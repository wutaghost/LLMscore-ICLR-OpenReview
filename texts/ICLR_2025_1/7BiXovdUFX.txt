009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is essential for pinpointing the root causes of fail-
012            ures in microservice systems. Traditional data-driven RCA methods are typically
013            limited to offline applications due to high computational demands, and existing
014            online RCA methods handle only single-modal data, overlooking complex interac-
015            tions in multi-modal systems. In this paper, we introduce OCEAN, a novel online
016            multi-modal causal structure learning method for root cause localization. OCEAN
017            employs a dilated convolutional neural network to capture long-term temporal de-
018
               pendencies and graph neural networks to learn causal relationships among system
               entities and key performance indicators. We further design a multi-factor atten-
019
               tion mechanism to analyze and reassess the relationships among different metrics
020
               and log indicators/attributes for enhanced online causal graph learning. Addition-
021            ally, a contrastive mutual information maximization-based graph fusion module
022            is developed to effectively model the relationships across various modalities. Ex-
023            tensive experiments on three real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and
024            efficiency of our proposed method.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is crucial for identifying the underlying causes of system failures and
029
      ensuring the high performance of microservice systems (Wang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2021; Wang
030   et al., 2023c). Traditional manual root cause analysis is labor-intensive, costly, and error-prone, given
031   the complexity of microservice systems and the extensive volume of data involved. Consequently,
032   effective and efficient root cause analysis methods are vital for pinpointing failures in complex
033   microservice systems and mitigating potential financial losses when system faults occur.
034
      Previous studies in data-driven RCA, particularly those utilizing causal discovery techniques, have
035
      primarily focused on constructing causal or dependency graphs (Ikram et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2017;
036   Li et al., 2021; Soldani & Brogi, 2022; Wang et al., 2023c; Zheng et al., 2024a). These graphs depict
037   the causal links between different system entities and key performance indicators (KPIs), thereby
038   enabling the tracing of underlying causes through these structures. For instance, Wang et al. (Wang
039   et al., 2023c) developed a hierarchical graph neural network method that automatically identifies
040   causal relationships both within and between networks to help pinpoint root causes.
041
      Despite significant advances, most of these approaches are designed for offline use and face challenges
042   with real-time implementation in microservice systems due to high computational demands. To
043   address this, Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2023a) introduced an online RCA method that decouples
044   state-invariant and state-dependent information and incrementally updates the causal graph. Li et
045   al. (Li et al., 2022) developed a causal Bayesian network that leverages system architecture knowledge
046   to mitigate potential biases toward new data. However, these online RCA methods are limited to
047   handling single-modal data.
048   Recently, multi-modal data, such as system metrics and logs, are commonly collected from microser-
049   vice systems, revealing the complex nature of system failures (Zheng et al., 2024a). For instance,
050   failures such as “Database Query Failures” might be overlooked if only system metrics are considered,
051   whereas issues like “Disk Space Full” are more effectively identified through combined analysis
052   of metrics and logs. This underscores the importance of using multi-modal data for a thorough
053   understanding of system failures. By integrating information from various sources, we can detect the
      abnormal patterns of system failures that might not be evident when analyzing single-modal data.

054
      To bridge this gap, this paper aims to propose an online multi-modal causal structure learning method
055   for identifying root causes in microservice systems. Formally, given the system KPI data along with
056   multi-modal microservice data including metrics and log data, our objective is to develop an online
057   multi-modal causal graph that identifies the top k system entities most relevant to the system KPI.
058   Three major challenges exist in this task. (C1) Capturing Long-term Temporal Dependencies:
059   Current auto-regressive based RCA methods (Wang et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024a) are limited to
060   capturing short-term temporal dependencies. However, some system faults, such as Distributed Denial
061   of Service (DDoS) attacks, may persist for extended periods. Effectively capturing these long-term
062   temporal dependencies is crucial for identifying various types of system faults. (C2) Capturing the
063
      Correlation of Multi-dimensional Factors: Existing RCA approaches (Ikram et al., 2022; Wang
      et al., 2023c; Zheng et al., 2024a) often analyze abnormal patterns from multiple factors individually,
064
      such as CPU usage or memory usage from system metrics and frequency or golden signal from system
065
      logs, overlooking potential relationships among these factors from both modalities. Furthermore,
066   these methods often consider all factors as equally important; however, in real applications, certain
067   factors prove to be considerably more crucial than others. It is vital, therefore, to reassess the
068   contributions of each factor to the learning of causal structures. (C3) Learning Multi-modal Causal
069   Structures: Effectively capturing the relationships between different modalities in an online setting
070   is crucial. Simply combining causal graphs from individual modalities can be problematic, especially
071   if one modality is of lower quality.
072   To tackle these challenges, we introduce OCEAN, Online Multi-modal Causal Structure LEArNing,
073   for root cause identification in microservice systems. Specifically, we propose to encode long-term
074   temporal dependencies using a dilated convolutional neural network (Yu & Koltun, 2016) and forecast
075   future values based on the p-th lagged historical data. We further develop a multi-factor attention
076   mechanism to analyze the correlations among various factors and reassess their importance for causal
077   graph learning. Additionally, we propose a contrastive mutual information estimation technique to
078   model the relationships of different modalities. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
079
080          • We introduce a novel online framework for multi-modality root cause analysis.
081          • We propose employing a dilated convolutional neural network to capture long-term temporal
082            dependencies and graph neural networks to model causal relations among system entities.
083          • We design a multi-factor attention mechanism to analyze the relationships among different
084            factors and reassess their impact on online causal graph learning.
085
             • We develop graph fusion techniques with contrastive multi-modal learning to model the
086            relationships between different modalities and assess their importance.
087
088
             • Extensive experiments on three real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and effi-
               ciency of our proposed method.

091   2   P RELIMINARY AND R ELATED W ORK
092
093   Key Performance Indicator (KPI). In a microservice system, KPIs serve as invaluable metrics
094   for assessing the effectiveness and productivity of the architecture (Podgórski, 2015). They play an
095   indispensable role in monitoring and managing different aspects of microservices to uphold optimal
096   performance levels. Common KPIs encompass latency and service response time. High values in
097   these metrics typically indicate suboptimal system performance or potential system failure.
098   Entity Metrics. Entity metrics are the measurable time-series attributes that provide insights
099   into the performance and status of services within a system (Bogner et al., 2017). These entities
100   encompass various components such as physical machines, containers, virtual machines, and pods.
101   In microservice architectures, typical entity metrics include CPU utilization, memory usage, disk I/O
102   activity, packet transmission rate, and etc. These metrics are extensively employed to detect anomalous
103   behavior and pinpoint potential causes of system failures in microservice environments (Wang et al.,
104
      2023a;b; Zheng et al., 2024a; Soldani & Brogi, 2023; Liu et al., 2021).
105   Root Cause Analysis. Current root cause analysis (RCA) methods can be categorized into two
106   main branches: single-modal RCA methods and multi-modal RCA methods. Single-modal RCA
107   methods primarily investigate causal relationships among system components using one type of data
      only (Sporleder et al., 2019; Duan et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2020; Soldani & Brogi, 2022; Aggarwal

134

      incidents. Similarly, Shan et al. (Shan et al., 2024) propose an approach that first identifies log
137   messages indicating configuration-related errors, then localizes suspected root-cause configuration
138   properties based on these log messages and LLM-generated configuration settings. While LLMs
139   could effectively learn temporal dependencies within the metric data of individual system entities, they
140   often struggle to capture interdependencies—such as causal relationships between different system
141   entities—leading to higher computational costs. Unlike existing RCA methods, this paper addresses
142   the online multi-modal RCA problem by uniquely modeling long-term temporal dependencies while
143   simultaneously capturing the cross-modal correlation of multiple factors.

148

      learning, multi-factor attention mechanism, and contrastive multi-modal learning. Subsequently, we
150   identify potential root causes through the network propagation-based root cause identification module
151   and establish stopping criteria. The overview of the proposed OCEAN is provided in Figure 1.

344   Optimization. The final objective function is written as:
345                          Lsparse = ||∆AL ||1 + ||∆AM ||1
346                                 L = −LM I + λ1 Ltemporal + λ2 Lsparse + λ3 h(A)                   (18)
347   where || · ||1 is the sparsity constraint imposed on the adjacency matrix and Lsparse aims to ensure
348   that the changes of the edges are expected to be sparse. The trace exponential function h(A) =
349   (tr(eA⊙A ) − n) = 0 holds if and only if A is acyclic (Pamfil et al., 2020), where ⊙ denotes the
350   Hadamard product of two matrices. λ1 , λ2 and λ3 are the positive constant hyper-parameters.

352   3.5   N ETWORK P ROPAGATION - BASED ROOT C AUSE I DENTIFICATION
353
354
      The propagation of malfunction effects from the root cause to adjacent entities implies that the
      immediate neighbors of system KPIs may not necessarily be the root causes themselves. To identify
355
      the root cause, we initially derive the transition probability matrix based on the causal graph G and
356
      then utilize a random walk with restart method (Tong et al., 2006) to simulate the spread patterns of
357   malfunctions as follows:
358                                                        βAj,i
                                                 Pij = Pn                                                (19)
                                                           k=1 Ak,i
359
360   The transition probability matrix P is the normalized adjacency matrix signified by the coefficient
361   β ∈ [0, 1]. During the visiting exploration process, we may restart from the KPI node to revisit other
362   system entities with the probability c ∈ [0, 1]. The equation for the random walk with restart is
363   formulated by:
364                                           rt+1 = (1 − c)P rt + cr0                                   (20)
365   where rt represents the jumping probability at the tth step, r0 denotes the initial starting probability,
366   and c ∈ [0, 1] stands for the restart probability. Upon convergence of the jumping probability rt , the
367   probability scores of the nodes are employed to rank the system entities and the top k entities are
368
      selected as the most probable root causes for system failure.
369   Stopping Criterion. As the number of new data batches increases, the identified causal structure
370   and its associated root cause list may gradually converge. To prevent unnecessary consumption of
371   computing resources, we employ them as indicators for automatic termination of the online RCA
372   process. We use the rank-biased overlap metric (RBO) (Webber et al., 2010) to measure the similarity
373   between two root cause lists, effectively capturing the evolving trend of root cause rankings. Given
374
      the rank lists from the previous and current batches, denoted as Rt−1 and Rt respectively, we quantify
      the similarity between these lists as follows:
375
376                                             γ = RBO(Rt−1 , Rt )                                    (21)
377   where γ ∈ [0, 1]. A higher value of γ indicates a greater similarity between the two root cause lists.
      The online RCA process is terminated when the similarity score s surpasses a predefined threshold.

384   4.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
385
386   Datasets. We evaluate the performance of OCEAN using three public real-world datasets: (1)
387   Product Review* (Zheng et al., 2024b): A microservice system dedicated to online product reviews,
388   encompassing 234 pods deployed across 6 cloud servers. It recorded four system faults between May
389   2021 and December 2021. (2) Online Boutique† (Yu et al., 2023): A microservice system designed
390
      for e-commerce, including five system faults. (3) Train Ticket (Yu et al., 2023): A microservice
      system for railway ticketing services, also with five system faults. All three datasets contain two
391
      modalities: system metrics and system logs.
392
393   Evaluation Metrics. We choose four widely-used metrics (Wang et al., 2023c; Meng et al., 2020):
394   (1) Precision@K (PR@K): Measures the accuracy of the top-K predicted root causes. (2) Mean
395   Average Precision@K (MAP@K): Evaluates the overall accuracy of the top-K predicted causes. (3)
396
      Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR): Assesses the ranking capability of the models. (4) Time: Measures
      the training time (in seconds) for each batch of data. Details on the first three metrics are provided in
397
      Appendix D and we provide the time complexity analysis in Appendix B.
398
399   Baselines. We compare OCEAN with seven causal discovery based RCA methods: (1) PC (Burr,
400   2003): A classic constraint-based algorithm that identifies the causal graph’s skeleton using an
401   independence test. (2) Dynotears (Pamfil et al., 2020): Constructs dynamic Bayesian networks
402
      through vector autoregression models. (3) C-LSTM (Tank et al., 2022): Utilizes LSTM to model
      temporal dependencies and capture nonlinear Granger causality. (4) GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020):
403
      Relaxes the hard Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) constraint in NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018) with a
404
      scoring function. (5) REASON (Wang et al., 2023c): Learns both intra-level and inter-level causal
405   relationships in interdependent networks. (6) MULAN (Zheng et al., 2024a): A multi-modal method
406   that captures both modality-invariant and modality-specific representations. (7) CORAL (Wang
407   et al., 2023a): A VAR-based online RCA method that decouples state-invariant and state-dependent
408   information.
409
      The first four baseline models were originally designed to learn causal structures solely from time
410
      series data. As outlined in (Wang et al., 2023c;a), these causal discovery models can be extended
411   to identify the root cause nodes. In this process, we first apply causal discovery models to learn the
412   causal graphs, then utilize random walk with restarts (Wang et al., 2023a) on these graphs to identify
413   the top K nodes as root causes.

530
531
      In this paper, we investigate the challenging problem of online multi-modal root cause localization in
532
      microservice systems. We introduce OCEAN, a novel online causal structure learning framework
533
      designed to effectively identify root causes using diverse data sources. OCEAN utilizes a dilated
534   convolutional neural network to capture long-term temporal dependencies and employs graph neural
535   networks to establish causal relationships among system entities and key performance indicators.
536   Additionally, we develop a multi-factor attention mechanism to evaluate and refine the contributions
537   of various factors to the causal graph. Furthermore, OCEAN incorporates a contrastive mutual
538   information maximization-based graph fusion module to enhance interactions between different
539   modalities and optimize their mutual information. The effectiveness of OCEAN is validated through
      extensive experiments on three real-world datasets, demonstrating its robustness and efficiency.

      A     M ORE R ATIONAL B EHIND THE M ODEL D ESIGN
757
758
759   The main issue of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Transformers is their high computational
760
      cost, which makes them impractical for online root cause analysis. We chose Dilated Convolutional
      Neural Networks (DCNNs) for their efficiency and ability to capture long-range dependencies, both
761
      of which are essential in online settings where computational efficiency is critical. Unlike RNNs and
762
      Transformers, which could capture temporal dependencies but often come with high computational
763   overhead, DCNNs are parallelizable and offer a lower time complexity of O(N T kC), where T is
764   the sequence length, L is the number of layers, C is the number of filters, and k is the filter size.
765   In contrast, the complexity of the Transformer is O(LT 2 d), where T is the sequence length, L is
766   the number of layers, and d is the hidden feature dimensionality. As a result, Transformers are
767   computationally more expensive than dilated convolutional neural networks. We further demonstrate
768   the efficiency and effectiveness of our method compared to LSTM and Transformers in the ablation
769   study presented in Table 6.
770   The rational behind the multi-factor attention mechanism is to capture complex relationships among
771   various factors (e.g., CPU usage and memory usage metrics in a microservice system) across two
772   modalities. A common limitation of existing methods is their focus on correlations between modalities,
773   often overlooking the importance of individual factors within each modality. Our mechanism
774   addresses this by reassessing the significance of each factor, enhancing causal structure learning. This
775   enables our model to dynamically adjust to the changing significance of factors, which is crucial for
776   accurate root cause analysis.
777   We integrate contrastive learning into our model because of its proven effectiveness in multi-modal
778   tasks. It extracts shared information across different modalities, enhancing robustness and addressing
779   challenges posed by low-quality data. By assigning weights to each modality based on its importance,
780   contrastive learning ensures that critical patterns are not obscured by noise. This approach helps
781   maintain the quality of causal graph learning, even when dealing with data of varying quality.
782
783
784   B    T IME C OMPLEXITY
785
786   The time complexity of dilated convolution based causal structure learning is O(N T kC). Here, T
787   is the sequence length, L is the number of layers, C is the number of filters, and k is the filter size.
788   The time complexity of multi-factor attention module is O(dM dL d), where d is the hidden feature
789   dimensionality, and dM and dL are the number of factors in two modalities. The time complexity of
790   contrastive learning module is O(n2 d), where n is the number of system entities.

793   C    L OG F EATURE /I NDICATOR E XTRACTION
794
795   In this subsection, we provide the details of converting the raw data into the time series, though this
796   is not within the scope of this work. Specifically, we first use the Drain parser He et al. (2017) to
797   transform the unstructured log event into structured log templates for each entity. Then, we partition
798   the log data with fixed time windows, such as 5 minutes, and set time steps at 10 seconds. Within
799   these intervals, we count the occurrence of each log template to derive the log frequency feature. The
800   extraction of the log frequency feature is inspired by the insight that the recurrence of a log event
801
      template often correlates with its significance. For instance, when a microservice system experiences
      Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks, the system will produce an unusual volume of system
802
      logs, indicating abnormal activity. Thus, the log frequency provides the information to identify
803
      unusual patterns indicative of potential failure scenarios. In addition to log frequency, we also extract
804   a second type of log feature known as the ‘golden signal.’ Notice that different from log frequency,
805   golden signal heavily relies on domain knowledge and it only focuses on the abnormal system logs.
806   More specifically, we are only interested in some keywords, including ‘error,’ ‘exception,’ ‘critical,’
807   ‘fatal’, and various others indicative of system anomalies. By identifying these keywords within log
808   event templates, we can discern abnormal occurrences for system failure localization. Similar to
809   the frequency-based feature, we compute the number of abnormal log events to derive the golden
      signal-based feature.
