011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In-context learning (ICL) achieves remarkable performance in various domains
014            such as knowledge acquisition, commonsense reasoning, and semantic under-
015            standing. However, its effectiveness deteriorates significantly in emotion detec-
016
               tion tasks, particularly in fine-grained emotion recognition. The reasons behind
               this decline still remain unclear. In this paper, we explore the underlying reasons
017
               of ICL’s suboptimal performance through the lens of prototype theory. Our in-
018
               vestigation reveals that ICL aligns with the principles of prototype theory when
019            applied to fine-grained emotion recognition tasks. According to prototype theory,
020            effective emotion recognition requires: Referencing well-represented emotional
021            prototypes that are similar to the query emotions, and making predictions based
022            on the closest emotional similarity. Building on this insight, ICL has three main
023            shortcomings: (i) It uses oversimplified single-emotion labels for prototypes, lead-
024            ing to inaccurate emotion representation. (ii) It references semantically similar but
025            emotionally distant prototypes. (iii) It considers all emotion categories as candi-
026            dates, leading to interference from irrelevant emotions and inaccurate predictions.
027            To address these shortcomings, we propose an Emotion Context Learning method
028            (E-ICL) for fine-grained emotion recognition. E-ICL first employs a dynamic
029
               soft-label strategy to create multi-dimensional emotional labels for accurate pro-
               totype representation. It then selects emotionally similar prototypes as refer-
030
               ences for emotion prediction. Finally, it uses an emotion exclusion strategy to
031
               eliminate interference from dissimilar emotions by selecting similar emotions as
032            candidates, resulting in more robust and accurate predictions. Note that our ap-
033            proach is implemented with the aid of a plug-and-play emotion auxiliary model,
034            requiring no additional training. Extensive experiments conducted on fine-grained
035            emotion datasets—EDOS, Empathetic-Dialogues, EmpatheticIntent, and GoE-
036            motions—demonstrate that E-ICL significantly outperforms existing methods in
037            emotion prediction performance. Moreover, even when the emotion auxiliary
038            model accounts for less than 10% of the LLMs’ capacity, E-ICL consistently
039            boosts LLM performance by over 4% across multiple datasets.

098
099         (i) Emotion Representation of Prototypes. ICL tends to use single emotion labels to repre-
100             sent the emotions of demonstrations (i.e., prototypes), which oversimplifies the complexity
101             of emotional states. For example, labeling the demonstration “This news makes me excited
102             and anticipatory” merely as “excited” fails to capture the full range of emotions expressed.
103        (ii) Prototype Selection. ICL often selects prototypes based on semantic rather than emotional
104             similarity, resulting in prototypes with limited emotional relevance. For example, the pro-
105             totype “This news makes me anticipatory” provides little emotional insight for the query
106
                “This news makes me sad,” even if they share semantic similarities.
107       (iii) Emotion Prediction. According to prototype theory, robust and nuanced emotion pre-
                diction requires focusing on emotions most similar to the prototype while eliminating in-

               terference from dissimilar emotion types. In contrast, ICL treats all emotion categories
109            as potential candidates, making it difficult to exclude irrelevant emotions, which leads to
110            unstable and imprecise predictions.
111
112   To address the above limitations, we propose Emotional In-Context Learning (E-ICL) for fine-
113   grained emotion recognition. E-ICL follows the three key steps of prototype theory to enhance
114   emotion prediction: First, it applies a dynamic soft-labeling strategy to assign multiple emotion
115   categories to demonstrations, constructing accurate emotional prototypes. Second, E-ICL choses
116
      emotionally similar examples as reference prototypes, rather than relying on semantic similarity.
      Finally, E-ICL employs an exclusion-based prediction strategy. It eliminates the interference of
117
      dissimilar emotions to prototypes, then guides LLMs to consider more similar emotions when pre-
118
      dicting the query’s emotion. Importantly, E-ICL achieves this through a plug-and-play emotion-
119   capable auxiliary model, requiring no additional training. This design significantly enhances the
120   method’s flexibility and applicability.

      We conduct extensive experiments on four fine-grained emotion recognition datasets: EDOS (We-
      livita et al., 2021), Empathetic-Dialogues (ED) (Rashkin et al., 2019), EmpatheticIntent (EI) (We-
123
      livita & Pu, 2020), and GoEmotions (GE) (Demszky et al., 2020). The experimental results demon-
124
      strate that compared to ICL, E-ICL guides LLMs to perceive fine-grained emotions more accurately
125   with the assistance of different emotion-capable auxiliary models. Furthermore, more analyses show
126   that E-ICL exhibits stable performance across different auxiliary models and LLMs. Notably, even
127   when the performance of the auxiliary model is 10% lower than that of LLMs, the proposed method
128   still enhances LLMs with a 4% higher performance than ICL on multiple datasets, indicating its
129   stable advantage.

131   cover that In-Context Learning (ICL) aligns with prototype theory. This insight us to identify ICL’s
132   limitations in fine-grained emotion recognition tasks and propose E-ICL as a solution. (ii) We im-
133   prove ICL’s demonstration construction by developing strategies for retrieving emotionally similar
134   examples and constructing dynamic soft labels, offering a new approach to demonstration construc-
135   tion. (iii) We introduce an exclusive emotion prediction strategy, enhancing the robustness and
136   accuracy of emotion recognition. (iv) Experiments show that E-ICL exhibits an stable advantage in
137   fine-grained emotion recognition across multiple datasets.

139   2   R ELATED W ORK
140
141   Fine-grained Emotion Recognition. The goal of the fine-grained emotion recognition task (FER)
142   is to detect subtle emotion categories in the query (Liew & Turtle, 2016; Abdul-Mageed & Ungar,
143   2017). As emotions are primarily influenced by situational and cognitive factors (Gross et al., 2014;
144   Siemer et al., 2007; Moors et al., 2013), existing works have mainly explored these two aspects and
145   can be divided into situation-based models and cognition-based models. Situation-based Models
146   mainly detect the subtle emotions implied in the query, without considering additional cognition
147
      information. These Models have explored word-level emotions (Li et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021b;
      Yang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024), mixed emotions (Majumder et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2019), and
148
      sentence-level emotions (Xie et al., 2019; Majumder et al., 2019; Ghosal et al., 2019). Cognition-
149
      based models mainly enhance emotions through additional cognitive factors. These models have
150   explored aspects such as emotion causes (Gao et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021a), and commonsense
151   knowledge (Sabour et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024a;b). Both types of models have
152   played an important role in the FER. However, these models are trained on specific datasets, lim-
153   ited by the corresponding data, and require certain computational resources and training time. In
154   contrast, we explore the FER task through In-Context Learning, without consuming computational
155   resources and training time.
156   In-Context Learning. In-Context Learning (ICL) improves LLMs’ performance by learning from
157   constructed demonstrations, circumventing the time and computational costs associated with fine-
158   tuning. One part of ICL enhances LLMs by breaking down the reasoning steps of demonstrations
159   into sub-steps and enabling LLMs to complete tasks by following these sub-steps (Wei et al., 2022;
160   Hendrycks et al., 2021; Kazemi et al., 2022). This type of ICL has demonstrated satisfactory results
161   in tasks such as arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning (Rae et al., 2021). However,
      these methods involve a high cost of manual construction, and for some tasks, the objectives cannot


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      be directly decomposed into sub-process problems. Another part of ICL, i.e., retrieval-based ICL,
163   mitigates this shortcoming by retrieving relevant demonstrations from training datasets. Retrieval-
164   based ICL primarily retrieves demonstrations that are similar to the query in terms of words (Rubin
165   et al., 2021; Agrawal et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), semantics (Li & Qiu, 2023; Liu et al., 2021;
166   Yang et al., 2023a; Xiao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021), structures (Levy et al., 2022), or other relevant
167   aspects (Fu et al., 2022; Gonen et al., 2022; Drozdov et al., 2022). Most of these methods rely
168   on the semantics between the query and the demonstrations. Owing to the potential for semantic
169   similarity to result in emotion misunderstanding issues, we propose an emotion-similarity-based
170   retrieval approach and integrate it with an exclusionary emotion prediction mechanism to facilitate
171
      more accurate emotion prediction.

176   query Q, the objective is to construct an effective prompt that guides a large language model (LLM)

      Overview. Our proposed E-ICL is an in-context learning method that constructs and references
188
      emotionally accurate prototypes (i.e., demonstrations) for exclusionary emotion prediction, assisted
189   by an auxiliary model. As shown in Figure 2, E-ICL consists of the following three steps: (i) Pro-
190   totype Construction (Section 4.1). E-ICL employs a dynamic soft-label construction strategy to
191   build prototypes (demonstrations) with accurate emotional representations. (ii) Prototype Selec-
192   tion (Section 4.2). It utilizes an emotion-similar example retrieval strategy to select prototypes that
193   are emotionally closer to the query as references. (iii) Emotion Prediction (Section 4.3). It cat-
194   egorizes the query’s emotions into those similar and dissimilar to the prototypes. It then prompts
195   LLMs to prioritize similar emotions while excluding the interference of dissimilar emotions, thereby
196   accurately predicting the emotion. Notably, the entire steps are facilitated by an emotion auxiliary
197
      model without requiring model training, thus enabling efficient emotion prediction while minimiz-
      ing computational resources and time demands.

286   Candidate Emotion Division. Previous studies (Yang et al., 2023a; Xiao et al., 2023) attempt to
287   predict emotions directly from a large set of categories. These approaches makes the choices made
288   by LLMs abrupt, often resulting in suboptimal predictions. In contrast, when humans face complex
289   choices, they tend to first eliminate unlikely options and then carefully consider the remaining possi-
290   bilities (Tversky & Shafir, 1992; Dhar, 1997; Shafir et al., 1993; Payne et al., 1993). Inspired by this
291   human decision-making process, we adopt an exclusionary emotion prediction strategy to enhance
292
      emotion prediction.
293   Our strategy begins by dividing the emotion categories into “possible” and “impossible” sets. To
294   achieve this, we apply the emotion auxiliary model to predict the query’s emotions. We then select
295   the top k3 emotions with the highest probabilities and consider them as possible emotions, which
296   we place in the set Spos . The remaining emotions are considered as impossible emotions and are
297   placed in the set Simp , so we have:

306   prototype, prioritizing emotions from the possible emotion set Spos before considering other emo-
307   tions for prediction. The emotions in this set are similar to those expressed in the query, while the
308   latter are similar to the prototype emotions. Indirectly, the emotions in the possible emotion set

423

425

      Impact of Dynamic Soft Label Quantity. We assess the impact of varying the number of dynamic

508   7   D ISCUSSION
509
510   Conclusion. This paper revealed that In-context learning (ICL) aligns with prototype theory for fine-
511   grained emotion recognition. Based on prototype theory, we proposed Emotion In-Context Learn-
512   ing (E-ICL), which improved ICL using three steps: E-ICL first employs dynamic soft labeling to
513   construct prototypes with more accurate emotion representations. It then retrieves examples with
514   emotions similar to the query as reference prototypes. Finally, E-ICL utilizes an exclusionary emo-
515   tion prediction strategy to eliminate interference from emotion types dissimilar to the prototypes,
516   identifying the most similar emotions as candidates for predicting the query emotion. Experimental
517   results and analysis have demonstrated that E-ICL achieves significant advantages on fine-grained
518
      recognition without requiring additional computational resources and training time.
519   Limitations. This paper has the following limitations: (i) Based on prior research (Xu et al., 2024;
520   Liu et al., 2021), ICL is likely to conform to prototype theory across a broader range of tasks as well.
521   (ii) While semantically similar example-label pairs to the query may not be the optimal choice for a
522   wider range of tasks, we did not explore this aspect in our study. (iii) The exclusionary prediction
523   strategy benefits ICL’s accurate and robust judgments by avoiding interference from irrelevant cat-
524
      egories, and it is likely applicable to multi-classification tasks. However, due to resource and time
      constraints, we do not further explore these limitations in this paper.
525
526   Future Work. In the future, we will investigate the following: (i) Explore whether ICL conforms
527   to prototype theory in more tasks; (ii) Explore better methods for constructing example-label pairs;
528   (iii) Study the applicability of the exclusionary prediction strategy in more tasks.

779   on the respective EDOS, ED, and EI datasets, it will introduce more noise, which is beneficial for
780   robustness experiments. Next, we select different numbers of candidate emotions to validate the
781
      robustness of Claude-haiku and ChatGPT-turbo. The experimental results are shown in Figure 3.
      The x-axis represents the number of candidate emotions, and the y-axis represents the metric values.
782
      As shown in Figure 8, as the number of candidate emotions (and noise) increases, the metric values
783
      of Claude-haiku fluctuate significantly, while those of ChatGPT-turbo remain stable within a certain
784   range. This indicates that ChatGPT-turbo is more robust to the noise introduced by E-ICL.

      As shown in Tables 3 and 4, the emotion auxiliary models exhibit different performance across dif-
      ferent datasets. Ignoring these differences and directly analyzing the experiments would lead to un-
790
      reliable results. To investigate their impact, we divide the emotion auxiliary models into two groups:
791
      (a) those that significantly outperform LLMs, and (b) those that underperform LLMs. Specifically,
792   we find that when using RoBERTaEI      large on the EDOS and ED datasets, its performance is signif-
793   icantly better than Claude-haiku and ChatGPT-turbo, while on the GE dataset, it underperforms
794   compared to them. Therefore, we categorize the experiments based on RoBERTaEI           large and con-
795   ducted on the EDOS and ED datasets as the (a) group experiments, while the experiments on the ED
796   dataset are categorized as the (b) group experiments. Simultaneously, we adopt the same approach
797   to divide the experiments based on RoBERTaGE                           GE
                                                       large . Since RoBERTalarge does not significantly out-
798   perform LLMs on the EDOS, ED, and GE datasets, we categorize its experiments as the (b) group.
799   When conducting parameter analysis experiments, due to the performance differences between the
800   emotion auxiliary models and LLMs, the (a) group experiments and (b) group experiments exhibit
801   different characteristics. This division of experiments better shows the impact of the emotion auxil-
802   iary models.
