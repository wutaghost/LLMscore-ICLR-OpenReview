011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The Obstacle Avoiding Rectilinear Steiner Minimum Tree (OARSMT) problem,
014            which seeks the shortest interconnection of a given number of terminals in a recti-
015            linear plane while avoiding obstacles, is a critical task in integrated circuit design,
016
               network optimization, and robot path planning. Since OARSMT is NP-hard, exact
               algorithms scale poorly with the number of terminals, leading practical solvers to
017
               sacrifice accuracy for large problems. However, for smaller-scale environments,
018
               there is no justification for failing to discover the true shortest path. To address
019            this gap, we propose and study MazeNet, a deep learning-based method that learns
020            to solve the OARSMT from data. MazeNet reframes OARSMT as a maze-solving
021            task that can be addressed with a recurrent convolutional neural network (RCNN).
022            A key hallmark of MazeNet is its ability to generalize: we only need to train the
023            RCNN blocks on mazes with a small number of terminals; mazes with a larger
024            number of terminals can be solved simply by replicating the same pre-trained
025            blocks to create a larger network. Across a wide range of experiments, MazeNet
026            achieves perfect OARSMT-solving accuracy with substantially reduced runtime
027
               compared to classical exact algorithms, and its perfect accuracy ensures shorter
               path lengths compared to state-of-the-art approximation algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Maze-solving algorithms are a special case of the solutions of an Obstacle-Avoiding Rectilinear
      Steiner Minimum Tree (OARSMT), with important applications in fields such as integrated cir-
034
      cuit design (Kahng et al., 2022), routing networks (Dong et al., 2013), and multi-path planning for
035
      robotics (Choset et al., 2005) and (Zang et al., 2022). In these domains, even small improvements
036   in path lengths can unlock new operational capabilities and reduce costs. For instance, in Very
037   Large Scale Integration (VLSI) design, minimizing the overall length of wiring directly decreases
038   the power consumption, reduces signal congestion, and minimizes timing delays; all of which con-
039   tribute to enhanced system performance Bricaud (2002). In this case, a maze can be expressed as a
040   graph where all edges have uniform cost, and therefore OARMST directly translates to reducing the
041   number of edges that are required to connect all the target nodes, which are termed terminals.
042   The Rectilinear Steiner Minimum Tree (RSMT) problem aims to connect a given set of points using
043   only horizontal and vertical lines while minimizing the total connection length Hwang et al. (1992).
044   OARSMT extends this problem by adding obstacles that must be avoided by the path solutions.
045   RSMT is however NP-complete (Garey & Johnson, 1977), and the inclusion of obstacles further in-
046   creases the problem’s complexity. Therefore, an exhaustive graph approach (i.e., a search algorithm
047   that mimics the exact solution of the Traveling Salesman Problem (Gutin & Punnen, 2002)) will be
048   perfectly accurate, but will have poor runtime scaling with an increasing number of terminals. This
049   method involves permuting the terminals and using Dijkstra’s algorithm Cormen et al. (2009) to
050
      compute the shortest path between each pair of consecutive terminals. We will refer to this method
      as Dijkstra’s exhaustive throughout our work.
051
052   Approximation algorithms to solve OARMST, such as by Kou et al. (Kou et al., 1981) and Mehlhorn
053   (Mehlhorn, 1988), offer improved scaling. However, these methods are not guaranteed to be exact
      and can produce incorrect maze solutions. Figure 1 illustrates a maze where approximation methods

055   multiple paths having nearly equal lengths. We observe that even for this relatively small 11 × 11

058
      Recent advancements in machine learning have introduced innovative approaches to tackling the
      Obstacle-Avoiding Rectilinear Minimum Steiner Tree (OARMST) problem. Among these,(Xiao
059
      et al., 2023), a mixed neural-algorithmic framework, offers a novel solution but does not account
060
      for obstacles in its computations. Similarly, while the methods proposed by (Chen et al., 2022),
061   (Lin et al., 2023), (Huang & Young, 2011) ,and (Chu & Wong, 2008) address obstacle-avoiding
062   Steiner tree construction, they are tailored for VLSI layouts and are not readily applicable to maze-
063   like configurations. This constraints our comparison to explicitly graph approximation methods that
064   handle more generalized environments, such as mazes with very specific obstacle patterns.

086   Rather than approaching OARMST as a traditional graph problem which has limitations of scal-
087
      ability, accuracy and generalization, we instead view mazes as images, and solve them via image
      processing techniques. Our deep neural network method, which we call MazeNet, makes use of Re-
088
      current Convolutional Neural Networks (RCNNs), which have been previously used to solve mazes
089
      of only two terminals Schwarzschild et al. (2021), (Bansal et al., 2022). MazeNet is scalable and
090   extendable, and can be used to solve mazes of variable sizes and variable numbers of terminals to
091   be connected: in this paper, we present results for mazes of up to a size of 11 × 11 and up to eight
092   terminals. MazeNet is able to offer perfect empirical accuracy for these scenarios, despite being
093   trained on smaller mazes that have up to five terminals.
094
      Since RCNNs may not be able to recognize a correct solution and terminate, MazeNet incorpo-
095
      rates a search-based algorithm that recognizes a correct solution to the maze. Our method combines
096   the runtime efficiency of graph-based approximate algorithms with the accuracy of the graph-based
097   exhaustive algorithms. This work addresses the gap between probabilistically correct approxima-
098   tion methods and the demand for deterministic accuracy in small-scale environments. For problems
099   within the 11×11 regime, probabilistic methods may be effective, but they lack justification for fail-
100   ing to discover the true shortest path. MazeNet is designed to achieve deterministic accuracy com-
101   parable to exact graph-based methods while maintaining the runtime efficiency of approximation
102   algorithms.
103   The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we formally define the OARSMT problem and
104   present its computational complexity. In Section 3, we present MazeNet’s architecture and train-
105   ing process, highlighting its key algorithmic features. In Section 4, we present the performance
106   of MazeNet and its state-of-the-art alternatives with regards to their accuracies and runtimes, and
107   analyze their scalabilities. We conclude in Section 5 and discuss future work and extensions for
      MazeNet, situating its broader significance in regards to Deep Learning for complex problems.

      The OARSMT problem is formally defined as follows. Given a set of terminals T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tN }
111   in a 2D plane, where each terminal ti has coordinates (xi , yi ), and a set of rectangular obsta-
112   cles O = {O1 , O2 , . . . , Om }, with each obstacle Oj defined by its vertices (xj min , yj min ) and
113   (xj max , yj max ), the objective is to find a tree T ′ that connects all terminals in T using horizontal
114   and vertical line segments (rectilinear paths) while avoiding all obstacles in O, such that the total
115   length of the tree T ′ is minimized. Defining E(T ′ ) as the set of edges in the tree T ′ , and L(T ′ ) as
116   the total length of the tree, we have:
117

121   such that T ′ is a connected acyclic graph spanning all terminals in T , and for every edge e ∈ E(T ′ )
122   between two points (xi , yi ) and (xj , yj ), the path is rectilinear and not intersecting any Oj ∈ O.
123
      With an exhaustive method, all possible permutations of the T terminals are considered to evalu-
124   ate every possible sequence of connections. For each permutation, the shortest path is computed
125   between each sequential pair of terminals in the order specified by the permutation. The number
126   of such permutations is O(T !). Dijkstra’s algorithm is used to compute the shortest path for each
127   sequential pair, with a time complexity of O(V log V + E) (Cormen et al., 2009), where V is the
128   number of vertices and E is the number of edges. In a grid graph with V vertices and E ≈ V edges,
129   this simplifies to O(V log V ). Therefore, the total complexity of Dijkstra’s exhaustive method is
130   O(T ! × (V + 1) log V ).

      3     OVERVIEW OF M AZE N ET
163
164   We now present the proposed MazeNet method, starting with its Recurrent Convolutional Neural
165   Network (RCNN) building blocks in Section 3.1. In Section 3.2, we introduce the termination con-
166   dition module and present its algorithm in detail. In Section 3.3, we present the overall MazeNet
167   architecture and its training procedure. Finally, in Section 3.4, we describe an algorithmic paral-
168   lelization feature that is useful for MazeNet’s extension to very large mazes.

170   3.1   RCNN APPROACH
171
172   The building block of the MazeNet method is the RCNN, which has been widely applied to ob-
173   ject recognition tasks Liang & Hu (2015). RCNNs can processes images by applying convolution
174
      operations recurrently, focusing on learning scalable algorithms without any graph as an input. Sepa-
      rately, maze-inspired algorithms have long been employed to tackle the OARSMT problem Lin et al.
175
      (2018); yet there has been little connection between RCNN-based methods and graph-theoretic ap-
176
      proaches. Schwarzschild et al. (2021) and Bansal et al. (2022) were the first to bridge this gap by
177   using RCNNs to solve maze-related problems, mimicking traditional algorithmic processes. How-
178   ever, these problems were in domains where traditional methods are both fast and accurate, leaving
179   open the question of whether RCNNs can provide similar advantages for more complex graph-based
180   problems.
181
      Since RCNNs are used for supervised learning in our case, it is necessary to have pairs of training
182
      input and target data images. We start by creating graphs as described in Appendix A.1, using the
183   DFS algorithm followed by random wall removal and uniformly distributing N terminals across the
184   grid. We then generate the solution graph with Dijsktra’s exhaustive, a graph-based algorithm as
185   described in Section 2, followed by a transformation to images as detailed in Section 2.1 in order to
186   create the input/target image pairs in Figures 2b and 2c.
187
      The architecture and the progressive training algorithm of Bansal et al. (2022) were designed to
188
      connect two terminals in perfect mazes, i.e., where a unique solution connects any two nodes. This
189   architecture has three key stages: a projection module that processes the input, followed by a re-
190   current module (RB) that operates sequentially, and finally, a head module that produces the output
191   (Appendix A.2). At each step of the recurrent module, a skip connection is maintained from the in-
192   put, ensuring that the input information is preserved throughout the recurrence. A final head module
193   transforms the network’s output into a single-channel prediction. The width of the network, defined
194   by the number of channels, is a tunable hyperparameter which represents the number of filters of the
195   recurrent module. In this work, we use their progressive training algorithm and make modifications
196   to the architecture to adapt it to this new problem, as detailed in Sections 3.3 and 3.2
197   RCNNs offer step-by-step interpretability of a trained method’s operations, since the head module
198   can be applied after any iteration, making it possible to observe the intermediate stages of the solu-
199   tion. These stages can be visualized directly as image outputs for insights into the solution process,
200   as demonstrated in Figure 3. Here, after suppressing the white permissible paths of the maze in
201   the input image, exploratory paths originate from the terminals, which intensify and become stable
202   when connections are established. Note however that the maze is essentially solved by iteration 23
203
      from a simple visual examination, and one does not have to wait for the paths to become solidly
      white. This observation motivates the introduction of a termination condition which can automate
204
      the recognition of the maze being essentially solved, and to return this correct solution, as discussed
205
      next.

      3.2   T ERMINATION C ONDITION
217
218   The RCNN architecture of Bansal et al. (2022) lacks a termination condition within its recurrent
219   modules, so its number of iterations must be predefined; which can lead to premature termination
220   or to excessive runtimes. Another issue is that if there are two nearly equidistant paths, the output
221   frequently oscillates between them. This event is also problematic with the approximation algo-
222   rithms Kou (Kou et al., 1981) and Mehlhorn (Mehlhorn, 1988). However, during this oscillation the
223   correct solution is occasionally highlighted as in Figure: 4. We therefore introduce a Termination
224   Condition (TC) module, which reduces unnecessary iterations by halting the network’s operation
225   once the correct path is identified, and also achieves the maximum empirical accuracy. TC does
226
      incur a computational overhead, making its optimization crucial.
227   After a set number of iterations, the head module is followed by the TC module which performs a
228   guided search, assessing the “whiteness” of the intermediate output which is measure of the presence
229   of white pixels that represent the connected path. Starting at the upper leftmost terminal in the maze,
230   TC specifically evaluates the “whiteness” in a 2 × 2 neighboring cell grid, averaging the 0-1 value
231   of the 4 pixels at neighboring cell. If the whiteness exceeds an empirically determined threshold of
232
      0.65, TC will continue the exploration in any direction North, East, West, and South (NEWS) above
      the threshold.
233
234   When encountering a junction where multiple directions exceed the whiteness threshold, TC initi-
235   ates a recursive branch of exploration while keeping track of the main branch with a from-junction
236   variable. If a branch leads to a previously visited position, indicating a cycle, the TC module stops,
237   and the image undergoes additional iterations to ensure valid tree convergence. A last-move variable
238
      keeps track of the previous direction to avoid moving backwards during the exploration.TC main-
      tains a record of visited positions, to ensure that all the terminals are reached or to terminate if no
239
      valid exploration direction remains.

      Algorithm 1 enables the TC module to identify the correct path, halting MazeNet once it confirms

302   To train this architecture, we generate a dataset of mazes with 2, 3, or 4 terminals, aiming to teach
303   MazeNet to minimize the length when connecting a variable number of terminals. The exact method
304   is computationally feasible for creating multiple optimal labels for low terminal mazes, allowing us
305   to generate a large dataset for training. Refer to Section 4 for details on the training and testing data
306   sizes. We use the progressive training strategy of Bansal et al. (2022), as it has demonstrated RCNN
307   generalization capabilities when recurrence is applied. Progressive training encourages the model to
308
      incrementally refine its solutions from any given starting point. Specifically, we begin by inputting a
      problem instance and running the recurrent module for a random number of iterations, n. After this,
309
      we take the resulting intermediate output, reset the recurrence (discarding gradients from the initial
310
      steps), and then train the model to produce the final solution after an additional number of iterations,
311   k. Here, n is sampled uniformly from [0, m], and k is then defined after n is randomly selected to
312   fulfill the constraint n + k = m.
313
314
      The output of the network, which matches the dimensions of the targeted image in Figure 2c and is
      a single-channel binary 0-1 valued matrix, is compared pixel-by-pixel with the labeled target using
315
      a cross-entropy loss function. The gradient calculation for progressive loss and backpropagation
316
      follows the procedure detailed in Algorithm 1 of Bansal et al. (2022).
317
318   Progressive training improves the quality of the network’s incremental solutions, and by selecting
319   a random iteration as the starting point for the next training phase, it also prevents the network
320
      from developing iteration-specific behaviors. Instead, the network is encouraged to learn iteration-
      agnostic behaviors.
321

323   accuracy with each iteration. However, in the following ten epochs, the network exhibits diminish-
      ing returns (Appendix A.3).Importantly, we do not apply the termination condition during training,

349   3.4   PARALLELIZATION FOR SCALABILITY
350
      To address future scalability challenges, particularly in handling larger mazes that correspond to
351
      more complex graphs, we integrate multi-GPU parallelization to MazeNet directly at the algorith-
352   mic level. Unlike traditional multi-GPU approaches, where parallelization typically focuses on the
353   gradient descent process, we parallelize the algorithms of MazeNet itself. This allows us to distribute
354   the GPU convolution operations, while still maintaining the accuracy of the method.
355
      In our approach, we divide the input image into smaller, equally-sized sections with each section
356
      processed independently through the projection, recurrent, and head modules in parallel, followed
357
      by a recombination of the sections at the end of this process. We divide the image into 2, 3, 4,
358   5, 6, 7, or 8 sections, as illustrated in detail in Appendix A.4. These image sections overlap by
359   10 pixels, which is sufficient to ensure that 2D convolution edge effects are discarded before the
360   sections are combined, resulting in an output identical to what would be produced by processing
361   the entire image as a single unit. Figure 7 also shows a specific example where two image sections
362   are processed simultaneously through one iteration. The network is inherently parallelizable across
363   all nine convolutional layers of a single feedforward pass without compromising any of MazeNet’s
364   accuracy. However, splitting and merging during every convolution introduces significant overhead.
365   To minimize this overhead, we parallelize all the 9 convolution layers before merging the image
366
      sections.
367   For MazeNet’s 48 × 48 images, the overhead introduced by parallelizing computations on a single
368   GPU outweighs the performance gains due to the small image size. However, for larger synthetic
369   images, such as 1000 × 1000, parallelization provides significant runtime improvements. The logic
370   follows that convolution operations are inherently parallelizable without introducing errors. For
371   larger synthetic images, parallelization gains runtime improvements in each iteration by efficiently
372
      distributing the computational load across sections.

605   A.1   DATA G ENERATION
606
607   We begin with a grid graph where each node is connected to four neighbors—two vertical and two
608   horizontal—except at the boundaries. From this uniform structure, we generate a perfect maze
609   using the DFS algorithm, a widely recognized method for maze construction. While DFS is well-
610
      documented in the literature, we detail the process here for our specific method.
611   In this approach, the maze is represented as a grid of cells, each initially enclosed by four walls:
612   North, East, South, and West. The algorithm starts from an arbitrary cell and inspects its neighboring
613   cells. If any of the neighbors have not been visited, the algorithm randomly selects one, removes the
614   wall between the two cells, and moves to the unvisited cell. This process is repeated, continuously
615   extending the path. If the algorithm encounters a dead end, where all neighboring cells have already
616
      been visited, it backtracks to the last cell with unvisited neighbors and continues from there.
617   In our implementation, the DFS algorithm is managed by defining classes for both the individual
618   cells and the overall maze structure. We track the path through the grid using a stack, which is
619   implemented as a Python list. When the algorithm encounters a dead end, it backtracks by popping
620   cells off the stack until it finds a cell with unvisited neighbors, allowing the exploration to continue.
621   This process results in a perfect maze—one in which each cell is reachable through a single con-
622   nected path, and in which no cycles exist. To increase the complexity and to introduce cycles, we
623   further modify the maze by randomly removing walls. Priority is given to nodes that are dead ends,
624   i.e., cells with three remaining walls, in order to break up isolated paths and to add variability. This
625   introduces cycles into the maze, making it more complex and extending MazeNet to a wider class
626   of problems.
627   Finally, after constructing the maze, we uniformly select N terminals at random locations throughout
628   the grid. These terminals are the points that will be connected in our OARSMT solution as seen in
629   Figure 9. By introducing these random elements, we ensure a diverse and challenging dataset for
630   our purposes.

      A.2   D ETAILS OF M AZE N ET ’ S ARCHITECTURE
670
      As depicted in Figure 11, MazeNet’s architecture consists of nine convolutional layers. The input
671
      image, which is 3-channel and of arbitrary size (48 × 48 in our case), first passes through the pro-
672   jection module. This module is a single convolutional layer (Conv 0) that expands the input image
673   to a predefined number of width channels (set to 128 in this project) while maintaining the spatial
674   dimensions of 48 × 48. A ReLU activation follows this layer, introducing non-linearity and allowing
675   for more expressive feature extraction.
676
      The core of the architecture is the recurrent module (RB), which plays an essential role in the model’s
677
      ability to generalize. The recurrent module consists of five convolutional layers (Conv 1-5). The
678   first layer in the module takes a concatenation of the input channels and the width channels from the
679   previous iteration (input + width channels), ensuring that information is retained throughout the it-
680   erations. It outputs the same number of width channels (128 in this case). The remaining four layers
681   are identical convolutional layers, processing and outputting width channels without additional ac-
682   tivations. This recurrent structure is applied iteratively during the testing phase, allowing the model
683   to adapt and generalize across different terminals.
684   The final component is the head module, which is responsible for reducing the channel width and
685   producing the (0-1) output. It achieves this by progressively decreasing the number of channels
686   through three convolutional layers (Conv 6-8) with ReLU activations between them. The first layer
687   reduces the channels from 128 to 32, the second reduces it further from 32 to 8, and the final
688   convolution narrows the channels from 8 to 2. An Argmax function is applied over the two channels,
689   generating a binary prediction (0-1) for each pixel. For the final model in our architecture in Figure
690   5, we use the Batch modules as as detailed in Figure 12.
