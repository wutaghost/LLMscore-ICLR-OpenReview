011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine unlearning of deep generative model refers to the process of modifying
014            or updating a pre-trained generative model to forget or remove certain patterns
015            or information it has learned. Existing research on Bayesian-based unlearning
016            from various deep generative models has highlighted low efficiency as a significant
017            drawback due to two primary causes. Firstly, Bayesian methods often overlook
018            correlations between data to forget and data to remember, leading to conflicts during
019            gradient descent and much slower convergence. Additionally, they require aligning
020
               updated model parameters with the original ones to maintain the generation ability
               of the updated model, further reducing efficiency. To address these limitations,
021
               we propose an Efficient Bayesian-based Unlearning method for various deep
022
               generative models called EBU. By identifying the relevant weights pertaining to
023            the data to forget and the data to remember, EBU only preserves the parameters
024            related to data to remember, improving the efficiency. Additionally, EBU balances
025            the gradient descent directions of shared parameters to adeptly manage the conflicts
026            caused by the correlations between data to forget and data to remember, leading to
027            a more efficient unlearning process. Extensive experiments on multiple generative
028            models demonstrate the superiority of our proposed EBU.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, there have been significant advancements in deep generative models, showcasing their
035   ability to produce synthetic images of exceptional quality (Wei et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Nichol
036   & Dhariwal, 2021). These models typically rely on large volumes of training data to effectively
037   learn and generate high-quality outputs (Wang et al., 2022; Cai & Zhu, 2015). However, the use of
038   unauthorized data for training can lead to issues such as data misuse and privacy breaches (Li et al.,
039
      2021; Deepa et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023c), raising concerns about the potential for these models
      to generate misleading or inappropriate content (Heng & Soh, 2024; Fan et al., 2024). Consequently,
040
      there is an urgent need to develop methods for mitigating the influence of specific data on pre-trained
041
      generative models.
042
043   The machine unlearning concept (Bourtoule et al., 2021) is proposed to demonstrate the problem
044   that requires the trained machine learning models unlearn from specific data instances. Significant
045
      efforts have been made to advance the field of machine unlearning (Gupta et al., 2021; Sekhari et al.,
      2021; Nguyen et al., 2022).When it comes to the unlearning of multiple deep generative models, prior
046
      research has tackled this task by proposing Bayesian-based unlearning methods, albeit with certain
047
      inefficiency limitations (Chen et al., 2021; Deepanjali et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Heng &
048   Soh, 2024; Fan et al., 2024). We attribute the inefficiency of Bayesian-based machine unlearning
049   methods to two primary causes. Firstly, they neglect correlations between data to remember and data
050   to forget (Heng & Soh, 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Fan et al., 2024), leading to conflicts during gradient
051   descent and much slower convergence, thereby exacerbating inherent inefficiencies. Secondly,
052   Bayesian-based unlearning methods require aligning updated model parameters with the original ones
053   to maintain generative ability of updated model (Heng & Soh, 2024; Wang et al., 2023a), introducing
      additional inefficiencies, notably prolonging the unlearning process.

      Given the inefficiency limitations of existing Bayesian-based unlearning methods (Chen et al., 2021;
055   Deepanjali et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2024), we seek to enhance the efficiency of
056   Bayesian-based forgetting methods. We propose EBU to stress the two fold limitations of Bayesian-
057   based unlearning methods. Firstly, we selectively retain memory of parameters crucial for data to
058   remember (Sener & Koltun, 2018; Désidéri, 2012), while updating parameters associated with data to
059   forget, preventing the alignment of the entire model parameters, thereby improving efficiency and
060   accelerating the unlearning process. Moreover, considering the correlation between data to forget and
061   data to remember, EBU balances gradient updates on shared parameters associated with both types
062   of data. This balancing mitigates conflicting gradient descent directions, narrowing conflicts, and
063
      further enhancing the efficiency of the unlearning process. Notably, the proposed EBU substantially
      improves the efficiency of the unlearning process and lays the groundwork for more effective model
064
      adaptation in deep generative models.
065
066   We summary our main contributions in short as follows:
067
068          • We introduce EBU, a groundbreaking framework that dramatically enhances the efficiency
069            of machine unlearning in deep generative models by effectively resolving the conflicts
070            between forgetting and remembering processes. This innovation significantly improves both
071
               concept-wise and class-wise unlearning.
072          • EBU strategically preserves critical parameters tied to the data that must be remembered,
073            making the unlearning process in Bayesian-based models far more efficient and streamlined
074            than previous approaches.
075
076          • By incorporating a novel mechanism to balance gradient updates for forgetting and remem-
077
               bering, EBU accelerates the entire unlearning process, ensuring faster and more reliable
               performance.
078
079          • Extensive experiments across diverse datasets and generative models clearly demonstrate the
080            superior performance of EBU, proving its effectiveness and efficiency compared to existing
081            baseline methods.

084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Machine unlearning for generative model In recent years, the researchers have made efforts in
087   unlearning of generative model. Several works proposed to unlearning from GANs by utilizing the
088   discriminator (Kong & Chaudhuri, 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023), but these methods
089   can’t be applied to other generative models due to they only suit for paradigm of GANs. There are
090   some works realizing unlearning of generative model by modifying the weights (Bau et al., 2020;
091   Tarun et al., 2023), but it is still a challenge to accurately identify model weights associated to the
092   forgetting tasks accurately. Some researchers proposed Bayesian based unlearning methods that can
093
      be applied to various generative models (Heng & Soh, 2024; Nguyen et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024; Fu
      et al., 2022; 2021), but they need to trade off between forgetting the posterior distribution of data to
094
      forget and not entirely forgetting posterior distribution of the original training data to preserve the
095
      generative models’ ability, causing conflicts due to the correlations between the data to forget and the
096   original training data.
097
098   Difference between EBU and prior methods Our proposed EBU is a Bayesian based unlearning
099
      method and can be compatible with any generative models, but advanced with the existing Bayesian
      based unlearning methods (Heng & Soh, 2024; Nguyen et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2024), our method
100
      reduce the conflicts by balancing the gradient descent directions of the parameters shared by the
101
      forgetting and remembering processes. We identify these shared parameters by analyzing correspond-
102   ing weight saliency maps during the unlearning process. In contrast to previous methods that use
103   saliency maps to identify parameters for updates (Fan et al., 2024), our approach dynamically selects
104   parameters specifically related to forgetting and remembering during the fine-tuning process. This
105   dynamic selection allows us to guide the gradient descent directions for forgetting and remembering
106   separately, while keeping unrelated parameters unchanged. By preventing optimization conflicts
107   between the two tasks, our method not only enhances unlearning efficiency but also ensures more
      precise parameter updates, leading to superior performance.

114   is the number of categories in the dataset. Without accessing the training data, we generate forgetting

                                                          n=1 denotes the set of data to remember, with N r + Nf ≤ N .
117   In this context, Xfn and Yfn denote the data to forget and corresponding labels for category n. Xrn
118   and Yrn denote the data to remember and the corresponding labels for category n. Our goal is to
119   forget the assigned set Df from the pre-trained generative model Gθ while keeping the generation
120   quality of the remaining samples in Dr by fine-turning the pre-trained deep generative model. The
121   fine-tuned model Gθ∗ with parameters θ ∗ is expected to forget all samples in Df while retaining the
122   ability to generate samples conforming to the distribution pθ∗ (Dr ) ∼ Gθ∗ (Xr |Yr ) that is expected
123   to align with the distribution of the data to remember p(Dr ). For clarity and ease of representation,
124   all model parameters used in this paper are denoted as the set of their individual elements. For
125   instance, θ = {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θi , . . . , θk }, where k represents the total number of elements within θ and
126
      θi denotes the ith element of the model parameters.

128   3.2   M OTIVATION
129
130
      We are interested in forgetting specified samples from a pre-trained diffusion model. Prior work
      attempted to forget data from a model to ensure the privacy in machine learning models by deleting
131
      only specific shards (Bourtoule et al., 2021), thereby forgetting these assigned shards. Moreover,
132
      building on the ideas from (Heng & Soh, 2024; Nguyen et al., 2020), they implemented forgetting
133   of model by forgetting the posterior belief of data Df while not forgetting the posterior belief
134   given the full data D. As suggested by (Heng & Soh, 2024), using Elastic Weight Consolidation
135   (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) keeps the posterior belief of the full data pθ (D), preventing
136   catastrophic forgetting. However, this approach hinders the forgetting process by maintaining the
137   posterior belief of full data pθ (D). A more reasonable solution is to remember the posterior belief of
138   Dr while forgetting the posterior belief of Df . The weights for forgetting pθ (Df ) and remembering
139   pθ (Dr ) are denoted as θf and θr , respectively. Fast forgetting of Df can be achieved by keeping θr
140   consistent with its original values and leaving θf unchanged. Moreover, the overarching unlearning
141
      process necessitates a delicate trade-off between forgetting pθ (Df ) while retaining aspects of pθ (D).
      The parameters affected by this trade-off are those within θf ∩ θ = θf . When compared to the
142
      general unlearning approach, focusing solely on addressing conflicts within θr ∩ θf ⊆ θf results in
143
      a smaller negative impact on the unlearning process, thereby potentially accelerating it.

148   We introduce a novel unlearning method EBU aimed at expediting the forgetting process of pre-
149   trained deep generative models while preserving the quality of the generated images. We present a
150   comprehensive theoretical analysis to underpin our proposed EBU in Section 4.1, elucidating the
151   underlying rationale behind our method’s efficacy in resolving conflicts inherent in the unlearning
152
      process. In particular, EBU comprises two key components. First, we introduce a partial parameter
      alignment method in Section 4.2, which significantly enhances unlearning efficiency. Second, in
153
      Section 4.3, we propose an effective approach to mitigate optimization conflicts between remembering
154
      and forgetting, further accelerating the unlearning process.

207
      Existing works (Chen et al., 2021; Deepanjali et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022) for unlearning
208

209

210   a technique which only needs to partially align the parameters between the unlearned model and

215   In this section, we propose efficient ways to select the corresponding parameters θf and θr , thereby

261   4.3   O PTIMIZATION C ONFLICTS M ITIGATION
262
      Forgetting data Df from the generative model changes part of the parameters, while replaying data
263
      Dr to the model also changes part of parameters, we denote the two parts of parameters as θf and
264   θr respectively. However, data from different categories may exhibit similar patterns, potentially
265   influencing a shared set of model parameters, i.e., θf ∩ θr . As shown in Figure 1, the changed
266   parameters θf and θr partially overlap. When optimizing Lr and Lf simultaneously, the gradient
267   update directions for forgetting and replaying conflict, hindering the model’s ability to forget or
268   remember effectively and slowing convergence. To address this, a trade-off strategy is needed
269   to balance forgetting and remembering during optimization. In this section, we propose efficient
      methods to mitigate these conflicts and accelerate the unlearning process.

      A     R ELATED W ORK OF M ACHINE U NLEARNING
703
704
      Machine unlearning dedicates to enabling the removal of specific data from trained machine learning
705   models (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022), encompasses methods ranging from data
706   reorganization to direct model manipulation. While some approaches focus on preventing model
707   learning by reorganizing data or constructing fake data (Felps et al., 2020; Tarun et al., 2023; Zhang
708   et al., 2022; Cao & Yang, 2015), they often introduce extra information that impacts learning. Some
709   researchers tried to unlearn sample through manipulating the model directly (Baumhauer et al., 2022;
710   Golatkar et al., 2020; Sekhari et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024; 2023b), i.e., modifying weights to remove
711   sample information or adjusting model updates based on data statistics during training (Golatkar
712   et al., 2020; Sekhari et al., 2021). Notably, research primarily focuses on unlearning for classification
713
      models, with relatively less attention to generative models, which is still in its early stages.
714
715   B     E XPERIMENT S ETTING
716
717
718
719   B.1   T HE DESCRIPTION OF BASELINE METHODS
720
721   General unlearning methods: We choose Retraining, FT (Warnecke et al., 2021) and GA (Thudi
722   et al., 2022) as basic unlearning baseline methods. Retraining realizes unlearning by updating on
723   the training data with removal of data. FT builds on close-form updates of model parameters to
724
      unlearn the features and labels. GA limits the overall change in weights during SGD to facilitate the
      approximate unlearning.
725
726   Unlearning for generative models: We use two most recent unlearning methods for generative
727   models SA (Heng & Soh, 2024) and ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023). SA derives from continual
728   learning to selectively forget concepts in pretrained deep generative models. ESD erases a visual
729   concept from a pre-trained diffusion model, given only the name of the style and using negative
730
      guidance as a teacher model.
731
732   B.2   I MPLEMENT DETAILS
733
         Class-wise forgetting: Class-wise forgetting targets the removal of generations belonging to
734
      specified classes from deep generative models. For the DDPM, we fine-tune it for 200 gradient
735   update steps using a batch size of 32 and a learning rate of 1e-5. We conduct comparisons on two
736   datasets, CIFAR-10 and STL10, employing various methods. Regarding the SD model, we fine-tune
737   it for 1000 gradient update steps with a learning rate of 1e-5. We focus on unlearning classes from
738   Imagenette, which comprises ten easily identifiable classes from ImageNet. The detailed experiment
739   results can be found in Section 5.2.
740
      Concept-wise forgetting: The concept-wise unlearning of deep generative models aims to eliminate
741   generations containing specific concepts. We fine-tune the SD model using 1000 gradient descent
742   steps, with a batch size set to 1 and a learning rate of 1e-5. We consider two types of concepts: art
743   style and nudity. Detailed experiments and results can be found in Section 5.3.
744
745   B.3   T HE DETAILS OF FORGETTING FROM DDPM
746
747   Baseline implement. For the baseline methods, we implement them as recommend. Note that the
748   baseline methods FT, GA are proposed for classification model, but they can be adapted to generative
749   model easily. For FT, it only needs data to remember during the unlearning process, thus we change
750   the original loss function of FT as Lr . For GA, it only needs data to forget, thus we set the loss
751   function of GA as −Lf . And for Retraining, we just fine-tune the pre-trained model with Dr directly.
752
      For SA, we implement it as recommend.
753   Experiment settings In our experimental setup, we utilize a simple yet effective Residual model
754   architecture with 3 input and output channels, employing a channel size of 128 and 2 residual blocks.
755   Attention resolutions are set at 16, with dropout probability at 0.1. For diffusion, we implement a
      linear beta schedule spanning from 0.0001 to 0.02 over 1000 diffusion timesteps. During training, we

      employ a batch size of 32 for 20,0 iterations, with logging every 50 iterations and visualization of
757   100 samples. Optimization is conducted using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001
758   and a weight decay of 0.000, while gradients are clipped at a threshold of 1.0. These settings ensure
759   robust experimentation and reliable evaluation of our proposed methods.

763   Baseline implement We use two baseline methods ESD and SD. For SD, it needs to generate the
764   data to forget and data to remember, we use the random samples generated by SD as the data to forget
765   and we then use a empty prompt to generate the data to remember. For ESD, we implement it as
766   recommended.
767
      Experiment settings In our experiment setup, we utilize the Latent Diffusion model with specific
768   configurations tailored for unlearning tasks. The diffusion process spans 1000 timesteps, with
769   linear beta scheduling from 0.00085 to 0.012. We employ a UNet model architecture with attention
770   resolutions at [4, 2, 1] and two residual blocks. Training involves a base learning rate of 1.0e-05,
771   and we utilize a LambdaLinear scheduler with a warm-up period of 1 step. The model consists of a
772   first stage autoencoder with embedded dimensions of 4 and a conditional stage encoder. And all the
773   unlearning methods are trained with ’xattn’ part parameters while keeping other parts frozen.
