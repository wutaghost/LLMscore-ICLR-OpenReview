009                                                                           A BSTRACT
010
011              Medical time series (MedTS) classification is critical for a wide range of health-
012              care applications such as Alzheimer’s Disease diagnosis. However, its real-world
013              deployment is severely challenged by poor generalizability due to inter- and intra-
014              dataset heterogeneity in MedTS, including variations in channel configurations,
015              time series lengths, and diagnostic tasks. Here, we propose FORMED, a founda-
016              tion classification model that leverages a pre-trained backbone and tackles these
017              challenges through re-purposing. FORMED integrates the general representa-
018
                 tion learning enabled by the backbone foundation model and the medical do-
                 main knowledge gained on a curated cohort of MedTS datasets. FORMED can
019
                 adapt seamlessly to unseen MedTS datasets, regardless of the number of chan-
020
                 nels, sample lengths, or medical tasks. Experimental results show that, without
021              any task-specific adaptation, the repurposed FORMED achieves performance that
022              is competitive with, and often superior to, 11 baseline models trained specifically
023              for each dataset. Furthermore, FORMED can effectively adapt to entirely new,
024              unseen datasets, with lightweight parameter updates, consistently outperforming
025              baselines. Our results highlight FORMED as a versatile and scalable model for
026              a wide range of MedTS classification tasks, positioning it as a strong foundation
027              model for future research in MedTS analysis. Code release upon acceptance.

053   Medical time series (MedTS) classification, such as on electrocardiograms (ECG) and elec-
      troencephalograms (EEG), is critical for a wide range of medical scenarios such as diagnosing

054
      Alzheimer’s Disease (AD; Jeong (2004)), Parkinson’s Disease (PD; Aljalal et al. (2022b;a)), and
055   heart Arrhythmia (Jin et al., 2024b). Despite significant advancements in developing deep learning
056   models for MedTS classification, several challenges still hinder their ability to generalize effectively
057   across different datasets, and even among patients within the same dataset, posing a critical barrier
058   to the successful translation of predictive algorithms into real-world clinical settings.
059
      As MedTS data can be of various modalities and dimensions, in real-world scenarios, they often
060
      differ from the training data in all aspects. Therefore, developing machine learning models that can
061   generalize across these diverse datasets is essential for translating predictive algorithms into clini-
062   cal settings. However, there are three unique key challenges in MedTS that a generalizable model
063   will have to face: (1) Inter-dataset Heterogeneity: The explicit characteristics of each dataset are
064   diverse due to factors like the domain of physiological data, the equipment for data collection, etc.
065   They require the model to be able to handle variations in the number of channels, sample duration,
066   sampling rates, diagnostic targets, and so on (Ganapathy et al., 2018). (2) Intra-dataset Hetero-
067   geneity: Given the intractable nature of the underlying physiological state, even within the same
068   dataset, heterogeneity still exists across the time of recording, experimental session, and most sig-
069
      nificantly, among patients due to the presence of noise and artifacts (Wang et al., 2024c; Ganapathy
      et al., 2018). As they are prevailing in the data, models are prone to overfitting to the training data,
070
      and thus show poor generalizability in real-world deployment. (3) Data Insufficiency: The other
071
      challenges could have been solved provided with sufficient data for deep learning methods, while
072   in reality, available MedTS datasets are often small due to costly data collection or simply privacy
073   concerns (Kaushik et al., 2020). Consequently, it increases the difficulty of training robust models
074   that handle the above challenges effectively (Ganapathy et al., 2018).
075
      To this end, developing a foundation model for MedTS classification requires capturing and sharing
076
      medical domain knowledge across tasks. Previous attempt such as Yang et al. (2023) adopts a
077
      Task-Specific Adaptation (TSA; see Figure 1) approach, in hope of capturing such knowledge in
078   the backbone model. Yet the results of negative performance gain indicate that the model may
079   only focus on extracting features that are informative for the training task, not of interest to future
080   datasets, and thus lack generalization from task to task. Meanwhile, recent advances in foundation
081   models bring sunlight for overcoming the above challenges by learning generic representations of
082   time series data (Liang et al., 2024). However, they have focused predominantly on forecasting tasks
083   (Ye et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2022), and simply applying TSA to adapt them for MedTS is not enough
084   for capturing the sophisticated patterns for specific tasks from our preliminary results. Therefore,
085   although they can serve as a great backbone model for extracting patterns in time series, dedicated
086
      effort in adaptation design is still required.
087   In this paper, we propose a novel approach to re-purpose foundation models pre-trained on large-
088   scale, generic time series data for MedTS classification. We introduce FORMED, a Foundation
089   model Repurposed for Medical time series classification, which achieves generalizable adaptation
090   by seamlessly handling datasets with arbitrary channel configurations, dynamic time series lengths,
091   and diverse diagnostic targets across multiple tasks (see Figure 1). Specifically, FORMED employs
092
      a pre-trained foundation model as a backbone, which captures general temporal features from time
      series data. We then adapt this backbone to the medical domain by integrating a specialized shell
093
      enriched with medical knowledge. This shell is trained on a curated cohort of MedTS data, enabling
094
      the model to effectively capture the unique characteristics of medical sequences. Even with far
095   fewer samples in the MedTS cohort than during pre-training, FORMED retains strong generalization
096   abilities and domain knowledge for MedTS classification tasks.
097
098
      We curate a repurposing cohort of 5 MedTS datasets, including 2 ECG’s and 3 EEG’s, containing
      only 340K samples or 90 million time-points in total. These datasets feature diverse channel con-
099
      figurations (ranging from 12 to 33 channels), sample lengths (from 250 to 300 time-points), and
100
      diagnostic tasks (ranging from binary neurological to 5-class cardiovascular classification). Our
101   evaluation focuses on two aspects: First, for datasets partially included in the repurposing cohort,
102   FORMED achieves superior performance on unseen patients, outperforming 11 state-of-the-art TSA
103   and TSM models across all five datasets. Second, for a completely new dataset not included in the
104   cohort, FORMED can be efficiently adapted by updating only a small number of parameters while
105   still achieving the highest accuracy and AUROC compared to TSM and TSA models, even across
106   different data availability scenarios.

112   Foundation Models for General Time Series. To date, no foundation model has been specifically
113   designed for time series classification tasks, let alone MedTS classification; instead, recent advances
114   in time series foundation models mainly concentrate on forecasting tasks (Liang et al., 2024). Notic-
115   ing their success in forecasting, it is as much tempting as theoretically and practically challenging
116   to re-purpose these models for MedTS classification, yet these models all have major limitations
117
      such as design for univariate time series and requiring Task-Specific Adaptations (mid-column in
      Figure 1) that prevent them from being directly applicable to MedTS classification tasks (Cao et al.,
118
      2024; Sun et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2023).
119
120   For instance, Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024a), UniTime (Liu et al., 2024) and GPT4TS (Zhou et al.,
121   2023a) are all backboned on large language models, therefore they naturally handle time series
122   data in a univariate manner, lacking the ability to integrate information across multiple channels for
123
      MedTS classification. Moreover, we empirically observe that time series foundation models that
      take LLM as backbone don’t work well on time series datasets, in agree with Tan et al. (2024).
124
      Similarly, although TimeGPT (Garza et al., 2024) and TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) are pre-trained
125
      on large scale time series data, they treat co-evolving multivariate time series data as independent
126   of each other, thus sharing the same limitation as the previous models. The one of its kind model,
127   UniTS (Gao et al., 2024), is able to handle multivariate time series data and trained on multiple task
128   domains including classification, yet due to its scale and design, it often requires fine-tuning of the
129   whole model or performing prompt learning for optimal performance. This is both computationally
130   expensive for gradients calculation (Figure 1), and more importantly, data-greedy due to the massive
131   parameters to tune, making it not suitable for small-scale MedTS datasets.
132   Therefore, despite their effort and success, current foundation models require significant adaptation
133   to meet the demands of MedTS classification effectively. This motivates the need for a specialized
134   foundation model tailored to the complexities of MedTS, which can address these limitations with
135   dedicated architectural components.
136
      Adaptation of Foundation Models for MedTS. As general-purpose models, foundation models
137
      usually require various techniques to be effectively adapted for specific downstream tasks, includ-
138   ing prompting, fine-tuning, re-programming and proposed re-purposing. Here we focus on re-
139   programming and re-purposing as they can serve our purpose, see rest in Appendix A.
140
141
      Re-programming: By reusing the pre-trained model’s backbone (usually all the Transformer layers)
      without altering its internal weights, it leverages the model’s existing capabilities. It is able to handle
142
      new domain of data or type of task, by wrapping the backbone model with input adapters and task
143
      heads (Figure 1 TSA). Yet on its dark side, the re-programmed model no longer serves as a general-
144   purpose model, as both the input adapters and the task heads are task-specific, making it incapable
145   of generalizing across tasks and datasets (Tan et al., 2024).
146
147
      Re-purposing: Proposed in this work, it focuses on adapting the model to a new type of task with
      minimal modification to the task head only and refrains from being specific to certain task. There-
148
      fore, the repurposed model remains a general-purpose model for the field, which can serve as a new
149
      foundation model and be further adapted to new datasets efficiently (see Section 3). Its generaliz-
150   ability, data-efficiency and domain-expert nature make it extremely suitable for adapting time series
151   foundation models for MedTS classification tasks.
152
153
      Forecasting v.s. Classification. Although both forecasting and classification are key tasks in time
      series analysis, they are fundamentally different in nature. The primary distinction lies in the re-
154
      lationship between the input and output spaces. In forecasting, the model predicts future values
155
      within the same domain as the input, i.e., mapping sequence → sequence (Lim & Zohren, 2021).
156   For example, using past EEG signals to predict future EEG signals (Wang et al., 2024a). In con-
157   trast, classification uses the input to predict a categorical label, i.e., sequence → category (Ali et al.,
158   2019), such as diagnosing a neurological disease from EEG data (Wang et al., 2024a). In essence,
159   forecasting only involves mapping input to output within the same domain, whereas in classification,
160   the mapping is from one domain to a variety of others. Therefore, adapting a forecasting model for
161   general classification tasks requires more than simply modifying the prediction layer; it demands a
      comprehensive redesign and a deeper understanding of the problem space.

288   4.2   ATTENTION - BASED C LASSIFIER FOR I NCONSTANT C HANNEL AND C LASS
289
290   Instead of using a simple linear classifier, as other time series classification models (Zerveas et al.,
291
      2021; Yang et al., 2023), which will require the input, output or both to have fixed number of
      channels and classes, we propose to use a Transformer decoder layer (Vaswani et al., 2017) for
292
      tackling such variability. Although inspired by techniques commonly employed in object detection
293
      (Carion et al., 2020) and image classification (Meng et al., 2023), it introduces key modifications
294   tailored to address the unique challenges in MedTS classification. Our design is optimized for
295   practical use, with an aim at handling dynamic shape input and outputing dynamic number of output
296   classes, while reducing the computational overhead for training on new dataset and lowering risk
297   of overfitting. Our attention-based classifier contains three key components: Channel Embeddings
298   (CEs), Label Queries (LQs) and Shared Decoding Attention (SDA).
299
      Channel Embeddings. As MedTS often exist in a multi-variate manner, injecting information
300   about the channel will help the classifier distinguish between channels, thus promoting a more ro-
301   bust correspondence between the task and specific channel features. The Channel Embeddings are
302   lightweight parameters that are grouped into a look-up table that maps the name of dataset to learn-
303   able channel embeddings E ∈ RC×D . These embeddings are then added to the feature tokens H to
304   form the prompted feature tokens H̃ ∈ RC×L×D :
305
306
                                                H̃:,i,: = H:,i,: ⊕ E                                      (7)
307
308   Label Queries. Just as CEs, the label queries Q ∈ RK×D are also task-specific, learnable embed-
309
      dings, where K is the number of classes for the given task. These task-specific queries are used
      to guide the attention mechanism to focus on the relevant features for the specific task. The label
310
      queries independently attend to the prompted feature tokens H′ to find evidence for each class.
311
312   Shared Decoding Attention. The core evidence-finding process in classification is achieved
313   through a shared decoding attention mechanism. It is a single decoder layer similar in Vaswani
314   et al. (2017), that performs multi-head attention using Q as queries and H̃ as keys and values,
315   where H̃ = Flatten(H̃) ∈ R(C·L)×D . It is followed by a residual block to obtain the logits
316   ŷ ∈ RK for each class, where the probability prediction can be obtained using softmax or sigmoid
317   functions depending on the type of task:

320   Note that all the parameters in SDA is independent on either input length, number of channels, or
321   number of classes, therefore it is able to handle the inconstant channel, length and class in MedTS
322   classification tasks. Moreover, as it defines how the task queries will interact with the prompted
323   feature tokens and is shared across datasets and tasks, it is coerced to learn a shared dynamics and
      form a domain knowledge that is fixed and can be reused in adapting to new classification tasks.

      4.3   R EPURPOSING AND A DAPTING
325
326   During repurposing, the backbone foundation model is frozen, and the weights in SDA is randomly
327   initialized. For each dataset in our MedTS cohort, a pair of E and Q is also randomly initial-
328   ized. These are then trained over the MedTS cohort to update their parameters (see Repurposing in
329   Figure 2). After repurposing, the SDA should already capture the domain knowledge required for
330   MedTS classification, thus it will be fixed and reused when adapting to new datasets and tasks. For
331   unseen datasets that need to be classified, a new pair of E and Q will be created and learned during
332
      adapting, while the majority of the model remains fixed (see Adapting in Figure 2).
333   Summary. Our approach enables generalizability to new datasets, making it particularly suited
334   for MedTS classification, and serves as a strong foundation model for all future MedTS tasks. In
335   particular, our design brings significant benefits in overcoming the aforementioned challenges:
336
      • Generalizability Across Datasets: The backbone foundation model is able to capture general
337     time series patterns and is fixed for all datasets, while the SDA is independent of channel num-
338     ber, input length or class number, so that it can ben shared across datasets, and gains domain
339     knowledge during repurposing. This ensures that the model never overlooks general patterns in
340     the data, and also gains sufficient domain knowledge for MedTS classification. FORMED can be
341     effectively generalized to datasets with different sample length, channels, and classes.
342   • Generalization Across Subjects Within Dataset: The E and Q are the only task-specific pa-
343     rameters that are dependent on the dataset and task, and they are used to guide the model to focus
344     on the relevant features for the specific task. As their number of parameters is very limited, it is
345
        highly unlikely to memorize the specific pattern of the training data, keeping the model highly
        performant across diverse patients.
346
      • Lowered Data Requirement: As the SDA is shared in all tasks, it can be trained on a joint of
347
        diverse small MedTS datasets as a whole, without the need for a single, large and comprehensive
348     dataset which doesn’t exist in practice. On the other hand, as the majority of the model parameters
349     is fixed during adapting, the task-specific E and Q can be easily tuned with a little data from the
350     new dataset. This design significantly reduces the data requirement for repurposing and adapting,
351     making it particularly suitable for MedTS classification tasks with limited data.

356   Datasets. We select 5 MedTS datasets to formulate a MedTS cohort and use it for repurposing
357
      (Figure 2). These datasets provide a broad range of physiological signals, capturing both cardiac
      and neurological activity, which are among the most commonly analyzed modalities in MedTS. See
358
      Table 3 for details on the datasets. Moreover, we also include an unseen, out-of-domain dataset
359
      (Liu et al., 2016) to assess our model’s ability to generalize to new tasks. All datasets are split into
360   train-test-valid sets following the patient-independent setting as in Wang et al. (2024c). The datasets
361   span a wide range of channels, sampling rates, sample durations, and disease labels, allowing for
362   the evaluation of inter-dataset heterogeneity. We use inter-subject variation, a key contributor to
363   intra-dataset heterogeneity (Wang et al., 2023), as a proxy to assess the generalization capability of
364   FORMED.
365

      6   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
498
499
      In this paper, we present FORMED, a foundation model for MedTS classification, that leverages
      a pre-trained backbone that can capture general time series patterns and a generalizable adapta-
500
      tion head to repurpose the model and capture domain-specific knowledge. We demonstrate that
501
      FORMED can effectively generalize both within and across datasets, providing superior perfor-
502   mance with more robustness against distribution discrepancies compared to state-of-the-art models,
503   and can be seamlessly adapted to unseen MedTS datasets with lightweight training. Next, we discuss
504   the potential impact of our work, the limitations, and future directions.
505
      Potential Impact. Our work has mainly focused on field of MedTS classification, where leakage of
506
      patient information and bias in the model are critical concerns. Regarding the former, we only use
507
      datasets that are publicly available and have been de-identified, and the details and sources of them
508   are provided in Table 3. As for the latter, we have taken steps to ensure that our model is fair, such
509   as using a backbone model that has been pre-trained on the largest dataset to capture more general
510   time series patterns, and no covariate information is used other than dataset-level embeddings. Yet,
511   we acknowledge that there may still be biases in the data that we have not accounted for, and we are
512   to release the weights of our model along with a detailed model card (Mitchell et al., 2019) for our
513   community to assess the potential bias and privacy concerns in a joint effort.
514   Relation to TimesFM. The TimesFM (Das et al., 2024) is a foundation model whose sole purpose
515   is time series forecasting, and by repurposing it, we create FORMED which is now a foundation
516   model for medical time series classification, fundamentally different from TimesFM.
517
      Backbone and Repurposing Domain. The proposed repurposing-and-adapting framework is not
518
      limited to specific backbone model, and can be applied to other time series foundation models with
519
      similar anatomy. Our framework can repurpose to other domains like weather forecasting, financial
520   tasks, etc., by simply using a domain-specific repurposing data cohort.
521
522   Computation Efficiency. Computational cost is of great concern for large foundation models, espe-
523
      cially so when the model needs to be frequently adapted to new downstream tasks (Hu et al., 2021).
      We have recognized such need and already incorporated several strategies to make our model more
524
      computationally efficient. By freezing the backbone model and omitting pre-backbone adapters, the
525
      gradients do not need to be back-propagated through the backbone model during repurposing (Fig-
526   ure 1), which significantly reduces the computational cost. Moreover, we take what is categorized as
527   an external memorization approach (Wang et al., 2024b), where new knowledge of specific tasks is
528   stored in the task-specific embeddings and queries, rather than tuning the model parameters, which
529   further reduces the computational cost at the adaptation stage. On the whole, our model is designed
530   to be computationally efficient and scalable to larger datasets and more complex tasks.
531
      Interpretability and Explainability. When it comes to medical applications, interpretability and
532   explainability are crucial for the model to be trusted and adopted by healthcare professionals. As
533   our model is fully transformer-based, it can harness the power of tools that dissect the attention
534   mechanism like Chefer et al. (2021); Hao et al. (2021). Moreover, the task-specific knowledge is
535   explicitly stored in channel embeddings and label queries, which can be used to compare and explain
536   the model’s behavior across different tasks. However, all these are beyond the scope of this paper
537   and deserves to be explored in future work.

      A     C OMPARISON OF A DAPTATION T ECHNIQUES
757
758
      As discussed in Section 2, adaptation techniques for foundation models mainly includes Prompt-
759   ing, Fine-tuning, Re-programming, and Re-purposing. We have introduced re-programming and
760   re-purposing, and here we provide a brief overview of prompting and fine-tuning, and compare
761   these techniques based on three aspects: Data Efficiency, New Task Type, and Generalizability.
762
      Prompting & Fine-tuning: Both are common adaptation techniques for foundation models, where
763
      prompting involves conditioning the model with specific instructions or cues, either handcrafted
764
      (Zhou et al., 2023b; Reynolds & McDonell, 2021) or learned through data (Zhou et al., 2022a),
765   and fine-tuning involves updating the model’s internal parameters on dedicated dataset (Howard &
766   Ruder, 2018; Ding et al., 2023). While they focus on different aspects of adaptation, they share the
767   commonality of not altering the model’s core architecture, therefore the functionality of the model
768   remains unchanged, e.g., model for forecasting remains a forecasting model. Moreover, fine-tuning
769   is often more data-greedy, as it requires updating the whole model’s parameters, while prompting
770   only requires learning a few task-specific embeddings or prompts.
771   In general, these techniques can be categorized based on three aspects: Data efficiency, as the scale
772   of dataset used for adaptation, typically measured by the number of parameters updated; New Task
773   Type, as the ability to adapt to new tasks that are different from the original task, such as from
774   forecasting to classification; and Generalizability, as the ability for the adapted model to be used
775   on unseen datasets and share knowledge across tasks. Table 2 provides a comparison of these tech-
776   niques based on these aspects.
