009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This work introduces a Transformer-based approach for graph signal processing
012            that leverages a novel task-specific attention mechanism, namely NTAttention.
013            Unlike conventional self-attention mechanisms, our method attends to all nodes
014            across multiple time steps, enabling the model to effectively capture dependencies
015            between nodes over extended time periods. This addresses a key limitation faced by
016            traditional methods. Additionally, we propose geometry-aware masking (GMask),
017            which incorporates the graph topology into the sparsification of the self-attention
018
               matrix. This enhances efficiency while preserving the rich temporal information
               conveyed by the nodes. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach on two
019
               critical applications: EEG seizure detection and traffic forecasting. Both tasks
020
               involve data collected from fixed sensors, such as electrodes or road sensors, where
021            data from one sensor can influence others temporally and spatially. Our model
022            enhances sensitivity in fast seizure detection by 20 percentage points compared to
023            state-of-the-art and significantly outperforms current methods in traffic forecasting.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   A significant portion of the time series data we utilize in various machine learning applications is
029   gathered thanks to fixed located sensors (Tang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2018). These sensors play a
030   crucial role in collecting information for different applications such as healthcare and time-series
031   forecasting (Jasper, 1958; Li et al., 2018). One notable application of these sensors is in neural
032   recordings, such as electroencephalography (EEG) signals, where electrodes are placed on a patient’s
033
      scalp (Jasper, 1958). Similarly, the deployment of traffic sensors along roadways for monitoring
      traffic flow is another significant example, greatly impacting our daily routines (Shao et al., 2022).
034
035   While these sensors remain fixed in their respective locations, it is crucial to recognize that the
036   data flow from one sensor can influence others both temporally and spatially (Tang et al., 2021;
037   Li et al., 2018). For example, effective EEG seizure detection require learning both (i) long-range
038   spatial dependencies, as seizure activity may originate at a focal electrode and then spread to other
039
      brain regions, and (ii) long-range temporal dependencies, as if a seizure occurs at the beginning
      of the window with no subsequent activity, the model need to classify based on this brief episode.
040
      In the context of traffic data, successful traffic forecasting needs to capture both (i) long-range
041
      spatial dependencies, as congestion at a major nodes can create ripple effects, and (ii) long-range
042   temporal dependencies, where forecasting typically operates with a 5-minute window resolution
043   and forecasting 1 or 2 hours ahead involves processing 12 or 24 data windows, which is considered
044   long-range for this task (Li et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2018; Shang et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2020).
045
      Motivated by these observations, data recorded from various fixed sensors or electrodes is often
046
      framed as a temporal graph representation, where the topology remains fixed over time. Several
047
      studies have leveraged different combinations of Graph Neural Network (GNN) and Recurrent Neural
048   Network (RNN) to capture the spatio-temporal dynamics inherent in such data (Yu et al., 2018;
049   zot; Song et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Ho & Armanfard, 2023). Two crucial
050   applications, EEG-based seizure detection and traffic prediction, have received considerable attention
051   in prior research (Tang et al., 2021; Ho & Armanfard, 2023; Li et al., 2018; Song et al., 2020). In
052   this study, we focus on these applications due to their significant impact and relevance. Over 50
053   million worldwide suffer from epilepsy (WHO; Begley et al., 2022), highlighting the critical need
      for effective seizure detection and prevention methods (Shoaran et al., 2016). Traffic congestion

078   While previous studies have modeled the spatio-temporal dynamics of temporal graphs using GNNs
079   and RNNs (Li et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2021; Ho & Armanfard, 2023; Shao et al.,
080
      2022), they often struggle to account for long-range dependencies among distant nodes over extended
      periods, affecting their accuracy in capturing long-range space-time dependencies (Tang et al., 2021;
081
      Ho & Armanfard, 2023; Song et al., 2020). Attempts have been made to use attention for graphs,
082
      e.g., Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021) or Graph Attention Networks (GAT) (Velickovic et al., 2017),
083   but they have not been investigated for graph signal representation with fixed nodes. While some
084   approaches, such as (Guo et al., 2019), have integrated transformers to capture long-range temporal
085   dependencies, they solely rely on node embeddings and graph convolutions for graph representation
086   (Morris et al., 2019). This reliance can lead to decreased accuracy, especially for graphs with a large
087   number of nodes, as it may struggle to effectively detect long-range space-time dependencies.
088
      In this study, we propose a new, simple yet highly effective self-attention module, namely NTAttention,
089   which attends to all graph nodes over extended time periods for learning spatio-temporal dynamics in
090   temporal graphs. Furthermore, to enhance efficiency in the spatial domain, we propose a geometry-
091   aware attention mask that ensures spatially distant nodes do not attend to each other. We illustrate
092   our method in Fig. 1. We evaluate our model on two key applications in graph signal processing:
093   EEG-based seizure detection and traffic forecasting. Our main contributions are as follows:
094
095          • We introduce a new self-attention mechanism, termed NTAttention, for graph signal pro-
096            cessing, which effectively attends to all nodes across multiple time steps. Our Transformer
097            design is easy to implement and strongly motivated by the characteristics of temporal graph
098            signals. Our architecture excels at addressing long-range space-time dynamics, a challenge
099
               that previous methods in graph signal processing have struggled to overcome.
100          • We introduce Geometry-Aware Masking (GMask) for NTAttention, which significantly
101            enhances the computational efficiency of our proposed attention mechanism, while simulta-
102            neously improving the model’s generalization performance.
103
             • Our model sets a new standard in both seizure detection and traffic forecasting. Specifically,
104            it improves sensitivity for long-term seizure detection by 20 percentage points compared to
105            previous methods, which is crucial for ensuring that seizure events are not missed. Addition-
106            ally, it achieves an impressive MAE of 2.94 for 1-hour traffic forecasting, outperforming all
107            other benchmarks while maintaining similar memory and computational requirements.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      We concentrate on two key applications of graph signal processing: 1) EEG-based seizure analysis,
111   and 2) Traffic forecasting. Accordingly, this section is divided into two parts to comprehensively
112   review the related work and advancements of various baseline methods in each area.
113
114   EEG-based Seizure Detection Various studies have attempted to develop machine learning and
115   deep learning models for EEG-based seizure detection (Asif et al., 2020; O’Shea et al., 2020; Ho
116   & Armanfard, 2023; Shoaran et al., 2016; Tang et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2022). For instance, (Asif
117   et al., 2020; Saab et al., 2020) used CNN-based architectures, either utilizing spectral features or
118   treating EEG data as multi-channel images, which neglects the time-series structure of EEG signals.
119   Furthermore, (Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al., 2020) employed a CNN-LSTM architecture that captures
120
      both spatial and temporal dependencies in EEG signals. However, these approaches overlook the
      non-Euclidean geometry inherent in EEG signals (Tang et al., 2021; Ho & Armanfard, 2023).
121
122   To address this, different variations of GNNs have been applied to the seizure detection task. For
123   example, (Tang et al., 2021) employed two versions of the diffusion convolution recurrent neural
124   network (DCRNN): one using a distance-based graph and the other a correlation-based graph. These
125   approaches leverage GNNs to capture spatial information considering non-Euclidean geometry
126
      and RNNs for temporal dependencies. However, these models still face challenges in capturing
      long-range node and time dependencies within the EEG structure (Yan et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022).
127
      This limitation arises because RNNs and GNNs are adept at capturing local spatial and temporal
128
      information but struggle with long-range space-time dependencies (Vaswani et al., 2017). A detailed
129   comparison of different models, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses, is provided in Table 1
130   and dataset description provided in Table 3.
131
132   Traffic Forecasting Traditional methods such as support vector regression (SVR) (Hong, 2011) and
133   Vector Auto-regressive (VAR) (Ermagun & Levinson, 2018) have been utilized for traffic forecasting.
134   These models, however, do not capture the information flow between different sensors and predict the
135   traffic flow for each sensor solely based on the data available for that sensor (Tang et al., 2021). Deep
136   fully connected neural networks have been employed for traffic forecasting tasks (Zhang et al., 2016),
137   leveraging data from all sensors to predict traffic flow. However, these models do not account for the
138
      locality of sensor placement, which is crucial for capturing the spatial dependencies and variations in
      traffic patterns. Recurrent neural network (RNN) based models, such as Long Short-Term Memory
139
      (LSTM), have also been utilized for capturing the temporal information of traffic data but similarly
140
      overlook the locality of sensors (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Laptev et al., 2017). To better
141   capture spatial information, convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures have been used to
142   model the information flow between traffic sensors (Huang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2017).
143
144
      Recent research has shown a growing interest in leveraging graph neural networks (GNNs) to address
      traffic forecasting challenges (Tang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2018; Song et al., 2020; Zheng et al.,
145
      2020; Shang et al., 2021). However, existing GNN-based models struggle to effectively capture
146
      long space-time dependencies (Song et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020). Inspired by developments in
147   language models, some studies have integrated attention mechanisms with convolutional layers for
148   this task (Guo et al., 2019), or introduced separate attention mechanisms for spatial and temporal
149   representation (Zheng et al., 2020). Despite these advancements, existing models still exhibit low
150   accuracy, particularly with long window sizes, and fail to incorporate long space-time correlations,
151   such as how distant nodes affect each other over time. In Table 2, we summarize the advantages and
152   disadvantages of current traffic forecasting models and in Table 4 we presented dataset description.
153   Additionally, since the connection and similarity between seizure detection and traffic forecasting
154   tasks have been established in Tang et al. (2021), but most models primarily focus on a single
155
      application, NTAttention has been specifically designed as a unified model capable of effectively
      handling both tasks.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING AND F ORMULATION
204
205   We represent the sensor network as a graph G = {V, E, P} with V = {v1 , ..., vN } as the nodes, E
206   representing the edges, and P = {p1 , . . . , pn } being a set of vectors pi ∈ R2 representing node
207   coordinates in space. We denote the data observed at time point t on graph G as a graph signal
208   X (t) ∈ RN ×M with M being the number of features per node. We present the sequence of T
209   observations of graph signal as a three-dimensional tensor X ∈ RT ×N ×M s.t.
210
211                                    Xt = [X (t) , X (t+1) , ..., X (t+T −1) ],                                  (1)
212
213   where t is the initial time point of T consecutive observations of graph signal. The problem of
214   seizure detection is formulated as a binary classification task predicting the label y ∈ {0, 1} for a
215   corresponding tensor Xt . The traffic forecasting problem is formulated as predicting the next tensor
      of T consecutive observations of graph signal Xt+T from a given tensor Xt .

      3.4   G EOMETRY-AWARE ATTENTION M ASKING (GM ASK )
285
      Masking the attention matrix can significantly enhance efficiency by skipping unnecessary compu-
286
      tations and improving performance (Zaheer et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020). In this section, we
287   introduce a geometry-aware masking approach, namely GMask, that aligns with the graph topology.
288   We use the node positions in space characterized by P to create the following attention mask:
289
290
                                                  ∥p −p ∥2
                                         (
291                                          exp − i σ2 j              if ∥pi − pj ∥ ≤ k,
                                 Gij =
292                                          0                         otherwise.
293
294
      Here, σ is set to the standard deviation of the distances, and k is the threshold for the Gaussian kernel
295
      (Shuman et al., 2013). We mask the attention by omitting the attention between two nodes i and j
296   in all time points if Gij = 0. This ensures that spatially distant nodes do not attend to each other,
297   thereby improving the computational efficiency and accuracy of the model. Specifically, by masking
298   the attention between nodes i and j, T 2 entries in the attention matrix are set to zero because the
299   model masks all the attention scores for all time points involving nodes i and j . Therefore, let N0 be
300   the number of non-zero entries of G, GMask avoids (N 2 − N0 )T 2 floating point computations. We
301   have also adapted dynamic GMask with correlation based edges (dynamic graph) in Appendix M.
302   The structured sparsity induced by GMask aligns with the graph topology, focusing attention on
303   relevant nodes and further enhancing optimization towards a solution that respects the underlying
304   graph structure.

323   labeled y = 1 if it contains at least one seizure event and y = 0 if it does not. Dataset descriptions

      5 C ONCLUSION
525   In this study, we proposed NTAttention for graph signal processing tasks, specifically targeting
526
      seizure detection and traffic forecasting. By incorporating spatial encoding into each node’s features
      and relative temporal encoding into the attention matrix, we effectively utilized the positional and
527
      temporal information inherent in the data. Our space-time attention mechanism, enhanced with
528
      geometry-aware masking based on graph topology, further improved model performance by focusing
529   attention on relevant nodes. We evaluated NTAttention on the TUH EEG seizure dataset and
530   the METR-LA traffic dataset, benchmarking it against several well-known models. For seizure
531   detection, NTAttention demonstrated superior performance, achieving significantly higher F1-scores
532   and sensitivity than other baselines, emphasizing its ability to reliably detect seizure events. In traffic
533   forecasting, NTAttention achieved the lowest RMSE and MAE, particularly excelling in long-term
534   forecasting horizons. NTAttention requires similar memory and computations as the baselines, where
535   efficiency can be further boosted with GMask while maintaining a similar level of performance.

      A     D ETAILS OF DATA PROCESSING
703
704
      A.1    EEG BASED SEIZURE DETECTION
705
706   Due to varying sampling frequencies in the Temple University EEG Seizure Corpus (TUSZ), we
707   standardize the signals to a uniform frequency of 200 Hz. We then preprocess the data to generate
708   EEG clips in the frequency domain along with their corresponding labels. For seizure detection,
709   we utilize both seizure and non-seizure EEGs. EEG clips are created by sliding a 12-second (or
710   60-second) window over the signals with no overlap, discarding the last window if it is shorter than
711   the clip length. Each clip is labeled as y = 1 if it contains at least one seizure event, and y = 0
712
      if no seizure event is present. For each 1-second window, we applied FFT to extract frequency
      domain features and selected the log-amplitude of non-negative frequency samples, resulting in
713
      X (t) ∈ RN ×M matrices for each 1-second window, with N = 19 EEG channels and M = 100
714
      frequency features (Tang et al., 2021; Ho & Armanfard, 2023). We then applied Z-score normalization
715
      to each window. For the seizure detection task, we used T = 12-second (or 60-second) consecutive
716   windows, resulting in an EEG clip tensor X ∈ RT ×N ×M for each input, with a corresponding label
717   y. Due to the imbalance between the number of seizure and normal samples, we down sampled
718   the normal samples during training to ensure an equal number of seizure and non-seizure samples.
719   However, all samples were used for testing. Additionally, following the methodology in (Tang et al.,
720   2021), we divided the TUSZ dataset’s training set into a 90-10 ratio for training and validation.
721
722   A.2    T RAFFIC F ORECASTING
723
724   Following previous studies (Li et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2020; Shang et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2019), we
725   processed the METR-LA (Tang et al., 2021) dataset by selecting 207 traffic sensors and aggregating
726   the traffic sensor readings into 5-minute windows, resulting in X (t) ∈ RN ×M tensors with N = 207
727
      nodes and M = 2 features per node for each input sample. We then selected T = 3 consecutive
      windows for 15-minute forecasting (Horizon 3), T = 6 for 30-minute forecasting (Horizon 6), and
728
      T = 12 for one-hour forecasting (Horizon 12), resulting in an input tensor X ∈ RT ×N ×M .
729
730   We split the data into 70% for training, 20% for testing, and 10% for evaluation. Z-score normalization
731   was applied to each 5-minute window of data. Additionally, each input tensor X overlaps with

      We conducted an ablation study to examine the effect of the threshold parameter k on the number

      of FLOPs for NTAttention + GMask. Our findings indicate that, in graphs with a larger number

902   how NTAttention + GMask achieves comparable model size and efficiency to other benchmarks, as

      I   E XTENSION OF NT ATTENTION TO GNN S
950
951   The spatial-temporal nature of the data we are using requires both spatial and temporal dependencies,
952   which cannot be handled by traditional GNNs alone. This is why the community is adapting methods
953
      like GMAN (Zheng et al., 2020), and GTS (Shang et al., 2021) for such tasks.
954   We would like to emphasize that NTAttention can be viewed as an extension of current GNNs,
955   particularly graph transformers like Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021), to temporal graphs. Traditional
956   GNNs lack the capability to handle time-series based features, making them unsuitable for the type
957   of temporal data and tasks we address.
958   In contrast, models such as DCRNN Tang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2018) have been adapted to this
959   setting, utilizing a combination of GConv (Morris et al., 2019) and GRU to capture spatio-temporal
960   dependencies. NTAttention extends these ideas by integrating temporal features directly into the
961   graph-based model, enhancing its ability to process and analyze temporal graph data effectively.
