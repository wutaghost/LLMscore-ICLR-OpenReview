009                                               A BSTRACT
010
               Randomized smoothing is the primary certified robustness method for accessing the
011
               robustness of deep learning models to adversarial perturbations in the l2 -norm, by
012            taking a majority vote over the multiple predictions of a random Gaussian perturbed
013            input of the base classifier. To fulfill the certified bound and empirical accuracy of
014            randomized smoothing, the base model either needs to be retrained from scratch
015            to learn Gaussian noise or adds an auxiliary denoiser to eliminate it. In this work,
016            we propose PEFTSmoothing, which teach the base model to learn the Gaussian
017            noise-augmented data with Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods in
018            both white-box and black-box settings. This design is based on the intuition that
019            large-scale models have the potential to learn diverse data patterns, including the
020
               noise data distributions. In addition, we explore the possibility of combining
               PEFTSmoothing with the fine-tuning for downstream task adaptation, which allows
021
               us to simultaneously obtain a robust version of the large vision model and its
022
               adaptation tailored to downstream datasets. Extensive results demonstrate the
023            effectiveness and efficiency of PEFTSmoothing, which allow us to certify over 98%
024            accuracy for ViT on CIFAR-10, 20% higher than SoTA denoised smoothing, and
025            over 61% accuracy on ImageNet which is 30% higher than CNN-based denoiser
026            and comparable to the Diffusion-based denoiser.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
030   Certified robustness is the primary method to evaluate the robustness of deep learning systems
031   to adversarial perturbations within specific bound (10; 1; 11), providing a reliable and provable
032   robustness guarantee to adversarial examples within specific norm bounds. In image classification,
033
      the state-of-the-art (SoTA) certified robustness to l2 -norm is randomized smoothing (1), which
      converts a deterministic base classifier into a probabilistic classifier by adding isotropic Gaussian
034
      noise to the input and returning the majority votes over the multiple predictions of noised inputs.
035
036   However, the empirical accuracy and certified bound of randomized smoothed model is not ideal
037   because the base model, initially trained on the original data distribution, fails to capture the noise-
038   augmented data distribution, thus unable to correctly predict the label of the original input when
039
      subjected to the corresponding Gaussian-noised counterpart (12). To address this, the base models
      either need to be trained from scratch to better learn the noise-augmented data distribution or
040
      incorporating a custom-trained denoiser to eliminate the Gaussian-noised inputs before they reach
041
      the base classifier (2; 50). Although these two approaches are intuitive, each of them holds its own
042   limitation. On one hand, training from scratch is impractical for large scale models and the trade-off
043   between time and computational cost in achieving a robust version of a large model is unworthy. On
044   the other hand, applying a denoiser brings both training and inference time adds-on, especially when
045   applying the SoTA diffusion-based denoiser architecture (50). In addition the certified performance
046   of denoised smoothing largely relies on the performance of the denoising procedure.
047
      The limitations of existing methods highlight the need for a more efficient and effective approach
048   to further optimize the empirical performance and the robustness bound of randomized smoothing.
049   In this work, we explore an alternative approach, changing from a reactive approach (denoised
050   smoothing) to a proactive approach that acquires the model’s ability to learn the underlying
051   noise-augmented data distribution. Instead of training the model from scratch, we propose
052   PEFTSmoothing to incorporate the Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (64) into
053   randomized smoothing procedure. The original goal of PEFTs is to adapt pre-trained models to
      downstream tasks with fewer fine-tuning parameters and lower memory usage, while maintaining

099   2 P RELIMINARIES
100   The SoTA certified robustness in l2 -norm is randomized smoothing. In this section, we first briefly
101   review the certified guarantee of randomized smoothing. Then, we explain denoised smoothing, the
102
      practical approach to optimize the empirical performance of randomized smoothing.

104   classifier G by generating the aggregated prediction over the Gaussian noise-augmented data via
105   majority voting. Specifically, for input x, G returns the class that is most likely to be returned by the
106   base classifier F under Gaussian perturbations of x, which can be stated as:
107

126    One major limitation of this method is that its implementation involves the training of multiple
127   denoisers for various noise types and scales. In addition, it diminishes the model’s accuracy and
128   its performance is not satisfactory. To further improve the denoising performance, researchers later
129   involved SoTA diffusion-based denoiser to optimize the performance of randomized smoothing
130   (62; 50). Stable diffusion (16) are well-equipped for Gaussian denoising due to their training
131   procedures, enabling them to effectively eliminate Gaussian noise and reconstruct clean images from
132   noisy inputs. Although diffusion-based denoiser has largely improved the empirical accuracy of
133
      randomized smoothing, one limitation is its significantly long inference time, as each input requires
      multiple samplings of Gaussian augmentation which leads to multiple times passing diffusion-based
134
      denoiser.

136   3   I NTUITION : PEFT G UIDES L ARGE V ISION M ODELS TO L EARN
137       N OISE -AUGMENTED DATA D ISTRIBUTION
138   In this section, we discuss the intuition behind PEFTSmoothing, using Parameter-Efficient Fine-
139   Tuning to achieve a certifiably robust classifier that large-scale models have the potential to learn
140   diverse data patterns effectively, including the noise data distributions, without applying a heavy-
141   structured denoiser. First, we will explain why randomized smoothing empirically fails without
142   training from scratch. Second, we will demonstrate that PEFT methods can more successfully guide
143   large vision models to learn noise-augmented data distribution than eliminate the noise with denoisers.
144   Theoretically, Theorem 2.1 of randomized smoothing holds regardless of how the base classifier is
145   trained. However, if the model is not trained from scratch with Gaussian augmented data, it fails to
146   predict accurately in the inference time with Gaussian noised inputs, thus, the defense capacity to
147   adversarial examples as well as the certified bound are significantly diminished. In other words, the
148   accuracy largely relies on how the model classifies the Gaussian noise-augmented data. Therefore,
149   how to improve the model’s prediction ability on noise-augmented data is the key component.
150   Denoised smoothing involves an auxiliary custom-trained denoiser to reactively eliminate the Gaus-
151   sian noise before feeding the inputs to the certified classifier. The denoiser allows the model to
152   predict accurately on the denoised input, which is close to the original data distribution. However, we
153   assume that large-scale models such as Vision Transformer (ViT), acquire such potential to capture
154   complex patterns and information if the models are trained with proper methods and training data,
155   making them well-suited for learning intricate data distributions, including noise-augmented data
156   which corresponds with our results in section 5.
157   The intuitive nature of PEFT aligns with the inherent ability of large models to understand and adapt
158   to diverse data patterns. Inspired by this, we propose PEFTSmoothing, utilizing PEFT methods to
159   guide the model to adjust its parameters more efficiently and effectively to the noise-augmented
160   data distribution compared to the potentially sub-optimal process of training an additional denoising
161   module. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to explore the PEFT methods in
      learning noised data distribution.

185   4     PEFTSmoothing
186
      As demonstrated in the previous section that PEFT methods have great potential to guide large vision
187
      models to learn noise-augmented data distribution, we introduce a novel paradigm PEFTSmoothing
188   for robustness enhancement, which not only empirically works but still holds the certifiable guarantee
189   of randomized smoothing as well.
190
191
      In this section, we first describe the white-box PEFTSmoothing with prompt-tuning (45), LoRA
      (46) and adapter (43; 42). Second, we introduce the black-box PEFTSmoothing, considering the
192
      more general cases where the user wants to have a robust version of a larger model without access to
193
      the private classification APIs. At the end, we demonstrate the potential of killing two birds with
194   one stone: achieving certified robustness and downstream task adaption with a single fine-tuning
195   procedure.
196
197
      4.1 W HITE - BOX PEFTSmoothing
      The training stage and inference are illustrated in Figure 1. In the training stage, training images
198
      are augmented with random Gaussian noise, before feeding into the classifier. We incorporate four
199
      PEFT methods to transform a base classifier into a PEFTSmoothed classifier including prompt-tuning,
200   LoRA and adapter, as well as the full fine-tuning. For all four fine-tuning methods, the blue blocks
201   indicated the frozen layers which will not be optimized or updated during training while the light red
202   blocks refer to the part that will be updated.
203

      standard fine-tuning method to optimize the prediction of the Gaussian perturbed input x + ε over

      ground-truth label y. The optimization process can be stated as follows:

      6   R ELATED W ORK
487
      6.1 A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES
488
489
      Adversarial Attacks. The concept of adversarial attacks was initially introduced by Szegedy et
      al. (36), who highlighted the susceptibility of neural networks to small perturbations in input data,
490
      capable of deceiving machine learning models. This foundational work brought significant attention to
491
      the field of adversarial machine learning. Following this, Goodfellow et al. proposed the Fast Gradient
492   Sign Method (FGSM), which efficiently generates adversarial examples by using the gradient of the
493   loss function with respect to the input data (7). Adversarial training. Among empirical defenses,
494   adversarial training has emerged as one of the most successful approaches. Initially proposed by
495   Goodfellow et al. (7), adversarial training involves incorporating adversarial examples into the
496   training dataset, thereby enhancing the model’s robustness. Madry et al. (34) extended this idea by
497   formulating adversarial training as a min-max optimization problem, which further improved the
498
      resilience of models against adversarial attacks. Empirical defense. Besides adversarial training,
      there are some other attack strategies adversarial detection (61; 27; 28; 51; 52) and gradient masking.
499   However, Carlini and Wagner (23) summarized most of these adversarial detecting methods cannot
500   defend adversarial examples in some cases by slightly changing the loss functions. In addition, many
501   heuristic defenses were later compromised by stronger adversarial attack methods, as demonstrated
502   by Athalye et al. (8) and Uesato et al. (72), who showed that many defenses relied on obfuscated
503   gradients, which provided a false sense of security and could be easily bypassed.
504   6.2 C ERTIFIED ROBUSTNESS
505   Certified defenses aim to provide provable guarantees on the robustness and reliability of models
506   against adversarial attacks. Lecuyer et al. (10) introduced PixelDP, which scales to large networks
507   and datasets by establishing a connection between robustness against adversarial examples and
508   differential privacy. This method provides formal guarantees on the model’s robustness by leveraging
509   the principles of differential privacy (55). Building on this concept, Cohen et al. (1) developed
510   randomized smoothing, a technique that creates robustness guarantees by adding random noise to the
511
      input data and averaging the model’s predictions over multiple noisy samples.
512   Subsequent research has focused on improving randomized smoothing to maximize its empirical
513   performance. For instance, Salman et al. (54) integrated adversarial training into the randomized
514   smoothing framework to further improve its defense capacity against adversarial examples. Zhai et al.
515   (63) sought to regularize the prediction consistency over noise, thereby enhancing the robustness of
516   the smoothed classifiers. Additionally, Kariyappa and Qureshi (37) investigated the robustness of
517
      ensemble models, demonstrating that diverse ensembles could provide better robustness compared
      to single models. Jeong and Song (38) proposed SmoothMix, a training scheme that enhances the
518
      robustness of smoothed classifiers through self-mixup, which blends inputs to create new training
519
      examples that help the model generalize better. Horvath et al. (40) explored the trade-off between
520   robustness and accuracy, proposing a compositional architecture that balances these two objectives.
521
      6.3 M ODEL FINE - TUNING
522
      Fine-tuning enhances a pre-trained model’s performance on specific tasks but can be costly for
523   large models. To address this, researchers propose parameter-efficient fine-tuning, optimizing model
524   parameters with minimal resource use For example, Houlsby et al. (42) introduced adapter layers,
525   which add a small number of trainable parameters to the model, allowing for efficient fine-tuning.
526   Pfeiffer et al. Lester et al. (44) developed prompt-tuning, which adjusts only the prompts used
527   to guide the model’s predictions, significantly reducing the number of parameters that need to be
528   updated. Hu et al. (46) proposed LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), which fine-tunes the model by
529   learning low-rank adaptations of the weight matrices, thereby reducing computational overhead.
530
      7 C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we present PEFTSmoothing to proactively adapt the base model to learn the Gaussian
532
      noise-augmented data distribution with Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning methods. PEFTSmoothed
533
      model can achieve high certified accuracy when applying randomized smoothing procedures. We
534   experimented PEFTSmoothing with different PEFT strategies and compared them with basic ran-
535   domized smoothing and denoised smoothing. Experimental results indicate that PEFTSmoothing
536   greatly outperforms the existing certified defense methods on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet while sig-
537   nificantly decreasing the computational cost of the defense. We further explored the black-box
538   PEFTSmoothing and the possibility of achieving a PEFTSmoothed model along with fine-tuning to
539   adapt to downstream datasets. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of
      PEFTSmoothing.

      Limitation. Overall, PEFTSmoothing achieves SoTA certified accuracy with significantly decreased
      computational cost in both white-box and black-box settings on CIFAR-10, while achieving slightly
814
      worse performance than diffusion-based denoised smoothing methods on ImageNet.
815
816   Deployment. Regarding the aforementioned limitation, we recommend the following deployment
817   guidelines to provide a comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of PEFTSmooth-
818   ing across different scenarios:
819
820          • PEFTSmoothing outperforms all state-of-the-art denoising smoothing approaches on the
821
               CIFAR-10 dataset. It achieves this superior performance while requiring the training of
               significantly fewer total model parameters, making it a more efficient option in terms of
822
               computational resources and training time.
823
824          • When applied to high-resolution images such as those in the ImageNet dataset, PEFTSmooth-
825
               ing combined with LoRA exhibits slightly lower certified accuracy compared to the diffusion-
               based denoising smoothing method.
826
827          • Among the four PEFTSmoothing methods evaluated, LoRA and prompt-tuning stand out
828            by achieving better top-1 accuracy with a smaller size of trained parameters, making them
829
               preferable choices for scenarios where parameter size and computational load are critical
               considerations.
830
831          • The black-box PEFTSmoothing using prompt-tuning can achieve performance levels similar
832            to those of the diffusion-based denoising smoothing methods.
833
834   Future work. In future research, we aim to enhance the performance of PEFTSmoothing on high-
835
      resolution images exploring more effective PEFT methods for large-scale models. Additionally, we
      plan to further investigate the integration of fine-tuning PEFTSmoothing with PEFT for downstream
836
      dataset adaptation. This approach presents a pathway toward scalable fine-tuning algorithms for certi-
837
      fied task-specific classifiers. Furthermore, it would be interesting to explore adapting PEFTSmoothing
838   to the certified robustness method in the large language model domain.
