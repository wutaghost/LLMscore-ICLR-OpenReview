009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             We introduce Planning-guided Retrieval Augmented Generation (Plan-RAG), a
012             novel framework that augments the retrieve-then-reason paradigm of existing
013             RAG frameworks to plan-then-retrieve. Plan-RAG formulates a reasoning plan as
014             a directed acyclic graph (DAG), decomposing queries into interrelated atomic sub-
015             queries. Answer generation follows the DAG structure, allowing significant gains
016
                in efficiency through parallelized retrieval and generation. While state-of-the-
                art RAG solutions require extensive data generation and fine-tuning of language
017
                models (LMs), Plan-RAG incorporates frozen LMs as plug-and-play experts to
018
                generate high-quality answers. Compared to existing RAG solutions, Plan-RAG
019             demonstrates significant improvements in reducing hallucinations and bolstering
020             attribution due to its structured sub-query decomposition. Overall, Plan-RAG of-
021             fers a new perspective on integrating external knowledge in LMs while ensuring
022             attribution by design, contributing towards more reliable LM-based systems.

073
074   To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel framework—Planning-guided Retrieval Aug-
075   mented Generation (Plan-RAG). We augment the conventional retrieve-then-reason paradigm to
076   plan-then-retrieve paradigm, fundamentally altering how LLMs interact with external knowledge.
077   Unlike traditional query decomposition methods such as RA-ISF (Liu et al., 2024) and RQ-RAG
078
      (Chan et al., 2024) that generate isolated or sequential sub-queries. Plan-RAG formulates a compre-
      hensive reasoning plan represented as a directed acyclic graph (DAG). This reasoning DAG decom-
079
      poses the main query into interrelated atomic sub-queries, providing a computational structure that
080
      enables efficient information sharing between sub-queries (see Fig. 1). In addition, the reasoning
081   DAG characterizes conditional independence relationships between queries, facilitating paralleliza-
082   tion, resulting in more efficient generation. Moreover, it provides improved explainability and de-
083   buggability, attribution, and efficient backtracking for correcting specific segments of the responses.
084   We cover these in detail in Sec. 3.1 and showcase the comparison of the key features between re-
085   cently proposed RAG frameworks and Plan-RAG in Table 1.
086
      In addition, unlike recent proposals for improving RAG (Asai et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2024;
087   Liu et al., 2024), Plan-RAG avoids the expensive step of finetuning an LM. It can work with any
088   pretrained LM, by incorporating a set of independent plug-and-play experts: critic expert, relevance
089   expert, etc.. The critic expert enables on-demand retrievals, assessing when additional information
090   is needed. The relevance expert refines the retrieval process, ensuring the most relevant documents
091   are selected. Notably, the critic expert enables on-demand retrievals, while the atomic nature of the
092   sub-queries limits retrieval to a single document. These features inherently promote attribution and
093   reduce hallucination. In Sec. 3.2, we discuss the experts and their benefits in detail.
094   Contributions (i) We propose augmenting the retrieve-then-reason paradigm to a plan-then-
095   retrieve paradigm, offering a new perspective on integrating external knowledge in LLMs. (ii) We
096   introduce a reasoning DAG that inherently enhances attribution and debuggability capability.
097   (iii) We demonstrate reduced hallucinations attributed to the plug-and-play experts and atomic
098   nature of sub-queries. (iv) We present a general and practical framework, as Plan-RAG uses a
099   frozen LLM without any assumptions.

      3.1     R EASONING PLAN : DAG
199
      At the core of Plan-RAG is a reasoning plan represented as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), gen-
200   erated by an LLM using a suitable prompt (see App. C.2). The DAG is structured so that the root
201   nodes can be answered independently, while each subsequent node can be answered based on the
202   answers to its parent nodes. Thus, the reasoning plan follows Markov assumption. Formally, the
203   reasoning DAG is represented as G(V, E) where V represents the set of generated subqueries for a
204   given query, and E denotes the edges. The answer to any subquery q ∈ V can be computed as,
205
                                 G(q) = f (G(Pa(v)), q, Dq ) ;       G = fReasoning (Q)                           (1)
206
207   where Pa(q) refers to all parents of q in the DAG and Dq represents the retrieved documents for
208   q, if any, and Q is the main query. The function f (typically the generator LM, fGenerator ) generates
209   the response text using the subquery, the responses from its parent subqueries, and the document
210   retrievals. When applied recursively, f provides a mechanism to generate the response to a query
211   by traversing the DAG from the root nodes to the leaves. In practice, G(v) may be computed in an
212
      auto-regressive manner with potentially multiple retrievals after each k-tokens as described above.
213   An example of the reasoning DAG is shown in Fig. 3. Each node is numbered as ⟨i.j⟩ where i refers
214   to the depth of the node from the root and j refers to its index among the nodes at the same depth. A
215   notable feature of the reasoning DAG is the use of a special tag ⟨AI.J⟩ that enables dynamic subquery
      generation. In the tag ⟨AI.J⟩, I and J are the integer values representing the Question IDs that are

      3.2   E XPERTS
220
221   Plan-RAG incorporates a set of independent, plug-and-play experts that enhance its capabilities and
222   helps in addressing key challenges in traditional RAG systems. These experts work in concert to
223   boost the accuracy, reliability, relevance, and interpretability of LLM-generated responses. Below,
224   we detail the role of each expert and how they contribute to the overall framework’s performance.
225
      1. Dynamic Query Expert: The dynamic query expert is responsible for generating subqueries
226
         by embedding the answers into the corresponding tags within the subquery. In addition to
227      generating the subquery, it captures the Markovian dependencies between subqueries, ensuring
228      that only the relevant information is passed while irrelevant details are abstracted. Formally, the
229      process is defined as q̃ ← EDynamicQuery (q, Tq ), where q̃ is the dynamically generated subquery, q
230      is the subquery within the DAG containing the special tag ⟨AI.J⟩, and Tq represents the set of
231      question-answer pairs associated to the special tags in q.
232   2. Critic Expert: The critic expert enables on-demand retrievals by assessing when additional in-
233      formation is needed during the generation process. This expert plays a crucial role in identifying
234      knowledge gaps, triggering retrievals, and reducing hallucination. By analyzing the current con-
235      text and the generation task, the critic determines when the LLM lacks sufficient information to
236      provide an accurate response. Upon identification, the critic initiates the retrieval process, ensur-
237      ing that the system acquires the necessary information dynamically. Formally, C ← ECritic (G, q̃),
238
         where G is the current generation, q is the (sub)query, and C is a boolean representing whether re-
         trieval is required or not. The critic expert can be configured to run after one sentence or k-tokens.
239
240   3. Relevance Expert: The relevance expert refines the retrievals, ensuring the selection of the
241      most relevant document to the subquery at hand. Its key tasks include relevance scoring, ranking
242
         and selection. The expert reduces the context window usage by ranking and selecting only the
         most relevant documents. Formally, r⋆ ← ERelevance (G, q̃, r), where G is the current generation,
243
         q̃ is the (sub)query, r is the set of retrieved document, and r⋆ is the set of relevant documents
244
         obtained from the retriever fRetriever . The relevance can be configured to output either a single
245      document or a set of relevant documents, depending on the task requirements.
246
      4. Aggregator: The aggregator expert combines multiple answers to a set of subqueries to
247
         generate a cohesive and comprehensive response to the original query. It plays a key role in
248      subquery integration and ensuring a balanced, holistic final response. The expert ensures that
249      the final response addresses all aspects of the original query in a balanced and thorough manner.
250      Formally, G ← EAggregator (q, G), where G is the combined generation, q is the set of queries, and
251      G is the set of their respective generations.
252
253
      All these experts work in tandem to create a robust and adaptive system, where each component
      plays a clear role in improving the overall performance of Plan-RAG. Plan-RAG reduces hallu-
254
      cination by incorporating on-demand retrievals and relevance expert, decreasing the likelihood of
255
      generating false or unsupported information. The system improves attribution by more easily trac-
256   ing generated content back to specific retrievals, enhancing the interpretability and trustworthiness
257   of the outputs. Plan-RAG ensures enhanced relevance through expert-guided retrieval and relevance
258   expert, guaranteeing that the most pertinent information is used in the generation process.
259
      As shown in Alg. 1 and Alg. 2, in the default setting, Plan-RAG involves 1 GPT-4o class to get the
260
      reasoning DAG and then for each node, the critic expert is called after every k-tokens (or at end-
261
      of-sentence). If it predicts that retrievals are needed, then the retriever is invoked and subsequently
262   the relevance expert is called. The dynamic query expert is only called once per subquery in case
263   it contains a special dependency tag, and the aggregator expert is called once for the entire query.
264   Therefore, for a reasoning DAG with n nodes, we expect O(2ns + n) calls where s is the number of
265   sentences/k-length phrases in the generation per node.

289   Attribution by design: The atomic nature of sub-queries improves attribution by limiting retrieval
290   to a single document per generation. In its default configuration, the relevance is constrained to se-
291
      lect only one relevant document per subquery, defined as r⋆ =ERelevance (Ḡq , q̃, r), where |r⋆ |=1. This
      ensures that each subquery generation is directly linked to a single retrieved document, establishing
292
      a clear, one-to-one mapping between the document and the subquery’s response. This setup guaran-
293
      tees attribution by design, allowing easy traceability of each generated response back to its specific
294   source document. When the relevance expert is permitted to retrieve multiple documents, this direct
295   attribution feature diminishes. However, empirical results indicate that even in these configurations,
296   the relevance expert typically selects only one document. This is largely due to the atomic nature
297   of the subqueries, where the relevant information for each subquery tends to be contained within a
298   single document. We showcase this with an experiment which we discuss in detail in App. E.1.
299
      Efficiency: The reasoning DAG enhances efficiency by leveraging queries that are on the same
300   depth within the DAG, or on the independent paths in the DAG. This significantly reduces latency,
301   and improves context handling through relevant flow of information i.e. abstracting unnecessary in-
302   formation such as subqueries, their responses, retrieved data that are not related. Plan-RAG achieves
303   greater efficiency in context utilization as compared to vanilla RAG models as well as RAG frame-
304   works like RA-ISF and RQ-RAG. By employing a focused approach where typically only one highly
305   relevant retrieval is used, Plan-RAG maximizes the use of the limited context window available to
306   SLMs. This targeted use of context allows the system to handle more complex queries and maintain
307   coherence over longer interactions without the need for extensive context management.
308   Debuggability: The reasoning DAG provides a robust mechanism for identifying and rectifying
309   erroneous generations by backtracking through the paths from the leaf nodes to the root node. This
310   allows us to analyze the subqueries and their corresponding responses at each step. Upon identifying
311   the error node, we can address the issue by providing additional context or clarifying the subquery,
312   among other strategies. After adjustments, we regenerate the outputs only for the affected path
313   and rerun the aggregator. This iterative debugging process enhances the explainability of the RAG
314
      system.More broadly, the reasoning DAG maps how various pieces of information contribute to the
      final generation.We demonstrate this debug-and-backtrack capability in Fig. 4, where Plan-RAG
315
      initially generates an incorrect response. However, by analyzing the DAG and its corresponding
316
      subqueries, we are able to identify and rectify the error, ultimately leading to a correct generation.

      Baselines and competing methods We evaluate the performance of Plan-RAG method against two
338
      recently proposed RAG methods that have achieved state-of-the-art results on the datasets above:
339
      Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) and RQ-RAG (Chan et al., 2024), outperforming SAIL (Luo et al.),
340   Toolformer (Schick et al., 2024), Alpaca models, and proprietary LLMs like Perplexity.ai and Chat-

342   are present in App. C.1. We also compare with the following baseline methods: (1) Vanilla LLMs
343   (i.e. with no retrieval), namely GPT-3.5, Llama2-7b-chat, Llama2-13b-chat, and Llama3-8B-instruct

347   retrieve top 10 documents and limit the relevant retrievals to maximum 1 using the relevance expert.
348   We employ the GPT-4o model for generating the query plan (DAG); and the Llama3-instruct8B
349   model for all the other experts in our method.
350
      Retriever We use the official Contriever-MS MARCO (Izacard et al., 2022) retriever. It consists of
351
      embeddings based on the 2018 English Wikipedia. The Wikipedia articles are segmented into non-
352   overlapping 100-word segments. For all vanilla LLM and RAG baselines, we retrieve 10 documents.
353   We retrieve 10 documents for Plan-RAG as well, but a relevance expert selects the single most
354   relevant document. In the relevance expert |r⋆ | ≥ 1 setting, it selects all documents deemed relevant.
355
      Evaluation metrics We employ three evaluation metrics: Accuracy, F1 score, and LLM-Eval. For
356
      accuracy, we consider an answer correct if the predicted answer contains the correct answer. This
357
      metric provides a relaxed version of exact-match, allowing for some flexibility in answer phrasing.
358   The F1 score is calculated based on the overlap between the prediction and the true answer, balancing
359   precision and recall. In addition, we utilize the recently developed LLM-Eval method (Lin & Chen,
360   2023), where we employ GPT-3.5-Turbo to compare the answers. This approach leverages the
361   language understanding capabilities of LLMs for evaluation. Due to computational costs, we apply
362   LLM-Eval to a subset of experimental datasets. The details are discussed in App. D.

400   Attribution Plan-RAG supports attribution by design, ensuring a one-to-one mapping between
401
      generation and the retrieved document. For this, we analyze the frequency of Plan-RAG generating
      answers with proper attribution to a retrieved passage. Using PopQA, we sample correctly answered
402
      queries and check whether generated answer is a substring of the corresponding retrieved document.
403
      This setup is inspired by Asai et al. (2023). Our experiment reveal that Plan-RAG correctly attributes
404   76% of its generations to the retrieved passage (i.e., 76% of the queries have the answer as a substring
405   in the retrieved documents), while 12% of answers come from its world knowledge (i.e., there is no
406   retrieve document). Consequently, only 12% of answers fall outside the retrieved passage (i.e.,
407   answer not present as a substring in the retrieve document) or require reasoning over the retrieved
408   data. These results demonstrate that Plan-RAG provides correct attribution or explicitly uses world
409   knowledge at-least 88% of the time, highlighting its strong attribution capability. We further evaluate
410   Plan-RAG using a random sample of 1500 HotpotQA queries and experiment with the configuration
411   employing a relevance expert (|r⋆ |≥1). In this setup, the expert returns a set of relevant documents
412
      rather than a single document, potentially compromising accuracy by losing the one-to-one mapping
      between retrieved documents and generated answers. However, we observe that due to the atomic
413
      nature of the subqueries, the relevance expert selects a single retrieved document 88% of the time.
414
      Thus, it maintains a high level of attribution even in this multi-document setting. This finding
415   suggests that Plan-RAG’s approach of generating atomic subqueries naturally gravitates towards
416   attribution even when given the option to use multiple documents. We detail the results in Table 5.

443   4.2   A BLATION S TUDY
444
      We conduct a series of ablation studies to evaluate the effectiveness of key components in the Plan-
445   RAG framework. Specifically, we focus on two critical elements: the critic expert and the relevance
446   expert. These studies aim to quantify the impact of each component on the overall system perfor-
447   mance, measured in terms of accuracy and F1 score.
448
      Effectiveness of the Critic Expert We evaluate the efficacy of the critic expert in Plan-RAG
449
      using 1500 random queries from HotpotQA. We compare two configurations: (1) a critic expert that
450
      dynamically decides whether to trigger retrievals, and (2) a baseline that consistently retrieves after
451   generating k-tokens. Both setups are constrained to a single retrieval (|r∗ |=1) per subquery. The
452   critic expert configuration achieved an accuracy of 36.60 and an F1 score of 40.72, while the baseline
453   attained an accuracy of 37.13 and an F1 score of 41.52. Across the 1500 queries (3926 subqueries),
454   the critic expert triggered 2530 retrievals, compared to 3163 in the always-retrieve setup. This rep-
455   resents a 600 reduction in retrievals. Notably, this significant decrease in retrievals resulted in only a
456   marginal performance drop of 0.5% in accuracy and 0.80 in F1 score. These results demonstrate that
457   the critic expert can improve retrieval efficiency while maintaining near-equivalent performance.
458   Effectiveness of the Relevance Expert We evaluate the relevance expert in the Plan-RAG frame-
459   work using 1500 random queries from HotpotQA. Two configurations are compared: (1) with a
460   relevance expert, and (2) a no relevance expert using all retrievals. Both setups initially retrieved
461   10 documents per subquery. The expert configuration significantly outperformed the no relevance
462   expert, achieving 39.33 accuracy and 42.01 F1 score, compared to 31.6 accuracy and 36.18 F1 score
463   for the no relevance expert configuration. The performance gap, despite the second configuration us-
464   ing more documents, can be attributed to the reasoning limitations of the Llama3-8B model and the
465
      negative impact of noisy retrievals on generation. These results highlight the crucial role of the rele-
      vance expert in enhancing performance by effectively filtering and providing only relevant retrievals.

468   5     D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we present Planning-guided Retrieval Augmented Generation (Plan-RAG), a novel
471   framework designed to tackle critical challenges in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), ad-
472   dressing performance and hallucinations in complex queries, and lack of attribution. Unlike tradi-
473   tional RAG systems that follow a retrieve-then-reason approach, Plan-RAG shifts to a plan-then-
474
      retrieve paradigm. This shift facilitates a more structured, efficient, and interpretable methodology
      for managing complex queries. Key innovations of Plan-RAG include a reasoning plan represented
475
      as a DAG and a set of plug-and-play experts. These advancements yield significant improvements
476
      across various critical dimensions like performance, attribution, as evidenced by the experimental
477   results. The plug-and-play experts allow for seamless substitution with alternative or emerging lan-
478   guage models, ensuring that Plan-RAG remains adaptable to evolving technologies. Furthermore,
479   the framework’s design enables easy integration with various language models without necessitating
480   fine-tuning, establishing it as a versatile and practical solution. Future work will focus on incorpo-
481   rating additional experts capable of early exiting from the reasoning DAG, developing specialized
482   experts for mathematical calculations and logical reasoning, and implementing dynamic expert al-
483   location conditioned on the input query.
484   Reproducibility statement We will open-source the code for Plan-RAG upon acceptance. We
485   provide all the expert prompts in the Appendix and use the official code for Self-RAG and RQ-RAG.

664
665   For multi-hop queries, we focus on the three datasets: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), StrategyQA
666   (Geva et al., 2021), and Arc-Challenge (Clark et al., 2018).
667   HotpotQA It is a multi-hop datasets from Wikipedia. The questions are diverse and not constrained
668   to any pre-existing knowledge bases or knowledge schemas. HotpotQA is a question-answering
669   dataset collected on the English Wikipedia, containing 7405 total queries in the dev-fullwiki setup.
670   Although, each question in the dataset comes with two gold paragraphs, as well as a list of sentences
671   in these paragraphs that crowd workers identify as supporting facts necessary to answer the question,
672   we in the experiments do not use them and use contriever to fetch the relevant documents.
673   StrategyQA It is a question-answering benchmark where multiple reasoning steps are required in
674   order to answer the question. Also, the answer should be inferred using a strategy. Questions are
675   short, topic-diverse, and cover a wide range of strategies. We use the dataset that is available on the
676   Self-RAG repository Asai et al. (2023). The dataset consists of 2,234 question-answer pairs, each
677   consisting of a strategy question.
678   Arc-Challenge It is a multiple-choice reasoning dataset created from scientific exam. It is a subset
679   of the broader ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge) dataset and is considered more challenging due to
680   the inclusion of harder questions that often require external knowledge. We use the dataset that is
681   available on the Self-RAG repository Asai et al. (2023). The dataset consists of total 1095 queries.

      C.1    C OMPETITIVE METHOD DETAILS
784
785   Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023): It is an open source framework wherein the base Llama2 models are
786   trained to learn special reflection tokens. The reflection tokens are then used to judge the requirement
787   for retrievals, relevance of the retrieved documents and the accuracy of the output. We tested Self-
788   RAG7b and Self-RAG13b on both single and multi-hop datasets, judging it using F1, subset match
789   as well as LLM-Eval. We use 10 documents as context; these are retrieved using contriever at the
790   beginning of each query iteration. The temperature was set to zero to maintain non-stochasticity.
791   RQ-RAG (Chan et al., 2024) It is an open source framework where in the base Llama2 model is
792   trained to enable it to dynamically refine search queries through rewriting, decomposing, and clari-
793   fying ambiguities. Control tokens are used to direct the generation process. Furthermore, they use
794   three different sampling methods which includes selection based on perplexity (PPL), confidence,
795   and an ensemble approach, in order to select the final answer. A total of three documents are re-
796   trieved at each depth for any given query and the maximum depth is set to 2. Both F1 and subset
797   match are used to judge the models accuracy on single and multi-hop datasets.

804

1045   E.1     R ELEVANCE E XPERT
1046
       We conducted experiments on 1500 randomly selected queries from the HotpotQA dataset, retriev-
1047
       ing k=10 documents per query and thereafter using the relevance expert (|r⋆ | ≥ 1) to get the set
1048
       of relevant documents r⋆ . The relevance expert returned a set of relevant retrievals, and the goal is
1049   to observe how often the retriever selected only one document due to the atomic nature of the sub-
1050   queries. In these cases, relevant information for each subquery tends to reside in a single document.
1051
1052
       Our findings reveal that 88.5% of the subqueries retrieved at most one relevant document (|r⋆ | ≤ 1),
       while only 11.5% retrieved more than one document (|r⋆ | > 1). This demonstrates that even when
1053
       multiple documents are available, the majority of generations maintain a one-to-one mapping with
1054
       a document, preserving attribution. Detailed statistics are shown in Table 5.
1055
1056
       E.2     E FFECTIVENESS OF THE C RITIC E XPERT
1057
1058   We conduct an experiment on 1500 randomly selected queries from the HotpotQA dataset, compar-
1059   ing two configurations: one where the critic expert triggers retrievals, and another where retrieval
1060   occurs after every k tokens. In both setups, we retrieve 10 documents using the Contriever retriever,
1061   and the relevance expert selects the most relevant document, i.e. |r⋆ | = 1. The objective is to
1062   demonstrate the effectiveness of the critic expert by showing that it reduces the number of retrievals
1063   while maintaining similar accuracy.
1064   Across the 1,500 queries, there were 3,926 subqueries. The always-retrieve setup used a total of
1065   3,163 retrievals, whereas the critic expert setup used 2,530 retrievals. The critic expert configuration
1066   achieved an accuracy of 36.60 and an F1 score of 40.72, while the always-retrieve configuration
1067   achieved an accuracy of 37.13 and an F1 score of 41.52. This represents a substantial reduction of
1068   600 retrievals, with only a minor performance drop of 0.5% in accuracy and 0.80 in F1 score. These
1069   results highlight that the critic expert can significantly enhance retrieval efficiency while maintaining
1070
       nearly equivalent performance. Detailed statistics are provided in Table 6. For both the experiments
       the default version of Plan-RAG was used with GPT-4o call for generating the reasoning DAG and
1071
       all the experts were Llama3-8B-instruct model.

       E.3     E FFECTIVENESS OF THE R ELEVANCE E XPERT
1074
1075   We conduct an experiment on 1500 randomly selected queries from the HotpotQA dataset, compar-
