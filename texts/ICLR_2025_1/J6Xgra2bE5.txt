009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                   Large Language Models (LLMs) can perform few-shot learning using either
012                   optimization-based approaches or In-Context Learning (ICL). Optimization-
013                   based methods often suffer from overfitting, as they require updating a large
014                   number of parameters with limited data. In contrast, ICL avoids overfitting but
015                   typically underperforms compared to optimization-based methods and is highly
016                   sensitive to the selection, order, and format of demonstration examples. To over-
017                   come these challenges, we introduce Context-aware Prompt Tuning (CPT), a
018
                      method inspired by ICL, Prompt Tuning (PT), and adversarial attacks. CPT
                      builds on the ICL strategy of concatenating examples before the input, extend-
019
                      ing it by incorporating PT-like learning to refine the context embedding through
020
                      iterative optimization, extracting deeper insights from the training examples.
021                   Our approach carefully modifies specific context tokens, considering the unique
022                   structure of the examples within the context. In addition to updating the context
023                   with PT-like optimization, CPT draws inspiration from adversarial attacks, ad-
024                   justing the input based on the labels present in the context while preserving the
025                   inherent value of the user-provided data. To ensure robustness and stability dur-
026                   ing optimization, we employ a projected gradient descent algorithm, constrain-
027                   ing token embeddings to remain close to their original values and safeguard-
028                   ing the quality of the context. Our method has demonstrated superior accuracy
029
                      across multiple classification tasks using various LLM models, outperforming
                      existing baselines and effectively addressing the overfitting challenge in few-
030
                      shot learning.

072   1    I NTRODUCTION
073
074   Fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) is a widely used technique that adapts models to spe-
075   cific tasks by modifying all their parameters. Despite its effectiveness, this approach requires han-
076   dling billions of parameters, which can be prohibitively expensive and inefficient, especially in terms
077   of computational resources and storage, making it challenging to scale effectively.
078
      To address the limitations of fine-tuning, several parameter-efficient methods have been introduced.
079   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) reduces the number of trainable parameters by
080   learning a low-rank decomposition. However, it still requires a portion of the model’s weights, which
081   remains burdensome, particularly since state-of-the-art models like Llama3 (AI@Meta, 2024) and
082   GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024) typically range from 7 billion to 1.7 trillion parameters. Another approach,
083   Prompt Tuning (PT) (Lester et al., 2021), offers a more efficient solution by updating a small set
084   of learnable token embeddings, which are concatenated before the input, while leaving the LLM’s
085   weights completely untouched. Alternatively, In-Context Learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) ad-
086   justs the model to new tasks without any parameter updates, relying on the straightforward con-
087
      catenation of training examples with the input context. Despite its computational efficiency, recent
      studies Zhang et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023); Perez et al. (2021) indicate that ICL falls short com-
088
      pared to supervised fine-tuning methods. To leverage the strengths of both PT and ICL, Instruction
089
      Prompt Tuning (IPT) (Singhal et al., 2022) was introduced. This approach involves concatenating
090   both learnable tokens and context to the input, training only the learnable tokens while keeping the
091   context and model weights frozen.
092
093
      Despite the recent advancements in parameter-efficient methods, determining the optimal method for
      few-shot learning remains highly unsettled. On one hand, optimization-based methods such as fine-
094
      tuning, LoRA, PT, and IPT are prone to overfitting, especially in few-shot settings where the number
095
      of trainable parameters is large – a condition known to exacerbate overfitting, as demonstrated in
096   fig. 1. Meanwhile, In-Context Learning (ICL) mitigates overfitting by avoiding model parameter
097   updates; however, it does not match the performance of other methods. Consequently, the most
098   effective method for various different scenarios remains uncertain (Sun et al., 2023).
099
      Context-aware Prompt Tuning (CPT), fuses concepts from In-Context Learning (ICL), Prompt Tun-
100
      ing (PT), and adversarial attacks (Blau et al., 2022; 2023; Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Athalye et al.,
101
      2018; Biggio et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kurakin et al., 2016; Nguyen et al., 2015; Madry
102   et al., 2017; Rebuffi et al., 2021; Gowal et al., 2020) into a cohesive approach, with the main dif-
103   ferences from the baselines illustrated in fig. 2. CPT follows the ICL technique of concatenating
104   training examples prior to the input. Similarly to PT, CPT updates only the context token embed-
105   dings through iterative optimization, leveraging again the training examples present in the context.
106   However, CPT carefully refines the context tokens while accounting for the context’s unique struc-
107   ture, keeping the label tokens intact, preserving their role as the ground truth. To effectively reduce
      overfitting and enhance performance, CPT adopts two strategies inspired by adversarial attacks: in-

         embeddings. By including context labels in the loss, CPT refines input adjustments, guiding the

         model to optimize the entire context rather than focusing solely on the training label. To further

133      by Zhao et al. (2021), where the model tends to prioritize examples located nearer the end of the
134      context. We recommend leveraging this property by applying an exponentially decaying weight to
135      examples as they approach the beginning of the context, thereby increasing the emphasis on more
136      recent examples in the optimization process.
137      We validate our method through a comprehensive evaluation of several classification tasks and in-
138      clude extensive ablations. We demonstrate that CPT outperforms other baselines across nearly every
139      scenario, as shown in fig. 3. We use diverse templates and seeds, which is crucial due to ICL’s sen-
140      sitivity to training examples and format selection, as highlighted by Sun et al. (2023); Zhao et al.
141      (2021).
142      To summarize, our key contributions are as follows:
143
144                   • We propose a novel few-shot method called Context-aware Prompt Tuning that enhances
145                     ICL with PT and adversarial methodologies. Our method carefully optimizes the context
146                     tokens while accounting for their unique structure.
147                   • Our method incorporates ground truth labels from the context into the loss term, optimizes
148                     with projected gradient descent, and applies recency-bias-inspired loss weighting.
149
                      • We achieve state-of-the-art results on several classification datasets and provide extensive
150
                        ablation studies for each design choice of our method.

153       2          R ELATED W ORK
154
155      Fine-Tuning Fine-tuning is a popular and effective method for adjusting LLMs to specific tasks.
156      Standard fine-tuning (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Howard & Ruder, 2018; Liu et al.,
157      2019; Lan et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019) retrains the model with new data.
158
         However, a key disadvantage is the large number of parameters that must be stored.
159      Efficient Fine-Tuning To alleviate the computational burden of fine-tuning, Adapter-BERT
160      (Houlsby et al., 2019) proposes training only the adapter layers inserted into the model, while BitFit
161      (Zaken et al., 2021) focuses on fine-tuning just the bias terms. Delta Tuning (Ding et al., 2022)
         explores parameter-efficient methods that adjust only a small portion of a model’s parameters. Low-


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Rank Adaptation methods (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) introduces a novel low-rank adaptation tech-
163   nique, where additional low-rank matrices are added to the weights during training. This allows
164   the model to fine-tune only these matrices, reducing the number of trainable parameters signifi-
165   cantly. VERA (Kopiczko et al., 2023) builds on LoRA by incorporating adaptive learning rates.
166   Compacter Karimi Mahabadi et al. (2021) leverages hypercomplex layers, and LoRA-Pro (Wang &
167   Liang, 2024) further refines optimization. Despite these advancements, large models like GPT-3,
168   which contain 175B parameters, require updating millions of parameters, such as 17.5M for LoRA.
169   Prompt Tuning (PT) Unlike fine-tuning methods, PT reduces the number of trainable parameters
170   by introducing learnable tokens optimized while keeping the model’s weights frozen. Lester et al.
171   (2021) propose appending continuous prompts to the input and optimizing them, while P-tuning (Liu
172   et al., 2023) and Prefix Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) extend this concept by incorporating learnable
173   tokens at intermediate layers. More recently, Wang et al. (2023) introduced the idea of training
174   a single prompt to be shared across multiple tasks. Although these methods significantly reduce
175   the number of trainable parameters, they face challenges in few-shot learning Gu et al. (2021) and
176   provide limited interpretability for the learned continuous tokens (Ghosal et al., 2024; Khashabi
177
      et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2022).
178   In-Context Learning (ICL) In contrast to earlier methods, ICL (Brown et al., 2020) avoids op-
179   timization entirely. Instead, it concatenates task-specific examples before the input, allowing the
180   model to learn a new task purely through observation, leveraging its pre-trained knowledge. Despite
181   its advantages, ICL has limitations, often underperforming compared to optimization-based methods
182   (Liu et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023).
183   Instruction Prompt Tuning (IPT) IPT (Singhal et al., 2022) combines key elements of PT and ICL,
184   utilizing learnable tokens that are optimized during training alongside static context tokens, similar
185   to ICL. The concept of using both soft and hard prompts was previously introduced by PPT (Gu et al.,
186   2021) and PTR (Han et al., 2022). Yet, IPT has struggled to consistently surpass PT in performance
187   (Sun et al., 2023). While our method shares similarities with IPT, we focus on optimizing context
188   tokens without introducing additional learnable tokens, and we are also leveraging context labels
189
      in the process. Another key difference lies in the optimization process, where our loss includes a
      regularization term, and we employ projected gradient descent to ensure the output stays close to
190
      the user-supplied reliable input.

      3.1   I NPUT P REPARATION
196
197   Our method takes as input a few-shot classification dataset containing N examples. Each example
198   consists of a pairing of x (an instruction) and y (a label). We embed (x, y) using input, output, and
199   separation templates, converting them into readable text that LLMs better understand, as done in
200   ICL Brown et al. (2020). The input and output templates, denoted Ti and To , along with separators
201   Sintra and Sinter , are provided in appendix E. To embed a single example (x, y) using the template, we
202   concatenate the input x embedded in Ti with Sintra , followed by the output y embedded in To , and
203   finally Sinter , resulting in XEmbi = [Ti (xi ), Sintra , To (yi ), Sinter ]. To generate the complete context,
204   we concatenate all XEmbi , forming XContext = [XEmbi ]N       i=1 . To construct a complete training example,
205
      we randomly select an embedded example from the training set XEmbi , and concatenate it after the
      context, resulting XTraini = [XContext , XEmbi ], which is then fed into the LLM. This process is also
206
207
      visualized in fig. 4. We supply additional concrete examples in appendix G.
208   Above, we described how we construct a training example XTraini , as a text sequence. However,
209   before feeding it into the model, we must process the text through a tokenizer, which splits the
210   text into tokens and returns an embedding vector for each token. Each example contains six types
211   of tokens: input, input template, intra-separator, output, output template, and inter-separator. For
212   simplicity, we ignore the separators and the fact that each part usually contains multiple tokens. For
                                                                                         (k) (k) (k) (k)
213   each training example i and its sub-example k, we focus on four token types: tIi , tITi , tOi , tOTi ,
214   which represent the input, input template, output, and output template, respectively. Each training
215   example i consists of N + 1 sub-examples, N sub-examples in the context and one training sub-
      example at the end.

243
244   In this section, we discuss the optimization process of our method, which is inspired by Adversarial
245
      Attacks (AT) Madry et al. (2017). Each AT step can be divided into two parts: optimization and

246
      in the second step, the attack limits its changes to avoid detection. Similarly, our method is split
247
      into two parts: optimization, including loss design, as discussed in section 3.2.1, and controlling the
248   token updates, as detailed in section 3.2.2.

      3.2.2    C ONTROLLED T OKEN E MBEDDING O PTIMIZATION
276
277   As mentioned in section 3.2.1, we use all the labels in each XTraini to optimize the tokens within
278   the context. However, some tokens in the context represent labels, and we do not update these label
279   tokens, as they carry valuable ground truth information. Instead, we update the other tokens in the
280   context, carefully managing these updates to ensure controlled and precise modifications.
281
      The controlled modification is designed with two key objectives. First, we trust the user to provide
282   meaningful examples representing the task, so the context should stay close to the user’s intent, min-
283   imizing significant changes. Second, few-shot optimization can lead to overfitting without proper
284   regularization. Controlled modification addresses both issues: it acts as a regularization mecha-
285   nism while preventing overfitting. For instance, as changes become smaller, our method converges
286   to ICL, which is robust against overfitting. We achieve this by using projected gradient descent,
287   which limits each token’s embedding change to an ℓ2 norm of size ϵ after each optimization step.

         5.2          S TANDARD D EVIATION
400
401      Standard deviation (std) plays a crucial role in few-shot learning due to the sensitivity of these
402      methods to both the training examples and the chosen template (Zhao et al., 2021; Voronov et al.,
403      2024). In fig. 5, we present accuracy along with two types of std bars: black bars represent the
404      mean std across different templates, while blue bars represent the mean std across different seeds.
405      We demonstrate that CPT significantly improves accuracy across various models and datasets in a
406      statistically significant manner. More information is presented in appendix A.
407      Our method’s standard deviation performs equivalently to other methods in most cases, while in cer-
408      tain cases, such as with DBpedia, CPT exhibits both higher accuracy and lower std, reinforcing its
409      robustness in complex tasks. However, the sensitivity of our method does not follow a clear pattern
410      across random seeds or templates. For instance, while randomness in templates and training exam-
411      ples has an equal influence on std in DBpedia and TREC , SST-2 shows a higher std for template
412      randomness, and AG News is more sensitive to variations in training examples.

468

      6     D ISCUSSIONS
474
475   CPT demonstrates significant advancements in few-shot learning by integrating ICL with PT and ad-
476   versarial strategies, refining context embeddings. Unlike traditional fine-tuning and other parameter-
477   efficient approaches, CPT optimizes only the context tokens, making it particularly effective in few-
478
      shot settings, where overfitting is a concern. CPT achieves improved generalization across a wide
      variety of tasks, demonstrates a significant advancement, and offers meaningful insights for future
479
      few-shot learning methods.
480
481
482   Limitation & Future Work The computational cost associated with the iterative optimization of
483   context embeddings is significant compared to ICL. Additionally, similar to ICL and IPT, CPT is
484   limited in the number of examples it can handle, as memory consumption scales with context length.
485   In contrast, traditional methods are better suited for larger datasets. Future work could explore more
      efficient optimization strategies to reduce computational overhead and improve scalability.
