011

               Language models (LMs) hallucinate. We inquire: Can we detect and mitigate
               hallucinations before they happen? This work answers this research question in the
013
               positive, by showing that the internal representations of LMs provide rich signals
014
               that can be used for this purpose. We introduce FACT C HECKMATE, which preemp-
015            tively detects hallucinations by learning a classifier that predicts whether the LM
016            will hallucinate, based on the model’s hidden states produced over the inputs, before
017            decoding begins. If a hallucination is detected, FACT C HECKMATE then intervenes,
018            by adjusting the LM’s hidden states such that the model will produce more factual
019            outputs. FACT C HECKMATE provides fresh insights that the inner workings of LMs
020            can be revealed by their hidden states. Practically, both the detection and mitigation
021            models in FACT C HECKMATE are lightweight, adding little inference overhead;
022            FACT C HECKMATE proves a more efficient approach for mitigating hallucinations
023
               compared to many post-hoc alternatives. We evaluate FACT C HECKMATE over LMs
               of different scales and model families (including Llama, Mistral, and Gemma),
024
               across a variety of QA datasets from different domains. Our results demonstrate the
025
               effectiveness of leveraging internal representations for early hallucination detection
026            and mitigation, achieving over 70% preemptive detection accuracy. On average,
027            outputs generated by LMs with intervention are 34.4% more factual compared to
028            those without intervention. The average overhead difference in the inference time
029            introduced by FACT C HECKMATE is around 3.16 seconds.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Language models (LMs) hallucinate, a phenomenon where they produce nonfactual or even mislead-
035
      ing outputs that often appear plausible (Ji et al., 2023a; Bang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Zhang
036   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023). Extensive efforts have been devoted
037   to mitigating their hallucination issues (Rawte et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2021). These approaches
038   are mostly reactive, addressing hallucinations after they occur, and often require resampling new
039   outputs (Li et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023), substantially increasing the inference overhead. In
040   addition, these approaches treat the LM as a black box, while relying on external LMs for detecting
041   hallucinations, missing the opportunity to gain deeper insights into the internal workings of these
042   models.
043   Recent findings by Azaria & Mitchell (2023) and Burns et al. (2022) show that probing the LMs’
044   representaions can effectively determine the factuality of their outputs. Marks & Tegmark (2023)
045   observe that the hidden states produced by the middle layers of LMs over complete statements exhibit
046   linear separability in binary factuality classification tasks. However, these studies have a relatively
047   narrow focus, primarily addressing hallucination detection in a reactive manner. A more thorough
048   investigation is needed.
049   The key hypothesis of this paper is that, the LMs’ hidden states reveals valuable information about
050   their internal working mechanisms, and provide signals that can be used to predict whether it is
051   likely to hallucinate before it happens. More formally, we propose FACT C HECKMATE to answer the
052   following research question (RQ): Can we preemptively predict and mitigate hallucinations with LMs’
053   internal representations? FACT C HECKMATE learns a classifier that, taking the models’ hidden states
      over the inputs, predicts whether the model is about to hallucinate. If a hallucination is detected,

402
403   One possible explanation for fθ ’s strong preemptive hallucination detection performance is that it
404   might be classifying the input questions rather than the LMs’ hidden states. It is true that more
405   difficult questions could lead to a higher chances of hallucinations by the LMs. However, our results
406   indicate that it is the LMs’ hidden states, rather than the questions themselves, that drive the success
407
      of fθ .
408   Table 4 summarizes the test accuracies for an fθ trained and tested on the word embedding layer of
409   Llama-2-7B, before any contextualization by the LM. Across the board, the accuracies are close to
410   50% random guess. This confirms that, the model is not skewed towards favoring a certain type of
411   question over another while doing the classification. The difficulty of the question is hence, not a
412   contributing factor to the accuracy calculated by classifying the hidden states.

415
      We explore three modes for aggregating the hidden states before passing them to the classifier: mean
416
      pooling, max pooling, and taking the last token. We see that the mean pooling gives us the best
417   accuracy as shown in Fig 5a. To test how different modes of aggregation work for the preemptive
418   experiments, we compare all the three modes. This is done across the same layer for a the same

422   pooling as our mode of aggregation in all our experiments.

424   5     R ELATED W ORK
425
426   Definitions. In this work, we investigate the phenomenon of hallucinations in language models that
427   generate responses based solely on their parametric knowledge, similar to Azaria & Mitchell (2023).
428   This contrasts with in-context generation scenarios where external knowledge sources are explicitly
429   incorporated within the prompt. We adopt the refined taxonomy proposed by Huang et al. (2023),
430   categorizing hallucinations into Factuality or Faithfulness. FACT C HECKMATE focuses its study on
431   addressing Factuality hallucinations, which are further divided into factual inconsistencies and factual
      fabrications.


                                                                8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Hallucination Detection. Hallucination remains a significant issue undermining the language
433   model’s usefulness. Existing research on hallucination detection has primarily focused on post-
434   processing methods applied after the inference process is completed and often utilizing external
435   knowledge sources for verification, as in (Manakul et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Chern et al., 2023).
436   For instance, Gou et al. (2024) introduce CRITIC, a framework that validates model outputs through
437   tool interaction, and FACTSCORE proposed by Min et al. (2023), is a fine-grained factual accuracy
438   metric that breaks down generated content into atomic facts, assessing their accuracy by comparing
439   them against reliable sources.
440   A recent promising line of research leverages the internal mechanics of language models to detect
441   hallucinations. Works such as (Burns et al., 2024; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Marks & Tegmark, 2023)
442   are pioneering efforts to assess the truthfulness of outputs by examining the hidden states of language
443   models. The work by Meng et al. (2022) locates where factual associations are stored in GPT models.
444   These studies have spurred further research into using LLMs’ internal representations in hallucination
445   detection (Chen et al., 2024a; CH-Wang et al., 2024). For instance, the MIND framework, introduced
446   by Su et al. (2024), generates training data in unsupervised approach for training hidden states based
447
      hallucination detectors. Duan et al. (2024) conducts an experimental examination of the hidden
      states of LLMs when processing factual versus nonfactual responses. Following this line of research,
448
      FACT C HECKMATE showcases the effectiveness of preemptive hallucination detection, i.e. identifying
449
      warning signals several tokens before the hallucinations actually occur, via solely exploiting the
450   language model’s hidden states.
451
452   Hallucination Mitigation. In the realm of hallucination mitigation at inference time, existing
453
      work has explored self-correction and automated feedback approaches, where the language model is
      prompted to fix its generation flaws, with or without leveraging feedback from the model itself or some
454
      external knowledge source, as detailed in (Pan et al., 2023; Dhuliawala et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023b).
455
      A recent approach to mitigating hallucinations involves utilizing activation engineering (Subramani
456   et al., 2022), first applied to hallucination mitigation by Duan et al. (2024). FACT C HECKMATE builds
457   on these findings and explores additional activation engineering techniques to intervene and mitigate
458   hallucinations during inference time.
459
460
461   6    C ONCLUSION
462
463
      In conclusion, FACT C HECKMATE demonstrates that the hidden states of language models encode
464
      rich information that can be used to predict hallucination preemptively, even before they appear in
465   the generated output. In FACT C HECKMATE, leveraging this insight, we develop an intervention
466   mechanism that steers the LM’s generation towards more factual outputs, once the hallucination
467   is detected. We achieve a preemptive hallucination detection accuracy of more than 70%, and an
468   average of 34.4% more factual output by LMs supported by FACT C HECKMATE, compared to the
469   base LMs. FACT C HECKMATE empirically proves the significant potential of utilizing the internal
470   working of LMs, through learning lightweight models for hallucination detection and mitigation,
471   introducing only a negligible average overhead of 3.16 seconds to the inference time.
472
473
474   7    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORK
475
476   We have only looked at the hidden states as an internal component for classification to predict the
477   factuality of a sentence. Exploring other LM’s internal components presents a potential direction
478   for future work. With our detection and intervention experiments, we see that different layers in
479   the LM have varying effects. The classifier fθ and the intervention model gϕ , are sensitive to the
480   hyperparameters selected. To solve this, we want find out a more robust and consistent approach that
481   is less sensitive to varying hyperparameters, to steer the generation of the model towards the truth.
482   Going ahead, we aim to build a pipeline that is more generalizable and is applicable to a variety of do-
483   mains. Expanding our evaluation to include diverse datasets with different distributions could provide
484   valuable insights and a potential future direction for improving model generalizability. FACT C HECK -
485   MATE has shown promising results in question-answering tasks, and it would be interesting to extend
      its application to other tasks, including dialogue-based and long-form generation tasks.
