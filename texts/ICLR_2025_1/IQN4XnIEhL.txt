009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The success of large language models in text processing has inspired their adapta-
012            tion to speech modeling. However, because speech is continuous and complex, it is
013            often discretized into tokens derived from self-supervised speech models. These
014            speech tokens typically focus on the linguistic aspects of speech and neglect its par-
015            alinguistic content. As a result, autoregressive models trained on these tokens may
016            generate speech with suboptimal naturalness. Previous methods attempted to ad-
017            dress this limitation by adding pitch features to speech tokens prior to autoregressive
018            modeling. However, pitch alone cannot fully represent the range of paralinguistic
019            attributes, and selecting the right features requires careful hand-engineering. To
020
               tackle this issue, we propose a variational approach that automatically learns to
               encode these continuous speech attributes to enhance the speech tokens. Our
021
               proposed approach eliminates the need for manual paralinguistic feature selection
022
               and extraction. Moreover, we demonstrate that our proposed approach maintains
023            or improves speech language modeling performance and enhances the naturalness
024            of generated speech compared to baseline approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved tremendous success in text processing OpenAI (2024),
031   offering new ways to interact with machines. This progress has motivated efforts to extend their
032   capabilities to speech to enable more natural spoken interactions with machines. However, modeling
033
      speech presents unique challenges due to its continuous and complex nature. As a result, previous
      works (Lakhotia et al., 2021; Borsos et al., 2023; Maiti et al., 2024) tokenized speech into simpler
034
      discrete units to enable the application of language modeling techniques originally developed for text.
035
      However, these speech tokens are typically derived by performing k-means clustering on features
036   extracted from self-supervised pre-trained speech models, such as HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021). These
037   models primarily capture the linguistic aspects of speech, such as phonetic information, while often
038   overlooking paralinguistic features, such as prosody (Weston et al., 2021). As a result, training an
039   autoregressive model solely with such speech tokens restricts the model’s ability to fully capture and
040   represent the diverse information encoded in speech.
041
      To address this limitation, Kharitonov et al. (2022) augmented the tokens with extracted fundamental
042   frequency (F0 , or pitch) to enable prosody-aware modeling. However, augmenting speech tokens
043   with manually defined paralinguistic attributes can be inherently suboptimal. First, pitch alone cannot
044   capture the full range of paralinguistic information encoded in speech. For instance, energy-related
045   (e.g., loudness, zero-crossing-rate) and spectral-related (e.g., mel-frequency cepstral coefficients)
046   features are also important paralinguistic features (Schuller et al., 2009; 2013; Eyben et al., 2015).
047   Additionally, training an accurate pitch tracker introduces additional complexity (Kim et al., 2018).
048   Instead of relying on hand-engineered paralinguistic features, we propose an approach to learning
049   these features directly from the input signal, within an autoregressive framework. These learned
050   features are optimized to simultaneously: 1) reconstruct the input speech and 2) enhance the au-
051   toregressive modeling process. Our approach allows the learned features to complement discrete
052   speech tokens, removing the need for pre-extracted paralinguistic features as required in previous
053   methods. As a result, our method generates more natural-sounding speech compared to baseline
      models, without sacrificing the meaningfulness of the syntheses.

      2        P RELIMINARIES
055
056
      In this work, we work on mel-spectrogram, and consider vocoding, the act of turning mel-spectrogram
057
      back to raw waveform, as a problem that has already been addressed. We denote the mel-spectrogram
058   as X = (xt ∈ Rdx )Tt=1 , where dx represents the number of filter-banks, T is the total number of time
059   frames in the spectrogram, and xt is the frame at time t. We use Xi:j to denote the sub-sequence
060   (xt )jt=i , and define X1:0 = ∅. Our goal is to model p(X) using a generative approach.
061
062
      Token-based Speech Language Model We describe the general framework of speech language
063
      models that rely on the use of speech tokens, as seen in works like Lakhotia et al. (2021); Borsos
064   et al. (2023); Maiti et al. (2024). This approach consists of three components: a speech tokenizer, an
065   autoregressive model, and a decoder. The speech tokenizer maps X1 to a sequence of discrete speech
066   tokens Zd = (ztd ∈ Nk )Tt=1 , where Nk = {1, 2, . . . , k}, and k is the vocabulary size of the speech
067   tokens. We use p(Zd | X) to denote the implicit distribution of the pre-trained speech tokenizer.
068   The autoregressive model, parameterized by ψ, models the probability of token sequences Zd as
                   QT
069   pψ (Zd ) = t=1 pψ (ztd | Zd1:t−1 ). Finally, the decoder, parameterized by θ, is trained to convert
070   Zd back to X by modeling pθ (X | Zd ). However, this framework is limited to the speech tokens
071   Zd , which primarily capture linguistic information and ignores paralinguistic information. As a
072   result, the decoder θ may struggle with accurate reconstruction, and the autoregressive model ψ can
073   have difficulty incorporating paralinguistic information. To address this limitation, we propose to
074   incorporate the variational autoencoder framework to learn continuous features to complement Zd .

171   From Equation 4, our training objective OELBO consists of three terms: Orec , Lckl , and Ldkl . Orec

      3.3      BALANCING THE LOSS TERMS
180   In Equation 4, the terms Orec , Lckl , and Ldkl can work against each other. For instance, the encoder ϕ
181   optimizes both Orec and Lckl . Maximizing Orec encourages the variational features Zc to encode
182   more information about X, while minimizing Lckl regularize Zc to be simpler for the autoregressive
183   model ψ to predict. Similarly, optimizing Lckl and Ldkl with the autoregressive model ψ a multi-task
184   learning scenario, where ψ learns to predict two different objectives given the same input. Moreover,
185   these terms may operate on different scales due to how the losses are computed, necessitating a
186   balancing mechanism. As a result, inspired by Higgins et al. (2017), we introduce two scalars, β and
187   γ, to balance the loss terms as follows:
188                                        OELBO = Orec − β · Lckl − γ · Ldkl .                                       (5)
189
      Here, β functions similarly to the parameter used in β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017). A larger β favors
190   a simple p(Zc ), while a smaller β encourages the variational features Zc to encode more information
191   about X. Larger γ encourages the autoregressive model ψ to prioritize accurate predictions of
192   Zd over Zc . In practice, we employ a linear warm-up strategy for β, increasing it from zero to
193   its final value during the early stages of training. This approach, inspired by prior works on text
194   generation (Bowman et al., 2016; Fu et al., 2019), helps mitigate posterior collapse. Empirically, we
195   find that this strategy allows for higher values of β without causing Lckl to collapse to zero.

      Diffusion Decoder We model the decoder pθ (X | Z) with Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model
217   (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020). We choose DDPM due to its flexibility in modeling complex distributions.
218   We condition the diffusion process on Z. For back-propagation through the encoder ϕ, we use the
219   reparameterization trick (Kingma & Welling, 2019) to sample from qϕ (Zc | X), and combine it with
220   embedded speech tokens Zd . The outcome is then concatenated with each intermediate layer of the
221   diffusion decoder for conditional diffusion. We train all diffusion decoders with 1000 DDPM steps.
222
223   Utterance Encoder Static features, such as speaker information and recording environments,
224   often vary little across a given utterance. In our current modeling approach, this static information
225   would be redundantly encoded at each time step. To address this issue, we introduce an additional
226   utterance-level feature encoder that encourages Z to focus on time-varying signals. Specifically, we
227   randomly segment a portion of the mel-spectrogram X and feed it to the utterance encoder to produce
228
      an utterance-level embedding. This embedding is then concatenated with Z before being provided to
      the diffusion decoder. The utterance encoder is trained end-to-end with the entire system.

247   We compare our proposed approach to methods that use only speech tokens in the autoregressive
248
      model, as well as methods that use speech tokens with added pitch features in the autoregressive
      model. To ensure a fair comparison, we fix the autoregressive model architecture to be the same for
249
      all methods, varying only the input and output layers. We also use the same configuration for the
250
      diffusion decoder and utterance encoder across all methods. For the neural vocoder (i.e., mapping the

282   4.3      E VALUATION M ETRICS
283
284   We evaluate the comparison methods on both reconstruction and speech continuation. The recon-
285   struction metrics, introduced in Section 4.3.1, involve only the encoder-decoder pair and indicate
286   how much information is preserved in the extracted representations. The remaining metrics focus on
287   speech continuation, which is our primary objective, where the performance of the autoregressive
288
      model is also assessed.
289
290   4.3.1      O BJECTIVE M ETRICS
291   Reconstruction Metrics We use F0 -RMSE, mel-ceptral distortion (MCD), and character error
292   rate (CER) to measure the quality of the reconstructed signal. F0 -RMSE measures the root mean
293   squared difference between the pitch contour of the ground-truth signal and the reconstructed one.
294   We use CREPE Kim et al. (2018) to extract pitch and only consider the voiced parts of the signal
295   when computing the difference. MCD measures the Euclidean distance between the 23 mel-cepstral
296   coefficients (MCEPs) extracted from the ground-truth and reconstructed signals. For calculating CER,
297   we use a pre-trained Whisper Radford et al. (2022) automatic speech recognition model.6 We use
298   the dev-clean and dev-other subsets of LibriSpeech for evaluating reconstruction. To ensure
299
      deterministic results, instead of sampling each ztc from qϕ (ztc | X), we directly use the Gaussian
      mean µϕ (X, t) from Equation 2. In practice, we observed that the stochastic noise of qϕ (ztc | X) has
300
      little effect on the reconstructed syntheses.
301
302
      ZeroSpeech Metrics We adopt the commonly-used metrics (Borsos et al., 2023; Hassid et al.,
303
      2023; Maiti et al., 2024) from the ZeroSpeech challenge (Nguyen et al., 2020): sWUGGY and
304
      sBLIMP to measure language capability objectively. For these two metrics, speech utterances are
305   given in positive-negative pairs, with each model scoring both utterances. The model’s accuracy
306   is the percentage of instances where the positive example receives a higher score than the negative
307   one. sWuggy measures if the model scores a real word higher than a phonetically similar non-word
308   (e.g., “brick” v.s. “blick”). sBLIMP measures if a model scores a grammatically correct sentence
309   higher than a similar but incorrect one (e.g., “the dogs sleep” vs. “the dog sleep”). Both metrics use
310   text-to-speech to generate the examples. In line with Borsos et al. (2023), we evaluate sWUGGY
311   using only words existing in LibriSpeech (referred as the “in-vocab” version). We use the test split for
312   evaluation. See Appendix G for detailed description on how we estimate the scores for the methods.
313
314   Perplexity Metric We use perplexity to evaluate the language modeling capability of our methods.
315   Specifically, perplexity measures the average negative log-likelihood of a test sequence generated
316
      by the language model. We use the dev-clean and dev-other subsets of LibriSpeech for
      computing perplexity.

465   6    R ELATED W ORK
466
467   Emerging speech language models typically use discrete speech tokens for autoregressive modeling.
468   These tokens are often obtained by k-means clustering of features extracted from self-supervised
469   pre-trained models (Hsu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022). Lakhotia et al. (2021) used discrete speech
470   tokens for generative spoken language modeling (GSLM). subsequently, Kharitonov et al. (2022)
471
      enhanced this approach by incorporating pitch information alongside speech tokens as joint inputs
      to the autoregressive model. Our proposed approach improves upon this line of research by using a
472
      variational autoencoder to automatically learn paralinguistic speech attributes in conjunction with the
473
      autoregressive model. Borsos et al. (2023) proposed a two-stage approach for the decoder that used
474   acoustic tokens (Zeghidour et al., 2022; Défossez et al., 2022). This type of framework is also widely
475   used in text-to-speech systems (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). In contrast, our approach
476   focuses on the joint modeling of linguistic and paralinguistic features by enhancing the inputs to the
477   autoregressive model rather than improving the decoder.
478
      Recently, a line of research has emerged focusing on improving speech language models through
479
      the integration of text-based models. Hassid et al. (2023) initialized their speech language model
480   using a pre-trained text-based large language model (LLM). Similarly, Rubenstein et al. (2023); Maiti
481   et al. (2024) expanded the vocabulary of pre-trained text-based LLMs by integrating discrete speech
482   tokens.Building on this, Yang et al. (2023); Du et al. (2024) further explored multi-task training
483   involving text-conditioned generative speech tasks, combining text and audio within a single LLM.
484   We note that our proposed approach takes a different direction but can still be integrated with these
485   approaches. For example, one could initialize the transformer in our autoregressive model using
      parameters from a text-based LLM.

      7   C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this work, we proposed an approach that combines a variational autoencoder with existing token-
489   based speech language models. We conducted experiments to evaluate its effectiveness in terms
490   of language capability and synthesis naturalness. Empirical evaluations suggest that our proposed
491   approach, in contrast with other recent techniques, is capable of producing synthesis with better
492   subjective meaningfulness and naturalness. Additionally, we examined the effects of the weights of
493   different loss terms, β and γ, on performance. Our findings indicate that β governs the amount of
494   information encoded from the mel-spectrogram into the variational feature, whereas γ controls the
495   type of information encoded within the variational feature.
496
497   8   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
498
499   Our results indicate that the performance of our proposed approach is sensitive to the choice of
500   hyperparameters β and γ. In future work, we plan to explore automated methods for tuning these
501   parameters. Additionally, this study does not examine how our method can be leveraged for other
502   downstream speech tasks. To address this, we plan to evaluate our pre-trained autoregressive
503
      transformer on downstream tasks using the SUPERB benchmark (Yang et al., 2024). Finally, our
      model has a relatively small number of parameters and requires less training data compared to many
504
      existing frameworks (Hassid et al., 2023; Rubenstein et al., 2023). We plan to scale our methods to
505
      assess whether the same conclusions hold with increased computational resources and larger datasets.
506
507   Broader Impact We proposed an approach that improves the naturalness of speech language models
508   without compromising their language proficiency, which can be leveraged by existing paradigms in
509   this literature. While a model that generates more natural speech can enhance the user experience in
510   conversational agents, it can also be exploited for harmful purposes, such as creating fake videos or
511   conducting spam phone calls.

886   For training the model, we use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with β1 =

890   600k update steps. We warm up β from 0 to the final value in the first 30k update steps. It takes about
891   14 days to train the Libri-light models.
892   For LibriSpeech models, we discovered that methods involving discrete tokens suffers from early
893   overfitting (but not in Libri-light). Therefore, we train these models (including our proposed approach)

895   them to 500k steps, where we observe marginal improvement of loss functions between epochs. For

898   models, we warm up β from 0 to the final value in the first 20k update steps. It takes about 2 days to

1030   J   C ONDITIONAL INDEPENDENCE ASSUMPTION OF ztd AND ztc
1031
1032   In general, Zc and Zd are not independent, since the language content can imply the paralinguistic
1033   information, and vice versa. However, our modeling assumes only conditional independence. Specifi-
1034
       cally, the past generations Z1:t−1 are first passed through the autoregressive transformer ψ to produce
       the intermediate representation ot = T ransf ormerϕ (Z1:t−1 ). Then, two separate heads predict ztc
1035
1036
       and ztd based on ot . This framework assumes that the transformer can learn ot such that ztc and ztd
       become conditionally independent given ot . Given the transformer’s modeling capacity, we believe
1037
       it can extract shared information (ot ) between ztc and ztd from Z1:t−1 , while delegating the distinct
1038
       information to their respective heads.
