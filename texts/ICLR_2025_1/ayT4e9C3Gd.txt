009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            An important problem in reinforcement learning is designing agents that learn
012            to solve tasks safely in an environment. A common solution is to define either a
013            penalty in the reward function or a cost to be minimised when reaching unsafe states.
014            However, designing reward or cost functions is non-trivial and can increase with the
015            complexity of the problem. To address this, we investigate the concept of a Minmax
016            penalty, the smallest penalty for unsafe states that leads to safe optimal policies,
017            regardless of task rewards. We derive an upper and lower bound on this penalty by
018            considering both environment diameter and solvability. Additionally, we propose
019
               a simple algorithm for agents to estimate this penalty while learning task policies.
               Our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach in enabling agents
020
               to learn safe policies in high-dimensional continuous control environments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Reinforcement learning (RL) has recently achieved success across a variety of domains, such as video
027
      games (Shao et al., 2019), robotics (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Kahn et al., 2018) and autonomous
      driving (Kiran et al., 2021). However, if we hope to deploy RL in the real world, agents must be
028
      capable of completing tasks while avoiding unsafe or costly behaviour. For example, a navigating
029
      robot must avoid colliding with objects and actors around it, while simultaneously learning to solve
030   the required task. Figure 1 shows an example.
031
032   Many approaches in RL deal with this problem by allocating arbitrary penalties to unsafe states
033
      when hand-crafting the reward function. However, the problem of specifying a reward function for
      desirable, safe behaviour is notoriously difficult (Amodei et al., 2016). Importantly, penalties that are
034
      too small may result in unsafe behaviour, while penalties that are too large may result in increased
035
      learning times. Furthermore, these rewards must be specified by an expert for each new task an agent
036   faces. If our aim is to design truly autonomous, general agents, it is then simply impractical to require
037   that a human designer specify penalties to guarantee optimal but safe behaviours for every task.

      When safety is an explicit goal, a common approach is to constrain policy learning according to
055   some threshold on cumulative cost (Schulman et al., 2015; Ray et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2017).
056   While effective, these approaches require the design of a cost function whose specification can
057   be as challenging as designing a reward function. Additionally, these methods may still result in
058   unacceptably frequent constraint violations in practice, due to the large cost threshold typically used.
059
      Rather than attempting to both maximise a reward function and minimise a cost function, which
060
      requires specifying both rewards and costs and a new learning objective, we should simply aim to have
061   a better reward function—since we then do not have to specify yet another scalar signal nor change
062   the learning objective. This approach is consistent with the reward hypothesis (Sutton & Barto, 2018)
063   which states: “ All of what we mean by goals and purposes can be well thought of as maximisation
064   of the expected value of the cumulative sum of a received scalar signal (reward). ” Therefore, the
065   question we examine in this work is how to determine the Minmax penalty—the smallest penalty
066   assigned to unsafe states such that the probability of reaching safe goals is maximised by an optimal
067   policy. Rather than requiring an expert’s input, we show that this penalty can be bounded by taking
068   into account the diameter and solvability of an environment, and a practical estimate of it can be
069
      learned by an agent using its current value estimates. We make the following contributions:
070         (i) Bounding the Minmax penalty: We provide analytical upper and lower bounds on the
071             Minmax penalty for unsafe transitions, and prove that using the upper bound results in
072             policies that minimise the probability of reaching unsafe transitions (Theorem 2).
073        (ii) Learning safety bounds: We show that these bounds can be accurately estimated using
074             policy evaluation (Sutton & Barto, 2018) (Theorem 1). Additionally, we show that
075             estimating the Minmax penalty or bounds is NP-hard since it requires solving a longest
076             path problem (Theorem 3).
077       (iii) Learning safe policies: Building on our theoretical analysis, we present a practical,
078             model-free algorithm that allows agents to learn a sufficient penalty for unsafe transitions
079             while simultaneously learning task policies (Algorithm 1). Since this approach only
080             modifies the reward received for unsafe transitions, it is easily integrated into any existing
081             RL pipeline that uses value-based methods.

122   3     AVOIDING U NSAFE A BSORBING S TATES
123
124   Given an environment, we aim to bound the smallest penalty (hence the largest reward) to use for
125   unsafe transitions to guarantee optimal safe policies. We define this penalty as the Minmax penalty
126   RMinmax , which is the largest reward for unsafe transitions that lead to optimal safe policies:
127
      Definition 2 Consider an environment ⟨S, A, P ⟩ where task rewards R(s, a, s′ ) are bounded by
128
      [RMIN RMAX ] for all s′ ̸∈ G ! . Let π ∗ be an optimal policy for one such task ⟨S, A, P, R⟩. We define
129
      the Minmax penalty of this environment as the scalar RMinmax ∈ R that satisfies the following:
130
131         (i) If R(s, a, s′ ) < RMinmax for all s′ ∈ G ! , then π ∗ is safe for all R;
132         (ii) If R(s, a, s′ ) > RMinmax for some s′ ∈ G ! reachable from S \ G, then there exists an R s.t.
133              π ∗ is unsafe.
134
135   Hence, the Minmax penalty represents the boundary where on one side no reward function has an
136   optimal policy that is unsafe, and on the other there exist a reward function with an optimal policy
137   that is unsafe. Interestingly, when R(s, a, s′ ) = RMinmax , there may exist optimal safe and unsafe
138   policies simultaneously—hence no RL algorithm with such rewards can be guaranteed to converge to
139
      optimal safe policies. We next demonstrate this using the Chain-walk running example.

154   optimal policy in s0 is to always pick a2 which always reaches s1 . For a sufficiently high penalty for
155   reaching s1 (any penalty higher than −2), the optimal policy in s0 is to always pick action a1 , which

236   We begin by proposing a simple model-based algorithm for estimating the diameter and solvability,
237   from which the penalties are then obtained. We describe the method here and present the pseudo-code
238

240
      the equation in Definition 3. Similarly, the solvability is estimated by estimating the reach probability
241   Psπ (sT ̸∈ G ! ) of each deterministic policy π using rewards of 1 for transitions into safe goal states
242   and zero otherwise. This approach converges via the convergence of policy evaluation (Theorem 1).

      compared to accounting for D only (R̄MAX ) and accounting for both C and D (R̄MIN ). Interestingly,

249   penalties may bound RMinmax in general. We show in Theorem 2 that this is indeed the case.

251   Theorem 2 (Safety Bounds) Consider a solvable environment where task rewards are bounded by
252   [RMIN RMAX ] for all s′ ̸∈ G ! . Then R̄MIN ≤ RMinmax ≤ R̄MAX .
253
      Theorem 2 says that for any MDP whose rewards for unsafe transitions are bounded above by
254
      R̄MIN , the optimal policy both minimises the probability of reaching unsafe states and maximises the
255
      probability of reaching safe goal states. Hence, any penalty R̄MIN − ϵ, where ϵ > 0 can be arbitrarily
256   small, will guarantee optimal safe policies. Similarly, the theorem shows that any reward higher
257   than R̄MAX may have optimal policies that do not minimise the probability of reaching unsafe states.
258   These can be observed in Figure 3. The figure demonstrates why considering both the diameter and
259   solvability of an MDP is necessary to guarantee safe policies, because the diameter alone does not
260   always minimise the failure rate.

      4                 P RACTICAL A LGORITHM FOR L EARNING S AFE P OLICIES
263
264   While the Minmax penalty of an MDP can be accurately estimated using policy evaluation (Algorithm
265   2), it requires knowledge of the environment dynamics (or an estimate of it). These are difficult
266   quantities to estimate from an agent’s experience, which is further complicated by the need to
267   also learn the true optimal policy for the estimated Minmax penalty. Hence, obtaining an accurate
268   estimate of the Minmax penalty is impractical in model-free and function approximation settings
269   where the state and action spaces are large. In fact, it is NP-hard since it depends on the diameter,
      which requires solving a longest-path problem.

468   7              D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
469
470   This paper investigates a new approach towards safe RL by asking the question: Is a scalar reward
471
      enough to solve tasks safely? To answer this question, we bound the Minmax penalty, which takes
      into account the diameter and solvability of an environment in order to minimise the probability of en-
472
      countering unsafe states. We prove that the penalty does indeed minimise this probability, and present
473
      a method that uses an agent’s value estimates to learn an estimate of the penalty. Our results in tabular
474   and high-dimensional continuous settings have demonstrated that, by encoding the safe behaviour
475   directly in the reward function via the Minmax penalty, agents are able to solve tasks while prioritising
476   safety, learning safer policies than popular constraint-based approaches. Our method is also easy to
477   incorporate with any off-the-shelf RL algorithms that maintain value estimates, requiring no changes
478   to the algorithms themselves. By autonomously learning the penalty, our method also alleviates the
479   need for a human designer to manually tweak rewards or cost functions to elicit safe behaviour.
480   Finally, while we show that scalar rewards are indeed enough for safe RL, the current analysis is
481   only applicable to unsafe terminal states—which only covers tasks that can be naturally represented
482   by stochastic-shortest path MDPs. Given that other popular RL settings like discounted MDPs can
483   be converted to stochastic shortest path MDPs (Bertsekas, 1987; Sutton & Barto, 1998), a promising
484   future direction could be to find the dual of our results for other theoretically equivalent settings.
485   In conclusion, we see this reward-only approach as a promising direction towards truly autonomous
      agents capable of independently learning to solve tasks safely.

      C.1    R EWARD SHAPING
759
760   The problem of designing reward functions to produce desired policies in RL settings is well-studied
761   (Singh et al., 2009). Particular focus has been placed on the practice of reward shaping, in which
762   an initial reward function provided by an MDP is augmented in order to improve the rate at which
763   an agent learns the same optimal policy (Ng et al., 1999; Devidze et al., 2021). While sacrificing
764   some optimality, other approaches like Lipton et al. (2016) propose shaping rewards using an idea of
765
      intrinsic fear. Here, the agent trains a supervised fear model representing the probability of reaching
      unsafe states in a fixed horizon, scales said probabilities by a fear factor, and then subtracts the scaled
766
      probabilities from Q-learning targets.
767
768   These approaches differ from ours in that they seek to find reward functions that improve convergence
769   while preserving the optimality from an initial reward function. In contrast, we seek to determine the
770   optimal rewards for terminal states in order to minimise undesirable behaviours irrespective of the
771
      original reward function and optimal policy.
772
773   C.2    C ONSTRAINED RL
774
      Disincentivising or preventing undesirable behaviours is core to the field of safe RL. A popular
775
      approach is to define constraints on the behaviour of an agent using CMDPs, tasking the agent with
776   limiting the accumulation of costs associated with violating safety constraints while simultaneously
777   maximising reward (Altman, 1999; Achiam et al., 2017; Chow et al., 2018; Ray et al., 2019; Hasan-
778   zadeZonuzy et al., 2021). Widely used examples of these approaches include constrained policy
779   optimisation (CPO) (Achiam et al., 2017), which augments TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015) with
780   constraints to satisfy a constrained MDP, and TRPO-Lagrangian (Ray et al., 2019), which combines
781   Lagrangian methods with TRPO. Another example is Sauté RL (Sootla et al., 2022), which incor-
782   porates the cost function into the rewards and augments the state with the remaining ”cost budget”
783   spent by violating safety constraints. Other constraint-based approaches include Projection-based
784
      CPO (Yang et al., 2020), which projects a TRPO policy onto a space defined by constraints, and PID
      Lagrangian methods (Stooke et al., 2020), which augment Lagrangian methods with PID control.
785
786   In deterministic environments with a cost threshold of 0, the set of safe policies for these approaches
787   are the same as ours. However, in stochastic environments, these approaches require the correct
788   choice of inequality constraints to even be well defined. If the cost threshold is not carefully chosen,
789   there may exist no policy that satisfies the CMDP constraints, implying there would exist no optimal
790
      safe policy to converge to. For example, in the L AVA G RIDWORLD or the P ILLAR domains with
      noise > 0, a cost threshold of 0 can never be satisfied by any policy for all states, making these
791
      approaches theoretically ill-defined in these environments with that cost threshold. That said, we found
792
      in practice that a cost threshold of 0 gave them the best performance in the safety-gym experiments
793   (compared to 1 and the default of 25). In contrast, we showed the existence of a Minmax penalty
794   irrespective of the stochasticity of the environment. Additionally, while these approaches in general
795   theoretically define or learn safety parameters—like Lagrange coefficients—for each reward function
796   even when the cost function and cost threshold remain unchanged, our minmax penalty approach is
797   theoretically defined and learned for all reward functions.
798
799   C.3    S HIELDING
800
801   Finally, another important line of work involves relying on interventions from a model (Dalal et al.,
802   2018; Wagener et al., 2021) or human (Tennenholtz et al., 2022) to prevent unsafe actions from being
803
      considered by the agent (shielding the agent) or prevent the environment from executing those unsafe
      actions by correcting them (shielding the environment). Other approaches here also look at using
804
      temporal logics to define or enforce safety constraints on the actions considered or selected by the
805
      agent (Alshiekh et al., 2018).
806
807   These approaches fit seamlessly into our proposed reward-only framework since they are primarily
808   about modifications on the transition dynamics and not the reward function—for example, unsafe
809
      actions here can simply lead to unsafe goal states.
