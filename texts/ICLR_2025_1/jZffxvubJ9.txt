011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013             In high-stakes areas like healthcare, retrospective counterfactual analysis—such
014
                as evaluating what might have happened if treatments were administered earlier,
                later, or differently—is vital for refining treatment strategies. This paper proposes
015
                a counterfactual treatment optimization framework using temporal point processes
016
                to model outcome event sequences. By sampling potential outcome events under
017             new treatment decision rules, our approach seeks to optimize treatment strategies
018             in a counterfactual setting. To achieve accurate counterfactual evaluation of new
019             decision rules, we explicitly introduce latent states into the modeling of tempo-
020             ral point processes. Our method first infers the latent states and associated noise,
021             followed by counterfactual sampling of outcome events. This approach rigorously
022             addresses the complexities introduced by latent states, effectively removing biases
023             in the evaluation of treatment strategies. By proving the identifiability of model
024             parameters in the presence of these states, we provide theoretical guarantees that
025
                enhance the reliability and robustness of the counterfactual analysis. By incorpo-
                rating latent states and proving identifiability, our framework not only improves
026
                the accuracy and robustness of treatment decision rules but also offers actionable
027
                insights for optimizing healthcare interventions. This method holds significant
028             potential for improving treatment strategies, particularly in healthcare scenarios
029             where patient symptoms are complex and high-dimensional.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   While online reinforcement learning policies have shown promise in designing treatment strategies
035   for sepsis patients in ICU ( Komorowski et al. (2018)), the direct deployment and testing of new
036   treatment strategies on patients raise practical and ethical concerns. Counterfactual evaluation offers
037
      a solution by retrospectively assessing the performance of different treatment policies using existing
      data, without intervening in ongoing patient care. Retrospective analysis is a safer method and has
038
      wide applications, as it allows for evaluating new treatments without posing risks to patients (Bal
039
      (2009)).
040
041   In this paper, we focus on answering the following what-if question:
042
          Given the observational treatment and outcome trajectories, can we modify specific treatment
043
                          actions to optimize the outcome in a counterfactual manner?
044
045   These modifications must adhere to predefined medical rules. For instance, if some patients respond
046   well to a particular drug, we might explore increasing the dose for better outcomes. Conversely, for
047   patients who do not respond, we could consider switching to alternative medications. These pertur-
048   bations must follow medical guidelines to ensure safety and efficacy. Similarly, when developing a
049   healthy exercise habit, any changes to the recommended actions must comply with behavior theory
050
      principles, such as gradual progression and sustainability. For example, it is inappropriate to recom-
      mend excessive exercise or drastic reductions in food intake, as these do not align with established
051
      theories of behavior change and can lead to adverse health effects.
052
053   Recently, a counterfactual off-policy evaluation method was developed for the partially observable
      Markov Decision Process (POMDP) Oberst & Sontag (2019). (Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez, 2022)


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      extended this method to temporal point processes setting. In this context, given a realization of a
055   temporal point process with a known intensity function, a counterfactual sampling algorithm was
056   developed to simulate counterfactual realizations of temporal point processes under a specified al-
057   ternative intensity function. This developed counterfactual temporal point can be deployed in coun-
058   terfactual treatment evaluation settings. For instance, it can be utilized to assess the counterfactual
059   treatment effect by sampling outcome events in what-if scenarios, where the occurrence of outcome
060   events is modeled by the temporal point processes whose intensity function depends on treatment
061   events.
062   We aim to extend existing counterfactual off-policy evaluation methods to a treatment decision rule
063   optimization setting, where outcome events are modeled using marked temporal point processes.
064   By integrating counterfactual reasoning with this modeling approach, our method enables the as-
065   sessment and optimization of treatment strategies in complex, high-dimensional healthcare environ-
066   ments. The framework is composed of two key components: an outer loop for optimizing treatment
067   decision rules and an inner loop for evaluating counterfactual treatment effects.
068   The outer loop systematically explores the treatment space to identify potential improvements in de-
069   cision rules. Concurrently, the inner loop evaluates these rules by retrospectively sampling symptom
070   events under counterfactual scenarios. To address the challenges posed by latent states, we intro-
071   duce a two-stage procedure. Initially, we infer latent states and associated noise to mitigate biases
072   in the marked temporal point processes data, ensuring that our analysis is both accurate and reli-
073   able. Importantly, we theoretically prove the identifiability of model parameters in the presence of
074   latent states, providing strong guarantees that enhance the robustness of our counterfactual evalua-
075
      tion. Following this, we conduct counterfactual sampling to rigorously assess the effects of different
      treatment strategies. This comprehensive approach not only refines existing treatment strategies but
076
      also generates new insights for optimizing patient outcomes in healthcare applications.

079   2    R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Latent states and latent confounders. The causal inference literature often make the assumption
082   that there are no unobserved confounders (Aglietti et al. (2021); Bica et al. (2021); Vanderschueren
083   et al. (2023)). However, in many practical settings, the NUC assumption could hardly hold. Also,
084   the confounders actually play a crucial role in the counterfactual reasoning process, since we might
085
      get a biased result if we ignore the impact of potential confounders on our target variables (Pearl
      (2009)). In a longitudinal setting, there are several ways to consider the unobserved confounders.
086
      One might replace the potential unobserved confounders by some proxies (Louizos et al. (2017);
087
      Madras et al. (2019); Kuzmanovic et al. (2021)), or learn substitutes for hidden confounders using
088   some factor models (Bica et al. (2020a); Hatt & Feuerriegel (2024)). In our work, we construct
089   a categorical variable for representing latent states, which can be seen as a partial representation
090   of the unobserved confounders and thus helps mitigate the potential influence (Bartolucci et al.
091   (2022)). Some related works also incorporate a categorical variable to represent latent states in
092   Hawkes processes setting.Xu & Zha (2017) consider a mixture model of Hawkes processes at the
093   sequence level, while Yang & Zha (2013) consider a setting for which the intensity has a mixed
094   kernel. Our setting provides a different view by considering the switching systems represented by
095   the categorical variable.
096
097   Counterfactual reasoning. Counterfactual reasoning has recently piqued interest in many ex-
098   plainable machine learning works. Note that different from the definition for counterfactual outcome
099
      in another line of works (Lim (2018), Melnychuk et al. (2022), Bica et al. (2020b), Hess et al. (2023),
      Frauen et al. (2024)), here we consider counterfactual works condition on all observed information.
100
      To illustrate, they typically refer a conditional average potential outcome related with probability
101
      P (Yt+τ [a[t,t+τ ] ]|Ht ), while our counterfactual objective is related to P (Y[0,T ] [a[0,T ] ]|HT ). The
102   main difference comes from the latter one would condition on the whole observed trajectory, thus
103   we need consider the posterior noise for a SCM. A discrete-time setup, such as POMDP, is consid-
104   ered in many existing works (Oberst & Sontag (2019); Tsirtsis et al. (2021);Aalen et al. (2020);Abid
105   et al. (2022);Tsirtsis & Rodriguez (2024)). The Gumbel-max SCM, a class of SCMs that meets
106   the counterfactual stability criteria for producing counterfactual trajectories in finite POMDPs, is
107   presented by Oberst & Sontag (2019). Noorbakhsh & Rodriguez (2022) apply this special SCM
      on the thinning process of temporal point process, allowing simulated counterfactual realizations in


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      continuous time under a given alternative intensity function. This method regards the Lewis’ thin-
109   ning algorithm (Lewis & Shedler (1979)) as the generative method. Therefore, it necessitates the
110   knowledge of an upper bound for both the observed and counterfactual intensity, which is challeng-
111   ing to get when the intensity is history-dependent. To overcome this limitation, Hızlı et al. (2023)
112   extend the counterfactual sampling algorithm to history-dependent point processes by regarding the
113   Ogata’s thinning algorithm (Ogata (1981)) as the generative process. However, all these works focus
114   on the univariate case, while we extend the related SCM to the multivariate case, and take the latent
115   states into consideration. Many existing works focus on finding the optimal actions in counterfactual
116   settings. Under static setting, works like (Karimi et al. (2021); Karimi et al. (2020)) focus on finding
117
      the actions that one could achieve a better outcome, which belongs to the framework called algo-
      rithmic recourse. As for time-varying settings, (Tsirtsis et al. (2021); Tsirtsis & Rodriguez (2024))
118
      provide several methods based on POMDPs which are suitable for different state types in order to
119
      find optimal action sequences, but their setting focuses on discrete-time setting. We focus on op-
120   timizing the specific meta-rules in a continuous time setting instead of the specific optimal action
121   sequence for an individual, which would be more informative and suitable for flexible situations.

226   Our goal is to answer “what-if” questions: Given the observed sequences of treatment and outcome
227   events, how can we optimize the treatment strategy to improve the final outcome denoted as Y in
228   a counterfactual manner? The final outcome Y , such as survival time, is either a direct function
229   of the outcome events or can be directly observed from the outcome events. We assume that Y is
230   measurable given the outcome events.
231   Objective: Instead of optimizing individual treatment actions, we aim to optimize decision rules
232   that are pre-specified by doctors. These rules determine the appropriate treatment action based on
233   the patient’s condition, reflected in the latent states z(t), and the history of treatment and outcome

314   We are interested in understanding the conditions our model must satisfy so that the following im-
315   plication holds for all (H, z) :
316                                ∀ (θ, θ ′ ) : Pθ (H) = Pθ′ (H) =⇒ θ = θ ′                             (16)
317                                                                 ′
      That is, if any two different sets of model parameters θ and θ result in the same marginal distribution
318   Pθ (H), then this would imply that these parameters are identical, leading to matching joint distri-
319   butions Pθ (H, z). This implies that if we learn parameters θ such that Pθ (H) = Pθ∗ (H) (the ideal
320   case where θ ∗ represents the true underlying parameters), then the corresponding joint distribution
321   also matches: Pθ (H, z) = Pθ∗ (H, z). If the joint distribution matches, it ensures that we have iden-
322   tified the correct prior Pθ (z) = Pθ∗ (z) and the correct posteriors pθ (z | H) = pθ∗ (z | H). This
323   guarantees that the EM algorithm, by maximizing the likelihood, correctly identifies the underlying
      parameters, ensuring the model’s identifiability.

342   parameters that entails the same distribution for the observed data, which ensures that for any revised
343   treatment plan, the corresponding intensity is uniquely determined, enabling a reliable and consistent
344
      counterfactual analysis. Note that to guarantee these parameters represents the causal relationships
      and identifiability for our counterfactual expected outcome, we need additional causal assumptions
345
      as we mentioned in previous section and provided in Appendix C.2. We provide detailed proof for
346
      model parameters identifiability in Appendix F.

419   Experimental setup. To validate our method, we constructed an 8-dimensional Hawkes process
420   with four dimensions representing treatments (A1 , A2 , B1 , B2 ) and four as outcomes. Indicators 1
421   and 2 reflect worsening symptoms, while indicators 3 and 4 reflect improvement. Drug A targets
422
      indicator 1, and drug B targets indicator 2, with A1 /B1 representing lower dosages and A2 /B2
      higher dosages. Treatments also increase the likelihood of positive outcomes (indicators 3 and 4).
423
      We incorporated two latent states to represent patient health stages, with healthier states having lower
424
      probabilities of adverse events. Intensity parameters were designed to reflect these relationships.
425
426   To evaluate patient outcomes, we defined a deterministic outcome Y , calculated as the square of the
427   weighted proportion of positive outcomes, with later events receiving higher weights. Our goal was
428
      to optimize decision rules that maximize Y , focusing on two meta-rules detailed in Appendix G.1.
429   We first generate 600 sequences from the ground truth model as we described above. Then based on
430   these sequences, we learn the model parameters by our EM methods and denoted as model 1. We
431   also applied original MLE method and have model 2 which does not take latent states into consid-
      eration. For optimizing our meta-rules, we simulate a synthetic baseline population dataset. This

433   ulating the outcomes and adopting some naive policies for choosing potential improper treatments
434   when some outcomes occurs, e.g., when outcome 1 occurs at state 0 we choose drug A1 instead of
435   drug A2 , thus obviously they are not the best policies. By performing our decision-rule optimization
436   algorithm, we could then compare our current policies performance with the baseline performance.

467
468   Sepsis, a life-threatening condition caused by the body’s overactive response to infection, leads to
469   inflammation, tissue damage, organ failure, and high mortality rates. Despite advances in critical
470   care, clinical recommendations for sepsis management remain uncertain, highlighting the need for
471
      decision-rule optimization techniques like ours (Evans et al. (2021)). To address this challenge,
      we utilized the MIMIC-III database (Johnson et al. (2016)), a widely used resource containing de-
472
      identified health data from over 60,000 ICU patients. While MIMIC-III supports predictive mod-
473
      eling and treatment evaluation, the common no unobserved confounders assumption is difficult to
474   meet, as unrecorded factors or omitted variables can influence outcomes. Our approach, designed
475   to account for latent states, leverages this database to mitigate confounding influences and optimize
476   decision rules for sepsis management.
477
      We extracted 2,000 patient sequences meeting the criteria for sepsis diagnosis (Saria (2018)). These
478
      patients formed the population for our EM algorithm so as to fit our mixture model. We then select
479
      the patients based on our meta-rules, ensuring those sequences containing the potential treatment
480   action to be revised, and we use this subset for our decision-rule optimization process. Treatments
481   for sepsis typically involve vasopressor therapy and fluid administration, with the aim of stabiliz-
482   ing patients by maintaining blood pressure and ensuring proper organ perfusion (Komorowski et al.
483   (2018)). For outcomes, we monitored real-time urine output and survival, key indicators in sep-
484   sis management. Low urine output is often an early sign of kidney dysfunction and septic shock,
485   potentially signaling inadequate treatment response or impending multi-organ failure. Ultimately,
      improving survival rates is the overarching goal of any sepsis intervention. We detailed these treat-


                                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ments and outcomes in Table 2. The reward design and specific meta-rules we aim to optimize is
487   described also in Appendix G.2.
488
489   Results. We hypothesized the existence of two latent states in the data and used the EM algorithm
490   to estimate parameters. Latent State 1, associated with stable conditions, showed lower baseline
491   event rates and inter-event influence, suggesting minimal need for intervention. In contrast, Latent
492   State 2 reflected acute conditions with higher event rates and stronger inter-event influence, requiring
493   more proactive care. In both states, predefined triggers like low urine output and low blood pressure
494   effectively prompted appropriate treatments, validating our rules.
495   Analyzing optimized meta-rules from the MIMIC-III dataset revealed state-dependent treatment pat-
496   terns. For fluids, stable conditions led to administration 0.6560 time units after low urine detection,
497   compared to 0.7994 in acute cases. Vasopressors were administered earlier in acute states (0.6027

499   shifted toward colloids in acute ones, while vasopressor usage balanced between norepinephrine
500   and dopamine in severe cases. Feedback from ChatGPT 4.0 confirmed the clinical validity of these
501   meta-rules, emphasizing their utility in distinguishing and managing patient conditions effectively.

527
528
      We introduced a counterfactual treatment optimization framework leveraging temporal point pro-
529
      cesses to model treatment-outcome event sequences while addressing challenges posed by latent
530   states. This framework provides insights into optimizing treatment strategies in complex healthcare
531   settings, enhancing clinical decision-making and patient outcomes. Future work could improve its
532   practical applicability by developing methods to automatically determine the optimal number of la-
533   tent states and extending the framework to model time-dependent latent states, capturing delayed or
534   evolving influences throughout the treatment course.

1325   Sepsis is a life-threatening condition that occurs when the body’s response to infection causes
1326   widespread inflammation, and is a major cause to tissue damage, organ failure, and mortality. Our
1327   decision-rule optimization method might be helpful since there is still a great deal of uncertainty
1328   regarding clinical recommendations in the management of sepsis (Evans et al. (2021)).
1329
1330   Dataset description. MIMIC-III (Johnson et al. (2016)) is a large, publicly available database
1331   containing de-identified health data from over 60,000 patients admitted to the ICUs at the Beth
1332   Israel Deaconess Medical Center. MIMIC-III is widely used in medical research for developing
1333
       predictive models, studying disease progression, and analyzing the effects of treatments in critical
       care settings. It includes detailed information on patient demographics, vital signs, laboratory test
1334
       results, medications, treatment procedures, and clinical outcomes. The no unobserved confounders
1335
       assumption is typically hard to be satisfied, since treatments and outcomes might be affected by some
1336   other factors those are not recorded in the database. Therefore, our method would be appropriate for
1337   learning decision-rules in this confounded setting.
1338
1339
       Patients: We extracted 2000 sequences with the criteria that those patients are diagnosed with
1340   sepsis (Saria (2018)) and the corresponding data were not missing. We regard this as our population
1341   to apply our EM algorithm for fitting our model. We then select the patients based on our meta-rules
1342   and use this subset for our decision-rule optimization process.
1343
1344   Treatments: Vasopressor therapy and fluid treatment are used in the management of sepsis with
1345   the goal of stabilizing the patient by preserving blood pressure and ensuring proper organ perfusion
1346   (Komorowski et al. (2018)). Vasopressors function to constrict blood arteries and boost cardiac
1347   output, while fluids aid in restoring intravascular volume. These actions are critical in preventing
1348   organ failure and guaranteeing the body receives enough oxygen and nutrients throughout the sepsis
1349   response. We list the detailed items in Table 2, with 3 types of fluids, 4 types of vasopressors, and 2
       rypes of inotroics.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       Outcomes: We treated real-time urine and survival condition as our main outcome indicators,
1351   also with several important lab measurements since they also have important impact on urine and
1352   survival condition. Low urine output is a critical indicator of kidney dysfunction and often signal the
1353   septic chock. Persistent low urine may indicate inadequate response to treatment, ongoing shock, or
1354   impending multi-organ failure, making it an essential parameter to monitor in sepsis management.
1355   Given the high death rate associated with sepsis, patient survival is a key outcome, and the goal of
1356   any treatment is to raise that probability.
1357
1358   Reward Y design: We design our final outcome Y as a deterministic function of all observed
1359   outcomes, distinct from the synthetic experiment approach. In this context, since there are no good
1360   outcomes, we utilize a squared weighted sum rather than a proportion. Specifically, Y is defined
1361
       as the square of the weighted sum of the counts of bad outcomes, where each outcome is assigned
       a dual weighting scheme. The first weight is based on the timing of the event, with later events
1362
       receiving higher weights to reflect their greater impact. The second weight is determined by the
1363

1364   events at 0.3, and low − BP events at 0.1. Therefore, our target is to find the best decision rules so
