009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Concept learning exploits background knowledge in the form of description logic
012            axioms to learn explainable classification models from knowledge bases. Despite
013            recent breakthroughs in the runtime of concept learners, most approaches still
014            cannot be deployed on real-world knowledge bases. This is due to their use of
015            description logic reasoners, which do not scale to large datasets. Moreover, these
016            reasoners are not robust against inconsistencies and erroneous data, both being
017            hallmarks of real datasets. We address this challenge by presenting a novel neural
018            reasoner dubbed E BR. Our reasoner relies on embeddings to rapidly approximate
019
               the results of a symbolic reasoner. We show that E BR solely requires retrieving
               instances for atomic concepts and existential restrictions to retrieve the instances
020
               of any concept in SROIQ. Importantly, our experiments also suggest that E BR
021
               is robust against missing and erroneous data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Description Logics (DLs) (Baader, 2003) offer a formal framework for structured knowledge repre-
      sentation and reasoning. Due to their well-defined semantics and favorable computational properties,
028
      DLs have become essential tools in fields such as ontology engineering (Keet, 2018), knowledge rep-
029
      resentation (Brachman & Levesque, 2004), and semantic web technologies (Horrocks et al., 2003).
030
031   Logical entailment is one of the most extensively studied reasoning mechanisms in computer sci-
032   ence (Tang et al., 2022). It is also a crucial task in the exploration of DL Knowledge Bases (KBs).
033
      Formally, a statement φ is logically entailed by a KB if φ is true in every model of the KB (Tang
      et al., 2022). In DL KBs, such entailments are typically computed by reasoners (termed as symbolic
034
      reasoners) including Pellet (Sirin et al., 2007), Fact++ (Tsarkov & Horrocks, 2006), HermiT (Glimm
035
      et al., 2014), and RacerPro (Haarslev et al., 2012). These reasoners are sound and complete; the
036   statements they derive are correct, and they derive every entailed statement.
037
038   Although symbolic reasoners are being successfully applied to infer missing knowledge on bench-
039
      mark datasets, their application at a large scale has been hindered by their inability to handle
      inconsistencies, inferring missing instance assertions, and their impractical runtimes. An incon-
040
      sistent KB logically entails every statement trivially. To illustrate this, let K = ({C ⊓ D ⊑
041
      A, B ⊑ ⊥}, {C(a), D(a), B(b)}) be a KB. In this case, a classical symbolic reasoner cannot
042   determine the membership of the individual a in A although it is not involved in any inconsis-
043   tency. Another issue that arises is incompleteness; illustrated via the following example. Let K =
044   ({Person(Bob), Person(P aul), Person(Ani), knows(Bob, P aul), knows(Ani, Joe)}). In
045   K, a symbolic reasoner cannot infer the membership for Joe in the class Person. Indeed, the is-
046   sues of inconsistency and incompleteness pose significant challenges, as most large-scale KBs, such
047   as Wikidata, DBpedia, and Yago, are often incomplete or inconsistent (Töpper et al., 2012; Nickel
048   et al., 2015; Krompaß et al., 2015). Furthermore, the aforementioned state-of-the-art reasoners oper-
049   ate on a single CPU, which hinders scalability to large real-world datasets by not leveraging modern
050
      parallel computing architectures.
051   Neural link predictors have been extensively investigated to deal with incompleteness on various
052   datasets (Dettmers et al., 2018; Ren & Leskovec, 2020). The likelihood of assertion (e.g. a class
053   membership Person(?) or knows(Ani, ?)) can be computed through learning continuous vector
      representations elucidated in Section 2.2. Recent works showed that neural link predictors can be

      effectively applied to answer complex queries involving multi-model reasoning (Arakelyan et al.,
055   2021; van Krieken et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023; Demir et al., 2023; Arakelyan et al., 2024).
056
057   Our approach dubbed E BR (Embedding Based Reasoner) leverages knowledge graph embeddings
058
      to perform reasoning over incomplete and inconsistent knowledge bases. We employ a neural link
      predictor to facilitate the retrieval of missing data and handle inconsistencies. Our contributions can
059
      be summarized as follows:
060
061          • We propose neural semantics to tackle the instance retrieval problem on incomplete or
062            inconsistent SROIQ KBs.
063
             • We provide an in-depth comparison for instance retrieval against symbolic reasoners on six
064
               datasets (Father, Family, Semantic Bible, Mutagenesis, Carcinogenesis, and Vicodi). We
065            show that on knowledge bases with varying numbers of missing assertions, our approach
066            outperforms symbolic approaches, which often return an empty result set in this case.
067
             • We show that the instance retrieval problem can be tackled without storing knowledge bases
068
               in memory. Storing the learned parameters of a neural link predictor suffices to retrieve the
069            instances of any SROIQ concepts. Importantly, the inference time can be decreased by
070            leveraging GPUs, enabling efficient handling of large-scale computations.

074
075   A DL KB K consists of a TBox T and an ABox A, where the former specifies the schema (i.e.,
076
      the axioms that describe the structure of the domain being modelled) and the latter contains the data
      (i.e., the assertions describing the objects in a domain of discourse). Precisely, a TBox contains
077
      general concept inclusions (GCIs) of the form C ⊑ D, where C, D are concepts. Moreover, the
078
      ABox includes assertions having the form C(a) (concept assertion) or r(a, b) (role assertion), for
079   individuals a, b, concept C, and role r. The syntax and semantics for concepts in SROIQ Baader
080   (2003); Hitzler et al. (2009) are given in Table 1.

      Let C ⊑ D be a GCI and I be an interpretation. Then, I satisfies C ⊑ D, denoted as I |= C ⊑ D
101
      iff C I ⊑ DI . Similarly, I satisfies an assertion C(a) iff aI ∈ C(a); and the assertion r(a, b) iff
102   (aI , bI ) ∈ rI . We write an axiom to mean either a TBox GCI or an ABox assertion. We say that
103   I is a model of the KB K, denoted by I |= K, iff I satisfies every axiom in K. Finally, let K be
104   a DL KB and α be an axiom, then K |= α iff I |= α for every model I of K. The DL SROIQ
105   additionally admits an RBox R which includes (1) a role hierarchy Rh consisting of (generalised)
106   role inclusion axioms of the form R ⊑ S, and (2) a set Ra of role assertions stating, for instance,
107   that a role R must be reflexive/irreflexive, symmetric/asymmetric, transitive, and that two roles R
      and S are disjoint. The semantics for RBox axioms is defined analogously Horrocks et al. (2006).

      Reasoning with expressive DL KBs is a computationally hard task. Specifically, the instance check-
109   ing problem, which, given K, a concept C, and an individual x, determines whether x is an instance
110   of C in K (denoted as K |= C(x)), has a high computational complexity. For the DL SROIQ, this
111   problem is non-deterministic double exponential time complete (Kazakov, 2008). Given such high
112   complexity and the additional challenges posed by incomplete and inconsistent data in real-world
113   scenarios, practical applications often require the use of approximation algorithms.
114
      In the following, we first briefly introduce symbolic DL reasoners suitable for small, consistent,
115   and complete datasets. Next, we introduce knowledge graph embeddings, able to deal with incom-
116   pleteness and inconsistency issues, albeit for simple 1-hop queries. We continue with neural query
117   answering approaches that generalize the capabilities of knowledge graph embeddings, e.g., sup-
118   porting multi-hop queries with conjunctions and disjunctions. Finally, we provide an overview of
119   approaches supporting expressive description logics including reasoning with type hierarchies.
120
121   2.1   S YMBOLIC R EASONING OVER K NOWLEDGE BASES
122
123   To the best of our knowledge, HermiT (Glimm et al., 2014) is the only reasoner that fully sup-
124   ports the OWL 2 standard, including all the datatypes specified in the standard and correctly reasons
125
      about properties as well as classes. It is based on a novel “hypertableau” calculus that addresses
      performance problems due to nondeterminism and model size—the primary sources of complexity
126
      in state-of-the-art OWL reasoners. HermiT reduces all basic reasoning tasks, including subsumption
127
      test, to satisfiability checking. HermiT was shown to outperform the previous reasoners Pellet (Sirin
128   et al., 2007), and Fact++ (Tsarkov & Horrocks, 2006). Similarly, OWL2Bench (Singh et al., 2020)
129   compares different reasoners across datasets and OWL profiles. Pellet and its extension Openllet
130   were found to perform best in terms of runtime. JFact, the Java implementation of Fact++, per-
131   formed worst on all the reasoning tasks across all OWL 2 profiles. While OWL2Bench assessed
132   the reasoners performance to detect inconsistent ontologies, they did not assess the robustness of
133   reasoners, i.e., how well they answer queries on incomplete/inconsistent data.
134
135   2.2   K NOWLEDGE G RAPH E MBEDDINGS
136
137   A plethora of Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE)/neural link predictors models have been de-
138
      veloped over the last decade (Dettmers et al., 2018). Most KGE models learn continuous vector
      representations tailored towards link prediction. They are often defined as parameterized scoring
139
      functions ϕΘ : E × R × E 7→ R, where Θ denotes parameters and often comprise entity embed-
140
      dings E ∈ R|E|×de , relation embeddings R ∈ R|R|×dr , and additional parameters (e.g., affine
141
      transformations, batch normalizations, convolutions). Since de = dr holds for many state-of-the-art
142   models, we will use d to signify the number of real parameters used for the embedding of an entity
143   or relation. Given (h, r, t) ∈ E × R × E, the prediction ŷ := ϕΘ (h, r, t) signals the likelihood
144   of (h, r, t) being true. Since G contains only assertions that are assumed to be true, assertions as-
145   sumed to be false are often generated by applying the negative sampling, 1vsAll or Kvsall training
146   strategies (Ruffinelli et al., 2020). KGE have been successfully applied to link prediction (Dai et al.,
147   2020; Wang et al., 2021), drug discovery (Bonner et al., 2022), community detection (Hamilton
148   et al., 2017), question answering (Hamilton et al., 2018), and product recommendation (Choudhary
149   et al., 2021).
150
151   2.3   N EURAL Q UERY A NSWERING ON I NCOMPLETE K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS
152
153
      In recent years, significant progress has been made on querying incomplete triple-based Knowledge
      Graphs (KGs) that are represented as subject-predicate-object triples, such as those in RDF. Hamil-
154
      ton et al. (2018) laid the foundations for multi-hop reasoning with graph query embeddings (GQE).
155
      Given a conjunctive query, they learn continuous vector representations for queries, entities, and
156   relations and answer queries by performing projection and intersection operations in the embedding
157   vector space. Ren et al. (2020) show that GQE cannot answer Existential Positive First-order (EPFO)
158   queries since GQE does not model the union operator. Hence, they propose Query2Box that repre-
159   sents an EPFO query with a set of box embeddings, where one box embedding is constructed per
160   conjunctive subquery. A query is answered by returning the entities whose minimal distance to one
161   of the box embeddings is smallest. TeMP (Hu et al., 2022) builds on top of GQE and allows each
      entity to have a set of types.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      All the aforementioned models learn query embeddings and answer queries via nearest neighbor
163   search in the embedding space. However, learning embeddings for complex, multi-hop queries
164   involving conjunctions and disjunctions can be computationally demanding. Towards this end,
165   Arakelyan et al. (2021) propose complex query decomposition (CQD). They answer EPFO queries
166   by decomposing them into single-hop subqueries and aggregate the scores of a pre-trained single-
167   hop link predictor (e.g., ComplEx-N3). Scores are aggregated using a t-norm and t-conorm—
168   continuous generalizations of the logical conjunction and disjunction (Arakelyan et al., 2021; Kle-
169   ment et al., 2004). Their experiments suggest that CQD outperforms GQE and Query2Box; it gener-
170   alizes well to complex query structures while requiring orders of magnitude less training data. Zhu
171
      et al. (2022) highlight that CQD is the only interpretable model among the aforementioned models
      as it produces intermediate results. Recently, Demir et al. (2023) extended CQD to answer multi-hop
172
      queries involving literals. Andresel et al. (2023) extend both GQE and CQD to answer queries in
173
      the presence of an ontolgoy. They do so via query rewriting and ontology-aware knowledge graph
174   embeddings. However, the expressiveness of their queries is limited. Unlike our approach, they only
175   support Existential Positive First-Order (EPFO) queries, but do not support negations, universal re-
176   strictions, and cardinality restrictions.

178   2.4   D ESCRIPTION L OGICS E MBEDDINGS
179
180   The previously discussed neural reasoning approaches assume a triple-based data model for knowl-
181   edge graphs consisting of subject-predicate-object triples. Recently, there has been a growing inter-
182   est in generating vector representations (embeddings) for OWL ontologies. However, most of them
183   do not allow answering instance queries, i.e., retrieve all instances in a given concept.
184   Several embedding techniques have been proposed for the lightweight DLs including EL and
185   EL++ (Kulmanov et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022; Lacerda et al., 2023; Jack-
186   ermeier et al., 2024). The underlying idea involves representing concepts as geometrical shapes
187   (boxes or balls). Further, Mondal et al. (2021) proposed to map the concepts and roles in an on-
188   tology to n-dimensional vector, and Singh et al. (2021) proposed a reinforcement learning-based
189   solution, both targeting the subsumption task in EL ontologies. However, these methods do not
190   support the construction of complex axioms involving negation or disjunction, nor do they support
191   instance retrieval.
192   For more expressive DLs such as ALC, embedding techniques have been proposed (Özçep et al.,
193   2020; Hohenecker & Lukasiewicz, 2020; Tang et al., 2022; Zhapa-Camacho & Hoehndorf, 2023a;b;
194   Özcep et al., 2023) with a primary focus on representing an ontology geometrically. The main
195   motivation of these approaches lies in proving that an ontology is satisfiable if it admits a satisfying
196   geometrical structure. For SROIQ, Holter et al. (2019) and Chen et al. (2021) proposed mapping
197   OWL ontologies to RDF graphs and applying Word2Vec (Church, 2017) over generated walks,
198
      considering concept membership and subsumption tasks. For an overview of existing works and
      their techniques, we refer the reader to the survey by Chen et al. (2024).
199
200   Finally, Hohenecker & Lukasiewicz (2020) proposed a neural architecture to perform logical entail-
201   ment over Datalog rules. Their approach considers instance checking (the entailment of instance
202   queries) in the context of data complexity. That is, the TBox in the KB is fixed, whereas the in-
203   put includes a query and a fixed-size ABox. Thus, their approach (1) is suitable in a setting where
204
      the TBox remains fixed, and (2) addresses only specific aspects of reasoning within an ontological
      framework.

208
209   The methodology of E BR consists of three main components: the embedding model, prediction
210   mechanisms, and the mapping of DL syntax to a neural semantic syntax. The source code of E BR is
211   provided in the supplemental material.

213   3.1   E MBEDDING M ODEL
214
215   As we will see in Section 3.3, mapping description logic syntax to our neural semantics requires only
      an engine that can answer queries of the form (x, rdf:type, ?), (x, r, ?), (?, r, y), and (x, ?, y).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Therefore, we first extract assertions and axioms of the form C(a) ≡ (x, rdf:type, C), r(x, y) ≡
217   (x, r, y), and C ⊑ D ≡ (C, rdfs:subClassOf, D) from a given knowledge base to construct
218   a knowledge graph G ⊆ E × R × E. We then use a KGE model to learn embeddings for entities
219   and relation types in the constructed graph. This yields a trained KGE model ϕΘ : E × R × E →
220   Vd (Vd is a vector space) which can answer the aforementioned queries. In our experiments, we
221   employ the state-of-the-art model K ECI (Demir & Ngonga Ngomo, 2023) (with p = 0, q = 1) for
222   embedding computation. In these settings, K ECI is equivalent to ComplEx (Trouillon et al., 2016),
223   which embeds entities and relation types into a complex vector space. Therefore, unless stated
224   elsewhere, ϕΘ is defined as
225                           ϕΘ : E × R × E → Cd ; ϕΘ (x, r, y) = Re(⟨x, r, ȳ⟩).
226   Here, x, r, and y are complex embeddings of the head entity x, the relation type r, and the tail entity
227   y, respectively; y denotes the complex conjugate of y.
228
229   3.2   P REDICTION MECHANISM
230
231   Once the model ϕΘ is trained, we construct a neural link predictor ϕ : E ∪ R 7→ [0, 1] which can
232   assign a score to every triple (x, r, y) based on the provided query. For a given query with missing
233
      tail (x, r, ?) or missing head (?, r, y), we rank all possible entities (individuals or atomic concepts)
      x ∈ E or y ∈ E based on the score ϕ(x, r, y). Higher scores indicate more likely matches. The
234
      same technique applies if there is a missing relation. That is, given a query (x, ?, y), we rank all
235
      possible relation types r ∈ R based on the score ϕ(x, r, y) and higher scores indicate potential
236   matches. Therefore, for any triple (x, r, y) in the knowledge graph representation of a knowledge
237   base, the score of the triple is computed as score(x, r, y) = ϕ(x, r, y).

      graph embeddings to perform robust reasoning on noisy and incomplete DL KBs. Our experiments

402

408   and effective solution for reasoning on real-world knowledge bases, where data imperfections are
