009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The scaling laws have become the de facto guidelines for designing large language
012            models (LLMs), but they were studied under the assumption of unlimited comput-
013            ing resources for both training and inference. As LLMs are increasingly used as
014            personalized intelligent assistants, their customization (i.e., learning through fine-
015            tuning) and deployment onto resource-constrained edge devices will become more
016
               and more prevalent. An urgent but open question is how a resource-constrained
               computing environment would affect the design choices for a personalized LLM.
017
               We study this problem empirically in this work. In particular, we consider the
018
               tradeoffs among a number of key design factors and their intertwined impacts on
019            learning efficiency and accuracy. The factors include the learning methods for LLM
020            customization, the amount of personalized data used for learning customization,
021            the types and sizes of LLMs, the compression methods of LLMs, the amount of
022            time afforded to learn, and the difficulty levels of the target use cases. Through
023            extensive experimentation and benchmarking, we draw a number of surprisingly
024            insightful guidelines for deploying LLMs onto resource-constrained devices. For
025            example, an optimal choice between parameter learning and RAG may vary de-
026            pending on the difficulty of the downstream task, the longer fine-tuning time does
027
               not necessarily help the model, and a compressed LLM may be a better choice than
               an uncompressed LLM to learn from limited personalized data.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The world has witnessed the growing interest in applying Large Language Models (LLMs) as a
033   potential solution to personalized AI assistants (Qin et al., 2024c). This is particularly true when
034   LLMs are deployed onto edge devices (edge LLMs) to meet the increasing needs of people’s daily life
035   assistance (Bevilacqua et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023), personalized companionship (et al., 2023b; Qin
036   et al., 2023), and real-time work assistance (et al., 2023a), where data privacy is of high priority Xu
037   et al. (2023) and constant internet connections may not be possible (Lin et al., 2022). Some exemplar
038   edge LLMs include LLM on Nvidia IGX (NVIDIA, 2024b), Chat with RTX (NVIDIA, 2024a), and
039
      TinyChat MIT (2024). edge LLMs can keep locally generated data from users and learn from that
      data locally. Such a high assurance of privacy coupled with LLMs’ strong reasoning capabilities can
040
      induce even more user interaction with the personal assistant at a deeper personal level than otherwise
041
      (Hämäläinen et al., 2023).
042
043   The scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020) has emerged as the standard for creating large language
044   models (LLMs), but a key assumption behind it is the reliance on unlimited computing power for
045
      both training and inference. However, the design of edge LLMs is no easy feat (Lin et al., 2024c)
      because of the limited resources available on edge devices. It requires allocating limited resources
046
      to accommodate multiple needs. An ideal resource allocation strategy needs to balance many key,
047
      yet sometimes conflicting, factors, such as the the types and sizes of pre-trained LLMs, the learning
048   methods and hyper-parameters for LLM customization, the amount of historical data used for learning
049   personalization, the compression methods of LLMs, the amount of time afforded to learn, and the
050   difficulty levels of the target user cases. For example, models can be selected from a wide range
051   of candidates with different model structures and sizes; while learning methods include parameter-
052   efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). A poorly designed edge
053   LLM may render a poor user experience as it cannot learn well from user locally-generated content.
      As such, a pressing yet unresolved issue is how those constraints of limited computing resources

      3.1                  O PTIMAL M ODEL AND C USTOMIZATION FOR LLM S ON THE E DGE
128
129   Study target. As illustrated in Figure 1, two primary questions need to be addressed within the
130   constraints of edge device resources: 1) Which LLM is best suited for the edge device? and 2) What
131   appropriate customization method should be applied to the chosen LLM? The performance of LLMs
132   of various sizes, combined with different customization methods, can vary significantly depending on
133   user tasks. These questions are particularly crucial for edge LLMs, as resource constraints necessitate
134   inevitable trade-offs between model size and customization methods.
135   Experiments and guidelines. To investigate these questions, we first compiled a collection of over 30
136   LLMs with diverse architectures and sizes, as described in Section A.2. We then selected five widely
137   used customization methods: LoRA(Hu et al., 2021), Prompt Tuning(Lester et al., 2021), Prefix
138   Tuning(Li & Liang, 2021), IA3(Liu et al., 2022), and RAG(Lewis et al., 2020). We evaluated these
139   LLMs on datasets designed for personalization tasks. It’s worth noting that LoRA, Prompt Tuning,
140   Prefix Tuning, and IA3 are categorized as Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT) methods(Ding
141   et al., 2023).
142   Table 1 presents results for 10 representative LLMs across the five customization methods on four tasks
143   of increasing difficulty. The featured models include Pythia(Py)-2.8B, OPT-2.7B, Llama(Ll)-2-3B,
144   StableLM(Sta)-3B, Gemma(Ge)-2B, Phi-2, Mistral(Mis)-7B quantized by GPTQ(G) (Frantar et al.,
145   2022), OpenChat(OpCt)-G, Gemma-7B-G, and Sheared(S)-Llama-2.7B pruned. Our experiments
146   reveal that LoRA consistently outperforms the other three PEFT methods, although it occasionally
147   underperforms compared to RAG. Consequently, we focus our subsequent investigations on LoRA
148   and RAG. Additional experimental results can be found in Appendix B.1.1.
149   Figure 2 showcases 13 models of varying sizes, ranging from 160M to 13B parameters, and their
150   performance on three tasks of increasing difficulty. The models, arranged on the x-axis based on their
151   weight sizes, include Pythia-160m, OPT-125m, OPT-350m, Pythia-410m, Phi-1.5, Gemma-2b-GPTQ,
152   Phi-2, Sheared-Llama-2.7b, Llama2-3b-GPTQ, Phi-3, Llama2-7b-GPTQ, OpenChat-3.5-GPTQ, and
153   Llama-13b-GPTQ. In our analysis, we selected LaMP-2 as the easy classification task, LaMP-6 as
154   the easy summarization task, and LaMP-4 as the hard summarization task. Through our extensive
155
      experiments, we conclude that the optimal choice of model and customization method depends on
      the complexity of the downstream task. We provide the general guidelines below:

        when an LLM is excessively small, like Pythia-70m or Pythia-160m shown in Appendix B.1.1, the

      Remark 1: Why does RAG work best with moderately complex tasks? We hypothesize that this
195   behavior stems from RAG’s reliance on the base LLM’s semantic understanding, which limits the
196   potential for performance improvement. Given the emergent abilities of LLMs (Wei et al., 2022),
197   where certain skills (particularly those required for solving more complex problems) only appear in
198   larger models, it is expected that smaller LLMs perform well on simpler tasks but struggle with more
199   complex ones. Furthermore, LLMs suited for edge deployment tend to be smaller in size, meaning
200   that even 7B-parameter models may lack the capacity to address highly complex tasks effectively
201   using RAG. In these cases, fine-tuning is more advantageous, as it teaches the models the semantic
202   structure of the expected answers, making parameter learning more effective than RAG for such
203
      difficult tasks.

205   3.2        C HOICE OF L O RA S TRATEGIES
206
207
      Study target. In Section 3.1, we identified the generally superior performance of LoRA and RAG,
      and determined the situations where LoRA is more suitable than RAG. This section delves deeper
208
      into LoRA settings, aiming to establish efficient strategies for optimizing LoRA configurations across
209
      various edge LLM use cases. This investigation is particularly crucial for edge LLMs, given the
210   limited resources available for conducting multiple experiments to determine optimal settings. In this
211   context, we address three key questions. First, is it necessary to experiment with different LoRA
212   settings to enhance model performance across various models and datasets? Second, is there an
213   optimal range or a universal LoRA setting that is effective for all edge LLM applications? Answering
214   these two questions can significantly benefit future edge LLM research and deployment by conserving
215   time and resources in performance optimization. Finally, we aim to understand the factors that cause
      LoRA to behave differently in edge LLMs compared to LLMs hosted on cloud servers.

234   Experiments and guidelines. Among the hyper-parameters of LoRA, rank and alpha are most
235   closely related to the number of trainable parameters, which directly impact resource usage. Moreover,
236   these two hyper-parameters significantly influence edge LLM customization (Zhang et al., 2024a).
237   While inappropriate combinations of rank and alpha can degrade edge LLM performance, identifying
238
      the optimal combination can be challenging. Different edge LLMs, operating under varying resource
      constraints, may require different optimal values for rank and alpha. Therefore, we investigate the
239
      impact of LoRA settings from two perspectives: the number of trainable parameters and the specific
240
      combinations of rank and alpha.
241
242   In Figure 3, we examine the performance of different-sized Pythia models given the value of rank
243   equal 8, 16, 32, 40, 48, 56, 64, 72, and 80, under the training time from one to eight hours. Additionally
244
      in Figure 4, we examine the performance of StableLM-2-1.6b and the larger StableLM-3b on eight
      commonly used rank and alpha combinations, under the training time 1 to 8 hours. Our findings are
245
      as follows:
246
      • Within the limited edge device resource, through experiments over various combinations of alpha
247     and rank, varying the value of alpha benefits less than fixing the value of alpha.
248
      • Increasing the training time might either have negligible LLM performance improvement or even
249
        lower the performance (A more detailed analysis on the training time will be provided in Section
250
        3.3). This situation remains consistent even when the value of rank is increased.
251
252
      • As shown in Figure 3 and Figure 4 and its supplemental results in Appendix B.1.2, Appendix B.1.3,
        and Appendix B.1.4, even in larger models like StableLM-3b and Pythia-2.8b deployed on edge
253
        devices with 10G or 16G RAM, increasing rank or alpha does not necessarily improve the model
254
        performance. Setting alpha and rank to (16, 16) or (16, 32) can work in most cases.

347   3.4          I MPACT OF U SER H ISTORY DATA VOLUME ON RAG P ERFORMANCE
348
349   Study target. Unlike LoRA, RAG involves simpler hyper-parameters, primarily related to max inner
350   product search (MIPS), its core functionality (Zhao et al., 2024). While LoRA’s challenge lies in
351
      parameter determination for edge LLMs, RAG’s difficulty stems from increasing stored user history
      data and search latency (Qin et al., 2024c). Cloud-hosted LLMs benefit from this data growth due
352
      to their strong reasoning capabilities. However, edge LLMs, with significantly smaller pre-trained
353
      knowledge bases and lower reasoning capabilities (Lin et al., 2024b), may not consistently gain
354   the same advantages. This study aims to investigate whether expanding stored user history data
355   consistently enhances RAG performance for edge LLMs or if there’s a threshold beyond which data
356   volume no longer improves performance, thus elucidating the relationship between data volume and
357   RAG performance in edge LLMs.
358
      Experiments and guidelines. We select 100 users and each user has up to 1000 samples of user
359
      history data. For the history data, we randomly maintain 0% to 100% of them, where 0% corresponds
360   to the case where no RAG is used. RAG takes an LLM as its content generator. We examine four
361   Pythia models with different sizes and four miscellaneous models including Llama-3b-v2-GPTQ,
362   TinyLlama-1.1b, TinyLlama-1.1b-GPTQ, and StableLM-2-1.6b. We calculate the performance
363   improvement brought by RAG using different amounts of user history data.
364
      As shown in Figure 7, we observe that increasing the amount of user history data to 1000 samples
365
      does not significantly enhance RAG performance compared to using only 100 samples. The RAG
366   performance based on eight models is quite consistent across all different sizes of user history data.
367   Noted, in Figure 7c, increasing user history data can even lower the RAG performance based on
368   Pythia-1b and Pythia-1.4b. The performance of RAG can be more related to the internal reasoning
369   capabilities of the LLM rather than the amount of user history data.
370
      Remark 4: Why does using less history data have marginal effects on RAG performance? These
371
      results are not surprising, particularly when considering that personalized data may not be as diverse
372   as general domain data. Additionally, connecting back to Section 3.1, we hypothesize that RAG
373   elevates the LLM close to a stage just before the “pre-power law” by providing grounded context
374   on the target’s format and basic information. Such a hypothesis might be the reason why RAG only
375   performs better than LoRA on tasks with moderate difficulty. For simple tasks, LoRA might be able
376   to reach a stage beyond “pre-power law” (which is the upper bound of RAG). For complex tasks,
377   RAG on edge LLMs cannot understand anything, whereas LoRA at least can learn some semantic
      structure, which helps the ROGUE metric.

413     3.5            C OMPARISON OF M ODEL C OMPRESSION T ECHNIQUES ON EDGE LLM S
414
415     Study target. Other than customization methods, the model itself is the other key component in our
416
        empirical study, shown in the right dashed rectangle in Figure 1. When deploying an LLM onto edge
        devices, it normally can be expected that the model needs to be compressed to fit the devices with
417
        various resource constraints. Hence, the critical question is: what is the best way to compress it? As
418
        such large-scale LLMs have demonstrated promising reasoning abilities when they are deployed on
419     cloud servers, their compressed versions are also expected to demonstrate similar reasoning abilities
420     when they are deployed on edge devices.
421
422
        Experiments and guidelines. Among various model compression techniques, there are three main
        approaches including quantization, pruning, and knowledge distillation. While each of them may
423
        have the potential to conduct a decent compressed LLM performance, it remains unknown which one
424
        of the methods can be more promising and appropriate under the constraints of edge devices. We take
425     the representative quantization method GPTQ, pruning method structure pruning, and knowledge
426     distillation model Phi to make investigations. Our findings through the investigations are as follows
427     (full experiments in Appendix C.1 to C.4):
428
429     • Using distilled models such as Phi is a safe option when the type and difficulty of the downstream
430       task cannot be determined. Phi-1.5 shows robustness towards both classification and summarization
431       tasks, where it generally has a ”brighter” performance heatmap shown in Figure 8, whereas Phi-3
          excels in classification tasks.


                                                                             8
        Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
        • Different compression techniques are good at different types of tasks. Summarization tasks require
433       larger (and better) LLMs to achieve emergent ability, thus GPTQ models such as OpenChat-3.5-
434       GPTQ work better than the other two compression techniques.
435
436     • Shearing is a very promising technique that preserves better performance, but they are less efficient
437       at saving RAM compared to quantization. Thus, shearing is generally not preferred for edge LLMs
438       where RAM capacity is a critical constraint.
439
440     Remark 5: What are the foundations behind each compression technique? From the results, we
441
        observe that model distillation is the most stable method for fitting a large LLM on edge devices,
        although it rarely (if ever) stands out as the best option. In contrast, GPTQ is less stable but often
442
        achieves the best performance when combined with certain LoRA parameters and training data.
443
        Shearing and pruning, however, perform poorly in comparison to these two methods. It should be
444     clear why sheared and pruned models are not ideal for edge devices. Pruning removes specific weights
445     from the model, but since the models must be fine-tuned for downstream tasks, the missing weights
446     are likely to be reintroduced during fine-tuning. As a result, pruned models offer no advantages in
447     RAM usage and do not retain the semantic understanding abilities of larger models. Additionally,
448     we hypothesize that the quality and memorization of pre-training datasets influence the fine-tuning
449     of these models. It is well known that LLMs memorize parts of their training datasets (Yin et al.,
450     2024; Carlini et al., 2023), but two uncertainties remain. First, we do not know the quality of the
451     memorized data (i.e., do they memorize only high-quality/significant data, or do they memorize
452
        anything?). Second, we do not know where they memorize the data. It is possible that models
        trained on low-quantity pre-training data exhibit unstable behavior in downstream tasks, but further
453
        investigation is needed to confirm this.

      3.6   C OMPARISON BETWEEN C OMPRESSED AND U NCOMPRESSED EDGE LLM S
487
488   Study target. After we compare the three compression techniques for their adaption on edge devices,
489   we further need to investigate the impact of such compression: Consider an edge device with 16G
490   RAM which is sufficient to host some uncompressed large-scale LLMs. While the compressed
491   models can learn and infer faster than their larger original versions, will such benefits compromise
492   the model’s performance? Other than model performance, learning efficiency under compressed and
493   uncompressed models is also important to consider.
494   Experiments and guidelines: Based on the experiments and their settings in Section 3.5, we
495   concentrate on two groups of comparisons: between quantized and un-quantized models, and between
496   pruned and un-pruned models. We select Gemma, Llama, and StableLM models. The results are
497   shown in Figure 9. Through our investigations, our findings are as follows:
498
      • While it is true that quantization will lead to some accuracy drops in most cases (Yin et al., 2024), it
499
        is noteworthy that with limited user history data for fine-tuning, the quantized model might perform
500
        better compared to unquantized counterparts on more challenging tasks.
501
502
      • Different from quantization which can benefit edge LLMs, pruning as shown from Figure 9j to
        Figure 9o indicates a clear performance drop in most LoRA settings and various datasets. Hence,
503
        pruning in edge LLM fine-tuning is not recommended.
504
505
      • A larger model is not always better. Llama3-8B is not performing well on many of the benchmarks,
        maybe because it is too big and not able to adapt to downstream tasks with limited user history data.
506
        Especially during classification tasks, we see that 2B to 3B models (quantized) are good enough.
507
        On the other hand, referring to Table 7, bigger models work better with summarization tasks that
508     require more semantic understanding of the context.
509
510   Remark 6: Why is a compressed model sometimes better? We hypothesize that such a phenomenon
511   shares the same intuition with the Section 3.5. An LLM memorizes its pre-training data, and they
512   are in the model’s weights in a numerical form that we cannot interpret. Fine-tuning toward the
513   downstream task is essentially the process of letting the LLM forget some of the pre-trained data
514   and memorize data from the target domain. Thus, quantization removes a lot of the pre-training
515
      information that is stored in the less significant bits of the LLM and makes the fine-tuning faster.
516
517   4     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
518
519   In conclusion, we present an empirical study for deploying large language models onto edge devices
520   with multiple resource constraints, and we provide guidelines for choosing the optimal strategy for
521   the deployment. Through experiments, we show that the optimal choice of LLM and customization
522   depends on the difficulty of the downstream task; during PEFT fine-tuning, the largest possible
523
      parameters and time are not the optimal settings; and compressed models are sometimes better than
      the original models on the edge due to their faster adaptation speed.
524
525   The insights provided by this study not only offer empirical guidelines for future deployment efforts
526   but also highlight key avenues for further research into adapting LLMs in resource-constrained
527   environments. In addition to these findings, we put forth several recommendations for the LLM
528   community, drawn from our experimental results:
529
      • Small LLMs could sometimes solve domain-specific problems better than larger models. The
530     community, especially the industry, should consider private small LLMs as alternatives to larger
531     models on many edge services.
532
      • When fine-tuning small LLMs on the edge, the tradeoff between time and performance shall be
533
        carefully considered. Larger trainable parameters and more training time are not always the best.
534     Different from the cloud LLMs where there are often no validation datasets, edge LLMs should
535     employ a validation set to ensure the best fine-tuning performance.
536
      • The RAG ability of edge LLMs is largely unexplored. Existing RAG frameworks heavily rely on
537     the semantic understanding ability of LLMs, which might not exist on the edge.
538
      • Model distillation is shown to be an effective solution that migrates the larger model’s semantic
539
        understanding ability to edge devices, but more research shall be directed into this area.

871
      Overview. Clusters and cloud servers that provide computing-as-a-service (CaaS) consist of millions
      of computation units like GPU with nearly unlimited RAM, storage, and energy (Qin et al., 2024a).
872
      Common edge devices such as smartphones, on the other hand, are usually equipped with a multi-
873
      core CPU with limited resources. Emerging technologies are beginning to integrate GPUs into
874   edge devices, exemplified by the Jetson Orin or phones powered by the Qualcomm Snapdragon 8
875   Gen, which offer computational powers nearly half that of a cluster-level GPU like the Nvidia RTX
876   2070. As computational power in edge devices increases, the feasibility of running edge LLMs is
877   significantly enhanced.
878
      Edge Devices for Experiments. In this paper, to prepare evaluations as we mentioned in Section 2,
879
      we selected some common edge devices with different RAM capacities and computation densities for
880   evaluation as shown in Table 2. RAM is a critical resource for enabling edge LLM learning. The
881   model weights must be loaded into RAM so that they can be updated and optimized to adapt to user
882   needs. In other words, when choosing to fine-tune an edge LLM, we should consider not only the
883   size of its weights but also the additional RAM usage due to parameter optimization and gradient
884   calculation. Conversely, when opting to use RAG to facilitate edge LLM learning, the consideration
885   shifts to only the weights size and data embedding size, as RAG requires storing all data embeddings
886   in RAM to perform MIPS.

       A.3     DATASETS
973
974    A.3.1    BACKGROUND
975
976    To prepare for evaluation in Section 2, it is crucial to utilize appropriate datasets. The edge LLM
977    is primarily employed for handling prompts made by a single user. Consequently, user-specific
978    datasets are necessary. Such datasets should feature data that is correlated and includes user-specific
979    information.
980    We have selected the LaMP datasets (Salemi et al., 2023) to evaluate our edge LLM. In LaMP, each
981    user has history data as shown in Table 5. The history data for each user consists of numerous
982    utterances that include a label and textual content. The edge LLM will primarily learn from this
983    historical data. The user prompts in the LaMP datasets are presented in Table 6. A well-trained edge
984    LLM should be capable of accurately responding to the prompts using the history data.
985
       The LaMP datasets consist of seven datasets. The first three datasets involve text classification,
986    including LaMP-1 for citation identification, LaMP-2 for movie tagging, and LaMP-3 for product
987    rating. The remaining four datasets pertain to text generation, including LaMP-4 for news headline
988    generation, LaMP-5 for scholarly title generation, LaMP-6 for email subject generation, and LaMP-7
989    for tweet paraphrasing.
990

       A.3.2    DATA P REPARATION
992
993    As we mentioned in Section 2, we prepare the data for our edge LLM evaluation from two perspectives:
994    user-level and task-level.
995
996
       User-level:
997    Each dataset contains many users, and each user has many samples of user history data and user
998    query data. One sample of user history data and user query data are shown in Table 5 and in Table 6.
999    In terms of generalization, we randomly select 100 users and calculate the average performance of
1000   edge LLMs over the 100 users in our evaluation.
1001   Task-level:
1002
1003
       When evaluating various edge LLMs across multiple datasets (tasks), it is important to account for
       the latent factor of task difficulty, which can influence the evaluation results (Ge et al., 2024). Within
1004
       a single dataset, we assume that utterances exhibit similar levels of difficulty (Balyan et al., 2020).
1005
       However, difficulty can vary significantly across different datasets, making it a critical consideration
1006   for accurate assessment. Previous research emphasizes the importance of considering task difficulty in
1007   evaluating machine learning models, as this factor can substantially impact performance comparisons
1008   across tasks and models. In particular, zero-shot learning has been used as an effective tool to estimate
1009   the inherent difficulty of datasets on strong pre-trained models, allowing for more refined assessments
1010   (Raina et al., 2024). To this end, we evaluate the performance of models such as GPT-4 (Achiam
1011   et al., 2023), Claude 3 Opus (Kevian et al., 2024), Gemini 1.0 Pro (Team et al., 2023), and Llama 3
1012   70B (Meta, 2023) on selected test data from each dataset, with the results summarized in Table 4.
1013   For classification tasks, where the number of choices varies across tasks, we adopt a more equitable
1014   assessment method inspired by (De Diego et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020), which normalizes accuracy
1015   relative to human expert performance. Since obtaining human performance benchmarks on these
1016   datasets was not feasible, we instead normalized accuracy by dividing the LLMs’ achieved accuracy
1017   by the accuracy expected from random guessing, a metric we refer to as ”normalized accuracy.”
                                                 accuracy
1018   This is mathematically expressed as 1/No.     choices . This approach allows us to address task difficulty
1019   disparities across datasets, as normalizing the model’s accuracy against random guessing helps isolate
1020   performance differences related to the models themselves rather than the intrinsic difficulty of the
1021   task. Similar methods for task difficulty normalization have been discussed in literature (Yin et al.,
1022
       2024), highlighting the importance of using a baseline such as random guessing or human experts
       when comparing performance across datasets of varying complexities.
1023
1024   For summarization tasks, normalized accuracy is equivalent to the raw accuracy, since random
1025   guessing does not apply in such contexts. This metric allows us to gauge task difficulty, where a
       higher normalized accuracy reflects a less challenging task.

1060   A.5    M ODEL C OMPRESSION
1061
1062
       As we mentioned in Section 2, model compression is another component in our empirical study.
       The LLMs were originally designed and trained for cluster computing (Qin et al., 2024b). Their
1063
       size can easily go beyond the size of edge device RAM size. Furthermore, the larger the model, the
1064
       longer time it takes to train and make inferences (Hoffer et al., 2017). Model compression can help
1065   such LLMs deploy on edge devices (edge LLMs) and improve their training and inference efficiency.
1066   There are three common implementation methods for model compression: quantization, pruning, and
1067   distillation.
1068
       Quantization The default precision for model weights in LLMs is FP32 or FP16. By reducing
1069
       the weight precision from 32 bits down to 3 or 4 bits (quantization), we can significantly decrease
1070
       the model size and inference time due to simpler gradient computations. However, reducing the
1071   number of bits in weights can compromise their precision and, consequently, model performance.
1072   The primary goal of quantization is to reduce weight bits without degrading performance. The main
1073   strategy for LLM quantization involves quantizing and then calibrating the pre-trained model, a
1074   technique known as post-training quantization (PTQ) (Yao et al., 2024). Several implementations of
1075   PTQ for LLMs exist, including SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2023), AWQ (Lin et al., 2023), LLM-QAT
1076   (Liu et al., 2023), and GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022).

       Pruning Different from quantization, pruning lightens the model by trimming off certain weights
       without lowering the model performance. Compared to quantization, pruning receives less attention
1085
       in the beginning. One reason can be the high weight complexity of LLMs which makes it difficult
1086
       to prune them while maintaining their performance. Furthermore, since the core modules of LLMs,
1087   the transformer blocks, make the structures of LLMs complex, pruning the LLMs can be more
1088   challenging than pruning on pure neural networks with simple structures. Within the existing pruning
1089   works including Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023), and Sheared-LLaMA (Xia
1090   et al., 2023), Sheared-LLaMA has demonstrated decent performance with published LLM weights.
1091
       To simplify the experiments and avoid potential issues with implementing Wanda or LLM-Pruner to
1092
       prune LLMs, we use Sheared-LLaMA in our experiments.
1093
1094   Distillation Other than quantization and pruning, another method involves using a smaller model to
1095   emulate and learn from a larger model, a process known as distillation. Microsoft introduced a small
1096
       language model (SLM) called Phi, which leverages synthetic datasets specifically created by GPT-3.5
       to learn domains such as common sense reasoning, general knowledge, science, daily activities, and
1097
       theory of mind (Gunasekar et al., 2023). This approach represents a novel departure from traditional
1098
       large language models (LLMs), allowing SLMs to acquire dense knowledge from LLMs while
1099   maintaining a smaller size. Although other works such as Distilling Step-by-Step (Hsieh et al., 2023),
1100   MetaIE (Peng et al., 2024a), and MLFS (Kundu et al., 2024) have proposed distillation methods, they
1101   have not yielded a well-trained model comparable to Phi, which benefited from Microsoft’s extensive
1102   resources and data. Therefore, we have chosen to use Phi in our experiments.

       A.6   M ETHODS FOR EDGE LLM CUSTOMIZATION
1105
1106   In Section 2, we also mention the customization. There are two methods for edge LLM customization:
1107   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Hu et al., 2023) and Retrieval-Augmented Generation
1108   (RAG) (Chen et al., 2024). PEFT is an approach that improves edge LLM performance by tuning a
1109   small portion of the model’s parameters. RAG, on the other hand, focuses on storing user history data
1110   and retrieving appropriate information to provide more context to user prompts. While both methods
1111   can generally enhance cloud-based LLM performance (Lakatos et al., 2024), their effectiveness can
1112
       vary significantly depending on different settings and datasets. It is crucial to investigate whether
       similar variations in performance exist for edge LLMs.
1113
1114   PEFT: Compared to various PEFT implementations, low-rank adaption (LoRA) has demonstrated
1115   promising capabilities (Hu et al., 2021) in fine-tuning a wide range of LLMs and significantly improv-
1116   ing their performance via updating a small portion of trainable parameters. The two hyperparameters
1117   rank and alpha can mostly impact the LoRA-tuning model performance (Lee et al., 2023). Hence, we
1118
       set all other hyperparameters to default, as described in Appendix A.7, and explore a wide range of
       rank and alpha value combinations and their corresponding trainable parameters in the performance
1119
       of the edge LLM.
1120
1121   RAG: RAG consists of a retriever that is commonly based on max inner product search (MIPS) and a
1122   generator which usually is an LLM. The retriever gets the appropriate query-relevant information
1123   from user history data to formalize the final prompt (Lewis et al., 2020). While this method does
1124
       not consume resources to fine-tune the model, it requires saving all user history data embeddings for
       information retrieval. As user history data accumulates, the data embeddings can become a significant
1125
       burden on the edge device (Qin et al., 2024c). In our experiments, we focus on the size of user history
1126
       data and set all other hyperparameters to default, as shown in Appendix A.7.
1127
1128   Our experiments investigate how different settings in each method can impact model performance
1129   and study which method can outperform the other under certain circumstances.

       D.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS C USTOMIZATION FOR D OWNSTREAM TASKS
2973
2974   Different from BERT family language models (Devlin et al., 2019; Sanh, 2019; Liu, 2019; Qin et al.,
2975   2021), Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama (Touvron et al.,
2976   2023), and T5 (Raffel et al., 2023), are pre-trained on vast amounts of text data (Pan et al., 2024).
2977   This pretraining enables them to learn a wide range of language patterns, structures, and knowledge.
2978   However, to achieve optimal performance on specific downstream tasks such as classification,
2979
       summarization, or translation, these models often require further customizations (Zhang et al., 2024b;
       Hu et al., 2024). Several approaches can be employed to tailor LLMs for specific downstream
2980
       tasks, and we can generally group them into two categories: fine-tuning and prompt engineering.
2981
       Fine-tuning uses targetted datasets to update the weights of LLMs so that the models can be familiar
2982   with the desired knowledge and structure of the outputs (Zhang et al., 2024b). The dominant approach
2983   uses variations of low-rank approximation (LoRA) techniques (Hu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2024;
2984   Dettmers et al., 2023; Babakniya et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024), which freeze the original LLM
2985   weights and add additional low-rank decomposition weights that simulate weight updates. On the
2986   other hand, prompt engineering achieves downstream optimization without changing the LLM’s
2987   weights. Typical approaches include few-shot in-context learning (Coda-Forno et al., 2023) and
2988   retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020). In particular, RAG retrieves relevant
2989   documents from a knowledge base and adds the knowledge pertinent to the prompt. It grounds
2990
       the generated answer with the retrieved knowledge and significantly increases response quality on
       commercial Cloud LLMs.
2991
2992
2993   D.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL E VALUATIONS
2994
       The field of Large Language Models (LLMs) has seen a significant amount of research focusing on
2995   various aspects of model evaluation (Guo et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024). Numerous
2996   studies have proposed benchmarks to systematically evaluate the capabilities of LLMs across different
2997   tasks. On the benchmark side, notable benchmarks include MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), GPQA
2998   (Rein et al., 2023), HumanEval (Peng et al., 2024b), and GSM-8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), but they are
