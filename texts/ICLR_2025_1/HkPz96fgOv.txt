010
011
               (Pseudo)random sampling, a costly yet widely used method in (probabilistic) ma-
012            chine learning and Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithms, remains unfeasible
013            on a truly large scale due to unmet computational requirements. We introduce
014            an energy-efficient algorithm for uniform Float16 sampling, utilizing a room-
015            temperature stochastic magnetic tunnel junction device to generate truly random
016            floating-point numbers. By avoiding expensive symbolic computation and map-
017            ping physical phenomena directly to the statistical properties of the floating-point
018            format and uniform distribution, our approach achieves a higher level of energy ef-
019            ficiency than the state-of-the-art Mersenne-Twister algorithm by a minimum factor
020
               of 9721 and an improvement factor of 5649 compared to the more energy-efficient
               PCG algorithm. Building on this sampling technique and hardware framework, we
021
               decompose arbitrary distributions into many non-overlapping approximative uni-
022
               form distributions along with convolution and prior-likelihood operations, which
023            allows us to sample from any 1D distribution without closed-form solutions. We
024            provide measurements of the potential accumulated approximation errors, demon-
025            strating the effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The widespread implementation of artificial intelligence (AI) incurs significant energy use, financial
032   costs, and CO2 emissions. This not only increases the cost of products, but also presents obstacles
033
      in addressing climate change. Traditional AI methods like deep learning lack the ability to quantify
      uncertainties, which is crucial to address issues such as hallucinations or ensuring safety in critical
034
      tasks. Probabilistic machine learning, while providing a theoretical framework for achieving much-
035
      needed uncertainty quantification, also suffers from high energy consumption and is unviable on a
036   truly large scale due to insufficient computational resources (Izmailov et al., 2021). At the heart
037   of probabilistic machine learning and Bayesian inference is Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
038   sampling (Kass et al., 1998; Murphy, 2012; Hoffman & Gelman, 2014). Although effective in
039   generating samples from complex distributions, MCMC is known for its substantial computational
040   and energy requirements, making it unsuitable for large-scale deployment for applications such as
041   Bayesian neural networks (Izmailov et al., 2021). In general, random number generation is an
042   expensive task that is required in many machine learning algorithms.
043   To address these challenges, this paper proposes a novel hardware framework aimed at improving
044   energy efficiency, in particular tailored for probabilistic machine learning methods. Our frame-
045   work builds on uniform floating-point format sampling utilizing stochastically switching magnetic
046   tunnel junction (s-MTJ) devices as a foundation, achieving significant gains in both computational
047   resources and energy consumption compared to current pseudorandom number generators. In con-
048   trast to existing generators, this device-focused strategy not only enhances sampling efficiency but
049   also incorporates genuine randomness originating from the thermal noise in our devices. Simultane-
050
      ously, this noise is crucial for the probabilistic functioning of the s-MTJs and is associated with low
      energy costs during operation.
051
052   We present an acceleration approach for efficiently handling probability distributions. Our experi-
053   ments confirm its effectiveness by quantifying potential approximation errors. This work does not
      seek to create a one-size-fits-all setting for all possible probabilistic algorithms that manage proba-

054
      bility distributions. Rather, we offer a solution approach that researchers can customize and utilize
055   based on individual required sampling resolution dependent on a specific algorithm.
056
057   Our contributions can summarized as follows:
058
            1. We present a novel, highly energy-efficient stochastically switching magnetic tunnel junc-
059
               tion device which is designed to improve both the energy efficiency and precision of our
060            sampling approach. The device is capable of generating samples from a Bernoulli distribu-
061            tion whose parameter p can be controlled using a current bias.
062
            2. We present a closed-form solution that defines the parameters for a collection of Bernoulli
063
               distributions applied to the bit positions of the floating-point format, leading to samples that
064
               adhere to a distribution without the need for symbolic calculations. Our simulations indi-
065            cate that this hardware configuration surpasses existing random number generators in terms
066            of energy efficiency by a factor of 5649 when using Float16. Additionally, our method
067            achieves genuine randomness through the use of thermal noise in our hardware devices. In
068            general, this approach is suitable for any entropy source device or even (pseudo)random
069            number generator that can produce bits in a reliable (and efficient) Bernoulli fashion.
070         3. We propose the representation of arbitrary one-dimensional distributions using a mixture
071            of uniforms model. This approach utilizes our highly efficient hardware-supported uniform
072            sampling approach to enable sampling from arbitrary 1D distributions. We introduce con-
073            volution and prior-likelihood transformations for this model to learn and sample from such
074            distributions without closed-form solutions. Our experimental evaluation shows that this
075            method is effective, as evidenced by the small approximation error in KL-divergence when
076            compared to sampling results from known closed-form solutions (0.0343 ± 0.1473 for the
077            convolution and 0.0141 ± 0.1073 for prior-likelihood) for basic usage scenarios.

      All code of our experiments is available at www.github.com/TBA.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   A majority of artificial intelligence algorithms rely on random number generators. Random num-
097   ber generators (RNG) are employed for weight initialization or dropout in deep learning or taking
098   random actions in reinforcement learning. In probabilistic machine learning, Markov-Chain-Monte-
099   Carlo (MCMC) algorithms utilize them for sampling from proposal distributions or for making de-
100   cisions on whether to accept or reject samples based on random draws.
101
      Hence, the research community focused on the development of efficient random number generators
102   (L’Ecuyer, 1994) and their infrastructure (Tan et al., 2021; Nagasaka et al., 2018) shares similar-
103   ities to this work. Physical (true) random number generators (TRNG) using physical devices is
104   an active research field since the 1950s (L’Ecuyer, 2017). Currently used random number genera-
105   tors are often feasability-motivated free-running oscillators with randomness from electronic noise
106   (Stipcevic & Koç, 2014). A very recent subfield are Quantum Based Random Number generators
107   (QRNG) (Mannalatha et al., 2023; Józwiak et al., 2024; Stipcevic & Koç, 2014; Herrero-Collantes &
      Garcia-Escartin, 2017). The concept of employing stochastic magnetic tunnel junctions for random


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      number generation has been investigated in recent years. Although these methods generally outper-
109   form traditional algorithmic random number generators in terms of energy efficiency, they lack three
110   crucial features for machine learning applications that our approach addresses. First, they lack the
111   ability to directly produce results using the floating-point format (Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
112   2022; Oosawa et al., 2015; Perach et al., 2019), which is critical for machine learning applications.
113   Converting results to floating-point format later (Fu et al., 2023) introduces unnecessary overhead,
114   reducing energy efficiency. In general, the unequal spacing characteristic of the floating-point format
115   complicates the transition from integers, making it non-trivial to maintain all possible floating-point
116   number candidates within a specific distribution. Second, most works lack the flexibility to generate
117
      arbitrary distributions. Zhang et al. (2024) propose using a conditional probability table for this
      purpose. However, their method involves adjusting the current bias for each bit in a sample and
118
      repeating this process for every required sample, which substantially increases energy consumption.
119
      In addition, sequential operations that scale with the number of bits reduce the achievable sampling
120   speed. Furthermore, they address integer generation only, making their work unsuitable for machine
121   learning applications. Finally, none of the works addresses directly sampling from a product of
122   likelihoods (distributions) as often encountered in probabilistic machine learning. It should be noted
123   that our conceptual approach can in principle be applied with any RNG that generates parametrizable
124   Bernoulli distributions, given that they are sufficiently (energy-)efficient.
125
      MCMC methods like Metropolis-Hastings (Hastings, 1970; Metropolis et al., 1953) and the state-of-
126   the-art Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) (Neal et al., 2011) algorithm are crucial for this research.
127   The use of MCMC for Bayesian inference and probabilistic machine learning represents the core
128   application area of this paper, aiming to achieve computational and energy-efficient deployment at
129   a large scale. Furthermore, (pseudo)random number generation is often discussed in the context of
130   Monte Carlo approaches as they are closely intertwined and take advantage of efficient random num-
131   ber sampling as proposed by us. On the other hand, we propose an alternative hardware-supported
132   approach to the MCMC algorithms themselves with our mixture model. In general, our approach
133   differs from traditional pseudorandom number generation of MCMC algorithms as we employ a
134
      genuinely random sampling method, making it less suitable for scenarios requiring reproducibility
      (L’Ecuyer, 2017; Holohan, 2023; Hill, 2015) or reversability (Yoginath & Perumalla, 2018), our
135
      objectives align in efficient random number generation and genuine statistical independence.
136
137   Antunes & Hill (2024) accurately measured the energy usage of random number generators
138   (Mersenne-Twister, PCG, and Philox) in programming languages and frameworks such as Python,
139   C, Numpy, Tensorflow, and PyTorch, thus providing a quantification of energy consumption in tools
140
      relevant to AI. The energy measurements of this benchmark serve as baseline against our approach.

      4.1   P ROBABILISTIC S PINTRONIC D EVICES
165
166   Spintronic devices are a class of computing (logic and memory) devices that harness the spin of
167   electrons (in addition to their charge) for computation (Žutić et al., 2004). This contrasts with tradi-
168   tional electronic devices which only use electron charges for computation. In essence, we interpret
169   the upwards and downwards electronic spin as binary states instead of their charge. Changing state
170   corresponds to changing the direction of the spin. The field of spintronics holds potential for low-
171   ering energy consumption in comparison to conventional electronics. Applying insufficient current
172
      results in the electronic spin states exhibiting probabilistic behavior due to ambient temperature. In
      this research, we utilize this probabilistic behavior by aligning it directly with algorithmic require-
173
      ments.
174
175   Spintronic devices are built using magnetic materials, as the magnetization (magnetic moment per
176   unit volume) of a magnet is a macroscopic manifestation of its correlated electron spins. The pro-
177   totypical spintronic device, called the magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ), is a three-layer device which
178
      can act both as a memory unit and a switch (Moodera et al., 1995). It consists of two ferromag-
      netic layers separated by a thin, insulating non-magnetic layer. When the magnetization of the two
179
      ferromagnetic layers is aligned parallel to each other, the MTJ exhibits a low resistance (RP ). Con-
180
      versely, when the two magnetizations are aligned anti-parallel, the MTJ exhibits a high resistance
181   (RAP ). By virtue of the two discrete resistance states, an MTJ can act as a memory bit as well as a
182   switch. In practice, the MTJs are constructed such that one of the ferromagnetic layers stays fixed,
183   while the other layer’s magnetization can be easily toggled (free layer, FL). Thus, by toggling the
184   FL, using a magnetic field or electric currents, the MTJ can be switched between its ‘0’ and ‘1’ state.
185
      An MTJ can serve as a natural source of randomness upon aggressive scaling, i.e. when the FL of
186
      the MTJ is shrunk to such a small volume that it toggles randomly just due to thermal energy in
187   the vicinity. It is worth noting that the s-MTJ can produce a Bernoulli distribution like probability
188   density function (PDF), with p = 0.5, without any external stimulus, by virtue of only the ambient
189   temperature. However, applying a bias current across the s-MTJ can allow tuning of the PDF through
190   the spin transfer torque mechanism. As shown in Figure 5c-f of Appendix A, applying a positive bias
191   current across the device makes the high resistance state more favorable, while applying a negative
192   current has the opposite effect. In fact, by applying an appropriate bias current across the s-MTJ,
193   using a simple current-mode digital to analog converter as shown in Figure 6a of Appendix A, we
194   can achieve precise control over the Bernoulli parameter (p) exhibited by the s-MTJ. The p-value of
195
      the s-MTJ responds to the bias current through a sigmoidal dependence. A more detailed version of
      this section on the physical principles, device structure and simulations of the s-MTJ device can be
196
      found in Appendix A.

348   5.1   E NERGY C ONSUMPTION OF THE S -MTJ A PPROACH
349
350   Figure 1 depicts our hardware configuration for sampling a single Float16 value. Each di is an s-
351   MTJ device. The devices d10 , · · · , d14 for the exponent are equipped with 4 control bits to adjust
352   the current bias ci , which corresponds to the Bernoulli probability. The other devices are set to a
353   fixed current bias equivalent to a Bernoulli of 0.5. The resolution, which determines how accurately
354   we can set the Bernoulli distributions for a device, is dependent on the number of control bits and is
355
      visualized in Figure 2. This Figure displays the specific Bernoulli values achievable with 4 control
      bits. Although additional control bits could allow for more precise settings, we restrict this number
356
      to 4 due to physical limitations in setting current biases in hardware with higher resolution while
357
      keeping the bias circuit simple (and hence energy-efficient). Our approach focuses on achieving
358   high accuracy around a probability of 1 (cf. configuration in Section 5.2) by taking advantage of
359   the characteristics of the sigmoid function, thus making 4 bits sufficient for achieving the required
360   probability density function.
361
      For our specific case, where the s-MTJs are being configured to generate a uniform distribution of
362
      Float16 samples, the p for each s-MTJ is predetermined and fixed. All the mantissa and sign bits
363
      require p = 0.5, which is exhibited by the s-MTJ without any current bias (cf. 4.1 and 4.2). Thus,
364   these eleven s-MTJs do not require a current biasing circuit. The predetermined p-values for the five
365   exponent bits correspond to specific current biases as shown in Figure 2, which amount to a total
366   power consumption of 20.86 W, as determined through SPICE simulations (see Appendix D). For a
367   sampling rate of 1 MHz, this corresponds to 20.86 pJ biasing energy per Float16 sample. Reading
368   the state of all sixteen s-MTJs, assuming a nominal resistance of 1 kΩ and 10 ns readout with 10 µA
369   probe current, amounts to an additional readout energy dissipation of 16 fJ per Float16 sample.
370   Given a hardware accelerator-style architecture, our system is designed with an embarrassingly par-
371   allel structure, capable of producing samples every 1 µs. Energy-wise, there is no difference between
372   parallel and sequential setups. Using min-max normalization, sampled intervals can be transformed

503   Control bit errors are not considered, attributing the error solely to the theoretical approach. We

      A    A DDITIONAL I NFORMATION ON THE S PINTRONIC D EVICE

      Spintronic devices are a class of computing (logic and memory) devices that harness the spin of
651   electrons (in addition to their charge) for computation. This contrasts with traditional electronic de-
652   vices which only use electron charges for computation. Spintronic devices are built using magnetic
653   materials, as the magnetization (magnetic moment per unit volume) of a magnet is a macroscopic
654   manifestation of its correlated electron spins. The prototypical spintronic device, called the mag-
655   netic tunnel junction (MTJ), is a three-layer device which can act both as a memory unit and a
656   switch (Žutić et al., 2004; Moodera et al., 1995). It consists of two ferromagnetic layers separated
657   by a thin, insulating non-magnetic layer. When the magnetization of the two ferromagnetic layers
658   is aligned parallel to each other, the MTJ exhibits a low resistance (RP ). Conversely, when the two
659   magnetizations are aligned anti-parallel, the MTJ exhibits a high resistance (RAP ). By virtue of the
660
      two discrete resistance states, an MTJ can act as a memory bit as well as a switch. In practice, the
      MTJs are constructed such that one of the ferromagnetic layers stays fixed, while the other layer’s
661
      magnetization can be easily toggled (free layer, FL). Thus, by toggling the FL, using a magnetic
662
      field or electric currents, the MTJ can be switched between its ‘0’ and ‘1’ state.
663
664   An MTJ can serve as a natural source of randomness upon aggressive scaling, i.e. when the FL
665   of the MTJ is shrunk to such a small volume that it toggles randomly just due to thermal energy
666
      in the vicinity. As schematically illustrated in Figure 4a, the self-energy of the magnetic layer is
      minimum and equal for the magnetization pointing vertically up or down, i.e. polar angle θM = 0o
667
      or 180o , respectively. The self-energy is maximum for the horizontal orientation (θM = 90o ). The
668
      corresponding energy barrier, ∆E dictates the time scale at which the magnet can toggle between
669   the up and down oriented states owing to thermal energy. This time scale follows an Arrhenius law
670   dependence (Camsari et al., 2019), i.e.
671
672                                                          ∆E
                                                  τ↑↓ = τ0 e kT ,                                        (19)
673
674   where, τ0 is the inverse of attempt frequency, typically of the order of 1 ns, k is the Boltzmann
675   constant and T is the ambient temperature. The energy barrier for a magnet is ∆E = KU V =
676   µ0 HK MS V /2, where KU , V , HK and MS are the magnet’s uniaxial anisotropy energy, volume,
677   effective magnetic anisotropy field and saturation magnetization, respectively. µ0 is the magnetic
678   permeability of free space. Thus, it can be observed that by reducing the volume V of the mag-
679   netic free layer, we can make its ∆E comparable to kT and achieve natural toggling frequencies
680   of computational relevance, as shown in Figure 4b. Figure 5a shows a time-domain plot of the
681   normalized state of such an s-MTJ, calculated using micromagnetic simulations with the MuMax3

      C    P OTENTIAL L IMITATIONS OF S IMULATED S -MTJ D EVICES
865
866
      While the proposed s-MTJ devices show great promise for energy-efficient true random number
867   generation, their practical implementation remains an active area of research. The materials system
868   integral to achieving reliable device performance at extreme scaling—particularly 2D magnetic ma-
869   terials—presents unique challenges due to their relative novelty. Key hurdles include the wafer-scale
870   growth of room-temperature monolayer 2D magnetic materials with BEOL compatibility and their
871   integration with tunnel barriers (e.g., 2D hBN or bulk MgO) and spin-orbit torque layers. These
872   challenges remain unmet at a wafer scale. Nonetheless, the promising benchmarking results pre-
873   sented in this study may serve as a catalyst for experimental advancements toward realizing these
874   hardware goals. Additionally, we must consider the effects of process-voltage-temperature (PVT)
875
      variations on s-MTJs. Leading semiconductor foundries have already established mature MTJ fab-
      rication processes for embedded MRAM (e.g., high-level caches), demonstrating the feasibility of
876
      fabricating PVT-robust MTJ devices for commercial applications. However, for s-MTJs specifi-
877
      cally, temperature variations may have unique implications. Unlike traditional deterministic MTJs,
878   the natural frequency of stochastic switching in s-MTJs is highly temperature-dependent. To ensure
879   uncorrelated samples, the sampling frequency must remain below the device’s natural frequency
880   across the entire rated operating temperature range. It is worth noting, however, that under typical
881   operating conditions, devices are likely to experience heating, which increases the natural frequency
882   of the devices. This inherent behavior provides a safety margin, ensuring that the samples remain
883   uncorrelated even in elevated temperature conditions.

      D    P OWER E STIMATION OF THE C URRENT B IASING C IRCUIT
919
920
      The current biasing circuit was simulated using Cadence Virtuoso using the Global Foundries
921   22FDX (22 nm FDSOI) process design kit. The circuit has been designed for a maximum bias
922   current of 20 µA to attain an s-MTJ with Bernoulli parameter p = 0.99. The current levels cor-
923   responding to p = 0.67 and p = 0.99 are divided into 4-bit resolution (Figure 2). The four bias
924   bits (B0-B3) are fed to the transistors P0, P1, P2, P3 (LSB to MSB), which are sized to produce
925   currents I0 , 2I0 , 4I0 and 8I0 , respectively, when the corresponding bias bit it ‘1’. A constant current
926   Ibase = 2.82 µA is additionally supplied through P4 to create a baseline of p = 0.67 for the s-MTJs.
927   The transistors are operated at a low supply voltage of 0.35 V to achieve a small I0 = 1.14 µA.
928   Thus, each exponent bit can be set to its requisite Bernoulli parameter by appropriately setting the
929
      4-bit bias word, and the power dissipation in the biasing circuit can be estimated for each of the
      exponent bits. Lengths of all the transistors are set to 20 nm. Width of P4 is set to 260 nm, while the
930
      widths of P0, P1, P2 and P3 are 100 nm, 200 nm, 400 nm, and 800 nm, respectively. As discussed
931
      in the main text, our proposed method requires only positive current biases for the stochastic MTJs.
932   Thus, the unipolar current mode DAC proposed here suffices for our application. For more general
933   use cases where both positive and negative bias currents may be needed, a bipolar current-steering
934   DAC can be utilized.
