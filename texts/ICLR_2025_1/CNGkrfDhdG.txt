011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Temporal knowledge graphs (TKGs) reflect the evolution patterns of facts, which
014            can be summarized as logical rules and applied to forecast future facts. However,
015            existing logical reasoning methods on TKGs face two limitations: 1) A lack of
016
               efficient strategies for extracting logical paths. 2) Insufficient utilization of struc-
               tural and textual information. To bridge these gaps, we propose CoLR, a two-stage
017
               framework that mines relation dependencies and textual semantics for Coherent
018
               Logical Reasoning over TKGs. In the first stage, we construct a temporal relation
019            structure graph (TRSG) composed of relations and cohesion weights between them.
020            Besides, we define a novel time-fusion search graph (TFSG) along with TRSG
021            to facilitate efficient and reliable temporal path searching. In the second stage,
022            the textual content and timestamp sequences from these paths undergo encod-
023            ing via a pre-trained language model and a time sequence encoder to accurately
024            capture potential logical rules. Additionally, for quadruplets missing paths, his-
025            torical edges sampled based on relation cohesion are used as supplements. Given
026            the limitations of existing benchmark datasets in evaluating accuracy, general-
027
               ization, and robustness, we construct three new datasets tailored to transductive,
               inductive, and few-shot scenarios, respectively. These datasets, combined with
028
               four widely-used real-world datasets, are employed to evaluate our model com-
029
               prehensively. Experimental results demonstrate that our approach significantly
030            outperforms existing methods across all three scenarios. Our code is available at
031            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CoLR-0839.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Temporal Knowledge Graphs (TKGs) are a pivotal method for representing the dynamic facts of the
037   real world. Due to their high application value in personalized recommendations (Wang et al., 2022)
038   and conversational systems Shang et al. (2022), they have attracted widespread research attention.
039   Reasoning tasks on TKGs encompass not only filling missing links within historical subgraphs
040   (interpolation reasoning) but also predicting future interactions among entities by analyzing historical
041   patterns (extrapolation reasoning). However, to achieve extrapolation reasoning, inductive capabilities
042   are significant, as new entities emerge over time. As shown in Figure 1, once Donald Trump assumes
043   the presidency, he is regarded as a new entity. Therefore, more fundamental connections between
044
      facts should be uncovered, such as the logical correlations between relations.
045   Recently, several TKG Reasoning (TKGR) methods have been proposed, where multi-hop logical
046   reasoning methods learn logical rules from the historical multi-hop relation paths Liu et al. (2022);
047   Niu & Li (2023); Xiong et al. (2023); Mei et al. (2024). During the reasoning process, these methods
048   predict target entities by applying these rules to give confidence scores to each candidate. Assuming
049   the stability of these logical rules, such methods can effectively generalize to inductive scenarios,
050
      with the logical rules offering interpretability for the reasoning process. For example, Imran Khan in
      Figure 1(a) can be found as logical correlation between relations Express intent to meet or negotiate
051
      and Make a visit has been learned in Figure 1(b)
052
053   The core of multi-hop logical reasoning lies in finding reliable multi-hop paths and their scoring
      strategies. Traditional symbolic methods Omran et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2022) perform extensive

065   random walks to sample relation paths and assess rule confidence with global frequency information.
066   However, this process may incur substantial computational overhead and potentially overlook low-
067   frequency rules. Recent methods Xiong et al. (2023); Mei et al. (2024) simplify the rule learning
068   process by encoding relation paths and evaluating confidence based on the semantic similarity
069   between paths. Nonetheless, the majority of paths between the subject and object are predominantly
070   irrelevant noise paths, which may potentially interfere with inference. Moreover, existing methods
071
      exhibit significant limitations when confronted with quadruplets lacking direct connectivity between
      subject and object entities. Furthermore, these methods insufficiently utilize the rich structural and
072
      textual information in temporal graphs. Symbolic methods leverage the frequency information of
073
      relations, while neural methods focus on graph structural information. Both overlook the positive
074   role of the structural dependencies between relations and textual semantics in logical reasoning.
075
076   To address these challenges, we propose a novel Coherent Logical Reasoning method, named CoLR,
077
      which is a two-stage portable framework designed to handle inductive scenarios. In the first stage, we
      introduce a temporal relation structure graph (TRSG), where nodes represent relations, and weighted
078
      edges reflect the cohesion between relations. The main idea comes from the concept of sentence
079
      cohesion in linguistics Halliday & Hasan (1976), where words in a coherent sentence should have
080   semantic associations. Similarly, effective relation paths should also demonstrate close connections
081   between their elements. We design a time-fusion search graph (TFSG) and develop a novel search
082   algorithm to guide path selection along with cohesion weights between relations, thus efficiently
083   extracting reliable paths. If no connected path exists, we sample historical edges based on relation
084   cohesion and temporal dependency as information supplements. In the second stage, we extract
085   textual sequences and timestamp sequences of paths, and introduce a pre-trained language model
086   (PLM) and time sequence encoder for temporal and logical semantic encoding. The textual semantics
087   embedded in textual descriptions helps reveal the logical correlations between relations, such as
088
      "Express intent to meet or negotiate" and "make a visit" in Figure 1. During the reasoning process,
      we combine the embeddings of text and timestamps and evaluate the confidence of quadruplets based
089
      on semantic similarity.
090
091   The proposed CoLR framework demonstrates significant transferability to previous logical reasoning
092   methods, effectively optimizing their learning efficiency and reasoning performance. Notably, we
093   introduce a novel confidence evaluation function for discrete logical reasoning methods, predicated on
094
      the concept of cohesion. This innovative approach circumvents the substantial redundant computations
      inherent in conventional methods while maintaining accuracy.
095
096   To comprehensively evaluate the efficacy of our methodology, we construct three novel datasets that
097   address the deficiencies in existing benchmarks, particularly in assessing accuracy, generalization,
098   and robustness. We conduct extensive experimentation across seven distinct datasets, encompassing
099   three pivotal experimental paradigms: transductive, inductive, and few-shot learning scenarios.
100
      Experimental results show that our proposed method consistently achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA)
      results under the three settings. Comprehensive ablation experiments and case studies verify the
101
      effectiveness of the components we proposed in capturing the logical correlations between relations
102
      and enhancing performance.

107   Representation-based methods for TKGR. Temporal Knowledge Graph Embedding (TKGE)
      is a classic representation-based method for TKGR tasks. These methods project the TKG into

      various complex embedding spaces, such as quaternion and hyperbolic manifold spaces, to learn
109   vector representations of entities, relations, and timestamps. They have achieved significant success
110   in interpolation settings, exemplified by methods like TTransE Leblay & Chekol (2018), TA-DisMult
111   García-Durán et al. (2018), TNTComplEx Lacroix et al. (2020), BoxE Abboud et al. (2020), TeLM
112   Xu et al. (2021), HGE Pan et al. (2024). However, due to a lack of scalability, these methods struggle
113   to adapt to extrapolation reasoning. Recent studies have utilized RGCN Schlichtkrull et al. (2018)
114   to aggregate entity representations across historical snapshots and evolve these representations over
115   time using RNNs to address future time predictions. Despite their advancements, these methods still
116   heavily rely on entity representations, which makes them less suitable for inductive scenario He et al.
117
      (2021); Jin et al. (2020b); Li et al. (2021; 2022b;a); Liang et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023a;b); Li
      et al. (2024). Additionally, the black-box nature of complex neural networks makes their predictive
118
      outcomes difficult to interpret intuitively.
119
120   Rule-based methods for TKGR. Rule-based methods learn first-order logical rules from the graph
121   and apply these rules to infer missing links. These methods also achieve stable predictions in inductive
122
      scenarios, as demonstrated by AMIE+ Galárraga et al. (2015), DRUM Sadeghian et al. (2019) and
      RNNLogic Qu et al. (2021). In TKGs, timestamps mark the sequence of events, making the logical
123
      associations within them easier to uncover. Therefore, symbolic methods like StreamLearnerOmran
124
      et al. (2019), TLogic Liu et al. (2022) and LCGE Niu & Li (2023) attempt to sample historical
125   multi-hop paths and extract reliable temporal logic rules from them. These rules, which do not
126   change with time or entities, are naturally suited for inductive prediction of future events and provide
127   interpretable reasoning. However, these methods require extensive random walks and corresponding
128   rule evaluations, leading to high computational costs. Recent approaches have introduced GRUs
129   to encode paths specific to query quadruplets and assess the credibility of rules based on semantic
130   similarity, thus simplifying rule learning and speeding up the reasoning process. This strategy, which
131   integrates neural methods, allows for more flexible and comprehensive learning of logical rules,
132   achieving superior performance in TKGR tasks. However, these methods Mei et al. (2022); Xiong
133
      et al. (2023); Mei et al. (2024) still struggle with the absence of connected paths from subject to
      object. Moreover, they overlook the positive influence of the logical semantics embedded in textual
134
      information on the learning of logical rules.

137   3    P RELIMINARIES
138   Temporal Knowledge Graph(TKG). A TKG G is a complex structure used to record known
139   facts, where G ⊆ E × R × E × T , E represents the set of entities, R represents the set of relations,
140   and T is the set of timestamps. At a specific point in time, all facts make up a temporal subgraph
141   Gi = {(sij , rij , oij , ti )}, where sij , oij ∈ E represent the subject and object of the jth event in
142   the subgraph, respectively, rij ∈ R represents the relation, and ti ∈ T is the i-th timestamp.
143   The entire TKG can be divided along timestamps into a series of consecutive temporal subgraphs:
144
      G = {Gi }1≤i≤|T | . To enhance the connectivity of the graph, each relation r ∈ R is supplemented
145   with an inverse relation r− , and the corresponding inverse edges are denoted as (o, r− , s, t).
146   Link Forecasting. Link forecasting is one of the core tasks of TKGR. This task involves a historical
147   graph G containing known facts, with the maximum timestamp t̂, and aims to predict future possible
148   links. Specifically, for a query quadruplet in the form (s, rq , ?, tq ), where tq > t̂, an ordered list
149   of candidate entities needs to be generated. During the training process, the goal is to increase the
150
      score of the quadruplets comprising the correct candidate entities and decrease the score of those
      with incorrect candidates. For cases where the subject is being predicted, the query quadruplet is
151
      formalized as (o, rq− , ?, tq ).
152
153   Temporal Logical Rule. A first-order logical rule is composed of a rule head and several binary
154
      rule body atoms connected in sequence, and can be formalized in a TKG as a first-order temporal
      logical rule of length l:
155
156                Υl : Pl+1 (x, y, tl+1 ) ← P1 (x, z1 , t1 ) ∧ P2 (z1 , z2 , t2 ), ..., ∧Pl (zl−1 , y, tl ),   (1)

      where x represents the subject entity, y represents the object entity, and zi can be any entity in the
      graph. Each Pi represents a predicate, typically instantiated as a relation. t1 , t2 , . . . , tl+1 denote the
159
      timestamps for each atom and must satisfy a non-decreasing constraint: t1 ≤ t2 ≤ . . . ≤ tl < tl+1 .
160

      satisfies the temporal constraints, then its HΥ is inferred to be true. In a TKG, if BΥ can be grounded,

183   4.1   R ELATION S TRUCTURE G RAPH
184
185   To capture the structural dependencies between relations, we define the positional patterns of relations
186   as follows:
187
      Definition 1. Given a directed knowledge graph G, a node e, and its connected edge set F, the
188
      positional pattern between any two relations r1 and r2 in F (r1 = r2 is possible) is:
189   (i) Coherent: e serves as the subject for r1 and the object for r2 , or as the object for r1 and the
190   subject for r2 ;
191   (ii) Homologous: e serves as the subject for both r1 and r2 ;
192   (iii) Converging: e serves as the object for both r1 and r2 .
193
194   If two relations are always connected to the same entity and exhibit the same positional pattern, then
195   there is a definite structural dependency between them. We define structural dependencies based
196   on positional patterns as cohesion, homology, and convergence. Some recent research endeavors
197   Lee et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2021) have explored the optimization of graph neural networks by
198   leveraging the homogeneity and homology between relations. Our approach, however, diverges
199
      significantly from these efforts. As a logical reasoning method, our primary focus is on the principle
      of cohesion. We estimate cohesion weights by calculating the frequency of two relations occurring in
200
      the coherent positional pattern.
201
202   For a static knowledge graph G = (E, R, F), we define two entity-relation matrices Es ∈ R|E|×2|R|
203   and Eo ∈ R|E|×2|R| , where the subscripts "s" and "o" represent subject and object, respectively.
204   Es [i, j] denotes the element at the i-th row and j-th column, recording the number of times the entity
205   ei in the graph is connected as a subject to the relation rj (including the inverse relation). Similarly,
206   Eo [i, j] records the number of times ei is connected as an object to the relation rj . Considering
207
      the variance in interaction frequencies of entities within the graph, we introduce a degree diagonal
      matrix for entities to compute the frequency of each entity’s interaction with relations, given by
208
      Ẽs = D−1                    −1

303

304   not just the relation path, to generate text representations. We utilize ";" to delimit the text of each

343   6.1       E XPERIMENT S ETUP
344   Datset & Evaluation. To validate the performance of our model in a transductive setting, we initially
345   selected four public datasets for comparative experiments on link forecasting task. These datasets
346   include ICEWS14 García-Durán et al. (2018), ICEWS18 García-Durán et al. (2018), ICEWS05-15
347   Jin et al. (2020a) and YAGO Mahdisoltani et al. (2015). However, traditional benchmark datasets
348   lack a comprehensive evaluation of accuracy, generalization, and robustness, which according to
349   transductive, inductive, and few-shot scenarios. Therefore, we design three datasets based on the
350   ACLED (The Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project)2 and ICEWS: ACLED2023, ACLED-
351   IND, and ICEWS14-FS. These datasets aim to address specific issues in existing transductive,
352
      inductive, and few-shot datasets:
353
                 • ACLED2023: To address the potential issue of information leakage due to the outdated
354
                   nature of existing datasets, where PLMs may learn from historical event texts, we compiled
355                all events from 2023 available in the ACLED database to create ACLED2023. This mitigates
356                the risk of information leakage since the release date of PLMs like ALBERT was in 2020,
357                well before the events in ACLED2023 occurred.
358
                 • ACLED-IND: Traditional approaches for partitioning inductive datasets typically segment
359                the entity set such that (Etrain ∩ Evalid ∩ Etest = ∅), while ensuring that (Rvalid ∈ Rtrain )
360                and (Rtest ∈ Rtrain ). However, this can disrupt the temporal and topological features
361                of TKGs. For instance, in the ICEWS14 dataset, China and United States are among
362                the most frequently occurring entities. If they are categorized in the same set, it could
363                result in many related facts being excluded from other sets, severely affecting the graph’s
364                connectivity and the capture of temporal features. Therefore, we retrieved events from
365                ACLED occurring in Asia between 2019-2022 for the training set, and events from 2023 in
366                Europe and the Americas for the validation and test sets, respectively. This design ensures
367
                   that (Etrain ∩ Evalid = ∅) and (Etrain ∩ Etest = ∅), maintaining the structural integrity of
                   the training [(Gtrain )], validation [(Gvalid )], and test [(Gtest )] graphs.
368
369              • ICEWS14-FS: To address the lack of exploration in few-shot scenarios in existing bench-
370                mark datasets, we created the ICEWS14-FS dataset based on the ICEWS14 dataset by
371
                   extracting 10% of events at each timestamp. This process is designed to test the performance
                   of models in information-sparse environments.

519
      in the dataset and serve as conjunctions in temporal logical paths. Notably, despite the different
520   numbers of relations in the three ICEWS datasets, their cohesion distributions are strikingly similar.

522   relations in the datasets and enhance the robustness of logical reasoning in inductive scenarios.

      7     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE WORK
526
      In this work, we propose a two-stage coherent logical reasoning framework, named CoLR, which
527
      integrates structural dependencies and text semantics of temporal paths to tackle the link forecasting
528
      task over TKGs. It searches for temporal logical paths in the first stage and encodes these paths using
529   a PLM and a novel time sequence encoder in the second stage. To efficiently search for reliable paths,
530   we construct TRSG based on cohesion between relations and design a temporal fusion search graph.
531   To address path-missing issues, we implement a path supplement strategy that samples historical
532   paths based on cohesion. Additionally, we introduced three new datasets to address the deficiencies
533   of existing benchmarks in transductive, inductive, and few-shot scenarios. Experimental results
534   demonstrate that CoLR comprehensively outperforms existing methods across six datasets. Since
535   temporal paths consist of only 1 to 3 hop edges, the corresponding text sequences pose challenges for
536   PLMs in capturing semantic dependencies. In future work, we plan to integrate LLMs to generate
537
      more detailed and precise texts to enhance the ability of PLMs to deeply understand the latent
      semantics of text sequences.

      A.3    C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY OF TRSG
784
785   As shown in Equation 2, the TRSG on each subgraph can be obtained by multiplying two entity-
786   relation matrices, which is efficiently implemented in PyTorch. Therefore, as the graph size expands,
787   the computational cost mainly arises from the increased number of matrix multiplications due to
788   the extension of the timestamp sequence. For a TKG with N timestamps, when the time window
789   ω is 1, the times of matrix multiplications is N ; when the time window size is N , the computation
790   times is 1/2(N ∗ (N + 1)). Thus, the time complexity for constructing a TRSG lies between O(N )
791   and O(N 2 ). Since the time window is typically much smaller than N , the time complexity of
792
      constructing TRSG approaches O(N ). Clearly, as the number of timestamps in the TKG increases,
      the computational cost does not grow dramatically. In fact, for the ICEWS05-15 dataset, which
793
      contains over 4000 timestamps, constructing a TRSG with a time window of 10 takes only 20 seconds.
794
      In summary, our proposed TRSG can flexibly scale to very large real-world graphs.

1067   to investigate the efficiency of our path search approach. As shown in Table 8, the time cost of CoLR
1068   for path search is significantly lower than that of TLogic, validating the effectiveness of the proposed
1069   time-fusion path search algorithm. By introducing TRSG and TFSG, our time-fusion path search
1070   algorithm can efficiently identify historical paths relevant to the query. Specifically, TRSG ensures
1071   that CoLR only needs to retrieve the top-K historical paths, avoiding the additional computational
1072   cost of retrieving unrelated paths. TFSG preserves temporal information while compressing the TKG
1073   into a static graph, eliminating the need for re-expanding the static graph back into a TKG.
