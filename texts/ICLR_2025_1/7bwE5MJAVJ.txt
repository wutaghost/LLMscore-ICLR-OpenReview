011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013               Hallucinations in large language models (LLMs) pose significant challenges in
014               tasks requiring complex multi-step reasoning, such as mathematical problem-
015               solving. Existing approaches primarily detect the presence of hallucinations but
016
                  lack a nuanced understanding of their types and manifestations. In this paper, we
                  first introduce a comprehensive taxonomy that categorizes the common halluci-
017
                  nations in mathematical reasoning task into six types: fabrication, factual incon-
018
                  sistency, context inconsistency, instruction inconsistency, logical inconsistency,
019               and logical error. We then propose FG-PRM (Fine-Grained Process Reward
020               Model), an augmented model designed to detect and mitigate hallucinations in
021               a fine-grained, step-level manner. To address the limitations of manually labeling
022               training data, we propose an automated method for generating fine-grained hal-
023               lucination data using LLMs. By injecting hallucinations into reasoning steps of
024               correct solutions, we create a diverse and balanced synthetic dataset for training
025               FG-PRM, which consists of six specialized Process Reward Models (PRMs), each
026               tailored to detect a specific hallucination type. Our FG-PRM demonstrates supe-
027
                  rior performance across two key tasks: 1) Fine-grained hallucination detection:
                  classifying hallucination types for each reasoning step; and 2) Verification: rank-
028
                  ing multiple LLM-generated outputs to select the most accurate solution, mitigat-
029
                  ing reasoning hallucinations. Our experiments show that FG-PRM outperforms
030               ChatGPT-3.5 and Claude-3 on fine-grained hallucination detection and substan-
031               tially boosts the performance of LLMs on GSM8K and MATH benchmarks.1

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   While considerable progress has been made in enhancing the general capabilities of large language
037   models (LLMs), solving complex reasoning tasks such as answering mathematical questions re-
038   mains a challenge. Recently, advanced techniques like Chain-of-Thoughts (Wei et al., 2022), Tree-
039   of-Thoughts (Yao et al., 2024) and Reasoning-via-Planning (Hao et al., 2023) are proposed. These
040   methods guide LLMs in breaking down complex reasoning tasks into simple steps, thus improv-
041   ing their performance and enhancing the interpretability of the reasoning process. However, while
042   generating multi-step reasoning chains can improve performance, LLMs often produce incorrect
043   or unverifiable statements—commonly known as hallucinations—that hinder their ability to solve
044
      complex problems that require multiple reasoning steps.
045   Prior methods of mitigating hallucinations in reasoning chains largely focus on detecting their pres-
046   ence, with limited exploration into the distinct types of hallucinations produced. Our research goes
047   beyond this by developing a fine-grained taxonomy that categorizes hallucinations based on their
048   nature and manifestation (see Figure 1 for an illustration comparing coarse-grained detection with
049   our method). We analyze reasoning steps to pinpoint the emergence of hallucinations and uncover
050
      patterns in their behavior.
051   Recent efforts have shown that training reward models (RMs) is an effective approach for detect-
052   ing and mitigating hallucinations, with the two primary categories being Outcome Reward Model

104   2   F INE - GRAINED H ALLUCINATION TAXONOMY
105
106   Large language models excel at solving tasks that require complex multi-step reasoning by gener-
107   ating solutions in a step-by-step and chain-of-thought format. Nevertheless, even state-of-the-art
      models are prone to inaccuracies, often producing content that is unfaithful, fabricated, inconsistent,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      or nonsensical. Categorizing and localizing these inaccuracies in reasoning steps is challenging but
109   provides explicit insights into which parts of the model output have specific types of problems. To
110   address the need for a more nuanced understanding of these hallucinations, we propose a refined
111   taxonomy for reasoning tasks.
112
      Building upon the prior work Ji et al. (2023), we develop a fine-grained taxonomy for two major
113
      categories of hallucinations: intrinsic and extrinsic hallucination, according to whether the hallu-
114
      cination can be verified by the input information or the context LLMs have previously generated.
115   To describe more complex errors surfacing in LM reasoning, we further divide the intrinsic hal-
116   lucination into contextual inconsistency, logical inconsistency and instruction inconsistency, while
117   extrinsic hallucinations are divided into logical error, factual inconsistency, and fabrication. To il-
118   lustrate our definition of LLM hallucinations more intuitively, we provide examples for each type
119   of hallucination in Appendix Table 4, along with corresponding explanations. The definition of our
120   proposed categories are elaborated below:
121
122         (1)    Context Inconsistency refer to instances where the model’s is unfaithful with the user’s
123               provided contextual information.
124         (2)    Logical Inconsistency refer to internal logical contradictions in the model’s output, mani-
125               fested as inconsistency both among the reasoning steps themselves and between the steps
126               and the final answer.
127         (3)   Instruction Inconsistency refer to instances where the model’s output does not align with
128               the user’s explicit request.
129
130
            (4)    Logical Error refer to instances where the model makes incorrect calculation or conclu-
                  sions that do not follow from the provided premises. For instance, it might draw incorrect
131
                  inferences or make errors in basic logical operations.
132
133         (5)    Factual Inconsistency refer to situations where the model’s output contains facts that can
134               be grounded in real-world information, but present contradictions.
135         (6) Fabrication refer to instances where the model’s output contains facts that are unverifiable
136             against established real-world knowledge or context information.
137
138   Compared with the simplified taxonomy in previous work (Golovneva et al., 2022; Prasad et al.,
139   2023), our refined taxonomy aims to comprehensively capture the unique complexities of LLM hal-
140   lucinations, providing a detailed description of the more intricate errors that occur during language
141   model mathematical reasoning.

145   In this section, we formulate the two primary tasks of fine-grained hallucination detection and miti-
146   gation, highlighting the importance of step-level and fine-grained supervision.

      3.1    TASK 1: F INE -G RAINED H ALLUCINATION D ETECTION
149
150   This task aims to detect hallucinations in language model reasoning output at a granular level, focus-
151   ing on individual reasoning steps. Specifically, the detector is tasked with identifying fine-grained
152   hallucinations in the output of a language model by assigning reward scores for each intermediate
153   step in a reasoning chain. The objective is to classify hallucination types at the step level, determin-
154   ing whether a specific hallucination type is present.
155
      Given a question x and its solution y consisting of L reasoning steps, we assume the ground-
156   truth annotations for hallucination types are available. These annotations, denoted as yi∗t ∈
157   {TRUE, FALSE}, provide a binary label for each hallucination type t at the i-th step, indicating
158   whether the hallucination t is present (TRUE) or absent (FALSE). The detector models predict yit ,
159   where yit is the model’s predicted label for the i-th step and hallucination type t. We evaluate the
160   model’s performance using standard metrics for classification as in previous work (Feng et al., 2023;
161   Mishra et al., 2024): precision and recall. For each hallucination type t, the precision measures the
      proportion of correct predictions out of all predictions where the model indicated the presence of

186   3.2   TASK 2: F INE -G RAINED H ALLUCINATION M ITIGATION
187
188   The verification task (Lightman et al., 2023) assesses a model’s ability to evaluate and rank multiple
189   candidate solutions for a given problem. In this task, a generator produces N possible solutions
190   {y 1 , y 2 , . . . , y N } for a problem x, which are then evaluated by a reward model (Section 4.1). The
191   reward model assigns a score to each candidate solution based on its correctness, with the goal of
192
      selecting the best solution among the candidates.
193   This task follows the best-of-N selection method, where the solution with the highest score is cho-
194   sen as the final answer. A well-performing reward model improves the likelihood of selecting the
195   correct solution, thereby enhancing the overall problem-solving accuracy. By providing meaningful
196   feedback on each candidate solution, the verification task helps ensure that the reasoning process is
197   grounded in correctness and consistency.

254   4.2     FG-PRM: F INE - GRAINED P ROCESS R EWARD M ODEL
255
256   In this Section, we introduce our FG-PRM, the Fine-Grained Process Reward Model for hallucina-
257   tion detection and mitigation. To reduce the annotation cost issues associated with PRM, we first
258   introduce an automated process annotation framework for step-level fine-grained dataset synthesis.
259   After that, we provide the training details for our FG-PRM on the synthetic dataset.
260
261   4.2.1     AUTOMATED H ALLUCINATION G ENERATION
262
263   To detect fine-grained hallucinations in language model reasoning tasks, we propose a framework
264
      based on fine-grained, step-level process supervision. Existing step-level datasets with fine-grained
      annotations (Golovneva et al., 2022) are limited in size, and collecting the necessary data for training
265
      models with such detailed labels is costly, as it requires human annotators to provide fine-grained
266
      feedback for each reasoning step. To overcome the scarcity of human-labeled data, we introduce an
267   automated reasoning process hallucination annotation framework, as illustrated in Figure 2. We treat
268   the golden chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning steps as positive examples, while negative examples
269   are generated by injecting hallucinations into these steps using Llama3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024).
      To synthesize the negative examples, we adopt a two-step process as follows.

                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Step 1: Identify where to inject hallucination In our taxonomy, each hallucination type has
271   distinct characteristics, requiring specific conditions and methods for generation. However, not all
272   reasoning steps are suitable for generating every type of hallucination. For instance, when a reason-
273   ing step is exclusively focused on numerical calculations, it becomes challenging to introduce factual
274   inconsistency. To effectively introduce a hallucination type into the reasoning process, we need to
275   identify steps that meet the necessary conditions for hallucination generation. To achieve this, we
276   have developed a set of tailored rules for the Llama3-70B model. These rules guide the model in
277   determining whether a given reasoning step provides the elements required for a specific type of
278   hallucination. For example, when evaluating whether a step can introduce factual inconsistency, the
279
      model checks if the reasoning step references objects (e.g., quantities, features) or named entities.
      This enables us to manipulate the information, allowing for the seamless integration of contextual
280
      inconsistencies in later steps. The complete set of rules for identifying hallucination injection points
281
      across the six hallucination types is detailed in Appendix D.
282
283
      Step 2: Hallucinate the ground truth reasoning steps To control the distribution of hallucina-
284   tions in the generated dataset and improve the success rate of incorporating our hallucination taxon-
285   omy, we prompt the Llama3-70B model to insert hallucinations one by one from our taxonomy. We
286   begin by inputting specific instructions for each hallucination type into the system prompt, guiding
287   the model on how to modify the reasoning process and introduce the desired hallucination. Detailed
288   instructions for each hallucination type are provided in Appendix E. Next, we employ an in-context
289   learning strategy by providing two demonstrations for each query type. Each demonstration includes
290   an example of an injected hallucination, along with an explanation of how it is introduced. These
291   demonstrations can be found in Appendix F. After confirming the appropriate location for injecting
292
      the hallucination, we present the problem and the correct reasoning history to the model, instructing
      it to generate the next reasoning step with the target hallucination. For cost efficiency, we delegate
293
      the task of hallucinating reasoning steps to the Llama3-70B model. We experimentally found that
294
      our method enables the language model to consistently generate hallucinatory reasoning steps with
295   a high success rate.

473   Hallucinations in LLM Numerous studies have explored the taxonomy of hallucinations in lan-
474   guage models, offering valuable insights. Mishra et al. (2024) identify six fine-grained types of
475   factual hallucinations in information search scenarios. Zhang et al. (2023) classify hallucinations
476   based on conflict types: input-conflicting, context-conflicting, and fact-conflicting. Huang et al.
477   (2023a) categorize hallucinations into factuality and faithfulness types, subdividing them further. Ji
478   et al. (2023) discussed intrinsic and extrinsic hallucinations, focusing on whether outputs contradict
479   input content. Our taxonomy, in contrast, concentrates on hallucinations in reasoning tasks, partic-
480   ularly through chain-of-thought reasoning. Closest to our work, Golovneva et al. (2022) propose
481
      ROSCOE, measuring semantic alignment, similarity, and language coherence in reasoning chains.
      Unlike ROSCOE that includes common issues like grammar, redundancy, and repetition, which are
482
      easier to spot in general, our taxonomy provides detailed distinctions between error types, providing
483
      a compact framework for diagnosing complex errors and improving model outputs.

      Improving reasoning abilities of LLMs For LLMs that have completed training, prompting tech-
516   niques is an effective approach to improve the performance of LLMs on reasoning tasks without
517   modifying the model parameters. Many studies have developed different prompting strategies in
518   reasoning tasks, such as the Chain-of-Thought Wei et al. (2022); Fu et al. (2022), Tree-of-Thoughts
519   Yao et al. (2024). Besides, instead of directly improving the reasoning performance of LLMs, veri-
520   fiers can raise the success rate in solving reasoning tasks by selecting the best answer from multiple
521   decoded candidates. Two types of verifiers are commonly used: Outcome Reward Model (ORM)
522   and Process Reward Model (PRM). PRM provides a more detailed evaluation by scoring each indi-
523   vidual step. However, training a PRM requires access to expensive human-annotated datasets, which
524
      can be a barrier to the advancement and practical application of PRM. To overcome this challenge,
      methods such as Math-Shepherd Wang et al. (2023) and MiPS Wang et al. (2024) have explored
525
      Monte Carlo estimation to automate the data collection process without human involvement, and
526
      OmegaPRM Luo et al. (2024) proposed a divide-and-conquer style Monte Carlo tree search al-
527   gorithm for automated process supervision data generation. Different from the above methods, we
528   directly generate negative examples with hallucinations instead of annotating the reasoning process.

533
534   In conclusion, we propose FG-PRM framework,introduces a nuanced approach for comprehensive
535   understanding and mitigations of hallucinations in language model reasoning, which are catego-
536   rized into six distinct types under our new paradigm. By leveraging a novel automatic data genera-
537   tion method, we significantly reduce the dependency on costly human annotations while enriching
538   the dataset with diverse hallucinatory instances. Our empirical results demonstrate that FG-PRM,
539   when trained on this synthetic data, significantly enhances the accuracy of hallucination detection,
      providing an effective mechanism for improving the LLM reasoning accuracy and faithfulness.
