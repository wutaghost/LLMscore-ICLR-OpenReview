009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating free energy differences between molecular systems is fundamental for
012            understanding molecular interactions and accelerating drug discovery. Current
013            techniques use molecular dynamics to sample the Boltzmann distributions of the
014            two systems and of several intermediate “alchemical” distributions that interpo-
015            late between them. From the resulting ensembles, free energy differences can be
016
               estimated by averaging importance weight analogs for multiple distributions. In-
               stead of time-intensive simulations of intermediate alchemical systems, we learn
017
               a fast-to-train flow to bridge the two systems of interest. After training, we obtain
018
               free energy differences by integrating the flow’s instantaneous change of variables
019            when transporting samples between the two distributions. To map between molec-
020            ular systems with different numbers of atoms, we replace the previous solutions
021            of simulating auxiliary “dummy atoms” by additionally training two autoencoders
022            that project the systems to a same-dimensional latent space in which our flow oper-
023            ates. A generalized change of variables formula for trans-dimensional mappings
024            allows us to employ the dimensionality collapsing and expanding autoencoders
025            in our free energy estimation pipeline. We validate our approach on systems of
026            increasing complexity: mapping between Gaussians, between subspaces of ala-
027
               nine dipeptide, and between pharmaceutically relevant ligands in solvent. All
               results show strong agreement with reference values. We provide an example
028
               anonymized Jupyter notebook for our method applied to Gaussian distributions
029
               here.

092   The common traditional approaches for such estimations are based on Free Energy Perturbation
093
      (FEP). The FEP identity (Zwanzig, 1954) reduces estimating free energy differences to importance
      sampling between distributions A and B: the negative log of the average importance weights is the
094
      free energy difference. For molecules, the distributions are sampled by running molecular dynam-
095
      ics (MD) simulations. The convergence of this estimate depends on the variance of the importance
096   weights, which is large if there is insufficient overlap between distributions A and B. Thus, in prac-
097   tice, one turns to alchemical FEP (Bash et al., 1987; Mey et al., 2020) where a series (typically a
098   few tens) of “alchemical” molecular systems between systems A and B are simulated (see Figure 2).
099   Modeling these additional intermediate distributions yields a lower variance free energy difference
100   estimate at the cost of additional molecular dynamics simulation time.
101
      Instead of bridging distributions A and B via additional MD simulations, FreeFlow learns a neural
102   map between them and estimates their free energy differences by observing the change of density
103   when transporting samples from system A to system B or back. The aim is to overfit and run infer-
104   ence with such a map faster than carrying out a series of MD simulations for FEP (while maintaining
105   or improving upon the accuracy of FEP). For this purpose, previous work (Wirnsberger et al., 2020)
106   employed normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2016) with the same input and output dimen-
107   sionality that do not accommodate different dimensionality (different numbers of atoms) between
      systems A and B.

      Concretely, we propose FreeFlow to map between distributions of arbitrary dimensions by encoding
109   the systems into a lower dimensional latent space and learning a flow model in that latent space
110   using flow matching. The autoencoders to produce the latent spaces for systems A and B are fast-to-
111   train and overfitting a flow between their small dimensional latent spaces is equally efficient. After
112   training this map, computing the free energy difference requires evaluating the change of density a
113   sample incurs when transporting it between the distributions. While this is trivial for normalizing
114   flows, FreeFlow involves changes in dimensionality, which we accommodate with a generalized
115   change of variables formula for “trans-dimensional” mappings.
116   Empirically, we evaluate FreeFlow on a series of free energy difference estimations of increasing
117   complexity. First, we confirm that FreeFlow is able to recover analytically computed free energy
118   differences between Gaussians of different dimensionalities. Next, we turn to a well-explored molec-
119   ular system, alanine dipeptide, and estimate free energy differences between partitions of its state
120   space. Lastly, we tackle the real-world task of computing free energy differences between different
121   pharmaceutically relevant ligands in solvent. In this experiment, we observe Spearman and Pear-
122   son correlations of up to 0.93 between our free energy difference and the Free Energy Perturbation
123
      reference values.
124   We summarize our key contributions as:
125
126         1. A simulation-free continuous normalizing flow training procedure based on flow match-
127            ing without constraints such as easy-to-compute Jacobian determinant or fast invertibility
128
               unlike older flows.
129         2. A map that translates between systems of arbitrary dimensionality via a same-dimensional
130            latent space, avoiding the introduction of dummy atoms and requiring an order of mag-
131            nitude fewer MD simulations compared to intermediate-window-based methods such as
132
               Alchemical FEP.
133         3. Using a generalized change of variables formulation for computing density changes in
134            trans-dimensional maps.
135         4. Validation on real-world pharmaceutically relevant ligands of varying numbers of atoms.

      Flow Matching. Flow Matching (FM) (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022)
140
      is a training framework for CNFs that avoids the need for simulation during training. Instead of
141   integrating the ODE, FM directly trains the vector field vθ (t, x) to match a target probability flow
142   defined by a prescribed time-dependent probability path pt (x). The objective minimizes the dis-
143   crepancy between the model’s vector field and the target vector field that transports pt (x) along the
144   flow:
                                                                            2
145                                   LFM (θ) = Et,x∼pt |ut (x) − vθ (t, x)| ,                           (1)
146   where ut (x) is the target vector field derived from the continuity equation. To construct more ex-
147   pressive probability paths pt (x), Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong
148   et al., 2023) introduces a conditioning variable z and expresses pt (x) as a combination of simpler
149   distributions pt (x|z) such as Gaussians conditioned on z.
150   Free Energy Calculations in Molecular Systems. Free energy calculations are essential for un-
151   derstanding molecular interactions and predicting binding affinities in drug discovery (Chipot et al.,
152   2007). The accuracy of these methods depends on the overlap between the configurations sampled
153   from the different states. Insufficient overlap can lead to high variance and unreliable estimates.
154   Techniques like stratification and the use of intermediate states help mitigate this issue but increase
155   computational complexity.
156   The Free Energy Perturbation (FEP) method, introduced by Zwanzig (1954), provides an exact
157   relationship for computing ∆F between two thermodynamic states A and B:

      4.3     P HARMACEUTICALLY R ELEVANT L IGANDS IN S OLVENT
466
467   Finally, we evaluate FreeFlow on a real-world use case commonly addressed by FEP: comparing the
468   binding free energies of ligands to a protein, a key task in drug discovery. This process involves two
469   main legs: the solvent leg, where the free energy difference between the two ligands is estimated in
470   solution, and the complex leg, where they are bound to the protein. For this evaluation, we focus on
471   the solvent leg, using a set of pharmaceutically relevant ligands of varying sizes. More specifically
472   for this task, the solvent leg involves system A, where one ligand is in water, and system B, where a
473   different ligand is in water. The task is to learn a mapping between the Boltzmann distributions of
474   these two systems in order to estimate the free energy difference between them.
475   Data Collection and Reference Values. We separately simulate each ligand in water at temperature
476   300 K for 400 ns with a step size of 2 fs using the OpenMM library (Eastman et al., 2017). As force
477   field, we use the implicit GBn2 solvation model (Nguyen et al.) with the gaff-2.11 force field
478   (Wang et al., 2004). We save a sample every 200 steps for a total of 1,000,000 samples from each
479   ligand. The reference values we use are free energy differences from the pmx library (Gapsys et al.,
480   2020) which were calculated using alchemical FEP (see Figure 2) and an explicit solvent potential.
481
      We use the OpenMM bridge within the bgmol1 library to evaluate the energy functions. Before we
      train FreeFlow on a pair of ligands, we align each sample of the two ligands to a single reference
482
      by rotating and translating to minimize the root-mean-square distance between the sample and the
483
      reference. This minimizes the distance between the samples while leaving their potential energies

      5   C ONCLUSION
500
      In this paper, we proposed FreeFlow, a novel method for estimating free energy differences between
501
      two systems by first encoding both systems into lower dimensional latent space, and training a flow
502
      model via Flow Matching to bridge the two latent distributions. This leads to fast training through
503   the simulation-free regression objective of Flow Matching, and has the main benefit that free energy
504   differences between systems of different dimensions can be estimated without resorting to nonphys-
505   ical modifications such as dummy atoms. The trans-dimensional latent map not being invertible
506   makes the typical formulations of change of variables inapplicable, and we build on previous work
507   to solve this challenge by separating the change of variables among the three components of the
508   map.
509   We evaluated FreeFlow first between simple Gaussian distributions to empirically validate our ap-
510   proach to learning a trans-dimensional map and our change of variables formulation. We then esti-
511   mated the free energy difference between two states of the small molecule alanine dipeptide, which
512   confirmed FreeFlow’s applicability to physical systems. We finally estimated the free energy differ-
513   ences between pairs of pharmaceutically relevant ligands of various dimensionality in water, which
514   represents one leg of the thermodynamic cycle commonly used to compare different molecules’
515   binding affinities to a protein, a critical task in drug discovery.
516   We anticipate that as future work FreeFlow can be extended to the other leg of the thermodynamic
517   cycle, learning a mapping between two bound protein-ligand complexes. This considerably in-
518   creases the dimensionality of the problem but can be tackled by FreeFlow since the lower dimen-
519   sional latent flow would still be fast to train.
