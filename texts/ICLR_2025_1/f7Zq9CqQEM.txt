011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-to-3D generation techniques signify a pivotal advancement in creating 3D
014            models from textual descriptions. Contemporary state-of-the-art methods utilize
015            score distillation processes, leveraging 2D priors to generate 3D assets. However,
016
               these approaches frequently encounter instability during the initial generation
               phases, primarily due to an distribution discrepancy between the score prediction
017
               network and rendered images. Specifically, the raw rendered images of the initial
018
               3D model lie out of the distribution (OOD) of the pretrained score prediction
019            network, which is trained on high-fidelity realistic images. To address this OOD
020            issue, we introduce an innovative Path-Tracing Distillation (PTD) technique that
021            refines the distillation process. Our method sequentially optimizes the 3D model
022            using intermediate score networks that exhibit closer distributional alignment,
023            thereby accelerating the convergence during the early stages of training. This
024            approach not only ensures a more stable increase in CLIP similarity initially but also
025            preserves the visual quality and diversity of the generated models. Comprehensive
026            experiments demonstrate that PTD significantly enhances both the stability and
027
               quality of text-to-3D generation, outperforming existing baselines in CLIP scores.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The emergence of 3D asset generation (Qian et al., 2024) has precipitated transformative shifts within
033
      the graphics industry, promising a future where 3D models are increasingly synthesized rather than
      traditionally rendered. A 3D asset, meticulously detailed with intricate textural properties, serves
034
      as a foundational element for a myriad of applications spanning animation, virtual reality, gaming,
035
      and more. Among the diverse array of techniques, text-to-3D generation (Wang et al., 2023) stands
036   out as a promising approach, offering an efficient and user-friendly mechanism to create 3D models
037   from textual descriptions. This methodology not only streamlines the creative workflow but also
038   democratizes 3D content creation, making it accessible to a broader creators.
039
      Currently, there are two predominant strategies for generating 3D assets from textual descriptions:
040
      directly training a generative model on 3D data and constructing 3D models based on 2D priors.
041
      The latter approach leverages large-scale diffusion models such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
042   2022b) and Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), which generate multi-view supervised signals to guide
043   the optimization of differentiably rendered images from an evolving 3D model. As the optimization
044   process iteratively refines the 3D model from various perspectives, it progressively converges toward
045   a realistic and coherent representation. This iterative refinement is crucial for ensuring that the
046   generated 3D models accurately reflect the semantic content of the input text while maintaining high
047   visual fidelity across different viewpoints.
048   At the forefront of these advancements is DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2022), which introduced Score
049   Distillation Sampling (SDS) to predict scores in noisy images rendered from 3D models. Despite its
050   innovative approach, DreamFusion’s technique sometimes results in images exhibiting over-saturation
051   or excessive smoothing, detracting from the model’s realism and detail. To address these limitations,
052   subsequent methods such as ProlificDreamer (Wang et al., 2023) introduced Variational Score
053   Distillation (VSD), modeling the distribution of multiple generated 3D representations to enable more
      diverse and accurate 3D model creation. Additionally, LucidDreamer (Liang et al., 2024) proposed

090   Interval Score Matching (ISM), employing deterministic diffusing trajectories and interval-based
091   score matching to mitigate the over-smoothing effect observed with SDS. More recently, Classifier
092   Score Distillation (CSD) (Yu et al., 2023b) was developed as an implicit classification model for
093   generation, achieving commendable results by refining the score prediction process. However, despite
094   these advancements, existing methods still grapple with instability during the early stages of 3D
095   model generation and require extended periods to achieve stable and high-quality outcomes.
096   We argue that the score prediction network struggles to accurately predict the noise added to initially
097   rendered images. Such argument is supported by our empirical evidence. Our findings reveal a
098   substantial disparity between the scores predicted by Stable Diffusion and an approximated score (Xu
099   et al., 2023b), given images rendered from the initial raw 3D models. This discrepancy becomes
100   increasingly pronounced when the added noise scale is small. In contrast, for images generated
101   directly by Stable Diffusion, the disparity between predicted and approximated scores is significantly
102   reduced. These observations conclude that the score prediction for images from the initial raw 3D
103   models is inaccurate. An intuitive explanation for this issue is that the rendered images from the
104
      low-quality initial 3D model tend to fall outside the support of Stable Diffusion which is trained
      exclusively on high-quality 2D images.
105
106   To address the identified issue, we propose Path-Tracing Distillation (PTD) method, which employs a
107   series of score prediction networks instead of the solitary pretrained score network to perform score
      distillation. Our core insight is, while the pretrained score network suffers from discrepancy, there

108
      are multiple intermediate distributions between the pretrained score network and the distribution of
109   rendered images. The 3D models may be guided by the intermediate score networks from close to far
110   and finally reach the pretrained score network. This is like when traveling to a remote city without
111   available direct flights, one may transfer several times through intermediate cities along the journey.
112   A planning of travel journey may start from the source to the destination, or from the destination back
113   to the source. We propose to obtain the intermediate score networks in a backward order.
114
      Based on the above insight, we design a two-stage text-to-3D generation pipeline. In the first stage,
115   given a raw 3D model initialized by SF3D (Boss et al., 2024), we finetune the pretrained score
116   model to fit the initially rendered images, thereby gradually degrading the pretrained score network
117   from high-quality real images to low-quality rendered images. Checkpoints are saved during this
118   finetuning, and they are also score networks which altogether represent a transformation path of
119   intermediate distributions connecting the high-quality pretrained score to the low-quality untrained
120   rendered image distribution. In the second stage, the 3D model is iteratively optimized using these
121   checkpoints of score networks as distillation targes in a reverse order one by one. The switch from
122   a current checkpoint to a next one occurs when rendered multi-view images nearly converges to
123
      the current checkpoint. The end of the reverse path is the original pretrained score network, so the
      proposed distillation approach share the same convergence target with other score distillation strategy.
124
      This distillation approach encourages a more stable optimization process, effectively mitigating the
125
      OOD issue and resulting in high-quality 3D models.
126
127   Compared to existing text-to-3D methods, our Path-Tracing Distillation approach predict more
128   accurately score and accerlarate the convergence speed in 3D generation while maintaining both
129
      diversity and quality. The proposed approach can be integrated with other existing score distillation
      3D generation methods. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
130
131         1. We identify the out-of-distribution (OOD) issue during the 3D generation process with
132            empirical evidence.
133         2. We propose Path-Tracing Distillation (PTD) to mitigate the instability of score prediction in
134            the early stages of generation.
135
            3. Experiments demonstrate that our PTD approach exceeds SOTA methods in CLIP similarity,
136            indicating higher quality of 3D assets.

      The out-of-distribution issue often refers to the machine learning model receives data which deviates
180   significantly from the model’s training set so the model fails to give reliable predictions. Specifically
181   in our scenario, the OOD issue refers to the score prediction network spretrain may receive rendered
182   images different from images in the training set so it gives unstable score. Intuitively, spretrain is a
183   large-scale network trained on high-quality realistic images to capture 2D visual prior. Whereas, the
184   initial 3D models, given different representation or initialization, are of low quality. Thus, spretrain
185   may not predict scores of the noisy rendered images well enough.
186   To investigate the OOD issue, we compare the predicted score spretrain (xt , t, y) with the approximated
187   score sapprox (xt , t, y). The approximated score is computed with Stable Target Field (Xu et al., 2023b)
188   (Appendix G), which includes an additional reference batch of training samples used to calculate
189   weighted conditional scores as the approximation. The comparison is reflected by a proposed
190   matching loss between the predicted and approximated score, formally defined as
191
                          Lmatching (xt ) = Ex0 ,t,ϵ ∥spretrain (xt , t, y) − sapprox (xt , t, y)∥2 ,       (5)
192
193   Here t indicates the timestep used for adding noise. If the noisy image given to the score prediction
194   network lies in the support of the training distribution, the predicted score is expectedly close to the
195   approximated score, and vice versa. The matching loss on the noisy rendered images xθt from initial

      3D models is 3.234 ± 0.042. This is significantly higher than the loss value 1.687 ± 0.075 on noisy

200
      is unstable. Thus, the early generation of 3D models are impeded. To solve this problem, we design
201   the path-tracing strategy as detailed in the next section.

208   given a raw initialized 3D model, we finetune the pretrained score model to fit the initially rendered
209   images and save checkpoints during this finetuning to form the transformation path. In the second
210   stage of tracing path, the 3D model is iteratively optimized using these checkpoints of score networks
211   as distillation targes in a reverse order one by one. The final target is the pretrained score network.

      5.1    D IRECT 3D SHAPE GENERATION
304
      Training neural networks using 3D models labeled with text description is a highly intuitive approach
305   to text-to-3D model, which meets people’s demand for more controllable 3D generation. For such
306   pre-trained models, a 3D shape (Gao et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2023; Nichol et al., 2022; Jun &
307   Nichol, 2023; He et al., 2024) or 4D motion (Dabral et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
308   2023) can be inferred within minutes or seconds by inputting text prompt. Relevant research includes
309   Point-E (Nichol et al., 2022), Shap-E (Jun & Nichol, 2023), Mofusion (Dabral et al., 2023), etc.
310   Despite the high efficiency of these methods, the quality of outputs is often suboptimal, which is
311   primarily due to the fact that the scale of 3D datasets cannot compare with that of 2D datasets. There
312   are also works to improve quality of direct 3D shape generation by introducing novel frameworks
313
      and using 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) as way of representing 3D shapes (He et al., 2024).

533   7   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we discuss the OOD issue in the current text-to-3D method with score distillation. We
536   find rendered images from the initial 3D models lie out of the distribution of the pretrained score
537   network, which is typically Stable Diffusion. Such issue cause unstable score prediction for the
538   generation process. To solve this issue, we propose a path tracing method. Experimental results
539   demonstrate that our proposed method enhances the generation process, achieving higher CLIP
      similarity and maintaining visual quality.

749   Our paper relies on the assumption that the intermediate checkpoints during the forwarding stage

751   at least only represent vector fields, and a vector field is a gradient field when several conditions
752
      are satisfied, including path independence, continuous partial derivatives, and zero curls (Matthews,
      1998). The violation may hinder the optimization in the generation process.
753
754   The claim of OOD issue may not be applicable when the pretrained score network spretrain includes
755   low-quality images similar to initial rendered images. Our claim is valid as current methods adopt
      Stable Diffusion as spretrain , which is trained on high-quality realistic images to form a 2D prior.

      F    T WO T YPES OF O UT OF D ISTRIBUTION I SSUES
835
836
837   In the context of 3D generation, there are two primary types of out-of-distribution (OOD) issues. We
838   have discussed one of it in the main context. Another type is associated with the inaccuracy of the
839   score prediction network sϕ in predicting the score from the noisy rendered images xt to the clear
840
      ones x0 . This type of OOD problem often arises in works that employ two networks to optimize
      the 3D representation, such as ProlificDreamer (Wang et al., 2023), where one network sϕ is used
841
      to predict the score of noisy rendered images. It also has a close relationship with the initialization
842
      strategy of the score prediction network sϕ which typically follows by three approaches:
843
844
            1. Random Initialization: This method initializes sϕ without any prior information, which
845            often results in sϕ being initially distant from the distribution of rendered images, leading to
846            inaccurate score predictions in the early stages.
847
848         2. LoRA Initialization: In this approach, sϕ is initially set as a Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
849            of a pretrained model spretrain , endowing it with robust prior knowledge. At the beginning
850            of the training, the elements in the LoRA matrix B are zero, making sϕ identical to the
851            pre-trained model. As training progresses, sϕ gradually adapts to fit the distribution of the
852            rendered images. Notably, ProlificDreamer employs this initialization strategy. However,
853
               due to the initial suboptimal quality of the rendered images, sϕ fails to predict the score
               accurately, presenting another OOD challenge.
854
855         3. Pre-training with 3D Model: Before training the 3D model, sϕ is initialized by fitting it to
856            the images rendered from the initial 3D model. This method ensures that sϕ starts with a
857            distribution that closely matches the rendered images, facilitating accurate score predictions
858            from the onset. Similar to the second method, during the subsequent training process, the
859            optimization of the 3D model and sϕ alternates to ensure sϕ can consistently predict the
860            noise accurately.
861
862   In methods using two score networks, the third initialization approach is necessary and advantageous
863   as it aligns sϕ ’s distribution with that of the 3D model-rendered images, thereby ensuring accurate
      and stable score predictions throughout the 3D generation process.

      I   S HIFT OF L O RA IN T RAINING 3D
919
920
      The traditional approach of changing LoRA target is to use the intermediate checkpoint of LoRA in
921   training. But three drawbacks determine its unconveniency:
922
923         1. Shift of Targets: Over the course of training 3D, a stable and solid target pretrained model
924            is important. If we change the LoRA of pretrained model just like changing a accessory, the
925            final target θ is also changing even though the Exponential Moving Average (EMA) trick is
926            used to train smooth LoRA. Reflected to current θ, the appearance and shape we construct
927            in current LoRA is destroyed when changing to the next one (Huang et al., 2024).
928         2. Limited Number of Checkpoints and Difficulty of Selecting Strategy of Loading LoRA:
929            We always train LoRA using different batch size, dataset size and some other parameters.
930            In some extreme situations, we only train several epochs which is so short to produce to
931            provide feasible path. In addition, with some intermediate LoRAs, we may be confused
932
               to select the suitable interval to change LoRA and the LoRA step gap. It always needs
               parameter search which will consume a lot of time.
933
934         3. Resources consumed: The intermediate LoRA checkpoints are needed to be saved locally,
935            requiring extra disk memory. And loading new LoRA and setting configuration consume
936
               time. Both need resources to implement.
937
      Base on these reasons, we adopt the new weighted LoRA method to trace path, showed in Section 4.3.
