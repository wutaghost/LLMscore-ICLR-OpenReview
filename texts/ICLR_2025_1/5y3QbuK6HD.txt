011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Average-reward Markov decision processes (MDPs) provide a foundational
014            framework for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. However, average-
015            reward MDPs have remained largely unexplored in reinforcement learning (RL)
016
               settings, with the majority of RL-based efforts having been allocated to episodic
               and discounted MDPs. In this work, we study a unique structural property of
017
               average-reward MDPs and utilize it to introduce Reward-Extended Differential
018
               (or RED) reinforcement learning: a novel RL framework that can be used to
019            effectively and efficiently solve various subtasks simultaneously in the average-
020            reward setting. We introduce a family of RED learning algorithms for prediction
021            and control, including proven-convergent algorithms for the tabular case. We then
022            showcase the power of these algorithms by demonstrating how they can be used to
023            learn a policy that optimizes, for the first time, the well-known conditional value-
024            at-risk (CVaR) risk measure in a fully-online manner, without the use of an explicit
025            bi-level optimization scheme or an augmented state-space.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Markov decision processes (MDPs) (Puterman, 1994) are a long-established framework for sequen-
031   tial decision-making under uncertainty. Episodic and discounted MDPs, which aim to optimize a
032   sum of rewards over time, have enjoyed success in recent years when utilizing reinforcement learn-
033
      ing (RL) solution methods (Sutton and Barto, 2018) to tackle certain problems of interest in various
      domains. Despite this success however, these MDP-based methods have yet to be fully embraced in
034
      real-world applications due to the various intricacies and implications of real-world operation that
035
      often trump the ability of current state-of-the-art methods (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021). We therefore
036   turn to the less-explored average-reward MDP, which aims to optimize the reward received per time-
037   step, to see how its unique structural properties can be leveraged to tackle challenging problems that
038   have evaded its episodic and discounted counterparts.
039
      In particular, we present results that show how the average-reward MDP’s unique structural prop-
040
      erties can be leveraged to enable a more subtask-driven approach to reinforcement learning, where
041
      various learning problems, or subtasks, are solved simultaneously (and in a fully-online manner)
042   to help solve a larger, central learning problem. Importantly, we find compelling case-study in the
043   realm of risk-aware decision-making that demonstrates how this subtask-driven approach can greatly
044   simplify problems that have proven to be challenging to solve in episodic and discounted MDPs.
045
      More formally, we introduce Reward-Extended Differential (or RED) reinforcement learning: a
046
      first-of-its-kind RL framework that makes it possible to solve various subtasks simultaneously in
047
      the average-reward setting. At the heart of this framework is the novel concept of the reward-
048   extended temporal-difference (TD) error, an extension of the celebrated TD error (Sutton, 1988),
049   which we leverage in combination with a unique structural property of the average-reward MDP
050   to solve various subtasks simultaneously. We first present the RED RL framework in a generalized
051   way, then adopt it to successfully tackle a problem that has exceeded the capabilities of current state-
052   of-the-art methods in risk-aware decision-making: learning a policy that optimizes the well-known
053   conditional value-at-risk (CVaR) risk measure (Rockafellar and Uryasev, 2000) in a fully-online
      manner without the use of an explicit bi-level optimization scheme or an augmented state-space.

066   2   R ELATED W ORK
067
068   Average-Reward Reinforcement Learning: Average-reward (or average-cost) MDPs, despite be-
069   ing one of the most well-studied frameworks for sequential decision-making under uncertainty (Put-
070   erman, 1994), have remained relatively unexplored in reinforcement learning (RL) settings. To
071   date, notable works on the subject (in the context of RL) include Mahadevan (1996), Tsitsiklis
072   and Van Roy (1999), Abounadi et al. (2001), Bhatnagar et al. (2009), and Wan et al. (2021). Most
073   relevant to our work is Wan et al. (2021), which provided a rigorous theoretical treatment of average-
074   reward MDPs in the context of RL, and proposed the proven-convergent ‘Differential Q-learning’
075   and (off-policy) ‘Differential TD-learning’ algorithms for the tabular case. Our work primarily
076
      builds on Wan et al. (2021), and we utilize their proof technique when formulating the conver-
      gence proofs for our algorithms. We note that the notion of a ‘subtask’, as explored in our work, is
077
      different to that of hierarchical RL (e.g. Sutton et al. (1999)), where the focus is on using temporally-
078
      abstracted actions, known as ‘options’ or ‘skills’, such that the agent learns a policy for each option,
079   as well as an inter-option policy. By contrast, in our work we learn a single policy, and the subtasks
080   are not part of the action-space. In the episodic and discounted settings, the notion of solving mul-
081   tiple objectives in parallel has been explored in various works (e.g. McLeod et al. (2021)), although
082   much of this work focuses on learning multiple features, options, policies, and/or value functions.
083   By contrast, in our work we learn a single policy and value function, and the subtasks are not part
084   of the state or action-spaces. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to explore solving
085   subtasks simultaneously in the average-reward setting.
086   Risk-Aware Learning and Optimization in MDPs: The notion of risk-aware learning and opti-
087   mization in MDP-based settings has been long-studied, from the well-established expected utility
088   framework (Howard and Matheson, 1972), to the more contemporary framework of coherent risk
089   measures (Artzner et al., 1999). To date, these risk-based efforts have almost exclusively focused on
090   the episodic and discounted settings. Critically, optimizing the CVaR risk measure in these settings
091   typically requires augmenting the state-space and/or having to utilize an explicit bi-level optimiza-
092   tion scheme, which can, for example, involve solving multiple MDPs. Seminal works that have
093
      looked at CVaR optimization in the standard discounted and episodic settings include Bäuerle and
      Ott (2011) and Chow et al. (2015); Hau et al. (2023a). In the distributional setting, works such as
094
      Dabney et al. (2018) have proposed a CVaR optimization approach that does not require an aug-
095
      mented state-space or an explicit bi-level optimization, however it was later shown by Lim and
096   Malik (2022) that such an approach converges to neither the optimal dynamic-CVaR nor the optimal
097   static-CVaR policies (Lim and Malik (2022) then proposed a valid approach that utilizes an aug-
098   mented state-space). Some works have looked at optimizing a time-consistent (Ruszczyński, 2010)
099   interpretation of CVaR, however this only approximates CVaR, as CVaR is not a time-consistent risk
100   measure (Boda and Filar, 2006). Other works have looked at optimizing similar objectives to CVaR
101   that are more computationally tractable, such as the entropic value-at-risk (Hau et al., 2023b).
102   Most similar to our work (in non average-reward settings) are Stanko and Macek (2019) and Miller
103   and Yang (2017). In Stanko and Macek (2019), the authors use a vaguely similar update to the
104   one derived in our work, however all of the methods proposed in Stanko and Macek (2019) require
105   either an augmented state-space or an explicit bi-level optimization. Similarly, while the approach
106   presented in Miller and Yang (2017) does not require an augmented state-space, it requires an ex-
107   plicit bi-level optimization. In the average-reward setting, Xia et al. (2023) recently proposed a set
      of algorithms for optimizing the CVaR risk measure, however their methods require the use of an

      augmented state-space and a sensitivity-based bi-level optimization. By contrast, our work, to the
109   best of our knowledge, is the first to optimize the CVaR risk measure in an MDP-based setting with-

208   4     A S UBTASK -D RIVEN A PPROACH
209
210   In this section, we motivate the need and opportunity for a subtask-driven approach to RL through
211   the lens of CVaR optimization. Let us begin by considering the standard approach used by exist-
212   ing MDP-based methods for optimizing CVaR, which requires an explicit bi-level optimization, as
213   described in Equation 8. In words, Equation 8 says that to optimize CVaR, we need to pick a wide
214   range of guesses for VaR, and for each guess, b, we need to solve an MDP. Then, out of all of the
215   MDP solutions, we pick the best one as our final solution. To further compound the computational
      costs, this approach typically requires that the state-space be augmented with a state that corresponds


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      to the VaR guess, b. Importantly, this computationally-expensive process would not be needed if we
217   somehow knew what the optimal value for b (i.e., VaR) was. In fact, in the average-reward setting,
218   if we knew VaR, then optimizing for CVaR ultimately amounts to optimizing an average (as per
219   Equation 7), which can be done trivially using the standard average-reward MDP.
220
      As such, it would appear that, to optimize CVaR, we are stuck between two extremes: a significantly
221
      computationally-expensive process if we don’t know VaR, and a trivial process if we do. But what
222
      if we could estimate VaR along the way? That is, keep some sort of running estimate of VaR that we
223   optimize simultaneously as we optimize CVaR. Indeed, such an approach has been proposed in the
224   discounted and episodic settings (e.g. Stanko and Macek (2019)), however, no approach has been
225   able to successfully remove both the augmented state-space and the explicit bi-level optimization
226   requirements. The primary difficulty lies in how one updates the estimate of VaR along the way.
227
      Critically, this is where the findings from Wan et al. (2021) come into play. In particular, Wan et al.
228
      (2021) proposed proven-convergent algorithms for the average-reward setting that can learn and/or
229   optimize the value function and average-reward simultaneously using only the TD error. In other
230   words, these algorithms are able to solve two learning objectives simultaneously using only the TD
231   error. Yet, the focus in Wan et al. (2021) was on proving the convergence of such algorithms, without
232   exploring the underlying structural properties of the average-reward MDP that made such a process
233   possible to begin with. In this work, we formalize these underlying properties, and utilize them to
234   show that if one modifies, or extends, the reward from the MDP with various learning objectives,
235   then these objectives, or subtasks, can be solved simultaneously using a modified version of the TD
236   error. Consequently, in terms of CVaR optimization, this allows us to develop appropriate learning
237
      updates for the VaR and CVaR estimates based solely on the TD error, such that we no longer need
      to augment the state-space or perform an explicit bi-level optimization.
238
239   In Section 5, we present the theoretical framework that enables the aforementioned subtask-driven
240   approach. Then, in Section 6, we adapt this general-purpose framework for CVaR optimization.

243   5    R EWARD -E XTENDED D IFFERENTIAL (RED) R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
244
245   In this section, we present our primary contribution: a framework for solving various learning ob-
246   jectives, or subtasks, simultaneously in the average-reward setting. We call this framework reward-
247   extended differential (or RED) reinforcement learning. The ‘differential’ part of the name comes
248   from the use of the differential algorithms from average-reward MDPs. The ‘reward-extended’ part
249
      of the name comes from the use of the reward-extended TD error, a novel concept that we will intro-
      duce shortly. Through this framework, we show how the average-reward MDP’s unique structural
250
      properties can be leveraged to solve various subtasks simultaneously in a fully-online manner. We
251
      first provide a formal definition for a (generic) subtask, then proceed to derive a learning frame-
252   work that allows us to simultaneously solve any given subtask that satisfies this definition. In the
253   subsequent section, we utilize this framework to tackle the CVaR optimization problem.
254
255
      Definition 5.1 (Subtask). A subtask, zi , is any scalar prediction or control objective belonging to
      a corresponding finite set Zi ⊂ R, such that there exists a linear (or piecewise linear) subtask
256
      function, f : R × Z1 × Z2 × · · · × Zi × · · · × Zn → R̃, where R is the finite set of observed
257
      per-step rewards from the MDP M, R̃ ⊂ R is a finite set of ‘extended’ per-step rewards whose
258                                                                                        .
      long-run average is the primary prediction or control objective of the MDP, M̃ = ⟨S, A, R̃, p⟩, and
259
      Z = {z1 ∈ Z1 , z2 ∈ Z2 , . . . , zn ∈ Zn } is the set of n subtasks that we wish to solve, such that:
260
261   i) f is invertible with respect to each input given all other inputs; and
262   ii) each subtask zi ∈ Z in f is independent of the states and actions, and hence independent of the
263   observed per-step reward, Rt ∈ R, such that E[f (Rt , z1 , z2 , . . . , zn )] = f (E[Rt ], z1 , z2 , . . . , zn ),

395
396   In the previous section, we derived a general-purpose framework and a corresponding set of algo-
397   rithms that enable a more subtask-driven approach to reinforcement learning, where various learning
398   problems, or subtasks, are solved simultaneously to help solve a larger, central learning problem. In
399   this section, we provide a compelling case-study which illustrates how this subtask-driven approach
400   can be used to successfully tackle the CVaR optimization problem without the use of an explicit
401   bi-level optimization scheme (as in Equation 8), or an augmented state-space.
402   First, in order to leverage the RED RL framework for CVaR optimization, we need to derive a valid
403   subtask function for CVaR that satisfies the requirements of Definition 5.1. It turns out that we can
404   use a modified version of Equation 7 as the subtask function. The details of the adaptation of Equa-
405   tion 7 into a subtask function are presented in Appendix D. Critically, as discussed in Appendix D,
406   optimizing the long-run average of the extended reward (R̃t ) from this subtask function corresponds
407   to optimizing the long-run CVaR of the observed reward (Rt ). Hence, we can utilize CVaR-specific
408   versions of the RED algorithms presented in Equations 15 and 16 (or their non-tabular equivalents)
409   to optimize VaR and CVaR, such that CVaR corresponds to the primary control objective (i.e., the
410   r̄π that we want to optimize), and VaR is the (single) subtask. We call the resulting algorithms, the
411
      RED CVaR algorithms. These algorithms, which are shown in full in Appendix D, update CVaR in
      an analogous way to the average-reward (i.e., CVaR corresponds to R̄t in Equations 15 or 16), and
412

      7   D ISCUSSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
503
504   In this work, we introduced reward-extended differential (or RED) reinforcement learning: a novel
505   reinforcement learning framework that can be used to solve various subtasks simultaneously in the
506   average-reward setting. We introduced a family of RED RL algorithms for prediction and control,
507   and then showcased how these algorithms could be adopted to effectively and efficiently tackle the
508   CVaR optimization problem. More specifically, we were able to use the RED RL framework to
509   successfully learn a policy that optimized the CVaR risk measure without using an explicit bi-level
510   optimization scheme or an augmented state-space, thereby alleviating some of the computational
511   challenges and non-trivialities that arise when performing risk-based optimization in the episodic
512
      and discounted settings. Empirically, we showed that the RED-based CVaR algorithms fared well
      both in tabular and linear function approximation settings. Moreover, our experiments suggest that
513
      these algorithms are robust to the initial guesses for the subtasks and primary learning objective.
514
515   More broadly, our work has introduced a theoretically-sound framework that allows for a subtask-
516   driven approach to reinforcement learning, where various learning problems (or subtasks) are solved
517   simultaneously to help solve a larger, central learning problem. In this work, we showed (both
518
      theoretically and empirically) how this framework can be utilized to predict and/or optimize any
      arbitrary number of subtasks simultaneously in the average-reward setting. Central to this result is
519
      the novel concept of the reward-extended TD error, which is utilized in our framework to develop
520
      learning rules for the subtasks, and satisfies key theoretical properties that make it possible to solve
521   any given subtask in a fully-online manner by minimizing the regular TD error. Moreover, we
522   built-upon existing results from Wan et al. (2021) to show the almost sure convergence of tabular
523   algorithms derived from our framework. While we have only begun to grasp the implications of our
524   framework, we have already seen some promising indications in the CVaR case study: the ability
525   to turn explicit bi-level optimization problems into implicit bi-level optimizations that can be solved
526   in a fully-online manner, as well as the potential to turn certain states (that meet certain conditions)
527   into subtasks, thereby reducing the size of the state-space.
528   Nonetheless, while these results are encouraging, they are subject to a number of limitations. Firstly,
529   by nature of operating in the average-reward setting, we are subject to the somewhat-strict assump-
530   tions made about the Markov chain induced by the policy (e.g. unichain or communicating). These
531   assumptions could restrict the applicability of our framework, as they may not always hold in prac-
532   tice. Similarly, our definition for a subtask requires that the associated subtask function be linear,
533   which may also limit the applicability of our framework to simpler functions. Finally, it remains to
534   be seen empirically how our framework performs when dealing with multiple subtasks, when taking
535
      on more complex tasks, and/or when utilizing nonlinear function approximation.
536   In future work, we hope to address many of these limitations, as well as explore how these promising
537   results can be extended to other domains, beyond the risk-awareness problem. In particular, we
538   believe that the ability to optimize various subtasks simultaneously, as well as the potential to reduce
539   the size of the state-space, by converting certain states to subtasks (where appropriate), could help
      alleviate significant computational challenges in other areas moving forward.

1599   A natural question to ask would be whether we can extend these convergence results to the prediction
1600   case. In other words, can we show that a tabular RED CVaR TD-learning algorithm will converge to
1601   the long-run VaR and CVaR of the observed reward induced by following a given policy? It turns out
1602   that, because we are not optimizing the expectation in Equation D.5e when doing prediction (we are
1603   only learning it), we cannot guarantee that we will eventually find the optimal VaR estimate, which
1604   implies that we may not recover the CVaR value (since Equation D.5f only holds to the optimal VaR
1605
       value). However, this is not to say that a RED CVaR TD-learning algorithm has no use. In fact, we
       do use such an algorithm as part of an actor-critic architecture for optimizing CVaR in the inverted
1606
       pendulum experiment (see Appendix E). Empirically, as discussed in Appendix E, we find that this
1607
       actor-critic approach is able to find the optimal CVaR policy.
1608
1609   We end this section by briefly noting that in the risk measure literature, risk measures are typically
1610   classified into two categories: static or dynamic. This classification is based on the time consistency
1611
       of the risk measure that one aims to optimize (Boda and Filar, 2006). Curiously, in our case the CVaR
       that we aim to optimize does not fit into either category perfectly. One could make the argument
1612
       that the CVaR that we aim to optimize most closely matches the static category, given that there is
1613
       some time inconsistency before t → ∞. Conversely, one could make a different argument that the
1614   CVaR that we aim to optimize most closely resembles the dynamic category since the sum over t for
1615   the average-reward is outside of the CVaR operator (see Theorem 1 of Xia et al. (2023)), such that
1616   an optimal deterministic stationary policy exists (unlike the static case; see Bäuerle and Ott (2011)).
1617   This does not affect the significance of our results, but rather suggests that a third category of risk
1618   measures may be needed to capture such nuances that occur in the average-reward setting.

1807   Follow-up Experiment: Varying the CVaR Parameter
1808   Given the results shown in Fig. 2a), we can see that, with proper hyperparameter tuning, the tab-
1809   ular RED CVaR Q-learning algorithm is able to reliably find the optimal CVaR policy for a CVaR
1810   parameter, τ , of 0.25. In the context of the red-pill blue-pill environment, this means that the agent
1811   learns to stay in the red world state because the state has a characteristic reward distribution with
1812   a better (higher) CVaR compared to the blue world state. By contrast, the risk-neutral differential
1813   algorithm yields an average-reward optimal policy that dictates that the agent should stay in the blue
1814
       world state because the state has a better (higher) average reward compared to the red world state.
1815   Now consider what would happen if we used the RED CVaR Q-learning algorithm with a τ of 0.99.
1816   By definition, a CVaR corresponding to a τ ≈ 1.0 is equivalent to the average reward. Hence, with
1817   a τ of 0.99, we would expect that the optimal CVaR policy corresponds to staying in the blue world
1818   state (since it has the better average reward). This means that for some τ between 0.25 and 0.99,
1819   there is a critical point where the CVaR-optimal policy changes from staying in the red world (let us
1820
       call this the red policy) to staying in the blue world state (let us call this the blue policy).
1821   We can estimate this critical point using simple Monte Carlo (MC). We are able to use MC in this
1822   case because both policies effectively stay in a single state (the red or blue world state), such that
1823   the CVaR of the policies can be estimated by sampling the characteristic reward distribution of each
1824   state, while accounting for the exploration ϵ. Fig. E.4 shows the MC estimate of the CVaR of the
1825   red and blue policies for a range of CVaR parameters, assuming an exploration ϵ of 0.1. Note that
1826
       we used the same distribution parameters listed in Appendix F for the red-pill blue-pill environment.
       As shown in Fig. E.4, this critical point occurs somewhere around τ ≈ 0.8.
1827
1828   Hence, one way that we can further validate the tabular RED CVaR Q-learning algorithm, is by
1829   re-running the red-pill blue-pill experiment for different CVaR parameters, and seeing if the optimal
1830   CVaR policy indeed changes at a τ ≈ 0.8. Importantly, this allows us to empirically validate whether
1831   the algorithm actually optimizes at the desired risk level. When running this experiment, we used
1832
       the same hyperparameters used to generate the results in Fig. 2a), with the exception of using a VaR
       η of 1e-1, as this showed slightly better performance for a broader range of CVaR parameters. We
1833
       ran the experiment for τ ∈ {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.85, 0.9}. For each τ , we performed 10 runs using
1834
       different random seeds, and the results were averaged across the runs.

       E.3   A DDITIONAL C OMMENTARY ON E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
1999
2000   Red-Pill Blue-Pill: In the red-pill blue-pill task, we can see from Fig. E.2 that for combinations with
2001   large step sizes, the RED CVaR Q-learning algorithm was able to successfully learn a policy, within
2002   the 100k time steps, that prioritizes maximizing the reward CVaR over the average-reward, thereby
2003   achieving a sort of risk-awareness. However, for combinations with smaller step sizes, particularly
2004   for low VaR η’s, the algorithm did not converge in the allotted training period. We re-ran some of
2005   the combinations with constant step sizes for longer training periods, and found that the algorithm
2006
       eventually converged to the risk-aware policy given enough training time. For combinations with
       the 1/n step size, we found that if the other step sizes in the combination were sufficiently small, the
2007
       algorithm would not converge to the correct policy (even with more training time). This suggests
2008
       that a more slowly-decreasing step size sequence should be used instead so that the algorithm has
2009   more time to find the correct policy before the step sizes in the sequence become too small.
2010
2011   Inverted Pendulum: In the inverted pendulum task, we can see from Fig. E.6 that both algorithms
2012
       achieved similar performance, as shown by the similar histograms for both the reward CVaR and
       average-reward during the final 1,000 time steps. These results suggest that both algorithms con-
2013
       verged to the same set of (sometimes sub-optimal) policies, as expected.
2014
2015   Overall: In both experiments, we can see that with proper hyperparameter tuning, the RED CVaR
2016   algorithms were able to consistently and reliably find the optimal CVaR policy. The VaR and CVaR
2017   estimates generally tracked with what one would expect (similar values, with the VaR value being
2018
       slightly larger than the CVaR value). However, these estimates were not always the same as the
       actual VaR and CVaR values induced by the policy because the solutions to the average-reward
2019
       MDP Bellman equations are only correct up to a constant. This is typically not a concern, given that
2020
       the relative ordering of the policies is usually what is of interest.
