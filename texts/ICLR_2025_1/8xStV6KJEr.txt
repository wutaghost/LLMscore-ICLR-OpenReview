006                                               A BSTRACT
007
008            This paper describes an efficient algorithm for solving noisy linear inverse prob-
009            lems using pretrained diffusion models. Extending the paradigm of denoising dif-
010            fusion implicit models (DDIM), we propose constrained diffusion implicit models
011
               (CDIM) that modify the diffusion updates to enforce a constraint upon the final
               output. For noiseless inverse problems, CDIM exactly satisfies the constraints; in
012
               the noisy case, we generalize CDIM to satisfy an exact constraint on the residual
013
               distribution of the noise. Experiments across a variety of tasks and metrics show
014            strong performance of CDIM, with analogous inference acceleration to uncon-
015            strained DDIM: 10 to 50 times faster than previous diffusion methods for inverse
016            problems. We demonstrate the versatility of our approach on many problems in-
017            cluding super-resolution, denoising, inpainting, deblurring, and 3D point cloud
018            reprojection.

020   1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) have recently emerged as powerful generative
023   models capable of capturing complex data distributions (Ho et al., 2020). Their success has spurred
024   interest in applying them to solve inverse problems, which are fundamental in fields such as com-
025   puter vision, medical imaging, and signal processing (Tropp & Wright, 2010; Hansen, 2010). In-
026   verse problems require recovering unknown signals from (possibly noisy) observations. Linear in-
027   verse problems, where the observations consist of linear measurements of a signal, encompass tasks
028   like super-resolution, inpainting, and deblurring.
029   Existing methods that apply diffusion models to linear inverse problems face several limitations.
030   First, many previous works require task specific training or fine-tuning (Li et al., 2022; Xie et al.,
031   2023). Second, methods that use pretrained diffusion models often introduce many additional net-
032   work evaluations during inference (Dou & Song, 2023; Zhu et al., 2024). Finally, popular diffusion
033   inverse methods such as diffusion posterior sampling (Chung et al., 2022b) fail to exactly recover
034   the input observations.
035   In this work, we propose constrained diffusion implicit models (CDIM), extending the inference
036   acceleration of denoising diffusion implicit models (Song et al., 2021) to efficiently solve noisy lin-
037   ear inverse problems using a single pretrained diffusion model. Our method modifies the diffusion
038   updates to enforce constraints on the final output, integrating measurement constraints directly into
039   the diffusion process. In the noiseless case, this approach achieves exact recovery of the observa-
040   tions. For noisy observations, we generalize our method by optimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL)
041   divergence between the empirical residual distribution and a known noise distribution, effectively
042
      handling general noise models beyond the Gaussian assumption.
043   Our contributions are as follows:
044
045          • Accelerated inference: we accelerate inference, reducing the number of model evaluations
046            and wall-clock time by an order of magnitude—10 to 50 times faster than previous posterior
047
               diffusion methods—while maintaining comparable quality.
048          • Exact recovery of noiseless observations: we can find solutions that exactly match the
049
               noiseless observation.
050          • General noise models: we extend the CDIM framework to accommodate arbitrary obser-
051
               vational noise distributions through distributional divergence minimization, demonstrating
               effectiveness given non-Gaussian noise, such as Poisson noise.

      4     METHODS
163
164   We are interested in solving linear inverse problems of the form y = Ax, where y ∈ Rd is a linear
165   measurement of x ∈ Rn and A ∈ Rd×n describes the form of our measurements. For example,
166   if A ∈ {0, 1}n×n is a binary mask (which is the case for, e.g., in-painting or sparse recovery
167   problems) then y describes a partial observation of x. We seek an estimate x̂ that is consistent
168   with our observations: in the noiseless case, Ax̂ = y. More generally, we seek to recover a robust
169   estimate of x̂ when the observations y have been corrupted by noise. Given a noise distribution
170   r, we seek to minimize DKL (r̂ ∥ r), where r̂ is the empirical distribution of d residuals, e.g.,
171   y − Ax̂ ∈ Rd , between noisy observations y and our estimates Ax̂.
172   We rely on a diffusion model pθ (x) to identify an estimate x̂ that is both consistent with the observed
173   measurements y and likely according to the model. In Section 4.1, we propose a modification of the
174   DDIM inference procedure to efficiently optimize the Tweedie estimates of x̂0 to satisfy Ax̂0 = y
175   during the diffusion process, resulting in a consistent and likely final result x0 . In Section 4.2 we
176   extend this optimization-based inference procedure to account for noise in the observations y. In
177   Section 4.3 we describe an early-stopping heuristic to avoid overfitting to noisy observations, which
178
      further reduces the cost of inference. Finally, in Section 4.4 we show how to set the step sizes for
      these optimization-based methods.

181
182   For linear measurements A, the Tweedie formula for x̂0 (and the corresponding plugin-estimate
183   Equation (5)) extends to a formula for the expected observations:
184                                      E [y|xt ] = AE [x0 |xt ] ≈ Ax̂0 .                              (8)

      For noiseless observations y, we propose a modification of the DDIM updates Equation (6) to find
186   xt−1 that satisfies the constraint Ax̂0 = y. I.e., at each time step t, we force the Tweedie estimate
187   of the posterior mean of q(y|xt ) to match the observed measurements y:
188
                                           arg min ∥xt−1 − fθ (xt )∥2
189                                          xt−1                                                       (9)
190                                       subject to Ax̂0 = y.

192   We can interpret Equation (9) as a projection of the DDIM update fθ (xt ) onto the set of values
193   xt−1 that satisfy the constraint Ax̂0 = y. The full inference procedure is analogous to projected
194   gradient descent, whereby we alternately take a step fθ (xt ) determined by the diffusion model, and
195   then project back onto the constraint Ax̂0 = y. We implement the projection step itself via gradient
                                 (0)
196   descent, initialized from xt−1 = fθ (xt ) and computing
                                       (k)     (k−1)
197                                  xt−1 = xt−1       + η∇xt−1 ∥y − Ax̂0 ∥2 .                            (10)

199   As t approaches 0, x̂0 converges to x0 and ∥y − Ax̂0 ∥2 becomes a simple convex quadratic, which
200   can be minimized to arbitrary accuracy by taking sufficiently many gradient steps. This allows us to
201   guarantee exact recovery of the observations y = Ax0 in the recovered inverse x0 .
202   For t close to T , we face two conceptual challenges in optimizing Equation (9). First, for large t, no
203   value xt will satisfy Ax̂0 = y and therefore the optimization is infeasible. Second, the estimate of
204   the score function ∇xt log q(xt ) using ϵθ (xt , t) may be inaccurate; we risk overfitting to a bad plug-
205   in estimate x̂0 . We illustrate both these claims by considering the Tweedie estimator Equation (5)
206   in the case t = T . In this case, xt ∼ N (0, I) is independent of x0 and therefore E[x0 |xt ] = E[x0 ],
207   the mean of the data distribution q(x0 ). Unless AE[x0 ] = y, the optimization is infeasible when
208   t = T . Furthermore, we observe that when t = T , the plug-in estimator x̂0 is not independent of xt
209
      and x̂0 ̸= E[x0 ]. This is indicative of error in the plug-in estimator, especially at high noise levels.
210   In light of these observations, we replace Equation (9) with a Lagrangian

214   is achieved implicitly by early stopping after k = K steps of gradient descent. In contrast to
215   projection, this Lagrangian objective is robust to both (1) the possible infeasibility of ŷ0 (xt ) = y

      4.4   C HOICE OF S TEP S IZE η
355
356   An important hyperparameter of these algorithms is the step size η. DPS sets η proportional to
357
      1/∥y − Ax̂0 ∥ Chung et al. (2022b). We find that this fails to converge for KL optimization, and also
      produces unstable results for L2 optimization when T ′ is small. This is because ∥y − Ax̂0 ∥ → 0
358
      towards the end of the optimization, leading to extremely large steps. One option is to set η inversely
359
      proportional to the magnitude of the gradient ∥∇xt−δ ∥ at every single optimization step. Although
360   this is the easiest solution, it can also result in unstable oscillations and slower convergence. Instead,
361   we propose to set η inversely proportional to Ex∼Xtrain ∇xt−δ , a common optimization heuristic
362   (Amari, 1998; Pascanu & Bengio, 2014). In Appendix A we describe how to compute this expecta-
363   tion. In Figure 5 we show qualitatively what happens with different η schedules.
364
      We find that for a specific optimization objective and task, the magnitude of the gradient ∥∇xt−δ ∥
365
      is highly similar across data points, datasets, and model architectures. While it is difficult to reason
366   analytically about these magnitudes due to backpropagation through the network ϵ(xt , t), we em-
367   pirically demonstrate this observation in Appendix A. This suggests that a learned step size based
368   on Ex∼Xtrain ∇xt−δ generalizes as a good learning rate for unseen data. For all experiments, we
369   estimate these magnitudes from FFHQ training data.

      In this paper we introduced CDIM, a new approach for solving noisy linear inverse problems with
480   pretrained diffusion models. This is achieved by exploiting the structure of the DDIM inference
481   procedure. By projecting the DDIM updates, such that Tweedie estimates of the denoised image x̂0
482   match the linear constraints, we are able to enforce constraints without making out-of-distribution
483   edits to the noised iterates xt . We note that our method cannot handle non-linear constraints, includ-
484   ing latent diffusion, because for a non-linear function h, E [h(x0 )] ̸= h(E [x0 ]). Therefore, unlike
485   the linear case of Equation (8), we cannot extend Tweedie’s estimate of the posterior mean of x0 to
      an estimate of the posterior mean of non-linear observations h(x0 ). However, for linear constraints,

673   A     C ALCULATING E ∇xt−δ
674
675   To calculate our expected gradient magnitude, we first start with simple gradient normalization:
676   η ← 1/ ∇xt−δ , which normalizes our step size by the gradient magnitude on the fly at every
677   optimization step. We run the full CDIM algorithm on the target task with the desired number of
678   steps T and K on images from the training set. We calculate and store each gradient magnitude
679     ∇xt−δ during the optimization process at every step. Finally, we average the empirical gradient
680   magnitudes at each step t − δ to find E ∇xt−δ across data points and inner optimization steps k.
681   In practice we find that very few images are required to calculate a stable value for the expected
682   gradient magnitude. In all experiments the value was calculated by running an initial optimization
683
      on 10 images from the training set.
