009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper presents a new self-supervised video representation learning frame-
012            work ARVideo, which autoregressively predict the next video token in a tailored
013            sequence order. Two key designs are included. First, we organize autoregressive
014            video tokens into clusters that span both spatially and temporally, thereby enabling
015            a richer aggregation of contextual information compared to the standard spatial-
016            only or temporal-only clusters. Second, we adopt a randomized spatiotemporal
017            prediction order to facilitate learning from multi-dimensional data, addressing the
018            limitations of a handcrafted spatial-first or temporal-first sequence order. Extensive
019            experiments establish ARVideo as an effective paradigm for self-supervised video
020
               representation learning. For example, when trained with the ViT-B backbone,
               ARVideo competitively attains 81.2% on Kinetics-400 and 70.9% on Something-
021
               Something V2, which are on par with the strong benchmark set by VideoMAE.
022
               Importantly, ARVideo also demonstrates higher training efficiency, i.e., it trains
023            14% faster and requires 58% less GPU memory compared to VideoMAE.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The transformer architecture, as introduced in Vaswani et al. (Vaswani et al., 2017), has fundamentally
030   transformed the field of natural language processing (NLP) through its ability to model long-range
031   dependencies with minimal inductive bias. A crucial catalyst for its success lies in self-supervised
032   learning of robust and transferable representations from large volumes of unlabeled data. Within
033
      this paradigm, masked language modeling (MLM) (Devlin et al., 2019) and autoregressive modeling
      (AR) (Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023) stand out as two leading approaches.
034
      Specifically, MLM masks random portions of input tokens and trains models to predict masked
035
      elements; whereas AR predicts subsequent words in a sequence based on all preceding words. These
036   methods have propelled state-of-the-art performance in various NLP tasks.
037
038   In the video domain, however, the landscape is different. Previous studies have predominantly relied
039
      on supervised pretraining using image datasets, often overlooking the critical aspect of temporal
      dynamics (Liu et al., 2022b; Bertasius et al., 2021). Recently, there has been a shift towards
040
      leveraging NLP-inspired mask language modeling (Devlin et al., 2019) or image-inspired mask
041
      image modeling (He et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2022) to directly exploit unlabeled video datasets for
042   pretraining. For instance, VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022; Feichtenhofer et al., 2022) introduces mask
043   autoencoder (He et al., 2022) for self-supervised video video representation learning; BEVT (Wang
044   et al., 2022a) learns spatial representations from image data and joint-masked image and video
045   modeling. Despite these advancements, autoregressive modeling—another powerful self-supervised
046   learning approach in NLP—has yet to be extensively explored within the context of video data
047   analysis.
048   Critically, applying autoregressive pretraining to video data entails the same principle of autore-
049   gressively predicting the next element in a sequential order based on its predecessors. In natural
050   language, these elements—words—are clearly defined and inherently follow a chronological order.
051   For images, elements could be conceptualized as pixels or patches arranged in a flattened sequence
052   (Chen et al., 2020; El-Nouby et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024). The further transition to video data,
053   however, introduces additional complexity due to its inherently multidimensional nature (i.e., in-
      cluding both spatial and temporal dimensions). This raises a crucial inquiry: how should we define

079   We note traditional methods, such as converting video into a sequence of cubes (Tong et al., 2022;
080   Bertasius et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2022b) and subsequently linearly mapping these
081   cubes into video tokens, generally reveal critical limitations in addressing this query. Specifically, the
082   granularity of these video tokens often fails to encapsulate the rich semantics typically represented by
083   words in text-based models—primarily because 1) these video tokens are too dimensionally limited,
084
      and 2) video inherently lacks a sequential order in its spatial dimensions, although it retains this
      feature in its temporal aspects.
085
086   To address these challenges, we hereby present ARVideo, a novel autoregressive-based video repre-
087   sentation learning paradigm with two key designs (see Figure 1). Firstly, we redefine ‘video elements’
088   by grouping video tokens into spatiotemporal video clusters, differentiating from conventional single-
089   dimensional strategies like spatial video clusters or temporal video clusters. This approach improves
090
      semantic representation by aggregating more contextually relevant multidimensional information.
      Secondly, we find that, compared to well-defined yet single-dimensional spatial-first or temporal-first
091
      sequence orders, a sequence order that randomly integrates both spatial and temporal dimensions
092
      empirically yields significantly stronger results. This suggests that effectively capturing the inherent
093   multidimensionality of video data is crucial for autoregressive modeling. Extensive experiments
094   establish our ARVideo as an effective paradigm for video representation learning. For example, while
095   the autoregressive video representation learning baseline only attains 74.2% on Kinetics-400 and
096   66.4% on Something-Something V2, ARVideo significantly boosts the results to 81.2% (+7%) and
097   70.9% (+4.5%), respectively. Notably, these results not only match but, in some aspects, surpass
098   the strong benchmark set by VideoMAE, particularly with respect to training efficiency—ARVideo
099   achieves faster training speeds by 14% and reduces GPU memory consumption by 58%.

104   2.1   V IDEO R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
105
106   Video representation learning has witnessed significant exploration, historically driven by supervised
107   learning methods (Tran et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Bertasius
      et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b) that pretrain backbone networks on labeled image or video data before


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      fine-tuning. However, such methods face challenges due to inherent discrepancy between image and
109   video data, compounded by the scarcity of comprehensively labeled video datasets.
110
111   In the era of self-supervised learning, recent work have designed pre-tasks incorporating temporal
112
      information for self-supervised video representation learning (Xu et al., 2019; Benaim et al., 2020;
      Huang et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2021; Ranasinghe et al., 2022) and leveraging contrastive learning for
113
      effective visual representations (Qian et al., 2021; Kuang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Diba et al., 2021;
114
      Han et al., 2020a;b). Additional, mask reconstruction-based methods inspired by masked language
115   modeling (Devlin et al., 2019) are introduced into self-supervised image and video representation
116   learning. For example, MAE (He et al., 2022) presents a scalable self-supervised learning method
117   to reconstruct masked image patches while VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022) extends this approach to
118   video data and reconstructs masked spacetime patches. BEVT (Wang et al., 2022b) separates spatial
119   learning from temporal dynamics, training on masked images initially before jointly on masked
120   images and videos. Christoph et al. (Feichtenhofer et al., 2022) introduce an efficient video-based
121   MAE extension with minimal biases and significant speedups. In contrast to prior works, our ARVideo
122   proposes a new path for self-supervised video representation learning via autoregressive pretraining.
123
124   2.2   AUTOREGRESSIVE P RETRAINING
125
      As a representative approach for autoregressive pretraining, Generative Pretrained Transformer (GPT)
126
      trains language models by autoregressively predicting the next word based on all preceding words
127
      in a sentence. Inspired by the success of autoregressive modeling in NLP, researchers start to apply
128   autoregressive pretraining in computer vision. ImageGPT (Chen et al., 2020) learns effective image
129   representations by training a Transformer to autoregressively predict image pixels without any prior
130   knowledge of their 2D structure. SAIM (Qi et al., 2023) adopts an encoder to autoregressively
131   learn contextual information like a standard vision transformer (ViT) and a decoder to predict the
132   current content, mutually reinforcing each other’s functions. RandSAC (Hua et al., 2022) arranges
133   image tokens into segments for parallel intra-segment and sequential inter-segment autoregressive
134   prediction. However, applying autoregressive pretraining on video data faces notable challenges due
135   to the extra temporal dimension. ARVideo explores the design of autoregressive video elements and
136
      visual sequence orders for video representation learning.

143   prediction order as the key ingredients in ARVideo for autoregressive prediction with videos.

186   3.3     ARV IDEO
187
188   Illustrated in Figure 1, ARVideo autoregressively pretrains on video data x ∈ RT ×H×W ×C . Note
189   that directly extending ImageGPT to videos faces significant challenges, primarily due to the added
190   temporal dimension, which would significantly escalate computational demands, even with low-
191   resolution videos like 4 × 32 × 32. Moreover, pixels as autoregressive elements lack semantic
192   richness compared to words in the language, further necessitating pixel grouping strategies to enhance
193
      representation learning. To better facilitate learning from multi-dimensional video data, we also
      explore prediction orders across spatial and temporal dimensions.
194

      3.3.1    P IXEL GROUPING
197   From Pixels to Video Tokens. With patch embeddings in ViT, videos can be patchified into non-
198   overlapping cubes (Tong et al., 2022; Bertasius et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2022b)
199   of size PT × PW × PH . Then, each cube is transformed into a video token through a linear
200   projection layer, resulting in N = PTT × PHH × PWW video tokens. This tokenization significantly
201   reduces operational elements, thus alleviating computational demands while ensuring that each video
202   token encapsulates richer semantics compared to individual pixels. For example, as reported in
203   Table 1, using video tokens as autoregressive elements for pretraining significantly outperforms
204   approaches without tokenization by 3.3% while keeping pretraining resolution consistent with
205   previous work (Tong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022a).
206

209                            Token      16 × 224 × 224                    64.0

212   videos and improves performance by 3.3%.

214   This promising transition from pixels to video tokens introduces a compelling query: Can further
215   performance gains be realized by aggregating more tokens? In pursuit of this, we examine three
      options: grouping video tokens into spatial, temporal, or spatiotemporal clusters. It is important

      to note that within each cluster, video tokens are always fully attended to each other. This full-
217   attention configuration helps to enable a more effective consolidation of semantic content within each
218   autoregressive element.

      From Tokens to Spatial Clusters. As shown in Figure 2(b), we strategically group spatially
      neighbored tokens—those sharing the same temporal positions but varying spatially—into spatial
222
      clusters. Following the patch embedding step, video tokens within the spatial domain PHH × PWW
223

      are grouped into one element, resulting in PTT autoregressive elements. For example, a video of
225
      size 16 × 224 × 224 with a cube embedding size of 2 × 16 × 16 (Tong et al., 2022) here will be
      transformed into 8 autoregressive elements, with each element comprising 14 × 14 tokens.
226
227
228   From Tokens to Temporal Clusters. As illustrated in Figure 2(c), our method integrates temporal
229   information by grouping tokens that are temporally adjacent into temporal clusters. Specifically,
230   tokens within the temporal domain PTT are grouped into one element, resulting in PHH × PWW au-
231   toregressive elements. For instance, a video of size 16 × 224 × 224 with a cube embedding size of
232   2 × 16 × 16 (Tong et al., 2022) here will transformed into 14 × 14 autoregressive elements, with each
233   element comprising 8 tokens.
234
235   From Tokens to Spatiotemporal Clusters. Moving beyond the single-dimensional grouping strate-
236   gies discussed above, we now consider the inherently multidimensional nature of video data by
237   grouping neighboring KT × KH × KW tokens into spatiotemporal clusters with no overlaps, as
238   illustrated in Figure 2(d). This strategy results in a total number of PTTKT × PHHKH × PWWKW clusters,
239   with each containing both spatial and temporal information as an autoregressive element.
240
241   3.3.2   S PATIALT EMPORAL P REDICTION O RDER
242
243   For the spatiotemporal cluster, we further explore its prediction order. Specifically, this strategy
244   is expected to yield PTTKT clusters at each spatial position, and PHHKH × PWWKW clusters at each
245   temporal position.
246
247
      Pre-defined order. We implement two systematic strategies: a spatial-first order and a temporal-first
248   order. The spatial-first approach prioritizes autoregressive pretraining within the PHHKH × PWWKW
249   spatiotemporal clusters along the spatial dimension, before transitioning to clusters in subsequent tem-
250   poral positions. Conversely, the temporal-first approach prioritizes within the PTTKT spatiotemporal
251   clusters along the temporal dimension, then proceeds to clusters in subsequent spatial positions.
252
253
254
      Random Rasteration. Inspired by the random sentence permutation technique used in XLNet (Yang
      et al., 2019) for enhancing autoregressive pretraining, our random rasterization approach scrambles
255
      the order of clusters randomly during autoregressive pretraining. This method avoids the constraints
256
      of fixed sequential patterns, such as spatial-first or temporal-first, and allows ARVideo to adaptively
257   model both long- and short-range spatial-temporal information. Such flexibility in autoregressive
258   prediction orders not only captures the inherent multidimensionality of video data more effectively
259   but also fosters a richer, more comprehensive video representation.
260
261
      3.3.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
262
263
      We adopt the ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Tong et al., 2022) as the encoder. For the decoder, we
264   take the Transformer decoder with cross attention but without self-attention. This design choice aims
265   to simplify the decoding process, emphasizing interaction between the encoded inputs while reducing
266   training costs. The query of the decoder is randomly initialized but includes position information
267   to facilitate sequence generation. Our model utilizes a strategically designed attention mask as in
268   previous work (Chen et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018) to enable efficient autoregressive prediction in
269   a parallel computation framework. When transferring to downstream tasks, we remove the decoder
      and only finetune the encoder.

293   4.1      DATASET AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
294
      We primarily evaluate ARVideo on Kinetics-400 (Kay et al., 2017) and Something-Something
295
      V2 (Goyal et al., 2017). Specifically, Kinetics-400 contains 400 classes and 260k videos of 10s, with
296
      240k for training and 20k for validation; Something-Something V2 contains 174 classes with 169k
297   videos for training and 25k for validation. While Kinetics-400 provides a broad spectrum of actions
298   with minimal context, Something-Something V2 focuses more on the interaction of actions with
299   objects.
300
      For our experiments, we first pretrain a vanilla video Transformer (Tong et al., 2022) with ARVideo,
301
      and then fine-tune the pretrained model on the target action recognition datasets. Additionally, we
302
      assess the feature transferability on AvA v2.2 (Gu et al., 2018) and HMDB (Kuehne et al., 2011).
303   AvA v2.2 is a human action localization dataset with 211k videos for training and 57k for validation;
304   HMDB is a small video dataset with 3.5k videos for training and 1.5k videos for validation.
305
306
      We follow the established protocol in prior work (Tong et al., 2022) to train our models. Instead of
      using negative log-likelihood as in GPT (Radford et al., 2018), we employ mean square error (MSE)
307
      loss to measure the discrepancy between the predicted and target cubes, as utilized in MAE (He et al.,
308
      2022). We randomly mask 80% tokens in each element to reduce the overall training costs; note that,
309   unlike MAE or VideoMAE, we do not reconstruct those masked regions.

      5    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduce ARVideo for self-supervised video representation learning, inspired by
534   the autoregressive principles of GPT in natural language processing. Diverging from conventional
535   methods, our approach innovatively uses video token clusters as the element for autoregressive
536   prediction, significantly reducing computational demands while still managing to capture essential
537   spatial-temporal dynamics. This advancement improves the efficiency of video data processing and
538   sets a new paradigm for self-supervised video representation learning. The promising results obtained
539   from ARVideo underscore its potential and advocate for further exploration and development of
      autoregressive pretraining methods within the video domain.
