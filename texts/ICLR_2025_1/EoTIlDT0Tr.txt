                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            Detecting deepfakes (i.e., AI-generated content with malicious intent) has become
012            an important task. Most existing detection methods provide only real/fake predic-
013            tions without offering human-comprehensible explanations. Recent studies lever-
014            aging multimodal large-language models (MLLMs) for deepfake detection have
015            shown improvements in explainability. However, the performance of pre-trained
016            MLLMs (e.g., LLaVA) remains limited due to a lack of understanding of their ca-
017            pabilities for this task and strategies to enhance them. In this work, we empirically
018
               assess the strengths and weaknesses of MLLMs specifically in deepfake detection
               via forgery-related feature analysis. Building on these assessments, we propose
019
               a novel framework called X 2 -DFD, consisting of three core modules. The first
020
               module, Model Feature Assessment (MFA), measures the detection capabilities
021            of forgery-related features intrinsic to MLLMs, and gives a descending ranking of
022            these features. The second module, Strong Feature Strengthening (SFS), enhances
023            the detection and explanation capabilities by fine-tuning the MLLM on a dataset
024            constructed based on the top-ranked features. The third module, Weak Feature
025            Supplementing (WFS), improves the fine-tuned MLLM’s capabilities on lower-
026            ranked features by integrating external dedicated deepfake detectors. To verify
027            the effectiveness of this framework, we further present a practical implementa-
028            tion, where an automated forger-related feature generation, evaluation, and rank-
029
               ing procedure is designed for MFA module; an automated generation procedure of
               the fine-tuning dataset containing real and fake images with explanations based on
030
               top-ranked features is developed for SFS model; an external conventional deep-
031
               fake detector focusing on blending artifact, which corresponds to a low detection
032            capability in the pre-trained MLLM, is integrated for WFS module. Experimental
033            results show that the proposed implementation enhances overall detection perfor-
034            mance compared to pre-trained MLLMs, while providing more convincing ex-
035            planations. More encouragingly, our framework is designed to be plug-and-play,
036            allowing it to seamlessly integrate with more advanced MLLMs and external de-
037            tectors, leading to continual improvement and extension to face the challenges of
038            rapidly evolving deepfake technologies.

      data distributions. Despite improvements in generalization performance, these methods typically
      only output a probability indicating whether a given input is AI-generated, without providing intu-
051
      itive and convincing explanations behind the prediction. This lack of reliable explanations confuses

078   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have shown remarkable potential in many research
079   areas (Yin et al., 2023). Given their advanced vision-language integration capabilities, MLLMs
080   hold promise for addressing the explainability gap. A few recent efforts (Jia et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
081   2024) have explored leveraging pre-trained MLLMs to obtain explainability for deepfake detection.
082   However, our preliminary studies reveal limitations in pre-trained MLLMs, primarily due to an
083   insufficient understanding of their capabilities specific to deepfake detection and a lack of effective
084
      strategies to enhance their performance. Specifically, we investigate the discrimination of several
      forgery-related features (e.g., blending, lighting) in the pre-trained MLLM (e.g., LLaVA), and find
085
      significant differences. As shown in Fig. 1, we see that some features exhibit strong discriminative
086
      capability for deepfake detection, while others do not. This discrepancy may explain the limited
087   detection performance of the pre-trained MLLM, as well as its unreasonable explanations.
088
089   Inspired by the above investigation, we propose X 2 -DFD, a novel framework that utilizes MLLMs
090   for eX plainable and eX tendable DeepFake Detection. The proposed X 2 -DFD operates through
091   three core modules. First, the Model Feature Assessment (MFA) Module aims to assess the intrinsic
092
      capability of the pre-trained MLLMs in deepfake detection. We provide a quantified assessment of
      the discriminative capability for detection of each forgery-related feature, leading to a descending
093
      ranking of all candidate features. Second, the Strong Feature Strengthening (SFS) Module aims to
094
      improve the overall detection performance of the model by fully leveraging strong features (i.e., top-
095   ranked intrinsic capabilities) for model fine-tuning. Third, the Weak Feature Supplementing (WFS)
096   Module aims to supplement the weak intrinsic capabilities of the model by leveraging the strength
097   of external dedicated detectors (EDDs) for weak features (i.e., low-ranked intrinsic capabilities).
098   Encouragingly, the modular-based design of the proposed X 2 -DFD framework enables seamless
099   integration with future MLLMs and EDDs as their capabilities evolve.
100
      Our main contributions are threefold. 1) Studying the intrinsic capabilities of MLLMs for deepfake
101
      detection: To our knowledge, we are the first to systematically assess the inherent capabilities of
102   MLLMs specifically in deepfake detection. We reveal that MLLMs have varying discriminating
103   capabilities on different forgery features. 2) Enhancing MLLMs’ explainability through designed
104   fine-tuning: Based on the identified strengths of MLLMs, we fine-tune them to generate explanations
105   grounded in their most “familiar” forgery features and abandon those they “unfamiliar” with, thereby
106   improving their ability to accurately detect and convincingly explain deepfakes. 3) For areas where
107   MLLMs show limitations, we integrate EDDs to supplement the model’s weakness. This allows us
      to leverage the strength of both MLLMs and EDDs for a better detection system.

      Conventional Deepfake Detection Early detection methods typically focus on performing feature
111
      engineering to mine a manual feature such as eye blinking frequency (Li et al., 2018), warping ar-
112   tifacts (Li, 2018), headpose (Yang et al., 2019), and etc. Recent conventional deepfake detectors
113   mainly focus on dealing with the issue of generalization (Yan et al., 2023d), where the distribution
114   of training and testing data varies. Until now, there have developed novel solutions from different di-
115   rections: constructing pseudo-fake samples to capture the blending clues (Li, 2018; Li et al., 2020a;
116   Shiohara et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021b), learning spatial-frequency anomalies (Gu et al., 2022; Liu
117   et al., 2021a; Luo et al., 2021a; Qian et al., 2020a), focusing on the ID inconsistency clues between
118   fake and corresponding real (Dong et al., 2023), performing disentanglement learning to learn the
119   forgery-related features (Yan et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2021), performing reconstruction learning
120
      to learn the general forgery clues (Cao et al., 2022b; Wang & Deng, 2021), locating the spatial-
      temporal inconsistency (Haliassos et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2021a; Yan et al.,
121
      2024b), and etc. Most of these methods improve the generalization ability compared to the early
122
      detection methods. However, these methods can provide only real/fake predictions without giving
123   detailed explanations behind the predictions. The lack of convincing and human-comprehensible
124   explanations might confuse users about why it is deemed fake.
125
126
      Deepfake Detection via Multimodal Large Language Model Vision and language are the two
      important signals for human perception, and visual-language multimodal learning has thus drawn a
127
      lot of attention in the AI community. Recently, the LLaVA series (Liu et al., 2023b; 2024; 2023a)
128
      have explored a simple and effective approach for visual-language multimodal modeling. In the field
129   of deepfake detection, (Jia et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024) have investigated the potential of prompt
130   engineering in face forgery analysis and proposed that existing MLLMs show better explainabil-
131   ity than previous conventional deepfake detectors. In addition, Li et al. (2024b) probe MLLMs
132   for explainable fake image detection by presenting a labeled multimodal database for fine-tuning.
133   More recently, (Zhang et al., 2024) proposed using pairs of human-generated visual questions an-
134   swering (VQA) to construct the fine-tuning dataset, but manually creating detailed annotations can
135   be costly. Another just-released work (Huang et al., 2024) proposes an automatic approach using
136   GPT-4o(Achiam et al., 2023) to generate annotations and train MLLM with the resulting VQA pairs.
137
      However, a new critical question was then raised: Can MLLMs (e.g., LLaVa) fully comprehend the
      fake clues identified by GPT-4o? It is reasonable to believe that there remains a capability gap be-
138
      tween LLaVa and GPT-4o. For this reason, we find that existing works lacking in understanding the
139
      limitations of capability and then find ways to enhance the strengths and augment the limitations.

196
      Step 1: Question Generation. For each candidate forgery-related feature, we formulate a corre-
197   sponding interrogative statement. For instance, the feature “blurry” is transformed into the question
198   “Is the image blurry?”. Recognizing that the candidate features are not pre-specified by developers,
199   we employ a Large Language Model (LLM), i.e., GPT-4o, to automatically generate a comprehen-
200   sive list of Nq questions, denoted as Qki for k ∈ {1, . . . , Nq }. These questions target key forgery
201   indicators, including but not limited to lighting anomalies, unnatural facial expressions, and mis-

206   lation to the question. These responses are aggregated into a confusion matrix for each question,
207   thereby quantifying the detection capability of the associated forgery-related features. Mathemati-
208   cally, for each question Qki and image xj , the MLLM produces:

311
      evaluation, and ranking. The methodology for each of these steps has been explained in detail in the
312   previous section. For more information, please refer to Sec. 3.3. These obtained questions are ranked
313   in descending order based on their accuracy scores, thereby prioritizing forgery-related features that
314   most effectively discriminate between real and fake images. After ranking the generated questions
315   by LLMs, we conduct human verification to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the fake features.
316   Note that most questions are generated well without any obvious errors or irrelevant information.

318   4.2.2      I MPLEMENTATION OF SFS M ODULE
319
320
      The SFS module fine-tunes the MLLM by strengthening its ability to detect features that have been
      identified as high-performing in the MFA module. This process consists of three key steps:
321
322   Step 1: Real/Fake Prompts Generation. Leveraging the strong features from the MFA module, we
323   generate specialized prompts to guide the MLLM’s focus during the fine-tuning phase. Specifically,
      we first utilize GPT-4o to summarize these strong features and construct two distinct prompts: one


                                                                                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      tailored for real images (Preal ) and another for fake images (Pfake ). These prompts are formulated
325   as: Preal = f (Freal ), Pfake = f (Ffake ), where Freal and Ffake denote the sets of strong features
326   relevant to real and fake images, respectively. Also, f represents any LLMs. Here, we employ
327   GPT-4o for implementation.
328
      Step 2: Fine-tuning Dataset Construction. A fine-tuning dataset Df t comprising VQA-style
329
      (visual question answering) pairs, which is constructed by pairing each image with the correspond-
330
      ing (real or fake) prompt. Each image is annotated with the specific features it exhibits, and the
331   standardized prompt Pfixed is defined as: Pfixed = “Is this image real or fake?” The model’s re-
332   sponse is structured to begin with a definitive statement—“This image is real/fake”—followed
333   by an explanation based on the identified features. Formally, the final answer is represented as:
334   Afinal = “This image is real/fake” + Areal/fake . Consequently, each VQA-style pair of the fine-tuning
335   dataset Df t is formalized as: VQA = (Image, Pfixed , Afinal ).
336
      Step 3: MLLM Fine-tuning. The initial MLLM is fine-tuned using Df t . The fine-tuning process
337   involves adjusting the projector to accurately associate image artifacts with the corresponding fake
338   labels. Additionally, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is employed to selectively
339   fine-tune a subset of the model’s parameters, thereby focusing the model’s reasoning on deepfake-
340   specific features while maintaining overall model integrity. The fine-tuning process can be denoted
341                  Df t
      as: Mbase −−→ Mfine-tuned , where Mfine-tuned is the enhanced MLLM with improved deepfake de-
342   tection capabilities.

362   4.2.3     I MPLEMENTATION OF WFS M ODULES
363
364   The WFS module enhances the MLLM by integrating external deepfake detectors, which are spe-
365   cialized in detecting features where the MLLM shows weakness. The overall pipeline after adding
366   the WFS module is illustrated in Fig. 5. This module follows three steps:
367   Step 1: External Detector Invocation. For features that the MLLM identifies as weak, we deploy
368   an external specialized deepfake detector (e.g., a blending-based detector (Lin et al., 2024)). This
369   external detector processes the input image and generates a prediction. Note that we also employ
370   other EDDs for implementation, and we provide an in-depth analysis for this in Sec. A.2. Specif-
371   ically, when utilizing a blending detector as an instance of EDD, a blending score s is produced:
372   s = σ(BlendDetector(x)), where x denotes the input image, and σ denotes the sigmoid function
373
      that transforms the logits output of the BlendDetector into the 0-1 range.
374
      Step 2: Integration of External Detection Results into the Fine-tuning Dataset.
375   The blending score s obtained from the external detector is incorporated into the fine-tuning dataset
376   by appending it to the existing prompts. This is done by adding a statement such as: “By the obser-
377   vation of the blending expert, blending score: s” Additionally, based on the score, a corresponding
      response aligned with the probability is included, as shown in Fig. 5, specifically in the Fine-tuning


                                                                                     7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Dataset Construction section of the SFS. This integration ensures that the MLLM benefits from both
379   its intrinsic detection capabilities and the specialized insights provided by the EDD.
380   Step 3: Integration of External Detection Results into Inference Prompts During inference,
381   the EDD’s results are integrated into the MLLM’s prompt-based reasoning process. The detailed
382   description and formulation can be seen in Sec. 4.2.4 below.
383
384   4.2.4 I MPLEMENTATION OF MI M ODULE
385   Generally, during the inference, the external detector’s blending score s is incorporated into
386   the MLLM’s prompt-based reasoning process. Specifically, the final output of the model
387   is structured, to begin with a definitive statement—“This image is real/fake”—followed
388
      by reasoning based on identified visual features.           Based on the blending score s, the
      model appends a descriptive statement: Afinal = “This image is real/fake” + Areal/fake +
389
      “ And this image contains obvious/minimal blending artifacts.” The model acquires this response
390
      pattern through training. This approach ensures that the MLLM effectively leverages EDDs to en-
391   hance its detection performance, particularly for features where it initially demonstrated weakness.

521   6      C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we propose X 2 -DFD, a novel framework that harnesses the power of Multimodal
524   Large Language Models (MLLMs) for explainable and extendable deepfake detection. For the first
525   time, we systematically evaluate the intrinsic capabilities of the pre-trained MLLMs, revealing their
526   varying effectiveness across different forgery-related features. Inspired by this, we implement a tar-
527
      geted fine-tuning strategy, which has largely improved the explainability of the MLLMs, specifically
      capitalizing on their strengths. Furthermore, by integrating external deepfake detectors (EDDs), we
528
      design a novel framework to combine the complementary advantages of both MLLMs and con-
529
      ventional detectors for better detection and explanation. In the future, we plan to implement our
530   framework in an automated, iterative system that will enable continuous updates based on collected
531   feedback. We hope our work can inspire future advancements in leveraging MLLMs for a better
532   deepfake detection system.

807
808   In addition to the used blending model Lin et al. (2024), we also try other instances to implement the
809   EDDs in the WCS module of our framework, each targeting specific types of artifacts, where SRI
      focusing on the generative artifacts by deep nets, F3-Net focusing on the frequency-level anomalies,


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      and SBI and CFDA focusing on the blending boundaries. Based on these empirical attempts, We
811   summarize the general criteria under which conditions the selected EDD instance can be used in
812   our framework. Specifically, the integrated EDD instance should meet the following criteria:
813
814                          • Criteria-1: Each EDD instance should focus on only one type of feature that is positively
815                            correlated with fake;
816                          • Criteria-2: The score given by the EDD instance can accurately reflect the characteristics
817                            of the corresponding feature;
818                          • Criteria-3: The data distribution of this feature in the dataset is relatively uniform.
819
820   Below, we show a detailed illustration of using other EDD instances for implementation one by one.
821
      AIGC Expert Integration. We first consider implementing an AIGC expert to learn the deep gener-
822
      ative artifacts. For implementation, we introduce the SRI model, based on self-reconstruction images
823
      generated by Simswap (Chen et al., 2020) and train on the Xception model, designed to capture self-
824   reconstruction generative features. However, from Fig. 8, integrating this model into our framework
825   results in only a minor performance improvement of 0.3%. Further analysis reveals a negative corre-
826   lation between the model’s features and fake labels in the training set (do NOT meet the Criteria-1),
827   indicating that these artifacts are poorly represented in the training data. Consequently, the model
828   struggles to leverage the expert-provided features effectively, offering limited benefits over not using
829   the expert model.
830   Frequency Expert Integration. We then integrate a frequency-based model F3-Net and train it on
831   the FF++ dataset (Rossler et al., 2019) to capture frequency anomalies. However, from Fig. 8, the
832   overall model’s performance is identical to that of the expert, with no improvement. Although the
833   expert features are positively correlated with fake labels, the frequency-based scores are overfitted
834   to the training set and do not accurately reflect the true feature quantity, with only near-1 (1 for fake)
835   and near-0 (0 for real) predictions (do NOT meet the Criteria-2) This leads to a shortcut, where
836   the model relies solely on the expert’s output without learning from the feature information, thus
837   limiting the extendability of the integrated model.

       A.8    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1113
       We use the LLava model and a 4090 GPU, with image cropping following the method from Deep-
1114   fakeBench. AUC is calculated by directly obtaining the token probabilities. Previous AUC calcu-
1115   lations for large models mostly relied on averaging methods, such as in (?), but this approach is
1116   not very accurate because: (1) multiple samplings are needed to approximate the true probability
1117   distribution, and (2) large models inherently perform inference with a default temperature, which
1118   itself involves sampling over probabilities. Averaging over multiple samples effectively results in a
1119   second layer of sampling, making the evaluation less accurate. Therefore, in this paper, we calculate
1120   AUC by directly obtaining token probabilities.
