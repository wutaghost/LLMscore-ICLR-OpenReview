010                                                                                   A BSTRACT
011                 As visual generation technologies continue to advance, the scale of video datasets
012                 has expanded rapidly, and the quality of these datasets is critical to the perfor-
013                 mance of video generation models. We argue that temporal splitting, detailed
014                 captions, and video quality filtering are three key factors that determine dataset
015                 quality. However, existing datasets exhibit various limitations in these areas. To
016                 address these challenges, we introduce Kinda-45M , a large-scale, high-quality
017                 video dataset featuring accurate temporal splitting, detailed captions, and superior
018                 video quality. The core of our approach lies in improving the consistency between
019
                    fine-grained conditions and video content. Specifically, we employ a linear clas-
                    sifier on probability distributions to enhance the accuracy of transition detection,
020
                    ensuring better temporal consistency. We then provide structured captions for the
021
                    segmented videos, with an average length of 200 words, to improve text-video
022                 alignment. Additionally, we develop a Video Training Suitability Score (VTSS)
023                 that integrates multiple sub-metrics, allowing us to filter high-quality videos from
024                 the original corpus. Finally, we incorporate several metrics into the training pro-
025                 cess of the generation model, further refining the fine-grained conditions. Our
026                 experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our data processing pipeline and the
027                 quality of the proposed Kinda-45M dataset.

      1    INTRODUCTION
055
056   Generative AI, particularly video generation tasks, has recently garnered significant interest from
057   researchers. These tasks involve generating high-quality videos from textual descriptions or images.
058   A critical factor in the success of these models is the quality of the datasets used for training. Several
059   open-source datasets (e.g. Panda-70M(Chen et al., 2024b), MiraData (Ju et al., 2024), OpenVid (Nan
060   et al., 2024), and VidGen (Tan et al., 2024)) have been introduced, each carefully selecting data
061   sources and applying various evaluation metrics for video filtering. Moreover, innovative approaches
062
      have been employed in the video captioning process, such as the multi-modal caption model (Chen
      et al., 2024b) or structured captions (Ju et al., 2024).
063
064   Despite the success of the data processing pipelines introduced by previous datasets, we argue that
065   the core challenge lies in establishing accurate and fine-grained conditioning for video data, which
066   is crucial for both reducing the complexity of the training process and improving the quality of the
067   generated outputs. To achieve this, we believe there are three key issues that need to be addressed:
068   First, the alignment between text and video semantics is essential. Unlike video question answer-
069   ing tasks, where captions are primarily driven by specific question-based details, video generation
070   requires captions that are directly tied to the visual content itself. Due to the infinite granularity of
071   visual signals, this necessitates captions that are rich and detailed. Furthermore, raw video data often
072   contains complex transitions, adding additional challenges in ensuring the accuracy of captions.
073   Second, the effective evaluation and filtering of low-quality data remains underexplored. Low-
074   quality video data, such as poor visual quality or excessive artificial effects, can impede the training
075   process. However, accurately assessing and filtering such data presents an ongoing challenge. Ex-
076   isting methods typically rely on manually selected quality metrics and heuristic threshold-based
077   filtering, which are often designed for other tasks and may not align with the specific requirements
078   of video generation. As a result, these approaches may not effectively ensure the desired data quality
079   for training.
080   Third, even with data filtering processes in place, the videos within the dataset still vary in quality,
081   with each video potentially exhibiting different strengths and weaknesses (e.g., one video may have
082   lower clarity but better aesthetic appeal). Training with such heterogeneous data in the same manner
083   may introduce ambiguity for the model, hindering its ability to learn effectively.
084   To address these issues, we present Kinda-45M , a large-scale high-quality video dataset with more
085   accurate video splitting, detailed captions, better data filtering methods and metric conditions. As
086   video content reaches considerable quality, the consistency between fine-grained conditions and
087   video content determines the performance of generation models. We propose a more refined data
088   processing pipeline based on this key insight. Since accurate video splitting leads to better temporal
089   consistency, we first employ a linear classifier on probability distributions to enhance the accuracy
090   of transition detection. Then We generate structured captions for the segmented video clips, with an
091   average length of 200 words, to improve text-video alignment. Sequentially, to prevent the erroneous
092
      deletion of high-quality data during filtering, we train a network to predict Video Training Suitability
      Score (VTSS) on human-aligned datasets to model the joint distribution of sub-metrics. This network
093
      takes videos and sub-metrics as input, and outputs a single value called Video Training Suitability
094
      Score as the only metric to filter data. Additionally, we introduce data metrics as extra conditions
095   (Metric Conditions) into the generation model during training, helping model distinguish data with
096   different quality and further improving the consistency between fine-grained conditions and video
097   content, which results in better performance and controllability of the generation model.
098
      To further validate Kinda-45M and our data processing pipeline, we train video generation models
099
      on different datasets. Both the dataset benchmark and the performance of the video generation
100
      model demonstrate the advantage of the Kinda-45M dataset. We perform more ablation studies to
101   demonstrate effectiveness of our data processing pipeline.
102
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
103
104          • We present a large-scale high-quality dataset called Kinda-45M , with accurate video split-
105            ting, detailed captions and higher-quality video content.
106          • We propose a refined data processing pipeline to further improve the consistency between
107            fine-grained conditions and video content, including transition detection methods, struc-
               tured caption system, Video Training Suitability Score and metric conditions.

      2     RELATED WORK
114
      Recent advancements in diffusion models have driven the evolution of image generation models
115
      into video generation models. In the field of text-to-video (T2V) generation, significant efforts have
116
      been made to develop large-scale T2V models, trained on extensive datasets using traditional U-Net-
117   based diffusion architectures (Zeng et al., 2024; Clark & Jaini, 2024; Ge et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023;
118   Khachatryan et al., 2023) and Transformer-based (DiT) architectures (Ma et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
119   2023b; Lu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Xing et al., 2024). The success of these video generation
120   models heavily depends on the quality of the video-text datasets.
121
122   2.1   V IDEO DATASETS
123
      While several video datasets (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015; Anne Hendricks et al., 2017; Rohrbach
124
      et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2023b; Sanabria et al., 2018; Wang
125
      et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023a) have been applied to tasks such as action recognition, video un-
126   derstanding, visual question answering (VQA), and video retrieval, there remains an urgent need for
127   a high-quality, open-source dataset specifically tailored for training video generation models, pro-
128   viding rich video-text pairs. Datasets such as YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2018), VATEX (Wang et al.,
129   2019), and ActivityNet (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015) offer high-quality human caption annotations.
130   Another set of datasets, including Miradata (Ju et al., 2024), VidGen-1M (Tan et al., 2024), and
131   OpenVid-1M (Nan et al., 2024), automatically generate high-quality captions and filter data using
132   manually selected thresholds on multiple dataset metrics.
133   However, these datasets are insufficient in size to support the training of large models. Datasets, in-
134   cluding YT-Temporal-180M (Zellers et al., 2021), HD-VILA-100M (Xue et al., 2022), ACAV (Lee
135   et al., 2021), etc., contain hundreds of millions of video-text pairs, but their captions are automati-
136   cally generated via speech recognition, leading to subpar quality. Panda70M (Chen et al., 2024b),
137   the largest publicly accessible video-text dataset, has become a popular choice for video genera-
138   tion due to its scale and considerable quality. However, its quality still needs further improvement.
139   Specifically, the captions in Panda-70M often provide simplistic, incomplete descriptions of video
140
      content, and the frequent transitions in the training videos can result in semantic inconsistencies,
      potentially leading to undesired or uncertain transitions in the generated videos.

194   4     METHOD
195
196   As shown in Fig. 3, we propose a refined data processing pipeline for Kinda-45M dataset. Our
197
      pipeline aims to further improve the consistency between fine-grained conditions and video content.
      Our main contributions are shown in the red box of Fig. 3. Specifically, we start from the same raw
198
      data with Panda-70M (Chen et al., 2024b) dataset. First, we propose a more accurate and efficient
199
      transition detection method for video splitting in section 4.1. Then we caption splitted videos with
200   an average length of 200 words based on our structured caption system in section 4.2. Subsequently,
201   we train a Video Training Suitability Score (VTSS) for data filtering to prevent high-quality data
202   from the erroneous deletion in section 4.3. Finally, we introduce multiple data sub-metrics as Metric
203   Conditions into the generation model to enrich the fine-grained conditions in section 4.4.
204
205   4.1   V IDEO SPLITTING
206   Splitting videos into temporal segments is crucial for creating video generation datasets. Transition-
207   free video data enable more accurate alignment between text and video, while reducing the difficulty
208   of model training and improving the temporal consistency of generated results. Current video split-
209   ting methods (Castellano) typically detect transitions based on changes in image features between
210   consecutive frames, relying on manually adjusted thresholds as criteria, but often overlook tempo-
211   ral information. As a result, these methods struggle to distinguish between gradual transitions and
212
      fast-motion scenes, leading to missed detections in the former and incorrect detections in the latter.
213   To address the above issues, we first propose a Color-Struct SVM (CSS) module that adopting
214   a learning-based approach for more accurate detection of changes between frames compared to
215   threshold-basd method. Then we leverage temporal smoothing and statistical features to differentiate
      between gradual transitions and fast-motion scenes.

293   4.3         DATA FILTERING
294
295   In the large-scale raw dataset, the quality of video content vary significantly. When the performance
296   of the generation model is built upon videos with considerable content quality, it is necessary and
297   crucial to filter out low-quality data and remain high-quality data accurately. Traditional methods
298
      often use various sub-metrics to evaluate video quality and then manually set thresholds to filter
      the desired data. Since these sub-metrics are not completely orthogonal with each other, the video
299
      quality is actually a joint distribution of all sub-metrics, which means these thresholds should have
300
      implicit constraints with each other. However, existing methods neglect the joint distribution of sub-
301   metrics, resulting in inaccurate thresholds. Meanwhile, since multiple thresholds need to be set, the
302   cumulative effect of inaccurate threshold lead to larger deviations during filtering. Therefore, not
303   only low-quality videos are not correctly filtered out as shown in Fig. 1, but also high-quality videos
304   are mistakenly deleted as shown in Fig. 5.

      4.3.1                       N EW CRITERIA AND HUMAN EVALUATION
320
321   We have defined a new annotation criterion that assigns a score reflecting whether a video is suit-
322   able as training data for video generation models. This criterion primarily considers the following
323   aspects of video quality: Dynamic Quality: A high-quality video should exhibit good dynamics,
      which are evaluated based on two factors: the extent of subject movement and the temporal stability

324
      of the motion. The motion area in the video should cover more than 30% of the frame; otherwise, the
325   score of the video will be decreased for insufficient dynamics. Temporal stability considers the cam-
326   era movement; non-professional videographers often produce videos with irregular and significant
327   shaking. We decrease the scores of such videos to distinguish them from professional works. Static
328   Quality: Each frame of a high-quality video should have rich subject details, reasonable compo-
329   sition, aesthetic appeal, clear and distinct subjects, and saturated colors. Although this metric may
330   involve some subjectivity, it is crucial for assessing the overall visual quality. Video Naturalness:
331   We prefer videos that are natural and unprocessed. Special effects, transitions, subtitles, and logos
332   can introduce biases in the video’s original distribution, making it harder for generation models to
333
      learn. Additionally, we consider the safety of the video content, rejecting videos with political,
      terrorist, violent, pornographic, gory, or otherwise disturbing content. In order to reduce the bias
334
      between the labeled scores and the true scores, each video is labeled by 8 experts and subjected to a

528   6          C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we present a large-scale high-quality dataset called Kinda-45M , with accurate video
531   splitting, detailed captions and higher quality video content. Kinda-45M dataset is currently the only
532   video dataset that simultaneously possesses a large number of videos (over 10M) and high-quality
533   fine-grained text captions (longer than 200 words), significantly improving the quality of large scale
534   video datasets. Additionally, we propose a refined data processing pipeline to further improve the
535   consistency between fine-grained conditions and video content, including better transition detection
536
      method, structured caption system, and data filtering method and fine-grained conditioning.
537   Limitations. Despite all the strength above, Kinda-45M is still insufficient to support the training
538   of an extremely large video generation model with over 1B parameters. A larger-scale datasets need
539   to be further collected and processed. Meanwhile, the performance, generalization, and scaling laws
      of generation models on high-quality datasets need further exploration.

743   A.2   E LIMINATION OF D EVIATIONS BETWEEN T RUE S CORES AND L ABELED S CORES

745   After establishing the criteria, we randomly sample a batch of data and have it annotated by trained
746   experts, with each video being scored by eight experts on a scale of 1 to 5. To ensure that the anno-
747
      tations closely reflect the true suitability scores, we need to address two types of errors: Individual
      Preference Bias: As shown in the Fig. 12, we visualize the violin plots of scores given by different
748
      experts. The expert on the left tends to give lower scores, while the expert on the right tends to give
749
      higher scores. These individual preferences can cause the final scores of some videos to be lower or
750   higher than their actual values. Therefore, we standardize the scores of each expert and then scaled
751   them using the mean and variance of the overall scores to eliminate the bias introduced by different
752   experts. From the figure, it can be seen that the scores processed through our normalization and
753   rescaling methods align more closely with the overall score distribution. Label Fluctuation Bias:
754   As shown in the Fig. 12, each video is annotated by eight experts, and different experts may assign
755   different scores due to varying interpretations of the criteria. This leads to label fluctuations. We use
      the mean score to reduce the error caused by these fluctuations.
