011                                              Abstract
012
013            Prior work has found that training very small language models (SLMs) on
014            synthetic children’s stories allows them to generate coherent text, compa-
015
               rable to much larger models. These stories are claimed to encompass the
               vocabulary and factual knowledge base of a 3-4-year-old child, capturing
016
               the “essence of natural language.” Because of these claims, it is tempting
017
               to attribute the findings to the high readability (i.e., simple language) of
018            children’s stories, drawing a parallel to how children learn language. Is the
019            human concept of readability relevant in the context of language model
020            training, or are these findings better explained by other properties of the
021            data? In this study, we investigate this by first validating several auto-
022            matic readability measures. We then create synthetic corpora with varying
023            levels of readability and assess the coherence of text generated by SLMs
024            trained on these corpora. We find that training on high readability text is
025            not a prerequisite for coherent SLMs. Specifically, SLMs trained on data
026
               with substantially more complex language also exhibit the same abilities as
               those trained on simple language. Moreover, training on simple language
027
               does not lead to the earlier development of coherence during training.
028

030   1    Introduction
031
032   How small can language models (LMs) be and still speak coherent English? Eldan & Li
033   (2023) find that transformer-based LMs produce coherent text with fewer than 10 million
034   parameters when trained on TinyStories, a synthetic dataset of children’s stories that uses
035   words that most 3 to 4-year-old children typically understand. The claim is that such a
036   dataset retains the essential elements of natural language—grammar, vocabulary, facts, and
037   reasoning—while being smaller and more focused. However, by focusing solely on children’s
038
      stories, it remains unclear whether these findings are due to the simple, easy-to-understand
      language (high readability) of the data, or other properties like its synthetic origin or limited
039
      diversity. 1
040
041   Readability refers to how easily a text can be read and understood. High readability is a
042   fundamental characteristic of children’s stories, as their intended audience typically lacks
043   the language skills needed to understand more complex texts. Rare vocabulary, complex
044
      grammatical structures, and abstract concepts can all reduce readability and hinder the
      learning process. Given that educators use readability assessments to align instructional
045
      material with the skill levels of their students, it is intriguing to consider that LMs might
046
      also benefit from a curriculum-based approach to learning language. Indeed, the findings of
047   Eldan & Li (2023) could be interpreted as preliminary evidence supporting this idea.
048
049   However, before accepting such an interpretation, we must consider an additional confound:
050
      the data generation process. TinyStories was constructed using an instruction-tuned LM
      and a template-based prompt, with minimal variations among the different instances of
051
      the template. While the variations may prevent the LM from generating nearly identical
052

      stories, the stories might still share redundant features. For example, they could all start
055   with “Once upon a time...” or follow a limited set of story templates where the details
056   differ but the overall structure and connective elements remain similar. In other words, the
057   resulting dataset may have low diversity, which in turn leads to low information content,
058   making it well-suited for an SLM to learn effectively.
059
      This alternative explanation may seem obvious to some readers. After all, LMs trained with
060
      maximum likelihood estimation on a homogeneous corpus will generate text that closely re-
061   semble that corpus. This phenomenon was even demonstrated with small character-level
062   RNNs by Karpathy (2015). However, the research community remains interested in devel-
063   opmentally inspired LM training, as evidenced by the BabyLM challenge (Warstadt et al.,
064   2023), which provides a pretraining corpus inspired by the language input received by chil-
065   dren, including child-directed speech and children’s literature. Additionally, citations of
066   Eldan & Li (2023) suggest that the community is open to the possibility that the simple
067   language properties of TinyStories aid the development of coherent SLMs (Yu et al., 2023;
068   Frank, 2023; Edman & Bylinina, 2023; Muckatira et al., 2024; Haga et al., 2024; Feng et al.,
069
      2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Nguyen, 2024). For example, Haga et al. (2024) write “Recent
      studies have shown the benefits of mimicking human language acquisition. For instance, us-
070
      ing child-oriented vocabulary and/or child-directed speech (CDS) as learning data improves
071
      learning efficiency.”
072
073   Readers might also argue that the findings of Eldan & Li (2023) are unsurprising, given
074   the well-documented effects of knowledge distillation (KD). Hinton et al. (2015) show that
075
      training a student model S on the real-valued outputs of a teacher model T is more effective
      than training S on the original data C used to train T . Informally, minimizing DKL (T ∥ S)
076
      is more effective than minimizing DKL (C ∥ S). Indeed, the procedure of Eldan & Li (2023)
077
      is a special case of KD. In the limit, sampling from T to create a synthetic corpus recovers
078   T itself. Thus, training S on this synthetic corpus is equivalent to minimizing DKL (T ∥ S).
079   However, Eldan & Li (2023) focus on a particular form of distillation: generating short
080   stories where the capacity of T and the size of C are many orders of magnitude larger than
081   S and the synthetic data sampled from T . Given these particularities, KD might not be the
082   only factor at play. And even if we accept that KD explains the coherence of SLMs, it does
083   not tell us which properties of the synthetic corpus facilitate learning.
084   In this work, we aim to better understand the properties of training data that result in
085   coherent SLMs. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
086
087        1. Through empirical analysis, we show that the coherence of text generated by SLMs
088           trained on TinyStories is not due to its developmentally inspired properties. In
089           particular, we show SLMs trained on data with complex language also exihibit the
090           same abilities as those trained on simple language. Moreover, training on simple
091           language does not lead to earlier development of coherence during training.
092        2. We release our datasets for studying SLMs and our data generation process. Specifi-
093           cally, we illustrate how to create datasets akin to TinyStories for training coherent
094           SLMs using only open-source LMs.
095
096
           3. As a supporting contribution, we provide evidence that open-source LMs can effec-
              tively evaluate text readability, presenting a viable alternative to human annotators.
097
              This finding has pedagogical implications, as traditional readability tools used by
098
              educators often depend on basic heuristics such as word count and word length.

101   2   Experimental Design
102
103   Our primary research question is: Are the coherent generation abilities of SLMs trained on
104   TinyStories due to the data’s developmentally inspired properties, namely high readability,
105   or are other data properties more influential? If the abilities stem from high readability,
106   then at least one of the following criteria must be true: (a) the abilities only manifest when
107   trained on high readability data and not on low readability data, or (b) the emergence
      of these abilities occur earlier during training on high readability data compared to low


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      readability data. Below, we outline our experimental design to investigate our research
109   question.
110
111   Measuring Readability (Section 3) We begin by validating the readability of
112
      TinyStories. Although the data is intended to be readable for 3-4 year old children, there
      has been no prior work confirming this claim. We employ several readability measures,
113
      including classic formulas, statistics derived from constituency parse trees, and LM-based
114
      evaluations. We find that all measures suggest TinyStories exhibits high readability. Given
115   that readability is an inherently fuzzy and difficult-to-define property of text, we also assess
116   how well each measure correlates with human judgments. We find that while all measures are
117   correlated with human judgments, instructing a LM to output a score in natural language
118   (LLM-as-a-Judge) demonstrates the highest correlation.
119
      Measuring Quality (Section 4) We also consider two additional measures that serve as
120
      proxies for the concept of text quality. Specifically, we focus on coherence, as measured
121   with LLM-as-a-Judge, and perplexity, computed by state-of-the-art LMs.2 Since we lack
122   a dataset of human judgments similar to the one used for validating readability measures,
123   we validate these quality measures by examining their correlation with a ranking of open-
124   source pretrained LMs. This ranking spans from toy (e.g., pythia-70m) to state-of-the-art
125   models (e.g., Llama-3.1-70B). Although this ranking is not as ideal as human judgments,
126   it is designed to be broadly acceptable to the research community by avoiding fine-grained
127   distinctions and instead categorizing models into reasonable tiers. Our analysis reveals that
128   both coherence and perplexity correlate with the ranking, with coherence demonstrating
129
      the strongest correlation. Importantly, coherence (but not perplexity) is uncorrelated with
      readability, reinforcing the idea that these are distinct properties of text.
130
131   Data Synthesis (Section 5) We generate our synthetic dataset of stories called
132   LlamaTales-GRE, which follows a data generation process similar to TinyStories, but incor-
133   porates vocabulary found in graduate school entrance exams. We aim for LlamaTales-GRE
134   to exhibit coherence similar to TinyStories but with lower readability. These properties
135
      are confirmed using the measures discussed above. Since the data generation process and
      the LMs used to create TinyStories are not open-source, there are potential confounds
136
      when comparing TinyStories and LlamaTales-GRE. To address these, we also generate
137
      LlamaTales-Jr, our open-source replication of TinyStories.
138
139   Results (Section 6) With our datasets generated and their properties validated, we pro-
140   ceed to train a suite of SLMs with varying capacities, ranging from 262K to 33M non-
141
      embedding parameters. We find that SLMs with as few as 9M parameters can produce
      text with coherence comparable to state-of-the-art LMs when trained on any of the three
142
      datasets described above, thus failing to meet criteria (a). Furthermore, we observe that
143
      the ability to generate coherent text does not emerge earlier in the training process when
144   using high readability text, thus failing to meet criteria (b). As we failed to meet both cri-
145   teria, we conclude that the high readability of TinyStories is not the cause of the coherent
146   generation abilities of SLMs.

148   3   Measuring Readability
149
150   We begin by validating the readability of TinyStories. Although its stories are intended
151   to be readable for 3-4 year old children, no prior work confirms this claim. But how does
152   one measure readability in the first place? As described by Trott (2024), readability refers
153   to the ease, accessibility, or comprehensibility of a text. Although defining this concept
154   precisely is challenging, its implications are generally understood. For instance, Gravity’s
155   Rainbow is more difficult to read than The Very Hungry Caterpillar, irrespective of their
156
      quality. Due to the subjective nature of readability, human evaluators are often considered
      the gold standard for judgement, despite their own limitations (Clark et al., 2021). However,
157
      acquiring human judgement across a large number of documents is both expensive and time-
158
      consuming. Consequently, several automatic measures have been proposed. In the following
159   sections, we introduce various measures such as classic readability formulas (3.1), grammar

198   to estimate the appropriate US grade level for a document is the Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level
199   (FKGL) formula (Kincaid et al., 1975):

222   phrase or a word, and the branches illustrate the relationships between these units. Figure
223   1 provides an example of such a tree.

234   parse trees can differ significantly, suggesting differences in grammatical complexity. To

240   3.3   LLM-as-a-Judge
241
      Finally, we consider leveraging instruction-tuned LMs to output readability scores in natural
242
      language, a technique commonly referred to as LLM-as-a-Judge. Although this approach is
243
      relatively new and the community has not yet reached a consensus on its efficacy, it offers
244   several advantages. One of the main benefits is its fully automated nature, which allows it
245   to scale well beyond the capabilities of human evaluations. Additionally, it is highly flexible,
246   as the prompts can be adjusted to fit specific judgment criteria. However, there are also
247   notable drawbacks, such as the need for prompt engineering and performance differences
248   among models. Despite these challenges, there is a growing body of evidence suggesting
249   that LLM-as-Judge is a reasonable alternative to hiring human annotators (Liu et al., 2023;
250   Dubois et al., 2023; Sachdeva et al., 2024). For our study, we use Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct
251   to generate readability scores for 10,000 documents from the training splits of TinyStories
252
      and FineWeb. We use the prompt proposed by Trott (2024), shown in Figure A10. Our
      findings in Table 1 again suggest that TinyStories exhibits higher readability than FineWeb.

276   4     Measuring Quality
277
278   Next, we tackle the challenge of measuring text quality, a concept that is inherently sub-
279   jective and difficult to define precisely, much like readability. Despite this, we believe that
280   most readers have an intuitive understanding of what differentiates high-quality text from
281
      low-quality text. In this study, we employ two specific measures as proxies for text quality,
      acknowledging that there exist many other reasonable alternatives.
282
283   Our first measure is perplexity, computed by state-of-the-art LMs. This approach is based
284   on the assumption that these LMs have learned a reasonable distribution over language. In
285   theory, well-formed text should yield lower perplexity scores, while nonsensical or poorly
286   constructed text should result in higher perplexity. However, we recognize that this method
287
      has limitations. For instance, it is possible for text that humans perceive as low quality to
      be assigned low perplexity, particularly if the text aligns with patterns frequently observed
288
      in the LM’s training data.
289
290   Our second measure employs LLM-as-a-Judge to assess coherence. The specific prompt is
291   presented in Figure A11. While text quality encompasses multiple dimensions, including
292   clarity and fluency, we focus on coherence to align with the titular question of Eldan & Li
293
      (2023). We also note that Eldan & Li (2023) uses LLM-as-a-Judge (GPT-4) to evaluate
      their SLMs but with a different prompt. As LLM-as-a-Judge was covered in Section 3.3, we
294
      refrain from further elaboration.
295
296
297   4.1   Correlation with Readability
298
      One of our running assumptions is that readability and text quality are orthogonal prop-
299
      erties. Therefore, it is desirable for our measure of text quality to be uncorrelated with
300
      readability. To check this, we compute perplexity and coherence for the CLEAR dataset (Sec-
301   tion 3.4). The results, presented in Figure A32, indicate that coherence is uncorrelated with
302   both human and LM judgments of readability. In contrast, perplexity shows some correla-
303   tion with readability. Consequently, we primarily focus on coherence as our measure of text
304   quality for the remainder of this study, while also reporting perplexity where relevant.
305
306   4.2   Correlation with a Ranking of LMs
307
308   Since we lack a dataset of human judgments specifically for text quality, we propose validat-
309   ing our quality measures against a ranking of open-source LMs. This ranking is designed to
310   be broadly acceptable by the community by categorizing models into reasonable tiers rather
311   than making fine-grained distinctions. We assign state-of-the-art LMs with 70B parameters
312   as the highest tier. The next tier includes their smaller counterparts with 7B parameters.
313   Finally, we include the Pythia suite (Biderman et al., 2023), ranking models in decreasing
314
      order based on parameter count. The ranking is detailed in Table A4.
315   Using prompts from the test splits of the datasets described in Section 5, we generate 1K
316   documents for each dataset and model in our ranking, for a total of 56K documents. We then
317   evaluate the generated text using our quality measures and present the Pearson correlation
318   coefficients in Figure A31. The results show that coherence has the highest correlation with
319   our ranking, followed by perplexity.
320   For the remainder of this study, we compute perplexity with Llama-3.1-8B, Qwen2-7B,
321   and Mistral-7B-v0.3. These models are chosen for their efficiency and their similar cor-
322   relation with our ranking compared to their larger counterparts. For coherence, we use
323   Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct because of its high correlation with our ranking and to ensure
      consistency with our readability measures.

      5   Data Synthesis
325
326
      To address our research question, we require a version of TinyStories with stories intended
327
      for a more advanced readership than children. This allows us to isolate the effects of read-
328
      ability from other characteristics of TinyStories. However, the data generation process
329   for TinyStories is not fully documented, and the data was generated using closed-source
330   models (GPT-3.5 and GPT-4). Fortunately, the authors released both the dataset and the
331   prompts used for its generation. Using this information, we reproduce TinyStories with
332   only open-source models and make our data generation process publicly available.
333
      Specifically, for each story, we prompt Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct to generate a story using
334
      the template shown in Figure A21. We uniformly select 3 words without replacement from
335   the same pool of 1,603 words used by TinyStories. These words include simple vocabulary
336   found in children’s stories such as “bed”, “dog,” and “snap”. Additionally, we select k
337   features from a pool of 6 possible features for the story where k follows a distribution
338   estimated from the prompts used to generate TinyStories. These features include elements
339   such as dialogue, a twist, or a bad ending. We instruct the generating model to incorporate
340   these words and features into the story with top-p sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020) where
341   p = 0.95. We call the resulting dataset LlamaTales-Jr, a fully open-source dataset of
342   children’s stories. LlamaTales-Jr and TinyStories exhibit similar scores when evaluated
343
      for readability, grammatical complexity, and coherence. However, LlamaTales-Jr stories are
      generally longer, contain a higher number of unique n-grams, and exhibit higher perplexity
344
      compared to TinyStories.
345
346   With our data generation process in place, we increase reading difficulty by making two
347   changes. First, we replace the pool of words from TinyStories with 2,880 words commonly
348   found in GRE study materials, such as “encomium,” “stevedore,” and “jingoism.” Second,
349
      we update the template to the one shown in Figure A22, allowing the model to generate
      stories beyond just children’s stories. We call the resulting dataset LlamaTales-GRE and
350
      confirm that it exhibits greater reading difficulty and grammatical complexity (similar to
351
      FineWeb) compared to LlamaTales-Jr, while it scores marginally higher in coherence and
352   perplexity.

356   6   Results
357
358
      Having established our datasets and evaluation measures, we proceed to train transformer
359
      LMs from scratch on the four datasets in Table 1. The LMs range from 262K to 33M
360   non-embedding parameters and share the same design as Llama-3. We train each model for
361   10 epochs (10B tokens). We also consider a number of pretrained LMs, namely the GPT-2,
362   Pythia, Mistral, Qwen-2, and Llama-3 families. We do not finetune these pretrained LMs
363   on our datasets. Details are shown in Table A2 and random generations are shown in Tables
364   A6-A20.
365

366   with p = 0.95, resulting in a total of 172K documents. To condition the generations, we
367   extract 50-token prompts from the test splits of our data. We then evaluate the generations
368   for coherence (Figure 2), perplexity (Figure A2), and readability (Figure A3). While our
369   discussion emphasizes coherence for the sake of brevity, our findings hold true for readability
370   and perplexity as well.

465   7   Discussion and Conclusion
466
467   Our findings suggest that the ability of SLMs to generate coherent text when trained on
468   TinyStories is not due to the simplicity of the language in the data. Training on a dataset
469   with more complex language, such as LlamaTales-GRE, results in similar levels of coherence.
470   Moreover, training on text with high readability does not accelerate the development of
471
      coherence during training; it may even delay it. However, the reasons for our observations
      remain unclear.
472
473   A seemingly straightforward explanation is that to develop a model with a specific property,
474   such as coherence, we should train it on data that exhibits that property. For instance, both
475   TinyStories and LlamaTales-GRE demonstrate similar levels of coherence, so it might not
476   be surprising that LMs trained on coherent text can generate coherent text. However, if we
477
      accept this explanation, we would expect models trained on FineWeb to produce text with
      coherence similar to FineWeb itself. Instead, as shown in Figure A1d, there is a substantial
478
      gap between the coherence of FineWeb and the text generated by SLMs trained on this data.
479
      This gap is not observed in TinyStories, LlamaTales-Jr, and LlamaTales-GRE. That said,
480   there are potential confounds to consider. First, the coherence of FineWeb is lower than
481   that of our other datasets. It is possible that for the SLM to learn coherence, the dataset’s
482   coherence must be above a certain threshold. This could be addressed by using LLM-as-
483   a-Judge to filter FineWeb documents to meet a certain coherence threshold. Additionally,
484   the noisy nature of web crawl data may result in a higher variance in coherence across the
485   documents of FineWeb compared to our other datasets. A low variance in a property like
      coherence might be necessary for effective learning of that property.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      We can also consider the n-gram profiles of our data to explain our findings. We specifically
487   consider the number of unique n-grams for each dataset. As shown shown in Figure A26,
488   FineWeb exhibits significantly higher n-gram diversity compared to our easy-to-learn syn-
489   thetic datasets, particularly for n > 2. Intuitively, high n-gram diversity suggests a dataset
490   with rich and varied content, making it more challenging for a SLM to learn due to exposure
491   to a broader range of linguistic structures and token combinations during training.
492
      To further analyze the n-gram profile of our data, we examine the perplexity of an n-
493   gram LM on its own training data. Unlike simply counting unique n-grams, this approach
494   incorporates frequency information. We train n-gram LMs on each of our datasets, and
495   the results, shown in Figure A30, reveal interesting patterns. For n ≤ 3, the easy-to-learn
496   datasets exhibit lower perplexity compared to FineWeb. However, this trend reverses for
497   n > 3.
498
      Why does this happen? Recall that the number of unique conditional probability tables
499   (CPTs) that form an n-gram LM is determined by the number of unique (n − 1)-grams in
500   the training data, which grows exponentially with n. For larger values like n = 4, FineWeb
501   has 413 million more CPTs than LlamaTales-GRE. This results in a dilution effect, where the
502   CPTs in FineWeb are much sparser compared to those in LlamaTales-GRE, leading to lower
503   entropy for the prior. In other words, given a 3-gram, there is less uncertainty in predicting
504   the next token for FineWeb compared to LlamaTales-GRE. Conversely, for smaller values
505   like n = 2, the number of unique CPTs remains relatively small, with FineWeb having only
506   57 thousand more than LlamaTales-GRE. This results in denser CPTs, where the higher
507
      diversity of FineWeb manifests as high-entropy CPTs. In FineWeb, there are simply many
      more possible completions given a 1-gram compared to LlamaTales-GRE.
508
509   We also observe that n-gram LM perplexities sometimes converge as we progress through a
510   dataset. Specifically, for n = 4, the perplexities of LlamaTales-GRE and FineWeb are nearly
511   identical when trained on the entire dataset. This could lead to the incorrect conclusion
512   that there are no differences between the two datasets. However, if we consider how an LM
513
      is actually trained, we observe stark differences in perplexity as training progresses. These
      differences could result in varying outcomes in terms of learnability.
514
515   In conclusion, we found that using simple language in training data is not a prerequisite for
516   coherence in SLMs. Measures like n-gram counts and n-gram LM perplexity might provide
517   a better assessment of how easily a dataset can be learned. However, it is important to
518   note that these metrics are currently only correlational, and we have not yet demonstrated
519
      a causal relationship. Future work will focus on validating these measures as indicators of
      learnability.
