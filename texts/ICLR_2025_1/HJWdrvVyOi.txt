009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training logistic regression over encrypted data has been a compelling approach in
012            addressing security concerns for several years. In this paper, we introduce an effi-
013            cient gradient variant, called quadratic gradient, for privacy-preserving logistic
014            regression training. We enhance Nesterov’s Accelerated Gradient (NAG), Adap-
015            tive Gradient Algorithm (Adagrad) and Adam algorithms by incorporating their
016            quadratic gradients and evaluate these improved algorithms on various datasets.
017            Experimental results demonstrate that the enhanced algorithms achieve signifi-
018            cantly improved convergence speed compared to traditional first-order gradient
019            methods. Moreover, we applied the enhanced NAG method to implement homo-
020
               morphic logistic regression training, achieving comparable results within just 4
               iterations. There is a good chance that the quadratic gradient approach could inte-
021
               grate first-order gradient descent/ascent algorithms with the second-order Newton-
022
               Raphson methods, and that it could be applied to a wide range of numerical opti-
023            mization problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Given a person’s healthcare data related to a certain disease, we can train a logistic regression (LR)
030
      model capable of telling whether or not this person is likely to develop this disease. However,
      such personal health information is highly private to individuals. The privacy concern, therefore,
031
      becomes a major obstacle for individuals to share their biomedical data. The most secure solution
032
      is to encrypt the data into ciphertexts first by Homomorphic Encryption (HE) and then securely
033   outsource the ciphertexts to the cloud, without allowing the cloud to access the data directly. iDASH
034   is an annual competition that aims to call for implementing interesting cryptographic schemes in a
035   biological context. Since 2014, iDASH has included the theme of genomics and biomedical privacy.
036   The third track of the 2017 iDASH competition and the second track of the 2018 iDASH competition
037   were both to develop HE-based solutions for building an LR model over encrypted data.
038
      Several studies on logistic regression models are based on homomorphic encryption. Kim et al.
039   (2018b) discussed the problem of performing LR training in an encrypted environment. They em-
040   ployed full-batch gradient descent during the training process and utilized the least-squares method
041   to approximate the sigmoid function. In the 2017 iDASH competition, Bonte & Vercauteren (2018),
042   Kim et al. (2018a), Chen et al. (2018), and Crawford et al. (2018) all addressed the same problem
043   explored by Kim et al. (2018b). In the 2018 iDASH competition, Kim et al. (2019b) and Blatt et al.
044   (2019) further developed the problem, focusing on efficient packing and a semi-parallel algorithm.
045   There are other related works (Kim et al., 2019a; Bergamaschi et al., 2019; Ogilvie et al., 2020)
046   focusing on various aspects but the papers most relevant to this work are (Bonte & Vercauteren,
047
      2018) and (Kim et al., 2018a). Bonte & Vercauteren (2018) developed a practical algorithm called
      the simplified fixed Hessian (SFH) method. Our study extends their work and adopts the ciphertext
048
      packing technique proposed by Kim et al. (2018a) for efficient homomorphic computation.
049
050   Our specific contributions in this paper are as follows:
051
052         1. We propose a new gradient variant, quadratic gradient, which can combine the first-
053            order gradient algorithms and the second-order Newton-Raphson method (aka Newton’s
               method).

            2. We develop three enhanced gradient algorithms by equipping the original ones with
055            quadratic gradient. The resulting algorithms all converge and, in most cases, demonstrate
056            strong performance in terms of convergence speed.
057
058         3. We implement privacy-preserving logistic regression training using the enhanced NAG
059            method, to our best knowledge, which seems to be a great choice without compromising
060            much on computation and storage.

069   2.1   F ULLY H OMOMORPHIC E NCRYPTION
070
071   Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) is a type of cryptographic scheme that can be used to com-
072   pute an arbitrary number of additions and multiplications directly on the encrypted data. It was
073   not until 2009 that Gentry constructed the first FHE scheme via a bootstrapping operation (Gen-
074   try, 2009). FHE schemes themselves are computationally time-consuming; the choice of dataset
075   encoding matters likewise to the efficiency. In addition to these two limits, how to manage the mag-
076   nitude of plaintext (Jäschke & Armknecht, 2016) also contributes to the slowdown. Cheon et al.
077
      (2017) proposed a method to construct an HE scheme with a rescaling procedure which could
      eliminate this technical bottleneck effectively. We adopt their open-source implementation HEAAN
078
      while implementing our homomorphic LR algorithms. In addition, it is inevitable to pack a vector
079
      of multiple plaintexts into a single ciphertext for yielding a better amortized time of homomorphic
080   computation. HEAAN supports a parallel technique (aka SIMD) to pack multiple complex numbers
081   in a single polynomial and provides rotation operation on plaintext slots. The underlying HE scheme
082   in HEAAN is well described in (Kim et al., 2018a;b; Han et al., 2019).
083
084
085   2.2   DATABASE E NCODING M ETHOD
086
      Kim et al. (2018a) proposed an efficient and promising database-encoding method by using SIMD
087
      technique, which could make full use of the computation and storage resources. Suppose a database
088
      contains a training dataset Z comprising n samples and (1 + d) covariates. The dataset matrix Z is
089   packed into a single ciphertext in a row-by-row manner.
090
091   When employing this encoding scheme, we can manipulate the data matrix Z through HE operations
092
      on the ciphertext Enc[Z], utilizing only three HE operations - rotation, addition, and multiplication.
      For instance, if we wish to isolate the first column of Enc[Z] and exclude the other columns, we can
093
      create a constant matrix F with ones in the first column and zeros elsewhere. Multiplying Enc[Z]
094
      by F will yield the desired ciphertext.
095
096   Han et al. (2019) mentioned several basic but important operations used by Kim et al. (2018a) in
097   their implementation, such as a procedure named “SumColVec” to compute the summation of
098
      the columns of a matrix. With these fundamental operations, more intricate computations, like
      computing gradients in logistic regression models, become achievable.

138   3     T ECHNICAL D ETAILS
139
140   It is quite time-consuming to compute the Hessian matrix and its inverse in Newton’s method for
141   each iteration. One way to limit this downside is to replace the varying Hessian with a fixed matrix
142
      H̄. This novel technique is called the fixed Hessian Newton’s method. Böhning & Lindsay (1988)
      have shown that the convergence of Newton’s method is guaranteed as long as H̄ ≤ ∇2β l(β), where
143
144   H̄ is a symmetric negative-definite matrix independent of β and “≤” denotes the Loewner ordering
145
      in the sense that the difference ∇2β l(β) − H̄ is non-negative definite. With such a fixed Hessian
146   matrix H̄, the iteration for Newton’s method can be simplified to:
147                                          βt+1 = βt − H̄ −1 ∇β l(β).
148
149   Böhning and Lindsay also suggest the fixed matrix H̄ = − 41 X ⊤ X is a good lower bound for the
150   Hessian of the log-likelihood function l(β) in LR.

230   Definition 3.2 (Quadratic Gradient). Given the previously defined B̃, we define the
231   quadratic gradient as G = B̄ · g with a new learning rate Nt , where B̄ is a diagonal matrix with di-
232   agonal entries B̄kk = 1/|B̃kk |. The learning rate Nt should always be no less than 1 and is designed
233   to decrease to 1 over a limited number of iterations. It is important to note that G remains a column
234   vector of the same dimension as the gradient g. To maximize the function F (x), we can employ the
235   iterative formula: xt+1 = xt + Nt · G, similar to the naive gradient approach.
236
      Minimizing the function F (x) is equivalent to maximizing the function −F (x). In this context, we
237
238
      need to construct B̃ using either a good lower bound H̄ for the Hessian −H of −F (x) or a good
      upper bound H̄ for the Hessian H of F (x). It is worth noting that H̄ can be taken as the Hessian
239
      H itself, thereby eliminating the need to find either lower or upper bounds.
240
241   Böhning & Lindsay (1988) did not propose a systematic method for determining or constructing a
242   constant Hessian approximation for their fixed Hessian method. This may be due to the absence of
243   such matrices for most objective functions. We note that our quadratic gradient approach does not
244
      require a constant (fixed) Hessian replacement and instead allows for the use of the original Hessian
245
      itself to construct our varying diagonal matrix B̃ for the quadratic gradient. Under this less restric-
      tive condition, we present a methodical approach to evaluate whether a fixed Hessian matrix can be
246
247
      employed for efficient computation. Specifically, we first construct B̃ from the Hessian of the given
248
      objective function and then check if, for each diagonal element B̃kk , there is a constant maximum
249
      for B̄kk = 1/|B̃kk | (or a constant minimum |B̃kk |). If each diagonal element |B̃kk | achieves its
250   minimum |M̃kk |, then a constant diagonal matrix with the corresponding diagonal elements |M̃kk |
251
      would satisfy the convergence condition for the SFH method. Finally, we should evaluate the per-
      formance of such a fixed Hessian replacement, as it might not always provide a good bound. This
252
      search method can be applied to any optimization function to identify a fixed Hessian. However, the
253
      absence of a constant Hessian replacement via this method does not necessarily imply that such an
254   approximation does not exist.

404

406   superior performance of the enhanced algorithms is that the quadratic gradient integrates curvature
407   information into first-order gradient methods.
408
      An important observation is that the enhanced algorithms demonstrate better performance with
409
      learning rates between 1 and 2 compared to other values. When the learning rate exceeds 3, the
410
      algorithm is nearly guaranteed not to converge. For quadratic gradient algorithms, we recommend
411   employing an exponentially decaying learning rate, such as 1 + A · γ t , where t denotes the iteration
412   number, A is a positive constant typically set to no less than 1, and γ is a positive number less than
413   1 that controls the rate of decay.

512   Implementation We implement the enhanced NAG based on HE with the library HEAAN. The
513   C++ source code is publicly available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/IDASH2017-245B . All
514   the experiments on the ciphertexts were conducted on a public cloud with 32 vCPUs and 64 GB
515   RAM.
516   For a fair comparison with the baseline (Kim et al., 2018a), we utilized the same 10-fold cross-
517   validation (CV) technique on the same iDASH dataset consisting of 1579 samples with 18 features
518   and the same 5-fold CV technique on the other five datasets. Like (Kim et al., 2018a), We consider
519   the average accuracy and the Area Under the Curve (AUC) as the main indicators. Tables 1 and 2
520   display the results of the two experiments, respectively. The two tables also provide the average
521   evaluation running time for each iteration and the storage (encrypted dataset for the baseline work
522
      and encrypted dataset and B̄ for our method). We adopt the same packing method that Kim et al.
      (2018a) proposed and hence our solution has similar storage of ciphertexts to (Kim et al., 2018a)
523
      with some extra ciphertexts to encrypt the B̄. We chose 1 + 0.9t as our learning rate configuration.
524
525   The parameters of HEAAN we set are same to (Kim et al., 2018a): logN = 16, logQ = 1200,
526   logp = 30, slots = 32768, which ensure the security level λ = 80. We use a larger logp = 40 to
527   encrypt the matrix B̄ for preserving the precision of B̄. Refer to (Kim et al., 2018a) for details on
528
      these parameters. Since our enhanced NAG method need one more ciphertext multiplication than
      the baseline work, consuming more modulus, our solution thus can only perform 4 iterations of the
529
      enhanced NAG method. Yet despite only 4 iterations, our enhanced NAG method still produces a
530
      comparable result.

533   6    C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we proposed a faster gradient variant, termed quadratic gradient, and imple-
536   mented the quadratic-gradient version of NAG in the encrypted domain to train a logistic regression
537   model. The quadratic gradient introduced in this work can be directly constructed from the Hessian,
538   effectively merging first-order gradient (descent) methods with the second-order Newton’s method.
539   There is a promising potential for the quadratic gradient to accelerate other gradient methods, and it
      may serve as an alternative or even a replacement for the traditional line-search method.
