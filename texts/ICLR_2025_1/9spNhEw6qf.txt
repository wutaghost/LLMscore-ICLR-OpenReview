009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we investigate the phenomenon of grokking, wherein models ex-
012            hibit delayed generalization following overfitting on training data. Our focus is
013            on studying grokking in data regimes where the amount of training data is be-
014            low the critical threshold necessary for grokking to occur naturally. We examine
015            several scenarios that provide insight on the grokking phenomenon and suggest
016            avenues for practical applications. We first consider training with a strong regu-
017            larizer, specifically Knowledge Distillation(KD) from a model that has grokked
018            on a distribution (p1 ) to induce grokking on a different distribution (p2 ). We find
019            that this can lead to much faster grokking and reduced critical data size. Further-
020
               more, we show that reducing the weight norm, a key focus in previous grokking
               studies, is not a necessary condition for grokking. We next, explore the scenario
021
               where we aim to train a larger size model on a joint distribution (p1 , p2 ). We
022
               demonstrate that achieving generalization under the critical data size is not possi-
023            ble through standard supervised training. However, we show that we can achieve
024            generalisation if we first perform grokking on two models with the individual dis-
025            tributions and distill this result into the larger model. Finally we consider a con-
026            tinual pretraining setup, where a grokked model transitions from distribution p1 to
027            p2 , we find that KD from the grokked model leads to faster generalization, even
028            when the available data constitutes as little as 10% of the dataset. This is note-
029            worthy because generalization might otherwise be unattainable in such low-data
030            conditions. Moreover, distillation mitigates catastrophic forgetting of previously
031
               learned knowledge. Our analysis offers new insights on the grokking phenomenon
               when knowledge transfer is feasible and illustrates the substantial role KD can
032
               play in accelerating generalization especially under low-data regime.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   In the rapidly evolving landscape of machine learning, the ability of models to adapt and generalize
039   across varying data distributions (Singh et al., 2024b;a; Van de Ven & Tolias, 2019; Fang et al., 2020;
040   Liang et al., 2024) remains a paramount challenge. Traditional training paradigms often struggle in
041   dynamic environments where data distributions shift or where data is scarce, leading to models that
042   either fail to generalize or require extensive computational resources to retrain. Recently, the phe-
043   nomenon of grokking (Power et al., 2022) has demonstrated new perspectives on the generalization
044
      behaviour and how a model can transition to a perfect generalization after long episode of overfit-
      ting and pure memorization (Arpit et al., 2017). Many recent studies attempted at providing a better
045
      understanding of grokking, attributing it to weight decay that steers the optimization towards gener-
046
      alization zone even after reaching a zero loss on the training data (Ishida et al., 2020). Grokking is
047   predominantly observed in low-data regimes; however, it has been shown that beyond a critical data
048   threshold, grokking cannot occur.
049
050
      To the best of our knowledge, grokking has only been studied in the context of a single training
      distribution, primarily focusing on weight decay as its underlying cause. In this work, we explore
051
      grokking in the data regimes lower than critical data, and systematically analyze the influence of
052
      grokked models on related varying distributions in conditions that trigger grokking.
053
      Specifically we address the following questions

092   To address the first question, we conducted extensive experiments by initially training a 1 layer
093
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) model to grok on a distribution p1 . This model serves as the
      Teacher (fT ), which is then used to train a Student model (fS ) on a different distribution p2 . We
094
      observed that not only does the Student model fS exhibit grokking on the new distribution p2 , but
095
      with distillation, the number of steps required to achieve grokking are also reduced. This approach
096   is especially relevant in data crunch situations where the availability of data for p2 is limited. By
097   utilizing a pre-grokked model on p1 we aim to facilitate rapid adaptation to p2 , thereby mitigating the
098   challenges posed by data scarcity. A natural question arises, why differ distributions? The primary
099   reason is to give a flavour of practical utility, where a perfectly generalizable model can be used to
100   assist other models in transferring knowledge under distribution shift. Our investigation is motivated
101   by the pressing need for models that can seamlessly transition between different data distributions
102   without incurring prohibitive computational costs. This is can be highly useful in various domains
103   like continual learning, multi-task learning, domain generalization, etc.
104   To address the second question, our investigation reveals that Knowledge Distillation(KD) offers
105   multiple advantages, one of which is reducing the number of iterations required for grokking. Uti-
106   lizing KD, we empirically demonstrate that grokking can occur even when the amount of data is less
107   than the critical data size. The critical data size as defined in (Liu et al., 2022b; Varma et al., 2023)
      is the minimum amount of data below which generalization is impossible.

      Previous studies have established that grokking occurs within specific data regimes. For in-
109   stance, Power et al. (2022) mentions that for large dataset sizes, training and validation losses track
110   each other closely. Similarly, Nanda et al. (2023) observes that with sufficient data, the gap be-
111   tween training and test loss vanishes. Varma et al. (2023) further investigate the behavior of learning
112   curves around the critical dataset size, identifying various manifestations of grokking. In contrast,
113   our experiments demonstrate that grokking can be observed even below the critical data regime,
114   highlighting the efficacy of KD in facilitating generalization under limited data conditions.
115   Finally, in addressing the third question, we empirically demonstrate that generalizing solutions
116   do not always lie on smaller weight norm spheres in parameter space, contrary to the arguments
117   presented in (Liu et al., 2022b; Varma et al., 2023).
118
      Nanda et al. (2023) propose that training can be divided into three phases: memorization of the train-
119
      ing data, circuit formation (where the network learns a mechanism that generalizes), and cleanup
120
      (where weight decay removes the memorization components). They further suggest that the sudden
121   transition to perfect test accuracy in grokking occurs during the cleanup phase, after the general-
122   izing mechanism has been learned. Through our rigorous experiments, we refute these ideas. We
123   consistently demonstrate examples of grokking occurring with zero weight decay and an increase
124   in parameter weight norm across different training settings, thereby ruling out these factors as the
125   primary reasons or explanations for grokking.
126
      To substantiate our claims, we conduct a series of experiments across various algorithmic tasks,
127   including addition and subtraction. These tasks provide a controlled environment to rigorously
128   evaluate the efficacy of our proposed methodologies. The results from these experiments underscore
129   the potential of grokking-based approaches in enabling efficient model training under constraints of
130   dynamic data distributions and limited data availability. Our findings contribute a comprehensive
131   framework for developing more robust and adaptable machine learning systems, paving the way for
132   advancements in fields where data variability and scarcity are prevalent.

138   Grokking was first observed for algorithmic datasets by (Power et al., 2022). Since then consider-
139   able efforts have been made to understand grokking.
140   Theoretical explanation of Grokking on simpler networks: Rubin et al. (2024) provides analyt-
141   ical predictions from a first-order phase transition perspective on feature learning and demonstrate
142   a mapping between Grokking and the theory of phase transitions. Similarly Levi et al. (2024) pro-
143   vided explicit analytical solutions for the training loss, generalization loss and accuracy dynamics
144   in a linear network. Analysing polynomial regression using a two-layer neural network Kumar et al.
145   (2024) hypothesized that grokking may arise from a transition from lazy to rich learning regime. Lyu
146   et al. (2024) suggest that the sharp transition in test accuracy may stem from a dichotomy of implicit
147
      biases between the early and late training phases.
148   Empirical demonstrations of Grokking: Humayun et al. (2024) explains that grokking mate-
149   rializes in a wide range of practical settings, such as training of a convolutional neural network
150   (CNN) on CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2012). They introduce the new concept of delayed robustness,
151   whereby a deep neural network groks adversarial examples and becomes robust, long after interpo-
152   lation and/or generalization. Notsawo Jr et al. (2023) proposed to predict grokking using the spectral
153
      signature from the Fourier transform to detect specific oscillations in the early training phase. Liu
      et al. (2022a) attributes grokking to the slow formation of good representations owing to the presence
154
      of four learning phases: comprehension, grokking, memorization, and confusion. They find repre-
155
      sentation learning to occur only in a “Goldilocks zone”(including comprehension and grokking)
156   between memorization and confusion. Nanda et al. (2023) demonstrated that grokking, rather than
157   being a sudden shift, arises from the gradual amplification of structured mechanisms encoded in
158   the weights, followed by the later removal of memorizing components. This process is followed by
159   the systematic elimination of memorization components. Barak et al. (2022) suggests that general-
160   ization is due not to random search, but to hidden progress of SGD to gradually amplify a Fourier
161   gap. Thilak et al. (2022) links grokking to the ”Slingshot mechanism” marked by cyclic transitions
      between stable and unstable training

162
      Relationship of Grokking and Dataset Size: Varma et al. (2023) employed circuit efficiency anal-
163   ysis to reveal that generalization is slower to learn but more efficient. They also introduced a concept
164   of ’critical data size’ below which it is extremely easy to memorise the training dataset, without gen-
165   eralisation. Training with these data points will result in suboptimal test loss (i.e., semi-grokking).
166   And fine-tuning grokked models with smaller data sizes will lead to poor test performance (i.e.,
167   ungrokking). Doshi et al. (2023) indicated that regularization methods could correct errors in the
168   training samples. Liu et al. (2022b) analyzed the loss landscapes of neural networks in explaining
169   many aspects of grokking: data size dependence, weight decay dependence, emergence of represen-
170   tations
171   Knowledge Distillation(KD): Knowledge distillation Hinton (2015) is a widely used technique for
172   model compression Sun et al. (2019); Sarfraz et al. (2021); Mishra & Marr (2017), building more
173   efficient neural network families (Huang et al., 2017; Singh et al., 2024a;b), quantizing existing
174   networks to use fewer bits for weights and activations Wu et al. (2016) and distilling knowledge
175   from larger networks into smaller ones (Tung & Mori, 2019). The method involves training a smaller
176   student model to replicate the behavior of a larger teacher model. This approach has been applied
177
      successfully in various domains, including natural language processing and computer vision. Our
      work builds on this foundation by focusing on task-level knowledge transfer in algorithmic tasks
178
      with varying data distributions.

245       WEIGHT DECAY ?

      We first train a 1 layer Transformer model(fT ) on 30% of training data p1 . As seen in Figure 2a,
248   grokking is observed within 30000 iterations. We further observe that weight decay helps in reducing
249   the number of iterations as shown in (Power et al., 2022). But we observe that weight decay is not the
250   only cause of grokking. Teacher model fT , which has grokked on distribution p1 , can be leveraged
251   to train a student model fS from scratch on a different distribution p2 , using the same fraction
252   (30%) of p2 . As shown in Fig 2b, distillation from fT not only enables fS to grok on p2 , but also
253   significantly accelerates the grokking process, regardless of the optimizer used. This demonstrates
254   a practical utility of grokked models, illustrating their effectiveness in training models on varying
255   distributions through KD. It is important to note that distillation occurs on the probability outputs
256
      from the operator token rather than the P token. This approach aims to learn generic operator-level
      representations instead of task-specific representations, which would depend on the choice of P .
257
258   We observe that utilizing KD significantly reduces the number of steps required to achieve grokking,
259   irrespective of the optimizer employed. It has been shown to provide multiple benefits in improv-
260   ing training dynamics. Menon et al. (2021) provided a statistical perspective on distillation, that
261   providing the true class-probabilities from the teacher model can lower the variance of the student
262
      objective, and thus improve performance. Further Phuong & Lampert (2019) provides a general-
      ization bound that establishes fast convergence of the expected risk of a distillation-trained linear
263
      classifier. It can be inferred from these studies (Tang et al., 2020; Cho & Hariharan, 2019; Yuan
264
      et al., 2020) that KD brings the following advantages towards training dynamics,
265
266          • Regularization Effect through Label Smoothing: KD smooths the labels, which acts as
267            a regularizer and prevents overfitting.
268
269          • Domain Knowledge Injection: The teacher model imparts class relationships that shape
               the geometry of the student’s logit layer.

      Additionally, as illustrated in Figure 3, the weight norm continuously increases for both addition
296
      and subtraction tasks, yet grokking still occurs. These findings challenge the theories proposed
297   by (Nanda et al., 2023) who suggest that the abrupt transition to perfect test accuracy during grokking
298   occurs in the cleanup phase (where weight decay removes memorization components), following the
299   establishment of the generalizing mechanism. Our empirical evidence contradicts these claims by
300   demonstrating grokking even without weight decay and with increasing weight norms.
301
      Similarly Liu et al. (2022b) induce grokking by increasing the initial weight norm and conclude
302
      that generalizing solutions lie on smaller norm spheres in parameter space. While we acknowledge
303   that an initially higher weight norm can facilitate grokking, our results indicate that generalizing
304   solutions do not necessarily lie on smaller norm spheres. Our modular arithmetic tasks serve as
305   counterexamples, where the final generalizing solutions exhibit larger parameter weight norms than
306   their initial states, and grokking occurs without the application of weight decay.
307
      Furthermore Varma et al. (2023) claim that the transition from memorizing to generalizing circuits
308
      occurs because the generalizing circuit is more “efficient” than the memorizing circuit, in the sense
309   that it can produce equivalent loss with a lower parameter norm. In contrast, our studies show that
310   modular arithmetic tasks can achieve generalizing solutions with higher parameter norms without
311   any weight decay, disproving the necessity of norm reduction for grokking.
312
      Therefore we assert that neither parameter weight decay nor decreasing weight norm during op-
313
      timization is inherently fundamental to observing grokking, as highlighted by the above previous
314
      studies on modular arithmetic tasks.

      This study advances our understanding of the grokking phenomenon by exploring its behavior below
      critical data regime. Unlike prior research that primarily focused on a single training distribution and
472
      the influence of weight norm and weight decay, our work broadens the scope by systematically inves-
473
      tigating how grokking can be induced with KD without relying only on weight decay and decreasing
474   weight norms. Our findings challenge the prevailing notion that weight decay and decreasing weight
475   norms are the sole drivers of grokking. Through rigorous experimentation, we demonstrated that
476   grokking can occur even in the absence of weight decay and with increasing weight norms, thereby
477   refuting earlier hypotheses that linked grokking exclusively to these factors. Additionally, we es-
478   tablished that KD not only accelerates the grokking process but also enables generalization below
479   the previously identified critical data threshold even in varying distributions which is significant for
480   scenarios characterized by data scarcity, where traditional training methods falter.
481   Future work may extend these insights to more complex and diverse real-world tasks, further elu-
482   cidate the underlying mechanisms of grokking, and explore additional strategies to harness pre-
483   grokked models for various transfer learning applications. By continuing to unravel the intricacies
484   of grokking, we can pave the way for the development of machine learning models that not only
485   generalize effectively but also adapt swiftly and efficiently to the ever-changing landscapes of real-
      world data.
