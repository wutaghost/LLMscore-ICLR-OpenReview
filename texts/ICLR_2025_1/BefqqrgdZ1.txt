011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce UltraLightUNet (2D and 3D), an ultra-lightweight,
014            multi-kernel U-shaped network for medical image segmentation. The core of Ul-
015
               traLightUNet consists of a new Multi-kernel Inverted Residual (MKIR) block,
               which can efficiently process images through multiple kernels while capturing
016
               complex spatial relationships. Additionally, our Multi-kernel Inverted Resid-
017
               ual Attention (MKIRA) block refines and emphasizes image salient features via
018            sophisticated convolutional multi-focal attention mechanisms. UltraLightUNet
019            strategically employs the MKIR block in the encoder for feature extraction and
020            the MKIRA block in the decoder for feature refinement, thus ensuring targeted
021            feature enhancement at each stage. With only 0.316M #Params and 0.314G
022            #FLOPs, UltraLightUNet offers an ultra-lightweight yet powerful segmentation
023            solution that outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods across twelve medical
024            imaging benchmarks. Notably, UltraLightUNet surpasses TransUNet on DICE
025            score while using 333× fewer #Params and 123× fewer #FLOPs. Compared
026
               to the lightweight model, UNeXt, UltraLightUNet improves DICE scores by
               up to 6.7% with 4.7× fewer parameters. UltraLightUNet also outperforms re-
027
               cent lightweight models such as MedT, CMUNeXt, EGE-UNet, Rolling-UNet,
028
               and UltraLight VM UNet, while using significantly fewer #Params and #FLOPs.
029            Furthermore, our 3D version, UltraLightUNet3D-M (1.42M #Params and 7.1G
030            #FLOPs), outperforms SwinUNETR (62.19M #Params, 328.6G #FLOPs) and
031            nn-UNet (31.2M #Params, 110.4G #FLOPs) on the FETA, MSD Brain Tumor,
032            Prostate, and Lung Cancer segmentation benchmarks. This remarkable perfor-
033            mance, combined with substantial computational gains, makes UltraLightUNet
034            an ideal solution for real-time and point-of-care services in resource-constrained
035            environments. We will make the code publicly available upon paper acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      The field of medical image segmentation has been revolutionized through the development of U-
040
      shaped convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Oktay et al.,
041
      2018; Zhou et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2020) such as UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), ResUNet (Zhang
042   et al., 2018), UNet++ (Zhou et al., 2018), AttnUNet (Oktay et al., 2018), PraNet (Fan et al., 2020),
043   UACANet (Kim et al., 2021), DeepLabv3+ (Chen et al., 2017), and ACC-UNet (Ibtehaz & Ki-
044   hara, 2023). These models excel at segmenting medical images, thus enabling precise segmentation
045   of critical areas like tumors, lesions, or polyps. The attention mechanisms (Oktay et al., 2018;
046   Fan et al., 2020; Woo et al., 2018) integrated into these architectures help refine the feature maps,
047   thus enhancing pixel-level classification. However, the substantial computational demands of these
048   models, including those with attention mechanisms, limit their applicability in resource-constrained
049   environments such as point-of-care diagnostics.
050   The introduction of vision transformers (Chen et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2021; Rahman & Marculescu,
051   2023b; Valanarasu et al., 2021), including TransUNet (Chen et al., 2021), SwinUNet (Cao et al.,
052   2021), MedT (Valanarasu et al., 2021), EMCAD (Rahman et al., 2024), and DeformableLKA (Azad
053   et al., 2024), marked a shift towards leveraging self-attention to capture long-range dependencies
      within images for a comprehensive global view. However, transformers tend to neglect crucial local

054
      spatial relationships among pixels which are essential for precise segmentation. Moreover, trans-
055   formers usually have high memory and computational demands for calculation and fusing attention
056   with convolutional mechanisms, which limits their practical uses.
057
058
      In recent years, a good number of lightweight architectures such as MobileNets (Howard et al.,
      2017; Sandler et al., 2018), UNeXt (Valanarasu & Patel, 2022), CMUNeXt (Tang et al., 2023),
059
      MALUNet (Ruan et al., 2022), EGE-UNet (Ruan et al., 2023), Rolling-UNet (Liu et al., 2024),
060
      and UltraLight VM-UNet (Wu et al., 2024), helped bridge this gap by combining the strengths of
061   CNNs and/or multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs). However, most of these architectures are designed
062   for less complex or easy-to-segment applications such as skin lesions, breast cancer with ultrasound,
063   and microscopic cell nuclei/structure segmentation. Consequently, these architectures show poor
064   performance in challenging applications like polyp segmentation due to the high variability in the
065   shape, size, and texture of polyps.
066
      Aiming to improve segmentation performance and accuracy, several 3D medical image segmen-
067   tation networks have been also introduced, such as 3D U-Net (Çiçek et al., 2016), SwinUNETR
068   (Hatamizadeh et al., 2021), 3D UX-NET (Lee et al., 2022), UNETR (Hatamizadeh et al., 2022),
069   nn-UNet (Isensee et al., 2021), and nn-Former (Zhou et al., 2021). However, the high computa-
070   tional demands (particularly the large #FLOPs and significant memory consumption) of these 3D
071   networks, make it challenging to implement them in clinical settings. Recently, SlimUNTER (Pang
072   et al., 2023) introduces a lightweight architecture, however, with the cost of significantly low seg-
073   mentation accuracy. These limitations highlight the need for extremely lightweight models that
074   can deliver highly accurate segmentation while being practical for use in real-time, particularly in
075
      resource-constrained settings.
076   Aiming to extreme lightweight efficiency for both 2D and 3D medical image segmentation in
077   resource-constraint settings, we design UltraLightUNet, a significant breakthrough in medical im-
078   age segmentation, which leverages multi-kernel (i.e., k1 = k2 or k1 ̸= k2 for k1 , k2 ∈ Kernels)
079   lightweight convolutions. Our lightweight convolution blocks drastically reduce the computational
080   cost, making the network ultra-lightweight without sacrificing the ability to capture detailed fea-
081
      tures within an image. Additionally, our multi-kernel property enables the model to effectively
      handle feature representations at same or varying receptive fields, thus allowing for a more robust
082
      and comprehensive analysis of complex images in diverse applications. Moreover, by incorporat-
083
      ing sophisticated convolutional multi-focal attention mechanisms only in our decoder further refines
084   the feature maps by capturing the image salient features. We note that our network is effective for
085   segmentation in both scenarios, whether the regions of interest vary significantly in size and shape
086   or remain relatively uniform. By integrating these new ideas, UltraLightUNet achieves a fine bal-
087   ance between computational efficiency and segmentation accuracy, thus offering an ultra-lightweight
088   model that not only surpasses the performance of heavyweight counterparts (in DICE scores), but it
089   does so with significantly fewer #Params and #FLOPs. Our contributions are as follows:
090
            • New Ultra Lightweight UNet: We propose a new end-to-end network, UltraLigh-
091           tUNet, for both 2D and 3D medical image segmentation, which encodes an image using
092           lightweight multi-kernel convolutions. UltraLightUNet also progressively refines the multi-
093           resolution spatial representations using multi-kernel convolutional attention. Of note, our
094           UltraLightUNet-T has only 0.027M and 0.062G #Params and #FLOPs, respectively, yet
095           provides SOTA performance. Moreover, UltraLightUNet has only 0.316M #Params and
096           0.314G #FLOPs. The extremely low model size (#Params) and computations (#FLOPs)
097           make our UltraLightUNet easy to deploy in point-of-care diagnostics or resource-constraint
098           environments (e.g., mobile or edge devices).

              computational costs.
105         • Lightweight Multi-kernel Inverted Residual Attention: We propose Multi-Kernel In-

143   2.1   C ONVOLUTIONAL N EURAL N ETWORKS (CNN S )
144
145   The advent of CNNs marks a significant shift in medical image segmentation (Ronneberger et al.,
146   2015; Oktay et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2017; Ibte-
147
      haz & Kihara, 2023). Pioneering works such as Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) (Long et al.,
      2015) laid the foundation for end-to-end segmentation models. FCNs replace fully connected lay-
148
      ers with convolutional layers, thus enabling pixel-wise predictions and efficient learning of spatial
149
      hierarchies in images. Afterward, U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) became a key model in medical
150   image segmentation due to its encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections. U-Net effec-
151   tively combines the high-resolution features from the encoder with the context information from
152   the decoder, hence leading to precise segmentations even with limited training data. The sophisti-
153   cated design of U-shaped architecture for pixel-level segmentation tasks motivates us to choose the
154   U-shaped design in our proposed network.
155
      U-Net’s success has inspired numerous variants and improvements. Inspired by residual learning in
156   ResNet (He et al., 2016), ResUNet (Zhang et al., 2018) employs residual blocks to facilitate gradient
157   flow and improve convergence, addressing the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks. Zhou
158   et al. (2018) introduce UNet++, which uses nested and dense skip connections to further enhance the
159   feature propagation and improve the segmentation accuracy. AttnUNet (Oktay et al., 2018) incorpo-
160   rates attention mechanisms that focus on the relevant regions in the feature maps, thus enhancing the
161   segmentation performance by suppressing irrelevant background noise. Fan et al. (2020) introduce
      PraNet for precise polyp segmentation by employing parallel reverse attention and edge-guidance to


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      refine segmentation boundaries. UACANet (Kim et al., 2021) leverages uncertainty-aware mecha-
163   nisms to improve the reliability and robustness of segmentation outcomes. DeepLabv3+ (Chen et al.,
164   2017) integrates atrous convolutions and spatial pyramid pooling to capture multi-scale context in-
165   formation. ACC-UNet (Ibtehaz & Kihara, 2023) employs adaptive context capture mechanisms to
166   dynamically adjust the receptive fields based on the input image.

      2.2   V ISION T RANSFORMERS
170
171   Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021) have emerged as a power-
172
      ful alternative to CNNs, i.e., a new paradigm for medical image analysis tasks that leverages the
      self-attention mechanism (Chen et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2021; Rahman et al., 2024; Rahman &
173
      Marculescu, 2023a; Valanarasu et al., 2021). Moreover, by combining the strengths of CNNs for
174
      local feature extraction and Transformers for capturing long-range dependencies, TransUNet (Chen
175   et al., 2021) achieves superior performance in medical image segmentation. SwinUNet (Cao et al.,
176   2021) is introduced based on the Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) architecture, which utilizes
177   shifted windows to achieve hierarchical feature representation, enabling efficient computation. Rah-
178   man et al. introduces, CASCADE (Rahman & Marculescu, 2023a), a cascaded-attention decoding
179   network using standard convolutions. Recently, EMCAD (Rahman et al., 2024) introduces a depth-
180   wise convolutions-based multi-scale decoder. Although, CASCADE and EMCAD perform well in
181   medical image segmentation, their segmentation accuracy and computational complexity solely de-
182   pend on the strength and complexity of the exiting pretrained transformer encoder they use, thus
183
      making them less suitable for resource-constrained settings. In contrast, we propose to design an
      extremely efficient (ultra-lightweight) end-to-end (both encoder and decoder) architecture using the
184
      multi-kernel trick (where, k1 = k2 or k1 ̸= k2 for k1 , k2 ∈ Kernels) with depthwise convolutions.
185
186
187   2.3   L IGHTWEIGHT N ETWORKS
188
189   Recent efforts have focused on making CNNs more efficient for real-time and resource-constrained
190   environments. MobileNets (Howard et al., 2017) and EfficientNets (Tan & Le, 2019) introduce
191   depthwise separable convolutions and compound scaling, respectively, to create lightweight mod-
192   els with competitive performance. Additionally, several novel lightweight architectures have been
193   developed to further enhance the efficiency of medical image segmentation (Valanarasu & Patel,
194   2022; Tang et al., 2023; Ruan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b).
195
      UNeXt (Valanarasu & Patel, 2022) leverages hybrid convolutional and transformer blocks to cap-
      ture both local and global features efficiently, improving segmentation accuracy while maintaining
196
      computational efficiency. CMUNeXt (Tang et al., 2023) combines convolutional and multi-scale
197
      features to enhance segmentation performance. EGE-UNet (Ruan et al., 2023) integrates edge-
198   guided mechanisms to refine segmentation boundaries. Rolling-UNet (Liu et al., 2024) incorporates
199   rolling convolutional blocks to enhance the model’s ability to capture long-range dependencies.
200
201
202   2.4   3D N ETWORKS
203
204   Recent advancements in 3D medical image segmentation have introduced several techniques to im-
205   prove performance, though many face challenges related to computational and memory efficiency.
206   3D U-Net (Çiçek et al., 2016) is a widely-used U-shaped network, but suffers from high #FLOPs
207
      and memory usage. nn-UNet (Isensee et al., 2021) automates the architecture optimization for spe-
      cific datasets, but still remains resource-intensive. Transformer-based models like nn-Former (Zhou
208
      et al., 2021), UNETR (Hatamizadeh et al., 2022), and SwinUNETR (Hatamizadeh et al., 2021)
209
      capture global dependencies, but are computationally heavy. Recently, mimicking the large-kernel
210   depthwise convolutions in ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) for global feature representation (like trans-
211   formers), 3D UX-Net (Lee et al., 2022) introduces a lightweight encoder using the 3D large-kernel
212   depthwise convolutions to improve global feature learning. However, 3D UX-Net has high compu-
213   tational costs due to using the existing heavyweight UNETR decoder (Hatamizadeh et al., 2022).
214   Recently, SlimUNETR (Azad et al., 2024) introduces a lightweight 3D network for volumetric seg-
215   mentation. However, SlimUNETR performs poorly on complex datasets. In contrast, we propose to
      design an ultra-lightweight 3D network without compromising the segmentation accuracy.

      3.1   M ULTI - KERNEL INVERTED RESIDUAL (MKIR)
248
      We first introduce the multi-kernel inverted residual (M KIR) block to generate and refine feature
249
      maps (Fig. 2c). By utilizing multiple (same or different) kernel sizes, M KIR allows for better
250   understanding of both fine-grained details and broader contexts, thereby enabling a comprehensive
251   representation of the input. As shown in Fig. 2c, the process begins by expanding the #channels
252   (i.e., expansion factor = 2) through point-wise convolution P W C1 , batch normalization BN (Ioffe
253   & Szegedy, 2015), and ReLU 6 activation (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2010). This is followed by multi-
254   kernel depth-wise convolution M KDC for capturing application-specific complex spatial contexts.
255   A subsequent point-wise convolution P W C2 and BN restore the original #channels. The MKIR
256   (Equation 1) significantly reduces the computational cost while ensuring rich feature representation:
257
                       M KIR(x) = BN (P W C2 (M KDC(ReLU 6(BN (P W C1 (x))))))                          (1)
258
259   where M KDC for multiple kernels (K) is defined in Equation 2 and Fig. 2d:
260                                                P
261                             M KDC(x) = CS( k∈K DW CBk (x))                                          (2)
262
      where DW CBk (x) = ReLU 6(BN (DW Ck (x))). Here, DW Ck (.) is a depth-wise convolution
263
      with the kernel k × k. To address the channel independence in depth-wise convolution, a channel
264   shuffle (CS) is used to ensure the inter-channel information flow. Our M KDC(.) differs from
265   M SCB (in EMCAD (Rahman et al., 2024)) in their core theoretical concepts. Our multi-kernel
266   trick supports both k1 = k2 (same-size kernels) and k1 ̸= k2 (different-size kernels) for k1 , k2 ∈ K
267   versus conventional multi-scale (only k1 ̸= k2 ) designs (Rahman et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023a;
268   Seo et al., 2022), thus allowing adaptable context extraction. This conceptual distinction allows
269   UltraLightUNet to adapt kernel sizes based on application-specific needs (e.g., large kernels for
      large objects, small kernels for small objects, or mixed for both objects segmentation).

      3.2   C ONVOLUTIONAL MULTI - FOCAL ATTENTION (CMFA)
271
272   Our CMFA (Fig. 2f) leverages a unified attention mechanism that effectively captures both channel-
273   wise and spatial features (Rahman & Marculescu, 2023a), thereby optimizing the network ability
274   to focus on critical aspects of the image while suppressing irrelevant details. We first enhance the
275   relevant channels by applying both adaptive max pooling (AM P ) and average pooling (AAP ) to
276   condense spatial information, which allows the network to maintain robustness to variations in local
277   structures. The pooled outputs are then passed through a series of point-wise convolutions (P W C)
278
      to reduce (r = 16) dimensions and are activated by ReLU (Nair & Hinton, 2010), followed by a
      second P W C layer for expansion. The Sigmoid activation generates the attention weights, which
279
      are then multiplied element-wise (⊛) with the input, thus emphasizing the important channels. This
280
      attention process is defined in Equation 3:
281
282
                CA(x) = Sigmoid(P W C2 (ReLU (P W C1 (AM P (x)))) + P W C2 (ReLU (P W C1 (AAP (x))))) ⊛ x (3)
283   Subsequently, to capture the spatial dependencies and refine the feature maps further, we ap-
284   ply pooling operations across channels to generate two spatial descriptors: Channelmax (x) and
285   Channelavg (x). By applying a large-kernel convolution (LKC) to the concatenated pooled values,
286   we capture contextual relationships across a broader spatial context, thus reinforcing the network
287
      focus on important regions of an image. The refined feature maps are derived as Equation 4:
288                  SA(x) = Sigmoid(LKC([Channelmax (x), Channelavg (x)])) ⊛ x                          (4)
289
      In essence, combining both mean and max pooling helps balance the focus between high-intensity
290
      (max) regions and overall feature consistency (mean). Similarly, the integration of both channel
291   and spatial attention facilitates precise reconstruction and segmentation, even in complex scenarios,
292   thereby leading to improved segmentation performance.
293

      3.3   M ULTI - KERNEL INVERTED RESIDUAL ATTENTION (MKIRA)
295   Our new MKIRA block (Fig. 2b) effectively refines the feature maps by leveraging a convolutional
296   multi-focal attention mechanism (CM F A) and a multi-kernel inverted residuals (M KIR). The use
297   of CM F A enhances the network ability to focus on critical channels and spatial regions, thereby
298   ensuring that the most salient features are enhanced (see the CMFA activation heatmaps in Fig. 3
299   in Appendix). This dual attention mechanism aids in improving feature discrimination and rep-
300
      resentation, especially in challenging scenarios where important structures may vary significantly.
      Additionally, the incorporation of the M KIR block further enriches the feature maps by capturing
301
      contextual relationships through multiple kernels (see the MKIRA activation heatmaps in Fig. 3).
302
      Taken together, these components enable the network to maintain high accuracy while minimizing
303   the computational overhead. M KIRA is given in Equation 5:
304
                                     M KIRA(x) = M KIR(CM F A(x))                                    (5)

306   3.4   G ROUPED ATTENTION GATE (GAG)
307
      We use a grouped attention gate (GAG, Fig. 2e) that mixes the feature maps with the attention
308
      coefficients for enhancing the relevant features and suppressing the irrelevant ones. By utilizing a
309
      gating signal from higher-resolution features, GAG directs the information flow, thus improving
310   medical image segmentation accuracy. Unlike Attention UNet (Oktay et al., 2018), which processes
311   signals with 1 × 1 convolution, our method applies 3 × 3 group convolutions to both gating (g) and
312   input (x) feature maps separately (Rahman et al., 2024). After convolution, the features undergo
313   batch normalization (BN ) and get combined via addition, followed by ReLU activation. Subse-
314   quently, a 1 × 1 convolution and batch normalization (BN ) produce a unified feature map which,
315   after the Sigmoid activation (σ), generates the attention coefficients. These coefficients adjust the
316   input feature x, and create an attention-enhanced output. GAG is defined in Equations 6:
317     GAG(g, x) = x ⊛ σ(BN (Conv(ReLU (BN (GroupConvg (g) + BN (GroupConvx (x))))))))) (6)

354   segmentation by replacing all 2D operations (such as Conv2d, BatchNorm2d, and channel shuffle)

      with their corresponding 3D operations. This adaptation allows the network to capture the volumet-
      ric features and spatial relationships inherent in 3D medical imaging data. Therefore, our MKDC
356
      is extended to MKDC3D, thus enabling our network to process complex volumetric features more
357
      efficiently across different layers in a 3D space. Additionally, attention mechanisms such as channel
358   attention (CA) and spatial attention (SA) are adapted to focus on relevant 3D regions to enhance

531   6      C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we have presented UltraLightUNet, a new network for medical image segmentation
533
      that achieves high accuracy with an ultra-lightweight design. UltraLightUNet outperforms state-of-
534   the-art models across multiple benchmarks while maintaining a significantly lower computational
535   footprint. For example, UltraLightUNet surpasses the performance of TransUNet in DICE scores
536   with 333× fewer #Params and 123× fewer #FLOPs. Similarly, UltraLightUNet improves segmen-
537   tation accuracy by up to 6.7% compared to UNeXt, while using 4.7× fewer #Params. Our design
538   efficiently captures complex spatial relationships and refines salient features, thus making it ideal
539   for resource-constrained environments such as point-of-care services, where real-time, high-fidelity
      diagnostics are essential.

759   A.1       DATASETS
760
761   We evaluate the UltraLightUNet’s efficacy across 11 datasets covering eight segmentation tasks.
762   Our six datasets from four binary segmentation tasks includes breast cancer (BUSI (Al-Dhabyani
763   et al., 2020), 647 images: 437 benign and 210 malignant), polyp (ClinicDB (Bernal et al., 2015) with
764   612 images, and ColonDB (Vázquez et al., 2017) with 379 images), skin lesion (ISIC18 (Codella
765   et al., 2019), 2,594 images), and cell nuclei/structure segmentation (DSB18 (Caicedo et al., 2019)
766   with 670 images, and EM (Cardona et al., 2010) with 30 images). These datasets, collected from
767   various imaging centers, offer a broad diversity in image characteristics, ensuring a comprehensive
768   evaluation. An 80:10:10 train-val-test split was applied across all the binary segmentation datasets
769
      and the DICE score of testset is reported.
770   Our two 2D multi-class segmentation datasets are Synapse Multi-organs 1 and ACDC cardiac organs
      2
771     . The Synapse multi-organ dataset is used for abdominal organ segmentation and includes 30
772   abdominal CT scans with 3,779 axial slices of 512 × 512 pixels. Following the TransUNet (Chen
773   et al., 2021), 18 scans (2,212 slices) are used for training and 12 for validation. We segment eight
774   organs: aorta, gallbladder, left kidney, right kidney, liver, pancreas, spleen, and stomach. For cardiac
775
      organ segmentation, the ACDC dataset contains 100 cardiac MRI scans segmented into three sub-
      organs: right ventricle, myocardium, and left ventricle. We follow the TransUNet protocol using 70
776
      cases (1,930 slices) for training, 10 for validation, and 20 for testing.
777
778   We perform experiments on five public multi-modality datasets for 3D volumetric segmentation: (1)
779   MICCAI 2021 FeTA Challenge dataset (FeTA2021) (Payette et al., 2021), (2) Medical Segmentation
780   Decathlon (MSD) (Antonelli et al., 2022) Task01 BrainTumour, (3) MSD Task05 Prostate, (4) MSD
781
      Task06 Lung, and (5) Synapse Multi-organ.
782   (1) For FeTA2021, we use 80 T2-weighted infant brain MRIs from the University Children’s Hos-
783   pital, acquired using 1.5T and 3T clinical whole-body scanners, for brain tissue segmentation with
784   annotations of seven distinct tissues. We perform a five-fold cross-validation and report the average
785   DICE score.
786   (2) The MSD Task01 BrainTumour dataset consists of multi-modal 848 MRI scans, including T1,
787   T1-contrast enhanced (T1ce), T2, and FLAIR modalities. The dataset focuses on the segmentation of
788   three tumor sub-regions: Tumor Core (TC), Whole Tumor (WT), and Non-enhancing Tumor (NET).
789   A major challenge in this dataset lies in the heterogeneous appearance and spatial distribution of the
790   tumor sub-regions, caused by differences in tumor morphology, size, and imaging characteristics
791   across patients. We use 396 scans for training and 96 scans for validation.
792   (3) The MSD Task05 Prostate dataset comprises 32 annotated MRI scans across two modalities,
793   targeting the prostate peripheral zone (PZ) and transition zone (TZ). One of the major challange
794   of this dataset is the significant inter-subject variability. We use 26 MRI scans for training and
795   remaining 6 scans for validation.
796
      (4) The MSD Task06 Lung dataset comprises 63 annotated CT scans, aiming to segment lung cancer
797   lesions. The dataset presents significant challenges due to the variable sizes, shapes, and locations
798   of lesions, as well as the presence of similar-looking non-tumorous structures like blood vessels and
799   nodules. The diversity in scan quality and inter-subject anatomical variability further adds to the
800   complexity in this dataset. We use 51 scans for training and 12 scans for validation.
801
      (5) As described earlier, Synapse Multi-organ dataset contains 30 CT scans with the annotation of
802
      13 abdominal organs (Spleen, Right Kidney, Left Kidney, Gallbladder, Esophagus, Liver, Stomach,
803   Aorta, IVC, Portal and Splenic Veins, Pancreas, Right adrenal gland, Left adrenal gland). Follow-
804   ing the splits of TransUNet (18 for training, 12 for validation), we perform both 13 and 8 class
805   segmentation. For all of these 3D datasets, we report the results on validation set.

      A.3     DATASET SPECIFIC IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
841
842
843
      For binary segmentation, training spans over 200 epochs with batches of 16, learning rate of 1e −
      4, and weight decay, during which we save the model achieving the highest DICE score. Image
844
      dimensions are set to 256 × 256 pixels for BUSI (Al-Dhabyani et al., 2020), ISIC18 (Codella et al.,
845
      2018), EM (Cardona et al., 2010), and DSB18 (Caicedo et al., 2019) datasets, while for ClinicDB
846   (Bernal et al., 2015) and ColonDB (Vázquez et al., 2017), the resolution is adjusted to 352 × 352
847   pixels. We utilize a multi-scale training approach, with scales of {0.75, 1.0, 1.25}, and enforce
848   gradient clipping at 0.5. For binary segmentation, we do not apply any form of augmentation and
849   use a hybrid loss function that combines (1:1) weighted BinaryCrossEntropy (BCE) and Intersection
850   over Union (IoU) loss.
851
      For multi-class segmentation in Synapse Multi-organs and ACDC datasets, we use an input size of
852   224 × 224, employ random rotation and flipping as data augmentation, and optimize the combined
853   Cross-entropy (0.3) and DICE (0.7) with a learning rate of 1e − 4. We train models for 300 and 400
854   epochs with a batch size of 6 and 12 for Synapse and ACDC datasets, respectively. In the case of
855   3D segmentation in MSD Prostate, FETA, and Synapse Multi-organs datasets, the DiceCELoss is
856   optimized for 40000 iterations with a learning rate of 1e − 3. We use an input size of 96 × 96 × 96
857   and augmentations the same as 3D UX-Net (Lee et al., 2022).
858   We consider MSD Task01 BrainTumour as a multi-level segmentation problem and use the Sigmoid
859   activation on the prediction. While we use Softmax activation on the prediction for multi-class
860   segmentation on MSD Task05 Prostate and binary segmentation on MSD Task06 Lung datasets.
861   We train the model for 60000 iterations on MSD Task01 BrainTumour and use 40000 iterations for
862   MSD Task05 Prostate and MSD Task06 Lung datasets. For these three MSD datasets, we use an
863   input size of 96 × 96 × 96, learning rate of 1e − 3, and augmentations the same as SwinUNETR
      (Tang et al., 2022).

1146
       While UltraLightUNet excels in computational efficiency, its focus on extreme lightweight design
       occasionally results in slightly lower performance compared to SOTA methods on complex datasets
1147
       (e.g., Synapse in Tables 2 and 12). This tradeoff reflects its primary goal of addressing resource
1148
       constraints in real-time and point-of-care applications.

1153

1154
       tUNet’s applicability across various computer vision tasks.
