025                                                    A BSTRACT
026
027                The rapid development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) is mak-
028                ing AI-driven scientific assistants increasingly feasible, with interpreting scientific
029                figures being a crucial task. However, existing datasets and benchmarks focus
030                mainly on basic charts and limited science subjects, lacking comprehensive evalu-
031
                   ations. To address this, we curated a multimodal, multidisciplinary dataset from
                   peer-reviewed, open-access Nature Communications articles, spanning 72 scientific
032
                   disciplines. This dataset includes figures such as schematic diagrams, simulated
033
                   images, macroscopic/microscopic photos, and experimental visualizations (e.g.,
034                western blots), which often require graduate-level, discipline-specific expertise to
035                interpret. We developed benchmarks for scientific figure captioning and multiple-
036                choice questions, evaluating six proprietary and over ten open-source models across
037                varied settings. The results highlight the high difficulty of these tasks and the signif-
038                icant performance gap among models. While many open-source models performed
039                at chance level on the multiple-choice task, some matched the performance of
040                proprietary models. However, the gap was more pronounced in the captioning
041                task. Our dataset also provide valuable resource for training. Fine-tuning the
042
                   Qwen2-VL-2B model with our task-specific multimodal training data improved
                   its multiple-choice accuracy to a level comparable to GPT-4o, though captioning
043
                   remains challenging. Continuous pre-training of MLLMs using our interleaved ar-
044
                   ticle and figure data enhanced their material generation capabilities, demonstrating
045                potential for integrating scientific knowledge. The dataset and benchmarks will be
046                released to support further research.

142   2    R ELATED DATASET W ORK
143
144   Scientific Figure Understanding Scientific figures in academic articles convey rich, valuable
145   information, and there has been extensive research on evaluating the understanding of these figures.
146   As seen in Table 1, existing datasets primarily focus on relatively simple chart figures, which require
147   minimal scientific knowledge but general chart interpretation capacities. Early efforts targeted data
148   visualization figures, such as synthetic datasets of plots and charts (Chen et al., 2020; Kahou et al.,
149   2017; Kafle et al., 2018). To capture more diverse and complex chart figures, FigureSeer (Siegel et al.,
150
      2016) and SciCap (Yang et al., 2023) extracted figures from computer science (CS) papers on arXiv.
      SciFiBench (Roberts et al., 2024) expanded on SciCap’s chart figures by introducing figure-to-caption
151
      and caption-to-figure matching tasks, while CharXiv (Wang et al., 2024b) hand-picked chart figures
152
      from arXiv papers. These datasets focus exclusively on chart figures. ArxivQA/Cap (Li et al.,
153   2024) extended the scope by collecting papers from 32 subjects on arXiv, including open-category
154   image types beyond charts. However, the collection still heavily focuses on CS and math, with less
155   comprehensive coverage of the natural sciences. Moreover, since arXiv papers are not peer-reviewed,
156   their quality is not guaranteed. In contrast, our dataset includes peer-reviewed articles from Nature
157   Communications, spanning 72 disciplines and covering a wide range of natural science subjects.
158   The figures are highly diverse and typically require graduate-level expertise in specific subjects to
159   interpret, ensuring the dataset’s quality, diversity, and complexity.
160

184   3       DATA C URATION
185
186   Source Data Collection Our dataset was collected from the Nature Communications website,
187   comprising open-access, peer-reviewed papers across five major categories and 72 subjects. The
188   top 30 subjects are shown in Figure 1, with the full list of all 72 subjects provided in the Appendix,
189   Table 6. Various information regarding each article is easily accessible on this website, providing a
190
      user-friendly platform for obtaining all necessary data. For each article, we collected information
      including the title, abstract, main body content, and references, directly from their respective sections
191
      on the article’s webpage (e.g., https://www.nature.com/articles/xxx, where “xxx” is
192
      the article’s unique ID). Figures and their captions were obtained from a dedicated figures page un-
193   der the article’s homepage (e.g., https://www.nature.com/articles/xxx/figures),
194   eliminating the need to extract figures from PDF files and thus ensuring image quality. We used
195   pylatexenc to convert LaTeX expressions of mathematical formulas in the article text and figure
196   captions into plain text.4 Since these papers are all peer-reviewed and the text, figures, and captions
197   are readily available from the website, ensuring the data is both authentic and high-quality. We
198   thus did not perform additional filtering or content extraction. We crawled articles up to the date
199   of 2024/04/15. The resulting source dataset comprises 131,393 articles and 742,273 figures. More
200   statistics are shown in Table 2.
201
202   Sub-caption Extraction Many figures in the dataset consist of multiple sub-figures in a single
203   image, with captions that include a main caption and descriptions of each sub-figure (sub-caption), as
204   illustrated in Figure 3. We developed a regular expression matching function to identify sub-figure
205   indices at the beginning of sentences in alphabetical order (a to z), extracting and identifying 514,054
206   sub-captions/figures, which aids in the consecutive construction of our benchmark.
207
208   Heterogeneous Figure Types in MMS CI We categorized the types of (sub-)figures in MMS CI
209   into seven major categories based on a subset of the figures, focusing on the smallest individual
210   components, such as sub-figures when present. Following this manual review, we used GPT-4o to
211   classify the images within the benchmark test set (see benchmark data splits in the next section).
212   Examples of image types are shown in Figure 2, with detailed statistics provided in the Appendix
213
      (Section A.1.3). In addition to charts in previous benchmarks, which make up half of the figures, we
      identified six other major types that vary significantly across different subjects.

234   4       B ENCHMARKS
235
236   We developed two benchmark tasks MMS CI C AP and MMS CI QA with varying settings to compre-
237   hensively test models’ understanding of scientific figures and content, as shown in Figure 3.
238
239   MMS CI C AP: Scientific Figure Captioning Scientific figure captioning in MMS CI presents
240   unique challenges compared to typical natural image captioning. Unlike natural images, interpreting
241   figures in Nature Communications articles often requires graduate-level expertise in specific subjects
242   and grounding in the article’s content. Additionally, these captions are significantly more detailed. In
243   MMS CI, captions average 153 words, much longer than those for natural images and ArxivCap (Li
244   et al., 2024). This complexity makes scientific figure captioning in our benchmark more demanding.
245
      In our task, we evaluate the scientific figure captioning with two settings: (1) ungrounded figure
      captioning: The model generates captions without access to any additional article content. (2)
246
      abstract-grounded figure captioning: The model is provided with the paper abstract to offer context
247
      for the figure. For evaluation metrics, we consider overlap-based metrics ROUGE (Lin, 2004),
248   METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and the similarity-based metric BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019),
249   which compare the generated captions to the reference captions.
250
251
      Additionally, we use a Large Language Model (LLM)-based metric, modified from FACT S CORE (Min
      et al., 2023) for evaluating detailed and complex scientific figure captions in our task. The key idea is
252
      to use LLMs to break down the generated caption y into a set of atomic units, denoted as Ay . Each
253
      atomic unit represents an independent description of either the overall figure or individual sub-figures.
254   We then evaluate whether each atomic unit is supported by the ground-truth caption C. To achieve a
255   more fine-grained evaluation, the LLM assigns a score ϕpa, Cq to each atomic unit a P Ay on a scale
256   from 0 to 1, representing the degree of support from the ground-truth caption. Additionally, a brevity
257   penalty is used to account for overly concise captions. The overall formulation is defined as follows:

      We set γ to 10 in our evaluation. Note that this metric focuses on precision rather than recall. We also
262   use G-E VAL (Liu et al., 2023b) to compare the generated caption with the reference caption on a
263   scale of 1 to 5. We use GPT-4o for the LLM-based evaluations.
264
265   MMS CI QA: Figure Caption Matching We also construct multiple-choice questions to evaluate
266   models’ ability to understand (sub-)figures and match them with the correct captions. To comprehen-
267   sively assess different capabilities and scenarios, we design three settings: (1) Figure-to-Caption
268   (Fig2Cap): The options include the correct main caption for a whole figure and three other main
269   captions from different figures within the same article. This tests the model’s ability to summarize the
      entire figure, demonstrating a holistic understanding of the entire figure. (2) Subfigure-to-Subcaption


                                                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      (SubFig2Cap): This setting evaluates the model’s ability to locate a sub-figure and match it with its
271   corresponding sub-caption. A random sub-figure is selected, along with its correct sub-caption and
272   three other sub-captions within the figure as choices. This challenges the model to first identify the
273   correct sub-figure, then interpret the captions to select the right answer. (3) Subcaption-to-Subfigure
274   (SubCap2Fig): In this reverse setting, the model is given a sub-caption and must select the matching
275   sub-figure from all available sub-figures in the figure. This requires the model to examine each
276   sub-figure and compare it with the captions. All sub-figures in the figure are included as choices.
277
278   Data Split To manage evaluation costs and align with popular benchmark sizes, we allocated 1%
279   of articles from each subject to the test set and another 1% to the validation (dev) set, resulting in
280   1,418 test articles and 1,414 validation articles, with 5 to 50 articles per subject. Each test sample
281   was derived from a single article, ensuring no content reuse. For the captioning task, captions
282
      were required to exceed 50 words. Each task and setting ultimately included around 1,200 samples,
      balancing coverage, diversity, and cost for effective benchmarking.

285   5    T RAINING R ESOURCES
286
287   Our dataset consists of rich articles and figure data, which we explore as training resources to enhance
288   models’ capabilities in comprehending scientific figures and content.
289
290   Task-specific Multimodal Training Data We created a task-specific multimodal training dataset
291   for visual supervised fine-tuning, formatted as single-turn or multi-turn conversations focused on
292   figure captions. The two tasks used for benchmark evaluation, multiple-choice questions and figure
293   captioning, are structured as single-turn interactions. For figure captioning, only abstract-grounded
294
      data is included. Additionally, we transformed figure captions into multi-turn conversations, where a
      human asks about content in different sub-figures, and the assistant responds with the corresponding
295
      sub-caption in each turn. To introduce diversity, we generated various conversation templates using
296
      GPT-4 to vary human instructions. All model responses are sourced from the original articles to
297   ensure data quality. This process produced 108,843 multi-turn conversations, resulting in a training
298   dataset with over 1 million instances.
299
300   Interleaved Text and Image Data for Pre-training MMS CI includes full article content and
301   figures, naturally forming interleaved text and image data suitable for pre-training MLLMs (Lin et al.,
302   2023). We discuss the utilization of this interleaved data in Section 7.

437
438   Material science is the subject with the most articles and figures in our dataset. It is an important and
439
      highly interdisciplinary field, requiring knowledge from various subjects. Therefore, we conducted
      a case study to enhance material science knowledge using our dataset. There has been research on
440
      using language models for material science tasks (Walker et al., 2021; Rubungo et al., 2023; Miret
441
      & Krishnan, 2024). A recent study (Gruver et al., 2024) achieved promising results by utilizing
442   LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023) for material generation. In this study, material crystal structures were
443   represented as text strings, and the LLaMA2 model was trained to generate these structure strings.
444   However, LLaMA2 may lack sufficient scientific knowledge to fully comprehend the principles
445   of material generation. Therefore, we explored the continuous pre-training of LLaMA2 using our
446   interleaved scientific article and figure data, aiming to enhance the model’s performance on the stable
447   material generation task.
448
449   Visual Pre-Training on MMS CI We continuously pre-trained the LLaMA2-7B model on our
450   collected interleaved article text and figure images, using data within materials science as well as other
451   eight related subjects in the same Physical Science category. To achieve that, we leverage LLaVA’s
452
      architecture (Liu et al., 2024), equipping LLaMA2 with a pre-trained CLIP ViT-L/14-336 (Radford
      et al., 2021) as the visual encoder and a 2-layer MLP as the projector. During training, we initially
453
      kept the LLM frozen and used data from general domains provided by (Liu et al., 2024) to initialize
454
      the projector. We then trained the model on the interleaved text and image data from general domains
455   in MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024) to further develop its image perception abilities, followed by our
456   collected interleaved articles and figures in MMS CI to infuse scientific knowledge. In this stage, we
457   tuned both the LLM and the projector, for one epoch. For the resulting multimodal model, we use its
458   LLM part, named LLaMA2-7B-MMS CI, for the subsequent material generation.

      8        C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we present MMS CI, a multidisciplinary multimodal dataset containing high-quality,
531
      peer-reviewed articles and figures across 72 scientific disciplines. Using this dataset, we construct
      a challenging benchmark to evaluate the capabilities of MLLMs in understanding scientific figures
532
      and content, revealing significant deficiencies. Additionally, we explore the use of our dataset
533
      as a training resource to enhance models’ scientific comprehension. By constructing the task-
534   specific multimodal training data and interleaving text and image data for pre-training, we achieve
535   improvements on both our benchmark and the material generation task. Our benchmark primarily
536   focuses on evaluating models’ understanding of scientific figures using figures and captions. The
537   dataset offers rich resources that could be leveraged to create additional tasks for assessing scientific
538   knowledge comprehension, which we plan to explore in future work. Overall, we anticipate that
539   MMS CI will serve as a valuable resource for evaluating and improving the scientific understanding
      of generative models, thereby advancing the development of AI-based scientific assistants.

974    Manual Review Initially, our authors conducted a thorough manual inspection of the figures and
975    sub-figures from 100 randomly sampled articles from the five major categories in MMS CI. This
976    involved summarizing and categorizing various potential figure types present in the benchmark test
977    set. From this detailed analysis, we identified and categorized the figures into seven primary types, as
978
       summarized in Table 7. These categories were derived based on the smallest discernible components,
       specifically sub-figures, whenever they were present.

       Automated Classification Using GPT-4o Following this review, we employed GPT-4o to automat-
981
       ically classify the images in the benchmark test set. We first used the human-annotated results of 200
982
       images from the previous step as the golden labels and then prompted GPT-4o to classify them into
983    categories. Cohen’s Kappa score was calculated to be 0.72, showing a very high agreement score
984    between humans and GPT-4o. The complete prompt for GPT-4o is:
985
986        Task for GPT-4o annotator
987

988        1) Quantitative Data Visualization Charts/Graphs: For charts and graphs displaying quantitative data,
989       such as scatter plots, bar graphs, and line charts.
990       2) Schematic Diagrams: Simplified and symbolic representations of systems, processes, or structures to
991       explain how something works or is constructed.
992
          3) Microscopic photographs: Photographs or images captured using a microscope, revealing details not
          visible to the naked eye.
993       4) Macroscopic photographs: Images or photographs of objects or scenes that are visible to the naked
994       eye, often used for visual analysis.
995       5) Simulated Images: Computer-generated images or visualizations created to model, predict, or illus-
996       trate theoretical scenarios, processes, or phenomena.
997
          6) Geographical and Environmental Maps: Visual representations of geographical areas or environmen-

1003      look like a: 1, b: 3.
1004      Do not return any other information.

1079   each unit based on credible sources. In our adaptation, we apply this approach to complex captions

1144   G-E VAL Evaluation Our G-E VAL evaluation follows the implementation in (Liu et al., 2023b).
1145   We provide the definition of evaluation criteria and evaluation steps without providing examples. The
1146   model is tasked with assigning a score in the range of 1-5. The detailed prompt is as follows:
1147
           Prompt for G-E VAL Evaluation
1148
1149      You will be given a oracle caption that describes a figure. You will then be given a second caption
1150      written for the same figure. Your task is to rate the second caption on one the following metric.
1151
1152       Evaluation Criteria:
           Relevance (1-5) - The extent to which the second caption is relevant to the key elements and context
1153
           described in the oracle caption. A relevant caption should focus on the same subjects, objects, actions,
1154       or context highlighted in the oracle caption, without introducing unrelated or extraneous details.
1155
1156      Evaluation Steps:
1157      1. Review the Oracle Caption: Carefully read the oracle caption to understand the main elements and

       Model Architecture Following the approach outlined in (Liu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023), we
1211   extend the LLaMA2-7B model from a text-only model to a multimodal model by augmenting the
1212   LLM with a visual encoder to learn visual embeddings and a projector to bridge the embeddings
1213   between the text and visual modalities. Specifically, the visual encoder processes the image and
1214   outputs visual features. These features are then mapped into the word embedding space by the
1215   projector, creating visual tokens. These visual tokens are concatenated with the word tokens and fed
1216   into the LLM, allowing the model to integrate both text and visual information for generation. The
1217   specific LLM, visual encoder, and projectors used in our experiments are presented in Table 10.
1218
1219   Training Stages The visual pre-training process (Lin et al., 2023) involves two stages:
1220
1221         1. Projection initialization: In this stage, the LLM and the visual encoder are both pre-trained
1222            and remain fixed. The projector, however, is randomly initialized. Only the projector is
1223            fine-tuned during this stage, using image-caption pairs from (Liu et al., 2024).
1224         2. Visual language pre-training: During this stage, both the LLM and the projector are
1225            fine-tuned on the interleaved image and text data. This includes data from general domains
1226            provided by MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024), as well as scientific articles and figures from our
1227            dataset MMS CI. Previous research (Lin et al., 2023) has shown that tuning both the LLM
1228            and the projector yields better results than tuning only one of them. Throughout this stage,
1229            the visual encoder remains fixed.
1230
       We did not conduct the further visual instruction-tuning for this model, as our primary objective was
1231
       to infuse scientific knowledge into the LLM for the consecutive text-only material generation task.
1232
       The two stages were conducted on a computing cluster equipped with eight NVIDIA A100 GPUs,
1233   each with 40GB of memory. The first stage took approximately 4 hours, and the second stage took
1234   around 36 hours.

1263   Evaluation Our evaluations follows (Xie et al., 2021; Gruver et al., 2024), including four key
1264   aspects. We reiterate some details here. Structural validity is assessed by ensuring that the shortest
1265   distance between any pair of atoms exceeds 0.5 Å. Compositional validity is evaluated by verifying
1266   that the overall charge is neutral, as calculated using SMACT (Davies et al., 2019). Coverage metrics,
1267   COV-R (Recall) and COV-P (Precision), measure the similarity between ensembles of generated
1268
       materials and ground truth materials in the test set. The property distribution metrics quantify the
       earth mover’s distance (EMD) between the property distributions of generated materials and those in
1269
       the test set, specifically for density (ρ, in g{cm3 ) and the number of unique elements (Nel ).
1270
1271   Metastability and stability are assessed based on the energy above the convex hull, denoted as
1272   Êhull . Two approaches are employed to estimate Êhull : M3GNet (Chen & Ong, 2022) and Density
1273   Functional Theory (DFT) using the VASP code (Hafner, 2008). For M3GNet, each sample undergoes
1274   relaxation using force and stress calculations before evaluating the energy of the final structure. For
1275   DFT, relaxation is performed using the VASP code, which provides more accurate results but requires
1276
       significantly more computational resources. A material is considered metastable by M3GNet if the
                                              M3GNet
       predicted energy above the hull, Ehull        , is less than 0.1 eV/atom. Furthermore, if validated by
1277                                    DFT
       DFT, the material must have Ehull ă 0.0 eV/atom to be considered stable. The percentages of such
1278
       materials are reported over the total 10,000 inferences. We use the Materials Project (Jain et al., 2013)
1279
       dated 2023-02-07.

1352   A.3.1     M OTIVATION
1353
1354   With the advancement of large language and multimodal models, there is a growing demand for
1355
       professional AI scientific assistants capable of comprehending and processing advanced, graduate-
       level scientific knowledge (noa, 2023; White, 2023; Vert, 2023). A crucial aspect of developing
1356
       effective AI scientific assistants is their ability to understand academic scientific literature, which often
1357
       includes complex figures such as data visualization plots, charts, schematic diagrams, macroscopic
1358   and microscopic photograph, and other specialized content from a variety of scientific fields. However,
1359   there is currently a lack of comprehensive evaluation for models’ understanding of advanced graduate-
1360   level multimodal scientific knowledge, especially in the context of complex figures across diverse
1361   scientific disciplines. Existing evaluations tend to focus on simpler charts and plots (Chen et al.,
1362   2020; Kahou et al., 2017; Siegel et al., 2016) and suffer from narrow scopes and lower quality (Li
1363   et al., 2024).
1364   Our dataset, MMS CI, is designed to address this gap. MMS CI is a multimodal, multi-discipline
1365   dataset comprising high-quality, peer-reviewed articles and figures from 72 scientific disciplines,
1366   predominantly within the natural sciences. We created a benchmark to evaluate models’ understanding
1367   of graduate-level multimodal scientific knowledge across these disciplines. Additionally, this dataset
1368   can serve as a training resource to enhance models’ understanding of multimodal scientific knowledge.
1369

       This dataset is used to evaluate and enhance the large multimodal models (MLLMs)’ understanding

       of advanced multimodal scientific knowledge.

       the article’s webpage (e.g., https://www.nature.com/articles/xxx, where “xxx” is the

1385

1387   eliminating the needs for quality control and data filtering.

       Our dataset MMS CI provides a comprehensive multimodal dataset across 72 scientific disciplines
