009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent research in Large Language Models (LLMs) has shown promising progress
012            related to LLM alignment with human preferences. LLM-empowered decision-
013            making systems are expected to be predictable, reliable and trustworthy, which
014            implies being free from paradoxes or contradictions that could undermine their
015            credibility and validity. However, LLMs still exhibit inconsistent and biased
016            behaviour when making decisions or judgements. In this work, we focus on
017            studying logical consistency of LLMs as a prerequisite for more reliable and
018
               trustworthy systems. Logical consistency ensures that decisions are based on a
               stable and coherent understanding of the problem, reducing the risk of erratic
019
               or contradictory outputs. We first propose a universal framework to quantify
020
               the logical consistency via three fundamental proxies: transitivity, commutativity
021            and negation invariance. We then evaluate logical consistency, using the defined
022            measures, of a wide range of LLMs, demonstrating that it can serve as a strong
023            proxy for overall robustness. Additionally, we introduce a data refinement and
024            augmentation technique that enhances the logical consistency of LLMs without
025            sacrificing alignment to human preferences. It augments noisy and sparse pairwise-
026            comparison annotations by estimating a partially or totally ordered preference
027            rankings using rank aggregation methods. Finally, we show that logical consistency
028            impacts the performance of LLM-based logic-dependent algorithms, where LLMs
029
               serve as logical operators.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

033
      Recent research in Large Language Models (LLMs; Brown et al. 2020; OpenAI 2023; Anil et al.
034   2023a;b) has achieved substantial progress concerning their instruction-following capabilities and
035   generating responses aligned with human preferences. Consequently, these advancements have
036   broadened their application to an even wider range of complex natural language tasks across diverse
037   domains, often with minimum or even no supervised data (Kojima et al., 2022). The instruction-
038   following abilities of LLMs are primarily achieved through supervised training with instruction-
039   following data (Wei et al., 2022a; Chung et al., 2022) and reinforcement learning from human
040   feedback (RLHF; Christiano et al. 2017; Stiennon et al. 2020; Ouyang et al. 2022b). During the
041   alignment phases, LLMs are enhanced to align with human values, enabling them to generate
042
      responses that better support their decision-making and problem-solving (Dai et al., 2024).
043   Despite these advancements, key challenges still exist regarding the reliability and trustworthiness
044   of LLMs. Issues such as hallucination (Zhang et al., 2023), bias (Gallegos et al., 2024), and
045   inconsistencies in reasoning (Huang & Chang, 2023) continue to affect their credibility. These
046   limitations hinder the full practical deployment of LLMs, particularly in professional and high-stakes
047   applications where trustworthiness and reliability are crucial.1 The foundation of a reliable and
048
      trustworthy system is the consistency of its predictions. A consistent system produces explainable and
      tractable decisions, enhancing its dependability and reliability. In this work, we focus on a key form
049
      of consistency in LLMs: logical consistency. This is especially critical for applications requiring
050
      structured reasoning and coherent decision-making (Creswell et al., 2023). Logical inconsistencies
051   can lead to unreliable conclusions (Restall, 2002) and even paradoxes (Hyde, 2011), posing significant

      2     M EASURING L OGICAL C ONSISTENCY
109
110
      We evaluate the logical consistency of LLMs by assessing their ability to predict logically consistent
111
      relations among a set of items. These items could represent diverse entities or events with a uniform
112
      relation between them; such a relation might be (i) comparing the preference among response
113   candidates to a query or (ii) determining the causal order of shuffled events, among other possibilities.
114   This evaluation is grounded in relational algebra and order theory (Abiteboul et al., 1995; Imieliński &
115   Lipski Jr, 1984), with the goal of assessing whether the model maintains coherent and self-consistent
116   judgments across its predictions.
117
      To formalize this concept, we define the logical consistency evaluation process by treating an LLM
118
      as an operator function that compares pairs of items and outputs a decision on the relation between
119   the items. Let X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN } represent a set of items, and we define a relation (e.g.,
120   comparison) function F : X × X → R, which compares two items, such as (xi , xj ), and assigns
121   a relational decision F (xi , xj ) = r, where r ∈ R denotes the directional relation between xi and
122   xj . For simplicity, we consider R to be a binary relation set, R = {rij , rji }, where rij represents a
123   preferential relation xi ≻ xj (i.e., item xi is preferred over item xj ), and rji indicates the reverse
124   preference xj ≻ xi .
125   In evaluating logical consistency, we focus on whether the function F adheres to the following key
126   properties over the item set X: 1) transitivity, 2) commutativity, and 3) negation invariance, as
127   demonstrated in Figure 1. These properties are foundational for logically consistent operations and
128   are critical for determining the reliability and reasoning capability of an LLM (Hamon et al., 2020).
129
      Transitivity ensures that the LLM’s predictions and judgements are internally coherent and do
130
      not suffer from logical contradictions within a given context. Commutativity tests whether the
131   model’s decisions are invariant to the order in which items are compared. The model should produce
132   consistent judgments regardless of whether the pair (xi , xj ) or (xj , xi ) is presented first. The negation
133   invariance checks whether the model maintains consistency when dealing with relational negations.
134   Inconsistencies here would indicate a failure to correctly understand and apply the complementary
135   nature of relations. By systematically applying these tests, we are able to assess the extent to which
136   the model’s outputs conform to logically consistent behavior, providing a quantitative proxy measure
137   of its decision reliability.
138
139   2.1   M EASURING T RANSITIVITY
140
141   Grounded in our problem setup and definitions above, respecting transitivity implies the following:
142   if a model predicts A ≻ B and B ≻ C, it must also predict A ≻ C. The ability to make transitive
143   predictions is critical to forming a robust, global understanding of relationships within a set of items.
144   Without transitivity, the model’s reasoning would be prone to contradictions, which could lead to
145
      unreliable outcomes in tasks requiring consistent decision-making or ranking (Liu et al., 2024; Li
      et al., 2019; Qin et al., 2024).
146
147   We define that the function F is fully transitive if it does not predict any intransitive relation within
148   the set X. This means that if F (xi , xj ) = rij and F (xj , xk ) = rjk , then F (xi , xk ) = rik must hold
149   for all i, j, k ∈ X. This can be visualized in Figure 2, where we represent the pairwise relations by F
150   as a relation graph. If F is transitive over X, the corresponding relation graph should be a Directed
151
      Acyclic Graph (DAG). A DAG implies that no cycles exist in the relation graph, denoting that there
      are no contradictions in the model’s relational judgments. Consequently, to determine if F is fully
152
      transitive over the item set X, we only have to verify whether the predicted relation graph contains
153
      any cycle or not. We show how to construct the relation graph from the judgements of the LLM
154   operator function F , and the algorithm to check whether a graph contains cycles in Appendix §A.
155
156   We introduce a metric, stran (K), designed to quantify transitivity over an arbitrary number of items.
157
      LLMs often struggle to maintain perfect/full transitivity, especially as the number of items increases.
      The proposed metric stran (K) captures the degree of transitivity across subsets of K sampled items,
158
      where 3 ≤ K ≤ |X|. The metric is defined as:

186   Here, SiK represents a randomly sampled sub-graph of size K, and the indicator function 1acyclic

192   Maintaining transitivity becomes increasingly difficult as the size of the subset grows. For any set of
193   K items, there are 2K possible combinations of pairwise relationships, but only K! of these can form
194   a transitive ranking. As a result, the degree of transitivity tends to decrease with larger sub-graph
195   sizes. This means that for the same LLM, stran (K) typically decreases as K increases, reflecting that
196   preserving consistent rankings over larger sets of items is increasingly challenging. Since stran (K)
197   measures transitivity for a fixed subset size, it allows for fair transitivity comparisons between item
198   sets of different sizes.

200   2.2     M EASURING C OMMUTATIVITY
201
      Commutativity refers to the logical property that ensures the model’s judgments remain consistent
202
      when the order of comparison between two items is reversed. Prior studies have shown that LLMs are
203
      susceptible to permutation bias, also referred to as positional bias (Wang et al., 2024b; Liusie et al.,
204   2024a). This bias can result in inconsistent outputs when the order of items in a comparison is altered.
205   To measure the degree of commutativity, we propose a metric scomm , which evaluates whether the
206   model’s judgment changes when the order of the items is swapped in the prompt. Specifically, it is
207   defined as follows:

211   Here, F (xi , xj ) represents the model’s judgment when comparing items xi and xj . The indicator
212   function 1 returns 1 if the model’s judgment remains consistent when the order of the items is
213   reversed, i.e., F (xi , xj ) = F (xj , xi ), and 0 otherwise. We visualize this comparison in Figure 3.

      that the model is completely robust to the order of item comparisons.

223   which examines if the model can correctly reverse its judgement when prompted with a negated

239   After defining the measures quantifying the three aspects of logical consistency in Section 2, we now
240
      proceed to evaluate LLMs’ judgements from the consistency angle on three representative tasks, each
      reflecting different levels of subjectivity.
241
242
243
      3.1   E VALUATION S ETUP
244
      Tasks and Datasets. We employ three representative tasks to evaluate LLMs’ logical consistency.
245   The first task, abstractive summarization evaluation, uses the SummEval dataset (Fabbri et al., 2021)
246   and focuses on the model’s ability to make preference judgments between summaries, particularly
247   regarding the coherence aspect. The second task, document reranking, leverages the NovelEval
248   dataset (Sun et al., 2023), where LLMs assess the relevance of retrieved documents in response
249   to queries. The final task, temporal event ordering, uses the CaTeRS dataset (Mostafazadeh et al.,
250   2016b) and tests the model’s capability to reason about temporal and causal relationships between
251   events, critical for maintaining consistency in narrative understanding. Further task and dataset details
252   are available in Appendix §B.
253   Metrics and Reliability Measurement. In Appendix §F, we show metric computation details and
254   the prompt templates used. We compute the logical consistency metrics at the instance level and
255   report averages across the test sets for each task. In addition, we report the human agreement rate
256   (abbreviated as H.) by calculating the pairwise judgement accuracy between judgements made by
257   LLMs and the provided human annotations. It serves as a reference for how closely the model’s
258   judgements align with human judgements. For the measurement of LLMs’ reliability, we perform
259
      Monte Carlo Sampling of Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022b) reasoning outputs, using a temperature
      of 0.7. Self-agreement is defined as the percentage of outputs that agree with the majority judgment
260
      across multiple samples. This measurement ranges from 0.5 to 1, with higher values indicating
261
      greater stability in the model’s reasoning processes.

294   The Phi-3 family consistently shows high logical consistency, which we hypothesize is due to
295   its reliance on synthesized data which is cleaner and less self-contradictory. This type of data
296   likely reduces internal conflicts, resulting in more consistent logical behavior. However, no strong
297   correlation is observed between logical consistency and human agreement accuracy. For instance,
298   while Gemma-2-9B and Llama-3-8B achieve similar levels of human agreement, Gemma-2-9B shows
299
      higher consistency in its outputs. This is further confirmed by the low correlations (below 0.05)
      between transitivity consistency and human preference accuracy across all datasets.
300
301   Another observation is that LLMs tend to perform more consistently on the CaTeRS dataset. We
302   attribute this to the dataset’s focus on temporal and causal relations. The more objective and logical
303   preferential relations may make it easier for models to maintain consistency.
304   Impact of CoT Prompting. We also investigated the effect of CoT prompting on logical consistency.
305   Surprisingly, CoT reasoning did not generally improve consistency, and in some cases, it led to a
306   decrease in transitivity performance. We hypothesize that this decline may be due to the additional
307   reasoning tokens generated during CoT prompting, which could introduce variations in the judgement
308   standards used for different comparisons. When a model’s understanding of the preferential relations
309   is not uniform, it may produce inconsistent (non-transitive) outcomes. This suggests that CoT
310   prompting, while beneficial for complex reasoning, might introduce unintended inconsistencies in
311
      certain logical judgement tasks.

313   3.3   C ONSISTENCY AND R ELIABILITY
314

420   4.1     E STIMATING R ANKINGS FROM N OISY PAIRWISE A NNOTATIONS U SING W IN -L OSS R ATE
421
422   Estimating global rankings from noisy pairwise annotations is essentially a rank aggregation problem.
423   We use the win-loss rate method due to its simplicity and two key advantages: 1) it performs well with
424   partial and sparse comparisons, which is common in real-world preference datasets, and 2) it remains
425   unaffected by the order in which comparisons are presented. While other rank aggregation methods
426   like ELO or the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) could be explored for improved results,
427   our choice serves as a proof-of-concept rather than an optimization for best performance.
428   The win-loss rate for each item is calculated as the number of comparisons it wins minus the number
429   of comparisons it loses and then divided by the number of comparisons it participates in. Following
430   that, as shown in Figure 6, we rank the item by the value of its win-loss rate. This way, we can
431   aggregate a full or partial ranking. We then split the ranking into a self-consistent pairwise comparison
      set. We can further augment the dataset by adding the comparisons using the negated relation.

444
      Experimental Setup. We use the Summarize From Feedback dataset (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016b),

446   factors. More details are available in Appendix §B. Given that the dataset annotations are sparse

448   refinement and augmentation techniques are then applied to improve the consistency and quantity of
449   pairwise comparisons, as shown in Table 2.
450
      To assess the effectiveness of our approach, we instruction-tuned three Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct
451
      models on: 1) the flipped/perturbed data, 2) the refined and augmented dataset, and 3) the ‘further
452   augmented’ dataset with additional negated relation comparisons. Training parameters are detailed in
453   Appendix § E. We randomly sample 200 instances from the test set and evaluate all models on this
454   subset. We assess the impact of the data augmentation on logical consistency and performance.
455
      Results and Findings. The results shown in Table 3 highlight three key findings: 1) Zero-shot
456
      inference shows considerable logical inconsistency. However, training on perturbed data can already
457
      substantially improve both human preference alignment and logical consistency. 2) Training with
458   the refined and augmented dataset significantly improves transitivity and commutativity, while
459   maintaining strong human alignment. 3) Only training further on negated relations improves the
460   model performance in negation invariance. However, adding negated relations to the broader dataset
461   may introduce distractions and cause a forgetting effect (Luo et al., 2023), resulting in a slight
462   reduction in performance on other logical properties. Overall, the findings support the effectiveness
463   of our data refinement and augmentation framework in improving the logical consistency of LLMs.

      5     I MPACT OF L OGICAL C ONSISTENCY ON D OWNSTREAM A PPLICATIONS
466
467
      LLMs are increasingly used as logical operators in high-level algorithms due to their ability to process
468
      and understand text semantics. For instance, Qin et al. (2024) use LLMs to enhance document
469   reranking in information retrieval systems. Similarly, Guo et al. (2024) and Yang et al. (2024) utilize
470   LLMs as optimizers in prompt-tuning algorithms, while Liu et al. (2024) and Liusie et al. (2024b)
471   employ LLMs as pairwise comparators to aggregate preference rankings. When LLMs are used as
472   logical operators, maintaining a high level of logical consistency is critical to ensure predictable
473   and efficient decision-making. In this section, we examine how logical consistency influences the
474   performance of LLM-based algorithms in such ‘logically grounded’ tasks.
475
476   5.1   A PPLICATION E XAMPLE : P REFERENCE R ANKING A LGORITHM
477
478   We illustrate the impact of logical consistency through the Pairwise-Preference Search (PairS)
479   algorithm proposed by Liu et al. (2024). PairS is a sorting-based rank aggregation method that uses
480   LLMs as pairwise evaluators (i.e., it is a particular ‘LLM-as-a-judge’ algorithm), comparing items
481   based on specific attributes using a merge-sort approach. Its performance is measured by comparing
482
      the LLM-generated rankings with human judgments via Spearman’s correlation. Sorting algorithms
      depend heavily on logical properties such as transitivity and commutativity. PairS assumes that LLMs
483
      used as evaluators have near-perfect transitivity and commutativity for optimal ranking results.
484
485   To evaluate this, we conduct controlled experiments on the coherence aspect of SummEval, using
      various LLMs with similar accuracy to human judgments. The results are shown in Table 4. As LLMs

      often exhibit positional bias, we follow Liu et al. (2024) by reporting both raw and calibrated results.
487   The calibration, performed as described in Zheng et al. (2023), averages the evaluation probabilities
488   across both possible pairwise orders. While this technique increases inference cost, it reduces bias by
489   balancing positional preferences.
490
      Our findings from Table 4 reveal two key insights: 1) Although Phi-3-mini has slightly lower accuracy
491
      with human judgments than GPT-3.5-turbo, its stronger transitivity leads to better ranking performance
492
      with or without calibration. 2) There is a clear correlation between an LLM’s commutativity and the
493   performance gains from calibration in the PairS algorithm. LLMs that are more commutative rely
494   less on calibration to achieve optimal performance, and therefore, require less computations.

497   6    R ELATED W ORK
498
499   Consistency of LLMs has been explored in several contexts before, where previous research has
500   predominantly focused on two domains: consistency of factual knowledge and entailment consistency
501
      across limited statements.
502   Consistency of Factual Knowledge. Previous work has demonstrated that in knowledge-intensive
503   question-answering tasks, concepts like symmetry and transitivity of knowledge snippets are impor-
504   tant (Asai & Hajishirzi, 2020). To this end, a benchmark for studying language model consistency
505   with paraphrased relations (Elazar et al., 2021) has been created. Additionally, different studies have
506   examined whether LLMs have a coherent understanding of knowledge graphs related to real-world
507
      entities (Jung et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023; Kassner et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023). The ‘reverse curse’
      phenomenon highlights that unlearned factual knowledge cannot be deduced by reversing learned
508
      knowledge (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023; Berglund et al., 2024).
509
510   Entailment and Inference Consistency. In Natural Language Inference (NLI), the consistency of
511   transitivity and symmetry in statement pairs has been explored using predefined inference rules (Li
512   et al., 2019; Jang & Lukasiewicz, 2023). Jang et al. (2022) proposed a test set to exam the consistency
513
      of LLMs for NLI tasks. Prediction consistency has been improved through the use of adversarial
      first-order logic examples (Minervini & Riedel, 2018).
514
515   Despite the wealth of knowledge regarding the logical consistency of LLMs within specific domains,
516   most studies have concentrated on first-order relations—logical connections or implications directly
517   linking two or three individual statements. Consequently, there is a notable gap in research addressing
518   the consistency and reliability of LLMs when applied to more complex decision-making scenarios or
519
      the evaluation of multiple items simultaneously. This limitation suggests a pressing need for further
      investigation into how LLMs can maintain coherence and consistency in broader, more comprehensive
520
      contexts, which is essential for their deployment in practical applications.
521
522
523   7    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we investigated the critical role of logical consistency in enhancing the reliability and
526   trustworthiness of LLMs, especially in their roles as decision-makers and reasoners. We introduced a
527   general framework for quantifying three key properties of logical consistency: transitivity, commu-
528   tativity, and negation invariance. Through comprehensive evaluation, we demonstrated that these
529   consistency measures serve as strong proxies for assessing the overall reliability of LLMs.
530   To improve the logical consistency of LLMs, we then proposed a data refinement and augmentation
531   framework, which effectively reduces logical inconsistencies at the source: directly in the data. Our
532   experimental results showed that models trained on this refined and augmented data achieve improved
533   logical consistency without compromising alignment with human preferences. Furthermore, we
534   demonstrated that integrating LLMs with higher logical consistency into a logic-driven algorithm
535   improves both the algorithm’s global performance and computational efficiency. This highlights the
536
      practical benefits of ensuring logical coherence in LLMs when applied to downstream tasks.
537   In summary, our work emphasizes the importance of logical consistency as a complementary factor to
538   human alignment in the development of more reliable LLMs. We hope this research will encourage
539   the broader community to recognize the importance of logical consistency as a fundamental aspect of
      any work aiming towards improved trustworthiness and reliability.

965   SummEval (Fabbri et al., 2021) is a summarization meta-evaluation dataset comprising 100 source
966
      texts, each paired with 16 summary candidates generated by various language models (LMs). The
      dataset is annotated based on four criteria: coherence (COH), fluency (FLU), consistency (CON),
967
      and relevancy (RE).
968
969   NovelEval (Sun et al., 2023) is a document reranking test set consisting of 21 novel questions. This
970   set was constructed by compiling questions and passages from four domains published after the
971   release of GPT-4. For each question, 20 candidate passages were retrieved through Google search,
      and their relevance was manually labeled.

       CaTerS (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016b) is a dataset focused on temporal event ordering. It contains
973    1,600 annotated sentences derived from 320 five-sentence short stories sampled from the ROCStories
974    corpus (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016a). These stories are organized based on causal and temporal
975    relations. We filtered the dataset to include only instances with seven or more events, resulting in 70

       drawn from the TL;DR (Cachola et al., 2020) and CNN/DM datasets (Nallapati et al., 2016). This
979    dataset is divided into two sections: pairwise comparisons and axis annotations. We concentrate on

981    training, we utilize the entire training set, while for testing, we randomly sample 200 instances from
982    the validation set.

       MS MARCO. (Nguyen et al., 2016) is a widely used large-scale benchmark for training and
984
       evaluating retrieval-based question answering systems. Following the methodology of Sun et al.
985    (2023), we leverage the dataset for a document reranking task. Specifically, they randomly sample a
986    subset of queries from the MS MARCO training set, retrieving 10 candidate passages for each query
987    using BM25. Ranking preferences for these passages were distilled using GPT-3.5-turbo. In this
988    work, we randomly sample 1K examples from their query subset and corresponding ranking labels.
989    We then split them into a training set of 800 examples and a test set of 200 examples.

1007   Improving Consistency of LLMs. Asai & Hajishirzi (2020) proposed an augmentation method and
1008   a training regularization term to improve the logical consistency of language models for QA tasks.
1009   Minervini & Riedel (2018) proposed an adversarial training procedure to improve the robustness of
1010
       NLI models to adversarial examples. Wang & Henao (2021) focus on improving the paraphrasing
       consistency during training for the task of low-resource Named Entity Recognition (NER).
1011
1012   Kumar & Joshi (2022) performed a symmetric consistency analysis on NLI and semantic textual
1013   similarity (STS) tasks in a more conservative manner, arguing that a model should generate not only
1014   the same predictions but also the same confidence scores if it is truly input-order invariant. They
1015   also observed that pretrained language models violated symmetric consistency and the authors then
1016
       introduced a consistency regularisation term to compensate for the issue.
1017   Theory of Consistency. The concept of transitivity in preferences has been extensively explored,
1018   revealing its significance in both psychological and logical frameworks. Tversky (1969) high-
1019   lighted various psychological factors that can lead to intransitive preferences, illustrating how human
1020   decision-making often deviates from rational models. Building on this, Arrow (1950) introduced
1021   Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem, which addresses the inherent challenges of constructing social choice
1022
       functions that maintain transitivity.
1023   Further contributions to the understanding of transitivity include a comprehensive review by Regen-
1024   wetter et al. (2011), which examines both theoretical and empirical perspectives on the subject. Their
1025   work underscores the critical role transitivity plays in preference structures. In a more formal context,
       Tarski (1941) examined the role of transitivity within relational calculus, an area that is foundational to


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       many logical systems. This connection demonstrates how transitivity not only influences preferences
1027   but also underpins logical reasoning.
1028
1029   Moreover, Hansson (2001) provided insights into the implications of violating transitivity in prefer-
1030
       ences, showing that such violations can lead to logical inconsistencies within decision theory. This
       theme is further explored by Hyde (2011), who proved that apparent violations of transitivity can
1031
       give rise to logical paradoxes. Collectively, these works emphasize the importance of transitivity in
1032
       ensuring consistency, both in decision-making and logical deduction.

1062   G    T HE C HOICE OF K
1063
1064   In this section, we discuss the considerations regarding the choice of K for the transitivity metric
1065   Stran (K).
1066
       Robustness of transitivity to K. Stran (K) is robust to variations in K. It is uncommon for a model
1067   to perform well on Stran (3) but poorly on Stran (5), as shown in the Figure 9. Additionally, it shows
1068   that larger values of K expand the value range of Stran (K), making comparisons between models
1069   more distinct.
1070
       Dependence on Prior Knowledge. Choosing K can depend on prior knowledge about the general
1071
       consistency performance of current LLMs. For example, we observe that the consistency performance
1072
       improves progressively from earlier LLMs, such as LLama-2 and Zephyr-7B-beta, to more advanced
1073   models like Gemma-2 and Llama-3. This trend suggests that considering the evaluated LLMs’
1074   expected consistency levels can inform an appropriate choice of K.
1075
1076
       Task-Specific Considerations. As discussed in Section 3.2, consistency performance varies de-
       pending on the task. For objective tasks, consistency tends to be higher, which means task-specific
1077
       nuances should also guide the selection of K.
1078
1079   In summary, there is no definitive “gold standard” for choosing K. Similar to selecting the N-gram
       size in BLEU-N , the choice depends on the models and tasks under evaluation.

1151   Theoretical Basis. The transitivity metric Stran (K) is computed as the average result of a binary
1152   indicator function (as defined in Equation 1), which follows a binomial distribution. Intuitively,
1153   Stran (K) can be interpreted as the probability (p) that a randomly sampled K-node sub-graph is
1154   transitive.
1155   Estimation Accuracy. Using the Central Limit Theorem, the binomial distribution of Stran (K)
1156   approximates a normal distribution when the sample size (n) is sufficiently large. To estimate p
1157   within a margin of error (E) at a confidence level z (e.g. z = 1.96 for 95% confidence), the required
1158   sample size is:
