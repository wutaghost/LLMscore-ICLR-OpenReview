011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The crystal structure can be simplified as a periodic point set repeating across the
014            entire three-dimensional space along an underlying lattice. Traditionally, methods
015
               for representing crystals rely on descriptors like lattice parameters, symmetry, and
               space groups to characterize the structure. However, in reality, atoms in material
016
               always vibrate above absolute zero, causing continuous fluctuations in their po-
017
               sitions. This dynamic behavior disrupts the underlying periodicity of the lattice,
018            making crystal graphs based on static lattice parameters and conventional descrip-
019            tors discontinuous under even slight perturbations. To this end, chemists proposed
020            the Pairwise Distance Distribution (PDD) method, which has been used to distin-
021            guish all periodic structures in the world’s largest real materials collection, the
022            Cambridge Structural Database. However, achieving the completeness of PDD
023            requires defining a large number of neighboring atoms, resulting in high com-
024            putational costs. Moreover, it does not account for atomic information, making it
025            challenging to directly apply PDD to crystal material property prediction tasks. To
026
               address these challenges, we propose the atom-Weighted Pairwise Distance Distri-
               bution (WPDD) and Unit cell Pairwise Distance Distribution (UPDD) for the first
027
               time, incorporating them into the construction of multi-edge crystal graphs. Based
028
               on this, we further developed WPDDFormer and UPDDFormer, graph transformer
029            architecture constructed using WPDD and UPDD crystal graphs. We demonstrate
030            that this method maintains the continuity and completeness of crystal graphs even
031            under slight perturbations in atomic positions. Moreover, by modeling PDD as
032            global information and integrating it into matrix-based message passing, we sig-
033            nificantly reduced computational costs. Comprehensive evaluation results show
034            that WPDDFormer achieves state-of-the-art predictive accuracy across tasks on
035            benchmark datasets such as the Materials Project and JARVIS-DFT.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Crystals are solids with a regular geometric shape formed by atoms, ions, or molecules arranged
040
      periodically in space during the crystallization process. Their structure is typically described using
041
      repeating unit cells and lattice vectors. However, this method of description brings a fundamental
042   challenge: the same crystal structure can be represented by different unit cells and lattice vectors,
043   as shown in Figure 1a. Additionally, in real-world scenarios, the experimental coordinates of unit
044   cells and atoms are inevitably affected by atomic vibrations and measurement noise. These subtle
045   disturbances can lead to discontinuous changes in any simplified unit cell (Kurlin, 2024), resulting in
046   numerous different unit cells for a given crystal structure, as shown in Figure 1b, thereby introducing
047   ambiguity in the representation of crystal data (Widdowson & Kurlin, 2022). Currently, many graph
048   neural networks (Batzner et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022; 2024a;b) typically use unit cell parameters,
049   simplified cell parameters, symmetry, and space groups to represent the periodic structure of crystals.
050
      However, these features are either non-invariant or discontinuous (Zwart et al., 2008) invariants,
      leaving the issue of ambiguity in crystal data unresolved (Patterson, 1944; Widdowson et al., 2022;
051
      Groom et al., 2016; Bartók et al., 2013; Wassermann et al., 2010; Ahmad et al., 2018).
052

      dowson and Kurlin (2022) addresses the ambiguity in crystal data representation by distinguish-

118   Definition 1: Pointwise Distance Distribution. For the infinite crystal structure S = (U, L)
119   mentioned in Section 2.1, fix a neighbor count k ≥ 1. For each point xi in the unit cell U, let

122

123
124   rows, which is called the Pointwise Distance Distribution PDD(S; k) ∈ Rn×(k+1) .
125   Definition 2: Isometric Crystal Graphs. According to the definition from Widdowson & Kurlin
126   (2022) and Yan et al. (2024a), an isometric transformation is a mapping that preserves Euclidean
127   distances, denoted as f (x) = Rx + b. Any isometric transformation f can be decomposed into
128   translation, rotation, and reflection. Specifically, suppose there exists a rotation matrix R ∈ R3×3 ,
129   with a determinant of 1 (|R| = 1), and a translation vector b ∈ R3 , then two crystal structures
130   S = (U, L) and Q = (U ′ , L′ ) are isometric, satisfying U ′ = RU + b, where RU + b denotes the
131
      application of the rotation R and translation b to each element in the infinite set U.
132   If S and Q are isometric, then their crystal graph representations satisfy G (S) = G (Q), which
133   means that the graphical representation of the crystal structure produces no false positives; that is,
134   there are no isometric pairs where G (S) ̸= G (Q) but S ≃ Q. Conversely, if G (S) = G (Q), then
135   S and Q are isometric, meaning f produces no false negatives, i.e., there are no non-isometric pairs
136   where G (S) = G (Q) but S ̸≃ Q. That is, if the crystal graph representations of artificially con-
137
      structed crystal structures are identical under isometric transformations, then they are geometrically
      equivalent.

140   (2022) and Yan et al. (2024a), if we construct crystal graphs G (S) = G (Q) =⇒ S ≃ Q, where ≃
141   denotes the isomorphism of two crystals as defined in Definition 2, then the crystal graph G is geo-
142   metrically complete. This means that if two crystal graphs G (S) and G (Q) are identical, the infinite
143
      crystal structures represented by G (S) and G (Q) are also identical. If the constructed crystal graph
      G can distinguish any subtle structural differences between different crystal materials, it is said to be

166   3     R ELATED WORK
167
168   Finite crystal graph representation. CGCNN (Xie & Grossman, 2018) predicts material proper-
169   ties by learning the connections between atoms in crystals by representing crystal structures as finite
170
      multi-edge crystal graphs. Building on the construction of multi-edge crystal graphs, MegNet (Chen
      et al., 2019) introduced global state attributes into graph networks, while GATGNN (Louis et al.,
171
      2020) utilized multiple graph attention layers (GAT) to learn the properties of local neighborhoods
172
      and employed global attention layers to weight global atomic features. ALIGNN (Choudhary & De-
173   Cost, 2021) and M3GNet (Chen & Ong, 2022) incorporated angular information into the message-
174   passing process to generate richer and more discriminative representations. CrysMMNet (Das et al.,
175   2023) adopted a multimodal framework, integrating graph and text representations to produce joint
176   multimodal representations of crystalline materials. CrysDiff (Song et al., 2024) is a pretraining-
177   finetuning framework based on diffusion models. However, the aforementioned methods represent
178   crystals as finite graph structures, failing to capture the periodicity of infinite crystals effectively.
179   Periodic representation of crystals. Recently, Matformer (Yan et al., 2022) encoded periodic pat-
180   terns by adding self-connecting edges to atoms based on lattice parameters, directly using lattice pa-
181   rameters to encode periodic structures under ideal conditions. PotNet (Lin et al., 2023) considered
182   the infinite summation of interatomic interactions. Crystalformer (Taniai et al., 2024) performed
183   infinite summations of interatomic potentials through infinitely connected attention while also uti-
184   lizing lattice parameters. ComFormer (Yan et al., 2024a) constructed cell parameters by adding
185   self-connecting edges to atoms and their copies in three different directions to encode periodic pat-
186   terns, employing equivariant vector representations and invariant geometric descriptors of Euclidean
187
      distances and angles to represent the geometric information of crystals. GMTNet (Yan et al., 2024b)
      aims to predict the tensor properties of crystalline materials while satisfying O(3) group equivari-
188
      ance and the symmetry of crystal space groups to ensure the accuracy and consistency of tensor
189
      predictions. However, while these methods achieve complete crystal graph representations, the
190   crystal structures they represent rely on non-invariants or discontinuous invariants, such as lattice
191   parameters, symmetry, and space groups, failing to address the issue of crystal data fuzziness.
192
193
      Continuity and complete representations for crystals. Addressing the continuity and complete-
      ness of crystal representations is a critical issue. Recent advancements in AMD (Wang et al., 2022)
194
      and PDD (Widdowson & Kurlin, 2022) have developed matrix forms that are both complete and
195
      continuous. However, in practical applications, using these matrix representations as inputs for pre-
196   dicting crystal properties without compromising continuity and completeness is challenging. The
197   AMD and PDD representations are designed for stable crystal structures and do not account for
198   atomic types. Their completeness relies on the assumption that no two crystals with identical struc-
199   tures differ solely by atomic type, which is only feasible for stable structures. Additionally, to
200   achieve completeness, a sufficiently large number of neighbors k must be predetermined for any
201   test crystal. Typically, hundreds of neighbors are required to distinguish all periodic structures in
202   the Cambridge Structural Database. Directly modeling PDD as edge information is impractical and
203   costly in real-world applications (Balasingham et al., 2022).

327   4.5   G EOMETRIC COMPLETENESS OF PROPOSED CRYSTAL GRAPHS
328
329   Proposition 2. The WPDD multi-edge crystal graph is geometrically complete.
330
      Inspired by Yan et al. (2024a). We prove this by mathematical induction. Suppose the number of
331
      atoms (nodes) in the unit cell of a crystal is n.
332
333   Base Case: When n = 1, the infinite crystal structure represented by the WPDD multi-edge crystal
334   graph is unique.
335   Induction Hypothesis: When n ≤ m, the infinite crystal structure is unique.
336
      Induction Step: Let n = m + 1. Without loss of generality, we safely assume that among the
337
      existing mmm nodes, Nj is the set of nodes forming the local region for node j. Then, j is the
338
      index of the (m + 1)-th node that is newly connected to these nodes. To prove that the infinite
339   crystal structure remains unique, we only need to demonstrate that the relative position of node j is
340   uniquely determined, given the WPDD multi-edge crystal graph. With this, the proposed WPDD
341   multi-edge crystal graph can define a unique infinite crystal structure.
342
      Here, we prove that the relative position of the newly added node j is uniquely determined by the
343
      proposed WPDD multi-edge crystal graph.
344
345   Proof: We use proof by contradiction. First, assume that there exist two distinct relative positions j
346   and j ′ that have the same WPDD multi-edge crystal graph, and we show that this assumption leads
347   to a contradiction.
348   Since UPDD is constructed based on the size of the unit cell, when the number of atoms in the
349   unit cell is relatively small, it could theoretically result in different crystal structures, where all
350   atoms have the same Euclidean distances and atom types but inconsistent atomic positions (which
351   do not exist in the real world), sharing the same crystal graph representation. According to the
352   WPDD multi-edge crystal graph construction process described in Section 4.3, if two distinct crystal
353   structures have the same WPDD crystal graph, their WPDD and the atomic types embedded by
354
      CGCNN must be identical. Since the WPDD crystal graph, which includes atomic information, is
      completely invariant, different crystal structures must have distinct WPDD crystal graphs, Relevant
355
      details can be found in Appendix 5. This contradicts the assumption. Hence, the proof is complete.
356
      Therefore, the proposed identical crystal graph can represent only the same infinite crystal structure.
357   Then, based on Definition 3, we complete the proof of Proposition 2.
358
359   Finally, we conclude that the UPDD crystal graph can only guarantee continuity, while the WPDD
360
      crystal graph can ensure both continuity and completeness.

533
534   In this study, we integrated WPDD and UPDD into the representation of crystal structures, achiev-
535   ing a complete and continuous construction of crystal graphs. This resolves the ambiguity in crystal
536   graph representations for predicting the properties of crystalline materials and bridges the gap be-
537   tween traditional crystal descriptors and dynamic atomic behavior. Experimental results demonstrate
538   the significant advantage of our WPDDFormer in various property prediction tasks. Ensuring the
539   completeness and continuity of crystal graphs after incorporating angular information is a problem
      that will be further explored in the future.

851   in the unit cell, P = [p1 , . . . , pN ] ∈ R3×N denotes the 3D Cartesian coordinates of these atoms,
852   L = [ℓ1 , ℓ2 , ℓ3 ] ∈ R3×3 is the lattice vector matrix. The infinite crystal structure is:
853
                   P̃ = {p̃i = pi + h1 l1 + h2 l2 + h3 l3 | h1 , h2 , h3 ∈ Z, i ∈ Z, 1 ≤ i ≤ n} ,

      have f (X , P, L) = f (X , QP + b, QL).

885   A.4.2    P ROOFS OF INVARIANCE
886
      Proof of Unit Cell E(3) Invariance and Periodic Invariance. If the PDD multi-edge graph we
887   construct exhibits E(3) invariance and periodic invariance, then every step in the crystal graph con-
888   struction process must conform to the crystal constraints. Therefore, we analyze the construction
889   process of the crystal graph to progressively demonstrate E(3) invariance and periodic invariance.
890
      First, we construct a crystal graph with n nodes using a minimal unit cell structure containing n
891
      atoms. This step has been handled by the JARVIS and MP datasets. Since all minimal unit cell
892
      structures for a given crystal share the same number of atoms and corresponding atomic features,
893   this step is E(3) invariant and periodically invariant.
894
895   After determining the selection of atoms, we begin to establish edge information connecting neigh-
896
      boring nodes for each atom. An edge is established from node j to node i when the Euclidean dis-
                     2                                                  2                                       2
897   tance |ej ′ i | between a duplicate of j and i satisfies |ej ′ i | = |pj + k1′ l1 + k2′ l2 + k3′ l3 − pi | ≤ r,
898
      where r ∈ R is the cutoff radius. We select the nearest t edges within the cutoff radius, each with
                                            2                                   2
899
      a corresponding edge feature |ej ′ i | . The Euclidean distance |ej ′ i | between duplicates j and i re-
      mains invariant under E(3) transformations and different representations of the unit cell structure.
900
      Thus, the neighborhood information for node i is E(3) invariant and periodically invariant.
901
902   Finally, we establish the crystal structure representations for WPDD and UPDD. For WPDD, we
903   select k nearest neighbors based on Euclidean distance to create the corresponding WPDD row for
904   each node i. For UPDD, we center around node i and select atoms from the reconstructed unit cell
905
      to create the corresponding UPDD row for each node i based on Euclidean distance. The Euclidean
      distance remains invariant under E(3) transformations and different unit cell structures. Thus, the
906
      PDD row of node i is both E(3) invariant and periodic invariant.
907
908   By combining these three steps in the construction process of crystal graphs, we complete the proof
909   that the proposed PDD crystal graph representation is E(3) invariant and periodic invariant.
