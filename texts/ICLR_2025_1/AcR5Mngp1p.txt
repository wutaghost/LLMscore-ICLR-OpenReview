009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models are exposed to privacy risks since they are trained on large
012            text corpus, which may include sensitive or private information. Therefore, ex-
013            isting studies have attempted to unlearn undesirable knowledge exposed without
014            permission from a language model. However, they are limited in that they have
015            overlooked the complex and interconnected nature of knowledge, where related
016
               knowledge must be carefully examined. Specifically, they have failed to evalu-
               ate whether an unlearning method faithfully erases interconnected knowledge that
017
               should be removed, retaining knowledge that appears relevant but exists in a com-
018
               pletely different context. To resolve this problem, we first define a new concept
019            called superficial unlearning, which refers to the phenomenon where an unlearn-
020            ing method either fails to erase the interconnected knowledge it should remove or
021            unintentionally erases irrelevant knowledge. Based on the definition, we introduce
022            a new benchmark, FaithUnBench, to analyze and evaluate the faithfulness of un-
023            learning in real-world knowledge QA settings. Furthermore, we propose a novel
024            unlearning method, KLUE, which identifies and updates only knowledge-related
025            neurons to achieve faithful unlearning. KLUE categorizes knowledge neurons us-
026            ing an explainability method and updates only those neurons using selected un-
027
               forgotten samples. Experimental results demonstrate that widely-used unlearning
               methods fail to ensure faithful unlearning, while our method shows significant
028
               effectiveness in real-world QA settings.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) are trained using a vast text corpus and perform various tasks,
034   demonstrating outstanding achievements (Radford et al., 2019; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Kassem
035   et al., 2023; Gemma et al., 2024). However, LLMs may show privacy risks since sensitive or private
036   information may be unintentionally included in the large text corpus used for training (Jang et al.,
037   2023; Patil et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). Therefore, prior studies have investigated unlearning
038   undesirable knowledge in language models (Jang et al., 2023; Chen & Yang, 2023; Maini et al.,
039
      2024; Jin et al., 2024). To assess the results of unlearning, most existing studies primarily focus
      on whether a model successfully forgets the specific knowledge to unlearn, while ensuring that
040
      irrelevant knowledge remains unaffected.
041
042   However, they are limited in that they have overlooked the complex and interconnected nature of
043   knowledge, where related knowledge must be carefully investigated. Specifically, these studies have
044   failed to evaluate whether an unlearning method effectively erases interconnected knowledge that
045
      should be removed, retaining knowledge that appears relevant but exists in a completely different
      context. This phenomenon can be further exacerbated when attempting to unlearn complicated world
046
      knowledge. Figure 1 presents an example of faithful unlearning in the world knowledge setting. In
047
      this case, an unlearning method aims to unlearn the specific knowledge related to the target question,
048   “What is the country of citizenship of Tom Cruise?” from a language model. To ensure successful
049   unlearning, the language model should forget the knowledge for answering the paraphrased question,
050   “Which country is Tom Cruise a citizen of?”, and the multi-hop question, “What is the continent of
051   the country where Tom Cruise holds citizenship?” since they share interconnected knowledge with
052   the target question. However, another question, “What country is Andy Warhol a citizen of?” should
053   be responded unchanged after the unlearning process, even though it shares the same answer as the
      target question and superficially appears to involve interconnected knowledge.

066   To address this gap, we first define superficial unlearning, which refers to the phenomenon where
067   an unlearning method either fails to erase the interconnected knowledge it should remove or uninten-
068   tionally erases irrelevant knowledge. Based on the definition, we introduce FaithUnBench (Faithful
069   Unlearning Evaluation Benchmark for Real-world Knowledge Question Answering), a new bench-
070   mark for more deep analysis and evaluation of unlearning methods. FaithUnBench consists of three
071   types of datasets for evaluating faithful unlearning: Paraphrased QA, Multi-hop QA, and Same-
072   answer QA datasets. Three datasets are used to evaluate whether unlearning methods faithfully un-
073   learn the interconnected knowledge while retaining knowledge that appears superficially relevant
074
      but exists in a different context.
075   Furthermore, we propose a novel method, KLUE, which stands for Knowledge-Localized
076   UnlEarning, to achieve faithful unlearning by precisely identifying and updating only knowledge-
077   related neurons. Specifically, we utilize attribution (Yang et al., 2023), an explainability method for
078   language models, to categorize neurons for updating by quantifying the amount of information each
079   neuron contains for predicting the answer to a particular question. However, the quantified score
080
      may include superficial knowledge simply influencing the probability of a target output, regardless
      of the context. Therefore, we newly propose a robust knowledge regularization method to accurately
081
      quantify the knowledge score of each neuron, removing the superficial contribution of neurons.
082
      After identifying knowledge neurons, our method precisely unlearns the target knowledge without
083   affecting other knowledge by updating only the knowledge-related neurons with selected unforgot-
084   ten samples. We demonstrate that most unlearning methods fail to ensure faithful unlearning in the
085   FaithUnBench setting. However, our method significantly outperforms the baselines in the FaithUn-
086   Bench setting, and these results prove that the knowledge-localized unlearning effectively achieves
087   faithful unlearning. In summary, this work makes the following contributions:
088       • We first define superficial unlearning and construct a new benchmark, FaithUnBench, to eval-
089         uate various aspects of it in real-world knowledge QA settings.
090       • We reveal that existing unlearning methods do not ensure faithful unlearning, raising new
091         research questions in the field of knowledge unlearning.
092       • To achieve faithful unlearning, we propose a novel unlearning method, KLUE, which accu-
093         rately identifies and updates only knowledge-related neurons. We demonstrate that KLUE sig-
094         nificantly outperforms the widely-used baselines in the FaithUnBench setting.

105   Benchmark datasets for evaluating these unlearning methods include WHP (Who is Harry Potter)
106   (Eldan & Russinovich, 2023), which prevents the generation of content related to Harry Potter, and
107   TOFU (Maini et al., 2024), which focuses on erasing information about fictionally created charac-
      ters. However, existing studies (Shi et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Li et al.; Maini et al., 2024; Jin

114   2.2   K NOWLEDGE L OCALIZATION FOR L ANGUAGE M ODELS
115
116   Although language models demonstrate remarkable performance, illuminating the exact role of each
117   parameter in dealing with specific knowledge remains challenging. Therefore, Yang et al. (2023;
118   2024) has identified knowledge neurons by extending the attribution (Shrikumar et al., 2016), an
119
      explainability method that determines the importance of individual features in solving a task. Yang
      et al. (2023; 2024) has confirmed that attribution effectively identifies knowledge neurons of various
120
      categories (e.g., language understanding, social bias) and introduced a knowledge neuron detection
121
      method suitable for language modeling tasks. In this study, we follow Yang et al. (2023; 2024) to
122   localize world knowledge neurons for unlearning language models, ensuring faithful unlearning.

268
269   To evaluate the faithfulness of unlearning methods, we first split the forget set Cf , the retaining set
      Cr , and the test set Ct from the entire Base QA dataset C. Then, we train a language model using the

      forget set and the retaining set to unlearn the forget set while maintaining knowledge of the retaining
271   set. Then, we evaluate an unlearned model to the test set to illuminate the knowledge retention for
272   unseen data. Furthermore, we evaluate the unlearned model with the other datasets (i.e., Cp , Cm , and
273   Cs ) mapped to the forget and test sets to analyze the aspect of superficial unlearning.
274
      Our unlearning framework consists of two types of input formats: (1) general QA format, and (2)
275
      multiple-choice QA (MCQA) format. We use the general QA format for unlearning and the MCQA
276
      format for evaluation. The general QA format inputs a question without an additional template and
277   the MCQA format uses a template including an instruction and answer options. Suppose we aim
278   to unlearn the knowledge of the question “Who is the mother of Barack Obama?”, then we train a
279   language model not to output the correct answer (i.e., Stanley Ann Dunham) using only the question
280   as an input. However, many users use a language model under various templates with instructions,
281   and an unlearned model should be evaluated in a more strict environment considering the general-
282   ization. Furthermore, evaluation considering all the possible answers to a question is one of the most
283   challenging aspects of evaluating QA tasks. Therefore, we utilize the MCQA form to evaluate an
284   unlearned model. This setting makes it easier for LLMs to derive knowledge since they are given
285
      answer options; thus, it makes unlearning algorithms harder to work. For this reason, we use the
      MCQA setting to evaluate unlearned models in more challenging and practical settings.

288   3.5     E VALUATION M ETRICS .
289
      We propose various metrics to evaluate the basic unlearning performance and the superficial unlearn-
290
      ing performance. We use exact match to calculate the score of all metrics. (1) Unlearning Accuracy
291   (UA): We compute accuracy for the forget set Cf to evaluate the basic unlearning performance. (2)
292   Extended Unlearning Accuracy (UA‡ ): We compute accuracy for the Paraphrased QA set Cp to
293   evaluate the generalized unlearning performance. (3) Test Accuracy (TA): We compute accuracy
294   for the test set Ct to evaluate whether unseen instances in the unlearning process are well maintained.
295   (4) Same-answer Test Accuracy (SA): We compute Accuracy for the Same-answer QA set Cs to
296   evaluate the preservation of irrelevant knowledge. An unlearning algorithm may only superficially
297   degrade the probability of the answer regardless of context. (5) Multi-hop Test Accuracy (MA):
298   We compute accuracy for Cm matched with each instance of Cf and Ct to evaluate whether the inter-
299   connected knowledge of instances is effectively unlearned. To derive the aggregated MA score, we
300
      first compute the individual accuracies, MAf for Cm mapped to Cf and MAt for Cm mapped to Ct ;
      then, we compute the aggregated score, MA, by averaging the scores, (100−MAf ) and MAt . Al-
301
      though the number of samples in Ct is generally higher than in Cf , we average the scores with equal
302
      weight, as we consider unlearning samples in Cf important due to significant privacy concerns. (6)
303
      Total Score (Score): We aggregate all the evaluation scores by averaging (100−UA‡ ), TA, SA, and
304
      MA, to present the overall performance.

309   In this section, we describe the process of quantifying and localizing a particular knowledge for
310   a language model. Specifically, we first compute an attribution score of each neuron for inferring
311   an answer to a given question. Then, we regularize the attribution scores to exclude superficial
312
      knowledge. Finally, we identify top-n neurons using the regularized attribution score and update
      only the gradients of those neurons, masking the gradients of others in the unlearning process.

      4.2     K NOWLEDGE - LOCALIZED U NLEARNING
357
      This section describes the process of knowledge-localized unlearning. We first select only unforgot-
358   ten samples from the forget set Cf and compute loss for the selected samples to mitigate overfitting
359   and superficial unlearning. Then, KLUE determines knowledge neurons to unlearn and finally up-
360   dates only the gradients of the selected knowledge neurons to achieve faithful unlearning.
361
      Unforgotten Samples Selection. If we repeatedly unlearn sufficiently unlearned samples, the train-
362
      ing procedure leads to overfitting and harms the generalization ability of a language model. There-
363
      fore, we select only the samples that have not been forgotten in the unlearning process to preserve
364   the generalization performance of the language model. Specifically, we select and unlearn only ques-
365   tions that satisfy the knowledge memorization criteria (Definition 1) for unlearning by each epoch’s
366   unlearning process.
367
      Knowledge Neuron Localization. After selecting unforgotten samples, we localize and update only
368
      the knowledge neurons corresponding to those selected samples in the language model. Specifically,
369
      we first compute gradients of parameters for the selected unforgotten samples. Then, we quantify the
370   knowledge relevance of each neuron by using the equations 3 and 4, and sort neurons of the whole
371   target layers by the knowledge relevance scores; then, we select the top-n knowledge neurons. We
372   finally mask gradients of the parameters for knowledge-irrelevant neurons to exclude them from
373   the unlearning process. Suppose that a weight matrix W ∈ Rd×k is a linear matrix multiplication
374   parameter of a language model, and the gradient computed for the parameter is ∇W L = ∂L/∂W .
375   Then, the gradient of i-th neuron (i.e., column) of the weight matrix after masking is denoted as
376   ∇W:,i L̃ = γ ⊙ ∇W:,i L, where γ ∈ {0d , 1d } and ⊙ means the Hadamard product. We also can
377   mask bias terms similar to the weight matrix. Notice that this method is model-agnostic since all
      neural network models consist of linear transformation layers.

      5.2      BASELINES
418
419   We evaluate widely-used unlearning algorithms to unveil the superficial unlearning. (1) Gradient
420   Ascent (GA): Unlike the gradient descent used during the pre-training phase, GA (Jang et al., 2023;
421   Yao et al., 2023) maximize the negative log-likelihood loss on the forget set. This method helps
422   shift the model away from its original predictions, aiding in the unlearning process. (2) Gradient
423   Ascent with a Retaining Loss (GAret ): GA tends to unlearn other unrelated knowledge since it
424   just maximizes the negative log-likelihood loss on the forget set. Therefore, we add an auxiliary
425
      retention loss to maximize the log-likelihood of the retaining set, securing the retention of other
      irrelevant knowledge. (3) Direct Preference Optimization (DPO): We adopt preference optimiza-
426
      tion to unlearn a language model to generate another answer. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024; Jin et al.,
427
      2024) utilizes positive and negative instances to train the model. Therefore, we select the correct
428   answer as the negative instance and also define two types of DPO methods to determine positive
429   ones: (1) DPOmis (DPO using a mismatched answer) and (2) DPOneg (DPO using a rejection an-
430   swer). DPOmis utilizes a randomly sampled answer as the positive instance. On the other hand,

      ”same-answer QA evaluation”, which assesses whether unlearning algorithms degrade output prob-

787

788

789   ’The Shining’?”. The two questions are quite related, but they are not completely interconnected
790   like multi-hop questions. In conclusion, the main contribution of our benchmark lies in evaluating
791   whether unlearning methods perform faithful unlearning while considering knowledge interconnec-
792   tion within the real-world entity unlearning setting.

848   B.2.2    S EQUENTIAL VS . BATCH U NLEARNING
849
850   We conduct experiments on Gemma-2 2B to show the performance variation for varying numbers
851   of samples unlearned in each batch. We select 5% of neurons to unlearn. We adopt various batch
852   size β ∈ {1, 4, 8, 16, 32} for the experiments, shown in Figure 7. The experimental results reveal
853   that KLUE is not effective for sequential unlearning (β = 1) and large batch unlearning (β = 32).
854
      Sequential unlearning localizes the neurons to unlearn for only the single data sample, causing the
      language model to forget all the knowledge since the number of neurons to unlearn is too large for
855
      each data sample; thus, the localized area covers not only the specific knowledge but also natural
856
      language understanding knowledge or general QA knowledge. In contrast, a large batch size makes it
857   hard for a language model to unlearn the knowledge since it can not identify appropriate knowledge
858   neurons from the attribution computed by large samples.
