009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have recently garnered widespread attention due
012            to their high computational efficiency and low energy consumption, possessing
013            significant potential for further research. Currently, SNN algorithms are primarily
014            categorized into two types: one involves the direct training of SNNs using sur-
015            rogate gradients, and the other is based on the mathematical equivalence between
016
               ANNs and SNNs for conversion. However, both methods overlook the exploration
               of mixed-timestep SNNs, where different layers in the network operate with dif-
017
               ferent timesteps. This is because surrogate gradient methods struggle to compute
018
               gradients related to timestep, while ANN-to-SNN conversions typically use fixed
019            timesteps, limiting the potential performance improvements of SNNs. In this pa-
020            per, we propose a Quantization-Aware Conversion (QAC) algorithm that reveals
021            a profound theoretical insight: the power of the quantization bit-width in ANN
022            activations is equivalent to the timesteps in SNNs with soft reset. This finding
023            uncovers the intrinsic nature of SNNs, demonstrating that they act as activation
024            quantizers—transforming multi-bit activation features into single-bit activations
025            distributed over multiple timesteps. Based on this insight, we propose a mixed-
026            precision quantization-based conversion algorithm from ANNs to mixed-timestep
027
               SNNs, which significantly reduces the number of timesteps required during in-
               ference and improves accuracy. Additionally, we introduce a calibration method
028
               for initial membrane potential and thresholds. Experimental results on CIFAR-10,
029
               CIFAR-100, and ImageNet demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms
030            previous approaches.

033   1   INTRODUCTION
034
035   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), as the third generation of neural networks, are inspired by the way
036   biological neurons transmit information through spikes(Maass, 1997). Neurons in SNNs communi-
037   cate using sparse and discrete spikes, with complex membrane potential updates and neural dynamic
038   processes (Izhikevich, 2003; Ghosh-Dastidar & Adeli, 2009), which gives SNNs higher biological
039
      plausibility compared to traditional Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs). Recently, SNNs have gar-
      nered widespread attention due to their energy-efficient computational paradigm. It is well known
040
      that the human brain operates at approximately 20W of power while containing around 86 billion
041
      neurons(Herculano-Houzel, 2009), enabling it to perform complex reasoning and decision-making
042   tasks. In contrast, current state-of-the-art artificial intelligence models require vast computational
043   resources(Brown, 2020; Patterson et al., 2021; Xu & Poo, 2023), including hundreds of server racks
044   and thousands of GPUs to support inference, consuming substantial amounts of energy. This signifi-
045   cant energy consumption has raised widespread concerns due to its high economic cost. As a result,
046   researchers are turning to SNNs, relying on their energy-efficient computing paradigm to reduce
047   computational energy costs.
048   There are fundamental differences in the computational mechanisms between SNNs and ANNs
049   (Pfeiffer & Pfeil, 2018). The computation in ANNs can be simplified as performing linear transfor-
050   mations within each layer, while continuous real-valued information is passed between layers via
051   differentiable nonlinear activation functions. In SNNs, the results of linear transformations accu-
052   mulate in the neuron’s membrane potential, and spikes are triggered by non-differentiable nonlinear
053   activation functions (Stöckl & Maass, 2021). The information passed between layers is in the form
      of discrete spike signals. Due to the advantage of sparse spike-based computation, recent studies

054
      have focused on fully leveraging the energy-efficient characteristics of SNNs to achieve low-power
055   computational models.
056
057   Currently, SNN learning methods are mainly categorized into two approaches: The first is through
058
      surrogate gradient methods, which replace the non-differentiable activation functions of SNNs with
      differentiable functions, enabling efficient training via backpropagation(Lee et al., 2016; Neftci
059
      et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2018b; Lee et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021b). The second
060
      approach exploits the mathematical equivalence between ANNs and SNNs, where the firing rates of
061   SNN spikes approximate the activations of ANNs, allowing pre-trained ANNs to be converted into
062   SNNs(Cao et al., 2015; Diehl et al., 2015; Rueckauer et al., 2016; Sengupta et al., 2019; Tavanaei
063   et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021a; Bu et al., 2023).
064
      Our work focuses primarily on the ANN-to-SNN conversion algorithm, aiming to achieve low-
065
      timestep, low-power, and high computational efficiency inference by converting pre-trained ANNs
066
      into SNNs. Specifically, our contributions include the following:
067
068          • We propose Quantization-Aware Conversion (QAC), an ANN-to-SNN algorithm based on
069            mixed precision quantization, which can obtain SNNs with mixed time steps and high
070            accuracy. In addition, we revealed the equivalence between the quantization bit-width of
071            ANN activations and the timesteps in SNNs with soft-threshold resets.
072          • We found that when the weights are fixed, the residual membrane potential is related to the
073            initial membrane potential and the threshold. We introduced a method for calibrating the
074            initial membrane potential and the threshold to further improve the model’s accuracy.
075
             • The results on the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet datasets demonstrate that, com-
076            pared to previous conversion methods, our approach achieves higher accuracy with fewer
077            time steps. For instance, ResNet18 achieves 95.29 % accuracy on CIFAR-10 using only
078            2.76 time steps.

      2   RELATED WORKS
082
      Mixed Precision Quantization. Early quantization approaches typically applied the same quanti-
083
      zation bit-width across different layers (Zhang et al., 2018; Choi et al., 2018; Bhalgat et al., 2020).
084
      However, different layers in neural networks exhibit varying sensitivities to quantization. When con-
085   strained by a uniform average bit-width, using the same quantization bit-width across all layers can
086   lead to performance degradation (Dong et al., 2019). To address this issue, mixed-precision quanti-
087   zation methods assign different bit-widths to different parts of the model to balance ”performance-
088   efficiency”: layers more sensitive to quantization are allocated higher bit-widths to minimize per-
089   formance loss due to quantization, while layers less sensitive to quantization are assigned lower
090   bit-widths to reduce storage and computational demands. The current mixed-precision quantiza-
091   tion approaches can be categorized into four types: (1) Optimization based on sensitivity metrics:
092   HAWQ (Dong et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2021) were among the first proposed methods for mixed bit-
093
      width quantization. These methods use loss and Hessian matrix information of model weights to
      gauge the quantization sensitivity of each layer and select quantization bit-widths accordingly. (2)
094
      Optimization using reinforcement learning: Methods like ReLeQ Elthakeb et al. (2020) and HAQ
095
      (Wang et al., 2019) employ reinforcement learning to allocate mixed bit-widths. Their state space
096   includes different quantization bit-widths, and the reward is either the ratio of quantized accuracy
097   to floating-point accuracy or a combination of task performance and simulated hardware perfor-
098   mance. (3) NAS-based solutions: DNAS (Wu et al., 2018a) draws on differentiable search works
099   DARTS(Liu et al., 2018), utilizing gradient-based methods to optimize bit-widths. Subsequent ef-
100   forts like HMQ (Habi et al., 2020) fall under this category. (4) Learning-based mixed precision
101   quantization solutions: (Uhlich et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Yang & Jin, 2021)
102   ANN-to-SNN Conversion. The initial studies on ANN-to-SNN conversion were undertaken by
103   (Cao et al., 2015), (Diehl et al., 2015; Rueckauer et al., 2016; Sengupta et al., 2019) further nar-
104   rowed the gap between ANNs and SNNs through scaling and normalization of weights. Han &
105   Roy (2020) proposed the use of soft-reset spiking neurons to further reduce conversion errors and
106   minimize information loss. (Deng & Gu, 2021; Li et al., 2021a)revealed conversion errors by catego-
107   rizing them into clipping and quantization error. (Ho & Chang, 2021) introduced Trainable Clipping
      Layers (TCL) to set thresholds effectively. (Bu et al., 2023) building on (Li et al., 2021a) work,

259   Motivation A key insight in spiking neural networks (SNNs) is that different layers exhibit varying
260   sensitivities to the number of timesteps. When all layers are constrained to use the same number
261   of timesteps, model performance is often suboptimal due to this uniform limitation. As a result,
262   attempting to enhance accuracy by uniformly increasing timesteps across layers can lead to substan-
263   tial inference delays on the order of O(N T ), where N is the number of layers and T is the timestep
264   count. To simultaneously improve performance and minimize inference latency, it is essential to
265   allocate different timesteps to different layers.
266   Theorem 1 establishes the relationship between quantization bit-width in ANNs and timesteps in
267   SNNs. Mixed-precision quantization assigns varying bit-widths across layers, denoted as {n1 , n2 ,
268   ..., nl }, to balance performance and efficiency: higher bit-widths are allocated to more sensitive
269   layers to mitigate performance loss, while lower bit-widths are used for less sensitive layers to
      reduce storage and computational overhead. Based on Theorem 1 and the conversion framework

348   Here, X̂ l+1 is the expected input of the l + 1-th layer and f (·) represents the temporal alignment
349   function, responsible for adjusting the temporal dimension from Tl to Tl+1 to ensure consistency in
350   the temporal dimension across layers. Depending on the specific scenario, f (·) can involve Tem-
351   poral Expansion (e.g., Averaging or Replication) or Temporal Reduction (e.g., Averaging or
352   Truncation) to align the timesteps between successive layers. Specifically, equation 11 can be writ-
                           PTl
353   ten as : X̂ l+1 [t] = i=1   w(t, i)X l+1 [i], t = 1, 2, . . . , Tl+1 , where w(t, i) is the f (·) function
354   that adjusts the contribution of the i-th timestep of X l to the new timestep t in X̂ l+1 . The specific
355   form of w(t, i) depends on the temporal alignment method: Replication: w(t, i) selects the near-
356   est corresponding timestep based on integer indexing. Averaging: w(t, i) = T1l evenly distributes
357   the influence of all timesteps from the previous layer. Truncation: w(t, i) = 1 if t ≤ Tl+1 and
358   i = t, otherwise w(t, i) = 0. Thus, truncation is effectively retaining the first Tl+1 time steps and
359   discarding the remaining ones from Tl .
360   Temporal Expansion: If T1 < Tl+1 , timestep expansion must be used to increase the temporal
361   resolution. For example , given the input activation X l , with Tl = 4, and the next layer Tl+1 = 8,
362   each timestep can be repeated twice by using timesteps replication method, which simply repli-
363   cates timesteps to match the required timesteps of the subsequent layer. However, when (Tl+1
364   mod Tl ) ̸= 0, simple timestep replication leads to uneven distribution of time information. For
365   instance, when Tl = 4 and Tl+1 = 5, it becomes impossible to evenly distribute the repeated time
366   steps across the new time dimension. In this case, some timesteps may be excessively duplicated,
367   while others may not receive the necessary expansion.
368   Temporal Reduction: If T1 > Tl+1 , timestep reduction can be applied to decrease the temporal
369   dimension. For example, if Tl = 8 and the next layer requires Tl+1 = 3, the timesteps truncation
370   method will select Tl+1 timesteps of data and discard the rest, thus matching the required dimension.
371   However, this may lead to the loss of some information contained in the discarded time steps.
372   Temporal Averaging Expansion Alignment: To achieve temporal alignment for temporal expan-
373   sion and reduction, we propose using the temporal averaging expansion method to adjust the input

      6                CONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a mixed-timestep SNNs conversion method Quantization-Aware Conver-
532
      sion (QAC) that enables low-timestep, high-accuracy SNNs. We first demonstrate that the power of
533
      the quantization bit-width of ANN activations is equivalent to the timesteps in SNNs, showing that
534   SNNs act as activation quantizers. Following this, we propose using mixed-precision quantization to
535   train activation-quantized ANNs, where each layer of the network is assigned an optimal bit-width,
536   and the converted SNNs achieve the best possible timesteps and accuracy, resulting in near-lossless
537   conversion from ANNs. To explore the initialization of membrane potentials, we introduce a cali-
538   bration method in which the final layer output of the quantized ANN serves as the target to calibrate
539   the initial membrane potential and thresholds of the SNNs. Experimental results on CIFAR-10,
      CIFAR-100, and ImageNet demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed methods.

1174
1175   Using QAC to build mixed timestep SNNs allows them to run on SNN hardware while preserv-
1176   ing the asynchronous nature of SNNs. SNN hardware has two mainstream implementation ap-
1177   proaches: ANN accelerator variants and non-Von Neumann distributed multi-core architectures
1178
       (e.g., TrueNorth Akopyan et al. (2015), Loihi Davies et al. (2018)) Li et al. (2024a).
1179   ANN accelerator variants primarily achieve asynchronous computation by sending non-zero inputs
1180   to processing element PE arrays and performing spike-based matrix calculations. These accelera-
1181   tors only compute one part of the neural network at a time, iterating to cover the entire network.
1182   Algorithm 2 Li et al. (2024b) shows the data flow, where the timestep for each layer is T . For
1183   mixed-timestep SNNs, we modify the timestep Tl for each layer and average the outputs of each
1184
       layer along the time dimension. These two operations do not change the original data flow, allowing
       the model to run on this type of hardware.
1185
1186   In contrast, multi-core neuromorphic hardware deploys the neurons of all layers across different
1187   cores. When neurons receive spike events, they immediately perform spike-based computations,
       achieving asynchronous execution. The network runs on hardware in a pipelined manner. As shown

       7.8     E NERGY C OMPARISON .
1228
1229
       To compare the energy consumption of SNNs (Spiking Neural Networks) and quantized ANNs
1230   (Artificial Neural Networks), we conducted experiments on VGG-16 using the CIFAR-100 dataset.
1231   We assume all weights are represented in 8-bit integers (INT8). Based on the energy consumption of
1232   32-bit and 8-bit fixed-point and floating-point operations provided in (Horowitz, 2014), we calculate
1233   the energy for a multiply-accumulate (MAC) operation, which is the sum of the energy required for
1234   multiplication and addition.
1235
       However, since the activations in the quantized ANN use mixed-precision quantization (with varying
1236   bit widths across layers), calculating the energy for MAC operations involves handling fixed-point
1237   multiplications with varying bit widths. To address this, we refer to the findings in (Potipireddi &
1238   Asati, 2013), which indicate that the energy consumption of an adder scales linearly with bit width,
1239   while the energy consumption of a multiplier scales quadratically with bit width . This enables us
1240   to calculate the energy consumption for MAC operations involving fixed-point numbers of varying
1241   bit widths.In contrast, SNNs only require addition operations, eliminating the challenges posed by
       varying bit widths in quantized ANNs.
