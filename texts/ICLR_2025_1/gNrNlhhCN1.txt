009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Asymmetric clustering has remained an unexplored problem in Clustered Federated
012            Learning (CFL), diverging from the traditional approach of forming independent,
013            non-interacting clusters. Previous methodologies have been limited to either sepa-
014            rating devices with different data quality into distinct clusters or merging all devices
015            into a single cluster, both of which compromise either data utilization or model
016            accuracy. We propose a new federated learning technique where some devices may
017            contribute to the training of the models of other devices, but without enforcing
018
               reciprocity, leading to a form of asymmetric clustering. This is beneficial in a
               variety of situations including scenarios where it is desirable for a device with high
019
               quality data to help train the model of a device with low quality data, but not vice-
020
               versa. This method not only enhances data utilization across the devices, but also
021            maintains the integrity of high-quality data. Through a rigorous theoretical analysis
022            and empirical evaluations, we demonstrate that our approach can efficiently find
023            high quality (asymmetric) clusterings for numerous devices, achieving competitive
024            performance metrics on existing CFL benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028   Federated learning (McMahan et al., 2017) is a machine learning technique designed to train algo-
029   rithms across decentralized devices while keeping data localized, thus addressing privacy, security,
030
      and data access challenges. Unlike traditional centralized machine learning methods where all data
      is uploaded to one server, federated learning allows for the model to be brought to the data source
031
      where training occurs. This approach is particularly valuable in scenarios where data privacy is im-
032
      portant, such as in healthcare, finance, and mobile computing. For example, smartphones that utilize
033   predictive text input features can improve their models using federated learning by learning from
034   user interactions without ever needing to upload individual typing data to a central server. However,
035   federated learning introduces complexities such as handling non-IID (independently and identically
036   distributed) data across various devices, dealing with devices that have varying computational and
037   storage capacities, and managing communication costs and efficiencies.
038   In practice, it is common for devices to encounter data from diverse distributions. Since heterogeneous
039   data may induce different optimal predictors at different devices, this has led to the development
040   of personalized federated learning techniques (Fallah et al., 2020). A popular approach consists of
041   training a global predictor that is adapted or fine-tuned for each device. However, this assumes that the
042   optimal predictors at each device are similar enough that fine-tuning / adapting a global predictor will
043   be sufficient. In cases where some optimal predictors are very different and fine-tuning is insufficient,
044   then clustered federated learning (Sattler et al., 2020; Mansour et al., 2020) becomes attractive. For
045
      instance, in mobile keyboard prediction, where users from different regions have distinct linguistic
      preferences and slang, fine-tuning a single global model may not be effective; clustered federated
046
      learning, on the other hand, allows for creating separate models for different linguistic groups to
047
      ensure that predictions remain relevant and accurate. In clustered FL, devices are partitioned in
048   clusters such that devices share models only with the other devices in their cluster. When clusters
049   combine devices with similar data while making sure that devices with very different data are in
050   different clusters, then learning will be more effective. Existing techniques for clustered FL (Sattler
051   et al., 2020) can learn clusters dynamically. However, most existing techniques assume a fixed number
052   of clusters that is known a priori and all existing techniques assume that each device contributes to a
053   single cluster. As we will explain later, this last assumption is suboptimal in asymmetric situations
      where training with the data of a device could help improve the prediction accuracy of other clusters

      in addition to the cluster that this device belongs to. We describe a technique that relaxes those two
055   assumptions.
056
      Determining the correct number of clusters beforehand can be challenging. This is particularly true in
057
      federated learning environments where data is distributed across numerous devices with potentially
058   diverse data distributions. Static clustering methods that assume a fixed number of clusters can lead
059   to inefficiencies and inaccuracies, as they might not accommodate the dynamic nature of real-world
060   data, which can vary in terms of volume, variety across different devices.
061   Adaptive clustering addresses this limitation by employing algorithms that dynamically adjust the
062   number of clusters based on the evolving characteristics of the data. Instead of pre-defining a cluster
063   count, adaptive clustering methods continuously analyze the incoming data and modify the cluster
064   count in real-time. This flexibility allows the learning process to maintain high levels of efficiency
065   and adaptability.
066   In federated learning, asymmetric scenarios often arise where the benefits of model sharing are not
067   reciprocal between devices. For instance, consider a situation involving two devices, device A and
068   device B. Device A has a large dataset characterized by the underlying conditional distribution
069   pA (y | x), whereas Device B has a smaller dataset with a similar conditional distribution pB (y | x)
070   that matches pA (y | x) for 90% of the inputs x. For device A, incorporating device B’s data could
071   potentially introduce a bias that might degrade the accuracy of its own model because of the 10%
072   divergence in their data distributions. Thus device A would not wish to train on data from device
073
      B. On the other hand, for device B, clustering with device A could significantly reduce variance
      owing to the greater volume of data it would benefit from, thereby enhancing its overall performance
074
      (reduction in variance outweighs the bias introduced). For example, regarding keyboard prediction,
075
      smartphone users of a rare dialect may benefit from the model trained with a large user base of a
076   similar common dialect, but not vice-versa.
077
      The key contributions of our research are outlined as follows:
078
079          • Introduction of Asymmetric Clustering: We propose the novel concept of asymmetric
080            clustering, enabling a more flexible and dynamic cluster formation that better reflects the
081            diversity of data quality and distribution among devices.
082
             • Development of FAACL: We implement asymmetric clustering within the framework of
               clustered federated learning, which not only groups non-reciprocal devices into distinct
083
               clusters but also establishes inter-cluster relationships.
084
             • Integration of statistical tests and bounds: We incorporate a robust statistical test, the
085            Wilcoxon signed rank test (Wilcoxon, 1992) and Hoeffding’s bound (Hoeffding, 1994) to
086            guide the cluster formation process.
087          • Empirical Validation: Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate FAACL’s competi-
088            tive performance compared to traditional baselines, and its effectiveness and scalability in
089            diverse federated environments.

132
      To enable asymmetric clustering, we propose to add a third component to the definition of each
133
      cluster Cj :
134
135          • Component 3: A set of supportive clusters, Cj .sup.
136   Supportive clusters are designed to establish inter-cluster relationships. Suppose that cluster CA
137   requires help from another cluster, CB , to train its model, then CB becomes a supportive cluster to
138   CA , indicated by CB ∈ CA .sup. Both members of Cj .D and members of supportive clusters in
139   Cj .sup contribute to training the model Cj .θ, though only the members of Cj .D will use model
140   Cj .θ for prediction. For instance, Cluster A may benefit from being grouped with Cluster B, while
141   Cluster B prefers to remain separate. Forcing a merger between Clusters A and B could reduce the
142   accuracy of Cluster B’s model. Conversely, keeping both clusters separate does not fully utilize the
143
      available data. To address this challenge, supportive clusters are introduced. By designating Cluster
      B as a supportive cluster to Cluster A, members of Cluster B can assist in training Cluster A’s model
144
      without directly using it for their own predictions. This approach enhances data utilization and model
145
      accuracy through inter-cluster collaboration.

147   3    R ELATED W ORK
148
      Clustered Federated Learning (CFL) represents a significant advancement in managing distributed
149
      data across various devices. This subsection reviews key methodologies and their respective contribu-
150   tions to the field.
151
152          • Iterative Federated Clustering Algorithm (IFCA) (Ghosh et al., 2020) starts with a
153
               predefined number of cluster models at the server. Devices determine their cluster identity
               based on which models minimize their local loss.
154
             • Federated Stochastic Expectation Maximization (FeSEM) (Xie et al., 2021) begins with
155
               a fixed number of clusters and iteratively assigns devices to the nearest cluster based on
156            the L2 distance of the model parameters. Each cluster updates its model by averaging the
157            models of the assigned devices.
158          • FedGroup (Duan et al., 2021) clusters devices according to their gradient cosine similarity
159            and facilitates both inter-cluster and intra-cluster training alongside device migration.
160          • FedSoft (Ruan & Joe-Wong, 2022) operates similarly to IFCA but introduces flexibility by
161            allowing devices to belong to multiple clusters. Each cluster’s importance is determined
               based on the local loss for each data point.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • FedDrift (Jothimurugesan et al., 2023) is designed for continual learning. It starts with an
163            assumption of homogeneous data distribution but can adapt to changes by initiating new
164            clusters when significant shifts in data distribution are detected through loss comparison.
165          • CFL-GP (Kim et al., 2024) partitions devices into groups with similar accumulated gradi-
166            ents by spectral clustering.
167          • SR-FCA (Vardhan et al., 2024) successively refines a clustering of devices by bottom up
168            aggregation based on a cross-model loss.
169   Despite these advancements, two primary limitations persist in CFL:
170
171          • Fixed Number of Clusters: Except for FedDrift and SR-FCA, most of the previous
172
               approaches use a fixed number of clusters for device grouping. This fixed cluster count
               presents a challenge, as it requires certain prior knowledge and needs to be accurate. If the
173
               initial guess for the number of clusters is too low, devices with varying data distributions
174
               may be incorrectly grouped together, leading to suboptimal predictors for those devices.
175            Conversely, an excessive number of clusters can scatter devices with similar data distributions
176            across different clusters, resulting in suboptimal predictors due to a reduced amount of data
177            for cluster model training.
178          • Symmetric Clustering Limitations: There is no natural extension from symmetric cluster-
179            ing to asymmetric clustering. Devices either support each other or they do not. Traditional
180            CFL approaches typically restrict each device to a single cluster (e.g., IFCA, FeSEM), or, as
181            seen in soft clustering methods like FedSoft, allow devices to influence multiple clusters
182            without adequately considering the overall impact on cluster integrity. In environments
183
               where data quality varies considerably, such strategies may compromise the robustness of
               clusters initially dominated by high-quality data, thus failing to balance individual benefits
184
               with collective goals effectively.

188   We propose a new method called Federated Adaptive Asymmetric Clustering (FAACL) that addresses
189   the two limitations identified in the previous section. We first describe how to initialize clusters
190
      (Sec. 4.1), merge clusters (Sec. 4.2) and train the model of each cluster (Sec. 4.3). These procedures

193   4.1   C LUSTERING I NITIALIZATION
194
      Our approach to clustering initialization in Clustered Federated Learning adopts an intuitive strategy.
195   In the initial phase, we create a unique cluster for each device, denoted as Ci .D={di }, effectively
196   forming singleton clusters (i.e., clusters containing only a single device). This is illustrated Algorithm
197   2 in Appendix A. Upon completion of the initialization, we obtain a total of n such singleton clusters.
198   Given the isolated nature of these initial clusters, the subsequent phase of our methodology focuses
199   on the inter-device communication. This is achieved by merging similar clusters to reduce the total
200   number of clusters, thereby enhancing the collaborative learning process among the devices.
201
      4.2   C LUSTER M ERGE
202
203   Following the initialization phase, which results in n distinct clusters, we employ an iterative process
204   to merge similar clusters based on support evaluations. The merging process and support evaluations
205   are outlined in Algorithms 3 to 5 in Appendix A. Each cluster’s supportive connections are updated
206
      by exchanging models among devices from different clusters.
207   Model Evaluation and Support Determination: For each pair of clusters, we assess if cluster C2
208   supports cluster C1 based on their model performances across all devices in cluster C1 . This is
209   determined by comparing the model parameters C2 .θ and C1 .θ. Specifically, we calculate losses for
210   each data point z in the validation set Zdval for a given device d: ℓ(θC1 , zdval ) and ℓ(θC2 , zdval ), where
211   ℓ(θC , z) denotes the loss of model parameters θC on data point z. To assess whether cluster C2 is
212
      supportive of cluster C1 , we implement two alternative approaches: direct mean comparison and the
      application of a statistical test.

318   cluster participates in subsequent iterations of cluster merging and cluster updating as usual. When a
319   device leaves the federation, it is simply removed from the support and membership of each cluster it
320   used to contribute to. If this device was part of a singleton cluster, that cluster is deleted. Subsequent
321   iterations of cluster merging and cluster updating proceed as usual.

342   4.6   P RIVACY
343
344   Although data does not leave each device in federated learning, the models that are shared may leak
345   information about the data used to train them (Mothukuri et al., 2021; Boenisch et al., 2023). Hence
346   it is common to combine federated learning with a differential privacy (DP) mechanism (Wei et al.,
347   2020) and secure multi-party computation (MPC) (Bonawitz et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020). MPC is
348
      typically used to secure the aggregation step at the server, but the resulting aggregated model may still
      leak data information, especially if it memorizes some of the data. Hence, to provably ensure privacy,
349
      DP is often the preferred mechanism. FAACL can be combined with a local DP mechanism (Shokri
350
      & Shmatikov, 2015) by adding noise to the weights of each model before sharing. In Algorithm 6,
351   each device can add Gaussian noise as a function of sensitivity to achieve a desired degree of privacy
352   at the end of each round of training. Models can then be shared with clusters and other devices while
353   statistically preventing membership attacks at the cost of a reduction in accuracy.

355   5     E XPERIMENTS
356   In this section, we present empirical results that validate our proposed methods within two distinct
357   experimental scenarios: asymmetric and symmetric.
358
             • Asymmetric Scenario: This scenario addresses the complexity arising from the diversity in
359            device data distributions. It explores situations where devices are not reciprocal.
360          • Symmetric Scenario: Devices either share identical data distributions or possess completely
361            contrasting distributions. The formation of clusters is such that devices within the same
362            cluster either mutually benefit or detrimentally affect each other’s model training outcomes.
363            Optimal performance is achieved when devices with similar distributions are clustered
364            together, and those with divergent distributions are separated.

385   Version 1 employs the Wilcoxon signed-rank test to evaluate support between clusters, making it
386   suitable for data with non-normal loss distributions. It leverages statistical testing to ensure that
387   differences in model performance are statistically insignificant before considering clusters supportive.
388
      Version 2 utilizes direct comparison of mean losses between clusters, applying Hoeffding’s bound
389
      to provide error estimation guarantees. It is beneficial when theoretical robustness is required,
390
      determining support based on if mean loss differences fall within a predefined acceptable range.
391
392   5.2 A SYMMETRIC S ETTING
393   In the asymmetric setting, we explore a more complex scenario where devices’ data distributions are
394   not entirely contradictory, and some devices may benefit from collaborating with others from different
395   distributions. This setting is designed to showcase the adaptability and performance advantages of
396   our proposed federated learning technique in creating asymmetric clusters. We design experiments
397   with both natural and synthetic partitions with respect to data quality and quantity.
398   Natural Data Partition (A0 ) Utilizing the FEMNIST subset of the LEAF dataset, we simulate a
399   realistic asymmetric scenario. For instance, in the FEMNIST dataset, each device corresponds to a
400
      unique writer, allowing us to simulate a realistic asymmetric scenario. Given that different writers
      produce images of varying quality for classification, asymmetric clustering becomes beneficial. In
401
      such a setup, devices associated with high-quality writers can lend support to those linked to lower-
402
      quality writers, demonstrating the utility of asymmetric clusters in enhancing overall performance.
403   Synthetic Image Partition (A1 ) In this setup, one group of devices receives data with pristine
404   quality (no noise), while another group handles data contaminated with Gaussian (Shannon, 1948)
405   and salt & pepper noise (Castleman, 1996). This partition serves to illustrate the need for asymmetric
406   clustering, where devices with noisy data can benefit from the cleaner inputs of other devices. It tests
407   the algorithm’s ability to optimize learning outcomes in the presence of varying data quality.
408   Synthetic Data Amount Partition (A2 ) We construct a scenario where one set of devices has access
409   to abundant data, while another set is limited in data quantity and exhibits slightly different predictors.
410   Asymmetric clustering plays a crucial role in such environments. Devices with enough data, although
411
      reluctant to merge due to predictor discrepancies, can still offer valuable insights to devices with
      sparse data. Conversely, devices with limited data can leverage the more extensive datasets of others
412
      to reduce variance, even at the risk of introducing some bias.
413
414   5.3 S YMMETRIC S ETTING
415   In the symmetric setting, our objective is to evaluate the adaptability and effectiveness of our proposed
416   federated learning technique under conditions of either homogeneous or extremely heterogeneous
417   data distributions among devices. We conduct these evaluations using both natural and synthetic data
418   partitions to simulate various distribution scenarios.
419   Natural Data Partition (S0 ): This setup simulates a scenario where each device’s data is indepen-
420   dently and identically distributed. This setup tests the algorithm’s ability to recognize and maintain
421   uniformity across devices in a federated environment.
422   Synthetic Label Partition (S1 ): In this setup, the dataset is allocated among devices based on
423
      distinct label ranges. For example, one set of devices might exclusively receive data corresponding to
      labels 0-4, while another set of devices receives data with labels 5-9. In this scenario, since devices
424
      with different label distributions are less likely to benefit each other, forming separate clusters for
425
      each label distribution is considered optimal.
426   Synthetic Predictor Partition (S2 ): In this partition, datasets of devices from different distributions
427   have different underlying predictors. For instance, in the MNIST dataset, one group of devices (set 1)
428   may map images directly to their corresponding labels, while another group (set 2) maps images to
429   shifted labels (e.g., mapping the image of digit 0 to label 1). This setup challenges the algorithm’s
430   ability to handle scenarios with significant variations in data mappings across devices. In such cases,
431   combining data from different distributions can be harmful, indicating the need for distinct clusters to
      maintain the integrity of each device’s predictive model.

483   6 C ONCLUSION
484   In this study, we introduced the novel concept of asymmetric clustering to address scenarios in
485   federated learning where clustering benefits are unevenly distributed—some devices benefit from
      joining a new cluster, while others may experience detrimental effects. To tackle this challenge,

487   facilitates the formation of asymmetric clusters.

926   B     S YNTHETIC DATA G ENERATION AND DATA PARTITION
927
928   Here, we provide a breakdown of how synthetic data is created for each setting.
929   Asymmetric Image Partition (A1 ): The devices are partitioned equally into two clusters. The
930   dataset is split equally and uniformly at random into the devices of the first cluster. The devices of
931   the second cluster receive copies of the data splits as for cluster 1, but each image is perturbed with
932   Gaussian, and salt and pepper noise. This configuration simulates scenarios where data quality or
933   noise levels vary among devices.
934
      Asymmetric Data Amount Partition (A2 ): The devices are partitioned equally into two clusters.
935   Devices in the first cluster receive abundant data while devices in the second cluster receive limited
936   data. The dataset is split equally and uniformly at random into the devices of the first cluster. For the
937   second cluster, one fifth of the dataset is sampled and partitioned equally and uniformly at random
938   among the devices. In addition, for the devices of the second cluster, the labels of digits 1, 2, and
939   3 are aggregated into a single class with label 2. This partitioning mimics scenarios where devices
940   receive varying amounts of data and varying label granularity.
941   Symmetric Label Partition (S1 ): The devices are partitioned equally into two clusters. The data
942   with labels 0 to 4 is split equally and uniformly at random into the devices of the first cluster. The
943   data with labels 5 to 9 is split equally and uniformly at random into the devices of the second cluster.
944   This design simulates scenarios where devices have access to data with distinct label ranges.
945
      Symmetric Predictor Partition (S2 ): The devices are partitioned equally into two clusters. The
946
      dataset is split equally and uniformly at random into the devices of the first cluster. The devices of
947   the second cluster receive data with shifted labels (i.e., class 0 is relabeled as 1, class 1 is relabeled as
948   2, ..., class 9 is relabeled as 0). The shifted version of the data set is split equally and uniformly at
949   random into the devices of the second cluster. This partitioning aims to simulate situations where
950   devices have different underlying predictors for the same inputs.
