009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series imputation is critical for many real-world applications and has been
012            widely studied. However, existing models often require specialized designs tailored
013            to specific missing patterns, variables, or domains which limits their generalizabil-
014            ity. In addition, current evaluation frameworks primarily focus on domain-specific
015            tasks and often rely on time-wise train/validation/test data splits, which fail to
016            rigorously assess a model’s ability to generalize across unseen variables or do-
017            mains. In this paper, we present NuwaTS, a novel framework that repurposes
018            Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) for general time series imputation. Once
019
               trained, NuwaTS can be applied to impute missing data across any domain. We
               introduce specialized embeddings for each sub-series patch, capturing information
020
               about the patch, its missing data patterns, and its statistical characteristics. By
021
               combining contrastive learning with the imputation task, we train PLMs to create a
022            versatile, one-for-all imputation model. Additionally, we employ a plug-and-play
023            fine-tuning approach, enabling efficient adaptation to domain-specific tasks with
024            minimal adjustments. To evaluate cross-variable and cross-domain generalization,
025            we propose a new benchmarking protocol that partitions the datasets along the
026            variable dimension. Experimental results on over seventeen million time series sam-
027            ples from diverse domains demonstrate that NuwaTS outperforms state-of-the-art
028            domain-specific models across various datasets under the proposed benchmarking
029            protocol. Furthermore, we show that NuwaTS generalizes to other time series tasks,
030
               such as forecasting.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Time series data are pervasive across numerous fields, including transportation (Li et al., 2018),
036   healthcare (Tonekaboni et al., 2020), education (Mao et al., 2024), and meteorology (Bi et al., 2023).
037   However, real-world time series data often suffer from missing values. Incomplete data complicate
038   various time series applications such as forecasting and classification, ultimately degrading the quality
039
      of data analysis (Ma et al., 2020). This makes time series imputation especially critical (Wang et al.,
      2024a). Traditionally, time series imputation methods have leaned heavily on statistical techniques
040
      like mean imputation and interpolation (Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011). Yet, with the
041
      rise of deep learning, there has been a notable shift towards deep learning models for imputation
042   tasks (Cao et al., 2018; Du et al., 2023).
043
044   In traditional deep learning-based imputation models, the typical protocol involves training a model
045
      on relatively complete or incomplete time series data within a closed domain from historical records.
      During the testing phase, the model is validated or tested on newly observed incomplete data,
046
      which are future observations of the variables from the training set (Cao et al., 2018; Wu et al.,
047
      2022) (Figure 1(b)). However, this approach presents a key limitation: the lack of cross-variable
048   generalization capability. Models trained in this manner may struggle to extend to variables not
049   observed in the training set. For instance, in one particular factory, a newly launched production
050   line generates several new time series data, and it’s uncertain whether the model trained on existing
051   production lines can generalize effectively to those new ones. A more challenging scenario arises
052   when we lack training data from a specific domain and must rely on a model trained from other
053   domains for imputation. This introduces a more complex cross-domain generalization problem (Jin
      et al., 2022; Wilson et al., 2020). For example, generalizing from traffic time series to factory time

079   In this paper, we propose a novel framework and benchmarking methodology specifically designed
080   to address and assess time series imputation methods that are capable of both cross-variable and
081   cross-domain generalization. We draw inspiration from the success of foundation models trained on
082   diverse datasets spanning multiple domains (Kirillov et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020; Peng et al.,
083   2023). These models have demonstrated remarkable ability to generalize across tasks and domains
084   using techniques like fine-tuning (He et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2020) or prompting (Jia et al., 2022;
085   Kirillov et al., 2023). In a similar vein, we propose a method that pushes time series imputation
086
      beyond the traditional single-domain paradigm, enabling generalization both across variables and
      across domains.
087
088   Specifically, we introduce NuwaTS, a foundation model designed for incomplete time series im-
089   putation. Our model segments the time series into patches and employs novel token designs to
090   capture both statistical information and missing data patterns. Additionally, we leverage contrastive
091   learning combined with missing data reconstruction to train an one-for-all imputation model on time
092
      series data from diverse domains. Finally, for cases requiring domain-specific adaptation, we have
      developed a domain-specific prefix embedding and a plug-and-play fine-tuning mechanism. This
093
      mechanism introduces continuous prompts at each layer of the frozen pre-trained foundation model
094
      without modifying its weights, enabling efficient domain specialization.
095
096   Furthermore, we introduce a novel benchmarking paradigm for time series imputation models. Rather
097   than the conventional time-wise train/validation/test partitioning, we partition multivariate time series
098
      data along the variable dimension, allocating different variables to the training, validation, and test
      sets (Figure 1(c)). This approach closely simulates real-world deployment scenarios, where models
099
      trained on one domain are required to impute missing data for entirely new variables and domains,
100
      effectively assessing the model’s cross-variable and cross-domain generalization capabilities.
101
102   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
103

                time series data along the variable dimension for time series imputation, which better
105
                assesses a model’s ability to generalize to new data.
106
107          2. We introduce NuwaTS, designed to handle missing data imputation tasks for any incomplete
                time series. NuwaTS is trained on data from diverse domains and incorporates a light-

118
119   2.1   I NCOMPLETE T IME S ERIES I MPUTATION
120
121   Many time series imputation models are tailored to specific missing data patterns and domains, such
122   as randomly missing traffic time series. For instance, matrix factorization models are designed for
123   multivariate time series imputation (Yu et al., 2016). For a dataset from a specific domain with a
124
      particular missing rate, it often requires optimizing a specialized model using a low-rank prior as the
      optimization target for imputation. Recent advancements in deep learning techniques have shown
125
      promising results in addressing missing data imputation. Generative models such as Generative
126
      Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Luo et al., 2019; Tashiro et al., 2021) and diffusion models are used
127   to learn the underlying distribution of the incomplete time series. Several architectures, such as
128   Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Cao et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Tang et al.,
129   2020) and attention mechanisms (Du et al., 2023), are proposed to capture temporal dependencies
130   within incomplete time series. While achieving good performance on narrow domains and missing
131   data patterns, these models lack versatility and generalizability to diverse domains and missing data
132   patterns.
133
134   2.2   F OUNDATION M ODEL FOR T IME S ERIES
135
136   In recent years, foundational models for time series analysis have made notable strides, primarily
137   leveraging pre-trained backbones from NLP and aligning modalities to extend their reasoning
138   capabilities to time series tasks. For example, Gruver et al. (Gruver et al., 2024) discovered that by
139   encoding time series as strings of numerical digits, LLMs can perform time series forecasting with
140   zero-shot capabilities. GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2024) trains time series models using pre-trained GPT
141
      weights. UniTime (Liu et al., 2024a) integrates domain-specific instructions into LLMs, enhancing
      their generalization across domains. Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) employs LLMs’ reasoning by
142
      framing time series data and statistical information in textual prompts, aligning time patches with
143
      word embeddings via cross-attention mechanisms for improved zero-shot learning. Autotimes (Liu
144   et al., 2024d) utilizes precomputed text embedding as positional embeddings and an autoregressive
145   approach for long-term forecasting. TEST (Sun et al., 2023) uses text-prototype-aligned embeddings
146   to enhance LLMs’ reasoning in time series data. S 2 IP-LLM (Pan et al., 2024) applies seasonal-trend
147   decomposition to time series and uses semantic space-informed prompting to retrieve appropriate
148   prompts from word token embeddings as prefixes. aLLM4TS (Bian et al., 2024) adapts LLMs for time
149   series representation learning by directly converting individual patches into time series sequences.
150   Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024) trains language models from scratch on a large collection of time series
151   data, using scaling and quantization for tokenization. This model demonstrates strong capabilities in
152
      zero-shot probabilistic forecasting. These models primarily focus on time series forecasting and do
      not specifically address missing data issues or the design of embeddings for missing data patterns.

      4        E XPERIMENT
282
283
      We conducted comprehensive experiments on NuwaTS across ten commonly used datasets from
284
      various domains. To facilitate comparison, we provided four versions of the model based on the
285   GPT2 architecture in Figure 4: (a) a specific model trained on a single domain, (b) an one-for-all
286   model trained on a general fused dataset (17.6M collected samples from various domains), (c) a
287   fine-tuned model for a specific domain (fine-tuned based on the one-for-all model) and (d) a cross-
288   domain model pretrained only on LargeST (Liu et al., 2024b) (comprising 100.1 million collected
289   samples). Cross-domain model will be evaluated on time series from other domains to verify the
290   domain-transfer zero-shot capability in Section 4.3. Moreover, we evaluated BERT (Devlin et al.,
291   2019) and LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023) as backbones for NuwaTS (details in Appendix D.5).
292   Baselines. Following the configuration outlined in (Du et al., 2023), we evaluated two naive
293   imputation methods: Median, where missing values are filled with the median value, and Last,
294   where missing values are filled with the last previous observations. To ensure a fair comparison,
295   all methods followed a unified pipeline1 . We assessed two classic deep learning-based imputation
296   models, BRITS(Cao et al., 2018) and SAITS(Du et al., 2023). We also compared our model with
297   other foundation models for time series such as DLinear(Zeng et al., 2023), PatchTST(Nie et al.,
298   2023), iTransformer(Liu et al., 2024c), TimesNet(Wu et al., 2022), Autoformer(Wu et al., 2021),
299
      Fedformer(Zhou et al., 2022), and GPT4TS(Zhou et al., 2024). We trained GPT4TS separately on
      single datasets with the number of layers set to 6 to align with NuwaTS. We trained all these models
300
      using a domain-specific approach. Since PatchTST is a transformer-based model with bi-directional
301
      attention, we also trained and tested it on the general fused dataset.
302
303   Setups. We partitioned the dataset along the sensor (variable) dimension into training,
304   validation, and test sets in a 1:1:1 ratio. This division simulates the process of collecting relatively
305
      complete time series data from a few sensors or variables with lower missing rates, training a model
      with this data, and then using it to impute data from other sensors or variables that have higher
306
      missing rates. The input time series length L is 96, and we trained all baselines under random missing
307
      rates (sampled from 0.1 to 0.9) and tested them separately under 9 missing rates: 0.1, 0.2,..., 0.9. We
308   used a total of ten datasets for domain-specific training and mixed them into a general fused dataset
309   for one-for-all training. Appendix A.1 shows the details of datasets. For the cross-domain model,
310   we chose LargeST2 , a large-scale traffic dataset including a total of 8,600 time series and over 100
311   million samples, to train NuwaTS and conduct zero-shot experiments on other domains such as ETTs,
312   weather and electricity.

503   5       C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this paper, we address the challenging problem of cross-variable and cross-domain generalization
506   in time series imputation. We introduce NuwaTS, a time series imputation model built on a PLM
507   backbone that explicitly captures patch-wise statistical information and missing patterns. Additionally,
508   we propose an efficient domain-specific fine-tuning technique that allows the model to seamlessly
509
      adapt from an all-in-one imputation model to a domain-specific one with minimal cost, and even
      transform into a forecasting model when needed. To benchmark the model’s performance, we
510
      design a novel variable-wise train/validation/test partitioning strategy, providing a rigorous evaluation
511
      framework. Experimental results show that our approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
512   methods, demonstrating strong adaptability across diverse domains and missing rates, even in zero-
513   shot scenarios. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first foundational time series imputation
514   model capable of generalizing across domains. We believe our method sets a new benchmark in the
515   field and provides valuable insights for future research.

      A.1     DATASET D ETAILS
707
708   Our research encompasses experimental evaluations utilizing a selection of ten popular multivariate
709   datasets, including (1) ETT3 reports seven factors of electricity transformers, encompassing four
710   subsets (ETTm1, ETTm2, ETTh1, ETTh2). These are divided into two categories based on temporal
711   granularity, with data recorded at intervals of 15 minutes (m) and 1 hour (h), respectively. (2)
712   ECL4 records the hourly electricity consumption of 321 customers. (3) Weather5 comprises 21
713   meteorological factors, recorded at ten-minute intervals throughout the year 2020, including variables
714
      such as temperature and humidity. (4) PEMS encompasses four datasets (03, 04, 07, and 08), each of
      which collects data on California’s public traffic network at a frequency of every five minutes. For
715
      the LargeST (Liu et al., 2024b), which consists of 8,600 sensors and spans from 2017 to 2021, we
716
      only selected the 2019 data for pre-training NuwaTS in this paper.
717
718   For the eleven datasets mentioned above, we split the data from the variable dimension rather than
719   the time dimension, using a 1:1:1 ratio for training, validation, and test datasets. This approach
720
      is necessary because some baselines use a channel-dependent method, which requires fixed input
      dimensions for time series.
721
722   For the general fused datasets, we processed each time variable separately. We sliced time variables
723   from all datasets (ETT, Weather, ECL, PEMS) of varying lengths with a stride of 1, creating a
724   combined dataset with 17.6 million time segments of fixed length. In this study, we set it to 96. These
725   segments were then randomly masked and used for model training.
726   Using the same approach, we sliced LargeST into 100.1 million segments to train NuwaTS and
727   PatchTST. This was done for zero-shot evaluation and fine-tuning for forecasting tasks on ETT,
728   weather, and ECL, ensuring no data leakage.
