                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Survival models are widely used to model time-to-event or survival data, which
012
               represents the duration until an event of interest occurs. In clinical research, sur-
013            vival analysis is used for estimating the effects of treatments on patient health
014            outcomes. Recent advancements in machine learning (ML) have aimed to im-
015            prove survival analysis methods, but current evaluation practices largely focus
016            on predictive performance, often neglecting critical factors such as the ability to
017            accurately estimate treatment effects and possible consequences on health equity.
018            Estimating treatment effects from time-to-event data presents unique challenges
019            due to the complex problem setting, the extensive assumptions required for causal
020            inference, biased observational data, and the ethical consequences of using model
021
               outcomes in real-world health decisions. In this work, we introduce a compre-
               hensive benchmarking framework designed to evaluate survival models on their
022
               ability to estimate treatment effects under realistic conditions and in the presence
023
               of potential inequalities. We formalize the discussion of bias in survival modelling,
024            identifying key sources of inequity, and outline practical desiderata for methods that
025            model time-to-event treatment effects. We clarify common assumptions in survival
026            analysis, discuss critical shortcomings in current evaluation practices, and propose
027            a new benchmarking metric that can be used to better evaluate model calibration.
028            Using this framework, we systematically compare traditional and modern survival
029            models across multiple synthetic and real world datasets, investigating, among
030            other challenges, model performance under mis-specification and observational
031            biases. Through this benchmark, we provide actionable insights for researchers to
032
               develop more robust and equitable survival models.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   With the rise of methods capable of processing large electronic health records (EHR) datasets, there
038   is growing excitement about using machine learning (ML) to extract new insights from existing
039
      medical data. In particular, observational data could be used to assess the potential impact of various
      clinical treatments on health outcomes, given personalized patient health data Liu et al. (2021); Tan
040
      et al. (2021). The focus of such analyses typically centers on time-to-event data (or survival data),
041
      representing the duration of time until a patient experiences a relevant health outcome (Klein and
042   Moeschberger, 1997; Tutz and Schmid, 2016; Hernan and Robins, 2023). For example, clinical
043   trials investigating cancer treatment often evaluate efficacy based on survival duration or time until
044   disease progression (Reck et al., 2016; Mok et al., 2019). Survival data may come from a randomized
045   controlled trial (RCT) or observational datasets, such as EHRs, which create additional modelling
046   complications (Hernan and Robins, 2023). While many ML for healthcare works focus on predicting
047   survival times directly (Huang et al., 2023), our interest lies in methods for estimating treatment-
048   specific survival models, such as survival or hazard functions, which can be used to determine
049   treatment effects (i.e. a conclusion that a treatment definitively impacts patient outcomes)–quantities
050
      critical to clinical decision-making (Singh and Mukhopadhyay, 2011; Faraone, 2008).
051   Despite the increased adoption of ML for survival analysis and treatment effects estimation, little
052   attention has been given to proper benchmarking and evaluation methods. Estimating treatment effects
053   from time-to-event data poses unique challenges, due to inherent complications such as censoring,
      the potential for biases, as well as the causal assumptions required for identifiability(Hernan and


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Robins, 2023). Many survival methods rely on restrictive modelling assumptions that may not hold
055   in practice. As treatment effect estimates can influence real-world medical decisions, a thorough
056   understanding of the methodology is crucial, especially of possible impacts on health equity. Key
057   challenges include: (1) complex data generating scenarios, (2) modelling assumptions violations, and
058   (3) sources of model bias (4) potential sources of inequity. In this paper, we propose a benchmarking
059   framework for the evaluation of survival methods used to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects
060   from time-to-event data, addressing these key challenges.
061   Related work. Survival models can be categorized into 1) classical statistical survival models, which
062   may be parametric, such as the logistic hazard model (Tutz and Schmid, 2016), semi-parametric, such
063   as the Cox proportional hazards model (CoxPH) (Cox, 1972), and non-parametric, such as the Kaplan-
064   Meier method (Kaplan and Meier, 1958); and 2) modern ML survival models, including tree-based
065   and neural network approaches (Wang et al., 2017). ML methods such as neural networks (Nagpal
066   et al., 2021; 2020; Katzman et al., 2016), random forests (Ishwaran et al., 2008; Cui et al., 2020), and
067   Gaussian processes (Fernandez et al., 2016; Alaa, 2017) have also been applied to survival analysis.
068   The Cox PH is widely used to estimate hazard ratios in clinical and epidemiological research, but its
069
      causal interpretation has faced controversy due to common methodological flaws and assumption
      violations in practice (Hernán, 2010; Martinussen et al., 2020; Martinussen, 2021). Tutz and Schmid
070
      (2016) provides a detailed discussion of discrete-time survival methods, Wang et al. (2017) provides
071
      a review of machine learning for survival analysis, and Wiegrebe et al. (2023) reviews deep learning
072   methods specifically. We provide details on notable approaches in Appendix A.1.
073
074   Existing benchmarks or comparison studies of ML (and classical) methods for survival analysis have
075
      focused on empirically evaluating models on predictive ability (Zhang et al., 2021; Spooner et al.,
      2020), rather than fidelity to the ground truth hazard or survival models, which are necessary to
076
      estimate treatment effects. These works also do not investigate model performance in the presence of
077
      assumption violations, known biases (such as confounding or informative censoring), or impacts on
078   health equity. Works proposing new methods for survival analysis often investigate model perfor-
079   mance over few synthetic scenarios, which cannot comprehensively inform model behavior (Katzman
080   et al., 2016; Cui et al., 2020). Related benchmarking works in the realm of ML for health have focused
081   on estimation of continuous treatment effects (Curth et al., 2021b; Crabbe et al., 2022) or fairness
082   in medical imaging (Zong et al., 2022). To our knowledge, our paper is the first to systematically
083   evaluate survival models from a causal perspective.
084   Contributions. 1) We provide a comprehensive discussion and formalization of key biases and
085   challenges that arise in survival analysis, particularly in the context of treatment effect estimation.
086   These include biases due to confounding, informative censoring, and model mis-specification, with a
087   focus on impacts on health equity. 2) We introduce a benchmarking framework, including a novel
088   evaluation metric, designed to evaluate the ability of survival models to estimate heterogeneous, time-
089   varying treatment effects in the face of various complications. 3) Through extensive experiments on
090   both synthetic and real-world datasets, we provide critical insights into the performance of traditional
091   and modern survival models as well as a guide for improved benchmarking practices.

      3    C HALLENGES : ESTIMATING TREATMENT EFFECTS FROM SURVIVAL DATA
134
135
      Heterogeneity and effect modification. While RCTs typically report population-level ATEs, these
136
      can be misleading when treatment effects vary across different values of covariates X, known as effect
137
      modifiers. In such cases, HTEs are more informative; relying solely on ATEs is problematic, especially
138   for health equity. For example, women and people of color have been historically under-represented
139   in clinical trials, likely leading to biased ATE estimates that do not reflect diverse populations (Chien
140   et al., 2022). As covariates are likely to influence treatment effects, HTEs are a more useful and fair
141   quantity to focus upon, particularly as developments in ML make HTE estimation feasible. Treatment
142   effects can also vary over time, complicating estimation further.
143
      Conditions for estimation of causal treatment effects. Clinical research aims to determine the
144   causal effects of treatments on health outcomes, reflecting how treatments would impact the same
145   population in a counterfactual world. Because multiple treatments cannot be applied to the same
146   individuals, estimating causal effects from observed data requires adherence to identifiability con-
147   ditions. Time-to-event data introduces additional challenges due to censoring and also relies on
148   exchangeability, the assumption that the counterfactual risk in the treated population is the same as
149   in the entire population if everyone were treated. If conditional exchangeability holds, conditional
150   causal effects can be estimated via methods like inverse propensity weighting (Hernan and Robins,
151   2023). We depict causal graphs of the time-to-event problem setting in Fig. 1, adapted from related
152
      work (Nagpal et al., 2022). See detailed discussion of causal identifiability in App. A.4.
153   Confounding and selection bias. Observational data may be subject to confounding (Fig. 1b), where
154   treatment effects are obscured by a common cause of both treatment assignment and patient outcome.
155   Both RCT and observational data may be subject to selection bias, where the analyzed population is
156   selected on a common cause (or effect) of both treatment and outcome (Hernan and Robins, 2023).
157   Censoring can be a form of selection bias, if the censoring mechanism depends on a covariate that
158
      affects treatment outcomes. Confounding and selection bias complicate treatment effects estimation
      by disrupting exchangeability and may also result in covariate shifts (Curth et al., 2021a) in the
159
      analyzed population, biasing model estimates.
160

      leads to bias during model estimation, particularly in the presence of model misspecification (Shi-

189   clinical trials, while researchers continue to investigate methods for causally interpretable estimation
190   of HRs (Axelrod and Nevo, 2022; Adib et al., 2020).
191
      Health equity. In survival analysis for clinical research, health equity concerns often arise across
192
      subgroups defined by protected attributes, such as race, ethnicity, or gender. Due to space constraints,
193   the relevant figure (Fig. 2) can be found in App. A.3. Fig. 2 highlights three ways subgroup member-
194   ship S can drive inequities. 1) Directly, where S affects both survival times and the mechanism of
195   bias (red arrows): this could lead to inequities via the strength of the impact (i.e., subgroup is strongly
196   associated with assignment to a certain treatment) or subgroup prevalence in the data. 2) Indirectly,
197   via covariate shift (blue arrows): patient covariates X and subgroup S may be dependent, such that
198   the distribution of X differs across subgroups, while P(Y |X), the outcome distribution conditional
199   on covariates, remains the same. Model performance can degrade for covariates that are underrep-
200   resented in the training data, due to the presence of ‘harder’ examples of X or infrequent/unseen
201
      values of X (Cai et al., 2023) and exacerbated by small datasets or mis-specified models (Shimodaira,
      2000). Because certain demographic groups have been historically underrepresented in clinical trials,
202
      unbalanced subgroup distributions leading to model bias is a significant equity issue. 3) Indirectly,
203
      where S affects the distribution of unmeasured variables Z (green arrows). This causes outcome shift,
204   such that P(Y |X) differs across subgroups and conditional exchangeability no longer holds. This
205   can exacerbate health inequity if the frailties Z are dependent on protected attributes S.

      4.1   C ONSTRUCTING AN EVALUATION SCENARIO
217
218   Discrete vs. continuous time. Survival times drawn from either discrete-time or continuous-time
219   distributions can be used for the evaluation of both discrete-time and continuous-time methods.
220   However, it is necessary to be mindful of the distinctions. For example, take a scenario where we
221   have generated survival times from a discrete-time model to test a continuous-time method. The
222   resultant continuous-time hazard function must be converted into discrete-time, using Equation 2, to
223   evaluate against a ground truth discrete-time model.
224   Data generating components. Generating synthetic survival data requires the following compo-
225   nents: a treatment assignment mechanism, A ∼ Bernoulli(α(x)), event and censoring processes
226   h(t|a, x) and hc (t|a, x) (or other survival models from Table 1), which define how frequently an
227   event/censoring event occurs, and patient covariates. Covariates can be generated synthetically or
228   sampled from real datasets to mimic realistic experimental conditions.
229   Model mis-specification. Parametric and semi-parametric methods require the underlying event
230   process to adhere to an assumed form that may not reflect the true data distribution. For example,
231   we can evaluate the CoxPH in situations where the proportional hazards assumption is violated. We
232   can evaluate parametric models, such as the exponential model, against misspecified data generated
233   from a log-logistic distribution. Time-varying models may be mis-specified with respect to the time
234   function. Table 5 summarizes types of mis-specification and what methods are affected.
235   Heterogeneous treatment effects. Treatment effects may be heterogeneous from two perspectives:
236   the event/censoring processes are dependent on covariates or the causal contrasts are dependent on
237   covariates. The former refers to a scenario where ha (t|x) ̸= ha (t). The latter refers to a scenario
238   where, if we define the causal contrast as the hazard ratio, HRA (t|x) ̸= HRA (t).
239
      Time-varying treatment effects. Similar to heterogeneity, treatment effects may be time-varying
240   from two perspectives: the event/censoring processes are dynamic, or the treatment effects (causal
241   contrasts) are dynamic. For example, survival times drawn from an exponential distribution reflect
242   a constant hazard function, where h(t) = λ. A time-varying hazard function does not necessarily
243   imply time-varying causal contrasts. For example, if ha (t) = λt exp(0.2a), HRA (t) = exp(0.2),
244   which is constant over time. A time-varying contrast is HRA (t) = exp(0.1t), which would result
245   from ha (t) = exp(0.1a · t). This complication can be combined with the above to generate
246   heterogeneous and time-varying treatment effects, such that HRA (t|x) = exp(0.1t + 0.2x), from
247   h(t|a, x) = exp(0.1a · t + 0.2a · x), or heterogeneously time-varying treatment effects, such that
248
      HRA (t|x) = exp(0.1t · x), from h(t|a, x) = exp(0.1a · t · x).
249   Observational bias. Bias can be incorporated into a synthetic scenario via the inclusion of common
250   effects, defined as patient covariates. Confounding occurs when there is a common cause of treatment
251   assignment and patient outcome. For example, if A ∼ Ber(σ(x1 )) and ha (t|x) = exp(a · x1 + x2 ).
252   Selection bias occurs when the at-risk population is selected based on two variables: treatment or
253   cause of treatment and outcome or cause of outcome. We can create a censoring mechanism that
254
      incorporates selection bias if we make it dependent on variables that satisfy this definition. For
      example, if treatment assignment and the hazard function are defined as above, the censoring hazard
255
      hac (t|x) = exp(a · x1 ) leads to censoring bias. Recall that survivorship bias occurs in any situation
256
      where there exist covariates (including treatment) that affect survival.
257
258   Violation of identifiability assumptions. Real world data is likely to violate causal identifiability
259   assumptions. The conditional exchangeability assumption does not hold if there are unmeasured
260
      confounders, or, in the presence of censoring, there are any unmeasured variables that affect both
      censoring and outcome. To create scenarios that violate these assumptions, we can incorporate
261
      variables into the data generating models for treatment assignment, event hazard, and censoring
262
      hazard that are withheld during model training.

269   as our primary evaluation metric. In the context of survival analysis, we say a model is perfectly
      calibrated if the estimated hazard function equals the true hazard function. It remains to determine

278   Motivation for MALE. MALE has two properties which make it an ideal performance metric. First,
279   it takes its minimum value 0 if and only if the model is perfectly calibrated, i.e., iff the estimated
280   hazard function is equal to the true hazard function. Second, a bound on MALE (which is in terms of
281   hazards) also corresponds to a natural bound on the difference in log failure probabilities between the
282   model and the ground truth. In contrast, while other seemingly natural measures such as the mean
283
      squared error of the true vs. estimated hazard share the property that they are minimized only by the
      ground truth, bounds on these quantities give no guarantees about the relative error of the computed
284
      survival probabilities. Formal statements and proofs of these qualities can be found in Appendix D.1.

      5.4   S UBGROUP FAIRNESS : SURVIVORSHIP BIAS
487
488   In this section, we investigate one possible source of inequity in the survival setting: survivorship
489   bias linked to protected attributes, s, which indirectly affect bias via covariate shift on measured
490   covariates, x. In the appendix, we also report results on an experiment investigating inequity caused
491   by covariate shift on unmeasured covariates, z, which are affected by subgroups s.
492   Setup. We now examine a setting where participants may belong to subgroups defined by protected
493   attributes, s. Even when there are no observational biases (such as confounding or informative
494   censoring) and all covariates that affect treatment outcome are measured, we still contend with the
495   issue of survivorship bias. Survivorship bias may be a source of inequity when, for example, subgroup
496   membership indirectly affects this bias via covariate shift on X, such that P(X|S). Here, the hazard
497   function h(τ |a, x, s) is complicated further by the presence of subgroups. In our experimental setup,
498   we draw x0 based on subgroup membership s, where x0 |s = 0 ∼ N (µ0 , σ 2 ) and x1 |s = 1 ∼
499   N (µ1 , σ 2 ). We vary subgroup means µ0 and µ1 across experiments (by column) to investigate
500
      impacts on model performance as the overlap between subgroups decreases. We also vary the
      proportion of subgroups P (s = 1) = π (shown on y-axis). We use hazard function piece1 , which is
501
      constant-time, non-linear, piecewise function where Ihaz = {0} (x0 is the only covariate that affects
502
      the hazard). We report MALE values, averaged over the first 75th percentile of survival times.

504   Results. We report findings in Figure 11 which can be found in Appendix C.3 due to space constraints.
505   Each row of the figure shows an experiment with different subgroup means, (µ0 , µ1 ), and each column
506
      is associated with a treatment a and subgroup s pair. When the subgroup distributions are closer
      together (first row), model performance generally remains similar across treatment, subgroup pairs,
507
      with the exception of DeepHit and RSF, where performance is significantly worse for (a = 1, s = 0)
508
      and (a = 0, s = 1). We find that this disparity increases as the subgroup means get further apart

511   effect in the columns corresponding to subgroup 1, particularly when a = 0; performance improves

520   6     D ISCUSSION
521
522   We have provided a comprehensive discussion of the time-to-event problem setting, alongside the
523   requisite assumptions for causal inference of treatment effects and significant challenges faced
524   during model design and estimation. We provide recommendations for benchmarking and evaluating
525
      methods for time-to-event treatment effect estimation, and evaluate the Cox proportional hazards
      model and the extended Cox model under this paradigm. In these experiments, we expose the
526
      common biases that the Cox model exhibits under different event settings, finding that the it is not
527
      robust to model misspecification (either due to the parameterization of the distribution or unmeasured
528   confounders/effect modifiers) and is easily biased by certain covariate shifts. Future work should
529   focus on further examining the Cox model with respect to these issues and towards the development
530   of methods to overcome these particular forms of covariate shift.
531
      This work also has several limitations. Most notably, we consider a specific setting for time-varying
532
      treatment effects, wherein the treatment is set at the beginning of analysis and covariates are measured
533
      at the beginning of analysis (but may contribute to time-varying effect modification). However,
534   clinical data may actually include time-varying covariates, time-varying treatments, and multiple
535   treatments. In addition, patients may be subject to competing events, where the patient outcomes are
536   caused by multiple mechanisms. There are also many more forms of selection bias, including selection
537   that occurs before a trial/before analysis, affecting generalizability of findings from analysis to the
538   general population. For example, eligibility criteria from clinical trials may restrict the investigated
539   population to a subset that is not reflective of the eventual patient population. This is a form of
      selection bias also known as sampling bias and affects the transportability of clinical trials outcomes.

794   A.1.1    T HE C OX PROPORTIONAL HAZARDS MODEL
795
796   We discuss the Cox proportional hazards model (Cox PH) (Cox, 1972) with more detail, due to its
797   popularity. The Cox PH is a semi-parametric approach to modelling hazard functions in continuous-
798
      time, with the primary goal of calculating hazard ratios. For estimation of average treatment effects,
      the hazard function for the Cox PH is defined as h(t|a) = h0 (t) exp(βA). Here, the baseline hazard
799
      h0 (t) is assumed to be the same across treatment groups and differs only by a constant (over time)
800
      scaling factor, exp(βA), which is dependent on the treatment assignment. This is the proportional
801   hazards assumption, which is violated if the treatment effect coefficients β vary over time. The Cox
802   PH model is estimated with a partial likelihood (Cox, 1975) that relies on the censoring at random
803   assumption and treats the baseline hazard, h0 (t), as a nuisance parameter that is not required in
804   the likelihood definition and not estimated during model inference. However, if a baseline survival
805   model is desired, the Breslow estimator is commonly employed to estimate the cumulative baseline
806   hazard (Lin, 2007). The Cox PH model is useful in that it does not require any assumptions regarding
807   the parametric form of the baseline hazard and the common issue of right-censoring is handled in
808   model inference. Additionally, the Cox PH presents a straightforward estimation of the hazard ratio,
809
      which is often desired as a metric for the comparison of treatment effects. The Cox PH marginal
                                            h(t|A=1)

      However, there are two major issues with this definition of the hazard ratio: 1) dependence on the
811   proportional hazards assumption, and 2) survivorship bias. Recalling the definition of the hazard
812   ratio from Equation 6, we note that the hazard ratio is meant to be interpreted as a contrast between
813   treatment groups of the probability of event at a specific moment in time, t, conditioned on survival
814   until that time. However, the hazard ratio given by the Cox PH model really reflects a weighted
815   average of the hazard ratios over the entire time period of 0 ≤ u ≤ t (Stensrud and Hernán, 2020),
816   rather than the hazard ratio at the specific moment in time, t. Researchers have proposed to resolve
817   this by reporting a series of period-specific hazard ratios in order to reflect time-varying hazards (Lin
818   et al., 2019). This is problematic due to issue (2) noted in the previous paragraph; if treatment-specific
819
      event processes differ, the distributions of the at-risk treatment groups diverge over time and lack
      exchangeability (Bartlett et al., 2020). The conditional Cox model follows by incorporating patient
820
      covariates X in the model in the same format as the treatment assignment variable, A. Under strict

      Consistency is required because treatments must be well-defined in order to then estimate their causal
865   effects (Rubin, 1980; 1986). Because it is not possible to administer the same treatments to the
866   same individuals to determine the impact of a single treatment, estimation of causal treatment effects
867   relies on the concept of exchangeability (Robins and Hernán, 2008). Exchangeability holds when the
868   counterfactual outcome and the assigned treatment are independent, such that the counterfactual risk
869   (of some health outcome) in the treated population is the same as the counterfactual risk (of some
870   health outcome) in the entire population, had the entire population been treated. However, as the
871   risk of treatment is actually observed in the treated population, it can be held true across the entire
872   population (Hernan and Robins, 2023). It is sufficient to require conditional exchangeability if there
873
      are no unmeasured confounders, as methods such as inverse propensity weighting can be used to
      adjust for confounders to estimate average causal effects (Robins and Hernán, 2008). Positivity is
874
      required as the causal effects of a treatment on patients can only be assessed if representative patients
875
      have received the treatment (Hernan and Robins, 2023). These conditions motivate the design of
876   RCTs, where, in ideal settings, all identifiability conditions are achieved by construction (Hernan
877   and Robins, 2023). Consistency is achieved through complete adherence to the assigned treatment
878   protocol, which is carefully designed. Exchangeability and positivity are achieved via randomization
879   of treatment assignments. While these assumptions are untestable for observational data, leading to
880   the presence of unmeasured variables (Z in Figure 7), practitioners can use expert knowledge and
881   careful problem framing in order to improve plausibility.

      A.5.3    I MPACT ON EXCHANGEABILITY
939
940   While either confounding or selection bias can lead to a lack of exchangeability, if conditional ex-
941   changeability holds, it is possible to estimate conditional causal effects and to recover exchangeability
942   via methods such as standardization or inverse propensity weighting (Hernan and Robins, 2023).
943   Conditional exchangeability is achieved in the presence of confounding if there are no unmeasured
944   confounders, such that any covariate that affects both treatment assignment and outcome is measured
945   and accounted for, and in the presence of selection bias, if there are no unmeasured covariates that
946   affect both the selection mechanism and outcome. For example, when selection occurs through cen-
947   soring, conditional exchangeability is achieved if all covariates that affects the censoring mechanism
948
      are measured (see Assumption 4, Section A.4).

1034   A.9    M ITIGATING CONFOUNDING AND SELECTION BIAS
1035
1036
       Confounding and selection bias can lead to (1) a lack of exchangeability, complicating causal effect
       estimation, and (2) covariate shift, which can lead to bias in model estimation, particularly if the model
1037
       is mis-specified (Shimodaira, 2000). If conditional exchangeability is satisfied, conditional survival
1038
       models are causal, and heterogeneous treatment effects calculated from causal conditional survival
1039   models can be considered valid (Hernan and Robins, 2023). To recover average treatment effects
1040   under conditional exchangeability, various methods such as stratification, standardization, and inverse
1041   propensity weighting can be used (Hernan and Robins, 2023). However, it remains difficult to adjust
1042   for potential issues of covariate shift, particuarly in the face of hetergeneous, time-varying treatment
1043   effects. Novel methods have been proposed that rely on the learning of balanced representations to
1044   overcome these issues (Chapfuwa et al., 2021; Curth et al., 2021a), but it remains an open area for
1045   further study.

1327   D.2    OTHER E VALUATION M ETRICS
1328
       Brier score The Brier score is a time-dependent measure of the quality of an estimated survival
1329   function, which computes the squared error of the survival probability predicted by the model vs. the
1330   binary label of whether or not the datapoint being evaluated has failed by the specified time. This
1331   squared loss is reweighted to account for censoring, and a time-independent version of the Brier score
1332   (called the integrated Brier score or IBS) is given by averaging the time-dependent score over the
1333   desired time interval. While the IBS is a proper scoring rule in the absence of censoring, Rindt et al.
1334   (2022) showed that it may not be a proper scoring rule when the censoring mechanism depends on
1335   the covariates. We refer the reader to Section 3.1 of Rindt et al. (2022) for more details.
1336
1337   1-Calibration 1-calibration compares predicted survival probabilities with outcomes in the data
1338   and measures how well the two agree. Specifically, the data are binned into pre-specified bins based
1339
       on their predicted survival probabilities at the time they experienced an event. The actual number of
       failures is compared to the expected number of failures (according to the model’s predicted failure
1340
       probabilities) for each bin. Under the null hypothesis that the model’s probabilities are correct, these
1341
       deviations can be used to define a test statistic which follows a χ2 distribution, which can be used
1342   to construct a hypothesis test for the calibration of the model. We refer the reader to Section 3.3
1343   of Haider et al. (2018) for complete details. As mentioned by Haider et al. (2018) in the following
1344   section of their paper (Section 3.4), 1-calibration is not effective for ranking multiple models beyond
1345   suggesting some of the models are calibrated (high p-value) and others are not (low p-value).
1346
1347   D-Calibration D-calibration is similar to 1-calibration in that it compares estimated failure prob-
1348   abilities to the expected number of events that would occur if these probabilities were accurate.
1349   However, instead of just comparing the predicted probabilities at the event time for each datapoint,
       D-calibration seeks to measure the goodness of fit of the entire survival distribution predicted by the


                                                               25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       model. This is again accomplished using a probability binning procedure followed by a hypothesis
1351   test. We refer the reader to Section 3.5 of Haider et al. (2018) for the complete details of this metric.
1352   While D-calibration does give a more nuanced evaluation of the calibration of a survival model as
1353   compared to 1-calibration, it suffers from the same problem, namely, it cannot be used to rank many
1354   survival models beyond suggesting that some are poorly calibrated (low p-value) while others are not
1355   (high p-value).
