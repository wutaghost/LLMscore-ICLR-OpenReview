009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011             We develop a new method HTBB for the multidimensional black-box approxima-
012             tion and gradient-free optimization, which is based on the low-rank hierarchical
013             Tucker decomposition with the use of the MaxVol indices selection procedure. Nu-
014             merical experiments for 14 complex model problems demonstrate the robustness
015             of the proposed method for dimensions up to 1000, while it shows significantly
016             more accurate results than classical gradient-free optimization methods, as well
017
                as approximation and optimization methods based on the popular tensor train
                decomposition, which represents a simpler case of a tensor network.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Many physical and engineering models can be represented as a real function (output), which depends
023   on a multidimensional argument (input) and looks like
024
025                              y = f(x) ∈ R,        x = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xd ]T ∈ Ω ⊂ Rd .                          (1)
026   Such functions often have the form of a black-box (BB), i. e., the internal structure and smoothness
027   properties of f remain unknown. Its discretization on some multi-dimensional grid results in a
028   multidimensional array (tensor1 ) Y ∈ RN1 ×N2 ×...×Nd that collects all possible discrete values of the
029   function (1) inside the domain Ω, i.e.,
030                                                                (n )   (n )        (n )
                                   Y[n1 , n2 , . . . , nd ] = f(x1 1 , x2 2 , . . . , xd d ).                          (2)
031
032   Storing such a tensor often requires too much computational effort, and for large values of the dimen-
033   sion d, this is completely impossible due to the so-called curse of dimensionality (the memory for
034   storing data and the complexity of working with it grows exponentially in d). To overcome it, various
035   compression formats for multidimensional tensors are proposed: Canonical Polyadic decomposition
036
      aka CANDECOMP/PARAFAC (CPD) Harshman et al. (1970), Tucker decomposition Tucker (1966),
      Tensor Train (TT) decomposition Oseledets (2011), Hierarchical Tucker (HT) decomposition Hack-
037
      busch & Kühn (2009); Ballani et al. (2013), and their various modifications. These approaches make
038
      it possible to approximately represent the tensor in a compact low-rank (i.e., low-parameter) format
039   and then operate with the compressed tensor.
040
041   The TT-decomposition is one of the most common compression formats Cichocki et al. (2016;
042
      2017). There is an algebra for tensors in the TT-format (i.e., TT-tensors): we can directly add
      and multiply TT tensors, truncate TT tensors (reduce the so-called TT-rank, i. e, the number of
043
      storage parameters), integrate and contract TT tensors. It is important that effective algorithms
044
      have been developed Kapushev et al. (2020); Ahmadi-Asl et al. (2021); Chertkov et al. (2023b)
045   for approximating BB like (1) and (2) in the TT-format, that is, for constructing an approximation
046   (surrogate model) using only a small number of explicitly computed BB values. In recent years, new
047   efficient algorithms have also been proposed Sozykin et al. (2022); Nikitin et al. (2022); Chertkov
048   et al. (2023a) for the second important problem associated with gradient-free optimization of such
049       1

      BB, that is, finding an approximate minimum or maximum value based only on queries to the BB.
055   Such TT-based methods of surrogate modeling (in particular, the TT-cross algorithm Oseledets &
056   Tyrtyshnikov (2010)) and gradient-free optimization (in particular, the TTOpt algorithm Sozykin et al.
057   (2022)) have shown their effectiveness for various multidimensional problems, including compression
058   and acceleration of neural networks, data processing, modeling of physical systems, etc.
059
      However, the TT-decomposition is one of the simplest special cases of a tensor network: it is a
060
      linear network or a degenerate tree, and it has a number of limitations related to weak expressiveness
061   and instability for the case of significantly large dimensions. The HT-format is potentially more
062   expressive and robust Buczyńska et al. (2015); thus, it makes it possible to approximate more complex
063   functions with fewer parameters. Taking into account TT-Cross and TTOpt algorithms which use the
064   well-known MaxVol approach Goreinov et al. (2010); Mikhalev & Oseledets (2018), in this work we
065   develop new methods of surrogate modeling and gradient-free optimization based on the HT-format,
066   and our main contributions are the following:
067
068

070          • we develop a new gradient-free optimization method HTOpt based on the HT-decomposition

072          • we implement the proposed HT-cross and HTOpt algorithm as a unified method HTBB

079            TTOpt, and classical gradient-free SPSA and PSO methods for optimization problems.

081   2    M OTIVATION AND OVERALL IDEA
082
083   HT-format (see Fig. 3 for a visual example of HT structure) is more expressive and robust Buczyńska
084   et al. (2015) than simpler forms of tensor networks (for example, the well-known TT-decomposition),
085   which makes it potentially possible to apply it for complex functions. Thus, it seems important to
086   develop new approximation and optimization methods based on it. We are inspired by a simpler, but
087   carefully designed TT-format and implement analogues of the known methods TT-cross and TTOpt
088   on its basis for the HT-decomposition. The TT-cross algorithm Oseledets & Tyrtyshnikov (2010)
089   adaptively calls the BB and iteratively builds the TT-surrogate until a given accuracy is reached or the
090
      BB access budget is exhausted. During this construction, the so-called Maximum Volume submatrix
      search (MaxVol) procedure Goreinov et al. (2010) is used to find a close to the dominant matrix of the
091
      tensor unfolding. As mentioned in the cited paper, the MaxVol algorithm is closely related to finding
092
      the maximum element in a given matrix — the submatrix, obtained by MaxVol, contains values close
093   to the maximum modulus values of the tensor. This effect can be used to find the quasi-maximal
094   element in the tensor, and the corresponding algorithm is called TTOpt Sozykin et al. (2022).
095
096
      Thus, our goal is to extend the above algorithms to the HT tree structure. To do this, we need to solve
      several algorithmic problems related to the fact that the two-dimensional HT structure has ambiguities
097
      that are not present in the one-dimensional TT structure. In particular, it is necessary to determine a)
098
      the sequence to traverse the HT tree (for TT structure the traversal is done sequentially from left to
099   right and back); and b) how to form a matrix A, which is the input to the MaxVol algorithm (in TT
100   cores there is a dedicated index, thanks to which it is unfolded, and we know exactly which indices
101   are row and which are column indices of the matrix A. In HT structure cores are more symmetric
102   and the choice of indices for rows and columns of the matrix A to which MaxVol is applied is not
103   obvious).
104
      Mathematically, we solve two problems using slight modifications of the same algorithm. The
105   first problem is the approximation of a given discrete black box, i. e., a function f (X) ∈ R whose

149   By Hierarchical Tucker (HT), we mean a tensor tree that is not necessarily balanced Ballani et al.
150   (2013). Let us describe this concept in detail in the context of our work. HT is such a low-parameter

152

153

154   the depth of the tree by L, and the number of nodes at level l (starting from the root node) by λl ; note

      4.3    T RAVERSAL PROCEDURE
343
      When updating indices, we walk sequentially to the neighboring (linked) node, going back only if
344   we reach a leaf node. At each visited node, we increment its visit counter by one, whereas at the
345   beginning all counters were reset to zero. When we pass through an edge, we update only one set of
346   index values at a time: if we go from parent to child, we update down indices values; if we go from
347   child to parent, we update upper indices values.
348
      To decide which of the two nodes to take the next step to (in case there are two options), we count
349
      the average number of visits in each part of the tree that separates each of the two paths. Namely,
350   we cut the edge that was traveled last, and we cut the edges connecting the current node to the two
351   candidate nodes. Since by definition there are no loops in the tree and each edge is a cut edge, we
352   get three components of connectedness. Then we calculate the average number of visits (the sum of
353   the number of visits on all nodes divided by the number of nodes) in each of the two connectivity
354   components and go where the number is smaller. If the average number of visits is close, namely,
355   they differ by no more than a given α value, then we randomly choose one side to go to. Please, see
356   Figure 3 for an example of path, where each number in a list inside a node (blue circle) represents the
357   number of steps when updates occur in this node.

      5     R ELATED WORK
404
405   In many practical situations, the problem-specific target function is not differentiable, too complex,
406
      or its gradients are not helpful due to the non-convex nature of the problem, and it has to be treated as
      a black box (BB). In this case, two important problems naturally arise: approximation Bhosekar &
407
      Ierapetritou (2018) and optimization Alarie et al. (2021). The approximation carried out in the offline
408
      phase allows us to build a surrogate (simplified) model of the BB, which can then be used in the
409   online phase to quickly calculate its values and various characteristics. In the multidimensional case,
410   it becomes difficult to construct a surrogate model, and low-rank tensor approximations are often
411   the most effective. Several recent works Kapushev et al. (2020); Ahmadi-Asl et al. (2021); Chertkov
412   et al. (2023b) proposed various new algorithms based on the TT-decomposition for approximating
413   high-dimensional functions. If we have access to the BB and can perform dynamic queries, then
414   the powerful TT-cross method Oseledets & Tyrtyshnikov (2010) is often used, and if only a training
415   dataset is available, then the TT-ALS method Holtz et al. (2012) is preferred. In this work, we
416   consider the case of adaptive queries to the BB, so we select the TT-cross method as the main baseline
417
      for the approximation problem.
418   Gradients are not available for the BB, so only gradient-free methods can be used for the optimiza-
419   tion problem. Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) Kennedy & Eberhart (1995) and Simultaneous
420   Perturbation Stochastic Approximation (SPSA) Maryak & Chin (2001) are rather useful methods in
421   this case. There is also a large variety of other heuristic methods for finding the global extremum.
422   Recently, the TT-decomposition has been actively used for black-box optimization, since it turns out
423
      to be more effective than standard approaches in the multidimensional case. An iterative method
      TTOpt based on the maximum volume approach is proposed in the work Sozykin et al. (2022). The
424
      authors applied this approach to the problem of optimizing the weights of neural networks in the
425
      framework of reinforcement learning problems in Sozykin et al. (2022) and to the QUBO problem
426   in Nikitin et al. (2022). A similar optimization approach was also considered in Selvanayagam et al.
427   (2022) and Shetty et al. (2016). One more promising algorithm, Optima-TT, which is based on the
428   probabilistic sampling from the TT-tensor, was proposed in recent work Chertkov et al. (2023a).
429   We also note the work Soley et al. (2021), where an optimization method based on the iterative
430   power algorithm in terms of the quantized version of the TT-decomposition is proposed. As a result,
431   we consider classical PSO and SPSA methods as well as the TTOpt method as baselines for the
      optimization problem.

525   7       C ONCLUSIONS
526
527   In this work, we presented a new method HTBB for simultaneously solving the problem of multidi-
528   mensional approximation and gradient-free optimization for functions given in the form of a black
529
      box. Our approach is based on the low-rank hierarchical Tucker decomposition, which makes it
      especially effective in the multidimensional case. The key features of the presented work are a) using
530
      the MaxVol algorithm which allows efficiently finding the required indices and b) using the sequential
531
      traversal of cores, allowing to move to one of the neighboring nodes and making it more efficient to
532   find indexes that need updating.
533
534   The HTBB method can be applied to a wide class of practically significant problems, including optimal
535
      control and various machine learning applications. As future work, we point out the possibility of a
      rather simple extension on the HT-structure of the algorithms that now exist for the TT-decomposition:
536
      rounding, orthogonalization, search for the maximum element by the top-k-like methods, etc.
