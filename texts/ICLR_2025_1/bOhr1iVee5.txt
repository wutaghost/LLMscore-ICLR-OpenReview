041
042
043            3D Gaussian Splatting is capable of reconstructing 3D scenes in minutes. De-
044            spite recent advances in improving surface reconstruction accuracy, the recon-
045
               structed results still exhibit bias and suffer from inefficiency in storage and train-
               ing. This paper provides a different observation on the cause of the inefficiency
046
               and the reconstruction bias, which is attributed to the integration of the low-
047
               opacity parts (LOPs) of the generated Gaussians. We show that LOPs consist

051            introduce global and local full-precision integrate-and-fire spiking neurons to the
052            opacity and representation function of flattened 3D Gaussians, respectively. Fur-
053            thermore, we enhance the density control strategy with spiking neurons’ thresh-
               olds and an new criterion on the scale of Gaussians. Our method can represent

      1   I NTRODUCTION
060
061   3D surface reconstruction from multiview RGB images is a challenging task in computer graphics
062
      and vision (Turkulainen et al., 2024; Verbin et al., 2022). Methods based on Neural Radiance Fields
      (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022; Martin-Brualla et al., 2021; Niemeyer et al.,
063
      2022; Wang et al., 2021) can extract geometry information from an implicit representation but re-
064
      quire extensive computational time for training, limiting their application scenarios. In contrast, 3D
065   Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) is exceptionally fast in 3D reconstruction, which is
066   gaining more and more attention.
067
068
      In particular, 3DGS-based methods integrate the opacity of Gaussians for view rendering and sur-
      face reconstruction. In the optimal scenario (Dai et al., 2024; Guédon & Lepetit, 2024), only a few
069
      high-opacity Gaussian tightly clustered around the surfaces contribute to integration, as shown in
070
      Fig. 1 (a) ⃝.

071   opacity are distributed around the surfaces, yielding the emergence of excessive low-opacity parts
072   (LOPs) in the 3DGS integration (Fig. 1 (a) ⃝- 2 ⃝3 and Fig. 1 (b)). Failing to suppress the emergence
073   of LOPs results in excessive 3D Gaussians, hindering training and storage efficiency. Besides, LOPs
074   also negatively influence surface reconstruction, leading to surface bias, as shown in Fig. 1 (a) ⃝-

078   (flattened 3D Gaussians, Chen et al. 2023; Dai et al. 2024; Huang et al. 2024). Despite the effective-
079
      ness of these methods, there is still considerable potential to improve the accuracy and efficiency of

081   In this paper, we show a detailed analysis (Sec. 3.2) about above observations in Fig. 1 (a) and
082   Fig. 1 (c), based on which we further identify two primary sources of LOPs: low-opacity Gaussians
083   (LOGs) and the low-opacity tails (LOTs) of Gaussians. To handle LOGs and LOTs, we introduce a

085

086
      reduces the number of LOGs; the latter introduces the discontinuity in the Gaussian representation
087

090   tion, it may overly remove Gaussians in occluded or sparsely observed regions, which slightly harms
091   the reconstruction accuracy. To avoid this, we introduce the scale-based clone to compensate for the
092   missing Gaussians in these regions. Moreover, we implement several regularization losses to further
093   enhance method’s performance. Extensive experiments show that Spiking GS achieves comparable
094   or superior 3D reconstruction performance on mainstream datasets with much fewer Gaussians and

096

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   Surface Reconstruction with implicit representation. Neural Radiance Field (NeRF, Mildenhall
112   et al. 2021) has explored a novel way to reconstruct 3D surface based on a neural radiance field.
113   However, such reconstruction suffers from significant artifacts due to a lack of constraints on ge-
114   ometry (Verbin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Yariv et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
115   2022; 2023) and defects of MLP in representing real-world scene (Liao et al., 2024). To improve
116   the reconstruction quality, some of the subsequent works focus on modifying the MLP-based rep-
117   resentation by either integrating with Signed Distance Functions (SDFs), a more explicit surface
118   representation (Wang et al., 2021; Yariv et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; 2023), or
119
      introducing discontinuities into the density field (Liao et al., 2024). Other works adopt strong priors
      to regularize the reconstruction, such as priors from depth (Deng et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022) and
120
      MVS (Zhang et al., 2021; Zuo & Deng, 2022). Nevertheless, these methods still require prolonged
121
      time for optimizing the neural radiance fields. In contrast, our method is developed on the fast 3DGS
122   method to achieve more accurate surface reconstruction with lower cost.
123
124   Surface Reconstruction with 3D Gaussians. Although 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023)
125
      achieves high fidelity on novel view synthesis, it struggles to reconstruct accurate surface due to
      the difficulty of aligning primitives with the actual surface (Dai et al., 2024) and the inconsistency
126
      between mesh extraction and view rendering (Yu et al., 2024). To address the issues, Chen et al.
127
      2024b; Guédon & Lepetit 2024 utilize a regularization to flatten the 3D Gaussian ellipsoids into
128   flat shapes, while Dai et al. 2024; Huang et al. 2024 directly use flattened 3D Gaussians as prim-
129   itives. Others redesigned the opacity modeling of Gaussians (Lyu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024),
130   improving the details of the reconstructed surfaces. However, these approaches still face challenges
131   in the accuracy of surface reconstruction in scenes including objects with complex materials (i.e,
132   semi-transparent). The proposed Spiking GS identifies a widely overlooked correlation between the
133   reconstruction bias and LOPs, achieving considerable improvement on the reconstruction quality
134   and efficiency by reducing the number of LOPs.
135   Efficient Geometry Representation of 3D Gaussians. The use of a large number of Gaussians to
136   accurately represent scenes often results in inefficient training and storage. To address this, recent
137   efforts have focused on utilizing compact feature encodings and pruning strategies to improve effi-
138   ciency in Gaussian representations (Chen et al., 2024c; Lee et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). However,
139   these methods sacrifice surface reconstruction accuracy. Other works (Dai et al., 2024; Guédon &
140   Lepetit, 2024) eliminate the occurrence of LOGs or introduce opacity-based regularization terms
141   to reduce the extra Gaussians. However, they suffer from limited representation capabilities, par-
142
      ticularly in scenes with semi-transparent objects, or limited effect on the occurrence of Gaussians.
      Although our method shares a similar target of reducing the number of Gaussians for improved ef-
143
      ficiency, we specifically focus on minimizing the number of LOPs in integration. As a result, we
144
      not only effectively achieve the target but also maintain the representation capabilities for accurate
145   surface reconstruction in various scenes.
146
147   Spiking Neural in Surface Reconstruction. Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have received
148
      extensive attention recently in many fields due to their energy efficiency (Kim et al., 2022; Ren
      et al., 2024). It has been utilized in processing video or event signals (Paredes-Vallés & De Croon,
149
      2021; Zhu et al., 2022), classification tasks (Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022), and adversarial
150
      attacks (Ding et al., 2022; Kundu et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021). Recently, Liao
151   et al. (2024) introduce the spiking neuron into the NeRF-based neural surface reconstruction for
152   better performance in representing discontinuities of the scene. The proposed spiking GS is also
153   equipped with the spiking neurons but for its efficiency in removing the LOPs in 3DGS. Experi-
154   ments show that it significantly improves the performance of 3DGS-based surface reconstruction.

      3.2   A NALYZING L OW- OPACITY PARTS
203
204   Upon analyzing the pipelines and generated Gaussians of 3DGS methods, we obtain insights below.
205
206   The suboptimal scenario leads to the emergence of excessive LOPs. As shown in Eq. (3), the
207
      pixel value in a given view is computed by integrating the color and opacity of the nearby projected
      Gaussians. If we focus on reducing the loss between the rendered and ground truth pixel values c(u)
208
      while overlooking regularizing the contribution of each Gaussian, there exists two possible scenar-
209
      ios for deriving the same pixel value c(u): the scenario where only a few high-opacity Gaussians
210   are involved, or the scenario where both a few high-opacity Gaussians and excessive low-opacity
211   Gaussians are involved. According to (Dai et al., 2024; Guédon & Lepetit, 2024), the latter is subop-
212   timal. We observe that existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods tend to converge to such suboptimal
213   scenario. We validate this by visualizing the representative pattern of the ω values along the view
214   rays generated by SOTA methods in Fig. 2 (a) and (b), showing that excessive LOPs are introduced
215   by excessively generated Gaussians in the SOTA methods. This is further validated by the statistic
      results in Fig. 2 (c) and (d).

366
367   Our training framework is developed on 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) with flattened Gaussians (Dai
368   et al., 2024) as primitive and customized CUDA kernels1 .
369   Evaluation metrics and datasets. To evaluate the accuracy of the 3D surface reconstruction, we
370   use the Chamfer distance as the metric (Dai et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2024). We
371   also report training time and the number of Gaussians (noted as #G) to evaluate the efficiency of our

464   5.2         A BLATION S TUDY
465
466   To validate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, we conduct an ablation study of the proposed
467
      Spiking GS. The results of a comparison between our method and two alternatives, ‘w/o glb’ and
      ‘w/o loc’, are shown in Tab.1 2 . Specifically, ‘w/o glb’ removes the global FIF neuron, while ‘w/o
468
      loc’ excludes the local FIF neuron. The results show that omitting either the global or local FIF
469
      neurons negatively impacts both the accuracy and efficiency of surface reconstruction. The two types
470   of FIF neurons play distinct roles in our method for reducing LOPs, resulting in different effects. For
471   instance, the global FIF neuron is primarily effective in reducing the number of LOGs to improve
472   reconstruction quality and efficiency. Particularly, we find method with global FIF neuron can better
473   capture the details on the surface, validated by a quality comparison between the full model and ‘w/o
474   glb’ in Fig. 5, column 1-3. In contrast, local FIF neurons decrease LOTs to minimize the overlap of
475   Gaussians, resulting in more separated centers of Gaussians, as evidenced by the visualized Gaussian
476   center’s distribution in Fig. 5, columns 5-6. Local FIF neurons are particularly effective in specific
477   scenes, such as scenes with flat surfaces (the round table in Fig. 4, column 2) where tail overlap
478
      is predominant. Incorporating local FIF neurons will be more efficient in these cases to reduce the
      number of Gaussians, compared to using global FIF neurons.
