009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion models excel at generative modeling (e.g., text-to-image) but sampling
012            requires multiple denoising network passes, limiting practicality. Efforts such as
013            progressive distillation or consistency distillation have shown promise by reducing
014            the number of passes at the expense of quality of the generated samples. In
015            this work we identify co-variate shift as one of reason for poor performance of
016            multi-step distilled models from compounding error at inference time. To address
017            co-variate shift, we formulate diffusion distillation within imitation learning (DDIL)
018            framework and enhance training distribution for distilling diffusion models on both
019            data distribution (forward diffusion) and student induced distributions (backward
020
               diffusion). Training on data distribution helps to diversify the generations by
               preserving marginal data distribution and training on student distribution addresses
021
               compounding error by correcting covariate shift. In addition, we adopt reflected
022
               diffusion formulation for distillation and demonstrate improved performance, stable
023            training across different distillation methods. We show that DDIL consistency
024            improves on baseline algorithms of progressive distillation (PD), Latent consistency
025            models (LCM) and Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD2).

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Diffusion models, while capable of producing high-quality images, suffer from slow sampling times
032   due to their iterative denoising process. To address this, distillation techniques have been proposed to
033
      reduce number of denoising steps. These techniques can be broadly categorized into trajectory-level
      (Luo et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2023; Salimans & Ho, 2021; Song et al., 2023) and distribution-
034
      matching approaches (Yin et al., 2023; 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Sauer et al., 2023) . While the former
035
      focuses on preserving the teacher’s trajectory at a per-sample level, the latter matches the marginal
036   distribution.
037
038   Multi-step student models offer a promising approach in balancing quality and computational effi-
039
      ciency. However, they often face a critical challenge: covariate shift. This occurs when the distribution
      of noisy input latents that the student model encounters during training differs from that seen during
040
      inference by the student. This mismatch can significantly impact generation quality especially when
041
      the number of denoising steps are low. Recent works Kohler et al. (2024); Yin et al. (2024) only
042   consider backward trajectories to obtain feedback on quality of generation, but these approaches are
043   often agnostic to data distribution and can exhibit mode-collapse.
044
045
      In this work, we identify ‘covariate shift’ as a critical factor that impacts the generation quality
      in multi-step distilled diffusion models. To address covariate shift and to preserve diversity, we
046
      introduce diffusion distillation within the imitation learning (DDIL) framework by improving the
047
      training distribution for distillation. We achieve this by incorporating both the data distribution
048   (forward diffusion) and the student’s predictive distribution (backward trajectory at inference time).
049   This approach combines the benefits of (1) Preserving Marginal Data Distribution: Training on
050   the data distribution ensures the student model maintains the inherent statistical properties of the
051   original data, and (2) Correcting Covariate Shift: Training on backward trajectories enables the
052   student model to identify and adapt to covariate shifts, thereby improving the accuracy of score
053   estimates, particularly in few-step settings. We illustrate instantiation of DDIL framework in context
      of progressive distillation in Figure 2. To this end, we make the following contributions:

123
124   Diffusion distillation methods. Progressive distillation Salimans & Ho (2021); Meng et al. (2023)
125   and many follow up works Li et al. (2023b); Berthelot et al. (2023) try to reduce the number of
126   iterations of student model by forcing student to mimic multiple steps of the teacher. Consistency
127   models Song et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023); Ren et al. (2024) assume deterministic probabilistic
128   flow at inference and enforce consistency in the data space for step-distillation. Additionally, recent
129   work decomposes the diffusion trajectory into multiple segments like in progressive distillation and
130
      performs distillation within consistency formulation Kim et al. (2023). Instead of using the real
      data, methods such as BOOT Gu et al. (2023) consider bootstrapping in the student trajectory to
131
      generate samples of high quality and diversity. Liu et al. (2023) approximates the underlying map of
132
      the pretrained diffusion model as linear paths. While above trajectory level distillation techniques
133   like progressive distillation and consistency-based approaches improve efficiency, the quality of the
134   generated samples exhibits low visual fidelity.
135
136
      Alternatively, diffusion distillation has been formulated in the distributional matching framework
      Yin et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2024); Yin et al. (2024); Salimans et al. (2024); Sauer et al. (2023;
137
      2024). Within distribution matching approaches instead of matching teacher for each trajectory or
138
      particle like in previous class of methods, we try to match marginals of distilled student model and
139   pretrained diffusion model. Further, adversarial loss has been applied to distillation approaches to
140   improve the visual quality of the generated images Sauer et al. (2023; 2024); Lin et al. (2024). Most
141   of distributional matching objectives like Sauer et al. (2024); Yin et al. (2023) are mode-seeking and
142   looses on diversity. EM distillation Xie et al. (2024) addresses this by richer sampling with langevian
143   MCMC to provide better target for distillation.
144
      Reverse diffusion as Markov decision process. Policy gradient methods have recently gained
145   traction in text-to-image generation with diffusion models by formulating the reverse diffusion
146   process as a markov decision process (MDP) Fan et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2023a). Recent work Fan
147   et al. (2024) proposes a policy gradient method for data distribution matching in diffusion models.
148   Black et al. (2023) introduces a policy gradient algorithm with reward function that optimizes a
149   diffusion model for downstream tasks. Yang et al. (2024) assumes a latent reward function of the
150   reverse denoising process by emphasing the text and image alignment on the coarser steps of image
151   generation. All these approaches have been applied to improve the alignment between the prompts
152   and generated images for high-fidelity synthesis. In our work, we leverage the formulation of reverse
153
      process as MDP for step-distillation. This formulation allows interactively update the student model
      with the observations of the teacher model using dataset aggregation Ross et al. (2011).

158   3.1   R EVERSE D ENOISING P ROCESS AS MDP
159
160   In imitation learning, an agent learns to perform tasks by observing and mimicking the behavior of
161   the expert. An MDP in imitation learning models the next action based on the previous action and the
      current knowledge of the environment (Ke et al., 2021; Spencer et al., 2021). In general, an MDP is


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      represented as ⟨S, A, P, ρ0 ⟩, where S is a finite set of states, A is the set of actions, P (s′ |s, a) is a
163   state transition kernel to transition from s to s′ under the action a and ρ0 is the set of initial states. An
164   MDP produces a trajectory which is a sequence of state-action pairs τ = (s0 , a0 , s1 , a1 , ..., aT , sT )
165   over T time steps.
166
      We formalize the reverse process of the diffusion models as a finite horizon MDP (Black et al., 2023;
167
      Fan et al., 2024) with the policy πθ (the diffusion model with parameters θ) where the states and
168
      the actions are st := (xt , t) and at := xt−1 respectively. The transition dynamics is defined by
169   P (st+1 |st , at ) := δ(xt−1 , t) and ρ0 (s) := (N (0, I), T ) denotes the initial state distribution. The
170   trajectory τ becomes (xT , xT −1 , . . . , x0 ).

      3.2   C O - VARIATE S HIFT IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
173
174   With in iterative denoising steps of generation within backward trajectory of diffusion models,
175   student’s current predictions determines what the student (learner) sees in next step within sequential
176   setting, which is classic feedback loop [1] in imitation learning. So if student makes any mistake or
177   has bad score estimate in one of early steps this discrepancy excarbates in later iterations and results
178   in accumulation of error. This error results in change in input distribution (covariate shift) of latents
179   between training time (forward diffusion) and latents student model encounters when it is unrolled in
180   iterative fashion at generation i.e., backward trajectory. Exposure bias is another closely related line
181
      of work Li et al. (2023a) which also discusses change in input distribution w.r.t pretrained diffusion
      model and propose training-free methods to improve it. Our work primarily focuses on distilling
182
      diffusion models and how this shift effects distillation.
183
184   Covariate shift is more prounced for distilled student diffusion model compared to pretrained diffusion
185   model. To further clarify why covariate shift poses more of a challenge for the student model
186   compared to the teacher model, we can consider inference as ancestral sampling (or annealing in
187
      score estimation). During generation i.e., within intermediate time-steps of backward trajectory
      of diffusion model, there is an implicit assumption that the marginal distributions between two
188
      consecutive denoising steps significantly overlap. While this is a reasonable assumption in continuous
189
      time diffusion models or when the number of denoising steps is sufficiently high for pretrained
190   diffusion model, when considering a diffusion model with only few steps, this assumption does not
191   hold. Consequently, any covariate shift would be more exacerbated for the student model, unlike the
192   continuous time teacher mode.
193
      DAgger to mitigate Co-variate shift: Imitation learning has long been used to learn offline sequential
194
      tasks wherein a student model is trained from teachers’ demonstrations. Standard imitation learning
195
      also suffers from covariate shift in discrepancy in states visited by student and the teacher. Interactive
196   methods such as DAgger (Ross et al., 2011) in Imitation learning augment training data by querying
197   the teacher model on student’s states, thereby obtaining teacher’s feedback on student’s predictive
198   distribution(backward trajecories). Building on the ideas of interactive methods in imitation learning,
199   in our work we aim to improve training distribution for diffusion distillation.
200
      Imitation Learning as Distribution Matching: Notably, Ke et al. (2021) has shown that the
201
      imitation learning algorithms can be formalized as f -divergence minimization algorithms where the
202   DAgger approach minimizes the total variation (TV) distance between the student and the teacher
203   trajectory distributions. If ρθ (τ ) and ρη (τ ) are teacher and student trajectory distributions respectively,
204   then DAgger minimizes upperbound on total variation. DAgger achieves O(T ϵ) error compared to
205   behavior cloning equivalent progressive distillation or any method with teacher forcing bounded by
206   O(T ϵ2 ) error.

226   Mode Seeking: The problem of covariate shift and distribution changes in diffusion distillation is
227   multifaceted. It’s not just about input distribution shifts caused by error accumulation. The reduction
228   of diversity in the intermediate steps of backward trajectories (generative process) also plays a crucial
229
      role. If diversity is lost early on, it cascades through subsequent steps, limiting the range of possible
      outcomes. This is akin to error accumulation, but instead of errors, we are consistently losing diversity
230
      across time. We can think of it like sequential Monte Carlo sampling in diffusion models: at each
231
      step, we are discarding a large number of potential paths (particles), leading to a narrower range of
232   possibilities in the later stages.
233
234   While EM Distillation (Xie et al., 2024) addresses this by employing Langevin MCMC for a richer
235
      reverse process and mode-covering divergences, it still doesn’t explicitly incorporate the data distri-
      bution into its sampling prior during distillation.

242   4.1     I MPROVING T RAINING D ISTRIBUTION WITH DDIL
243
      We introduce Diffusion Distillation with Imitation Learning (DDIL), a novel framework inspired
244
      by the DAgger algorithm from imitation learning to enhance the sampling distribution of interme-
245
      diate noisy latents for distilling diffusion models. Diffusion model distillation involves two key
246   considerations: (1) the training distribution of latent states encountered by the student model, and
247   (2) the feedback mechanism employed during distillation. DDIL specifically focuses on improving
248   the training distribution, remaining agnostic to the specific feedback mechanism utilized by different
249   distillation techniques.
250
      To achieve this, DDIL strategically samples intermediate latent variables from three sources: (1)
251
      forward diffusion of the dataset, captured by the sampling prior βf rwd (as illustrated in Algorithm 1);
252   (2) backward trajectories (unrolled latents) from the student model, denoted by the sampling prior
253   βstudent_bckwrd ; and (3) backward trajectories from the teacher model, denoted by the sampling prior
254   βteacher_bckwrd , which is particularly advantageous in data-free settings for preserving marginal data
255   distribution. Combining these sampling strategies leads to improved distillation performance.
256
      DDIL is a unified training framework for distilling diffusion models w.r.t sampling prior for distil-
257
      lation. DDIL incorporates teacher feedback on student trajectories, aligning with the principles of
258   DAgger Ross et al. (2011) in case of progressive distillation and latent consistency models (LCM).
259   Furthermore, while methods like Kohler et al. (2024); Yin et al. (2024) perform distillation only
260   on backward trajectories and don’t account for marginal data distribution during distillation, DDIL
261   addresses this by consistently incorporating feedback from the chosen distillation algorithm on
262   both forward and backward trajectories. Our flexible framework thus allows for improved training
263   distribution to boost the performance of diffusion distillation methods.

265   (DDIL). This framework leverages a pre-trained diffusion model (teacher) and a student diffusion
266   model, typically initialized with the teacher’s parameters. Additionally, access to real data is assumed,
267   providing representative samples from the marginal data distribution during the distillation process.
268   The framework necessitates specifying hyperparameters for both the teacher and student models,
269   including their respective discretization schemes. For simplicity we assume DDIM solver in 1.

307   Reflected Diffusion Distillation: When distilling diffusion models either the teacher or student
308   model might not satisfy implicit assumed support during distillation which could makes training
309
      unstable and require large batch sizes, etc. We adopt reflected diffusion models Lou & Ermon

310
      stability of training, lower required batch size with improved performance.
311
312   Thresholding is applied to the teacher model’s estimates consistently across all investigated methods:

314   consistency distillation framework, the target derived from the student model is also threshold-ed.
315
      For DMD2, thresholding is applied to the score estimates of the pre-trained diffusion model, the
      fake critic, and the student model. Without thresholding our gradient feedback could be noisy and
316
      negatively impacting training stability.

319   4.2   DDIL I NTEGRATION
320
      This section examines the integration of DDIL with various distillation techniques. Detailed design
321
      choices are further elaborated in the appendix (section to be updated).
322
323   PD + DDIL: DDIL is integrated with progressive distillation using a DAgger-inspired approach
      Ross et al. (2011). Distillation is performed on mixed rollouts generated by alternating between the


                                                             6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      pre-trained and student diffusion models within each generation. A stateless DDIM solver facilitates
325   this interleaved sampling process.
326
327   LCM + DDIL: DDIL is also applied to consistency distillation. Due to the pre-trained model’s
328
      lack of prior consistency training, the mixed-rollout strategy used in progressive distillation is not
      directly applicable. Therefore, DDIL is extended to LCM by applying consistency distillation to both
329
      forward and backward trajectories of the student model, leveraging the student-induced distribution
330
      and demonstrating performance improvements.
331
332   DMD2 + DDIL: Mirroring the progressive distillation approach, mixed rollouts are employed within
333   the DMD2 framework. Trajectories are sampled up to a predetermined noise level or timestep (e.g.,
334
      t=500) using either the student or teacher model. The resulting latent serves as input to the student
      model for gradient feedback within the DMD2 formulation. Distribution Matching aligns well
335
      within our DDIL framework as discussed in Section 3.2 where within imitation learning framework
336
      we consider matching generated trajectory distributions of student model with teacher or expert’s
337   trajectory distribution (of states or equivalently noisy latents in case of diffusion).

340   5    E XPERIMENTS
341
342   Datasets and metrics: Following standard practice for evaluating text-to-image diffusion models
343   Rombach et al. (2022); Meng et al. (2023), we evaluate our distilled models zero-shot on two public
344   benchmarks: COCO 2017 (5K captions), and COCO 2014 Lin et al. (2014) (30K captions) validation
345
      sets. We use each caption to generate an image with a randomized seed and report CLIP score using
      OpenCLIP ViT-g/14 model Ilharco et al. (2021) to evaluate image-text alignment. We also report
346
      Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) Heusel et al. (2017) to estimate perceptual quality. To measure
347
      diversity of generation, we report LP IP SDiversity , where for a given prompt we generate output for
348   10 different seeds and obtain pair-wise LPIPS score and finally average over 50 randomly sampled
349   COCO 2017 prompts.
350
351
      Training: For all our experiments, we choose AdamW optimizer Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) with
      1e − 05 learning rate with warmup and linear schedule on a batch size of 224 in case of progressive
352
      distillation, 360 in case of LCM and 7 in case of DMD2 on SSD1B. To optimize for GPU usage, we
353
      adopt gradient checkpoint and mixed-precision training. Please refer to Appendix A for additional
354   training details.
355
356   PD + DDIL:In case of progressive distillation, we train the model for 4k steps for ϵ to v space
357
      conversion to perform step distillation in v space Salimans & Ho (2021). Then we perform guidance
      conditioning following the same protocol as Meng et al. (2023) where we sample guidance scale
358
      ω ∼ [2, 14] and incorporate additional guidance embedding as in Rombach et al. (2022) followed
359
      by step distillation. Overall we train 10K steps to obtain guidance conditioned checkpoint SD(gc).
360   For progressive distillation, we start with a 32-step discretization assumption for the pre-trained
361   diffusion model and perform 32 → 16 step distillation for 5K iterations with 500 steps of warm-up.
362   We progressively increase training compute or gradient steps as we go towards fewer iteration student.
363   For 16 → 8, we follow a similar protocol, but we further split each stage of training into two parts.
364   First, we do distillation for 6K steps to obtain a checkpoint and resume with warmup and 1e − 05
365   learning rate for another 4K steps of training. For 8 → 4 and 4 → 2 distillation, we first distill model
366   for 8K steps followed by another 6K steps.We split single stage of training into two parts to exactly
367   match training protocol of Step Distillation and DDIL. See supplemental for additional details. We
368
      adopt timesteps for discretization from the default config of the DPM++ solver i.e., for 4-step models
      our timesteps are {999, 749, 500, 250}.

      6     C ONCLUSION
466
467
      This work introduces DDIL, a novel framework for distilling diffusion models that addresses the
468
      challenge of covariate shift while preserving the marginal data distribution. Integrating DDIL with
469   established distillation techniques, including Progressive Distillation, Consistency Distillation (LCM),
470   and Distribution Matching based Distillation (DMD2), consistently yields quantitative and qualitative
471   improvements. Furthermore, we also show that integrating DDIL within the DMD2 framework
472   enhances training stability, reduces required batch sizes, and improves computational efficiency
473   demonstrating wider applicability and practical usefulness.
