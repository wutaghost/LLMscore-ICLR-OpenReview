009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We study whether Large Language Models (LLMs) inherently capture domain-
012            specific nuances in natural language. Our experiments probe the domain sensi-
013            tivity of LLMs by examining their ability to distinguish queries from different
014            domains using hidden states generated during the prefill phase. We reveal latent
015            domain-related trajectories that indicate the model’s internal recognition of query
016            domains. We also study the robustness of these domain representations to varia-
017            tions in prompt styles and sources. Our approach leverages these representations
018
               for model selection, mapping the LLM that best matches the domain trace of the
               input query (i.e., the model with the highest performance on similar traces). Our
019
               findings show that LLMs can differentiate queries for related domains, and that
020
               the fine-tuned model is not always the most accurate. Unlike previous work, our
021            interpretations apply to both closed and open-ended generative tasks.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various tasks,
027   yet the internal mechanisms driving these capabilities remain poorly understood. Different domains
028   require distinct knowledge and reasoning patterns, necessitating LLMs to adjust decision-making
029   based on-the-fly for input queries. This is crucial for applications demanding high reliability, such
030   as legal and medical fields, where errors can lead to significant consequences.
031   The research question of how LLMs adapt their decision-making and reasoning patterns across
032   different domains is distinct from a growing body of work on locating factual associations from
033   language models behavior (Meng et al., 2024; Hernandez et al., 2024a;b; Mitchell et al., 2022;
034   Meng et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2022; Belrose et al., 2023). While these studies aim to identify the
035   modules and computations that recall specific facts, primarily monitoring and controlling language
036   generation, they often fall short in addressing the complexities of generative tasks.
037   Understanding how LLMs adapt their reasoning across generative tasks is important for enhancing
038   transparency in their decision-making processes. This insight not only deepens our understanding of
039   generalization capabilities but also promotes interdisciplinary collaboration and improves the design
040   of evaluation metrics that consider domain-specific nuances. Our research focuses on the patterns
041   models reveal as they tackle domain-specific challenges, rather than merely retrieving factual infor-
042   mation.
043   Recently, Guo et al. (2024) evaluated GPT-4’s ability to infer domain knowledge using a ReAct-
044   based LLM chain. The experiment involved generating reasoning paths and actions from unlabeled
045   coding exemplars without explicit domain descriptions. Their findings show that GPT-4, when
046   given domain-relevant exemplars, significantly outperforms its generic counterpart, suggesting that
047   the model can discern domain essence from the exemplars. However, it remains unclear whether
048   the model truly ”understands” the content or merely imitates the exemplars based on its outputs.
049   Similarly, other efforts focus on creating probing representations for individual context-dependent
050
      situations Li et al. (2021); Pimentel et al. (2020), where performance varies significantly based on
      task-specific metrics.
051
052   Our research, motivated by studies on neural network activation Abdelnabi et al. (2024); He et al.
053   (2024); Mallen & Belrose (2024), aims to interpret how hidden states represent context for domain-
      related queries before the generation phase. We aim to determine if models inherently encode gen-

084   2   R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Understanding Transformers-based Models. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) play a key role
087
      in Natural Language Processing tasks. As a result, understanding their internal working mechanisms
      is critical. Research on interpreting Transformer states is based on forwarding data into the model
088
      to analyze attention heads (Clark et al., 2019; Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) and embedding spaces (Dar
089
      et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2022; 2021) that connect “interpretability” with different data distributions
090   and equivalent predictions. However, these techniques are task-specific and not related to gradient-
091   based measures of feature importance (Jain & Wallace, 2019).
092
093
      LLMs Hidden States as Internal Representations: Hidden states have been studied to investigate
      factual knowledge (He et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Burns et al., 2023), hallucination (Zhao et al.,
094
      2024; Dombrowski & Corlouer, 2024), locating and modifying factual data (Meng et al., 2022; Her-
095
      nandez et al., 2024a) and task drifts (Abdelnabi et al., 2024; Zverev et al., 2024). Most research
096   is limited to closed-ended scenarios that involve probing a white-box model to uncover contrast-
097   ing behaviors (often impractical for generative tasks). Bricken et al. (2023) decompose activations
098   into more interpretable monosemantic features using a sparse autoencoder. Feature decomposition
099   can determine the contribution of the layers’ activations on a specific example, making it easier to
100   monitor the network activation for specific features. In contrast, we aim to reuse the hidden states
101   generated by the LLM from the context, without using an external autoencoder.
102   Domain Representations for Routing Mechanisms. Semantic Layer approaches (Sun et al., 2024;
103   Manias et al., 2024a) have emerged as a particularly lightweight and effective solution: by com-
104   paring the embeddings of semantic representations (e.g. cosine, Manhattan distances), these layers
105   perform a preselection of language models or tools that need to be retrieved for specific domain
106   tasks. These methods can be restricted when data is scarce, or we do not have a predefined in-
107   struction structure. Within the recommendation systems literature, there are works that leverage
      deep neural networks discriminatively to learn better representations of users/items based on con-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      textual information that can be used for downstream tasks (Liang et al.; Li et al., 2023). Yet, to
109   our knowledge, none of these have delved into how to generalize to more complex generative tasks.
110   Alternative routing strategies for model selection (Ding et al., 2024; Ong et al., 2024; Šakota et al.,
111   2024; Jiang et al., 2023b) aim to estimate query complexity and redirect “easy” requests to smaller
112   LLMs, balancing model performance and inference costs. Within this area, some routers based on
113   domain clustering have emerged (Pichlmeier et al., 2024; Ostapenko et al., 2024), demonstrating the
114   ability to efficiently distribute incoming requests by directing them to the nearest cluster of instruc-
115   tions. A limitation of these approaches is their dependency on access to a subset of the expert/cluster
116   training data, which must be adequately representative for comparison purposes, a requirement that
117   may be infeasible for models trained on proprietary data.
118   In contrast with previous work, our research aims to investigate whether LLMs can inherently dis-
119   tinguish between queries from various domains, despite differences in prompt style and source and
120   show which value these representations provide, compared with other semantic and token-level rep-
121   resentations.

125   Operational definitions: To assess the ability of LLMs to capture domain-specific representations,
126   we rely on hidden states generated during the prefill phase – the stage in which the model processes
127   input tokens to generate intermediate states before producing the first new token. Below are the key
128   operational terms used throughout our experiments:
129
130          • Hidden states are the intermediate activations produced by the model at each layer when
131            processing input tokens, building the model’s contextual understanding. For each query, the
132            model generates a set of hidden states in the shape (batch size, dimension, num layers),
133
               where batch size refers to the number of samples processed at once, dim refers to the size
               of the hidden representation (i.e., the number of features in each state) and num layers
134
               indicates the total number of layers in the model, each producing its own hidden states.
135
136          • Mean activation: To simplify analysis, we compute the mean activation across both the
137
               batch and dimension axes. The mean for each layer l is given by:

175   Building on these observations, we propose the following main hypothesis: LLMs’ hidden states
176   encode generalizable representations for specific domains, revealing domain-related traces from
177   the context understanding phase. Testing this hypothesis requires:
178            • Generalizability: We aim to determine whether this ability is consistent across different
179              LLM architectures, training recipes, and model parameters. We also investigate whether
180              these representations are retained after fine-tuning and assess how robust they are when
181              prompts are perturbed.
182            • Evaluation: We develop a method that leverages these hidden state representations to
183              benchmark model performance against traditional approaches. Specifically, we quantify
184              the value of these representations compared to token-based and semantic representations.

187   and evaluate the robustness of hidden states across various models, prompts, and domains.

      5.1     A NALYZING THE POWER OF THE LLM HIDDEN STATES
242
243   Based on our initial observations on the behavior of the Phi-3-mini-3.8B model, we aim to in-
244   vestigate whether the ability to encode domain-specific information in hidden states is an emergent
245   property of LLMs in general, rather than a model-specific phenomenon. To this end, we conducted a
246   comparative analysis between different generative LLMs (Gemma-2B, Phi-3-mini-3.8B, Llama2-7B
247   and Mistral-7B) and a pretrained encoder model (DeBERTa) to explore the contextual representa-
248   tions captured during the prefill phase. We structured our investigation around the following key
249
      questions:
250         a) Comparison with encoder models: How do the hidden states of generative language mod-
251
               els compare with those of an encoder model designed to capture more fine-grained semantic
               and positional information?
252
253         b) Impact of finetuning: After fine-tuning on specific tasks, do LLMs retain the same
254            domain-specific hidden state traces, or do these traces shift significantly?
255   To answer these questions, we randomly selected 5,000 samples from the Base Pool, a collection
256   of domain-specific queries, and fed them into the various models. We extracted the hidden state
257   activations from each layer, focusing on the last token in the input query. This process was repeated
258   for all layers of the models, ensuring a comprehensive analysis of the hidden state behavior. As
259   a safety check, we introduced 5,000 samples from the Specialized Pool, which contained queries
260   from a different distribution with no overlap with the MMLU dataset. This helped us ensure that
261
      the observed patterns were not merely the result of similar instructions or query semantics. Table 1
      provides examples of the prompt variations across these datasets.

      We also compared the LLM Sequence Classifier and the LLM Hidden States Classifier. The results
438
      indicate that separating the analysis of the input from the generation phase (as done in the hidden
439   states approach) leads to more robust representations. By leveraging the entire sequence of hidden
440   states, the LLM Hidden States Classifier captures more detailed information about the input se-
441   quence, improving its ability to make accurate predictions. Additionally, in the generation process,
442   hidden states are influenced by previous tokens, which can narrow the representation of domain
443   information. In contrast, the prefill phase retains the rich, diverse embeddings from the pretrained
444   model, offering a more flexible and unbiased understanding of the domain.
445   In summary, our results show that moving away from rigid domain-labeled model selection strate-
446   gies toward approaches that rely on hidden state representations can lead to improved generalization
447   across domains, questions, and input structures.

483   While our study provides valuable insights into the utility of hidden state representations in LLMs,
484
      several limitations should be acknowledged:

            • Focus on smaller LMs: Our work primarily focuses on interpreting small LLMs (up to
487           7B parameters). While we demonstrate that even the smallest models can provide useful
488           information on domain interpretation, the applicability of our approach to larger models
489           remains to be explored.
490
            • Domain traces may not generalize: The domain traces we show per model may not be
491           a definitive representation of that domain, but rather an infusion of multiple subdomains
492           reflected in the queries. Therefore, the “clustering” might not generalize to other datasets
493           sharing the same domain label. We argue, however, that this variability reflects how the
494           model internalizes new data distributions. This characteristic represents a key advantage
495           of using hidden states for interpretation, as it allows us to gain feedback directly from the
496           model itself.

498   7   D ISCUSSION
499
500   When creating strategies for interpreting domain questions, it is more beneficial to focus on the
501   model’s comprehension of the question itself rather than the domain labels. This approach can lead
502   to a variety of advantages and interesting scenarios. In this paper, we aimed to uncover the LLM’s
503   ability to differentiate between well-defined domains and leverage the context understanding into
504   domain representations that can be harnessed in the model routing scenario, showing an improve-
505   ment of 12% over baseline methods. However the applicability of this approach can be extended to
506   tasks with:
507         • Interdisciplinary collaboration: For instance, biomedical ethics, where questions involve
508           ethical reasoning but also medical knowledge, selecting models or agents, based on their
509           interpretation of the question’s complexity or reasoning requirements rather than a domain
510           label can improve performance.
511         • Unsupervised model selection: In the absence of labeled data, selecting models based on
512           their ability to interpret the structure or type of reasoning involved can be helpful in zero-
513           shot learning tasks. Models can be chosen that are good at recognizing the tone or domain
514           of the question, which can be more beneficial than relying on static routing to domain
515
              fine-tuned models.
516         • Remove manual bottleneck: These mechanisms can be leveraged to enhance scalability
517           by eliminating the bottleneck associated with manual sample selection, thereby streamlin-
518           ing the processing of large datasets on well-defined domains.
519         • Enhancing LLM-Human collaboration: The domain representations can be harnessed
520           to generate summaries or feedback of the LLM context understanding when there is uncer-
521           tainty on how the model would process an specific request.
522
      We have demonstrated that LLMs are capable of encoding domain representation, capturing contex-
523
      tual information in their hidden states –before the generation phase– distinguishing between queries
524   from different domains, regardless of the prompt style and query source. This approach is particu-
525   larly useful in domains where the labels are insufficient to capture the complexity of the underlying
526   data. Our approach can be used to identify the most relevant model for a given domain-related
527   trajectory and improve results over semantic and token-based approaches. Comparing these three
528   methods has provided us with new insights into their strengths and limitations, which can be useful
529   for future research in this area. Our approach shows promise for improving the interpretability of
530   language models, which will hopefully lead to a better understanding of their underlying mecha-
531   nisms and discriminative power.

851   A.2    MLP C LASSIFIER USED FOR HIDDEN STATES TRAJECTORIES
852
      We employed a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) as a classifier to process the hidden states generated
853
      by Phi-3-mini-128k. The MLP is structured with three fully connected layers. The input layer,
854
      which takes the hidden states, is followed by two hidden layers. The first fully connected layer (fc1)
855   maps the input to a hidden dimension of size hidden size using a linear transformation, followed
856   by a ReLU activation function. The output of the first hidden layer is then passed through a second
857   fully connected layer (fc2), which retains the same hidden dimension, again followed by a ReLU
858   activation. The final layer (fc3) maps the hidden representation to the output space, producing a
859   prediction over 4 classes.

951   A.5   OVERLAPPING ACROSS M ATHS AND B IOMEDICAL D OMAINS
952
953   The overlap in hidden states when computing queries from the mathematical and biomedical do-
954
      mains contrasts with domains like law and humanities, where reasoning processes differ. Math and
      biomedicine rely heavily on structured, logical reasoning and problem-solving, leading to more
955
      precise, analytical neural activations. In contrast, law and humanities emphasize interpretative,
956
      narrative-driven reasoning, which involves greater flexibility, ambiguity, and context-dependent
957   thinking. While math and biomedical domains focus on clear relationships between variables and
958   technical language, law and humanities require models to capture complex human experiences, ethi-
959   cal considerations, and persuasive argumentation. As a result, the hidden states for law and humani-
960   ties queries would likely reflect more diverse and abstract linguistic patterns, with less direct overlap
961   compared to the more systematic reasoning used in mathematics and biomedicine.
