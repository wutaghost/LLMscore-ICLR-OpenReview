009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce FaceGPT, a self-supervised learning framework for large vision-
012            language models (VLMs) to reason about 3D human faces from images and text.
013            Typical 3D face analysis algorithms are specialized and lack semantic reasoning
014            capabilities. FaceGPT overcomes this limitation by embedding the parameters of
015            a 3D morphable face model (3DMM) into the token space of a VLM, enabling
016
               the generation of 3D faces from both textual and visual inputs. FaceGPT is
               trained as a model-based autoencoder in a self-supervised manner from in-the-wild
017
               images. In particular, a dedicated face token is projected to 3DMM parameters
018
               and then rendered as a 2D face image to guide the self-supervised learning process
019            through image-based reconstruction. Without relying on expensive 3D annotations,
020            FaceGPT learns to generate 3D faces based on visual or textual inputs, achieving
021            a competitive performance compared to methods that are specialized to each of
022            these tasks. Most importantly, FaceGPT is able to leverage the world knowledge in
023            VLMs to achieve semantic reasoning capabilities, allowing the model to perform
024            speculative generation of 3D faces purely from subtle textual prompts that do not
025            explicitly describe facial features. This opens a new way of generating 3D faces
026            from subtle descriptions of emotions or general everyday situations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      In this work, we address the problem of reasoning about 3D human faces from images and text.
031   Related work on monocular 3D face reconstruction aims to estimate the parameters of a 3D morphable
032   model Blanz & Vetter (1999); Tewari et al. (2017); Deng et al. (2019b); Feng et al. (2021a); Li
033   et al. (2023) given 2D face images as input. However, these methods lack the capability to reason
034   about faces from text input. Unlike these systems, humans can vividly imagine and even draw faces
035   based solely on either face images or textual descriptions. Motivated by recent advances in large
036   vision-Language models (VLMs) Liu et al. (2023b); Zhu et al. (2023), we aim to explore a path
037   forward towards enabling VLMs to obtain an in-depth reasoning-based understanding of 3D faces.
038   To investigate this question, we present FaceGPT, a vision-language model with an intricate ability to
039   reason about 3D human faces from visual and textual input. We represent faces as 3D morphable
040   model (3DMM) parameters that include parameters for the 3D face shape, expression, albedo, and
041   scene illumination. Following related work on image segmentation Lai et al. (2024) and human pose
042   estimation Feng et al. (2024), we extend the original token space of the VLM by incorporating a new
043   <FACE> token that is decoded into 3DMM parameters using an MLP (Fig. 1). Thus, when combined
044   with a differentiable computer graphics renderer Ravi et al. (2020), the VLM model becomes capable
045
      of synthesizing face images. This enables us to formulate FaceGPT within a model-based autoencoder
      framework Tewari et al. (2017), and hence to train our model in a fully self-supervised manner from
046
      in-the-wild images. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work combining vision-language
047
      model with an inverse graphics pipeline. During training, we freeze the visual encoder of the VLM
048   while training the MLP and the LLM using LoRA Hu et al. (2022). The model is trained with three
049   types of data: (1) In-the-wild face images for the self-supervised training of the <FACE> token
050   and 3DMM projection layers via inverse rendering. (2) Text-to-3DMM data for generating 3DMM
051   parameters from text that explicitly describes facial features. (3) Standard multi-modal instruction
052   tuning data to retain the general capability and quality of the VLM. We construct this dataset from a
053   set of face images by running an off-the-shelf self-supervised monocular face reconstruction method
      Li et al. (2023) and by generating textual descriptions of the faces via the original VLM.

116   2.1   M ONOCULAR MODEL - BASED FACE RECONSTRUCTION
117
      Realistically reconstructing digital human faces has been a longstanding challenge in computer vision
118
      and graphics due to their vast potential applications. Traditional methods primarily use parametric
119
      3D Morphable Models (3DMM) Blanz & Vetter (1999); Paysan et al. (2009); Li et al. (2017) with
120   PCA for dimensionality reduction to simplify high-dimensional 3D face scans, serving as a 3D
121   prior for representing unique facial characteristics and providing precise control. Recently, deep
122   learning-based methods that map 2D images to 3D face models have gained popularity. Early
123   methods struggled with the need for extensive 3D facial scan data paired with 2D images, which
124   was labor-intensive and costly. This limitation was addressed with the introduction of model-based
125   face autoencoders (MoFA) Tewari et al. (2017) that enabled self-supervised 3D face reconstruction.
126   MoFA uses a differentiable rendering layer to minimize differences between input and rendered
127   images, enabling end-to-end learning without ground-truth 3D faces, leading to a number of effective
128
      extensions in the self-supervised learning strategies Tewari et al. (2018b;a); Bas et al. (2017); Genova
      et al. (2018); Daněček et al. (2022). RingNet Sanyal et al. (2019) and DECA Feng et al. (2021b)
129
      use landmark-based training, predicting landmarks for input images and treating them as pseudo
130
      ground truth, measuring the distance between 2D face landmarks and their projections on the 3DMM
131   surface. The FOCUS Li et al. (2023) framework jointly trains a face autoencoder and an outlier
132   segmentation network, which makes the method robust to outliers such as occlusion and make-up.
133   These advancements significantly improved monocular model-based face reconstruction, making
134   it more efficient and effective. However, these methods are highly specialized and lack a deep
135   understanding of the semantics of human faces or the ability to relate faces to language, limiting their
136   overall scope and effectiveness.
137
138   2.2   T EXT- TO -3D FACE G ENERATION AND M ANIPULATION
139
140   Text-to-3D face generation and manipulation methods aim to use textual information for creating and
141   editing 3D faces. Methods like Dreamface Zhang et al. (2023) and Describe3D Wu et al. (2023b)
142   generate text-conditioned texture maps to render 3D morphable models (3DMM). TG-3DFace Yu
143
      et al. (2023) advances this by using tri-plane neural representations and extending the 3D-aware
      GAN, EG3D Chan et al. (2022), for end-to-end text-conditioned generation. For text-guided 3D
144
      face manipulation, methods like Latent3D Canfes et al. (2022) and ClipFace Aneja et al. (2023)
145
      optimize intermediate layers with a CLIP-based loss to generate UV-texture maps or predict texture
146   and expression latent codes. These methods, however, rely on 3D scan data and to train new mappers
147   for each text instruction. Unlike these task-specific approaches, FaceGPT is a general-purpose model
148   that reasons about 3D human faces from images, text, or both by leveraging general visual knowledge.
149   Our model can interact with users through conversations, discussing facial features and providing
150   relevant responses, while also being capable of following general user instructions.

      3     M ETHOD
191
192
      In this work, our aim is to augment existing large Vision Language Models (VLMs) with the ability
193
      of reasoning about 3D human faces without requiring manual human efforts. Inspired by established
194
      model-based face reconstruction methods, we represent 3D human face with 3D Morphable Model
195   (3DMM) Blanz & Vetter (1999) parameters, representing the face shape, expression, albedo, illumi-
196   nation and pose. In particular, we introduce a <FACE> token into the language space of the LLM,
197   which is mapped into the 3DMM parameter space and subsequently rendered into a 2D image, hence
198   enabling self-supervised 3D facial reconstruction. Fig. 2 presents the whole pipeline of FaceGPT.
199
200   3.1   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
201
202   Representing Face in language space. Inspired from recent advancements in LMMs, we treat the
203   human face as a distinct modality and incorporate its representation into the language space of VLM.
204   Specifically, we extend the vocabulary of VLM to include a new token <FACE> that specifically
205
      represents the human face. Given an input text prompt xtxt and/or input image ximg , the VLM f
      predicts text responses:
206
207

208   where ytxt = [t1 , . . . , tN ] is the output sequence of tokens with corresponding hidden states
209   [h1 , . . . , hN ]. When xtxt contains a face generation instruction, the resulting output ytxt will
210   include a <FACE> token, facilitating further 3DMM predictions.
211   From <FACE> token to 3DMM. If one of the output tokens tn ∈ ytxt is our defined <FACE> token,
212   we can extract the hidden state as h<FACE> = hn ∈ R4096 and project it using an MLP σ into the
213   latent 3DMM parameters θ = σ(h<FACE> ) = [α, δ, γ, ϕ, c] ∈ R257 , i.e. the 3D face shape α ∈ R80 ,
214   facial expression parameters δ ∈ R64 and texture γ ∈ R80 of a 3DMM, as well as the spherical
215   harmonics illumination ϕ ∈ R27 and camera parameters c ∈ R6 of the scene. The 3D vertices and
      triangles, as well as the color of the face mesh are then determined using the standard 3DMM model

      3.2   S ELF - SUPERVISED T RAINING
226
227   Our objective is to develop a VLM that is able to learn an enhanced comprehension of 3D human
228   faces in a self-supervised manner. Ultimately, the model should not only understand user instructions
229   reliably, but also to accurately reconstruct 3D faces from visual or text input. To accomplish this, we
230   have devised a self-supervised approach that incorporates 3DMM understanding into existing VLMs.
231   This method allows us to leverage face data without the need for costly 3DMM annotations. We also
232   construct a text-to-3DMM dataset in an unsupervised manner that supports this training paradigm,
233   enabling our model to learn these capabilities effectively and efficiently.
234   Self-supervised face reconstruction loss. To incorporate the 3DMM as a new modality into an
235   existing VLM, we add a new token <FACE> in the vocabulary of the LLM and fine-tune the language
236   modelling head. For fine-tuning the VLM without relying on manually annotated data, we introduce
237   a 2D self-supervised loss Lf ace , following established protocols of specialized face reconstruction
238   models Li et al. (2023):
239                      Lf ace = λpixel Lpixel + λper Lperc + λLM LLM + λreg Lreg ,                      (3)
240
241
      Specifically, Lf ace incorporates the following components:
242
243      Reconstruction loss. Lpixel = ||A ⊙ (ximg − ŷrec )||22 refers to the pixel-wise reconstruction loss
244   between reconstructed images ŷrec and the input images ximg . To avoid distortions when training
245   from in-the-wild data, a 2D skin mask A is estimated from the input images using a simple pre-trained
246
      Gaussian mixture model for the skin color Deng et al. (2019b).
247      Perceptual loss. Lperc = sim(fperc (ximg ), fperc (ŷrec )) estimates the cosine similarity between
248   the reconstructed image ŷrec and the input image ximg at the perceptual level using a pre-trained
249   feature extractor fperc .
250
         Landmark loss. LLM = ||LMimg − LMrec ||22 measures the L2 distance between the projected
251   2D landmarks of the estimated 3DMM LMrec and predicted 2D facial landmarks LMimg using an
252   off-the-shelf facial detector (Bulat & Tzimiropoulos, 2017).
253
254
        Parameter regularization. Lreg = ||θ||22 regularizes the estimated 3DMM parameters towards the
      mean value of the multi-variate Gaussian distribution of the 3DMM.
255
256      The weight parameters λf ace = [λpixel , λper , λLM , λreg ] balance the respective losses, and we
257   set them as established in prior work Li et al. (2023).
258   Preserving the ability for natural conversations about faces. We noticed that VLM’s loose the ability
259   to conduct general conversations about faces when trained self-supervised face reconstruction loss,
260   and tend to always output 3DMM parameters when queried with a face image. To resolve this
261   problem, we generate a face conversation dataset with accurate textual face descriptions, by querying
262   the VLM with face images and conversations that include multiple questions about facial attributes.
263   During training, we mix task-specific instructions that explicitly ask for 3DMM parameters with
264   general conversational data to regularize the training and preserve the ability for general non-3DMM
265   related conversations about faces. In contrast to prior works (Lai et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024)
266
      which generally employ a simple static question-answer template that restrains the model from
      providing diverse responses, our proposed strategy can effectively improve the instruction following
267
      performance for general face conversations.
268
269   Self-supervised Text-to-3DMM loss. We aim to enable the VLM to perform semantic face under-
      standing tasks, i.e. to predict faithful 3DMM parameters when only having textual input f (·, xtxt ).

      While related works for 3D human pose estimation Delmas, Ginger and Weinzaepfel, Philippe and
271   Lucas, Thomas and Moreno-Noguer, Francesc and Rogez, Grégory (2022); Feng et al. (2024) rely on
272   human-written fine-grained language descriptions of 3D human meshes, we aim to achieve text-to-

      3.3   S EMANTIC REASONING ABOUT HUMAN FACES
294
295   After being trained as described in the previous section, FaceGPT becomes a general model that
296   can estimate 3D facial expressions from single images, create facial features based on detailed
297   descriptions, and participate in question-and-answer dialogues. Most remarkably, even without
298   directly training the model for linking 3D facial attributes to subtle phrases that do not directly
299   contain specific facial traits, our model shows a zero-shot ability to reason about human faces from
300   descriptions that contain emotions or general descriptions of everyday situations. This means the
301   model can combine reasoning and world knowledge with the 3D facial representation. Similar as in
302   ChatPose Feng et al. (2024), our aim is to highlight these emerging capabilities by introducing the
303
      task of speculative face generation, which focuses on the model’s ability to reason about 3D human
      faces from speculative queries.
304
305   Speculative Face Generation. In this task, rather than providing explicit facial descriptions that
306   directly detail the shape and texture of features, we pose speculative queries related to a person’s
307   emotional state or general everyday situations. The model is then tasked to infer a plausible 3D
308   face, based on the assumption that the person is experiencing the described situation or emotion.
309
      For instance, a user might say, “Predict the face of a person who is excited about a surprise party.”
      Answering such queries requires an understanding of broader concepts like “excitement” and the
310
      ability to deduce the appropriate facial features, followed by generating the relevant 3D facial
311
      parameters. To build an evaluation dataset, we draw from the CelebAText dataset Sun et al. (2021)
312   for facial descriptions. We then use GPT-4 to rephrase these descriptions into questions about the
313   emotions tied to each expression, resulting in a total of 444 responses, with 64 examples selected for
314   evaluation. These responses undergo manual review and corrections in order to remove any direct
315   descriptions of facial features. We further estimate the 3DMM face parameters of every test image
316   using a state-of-the-art face autoencoder Li et al. (2023), resulting in paired data with speculative
317   descriptions and corresponding 3D face parameters. More details can be found in supplementary.

      3DMM parameters of faces. During training, only text is taken as input and the correspond-
325   ing face images are only used for loss computation. Given the absence of publicly available
326   datasets linking text descriptions to 3D face meshes and existing VLMs like LLaVA present

334   face images will be formatted with a template like "USER: <IMAGE> Can you give the
335   3DMM parameters of this person. ASSISTANT: Sure, it is <FACE>.".
336   To enhance the diversity and relevance of the conversations centered around human face images,
337   we also generate face-centric conversations for each face image. This approach enriches the
338   contextual understanding of the VLM concerning the newly introduced token <FACE>. We adopt
339
      the CelebA-HQ trainset as the image to face reconstruction dataset.
340   Multimodal Instruction-Following Data. This data is general-purpose VQA data, and it is used to
341   preserve the VLM’s ability of understanding a user’s instructions. Following LLaVA v1.5, We use

344   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
345
      Network Architecture. We build our model on Large Vision Language Model LLaVA-1.5-7B (Liu
346
      et al., 2023a) with CLIP-ViT-L-336px as vision encoder and Vicuna v1.5 as the LLM backbone.
347
      LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) is applied to efficiently fine-tune the VLM. An MLP head with GeLU
348   activations (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) and channels [5120, 5120, 257] is appended to the last layer
349   of the VLM to predict the 3D human face parameters.
350
351
      3D Face Model. We use the Basel Face Model (BFM) 2017 Gerig et al. (2018) as the 3D face model.
      The face is parameterized as the semantic code vector θ = [α, δ, γ, ϕ, c] ∈ R257 in (Tewari et al.,
352
      2017), which includes 3D shape parameters α ∈ R80 , facial expression parameters δ ∈ R64 , texture
353
      of 3DMM γ ∈ R80 , illumination ϕ ∈ R27 and camera parameters c ∈ R6 .
354
355   LLaVa-Key baseline. As there are no VLM-based methods that can perform 3D face reconstruction,
356   we introduce the LLaVa-Key. The model is finetuned to predict facial landmarks in the format of pure
357
      text given either images or textual description of human faces as input . For each input, gradient-based
      optimization is applied to these predicted landmarks to fit the 3DMM using Eq. (3) to obtain a 3D face
358
      reconstruction. It is important to note that LLaVa-Key employs test-time fine-tuning, making it an
359
      inherently different baseline compared to all other feed-forward methods. Nevertheless, LLaVA-Key
360   is useful to show the effect of a directly using the token space of VLMs to encode 3D information.
361
362   Implementation Details. The training uses 8 NVIDIA 48G A40 GPUs. We utilize deepspeed (Rasley
363
      et al., 2020) engine and ZeRO optimizer (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) for efficient training. We use
      AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with learning rate and weight decay set to 2e − 5 and
364
      0, respectively. We also follow the standard setting of using a WarmupDecayLR as the learning rate
365
      scheduler, where the warm-up iterations are set to 100. The weights of the text generation loss λtxt
366   and the face reconstruction loss λf ace are set to 1.0 and 0.1, respectively. Following (Li et al., 2023),
367   those of the pixel loss λpixel , the perceptual loss λper , the landmark loss λLM , and the regularization
368   loss λreg are set to 0.5, 0.25, 5e-4 and 0.1, respectively. The landmarks of human faces are obtained
369   with (Bulat & Tzimiropoulos, 2017) and pre-trained ArcFace (Deng et al., 2019a) is used to compute
370   perceptual loss. The batch size is set to 8 per GPU and gradient accumulation step is set to 4.

      Evaluation Metrics. For text-based face generation task, we compare the estimated 3D human face
372   with the ground truth mesh to measure the Chamfer Distance(CD), Complete Rate(CR) and Relative
373   Face Recognition Rate(RFRR) following Describe3D (Wu et al., 2023a). Both Chamfer Distance and
374   Complete Rate would be used to reflect the accuracy of 3D meshes. And Relative Face Recognition
375   Rate can measure the identity similarity of the textured 3D face rendering. We also additionally
376   perform a user study for speculative face generation task as it is a difficult task requiring common
377   sense of human. For image-based face reconstruction task, we measure the L2 photometric error
      in RGB space and the L2 landmark error to reflect the quality of 3D face’s eometry and texture.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      FaceGPT is the first self-supervised learning framework for Large Vision-Language Models to reason
534   about 3D human faces. We show that VLMs can learn to predict detailed 3D human faces from not
535   only images, but also from textual inputs, in a fully self-supervised manner via inverse rendering. As
536   a generalist model, FaceGPT achieves strong results across various tasks, including text-based face
537   generation, traditional 3D face reconstruction, visual instruction following. FaceGPT also presents
538   impressive ability to infer 3D faces from abstract and indirect text descriptions. We believe our work
539   also has general implications beyond face analytics, as it points towards a way forward to enable
      large multi-modal language-models to reason about our 3D world without supervision.

      A.1    S PECULATIVE FACE G ENERATION B ENCHMARK
813
814
815       As an AI visual assistant specializing in human face analysis, your task is to infer possible
816       activities, events, and emotional states a person might be experiencing based on a detailed
817       visual and textual description of their face. Your goal is to focus on the high-level emotional
818       context and plausible scenarios that this person could be engaging in, rather than anatomical
819
          details. For each facial description, consider the following key questions before formulating
          your response:
820
821
          1. What emotion is the person likely experiencing? How does this emotion differ from a
822       typical representation of this feeling?
823       2. What specific activity might this individual be participating in, based on their emotional
824       state and facial expression?
825       3. What event could have triggered the current emotional expression or facial state?
826       4. Could the individual be engaged in other parallel activities that are influencing their
827       expression?
828
829       Once you’ve considered these questions, craft 5 distinct facial descriptions, each beginning
830
          with "This person," followed by one or two sentences that clearly suggest a plausible activity
          or situation. Ensure that each description provides a rich context that allows the user to
831
          imagine and even replicate the facial expression if they were in that situation. Avoid vague or
832
          general terms, and be as diverse as possible in your interpretations.
833
834       Example answers and face descriptions:
835       Answer to the questions:
836       1. The individual appears to be deeply worried, but there’s an undertone of surprise not typical
837       of worry alone.
838       2. The individual could be reviewing a critical work email and is taken aback by unexpected
839       information.
840       3. It seems like the individual just encountered an unexpected delay for an important event.
841
          4. Additionally, the person might be contemplating a difficult decision while trying to process
          sudden news.
842
843
          Facial descriptions:
844       1. This person looks like they have just received news that their flight has been canceled at
845       the last minute.
846       2. This person is struggling to concentrate during a crucial work presentation, trying to mask
847       their frustration.
848       3. This person might be reading an intense plot twist in a book, causing both confusion and
849       intrigue.
850       4. This person seems to be on the verge of delivering uncomfortable feedback to a colleague.
851       5. This person appears to be caught off guard in a meeting, unexpectedly asked to answer a
852
          difficult question.

858   corresponding 3D faces, , we choose to construct the Speculative Face Generation Benchmark with

862   their explicit descriptions from CelebAText dataset Sun et al. (2021). Using the prompt detailed in

871
872   For Image-based 3D Face Reconstruction and text-based 3D Face Reconstruction, we develop
873   a baseline based on VLM called LLaVA-Key where the human face is represented as text.
874
      Following Feng et al. (2024), we represent the human face through the 68 landmarks on 2D
      images as landmarks tracks the locations of eyes, nose, mouth and so on, which also contains rich
875
      information about faces. We utilize templates like "USER: <IMAGE> Please estimate
876
      the 68 facial landmarks coordinates. The output format should be
877   Jawline-1:(x1,y1),Jawline-2:(x2,y2),... ASSISTANT: The detected
878   landmarks are Jawline-0:(41, 88),Jawline-1:(43, 107), ...,Inner
879   Lip-67:(101, 159)." for image-to-face data and templates like "USER: There
880   is a person with the following description:{description} Please
881   estimate the 68 facial landmarks coordinates. The output format
882   should be Jawline-1:(x1,y1),Jawline-2:(x2,y2),... ASSISTANT: The
883   detected landmarks are Jawline-0:(41, 88),Jawline-1:(43, 107),
884   ...,Inner Lip-67:(101, 159)." for text-to-face data where {description} would
885
      be replaces with text description for faces generated by LLaVA. And we use an off-the-shelf face
      detector to provide the coordinates landmarks for each face. LLaVA model is finetuned to produce
886
      2D coordinates of 68 faicl landmarks with the formatted data. After finetuning, LLaVA would be
887
      prompted to predict the landmarks in the test set and an optimization-based method is used to fit
888   3DMM for the estimated landmarks. For image-to-face data, we utilize lossf ace defined in 3 to
889   optimize the 3DMM parameters on estimated landmarks and input image. For text-to-face data,
890   we only utilize the landmark loss and regularization defined in 3.2 for optimization. Experiments
891   reflect that representing human face as a new modality outperform naively encoding human face in
