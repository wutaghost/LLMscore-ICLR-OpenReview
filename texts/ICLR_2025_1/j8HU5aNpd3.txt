011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013               Language models (LMs) have demonstrated an improved capacity to handle long-
014               context information, yet existing long-context benchmarks primarily measure
015               LMs’ retrieval abilities with extended inputs, e.g., pinpointing a short phrase from
016
                  long-form text. Therefore, they may fall short when evaluating models’ global
                  context understanding capacity, such as synthesizing and reasoning over content
017
                  across input to generate the response. In this paper, we study long-context lan-
018
                  guage model (LCLM) evaluation through many-shot in-context learning (ICL).
019               Concretely, we identify the skills each ICL task requires, and examine models’
020               long-context capabilities on them. We ask the first question: What types of ICL
021               tasks benefit from additional demonstrations, and are these tasks effective at eval-
022               uating LCLMs? We find that classification and summarization tasks show notable
023               performance improvements with additional demonstrations, while translation and
024               reasoning tasks do not exhibit clear trends. This suggests the classification tasks
025               predominantly test models’ retrieval skills. Next, we ask: To what extent does each
026               task require retrieval skills versus global context understanding from LCLMs? We
027
                  develop metrics to categorize ICL tasks into two groups: (i) retrieval tasks that re-
                  quire strong retrieval ability to pinpoint relevant examples, and (ii) global context
028
                  understanding tasks that necessitate a deeper comprehension of the full input.
029
                  We find that not all datasets can effectively evaluate these long-context capabili-
030               ties. To address this gap, we introduce a new many-shot ICL benchmark, M ANY-
031               ICLB ENCH, designed to characterize LCLMs’ retrieval and global context un-
032               derstanding capabilities separately. We benchmark 11 open-weight LCLMs using
033               M ANY ICLB ENCH. We find that while state-of-the-art models demonstrate satis-
034               factory performance up to 64k tokens in retrieval tasks, many models experience
035               significant performance drops at only 16k tokens in global context understanding
036               tasks.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Long-context language models (LCLMs) have revolutionized the way users interact with language
      models by extending the context size from 2K to 128K or even 1M tokens (Team et al., 2024a; GLM
042
      et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024), which unlock challenging applications, such as long- and multi-
043
      document summarization, multi-turn dialogue, and code repository comprehension. Despite the
044   recent progress in building LCLMs, existing benchmarks primarily evaluate these models’ retrieval
045   capabilities (Liu et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2024). From synthetic tasks such as Needle-in-A-Haystack
046   (Kamradt, 2023) and RULER benchmark (Hsieh et al., 2024) to real-world challenges like long-
047   novel QA (Karpinska et al., 2024), the majority of benchmarks assess how well LCLMs retrieve
048   specific pieces of information from extensive contexts. As a result, evaluating models’ global
049   understanding of the full context remains lacking.
050   To fill the gap, Li et al. (2024) introduce LongICLBench, which uses many-shot ICL classification
051   tasks to evaluate models’ long-context performance, arguing that these tasks require the compre-
052   hension of the entire input. A few other works have also explored many-shot ICL for long-context
053

054
      models (Agarwal et al., 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024). Yet, they have mainly relied on classification
055   tasks (Li et al., 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024), which are insufficient to distinguish which skills LCLMs
056   require to perform well on many-shot ICL classification tasks. Recently, Agarwal et al. (2024) study
057   non-classification ICL tasks but only on Gemini 1.5 Pro. In this work, we want to conduct a com-
058   prehensive study on many-shot ICL across a wide range of models, with a goal of identifying tasks
059   that benefit from additional demonstrations and explore their utility in evaluating long-context
060   models. Moreover, we seek to determine the extent to which these tasks rely on retrieval versus
061   global context understanding.
062
063
064   RQ1: Which tasks benefit from many-shot ICL? First, we investigate ICL tasks that are used in
065   prior work, including classification, summarization, and reasoning, under many-shot settings with
066   context lengths from 1k to 128k (Agarwal et al., 2024). We find that classification and summa-
067   rization tasks show strong positive correlation between context lengths and model performance.
068
      Our findings indicate that translation and reasoning tasks such as ARC (Clark et al., 2018) and
      FLORES-200 (NLLB Team, 2022) do not gain much performance with an increasing number of
069
      demonstrations. Science and symbolic reasoning tasks exhibit inconsistent trends between context
070
      lengths and model performance. This variance in performance is mainly attributed to the specific na-
071   ture of tasks, where more demonstrations do not boost the models’ task understanding. Interestingly,
072   math tasks benefit from additional demonstrations only when step-by-step solutions are derived and
073   using strong LCLMs.
074
      RQ2: What skill does each task primarily measure? We then analyze the retrieval and global
075
      context understanding skills necessary for each ICL task. We use the ratio between the performance
076
      change of removing dissimilar examples and the change of removing similar examples. A high ratio
077   means a more pronounced drop in performance upon removing similar examples, which indicates
078   the task’s heavy reliance on retrieval capabilities. Our analysis indicates that existing many-shot
079   ICL classification tasks (Li et al., 2024) predominantly assess retrieval abilities rather than global
080   context understanding. This leads us to categorize tasks into retrieval and non-retrieval groups.
081
      Subsequently, we explore whether non-retrieval tasks genuinely benefit from additional demon-
082
      strations and assess models’ global context understanding skills. By comparing the performance
083   of models with unique demonstrations versus duplicated examples on non-retrieval tasks, we aim to
084   determine if duplicating examples adversely affects performance compared to adding new examples.
085   If this is the case, it signifies that unique demonstrations provide additional beneficial information,
086   reinforcing the notion that these tasks require global context understanding. Using this method, we
087   identify a subset of non-retrieval tasks that evaluate models’ comprehension of global content.
088
      Following the categorization, we propose a new many-shot ICL benchmark, M ANY ICLB ENCH,
089   designed for evaluating long-context models and advocate for the inclusion of many-shot ICL tasks
090   as effective evaluation candidates. Importantly, on M ANY ICLB ENCH, models are tested to either
091   retrieve the most similar demonstrations or assimilate all demonstrations to enhance their under-
092   standing of the task (Lin & Lee, 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024). Therefore, M ANY ICLB ENCH evalu-
093   ates both retrieval skills and global context understanding, thus providing a holistic assessment of
094   long-context models’ capabilities.
095   In summary, we make the following contributions in this paper:
096
097          • Investigate whether ICL tasks benefit from additional demonstrations and assess their suit-
098            ability for evaluating LCLMs with a context length up to 128k tokens.
099
100          • Develop methods to characterize the primary skills evaluated by ICL tasks, where we focus
101            on distinguishing between retrieval capabilities and global context understanding.
102
103          • Construct a many-shot ICL benchmark, named M ANY ICLB ENCH, designed for evaluat-
104            ing LCLMs on both retrieval and global context understanding, while excluding irrelevant
105            datasets previously used in LCLM evaluation.
106
107          • Benchmark 11 widely-used state-of-the-art LCLMs on M ANY ICLB ENCH to assess their
               performance comprehensively.

      2.1   L ONG -C ONTEXT L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND E VALUATION
111
112
      As large language models grow in scale, there is an increasing demand for handling tasks that require
113   extended contexts. Tasks such as long document summarization (Kryściński et al., 2022), conver-
114   sations with long-context memory (Xu et al., 2021), and repository-level code completion (Zhang
115   et al., 2023) have garnered significant interest. Advances in efficient attention mechanisms, such as
116   flash attention (Dao et al., 2022) and grouped query attention (Ainslie et al., 2023), alongside the
117   development of GPUs with larger memory capacities, have enabled LLMs to be trained on extended
118   contexts. Techniques like position interpolation (Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023) and context
119   compression (Chevalier et al., 2023; Mohtashami & Jaggi, 2023; Jiang et al., 2024) have further
120   extended the context window size to up to 1 million tokens.
121   Despite these advancements, the NLP community still seeks a universal and effective method for
122   evaluating long-context models. One prominent task is Needle-in-a-Haystack (Kamradt, 2023),
123   which requires models to retrieve the most relevant document from a large set of documents. Cur-
124   rently, most evaluation benchmarks focus on synthetic tasks that primarily assess the retrieval capa-
125   bilities of long-context models (Hsieh et al., 2024; Kamradt, 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024).
126   Only a few benchmarks, such as Karpinska et al. (2024) and Zhang et al. (2024), emphasize the
127
      model’s ability to comprehend the global context. For example, Karpinska et al. (2024) manually
      curated a set of challenging questions based on various novels to evaluate global context understand-
128
      ing. It is the first work to create a realistic long-context benchmark emphasizing retrieval and global
129
      context understanding skills.

132   2.2   M ANY- SHOT ICL WITH LCLM S
133
134
      Because the context length of large language models expands, the number of demonstrations that can
      be utilized in ICL has also increased. Studies by Li et al. (2024), Bertsch et al. (2024), and Agarwal
135
      et al. (2024) have examined various properties of ICL under the many-shot setting. Bertsch et al.
136
      (2024) explore whether models are merely performing retrieval tasks or genuinely understanding
137   the tasks during many-shot ICL classification. Similarly, Agarwal et al. (2024) analyzes the per-
138   formance of tasks beyond classification in the many-shot context, using Gemini-Pro, and finds that
139   additional demonstrations generally enhance task performance. Furthermore, Li et al. (2024) pro-
140   pose a long-context evaluation benchmark LongICLBench comprising many-shot ICL classification
141   tasks, noting that current long-context models still face challenges in this area. None of the prior
142   works has studied what skill each ICL task measures LCLMs for. LongICLBench mostly focuses
143   on classification tasks, which may only evaluates the retrieval ability of LCLMs. Unlike previous
144   studies, our work provides a more comprehensive analysis of many-shot ICL across a diverse set
145
      of tasks and multiple models. We introduce novel metrics to measure retrieval skills and the level
      of task understanding required for each task. We identify a set of ICL tasks suitable for evaluation
146
      and present a refined long-context evaluation benchmark with fine-grained categorization based on
147
      required retrieval skills and task understanding.

150   2.3   I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
151
152   In-context learning (ICL) enables models to quickly recognize and perform tasks during inference
153
      by conditioning on a set of provided demonstrations (Brown et al., 2020). Many previous works have
      sought to understand the mechanisms behind in-context learning (ICL). Xie et al. (2022) suggests
154
      that models implicitly perform Bayesian inference during inference, retrieving relevant skills learned
155
      during pretraining. Additionally, Lin & Lee (2024) introduces the concept of a dual operating mode
156   in ICL: task learning and task retrieval. With sufficient demonstrations, models can adapt to unseen
157   tasks learned during pretraining, thereby enhancing performance as the number of demonstrations
158   increases. To explore how many-shot ICL operates, Bertsch et al. (2024) modified the attention
159   patterns by restricting attention among individual examples. Their findings suggest that performance
160   improvements primarily arise from retrieving similar examples rather than comprehending the task.
161   However, their experiment is limited to classification tasks. It may also be biased when comparing
      full attention and block attention, as block attention allows access to more demonstrations. Our work

      To investigate many-shot ICL across various tasks and model sizes, we select 11 models ranging
185
      from 3.8B to 123B parameters. Our evaluation includes 12 datasets with 22 subtasks, spanning clas-
186
      sification, summarization, reasoning, and translation domains. For each task, we randomly sample
187   200 data points from the test set, using the full test set if it contains fewer than 200 samples.
188
189   For each task, we construct prompts for different context window sizes by incrementally adding new
190
      demonstrations from the training set to the prompt of the shorter context window size and duplicate
      training examples if they are insufficient to fill the context window. To ensure a fair comparison, we
191
      randomize the order of demonstrations and consistently use the same set of examples across all con-
192
      text sizes. For simplicity, we apply greedy decoding across all models and conduct each experiment
193   using three different random seeds. For the prompt construction, we only include demonstrations
194   and provide minimal task instruction.

511   7   C ONCLUSION
512
513   We investigated many-shot in-context learning (ICL) across various tasks using different open-
514   weight models, assessing their suitability for evaluating long-context language models (LCLMs).
515   Our findings indicate that classification and summarization tasks consistently benefit from addi-
516
      tional demonstrations, while other tasks do not. To identify a set of tasks suitable for long-context
      evaluation, we introduced the concept of retrieval load ratio to assess the retrieval demands of differ-
517
      ent tasks. This analysis revealed that classification tasks predominantly rely on the model’s retrieval
518
      capabilities. For non-retrieval tasks, we conducted duplication experiments to differentiate global
519   context understanding tasks from those that introduce noise. Based on these insights, we categorized
520   tasks into two distinct groups: retrieval tasks and global context understanding tasks. Furthermore,
521   we introduced a novel many-shot ICL benchmark, ManyICLBench, designed to evaluate both re-
522   trieval and global context understanding skills of LCLMs. Benchmarking open-weight LCLMs
523   on ManyICLBench revealed that most models struggle with global context understanding tasks at
524   lengths below 16k tokens. In contrast, performance on retrieval tasks tends to decline after 32k
525   tokens.

       A    DATASETS
1243
1244
1245   BANKING77 (Casanueva et al., 2020) is an intent classification task in the banking domain. It has
1246   over 10k customer service queries labeled with 77 intents.
1247   GoEmotions (Demszky et al., 2020) contains 58 Reddit comments labeled for 27 emotion categories
1248   or Neutral.
1249
       DialogRE (Yu et al., 2020) is a relation extraction dataset that is built based on transcripts of an
1250
       American TV show Friends. It comprises 10,168 relation triples for 1,788 dialogues and 36 total
1251   relations types. We only focus on relation classification for this dataset.
1252
1253   TREC (Li & Roth, 2002; Hovy et al., 2001) is a question classification dataset with six coarse and
1254
       50 fine class labels. It contains 5,500 questions in the training set and 500 in the test set.
1255   CLINC150 (Larson et al., 2019) is an intent classification dataset with 150 intents from 10 domains.
1256
       MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a dataset of 12,5000 challenging completion mathematics prob-
1257
       lems. Each problem has a full step-by-step solution. We use four subdomains from the dataset:
1258   algebra, geometry, counting and probability, and number theory.
1259
1260   GSM8K (Hendrycks et al., 2021) consists of 8.5K high quality grade school math problems created
1261
       by human problem writers. These problems take between 2 and 8 steps to solve, and solutions pri-
       marily involve performing a sequence of elementary calculations using basic arithmetic operations
1262
       (+ - / *) to reach the final answer.
1263
1264   BBH (Srivastava et al., 2022) is a subset of 23 challenging BIG-Bench tasks (Suzgun et al., 2022),
1265   which include task categories such as mathematics, commonsense reasoning, and question answer-
1266   ing. We use four subtasks from BBH-Hard: geometric shape, salient translation error detection,
1267
       word sorting, and dyck languages.
1268   ARC (Clark et al., 2018) is a dataset of 7,787 genuine grade-school level, multiple-choice science
1269   questions. The dataset is partitioned into a Challenge Set and Easy Set, where the former contains
1270   only questions answered incorrectly by both a retrieval-based algorithm and a word co-occurrence
1271   algorithm.
1272   GPQA (Rein et al., 2023) is a dataset of 448 multiple-choice questions with detailed explanations
1273   written by domain experts in biology, physics, and chemistry.
1274
       XLSUM (Hasan et al., 2021) is a summarization dataset that focuses on news articles from BBC. In
1275
       this work, we focus only on English news articles.
1276
1277   FLORES-200 (NLLB Team, 2022) is a translation benchmark that contains many low-resource
1278   languages. We follow Agarwal et al. (2024) and choose the translation task from Tamil to English.
1279   Additionally, we also test models on Chinese and Spanish.
1280
1281
1282   B    M ODELS
1283
1284   Llama-3.1 8B and 70B (Dubey et al., 2024): We use both the 8B and 70B Llama 3.1 Instruction
1285   models. These multilingual models are trained on a 128k context window using position interpola-
1286   tion. The models are further fine-tuned with synthetic long-text Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) data
1287   and also undergo Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024).
1288
       GLM-4-9B-Chat (GLM et al., 2024): This is a 9-billion-parameter multilingual model, also trained
1289   on a 128k context window with position interpolation. It is further fine-tuned with labeled long-text
1290   SFT data and undergoes a DPO stage.
1291
1292
       Mistral Family (Mistral AI, 2024): We use both 12-billion-parameter and 123-billion-parameter
       multilingual models, trained on a 128k context window.
1293
1294   Qwen2 7B and 72B (Yang et al., 2024): These two models are trained with a context size of 32k
1295   tokens, and their context window is extended to 128k by YARN (Peng et al., 2023), a dynamic
       position interpolation technique.
