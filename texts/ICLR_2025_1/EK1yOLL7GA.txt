009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we introduce Token Condensation as Adaptation (TCA), a training-
012            free approach designed to mitigate distribution shifts encountered by vision-
013            language models (VLMs) during test-time inference. TCA bridges distribution
014            gaps at the patch level by condensing image tokens that exhibit low attentiveness
015            to the <cls> token. Recognizing the <cls> token may correspond to universal
016            concepts, TCA identifies and tracks the most reliable <cls> tokens that align
017            specifically with target classes from historical data streams. To achieve this, we
018            propose a context token reservoir (CTR), which retains tokens with the lowest
019
               uncertainty as “anchors” to guide the preservation of class-relevant tokens during
               inference. These anchors, in turn, act as token-level classifiers to correct VLM
020
               predictions and improve visual-text alignment. Utilizing anchors sampled from
021
               CTR, TCA condenses tokens through two operations: (1) pruning class-irrelevant
022            tokens that consistently rank low across all attention heads to reach cross-head
023            consensus on their irrelevance, and (2) merging the remaining class-ambiguous
024            tokens into representative centers using coreset selection, maintaining linear com-
025            putational complexity. As the first method to explore token efficiency in test-time
026            adaptation, TCA consistently demonstrates superior performance across cross-
027            dataset and out-of-distribution adaptation tasks, reducing GFLOPs by 12.2% to
028            48.9% while achieving accuracy improvements up to 21.4% against the strongest
029            baseline without introducing additional parameters.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Online test-time adaptation (TTA) (Wang et al., 2023c) has emerged as a promising strategy to han-
034
      dle distribution shifts encountered during inference (Liang et al., 2023). TTA dynamically fine-tunes
035
      pretrained models on unlabeled data batches, enhancing generalization by aligning intermediate-
036   layer batch statistics (Niu et al., 2023), optimizing for first-order flatness in the loss landscape (Foret
037   et al., 2021), promoting self-supervised consistency across augmentations (Zhang et al., 2022), or
038   tracking model historical weights (Lee & Chang, 2024). Despite the success of traditional TTA
039   methods, they often require computationally expensive tuning of the backbone’s parameters. This
040   challenge is further amplified in vision-language models (VLMs), which consist of vast parameter
041   sets and require large batch sizes (e.g., 256) for stabilizing adaptation (Döbler et al., 2024).
042   Test-time prompting (TPT) offers a more efficient alternative for TTA by shifting adaptation focus
043   to the language side of VLMs, learning a small set of task-specific context prompts for downstream
044   tasks while freezing the visual backbone. Nevertheless, TPT largely overlooks the impact of visual
045   distribution shifts. Adapting to high-variance target images through prompts often relies on external
046   source data (Samadh et al., 2023) or extensive data augmentation (Feng et al., 2023) (e.g., 60× more
047   AugMix or diffusion-based synthetic samples). In strict online TTA settings, where the batch size is
048   constrained to one, this reliance on augmentation significantly inflates computational costs, leading
049   to a 60× rise in GFLOPs compared single-sample processing (i.e., 1108.61 vs. 17.59 GFLOPs).
050
      The need for gradient backpropagation during inference further increases the computation burden,
      making exiting TPT suboptimal for many resource-constrained applications.
051
052   In this paper, we attempt to tackle visual shifts at a patch level by introducing a novel approach
053   named Token Condensation as Adaptation (TCA). TCA allows the model to adapt on the fly to
      unseen target domains while accelerating VLM inference by 20% fewer GFLOPs without requiring

071   as depicted in Figure 2a. The results reveal that discarding tokens with lower attentiveness to its

      chors serve as proxies to bridge visual and textual representations, guiding <cls> attentiveness to

086
087   Building on these two insights, we design a context-aware token pruning and merging mechanism
088   to condense tokens within the ViT blocks. As shown in Figure 1, the context from the target class
089   is incorporated into the token condensation function via a context token reservoir (CTR). At each
090   adaptation step, the most reliable <cls> tokens are retained as “anchors” and used to guide the
091
      selection and preservation of class-relevant tokens. These anchors, in turn, act as token-level clas-
      sifiers to correct VLM predictions and improve the final visual-text alignment. Specifically, our
092
      token pruning strategy utilizes consensus across attention heads to retain only the most relevant
093
      class-specific patches, while class-ambiguous tokens are merged into representative centers using a
094   coreset selection algorithm. Empirically, we show that a minimal number of centers is sufficient to
095   stabilize performance, allowing TCA to scale efficiently with linear complexity.
096
097
      To our knowledge, this is the first work to explore token condensation in the context of test-time
      adaptation. The efficiency and effectiveness of our training-free TCA approach have been rigor-
098
      ously validated across multiple TTA benchmark datasets, including ImageNet and four associated
099
      natural distribution shift datasets, along with ten fine-grained classification datasets. In extensive
100   evaluations with traditional TTA baselines, prompting, and test-time prompting approaches, our
101   method consistently outperforms the baselines by up to 21.4% compared with the strongest base-
102   line, while reducing GFLOPs cost by 12.2% to 48.9%. For reproducibility, we provide the complete
103   benchmarking toolkit in the supplementary material for reference.

107   Online Test-time Adaptation. To address performance degradation during test time, online test-
      time adaptation (TTA) has gained significant attention. Current TTA methods can be categorized

108
      into three main types (Wang et al., 2023c): optimization-based, data-based, and model-based ap-
109   proaches. Optimization-based methods focus on model updates and optimization objectives (Goyal
110   et al., 2022; Marsden et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022). A prominent example is Tent (Wang et al.,
111   2021), which adapts Batch Normalization layers (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) using entropy minimiza-
112   tion. SAR (Niu et al., 2023) extends this approach to Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016) and
113   Group Normalization (Wu & He, 2018) with sharpness-aware minimization (Foret et al., 2021).
114   Data-based methods include augmentations like selective augmentation (Wang et al., 2022) and ad-
115   versarial augmentation (Tomar et al., 2023), as well as the use of memory banks (Gong et al., 2022;
116   Yuan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022). Model-based approaches involve architectural modifications
117
      to enhance model adaptability during testing (Liu et al., 2024a; Iwasawa & Matsuo, 2021; Jang
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). However, these methods typically depend on large batch sizes and
118
      augmentations, which introduce significant latency for online prediction.
119
120   Recently, vision-language models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) have excelled beyond fixed label
121   sets, rendering traditional TTA methods less suitable (Döbler et al., 2024). As a result, various on-
122   line adaptation strategies have been proposed to improve zero-shot generalization. Test-time prompt
123
      tuning has emerged as a key approach in this context. TPT (Shu et al., 2022) optimizes learnable
      prompts using data augmentations and soft entropy minimization, Diff-TPT (Feng et al., 2023) en-
124
      riches this with more diverse augmentations (Rombach et al., 2022), while C-TPT (Yoon et al., 2024)
125
      focusing on model calibration. Other methods like VTE (Döbler et al., 2024) and DART (Liu et al.,
126   2024b) leverage prompt ensembles with DART further employing moving averages to boost perfor-
127   mance. SwapPrompt (Ma et al., 2023) incorporates an EMA-updated target prompt. AdaPrompt
128   (Zhang et al., 2024) utilizes a class-balanced memory bank to enhance adaptability. SCP (Wang
129   et al., 2024) builds on TPT with a teacher-student framework to prevent semantic drift, while RLCF
130   (Zhao et al., 2024) incorporates reinforcement learning strategy (Williams, 1992) to optimize the
131   adaptation process. Beyond these, MTA (Zanella & Ayed, 2024) introduces a new objective based
132   on test-time augmentation to optimize visual features in the semantic space. TDA (Karmanov et al.,
133   2024) further improves CLIP’s zero-shot ability by incorporating positive and negative caches with
134
      a training-free adapter. However, it relies on a large number of hyperparameters and is highly sen-
      sitive to them, while incurring significant computational costs during inference. In contrast, our
135
      approach strikes a better balance between computational efficiency and performance, outperforming
136
      both training-required and training-free methods.
137
138   Token Condensation in Vision Transformers. Vision transformers have achieved notable success
139   in image recognition tasks, but their deployment is often limited by resource-constrained environ-
140
      ments. To address this, various token condensation methods (Meng et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2021;
      Ryoo et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Zong et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a) have been
141
      proposed to reduce the computational overhead, primarily through two strategies: token pruning
142
      and token merging. Token pruning eliminates less informative tokens to save computation, as seen
143   in methods like EViT (Liang et al., 2022), which retains tokens based on their attentiveness to the
144   <cls> tokens. ATS (Fayyaz et al., 2022) introduces input-dependent token pruning to adapt to vari-
145   ability across inputs. Token merging, on the other hand, seeks to combine similar tokens to reduce
146   redundancy. For instance, ToME (Bolya et al., 2023) uses bipartite soft matching to merge neigh-
147   boring tokens that exhibit similarity. Hybrid approaches have also emerged, such as TPS (Wei et al.,
148   2023), which prunes tokens and transfers information to retained ones using nearest-neighbor match-
149   ing, and PruMerge (Shang et al., 2024), which prunes inattentive tokens using interquartile range and
150   merges via k-nearest neighbors. While previous works have focused on enhancing efficiency within
151
      pure ViT models, our approach utilizes token condensation from a different perspective: addressing
      multimodal distribution shifts in VLMs. This shift remains underexplored, particularly in how to
152
      use semantic guidance to prune irrelevant visual tokens that introduce ambiguity. By condensing
153
      these tokens, we effectively reduce such distribution shifts, enhancing test-time performance while
154   simultaneously lowering computational costs.

      Datasets. Following prior works, we conducted two main benchmarks: the cross-dataset (CD)
351
      benchmark and the out-of-distribution (OOD) benchmark. The CD benchmark assesses the model’s
352
      performance on unseen classes across 10 datasets: Aircraft (Maji et al., 2013), Caltech101 (Fei-Fei
353   et al., 2007), Cars (Krause et al., 2013), DTD (Cimpoi et al., 2014), EuroSAT (Helber et al., 2019),
354   Flower102 (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008), Food101 (Bossard et al., 2014), Pets (Parkhi et al., 2012),
355   SUN397 (Xiao et al., 2010), and UCF101 (Soomro et al., 2012). In contrast, the OOD benchmark
356   focuses on evaluating the model’s effectiveness on shifted data using label sets previously seen by
357   CLIP. This includes variants of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009): ImageNet-A (Hendrycks et al., 2019),
358   ImageNet-V2 (Recht et al., 2019), ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and ImageNet-S (Wang
359   et al., 2019).
360   Baselines. To provide a comprehensive evaluation, we compare TCA with existing approaches
361   across four categories: (1) Prompt-tuning methods like CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) and CoCoOp
362   (Zhou et al., 2022a), which require multi-epoch adaptation; (2) Conventional online test-time adap-
363   tation (TTA) methods such as Tent (Wang et al., 2021) and SAR (Niu et al., 2023). Tent updates
364   batch normalization layers, while SAR further incorporates sharpness-aware minimization for reli-
365   able model updates. Following Döbler et al. (2024), we reran these experiments with adjusted batch
366   sizes to align with our settings; (3) Test-time prompting methods, including TPT (Shu et al., 2022),
367
      C-TPT (Yoon et al., 2024), and Diff-TPT (Feng et al., 2023), as well as TTA methods for CLIP
      such as MTA (Zanella & Ayed, 2024) and TDA (Karmanov et al., 2024); and (4) Token pruning
368
      and merging methods for ViTs, such as EViT (Liang et al., 2022), ToMe (Bolya et al., 2023), and
369
      ATS (Fayyaz et al., 2022). As ATS is an adaptive token pruning method with no fixed budget, we
370   constrain its computational cost by an upper bound to ensure fair comparison.
371
372   Implementation Details. We utilize the official CLIP 1 prompts as text inputs. The batch size is
373
      set to 1 without data augmentations to mimic realistic deployment scenarios. All experiments are
      conducted using the pre-trained CLIP models, specifically using ViT-B/16 and ViT-L/14 architec-
374
      tures as the visual backbone. For both CD and OOD benchmarks, we set K to 2. Notably, our
375
      method is training-free, which achieves rapid adaptation with no need for any hyperparameters for
376   optimization. All experiments are performed on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU.

      4.2.1 CROSS - DATASET BENCHMARK
400   Table 1 presents the results for fine-grained cross-dataset benchmark using the ViT-B/16 architec-
401   ture. As observed in Figure 2a, the core idea behind TCA is that condensing inattentive tokens
402   can effectively mitigate distribution shifts caused by visual-text misalignment. This concept is first
403
      validated by the improved performance of token pruning baselines over CLIP inference, where a
      condensed token set yields a 0.9% increase in average accuracy when R = 0.9. TCA further en-
404
      hances its performance by dealing with visual-text misalignment, moving visual features toward
405
      historical anchor tokens from CTR. As a result, TCA achieves an average accuracy of 68.69%, out-
406   performing both train-required and training-free baselines without augmentation. Conventional TTA
407   methods perform poorly on all datasets even with the requirement of fine-tuning a large amount of
408   learnable parameters. In contrast, prompt-tuning methods, although requiring fewer learnable pa-
409   rameters, rely heavily on augmentation and struggle to effectively handle visual shifts. While TDA
410   is a training-free method, it requires a large number of hyperparameters (a total of 10 for managing
411   positive and negative caches) to achieve optimal performance. On the other hand, TCA uses signif-
412   icantly fewer hyperparameters and delivers a 1.72% improvement in average accuracy over TDA,
413   with approximately 12.2% fewer GFLOPs. Further details on TDA combined with token conden-
414
      sation baselines can be found in Figure 4c. To verify the universality of the proposed TCA, we
      examine the impact of the visual backbone (ViT-L/14), where we reduce 48.9% GFLOPs without
415
      compromising adaptation. The results are presented in Section A.1.

      5    C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we introduced Token Condensation as Adaptation (TCA), a novel training-free test-
532   time adaptation method for CLIP models. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrated that token
533   condensation significantly benefits the visual-text alignment in CLIP, which can further serve as
534   an interpretation of visual semantics. Additionally, our method reduces GFLOPs as a beneficial
535   byproduct, enhancing computational efficiency. For fair comparisons, we fixed the GFLOPs budget
536
      by pre-setting R in our experiments; however, the condensing rate can be adaptively estimated
      using the distances to merging center as an indicator, which can be a promising direction for future
537
      research. We also acknowledge the limitations of TCA as a training-free method, particularly its
538
      bottleneck in handling datasets with severe distribution shifts, as discussed in Section A.4.
