011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies in multivariate time series (MTS) forecasting reveal that explicitly
014            modeling the hidden dependencies among different time series can yield promising
015
               forecasting performance and reliable explanations. However, modeling variable de-
               pendencies remains underexplored when MTS is continuously accumulated under
016
               different regimes (stages). Due to the potential distribution and dependency dispar-
017
               ities, the underlying model may encounter the catastrophic forgetting problem, i.e.,
018            it is challenging to memorize and infer different types of variable dependencies
019            across different regimes while maintaining forecasting performance. To address
020            this issue, we propose a novel Structural Knowledge Informed Continual Learn-
021            ing (SKI-CL) framework to perform MTS forecasting within a continual learning
022            paradigm, which leverages structural knowledge to steer the forecasting model
023            toward identifying and adapting to different regimes, and selects representative
024            MTS samples from each regime for memory replay. Specifically, we develop a fore-
025            casting model based on graph structure learning, where a consistency regularization
026
               scheme is imposed between the learned variable dependencies and the structural
               knowledge (e.g., physical constraints, domain knowledge, feature similarity, which
027
               provides regime characterization) while optimizing the forecasting objective over
028
               the MTS data. As such, MTS representations learned in each regime are associated
029            with distinct structural knowledge, which helps the model memorize a variety of
030            conceivable scenarios and results in accurate forecasts in the continual learning
031            context. Meanwhile, we develop a representation-matching memory replay scheme
032            that maximizes the temporal coverage of MTS data to efficiently preserve the
033            underlying temporal dynamics and dependency structures of each regime. Thor-
034            ough empirical studies on synthetic and real-world benchmarks validate SKI-CL’s
035            efficacy and advantages over the state-of-the-art for continual MTS forecasting
036            tasks. SKI-CL can also infer faithful dependency structures that closely align to
037
               structural knowledge in the test stage.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Multivariate time series (MTS) forecasting aims to predict future samples from multiple time series
042   based on their historical values and has shown its importance in various applications, e.g., healthcare,
043   traffic control, energy management, and finance Jin et al. (2018); Gonzalez-Vidal et al. (2019); Guo
044   et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2017). Accurate MTS forecasting not only relies on capturing temporal
045   dynamics of the historical time series data van den Oord et al. (2016); Bai et al. (2018); Borovykh
046   et al. (2017); Lai et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022); Nie et al. (2022), but also relies
047   on modeling dependency structures among different variables Lai et al. (2018); Shih et al. (2019);
048   Wu et al. (2020); Shang & Chen (2021); Liu et al. (2023).
049   Despite the promising forecasting accuracy and explainable structure characterization, capability of
050   these MTS forecasters is limited to one regime (stage) of MTS data characterized by a set of similar
051   dependency patterns. In real-world applications, different regimes of MTS data are often continuously
052   collected under different operational logic of the target system. In this learning scenario, where
053   regimes arrive sequentially, the major challenge in MTS forecasting is to keep track of the latest
      regime while maintaining forecasting capability on the past ones. For example, in the context of solar

105   individually for sample selection. Given the memory budget, we select a subset of MTS samples
106   whose representations are the most similar to that of the entire mode, measured by CORAL Sun
107   & Saenko (2016). Thereby the coverage of each regime is efficiently preserved by the union of

111   In summary, our work makes the following four contributions. (1) We present a novel Structural
112
      Knowledge Informed Continual Learning (SKI-CL) framework to perform MTS forecasting and infer
      dependency structures in the continual learning setting. (2) We develop a graph-based forecaster
113
      that contains a structure learning module to capture temporal dependencies and dynamically infer
114
      dependency structures, and employ a consistency regularization scheme that exploits structural
115   knowledge to facilitate continual forecasting. (3) We propose a novel representation-matching
116   memory replay scheme to maximize the temporal coverage of MTS data and preserve the underlying

118   on one synthetic dataset and three benchmark datasets demonstrate the superiority of SKI-CL over
119   the state-of-the-art in continual MTS forecasting and dependency structure inference.

      2.1   M ODELING D EPENDENCIES IN M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
124
125   In recent years, modeling variable dependencies of MTS has received increasing attention for
126   forecasting tasks. Early methods apply linear or convolution transformations to capture variable
127   dependencies in an implicit recurrent process Lai et al. (2018); Graves (2013); Shih et al. (2019),
128   which fall short of modeling the non-Euclidean interactions due to the underlying fully-connected
129   or translation-invariant assumptions. The advent of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) has inspired
130   the formulation of variable dependencies as a given or learnable graph, with variables being nodes
131   and pairwise relationships being edges. Existing literature models the dependency structures based
132
      on different topological perspectives and temporal granularity (e.g., undirected Yu et al. (2018) and
      directed graph Li et al. (2017), static Bai et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2020) and dynamic graphs Ye
133
      et al. (2022); Cao et al. (2020), single or multiple layers Lin et al. (2021)). On the other hand,
134
      structural knowledge has been an important component in GNN-based forecasting methods. In many
135   tasks such as the traffic Guo et al. (2019); Li et al. (2017) and skeleton-based action prediction Yan
136   et al. (2018), structural knowledge is explicitly presented as spatial connections. In other cases
137   without an explicit topological structure, the structural knowledge can be drawn from either domain
138   knowledge (e.g., transfer entropy Duan et al. (2022), Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients Lin et al.
139   (2021)) or feature similarity (e.g., the correlations of decomposed time series Ng et al. (2022), kNN
140   graph Shang & Chen (2021)). Their promising results suggest the capability of structural knowledge
141   to convey meaningful dependency information. Our proposed method enforces the consistency
142   between the learned graph structures and the structural knowledge so as to characterize the underlying
143
      relation-temporal dependencies and improve the continual MTS forecasting performance.
144
145   2.2   C ONTINUAL L EARNING IN M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
146
147
      Deep learning models use continual learning to address the catastrophic forgetting issue when sequen-
      tially adapting to new tasks. Existing literature in continual learning can be roughly classified into
148
      three categories: experience-replay methods Rolnick et al. (2019), parameter-isolation methods Rusu
149
      et al., and regularization-based methods Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Li & Hoiem (2017). Current
150   continual learning works have been extensively studied on images Wang et al. (2022), texts Ke
151   & Liu (2022), and graph data Zhang et al. (2022a;b). However, much less attention is drawn to
152   time series data, and the focus has primarily been on classification and forecasting tasks without
153   explicitly addressing complex variable dependencies Gupta et al. (2021); He & Sick (2021). How to
154   maintain the meaningful dependency structures and forecasting performance over different regimes is
155   underexplored. Our proposed SKI-CL tackles this issue by jointly optimizing the inferred structure
156   toward the structural knowledge and forecasting objectives based on samples drawn from the current
157   regime and a representative memory.
158   Recent progress has been made in forecasting Pham et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023) and topology
159   identification Money et al. (2021); Natali et al. (2022); Isufi et al. (2019); Zaman et al. (2020)
160   from MTS data in an online learning setting, which focuses on adapting the forecasting model and
161   dependency structure from the historical MTS data to future unseen data. In contrast, our study aims
      to maintain the forecasting performance and infer the learned structures from the existing regimes

      Dependencies Characterization with Structural Knowledge To learn a faithful dynamic graph
232   structure that characterizes the underlying dependencies of each regime, we incorporate structural
233   knowledge as a reference in learning objectives. In real-world MTS modeling, the edges can be
234   either continuous, if we can quantify the strength in the context, or binary if we are more confident
235   of the connection in a qualitative sense (e.g., physical connections). It also interleaves with the fact
236   that if the structural knowledge can be fully observed, as in many cases we are only confident in the
237   existence of certain relationships.
238   To fully leverage different forms of structural knowledge in dependency structure learning, we
239   design an adaptive scheme that imposes different constraints on the parameterized graph in the
240   objective function, which is denoted as LG . If an edge is treated as a binary variable, we activate
241   the parameterized edge with a sigmoid function to approximate the Bernoulli distribution Âij ∼
242   Bern(θij ), where P (Âij = 1) = θij is the probability that an edge is forming between node i and
243   node j. Then, we encourage the probability Pof edge to be consistent with the prior, which essentially
244   minimizes the binary cross entropy: LG = i,j −Aij log θij − (1 − Aij ) log (1 − θij ).
245
246
      If an edge is treated as a continuous variable, we activate the parameterized edge with a ReLU
      function to remove the weak connections, and enforce the consistency between the numerical values
247
      of the parameterized edge and the prior, representing a similar interaction strength. This is achieved
248
      by minimizing the MSE objective LG = N12 i,j ∥Aij − Âij ∥2 . So far, we have discussed the

321   We briefly introduce the pipeline of continual MTS forecasting, as shown in Figure 2. At the training
322   phase of i-th regime, the training objectives Lregime-i contains the objective for the current training
323   samples, denoted as Lcurrent and that for the memory of previous i-1 regimes, denoted as Lmemory ,

      After training, the structural knowledge of the current regime is saved in the structural memory and
325   the training samples are selected to enrich the MTS memory as aforementioned. At the testing phase,
326   SKI-CL is able to maintain the forecasting performance on the queries of testing samples from all
327   regimes up to the current one, and accordingly recover the learned dependency structures informed by
328   the structural knowledge of each regime (as shown in Figure 2(right)). Unlike other graph structure
329   learning methods for continual MTS forecasting, our method is able to dynamically infer faithful
330   dependency structures for existing and current regimes without accessing the memory buffer, which
331   is more practical in real world applications.

334
335   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
336
337   Datasets To evaluate the performance of SKI-CL on continual MTS forecasting, we conduct ex-
338   periments on three public benchmark MTS datasets including the traffic (Traffic-CL), solar energy
339   (Solar-CL), and human activity recognition (HAR-CL), as well as one synthetic dataset based on
340   Non-repeating Random Walk Denton (2005) that is used in Liu et al. (2022b) under continual learning
341   setting. The statistics of these datasets are summarized in Table 4 in Appendix C. For Traffic-CL and
342
      Solar-CL, the structural knowledge is the spatial proximity of the sensor/station. For HAR-CL, the
      partial structural knowledge is drawn from the domain-specific motion dynamics. For synthetic data,
343
      structural knowledge is the feature similarity of different variables. The edges from the structural
344
      knowledge are binary for Traffic-CL and HAR-CL, and continuous for Solar-CL and Synthetic-CL.
345
346   Baselines We compare SKI-CL with a number of dependency-modeling-based forecasting methods
347   and commonly used continual learning methods to resolve the catastrophic forgetting issue in
348
      sequential training. The forecasting methods include statistical model VAR Lütkepohl (2005),
      ARIMA Box et al. (2015), and deep learning models including TCN Bai et al. (2018), LSTNet Lai
349
      et al. (2018), STGCN Yu et al. (2018), MTGNN Wu et al. (2020), AGCRN Bai et al. (2020),
350
      GTS Shang & Chen (2021), ESG Ye et al. (2022), StemGNN Cao et al. (2020), Autoformer Wu
351   et al. (2021), PatchTST Nie et al. (2022), Dlinear Zeng et al. (2023), TimesNet Wu et al. (2022),
352   iTransformer Liu et al. (2023), and OFA Tian Zhou (2023) where STGCN and GTS use structural
353   knowledge and ESG learns a dynamic graph in MTS modeling. All forecasting methods are first
354   evaluated on sequential training without any countermeasures (denoted as seq). The continual
355   learning methods employ the memory-replay-based methods including the herding method (denoted
356   as herd) Rebuffi et al. (2016), the randomly replay training samples (denoted as er), a DER++ method
357   Buzzega et al. (2020) that enforces a L2 knowledge distillation loss on the previous logits (denoted as
358   der++) and a MIR method that selects samples with highest forgetting for experience replay (denoted
359
      as mir). For the proposed SKI-CL, we also evaluate our proposed representation-matching sample
      selection scheme.
360
361   Evaluation Metrics We adopt two metrics based on Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean
362   Squared Error (RMSE) to evaluate the performance on continual MTS forecasting, i.e., the Average
363   Performance (AP) and Average Forgetting (AF) Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017); Zhang et al. (2022a).
                                                 Pi
364   The AP at i-th regime is defined as AP = j=1 Pi,j /i for i ≥ 1, where Pi,j denotes the performance
365   on regime j after the model has been sequentially trained from stage 1 to i. Similarly, the Average
                                         Pi−1
366   Forgetting is defined as AF =         j=1 (Pi,j − Pj,j )/(i − 1) for i ≥ 2. Besides the forecasting
367   performance, we also evaluate AP and AF on the learned dependency structures, where the average
368   precision (Prec.) and average recall (Rec.) are used for a binary graph, MAE and RMSE are used for
369   a continuous graph. More details of datasets, baselines, and evaluations are provided in Appendix C.

      5       C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a novel Structural Knowledge Informed Continual Learning (SKI-CL)
533
      framework to perform MTS forecasting and infer dependency structures inference in the continual
534   learning setting. We develop a forecasting model based on dynamic graph learning and impose a
535   consistency regularization that exploits structural knowledge to facilitate continual learning. We
536   further alleviate the catastrophic forgetting by proposing a novel representation-matching memory
537   replay scheme, which maximizes the temporal coverage of MTS data to efficiently preserve each
538   regime’s underlying temporal dynamics and dependency structure. Experiments on one synthetic
539   dataset and three real-world benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and advantages of the
      proposed SKI-CL on continual MTS forecasting tasks.

786   B     R ELATION TO OTHER R ESEARCH T OPICS
787
788   Our method characterizes dynamic variable dependencies of MTS data within each regime to perform
789   continual MTS forecasting tasks. The dynamic variable dependencies modeling and continual
790   adaptation nature relate our method to two research topics, i.e., the dynamic graph learning and
791   learning with temporal drift of MTS. Dynamic graph learning focuses on adapting to the changes in
792   the explicit graph structure and possibly features over time, and leverage it to facilitate downstream
793   tasks (e.g., node classification Liu et al. (2022a), link prediction Wang et al. (2021), community
794   detection Park et al. (2022)). In contrast, our proposed continual multivariate time series forecasting
795
      method needs to discover underlying dependencies structure of variables over different regimes
      (stages), by capturing the temporal dynamics of MTS data. On the other hand, temporal drift of
796
      MTS represents the phenomenon where the data distribution of the target MTS changes over time in
797
      unforeseen ways, where the approaches are often more reactive and focused on adapting to changes
798   in data distribution for a specific task Du et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2021); Lee et al. (2022). Based on
799   the notion, it is important to emphasize the continual adaptation nature of continual learning setting
800   regarding the temporal drifts, where the catastrophic forgetting happens due to the shifts over multiple
801   regimes. That being said, continual MTS forecasters need to handle a variety of regimes, not just
802   adapt to changes in data distribution for a single one, which is to some degree more realistic and
803   challenging. Temporal drift represents a specific challenge within this broader spectrum of adaptation.
804   Moreover, while the aforementioned topics as well as our study deal with the evolving data, the
805   learning objectives can be very different. For the temporal drift methods, the objective is to adapt
806   the forecaster to these new MTS patterns by effectively detecting, responding to, and learning from
807   these changes. Similarly, the focus of dynamic graph learning is to adapt its model to cope with the
808   structural and possible feature changes. However, the main focus this paper is to prevent the model
809   from forgetting previously learned knowledge (coupled temporal dynamics and variable dependencies
      in our scope) when adapting to new MTS distributions.

      HAR-CL We leverage the class boundaries in MTS classification data to construct different regimes
865   in forecasting tasks. Specifically, we build our continual forecasting dataset based on a commonly
866   used MTS Classification benchmark, the Human Activity Recognition (HAR) dataset Anguita et al.
867   (2013b;a) in the UCI database3 , where the data is collected from a group of 30 volunteers from
868   19-48 years, wearing a Samsung smartphone on the waist and performing six activities of daily living
869   (Walking, Walking upstairs, Walking downstairs, Standing, Sitting, Lying). Each MTS sequence
870   contains 128 time steps and 9 variables that are recorded based on the accelerometer and gyroscope,
871   including the linear accelerations, the angular velocities, and total accelerations along the X-Y-Z axis.
872   The detailed setup for data collection can be found in Figure 1 of Anguita et al. (2013a).
873   We notice that different human activities naturally form distinct regimes with unique temporal
874   dynamics, where deep learning methods easily achieve over 94% classification accuracy Wang et al.
875   (2019). Motivated by this fact, we construct the continual HAR forecasting dataset considering the
876   following details. Firstly, we select Walking, Walking upstairs, Walking downstairs, and Lying as
877   four different regimes in our task. Secondly, each regime contains over one thousand sequences that
878   are not always temporally connected due to different volunteers. As such, we iterate all sequences
879
      and construct the input-output MTS windows in each 128-step sequence, after which all windows
      are stacked as the training/validation/testing data in one regime. Secondly, we try to build the
880
      structural knowledge for each regime based on the domain knowledge. We’ve already known that
881
      linear accelerations are highly correlated to total accelerations along each axis, regardless of the
882   activities, but the dependencies among other variables are not very clear. Therefore, we examine
883   the Pearson correlations of all training sequences for each regime, where the mean of correlation
884   matrices demonstrate distinct patterns, and validate the strong correlations between linear and total
885   accelerations. However, the standard deviations are only small at the diagonal and the aforementioned
886   entries, suggesting a varying and uncertain structure for other variable pairs. To this end, we check a
887   small (15-th) percentile of each entry in the absolute correlation matrix, and apply a threshold to get
888   a binary mask representing the variable dependencies that we are confident of. Note that we only
889   regularize the learned structures to be consistent with the partially observed structural knowledge
890
      at the masked entries. As such, we simulate the structured scenario when we are not aware of the
      completed structural knowledge of MTS. The input for the HAR-CL dataset has 12 steps.

1001   Compared with the state-of-the-art method, our proposed SKI-CL backbone model learns a dynamic
1002   graph for MTS modeling, which is also capable of incorporating structural knowledge with different
1003   forms and availability scenarios to characterize the dependency structure and temporal dynamics of

1005
       Training Details The dynamic graph inference module consists of 3 stacked 2D convolutional layers.
1006
       Using Cin , Cout to denote the number of channels coming in and out, the parameters of these
1007   convolutional layers are [ Cin = 1, Cout = 8, kernel size = (1,2), stride = 1, dilation = 2 ], [Cin =
1008   8, Cout = 16, kernel size = (1,3), stride = 1, dilation = 2 ] and [Cin = 16, Cout = 32, kernel size
1009   = (1,3), stride = 1, dilation = 2 ] respectively. Each batched output is normalized using the Batch
1010   Norm2d layer. The hidden dimension for the node feature project is set at 128. For optimization,
1011   we train SKI-CL with 100 epochs for every stage under Adam optimizer with a linear scheduler.
1012   For the learning rate schedule, we use a linear scheduler, which drops the linear rate from 0.0001
1013   to the factor of 0.8 for every 20 epochs. The data split is 6/2/2 for training/validation/testing. We
1014   use a batch size of 32/64/64 for the Traffic-CL, Solar-CL and HAR-CL datasets and use a batch
1015
       size of 8 for our synthetic dataset. Considering the sizes of datasets, the default memory for each
       regime is 1% for Traffic-CL and Synthetic-CL and 0.1% for Solar-CL and HAR-CL, respectively.
1016
       We also weigh the examples in the current stage and in memory differently. We apply a weighted loss
1017
       regarding the sizes of memory and training data, as stated in the manuscript, and we also construct a
1018   data loader that guarantees the balance between training data and memory data. For the setting of our
1019   distribution characterization scheme, the default values are N = 10 and K = 7. We implement our
1020   models in Pytorch. All experiments are run on one server with four NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs. We
1021   will release our code upon paper acceptance.
