009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite significant advancements in post-hoc explainability techniques for neural
012            networks, many current methods rely on approximations and heuristics and do
013            not provide formally provable guarantees over the explanations provided. Recent
014            work has shown that it is possible to obtain explanations with formal guarantees by
015            identifying subsets of input features that are sufficient to determine that predictions
016            remain unchanged by incorporating neural network verification techniques. Despite
017            the appeal of these explanations, their computation faces significant scalability
018
               challenges. In this work, we address this gap by proposing a novel abstraction-
               refinement technique for efficiently computing provably sufficient explanations of
019
               neural network predictions. Our method abstracts the original large neural network
020
               by constructing a substantially reduced network, where a sufficient explanation
021            of the reduced network is also provably sufficient for the original network, hence
022            significantly speeding up the verification process. If the explanation is insufficient
023            on the reduced network, we iteratively refine the network size (by gradually increas-
024            ing it) until convergence. Our experimental results demonstrate that our approach
025            substantially enhances the efficiency of obtaining provably sufficient explanations
026            for neural network predictions while additionally providing a fine-grained interpre-
027            tation of the network’s decisions across different abstraction levels. We thus regard
028            this work as a substantial step forward in improving the feasibility of computing
029
               explanations with formal guarantees for neural networks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Despite the widespread use of deep neural networks, they remain black boxes that are uninterpretable
034
      to humans. Various methods have been proposed to explain neural network predictions. Classic
035
      additive feature attributions like LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), and
036   integrated gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) assume that neural networks exhibit near-linear
037   behavior in a local region around the interpreted instance. Following these works, methods like
038   Anchors (Ribeiro et al., 2018) and SIS (Carter et al., 2019) aim to compute a subset of input features
039   that is (nearly) sufficient to determine the prediction. We refer to this subset of features as an
040   explanation of the prediction. A common assumption in the literature is that a smaller explanation
041   provides a better interpretation, and for this reason, the minimality of the explanation is also a desired
042   property (Ignatiev et al., 2019; Carter et al., 2019; Darwiche & Hirth, 2020; Ribeiro et al., 2018;
043   Barceló et al., 2020).
044   Methods like Anchors and SIS rely on probabilistic sampling of the input space and lack formally
045   provable guarantees for the sufficiency of the subsets they identify. In contrast, recent approaches
046   have demonstrated that incorporating neural network verification tools can produce explanations that
047   are provably certified as sufficient (Bassan & Katz, 2023; Wu et al., 2024; La Malfa et al., 2021).
048   This makes such explanations more suitable for safety-critical domains where formally certifying the
049   reliability of the explanation is vital (Marques-Silva & Ignatiev, 2022).
050   Despite the appeal of such explanations, the computational complexity of producing them limits
051   their feasibility for large neural networks (Barceló et al., 2020). Verifying a single query on a
052   neural network is NP-Complete (Katz et al., 2017), with exponentially increasing complexity based
053   on the number of nonlinear activations. Consequently, larger and deeper neural networks are
      significantly harder to verify. While there have been rapid advances in the scalability of neural

078   Our Contributions. In this work, we propose a novel algorithm that significantly enhances the
079   efficiency of generating provably sufficient explanations for neural networks. Our algorithm is based
080   on an abstraction refinement technique (Clarke et al., 2000; Wang et al., 2006; Flanagan & Qadeer,
081
      2002), which is widely used to improve the scalability of formal verification and model checking. In
      abstraction-refinement, a complex model with many states is efficiently optimized through two steps:
082
      (i) abstraction, which simplifies the model by grouping states, and (ii) refinement, which increases
083
      the precision of the abstraction to better approximate the model.
084
085   In the context of explainability, we propose an algorithm that constructs an abstract neural network
086   — a substantially reduced model compared to the original. This reduction is achieved by merging
087
      neurons within each layer that exhibit similar behavior. The key component of this approach is
      to design the reduction such that a sufficient explanation for the abstract network is also provably
088
      sufficient for the original network. Hence, we define an explanation for the abstract neural network
089
      as an abstract explanation. Verifying the sufficiency of explanations for an abstract neural network
090   is much more efficient than for the original model due to its reduced dimensionality. However, if a
091   subset is found to be insufficient for the abstract network, its sufficiency for the original model is
092   undetermined. Consequently, while sufficient explanations for the abstract network will be sufficient
093   for the original network, minimal sufficient explanations for the abstract network, though sufficient,
094   may not be minimal for the original network.
095
      To address this issue, we incorporate a refinement component as typical in abstraction-refinenemt
096   techniques: We construct an intermediate abstract network, which is slightly larger than the initial
097   abstract network but still significantly smaller than the original. The explanations computed for
098   this refined network are still provably sufficient for the original network and are also guaranteed
099   to be a subset of the explanation from the initial abstract network. Hence, this phase produces a
100   refined explanation based on the refined network. After several refinement steps, the sizes of the
101   neural networks will gradually increase while the sizes of the explanations will gradually decrease
102   until finally converging to a minimal explanation for some reduced network, which is also provably
103   minimal for the original neural network. An illustration of this entire process is shown in Fig. 1.
104   We evaluate our algorithm on various benchmarks and show that it significantly outperforms existing
105   verification-based methods by producing much smaller explanations and doing so substantially
106   more efficiently. Additionally, we compare our results to heuristic-based approaches and show
107   that these methods do not provide sufficient explanations in most cases, whereas the explanations
      of our approach are guaranteed to be sufficient. An additional advantage of our method is that it

      Besides these practical aspects, we view this work as a novel proof-of-concept for using abstraction-

      refinement-based techniques in explainability, by obtaining formally provable explanations over
114
      abstract neural networks, which allow significantly more efficient verification, and a fine-grained
115   interpretation over abstracted and refined networks. We hence consider this work as a significant step

      2.1   N EURAL N ETWORK V ERIFICATION
121
122   We specify a generic neural network classifier architecture that can utilize any element-wise nonlinear
123   activation function. For an input x ∈ Rn , the neural network classifier is denoted as f : Rn → Rc .
124   Numerous tools have been proposed for formally verifying properties of neural networks, with
125   adversarial robustness being the most frequently examined property (Brix et al., 2023). The neural
126   network verification query can be formalized as follows:
127
128   Neural Network Verification (Problem Statement):
129   Input: A neural network f , such that f (x) = y, with an input specification ψin (x), and and unsafe
130   output specification ψout (y).
131   Output: No, if there exists some x ∈ Rn such that ψin (x) holds and for y = f (x) : ψout (y) holds, and
132
      Yes otherwise.
133   There exist many off-the-shelf neural network verifiers (Brix et al., 2023). If the input specifications
134   ψin (x), output specifications ψout (y), and model f are piecewise-linear (e.g., f uses ReLU activa-
135   tions), this task can be solved exactly (Katz et al., 2017). For non-piecewise-linear activations like
136   sigmoid, the output is usually enclosed by bounding all approximation errors (Singh et al., 2018).
137
138   2.2   F ORMALLY P ROVABLE M INIMAL S UFFICIENT E XPLANATIONS
139
      In this study, we concentrate on local post-hoc explanations for neural network classifiers. Specifically,
140
      for a neural network classifier f : Rn → Rd and a given local input x ∈ Rn that has been assigned to
141
      class t := arg maxj f (x)j , our objective is to explain why f (x) was classified as class t.
142
143   Sufficient Explanations. A common method for interpreting the decisions of classifiers involves
144   identifying subsets of input features S ⊆ [n] such that fixing these features to their specific values
145
      guarantees the prediction remains constant. Specifically, these techniques guarantee that the clas-
      sification result remains consistent across any potential assignment within the complementary set
146
      S̄, thereby allowing for the formal validation of the explanations’ soundness. While in the classic
147
      setting approaches, the complementary set S̄ := [n] \ S is allowed to take on any possible feature
148   values (Ignatiev et al., 2019; Darwiche & Hirth, 2020; Bassan & Katz, 2023), a more feasible and
149   generalizable version restricts the possible assignments for S̄ to a bounded ϵp -region (Wu et al., 2024;
150   La Malfa et al., 2021; Izza et al., 2024). We use (xS ; x̃S̄ ) ∈ Rn to denote an assignment where the
151   features of S are set to the values of the vector x ∈ Rn and the features of S̄ are set to the values of
152   another vector x̃ ∈ Rn . Formally, we define a sufficient explanation S as follows:

177   Minimal sufficient explanations can also be determined using neural network verifiers. Unlike simply
178   verifying the sufficiency of a specific subset, this process requires executing multiple verification

181   with S encompassing the entire feature set [n] and iteratively tries to exclude a feature i from S, each
182   time checking whether S \ {i} remains sufficient. If S \ {i} is still sufficient, feature i is removed;
183

      3    F ROM A BSTRACT N EURAL N ETWORKS TO A BSTRACT E XPLANATIONS
198
199   A primary challenge in obtaining minimal sufficient explanations in neural networks is the high
200
      computational complexity involved. Verifying the sufficiency of a subset through a neural network
      verification query is NP-Complete (Katz et al., 2017), with complexity increasing exponentially
201
      with the number of activations, making it especially difficult for larger networks (Brix et al., 2023).
202
      Obtaining a minimal subset also requires a linear number of verification queries relative to the input
203   size (Ignatiev et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2024), making the process computationally intensive for large
204   inputs. One potential solution is to replace the original neural network f with a much smaller, abstract
205   neural network f ′ , and then run verifying queries on f ′ instead of f . However, a key challenge here is
206   to ensure that a sufficient explanation for f ′ is also a sufficient explanation for f . Although abstraction
207   techniques have been applied to improve the efficiency of adversarial robustness verification (Elboher
208   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024; Ladner & Althoff, 2023), to our knowledge, we are the first to use such
209   an approach to obtain provable explanations for neural networks.
210   Abstract Neural Networks. When abstracting a neural network, rather than using a traditional
211   network f : Rn → Rc , it is common to employ an abstract neural network f ′ that outputs a set
212   that encloses the actual output of f (Ladner & Althoff, 2023; Prabhakar & Rahimi Afzal, 2019;
213   Boudardara et al., 2022). This approach facilitates a more flexible propagation of the network’s inner
214   bounds capturing the error due to the abstraction. More formally, we define the domain of our abstract
                             c              c
215   network f ′ : Rn → 2(R ) , where 2(R ) denotes the power set of Rc . In the simplest case, this means
      that our abstract network outputs a c-dimensional interval rather than a c-dimensional vector.

226   Neuron-Merging-Based Abstraction. Various strategies can be employed to abstract a neural
227   network to reduce its size. In this work, we use a fully-automatic abstraction technique (Ladner
228
      & Althoff, 2023), which involves merging neurons that exhibit similar behavior within the neural
      network for some bounded input set. For instance, numerous sigmoid neurons may become fully
229
      saturated, producing outputs close to 1. Hence, the corresponding abstraction approach involves
230
      merging these saturated neurons and establishing corresponding error bounds for the given input set.
231   This can be realized without large computational overhead to a desired reduction rate ρ ∈ [0, 1] such
232   that the overall verification time, including abstraction, mainly depends on the remaining number of
233   neurons. For convenience, we also say that setting ρ = 1 produces the original network. We give
234   details about the construction of the abstract network in Appendix A.

      6    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
489   Our work is closely related to formal explainable artificial intellicene (XAI) (Marques-Silva &
490   Ignatiev, 2022), which aims to provide explanations with formal guarantees. Previous research has fo-
491   cused on deriving provable sufficient explanations for simple models like decision trees (Huang et al.,
492   2021; Izza et al., 2022), linear models (Marques-Silva et al., 2020), monotonic classifiers (Marques-
493
      Silva et al., 2021; El Harzli et al., 2022), and tree ensembles (Izza & Marques-Silva, 2021; Ignatiev
      et al., 2022; Boumazouza et al., 2021). More closely related to our work are methods that derive
494
      minimal sufficient explanations for neural networks (Bassan & Katz, 2023; La Malfa et al., 2021;
495
      Wu et al., 2024). These explanations often rely on neural network verification tools, which are
496   rapidly improving in scalability (Katz et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Brix et al., 2023), though their
497   scalability remains a key challenge, as they require executing multiple verification queries (Barceló
498   et al., 2020; Ignatiev et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2024).
499
      Additionally, our algorithm uses abstraction-refinement, a technique predominantly used to improve
500
      the efficiency of symbolic model checking (Clarke et al., 2000; Wang et al., 2006). This approach
501
      has also been successfully applied in software (Jhala & Majumdar, 2009; Flanagan & Qadeer, 2002),
502   hardware verification (Andraus et al., 2007), and hybrid systems verification (Alur et al., 2000). More
503   recently, techniques have been proposed to use abstraction-refinement by abstracting neural network
504   sizes to improve the efficiency of certifying adversarial robustness (Elboher et al., 2020; Ladner &
505   Althoff, 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Siddiqui et al., 2024). However, to the best of our knowledge, we are
506   the first to adopt such an abstraction-refinement-based technique to reduce neural network sizes for
507   providing provable explanations of neural network predictions.
508
509
510   7    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
511
512
513   The primary limitation of our framework is its reliance on neural network verification queries, which
514   currently face scalability challenges. While verification techniques are still limited in applying to
515
      state-of-the-art models, their scalability is improving rapidly (Wang et al., 2021; Brix et al., 2023).
      Our method adds an orthogonal step in using these tools to derive provable explanations for neural
516
      network decisions. Hence, as the scalability of verification tools improves, so will that of our approach.
517
      Additionally, our focus is on obtaining minimal sufficient explanations for predictions, but other
518   methods exist for explaining model decisions. Future work could explore abstraction-refinement
519   strategies for scaling various explanation types with formal guarantees by reducing the network size.
520   If a formal explanation holds for a smaller network f ′ , it should apply to the original model f . For
521   example, one could construct a smaller network where a minimal counterfactual explanation for f ′
522   is also minimal for f (Mothilal et al., 2020). Other approaches might study attributes of Shapley
523   values, such as symmetry and efficiency (Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020), or properties like infidelity
524   or consistency (Yeh et al., 2019) of feature attributions. Each property might necessitate distinct
525   methods of abstraction between f and f ′ , presenting compelling avenues for future research.
526

528   8    C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      Obtaining minimal sufficient explanations for neural networks offers a promising way to provide
      explanations with formally verifiable guarantees. However, the scalability of generating such ex-
532
      planations is hindered by the need to invoke multiple neural network verification queries. Our
533
      abstraction-refinement approach addresses this by starting with a significantly smaller network and
534   refining it as needed. This ensures that the explanations are provably sufficient for the original net-
535   work and, ultimately, both provably minimal and sufficient. The method also produces intermediate
536   explanations, allowing for an early stop when sufficient but non-minimal explanations are reached,
537   while also offering a more fine-grained interpretation of the model prediction. Our experiments
538   demonstrate that our approach generates minimal sufficient explanations substantially more efficiently
539   than traditional methods, representing a significant step forward in producing explanations for neural
      network predictions with formal guarantees.

887   B.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS OF ALGORITHM 1 AND ALGORITHM 2
888
889   Both Alg. 1 and Alg. 2 iterate over the features in a specified order. This approach aligns with the
890   methodologies used in (Wu et al. (2024); Izza et al. (2024); Bassan & Katz (2023)), where a sensitivity
891   traversal over the features is employed. We prioritize iterating over features with the lowest sensitivity
892   first, as they are most likely to be successfully freed, thus leading to a smaller explanation. As we
893   refine the abstract network following Prop. 1, we define a series of reduction rates used during each
894   refinement step. For simplicity, we start with a coarsest abstraction at a reduction rate ρ = 10% of
895   the original network’s neurons and increase ρ by 10% at each subsequent refinement, until ρ = 100%
896   is reached, which restores the original network.
897   We also note that while MNIST utilizes grayscale images, both CIFAR-10 and GTSRB use RGB
898   images. Following standard practices (Wu et al. (2024); Ribeiro et al. (2018); Carter et al. (2019;
899   2021)) for colored images, we provide explanations for CIFAR-10 and GTSRB on a per-pixel basis,
900   rather than at the neuron level; this means we either include/exclude all color channels of a pixel
901   within the explanation or none. Consequently, the maximum size of an explanation, |S|, is 32 · 32
902   instead of 32 · 32 · 3. For MNIST, which has only one color channel, the maximum size is always
903
      |S| = 28 · 28.
