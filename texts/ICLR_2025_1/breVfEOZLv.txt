                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Recent advancements in large language and vision models have demonstrated ex-
011
               traordinary capabilities, driving researchers to train increasingly larger models in
               pursuit of even greater performance. However, smaller, easier-to-train models of-
012
               ten exist prior to these larger models. In this paper, we explore how to effectively
013
               leverage these smaller, weaker models to assist in training larger, stronger models.
014            Specifically, we investigate the concept of weak-to-strong knowledge distillation
015            within vision models, where a weaker model supervises a stronger one, aiming
016            to enhance the latter’s performance beyond the limitations of the former. To this
017            end, we introduce a novel, adaptively adjustable loss function that dynamically
018            calibrates the weaker model’s supervision based on the discrepancy between soft
019            labels and hard labels. This dynamic adjustment allows the weaker model to pro-
020            vide more effective guidance during training. Our comprehensive experiments
021            span various scenarios, including few-shot learning, transfer learning, noisy label
022
               learning, and common knowledge distillation settings. The results are compelling:
               our approach not only surpasses benchmarks set by strong-to-strong distillation
023
               but also exceeds the performance of fine-tuning strong models on full datasets.
024
               These findings highlight the significant potential of weak-to-strong distillation,
025            demonstrating its ability to substantially enhance vision model performance. Code
026            will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031                      ”Big things have small beginnings.” — Movie ”Prometheus”.
032
033   This adage aptly encapsulates the developmental journey of high-performance models in the fields
034   of computer vision and natural language processing. The remarkable models that currently drive
035   advancements in these areas did not appear out of nowhere; they evolved incrementally from simpler,
036   less powerful architectures.
037
      In the realm of NLP, the journey began with models like RNN and LSTM networks. These early
038
      models laid the foundation for more advanced architectures, gradually evolving into models like
039   GPT (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). With its 175 billion parameters, GPT-3 showcased
040   the transformative power of scaling up, ultimately paving the way for today’s state-of-the-art large
041   language models that excel in various tasks, from translation to creative writing. Similarly, the evo-
042   lution in vision began with the pioneering LeNet (LeCun et al., 1998) architecture, designed for digit
043   recognition. ResNet (He et al., 2016) then addressed the vanishing gradient problem, allowing for
044   much deeper networks. Today, large vision models like ViTs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) continue to
045   push the boundaries, achieving unprecedented performance surpassing human across various visual
046   tasks.
047   As demonstrated by empirical studies on scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020), model performance
048   typically scales with model size, dataset size, and the amount of compute used for training. This
049   suggests that training larger models with more data holds the greatest potential for improvement,
050   while other factors like training recipes or network architectures have relatively minimal impact
051   across a wide range. However, before training a new large model, there often exists a smaller,
052   weaker model. It’s natural to ask whether these existing weaker models can be leveraged to assist in
053
      training larger ones. In this paper, we focus on addressing the challenge of how to efficiently utilize
      these weaker models to optimize and guide the training of more powerful, larger models.

      We delve into the benefits brought via “vision superalignment”, specifically investigating the appli-
      cability of Weak-to-Strong enhancement (W2S) within the context of vision tasks. Take the image
100
      classification task as an example, models typically develop from small to large, and the cost of train-
101
      ing a small (weak) model is far less than that of training a large (strong) model. As shown in Table 1,
102   larger models generally perform better. However, even when a model is 100 times larger and the
103   top-1 accuracy is 22% higher, there are still many samples correctly identified by the weak model
104   but misclassified by the strong model. This indicates that there are always opportunities to boost
105   performance by leveraging the weak model. Therefore, when attempting to train a large, strong
106   model, a natural question arises: how can we leverage existing weak models to achieve further per-
107   formance gains? Our study meticulously designs and examines multiple scenarios in computer vi-
      sion, including few-shot learning, transfer learning, noisy label learning, and traditional knowledge

116   2   R ELATED W ORKS
117
118   The pursuit of enhancing the performance of deep neural networks in computer vision has led to
119   the development of the teacher-student learning paradigm (Hinton et al., 2015; Roth et al., 2023).
120   This approach typically involves a stronger model (teacher) improving the performance of a weaker
121   model (student), with extensive research focusing on optimizing the capabilities of the weaker
122   model. Various strategies have been proposed to achieve this. For instance, (Romero et al., 2014)
123   suggests that in addition to the output logits, incorporating intermediate layer features for supervi-
124   sion can significantly boost the student’s learning. (Park et al., 2019) posits that the relationships
125   between samples can serve as valuable supervisory information.
126   In a further refinement of this approach, (Zhao et al., 2022) redefines classical knowledge distilla-
127   tion (KD) loss, segmenting it into target-class and non-target-class distillation to balance the transfer
128   of these two types of information more effectively. (Heo et al., 2019) delves into the details and
129   components of feature distillation, arriving at an improved method for the transfer of feature knowl-
130   edge. Meanwhile, (Chen et al., 2021a) explores cross-stage feature transfer as an alternative to the
131   conventional same-stage feature transfer. These methods have proven effective for strong-to-weak
132
      generalization scenarios.
133   However, with the gradual increase in the size and complexity of vision foundation models, the focus
134   has shifted towards weak-to-strong boosting, i.e., how a weak model can improve a strong model.
135   In this context, (Furlanello et al., 2018) investigates knowledge distillation between teachers and
136   students of equal size, demonstrating the feasibility of distilling models of the same size. Building
137   upon this, (Xie et al., 2020) introduces the use of additional unlabeled data for knowledge distilla-
138
      tion among models of equal size, further validating the effectiveness of strong-to-strong boosting,
      especially in scenarios with abundant data availability. This body of research sets the stage for our
139
      exploration into weak-to-strong boosting, a relatively uncharted yet promising domain in the field
140
      of vision foundation models.
141
142   Part of our experimental settings are similar to weakly supervised learning (Durand et al., 2017;
143   Joulin et al., 2016), where there are no annotations (ground truth labels) in training machine learning
144
      models. However, unlike weak supervision, which focuses on obtaining a large amount of annotated
      data at a low cost, we are more interested in the weak-to-strong boosting process itself rather than
145
      the availability of annotations.
146

148   3   W EAK - TO -S TRONG E NHANCEMENT
149
150   To advance towards super-human AGI models, a weak-to-strong approach is critical. This means us-
151   ing human-level intelligence as a foundation to guide and refine the development of more advanced,
152   super-human systems. We begin by focusing on foundational tasks and models that can progres-
153
      sively support the growth of stronger architectures. In the following, we examine the feasibility
      of weak-to-strong enhancement, where simpler, weaker models provide useful supervision to more
154
      complex models. This step-by-step approach ensures that as models evolve, they can benefit from
155
      earlier stages, steadily improving in both capability and accuracy. One of the key challenges in this
156   weak-to-strong framework is dealing with the noisy or incomplete supervision signals provided by
157   weaker models. To mitigate this, we introduce a novel technique: adaptive confidence distillation.
158   This method leverages the insights from weaker models while dynamically adjusting the level of
159   trust placed in them. By modulating the influence of weak supervision based on its confidence, our
160   approach ensures that the stronger model can benefit from imperfect labels without being misled.
161   This adaptive mechanism allows stronger models to distill meaningful knowledge, even from less
      accurate outputs.

      3.1   S ELECTION OF V ISION M ODEL
163
164   In our exploration of weak-to-strong enhancement for vision models, it is essential to first define
165   which models are suitable for this foundational research. Several categories of models can serve
166   as strong candidates for vision foundation models, including text-vision models (Radford et al.,
167   2021), image generation models (Rombach et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020), and general zero-shot
168   models (Bai et al., 2023; Kirillov et al., 2023). Each of these models brings unique strengths and
169   approaches to solving computer vision tasks. To identify the most appropriate models for weak-to-
170
      strong boosting, we propose a definition that emphasizes both versatility and effectiveness. Vision
      foundation models should be capable of addressing a wide range of visual tasks while consistently
171
      delivering high-quality performance.
172
173   Based on these criteria, we propose that backbones pretrained on ImageNet are strong candidates for
174   vision foundation models. The rationale behind this choice is threefold. First, ImageNet-pretrained
175   backbones have consistently proven to be highly adaptable and effective across downstream vision
176
      tasks such as classification, detection, segmentation, tracking, colorization, etc.. Fine-tuning these
      backbones often results in state-of-the-art performance, underscoring their robustness and versatil-
177
      ity. Second, there is a wealth of pretraining algorithms specifically developed for these models (He
178
      et al., 2022a; Xie et al., 2022), positioning them as universal tools for a variety of vision tasks. Fur-
179   thermore, these models frequently serve as one branch in vision-language multimodal models (Du
180   et al., 2022), further validating their applicability in cross-modal tasks. Finally, compared to other
181   foundation models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or Diffusion-based (Rombach et al., 2022)
182   models trained on massive web-scale datasets, ImageNet-trained backbones are far more accessi-
183   ble. Their moderate computational demands make them a practical choice for a broader range of
184   researchers with limited resources.
185   To validate the feasibility of weak-to-strong enhancement, we focus on these pretrained backbones
186   and use image classification as the fundamental task. By selecting this well-established task, we
187   create a controlled environment to rigorously test the effectiveness of our adaptive confidence dis-
188   tillation method. This will provide a solid baseline for expanding the weak-to-strong paradigm to
189   more complex vision tasks and models in future work.

191   3.2   A DAPTIVE C ONFIDENCE D ISTILLATION
192
193   In this section, we explore the methodology for implementing weak-to-strong boosting in vision
194
      foundation models. The central question we address is how a weak vision foundation model can
      supervise a stronger counterpart effectively. (Burns et al., 2023) proposes an augmented confidence
195
      loss approach, which is formulated as:
196
197                        Lconf (f ) = (1 − α)CE(f (x), fw (x)) + αCE(f (x), fˆ(x)),                      (1)
198
      where f represent the strong model that needs to be optimized, and fw denote the weak model,
199
200
      fˆ(x) refers to the hard label predicted by the strong model for an input image x. The loss function
      incorporates the cross-entropy loss (CE) and is balanced by a hyperparameter α. In this formulation,
201
      the first term of the loss function resembles the traditional knowledge distillation loss, signifying the
202
      learning process of the strong model from the weak model. Given that the labels provided by the
203   weak model may not always be accurate, the second term of the loss function encourages the strong
204   model to leverage its superior generalization abilities and prior knowledge to refine its predictions.
205
206
      The strength of this approach lies in its ability to balance direct learning from the weak model with
      the strong model’s intrinsic capacity for understanding and interpreting the visual data. This method
207
      paves the way for the strong model to surpass the limitations of the weak model, utilizing the latter’s
208
      guidance while simultaneously enhancing its predictions through its advanced capabilities.
209
210   Addressing the limitations inherent in the supervision provided by weak models and the inaccuracies
211   of strong models’ self-generated hard labels, a more sophisticated approach is required beyond a
212
      simple weighted combination of these labels. Given the challenge in directly discerning the accuracy
      of each label, leveraging confidence as a metric for selecting the most probable correct label emerges
213
      as a viable solution.
214
215   We propose to use the discrepancy between the soft label and the hard label as an indicator of the
      model’s confidence. The underlying rationale is that when a model’s soft label closely aligns with

477
       Robustness of confidence distillation. In this study, we investigate the necessity of devising a
478
       method that goes beyond a mere weighted combination of labels. As depicted in Eq. 1, despite
479
       its straightforward approach of integrating direct learning from a weaker model with the intrinsic
480    capacity of a stronger model, AugConf (Burns et al., 2023) still requires manual tuning of a hyper-
481    parameter α to balance the ratio of two different objectives. The setting of different α values can
482    have varying impacts across different contexts. Similarly, although our proposed AdaptConf does
483    not require manual adjustment of α to balance the proportions of objectives, we can manipulate the
484    temperature T to control the degree of probability distribution in soft labels during the computa-
485    tion of the cross-entropy CE(·), following a conventional distillation method (Hinton et al., 2015).
       Therefore, we explore the effects of these two methods under different hyper-parameter settings on

      the final outcome. Overall, the performance of KD, AugConf, and AdaptConf improves sequentially
487
      across various architectural settings. Moreover, it can be observed that AugConf exhibits a larger
488   fluctuation in results compared to AdaptConf, indicating that the influence of α on AugConf is more
489   significant than the effect of T on AdaptConf, which suggests that our AdaptConf has superior ro-
490   bustness. Additionally, the average outcomes achieved by AdaptConf are consistently higher than
491   those of AugConf under different hyper-parameter settings.

      Robustness of confidence distillation. In this section, we perform a quantitative analysis of the
493
      confidence weight determined by our dynamic function β(x) as delineated in Eq. 2, with the findings
494   illustrated in Figure 3. We selected checkpoints from four distinct training phases and calculated
495   their specific β(x) values on the validation set. It can be observed that as training progresses, the
496   proportion of samples with β = 0.5 increases, indicating that the student model’s performance is
497   improving and being aligned with the weak teacher’s correct classifications. A higher temperature
498   setting T reduces the cross-entropy (CE) discrepancy between the teacher and student, promoting a
499   more uniform balance between the weak teacher’s guidance and the strong student’s own predictions.
500   Consequently, the number of samples where β = 0.5 also increases with training. These phenomena
501   collectively validate that our proposed AdaptConf can dynamically adjust the learning ratio between
502
      the two components.
