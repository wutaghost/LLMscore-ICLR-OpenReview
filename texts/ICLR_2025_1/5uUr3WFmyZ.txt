009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Gradient normalization and soft clipping are two popular techniques for tackling
012            instability issues and improving convergence of stochastic gradient descent (SGD)
013            with momentum. In this article, we study these types of methods through the lens
014            of dissipative Hamiltonian systems. Gradient normalization and certain types of
015            soft clipping algorithms can be seen as (stochastic) implicit-explicit Euler dis-
016            cretizations of dissipative Hamiltonian systems, where the kinetic energy function
017            determines the type of clipping that is applied. We make use of dynamical systems
018
               theory to show in a unified way that all of these schemes converge to stationary
               points of the objective function, almost surely, in several different settings: a) for
019
               LвИТsmooth objective functions, when the variance of the stochastic gradients is
020
               possibly infinite b) under the (L0 , L1 )вИТsmoothness assumption, for heavy-tailed
021            noise with bounded variance and c) for (L0 , L1 )вИТsmooth functions in the em-
022            pirical risk minimization setting, when the variance is possibly infinite but the
023            expectation is finite.

150   2    C ONTRIBUTIONS
151
152   Making use of Hamiltonian dynamics, we consider a large class of stochastic optimization algo-
153
      rithms (9) for large-scale optimization problems, for which we perform a rigorous convergence
      analysis. Our assumptions on the dissipation term ѕХ are fairly permissive, and thus the class of algo-
154
      rithms covers both interesting cases like normalized SGD with momentum and various soft-clipping
155
      methods with momentum, as well as novel methods. Our analysis shows that the iterates generated
156   by any method in this class are finite almost surely, and that they converge almost surely to the set of
157   stationary points of the objective function F . This means that the methods вАЬalwaysвАЭ work in prac-
158   tice, in contrast to what can be guaranteed by analyses that show convergence in expectation. These
159   results are valid in many applications, due to fairly weak assumptions on the optimization problem.
160   The exact assumptions are listed in Section 5 but essentially consist of either
161
             вАҐ L-smooth objective functions and stochastic gradients with possibly infinite variance, or

239   4.3      A LMOST SURE CONVERGENCE
240
241   The analysis in this paper is based on the ODE method, emanating from Ljung (1976). The particular
242   proof strategy is due to Kushner & Clark (1978), and is based on linear interpolation of the sequence
243   of iterates. The technique was extended to piecewise constant interpolations in Kushner & Yin
244   (2003). The approach relies on the assumption that the iterates generated by the algorithm are finite
245
      almost surely; an assumption that has to be verified independently.
246   A similar analysis of the SGD with momentum was performed in Gadat et al. (2018). It was extended
247   in Barakat et al. (2021), to a class of schemes that encompasses (5). The analytical approach is
248   slightly different and does not cover the normalization- and clipping algorithms that we analyze in
249   this article.
250   We also note that one can employ an analysis similar to that in e.g. Bottou et al. (2018), along with
251   martingale results like that in Robbins & Siegmund (1971) to obtain almost sure convergence of a
252   subsequence of the iterates. This is for instance the case in Sebbouh et al. (2021) where almost sure
253   convergence guarantees of the type min0вЙ§kвЙ§K kвИЗF (qk )k2 вЖТ 0 almost surely for SGD and SGD
254   with momentum are established. These types of results are weaker than those obtained in this paper,
255   since they cannot guarantee that the whole sequence of iterates {qk }kвЙ•0 converges to a stationary
256   point.

260   The (L0 , L1 )вИТsmoothness assumption was introduced in Zhang et al. (2020b) as a more appropriate
261

262

263

1895   The experiments in the previous section verify the theoretical results in the paper and we see that
