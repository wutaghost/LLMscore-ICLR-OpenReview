009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper considers the problem of utilizing a large-scale text-to-image diffusion
012            model to tackle the challenging Inexact Segmentation (IS) task. Unlike traditional
013            approaches that rely heavily on discriminative-model-based paradigms or dense
014            visual representations derived from internal attention mechanisms, our method
015
               focuses on the intrinsic generative priors in Stable Diffusion (SD). Specifically, we
               exploit the pattern discrepancies between original images and mask-conditional
016
               generated images to facilitate a coarse-to-fine segmentation refinement by establish-
017
               ing a semantic correspondence alignment and updating the foreground probability.
018            Comprehensive quantitative and qualitative experiments validate the effectiveness
019            and superiority of our plug-and-play design, underscoring the potential of lever-
020            aging generation discrepancies to model dense representations and encouraging
021            further exploration of generative approaches for solving discriminative tasks.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Recent breakthroughs in Diffusion Models (DMs) have empowered the field of visual generation for
026   images (Rombach et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023) and video (Cho et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2022), demon-
027   strating their capacity of high-fidelity and diverse content synthesis. Meanwhile, there is a growing
028
      interest in unlocking DMs for performing the discriminative task of visual dense recognition (Xu
      et al., 2023a; Barsellotti et al., 2024a). However, similar to discriminative-model-based segmentation
029
      frameworks (Kirillov et al., 2023; Huynh et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022b), these DM-based methods
030
      rely heavily on large-scale pixel-level training datasets, which require costly and labor-intensive
031   labeling efforts. To relieve this, this paper explores the potential of DMs in tackling the Inexact
032   Segmentation (IS) problem, a more challenging task that achieves segmentation using only text
033   or image-level class labels, essentially merging two existing settings: Text-Supervised Semantic
034   Segmentation (TSSS) (Xu et al., 2022a; Ren et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b) and Weakly-Supervised
035   Semantic Segmentation (WSSS) (Ahn & Kwak, 2018; Wang et al., 2020b).
036
      One line of current DM-based IS research is dedicated to excavating and refining the image-text
037
      cross-attention map embedded in the noise predictor network (Wang et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2023b).
038
      Specifically, these methods leveraged the object-shape-characterized self-attention module to refine
039   the cross-attention map, yielding a segmentation mask for the query object. Another line of research
040   focuses on treating a diffusion process as a self-supervised denoising task and employing a diffusion
041   model as a general feature extractor (Xu et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023). In these studies, diffusion
042   models serve as attention-guiding feature extractors, indirectly assisting segmentation tasks. In con-
043   trast, research on using generative paradigms to directly optimize segmentation remains unexplored,
044   leaving the fundamental generative ability of large-scale pretrained diffusion models underutilized.
045
      In this paper, we delve into the generative nature of pretrained diffusion models to refine a
046
      coarse segmentation mask from inexact segmentation. Specially, we are inspired by cases that
047
      GPTs (Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023) can generate responses closer to the alternative an-
048   swers under certain prompts to solve discriminative tasks without any extra training. For vi-
049   sual diffusion models, better condition guidance similarly results in a smaller discrepancy be-
050   tween the generated and initial images. Under such an implication, we can use the discrep-
051   ancy to obtain feedback to improve the condition itself. Prior work, DiffusionClassifier (Li
052   et al., 2023a), has proved that using a correct text prompt leads to a better denoising result for
053   a specific image, indicating better category classification. We incorporate this spirit into IS, a
      more challenging discriminative grounding task without pixel-level supervision. A new frame-

      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
079       • Different from the popular discriminative-based segmentation paradigm and previous DM-based
080         training methods, we propose a novel training-free framework in a generation manner for inexact
081         segmentation refinement empowered by the condition capacity of pretrained diffusion models.
082
          • We are among the first attempts to leverage the discrepancy between original and generated
083
            images to refine the coarse mask by technically establishing a principled alignment to build the
084         correspondence and updating the foreground probability of each pixel with its paired pixel.
085
086       • Our framework has achieved a consistent performance gain in both open-vocabulary and weakly
087         supervised segmentation tasks on top of current state-of-the-art methods leveraging complemen-
088         tary knowledge sources from other post-refinement methods. The promising potential sheds
089
            light on using generative models to solve discriminative tasks without training.

      2    R ELATED WORK
092   Diffusion Model-based Segmentation. Diffusion Models (DMs), while demonstrating powerful
093   image generation capabilities, have also exhibited emergent perception in object segmentation. A
094   line of segment-after-synthesize works intuitively turns to Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al.,
095   2022), representing the most powerful DM, to first synthesize extra high-quality pixel-level training
096   datasets, which are then used to enhance the segmentor’s performance (Li et al., 2023b; Nguyen
097   et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023). Specifically, these two-stage methods either focus on
098   exploiting the cross-attention map from SD for generating the first-stage mask or directly use the
099
      fused visual features from SD to train the second-stage segmentation module. Differentiating from
      such a two-stage pipeline, some methods shed light on directly transferring DMs into a discriminative
100
      segmentation model by generating the pixel-level output conditioned on the input image (Amit et al.,
101
      2021; Xu et al., 2023a; Burgert et al., 2022). For instance, ODISE (Xu et al., 2023a) proposed to
102   train an SD-based segmentation framework by aligning the generated visual mask output with the
103   corresponding caption and category labels. Contrary to these training-based frameworks, a stream
104   of works Tang et al. (2022); Karazija et al. (2023); Barsellotti et al. (2024a;b); Marcos-Manchón
105   et al. (2024); Yoshihashi et al. (2023), liberating from the costly pixel-level training process, has been
106   dedicated to treating SD as an explicit training-free segmentor by directly mining its inner dense visual
107   representation. OVDiff (Karazija et al., 2023) and FreeDA (Barsellotti et al., 2024b) tend to adopt the
      SD-based visual feature to generate the visual semantic prototype, serving as the nearest neighbor

129   Discriminative Models for Inexact Segmentation. To liberate humans from exhaustive pixel-level
130   annotation, recent years have witnessed extraordinary progress in Inexact Segmentation (IS), which
131   aims to achieve a segmentation network equipped with coarse-grained labels. In this paper, we
132   mainly discuss two derivative streams, i.e., Weakly-supervised Semantic Segmentation (WSSS) (Ahn
133   & Kwak, 2018; Ahn et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020b; Zhu et al., 2023), and
134
      Text-Supervised Semantic Segmentation (TSSS) (Xu et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023; Cha et al.,
      2022; Shin et al., 2022). WSSS regulates a segmenter trained with merely image-level labels.
135
      Most methods addressing WSSS focus on refining the seed areas generated by Class Activation
136
      Mapping (CAM) (Zhou et al., 2016), which merely captures the highly discriminative object regions.
137   These methods, starting from early pooling-based mechanism modifications (Kwak et al., 2017) and
138   regularized data augmentation enhancements (Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020b), have gradually
139   shifted to inter-pixel or semantic relation mining (Ahn et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2022b; Zhu et al., 2023).
140
      TSSS aims to develop a segmentation model, trained with merely image-text pairs, that is able to
141
      segment arbitrary objects beyond predefined classes. This ability is also known as open-vocabulary
142
      segmentation. Most discriminative-model-based works addressing this can be categorized into
143   two groups based on whether CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is adopted for mask generation. The
144   first category concentrates on extracting coarse localization features from CLIP through either the
145   image-text cross-attention map (Shin et al., 2022; Cha et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022a) or the
146   CAM (Zhou et al., 2016)-based attention map Lin et al. (2023), which are subsequently refined to
147   achieve fine-grained segmentation performance. The second category, different from those CLIP-
148   based training-free methods, focuses on enhancing plain Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy
149   et al., 2020) by injecting grouping and clustering recognition from massive image-text training pairs,
150   leading to a foundational segmentation model (Xu et al., 2022a; Ren et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2022;
151
      Zhang et al., 2023).
152   Segmentation Post-Refinement The segmentation post-refinement enhances the quality and pre-
153   cision of initial segmentation outputs by leveraging additional priors to address inaccuracies and
154   improve overall performance. Dense CRF (Krähenbühl & Koltun, 2011) refines segmentation results
155   by applying a fully connected Conditional Random Field (CRF) to the predicted probability map,
156   leveraging pixel similarity and spatial relationships from the image. CascadePSP (Cheng et al., 2020)
157
      refines local boundaries with a novel refinement module, achieving pixel-accurate, class-agnostic
      segmentation across resolutions. SegRefiner (Wang et al., 2023c) enhances object masks using a
158
      discrete diffusion-based refinement approach. In comparison, our approach exploits image generation
159
      discrepancies empowered by a pretrained diffusion model to refine existing segmentation masks.
160
161   Visual Correspondence. Visual correspondence typically describes the matching relationship
      between specific points or features across different images that represent the same semantic, geometric,

163   beneficial to various vision tasks, such as object segmentation (Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020;

172

173

174   et al., 2022). This work exploits DM-based semantic correspondence to improve the segmenter’s
175   performance explicitly.

209

214   is detailed in Section 3.3.1. Then, x̃0 can be calculated under such a mask injection. Finally, the

335   trained on LAION Schuhmann et al. (2022). In our experiment, all images are resized to (512, 512).
336   All experiments are merely conducted on 1 RTX 3090 GPU equipped with 24 GB of memory without
337   any extra training. Our method, working in an on-top-of manner, follows a refine-after-generate
338
      paradigm: generating the masks from the selected mask-free baseline first and then refining them
      via our proposed method without additional training. For the mask generation process, we strictly
339
      follow the settings in the selected baselines. For explicit mask injection, our parameter follows the
340
      DenseDiffusion Kim et al. (2023) and the added noise step is 400. For the semantic correspondence
341   alignment, the feature extractor we adopt is a CLIP image encoder to better distinguish between
342   generated and initial image. The pixel correspondence mixing coefficient β is set to 0.8 for open-
343   vocabulary segmentation and 0.9 for weakly-supervised semantic segmentation. For each specific
344   class, We treat all other segments as background and update the current segment logit independently.
345   Then the final segmentation is refined with updated logit after normalization. As the framework is
346   dedicated to a mask refining task, we only select the confusion areas in the coarse mask Sc , providing
347   the upper and lower bounds of the foreground probability. The confusion area is selected as where the
348   foreground probability value is within the range of [0.2, 0.6] of the maximum foreground probability
349
      value for the current class, and the distance to the edge does not exceed 40 pixels.

      5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   This paper explored an intuitive yet feasible training-free solution based on Stable Diffusion (SD),
529   a representative large-scale text-to-image diffusion model, to tackle the challenging vision task of
530   Inexact Segmentation (IS), which aims at achieving segmentation using merely texts or image-level
531
      labels as minimalist supervision. Most SD-based trials, following the discriminative-model-exploited
      pipelines, fall into the pure exploitation of the visual dense representations inherently arising from
532
      the inner attention mechanism. In contrast, this paper emphasized the underlying generation prior in
533
      SD, i.e., the pattern discrepancy between the original and mask-conditioning reconstructed images, to
534   encourage a coarse-to-fine segmentation refinement by progressively aligning the generated-original
535   representations. Furthermore, we proposed establishing the pixel-level semantic correspondence
536   between the generated-original patterns, yielding a delicate correction towards flawless segmentation
537   for the matched point. Through quantitative and qualitative experiments, we have demonstrated
538   the effectiveness and superiority of this plug-and-play design. Our results highlight the potential of
539   utilizing generation discrepancies to model dense representations in diffusion models. We hope this
      work inspires further exploration of diffusion models in discriminative tasks.

1175   in-exact nature, we only use the attention perturbation in diffusion backbone avoiding involving exact
1176   pixel-level annotation.

1178   stronger mask injection method, Controlnet (Zhang et al.). The ControlNet consists of approximately
1179   half of a diffusion backbone and functions as a feature extractor that can accept arbitrary signals
1180   (such as segmentation masks) as input. The extracted features are then integrated into the diffusion
1181   backbone to control the generative output, ϵ(xt , t, S). For images-annotation pairs(x0 and S), then

1195
1196   On-top-of. Our method, as a plug-and-play framework, can be simply and efficiently integrated into
1197   various existing segmentation modules to enhance the performance online with the current single
1198   sample.
1199
       Generative content with generated-original bias. Some work such as VPD (Zhao et al., 2023)
1200
       and ODISE (Xu et al., 2023a) use pretrained diffusion model as feature extractor with a self-
1201
       supervised denoising loss. While another line of research, such as OVDiff (Karazija et al., 2023) and
1202   Freeda Barsellotti et al. (2024c), merely utilize the content directly generated by diffusion models for
1203   target prototype retrieval. In our work, we explore the discrepancy between the generative content
1204   and the initial image to refine the discriminative result.
1205
1206   Discriminative assistance. Some diffusion-based training-free segmentation works such as
1207   Freeda (Barsellotti et al., 2024c) and OVDiff (Karazija et al., 2023) employ pre-trained discriminative
1208   models such as DINO (Caron et al., 2021) as assistance, while the performance of the framework
1209   heavily depends on these discriminative models.
1210
       Cross attention initialization. Most works employ the attention between text and image as a
1211
       segmentation prior to the diffusion model, such as DatsetDiffusion (Nguyen et al., 2024) and
1212
       DiffSegmentor (Wang et al., 2023b). The most significant difference between our work and others is
1213   that the attention mechanism we used is EMI as injecting the coarse mask prior to the generation
1214   pipeline. The EMI part could be substituted without attention with another more advanced mask-
1215   injecting module.

       O.1 DATASET D ETAILS
1267   Datasets. In Section 4, we evaluate our G4Seg on 3 prevalent benchmarks, which are PASCAL
1268   VOC12 2012 Everingham et al. (2015), COCO Lin et al. (2014), PASCAL Context Mottaghi et al.
1269
       (2014). Here is the detailed introduction of these five datasets as follows:
1270   • PASCAL VOC2012 Everingham et al. (2015): The PASCAL VOC12 dataset consists of a diverse
1271     collection of images spanning 21 different object categories (including one background class), such
1272     as a person, car, dog, and chair. The dataset provides annotations for both training and validation
1273     sets, with around 1,464 images in the training set and 1,449 images in the validation set. We use
1274     the validation set for the downstream evaluation.
1275   • COCO Lin et al. (2014): The COCO Object dataset covers a wide range of 80 object categories,
1276     such as cars, bicycles, people, animals, and household items. For semantic segmentation, it has
1277     118,287 training images and 5,000 images for validation.
1278
       • Context Mottaghi et al. (2014): The dataset contains a diverse set of images taken from various
1279     scenes, including indoor and outdoor environments. It covers 59 common object classes, such as a
1280     person, car, bicycle, and tree, as well as 60 additional stuff classes, including sky, road, grass, and
1281     water. It has 118,287 training images and 5,000 images for validation. Here we merely consider
1282     the object dataset part and use the validation set.
