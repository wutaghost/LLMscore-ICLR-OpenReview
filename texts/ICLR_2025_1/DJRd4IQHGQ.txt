011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated fine-tuning (FFT) aims to fine-tune a pre-trained model with private
014            data from distributed clients by exchanging models rather than data under the
015
               orchestration of a parameter server (PS). However, as large models are acing in
               almost every machine learning task, the communication overhead and memory
016
               demand are surging accordingly, hindering the practical deployment on consumer
017
               devices. To overcome the bottleneck forged by the growing communication over-
018            head of federated learning and lower the high memory demand of large model
019            fine-tuning, we propose FeedSign, an FFT algorithm where a client uploads its
020            update model and downloads the global model of any size using exactly 1 bit
021            per step, while the memory demand is squeezed to the amount needed for infer-
022            ence. This is realized by utilizing zeroth-order (ZO) optimizers on large models
023            and shared pseudo-random number generators (PRNG) across devices to split the
024            gradient estimate from the clients to 1) a direction corresponding to a designated
025            random seed and 2) a binary vote from the client indicating whether the seed-
026
               corresponding direction grants a local loss descent, which is the only information
               the clients should convey to the PS. We conduct theoretical analysis on FeedSign
027
               and show that it converges at an exponential rate O(e−t ), where t is the number
028
               of elapsed steps, the same rate as in first-order (FO) methods can attain in big O
029            notation. Moreover, it is also found that FeedSign enjoys good robustness against
030            data heterogeneity and Byzantine attacks. We conduct extensive experiments on
031            models across different structures and sizes (11M to 13B) and found that the pro-
032            posed method performs better or closely, depending on scenarios, compared to
033            its ZO and FO counterparts albeit an orders-of-magnitude lower communication
034            overhead. We also discuss some interesting advantages as byproducts guaranteed
035            by the minimalistic design of FeedSign.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      The development of deep learning (DL) has allowed us to enjoy better intelligent services by training
040
      larger models on broader data. While large models demonstrate good performance in general cases,
041
      they hold promise to provide more tailored services and greatly improve their ability on intelligent
042   applications if the rich but privacy-sensitive local data of users can be accessed for learning. Fed-
043   erated learning (FL) McMahan et al. (2017) stands out as a solution to achieve privacy-preserving
044   distributed learning by frequently averaging the model parameters generated by the local data but
045   leaving the data intact at their holders. The algorithm is known as federated averaging (FedAvg).
046   When the paradigm is applied on a fine-tuning task, it is often termed federated fine-tuning (FFT)
047   Popov et al. (2018).
048   Such a learning paradigm demands that stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms to be run on
049   client devices. However, the assumed participating client devices are usually resource-restrictive
050   devices like phones and tablets, where SGD can be a heavy computation burden. Moreover, as large
051   models become increasingly popular due to their versatility and great performance, model update
052   aggregation under the FFT paradigm becomes prohibitively expensive. Different methods have been
053   proposed to lower communication and computation costs. Pioneering works include model splitting
      Thapa et al. (2022) that proposed to split the DL model into two parts so that most of the computation

      2.1   F EDERATED L EARNING
120
121   Federated learning (FL) contrasts with centralized learning by training a shared model using data
122   from distributed owners without directly sharing the data thereby preserving data privacy McMahan
123   et al. (2017). Although centralized learning usually provides an upper bound of performance, FL
124   has its unique advantages as it can access data originally unavailable to centralized learning due to
125   privacy concerns Yang et al. (2018); Hard et al. (2018); Cormode et al. (2018). It is also suitable
126   for uniting siloed raw data without compromising confidentiality as in various fields like healthcare
127   Ogier du Terrail et al. (2022); Rieke et al. (2020) and financing Long et al. (2020).
128   However, FL’s privacy protection comes at the cost of frequent model parameter exchanges, creat-
129   ing a communication bottleneck Konečnỳ et al. (2016); Kairouz et al. (2021). The success of large
130   pre-trained models in various tasks Liu et al. (2019); Achiam et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023a); Doso-
131   vitskiy et al. (2020) highlights the need to address this bottleneck. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning
132   techniques, which can reduce the number of trainable parameters, show promise when combined
133   with FL to minimize communication overhead Sun et al. (2024); Cho et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
134
      (2023); Kim et al. (2023). However, the communication overhead inevitably scales to the number
      of trainable parameters in all of methods above.
135
136
137   2.2   ZO O PTIMIZATION FOR DL AND FL
138
      Over the years, FO methods like SGD and its variants have been the default choice for DL model
139
      training Gardner (1984); Amari (1993); Bottou (2010); Kingma (2014); Bottou et al. (2018). This
140   method aims to minimize an objective L(w) that characterizes how bad a function fw parameterized
141   by a numerical vector w is mapping from an input space X to an output space Y using the chain
142   rule and automatic differentiation Griewank (2014); Paszke et al. (2017) to approach the deriva-
143   tive of L(w) with respect to w. Nonetheless, some objectives of interest are non-differentiable or
144   whose gradients are expensive to compute calling for alternatives. They are usually known as ZO
145   optimization since they do not require explicit gradient information for objective minimization.
146   The combination of ZO optimization and FL has been a hot research topic in recent years since in
147   FL settings clients are usually resource-limited and ZO can make the estimation of gradients less
148   expensive Fang et al. (2022); Qiu et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024a); Ling et al. (2024); Maritan et al.
149   (2024). However, the communication bottleneck is still a huge problem for real deployment.
150
      Notably, FwdLLM Xu et al. (2024) and FedKSeed Qin et al. (2023) are the closest works to ours,
151
      where the authors discuss a federated fine-tuning framework that exchanges models by exchang-
152   ing seed-projection pairs. However, our work aims to push the method of seed-projection pairs
153   for model exchange to its limits. We show that our method enjoys numerous surprising benefits
154   compared to its predecessors. Moreover, we extend the experiments to models of larger scales and
155   account for vision models also.
156
157   2.3   DATA H ETEROGENEITY, B YZANTINE ATTACKS , AND C OMPRESSION IN FL
158
159   Data heterogeneity is a critical concern in federated learning Ye et al. (2023) where each user holds
160   inconsistent shards that do not represent the overall data distribution well, causing divergent updates
161   and undermining training effectiveness. This can cause the global model to converge to suboptima
      with potential performance loss Karimireddy et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020). Efforts to address this


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      challenge under an FO setting are ongoing Qu et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023b);
163   Chen et al. (2024b). Notably, Li et al. (2019) proposes to do a one-bit element-wise compression on
164   the model weights to simultaneously promote data heterogeneity resilience and reduce communica-
165   tion load, pushing elementwise compression to its limit. However, the communication overhead still
166   scales to the parameter size, hindering integration with large models.
167
      Additionally, FL performance can be degraded by Byzantine clients who maliciously alter their
168
      data or models Fang et al. (2020), necessitating robust FL algorithms So et al. (2020); Tian et al.
169   (2022). Within the context of zeroth-order (ZO) optimization, CYBER-0 Delgado Neto et al. (2024)
170   marks an initial attempt by using trimmed mean aggregation to enhance the Byzantine resilience
171   of ZO-based FL. Various aggregation methods have been proposed to improve FO-based FL re-
172   silience against such attacks Blanchard et al. (2017); Yin et al. (2018); Alistarh et al. (2018); So
173   et al. (2020). Allouah et al. (2023) explores a joint defense method against data heterogeneity and
174   Byzantine attacks. However, the communication load is not reduced and will be prohibitively high.
175   Notably, Lang et al. (2023a;b) introduces a Byzantine resilient compressed aggregation method for
176   FO-based FL systems where the communication overhead is reduced to 1 bit per step using a nested
177
      lattice coding with strict privacy guarantees, demonstrating that well-designed lossy compression
      can induce strong robustness without obviously compromising the performance of FL systems.
178
179   However, we notice that most efforts addressing this issue are separately doing accurate gradient
180   estimation followed by lossy compression, leading to potentially unnecessary computational loads,
181   as the compression eventually negates the costly effort of acquiring an accurate gradient estimation
182   in FO-based methods. Motivated by this, we envisage a more integrated and efficient framework that
183
      runs on gradient estimation that is less accurate but attainable and communicable with much lower
      overheads with marginal performance loss. This marks a difference in rationale between our work
184
      and conventional methods addressing data heterogeneity and Byzantine attacks by compression.

356   To validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, we conducted extensive experiments across

362   perturbations consistent with that adopted in MeZO. As a result, the number of elapsed steps of

526   6     C ONCLUSION
527
528   We have presented a novel FFT framework FeedSign that can operate in an extremely deficient
529
      communication and memory budget. Facilitated by ZO optimization and shared PRNG, each client
      needs only to upload one bit to the PS and then download one bit as a global update direction
530
      metric in a step, and use up the memory amount equaling to that needed for inference. We conduct
531
      theoretical analysis implying that FeedSign has many interesting properties including different kinds
532   of robustness. Extensive experiments have shown that reducing communication overhead affects the
533   performance of FeedSign little. We discuss some surprising advantages brought by the minimalistic
534   design of FeedSign and how it can facilitate better FL deployment.

1061   D    W HEN W ILL FeedSign BE U NBIASED ?
1062
1063   It is clear that FeedSign does not provide general unbiased gradient estimation. We will discuss a
1064   specific case where FeedSign is unbiased.
1065   Assume the true gradient projection for a specific model is p, and the estimation yielded by ZO-
1066   FedSGD is p1 and FeedSign p2 . Consider a noise n with CDF F (x) on p due to batch gradient
1067   estimation, specifically, p1 = p + n, then
1068                                           Prob(p2 = 1) = F (p),                                (52)
1069
                                            Prob(p2 = −1) = F (−p).                                 (53)
1070
1071   To make p2 an unbiased estimator of p, we need
1072                                       E[p2 ] = F (p) − F (−p) = p                                       (54)
1073
       for any p in the support of F (x). This result implies that FeedSign is unbiased only when
1074
1075         1. the noise CDF is uniform on [−1, 1],
1076         2. p takes value on [−1, 1] only.
1077
1078   This is an obviously unrealistic setting. However, there could be methods that distort the true dis-
1079   tribution of p and n so that they behave similarly to the abovementioned case, making FeedSign an
       unbiased FL method. This could be a topic for future investigation.

1302    H.3    I MPLEMENTING FedSGD OVER LLM
1303
1304    A common way of building an FL system simulation is maintaining K + 1 model instances in the
1305    memory (K as the clients, one as the PS). However, the largest experiment we have carried out is
1306    FO full-parameter fine-tuning OPT-13B with FedAvg over K = 5 clients. Fine-tuning an instance
1307
        of an OPT-13B model costs 316 GB (4xA100 GPU) GPU memory, and 6 of them is unaffordable
        for us. So we make use of the behavior of the auto-differentiation of PyTorch to detour the problem.
1308
1309    In a nutshell, we simulate only the global model on a virtual PS in the memory, and the local up-
1310    dates from different clients are accumulated to param.grad by calling loss.backward() for
1311    K times, each time on the loss computed from a corresponding client. The loss.backward()
1312    implicitly simulates three steps in a communication round: 1) clients computing local updates,
1313
        2) clients sending local updates to the PS, 3) PS aggregates the local gradients. Next, a call of
        optimizer.step() subtracts the parameters by the corresponding entries of param.grad,
1314
        simulating the global model marching a step along the gradient direction and broadcasting the up-
1315
        dated model. In this way, we simulate the FedSGD over K clients using only 1x inference memory.
1316
1317    We illustrate the process using the following snippet.
1318   1 for t in range(T):
1319   2     optimizer.zero_grad()
       3     for c in range(C):
1320   4         ’’’
1321   5         sample a batch from its private dataset
       6         calculate local loss
1322   7         ’’’
1323   8         loss.backward()      # accumulate local gradients to global model
       9     optimizer.step()
