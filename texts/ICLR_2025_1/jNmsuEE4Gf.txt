009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual reasoning is an essential ability of state-of-the-art multi-modal AI systems.
012            Improving these systems requires high-quality vision-language data at scale. De-
013            spite the abundance of internet image and text data, knowledge-rich and well-
014            aligned image-text pairs are rare. In this paper, we present a scalable data gener-
015
               ation pipeline built with our diagramming agent, F EYNMAN. To create diagrams,
               F EYNMAN first enumerates domain-specific knowledge components (“ideas”) and
016
               performs code planning based on the ideas. Given the plan, F EYNMAN trans-
017
               lates ideas into simple declarative programs and iterates to receives feedback and
018            visually refine diagrams. Finally, the declarative programs are rendered by the
019            P ENROSE diagramming system. The optimization-based rendering of P ENROSE
020            preserves the visual semantics while injecting fresh randomness into the layout,
021            thereby producing diagrams with visual consistency and diversity. As a result,
022            F EYNMAN can author diagrams along with grounded captions with very little
023            cost and time. Using F EYNMAN, we synthesized a dataset with more than 100k
024            well-aligned diagram-caption pairs. We also curate a visual-language benchmark,
025            D IAGRAMMA, from freshly generated data. D IAGRAMMA evaluates the visual
026
               reasoning capabilities of vision-language models. We plan to release the dataset,
               benchmark, and the full agent pipeline as an open-source project.

074   When generating conceptual diagrams, models are tasked to perform both knowledge elicitation and
075   visual production. When prompted to produce a diagram representing some high-level concepts, the
076   model needs to elicit the relevant concepts (abstract knowledge, e.g., H2 O has 2 hydrogen and 1

079

082   diagram generation, and ask:

085
086   To address our questions, we elicit domain knowledge from LLMs and iteratively produce high-

088

      2   D IAGRAMMING AGENT P IPELINE
142
143   In this section, we present the workflow of our diagramming agent, F EYNMAN. F EYNMAN’s di-
144
      agram synthesis pipeline includes four steps: idea, plan, iterate, and render. By leveraging the
      knowledge capacity of LLMs and a conceptual diagramming tool, F EYNMAN can generate grounded
145
      and diverse visual representations of scientific concepts at scale. Our pipeline has the following
146
      characteristics:
147
148
            1. Knowledge scalability: Our choice to use an LLM to provide general “knowledge-focused
149            planning” decouples the domain knowledge elicitation and the domain-specific visual de-
150            sign. This choice alleviates the cost of obtaining diverse and high-quality knowledge for
151            the generation of domain-specific diagrams (Section 2.2).
152
153         2. Visual diversity: The optimization-based approach of the P ENROSE rendering engine pro-
154
               vides visual diversity even given the same visual concept (Section 2.1), boosting visual
               diversity of the synthesized diagrams.
155
156         3. Image-text alignment: The programs written by F EYNMAN simply encode the conceptual
157            ideas and relationships, from which the visuals are automatically derived. These programs
158            resemble natural language descriptions of the concepts, enabling smooth translation be-
159            tween concepts and code.
160
161   In this section, we first present the background of conceptual diagramming, which is the foundation
      of our approach, and then introduce each step in the agentic pipeline of F EYNMAN.

      2.1     BACKGROUND : CONCEPTUAL DIAGRAMMING
174
175   Conceptual diagrams refer to abstract images
176   that visually represent “a set of ideas and
177   their relations” (Tversky, 2017). At present,
178   most conceptual diagrams are created by ei-
179   ther a drawing tool like Adobe Illustrator or a
180   low-level graphical programming language like
181   PGF/TikZ or SVG. Using these tools is highly
182   manual and it is extremely difficult to use them
183
      to automating diagram production (Ma’ayan &
      Ni et al., 2020). As a result, AI generation of
184
      diagrams through TikZ has proved challenging
185
      (Belouadi et al., 2023) (Fig. 7).
186

188   targeting conceptual diagrams. P ENROSE sepa-

      rates the abstract concepts in the diagram (the
      Substance) and the visual representations of

199   shapes S = {S1 , . . . , Sn }, each with degrees of freedom p⃗ = (p1 , . . . , pm ) such as width, height,

234   2.2   K NOWLEDGE P LANNING : E NUMERATING THE DOMAIN KNOWLEDGE WITH AN LLM
235
236   State-of-the-art LLMs learn vast knowledge during their large-scale pretraining. For example, GPT-
237   4o attains 53% accuracy in the GPQA benchmark (Rein et al., 2023), close to the 57% achieved
238   by human experts who have or are pursuing Ph.D. degrees. We leverage this large capacity of
239   knowledge of LLM by designing domain-specific prompts to ask an LLM to enumerate pieces of
240   knowledge (“ideas”) related to the selected domain. The prompts are designed to encourage LLM
241   to perform creative knowledge enumeration. For example, LLM is given the question “Enumerate
242
      N chemical reactions that are pedagogical and important.” in the chemical-reactions domain (see
      examples of full domain planning prompts in Appendix B.2). We feed the LLM’s response to the
243
      coding agent, F EYNMAN, to program the concepts into diagrams. In cases where we can’t parse the
244
      output format, we try multiple rounds until we reach a maximum number of rounds.
245
246
247   2.3   D IAGRAMMING CODE PLANNING : REASONING FOR CONCEPTUAL DIAGRAMMING
248
249   In this stage, the F EYNMAN agent generates a coding plan for each of proposed knowledge compo-
250
      nents. The agent first attempts to organize the knowledge components into visual concepts, aiming
      to prepare for translation into P ENROSE code. To make the F EYNMAN agent aware of the P EN -
251
      ROSE syntax, we provide the official P ENROSE documentation in the prompt, akin to Wu et al.
252
      (2024a). Moreover, for each domain, we provide a few in-context examples for LLM to learn the
253   syntax for that specific domain. We then instruct F EYNMAN to plan the visual elements that are
254   described in each knowledge component. This involves listing important steps to write a Substance
255   program that corresponds to the sampled knowledge component (see full code planning prompts in
256   Appendix B.4). Note that we do not instruct the model to write runnable code in this step, which is
257   an explicit design choice. In Section 4, we show that they serve as crucial foundations for successful
258   and diverse code generation.
259
260
      2.4   I TERATIVE V ISUAL -R EFINE WITH A PANEL OF VISUAL JUDGES
261
262   After the planning stage, F EYNMAN translates the plan into compilable and correct P ENROSE pro-
263   grams. We found in our preliminary experiments that even though LLMs such as GPT-4o fail to
264   write the correct program in their first attempt, they can improve if given suitable suggestions in
265   multi-round conversations. This is close to the interactive self-refine approach (Madaan et al.,
266   2024), which improves LLM output quality via multi-round self-reflection. The refinement of dia-
267   grams, which involves visual judgments, posed different challenges. While the prior work adopt tree
268   search to perform refinement (Belouadi et al., 2023; 2024a), their refinement process primarily aims
269
      to achieve better compilation success rate. Without visual judgments and feedback, the generated
      visual artifacts might include incorrect representation of knowledge.

271
      which we term it as Iterative Visual-Refine. The process is illustrated in Figure 3. In the first round,
272   F EYNMAN receives the plans from the previous planning steps and attempts to generate the first
273   code sample. If the code is successfully compiled into a diagram, then F EYNMAN run P ENROSE to
274   generate variations of this diagram and send them to a panel of visual judges to assess its quality.
275   Each visual judge is a vision-language model asked to provide critical feedback to the diagramming
276   agent based on a set of criteria on various aspects of diagram qualities. To keep the task simple,
277   we prompt the judges to answer in boolean values, which are then collected and aggregated. For

279   diagram. We provide an aggregated feedback message and move to the next iteration if the program

      generated arrives at any of these states: a) cannot be extracted from the LLM response; b) fail to

      4.3   A BLATIONS FOR AGENT WORKFLOW
463
464   To provide quantitative insights into the effectiveness of F EYNMAN, we evaluate our pipeline on
465   two metrics: the final yield rate is percentage of successfully complied images after de-duplication;
466   the visual judge scores is an average of rule-based critic score given by MLLM judges for de-
467   duplicated images at the end of the generation. We perform an ablation study on 10 subdomains that
468   encompass wide range of knowledge. As shown in Table 3, we ablate key components of F EYNMAN:
469   (1) explicit knowledge planing (KP, Section 2.2), (2) explicit code planning (CP, Section 2.3), and
470   (3) early stop mechanism based on judge scores (S, Section 2.4).
471   We discuss three notable findings from our ablation results. First, the pipeline with all components
472   (KP + CP + S) achieves the best average judge score. Second, code planning (CP), together with
473   use score to early stop, helps the generation pipeline end in much lower number of rollout rounds.
474   Finally, when knowledge planning (KP) is present, the gap between compiled success rate and final
475   yield rate is very low. The observations above suggest that knowledge planning (KP) is essential for
476   generating diverse scientific diagrams. Additionally, code planning (CP) improves the scalability
477
      of the data generation pipeline. When combined with early-stop, CP helps generate better-quality
      figures with fewer iterations and reduce the overall cost. Combing both steps achieves our goal of
478
      generating scalable and diverse diagrams.

      Synthetic data generation The success of large AI models depends primarily on the scaling law of
511
      model size and training data (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), which stimulated efforts
512
      to curate datasets (Gao et al., 2020; Soboleva et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c). For domains where
513   data collection is expensive, synthesizing data has become the dominant approach (Haluptzok et al.,
514   2022; Zelikman et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023).
515   Synthetic data have long existed in the vision domain (Little & Verri, 1989). For multi-modal AI
516   models, teaching the model to harness their visual reasoning capabilities also relies on synthetic data
517   (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b;a). One work that tackled similar problems to ours is by Zhang
518   et al. (2024b), who synthesized charts and figures via LLM knowledge and program synthesis, but
519   their approach is limited by the tool of choice and lack of agentic ability such as iterative refinement.

521   Vision-language benchmarks Sustainable progress of AI research relies on the continuous de-
522
      velopment of benchmarks to measure the capabilities of AI systems. Benchmarks like HellaSwag
      (Zellers et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2020; Cobbe et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023) contributed sig-
523
      nificantly to the progress measurement of building state-of-the-art LLMs. Vision-language bench-
524
      marks serve the same role for the visual understanding and reasoning abilities of MLLMs. For exam-
525   ple, benchmarks like VQA-v2 (Antol et al., 2015), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) and MMMU
526   (Yue et al., 2024) measure the visual knowledge of MLLMs comprehensively. Other benchmarks
527   like (Methani et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021b;a; Masry et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b)
528   measure domain-specific capabilities like chart understanding and math visual reasoning.

531
532
      In this paper, we presented F EYNMAN, a diagramming agent that authors conceptual diagrams at
533
      scale. F EYNMAN decouples knowledge elicitation from visual production of diagrams to achieve
534   scalability of diagram synthesis. Grounded by a knowledge-infused diagramming language, F EYN -
535   MAN produces text-image pairs to scale up the synthetic data across multiple subjects, such as com-
536   puter science and mathematics. We conducted systematic ablation of key design choices for F EYN -
537   MAN , and showed the production-to-scale curves to demonstrate the scalability of our pipeline.
538   Additionally, we released a new benchmark D IAGRAMMA with question-answer pairs generated
539   by F EYNMAN and curated to ensure correctness, further contributing to research in diagram-based
      reasoning.

845   A     L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
846
847   Dependency on Implicit Knowledge: F EYNMAN relies heavily on the implicit knowledge embed-
848   ded in large language models (LLMs) during knowledge elicitation. This reliance can reduce the
849   model’s effectiveness in domains where LLMs have incomplete or biased knowledge, resulting in
850   less diverse outputs. A key area for future work is improving how knowledge is elicited from LLMs.
851
      For example, this may involve integrating a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline to
      supplement LLMs with external, domain-specific information.
852
853   Limited Diagram Style Variation: While the generated diagrams exhibit layout diversity, control
854   over stylistic elements, such as color schemes or visual aesthetics, is limited to the default capabil-
855   ities of the P ENROSE language. To address this, future efforts will focus on systematically varying
856   Style and Domain programs in P ENROSE, enabling more flexible and customizable diagram gener-
857
      ation.
