009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Electrocardiogram (ECG) is of great importance for the clinical diagnosis of car-
012            diac conditions. Although existing self-supervised learning methods have ob-
013            tained great performance on learning representation for ECG-based cardiac con-
014            ditions classification, the clinical semantics can not be effectively captured. To
015            overcome this limitation, we proposed a Deep ECG-Report Interaction (DERI)
016
               framework to learn cross-modal representations that contain more clinical seman-
               tics. Specifically, we design a novel framework combining multiple alignments
017
               and mutual feature reconstructions to learn effective cross-modal representation of
018
               the ECG-Report, which fuses the clinical semantics of the report into the learned
019            representation. An RME-Module inspired by masked modeling is proposed to im-
020            prove the ECG representation learning. Furthermore, we extend ECG represen-
021            tation learning with a language model to report generation, which is significant
022            for evaluating clinical semantics in the learned representations and even clinical
023            applications. Comprehensive experiments on various datasets with various exper-
024            imental settings show the superior performance of our proposed DERI.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Electrocardiogram (ECG) is a widely used data for reflecting heart electrical activity (Attia et al.
029
      (2019); Al-Zaiti et al. (2023)), which is of great importance in cardiac conditions classification.
030
      Supervised learning methods have obtained effective performance in ECG signal classification with
031   high-quality annotations (Huang et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024a)). However, there are a large
032   number of unlabeled ECG signals in the real world, and supervised learning methods have difficulty
033   utilizing this resource effectively. To reduce the dependence on labeled data, ECG representation
034   learning methods based on self-supervised learning demonstrate their powerful performance (Oh
035   et al. (2022); Mehari & Strodthoff (2022); Lan et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024c)). Compared to
036   supervised learning methods, self-supervised learning methods aim to learn effective representations
037   from ECG signal data in a detached labeling context, and thus tend to be more generalizable and
038   adaptable to different downstream tasks, showing great potential.
039   Existing self-supervised methods for ECG signals can be broadly categorized into generative learn-
040   ing and contrastive learning approaches. Generative learning focuses on masked modeling to recon-
041   struct or generate the input data itself, while contrastive learning aims to learn more discriminative
042   representations by comparing similar and dissimilar samples. However, most of these methods are
043   single-modal, limiting their ability to effectively capture comprehensive, deep semantic representa-
044   tions of ECG signals. Specifically, generative learning methods, which emphasize signal recovery
045
      through masked modeling, primarily focus on low-level signal patterns (e.g., local intensities and
      waveforms), often overlooking high-level semantic features related to clinical conditions (Zhang
046
      et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b)). Contrastive approaches, on the other hand, typically create aug-
047
      mented views as positive pairs by applying data transformations at the input level. However, these
048   transformations can distort the inherent semantics of the ECG signal, leading to a loss of critical
049   information (Lan et al. (2024); Na et al. (2024)). Multi-modal learning has emerged as a promising
050   solution for these limitations due to its ability to learn effective representations from multiple data
051   sources (Chen et al. (2024b;c)). Compared to ECG signals, clinical reports offer direct high-level se-
052   mantic insights. Inspired by advances in medical imaging and radiology reports (Chen et al. (2023);
053   Liu et al. (2023a)), Liu et al. proposed a multi-modal representation learning approach by aligning
      ECG signals with clinical reports (Liu et al. (2024b)). However, their method aligns ECG features

054
      with report features in the feature space, drawing inspiration from CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)),
055   but the interaction between modalities is relatively shallow. Furthermore, although the ECG repre-
056   sentations learned by MERL perform well in classification tasks, they fail to effectively convey the
057   underlying semantics of ECG recordings, which are crucial for understanding cardiac conditions.
058
      To overcome these limitations, we proposed a novel Deep ECG Report Interaction (DERI) frame-
059
      work for cross-modal representation learning. To better capture the clinical semantics of ECG sig-
060
      nals and reports, we design an encoder-decoder structure to conduct multiple alignments and fea-
061   ture reconstruction. Specifically, the ECG signals and their corresponding clinical reports are first
062   encoded and projected into a shared alignment space to achieve an initial alignment. To enhance
063
      interaction, two specialized decoders are employed to reconstruct features by decoding the aligned
064
      representations into the encoding of the other modality. This reconstruction process captures the la-
065
      tent structures in both modalities, enabling the learning of richer cross-modal representations. Sub-
066
      sequently, the decoded features are fused with the modality-specific aligned features to create mixed
067
      representations incorporating both ECG signal and clinical report semantics. These mixed represen-
068
      tations are further utilized for a second alignment. Additionally, we introduce a Random Masked
069
      Enhancement Module (RME-Module) to improve ECG representation learning. Furthermore, the
070
      proposed DERI model is integrated with language models to generate clinical reports, providing a
071
      way to assess the learned clinical semantics embedded in the representations. Extensive experiments
072
      across various settings and datasets are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed
073
      approach. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
074
075   • To learn effective ECG representation for cardiac conditions from reports, we propose a novel
076     cross-modal framework of ECG-Report via multiple feature alignment and mutual feature recon-
077     struction. An RME-Module is also designed for ECG representation learning enhancement.
078   • To better illustrate the clinical semantics learned by DERI, we combine it with a language model
079     for report generation. The pre-trained model provides effective ECG representation and a language
080     model is used to decode it into clinical reports, which can provide clinical semantics visually.
081
      • Comprehensive experiments on downstream datasets are conducted to evaluate the proposed DERI
082     method, including zero-shot classification, linear probing, and even report generation. Experi-
083     mental results illustrate that our proposed DERI method surpasses all SOTA methods by a large
084     margin, which represents that our method can learn more effective clinical semantics.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

      2.1   S INGLE - MODAL ECG R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
089
090   There are various self-supervised learning methods for ECG representation learning. Most of these
091   methods are single-modal, which conduct generative learning or contrastive learning on unannotated
092   ECG signals. CLOCS (Kiyasseh et al. (2021)) and ASTCL (Wang et al. (2023)) are the SOTA single-
093   modal contrastive learning methods that explore temporal and spatial correlations of ECG signals.
094   Similarly, ST-MEM (Na et al. (2024)) proposes to learn ECG representation by spatial-temporal
095   masking modeling and reconstruction of 12-lead ECG signals. Although all these unimodal methods
096   have achieved good performance, they still fall short in learning the clinical semantics of ECG
097
      signals (Liu et al. (2024b)). Single-modal contrastive and generative methods extract representations
      only from ECG signals and are therefore not related to diagnostic reports.

105   better conduct blood pressure estimation (Long & Wang (2023)). However, these methods do not
106   use diagnostic report data, making it difficult to learn the clinical semantics effectively. To learn the
107   clinical semantics of ECG signals, Liu et al. proposed to align ECG features with clinical reports in-

      2.3    C LINICAL R EPORT G ENERATION
129
130   Clinical report generation in radiology has obtained great performance inspired by imaging caption-
131   ing You et al. (2016; 2021). R2Gen (Chen et al. (2020b)) uses a memory-driven Transformer to
132   generate a radiology report directly with the representation of the medical image. CvT2DistilGPT2

134   report generation as well. X-REM proposed to fuse the image-text multi-modal representation and
135
      then used retrieval-based methods to generate the predicted reports from the retrieval corpus (Jeong
      et al. (2024)). Inspired by these image-based clinical report generation methods, we extend our pro-
136
      posed ECG representation learning method to ECG-based clinical report generation, which can help
137
      understand the clinical semantics of the cardiac conditions from the ECG signals.

140   3     METHODOLOGY
141
      3.1    OVERVIEW
142
143   Our DERI framework is designed to learn effective multi-modal representation from ECG signals
144   and corresponding clinical reports. Compared to learning ECG representation alone, our DERI can
145   obtain much deeper interaction between signal modal and text modal via feature aligning and re-
146   construction. As depicted in Figure 1, we adopted a dual-encoder framework as the backbone. Our
147   DERI performs modal interaction representation learning from feature alignment and feature recon-
148   struction. We also design an RME-Module to enhance the ECG representation learning. Deeper
149   modal interactions enable learned representations to include more clinical information, from satis-
150
      fying cardiac status classification to generating diagnostic reports.
151

155

233   3.4    L ATENT R ANDOM M ASKING E NHANCEMENT
234
235   We further conduct an RME module on latent ECG encoding to facilitate representation learning.
236
      Considering that augmentation directly at the data level entails the loss of semantic information
      about the signal, we use our proposed RME-Module on the ECG encoding. Specifically, as the
237
      Encoder extracts encodings, it tends to focus on the local features of the signal to form an encod-
238
      ing sequence. Rather than applying global average pooling to the ECG encoding sequence and
239   using two separate dropout operations to create augmented views, we instead randomly mask the se-
240   quence twice, independently. Then, a multi-head attention mechanism is employed to aggregate the
241   sequence, producing two augmented views of the encoding as a positive pair. This random masking
242   approach helps preserve sequence-level semantic features while enabling the model to learn global
243   features more effectively. We then use standard contrastive loss on these two augmented encoding
244   views. The whole process can be illustrated in Eq. 8

264   3.5    D OWNSTREAM TASKS ON DERI F RAMEWORK
265
266   After training the proposed DERI model, we can obtain an effective representation of ECG signals
267   that contains clinical report information. Then we can use the representation to conduct zero-shot
268   classification and report generation. Considering the quality of the category prompts for zero-shot
269   classification will have a great impact on the performance (Pratt et al. (2023); Maniparambil et al.
      (2023)), we adopt the CKEPE prompts which are constructed by combining large language model


                                                                                                   5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      (LLM) and clinical knowledge (Liu et al. (2024b)). The whole process of these two downstream
271   tasks is illustrated in Figure 2.
272
273   Zero-shot Classification. We adopted CKEPE (Liu et al. (2024b)) as the category prompts and used
274
      the trained report encoder Ft and the projector to obtain the prompt embeddings of all categories.
      We then use the trained DERI model to obtain the Mix Encoding, which is the final representation
275
      containing both ECG signal features and the corresponding clinical report features. Finally, we cal-
276
      culate the similarity between the Mix Encoding and the prompt encoding and then adopt the category
277   to which the prompt with the highest similarity belongs as the classification result. Importantly, all
278   the parameters of the proposed DERI are frozen in this process.
279
280
      Report Generation. After we obtain the final representation of ECG, we adopt GPT-2 as the text
      decoder to construct an encoder-decoder structure since DistilGPT2 (Sanh (2019)) has shown its
281
      great performance on radiology report generation (Nicolson et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024)). We
282
      adopt a trainable linear layer to transform the dimension of the input encoding to meet the dimension
283   of the GPT-2 and conduct fine-tuning on the GPT-2 to minimize a cross-entropy loss LCE between
284   the generated report to the ground truth reports. After training the linear layer and fine-tuning the
285   GPT-2, we can generate corresponding diagnostic reports with ECG signals alone.

290   4.1   DATASETS
291
292   MIMIC-ECG The proposed DERI model is pre-trained on the MIMIC-ECG dataset (Gow et al.
293   (2023)), which contains 800,035 paired ECG signals and clinical reports from 161,352 unique sub-
294   jects. We removed all the samples without reports containing more than 3 words and replaced
295
      ’NaN’ or ’Inf’ in the ECG signal with mean interpolation. Finally, the dataset used for pre-training
      has 771,693 samples. We also conduct report generation tasks on this dataset.
296
297   PTBXL (Wagner et al. (2020)) contains 21,837 12-lead ECG signals recorded from 18,885 patients
298   at a sample rate of 500 Hz with a duration of 10 seconds. It contains four multi-label classification
299   tasks: Superclass, Subclass, Form, and Rhythm. The four different tasks are divided based on the
300   ECG annotation protocol. These four tasks are multi-label classification tasks.
301   CPSC2018 (Liu et al. (2018)) is a publicly accessible dataset that contains 6,877 12-lead ECG
302   recordings with a sampling rate of 500 Hz. The duration of these signals ranges from 6 to 60
303   seconds. Each recording has one corresponding label within nine categories.
304
      Chapman-Shaoxing-Ningbo (CSN) (Zheng et al. (2020; 2022)) is a publicly accessible dataset
305   that contains 45,152 12-lead ECG recordings with a sampling rate of 500 Hz. Each recording has
306   a duration of 10 seconds and the ECG signals with ”unknown” annotation are removed. Therefore,
307   23,026 ECG records with 38 categories were used for classification.
308
309
      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
310
311
      Pre-training. For the encoders used for ECG and reports, we adopt a random initialized 1D-
312   ResNet18 and the Med-CPT (Jin et al. (2023)) respectively. For decoders, we adopt two transformers
313   with 8 attention heads, a depth of 2, and a hidden size of 256 respectively for ecg encoding recon-
314   struction and report encoding reconstruction. We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of
315   1e-3 and a weight decay of 1e-8. The epoch for pre-training is set as 50 with a cosine annealing
316   scheduler to adjust the learning rate. We conduct all the pre-trained experiments on four NVIDIA
317   GeForce RTX 4090 GPUs with a batch size of 512 per GPU.
318   Classification. We freeze the whole DERI and conduct zero-shot classification as illustrated in
319   Section 3.5. For linear probing, we add a new linear classifier and freeze all other parameters in
320   our DERI. We adopt three different settings, which utilize 1%, 10%, and 100% of the training data
321   to train the linear classifier. Since these tasks are all classifications that contain many categories,
322   we adopt the macro AUC as the evaluated metric. We conduct these experiments on one NVIDIA
323   GeForce RTX 4090 GPU and more details about the implementation are provided in the Appendix B.
      The baselines we compared include single-modal methods such as ASTCL. (Wang et al. (2023)),


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      CRT (Zhang et al. (2023)), STMEM (Na et al. (2024)) and multi-modal method MERL. More
325   details can be found in the Appendix C.
326
327   Report generation. We adopt the Natural Language Generation (NLG) metrics to evaluate our
328
      report generation performance first, which include BLEU-n for n-gram overlap evaluation (Papineni
      et al. (2002)) and ROUGE-L with the longest common sub-sequence between the original report and
329
      generated report (Lin (2004)). Considering that NLG metrics can not effectively reflect the clinical
330
      accuracy of the generated report, we further integrated Clinical Efficiency (CE) metrics inspired by
331   the zero-shot classification (more details can be found in Appendix E). Specifically, we use the pre-
332   trained text decoder to obtain prompt embeddings of all categories in the CKEPE. We feed the pre-
333   trained text decoder with the ground clinical report and calculate the similarity as the classification
334   probability for the category. We adopt the category with the highest classification probability as the
335   ground truth label. Then we use the generated report to obtain the predicted label in the same way.
336   The predicted labels are then used to compute precision, recall, and F1 scores against ground truths
337   as the CE metrics. We conduct the experiments on two NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPUs.

525   5   CONCLUSION
526
527   In this study, we proposed DERI, an innovative deep ECG-Report interaction framework for cross-
528
      modal representation learning. To obtain deep ECG-Report interaction, we design multiple align-
      ments and cross-modal mutual reconstruction. Besides, an RME-Module is conducted on the ECG
529
      latent encoding for representation learning enhancement. Moreover, we extended ECG representa-
530
      tion learning to clinical diagnostic report generation, aiming to deliver more intuitive ECG clinical
531   insights. Our extensive experiments demonstrate the DERI’s capability to learn the clinical se-
532   mantics of ECG signals with the help of reports, which achieves the best performance on ECG
533   classification and report generation.
534
      Limitations and Future Work. One potential limitation of our work is that the report used is
535
      closer to a clinical semantic description of the signal and remains structurally different from a real
536
      diagnostic report. Additionally, we plan to expand our DERI into a more comprehensive cross-
537   modal representation learning model, which can learn from other modal data, such as electronic
538   medical records, further enhancing its relevance in clinical medicine.

733   A    R ELATED WORK ON ECG R EPORT L EARNING
734
735   Exploration of ECG signals with the report is now more and more popular. Ref. (Yu et al. (2023)) is
736   proposed to conduct ECG zero-shot classification based on LLMs and retrieval-augmented genera-
737   tion (RAG) and then obtain a great performance on ECG classification. However, this method needs
738   to construct a vector database first for retrieval and the performance depends on the quality of the
739   database. This RAG method has high storage requirements and high computing costs. In addition,
740   although this method is also a multimodal method, it does not use the diagnostic report correspond-
741   ing to the ECG signal, and cannot extract the high-level semantics in the report as efficiently as our
742   DERI method.
743
      METS (Li et al. (2024)) is proposed to simply calculate the cosine similarity between the ECG em-
744
      bedding and the report embedding for multi-modal ECG-Text SSL pre-training. However, this is too
745
      simple to learn the cross-modal representation of ECG and reports. Actually, it’s the previous works
746
747
      of the MERL with simpler methods with lower results. MERL is the work that further improved
748
      METS.
749   MEIT (Wan et al. (2024)) aims to use an instruction prompt to generate reports based on the ECG
750   signal input with LLMs. However, in our work, our DERI framework is not designed only for report
751   generation, but also for ECG classification. It is used to learn effective representation with more
752   clinical cardiac information from ECG signals. Although we also conduct report generation, it is
753   just one of the ways to verify the representation of ECG learned.
754
755   ECG-Chat (Zhao et al. (2024)) is a great work for multi-modal ECG learning, which combines the
      ECG encoder and classification results to construct instructions for LLM. In addition to the ECG

790   C    BASELINE .
791
792   More details about the compared baselines in our experiment are shown as below.
793   SimCLR. (Chen et al. (2020a)) This method aims to maximize consistency between differently
794   augmented views of the same data example including random cropping, random distortion, and
795   random Gaussian blur. Contrastive loss obtained by the representation of augmented views is used
796   to optimize the base encoder.
797   BYOL. (Grill et al. (2020)) This method uses an online network to predict the presentation of other
798   augmented views of the same sample obtained by a target network. Therefore, BYOL conducts
799   contrastive learning without negative pairs.
800
      BarlowTwins. (Zbontar et al. (2021)) This proposes to measure the cross-correlation matrix be-
801
      tween the outputs obtained by two identical networks with distorted versions of a sample. The
802
      cross-correlation matrix is made as close to the identity matrix as possible since it can make the
803   representation of distorted versions from the same simple to be similar.
804
805   MoCo-v3. (Chen et al. (2021)) This method proposes to train the transformer for self-supervised
806
      learning based on investigation of the fundamental components during training. In this way, they
      aim to overcome the instability of the transformer for a better representation.
807
808   SimSiam. (Chen & He (2021)) This method uses an encoder to process two augmented views of
809   one sample and then a prediction MLP is applied on one side while stop-gradient is applied on the
      other side. Neither negative pairs nor momentum are used to learn meaningful representations.


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      TS-TCC. (Eldele et al. (2021)) This method first augments the sample using weak and strong aug-
811   mentation and then learns robust temporal representation with a cross-view prediction task. Finally,
812   the similarity among contexts from the same sample is minimized as contextual contrasting learning.
813
814
      CLOCS. (Kiyasseh et al. (2021)) This method proposed to learn patient-specific representations of
      ECG signals via contrastive learning with consideration of both temporal and spatial information.
815
816   ASTCL. (Wang et al. (2023)) This method proposes a novel ECG augmentation method based on the
817   noise attributes and then combines an adversarial module and a spatial-temporal contrastive module
818   to learn the spatial-temporal and semantic representations of ECG signals.
819   CRT. (Zhang et al. (2023)) This method proposes to model temporal-spectral correlations of tempo-
820   ral time series by a cross-reconstruction transformer. Through cross-domain dropping reconstruc-
821   tion, the model can adequately capture the cross-domain correlations between temporal and spectral
822   information of time series data.
823
      STMEM. (Na et al. (2024)) This method is proposed to learn the spatial-temporal relations of ECG
824   signals for masked modeling. ECG signals are divided into patches on the temporal and spatial
825   dimensions and then the model reconstructs the masked patches to learn spatial-temporal features of
826   12-lead ECG signals for classification.
827
      MERL. (Liu et al. (2024b)) This method extends ECG self-supervised learning to multi-modal
828
      learning by directly aligning the ECG signal encoding and the text encoding to learn ECG repre-
829
      sentation for classification. However, shallow interaction between ECG signals and reports can not
830   provide effective clinical semantics for the representation.
