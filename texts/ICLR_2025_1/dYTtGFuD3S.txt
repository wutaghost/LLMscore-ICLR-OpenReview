037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      The precise prediction of drug-drug interactions (DDIs) remains a critical challenge in biomedicine
040
      and healthcare, with significant implications for both combinatorial therapies and adverse drug re-
041
      actions Juurlink et al. (2003); Bangalore et al. (2007); Scavone et al. (2020); Chakraborty et al.
042   (2021); Akinbolade et al. (2022). While traditional methods of identifying DDIs through clinical
043   evidence are time-consuming and expensive Percha & Altman (2013); Jiang et al. (2022), computa-
044   tional approaches, particularly those leveraging deep learning, have shown promise in accelerating
045   the discovery of potential interactions. However, the scarcity of known DDI fact triplets, exempli-
046   fied by the DrugBank database containing only 365,984 known DDIs among 14,931 drug entries
047   Wishart et al. (2018), poses a significant challenge to these data-driven methods.
048   Recent advances in domain adaptation (DA) techniques offer a promising avenue for addressing the
049   data scarcity problem in DDI prediction. By leveraging knowledge from related domains or datasets,
050   DA methods can potentially improve the generalization and robustness of DDI prediction models.
051   However, the complex nature of drug interactions and the heterogeneity of biomedical knowledge
052   graphs (KGs) Bonner et al. (2022); Himmelstein & Baranzini (2015); Zheng et al. (2021); Chandak
053   et al. (2023) necessitate a more sophisticated theoretical framework that can capture the intricate
      geometry of the problem space.

054
      In this paper, we introduce GraphPharmNet, a novel approach that seamlessly integrates advanced
055   mathematical concepts from differential geometry, optimal transport theory, and quantum field the-
056   ory with state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques. Our framework reformulates the DDI pre-
057   diction problem using the language of fiber bundles and gauge theory, viewing the domain adaptation
058   process as a Hamiltonian flow on a statistical manifold. This perspective allows us to leverage pow-
059   erful tools from symplectic geometry and information geometry to analyze the dynamics of domain
060   adaptation in the context of DDI prediction.
061   A key innovation of our approach is the introduction of the DDI-DA bundle, a geometric structure
062   that encapsulates both the space of drug features and the associated knowledge graphs. By equip-
063   ping this bundle with a connection and a symplectic form, we are able to define gauge-equivariant
064   convolution operations that respect the local symmetries of the underlying drug interaction space.
065   This formulation leads to more robust and generalizable representations of drug interactions across
066   different domains.
067   Our framework also incorporates ideas from topological data analysis and persistent homology to
068   characterize domain-invariant features in DDI prediction. By analyzing the persistent homology
069   groups of the DDI-DA bundle, we provide a topological perspective on the stability of domain
070   adaptation in the context of drug interactions. This approach allows us to identify robust, scale-
071   invariant features that persist across different domains and scales of analysis.
072
      To address the quantum nature of certain drug interactions and the discrete structure of some fea-
073   ture spaces, we extend our framework to the realm of noncommutative geometry. By introducing
074   DDI-DA spectral triples, we provide a noncommutative analogue of Riemannian geometry for DDI
075   prediction, allowing us to apply powerful tools from index theory and K-homology to the analysis
076   of domain adaptation in this context.
077
      The theoretical advancements in GraphPharmNet are complemented by practical innovations in
078
      graph neural network architectures and optimization techniques. We develop a novel graph encoder
079   that operates directly on the DDI-DA bundle, leveraging gauge-equivariant convolutions to capture
080   the geometric structure of drug interactions. Our optimization procedure is guided by a functional
081   renormalization group equation derived from quantum field theory, providing a principled approach
082   to multi-scale analysis of the DDI prediction model.
083
      Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate that GraphPharmNet significantly out-
084
      performs existing methods, particularly in scenarios with limited data or when transferring knowl-
085   edge across disparate domains. Our results highlight the power of this unified mathematical frame-
086   work in capturing complex drug interactions and adapting to new domains, paving the way for more
087   accurate, robust, and interpretable DDI prediction models.
088
      The contributions of this work extend beyond the specific problem of DDI prediction. By establish-
089
      ing a rigorous theoretical foundation for domain adaptation in graph-structured data, our framework
090
      opens new avenues for research in a wide range of scientific disciplines. The combination of differ-
091   ential geometry, optimal transport, information geometry, topological data analysis, and noncommu-
092   tative geometry provides a powerful toolset for analyzing and solving complex domain adaptation
093   problems in various fields.
094
      In the following sections, we provide a detailed exposition of the mathematical foundations
095
      of GraphPharmNet, including the construction of DDI-DA bundles, the formulation of gauge-
096
      equivariant graph neural networks, and the analysis of domain adaptation dynamics using tools
097   from symplectic geometry and renormalization group theory. We then present our experimental
098   results, demonstrating the superior performance of GraphPharmNet on benchmark DDI prediction
099   tasks and providing insights into the interpretability of our model through case studies of predicted
100   drug interactions.
