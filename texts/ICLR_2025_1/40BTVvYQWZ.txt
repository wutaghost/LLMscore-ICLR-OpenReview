009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Game dynamics, which describe how agents’ strategies evolve over time based on
012            past interactions, can exhibit a variety of undesirable behaviours including con-
013            vergence to suboptimal equilibria, cycling, and chaos. While central planners can
014            employ incentives to mitigate such behaviors and steer game dynamics towards
015            desirable outcomes, the effectiveness of such interventions critically relies on ac-
016            curately predicting agents’ responses to these incentives—a task made particu-
017            larly challenging when the underlying dynamics are unknown and observations
018
               are limited. To address this challenge, this work introduces the Side Information
               Assisted Regression with Model Predictive Control (SIAR-MPC) framework. We
019
               extend the recently introduced SIAR method to incorporate the effect of control,
020
               enabling it to utilize side-information constraints inherent to game-theoretic ap-
021            plications to model agents’ responses to incentives from scarce data. MPC then
022            leverages this model to implement adaptive incentive adjustments. Our experi-
023            ments demonstrate the efficiency of SIAR-MPC in guiding systems towards so-
024            cially optimal equilibria, stabilizing chaotic and cycling behaviors. Comparative
025            analyses in data-scarce settings show SIAR-MPC’s superior performance com-
026            pared to pairing MPC with state-of-the-art alternatives like Sparse Identification of
027            Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) and Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs).

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Game theory provides a mathematical framework for studying strategic interactions among self-
033   interested decision-making agents, i.e., players. The Nash equilibrium (NE) is the central solution
034   concept in game theory, describing a state where no player has an incentive to deviate (Nash, 1950).
035
      Over time, research has shifted from simply assuming that an NE exists and players will eventu-
      ally play it, to understanding how equilibrium is reached (Smale, 1976; Papadimitriou & Piliouras,
036
      2019). This shift has led to a focus on learning in games, exploring how strategies evolve over time
037
      based on past outcomes, adopting a dynamical systems perspective (Fudenberg & Levine, 1998;
038   Sandholm, 2010). It has been shown that game dynamics do not necessarily converge to NE but in-
039   stead can display a variety of undesirable behaviors, including cycling, chaos, Poincaré recurrence,
040   or convergence to suboptimal equilibria (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2003; Sato et al., 2002; Mertikopoulos
041   et al., 2018; Milionis et al., 2023). Motivated by these challenges, our primary objective in this work
042   is to determine:
043
044                       Can we steer game dynamics towards desirable outcomes?
045
      To address this problem, we adopt the perspective of a central planner who seeks to influence player
046
      behaviour by designing incentives. Our goal is to achieve this with minimal effort, ensuring that
047
      the incentives are both cost-effective and efficient. More importantly, we operate in a setting with
048   unknown game dynamics and limited observational data, reflecting real-world scenarios where in-
049   formation is often incomplete or uncertain. To tackle these challenges, we introduce a new com-
050   putational framework called Side Information Assisted Regression with Model Predictive Control
051   (SIAR-MPC), designed to steer game dynamics by integrating cutting-edge techniques for real-time
052   system identification and control. In the system identification step, we predict agents’ reactions to
053   incentives, which is especially challenging for settings where observational data is limited, difficult
      to obtain, or costly. To address this problem, we extend the recently introduced SIAR method

055   short burst of a system trajectory. To compensate for the absence of data, SIAR searches for poly-

057   game theoretical applications. To adapt it to our needs, we broaden the scope of SIAR to incorpo-

      Once agent responses to incentives are modeled with SIARc, we use MPC in the subsequent control
      step to develop a dynamic incentive scheme that steers the system towards desirable outcomes (see
079
      Fig. 1 for an example illustrating the impact of control). MPC is a control technique that leverages
080
      a mathematical model of the system to predict future behavior and calculate optimal control inputs
081   that minimize a given objective function (Camacho & Bordons, 2007). A key advantage of MPC
082   is its ability to handle input constraints, which is particularly relevant in our context since there are
083   practical limits to the incentives that can be offered to agents. However, the effectiveness of MPC de-
084   pends on having an accurate system model, highlighting the importance of the system identification
085   step for its successful application.
086
      Our key contributions are:
087
088              • Framework for steering game dynamics: We introduce the SIAR-MPC framework for
089                steering game dynamics towards desirable outcomes when the underlying agent behaviours
090                are unknown and observational data are scarce.
091              • Demonstration of performance across diverse game types: We demonstrate the effec-
092                tiveness of our framework across a diverse range of game types, from zero-sum games like
093                Matching Pennies and Rock-Paper-Scissors to coordination games such as Stag Hunt. Our
094                experimental results show that SIAR-MPC can successfully steer system dynamics towards
095                socially optimal equilibria and stabilize chaotic and cycling learning dynamics.
096              • Superior performance in data-scarce settings: We benchmark SIAR-MPC against an
097                unconstrained regression method, Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics with con-
098                trol (SINDYc), as well as Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) coupled with MPC.
099                Our results demonstrate that SIAR-MPC consistently achieves superior convergence and
100                reduced control costs in data-scarce settings.

103
104   Control of Game Dynamics: Recently, incentive-based control has been applied extensively in
105   multi-player environments (see, e.g., Riehl et al. (2018) and references therein). In addition, optimal
106   control solutions have been given for specific evolutionary games and dynamics (Paarporn et al.,
107   2018; Gong et al., 2022; Martins et al., 2023). However, in the aforementioned works the player
      behavior is known, and to the best of our knowledge a setup where the game dynamics are not a

108
      priori known to the controller has only been explored in the recent works of Zhang et al. (2024)
109   and Huang et al. (2024). Zhang et al. (2024) study the problem of steering no-regret agents in
110   normal- and extensive-form games, under both full and bandit feedback. However, in their setup the
111   agents are adversarial and not bound to fixed dynamics, making their setup incompatible with system
112   identification. On the other hand, Huang et al. (2024) study this problem in the richer environment
113   of Markov games under the additional assumption that the players’ dynamics belong to some known
114   finite class. This assumption allows the controller to utilize simulators of the class of dynamics to
115   optimize for controls that identify the exact update rule with high probability. In contrast, SIAR-
116   MPC does not rely on this assumption and instead takes advantage of the approximation guarantees
117
      of polynomial regression to acquire an accurate representation of an unknown system.
118   System Identification: In the last decades, the overall success of data-driven approaches in the sci-
119   entific communities has inspired the development of Deep Learning (DL) architectures focused on
120   the discovery of dynamical systems, e.g., Neural ODEs (Lu et al., 2018). More recently, increasing
121   demand for more interpretable ”black-box” models led to the development of various specialized ar-
122   chitectures including Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019), Kolmogorov-
123
      Arnold Network ODEs (KAN-ODEs) (Koenig et al., 2024), Learning Across Dynamical Systems
      (LEADS) (Yin et al., 2021), and Deep Projection Networks (DPNets) (Kostic et al., 2024). Among
124
      the aforementioned data-driven technologies, we single out PINNs, and PINN-based architectures
125
      (Geneva & Zabaras, 2020; Ren et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2021), due to their ability to incorporate side
126   information, e.g., physical laws, in their training process. This allows PINNs to also be used in
127   data-scarce environments provided that the amount of side information available is adequate.
128
129
      Two recent non-DL system identification frameworks that account for data scarcity are Sparse Iden-
      tification of Nonlinear Dynamics (SINDy) (Brunton et al., 2016) and the work of Ahmadi & Khadir
130
      (2023), which rely respectively on Sparse-Polynomial Regression and Sum-of-Squares Optimiza-
131
      tion. SINDy is a sparsity-promoting method initially developed for the discovery of ODEs which
132   has been extended through different approaches (Rudy et al., 2017; Mangan et al., 2016; Quade
133   et al., 2018; Chu & Hayashibe, 2020) and applied extensively. An important extension of SINDy is
134   SINDy with control (SINDYc) which incorporates the effects of control inputs (Kaiser et al., 2018).
135   However, SINDy-based frameworks only allow for the incorporation of linear constraints with the
136   notable exception of SINDy-SI (Machado & Jones, 2024), which allows for the integration of poly-
137   nomial nonnegativity constraints based on the novel framework of Ahmadi & Khadir (2023). Even
138   so, SINDy-SI is not tailored for the discovery of game dynamics. On the other hand, the recently in-
139   troduced Side Information Assisted Regression (SIAR) (Sakos et al., 2023) method extends the work
140
      of Ahmadi & Khadir (2023) to games, incorporating various well-studied game-theoretic properties
      as polynomial nonnegativity constraints on the dynamics. At a technical level, both SINDy-SI and
141
      SIAR solve a hierarchy of semidefinite problems. In this work, we follow this approach and develop
142
      the SIAR-MPC framework with a focus on the identification and control of game dynamics through
143   semidefinite programming in a data-scarce environment.

179   4     T HE SIAR-MPC F RAMEWORK
180
181   In this section, we describe the SIAR-MPC framework for the real-time identification and control of
182   game dynamics. As outlined in the introduction, SIAR-MPC involves two steps. First, the system
183   identification step aims to approximate the controlled dynamics in (2) using only a limited number of
184   samples. Second, once the agents’ reactions to payoffs are modeled, the control step employs MPC
185
      to steer the system towards a desirable outcome by optimizing specific objectives. The following
      subsections details these two steps.
186

189
      To model the controlled dynamics in (2) we extend the SIAR framework introduced in Sakos et al.
190   (2023), which was in turn motivated by recent results in data-scarce system identification (Ahmadi
191   & Khadir, 2023). SIAR relies
                                  on polynomial regression to approximate agents’ learning dynamics
192   of the form ẋ(t) = f x(t) based on a small number of potentially noisy observations x(tk ), ẋ(tk )
193   (typically, K = 5 samples) taken along a short burst of a single system trajectory. To ensure
194   the accuracy of the derived system model, SIAR searches for polynomial regressors that satisfy
195   side-information constraints native to game-theoretic applications, which serve as a regularization
196   mechanism.

200   field pi (x, ω). To do so, during the data collection phase, we assemble a dataset x(tk ), ω(tk ), ẋ(tk ),

253

254

255

285   5     E XPERIMENTS
286
287   In this section, we demonstrate the applicability and the performance improvements of the
288   SIAR-MPC framework compared to combining MPC with solutions obtained from SINDYc and
289   PINN, across various normal-form games of independent interest. The implementation of SINDYc
290
      follows the methodology described in Kaiser et al. (2018). For solving the sparse regression prob-
      lem, we employ a sequential least squares procedure, as detailed in the supplementary information
291
      of Brunton et al. (2016). For PINN, the method is straightforward: side-information constraints are
292
      integrated into the neural network during its training as terms in the loss function that penalize viola-
293   tions of the desired constraints. In our examples, we enforce RFI and PC as such constraints. Given
294   the limited number of training samples—in most cases, 5 samples—we are restricted to a simple
295   neural network architecture consisting of two hidden layers of size 5. This limitation in the neural
296   network’s expressivity is counterbalanced by the simplicity of the ground-truth update policies f
297   (which, in most cases, are polynomial). As activation functions we use the tanh function. Finally,
298   the side-information constraints are enforced using 2, 500 collocation points. Further details on the
299   construction of the loss function and the generation of collocation points can be found in Appendix
300   B.
301   We begin our demonstration with a well-known coordination game: the stag hunt. As coordina-
302   tion games correspond to non-cooperative setups where convergence to a socially optimal equi-
303   librium is desirable, the stag hunt game is a natural candidate for comparing the performance of
304   different system identification methods, combined with MPC, in guiding agents towards the game’s
305   optimal outcome. Next, moving away from the ideal landscape of coordination games, and into
306   the more challenging regime of two-player zero-sum games, we demonstrate the performance of
307
      the SIAR-MPC framework in achieving two primary objectives: steering possibly non-polynomial
      learning dynamics with non-vanishing regret towards a Nash equilibrium of the game; and steering
308
      a provably chaotic system. In addition to the detailed analyses presented in this section, we conduct
309
      simulations across a large and diverse set of settings to gain a statistically significant understanding
310   of each methods’ performance. The results of these simulations can be found in Appendix A.

385
386   The stag hunt game arguably provides an ideal landscape for the steering of game dynamics to a
387   Nash equilibrium. This is because of the existence of a socially optimal Nash equilibrium (the hunt
388   of a stag) that has a positive-measure basin of attraction. The class of zero-sum games, on the other
389
      hand, does not possess such merit, since in a zero-sum game, by definition, the player rewards sum
      up to zero independently of the strategies chosen by the players. To make matters worse, well-known
390
      game dynamics, e.g., the replicator dynamics, are known to exhibit undesirable behavior in zero-sum
391
      games such as Poincaré recurrence (Akin & Losert, 1984), or even chaos (Sato et al., 2002). Even
392   if we amuse ourselves by instead pondering the more relaxed notion of time-average convergence,
393   positive results in that area revolve around the notion of no-regret dynamics (Sorin, 2024), and the
394   steering of learning dynamics of non-vanishing regret is, to the best of our knowledge, unexplored.
395   In that regard, in the next couple of examples we demonstrate the performance of SIAR-MPC in
396   steering the log-barrier dynamics—well-known learning dynamics of non-vanishing regret—in the
397   matching pennies game and in the steering of chaotic replicator dynamics in an ϵ-perturbed rock-
398   paper-scissors (ϵ-RPS) game. In both cases, we demonstrate the SIAR-MPC framework is able to
399   steer the system towards the desired Nash equilibrium of the game.

472   6    C ONCLUSIONS
473
474   In this work, we introduced SIAR-MPC, a new computational framework that extends SIAR for sys-
475   tem identification of controlled game dynamics and integrates it with MPC for dynamic incentive
476   adjustments, aiming to steer game dynamics towards desirable outcomes with limited data availabil-
477   ity. Our results demonstrated that SIAR-MPC effectively steers systems towards optimal equilibria,
478   stabilizes chaotic and cycling dynamics. Comparative analysis showed that SIAR-MPC outperforms
479   alternative methods in data-scarce settings.
480   Future research can explore several potential directions. First, we intend to address a broader ques-
481   tion: Given the inherent limitations of game dynamics, where convergence to NE is not always
482   guaranteed, what are the necessary and sufficient conditions for achieving global stability in con-
483   trolled game dynamics? Second, we aim to extend our framework to encompass games beyond the
484   normal form, thereby expanding its applicability to a wider range of strategic interactions. Finally,
485   we plan to investigate the scalability of our approach by exploring the uncoupling assumption, which
      could potentially enable its application to larger systems involving numerous agents.
