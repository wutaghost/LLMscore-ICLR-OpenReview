009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we introduce AVCaps, an audio-visual captioning dataset that con-
012            tains separate textual captions for the audio, visual, and audio-visual contents of
013            video clips. The dataset contains 2061 video clips constituting a total of 28.8
014            hours. We provide up to 5 captions for the audio, visual, and audio-visual con-
015            tent of each clip, crowdsourced separately. Existing datasets focus on a single
016            modality or do not provide modality-specific captions, limiting the study of how
017            each modality contributes to overall comprehension in multimodal settings. Our
018
               dataset addresses this critical gap in multimodal research by offering a resource
               for studying how audio and visual content are captioned individually, as well as
019
               how audio-visual content is captioned in relation to these individual modalities.
020
               To counter the bias observed in crowdsourced audio-visual captions, which often
021            emphasize visual over audio content, we generated three audio-visual captions for
022            each clip using our crowdsourced captions by leveraging existing large language
023            models (LLMs). We present multimodal and crossmodal captioning and retrieval
024            experiments to illustrate the effectiveness of modality-specific captions in evaluat-
025            ing model performance. Notably, we show that a model trained on LLM-generated
026            audio-visual captions captures audio information more effectively, achieving 14%
027            higher Sentence-BERT similarity on ground truth audio captions compared to a
028            model trained on crowdsourced audio-visual captions. We also discuss the pos-
029
               sibilities in multimodal representation learning, question answering, developing
               new video captioning metrics, and generative AI that this dataset unlocks. The
030
               dataset will be freely available online.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The growth of multimodal large language models (LLMs) and multimedia content has created a
036   growing demand for datasets and methods capable of a comprehensive understanding of audio-visual
037   data Nagrani et al. (2022). In this context, captioning datasets play an important role in advancing
038   AI and machine perception research helping models learn to interpret and describe complex audio-
039   visual scenes. These datasets enable machines to generate meaningful descriptions, bridging the gap
040   between humans and machines, and are essential for advancing tasks like image or video captioning
041   Xu et al. (2015), multimodal scene understanding Li et al. (2022), and improving human-computer
042   interaction Baltrušaitis et al. (2019). Specifically, audio-visual datasets with accompanying captions
043   enable models to learn and interpret the rich, multimodal information in such data.
044   Multimodal perception models developed using captioning datasets have diverse real-world applica-
045   tions. For example, audio-visual captioning enhances accessibility by providing transcriptions and
046   descriptions for multimedia content, making it accessible to individuals with disabilities Mocanu
047   et al. (2019). They are also essential in automating subtitle generation Chen et al. (2023), improving
048   content searchability Dong et al. (2018), supporting educational tools Zhou et al. (2018), and video
049   analysis in security and surveillance Yuan et al. (2024).
050   Captioning datasets typically focus on descriptions for specific input modalities, such as audio,
051   image, or video. Image captioning datasets like MS COCO Chen et al. (2015), and Flickr30k Young
052   et al. (2014) provide textual descriptions for images that include various objects and people engaged
053   in everyday activities. These datasets enable the development of models that can generate textual
      descriptions for static visual content.

054
      Similarly, audio captioning datasets, such as AudioCaps Kim et al. (2019), Clotho Drossos et al.
055   (2020), and MACS Martın-Morató & Mesaros (2021) provide textual descriptions that capture the
056   acoustic scenes in audio clips. AudioCaps has captions for audio files from AudioSet Gemmeke
057   et al. (2017), a dataset for a general-purpose audio classification from YouTube. Clotho contains
058   captions for sounds occurring in nature such as water, birds, rain, etc., sourced from FreeSound
059   Font et al. (2013) while avoiding sounds such as music, speech, and sound effects. MACS provides
060   captions for audio files recorded in different European cities, from three acoustic environments -
061   airports, public squares, and parks.
062   While these datasets have significantly advanced the field of multimedia understanding, they con-
063   centrate on describing one input modality, making it challenging to model the relationships between
064   different modalities to get a complete context. Image captions emphasize objects present in the im-
065   ages describing spatial relationships and object properties. Audio captions contain descriptions of
066   sound sources, environments, and temporal patterns present in the audio. For instance, consider a
067   video clip of a dog barking at the door while a bell rings in the background. An image captioning
068   dataset might focus solely on the visual aspect, describing the scene as “A dog is standing near a
069
      door”, without acknowledging the audio elements. On the other hand, an audio captioning dataset
      might describe the sound as “A dog barking and a bell ringing” without any context of the visual
070
      scene. The lack of integration between the two modalities means that the complete context - “The
071
      dog is barking at the door because someone rang the bell” is not captured. This demonstrates the
072   need for datasets that can handle both modalities simultaneously to generate richer and more con-
073   textually accurate captions.
074
075
      Video captioning datasets, like MSR-VTT Xu et al. (2016) and ActivityNet Captions Krishna et al.
      (2017) offer captions that describe the content of video clips. MSR-VTT consists of 10000 video
076
      clips from various categories such as people, animation, TV shows, vehicles, etc., and 20 associated
077
      crowdsourced English captions per clip. ActivityNet Captions consists of video clips capturing
078   various human activities annotated with natural language captions. Although these datasets include
079   audio content as a part of the video clips, the primary focus of the descriptions is on the visual
080   content. Winter (2019) also presents a study of how the descriptions vary depending on the sensory
081   input and demonstrates visual as the dominant sensory input. Hence, current captioning datasets
082   hamper our ability to understand how multimodal models perceive and integrate information from
083   different modalities, and to quantify the extent to which each modality contributes to the overall
084   representation.
085   To address these challenges, there is a pressing need for a dataset that provides captions for audio,
086   visual, and audio-visual content for the same video clips. To this end, we designed a new audio-
087   visual captioning dataset AVCaps, that contains modality-specific captions for audio, visual, and
088   audio-visual content of the same video clips.
089
      In this paper, our contributions are threefold:
090
091          • We introduce a novel audio-visual captioning dataset AVCaps, that contains 2061 videos,
092            spanning a total duration of 28.8 hours, and includes crowdsourced captions for audio,
093            visual, and audio-visual content of the same video clips.
094
             • Since human annotators tend to focus more on visual elements than audio when describing
095            audio-visual content Winter (2019), we also present as part of the dataset, three audio-visual
096            descriptions for each clip generated by an LLM using the modality-specific crowdsourced
097            captions.
098
             • We develop audio-visual captioning and retrieval models on the AVCaps dataset and
099
               demonstrate how modality-specific captions can be used to enhance and evaluate the ability
100
               of these models to capture information from audio and visual modalities.

      2.1      DATA C OLLECTION
109
110   The first step involved selecting videos for the dataset. Our goal was to choose publicly available
111   videos that can be reused, redistributed, and encompass a broad range of topics. To achieve this, we
112   sourced the videos from the VidOR dataset Shang et al. (2019); Thomee et al. (2016). The VidOR
113   dataset comprises 10000 Creative Commons-licensed video clips from Flickr, featuring a variety of
114   scenarios such as indoor and outdoor family moments, musical performances, speeches, pets, and
115   babies. The VidOR dataset also contains annotations of 80 object classes with their spatiotemporal
116   bounding boxes and 50 categories of action and spatial predicates that describe the relationships
117
      between different objects within the videos.
118   We filtered this dataset to select videos with at least three object classes and a minimum duration
119   of 10 seconds to aid diverse and descriptive captions. After this filtering, we had 2176 videos for
120   further data annotation. We isolated audio and visual content from the selected videos, to provide
121   them as separate modalities for the annotators. Using Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT), we crowd-
122   sourced five captions per clip for the audio, visual, and audio-visual content in the selected clips,
123
      annotated by different workers. To ensure quality, we restricted the task to AMT workers with a 97%
      approval rate. Since we collected English captions, we limited the task to workers from English-
124
      speaking countries such as the USA, UK, and Australia. The workers were instructed to complete
125
      a Human Intelligence Task (HIT) by describing the input clip (audio, visual or audio-visual) using
126   a complete sentence with at least five words in the caption. They were also provided with some
127   example captions to assist them. The workers were compensated at a rate of $0.25 per HIT. In total,
128   4421 workers annotated the entire dataset. The instructions provided to the workers for annotating
129   different modalities are provided in Appendix B.
130
131   2.2      DATA C LEANING
132
133   We implemented a two-stage data cleaning process to ensure high data quality.
134
      Stage 1: Automated Error Correction
135
136   Following the crowdsourcing phase, each video clip was paired with 15 captions, 5 each for the
137   audio, visual, and audio-visual content. Despite limiting the tasks to workers from English-speaking
138   countries, a notable proportion of captions contained spelling and grammatical errors. To address
139
      these issues, we implemented an automated error correction step using the GPT-3.5 language
      model. Following the grammar correction prompt outlined in the OpenAI API documentation1 , the
140
      following template was employed:
141
142
                    You will be provided with statements.                Your task is to
143                 convert them to standard English.

                    crowdsourced caption 1
                    crowdsourced caption 2
146                 ..
147                  .
148                 crowdsourced caption n
149
150   After this error correction process, we observed a decrease in the vocabulary of the corrected cap-
151
      tions due to the removal of misspellings. The average length of the corrected captions increased,
      due to adding articles and determinants to the sentences. Additionally, readability indices Thomas
152
      et al. (1975) showed that the comprehension level required increased, from 7 years old to 9-10 years.
153
      Examples illustrating this error-correction process and its effects are provided in Appendix C.
154
155   Stage 2: Manual Relevance Screening
156   Next, we found that some captions were not relevant to the scenes, as the annotations were noisy.
157   For instance, one worker repeated the caption “I enjoy it greatly” 10 times and another worker
158   wrote “The voice is very nice and likable”, 19 times. Some workers copied the example captions
159   provided for reference. The audio modality captions were the most affected with a total of 427 HITs
160   completed using one of the example captions.

      2.3      T RAINING , VALIDATION AND TEST SPLITS
173
174   The dataset is split randomly approximately into 80%-10%-10% for training, validation, and testing,
175   respectively. The training set contains 1661 clips, while the validation and test set each consist of
176   200 clips. The data is split in such a way that all files in the validation and test splits have at least
177   three ground truth captions for audio, visual, and audio-visual content of the clip while all the files
178   in the training split have at least one caption for each category. The content of the videos in the
179   training split contains 73 unique VidOR object categories while the validation and test videos have
180
      49 and 48 respectively.

      In this section, we present various captioning and retrieval tasks realized with the AVCaps dataset.
303   Specifically, we build audio-visual captioning and retrieval models and show how modality-specific

308   3.1       C APTIONING
309
310   Captioning is the task of generating a natural language description for an input signal like an image
311   or audio. A typical deep-learning captioning model consists of an encoder and a decoder. The
312   encoder processes the input signal, extracts features, and creates a representation. The decoder then
313
      generates the caption in an auto-regressive manner using this encoded representation. For unimodal
      tasks like image or audio captioning, encoders are selected based on the input modality. ResNet
314
      He et al. (2016) or VGG Simonyan & Zisserman (2014) models are common for image feature
315
      extraction, while PANNs Kong et al. (2020) or VGGish Hershey et al. (2017) are used for audio.
316   With the advancements in natural language processing, transformers have become the gold standard
317   for decoders to generate textual descriptions.
318
319
      Audio-visual captioning: Using our AVCaps dataset, we developed an audio-visual captioning
      system. This model consists of two encoders - PANNs Kong et al. (2020) for the audio feature
320
      extraction and ResNet-3D Tran et al. (2018) for the visual feature extraction. We used pre-trained
321
      PANNs finetuned on the Clotho dataset Drossos et al. (2020) for the audio retrieval task as a part
322   of the DCASE 2023 challenge 3 , and for visual feature extraction we used the ResNet-3D model

422   3.2    R ETRIEVAL
423
      Retrieval is the task of identifying and returning relevant information based on an input query. For
424
      example, in text-based image retrieval, images from a database that corresponds to a textual query
425
      are returned. Similarly, retrieval tasks can be extended to return audio clips, audio-visual clips, and
426   so on. One common way in deep learning to build retrieval models is to have two encoders. A
427   textual encoder encodes the information in the query text and an audio or visual encoder encodes all
428   the audio or visual files in the database. Then the cosine similarity between the text representation
429   and all the audio or visual representations is calculated and the files with top similarity scores are
430   returned. To train these retrieval models, a contrastive training approach is adopted, wherein the
431   similarity between media files and their corresponding ground truth descriptions is increased while
      the similarity with the other textual descriptions and other media files in the database is decreased.

      4   P OSSIBILITIES WITH THE DATASET
487
488
      Representation Learning: Traditionally, multimodal representation learning has concentrated on
489   two modalities, such as image and text (e.g., CLIP) Radford et al. (2021) and audio and text (e.g.,
490   CLAP) Elizalde et al. (2023). Some studies, such as Wu et al. (2022) employ a two-step approach to
491   create a shared representation across audio, visual, and textual modalities. First, a model is trained
492   to align images and text in a shared space using an image-text dataset. The text/image encoder is
493   then frozen or fine-tuned to create a shared representation with audio using a separate audio-text
494   or audio-image dataset. A key disadvantage of this approach is the suboptimal alignment due to
495   the independent training phases and different datasets used in these phases. In contrast, our dataset
496   provides paired audio, visual, and audio-visual captions, enabling the development of a single-stage
497
      shared representation encompassing all three modalities. This approach has the potential to signif-
      icantly enhance performance in tasks such as video understanding, video classification, captioning,
498
      and retrieval.
499
500   Multimodal Question Answering: Using LLMs and modality specific captions, our dataset enables
501   the creation of multimodal question-answering datasets by forming questions and answers about
502   different aspects of the video content, such as sound events, visual actions, or combined audio-visual
503
      occurrence. We can then train models that provide contextually accurate responses about various
      aspects of video clips compared to previous approaches that often focus on isolated modalities.
504
505   Benchmarking and Metrics: This dataset establishes a new benchmark for evaluating video cap-
506   tioning models. With ground truth available for each modality, we can assess how effectively these
507   models capture and integrate information from both audio and visual inputs, enabling the develop-
508   ment of new metrics for video captioning. This capability is essential for advancing the state-of-the-
509
      art in multimodal scene understanding.
510   Rich Video Descriptions: Using captions from multiple modalities, incorporating additional meta-
511   data from the VidOR dataset such as spatiotemporal bounding boxes, object detection, and sound
512   event detection models, we can generate comprehensive and descriptive video summaries. Unlike
513   existing datasets that focus on a single modality, our dataset combines information from various
514   sources, providing a richer context for a more holistic understanding of the video.
515   Generative Modeling: State-of-the-art video generation models lack the capability to produce
516   videos accompanied by their corresponding audio. However, this dataset facilitates the training
517   of such models since it has ground truth captions for individual modalities. We can train models
518   to generate visual content for a given audio caption, and conversely, generate audio from a visual
519   caption. This capability significantly enhances the potential for multimodal content generation.
520
521   5   C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we presented AVCaps, an audio-visual captioning dataset consisting of 2061 video
524   clips, each paired with up to five human-annotated captions for the audio, visual, and audio-visual
525   content. To address the bias toward visual cues in crowdsourced audio-visual captions, we included
526   three audio-visual captions generated by large language models (LLMs) based on the ground truth
527   annotations. Using multimodal and crossmodal captioning and retrieval experiments, we demon-
528   strated the effectiveness of modality-specific captions for model evaluation. Our captioning experi-
529   ments showed that models trained on LLM-generated audio-visual captions capture audio informa-
530
      tion more effectively, achieving 14% higher Sentence-BERT similarity on ground truth audio cap-
      tions compared to models trained on crowdsourced audio-visual captions. In retrieval experiments,
531
      we found that audio-visual text queries improve recall@10 in audio retrieval by 7% and visual re-
532
      trieval by 5%, demonstrating the advantage of incorporating crossmodal information. Finally, we
533   discussed potential applications of the dataset in advancing multimodal learning, highlighting the
534   value of AVCaps for future research in this domain.

      Finally, we calculated if a sentence is grammatically correct by defining grammar rules. We defined
945   the rules such as there should be noun phrases, verb phrases, prepositional phrases, and a clause.
946   A clause is defined as a group of words that contains a subject and a predicate; we defined it as a

1002   E    OTHER EXPERIMENTS
1003
1004   In addition to the experiments presented in Section 3, we developed various other unimodal and
1005   crossmodal captioning and retrieval models that are presented in this section.
1006
       Audio captioning: The audio captioning model takes audio files cropped or padded for 2 minutes,
1007
       sampled at 16 kHz as inputs, and their corresponding ground truth captions as labels. Log-mel
1008   spectrograms with 64 mel bins are extracted and fed into the pre-trained PANNs model, producing
1009   an encoded representation R75×2048 , where 75 is the temporal dimension and 2048 is the feature
1010   dimension. This is transformed via a learnable linear layer to a 75x512 representation and passed
1011   to a 6-layer transformer decoder with an attention size of 512 that predicts the next token auto-
1012   regressively.
1013
       Visual captioning: Similarly, a visual captioning model was trained using muted video clips as input
1014   and ground truth captions as labels. The clips were cropped or padded to 2 minutes and sampled
1015   at 5 fps and visual features were extracted using a pre-trained ResNet-3D. It produces an encoded
1016   representation R75×512 , where 512 is the temporal dimension and 512 is the feature dimension that
1017   is passed to the decoder. The decoder architecture is identical to that used for the audio captioning
1018   model. Note that in both cases, the transformer decoders are trained from scratch. The results of the
1019   unimodal captioning experiments are presented in Table 12. It can be seen that the audio captioning
1020   model performs slightly better in the SPICE metric, while the visual captioning model shows better
1021   performance in ROUGE L and CIDEr.
1022   Crossmodal captioning: Next, we developed additional crossmodal captioning models. Specifi-
1023   cally, we trained models that generate visual captions using audio input and vice-versa. During the
1024   training phase, these models were provided inputs from one modality and captions from the other
1025   modality. During inference, the predicted captions are evaluated against the ground truth captions of
       the cross-modality. In Table 13, we present the performance of these crossmodal captioning models.
