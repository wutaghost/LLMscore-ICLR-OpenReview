009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Non-invasive brainwave decoding is usually done using Mag-
012            neto/Electroencephalography (MEG/EEG) sensor measurements as inputs.
013            This makes combining datasets and building models with inductive biases
014            difficult as most datasets use different scanners and the sensor arrays have a
015            nonintuitive spatial structure. In contrast, fMRI scans are acquired directly in
016            brain space, a voxel grid with a typical structured input representation. By
017            using established techniques to reconstruct the sensors’ sources’ neural activity
018
               it is possible to decode from voxels for MEG data as well. We show that this
               enables spatial inductive biases, spatial data augmentations, better interpretability,
019
               zero-shot generalisation between datasets, and data harmonisation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Real-time non-invasive neural decoding is typically done using sensor measurements, with models
025   translating their time series to useful predictions like which word is read (Duan et al., 2023) or
026   picture is seen (Benchetrit et al., 2023). Raw non-invasive sensor data resembles a static random
027   point cloud where each point has an associated time series, making the relation between sensor data
028   points unintuitive. This renders tasks like developing data augmentations, interpreting decoding
029   models, designing strong inductive biases, generalising between datasets, and combining them non-
030   trivial. Many common deep learning modalities, such as images, do not suffer from this as they have
031   a clear structure — they can be augmented by cropping, interpreted by masking regions, learnt using
032   hierarchical local feature detectors, and combined by rescaling. Combining datasets is especially
033
      important as deep learning typically performs best with lots of data (LeCun et al., 2015).
034   These challenges are evident in existing works. Défossez et al. (2023) learn to predict perceived
035   speech from magnetoencephalography (MEG) and electroencephalography (EEG) data, where de-
036   spite their impressive engineering they do not combine datasets or use data augmentations. They do
037   however use a spatial attention mechanism over the sensors’ locations, thereby using spatial sensor
038   space information. Jayalath et al. (2024) pre-train and fine-tune on different datasets, thereby requir-
039
      ing projecting their data into a learnt latent space which lacks explicit geometric structure. More-
      over, it requires fine-tuning for datasets not seen during training. Afrasiyabi et al. (2024) learn a
040
      latent cross-modality representation but require corresponding functional magnetic resonance imag-
041
      ing (fMRI) and MEG/EEG data. Wang & Ji (2022) and Wang et al. (2023) transform sensors’ time
042   series to construct better input representations but treat each sensor independently. While some
043   works use spatial data augmentations over sensors, these tend to be simple like swapping the left
044   and right hemispheres or masking random channels (Rommel et al., 2022).
045
      Other neural decoding modalities are amenable to more deep learning techniques as their inputs are
046
      spatially structured. fMRI scans are processed as 3D voxels, allowing the use of 3D convolutional
047
      networks (Mzoughi et al., 2020), data augmentations (Chlap et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2024), inter-
048   pretability (Zintgraf et al., 2017), and transfer learning by combining datasets (Wen et al., 2023).
049   Moreover, fMRI has a higher spatial resolution (∼ 1mm) than MEG and EEG (several millimetres).
050   However, its slow sampling speed (∼ 0.5Hz) and bulky equipment requirements make it difficult
051   to use for real-time decoding (Benchetrit et al., 2023). Invasive approaches have high spatiotempo-
052   ral resolutions and are spatially structured but require subjects undergoing surgery, thereby limiting
053   their use (Peksa & Mamchur, 2023). MEG and EEG can record brain signals at cognitively relevant
      timescales (on the order of ms) while being non-invasive. So far, these data are not typically decoded

054
      in voxel space, possibly due to this mapping between measurements and their spatial sources being
055   ill-posed and nontrivial (Mattout et al., 2006).
056
057   Still, approximate MEG and EEG source reconstruction is possible. This converts the sensor data
058
      to a 3D voxel grid of neural activity at different points in the brain, known as brain or source space.
      Being a regular grid with an underlying physical meaning source space has an inherent and intuitive
059
      spatial structure. This allows it to benefit from research on learning for 3D images (Maturana &
060
      Scherer, 2015) in general and medical imaging (Lu et al., 2019) in particular.
061
062   There is also a naturalness argument for why neural data should be analysed from a source instead
063   of sensor perspective. Although representation learning should make different input representations
064
      equivalent, in practice structured inputs yield better performance as they enable data augmentations
      (Park et al., 2019; Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019) and architectures with inductive biases (LeCun
065
      et al., 1995; Gilmer et al., 2017; Ho et al., 2020). In well studied modalities input representations
066
      that fit human perception tend to work best. Bitmap images are standard inputs in computer vision
067   and not spatial gradient information, which were prevalent in pre-deep learning handcrafted features
068   (Lowe, 2004; Dalal & Triggs, 2005). In speech processing log mel spectrograms — a logarithmic
069   scale that approximates the human auditory response — are the de-facto standard input represen-
070   tations (Choi et al., 2018; Park et al., 2019). On the other hand, text has complex dependencies
071   that make it difficult to design effective data augmentations, with state-of-the-art models often not
072   using any (Vaswani et al., 2017). Structured inputs are also easier to interpret — practitioners care
073   which brain regions play a role in decoding stimuli more than whether the most important sensor
074   was number 15 or 32. While neural activations are not perceived in a sensory sense, they describe
075
      an underlying biological process instead of a measurement thereof.
076   Decoding from MEG/EEG source space has been done before, but previous works are either im-
077   practical for real-time decoding or do not use deep learning. For example, Daly (2023) rely on
078   simultaneous fMRI recordings, Westner & King (2023) use only linear models and either simulated
079   or small datasets, and Westner et al. (2018) use only random forests. Still, their results are promising
080   — Westner et al. (2022) remarks that source reconstruction can induce denoising and for Westner &
081
      King (2023) decoding from sources outperforms sensors.
082   In this work we compare decoding in source to sensor space, with an emphasis on large-scale deep
083   learning. After tuning a preprocessing pipeline (section 2) we show that source space’ structure
084   can help decoding, enabling models with inductive biases (3.1), spatial data augmentations (4),
085   interpretability (5), zero-shot cross dataset generalisation (6), and learning from a combination of
086   datasets without requiring projecting to a shared learnt latent space (7), which is sometimes known
087
      as “harmonisation” (Cheng et al., 2024). We are unaware of other works that accomplish the last
      two points.

134   Source reconstruction usually uses a subject’s anatomical scans, known here as structurals, to better
135
      model the relation between the sensor measurements and their sources. We use the Armeni et al.
      (2022) dataset for tuning the pipeline as it has such scans and multiple recording sessions per subject,
136
      allowing single-subject decoding. It contains MEG measurements of people listening to stories
137
      in English. For tuning the pipeline we train on the first subject’s first session’s recording while
138   validating on the second session and testing on the third, each being 1-2 hours long.
139
140   The logistic regression classifiers showed several trends. For the sensor preprocessing a) lower
141
      highpass and lowpass frequencies tend to perform better, and b) the downsampling frequency and
      whether a notch filter is applied do not have a meaningful effect. Lowpass frequencies as low as
142
      10Hz were found to perform well, surprising given that meaningful auditory processing occurs at
143
      higher frequencies (Hyafil et al., 2015). On the other hand much of the auditory response is low
144   frequency, see Figure 4. Thus, as this is possibly due to logistic regression not having representation
145   learning and hence latching onto coarse features we chose to use a lowpass frequency of 48Hz for
146   the final pipeline, below the power line frequencies (50Hz for European and 60Hz for American
147   measurements) but higher than typical neural auditory processing (Hyafil et al., 2015).
148
      The source reconstruction parameters also exhibited some patterns. Tomographic (global) source
149
      reconstruction methods that assume correlated voxels (minimum norm (Hämäläinen & Ilmoniemi,
150   1994), sLORETA (Pascual-Marqui et al., 2002), and dSPM (Dale et al., 2000)) outperform a local
151   one which does not (LCMV beamformer (Van Veen et al., 1997)). Vector source estimates —
152   keeping the source activity’s 3 vector components — perform better than magnitude based ones
153   — taking only its magnitude. No typical covariance matrix, signal-to-noise ratio, or voxel size are
154   significantly better than any others. Voxel sizes of 10, 15, and 20mm had similar results, likely due
155   to MEG’s poor spatial resolution as larger voxels may yield a higher signal-to-noise ratio as a larger
156   volume is averaged over. Smaller voxels were not used due to memory constraints.
157   Given this, the best value was chosen for sensitive parameters while the rest were given typical
158   default values, yielding a standard MEG preprocessing pipeline (Gross et al., 2013) with a few
159   notable exceptions. The sensor data is lowpass filtered at 48Hz and downsampled to 150Hz to
160   reduce the amount of data while preventing aliasing by having the lowpass filter be about 50% less
161   than the Nyquist frequency. Although the covariance matrix’ form seems insignificant, later larger-
      scale experiments showed that using a diagonal covariance yields slightly better results. Vector

184   The final pipeline yields 400 − 900 voxels, with the exact number depending on the subject’s
185   anatomy. Although decoding from a variable number of voxels is possible, fixed-domain decod-
186   ing reduces information loss by not requiring domain-agnostic pooling. For multi-subject decoding
187
      source activity estimates are morphed into a standard template brain using both the original subject’s
      and the template’s anatomy, as per Avants et al. (2008). Usually this increases the number of voxels.
188
189   The template brain has ∼ 900 voxels with three components each, giving an input dimension of
190   ∼ 2700 ≈ 10× the number of sensors. Although MEG/EEG data is known to be low rank (Barysh-
191   nikov et al., 2004) and vector source estimates are assumed to have only two relevant components,1
192   we did not use dimensionality reduction techniques such as applying PCA to a voxel’s channels
193
      or grouping meaningful regions using parcellations (Eickhoff et al., 2018). This is to prevent in-
      putting biases that may or may not help but to have the models learn which information is relevant,
194
      as often works best (Sutton, 2019). Indeed, Table 2a shows that dimensionality reduction harms
195
      performance.

381   on one dataset on subjects from another. Here we test how well the inter-subject (Schoffelen) models
382   perform when evaluated on the single-subject (Armeni) data and vice-versa. Although all models
383   are trained on heard speech detection the Schoffelen data is for native Dutch speakers listening
384   to randomly shuffled Dutch words while Armeni subjects are native English speakers listening to

457   8   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
458
459   This work demonstrates source space’ utility as a non-invasive neural decoding input representation
460   instead of sensor data. Beyond simple performance improvements — either through enabling spatial
461   inductive biases or domain-specific spatial data augmentations — it allows using techniques that
462   decoding from sensors does not, namely model-agnostic cross-dataset evaluation and combination.
463
      Moreover, source space is more interpretable as it represents the object of interest, the brain, while
      sensor data is a proxy thereof.
464
465   Models that handle variable-size inputs, like transformers and graph neural networks, may carry
466   these benefits over to sensor space. We are unaware of any works that try this and our attempt
467   to train a graph attention network underperformed an MLP baseline. Computer vision still relies
468   on fixed domain models although enough data allows relaxing that requirement (He et al., 2015;
469
      OpenAI, 2024), perhaps neural decoding will follow suit.
470   Still, source space has some clear limitations, eg. a higher input dimensionality. Although this
471   was not a bottleneck here memory may become an issue when scaling to more subjects. Source
472   reconstruction results in additional preprocessing but this is a one-off computational cost. Finding
473   the right parameters for it is nontrivial, with our ablations being a step towards a standard pipeline.
474   Unlike Westner & King (2023) we do not find source space’ preprocessing to be prohibitively long
475
      as long as results are cached, with optimal batch sizes chiefly dictating a model’s training time. As
      sensor space in some cases has better performance out-of-the-box it might be preferred in simple
476
      applications.
477
478   Source space could allow combining not only different MEG datasets but different neural modalities
479   altogether, eg. EEG and fMRI. Given these domains’ limited data cross-modal learning could enable
480   valuable knowledge transfer.

845   Technical details. The ablations’ setup is similar to that used in the rest of the paper. Specifically,
846   sensor data for the source reconstruction ablations uses the final preprocessing parameters given
847   in Table 1. Both sensor and source data were standardised independently channelwise, with each
848
      session standardised independently and without any epoching, as detecting heard speech is a contin-
      uous task. The ablations’ MLPs have two hidden layers with width tuned to have 0.25M parameters.
849
      The batch size is 128, learning rate is 10−5 , and early stopping with a patience of 10 epochs and a
850
      minimum delta of 0.1% validation accuracy is used to terminate training. Test accuracies are given
851   for the model with the best validation accuracy throughout training. Parcels were made using the
852   Harvard-Oxford atlas, with each parcel having 12 features — the average, standard deviation, max,
853   and min of its voxels’ activities in each of the three axes.
854
      Results. Ablations for all preprocessing parameters are given in Tables 9 and 10. Small variations in
855
      the final pipeline’s accuracy, eg. 67.0% in Table 9a vs 67.1% in Table 9b, are due to random chance,
856
      with an estimated uncertainty of ±0.3%. As these differences seem sufficiently small to not change
857   the conclusions each ablation is run once.

       E     T ECHNICAL D ETAILS
1013
1014   Setup. All experiments were performed using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). The preprocessing
1015   was done using MNE (Gramfort et al., 2014), MNE BIDS (Appelhoff et al., 2019), and FreeSurfer
1016   (Fischl, 2012). Experiments were run on a variety of GPUs, with the biggest using an Nvidia A100.
1017   No parallelism was used for training.
1018
       Models. Models were trained using AdamW (Loshchilov et al., 2017). Hidden dimensions were
1019
       tuned to ensure models have the desired number of parameters. The SE blocks in the CNN have
1020   a reduction factor of 16 and use adaptive average pooling, with all other models having the same
1021   hidden dimension throughout their layers. One exception is the SECNN which has a pooling layer
1022   before two fully connected ones. The SECNN and GAT were chosen over similar variants, eg.
1023   a regular 3D CNN for the former and a graph convolutional network (Kipf & Welling, 2016) or
1024   simple graph convolution (Wu et al., 2019) for the latter, as they gave the best results. Positional
1025   embeddings were normalised to be in [−1, 1] along each axis. Hidden dimensions are tuned so
       models approximately have the desired number of parameters.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Benchmarking. Many papers use the standard error of the mean instead of standard deviation,
1027   thereby showing errors which are not indicative of the setting’s variance and are smaller when more
1028   seeds are used. We believe standard deviation is more appropriate here as it indicates a setup’s
1029   consistency. A ± indicates at least 3 random seeds. More were used when employing statistical
1030   tests or by accident.
1031
       We use probability of improvement (Agarwal et al., 2021) instead of statistical comparisons like
1032
       Welch t-tests as the former does not assume a normal distribution and is known to be robust under
1033   few samples. We found that Welch t-tests sometimes gave misleading results due to non-normal
1034   samples having a high variance. For example, there are 7 “Schoffelen only” subject 002 samples
1035   in Table 8 and 4 “Combined”, with all “Schoffelen only” samples being less than the “Combined”
1036   ones. Due to the latter having a high variance a Welch t-test gives a p-value of 0.11.
1037
       Data augmentations. Cube masking was done by randomly choosing two voxels in a grid and
1038
       masking all points between them.

1043   This work proposes an alternative input representation for neural decoding. Potential issues caused
1044   by advancing this technology are present here as they are in other works in the field, namely loss

       while others do not, and so on. The potential benefits exist here as well, such as allowing those with
