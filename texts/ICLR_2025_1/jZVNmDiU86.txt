009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this study, we investigate whether attention-based information flow inside large
012            language models (LLMs) is aggregated through noticeable patterns for long context
013            processing. Our observations reveal that LLMs aggregate information through
014            Pyramidal Information Funneling where attention is scattering widely in lower
015            layers, progressively consolidating within specific contexts, and ultimately focusing
016            on critical tokens (a.k.a massive activation or attention sink) in higher layers.
017            Motivated by these insights, we developed PyramidKV, a novel and effective KV
018            cache compression method. This approach dynamically adjusts the KV cache
019
               size across different layers, allocating more cache in lower layers and less in
               higher ones, diverging from traditional methods that maintain a uniform KV cache
020
               size. Our experimental evaluations, utilizing the LongBench benchmark, show
021
               that PyramidKV matches the performance of models with a full KV cache while
022            retaining only 12% of the KV cache, thus significantly reducing memory usage.
023            In scenarios emphasizing memory efficiency, where only 0.7% of the KV cache
024            is maintained, PyramidKV surpasses other KV cache compression techniques,
025            achieving up to a 20.5 absolute accuracy improvement on TREC dataset. In the
026            Needle-in-a-Haystack experiment, PyramidKV outperforms competing methods in
027            maintaining long-context comprehension in LLMs; notably, retaining just 128 KV
028            cache entries enables the LLAMA-3-70B model to achieve 100.0 Acc. performance,
029            matching that of a full KV cache.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023)
035   are integral to various natural language processing applications, including dialogue systems (Chiang
036   et al., 2023), document summarization (Fabbri et al., 2019a), and code completion (Roziere et al.,
037   2023). These models have recently been scaled up to handle long contexts (Fu et al., 2024; Ding
038   et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), with GPT-4 processing up to 128K tokens and
039
      Gemini-pro-1.5 handling 1M tokens. However, scaling LLMs to extremely long contexts naturally
      leads to a significant delay due to the quadratic computation of attention over long contexts. A
040
      common solution to mitigate such inference delays involves caching the key and value states (KV) of
041
      previous tokens (Waddington et al., 2013), with the trade-off of requiring extensive GPU memory
042   storage. For instance, maintaining a KV cache for 100K tokens in LLaMA-2 7B requires over 50GB
043   of memory, while a 2K context requires less than 1GB of memory (Wu et al., 2024).
044
045
      To tackle these memory constraints, recent studies have explored the optimization of KV caching,
      including approaches such as low-rank decomposition of the KV cache (Dong et al., 2024) or
046
      pruning non-essential KV cache (Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2023). Notably, it
047
      has been shown that maintaining merely 20% of the KV cache can preserve a substantial level of
048   performance (Zhang et al., 2024). Moreover, extreme compression of the KV cache for tasks of
049   longer contexts (e.g., retrieval augmented generation or RAG for short) can drastically improve
050   efficiency and further reduce resource use. However, questions about the universal applicability of
051   these strategies across all layers of an LLM remain open. (1) Are these KV cache strategies applicable
052   to all layers? (2) Is it computationally efficient to use the same KV cache size across layers as
053   previous studies have done? These considerations suggest a need for an in-depth, more nuanced
      understanding of KV cache optimization in LLMs.

      To examine these questions, we aim to systematically investigate the design principles of the KV
078
      cache compression across different layers, specifically tailored to the behaviors of the attention
079   mechanism. We first investigate how information flow is aggregated via attention mechanisms across
080   different layers in multi-document question answering (QA), a classic task involving long contexts.
081   Our analysis identifies a notable transition of attention distribution from a broad coverage of global
082   contexts to a narrow focus of local tokens over layers in LLMs. This pattern suggests an aggregated
083   information flow where information is initially gathered broadly and subsequently narrowed down to
084   key tokens, epitomizing the massive attention phenomenon. Our findings provide unique insights
085   beyond the previously documented “massive activation” (Sun et al., 2024) that very few activations
086   exhibit significantly larger values than others when calculating multi-head attention in LLMs and
087
      “attention sink” (Xiao et al., 2023) that keeping the KV of initial tokens will largely recover the
      performance of window attention.
088
089   Building on these insights on how information flows are aggregated through a pyramid pattern, we
090   design a novel and effective KV cache pruning approach that mirrors the geometric shape, named
091   PyramidKV. As shown in Figure 1, unlike the fixed-and-same length KV cache pruning common
092   in prior works (Zhang et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), PyramidKV allocates more KV
093
      cache to the lower layers where information is more dispersed and each KV holds less information
      while reducing the KV cache in higher layers where information becomes concentrated in fewer key
094
      tokens. To the best of our knowledge, PyramidKV is the first KV cache compression method with
095
      varied cache retention across layers, tailoring cache amounts to the informational needs of each layer
096   and paving the way for future research.
097
098   We conducted comprehensive experiments on LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) using 17 datasets across
099
      various tasks and domains with three backbone models (LLaMa-3-8B-Instruct, LLaMa-3-70B-Instruct
      and Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023)). The results show that PyramidKV preserves performance
100
      using just 12.0% of the KV cache (KV Cache size = 2048) on the LongBench benchmark and
101
      significantly outperforms other methods in extreme conditions, retaining only 0.7% of the KV cache.
102   Moreover, PyramidKV outperforms baseline models (H2O (Zhang et al., 2024), SnapKV (Li et al.,
103   2024), StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023)) across all tested cache sizes (64, 96, 128, 256), with
104   its advantages most pronounced at smaller cache sizes. In the Needle In A Haystack experiment,
105   PyramidKV effectively maintains the long-context comprehension in LLMs, outperforming than
106   competing methods. Remarkably, with PyramidKV, retaining only 128 KV cache entries allows the
107   LLaMa-3-70B-Instruct model to achieve 100.0 Acc. performance, matching the performance of a
      full KV cache.

      Interpretation of LLMs Prior research has shown that attention matrices in LLMs are typically
111
      sparse (Chen et al., 2024a; Xiao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), focusing disproportionately on a few
112   tokens. For instance, Xiao et al. (2023) identified an “attention sink” phenomenon, where maintaining
113   the Key and Value (KV) states of the first few tokens can substantially restore the performance of
114   windowed attention, despite these tokens not being semantically crucial. Similarly, Sun et al. (2024)
115   identified a “massive activations” pattern, where a minority of activations show significantly larger
116   values than others within LLMs. Interestingly, these values remain relatively constant across different
117   inputs and act as critical bias terms in the model.
118   Further explorations in this field reveal distinct patterns across various attention heads and layers.
119   Li et al. (2024) observed that certain attention heads consistently target specific prompt attention
120   features during decoding. Additionally, Wang et al. (2023) discovered that in In-Context Learning
121   scenarios, label words in demonstration examples serve as semantic anchors. In the lower layers
122   of an LLM, shallow semantic information coalesces around these label words, which subsequently
123   guide the LLMs’ final output predictions by serving as reference points. Recently, Wu et al. (2024)
124   revealed that a special type of attention head, the so-called retrieval head, is largely responsible for
125   retrieving information. Inspired by these findings that the attention mechanism exhibits varying
126
      behaviors across different layers, we discovered that “Massive Activation” does not consistently
      manifest across all layers in long context sequences; instead, it predominantly occurs in the upper
127
      layers. Additionally, we identified a novel trend of information aggregation specific to long-context
128
      inputs, which will be further explained in §3.
129
130
      KV Cache Compression There has been a growing interest in addressing LLMs’ memory con-
131
      straints on processing long context inputs. FastGen (Ge et al., 2023) introduces an adaptive KV cache
132   management strategy that optimizes memory use by tailoring retention tactics to the specific nature
133   of attention heads. This method involves evicting long-range contexts from heads that prioritize local
134   interactions, discarding non-special tokens from heads focused on special tokens, and maintaining a
135   standard KV cache for heads that engage broadly across tokens. SnapKV (Li et al., 2024) improves
136   efficiency by compressing KV caches via selecting/clustering significant KV positions based on their
137   attention scores. Heavy Hitter Oracle (H2O) (Zhang et al., 2024) implements a dynamic eviction
138   policy that effectively balances the retention of recent and historically significant tokens, optimizing
139   memory usage while preserving essential information. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023) enables
140
      LLMs trained on finite attention windows to handle infinite sequence lengths without fine-tuning,
      thus expanding the models’ applicability to broader contexts. LM-Infinite (Han et al., 2023) allows
141
      LLMs pre-trained with 2K or 4K-long segments to generalize to up to 200M length inputs while
142
      retaining perplexity without parameter updates.
143
144   While these approaches have significantly advanced the efficient management of memory for LLMs,
145   they generally apply a fixed KV cache size across all layers. In contrast, our investigations into the
146
      attention mechanisms across different layers of LLMs reveal that the attention patterns vary from
      layer to layer, making a one-size-fits-all approach to KV cache management suboptimal. In response
147
      to this inefficiency, we propose a novel KV cache compression method, called PyramidKV that
148
      allocates different KV cache budgets across different layers, tailored to the unique demands and
149   operational logic of each layer’s attention mechanism. This layer-specific strategy takes a significant
150   step toward balancing both memory efficiency and model performance, addressing a key limitation in
151   existing methodologies.

213   4.1   P RELIMINARIES AND P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
214
215   In an autoregressive transformer-based LLM, the generation of the i-th token requires that the
      attention module computes the query, key, and value vectors for all previous i − 1 tokens. To speed


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      up the inference process and avoid duplicate computations, the key and value matrices are typically
217   stored in the GPU memory. While the KV cache enhances inference speed and reduces redundant
218   computations, it can consume significant memory when dealing with long input contexts. To optimize
219   memory usage, a strategy called KV cache compression is proposed (Zhang et al., 2024; Xiao et al.,
220   2023; Li et al., 2024), which involves retaining only a minimal amount of KV cache while preserving
221   as much information as possible.
222
      In a language model with m transformer layers, we denote the key and value matrices in the l-th
223   attention layer respectively as K l , V l ∈ Rn×d , ∀l ∈ [0, m − 1] when encoding a sequence of n
224                                                                                                l
      tokens. The goal of KV cache compression is to seek two sub-matrices Ksl , Vsl ∈ Rk ×d from the
225                   l       l                         l
      full matrices K and V , given a cache budget k < n for each layer l ∈ [0, m − 1] while maximizing
226   performance preservation. That is, a language model with KV cache compression only uses Ksl and
227   Vsl in the GPU memory for inference on a dataset D, and obtains a similar result to a full model
228   according to an evaluation scoring metric, i.e., score(K l , V l , D) ≈ score(Ksl , Vsl , D).

      4.2     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
231
232   In this section, we introduce our method, PyramidKV, based on the pyramidal information funneling
233   observed across different layers in §3. PyramidKV consists of two steps: (1) Dynamically allocating
234   different KV cache sizes/budgets across different layers (§4.2.1); and (2) Selecting important KV
235   vectors in each attention head for caching (§4.2.2).
236
237   4.2.1    KV C ACHE S IZE /B UDGET A LLOCATION
238
239
      Previous work on KV cache compression (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023) often
      allocates a fixed KV cache size across LLM layers. However, as our analysis in §3 demonstrates,
240
      attention patterns are not identical across different layers. Particularly dense attention is observed in
241
      the lower layers, and sparse attention in higher layers. Therefore, using a fixed KV cache size across
242   different layers may lead to suboptimal performance. These approaches may retain many unimportant
243   tokens in the higher layers of sparser attentions while potentially overlooking many crucial tokens in
244   the lower layers of denser attentions.
245
      Thus, we propose to increase compression efficiency by dynamically allocating the cache budgets
246
      across layers to reflect the aggregated information flow based on attention patterns. Specifically,
247
      PyramidKV allocates more KV cache to the lower layers where information is more dispersed
248   and each KV state contains less information, while reducing the KV cache in higher layers where
249   information becomes concentrated in a few key tokens.
250
251
      Following the common practice in KV cache compression (Li et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023), we
      first retain the KV cache for the last α tokens of the input across all layers, as these tokens have
252
      been shown to contain the most immediate task-related information, where α is a hyperparameter,
253
      controlling the number of last few tokens being included in the KV cache. For simplicity, we call these
254   tokens “instruction tokens”, which is also referred to as “local window” in previous literature (Zhang
255   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023).
256
      Subsequently, given the remaining total cache budget k total = l∈[0,m−1] k l that can be used over
                                                                         P

259

260
      reflecting a monotonically decreasing pattern of important tokens for attention from lower layers to

      4.2.2    KV C ACHE S ELECTION
271
272   Once the KV cache budget is determined for each layer, our method needs to select specific KV states
273   for caching within each layer in LLMs. As described in the previous section, the KV cache of the
274   last α tokens, referred to as instruction tokens, are retained across all layers. Following SnapKV (Li
275   et al., 2024), the selection of the remaining tokens is then guided by the attention scores derived from
276
      these instruction tokens—tokens receiving higher attention scores are deemed more relevant to the
      generation process and are thus their KV states are prioritized for retention in the GPU cache.
277

      5.1.3    BASELINES
337
338   We compare PyramidKV with three baselines, all of which keep the same KV cache size across
339   different layers, with different strategies for KV cache selection.
340   StreamingLLM (SLM) (Xiao et al., 2023) is an efficient framework that enables LLMs trained
341   with a finite length attention window to generalize to infinite sequence length without any fine-tuning.
342   They propose StreamingLLM based on the attention sink phenomenon that keeping the KV of the first
343   few tokens will largely recover the performance of window attention. StreamingLLM is a competitive
344   method to solve long-context tasks. In our experiments, to be consistent with other methods, we
345   simply keep the KV cache of the last α tokens and the first k − α tokens, as suggested in the paper.
346
347   Heavy Hitter Oracle (H2O) (Zhang et al., 2024) is a KV cache compression policy that dynami-
348   cally retains a balance of recent and Heavy Hitter (H2) tokens. H2O keeps a fixed cache size of the
349   Key and Value matrix across Transformer layers. The selection process for the KV cache is driven by
350   attention scores, specifically utilizing the average attention scores from all queries across all tokens
351
      to guide the selection.
352
353
      SnapKV (SKV) (Li et al., 2024) automatically compresses KV caches by selecting clustered
      important tokens for each attention head. This method discerns the attention patterns of the Key and
354
      Value matrices using a localized observation window positioned at the end of the prompts. However,
355
      unlike H2O, SnapKV employs a more nuanced clustering algorithm that includes a pooling layer.
356   Additionally, SnapKV captures attention signals using patterns from a localized window (Instruction
357   Tokens), rather than aggregating attention across all queries, allowing for more targeted and efficient
358   compression.
359
360   FullKV (FKV) caches all keys and values for each input token in each layer. All methods are
361   compared to the FullKV simultaneously.

452   5.3.1    P YRAMID KV P RESERVES THE L ONG -C ONTEXT U NDERSTANDING A BILITY
453
454   We conduct the "Fact Retrieval Across Context Lengths" (Needle In A Haystack) experiment (Liu
455
      et al., 2023a; Fu et al., 2024), which is a dataset designed to test whether a model can find key
      information in long input sequences, to evaluate the in-context retrieval capabilities of LLMs when
456
      utilizing various KV cache compression methods. For this purpose, we employ LlaMa-3-70B-
457
      Instruct as our base, with context lengths extending up to 8k. We compared several KV cache
458   compression techniques (PyramidKV, SnapKV (Li et al., 2024), and H2O (Zhang et al., 2024)) at
459   cache sizes of 128 and full cache. The results, presented in Figure 4 1 . The results demonstrate that
460   with only 128 KV cache retained, PyramidKV effectively maintains the model’s ability to understand
461   short contexts, and shows only modest degradation for longer contexts. In contrast, other KV cache
462   compression methods significantly hinder the performance of LLMs. Notably, for the larger model
463   (LlaMa-3-70B-Instruct), PyramidKV achieves 100.0 Acc. performance, matching the results of
464   FullKV, thereby demonstrating its ability to preserve long-context comprehension with a substantially
465   reduced KV cache. We adopt the haystack setting of haystack formed from a long corpus for the
466
      Needle In A Haystack task as Wu et al. (2024).

784   Figure 5 and Figure 6 shows the attention patterns of one QA example over six different layers (i.e.,

789
      universality of the Pyramidal Information Funneling phenomenon across diverse model families. We
      hope this addresses your concern and underscores the generalizability of our findings.
790
791   Our analysis uniquely examines attention metrics across all transformer layers, from 0 to 30, leading
792   to the discovery of a key phenomenon we term Pyramidal Information Funneling.
793    Lee et al. (2024) conducted a limited investigation into attention patterns, focusing only on the lower
794   layer (layer 0) and a single upper layer (layer 18). While Lee et al. (2024) noted that attention
795   becomes more skewed in upper layers, it did not provide a fine-grained observation of attention
796   patterns across all layers. In contrast, our study reveals several novel findings:
797
798
799          • Localized Attention: We observe that attention progressively narrows its focus, targeting
800            specific components within the input sequence.
801          • Massive Attention Mechanism: In the upper layers, attention heavily concentrates on a
802            small set of critical tokens. Notably, these tokens are not limited to the leading positions,
803            as observed in Lee et al. (2024), but also appear at regular intervals across the sequence.
804            The discrepancy arises from differences in input settings, with Lee et al. (2024) identifying
805            massive attention only at the initial tokens.
806
      These insights motivated us to propose a token-selection method based on the highest attention scores
807
      in the upper layers, rather than solely relying on tokens from earlier positions.
808
809   To the best of our knowledge, Chen et al. (2024b) has not analyzed attention patterns across
      transformer layers.

844
845   Based on the pyramidal information funneling observed across different layers, PyramidKV consists
846   of two steps: (1) Dynamically allocating different KV cache sizes/budgets across different layers;
847
      and (2) Selecting important KV vectors in each attention head for caching as Figure 7.
848   Our decision to use an arithmetic sequence is driven by three key factors:
849
            • Alignment with Pyramidal Information Funneling Pattern: Empirical observations
850
              reveal a pyramidal information funneling pattern, where lower layers exhibit dispersed
851           attention while higher layers concentrate on fewer tokens. Inspired by this, we adopt the
852           arithmetic sequence design to align with this natural progression.
853
            • Superior Empirical Performance: Through extensive experimentation across diverse
854
              datasets, we compared various methods, including the arithmetic sequence and adaptive
855           approaches. Results consistently showed that the arithmetic sequence method outperformed
856           others.
857
            • Computational Efficiency: The arithmetic sequence method introduces minimal computa-
858
              tional overhead compared to adaptive approaches, which require dynamically computing
859           cache budgets across layers.
860
861   To perform KV cache eviction, we use torch.gather. Below, we outline the memory allocation and
862   release process of torch.gather:
863
            • Index Selection: Identify the positions of the elements to extract from the input tensor.


                                                      16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
              • Memory Location Calculation: Compute the specific memory locations of the elements to
865             be extracted using the strides of the input tensor across each dimension.
866
867           • Output Tensor Creation: Allocate memory to create a new output tensor and copy the
868             selected elements to their corresponding positions in the output tensor.
869           • Memory Management: Since torch.gather is not an in-place operation, it creates a new
870             tensor to store the results, while the memory of the original input tensor is released.
871
872   The speed-up offered by PyramidKV is complementary to that achieved through tensor parallelism and
873   pipeline parallelism, as these approaches are not mutually exclusive. PyramidKV can be seamlessly
874   integrated with both tensor parallelism and pipeline parallelism.

922   We keep the rotary embedding unchanged after tokens are removed, so that LLMs can still capture
923
      the exact position information even if the tokens are removed. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023)
      shows that rolling kv cache with the correct relative position is crucial for maintaining performance.
924
      This is because StreamingLLM is designed to mainly handle unlimited context sizes, where con-
925
      texts exceed the LLM’s fixed context length. Without changing the rotary embedding after token
926   removal, LLMs would receive rotary embedding of a non-monotonic position sequence. For ex-
927   ample, after the first KV cache compression, LLMs might receive the input position embedding as
928   [0, 1, 2, 3, 3096, 3097, · · · , 4096], and the position embedding of the generated sequences could be
929   [1005, 1006, 1007, · · · ]. The position sequence of [0, 1, 2, 3, 3096, . . . , 4096, 1005, 1006, 1007, · · · ]
930   is a non-monotonic sequence, which may negatively hurts the performance. In contrast, our targeting
931   settings will not process unlimited context size. For example, given a input sequence of 4012 length,
932   after KV cache compression, the position sequence would be [0, 4, 6, 16, · · · , 3927, 3987, 4012], and
933   the position sequence of the generated tokens would be [4013, 4014, · · · ]. By keeping the rotary
934
      embedding unchanged after the tokens are removed, the LLM avoids non-monotonic position se-
      quences, and the LLM can capture the exact position information even if the tokens are shifted. Our
935
      preliminary results show that rolling KV cache with the correct relative position will slightly decrease
936
      the performance.

1060                  tifying and leveraging the pyramidal information funneling phenomenon within attention
1061                  mechanisms. Through in-depth analysis, we observe that attention tends to disperse in
1062                  earlier layers and progressively concentrates on crucial tokens in higher layers. This insight
1063                  forms the foundation of our arithmetic decay strategy, ensuring that our method aligns more
1064                  naturally with these intrinsic patterns.

       With a small budget, our proposed method enables more effective allocation, better preserving useful

       We find that allocating/releasing/moving/accessing very small chunks of memory may cause ineffi-
1945   ciency and fragmentation in a naive implementation of PyramidKV at vLLM. As PyramidKV applies
1946   different allocation budgets for different layers. The top layers have less budget, while the bottom
1947   layers have more budget. The application of KV cache eviction with different budgets across layers
1948   at the standard paged attention frameworks (i.e., vLLM) is ineffective as it only reduces the cache
1949   size proportionally to the layer with the lowest compression rate, and all evictions beyond this rate
1950   merely increase cache fragmentation.
1951   However, the problem could be solved by adapting paged attention to page out cache on a per-layer
1952   basis. We expand the block tables of each sequence to include block tables for each layer of the
1953   cache so that they can be retrieved for each layer’s KV cache during attention without the use of fixed
1954   memory offsets.
1955
       The implementation of PyramidKV is orthogonal to multi-gpu setting at vLLM because vLLM
1956
       shards attention by head in tensor parallel, so the performance bonus would not change too much
1957   with tensor parallel or pipeline parallel. Each TP rank will have it’s corresponding heads of size
1958   (num_heads/tp_size). To help see the performance of PyramidKV implementation at vLLM
1959   compared with the vLLM dense attention backend at tensor parallel and pipeline parallel, we perform
1960   the experiment as Figure 20. We present the throughput comparison between PyramidKV vLLM

1963   Results show that the performance bonus remains similar with tensor parallel and pipeline parallel.
