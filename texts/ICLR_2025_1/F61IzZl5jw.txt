009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             Recent works have shown that diffusion models are able to memorize training
012             images and emit them at generation time. However, the metrics used to evaluate
013             memorization and its mitigation techniques suffer from dataset-dependent biases
014             and struggle to detect whether a given specific image has been memorized or not.
015             This paper begins with a comprehensive exploration of issues surrounding memo-
016             rization metrics in diffusion models. Then, to mitigate these issues, we introduce
017             S OLID M ARK, a novel evaluation method that provides a per-image memorization
018             score. We then re-evaluate existing memorization mitigation techniques and show
019
                that S OLID M ARK is capable of evaluating fine-grained pixel-level memorization.
                Finally, we release a variety of models based on S OLID M ARK to facilitate further
020
                research for understanding memorization phenomena in generative models.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have gained
026   prominence because of their ability to generate remarkably photorealistic images. However, they
027   have also been subject to scrutiny and litigation (Saveri & Butterick, 2023) owing to their abil-
028   ity to regurgitate potentially copyrighted training images. Additionally, commonly used datasets
029   (Schuhmann et al., 2021) have been shown to contain sensitive documents such as clinical images of
030   medical patients, whose recreation poses incredibly intrusive privacy concerns. As a result, recent
031
      works (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b; Carlini et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024; Kumari
      et al., 2023b) have looked to quantify, explain, and mitigate memorization in diffusion models.
032
033   Crucially, reliable and effective quantification of memorization requires sound metrics. Although a
034   few proposed metrics serve as powerful memorization indicators, there exist disagreements in terms
035   of how they should be applied (Chen et al., 2024). The typical way in which a given image is
036   declared to be memorized is if it is produced in a pixel-exact manner at inference time. However,
037
      such a generation can be challenging to induce, even if the training prompt is known, due to inherent
      stochasticity present in diffusion model inference. This problem is even harder in unconditional
038
      models, where there are no knobs to guide the generation towards a given target image. If such a
039
      generation is not observed, the user is not provided with any strong indication on whether the model
040   has knowledge of the image.
041
042   Memorization metrics usually consist of (i) some distance measure ℓ between a model generation
043
      and its training dataset1 and (ii) some scoring function that takes in a large number of these distance
      values (from many generations) and outputs a scalar metric. For example, a commonly used metric
044
      for memorization is the 95th percentile (scoring function) of SSCD similarities (Pizzi et al., 2022;
045
      Somepalli et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024), an embedding-based distance between each generation
046   and its nearest training image.
047
048   In this paper, we propose S OLID M ARK, an approach that allows for the precise quantification of
049
      pixel-level memorization. The basic idea is simple: S OLID M ARK augments each image with a
      grayscale border of random intensity (see Fig. 1). At evaluation time, we prompt the model to fill
050
      in only the image’s border in a task we call outpainting (as an analogy to inpainting). Since the
051
      pattern is randomized independently for each image, a correct reconstruction of the pattern’s color

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Detecting Memorization in Diffusion Models. Many works have aimed to detect memorization
111
      in diffusion models (Somepalli et al., 2023a; Carlini et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023b). A generative
112   model that memorizes data might be especially vulnerable to membership inference attacks, in which
113   the goal is to determine whether an image belongs to the original training set (Carlini et al., 2022;
114   Hu & Pang, 2021; Wen et al., 2023). One notable example of a membership inference attack is an
115   inpainting attack from Carlini et al. (2023), who show that a diffusion model’s performance on the
116   inpainting task significantly increases for memorized images.
117
118   Mitigating Unwanted Generations. A number of works (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Chen et al.,
119   2024; Wen et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024) have introduced methods to mitigate memorization in dif-
120   fusion models. These methods either perturb training data to decrease memorization as the model
121   trains or perturb inputs at test time to decrease the model’s chances of recalling memorized informa-
122
      tion. Although most mitigation techniques usually involve augmenting data with some type of noise
      (Somepalli et al., 2023b), other works attempt to alter generation trajectories using intuition about
123
      the causes for memorization (Chen et al., 2024). To prevent Stable Diffusion models from gener-
124
      ating unwanted outputs, various concept erasure techniques have been proposed (Gandikota et al.,
125   2023; Pham et al., 2024; Gandikota et al., 2024; Kumari et al., 2023a). Although these methods
126   were initially developed to erase broad concepts, they can also target specific images.
127
128   Image Watermarking.           Classically, image watermarking allows for the protection of intellec-
129   tual property and has been accomplished for years with simple techniques like Least Significant
130   Bit embedding (Wolfgang & Delp, 1996). Recently, more complex deep learning-based methods
131   (Zhu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019; Lukas & Kerschbaum, 2023) have been suggested. For gen-
132   erative models, watermarking allows developers to discreetly label their model-generated content,
133   mitigating the impact of unwanted generations by increasing their traceability. Some works attempt
134   to fine-tune watermarks into existing diffusion models (Zhao et al., 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023;
135   Xiong et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
136
137   Needle-in-a-Haystack Evaluation for LLMs. Some recent works (Fu et al., 2024; Kuratov et al.,
138   2024; Wang et al., 2024; Levy et al., 2024) have used Needle-in-a-Haystack (NIAH) evaluation
139
      (Kamradt, 2023) to test the long-context understanding and retrieval capabilities of Large Language
      Models (LLMs). In this test, a short, random fact (needle) is placed in the middle large body of text
140
      (haystack). This augmented corpus is passed into the model at inference. Subsequently, the model
141
      is asked to recall the needle; by changing the size of the context window and shifting the needle
142   around, testers are able to evaluate the in-context retrieval capabilities of LLMs. If the model is
143   able to successfully retrieve the needle from the haystack with a high consistency, developers can be
144   more confident that it will be able to recall specific information from large context windows. Similar
145   to how NIAH evaluation takes a large context window and injects a small, unrelated phrase as a key,
146   we inject our training images with scalar keys using a small, unrelated border.

      Types of Memorization. Memorization in diffusion models can usually be classified into either
151
      pixel-level or reconstructive. Pixel-level memorization (Carlini et al., 2023), is identified by a near-
152
      identical reconstruction of a particular training image. That is, even if a generation contains recre-
153   ations of certain objects or people from the training data, a given generation would only be consid-
154   ered reflective of pixel-level memorization if the full image was almost entirely identical to a specific
155   training image. In this sense, the process of recovering a pixel-level memorized image is analogous
156   to extracting a training image from the model. Alternatively, reconstructive memorization repre-
157   sents a more semantic type of data replication. It is identified by the replication of specific objects
158   or people found in training images, even if the generation in question has a high pixel distance from
159   all training images (Somepalli et al., 2023a).
160
161   Measuring Memorization.        Neither pixel-level nor reconstructive memorization have precise
      mathematical definitions, making it rather difficult to declare whether or how strongly a training


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      image is memorized. Instead, when constructing metrics, the prior works attempt to construct math-
163   ematical measures for a given generation’s similarity to the model’s training set. These measures,
164   in turn, can identify memorizations when they occur at generation time. Specifically, for a training
165   dataset X and a generation x̂0 , researchers will either use some distance function ℓ(x̂0 , X), with
166   lower values indicating a higher likelihood of memorization, or a similarity function σ(x̂0 , X),
167   with higher values indicating a higher likelihood of memorization. After collecting these values for
168   a large number of generations, they are converted into an overall score for a model: for example, the
169   95th percentile of all similarities is a common scoring function (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Chen et al.,
170   2024). Past works also track the overall maximum similarity value (Chen et al., 2024). Notably,
171
      Carlini et al. (2023) track the proportion of generations with distances under a certain threshold,
      defined as “eidetic” memorization. We use similar language, which we define in Definitions 1, 2.
172
173   Definition 1 (Eidetic Metric). A metric that counts the number of distances ℓ below a threshold δ.
174   Definition 2 (Eidetic Memorization). A training image x is said to be (ℓ, δ)-eidetically memorized
175   if the respective model returns a generation x̂0 where ℓ(x̂0 , x) ≤ δ.

      Motivation. Performance on inpainting tasks significantly increases for memorized images (Car-
      lini et al., 2023; Daras et al., 2024). Therefore, we choose inpainting as the foundation of our
279
      method. This task also stands out because of its ability to naturally function as a key-query mecha-
280
      nism: by masking out part of a training image, we can provide the unmasked portion to the model
281   as a ‘query’ and ask it to recall the ‘key’ (the masked portion) from memory. Yet, two issues need
282   consideration:
283
284
      First, with inpainting, the key is almost definitely semantically related to the query, meaning the
      model still has a good chance to infer the masked portion of an unseen image. Additionally, the
285
      amount of useful information in the unmasked portions of different images may vary significantly,
286
      making it difficult to develop a general baseline for the model’s performance on an unmemorized
287   image. That is, it would be harder to inpaint a memorized complex image than certain unmemorized
288   simple images. Second, since the key for inpainting is essentially a smaller image, the problems with
289   earlier distance metrics could just propagate. For example, relatively accurate inpaintings might still
290   produce high ℓ2 distances for various reasons.
291
292   Method Structure.       To solve these issues, we assign a random scalar key to each image and
293   embed it as the intensity of a grayscale border around that training image. By training the model
294   on these augmented images, we teach it to output the correct grayscale intensity in the borders of
295   an image, if memorized (see Fig. 1). Since the keys and queries are unrelated, the model outputs
296   random grayscale borders from the distribution of the training keys for an unmemorized image.
297   At evaluation time, we prompt the diffusion model to outpaint the border (key) for a training image
298   using the training caption as the text prompt and evaluate its accuracy with a scalar distance function
299   between the grayscale intensities. This strategy solves both of our previous issues: First, since the
300   key is unrelated to the query, we minimize the probability of inferring the key by chance. Second,
301   since our key is a scalar, we can directly use a scalar distance function between keys (grayscale
302   border intensities) instead of using a pixel distance function. We refer to this distance as ℓSM (SM
303
      = S OLID M ARK). We provide pseudocode and explain all of S OLID M ARK’s hyperparameters in
      Appendix Section C.

      5.2   H OW F INE -G RAINED IS S OLID M ARK ?
356
      In order to understand the cues the model uses to construct memorized borders, we evaluate whether
357   the information the model utilizes is based on the semantics of the image or on more fine-grained
358   pixel-exactness. We evaluated changes in reported memorization as a response to small perturba-
359   tions applied to the query image. To do this, we augmented LAION-5K with S OLID M ARK’s borders
360   and finetuned SD 2.1 on the augmented dataset. At evaluation time, we applied different augmen-
361   tations like cropping, rotation, or blurring to the query image and observed changes in the model’s
362   memorization performance. Examples of these augmentations are in Figure 5.2 with implementation
363   details in Appendix Section G. Overall, our results in Table 5 show that even minor perturbations
364   to query images significantly disrupt the model’s ability to recall the border color, especially when
365
      the required accuracy δ is small. These changes are not semantically meaningful and are sometimes
      barely visually perceptible. For this reason, we classify S OLID M ARK’s reported memorizations as
366
      instances of fine-grained pixel-level memorization.

480   Limitations. By the nature of the difficulty of the setting, our method may not report memoriza-
481   tions that are not strong enough to capture the key. Additionally, our evaluation method has a false
482   positive probability based on the chance of an unmemorized color randomly fitting to the key of a
483   specific image. Additionally, S OLID M ARK may struggle with accurately reporting memorization
484   caused by excessive exact duplication. For this reason, we encourage its use in tandem with other
485   metrics. For an in-depth guide on how we recommend choosing a metric for a specific use case, see
      Appendix Section K.

492
      We introduce S OLID M ARK as a non-intrusive framework that can help developers evaluate and

      study memorization in their models. With our recommendations for how memorization metrics

744   We adapt the inpainting algorithm from (Lugmayr et al., 2022), which originally involves masking
745   in a noised version of the known portion of an image at every timestep t of the diffusion process. The
746   diffusion process is thus run as normal for the unknown portion of the image. However, this kind
747   of remasking would be impractical to do in the latent space in which LDMs operate. Instead, we
748   chose to decode the latent (with decoder D) every s = 10 steps, apply this remasking, and re-encode
749   the latent (with encoder E). This allows us to achieve a similar effect to RePaint without too much
750
      computational overhead. Algorithm 1 describes our adapted outpainting algorithm for a model ϵθ to
      outpaint an image x with conditional (prompt) embedding c and mask m.

869
870   We evaluated Gaussian Noise at Inference (GNI), Random Token Replacement and Addition (RT),
871
      Caption Word Repetition (CWR), and Random Numbers Addition (RNA). GNI adds a small amount
      of random noise to text embeddings, usually from a distribution of N (0, 0.1). To tune this method,
872
      we increased the magnitude of the perturbations in order to reduce memorization further at the cost
873
      of adherence to the conditional prompt. RT randomly replaces tokens in the caption with random
874   words and adds random words to the caption. On each iteration, RT has a chance to randomly replace
875   each individual token in the prompt; this method was tuned by changing the number of iterations.
876   CWR randomly chooses a word from the given prompt and inserts it into one additional random
877   spot in the prompt. RNA, at each iteration, randomly adds random numbers in the range {0, 106 }
878   to the prompt, hoping to perturb the prompt without changing its semantic meaning. Similar to RT,
879   CWR and RNA were tuned by changing the number of iterations.

      K    H OW TO C HOOSE A M ETRIC
919
920
      Given the outlined qualities of a stable metric, we suggest that eidetic scoring always be used,
921   regardless of the choice of distance function. A few values of δ should be chosen and tracked
922   simultaneously, especially if a memorization reduction technique is being applied (to give an idea
923   of how fine-grained the changes in memorization are).
924
      If reconstructive memorization is to be tracked, one should first consider the training dataset. If
925
      a large dataset of natural images such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022) was used to train
926
      the model, then, following the literature, SSCD similarity is likely the most consistent and precise
927   metric. If a smaller or more niche dataset is being used, then the optimal choice of distance function
928   is a comparatively unexplored question. Intuitively, two suggestions could be to pretrain a self-
929   supervised model on the dataset or to finetune SSCD on the dataset. Whichever approach is chosen,
930   it is important to evaluate samples by hand and ensure that the metric is sufficiently specific and
931   sensitive.
932
      For pixel-level memorization, one might feel inclined to only use an ℓ2 -based metric because of
933   their simplicity. This approach is problematic, though, as ℓ2 -based metrics are not robust against
934   small shifts in pixel space and tend to report false positives with their biases towards monochro-
935   matic images. We instead recommend that S OLID M ARK be used in this situation. If the dataset
936   in question contains lots of exact duplication, then an ℓ2 -based metric should be used in tandem
937   with S OLID M ARK while manually validating the ℓ2 metric’s reported memorizations. This way, no
938   memorizations will be missed, but the evaluation will remain fine-grained.
