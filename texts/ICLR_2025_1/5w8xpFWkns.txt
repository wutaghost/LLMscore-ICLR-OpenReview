011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding the neural code requires identifying different functional units in-
014            volved in the neural circuits. One way to identify these functional units is to solve
015            a neuron type classification problem. For decades, current-clamp electrophysiol-
016            ogy recordings have provided the means to classify the neurons based on subtle
017            differences in action potential shapes and spiking patterns. However, significant
018
               variations in neuronal type definitions, classification pipelines, and intrinsic vari-
               ability in the neuronal activities make unambiguous determination of neuron type
019
               challenging. Previous solutions to this electrophysiology-based cell type classi-
020
               fication problem consisted of dimensionality reduction juxtaposed with cluster-
021            ing using hand-crafted action potential features. Recent discoveries have allowed
022            genetics-based cell-type classifications, which have fewer ambiguities, but they
023            are less practical in vivo and have even lower throughput. Leveraging the unprece-
024            dented ground truth data published in the Allen Institute Cell Types Database,
025            which contains anatomical, genetic, and electrophysiological characterizations
026            of neurons in the mouse neocortex, we construct a robust and efficient convo-
027            lutional neural network (CNN) that successfully classifies neurons according to
028            their genetic label or broad type (excitatory or inhibitory) solely using current-
029
               clamp electrophysiology recordings. The CNN is configured as a multiple-input
               single-output network consisting of three subnetworks that take in the raw time
030
               series electrophysiology recording as well as the real and imaginary components
031
               of its Fourier coefficients. Our single pipeline method is fast and streamlined
032            while simultaneously outperforming a previous method. Furthermore, our method
033            achieves classification with more classes using only a single current-clamp time
034            series trace as the input. This end-to-end convolutional neural network-based clas-
035            sification method removes the need for hand-crafted features, specific knowledge,
036            or human intervention for quick identification of the neocortical cell type with
037            high accuracy, enabling interpretation of experimental data in a bias-free manner
038            and understanding of a much broader scientific context.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   The neuronal type classification problem has been present in neuroscience since Ramón y Cajal’s
044   presentation of the neuron doctrine, which highlighted the ample diversity of neurons. Neurosci-
045   entists hypothesized that morphological differences play a functional role in neural circuits. This
046   intuition was extended to the investigation of the differences in neuronal activity with the appear-
047   ance of the current clamp technique, which allowed observations of various action potential shapes
048   and patterns in neurons.
049   Features such as action potential (AP) threshold and frequency, full width at half maximum (FWHM)
050   of an action potential (AP width), or afterhyperpolarization values and their common ratios in subse-
051   quent action potentials in a train, which are easily distinguishable by a person, aimed to describe the
052   differences among the observed variabilities in neuronal activities. Although no two neurons have
053   the same activity, these hand-crafted features were used to define the electrophysiological types of
      neurons (Beierlein et al., 2003; Nowak et al., 2008). Due to the intrinsic neuron-to-neuron variabil-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ities and the lack of established features that definitively separate neuron types, no single pipeline is
055   sufficient enough for unambiguous classification.
056
057   Several electrophysiology-based classification pipelines including methods that consider more
058
      features and ones that focus only on a single neuronal subpopulation coexist today
      (Petilla Interneuron Nomenclature Group; PING and others, 2008; Markram et al., 2004). Previous
059
      solutions to this cell type classification problem were comprised of a combination of dimensional-
060
      ity reduction and clustering using calculated action potential features and cell morphology. These
061   approaches suffered in classification accuracy as they relied on AP shape, spiking pattern, or cell
062   shape parameters that span a continuous feature space, which often do not have clear separation
063   boundaries.
064
      In addition to the aforementioned classification method based on morphology and electrophysiol-
065
      ogy, one based on the genetic makeup of neurons appeared recently in systems neuroscience (Tasic
066
      et al., 2018). A vast majority of neurons can be clustered according to their genetic encoding charac-
067   teristic of the group proteins. This gene-based consideration of neuron type allows a less ambiguous
068   classification pipeline. The most commonly used genetic types of neurons are neuron-derived neu-
069   rotrophic factor (Ndnf), parvalbumin (Pvalb), somatostatin (Sst), and vasoactive intestinal peptide
070   (Vip) interneurons, and excitatory (Exc) neurons that are predominantly pyramidal cells.
071
      Furthermore, the emergence of genetically modified transgenic lines of animals, where cells of a
072
      selected genetic type can be marked by a fluorophore, enabled studies relating electrophysiological
073   neuronal activity to the genetic type of neurons (Taniguchi et al., 2011). Attempts to characterize the
074   electrophysiological features of specific genetic types of neurons revealed that the majority of Pvalb
075   interneurons correspond to Fast Spiking (FS) electrophysiological cell type, but both Vip and Sst
076   interneurons as well as excitatory neurons can appear as Regular Spiking (RS) or Low Threshold
077   Spiking (LTS) types (Contreras, 2004). Therefore, we can conclude that there does not exist a
078   clear mapping from one classification scheme to another, partially due to existing methods’ low
079   throughput and differences in definitions and experimental pipelines.
080   To address the challenges presented by the above neuron type classification problem and to im-
081   prove on the existing classification pipeline architectures, a robust and efficient convolutional neural
082   network (CNN) that successfully classifies neurons according to their genetic label or broad type
083   (excitatory or inhibitory) solely using short snippets of current-clamp electrophysiology recordings
084   has been developed. The method presented in this paper relies on the ground truth data published
085   in the Allen Institute Cell Types Database, which contains anatomical and morphological descrip-
086   tions, genetic types, and electrophysiology features of thousands of neurons in the mouse neocortex
087
      (Gouwens et al., 2019). We use this open access database, which is one of the flagship initiatives of
      the Allen Institute for Brain Science, to obtain the genetic type label of a given neuron, bypassing
088
      supervised definitions of all signal features. The following sections detail the related state-of-the-
089
      art methods, the specifications of our novel deep neural network-based neuron type classification
090   method, as well as the results obtained using our custom CNN architecture.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096
097   Gouwens et al. (2019) built and made publicly available the Allen Institute Cell Types Database.
098   From this database, 17 electrophysiological, 38 morphological, and 46 morpho-electric neuron types
099
      were identified using a custom classification pipeline. The authors employed biocytin-based neu-
      ronal reconstruction to extract morphological features and used raw current-clamp electrophysi-
100
      ology recordings of cells from the mouse visual cortex in vitro as inputs for electrophysiological
101
      features. After computing handcrafted single action potential features including action potential
102   height, threshold, upstroke speed, and downstroke speed, as well as features corresponding to action
103   potential trains, such as interspike intervals and spiking frequency, principal component analysis and
104   t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding techniques were applied to project the high-dimensional
105   electrophysiological feature space into two dimensions. With clustering, the authors were able to
106   identify 17 electrophysiological neuron types, 4 of which were classified as excitatory subtypes and
107   13 of which were inhibitory. The 13 inhibitory subtypes were further mapped to the four inhibitory
      interneuron types based on their genetic tags: Sst, Vip, Pvalb, and Ndnf.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Ghaderi et al. (2018) successfully developed a semi-supervised technique to classify neuron types
109   using limited in vivo electrophysiology recordings data. The authors considered only 3 types of
110   neurons: excitatory pyramidal (Pyr) cells, parvalbumin positive (Pvalb) interneurons, and somato-
111   statin positive (Sst) interneurons from layer 2/3 of the mouse primary visual cortex. After extracting
112   single spikes, they extracted discriminative action potential features by computing the Discrete Co-
113   sine Transform of the recorded electrophysiology traces. Principal component analysis and fuzzy
114   c-means clustering were then performed, and neurons were subsequently classified using a minimum
115   distance classifier. The authors achieved accuracies of 91.59 ± 1.69, 97.47 ± 0.67, and 89.06 ± 1.99
116   for Pvalb, Pyr, and Sst, respectively, which yielded an overall accuracy of 92.67 ± 0.54%. This
117
      classification algorithm pipeline was further applied to the in vitro data from the Allen Institute Cell
      Types Database containing Pvalb, Sst, 5HT3a, and Vip genetic types. Testing on a dataset comprised
118
      of a pool of 50 neurons where multiple electrophysiology traces have been recorded for each neuron,
119
      the authors achieved accuracies of 93.57 ± 0.59%, 89.15 ± 0.63%, 81.69 ± 0.56%, 79.23 ± 1.38%,
120   and 77.02 ± 0.91% for Pvalb, Sst, Vip, 5HT3a, and Pyr, respectively.

123   3     M ETHODS
124
125   In this paper, we present a neuron type classification technique based on a simple convolutional
126   neural network (CNN) architecture. Using in vitro current-clamp electrophysiology recording traces
127
      of neurons in the mouse neocortex, the CNN is configured as a multiple-input single-output network
      consisting of three subnetworks. The first subnetwork takes in a portion of the raw time series
128
      recording that is 50 ms in duration and contains at least one action potential. The real and imaginary
129
      components of a trace’s Fourier coefficients are fed into the second and third subnetworks of the
130   CNN, respectively. A graphical representation of the CNN architecture is shown in Figure 3.

      3.1   DATASET
149
      We use data collected from 1947 cells in the Allen Institute Cell Types Database (Gouwens et al.,
150   2019). Of these, we omitted 81 cells containing only morphological features and lacking electro-
151   physiology recordings, and only the remaining 1866 cells containing electrophysiology recordings
152   were used to build our training, validation, and test sets. Every one of these 1866 cells was ob-
153   tained from transgenic animal lines, and thus is associated with a genetic label (Exc, Ndnf, Vip,
154   Sst, and Pvalb). Each neuron contains approximately 50 electrophysiology trace recordings that are
155   responses to multiple current-clamp stimuli including short square, long square, ramp, and noise. Of
156   these traces, we only considered responses to the short square stimulus, which are 3 ms in duration,
157   which is just long enough to induce a single action potential. Each of these recordings is collected
158
      at either 200 kHz or 50 kHz sampling rate. Because each neuron is recorded at a different stimulus
      level, we only consider the traces that contain an action potential.
159
160   To obtain the most useful information about the neuron type being assessed, the type of classification
161   task that our architecture solves can be dictated by the needs of a neurophysiologist. One task
      is to distinguish neuronal activity coming from an excitatory (Exc) or an inhibitory (Inh) neuron.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      This task is less informative, but provides explanation useful for analyzing electrophysiological
163   recordings. The more interesting task is to discriminate among the 5 genetic types (Pvalb, Sst, Vip,
164   and Ndnf inhibitory interneuron types and Exc neuron type), which constitute the broader excitatory
165   and inhibitory categories. Reliable and accurate classification among the 5 genetic types is highly
166   valuable to the neuroscience community. We train our network to perform the two aforementioned
167   tasks.

169   3.2   P REPROCESSING
170
171   One of the main objectives of our approach is to remove as much supervised overhead in the data
172   processing stage of the neuron type classification pipeline as possible. The only preprocessing steps
173   we perform on the raw time series data is removing excess temporal portions of recordings that do
174
      not provide useful information. We are only interested in the portion of the recording containing an
      action potential, so only 50 ms of recording, 25 ms before and 25 ms after the onset of the short
175
      square stimulus, was considered. The 25 ms of pre- and post-stimulus time duration was chosen
176
      to ensure that potential discriminative features that may be present before or after the onset of the
177   stimulus would be captured by the representation learning performed by our convolutional neural
178   network. The 25 ms of post-stimulus time guarantees that a single action potential has returned to
179   its resting membrane potential after depolarization and hyperpolarization.
180
      We also take the fast Fourier transform of these 50 ms time series traces. The real and imaginary
181
      components of the resulting Fourier coefficients, in addition to the corresponding time series traces,
182
      are used as inputs to the subnetworks of our convolutional neural network architecture.
183
184   For model selection and performance, we divide the aforementioned dataset collection from the
185   Allen Institute Cell Types Database based on the unique cell identification numbers. The ratio
186
      between the training and validation sets was fixed at 8:2. Once the best generalized performing
      model was identified, we independently split the dataset again based on the unique cell identification
187
      numbers. Eighty percent of the data were reserved for training and the remaining 20% were set
188
      aside as the test set data. The test set was further split as follows: 80% test and 20% validation. The
189   validation set was used to tune the network hyperparameters. This dataset separation by cell prevents
190   overfitting, and provides both significant advantage and improvement to the existing methods. Due
191   to natural cell-to-cell variations, basic action potential features like resting membrane potential can
192   vary considerably among cells of the same genetic type. We therefore use and report the maximum
193   validation accuracy we obtain over 100 epochs.
194
195   3.3   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
196
197   We use a multiple-input single-output convolutional neural network (CNN) for training. To remove
198   the need for handcrafted features, our deep neural network uses a one-dimensional convolutional
199   neural network as a feature encoder and employs dense layers to output class predictions. The
200
      standard one-dimensional CNN encoder is implemented using PyTorch. The encoder contains 6
      convolutional layers, and each layer is passed through a Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation
201
      function. Batch normalization introduced by Ioffe & Szegedy (2015) is applied to each activated
202
      layer. The exact specifications of each layer is shown in c) of Figure 3.3.
203
204   For training, we employ the Adam optimizer—with the learning rate set to 10−3 and the `2 regular-
205   ization parameter set to 10−5 —to minimize the cross entropy loss with sum reduction (Kingma &
206
      Ba, 2014). The initial weights were randomly generated.
207   An 8:2 ratio training-validation data set split was used to select the optimal network model config-
208   uration. We first tried a single-input single-output configuration, where only the top stream in c) of
209   Figure 3.3 was used. This architecture resulted in a validation accuracy of 88.52% for classification
210   among the 5 genetic neuron types: Exc, Pvalb, Sst, Ndnf, and Vip; and a validation accuracy of
211   96.35% for classification between excitatory and inhibitory broad types.
212   We then tested using a dual-input single-output configuration. Features were trained independently
213   on each subnetwork and were concatenated at the final step for classification. Using only the real
214   component of the Fourier coefficients as input to the additional subnetwork resulted in validation
215   accuracies of 91.43% for classification among the 5 genetic neuron types, and 99.38% for classifi-
      cation between excitatory and inhibitory broad types. Similarly, using only the complex component

      3.4                                    T RANSFER L EARNING
264
265   One of the biggest challenges in decoding the neural circuit and in systems neuroscience in general
266   is obtaining sufficient amount of data for each neuron type. Supervised learning requires a large
267   number of sample data points to train efficient classifiers. One of the main difficulties we encoun-
268   tered in this work was the problem of class imbalance present in the datasets. For example, there are
269   many more data points from excitatory (466) and parvalbumin (241) cells than Ndnf (46), Sst (86),
      and Vip (45) cells in the 5 class validation set; and many more data points from excitatory (644)


                                                                                                                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and parvalbumin (260) cells than Ndnf (38), Sst (130), and Vip (72) cells in the 5 class test set. We
271   attempt to solve this problem using transfer learning.
272
273   From the results of our triple-input single-output network, we can conclude that the 2 class classifi-
274
      cation task of identifying excitatory versus inhibitory cells achieves high accuracy. This observation
      inspired us to first train the model on the task it does well, and then conduct fine-tuning. We there-
275
      fore trained the aforementioned CNN network for the 2 broad type (Exc and Inh) classification task
276
      first for 100 epochs. We then changed the task to the 5 genetic type (Exc, Pvalb, Sst, Ndnf, and
277   Vip) classification using the network trained on the two broad type classification task. The network
278   uses the same CNN weights from the 2 type classification task, but has an output layer changed
279   to the 5 type classification task. Then, the updated network underwent fine-tuning on the 5 type
280   classification task for 100 epochs.
281
      The same Adam optimizer with the learning rate of 10−3 and `2 regularization parameter of 10−5
282
      was used for training. In order to investigate the model’s ability to undergo fine-tuning on a limited
283   number of data points, we subjected the network to endure fine-tuning on the following training-test
284   data set split ratios: 3:7, 5:5, and 8:2. For the 5 class classification task, at the epoch corresponding to
285   the highest validation accuracy, the computed test set precisions were 86.87%, 87.46%, and 89.22%,
286   for the above three training-test set split ratios, respectively. Table 1 reports the details of the test set

425   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
426
427   We presented in this paper a solution to the neuron classification problem that avoids the use of exist-
428   ing imperfect, non-standardized, and cumbersome electrophysiological classification schemes. The
429   triple-input CNN architecture described in this paper maps the pool of neurons into less ambiguous
430   genetic classifications that is normally not widely accessible or practical in experimental pipelines.
431   After training, our streamlined end-to-end convolutional neural network-based classification method
      does not require any domain specific knowledge or human intervention for quick identification of the


                                                                   8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      neuron’s cell type with high accuracy. The method presented in this paper provides an efficient and
433   standardized tool for the neuroscience community to use, thus enabling data analyses in a broader
434   scientific context. We also showed that the network architecture learns representations that suc-
435   cessfully distinguishes the neuron types, even when these features are not immediately recognizable
436   upon inspection as shown by the histograms of the time series data in the Appendix.
437
      Although we achieved the state-of-the-art results, there exists a potential source of noise in our data.
438
      The genetic labels we use in the training, validation, and test sets are currently based on the animal
439   type used to obtain the electrophysiology recording traces. The transgenic animal lines however are
440   not guaranteed to be accurate. It is known that some recordings obtained from a specified given
441   type will in fact belong to another genetic type (Hu et al., 2013). Quantifying how much error is
442   propagated from this potential noise source across all genetic types is of importance. Obtaining a
443   more accurate genetic labels from genetic sequencing data needs to be considered in the future.
444
      Furthermore, we plan to augment our dataset with in vivo electrophysiology recordings, which are
445   inherently coupled with more background noise. Such an extension will be particularly beneficial
446   for rare, but highly valuable in vivo current-clamp recordings, which are often blind to cell type. By
447   removing the need for labor-intensive handcrafted feature computation, which traditionally requires
448   domain expertise, our approach can enhance reproducibility and lead to faster, less biased scientific
449   outcomes. Additionally, analyzing the network’s learned features may provide insights into which
450   neuronal activity patterns best define specific neuron types. It is widely assumed that genes encode
451   the ion channel repertoire that underlies electrophysiological activity (Nandi et al., 2022). Identi-
452   fying correlations or causal relationships between genetic and electrophysiological classifications
453
      remains a key objective in neurophysiology. This pursuit aligns with Ramón y Cajal’s intuition that
      neuron groups possess distinct, intrinsic properties.
454
455   As foundational models continue to shape the future of machine learning and neuroscience, our ap-
456   proach serves not only as a current solution, but also as a potential blueprint. It provides an encoding
457   mechanism for electrophysiology data that can seamlessly integrate into larger foundational models,
458   ultimately bridging the gap between cellular-level neural activity and large-scale neural representa-
459
      tions.
