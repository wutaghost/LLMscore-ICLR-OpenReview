011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We initiate the study of Preference-Based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning
014            (PbMARL), exploring both theoretical foundations and empirical validations. We
015            define the task as identifying the Nash equilibrium from a preference-only of-
016            fline dataset in general-sum games, a problem marked by the challenge of sparse
017            feedback signals. Our theory establishes the upper complexity bounds for Nash
018
               Equilibrium in effective PbMARL, demonstrating that single-policy coverage is
               inadequate and highlighting the importance of unilateral dataset coverage. These
019
               theoretical insights are verified through comprehensive experiments. To enhance
020
               the practical performance, we further introduce two algorithmic techniques. (1)
021            We propose a Mean Squared Error (MSE) regularization along the time axis to
022            achieve a more uniform reward distribution and improve reward learning outcomes.
023            (2) We propose an additional penalty based on the distribution of the data set to
024            incorporate pessimism, improving stability and effectiveness during training. Our
025            findings underscore the multifaceted approach required for PbMARL, paving the
026            way for effective preference-based multi-agent systems.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Large language models (LLMs) have achieved significant progress in natural language interaction,
      knowledge acquisition, instruction following, planning and reasoning, which has been recognized as
032
      the sparks for AGI (Bubeck et al., 2023). The evolution of LLMs fosters the field of agent systems,
033
      wherein LLMs act as the central intelligence (Xi et al., 2023). In these systems, multiple LLMs
034   can interact with each other as well as with external tools. For instance, MetaGPT assigns LLM
035   agents various roles, akin to those in a technology company, enabling them to cooperate on complex
036   software engineering tasks (Hong et al., 2023).
037
      Despite some empirical successes in agent systems utilizing closed-source LLMs, finetuning these
038
      systems and aligning them with human preferences remains a challenge. Reinforcement learning from
039
      human feedback (RLHF) has played an important role in aligning LLMs with human preferences
040   (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019). However, unexpected behavior can arise when multiple
041   LLMs interact with each other. In addition, reward design has been a hard problem in multi-agent
042   reinforcement learning (Devlin et al., 2011). Thus, it is crucial to further align the multi-agent system
043   from preference feedback.
044
      We address this problem through both theoretical analysis and empirical experiments. Theoretically,
045
      we characterize the dataset coverage condition for PbMARL that enables learning the Nash equilib-
046   rium, which serves as a favorable policy for each player. Empirically, we validate our theoretical
047   insights through comprehensive experiments utilizing the proposed algorithmic techniques.

277
278   The proof is derived by a reduction from standard offline MARL to MARLHF. Suppose that MARLHF
279
      with single policy coverage suffices, we could construct an algorithm for standard offline MARL,

284
285   While single policy coverage is too weak to learn a Nash equilibrium, uniform policy coverage,

288   policy coverage.
289
290
      Theorem 2. (Informal) If the dataset has unilateral coverage on the Nash equilibrium policy, there

      and policy optimization subroutines. Intuitively, the algorithm identifies a policy that minimizes a

      5     A LGORITHMIC T ECHNIQUES FOR P RACTICAL P ERFORMANCE
300
301
302   In Section 4, we provided a theoretical characterization of the dataset requirements for MARLHF.
303   However, the algorithm used in Theorem 2 is not computationally efficient. In this section, we propose
304   a practical algorithm for MARLHF and validate our theoretical findings through experiments.
305

      5.1   H IGH - LEVEL M ETHODOLOGY
307
308
      Our MARLHF pipeline consists of two phases: In the first step, we train a reward prediction model
309
      and approximate the behavior policy ⇡b using imitation learning; in the second step, we then apply an
310
      MARL algorithm to maximize a combination of the KL-divergence-based reward and standardized
311   predicted reward r , ultimately deriving the final policy ⇡w .
312
313   Step 1: Reward Training and Dataset Modeling. Given the preference signals of trajectories,
314
      we use neural networks to predict step-wise rewards r (sh , ah ) for each agent, minimizing the loss
      defined in (1). The objective is to map (s, ai )-pairs to reward values such that the team returns align
315
      with the preference signals. At the same time, in order to utilize distribution-based penalty term
316
      log ⇡b (s, a) to cope with the extrapolation error in offline learning, an imitation learner is trained
317   over the entire dataset to model the behavior policy ⇡b .
318
319   Step 2: Offline MARL. Although in this work, VDN is chosen as the MARL oracle, it should
320
      be noted that other MARL architectures are also applicable. With the reward model r and the
      approximated dataset distribution learned in Step 1, we are now able to construct a virtual step-wise
321
      reward for each agent. The agents are then trained to maximize the target defined in (3).
322
323   Given this framework, additional techniques are required to build a strong practical algorithm, which
      we provide more details below.

      5.2   R EWARD R EGULARIZATION
325
326   Compared to step-wise reward signals, preference signals are H times sparser, making them more
327   challenging for a standard RL algorithm to utilize effectively. Concretely, this reward sparsity causes
328   the naive optimization of the negative log-likelihood (NLL) loss to suffer from two key problems:
329
            1. Sparse and spiky reward output. When calculating NLL losses, spreading the reward
330
               signal along the trajectories is equivalent to summing it at the last time step (Figure 2a).
331
               However, a sparse reward signal is harder for traditional RL methods to handdle due to
332            the lack of continuous supervision. More uniformly distributed rewards across the entire
333            trajectory generally leads to more efficient learning in standard RL algorithms.
334
            2. Over-reliance on irrelevant features. The model may exploit redundant features as
335            shortcuts to predict rewards. For instance, expert agents in cooperative games usually
336            exhibit a fixed pattern of collaboration from the very beginning of the trajectory (such as
337            specific actions or communication moves). The reward model might use these patterns
338            to differentiate them from agents of other skill levels, thereby failing to capture the true
339            reward-observation causal relationships.
340
341
      To mitigate these problems, we introduce an extra Mean Squared Error (MSE) regularization along
      the time axis (Equation 1, 2). By limiting the sudden changes in reward predictions between adjacent
342
      time steps, this regularization discourages the reward model from concentrating its predictions on
343
      just a few time steps. While these issues can also be mitigated by using more diversified datasets and
344   adding regularization to experts to eliminate reward-irrelevant action patterns, these approaches can
345   be costly and sometimes impractical in real-world applications. In contrast, our MSE regularization
346   is both easy to implement and has been empirically verified to be effective, creating more uniform
347   reward distribution (Figure 2) and better performances.

383   6     E XPERIMENTS
384
385   We design a series of experiments to validate our theories and methods in common general-sum
386   games. Specifically, we first use online RL algorithms to train expert agents, and take intermediate
387   checkpoints as rookie agents. Then, we use these agents to collect datasets and use the Bradley-Terry
388   model over standardized returns to simulate human preference. Experiments are carried out to
389
      verify the efficiency of our approach with unilateral policy dataset coverage (in Theorem 2) while
      single policy coverage is insufficient (stated in Theorem 1). We also design ablation studies to
390
      showcase the importance of our methods, particularly focusing on reward regularization and dataset
391
      distribution-based pessimism.
392
393
394
      6.1    E NVIRONMENTS
395
      Our experiments involved 3 Multi-Agent Particle Environments (MPE), including Spread-v3, Tag-v3
396   and Reference-v3, and Overcooked environment implemented with JaxMARL codebase (Rutherford
397   et al., 2023). Spread-v3 contains a group of agents and target landmarks, where the objective is to
398   cover as many landmarks as possible while avoiding collisions. Tag-v3 contains two opposing groups,
399   where quicker "preys" need to escape from "predators". To ensure a fair comparison of different
400   predator cooperation policies, we fixed a pretrained prey agent. Reference-v3 involves two agents
401   and three potential landmarks, where the agents need to find each one’s target landmark to receive a
402   high reward. The target landmark of each agent is only known by the other agent at first. Overcooked
403   involves two agents moving and operating objects in a gridworld. A more detailed description of the
404
      tasks and their associated challenges is provided in Appendix B.2.
405
406   6.2    T HE I MPORTANCE OF DATASET D IVERSITY
407
408   To study the influence of diversity of dataset, we manually designed 4 kinds of mixed joint behavior
409
      polices, and change their ratios to form different datasets.
410       • Expert policy: n expert agents. Trained with online RL algorithms till convergence.
411       • Rookie policy: n rookie agents. Trained with online RL algorithms with early stop.
412
          • Trivial policy: n random agents. All actions are uniformly sampled from the action space.
413
          • Unilateral policy: n 1 expert agents and 1 rookie agent of different proficiency level.
414
415   Table 1 presents the ratio of trajectories collected by the four different policies. The experiments are
416   designed to hierarchically examine the roles of diversity (Diversified vs. Mix-Unilateral), unilateral
417   coverage (Mix-Unilateral vs. Mix-Expert), and trivial comparison (Mix-Expert vs. Pure-Expert).
418   The ranking of diversity follows the order:
419
420                        Pure-Expert < Mix-Expert < Mix-Unilateral < Diversified
421
422   Due to the inherent limitations of offline reinforcement learning (RL) in action selection dictated
423   by the dataset, the effectiveness of learning is often strongly correlated with dataset quality, i.e. the
424   level of expertise demonstrated in the dataset. However, the results in preference-based MARL
425   experiments partially diverge from this conventional conclusion. While the quality of the dataset
426   remains critical, experiments on Reference-v3 and Overcooked (Table 2) indicate that diversity and
427   unilateral data can significantly enhance the performance of the reward model, thereby facilitating
428
      learning.
429   The main experimental results are presented in Table 2 and Table 3.Among all the experiments,
430   apart from the experiments on Tag-v3, where the high operational precision requirements make data
431   quality more critical than diversity, the other three environments validate our conclusions across all
      algorithms.

      6.3   E XPERIMENTS FOR R EWARD R EGULARIZATION
455
456   In Figure 2, we examined the effectiveness of our proposed reward regularization technique. Figure 2a
457   demonstrates that without regularization, the learned rewards tend to be sparse and spiky compared
458   to the ground truth rewards.
459
      We also observe that the rewards often exhibit temporal continuity, which can create greater discrep-
460
      ancies with the sparse, pulse-like ground truth. Notably, we found that adding stronger regularization
461   does not necessarily lead to underfitting of the reward model; in some cases, it even helps the model
462   converge to a lower training loss. Detailed parameters and experimental results are provided in the
463   appendix (cf. Table 8). We attribute this to the role of regularization in preventing the model from
464   overly relying on shortcuts.
