009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive learning has found extensive applications in computer vision, natural
012            language processing, and information retrieval, significantly advancing the fron-
013            tier of self-supervised learning. However, the limited availability of labels poses
014            challenges in contrastive learning, as the positive and negative samples can be
015            noisy, adversely affecting model training. To address this, we introduce instance-
016            wise attention into the variational lower bound of contrastive loss, and proposing
017            the AttentionNCE loss accordingly. AttentioNCE incorporates two key compo-
018
               nents that enhance contrastive learning performance: First, it replaces instance-
               level contrast with attention-based sample prototype contrast, helping to mitigate
019
               noise disturbances. Second, it introduces a flexible hard sample mining mecha-
020
               nism, guiding the model to focus on high-quality, informative samples. Theoret-
021            ically, we demonstrate that optimizing AttentionNCE is equivalent to optimizing
022            the variational lower bound of contrastive loss, offering a worst-case guarantee for
023            maximum likelihood estimation under noisy conditions. Empirically, we apply
024            AttentionNCE to popular contrastive learning frameworks and validate its effec-
025            tiveness. The code is released at: https://anonymous.4open.science/
026            r/AttentioNCE-55EB

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The pursuit of learning effective feature representations from unlabeled data has long been a long-
032   standing goal in machine learning Wu et al. (2018); Zhuang et al. (2019); Chuang et al. (2020); Chu
033
      et al. (2023). Contrastive learning, as a powerful branch of self-supervised learning, driven by the
      pretext tasks of contrasting semantically similar positive examples with semantically unrelated neg-
034
      ative examples to facilitate model pretraining. Contrastive learning and has demonstrated promising
035
      results Grill et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Tong et al. (2023), garnering extensive adoption across
036   various domains such as computer vision Chen et al. (2020a), natural language processing Radford
037   et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2023), information retrieval Liu & Wang (2023), and other domains, even
038   outperforms supervised learning in certain tasks Misra & Maaten (2020); He et al. (2020).
039
      Within instance-level contrastive learning Wu et al. (2018); Oord et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2020a);
040
      Grill et al. (2020); He et al. (2020), positive and negative labels are typically assigned based on
041
      co-occurrence Liu et al. (2021) of input data, which often leads to label noise. For instance, in
042   methods like CPC, SimCLR, and MOCO, positive examples are samples that co-occur with anchor
043   data (e.g., augmented images or multimodal signals in videos). However, these positive examples
044   may not always share semantic meaning with the anchor data, such as in cases of excessive cropping
045   in images, leading to false positives. Negative instances typically comprise random samples that do
046   not co-occur with the anchor data, yet they can unintentionally share semantic similarities with the
047   anchor, resulting in false negatives. This noise arises from the absence of supervisory signals and
048   the reliance on co-occurrence for automatic labeling, a process that inevitably generates both false
049   positives and false negatives.
050   Noisy labels pose several challenges for contrastive learning. Firstly, they complicate likelihood
051   modeling. The process of optimizing contrastive loss essentially aims to maximize the likelihood of
052   identifying positive samples from a set of negatives Li et al. (2021). However, since the ground -
053   truth labels for both positive and negative samples are not available, likelihood modeling becomes
      complicated. Secondly, the occurrence of false positives and false negatives result in semantically


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      unrelated samples are erroneously pulled together while semantically related samples are pushed
055   apart, which will disrupt the semantic structure of embeddings Wang & Liu (2021). Previous re-
056   search Chuang et al. (2022; 2020); Robinson et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2023) has shown that the
057   presence of false positive and false negative examples significantly lead to performance drop. More-
058   over, while contrastive learning benefits from the mining of hard samples Robinson et al. (2021),
059   existing research predominantly concentrates on hard negative mining, overlooking the potential
060   benefits that could arise from the incorporation of hard positive mining. This oversight restricts the
061   potential for further performance enhancements.
062   In this paper, we introduce a latent space to decompose the contrastive loss and derive its variational
063   lower bound, which results in the proposal of the AttentionNCE loss as an alternative optimization
064   target. AttentionNCE enables us to optimize the contrastive loss indirectly and provides a worst -
065   case guarantee for maximum likelihood estimation under noisy conditions. To tackle the challenge
066   of noisy perturbations, we utilize an attention mechanism to derive sample prototypes for contrast.
067   By aggregating information from multiple samples, these prototypes assist in mitigating the effects
068   of noisy perturbations during instance - level contrast. Finally, as we realize that high - quality hard
069
      samples can enhance contrastive learning, we incorporate a flexible and lightweight mechanism to
      mine both hard positive and hard negative samples, ensuring that the model focuses on high - quality
070
      and informative samples.
071
072   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
073
074          • We propose the AttentionNCE contrastive loss and theoretically prove that optimizing At-
075            tentionNCE is equivalent to optimizing the variational lower bound of the original con-
076            trastive loss, which provides a worst - case guarantee for maximum likelihood estimation
077
               (MLE) under noisy conditions.
078          • AttentionNCE incorporates attention - based sample prototype contrast to alleviate the im-
079            pact of noise perturbations. Moreover, it includes a flexible hard - sample - mining mecha-
080            nism to guide the model to focus on high - quality and informative samples.
081
             • We apply the AttentionNCE loss to popular contrastive learning frameworks and validate
082            its effectiveness.

087   Self-supervised learning is a branch of unsupervised learning that aims to exploit the internal struc-
088   ture of data Wu et al. (2018) for learning without relying on manual annotations. It achieves this
089   through carefully designed pretext tasks He et al. (2020). These tasks typically include predicting
090   arbitrary parts of the input based on observed parts Liu et al. (2021), such as autoencoder-based re-
091   construction Bengio et al. (2006), context prediction Doersch et al. (2015), colorization Zhang et al.
092   (2016), rotation prediction Gidaris et al. (2018), among others. Another common type of pretext
093
      task involves predicting similar or not, or formulating it as classifying semantically related positive
      samples from semantically unrelated negative samples Gutmann & Hyvärinen (2010); Oord et al.
094
      (2018). This paradigm is also known as contrastive learning, which relieves the encoder from pixel-
095
      level information reconstruction and has shown promising results in various tasks Robinson et al.
096   (2021); Tian et al. (2020); Wang & Isola (2020); Saunshi et al. (2019).
097
098   Contrastive learning has garnered significant attention in recent years as a self-supervised tech-
099
      nique for representation learning Bachman et al. (2019); Hjelm et al. (2019); Henaff (2020); Misra
      & Maaten (2020); Wang & Isola (2020); Chen et al. (2020b); Radford et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021).
100
      Although the specific choices of representation encoder f and similarity measure may vary depend-
101
      ing on the task Gutmann & Hyvärinen (2010); Devlin et al. (2018); He et al. (2020); Dosovitskiy
102   et al. (2014), they all share a common underlying principle of bringing positive pairs closer while
103   pushing negative pairs apart to train the representation encoder f through the optimization of a
104   contrastive loss Wang & Isola (2020); Gutmann & Hyvärinen (2010); Oord et al. (2018); Hjelm
105   et al. (2018); Wang & Isola (2020). Building upon this idea, several popular contrastive learning
106   frameworks have been proposed, such as SimCLR Chen et al. (2020a), MOCO He et al. (2020),
107   BYOL Grill et al. (2020), and SimSiam Chen & He (2021). However, in the self-supervised setting
      of contrastive learning, the issues of false positive samples Chuang et al. (2022) and false negative

      examples Chuang et al. (2020); Robinson et al. (2021) can arise, which can degrade the performance
109   of contrastive learning Wang & Liu (2021); Wu et al. (2023).

508   label as the anchor point, while negative samples are those with different labels. This process elim-
509   inates the cost of false - negative samples. Subsequently, we calculate the AttentionNCE loss. For
510   SimCLR, labels are utilized to exclude false - negative samples. The data augmentation methods,
511   network architecture, optimizer, and linear evaluation protocol remain unchanged. Table 4 displays
512   the performance of AttentionNCE in a supervised setting. Without the cost related to false negative
513   samples, AttentionNCE shows a significant improvement. This is because the sample prototypes
514   generated from multiple samples by means of attention can encode more information of semantic
515
      classes. In contrast to instance-level contrast, when using prototypes, they are capable of capturing
      more general and representative features within a semantic class, which is beneficial for reducing the
516
      influence of noise and variability within individual samples. This advantage backs up the underlying
517
      motivation for generating prototypes using the attention function for contrast.

528   5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   This paper introduces instance-level attention into contrastive learning by integrating attention-based
531
      sample prototypes into the variational lower bound of contrastive loss, resulting in the proposed
      AttentionNCE loss. AttentionNCE directs the model’s focus toward more informative and relevant
532
      samples, offering a worst-case guarantee for maximum likelihood estimation under noisy conditions.
533
      Despite its simplicity and ease of implementation, AttentionNCE includes two key components that
534   enhance performance. First, attention-based sample prototypes help mitigate the impact of noise in
535   instance-level contrast. Second, the flexible incorporation of hard positive and hard negative sample
536   mining further boosts performance. However, the balance between exploiting easy (clean) samples
537   and exploring hard (potentially noisy) samples requires further study. This balance is crucial for
538   generating more effective sample prototypes, h, and achieving a tighter variational lower bound.
539   We hope this study will inspire further theoretical analyses in self-supervised contrastive learning
      and promote the extension of instance-level attention mechanisms in future methods.
