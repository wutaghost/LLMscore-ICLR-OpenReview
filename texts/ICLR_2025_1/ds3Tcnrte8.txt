                                                 A BSTRACT
009
010
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant advancements in
011            various natural language processing tasks, yet they often struggle with tasks that
012            require external domain-specific knowledge, such as Multiple Choice Question
013            Answering (MCQA). Integrating Knowledge Graphs (KGs) with LLMs has been
014            explored as a solution to enhance LLMs’ reasoning capabilities, while existing
015            methods either involve computationally expensive finetuning processes or rely on
016            the noisy retrieval of KG information. Recent efforts have focused on leveraging
017            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to generate KG-based soft prompts for LLMs,
018            which face challenges of lacking question-relevance assessment in GNN and uti-
019
               lization of relations among options. In this paper, we propose a novel approach,
               QAP, to address these challenges by optimizing the utilization of KG in MCQA
020
               tasks. Our method introduces question embeddings into the GNN aggregation pro-
021
               cess, enabling the model to assess the relevance of KG information based on the
022            question context. Additionally, QAP facilitates inter-option interactions by em-
023            ploying an attention module that explicitly models relationships between answer
024            options. Specifically, we use multiple attention heads for the GNN output, allow-
025            ing the model to capture and compare features across different options, thereby
026            enhancing cross-option reasoning. Our approach not only enhances the connec-
027            tion between GNNs and LLMs but also enables the model to better utilize the re-
028            lationships between answer options. Experimental results demonstrate that QAP
029            outperforms state-of-the-art models on multiple public MCQA datasets, validating
030
               its effectiveness and scalability.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
035   2023) have made significant strides in natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Wei et al., 2022b;
036   Cohen et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Leveraging vast amounts of data and computational resources,
037   LLMs have demonstrated remarkable performance in tasks such as language generation (Cheng
038   et al., 2023) and text comprehension (Lewis et al., 2020). However, despite their impressive achieve-
039
      ments, LLMs still face challenges when it comes to tasks that require domain-specific knowledge
      or external information (Zheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). A typical example is the Mul-
040
      tiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA) task, where the correct answer may rely on complex
041
      background knowledge that goes beyond what LLMs have learned from pretraining corpora (Asai
042   et al., 2024). To address this limitation, researchers have started exploring ways to integrate exter-
043   nal knowledge bases, such as Knowledge Graphs (KGs), into LLMs to enhance their reasoning and
044   answering capabilities (Jiang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).
045
      Several existing studies have proposed to leverage KGs for assisting LLMs in answering ques-
046
      tions (Jiang et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024). Some approaches incorporate KG information directly
047
      into the training or finetuning process of LLMs (Zhang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021). For in-
048   stance, K-Adapter (Wang et al., 2021) introduces entity and relation knowledge during model train-
049   ing, leading to improved performance in knowledge reasoning tasks. However, such methods re-
050   quire retraining or finetuning the LLMs, which is computationally expensive and challenging to
051   scale in resource-limited environments. Another class of methods retrieves relevant information
052   from KGs and appends it to the LLM input, enabling the model to utilize this information during
053   inference (Baek et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024). While these approaches avoid finetuning, the re-
      trieval quality is often suboptimal, especially when the retrieved KG content is not semantically

      aligned with the question. This misalignment introduces noise and degrades the quality of gener-
055   ated answers (Xu et al., 2024). More recently, researchers have proposed to combine the benefits of
056   finetuning and retrieving by utilizing KG-based soft prompts (Lester et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2021).
057   KG-based soft prompts are lightweight and flexible input prefixes obtained from KGs using Graph

102   In this section, we introduce the task of Multiple Choice Question Answering (MCQA) based on
103   knowledge graphs. We aim to answer a question q by selecting one of the n answer options from
104   the candidate set A = {ak |k = 1, 2, . . . , n}. This is performed with the assistance of a knowledge
105   graph G = (E, R, T ), where E and R are sets of entities and relations, respectively, and T =
106   {(h, r, t)|h, t ∈ E, r ∈ R} is the set of knowledge triplets, each containing a head entity h, a relation
107   r and a tail entity t. We utilize a pretrained large language model denoted as LM to generate the
      final answer to the question q, which is the input.

133   3       QAP
134
135   In this section, we introduce the details of our proposed framework QAP. As presented in Figure 2,
136   QAP is structured into three phases: (i) Subgraph Retrieval, (ii) Question-Aware Neighborhood Ag-
137   gregation (QNA) and (iii) Inter-Option Attention (IPT). During the Subgraph Retrieval phase, we
138
      extract a contextualized subgraph from the KG, containing the information relevant to the ques-
      tion and each answer option. In the Question-Aware Neighborhood Aggregation phase, we utilize
139
      a specialized GNN, where the aggregation process is impacted by the question, allowing the sub-
140
      graph processing to generate outputs that are more closely aligned with the query. Finally, in the
141   Inter-Option Attention phase, we employ an attention module to capture the relationships between
142   different options and transfer the output of the GNN into a form that is easier for LLM to understand
143   and process. The outputs of IPT serve as soft prompts to guide LLMs. Notably, the entire framework
144   is optimized in an end-to-end manner and no intermediate training objective is needed.
145
146
      3.1       S UBGRAPH R ETRIEVAL
147
148   To effectively utilize and retrieve the useful information in the KG that is relevant to the given
149   question, we extract the contextualized subgraphs of the questions to reduce the size of the KG used
150   while capturing useful data. Specifically, for an answer option ak , we first establish the set of all
151   entities in G that appears in the question q and answer option ak , denoted as Eqk . We then extract the
152   N -hop neighbors and the relation connecting them as the contextualized subgraph of ak , denoted as
153   Gqk (Yasunaga et al., 2022). This contextualized subgraph contains the necessary information that is
154   potentially helpful for determining whether the option ak is correct for the given question q.
155
156   3.2       Q UESTION -AWARE N EIGHBORHOOD AGGREGATION
157
158   After obtaining the contextualized subgraphs during the Subgraph Retrieval phase, we introduce
159   a Question-Aware Neighborhood Aggregation mechanism (QNA) within each subgraph Gqk , guided
160   by the question embedding given by LLM. The goal is to generate node representations that not only
161   capture the structural properties of the KG but also emphasize the triplet relevant to the question q,
      thus making the final output more compatible with the question.

      3.3      I NTER -O PTION ATTENTION
217
218   In this subsection, we describe the Inter-Option Attention (IPT) mechanism. IPT incorporates the
219   relationships among different options to soft prompt generating and transforms the output of QNA
220   into a form that is more interpretable by the LLM. The idea is to align the GNN node representations
221   from each contextualized subgraph with the token embeddings of all options along with the question,
222   which contain text information interpretable by LLM across all options.
223
224   Cross-Option Node-Token Attention. After processing each subgraph Gqk through QNA, we ob-
225   tain node representations for each node in the subgraph. Let Hk ∈ RNk ×dg denote the node repre-
226   sentations for the subgraph corresponding to the answer option ak . Additionally, for the question q
227
      and its n answer options, we construct n different sequences T1 , T2 , . . . , Tn , where each sequence
      Tr is a concatenation of the of token embeddings from question q and the r-th answer option ar .
228
      We denote Tr = {Tr,1 , Tr,2 , . . . , Tr,m }, where m is the number of tokens in Tr and each token
229
      embedding Tr,s ∈ Rdt , with dt as the token embedding dimension. To ensure compatibility be-
230
      tween the node and token embeddings, we first project these embeddings into the same dimensional
231
      space using a linear transformation. For the i-th node in Hk and the s-th token in Tr :

269   in the KG and the sequential processing of the LLM and enriches the prompt with inter-option

      4     O PTIMIZATION
271
272   The optimization of our proposed framework QAP is focused on aligning the LLM’s output with the
273   correct answer. Let y denote the ground truth text associated with the correct answer option. The
274   loss function used to optimize the QAP model is the cross-entropy loss as:
275
                                           \mathcal {L} = -\log P(\mathbf {y}|\mathbf {S}_p+\mathbf {Q}+\mathbf {A}).    (14)
276
277   Here Q, A represent the token embeddings of the question q and all options a1 , a2 , · · · , an . This
278   loss function is used to adjust the parameters of the Question-Aware Neighborhood Aggregation
279
      and the Inter-Option Attention module in an end-to-end manner while keeping the LLM parameters
      frozen. By minimizing the cross-entropy loss, the QAP model learns to produce outputs that are
280
      increasingly aligned with the ground truth text, thereby improving its ability to generate soft prompts
281
      that can effectively guide the LLM to generate the correct textual output based on the information
282   provided by the knowledge graph and the associated question context.

292
293   We evaluate our model on both general domain and biomedical domain MCQA datasets, utiliz-

300   5.2   BASELINES
301
      We compare the performance of our proposed method QAP with the following five baselines:
302
303   • LLM (LLM-Only): This baseline uses the Large Language Model directly to answer the ques-
304     tions, without any additional prompt enhancements or external knowledge integration.
305
      • PE (Prompt-Enhanced LLM): In this method, we utilize the same LLM but with designed
306     prompts to guide the model’s reasoning. These prompts are crafted to better align with the specific
307     requirements of the question.
308
      • KGEP (KG Evidence Prompting) (Baek et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024): This approach incorpo-
309     rates Knowledge Graph triplets into the prompt. A decoder first ranks the similarity between KG
310     triplets and the given question, selecting the highest-scoring triplets as evidence. These triplets
311     are then added to the prompt to aid the LLM in generating a more informed answer.
312
      • SP (Soft Prompting) (Lester et al., 2021): We train soft prompts without utilizing any external
313     KG information. These soft prompts assist the LLM in generating answers and the knowledge
314     comes solely from the pretrained language model without external knowledge.
315
      • GNP (Graph Neural Prompting) (Tian et al., 2024): GNP uses a graph neural network to encode
316     KG information into the LLM’s prompts. GNP consists of a GNN encoder, a cross-modality
317     pooling module, and self-supervised link prediction to better incorporate KG knowledge into the
318     language model as soft prompts.

346   the biomedical domain (MedQA).
347
      QAP shows improvements on OBQA, which relies more on direct factual recall from elementary-
348   level science concepts. This may already be well captured by the LLM itself, reducing the potential
349   benefit from external KG integration. However, QAP still demonstrates the effectiveness of incor-
350   porating structured knowledge and multi-head attention mechanisms to enhance reasoning in these
351   cases. Specifically, QAP outperforms the best baseline by 7.61% with the 3B LLM and 3.28% with
352   the 11B LLM. For Riddle, which requires complex commonsense reasoning, the inclusion of Con-
353   ceptNet and our QNA mechanism enabled better extraction and utilization of relevant knowledge
354   to solve riddles. The ability of IPT to model relationships between options also contributed to im-
355   proved reasoning, as our method can better infer the correct answer by comparing options against
356
      one another. Here, the performance gains were more substantial, with improvements of 20.61% for
      the 3B LLM and 13.59% for the 11B LLM over the baseline. On MedQA, the use of UMLS as a
357
      knowledge graph proved critical. Medical questions often require highly specialized knowledge, and
358
      by leveraging UMLS through our method, the model could access domain-specific information not
359   present in standard language models. This integration allowed QAP to better interpret the biomedi-
360   cal context of questions, leading to an improvement of 11.83% with the 3B LLM and 11.00% with
361   the 11B LLM.
362
      These results highlight the importance of leveraging question-aware external structured knowledge
363
      and modeling inter-option relationships for enhancing the reasoning capabilities of large language
364
      models, particularly in complex domains such as biomedical reasoning.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
407
408   6.1    L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND Q UESTION A NSWERING
409
410   Large Language Models, such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and Flan-T5 (Wei et al., 2022a), have
411   shown remarkable performance across various natural language processing tasks (Wei et al., 2022b),
412   including MCQA (Tian et al., 2024). However, LLMs still face limitations in reasoning tasks that
413   require access to factual knowledge beyond their pre-training corpus (Luo et al., 2024). Several
414   approaches have been proposed to augment LLMs with external knowledge sources, such as knowl-
415
      edge graphs, to enhance their factual accuracy and reasoning capabilities (Baek et al., 2023). For
      example, methods like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) have introduced mechanisms to
416
      retrieve relevant information from external sources, including KGs, and incorporate it into LLM
417
      inputs (Xu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). While effective in some scenarios, these
418   approaches often struggle with noisy retrievals or insufficiently grounded knowledge, limiting their
419   impact on complex reasoning tasks.
420
421   6.2    K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS FOR E NHANCING QA
422
423
      Knowledge graphs provide structured representations of entities and their relationships, making
      them valuable resources for improving the reasoning abilities of LLMs in knowledge-intensive
424
      tasks (Zhang et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). Prior work, such as QA-GNN (Ya-
425
      sunaga et al., 2021), has demonstrated the effectiveness of using graph neural networks (GNNs) to
426   model the relationships within KGs and integrate these relationships into the question-answering
427   process. These approaches allow LLMs to reason over multi-hop knowledge, bridging gaps in
428   factual knowledge that are not readily accessible through text-based models alone (Jiang et al.,
429   2023b;a). However, many existing methods treat KGs as static resources, retrieving specific facts
430   based on direct entity matches without fully leveraging the contextual relevance of knowledge to
431   the question (Sun et al., 2024). This limitation can reduce the potential of KGs to support deeper
      reasoning tasks, such as commonsense or biomedical question answering.

      6.3   GNN S AND S OFT P ROMPTS FOR KG I NTEGRATION
461
      Recent advances in integrating GNNs with LLMs have introduced the use of GNNs to generate soft
462
      prompts (Lester et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2023), and guide the LLM’s reasoning process by encoding
463   KG information directly into the model’s input. For instance, Graph Neural Prompting (GNP) (Tian
464   et al., 2024) incorporates a GNN to learn from KG data and produce neural prompts that enhance
465   the LLM’s performance on both commonsense and domain-specific reasoning tasks. GNN-based
466   approaches, however, often rely on static KG structures and fail to incorporate the question directly
467   into the KG aggregation process (Pan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022). This lack of interaction
468   between the question and KG during the aggregation phase can result in suboptimal utilization of
469   the KG, especially for questions requiring nuanced reasoning.

471   7     C ONCLUSION
472
473   In this paper, we proposed a novel approach, QAP, to enhance the performance of Multiple Choice
474   Question Answering (MCQA) tasks by integrating Knowledge Graphs with Large Language Mod-
475   els. Our method addresses two key challenges in existing approaches. First, we introduced a
476   Question-Aware Neighborhood Aggregation (QNA) mechanism that incorporates question embed-
477
      dings into the aggregation process of GNNs, improving the model’s ability to assess the relevance
      of KG information based on the question context. This allows the GNNs to focus on the most rel-
478
      evant knowledge for answering the question to capture useful information. Second, we designed a
479
      novel attention mechanism, IPT, enabling inter-option interactions. By utilizing multi-head attention
480   where each head attends to a different answer option, we allowed the model to leverage the relation-
481   ships between the answer options. This strategy improves the model’s ability to eliminate incorrect
482   options and enhance overall performance. Our method was evaluated on three MCQA datasets of
483   two domains, and experimental results demonstrated that QAP outperforms state-of-the-art models,
484   validating its effectiveness and scalability. We believe that integrating structured external knowledge
485   with LLMs through attention and interaction mechanisms will continue to be a promising direction
      for advancing question answering systems.

730   A.4   L IMITATIONS
731
      While our proposed method demonstrates significant improvements in Multiple-Choice Question-
732
      Answering (MCQA) tasks by integrating knowledge graphs and leveraging Question-Aware strate-
733   gies, several limitations still remain. First, our approach relies heavily on the quality and complete-
734   ness of the external knowledge graph. In domains where the KG is sparse or lacks coverage, such as
735   less-studied areas or questions involving uncommon entities, the model’s performance may degrade.
736   Moreover, our method does not explicitly handle cases where external knowledge is ambiguous or
737   conflicts with the question context, potentially leading to confusion in the model’s final predictions.
738   Second, the computational complexity of our design can lead to increased inference time, making it
739   less suitable for real-time applications or deployment in resource-limited environments.

741   A.5   E THICS S TATEMENT
742
743
      Our work focuses on improving the performance of large language models for Multiple-Choice
      Question Answering by integrating structured knowledge from knowledge graphs. While our
744
      method enhances the factual accuracy and reasoning capabilities of LLMs, we acknowledge sev-
745
      eral ethical considerations. First, the use of external knowledge sources such as knowledge graphs
746   introduces potential biases inherent in the data. Knowledge graphs may reflect the biases of their
747   creators, including historical, cultural, and societal biases, which could inadvertently affect the fair-
748   ness and neutrality of the model’s predictions. Second, in sensitive domains such as healthcare (e.g.,
749   MedQA), the reliance on imperfect knowledge graphs may lead to incorrect or harmful predictions,
750   especially in situations where the information in the KG is outdated or incomplete. This underscores
751   the importance of validation and continuous updating of the external knowledge sources used by the
752   model. We are committed to promoting fairness and effectiveness in AI, and we encourage the
753   responsible use of our method, particularly in high-stakes applications.
