009                                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011                 In order to unlock the potential of diverse sensors, we investigate a method to
012                 transfer knowledge between modalities using the structure of a unified multimodal
013                 representation space for Human Action Recognition (HAR). We formalize and ex-
014                 plore an understudied cross-modal transfer setting we term Unsupervised Modality
015                 Adaptation (UMA), where the modality used in testing is not used in supervised
016                 training, i.e. zero labeled instances of the test modality are available during training.
017                 We develop three methods to perform UMA: Student-Teacher (ST), Contrastive
018                 Alignment (CA), and Cross-modal Transfer Through Time (C3T). Our extensive
019
                    experiments on various camera+IMU datasets compare these methods to each other
                    in the UMA setting, and to their empirical upper bound in the supervised setting.
020
                    The results indicate C3T is the most robust and highest performing by at least a
021
                    margin of 8%, and nears the supervised setting performance even in the presence
022                 of temporal noise. This method introduces a novel mechanism for aligning signals
023                 across time-varying latent vectors, extracted from the receptive field of temporal
024                 convolutions. Our findings suggest that C3T has significant potential for devel-
025                 oping generalizable models for time-series sensor data, opening new avenues for
026                 multi-modal learning in various applications.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Humans can naturally actuate a motion they have only seen before; however, transferring motion
032   knowledge across sensors for machine learning models is nontrivial. Our interaction with computing
033   has historically been centered around visual and textual modalities, which has provided these models
034   an abundance of data. Thus, deep learning based human action recognition (HAR) systems often
035   collapse 3D motion into related but imprecise modalities such as visual data (Ji et al., 2012; Simonyan
036
      & Zisserman, 2014; Lin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) or language models (Radford et al., 2021;
      Wang et al., 2022a; Tong et al., 2022; Piergiovanni et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023).
037
038   Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs), which typically provide 3-axis acceleration and 3-axis gyroscopic
039   information on a wearable device, emerge as strong candidates for understanding human motion
040   in a nonintrusive fashion. Smartwatches, smartphones, earbuds and other wearables have enabled
041   the seamless integration of IMUs into daily life (Mollyn et al., 2023). Unfortunately, IMUs remain
042
      underutilized in current machine-learning approaches due to several challenges including (1) the
      lack of abundant labeled data (2) the difficulty in interpreting the raw sensor readings, and (3) a
043
      low-dimensional representation, which limits the amount of information that can be stored and
044
      learned from.

      Beyond IMUs, various sensing modalities are gaining popularity in wearables (e.g. surface electro-
055   cardiogram, electromyography) and ambient monitoring systems (e.g. WiFi signals, Radar). While
056   numerous AI methods have been developed for HAR using these modalities, they struggle to achieve
057   the level of generalization and capability demonstrated by visual and textual modalities, which benefit
058   from internet-scale data, and new modalities are continuously emerging. This raises the critical
059   question of how to integrate new sensors with existing ones in the absence of labeled data. One
060   promising solution is to leverage a well-documented modality to transfer knowledge to another
061   modality, a process known as cross-modal transfer (Niu et al., 2020). Ideally, the model for this
062   source modality would teach the model for the new target modality without additional human annota-
063
      tion effort. Existing cross-modal learning techniques assume a semi-supervised or fully supervised
      setup where there exists some labels for each modality during training (An et al., 2021; Niu et al.,
064
      2020). Cross-modal learning has not thoroughly been investigated in a setting where one modality
065
      is completely unlabeled during training, which we refer to as Unsupervised Modality Adaptation
066   (UMA), demonstrated in Figure 1.
067
068   Multimodal learning and sensor fusion has long been studied for human action recognition, however,
069
      few methods study the UMA setting. In addition, existing works typically compress entire time-series
      sequences into single latent representations or segment data into fixed-size chunks for transformer
070
      inputs. However, this approach fails to account for the continuous nature of real-world data and the
071
      variable time spans over which actions occur. We propose 3 methods for the UMA setting. The first is
072   called Student-Teacher (ST) akin to many existing knowledge distillation methods for other domain
073   adaptation or semi-supervised settings. The second method, Contrastive Alignment (CA), aligns the
074   latent representation of each data sample across modalities and uses a shared task head to perform
075   transfer across modalities. The third method, entitled Cross-modal Transfer Through Time (C3T),
076   extracts a time-varying latent dimension through the temporal receptive field of 3D convolution and
077   uses a shared task self-attention head to perform transfer.
078   Our research evaluates these three UMA methods—ST, CA, and C3T—by focusing on the challenging
079   task of transferring knowledge from RGB video data to IMU signals across four diverse human action
080   recognition datasets. Cross-modal Transfer Through Time (C3T) surpasses the other methods on all
081   datasets by at least an 8% margin on Top-1 Accuracy. Remarkably, C3T in unsupervised modality
082   adaptation even outperforms the RGB unimodal supervised setting, underscoring its effectiveness in
083   cross-modal learning.
084   Our qualitative analysis suggests that the success of CA and C3T lies in their ability to uncover
085   hidden correlations between modalities within the latent space. This capability allows the model
086   to leverage structural information from one modality to inform its understanding of another, even
087   when labeled data is unavailable. To enhance performance over CA, C3T introduces a mechanism for
088   aligning signals across time-varying latent vectors. This novel approach enables the model to capture
089   temporal dynamics more effectively, resulting in enhanced robustness against time-related noise—a
090   common challenge in real-world sensor data. We conduct additional experiments that demonstrate
091   C3T’s superior resilience to time-shift, misalignment, and time-dilation noise compared to other
092
      methods. The promising results of C3T open new avenues for developing highly generalizable
      models for time-series sensor data. This has far-reaching implications across various domains,
093
      including healthcare monitoring, smarthomes, industrial IoT, and human-computer interaction, where
094
      multi-modal learning from limited labeled data is crucial.
095
096   Our contributions are as follows:
097   • A novel motivation and setup for cross-modal transfer learning to an unlabeled modality, referred
098     to as Unlabeled Modality Adaptation, UMA.
099   • Development and comparative analysis of three methods—Student-Teacher (ST), Contrastive
100     Alignment (CA), and Cross-modal Transfer Through Time (C3T)—for performing UMA in human
101     action recognition using RGB and IMU data (Sections 4 and 5)
102   • Insights into the mechanisms of latent space alignment that enable effective knowledge transfer
103     between modalities, potentially applicable to other multi-modal learning tasks beyond human
104
        action recognition (Section 5).
      • An in-depth analysis of how C3T’s structure, in particular its use of temporal tokens, provides
105
        superior performance with minimal model size and enhanced robustness to temporal distortions,
106
        showcasing its potential for real-world applications with sensor data (Sections 5.1 and 5.2)

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
111   We outline our motivation for the term UMA and then review the works related to each of our
112   methods: Student-Teacher, Contrastive Alignment, and Cross-modal Transfer Through Time.
113
      Unsupervised Modality Adaptation Cross-modal transfer learning is a powerful technique that
114
      enables the transfer of knowledge from a data-rich modality to one with limited data availability (Niu
115
      et al., 2020). This approach can be viewed as a specialized form of domain adaptation, where a
116   model, trained in a source domain, is tasked with efficiently adapting to a related target domain for
117   the same output task, despite having access to fewer labeled data points (Pan & Yang, 2009; Farahani
118   et al., 2021). Given the focus on domains with scarce labels, adaptation is often achieved through
119   unsupervised (Chang et al., 2020) or semi-supervised (An et al., 2021) methods. In the context
120   of human activity recognition (HAR), domain adaptation may involve adapting between different
121   sensors (Bhalla et al., 2021), adjusting to varying positions of wearables on the body (Wang et al.,
122   2018; Chang et al., 2020; Prabono et al., 2021), accommodating different users (Hu et al., 2023;
123   Fu et al., 2021), or adapting to different IMU device types (Khan et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2020;
124
      Chakma et al., 2021). Our work focuses on domain adaptation where the target domain is a new
      completely unlabeled modality, hence we introduce the term Unsupervised Modality Adaptation
125
      (UMA). Previous research explored similar concepts under various terms like knowledge distillation,
126
      missing modality, robust sensor fusion, multi-modal alignment, and zero-shot cross-modal transfer.
127   We propose UMA as an encompassing term to unify the discussion for performing inference with a
128   completely unsupervised modality.
129
130
      Student Teacher: Various knowledge distillation methods use student-teacher models for missing
      modality inference. They often use an extra auxiliary modality during training to increase a different
131
      modality’s performance during testing, however, they assume the availability of labeled training
132
      data from both modalities (Xue et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Bruce et al.,
133   2021). Notably, Thoker & Gall (2019) performs knowledge distillation without labels for one
134   modality, i.e. in the UMA setting. However, they require an ensemble of students to perform mutual
135   learning and limit their analysis to one method using two visual modalities on one dataset. Similarily,
136   IMUTube (Kwon et al., 2020) and ChromoSim (Hao et al., 2022) simulate IMU data from videos to
137   teach an IMU model and then perform zero-shot transfer to real IMU data. However, these approaches
138   are resource-intensive, cannot easily extend to other modalities, and face simulation-reality gaps.
139   Contrastive Alignment: Unsupervised contrastive methods, such as IMU and vision-based examples
140   like ImageBind (Girdhar et al., 2023), IMU2CLIP (Moon et al., 2022), and mmg-Ego4d (Gong
141   et al., 2023), have emerged as powerful tools for robust multimodal representation learning and are
142   relevant to our Contrastive Alignment (CA) method. While these approaches have shown promise
143   in alignment for retrieval and generation tasks, they have limitations. ImageBind and IMU2CLIP
144   focus exclusively on egocentric data and IMU2CLIP aligns IMU data with text which violates our
145   UMA setting. Although mmg-Ego4d, addresses UMA in their zero-shot cross-modal transfer task,
146   but their work is limited to a single, private dataset and focuses mainly on few shot label learning.
147
      Our CA method addresses these limitations by encompassing both egocentric and third-person public
      datasets, tackling the UMA setting effectively, and offering flexibility during inference. Uniquely,
148
      our approach enables fusion of all available modalities during testing, even when trained on only one
149
      labeled modality. We also test for temporal robustness, visualize the latent space, and compare with
150   other UMA methods.
151
152   Cross-modal Transfer Through Time: Existing video understanding and robust sensor fusion
153
      methods often leverage temporal data heavily, employing techniques such as temporal masking
      and reconstruction (Tong et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2019), spatio-temporal memory banks (Islam
154
      et al., 2022), or fusion of temporal chunks through transformer self-attention (Shi et al., 2024; Zhao
155
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b). However, these approaches typically assume the availability of
156   labeled data for all modalities during training, limiting their applicability to the UMA setting. While
157   these methods, inspired by ViT (Arnab et al., 2021) and SwinTransformers (Liu et al., 2022), chunk
158   continuous data to create input tokens for a transformer, they fundamentally differ from our approach.
159   Traditional methods restrict each token to a specific time segment, or shifted time segments, whereas
160   C3T learns time segments through temporal convolutions and performs alignment on these segments
161   across modalities. This novel technique of cross-modal temporal alignment showcases significant
      potential for further investigation across various research domains.

      3    BACKGROUND
163
164
      We investigate the creation of a multi-modal latent space for human action recognition, denoted as Z,
165
      that can be leveraged for UMA and exhibits robustness to input variations and noise. In this context,
166   robustness refers to the ability of the latent space to maintain consistent representations despite
167   shifts in input data distribution across modalities or variations in action execution speed or style. We
168   assume that there exists a learnable projection f (k) from every modality k ∈ 1, . . . , M to this latent
169   space Z, i.e., f (k) : X (k) → Z, such that the same actions viewed from different modalities map to
170   proximal points in Z, while distinct actions map to disparate regions. We further assume there exists
171   a learnable mapping h from the latent space Z to the label space of human actions Y, i.e., h : Z → Y.
172   This mapping should be invariant to the originating modality of the latent representation. Our method
173   leverages the intuition that proximity in the high-dimensional space Z indicates semantic similarity.
174   Points near zi ∈ Z likely map to the same class, allowing classification of neighboring vectors
175   regardless of their originating modality using the same decision boundary h. We use cosine similarity
176
      to measure "nearness," as it’s more effective in high-dimensional spaces than Euclidean distance.
      This choice: (1) focuses on directional similarity, (2) mitigates the "curse of dimensionality," and
177
      (3) aligns with the distributional hypothesis in representation learning. This approach is common in
178
      multimodal alignment (Radford et al., 2021).
179

312   Implementation: Our methods utilize three key neural network modules:
313
      (1) Video Feature Encoder f (1) : X (1) → Z: We employ a pretrained ResNet18 on every frame of
314
      the video followed by 3D convolution and a 2-layer MLP with ReLU activations. The Resnet is a
315
      well-established lightweight spatial feature extractor (Hara et al., 2017), and the 3D convolution is an
316   effective temporal feature extractor (Tran et al., 2018).
317
318   (2)IMU Feature Encoder f (2) : X (2) → Z: A 1D CNN followed by an MLP is used here. CNNs have
319   shown superior performance in extracting features from time-series data like IMU signals, efficiently
320
      capturing local patterns and temporal dependencies (Valarezo et al., 2017). Both feature encoders
      output the label for ST, the latent dimension vector for CA, and trec latent vectors for C3T.
321
322   (3) HAR Task Decoder h : Z → Y: ST does not require this module and CA uses a simple MLP. C3T
323   employs a self-attention module to better capture long-range dependencies in the latent space, which
      is particularly beneficial for complex action sequences (Moutik et al., 2023).

421   We conduct three primary sets of experiments to further gain insights from our UMA methods: one
422   investigates various training mechanisms of CA and C3T, the second examines the deployment of
423
      each model in different testing scenarios, and the third tests performance under temporal noise.
424   How do we train the CA and C3T Architectures?
425
      Experimental setup: We experimented with four ways to perform the two phases of training for CA
426
      and C3T. In this setup, all tests were done on the IMU data in DT est .
427
428   1. Align First: First align the representations generated by the RGB and IMU encoders, f (1) and
429      f (2) RGB on DAlign (phase b depicted in Figure 2). Then freeze the weights for both encoders and
430      train the HAR module h on RGB data in DHAR (phase a in Figure 2).
431   2. HAR First: Reverse the order from the previous method, first perform phase a), RGB HAR
         training, then freeze the RGB encoder f (1) and align the IMU encoder f (2) , phase b).


                                                               8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      3. Interspersed Training: Intermittently learn from DAlign and DHAR . The model learns an epoch
433      from a) and updates its weights to train the RGB HAR model, then learns an epoch from b) and
434      updates it’s weights to align the encoders. The model continues to iterate between the two losses.
435   4. Combined Loss: Train both phases a) and b) but within the same loss iteration. The loss from a)
436      on a batch of data from DHAR is added to the loss from b) on a batch of data from DAlign and the
437      total loss is then used to update the weights of the model: LTotal = LCE + LCL .
438
      Results: As shown in Table 3 Training, method 1. Align First performs the best for C3T, whereas 4.
439   Combined Loss performed the best for CA. The main experiments reported in this work (Table 1 use
440   training methods 1. and 4. for C3T and CA, respectively. We hypothesize that in method 2. training
441   the HAR model first yields a latent space that captures the best HAR features for RGB data, which is
442   not directly applicable to IMU data. This implies that cross-modal alignment may be a more difficult
443   task, i.e. adding a modality may require more restructuring of the latent space than adding an output
444   head. This reaffirms findings from existing methods such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), where linear
445   probing is used on aligned representations to learn new tasks. Method 3. faced instability in training
446   and was unable to converge. In method 4, for CA we believe one loss acted as a regularizer for the
447
      other pushing the latent space Z to the ideal balance for cross-modal transfer in HAR. However,
      given that C3T is a more difficult alignment problem, the contrastive loss was dominating the overall
448
      loss term preventing the model from learning to perform HAR well.

515
      We conducted comprehensive ablation experiments on our model architecture (Table 5), comparing
516
      convolutional and attention modules for RGB (spatial and temporal) and IMU (temporal) encoders.
517   Results generally favored convolutional architectures across various methods in our UMA setting.
518   Notably, C3T’s superior performance cannot be attributed solely to its attention head leveraging
519   temporal information, as ST or CA models with temporal attention did not perform comparably well.
520   Instead, C3T’s effectiveness stems from its unique method of alignment in the temporal space.
521
      Further ablation on C3T head architectures (Table 4) compared the class token-based self-attention
522
      head with alternatives: concatenating output attention tokens and projecting, adding latent vectors
523   Z1 . . . Ztrec and passing through an MLP, and concatenating vectors and using an MLP. The latter two
524   methods do not use attention. While concatenating latent vectors and using an MLP performed best
525   on clean data, the class token attention mechanism offered superior robustness to noise. The attention
526   visualization in Figure 5 corroborates these findings, showing the class token approach’s resilience to
527   shift noise. In addition, we notice all C3T variants outperformed CA and ST in UMA performance
528   (Table 1) on the UTD-MHAD dataset, emphasizing C3T’s strength in temporal alignment, regardless
529   of the classification head.

533
534   We explored the UMA framework for human action recognition, challenging models to perform
535   inference with an unlabeled modality during training. Our experiments focused on constructing a
536   unified latent space between modalities and comparing three UMA methods in various settings. Our
537   C3T method, integrating temporal convolutions with self-attention, showed promising results for
538   robust cross-modal transfer in UMA. Future work could explore continuous HAR with temporal
539   localization, and generalization to additional modalities and tasks.We hope our results inspire further
      exploitation of cross-modal latent spaces for more robust human motion understanding in AI models.

1069
1070         1. Early or data-level fusion combines the raw sensor outputs before any processing.
1071         2. Middle/intermediate or feature-level fusion combines each sensor modality after some

1074            models.

1129   modality, although sequential in nature, does not have a time dimension, thus it cannot leverage correlations
1130   between modalities in time like C3T.

1152   How quickly can our models learn from labeled IMU samples?
1153   As illustrated in Figure 12, CA demonstrates faster learning, reaching peak performance within 20 shots, while
1154   C3T requires about 40 shots. Neither method matches the supervised IMU performance of 87.9% reported in
1155   Table 1, but they approach the fusion performance of approximately 62%.
1156   It’s important to note that this comparison with the supervised baselines may not be entirely fair, as the supervised
1157   baselines had access to the entire Train HAR dataset (40% of the data), whereas, the few-shot learning was
1158   conducted on the validation set (10% of the data). Given that the supervised IMU and fusion models share the
       same architecture as CA, repurposed for the supervised setting, we would expect similar performance under
1159
       equal conditions.
