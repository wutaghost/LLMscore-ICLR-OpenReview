                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               We introduce a framework for expanding residual networks using jets, opera-
010
               tors that generalize truncated Taylor series. Our method provides a systematic
011            approach to disentangle contributions of different computational paths to model
012            predictions. In contrast to existing techniques such as distillation, probing, or
013            early decoding, our expansions rely solely on the model itself and requires no
014            data, training, or sampling from the model. We demonstrate how our framework
015            grounds and subsumes the logit lens, reveals a (super-)exponential path structure
016            in the network depth and opens up several applications. These include the ex-
017            traction of n-gram statistics from a transformer large language model, and the
018            definition of data-free toxicity scores. Our approach enables data-free analysis of
019
               residual networks for model interpretation, development, and evaluation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Machine learning models, particularly large-scale foundation models, have become increasingly
024
      prevalent and impactful across a wide range of domains (Wei et al., 2021; Bommasani et al., 2023;
025   Touvron et al., 2023b). While delivering strong results, their black-box nature has led to the de-
026   velopment of techniques to assess their behavior and gain insights into their internal mechanisms.
027   In this space, mechanistic interpretability (MI) (see e.g. Bereska & Gavves, 2024; Ferrando et al.,
028   2024, for recent surverys) has emerged as an alternative to more classic local attribution methods
029   such as SHAP (Lundberg, 2017) or integrated gradient (Sundararajan et al., 2017). Contrary to these
030   methods, which seeks to trace output behavior back to the network input, MI focuses on tracing be-
031   havior back to the model itself. It seeks to uncover learned “algorithms” that are embedded in the
032   model weights and computational structure, with the aim of developing a global understanding of –
033
      and, ultimately, to reverse engineer – neural computation.
034   The great majority of MI work uses a hypothesis-and-dataset-driven approach (see for example
035   Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023)), in that it first formalizes a hypothesis, then chooses or curates a
036   dataset to probe the model, it applies techniques such as path patching (Wang et al., 2022) or causal
037   tracing (Meng et al., 2022), and then possibly refines the initial hypothesis. While this approach
038   to MI is valuable, it can limit the ability to perform open-ended exploration-driven studies aimed
039
      at uncovering global behavior and charting “maps” that connect computation to behavior. In this
      regard, studies such as Veit et al. (2016) or Elhage et al. (2021) focus on the intrinsic computation
040
      that is carried out by a model, offering complementary views to the hypothesis-and-dataset-driven
041
      approach. Yet, these studies often make unrealistic assumptions of the model, making it unclear how
042   much of the derived understanding can be transferred to real-world models and applications.
043
044   This paper contributes to this latter direction, presenting a general-purpose framework to manipulate
045
      a residual computational graph with the aim of decomposing it into individual input-to-output com-
      putational paths, which we can then further analyze to extract behaviors. Our method is based on
046
      the simple observation that we can recursively expand a residual computational graph by selectively
047
      applying jet operators (Ehresmann, 1951), which one can think of as the functional counterpart of
048   truncated Taylor series. This process, which we call the jet expansion of a model, gives rise to a class
049   of equivalent functional rewritings of the original network into the sum of polynomial terms (that
050   we see as input-to-output functions and dub jet paths) and non-linear remainders. The framework
051   does not make particular assumptions on the input model and, as it operates in the space of func-
052   tions (rather than function evaluations), it requires no input data. For transformer language models,
053   we show how specific instantiations linked to n-gram models make it feasible to exhaustively eval-
      uate the jet paths over the entire input space, enabling end-to-end data-free global interpretability.

      We focus on residual networks (He et al., 2016), particularly transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017),
055   operating at the granularity of residual blocks (e.g., self-attention or MLP blocks). This approach
056   simplifies our presentation, aligns with (Veit et al., 2016), and remains relevant given the ubiquity
057   of residual computation in practice. In section 4, we describe several instantiations of our frame-
058   work, some encompassing previously proposed interpretability tools like LogitLens (nostalgebraist,
059   2021b). Based on these instantiations, we present extensive case studies on auto-regressive large
060   language models (LLMs) from varying families and sizes, including GPT, Llama and OLMo. Our
061   findings demonstrate that jet expansion offers a versatile toolkit – jet lens, jet paths and jet n-grams
062   – for interpreting LLMs: i) analyzing their inner working (section 5.2); ii) debugging pretraining
063
      dynamic (section 5.3); and iii) examining fine-tuning effects (section 5.4), contributing to more
      transparent and responsible LLM usage. We conclude with a discussion about potential future re-
064
      search directions that this work opens, alongside its current limitations.

      Runtime The runtime of algorithm 1 is negligible as it operates on the original computational
199   graph. Evaluating ξ (and δ) at any z ∈ Z requires computing kth-order jets with a complexity of
200   O(|C|(F +kB)), where F and B are the costs of forward and backward evaluations of q. In practice,
201   higher-order jets can be computed efficiently using stored computation (Griewank & Walther, 2008;
202   Bettencourt et al., 2019). Specifically, k-th order derivatives can be computed using recurrence
203   Dk f (x)(y − x) = jvp(Dk−1 f, x, y − x), where jvp computes the Jacobian-vector product and
204   it is available in most mainstream automatic differentiation frameworks like Pytorch. Appendix B
205   reports an example of runtime scaling with the jet order k in our implementation.

      The logit lens, aimed at highglighting the iterative refinement of the prediction across blocks, is
244   related to early exiting (or early decoding) in the context of conditional computation (see e.g. Panda
245   et al., 2016; Elbayad et al., 2020; Geva et al., 2022). It is immediate to verify that LogitLensl
246   is equivalent to the expansion yielded by jet expand(q, L, {hl }, 0). This suggests two general-
247   izations, which we dub iterative and joint jet lenses, respectively. The iterative jet lens is a direct
248   extension of the logit lenses that allows for higher order jets: jet expand(q, L, {hl }, k). The
249   joint jet lenses are expansions obtained through jet expand(q, L, {γl ◦ hl−1 }l∈[L] , k) that are
250   aimed at highlighting the residual contributions of each block non-linearity, rather than the iterative
251   refinement of the residual stream.
252   Jet bi-grams and skip-n-grams statistics. We consider transformer-based large language mod-
253   els with alternating self-attentions and MLPs, which are particular instances of residual nets. 4
254   Our framework allows us to directly extract n-gram statistics from an existing LLM without any
255
      probing datasets. Concretely, we can systematically evaluate relevant jet paths (for small n’s)

256
      individual contexts. For example, bi-grams statistics related to Pq (z2 |z1 , . . . ) can be computed
257

265   them into (symbolic) n-gram databases. Thus they allows us to perform symbolic model diffing

282   5       I NTERPRETING LLM S WITH JET EXPANSIONS
283
284   Our framework provides users with freedom in terms of choosing the computational paths they
285   wish to focus on. Jet expansions support studies across various levels, including model-level global
286   analysis (jet n-grams), component-level analysis (jet paths), and example-level analysis (jet lens).

302   We define some metrics used in our empirical study. 1) ∆ Logit after Intervention. We measure
303   the logit for an n-gram before and after applying an intervention (e.g., removing an attention head)
304   and compute the change at the last position. 2) One-to-One and Many-to-Many Bi-grams. One-
305   to-one bi-grams are unimodal, concentrating probability on a single token (e.g., z1 = &, z2 = amp).

308   metric measures the cumulative probability of the  P top 1K bigrams, weighted by an empirical un-
309

311
      ity of z2 given z1 , PD (z1 ) is the unigram probability of z1 from the empirical distribution. This
      metric evaluates how well the model assigns “correct” probability mass to bigrams, considering the
312

471   6   R ELATED WORK
472
473   Interpreting transformers. There has been much effort in interpreting the inner computations
474   of transformer models. In particular, mechanistic interpretability Ferrando et al. (2024), focuses
475   on reverse-engineering such computations by identifying, clustering and labelling model behavior
476   (Shah et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2022; Bricken et al., 2023) in human understandable terms and
477
      attributing them with certain model components, e.g., MLPs Geva et al. (2021; 2022), or typical
      “circuits” (Conmy et al., 2023; Ferrando & Voita, 2024). Authors discussed limitations of cur-
478
      rents approaches to MI. For example, Templeton et al. (2024) found it generally hard to conclude
479
      neuron-level intepretabilities, compared with feature representations; while Bolukbasi et al. (2021);
480   Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023) points out that conclusions drawn are generally limited to the chosen
481   data distribution. On a high level, allowing taking any portion of compute out of the original trans-
482   former, jet expansions abstract and generalize previous characterizations on the computational paths
483   (Veit et al., 2016; Elhage et al., 2021), where non-linear components with significant roles, e.g. lay-
484   ernorm and MLPs, are either ignored or over-simplified for the ease of analysis. Our approach also
485   does not require extra datasets that are used for probe fitting in methods such as Belrose et al. (2023)
      nor sampling, as needed in (Conmy et al., 2023; Ferrando & Voita, 2024; Voita et al., 2024).


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      n-gram models. The early applications of n-gram models in languages dates back to (Shannon,
487   1948), where n-grams modeled the statistics of English. The n-gram based approaches have been
488   an important baseline in language processing, e.g., general language modelling (Goodman, 2001)
489   with applications like machine translation (Brants et al., 2007). There have been regained interests
490   on combining n-gram with neural network model-based approaches (e.g. Liu et al., 2024). Several
491   recent works have explored the relationships between LLMs and n-gram language models, such as
492   analyzing the representational capacity of transformers to simulate n-gram LMs (Svete & Cotterell,
493   2024) and measuring agreement between LLM predictions and n-gram rulesets (Nguyen, 2024).
494
      Taylor expansion and jets Taylor expansions are popular tools in analyzing learning behaviours
495
      (Jastrzebski et al., 2017), notably linearization (k = 1). For example, Belrose et al. (2024) applied
496
      Taylor expansion on the loss function to demonstrate the learning preference of neural network
497   models. Xu et al. (2022) introduced a second-order Taylor expansion over the data distribution to
498   interpret optimal features. The generalized jet notions was introduced in machine learning in the
499   context automatic differentiation tools by Bettencourt et al. (2019), and is an experimental feature
500   in Jax (Bradbury et al., 2018), but has been studied before (see e.g. Griewank & Walther, 2008).

503   7   C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
504
505   We introduced jet expansion, a novel framework for expanding the computational graphs of neu-
506   ral networks. The method, which we specialize in this paper to deep residual nets, can be used
507   to disentangle contributions of user-selected computational paths from the overall graph. Comple-
508   mentary to other dataset-dependent methods in MI, our method enables various dataset-free global
509   interpretability studies, such as mapping computation to linguistic roles. We have validated jet ex-
510   pansions in terms of cosine similarity against model outputs and through interventional experiments
511
      (section 5.2). We applied our data-free method to transformer LMs, showing how we can sketch the
      original model with input-output probability databases, extracting LM bi-and-tri-gram statistics.
512
513   Limitations. Although rooted in Taylor series theory, expansions obtained via our frameworks do
514   not (seek to) approximate the input function in any strict sense. Rather, our framework is amed at
515   facilitating interpretation of model behavior: we can use jet expansion to rewrite an input compu-
516   tational graph as a sum of “interpretable” polynomial terms and a (computable) remainder. How
517   large is a reminder and how expansions align with model outputs remains at the moment an em-
518   pirical question, implying that the jet order and weight optimization routines should generally be
519   considered as hyperparameters of the method. Furthermore, expansions are not unique (but higher
520   order expansions ”contain” lower order one). We leave a deeper investigation of these aspects to
521
      future work. From a runtime standpoint, we note that even though graph manipulation is almost
      immediate, systematic evaluation of jet paths may be time consuming (especially for k ≫ 0 and
522
      when optimizing jet weights). If the input space is large, one may need to resort to sub-sampling or
523
      search heuristics. Finally, we limited our study of n-gram expansions of LMs to bi-and-tri-grams,
524   unearthing compelling behaviors. This leaves the study of longer-context expansions to future work.
525
526   Implications and future work. Our work opens up several research directions. From a theoretical
527   standpoint, we will extend the expansion procedure to cover finer granularities, e.g. at neuron (sub-
528   space) levels; incorporate established attribution methods such as the Shapley value (Shapley et al.,
529   1953), including recent extensions to deal with probabilistic models (Franceschi et al., 2024); de-
530   velop concepts of (approximate) equivalence classes over models leveraging the jet spaces, which, in
531
      turn, may further ground the model diffing procedure sketched in our case studies. Furthermore, we
      will take inspiration from the numerous tools in linear algebra to provide further depth into the anal-
532
      ysis, deepening the link to linear residual structures and establishing relations with Markov chains
533
      and hidden Markov models, recently employed e.g. by Zhang et al. (2023) for constrained (struc-
534   tured) decoding. We plan to investigate the implication of the super-exponential number of paths
535   in the residual networks depth unearthed by algorithm 2. From an applications standpoint, besides
536   studying jet n-grams for n > 3, we envision several fruitful applications in safety and transparency,
537   such as developing “search features” to systematically detect unwanted associations, or leaked pri-
538   vate content. Although our experiments are primarily observational, we speculate that jet expand
539   may also become an useful tool to guide interventions, supplementing other techniques like causal
      tracing (Meng et al., 2022) and path patching (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023).

758   A jet of a function represents an equivalence class. We thus can perform algebraic operations among
759   functional equivalence classes using jet algebra stated below.
760   Proposition 1 (Jet algebra). Let f, g ∈ C ∞ (Rd , Rd ) and k ∈ N+ . Then,
761
762        (i) Jk (af + bg)(x) = a Jk (f )(x) + b Jk (g)(x), for a, b ∈ R (linearity);
763
          (ii) Jk f (x) ◦ g ∈ Jk f (x) and Jk f (x) ◦ g(y) = Jk f (x)(g(y)) (jet after endomorphisms);
764
765       (iii) g ◦ Jk f (x) = {g ◦ u : u ∈ Jk f (x)} (endomorphism after jet);
766       (iv) Jk (f ◦ g)(x) = Jk f (g(x)) ◦ Jk g(x) (composition of jets);
767
768   Properties (i)-(iii) follow directly from the definition; (iv) is a consequence of the chain rule and
769   truncation.

      Jet Expansions for In-Model n-Grams Jet expansions allow us to define n-grams statistics that
      are derived solely and directly from the model itself – producing in-model n-grams rather than
826
      in-data n-grams. This approach offers at least two significant advantages:
827
828          • No Dataset Preparation: It eliminates the need for dataset preparation to collect activation
829            patterns when interpreting the model globally, thereby saving time and computational re-
830            sources. This process can be conducted entirely on CPU, which is approximately 10 times
831            cheaper per hour compared to GPUs in the current market.
832
             • Avoidance of Fitting Artifacts: It avoids potential artifacts that could arise from the se-
833            lection of external n-gram fitting methods.
834

836   studies, and the jet expansion as follows.

877   One-to-one bi-grams like and Many-to-many bi-grams One-to-one bi-bigrams are (approxi-
878   mately) unimodal bi-grams that concentrate all mass on a single token: i.e. given z1 , P D(z2 |z1 ) ≈ 1
879   and given z2 , PD (z1 |z2 ) ≈ 1 for a specific pair of token and close to 0 for all others. In the example
880   in the paper, z1 = “&”, and z2 = “amp”. PD is the probability distribution induced by the pre-
881   training data. Many-to-many bi-grams we refer to the opposite scenario where both the conditional
882   probabilities are highly multi-modal. In the example z1 =”make” and z2 =”sure” we have that
883   many other tokens can succeed z1 =”make” or precede z2 =”sure”.
884
885   Hit Ratios of bi-grams The Hit Ratio (HR@n), often referred to as hit rate, is a metric commonly
886   used in ranking tasks. In our context, we treat each checkpoint of the language model as a ”ranker”
887   of bigrams. The Hit Ratio measures how effectively the current model checkpoint retrieves high-
888
      quality bigrams from the set of all possible bigrams. To quantify the model’s progress, we define
      the bigrams at the final step as the “good” bigrams and measure how quickly the model approaches
889
      these high-quality bigrams. Specifically, we compute the HR@n to evaluate how often the model’s
890
      output bigrams match those in the “true” top n ranked bi-grams given by the final step. Formally,
891   the Hit Ratio@n is given by
