009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning methods have yielded exceptional performances in material prop-
012            erty regression (MPR). However, most existing methods operate under the as-
013            sumption that the training and test are independent and identically distributed
014            (i.i.d.). This overlooks the importance of extrapolation - predicting material prop-
015            erties beyond the range of training data - which is essential for advanced material
016            discovery, as researchers strive to identify materials with exceptional properties
017            that exceed current capabilities. In this paper, we address this gap by introduc-
018
               ing a comprehensive benchmark comprising seven tasks specifically designed to
               evaluate extrapolation in MPR. We critically evaluate existing methods includ-
019
               ing deep imbalanced regression (DIR) and regression data augmentation (DA)
020
               methods, and reveal their limitations in extrapolation tasks. To address these is-
021            sues, we propose the Matching-based EXtrapolation (MEX) framework, which
022            reframes MPR as a material-property matching problem to alleviate the inherent
023            complexity of the direct material-to-label mapping paradigm for better extrapola-
024            tion. Our experimental results show that MEX outperforms all existing methods
025            on our benchmark and demonstrates exceptional capability in identifying promis-
026            ing materials, underscoring its potential for advancing material discovery.

064   In response, we propose Matching-based EXtrapolation (MEX), a novel framework that reframes
065   MPR as a material-property matching problem, aimed at simplifying the complexity of target func-
066   tions to enhance model extrapolation. Our intuition is that matching - focusing on the proximity
067   between material and property representations rather than precise value predictions - reduces learn-
068   ing difficulty and improves extrapolation. Specifically, MEX employs two complementary training
069
      objectives to learn aligned feature spaces for material and property representation matching. First,
      it performs absolute matching optimization using negative cosine similarity loss, which pulls paired
070
      material and label representations closer together. Second, MEX leverages Noise Contrastive Esti-
071
      mation (NCE) (Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010) to force the model to distinguish between target and
072   noisy labels, thus capturing fine-grained relative matching relationships. Within the well-aligned
073   latent spaces, MEX predicts by optimizing for the nearest target value for a given sample. Experi-
074   ments show that MEX not only achieves the best performance on our benchmark but also exhibits
075   extraordinary detection capability for promising materials, demonstrating superior extrapolation ca-
076   pabilities and potential for more robust material discovery.
077
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
078
079   • We highlight the critical importance of extrapolation in MPR, an area that has been previously
080     understudied yet holds significant implications for realistic material design scenarios.
081   • We curate a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to evaluate extrapolation in material
082     properties regression, and thoroughly investigate the effectiveness of deep imbalanced regression
083     (DIR) and regression data augmentation (DA) methods on extrapolation tasks, revealing their
084     limitations in handling the complexities of MPR.
085   • We propose MEX, a simple yet effective framework that substantially enhances extrapolation
086     capabilities, achieving state-of-the-art performance on our benchmark.

088   2   RELATED WORK
089
090   Material property prediction. Recent years have witnessed the tremendous impact of deep learn-
091   ing on predicting material properties (Schütt et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2022; Shoghi et al., 2024).
092   Considering the 3D atomic systems’ essence of material data, numerous studies have aimed to
093   enhance neural architectures to effectively capture the intrinsic physical symmetries of such data.
094   SchNet (Schütt et al., 2018) and CGCNN (Xie & Grossman, 2018) pioneered the use of graph neu-
095   ral networks for 3D atomic systems, which modeled the pairwise atomic distance variant with regard
096   to Euclidean transformations. Since then, a body of research has focused on encoding higher-order
097   geometric invariants (Klicpera et al., 2020; Gasteiger et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2022) and equivari-
098
      ants (Schütt et al., 2021; Passaro & Zitnick, 2023; Liao et al., 2024).
099   Another area of focus lies in pre-training to learn transferable material representations (Zhang et al.,
100   2023; Shoghi et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024). For instance, Shoghi et al. (2024) propose joint pre-
101   training on force and energy prediction tasks across different chemical domains and show impres-
102   sive transfer performance to downstream tasks. Song et al. (2024) employed a self-supervised pre-
103   training task via crystal structure reconstruction based on diffusion models. Orthogonal to existing
104
      research efforts, our work focuses on the overlooked issue of extrapolation in MRP and approaches
      it from a unique training strategy perspective, which can use any model architecture and pre-trained
105
      model as backbones.

139   3        METHODOLOGY
140
141   3.1        P ROBLEM D EFINITION
142
143   We define MPR extrapolation tasks as predicting unobserved material property values that lie outside
144   the training label range. Formally, let the input space and label space be denoted as X and Y, where
145
      X contains the structural data of materials, and Y ⊂ R corresponds to a continuous range of labels.
      The training domain and target domain are respectively defined as Dtrain = {(x, y) | (x, y) ∈
146
      X × Ytrain } and Dtarget = {(x, y) | (x, y) ∈ X × Ytarget }, where Ytrain and Ytarget are two disjoint
147
      subspaces of Y, i.e.,
148
149                          Ytarget ⊂ y ∈ Y | y > max(Ytrain ) ∨ y < min(Ytrain )
150
      The goal of extrapolation is to learn a model f : X → Y that minimizes the extrapolation error
151
      E(x,y)∼Dtarget [ℓ(f (x), y)], where ℓ : R × R → R is the loss function. Note that the model can only
152
      utilize Dtrain without further adapting to Dtarget during training.
153
154
      3.2        M ATCHING - BASED EXTRAPOLATION
155
156   In contrast to directly mapping materials to properties, we argue that learning the matching relation-
157   ship between them presents a simpler learning target, facilitating model generalization in previously
158   unseen label ranges. Given a training set comprising N examples Dtrain = {(xi , yi )}N i=1 , we aim to
159   learn a binary matching function M(x, y) that output high values for a paired sample x and label y,
160   while assigning lower values to unpaired ones. MEX parameterizes M(x, y) as Sim(Es (x), El (y)),
161   where Es (·) : X → Rd represents the material encoder, El (·) : R → Rd represents the label en-
      coder, and Sim(·, ·) : Rd × Rd → R is the cosine similarity between two vectors. We denote the


                                                          3

162
      encoded material and label as z s and z l , respectively. Variables with subscript i correspond to the
163   i-th example in the training set.

      4.1    DATASET
251
252   We curated a range of extrapolative MPR benchmark datasets using four datasets from
253   Matminer (Ward et al., 2018) covering the following properties: formation energy, shear mod-
254   ulus, refractive index, and phonons mode peak, with dataset size ranging from 1,265 to 18,928.
255   Figure 3 shows the label distribution of the raw datasets and more dataset characteristics are pro-
256   vided in Table 5. These datasets are split into train/validation/test sets with a ratio of 7:1.5:1.5.
257   Rather than employing conventional partition methods like random splitting, we selected extreme
258
      target values for extrapolation evaluation. Specifically, each dataset is first sorted by the property
      values, and the top (or bottom) 15%, along with the second top (or bottom) 15%, were used as the
259
      test and validation sets, respectively. Whether we select the top or bottom extremes is determined
260
      by the specific property desired in material design scenarios. For example, lower formation energy
261   indicates higher material stability, prompting researchers to search for materials with extremely low
262   formation energies. Thus, for such properties, we select the bottom values for evaluation. For prop-
263   erties where both low and high values are of interest, e.g., shear modulus, the dataset is split with the
264   top and bottom configuration once each to ensure comprehensive evaluation across the spectrum.
265   Details of the resulting seven benchmark datasets are shown in Table 1.

       4.4       P OTENTIAL IMPACT ON CUTTING - EDGE MATERIAL DISCOVERY
401
402
       As discussed in Section 4.3, extrapolation presents a significant challenge for methods in the lit-
403    erature, and our approach is no exception. Given the inherent limitations of neural networks in
404    extrapolating (Xu et al., 2021), one may wonder: To what extent can current deep learning methods
405    assist in the discovery of cutting-edge materials?
406
       In addition to accurately predicting the property values of extrapolative samples, we contend that the
407
       ability to detect materials with potentially groundbreaking properties is also crucial. Once identified,
408
       these candidates can be further refined using first-principles methods, such as Density Functional
409    Theory (DFT), to compute more precise properties. Consequently, models’ detection capabilities
410    could become vital tools in advancing material discovery.
411
412
       To assess the effectiveness of different methods in detecting materials within extrapolation regions,
       we present their recall rates in Figure 5. Specifically, for samples from the validation and test sets in
413
       our benchmark, a sample is considered detected if its predicted value falls within the extrapolation
414
       interval, and the recall rate is calculated as the proportion of such samples correctly identified. As
415    shown, MEX outperforms previous methods in 6 out of 7 detection tasks. Notably, it achieves a
416    recall rate of over 80% on three datasets and exceeds 60% on six datasets. This substantial per-
417    formance advantage demonstrates the robustness of MEX and highlights its potential to identify
418    cutting-edge materials that might otherwise be overlooked.

       Score module analysis. The score module is a critical component in learning fine-grained rela-
       tionships between sample and label. Here, we investigate the effects of various design choices. The
424
       first, referred to as MEX (mlp+cos), employs two independent 2-layer MLPs to project the sam-
425
       ple and label representations into a new space, after which the cosine similarity between the two
426    projections is computed. The second approach, MEX (cos), directly computes the cosine similarity
427    between the original sample and label representations. As illustrated in Table 4, MEX and MEX
428    (mlp+cos) exhibit comparable performance, while MEX (cos) demonstrates inferior performance
429    relative to the other designs. This observation aligns with findings in SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020),
430    which indicate that incorporating a learnable nonlinear transformation on the representations be-
431    fore applying the contrastive loss, rather than directly optimizing the representations, significantly
       enhances the quality of the learned features.

      5   CONCLUSION
472
473
      In this work, we shed light on the challenging task of extrapolation in material property regres-
      sion (MPR), which aims to generalize to materials with unseen property values. We introduce a
474
      new benchmark consisting of seven MPR tasks and provide a comprehensive evaluation of existing
475
      methods’ extrapolation capabilities. To address the task, we propose a simple yet effective frame-
476   work that captures the sample-label matching relationship in the latent space. Extensive experiments
477   demonstrate the superior performance of our approach and highlight its potential application in the
478   discovery of cutting-edge materials.

682   ns ← initial noise scale;

               C                    C
                                            i=1 );

689       for i ← 1 to C do

              ϵi ∼ N (0, 1);
              yi ← yi + ϵi ∗ ns;

              yi ← clip(yi , l, u);
