009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we address the problem of cost-sensitive multi-fidelity Bayesian
012            Optimization (BO) for efficient hyperparameter optimization (HPO). Specifically,
013            we assume a scenario where users want to early-stop the BO when the performance
014            improvement is not satisfactory with respect to the required computational cost.
015            Motivated by this scenario, we introduce utility, which is a function describing
016            the trade-off between cost and performance of BO and can be estimated from the
017            user’s preference data. This utility function, combined with our novel acquisition
018
               function and stopping criterion, allows us to dynamically choose for each BO step
               the best configuration that we expect to maximally improve the utility in future, and
019
               also automatically stop the BO around the maximum utility. Further, we improve
020
               the sample efficiency of existing learning curve (LC) extrapolation methods with
021            transfer learning to develop a sensible surrogate function for multi-fidelity BO.
022            We validate our algorithm on various LC datasets and found it outperform all
023            the previous multi-fidelity BO and transfer-BO baselines we consider, achieving
024            significantly better trade-off between cost and performance of BO.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Hyperparameter optimization (HPO) (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Bergstra et al., 2011; Hutter et al.,
030   2011; Snoek et al., 2012; Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022; Li et al., 2018; Franceschi et al., 2017) stands as a
031   crucial challenge in the domain of deep learning, given its importance of achieving optimal empirical
032   performance. Unfortunately, the field of HPO for deep learning remains relatively underexplored,
033
      with many practitioners resorting to simple trial-and-error methods (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012; Li et al.,
      2018). Moreover, traditional black-box Bayesian optimization (BO) approaches for HPO (Bergstra
034
      et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022) have limitations when applied to deep
035
      neural networks due to the impracticality of evaluating a vast number of hyperparameter configurations
036   until convergence, each of which may take several days.
037
038   Recently, multi-fidelity (or gray-box) BO (Li et al., 2018; Falkner et al., 2018; Awad et al., 2021;
039
      Swersky et al., 2014; Wistuba et al., 2022; Arango et al., 2023; Kadra et al., 2023; Rakotoarison
      et al., 2024) has gained increasing attention to improve the sample efficiency of traditional black-box
040
      BO. Multi-fidelity BO makes use of lower fidelity information (e.g., validation accuracies at fewer
041
      training epoches) to predict and optimize the performances at higher or full fidelity (e.g., validation
042   accuracies at the last training epoch). Unlike black-box BO, multi-fidelity BO dynamically selects
043   hyperparameter configurations even before finishing a single training run, demonstrating its ability of
044   finding better configurations in a more sample efficient manner than black-box BO.
045
      However, one critical limitation of the conventional multi-fidelity BO frameworks is that they are
046
      not aware of the trade-off between the cost and performance of BO. For instance, given a limited
047
      amount of total credits, customers of cloud computing services (e.g., GCP, AWS, or Azure) may
048   choose to heavily penalize the cost of BO relative to its performance, in order to conserve credits
049   for other tasks. A similar scenario applies to users of task managers such as Slurm, who aim to
050   optimize their allocated time within a computing instance. In those cases, users may want the BO
051   process to focus on exploiting the current belief on good hyperparameter configurations than trying
052   to explore new configurations, in order to efficiently consuming their limited resources. Yet, the
053   existing multi-fidelity BO methods tend to over-explore because they usually assume a sufficiently
      large budget (e.g., total credits, or allocated time) for the BO and aim to obtain the best asymptotic

056   maximum budget, but in practice it is hard to specify the target budget in advance as it is difficult to

088

089   assume that users want to stop the BO as early as possible, LC extrapolation should be accurate even

091   improve the sample efficiency of BO (Arango et al., 2023) and to prevent inaccurate early-stopping
092   when there are only few or even no observations in the BO.
093
      Based on those criteria, we introduce our novel Cost-sensitive Multi-fidelity BO (CMBO) that can
094
      effectively maximize the utility based on the three components mentioned above. We first introduce
095   the detailed notion of utility function and how to estimate it from the user’s own preference data1
096   (§3.1) . We then describe the acquisition function and stopping criterion specifically developed
097   for our framework, and explain how to achieve a good trade-off between cost and performance
098   of multi-fidelity BO with them (§3.2). Building on the recently introduced Prior-Fitted Networks
099   (PFNs) (Müller et al., 2021; Adriaensen et al., 2023) for in-context Bayesian inference, we explain
100   how to train a PFN with the existing LC datasets to develop a sample efficient in-context surrogate
101   function for freeze-thaw BO that can also effectively capture the correlations between different
102   hyperparameter configurations (§3.3). Lastly, we empirically demonstrate the superiority of CMBO
103
      on a set of diverse utility functions, three multi-fidelity HPO benchmarks, and one real-world
      object-detection LC dataset we collected, showing that it significantly outperforms all the previous
104
      multi-fidelity BO and the transfer-BO baselines we consider (§4).

      We summarize our contributions and findings as follows:
109
110   • We introduce the concept of utility, which describes the trade-off between cost and performance of
111     multi-fidelity BO, along with the method to quantify it with user preference data.
112   • We propose a new problem formulation, cost-sensitive multi-fidelity HPO, where we aim to
113     maximize utility instead of maximizing the asymptotic validation performances.
114   • We introduce our novel acquisition function and stopping criterion specifically designed for our
115     problem formulation, along with the transfer learning of in-context LC extrapolation.
116   • We extensively validate the superiority of CMBO on various cost-sensitive multi-fidelity HPO set-
117     tings, with three popular benchmarks and one real-world object detection LC dataset we collected.

119   2     R ELATED W ORK
120
121   We briefly discuss the related work in this section. See §A for the other related work on multi-fidelity
122   HPO, transfer BO, cost-sensitive HPO, and BO with user preference.
123   Freeze-thaw BO. Freeze-thaw BO (Swersky et al., 2014) dynamically pauses (freezes) and resumes
124   (thaws) configurations based on the last epoch performances extrapolated from a set of partially
125   observed LCs obtained from other configurations, leading to an efficient and sensible allocation of
126   computational resources. DyHPO (Wistuba et al., 2022) and its transfer version (Arango et al., 2023)
127   improve the computational efficiency of freeze-thaw BO with deep kernel GP (Wilson et al., 2016),
128   but their acquisition extrapolates the LCs only a one-step forward, producing a greedy strategy. Other
129   recent variants of freeze-thaw BO include DPL (Kadra et al., 2023) and ifBO (Rakotoarison et al.,
130   2024) which are not greedy, and their acquisitions maximize the performance either at the last BO
131
      step or random future steps. On the other hand, we maximize the utility specified by each user.
132   Learning curve extrapolation. Freeze-thaw BO requires the ability of dynamically updating
133   predictions on future performances from partially observed LCs, thus heavily relies on LC extrap-
134   olation (Baker et al., 2017; Gargiani et al., 2019; Wistuba & Pedapati, 2020). DyHPO (Wistuba
135   et al., 2022) and Quick-Tune (Arango et al., 2023) propose to extrapolate LCs for only a single
136
      step forward. Freeze-thaw BO (Swersky et al., 2014) and DPL (Kadra et al., 2023) use non-greedy
      extrapolations but limit the shape of the LCs. Domhan et al. (2015) consider a broader set of basis
137
      functions, but requires computationally expensive MCMC, and also do not consider correlations
138
      between different configurations. Klein et al. (2017b) models interactions between configurations
139   with a Bayesian neural network (BNN), but suffers from the same computational inefficiency of
140   MCMC and online retraining. LC-PFNs (Adriaensen et al., 2023) are an in-context Bayesian LC
141   extrapolation method without retraining, but they do not consider interactions between configurations.
142   Recently, ifBO (Rakotoarison et al., 2024) further combine LC-PFNs with PFNs4BO (Müller et al.,
143   2023) to develop an in-context surrogate function for freeze-thaw BO, but they train PFNs only with
144   a prior distribution. On the other hand, we use transfer learning, i.e., train PFNs with the existing
145   LC datasets, to improve the sample efficiency of freeze-thaw BO while successfully encoding the
146   correlations between configurations at the same time.

      3.3   T RANSFER L EARNING OF LC E XTRAPOLATION
279
280   Since users may want to early-stop the BO, we should have a sample efficient LC extrapolation
281   for preventing inaccurate early-stopping at the early stage of BO. We thus propose to use transfer
282   learning to maximally improve the sample efficiency of our LC extrapolator.
283   Transfer learning with LC mixup. Among many plausible options, in this paper we propose to
284   use Prior Fitted Networks (PFNs) (Müller et al., 2021) for LC extrapolation. PFNs are an in-context
285   Bayesian inference method based on Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017), and show good
286   performances on LC extrapolation (Adriaensen et al., 2023; Rakotoarison et al., 2024) without the
287   computationally expensive online retraining (Kadra et al., 2023). A major difficulty of using PFNs
288   for our purpose is that their training examples are generated only from a prior distribution, and to
289   our knowledge, there are no existing ways to train PFNs with the given datasets. Also, PFNs require
290   relatively a large Transformer architecture as well as huge amounts of training examples for good
291
      generalization performance (Adriaensen et al., 2023), which makes it risky to train PFNs with a finite
      set of examples.
292

      m-th dataset and the n-th configuration, forming a complete LC of length T . Further define the matrix
                  ⊤               ⊤
      Lm = [lm,1       ; . . . ; lm,N ]⊤ , the row-wise stack of those LCs. In order to augment training examples,
298
      we propose to perform the following two consecutive mixups (Zhang et al., 2018):
299
300   1. Across datasets: L′ = λ1 Lm + (1 − λ1 )Lm′ , with λ1 ∼ Unif(0, 1), for all m, m′ ∈ [M ].
301   2. Across configurations: (x′′ , l′′ ) = λ2 (xn , ln′ ) + (1 − λ2 )(xn′ , ln′ ′ )
302      .                    with ln′ the n-th row of L′ , λ2 ∼ Unif(0, 1), for all L′ and n, n′ ∈ [N ].
303
304
      In this way, we can sample infinitely many training examples {(x′′ , l′′ )} by interpolating between the
      LCs, leading to a robust LC extrapolator with less overfitting. Note that in the first step, we do not
305
      individually perform the mixup over the configurations but apply the same λ1 to all the configurations,
306
      in order to preserve the correlations between the configurations encoded in the given datasets.
307
308   As for the network architecture and the training objective, we mostly follow Rakotoarison et al. (Rako-
309   toarison et al., 2024). We use a similar Transformer architecture that takes a set of partial LCs and
310
      the corresponding configurations as an input and extrapolates the remaining part of the LCs. The
      training objective then maximizes the likelihood of those predictions conditioned on the partial LCs.
311
      We defer more details on the training to §E. Also, see §G for more discussion about the connection of
312
      our transfer learning method with ifBO (Rakotoarison et al., 2024) TNPs (Nguyen & Grover, 2022).

      A    R ELATED W ORK
757
758
759   Multi-fidelity HPO. Unlike traditional black-box approaches for HPO (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012;
760   Hutter et al., 2011; Bergstra et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; 2015; 2014; Cowen-Rivers et al., 2022;
761   Müller et al., 2023), multi-fidelity (or gray-box) HPO aims to optimize hyperparameters in a sample
762   efficient manner by utilizing low fidelity information (e.g., validation set performances with smaller
763   training dataset) as a proxy for higher or full fidelities (Swersky et al., 2013; Kandasamy et al.,
764   2016; Klein et al., 2017a; Poloczek et al., 2017; Kandasamy et al., 2017; Wu & Frazier, 2018;
765   Wu et al., 2020), dramatically speeding up the HPO. In this paper, we focus on making use of
766
      performances at fewer training epochs to better predict/optimize the performances at longer training
      epochs. One of the well-known examples is Hyperband (Li et al., 2018), a bandit-based method
767
      that randomly selects a set of random hyperparameter configurations, and stops poorly performing
768
      ones using successive halving (Karnin et al., 2013) even before reaching the last training epoch.
769   While Hyperband shows much better performance than random search (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012),
770   its computational or sample efficiency can be further improved by replacing random sampling of
771   configurations with Bayesian optimization (Falkner et al., 2018), adopting evolution strategy to
772   promote internal knowledge transfer (Awad et al., 2021), or making it asynchronously parallel (Li
773   et al., 2020).
774
775
776
      Transfer BO. Transfer learning can be used for improving the sample efficiency of BO (Bai et al.,
777   2023), and here we list a few of them. Some of recent works explore scalable transfer learning with
778   deep neural networks (Perrone et al., 2018; Wistuba & Grabocka, 2020). Also, different components
779   of BO can be transferred such as observations (Swersky et al., 2013), surrogate functions (Golovin
780   et al., 2017; Wistuba & Grabocka, 2020), hyperparmater initializations (Wistuba & Grabocka, 2020),
781   or all of them (Wei et al., 2021). However, most of the existing transfer-BO approaches assume the
782   traditional black-box BO settings. To the best of our knowledge, Quick-Tune (Arango et al., 2023)
783   is the only recent work which targets multi-fidelity and transfer BO at the same time. However, as
784   mentioned above, their multi-fidelity BO formulation is greedy, whereas our transfer-BO method can
785
      dynamically control the degree of greediness during the BO by explicitly taking into consideration
      the trade-off between cost and performance of BO.
786
787
788
789   Cost-sensitive HPO. Multi-fidelity BO is inherently cost-sensitive since predictions get more
790   accurate as the gap between the fidelities becomes closer. However, the performance metric of such
791   vanilla multi-fidelity BO monotonically increases as we spend more budget, whereas in this paper we
792   want to find the optimal trade-off between the amount of budget spent thus far and the corresponding
793
      intermediate performances of BO, thereby automatically early-stopping the BO around the maximal
      utility. Quick-Tune (Arango et al., 2023) also suggests a cost-sensitive BO in multi-fidelity settings,
794
      but unlike our work, their primary focus is to trade-off between the performance and the cost of BO
795
      associated with pretrained models of various size, which can be seen as a generalization of more
796   traditional notion of cost-sensitive BO (Snoek et al., 2012; Abdolshah et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020),
797   from black-box to multi-fidelity settings. In addition to the above discussion, Makarova et al. (2022)
798   propose a stopping criterion which terminates the BO when the suboptimality in optimizing validation
799   performance (instead of the test performance) is dominated by the statistical estimation error. Roughly
800   speaking, this stopping criterion can be seen as an instance of our utility-based stopping criteria,
801   where the user preference is not willing to spend further computational budgets after reaching the
802   certain BO performance. Furthermore, in this paper we focus on maximizing any possible utility
803   functions defined by user, instead of minimizing population error.
804
805
806   BO with user preference. Several works have tried to encode user’s initial belief on good hyperpa-
807   rameter configurations into BO frameworks (Souza et al., 2021; Hvarfner et al., 2021; Mallik et al.,
808   2024). On the othet hand, our paper suggests encoding user’s preference about the trade-off between
809   cost and performance of multi-fidelity BO. Therefore, the notion of user preference in this paper is
      largely different from the previous literature.

812
      As mentioned in the main paper, it is not easy for users to define or quantify their utilities. Here we
813
      first briefly remind the notion of utility and then detail how we simulate the estimation of user utility
814
      with Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952).
815
816
817   Utility. A utility function U describes the trade-off between the BO step b and the BO performance
818
      ỹb . Its values U (b, ỹb ) negatively correlate with b and positively with ỹb . For instance, we can
      assume a linear utility function U (b, ỹb ) = ỹb − αb for some α > 0, such that the utility gives linear
819
      incentives and penalties to the performance and number of BO steps, respectively.
820
821
822   Functional forms. In real-world scenario, however, one can have much more complex utility
823   function other than the above linear case. We therefore consider the following additional functional
824   formsP  including linear one: 1) Linear: U (linear) (b, y) = ỹ − αb, 2) Staircase: U (stair) (b, ỹb ) =
825
      ỹb − i αi 1(b ∈ Ai ) (1(·) is an indicator function, and Ai is an i-th interval), 3) Quadratic:
826
      U (quad) (b, ỹb ) = ỹb − αb2 , and 4) Square Root: U (sqrt) (b, ỹb ) = ỹb − αb1/2 . In contrast to the
      notation of main paper, we normalize the budget b by the allowed total computation budget B, i.e.,
827
      b ∈ [0, 1] instead of b ∈ {1, . . . , B}. Furthermore, we assume that these utility functions can be
828
      linearly combined, e.g., U = w(linear) U (linear) + . . . + w(sqrt) U (sqrt) , where w(linear) + . . . + w(sqrt) = 1.
829
830
831   Data collection. We now describe how we roughly estimate user utility based on the user preference
832   data pairs. First of all, we assume that it is possible for users to decide whether they prefer one point
833   to the other one, instead of quantifying their utility, i.e., we can collect user preference data. For
834
      simulation, we assume that we are given these preference data generated by true utility function. True
      utility function U is randomly defined by sampling penalizing coefficient from U(0, 1) and linear
835
      combination coefficients from Dirichlet distribution. We randomly select meaningful data pairs from
836
      b ∼ U(0, 1) and ỹb ∼ U(0, 1). Here, meaningful data pairs means that one datapoint of each pair
837   is not trivially preferred by user, for example, one has larger performance ỹb with smaller budget b
838   than the other. A user then label their preference on these data pairs; for simulation, we label them by
839   using the true utility functions.

1090   3. DPL. We follow the official code5 provided the authors of DPL (Kadra et al., 2023), and slightly
1091      modify the benchmark implementation to incorporate our experimental setups.
1092   4. ifBO. We follow the official code6 provided the authors for surrogate model of ifBO (Rakotoarison
1093      et al., 2024), and incorporate the surrogate model in our code base to be aligned with our
1094      experimental setups.
1095   5. DyHPO and Quick-Tune† . We follow the official code7 provided the authors of DyHPO (Wistuba
1096      et al., 2022), and slightly modify the benchmark implementation to incorporate our experimental
1097      setups. For Quick-Tune† , we pretrain the deep kernel GP for 50000 iterations with Adam optimizer
1098      with mini-batch size of 512. The initial learning rate is set to 1e-03 and decayed with cosine
1099      scheduling. To leverage the transfer learning scenario, we use the best configuration among the
1100      LC datasets which is used for training the GP as an initial guess of BO.
1101   6. FSBO. FSBO does not provide an official code, therefore, we follow an available code in the
1102      internet8 . We also slightly modify the benchmark implementation, and use the best configuration
1103      among the LC datasets as an initial guess.
1104
1105   G       C ONNECTION BETWEEN OUR M IXUP S TRATEGY WITH IF BO AND TNP
1106
1107   Our mixup strategy is reminiscent of the data generation scheme of ifBO (Rakotoarison et al., 2024),
1108   a variant of PFNs for in-context freeze-thaw BO. Similarly to our ancestral sampling, ifBO first
1109   samples random weights for a neural network (i.e., a prior distribution) to sample a correlation
1110   between configurations (the first mixup step), and then linearly combines a set of basis functions
1111   to generate LCs (the second mixup step). Our training method differs from ifBO in that our prior
1112   distribution is implicitly defined by LC datasets and the mixup strategy, whereas ifBO resorts to a
1113
       manually defined distribution.
1114   Indeed, our training method is more similar to Transformer Neural Processes (TNPs) (Nguyen &
1115   Grover, 2022), a Transformer variant of Neural Processes (NPs) (Garnelo et al., 2018). Similarly to
1116   PFNs, TNPs directly maximize the likelihood of target data given context data with a Transformer
1117   architecture, which differs from the typical NP variants that summarize the context into a latent
1118   variable and perform variational inference on it. Moreover, as with the other NP variants, TNPs
1119
       meta-learn a model over a distribution of tasks to perform sample efficient probabilistic inference. In
       this vein, the whole training pipeline of our LC extrapolator can be seen as an instance of TNPs – we
1120
       also meta-learn a sample efficient Transformer-based LC extrapolator over the distribution of LCs
1121
       induced by the mixup strategy.
