011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013             Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is crucial for ensuring the robustness of ma-
014             chine learning models in open-world scenarios by identifying inputs from un-
015             known classes. Vision-language models like CLIP have enabled zero-shot OOD
016             detection without requiring labels or training on in-distribution (ID) data. How-
017             ever, current approaches are limited by their dependence on closed-set text-based
018             labels and full image feature representations, constraining CLIP’s capacity to gen-
019             eralize across diverse labels. In this work, we propose GRIC, a novel method
020
                based on few-shot multi-modal OOD detection by leveraging two key insights: (1)
                OOD detection is driven by general ID representations rather than class-specific
021
                features, and (2) large language models (LLMs) can enrich the model’s under-
022
                standing of ID data and simulate potential OOD scenarios without actual OOD
023             samples. GRIC demonstrates simplicity and effectiveness, achieving up to a 19%
024             reduction in FPR95 on the MS-COCO dataset and up to 5% on ImageNet, outper-
025             forming state-of-the-art methods.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is essential in real-world machine learning applications,
031   where novel, previously unseen classes require special attention. While there has been grow-
032   ing interest in OOD detection, most existing methods rely heavily on single-modal learning ap-
033   proaches (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
034   2021; Zhan et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021) (see Fig. 1(a)). Dependence on visual
035   data alone can be limiting, especially when OOD inputs closely resemble in-distribution (ID) data
036   visually but differ in their semantic content. For example, in image classification tasks, labels are
037   often encoded as one-hot vectors, which fail to capture the rich semantic information embedded in
038   textual descriptions (Ming et al., 2022).
039   Conventional single-modality OOD detection
040   techniques in vision rely on robust feature rep-
041   resentations (Sehwag et al., 2021; Tack et al.,
042   2020) and predefined distance metrics (Lee
043   et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2022) to separate
044   OOD data from ID data. Recent advance-
045
      ments in multi-modal (vision-language) pre-
      training, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
046
      and ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), have opened
047
      new avenues for learning representations by
048   aligning images with textual descriptions. As
049   a result, OOD detection has begun shifting
050   from traditional single-modal approaches to-
051   ward multi-modal frameworks (Fort et al., Figure 1: Different paradigms of OOD detection methods. In multi-

      1) Defining OODness in a multi-modal framework, especially when using vision-language mod-
055   els like CLIP, where training datasets are not explicitly disclosed. In such cases, OODness is typi-

064   full vision feature representations, overlooking the potential benefits of combining specific vision
065   feature segments with textual data.
066
      In response to these challenges, we introduce GRIC, a post-hoc OOD detection method inspired
067   by low-rank factorization in deep neural networks (Gao et al., 2020; Elhamifar & Vidal, 2013; Li
068   et al., 2019b) and advancements in LLMs and prompt engineering (Novack et al., 2023; Ge et al.,
069   2023). Unlike prior methods that focus on entire vision features, GRIC emphasizes the significance
070   of general/common features in representing datasets and distinguishing between ID and OOD data.
071
      Based on key findings from classification and fine-grained classification research (Fei-Fei et al.,
072
      2004; Russakovsky et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), we observe that
073   class-specific features—those critical for recognizing ID data—are often contained within the sub-
074   space of the ID data itself. By focusing on features outside of this subspace, which represent a more
075   generalized depiction of ID data, we can better differentiate OOD from ID samples.
076
      GRIC distinguishes OOD samples by leveraging the general/common feature representation of
077
      ID data, while masking class-specific features, and leveraging LLMs to access more informative
078
      prompts. It incorporates a new scoring function that disregards low-rank subspaces in ID data and
079   enriches prompts with ID super-class information, improving their effectiveness.
080
081   Our key contributions include:
082   - GRIC: A novel, training-free OOD detection method that highlights the importance of general
083   ID data representation and informative prompts. GRIC demonstrates consistent performance, oper-
084   ates without the need for downstream fine-tuning, and shows strong generalizability across various
085   tasks.
086   - Performance improvement: GRIC reduces FPR95 by approximately 19% compared to robust
087   baseline methods, demonstrating its superior performance in distinguishing OOD samples while
088   maintaining accuracy for ID data.
089
      - Comprehensive analysis: Extensive ablation studies provide insights into the effectiveness and
090
      mechanisms of GRIC, offering a deeper understanding of its design and performance.

093   2   BACKGROUND AND N OTATIONS
094
      Contrastive Vision-Language Pre-Training In the realm of visual representation learning, models
095
      emphasizing vision-language representation demonstrate superior efficacy in image classification
096   tasks compared to those focused solely on visual features, such as ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
097   A prominent example is CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which employs a self-supervised contrastive
098   objective, akin to the InfoNCE loss (Van den Oord et al., 2018). CLIP aligns images with their
099   textual descriptions, utilizing a dual-stream model with a prompt (p) text encoder T : p → Rd
100   (e.g., Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017)) and an image encoder I : x → Rd (e.g., ViT (Doso-
101   vitskiy et al., 2021)). Despite its success, models like CLIP typically operate in a ’closed-world’
102   setting, conducting zero-shot classification within a predetermined set of classes, even if the input is
103   irrelevant.
104   Few-shot and Zero-Shot OOD Detection In employing a pre-trained model, we establish a clas-
105   sification task with a defined set of class labels, referred to as Yin , which represent the known (ID)
106   classes. These ID classes are defined in relation to the specific classification task, rather than the
107   classes utilized during pre-training. Consequently, out-of-distribution (OOD) instances are identi-
      fied with respect to the ID classes, not the data distribution during pre-training. zero-shot multi-

                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      modal OOD detection (Fort et al., 2021; Ming et al., 2022; Esmaeilpour et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
109   2023) addresses two primary objectives: (1) identifying samples not corresponding to any known
110   (ID) classes, and (2) assigning test samples to known classes when applicable. The OOD detector,
111   denoted as G(x; Yin, T , I) : X → {ID, OOD}, serves as a protective mechanism for the classifi-
112   cation model. Our approach (Fig. 1 (b)) leverages a pre-trained model and avoids training on ID
113   samples; however, it is considered a few-shot multi-modal method as it requires access to a limited
114   number of ID samples to compute PCA, without requiring access to their labels. This reliance on
115   minimal ID data allows our method to effectively identify OOD samples while maintaining scala-
116   bility and efficiency.
117   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Wold et al. (1987); Joliffe & Morgan (1992); Bro & Smilde
118   (2014) stands as a foundational technique integral to extracting essential information and discerning
119   the low-rank subspace within data. Operating as a potent feature extraction method, PCA trans-
120   forms original features into linearly uncorrelated variables, known as principal components. These
121   components are meticulously chosen to maximize variance in the dataset, capturing the most in-
122   formative and discriminative features. In the realm of class-specific classification tasks, PCA plays
123
      a pivotal role by identifying principal components contributing significantly to the variance within
      each class. This focused representation enhances the efficiency of class-specific models, enabling
124
      a more streamlined exploration of the intrinsic structure of the data. However, for robust OOD de-
125
      tection, a nuanced shift in focus from class-specific to general dataset representation is imperative.
126   Leveraging PCA, we strategically neutralize features associated with specific classes, obtaining the
127   general feature representation essential for effective OOD detection. This approach ensures a more
128   efficient exploration of the intrinsic structure of the data, underscoring its relevance in tasks that
129   demand distinct features for precise classification.

141   3.1 G ENERAL ID I MAGES F EATURES R EPRESENTATION
142
      The accuracy of OOD detection is notably enhanced by both vision and text feature representa-
143
      tions. To achieve the most informative vision feature representation of ID data, we only leverage
144   features corresponding to the general representation of ID data (GRID) and mask class-specific
145   features. Class-specific features, which reside on boundaries between different classes, are crucial
146   for tasks like image classification and masking them results in more generalization. The general
147   feature representation captures patterns that are shared across all ID classes. These patterns are
148   more general and robust, making them more effective at distinguishing between OOD and ID data.
149   This approach aligns with principles of data
150   augmentation and regularization, aiming to en-
151   hance the model’s robustness to variations in
152
      the input data that it has not explicitly en-
      countered during training (Dietterich, 1998; Ma
153
      et al., 2018). Feature masking serves as a form
154
      of regularization, preventing the model from re-
155   lying excessively on class-specific features.

      3.2 I NFORMATIVE C ONTENT /P ROMPT (IC)
255
      The efficacy of Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) critically relies on careful prompt
256
      engineering, serving as pivotal cues directing the model’s comprehension of the intricate interplay
257
      between textual descriptions and corresponding visual content (Radford et al., 2021). Thoughtful
258   consideration and formulation of prompts wield considerable influence over the model’s capac-
259   ity to capture nuanced semantics and contextual information (Carion et al., 2020). By tailoring
260   CLIP to specific tasks through prompt engineering, researchers amplify its adaptability and perfor-
261   mance across a diverse array of applications, unlocking its full potential in real-world scenarios.
262   The paramount importance of prompt-related considerations becomes evident as practitioners seek
263   optimal results and extend CLIP’s applicability in complex, dynamic environments (White et al.,
264   2023; Liu & Chilton, 2022; Zhou et al., 2022c).
265   In recent investigations into zero-shot multi-modal OOD detection, scant attention has been directed
266   towards the significance of text prompts, as noted by (Fort et al., 2021; Ming et al., 2022; Wang
267   et al., 2023; Shu et al., 2023). Ming et al. (2022) introduces a set of prompts structured as ”a photo
268   of a ⟨yi ⟩” based on an in-distribution (ID) dataset with n labels. However, this approach simplifies
269   prompts to a class name without incorporating any additional knowledge. Fort et al. (2021) and
      Esmaeilpour et al. (2022) extend the label set, broadening the exploration scope. However, consid-


                                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      ering potential OOD classes or addressing computational hurdles to train an extra text encoder poses
271   challenges. Wang et al. (2023) equip CLIP with the ability to distinguish OOD and ID samples us-
272   ing positive-semantic and negation-semantic prompts, introducing computational costs in training a
273   learnable ”no” prompt and a ”no” text encoder. However, all these approaches rely only on simple
274   prompt with no extra information.

      3.2.1 I NFORMATIVE P ROMPT AUGMENTATION
276
      In our pursuit to improve OOD detection performance, we investigated the augmentation of prompts
277   with supplementary information. A significant challenge arises from the absence of knowledge con-
278   cerning potential OOD labels. To overcome this hurdle, we utilize existing knowledge, specifically
279   ID labels, and enhance it by incorporating high-level information. This involves identifying the hier-
280   archy within our ID dataset and integrating super-class names into the prompts. For instance, if the
281   ID dataset focuses on dog images, we add ”animal” as the super-class name, resulting in prompts
282   like ”an image of a dog, animal, living entity.”. In our formulation, we present this informative con-
283   tent by IC(yi ). For this sake, we leverage large language models (LLMs) to generate the taxonomy
284   of ID dataset labels. This simple yet effective approach requires no training or knowledge of OOD
285
      data, making it cost-free, straightforward, and effective.
286   Insight: Relying solely on ID labels for OOD classifications presents several limitations. Firstly, it
287   restricts the model’s understanding of potential labels and hinders its ability to encompass diverse
288   data points due to the inherent constraint of a limited set of ID labels. Secondly, insufficient exposure
289   to ID general patterns diminishes the model’s capacity for comprehensive generalization, making
290   it challenging to recognize and classify novel instances accurately. This narrow scope results in
291
      a diminished gap between ID scores and OOD scores, complicating the discernment between in-
      distribution and out-of-distribution instances.
292
293   To overcome these limitations, integrating hierarchical information, such as super-class names, into
294   OOD detection offers significant benefits. It enriches the understanding of ID labels and patterns,
295   facilitating improved generalization capabilities and better recognition of novel instances. Addition-
296   ally, the incorporation of hierarchical information widens the contextual understanding, enabling a
297
      more effective differentiation between in-distribution and out-of-distribution instances. This broader
      context fosters a stronger association between textual prompts and visual content, enhancing the
298
      model’s interpretative abilities.
299
300   In summary, leveraging hierarchical information in OOD detection enhances knowledge of ID labels
301   and general patterns, improves generalization, widens the context for classification, and enriches
302   the semantic understanding of input data. This integration fosters a robust association between
303
      textual and visual information, ultimately enhancing the accuracy and reliability of OOD detection
      methodologies.

      Metrics: In our comprehensive evaluation, we employ three well-known metrics in OOD detection

      to assess the performance of our approach. These metrics are:
396
397   1) False Positive Rate at 95% True Positive Rate (FPR95): This metric measures the rate of false
398   positives among OOD samples when the true positive rate of ID samples reaches 95%. It provides a
399   nuanced assessment of the model’s ability to discern between ID and OOD instances.
400   2) Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC): is a widely used metric
401   that quantifies the model’s ability to discriminate between in-distribution and out-of-distribution
402   samples across various decision thresholds. A higher AUROC value indicates improved overall
403   performance in distinguishing between the two classes.
404   3) ID classification accuracy (ID ACC). We provide the details in appendix, Section B.3.

530   5 C ONCLUSION
531
      In conclusion, this research study addresses the critical challenge of out-of-distribution sample de-
532
      tection in machine learning applications by introducing GRIC leveraging general representation of
533
      the ID data and informative content. By emphasizing general feature representations within the
534   in-distribution data and employing informative prompts enriched with ID super-class information,
535   GRIC showcases its efficacy in nuanced OOD detection. Our contributions include the introduction
536   of GRIC as a novel and effective technique, which, through comprehensive evaluations, outperforms
537   various OOD detection baselines, achieving an average reduction of ∼ 19% in FPR95 compared to
538   robust baseline methods. These findings offer valuable insights into the intricacies and efficacy of
539   GRIC, positioning it as a promising solution for advancing training-free OOD detection in multi-
      modal machine learning applications.

       F.1   P ROCEDURE :
1243
1244
1245   In this section, we explain the procedure that is followed in PCA analysis to extract the most infor-
1246   mative features.
1247   Data Standardization:
1248
       Standardize the features of the dataset to have zero mean and unit variance.
1249
1250   Covariance Matrix: Compute the covariance matrix of the standardized data. The covariance
1251   matrix represents the relationships between different features.
1252   SVD of Covariance Matrix:
1253
1254   Perform SVD on the covariance matrix. The singular value decomposition of the covariance matrix
1255
       results in the principal components. Selecting Principal Components:
1256   Sort the singular values in descending order. The corresponding singular vectors are the principal
1257   components. Choose the top k principal components to form a reduced-dimensional space.
1258
       Projection:
1259
1260   Project the original data onto the selected principal components to obtain the lower-dimensional
1261   representation.
1262   Benefits:
1263
       Dimensionality reduction facilitates easier visualization and interpretation of data. Reduced dimen-
1264
       sions often lead to computational efficiency. Principal components capture the most significant
1265
       patterns in the data.
1266
1267   More Explanation Regarding SVD Computation: Consider an m×n matrix R, where m denotes
1268   the number of rows, and n represents the number of columns. The primary objective of Singular
1269
       Value Decomposition (SVD) is to decompose matrix R into three distinct matrices: U , Σ, and V T
       (transpose of matrix V ). This decomposition is expressed as R = U ΣV T ∈ Rm×n , as illustrated
1270
       in Fig. 4.
1271
1272   U : An m × m orthogonal matrix, where its columns signify the left singular vectors of R.
1273   Σ: An m × n diagonal matrix, featuring singular values of R (non-negative and arranged in de-
1274   scending order).
1275
1276
       V T : An n × n orthogonal matrix, with its columns representing the right singular vectors of R.
1277   In addition to singular values and singular vectors, eigenvalues and eigenvectors are also integral to
1278   understanding matrix properties. An eigenvalue λ and its corresponding eigenvector v of a square
1279   matrix R satisfy the equation Rv = λv. Eigenvectors denote directions in the vector space that are
1280   solely scaled by the matrix R, while eigenvalues represent the scaling factors for these eigenvectors.
1281   SVD and its Relationship to Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors: SVD establishes a crucial connection
1282   between eigenvalues and eigenvectors with the singular values and singular vectors of a matrix. The
1283   singular values of R are the square roots of the eigenvalues of either RRT or RT R, and the left
1284   and right singular vectors are the eigenvectors of RRT and RT R, respectively.
1285
       Rank and Matrix Approximation: The rank of a matrix R is determined by the count of non-zero
1286   singular values in Σ. By retaining only the largest singular values and their corresponding singular
1287   vectors, it becomes feasible to approximate the original matrix R with a lower-rank approximation.
1288   This technique is valuable for tasks such as dimensionality reduction and noise reduction, and we
1289   leverage this feature in our approach.
1290
       Properties of SVD:
1291   The singular values in Σ are non-negative and arranged in descending order. The columns of U
1292   and V are orthonormal, forming an orthogonal basis for their respective vector spaces. The SVD
1293   decomposition is unique, except for the sign of the singular values and the order of the singular
1294   vectors. SVD is a potent matrix factorization technique, offering a concise representation of a matrix
1295   while preserving essential structural properties. Its applications span diverse fields, including data
       analysis, image processing, recommendation systems, and more (Deisenroth et al., 2020).
