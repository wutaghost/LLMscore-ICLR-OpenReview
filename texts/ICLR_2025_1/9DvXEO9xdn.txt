011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Millions of new pieces of malware are introduced each year. This poses signif-
014            icant challenges for antivirus vendors, who use machine learning to detect and
015            analyze malware, and must keep up with changes in the distribution while retain-
016            ing knowledge of older variants. Continual learning (CL) holds the potential to
017            address this challenge by reducing the storage and computational costs of regu-
018            larly retraining over all the collected data. Prior work, however, shows that CL
019
               techniques designed primarily for computer vision tasks fare poorly when applied
               to malware classification. To address these issues, we begin with an exploratory
020
               analysis of a typical malware dataset, which reveals that malware families are di-
021
               verse and difficult to characterize, requiring a wide variety of samples to learn
022            a robust representation. Based on these findings, we propose M alware Analysis
023            with Diversity-Aware Replay (MADAR), a CL framework that accounts for the
024            unique properties and challenges of the malware data distribution. We extensively
025            evaluate these techniques using both Windows and Android malware, showing
026            that MADAR significantly outperforms prior work. This highlights the impor-
027            tance of understanding domain characteristics when designing CL techniques and
028            demonstrates a path forward for the malware classification domain.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Advances in machine learning have significantly improved detection and classification of malicious
034
      software, with notable success across various settings, such as Windows executables (Dahl et al.,
035
      2013; Kovacs, 2018), PDFs (Maiorca et al., 2012), and Android applications (Arp et al., 2014).
036   Traditional models, trained on static datasets, are expected to perform well on new data under the
037   assumption of a constant data distribution. In reality, though, both malicious (i.e., malware) and
038   benign software (i.e., goodware) are ever-evolving and require regular model updates to keep up with
039   these changes in data distribution to maintain effectiveness. For example, the AV-TEST Institute logs
040   about 450,000 new malware samples daily (AV-TEST, 2023), and VirusTotal processes about one
041   million new submissions each day (VirusTotal, 2023).
042   Training a malware classification model solely on new data can lead to catastrophic forgetting
043   (CF) (French, 1999), which may result in both misclassifying goodware and allowing attackers
044   to bypass detection with older malware strains. To address this, antivirus vendors can keep older
045   samples and retrain over all of them during model updates, but the enormous volume of these sam-
046   ples makes the storage and computational costs of this approach excessive. Continual learning (CL)
047   offers a solution to this problem by enabling models to adapt to new data without the need for
048   maintaining large datasets or extensive retraining (van de Ven et al., 2020; Bhat et al., 2023).
049   While designs for CL have been extensively studied in the context of computer vision (Shin et al.,
050   2017; Hsu et al., 2018; van de Ven et al., 2020), there are very few such studies in the malware
051   classification domain. An exception is the work of Rahman et al. (2022), who found that none of
052   the CL techniques originally designed for computer vision problems offer acceptable performance
053   in malware classification, due in part to the strong semantics of malware features and the high level
      of diversity found in the malware ecosystem.

054
      In this study, we first delve into the complexities of malware data distributions using the EMBER
055   dataset (Anderson & Roth, 2018) of Windows malware and goodware. Our analysis highlights the
056   diversity in malware, both between and even within families—groups of related malware. Lever-
057   aging this insight, we devise MADAR, M alware Analysis with Diversity-Aware Replay, a replay-
058   based continual learning strategy that accounts for diversity and achieves improved malware clas-
059   sification performance. MADAR replays a mix of representative samples and novel samples (i.e.,
060   outliers) to enhance the model’s ability to retain knowledge and identify new malware variants de-
061   spite memory constraints. Our techniques use Isolation Forests (IF) to find these novel samples.
062   We then evaluate MADAR with comprehensive experiments on the EMBER dataset in three CL
063   scenarios that mirror common malware classification tasks: domain incremental learning (Domain-
064   IL), class incremental learning (Class-IL), and task incremental learning (Task-IL). Additionally, we
065   have curated two new benchmarks of Android malware from the AndroZoo repository (Allix et al.,
066   2016): AZ-Domain for Domain-IL experiments and AZ-Class for Class-IL and Task-IL scenarios.
067   Our results on these datasets confirm that MADAR is indeed effective and much better than prior
068   state-of-the-art CL methods in the face of realistic data distribution shifts.
069   In summary, the contributions of this study are:
070
071
      • We provide an exploratory analysis of the diversity of malware distributions and show how it
        creates unique challenges for continuous learning.
072
      • We develop two large-scale, realistic Android malware benchmarks covering all three CL scenar-
073
        ios – Domain-IL, Class-IL, and Task-IL.
074
      • In Domain-IL scenarios, we show that MADAR performs much better than prior CL techniques.
075
        On the AZ dataset, for example, MADAR comes within 0.4% average accuracy of the joint train-
076     ing baseline using just 100K training samples versus 680K for joint.
077   • MADAR is also effective in Class-IL scenarios, where it consistently outperforms all prior meth-
078     ods over a wide range of budgets. With a budget of 20K training samples on EMBER, MADAR
079     gets an average accuracy of 85.8% versus 66.8% for the best prior method.
080   • For Task-IL, MADAR outperforms all prior methods across all memory budgets for both the
081     EMBER and AZ datasets. For example, in the AZ dataset, the MADAR-U variant achieves an
082     average accuracy of 98.7% (within 0.1% of joint) with a budget of only 20K replay samples
083     (versus approximately 250K for joint).

088   Replay in Continual Learning. The fundamental challenge in developing a CL system is ad-
089   dressing catastrophic forgetting (CF), and one of the widely studied methods to overcome CF is
090   replay (Zhang et al., 2024; Elsayed & Mahmood, 2024; Bhat et al., 2023; Rebuffi et al., 2017).
091   These techniques can further be classified into one of two major subcategories – exact replay and
092   generative replay.
093   Exact-replay techniques are designed to choose replay samples from previously learned data to
094   be combined with new samples for retraining. The goal of these techniques is to maximize the
095   performance with minimal replay samples (Rolnick et al., 2019; Chaudhry et al., 2019a; Rebuffi
096   et al., 2017; Bhat et al., 2023). Selecting and using the replay samples involves determining a
097   memory budget, denoted as M, and choosing an optimal value of M remains an open research
098   question (Aljundi et al., 2019; Chaudhry et al., 2019b). Generative or pseudo-replay strategies are
099   designed to replicate the original data (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Shin et al., 2017; van de Ven et al., 2020).
100   These techniques either generate a representative of the original data using a separate generative
101
      model or generate pseudo-data by using an earlier model’s predictions as soft labels for training
      subsequent models. We experimentally compare the performance of MADAR against the leading
102
      exact and generative replay techniques, and we find that it’s diversity-aware approach outperforms
103
      all of them in the malware domain.
104
105   CL in Malware and Related Domains. Despite extensive work in CL, very few studies have
106   studied CL in the malware domain. Rahman et al. (2022) were the first to explore CL for malware
107   classification. They concluded that existing CL methods fall short in tackling CF in malware classi-
      fication systems due to differences in the underlying nature of the data distribution shifts that occur

133   3   CL S CENARIOS IN THE M ALWARE A NALYSIS P IPELINE
134
135   Continual Learning (CL) is categorized into three scenarios: Domain Incremental Learning
136   (Domain-IL), Class Incremental Learning (Class-IL), and Task Incremental Learning (Task-
137   IL) (van de Ven et al., 2022). In this section, we explain how the three CL scenarios fit into a
138   typical malware analysis pipeline (see Figure 1).
139
140
      Domain-IL. The first step in the pipeline is the binary classification problem of distinguishing
      between goodware and malware. Each day, VirusTotal receives one million never-before-seen sam-
141
      ples (VirusTotal, 2023), highlighting the persistent and evolving nature of software.This underscores
142
      the importance of rapidly integrating these new samples into operational systems to protect against
143   evolving threats. In addition, with the continual emergence of new benign software programs and
144   the massive class imbalance in practice (i.e., significantly more goodware than malware), it is of
145   utmost importance to not increase the false positive rate of the classifiers.
146
      In this adversarial context, attackers may deploy older malware to evade detection by systems that
147
      have forgotten their patterns, necessitating a balance between adapting to new threats and preventing
148
      CF. To evaluate CL schemes in this regard, we segment our Domain-IL datasets into blocks of time
149   based on the date of each sample.
150
151   Class-IL. Once software is determined to be malicious, the next task in malware analysis involves
152   classifying malware into families—groups of programs with substantial code overlap and similar
153   functionality, as recognized by experts (Zhu et al., 2020). For instance, the zeus banking trojan
154   has evolved into 556 variants across 35 families, including citadel and gameover. Defining a
155
      new class relies on consensus from multiple anti-virus engines and occurs when a significant set of
      similar samples forms a new family (Kantchelian et al., 2015; Zhu et al., 2020). In our incremental
156
      multi-class model, we start with a set of known malware families and add new ones as they emerge,
157
      continuously adjusting and assessing the model across all known classes.
158
159   Task-IL. In malware analysis, leveraging insights from additional methods can prove beneficial.
160   This may involve identifying the broader malware category (e.g., adware, ransomware, etc.), mal-
161   ware behaviors (Berlin et al., 2015), or the infection vector (e.g., phishing, downloader, etc.). Task-
      IL encapsulates this concept of constrained tasks, where the introduction of a new task may sym-


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      bolize a new category or behavior set. This event occurs less frequently than adding a new family,
163   as found in Class-IL.Unlike Class-IL, the task identity is provided to the model at test time, sig-
164   nificantly simplifying the problem. In malware, this could mean learning the task identity from a
165   separate model, manual analysis, or field reports of the malware’s behavior. As our datasets do not
166   possess naturally defined tasks, we partition our dataset into tasks comprising an equal number of
167   independent and non-overlapping classes to act as a proxy to new behaviors, following common
168   practice in the CL literature (van de Ven et al., 2022; 2020). In other words, a given task would be to
169   perform family classification among one subset of families, and the subset that each sample belongs
170   to is known to the classifier. The model is expected to be able to handle multiple tasks at once, and
171
      new tasks are being added during each experiment.

      4.1   E XPLORATORY A NALYSIS OF THE EMBER DATASET
177
178
      In this section, we provide an analysis of the EMBER dataset (Anderson & Roth, 2018), a widely
179
      used benchmark for Windows malware classification. The analysis sheds light on the data distri-
180   bution across various families and tasks, aiding in selecting representative samples for replay. We
181   identified 2,899 unique malware families within a subset of EMBER, and an additional 11,433 sam-
182   ples lacking clear family labels were assigned the label Other.
183
      We investigate the prevalence of malware families over time, distinguishing between recurring and
184
      newly identified families each month. Unlike many datasets used in CL research, we see significant
185
      churn in the representation of families over time. Of the 913 families seen in January, for example,
186   only 551 are seen in February, while 425 new families emerge. This churn indicates a potential issue
187   in training data continuity, which may aggravate catastrophic forgetting and underscores the need
188   for different CL strategies in the malware domain. Generally, each family has its own distribution
189   pattern, and together these patterns make up the total distribution of malware for a particular month.
190
      Worse, many malware samples do not have family labels at all. Correctly labeling samples is
191
      challenging, requiring time and expert knowledge (Kantchelian et al., 2015), so the lack of labels
192   matches real-world conditions. Family labels for malware are based on the av-class labels provided
193   by the av-test engine (AV-TEST, 2023). The Other-labeled samples do not seem from our analysis
194   to align with other families, meaning that many of them indeed come from unknown families.
195
      Furthermore, the prominent malware families change with
196
      the evolution of tasks. The 10 most common families vary
197
      greatly across tasks. For example, the emotet malware
198   family was the most consistent, appearing in 11 out of 12
199   tasks. The next most consistent families were fareit
200   and zusy, appearing in eight and seven tasks, respectively.
201   This indicates considerable concept drift in malware data,
202   highlighting the need to regularly update classifiers.
203
      Challenges in the malware domain also arise from its com-
204   plexity. Many malware families display complex distribu-
205   tional patterns in feature space, making for additional di-
206   versity within classes. Figure 2 shows a t-SNE projection
207   of EMBER features for all malware samples from January
208   2018. Each family (represented by color) is not clustered
209   into a single well-defined region. Rather, the larger families Figure 2: t-SNE projection of EMBER
210   are split up and spread out in feature space. To accurately malware, Jan. 2018
211   represent the malware distribution, it is thus important to
212
      select samples not only from each family, but also from multiple areas within each family. This
      may explain why prior CL techniques designed for computer vision datasets are less effective when
213
      applied to the malware domain (Rahman et al., 2022).
214
215   In light of these results, we propose that selecting replay samples based on families and variations
      within families could more effectively capture the diversity within the data and help mitigate CF.

240        4.2      MADAR
241
           Here, we introduce the MADAR framework for CL in malware classification that uses a diversity-
242        aware replay buffer to account for the diversity of samples we saw in EMBER.
243
244        Building on our analysis in Section 4.1, we postulate that stratified sampling—where replay samples
245
           are chosen based on their representation in malware families—may better preserve the model’s
           stability compared with random sampling as used in global reservoir sampling (GRS) (Vitter, 1985;
246
           Rahman et al., 2022). Moreover, we also seek to capture the diversity within each family’s data
247
           distribution. Let Df = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn } represent the data samples belonging to a specific family
248        f . We define two subsets of interest within this data: the representative samples Sf ⊂ Df capture
249        the frequently occurring samples within the family and the anomalous samples Af ⊂ Df capture
250        rare samples. The selected set of replay samples S = Sf ∪ Af captures the diversity within the
251        family’s data distribution by balancing representative and anomalous samples. While any single
252        anomalous sample is not as important to learn and remember as a single representative sample, a
253        collection of anomalous samples helps to track the diversity within a class.
254        Isolation Forest (IF) (Liu et al., 2008) is a technique for identifying outliers in high-dimensional
255        data. IF uses decision trees to isolate anomalous data points based on the intuition that they are easy
256        to separate from the rest of the data. An important parameter in IF is the contamination rate Cr ,
257        which represents the expected fraction of outliers in the data. We found that Cr = 0.1 works best
258        and used it in all our experiments. The algorithm for MADAR in the Domain-IL setting is provided
259        in Algorithm 1. The algorithms for Class-IL and Task-IL are presented in Appendix A.
260

262

263
           and Task-IL. The procedure begins by initializing a global data pool P, containing both goodware
264        and malware samples, and a dictionary D that tracks malware families and their frequencies in the
265        data up to the current task.
266
267
           For each new task c, MADAR divides the data into goodware (Xgood ) and malware (Xmal ) subsets
           from P, allocating memory budgets βM for malware and βG for goodware from the total memory
268
           budget β, based on a split ratio γ:
269

271   For an imbalanced dataset, it is better to tune γ. Our Android malware (AZ) datasets, for example,
272   have a 9:1 ratio of goodware to malware, so we use γ = 0.9.
273
274
      Before training for a new task, MADAR incrementally trains the classifier using a combination of
      new samples from the current task and replay samples from previous tasks. The replay samples
275
      include both goodware (Rgood ⊂ Xgood ) and malware (Rmal ⊂ Xmal ), with Rmal sampled from
276
      specific malware families rather than randomly from all of Xmal .
277
278   For each family f , we set its family budget Bf — the number of samples to select from f —using
279   two sub-sampling variants: Ratio budgeting and Uniform budgeting.
280   • Ratio Budgeting: Select the number of samples from a family f proportional to that family’s

282

283

      5.5      D ISCUSSION
457
      Our results show that MADAR outperforms previous methods in Domain-IL, Class-IL, and Task-
458
      IL scenarios on both the EMBER and AZ datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of diversity-
459
      aware replay in maintaining stability in continual malware classification. In all scenarios, MADAR
460   outperforms prior replay-based methods that were designed for computer vision tasks. These prior
461   approaches generally performed quite poorly in the malware domain, echoing findings of Rahman
462   et al. (2022). For larger budgets, MADAR’s performance approaches the Joint baseline while using
463   a much smaller training budget, e.g., as little as 3% of the cost.
464
      The Ratio variant performed better in Domain-IL, while the Uniform variant worked well in Class-
465
      IL and Task-IL. Intuitively, this makes sense, as ratio budgeting captures the contributions of each
466   family to the overall malware distribution for binary classification in the Domain-IL setting. Addi-
467   tionally, since there are many small families in the Domain-IL datasets, uniformly sampling from
468   them consumes budget while offering little improvement in malware coverage. In contrast, Class-IL
469   and Task-IL require classification across families, where uniform budgeting ensures class balance
470   and comprehensive coverage.
471
      GRS performs well, often close to MADAR’s performance, especially at higher budgets in Domain-
472   IL. GRS provides an unbiased estimate of the underlying distribution, which makes it a strong
473   baseline. MADAR is particularly effective in Class-IL, Task-IL, and lower-budget Domain-IL, while
474   GRS generally performs as well as MADAR in higher-budget Domain-IL. We hypothesize that
475   diversity is more important when the number of samples per class is limited.

479
480   In this paper, we propose MADAR, a framework for diversity-aware replay in continual learning
481   specially designed for the challenging setting of malware classification. Our comprehensive eval-
482   uation across Domain-IL, Class-IL, and Task-IL scenarios against Windows executable (EMBER)
483   and Android application (AZ) datasets demonstrates that diversity-aware sampling is helpful for
484   effective CL in malware classification. As malware and goodware continue to evolve, we hope
485   these insights steer continual learning towards strategic, resource-efficient methods, ensuring model
      effectiveness amid the constantly shifting landscape of cybersecurity threats.

      B.4   T RAINING AND E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
682
683
      A continual learning (CL) model is sequentially trained to learn tasks from t1 , t2 , ..., tT , each with
684   its distinct data distribution p(x, y|ti ). The goal is to adapt to new tasks without forgetting the
685   old ones. CL training involves three sets of parameters: shared parameters (θs ) across all tasks,
686   old task-specific parameters (θ0 ), and new task parameters (θn ) (Li & Hoiem, 2017). The Joint
687   training benchmark trains the model with all the available training samples up to the current task and
688   optimizes all these parameters simultaneously; however, it incurs incremental storage and training
689   costs. In contrast, CL training strives to optimize and update θs and θn , while maintaining θ0
690   in a relatively fixed state for each new task tn . However, updating any of the shared weights θs
691   risks confusing the classifier when faced with older data, as those classification decisions depend
692
      not only on θ0 but also on θs . CL training typically boasts significantly faster speeds and far less
      storage requirements than Joint training, thus permitting more frequent model retraining to adapt to
693
      evolving data distributions or other requirements.
694
695   In our evaluations, we use a non-overlapping hold-out set corresponding to each task. For example,
696   the AZ-Domain dataset contains 9 years of training samples from 2008 to 2016, resulting in 9 hold-
697   out sets, one for each year. A CL model is evaluated on all the hold-out sets up to the current task;
698
      formally, the model is evaluated on tasks ti to tT , for 1 ≤ i ≤ T , after it been trained on the current
      task tT .
699
700   In this work, each set of experiments is performed around 10-15 times with different random param-
701   eter initializations. We use PyTorch on a CentOS-7 machine with an Intel Xeon processor, 40 CPU
      cores, 128GB RAM, and four GeForce RTX 2080Ti GPUs, each with 12GB memory.
