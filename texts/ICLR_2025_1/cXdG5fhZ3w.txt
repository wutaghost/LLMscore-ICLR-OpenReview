009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Personalized Federated Learning (FL) algorithms collaboratively train customized
012            models for each client, enhancing the accuracy of the learned models on the
013            client’s local data (e.g., by clustering similar clients, by fine-tuning models locally,
014            or by imposing regularization terms). In this paper, we investigate the impact of
015            such personalization techniques on the group fairness of the learned models, and
016            show that personalization can also lead to improved (local) fairness as an unin-
017            tended benefit. We begin by illustrating these benefits of personalization through
018            numerical experiments comparing several classes of personalized FL algorithms
019
               against a baseline FedAvg algorithm, elaborating on the reasons behind improved
               fairness using personalized FL, and then providing analytical support. Motivated
020
               by these, we then show how to build on this (unintended) fairness benefit, by fur-
021
               ther integrating a fairness metric into the cluster-selection procedure of clustering-
022            based personalized FL algorithms, and improve the fairness-accuracy trade-off
023            attainable through them. Specifically, we propose two new fairness-aware feder-
024            ated clustering algorithms, Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC, extending the existing
025            IFCA and FL+HC algorithms, and demonstrate their ability to strike a (tuneable)
026            balance between accuracy and fairness at the client level.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a pivotal paradigm for collaboratively training a shared
032   machine learning model across distributed datasets/clients in a privacy-preserving manner (Kairouz
033   et al., 2021). Shared, global models learned through FL can (potentially) outperform standalone
034   models (those trained individually by each client in the absence of collaboration), owing to their
035   ability to effectively aggregate knowledge from diverse clients. Popular FL algorithms such as
036   FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017) have been found to perform well, particularly when clients have in-
037   dependent and identically distributed (iid) datasets. However, real-world applications often involve
038   heterogeneous datasets across clients, a situation where the performance of FedAvg can consider-
039
      ably deteriorate (Li et al., 2019b). Moreover, the standard FL approach yields a single global model,
      lacking customization for each client. Consequently, clients with heterogeneous datasets may expe-
040
      rience low local accuracy (Li et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2022; Karimireddy et al., 2020). To address
041
      these limitations, a spectrum of personalized FL techniques has been proposed, to enhance the local
042   accuracy of the learned models, while keeping some of the benefits of collaborative learning.
043
044   Aside from handling issues of data heterogeneity, training FL models that can uphold societal val-
045
      ues, as formalized through notions of algorithmic (group) fairness (Barocas et al., 2019), has been a
      focus of recent research. Consider as an example the scenario where FL is used to train a foundation
046
      model/LLM on local datasets, contributed by diverse participants from different regions and com-
047
      munities (Kuang et al., 2023). Without careful oversight, the resulting model will favor language
048   and content preferred by the majority contributors, often disregarding the unique linguistic nuances
049   and cultural contexts of minority groups (Durmus et al., 2023). As another example, existing re-
050   search (Kirdemir et al., 2021) finds structural and systemic bias in YouTube video recommendation
051   systems. In this case, the collaboratively trained recommendation model begins to favor certain
052   perspectives over others, inadvertently reinforcing biases present in the data. As a result, users are
053   fed information that perpetuates stereotypes, causing harm to underrepresented communities. These
      examples motivate the development of FL algorithms that can meet certain fairness criteria.

054
      In this paper, we establish an alignment between these two considerations in developing FL algo-
055   rithms: personalization can also improve fairness. That is, we show that personalization techniques
056   (which are designed to improve the local accuracy of the learned models) can also enhance algorith-
057   mic fairness as an unintended benefit (i.e., they can reduce the disparities in how the resulting model
058   treats individuals from different protected groups, once the model is deployed by the client).
059
      We begin by illustrating this alignment between personalization and fairness through numerical
060
      experiments comparing the average local fairness achieved by several personalized FL algorithms
061   (IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020), MAML-FL (Fallah et al., 2020), FedProx (Li et al., 2020), FL+HC
062   (Briggs et al., 2020), and CFL-Cosine (Sattler et al., 2020)), against FedAvg (McMahan et al.,
063   2017) and standalone learning (where each client learns by itself, and there is no collaboration),
064   under three notions of fairness (Statistical Parity, Equality of Opportunity and Equalized Odds), and
065   using both synthetic and real-world data (“Adult” dataset (Dua & Graff, 2017), and combinations
066   of two different prediction tasks and two protected attributes in the “Retiring Adult” dataset (Ding
067   et al., 2021)). Based on these experiments, we highlight two potential factors driving the alignment:
068        1. Statistical advantages of diverse data. Collaborative learning algorithms in essence have
069
      access to more “information” (data) from diverse clients. They can therefore enhance both accuracy
      and fairness, especially when dealing with imbalanced datasets, as in these scenarios issues of algo-
070
      rithmic unfairness can be attributed to the under-representation of samples from the disadvantaged
071
      group in the data. Further, personalized algorithms can outperform non-personalized collaborative
072   learning (e.g. FedAvg), as (we posit) they are less prone to overfitting to the majority group’s data.
073        2. Computational advantages due to alignments in local accuracy and fairness. Moreover, we
074   identify cases where the sample distribution within clients is such that improving accuracy also pro-
075   motes fairness. More intuitively, consider the concept of clustered FL, a personalized FL technique,
076   where clients are organized into groups based on similarities in model performance (a proxy for
077   data similarities). We argue that this clustering can be seen as a means to foster a model that is both
078   more precise and more equitable, as it effectively treats information originating from other clusters
079   as noise, which, if left unaddressed, would have led to model divergence. (Building on this intuition,
080
      we also provide analytical support under certain conditions in Propositions 1 and 2 in Appendix B).
081   To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to identify the (unintended) fairness benefits of
082   personalization in FL. Most existing works primarily focus on refining algorithms to attain either
083   improved local accuracy (the personalized FL literature), or, enhanced (global) fairness (the fair FL
084   literature); we discuss related work from each direction in Section 2. In contrast, our work exam-
085   ines the influence of existing personalization techniques on (local) fairness, and points to inherent
086
      features of collaborative learning and personalization that can advance fairness. Additionally, prior
      work points to the challenge of balancing the trade-off between fairness and accuracy of an algo-
087
      rithm, as enhancing fairness often comes at the cost of lower accuracy (Gu et al., 2022; Ezzeldin
088
      et al., 2023; Chien & Danks, 2023). In contrast, we identify instances that FL in general, and per-
089   sonalized FL in particular, can improve both accuracy and fairness compared to standalone learning.
090
091   Furthermore, inspired by our findings, we propose two new fairness-aware federated clustering algo-
092
      rithms, Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC, based on the existing IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020) and FL+HC
      (Briggs et al., 2020) personalized FL algorithms, respectively. Both Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC
093
      algorithms modify the cluster identity assignment step to encompass a fairness criterion. Build-
094
      ing on our finding that personalization can offer dual benefits, we demonstrate that this additional
095   modification can be used to strike a better balance between accuracy and fairness at the client level.
096
097   Summary of findings and contributions.
098
      1. Unintended fairness benefits of personalization. We conduct extensive numerical experiments,
      under different notions of fairness, and using both real-world and synthetic data, to show that per-
099
      sonalization in FL (which is intended to improve local accuracy) can also improve local fairness as
100
      an unintended benefit (Section 4). We highlight the potential statistical and computational reasons
101   leading to this alignment, and provide analytical support under certain conditions (Prop. 1 and 2).
102   2. New fairness-aware, personalized federated clustering algorithms. We propose two new algo-
103   rithms, Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC, by modifying the existing IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020) and
104   FL+HC (Briggs et al., 2020) personalized FL algorithms, to take fairness into account when (itera-
105   tively) determining clients’ cluster memberships (Algorithms 1 and 2). We show that this can pro-
106   vide desired fairness-accuracy trade-offs by adjusting a hyperparameter (Section 5), and elaborate
107   on when/how this technique can be applied to other (clustering-based) personalized FL methods.

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110   Our work is at the intersection of two literatures: personalized FL, and achieving fairness in FL.
111
      Here, we discuss the most closely related works; we review additional related work in Appendix A.
112   Personalized FL. Existing literature can be categorized based on how personalization is achieved.
113   Here, we investigate personalization through clustering, local fine-tuning, and regularization.
114   Clustering: Mansour et al. (2020) use a hypothesis-based clustering approach by minimizing the
115   sum of loss over all clusters. Sattler et al. (2020) use the idea that cosine similarity between weight
116   updates of different clients is highly indicative of the similarity of data distribution. Nardi et al.
117
      (2022) use a decentralized learning idea by exchanging local models with other clients to find the
      neighbor/group which has a high accuracy even using other clients’ models. Zheng et al. (2022)
118
      learn a weighted and directed graph that indicates the relevance between clients. Ghosh et al. (2020)
119
      use a distributed learning idea by broadcasting all clients models to others, and collecting back the
120   cluster identity from clients who can identify good performance when using others’ models.
121   Local fine-tuning: Fallah et al. (2020) propose using a Model Agnostic Meta Learning (MAML)
122   framework, where clients run additional local gradient steps to personalize the global model. Ari-
123   vazhagan et al. (2019); Jiang & Lin (2022) propose using deep learning models with a combination
124   of feature extraction layers (base) and global/local head (personalization). Jiang & Lin (2022), in-
125   spired by Arivazhagan et al. (2019), further consider robustifying against distribution shifts.
126   Model regularization: Hanzely & Richtárik (2020); Sahu et al. (2018); Li et al. (2020; 2021b) add
127   a regularization term with mixing parameters to penalize the distance between the local and global
128
      models. In particular, Sahu et al. (2018) has a pre-set regularization parameter and allows for system
      heterogeneity. Li et al. (2021b) consider improving accuracy, while being robust to data and model
129
      poisoning attacks, and fair. Similarly, T Dinh et al. (2020) formulate a bi-level optimization prob-
130
      lem, which helps decouple personalized model optimization from learning the global model. Huang
131   et al. (2021) propose the FedAMP algorithm which also introduces an additional regularization term,
132   but differs from previous works in that they encourage similar clients to collaborate more.
133
134
      In this paper, we will consider three variants of clustered FL algorithms (IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020),
      FL+HC (Briggs et al., 2020), and CFL-Cosine (Sattler et al., 2020)) from the clustering category.
135
      Additionally, we consider MAML-FL by Fallah et al. (2020) from the local fine-tuning category, and
136
      FedProx by Li et al. (2020) from the model regularization category. Our new proposed algorithms,
137   Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC, will belong to the clustering category.
138
139   Fairness in FL. This literature, surveyed recently in Shi et al. (2023); Rafi et al. (2023), can also
140
      be categorized depending on the adopted notion of fairness. Notably, several works (e.g., (Li et al.,
      2019a; 2021b; Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021)) on fair FL consider a notion of performance
141
      fairness, assessing whether the learned model(s) achieve uniform accuracy across all clients. In con-
142
      trast, we focus on notions of social (group) fairness (also studied in, e.g., (Abay et al., 2020; Zhang
143   et al., 2020; Du et al., 2021; Gálvez et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2021; Ezzeldin et al., 2023)), which
144   compare statistics such as selection rate, true positive rate, or false positive rate, across different
145   demographic/protected groups, to identify any bias in how humans will be treated once the learned
146   models are deployed. In contrast to all these works, we do not aim to impose a fairness constraint
147   during training or aggregation of the (global) models, but show that improved (local) fairness (and
148   a better fairness-accuracy tradeoff) can be achieved either by personalization alone, or by grouping
149   clients into clusters based on a fairness-aware cluster assignment metric.

      3   P ROBLEM SETTING AND PRELIMINARIES
151
152
      We study a federated learning setting consisting of n clients. Each client i is tasked with a binary
      classification problem. The client’s dataset consists of samples z = (x, y, g), where x 2 Rn is a fea-
153
      ture, y 2 {0, 1} is the true label of the sample, and g 2 {a, b} is a demographic group membership
154
      based on a protected attribute (e.g., race, gender, or age). Client i’s samples are drawn independently
155   at random from joint feature-label-group distributions with pdfs {fgy,i (x), y 2 {0, 1}, g 2 {a, b}};
156   note that these distributions are client-dependent and potentially different, motivating the need for
157   personalization of the learned models. Let Di denote the local dataset of client i.
158
      Standalone learning. Clients can use their local data Di to (independently) learn binary classifiers
159
      h✓i (x) : Rn ! {0, 1}, where ✓i are the parameters of the classifier (e.g., weights of a NN).
160
161   Federated learning. While clients can learn standalone models, Federated Learning (FL) paradigms
      enable them to leverage the power of collaborative learning. This is exemplified by the FedAvg al-


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      gorithm (McMahan et al., 2017), in which the objective is to learn a single global model ✓ that
163   minimizes the average loss across all clients. Learning happens over rounds. During each commu-
164   nication round t, each client i receives the latest global model ✓t 1 from the server, performs a local
165   update on this model based on its local data Di , and sends back its updated model ✓it to the server.
166   The server then aggregates all the received local models (through a weighted sum) to create the
167   new global model for the next round. While FedAvg can successfully learn a global model, if the
168   clients’ datasets Di are heterogeneous, the local loss of the shared global model may be high locally
169   for each client. This has motivated the development of personalized FL algorithms, five of which we
170   consider in this work: clustering-based methods (specifically, IFCA, FL+HC, and CFL-Cosine),
171
      a local fine-tuning method (MAML-FL), and one that imposes model regularization (FedProx).
172   Personalized Federated learning. We provide a high-level description of the personalized FL
173   methods considered in this paper. The IFCA algorithm of Ghosh et al. (2020) alternates between
174   two steps: clustering/grouping clients based on model performance, and optimizing model parame-
175   ters via gradient descent in each cluster separately, leading to distinct, cluster-specific models. The
176   FL+HC algorithm of Briggs et al. (2020) uses a hierarchical clustering technique to group clients
177
      while minimizing intra-cluster variance, measured by the Euclidean distance of their models. The
      CFL-Cosine algorithm of Sattler et al. (2020) bipartitions clients, aiming to minimize the maxi-
178
      mum cosine similarity of clients’ gradient updates. The MAML-FL algorithm of Fallah et al. (2020)
179
      builds upon the FedAvg approach by allowing clients to further fine-tune the received global model
180   through additional local gradient steps, thereby specializing it to their data and reducing local loss.
181   Similarly, the FedProx algorithm of Li et al. (2020) introduces a l2 regularization term for each
182   client to learn a mix of local and global models.
183
      Assessing Fairness. Given the sensitive demographic information g (e.g., race, gender, age) con-
184
      tained in each data point in our problem setting, we further assess the group fairness of the learned
185
      models. Different notions of group fairness have been proposed, each assessing a specific statis-
186   tical disparity in how the model treats individuals from different groups. Here, we consider three
187   notions of fairness: (1) Statistical Parity (SP) (Dwork et al., 2012; Ezzeldin et al., 2023), which
188   compares the difference in selection rate between the two groups (i.e., SP (✓) := |P r(ŷ(✓) =
189   1|g = a) P r(ŷ(✓) = 1|g = b)|, where ŷ(✓) is the label assigned to data points under model
190   ✓); (2) Equality of Opportunity (EqOp) (Hardt et al., 2016), which compares the differences in true
191   positive rates across groups (i.e., EqOp (✓) := |P r(ŷ(✓) = 1|g = a, y = 1) P r(ŷ(✓) = 1|g =
192   b, y = 1)|); and (3) Equalized Odd (EO) (Hardt et al., 2016), which is set to either the true posi-
193   tive rate or false positive rate difference between the two protected groups, whichever larger (i.e.,
194      EO (✓) := maxi2{0,1} |P r(ŷ(✓) = 1|g = a, y = i)    P r(ŷ(✓) = 1|g = b, y = i)|).
195   Comparing different algorithms. To compare the different algorithms’ performance, we will con-
196   trast the (average of the) local accuracy of each client’s local model ✓i (which can be obtained by
197   finding its standalone model, the global FedAvg model, a cluster-specific local model, or a fine-
198   tuned local model, or a regularized local model) when applied to the client’s local data Di . We will
199   similarly contrast the different algorithms in terms of the (average of) local f -fairness, evaluating
200
      each client’s local model on its local data under f 2 {SP, EqOp, EO}.

351   4.5 A NALYTICAL SUPPORT
352   To support and validate our findings from the numerical experiments, we also analytically show that
353   personalized Federated clustering algorithms (which cluster/group similar clients to improve their
354   models’ local accuracy) can also lead to better local fairness, when compared to a (non-personalized)
355   shared global model, under certain conditions. Our results are detailed in Appendix B. Specifically,
356
      we consider a setting where data has single-dimensional, normally distributed features, leading to

      6   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We have shown, both numerically and analytically, that there can be (unintended) fairness benefits
527
      to personalization in Federated Learning. We have identified potential statistical reasons (improved
528   data diversity) and computational reasons (alignments between local accuracy and fairness while
529   preventing overfitting) for this alignment. Motivated by these findings, we further proposed new
530   fairness-aware Federated clustering algorithms, Fair-FCA and Fair-FL+HC, which take both
531   the fairness and model performance into account when clustering clients. We find that this modifi-
532   cation can lead to an improved fairness-accuracy tradeoff, (partly) as personalization technique offer
533   dual benefits in terms of accuracy and fairness. Extending our analytical findings (both for clustered
534   FL algorithms, and to other classes of personalized FL methods), and identifying methods to inte-
535   grate fairness considerations into other (non-clustering based) personalized FL algorithms, are main
536
      directions of future work.
