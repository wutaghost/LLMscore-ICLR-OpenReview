011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Low-Dimension-to-High-Dimension (LDHD) generalization is a special case of
014
               Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization, where the training data are restricted
               to a low-dimensional subspace of the high-dimensional testing space. Assuming
015
               that each instance is generated from a latent variable and the dimension of the
016
               latent variable reflects the problem scale, the inherent scaling challenge in length
017            generalization can be captured by the LDHD generalization in the latent space. We
018            theoretically demonstrate that LDHD generalization is generally unattainable with-
019            out exploiting prior knowledge to provide appropriate inductive bias. Specifically,
020            we explore LDHD generalization in Boolean functions. We verify that different
021            architectures trained with (S)GD converge to min-degree interpolators w.r.t. dif-
022            ferent linearly independent sets. LDHD generalization is achievable if and only if
023            the target function coincides with this inductive bias. Applying the insights from
024            LDHD generalization to length generalization, we explain the effectiveness of CoT
025
               as changing the structure latent space to enable better LDHD generalization. We
               also propose a principle for position embedding design to handle both the inherent
026
               LDHD generalization and the nuisances such as the data format. Following the
027
               principle, we propose a novel position embedding called RPE-Square that remedies
028            the RPE for dealing with the data format nuisance.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The field of learning to reason has gained significant popularity in the machine learning community
034   due to its impressive performance in reasoning tasks such as natural language processing (OpenAI,
035   2023a;b), mathematics (Frieder et al., 2023; Jelassi et al., 2023), coding (Zhang et al., 2022a),
036
      symbolic logic (Abbe et al., 2023; Garcez et al., 2022), and planning (Zhao et al., 2023; Valmeekam
      et al., 2023). Reasoning problems inherently require generalization beyond the training distribution,
037
      a challenge known as Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization (Krueger et al., 2021; Ye et al.,
038
      2021). One of the most significant challenges in OOD generalization is scaling, also known as length
039   generalization (Anil et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b), where models trained on small-scale instances
040   must generalize to large-scale instances. Length generalization is crucial because the size of sample
041   spaces often increases exponentially with the complexity of reasoning problems, leading to intractable
042   sample complexity and computational costs for models that do not achieve length generalization.
043
      Numerous works have investigated the length generalization problem in various tasks and proposed
044
      various techniques that help scaling in practice, including modifications to model architectures (Shaw
045   et al., 2018; Jelassi et al., 2023; Kazemnejad et al., 2024), transformations in data formats (Lee et al.,
046   2023; Zhou et al., 2023), prompt engineering for Large Language Models (LLMs) Wei et al. (2022);
047   Feng et al. (2024). While some techniques work uniformly well across a wide class of problems,
048   many are fragile and even ad-hoc, applicable only to specific problems with certain formats (Zhou
049   et al., 2024). This can be largely attributed to the mismatch between the inherent problem scale and
050   the formal scale in the input domain (e.g., the length of the input string in language models). We
051   further elaborate the statement in Example 1.
052   Example 1. Consider the addition learning problem in the language domain, where the input is a
053   string. For an N -digit-plus-N -digit (written as N -addition) addition of two numbers x and y, where
      x = xN −1 . . . x0 , y = yN −1 . . . y0 , xi , yi ∈ {0, . . . , 9} (xN −1 > 0 or yN −1 > 0), consider two


                                                          1

054
      formats of the instance: the Aligned and Reverse Format (ARF), where the instance is represented
055   as “x0 . . . xn−1 + y0 . . . yn−1 =”; and the Unaligned and Reverse Format (URF), where the instance
056   is represented as “x0 . . . xnx −1 + y0 . . . yny −1 =”, nc = arg maxi {ci ̸= 0}, c ∈ {x, y}. The formal
057   scale in the language domain is the length of the string. However, the length of the input strings in
058   neither format faithfully reflects the inherent problem scale: in ARF, the length of the input strings
059   is always invariant; in URF, a shorter string may correspond to a larger scale than a longer string
060   (e.g., s1 =“1 + 1234 =” is 4-addition, s2 =“123 + 123 =” is 3-addition; however, |s1 | < |s2 |).
061   Furthermore, we find in experiments that Relative Position Embedding (RPE) improves the length
062   generalization performance of Transformers for ARF, while it provides little benefit for the length
063
      generalization of Transformers for URF.
064
      To develop robust and transferable methods for scalable models, it requires a formulation that captures
065   the inherent scaling challenge of length generalization and is invariant to the nuisances such as data
066   format. To do so, we first notice that in the length generalization of a wide range of problems, the
067   instances can be seen as generated from some latent space whose dimension reflects the scale.
068
      Example 2 (Addition). Let Σ = {0, . . . , 9} × {0, . . . , 9}. The instance of n-addition xn−1 . . . x0 +
069
      yn−1 . . . y0 can be represented by the state vector hn = [(x0 , y0 ) , . . . , (xn−1 , yn−1 )]. The k-th
070   dimension of hn corresponds to the (k − 1)-th digits of the two addenda (i.e., xk−1 and yk−1 ). The
071   expansion of the state vector in the dimension corresponds to the increase in the addendum digits.
072   Therefore, the length generalization from N0 -addition to N -addition can be seen as a generalization
073   from the low-dimensional latent space ΣN0 to the high-dimensional latent space ΣN .
074
075   To catch the above intuition, we make the following assumption about the underlying data generation.
076   Assumption 1 (Data Generation in Length Generalization). The data generation process of an
077   instance of scale n with the concept c is as follows:
078
079   1. A latent variable h is sampled from a subspace Σn of dimension n;
080
      2. The label y is determined by the concept c and the latent variable h, i.e., y = c(h);
081
082   3. The hidden variable h is transformed to an input sequence by the data format mapping ϕ.
083
084   According to Assumption 1, the inherent scaling challenge can be captured by Low-Dimension-to-
085
      High-Dimension (LDHD) generalization, a special scenario of OOD generalization with structural
      distributional shifts. In an LDHD generalization problem, a model is trained with samples from
086
      a low-dimensional subspace while evaluated with samples from a higher-dimensional superspace.
087
      See Figure 2 in Appendix B for an intuitive illustration of LDHD generalization, compared to in-
088   distribution and typical OOD generalization. To further illustrate, we can consider that the training
089   samples are from the space ΣN0 and the testing samples are from ΣN , where Σ is a domain and
090   the numbers N0 , N satisfy N0 < N . With slight abuse of notation, we also use Σn to denote the
091   embedding space of the subspace Σ into a space ΣN̄ for some sufficiently large N̄ ≥ N > N0 (we
092   use N̄ = N below if there is no special statement).
093
      The main challenge of LDHD generalization is that the testing space has extra dimensions compared
094
      to the training space. As a result, the testing space contains instances with orthogonal components
095
      to the training space. The training samples can reveal no information about how these components
096   contribute to the results. For instance, in Example 2, learning with N0 -addition solely does not tell
097   how (N0 + 1)-th digits to N -th digits contribute to the result unless we have the prior knowledge that
098   the addition of each digit shares the same process. We formally this challenge as No-Free-Lunch
099   Theorem of LDHD Generalization in Section 3.
100
      The No-Free-Lunch Theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997; Wolpert, 2002) of LDHD Generalization
101
      necessitates the use of prior knowledge in the learning process in order to achieve LDHD generaliza-
102   tion. In practice, the prior knowledge is usually incorporated via the inductive bias of the learning
103   algorithms and the models. Abbe et al. (2023) claimed that models with (S)GD converge to min-
104   degree interpolators. However, the minimal degree-profile bias neither holds uniformly for all model
105   architectures nor is sufficient for achieving LDHD generalization. To develop LDHD generalizable
106   models, we investigate different architectures whose inductive bias with (S)GD can potentially go
107   beyond the min-degree interpolators. While random feature models are shown to be min-degree
      interpolators, we prove that random feature models with projections, where inputs are transformed

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      by projections before fed to the random feature models, converge to min-degree interpolators under
109   different linearly independent sets. We further consider Position-Only Linear Attentions with Advice
110   (PLAA), i.e., linear attentions whose attention scores are determined only by positions, with addi-
111   tional hints about the scales of the instances. This model can be seen as a simplified abstraction of
112   decoder-only transformers with a special focus on positional relations that are considered crucial for
113   length generalization. We demonstrate the failure of PLAA models equipped with Absolute Position
114   Embeddings (APEs) in general cases and show the PLAA models with Relative Position Embeddings
115   (RPEs) are min-degree interpolators w.r.t. some linearly independent sets. These results show that
116   for models biased towards min-degree interpolators w.r.t. some linearly independent sets, LDHD
117
      generalization can be achieved if and only if the target mapping is the min-degree interpolator w.r.t.
      the same set.
118
119   The LDHD generalization perspective further provides insights on how Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei
120   et al., 2022) can help length generalization. We show that CoT can be seen as a change of the hidden
121   space, where each dimension of the latent space is augmented with an additional middle state. This
122   transformation could facilitate LDHD generalization in the latent space for some problems. Another
123
      implication is a principle of position embedding design for length generalization with Transformers:
      we need to handle both the inherent LDHD generalization and the nuisances such as the data format
124
      in the design of the position embeddings. Following this principle, we propose a novel position
125
      embedding named RPE-Square. The RPE-Square enhances the RPE with the ability to handle certain
126   data format nuisances, evidently improving the length generalization of the URF addition over the
127   Transformer with the RPE.
128
129
      The main contributions of the paper are summarized as follows:
130
      • We propose to study LDHD generalization, which captures the inherent scaling challenge of a wide
131
        range of length generalization problems. LDHD generalization provides a formulation under which
132     we can perform theoretical analysis for general length generalization problems.
133
134
      • We study the inductive bias of different model architectures under (S)GD, which can be exploited to
        incorporate prior knowledge to achieve LDHD generalization. In the context of Boolean functions,
135
        we show that different architectures under (S)GD can have inductive bias other than min-degree
136
        interpolators, which enables LDHD generalization for a wider class of problems.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
146
147
      Length generalization in reasoning problems. Length generalization is a key challenge in learning
148
      to reason, typically interpreted as the ability to learn with small-scale instances of a task and generalize
149
      to unseen large instances of the same task (Anil et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). Various reasoning
150   tasks are considered to investigate length generalization, including arithmetic (Jelassi et al., 2023;
151   Feng et al., 2024), Boolean logic (Abbe et al., 2023; d’Ascoli et al., 2023), symbolic reasoning (Zhang
152   et al., 2022b), etc. Despite the rich literature on length generalization on specific reason tasks, few
153   works have considered challenges and overconditions of length generalization for general problems.
154   The existing works that analyze general length generalization mainly focus on the change of the input
155   sequence length (Xiao & Liu, 2023; Ahuja & Mansouri, 2024), which does not precisely capture the
156   difficulties of length generalization. Our work proposes to consider LDHD generalization, which
157   characterizes the inherent challenges of length generalization in many tasks.
158   Inductive Bias of Model Architectures and Algorithms. In the situation of learning with over-
159   paratermization or incomplete information, proper inductive bias is essential to select the true model
160   from the hypothesis (Neyshabur, 2017; Bartlett et al., 2021). One of the most studied scenarios is
161   the inductive bias of different model architectures under (S)GD. Previous research shows (S)GD
      combined with different model architectures lead to different effects of implicit regularization, such as


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      different norms of the learnable parameters Gunasekar et al. (2017); Bartlett et al. (2021) and different
163   complexity characterizations of the models (Razin & Cohen, 2020; Razin et al., 2021; Abbe et al.,
164   2023). We specially mention Abbe et al. (2023), which proposes that models trained with (S)GD are
165   biased towards min-degree profile interpolators in the context of Boolean functions, which do not
166   achieve general LDHD generalization. Our results show that the min-degree profile bias does not hold
167   for all models. We further show that models with different architecture can converge to min-degree
168   profile interpolators under different linearly independent sets when trained with (S)GD. This partially
169   explains how model architectures can affect length generalization in reasoning problems.

209   We further investigate how model structures can affect LDHD generalization. We show in Section 4
210   that different models trained with (S)GD can be seen as min-degree interpolators under different

215   Many generalization challenges in reasoning problems can be attributed to LDHD generalization
      inherently. Length generalization is representative of these challenges and of significance in reasoning

      problems. Intuitively, length generalization means that a model trained with small-scale instances
217   of a reasoning problem can perform well on large-scale instances of the problem. To formalize the
218   intuition, we consider a typical instance being generated from a hidden state variable h that represents
219   the core of the instance and is transformed to the model input by a data format mapping. The hidden
220   state space is of the form Σ∗ for some domain Σ. The dimension n of the subspace from which the

319   We further elaborate on the intuition behind the PLAA models. In the generation process of a
320   decoder-only (linear) Transformer with position embeddings given input s = s1 . . . sn , the attention
321   is computed by the query at the position n and the keys at the positions i ≤ n. The position of the

443   5     F URTHER I MPLICATIONS FOR L ENGTH G ENERALIZATION
444   We discuss further implications of the LDHD generalization perspective for length generalization.
445
446   5.1    C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT FOR L ENGTH G ENERALIZATION
447
448   While the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) can lead to more variety in the length of testing samples,
449   it is widely used as an effective technique to improve the length generalization in various rea-
450
      soning tasks. This seems contradictory if considered in the original space of the input se-
      quence. However, when considering the abstraction of the data generating process from the
451
      hidden space, we can see that CoT intrinsically changes the underlying hidden space by extend-
452
      ing each dimension with a “middle” variable and does not lead to the dimensional increase in
453   the hidden space. For example, consider the n-addition without CoT and the n-addition with
454   CoT. In the case without CoT, the instance xn−1 . . . x0 + yn−1 . . . y0 = zn . . . z0 corresponds
455   to the latent state hn = [(x0 , y0 ) , . . . , (xn−1 , yn−1 )] ∈ Σn for Σ = {0, . . . , 9} × {0, . . . , 9}.
456   In the case with CoT, one step of predicting zt in solving the instance corresponds to the la-
457   tent state hn = [(x0 , y0 , z0 ) , . . . , (xt−1 , yt−1 , zt−1 ) , (xt , yt , ∗) . . . , (xn−1 , yn−1 , ∗)] ∈ Σ̄n for
458   Σ̄ = Σ × {∗, 0, . . . , 9}, where ∗ is a special element indicating undetermined values. CoT does not
459   cause the LDHD generalization challenge but extends the domain Σ, which could potentially lead to
460   a more easily learnable target concept under the model architecture.
461
462   5.2    P OSITION E MBEDDINGS FOR L ENGTH G ENERALIZATION
463
      Position embeddings are considered closely related to the length generalization in Transformers Our
464
      analysis suggests a principle to consider both the inherent LDHD generalization and the nuisances
465   such as the data format in the design of the position embeddings. To further elaborate, consider the
466   length generalization of the URF addition with CoT. While RPE can capture the recursive structure
467   of the addition problem and could lead to LDHD generalization in the latent space, it fails to work for
468   the URF addition with CoT. The failure of RPE is attributed to the nuisance of the URF.
469
      Following the principle, we design a novel position embedding called RPE-Square to handle the
470
      nuisance of the URF. On the one hand, we keep the RPE structure for the inherent LDHD generaliza-
471   tion. On the other hand, we deal with the unalignment by considering the distances to several special
472   tokens (i.e., [BOS], +, and =). These considerations lead to the RPE-Square, in which we compute
473   the relative values between the distances to the special tokens. More concretely, the RPE-Squarei,j
474   for the query at j and the key at i is

1000   E.1    D ETAILED E XPLANATION OF RPE-S QUARE
1001
       The design of RPE-Square follows the principle: when devising position embeddings for length
1002
       generalization, one needs to consider both the LDHD generalization in the latent space and the
1003   nuisance of the data format in the input sequence space. This principle is derived from the LDHD
1004   generalization perspective for length generalization, illustrating the insights of LDHD generalization
1005   for practical models.
1006
       To further elaborate, we show how RPE-Square is constructed in the guidance of the proposed
1007
       principle to achieve length generalization of the URF addition (with CoT).
1008
1009
       • LDHD generalization in the latent space. For the addition, the LDHD generalization in the latent
1010     space can be handled by RPE.
1011
       • The data format nuisance of URF. To handle the data format, we need to consider the
1012

1038   RPE-Square is designed to encode the prior knowledge of both the LDHD generalization and the data
1039
       format, combining RPE and the approach to deal with the URF data format. The position embedding
       for the query j and the key i is determined by the relative distance between the relative distance
1040
       of j to the position of some token sl , and the relative distance of i to the position of some token xk ,
1041
       parameterized by R(j−l)−(i−k) . The “relative distance of relative distances” design is where the
1042   name RPE-Square comes from. Furthermore, we want to learn the special tokens automatically rather
1043   than specify them by hand-craft. To achieve this, we adopt a “soft” design. taking a weighted average
1044   over all 1 ≤ l ≤ j, 1 ≤ k ≤ i. The weight of R(j−l)−(i−k) depends on the tokens, which is the
1045   multiplication between the attention score of the query xj and the key xl , and the attention score of
1046   the query xi and the key xk . This design makes RPE-Square more flexible and potentially applicable
1047   to other problems of structures similar to the URF addition. We conduct an addition experiment in
1048   Appendix E.3, showing that RPE-Square can achieve length generalization beyond the URF addition.
