011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Models based on Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) have been widely employed
014            for text classification tasks. Traditional RNNs primarily emphasize long-term
015            memory capabilities. However, this approach does not fully align with human
016
               cognitive learning processes, particularly in the context of classification tasks.
               The human brain typically extracts essential information relevant to the classi-
017
               fication categories, disregards irrelevant details, and compresses the input to ac-
018
               celerate decision-making. Inspired by this, we propose a novel architecture, the
019            Fast Salient Factor Concentration (FSFC) RNN, specifically designed for classi-
020            fication tasks. FSFC dynamically clusters and compresses semantic information
021            by leveraging the short-term memory capabilities of recurrent neural networks.
022            Experimental results demonstrate that FSFC achieves performance comparable to
023            existing RNNs, while significantly improving training efficiency in classification
024            tasks. Based on the YelpReviewFull dataset, FSFC improves accuracy by 1.37%
025            over Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), while reducing training time by 86%.
026            Additionally, we propose a new evaluation metric, E-score, which integrates both
027
               accuracy and time efficiency to comprehensively assess the overall performance
               of each network.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Text classification is an important and fundamental problem in the field of natural language process-
      ing (NLP) (Du et al., 2020; Joulin et al., 2016; Magalhães et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b), with
034
      wide applications such as spam filtering, sentiment analysis, and news categorization (Wang et al.,
035
      2018; Yao et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2018). With the advancement of deep learning technologies, nu-
036   merous deep learning models have been introduced into text classification tasks. Recurrent Neural
037   Networks (RNNs), especially Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTM) (Hochreiter & Schmid-
038   huber, 1997) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) (Cho, 2014), have garnered significant attention in
039   the field of text classification (Liu & Guo, 2019; Luan & Lin, 2019).
040
      However, in classification tasks, the long-term memory mechanisms of traditional RNNs do not
041
      fully align with human cognitive learning processes. When processing long texts or audio, humans
042   typically rely on short-term memory, focusing on task-relevant key information while ignoring ir-
043   relevant content. Through selective attention mechanisms, working memory prioritizes important
044   information and dynamically adjusts the focus and granularity of information processing (Hu et al.,
045   2024; Jeanneret et al., 2023). This ability to compress and organize information allows humans to
046   make more efficient decisions in complex tasks (Hu et al., 2024).
047
      Inspired by cognitive mechanisms, we propose a novel RNN architecture specifically designed for
048   classification tasks, called Fast Salient Factor Concentration Recurrent Neural Network (FSFC).
049   Unlike traditional RNNs that predominantly rely on long-term memory (Duarte & Berton, 2023; Lu
050   et al., 2023; Soni et al., 2022), FSFC fully exploits the short-term memory capabilities of RNNs
051   while simplifying the network by removing complex gating mechanisms, leading to a significant
052   improvement in computational efficiency. Moreover, FSFC enhances the processing of crucial in-
053   formation by employing dynamic clustering and semantic compression techniques. Experimental
      results indicate that FSFC achieves performance on par with existing RNN models, while consid-

      erably reducing training time in classification tasks. The primary contributions of this work are as
055   follows:
056
057         1. We propose a novel RNN architecture, FSFC (Fast Salient Factor Concentration), devel-
058            oped as an alternative to traditional RNN components. By integrating a semantic segmen-
059            tation and clustering mechanism, FSFC effectively compresses textual information while
060            utilizing the short-term memory capabilities of RNNs, leading to a significant enhancement

062         2. We introduce a cognitive function for FSFC, inspired by the human learning process that

070   2.1   T RADITIONAL T EXT C LASSIFICATION
071
072   Traditional research in text classification primarily focuses on feature engineering and classification
073   algorithms (Yao et al., 2019). In early studies, conventional machine learning methods, such as
074
      Support Vector Machines (SVM) (Zhang et al., 2010) and logistic regression (Genkin et al., 2007),
      relied on sparse representation techniques, including the Bag of Words (BoW) model and TF-IDF.
075
      These methods classify text by converting it into word frequency or weighted frequency matrices.
076
      However, sparse representations fail to capture the contextual relationships between words, leading
077   to significant limitations when handling complex texts (Wang et al., 2024).
078
079   To address the limitations of sparse representations, (Mikolov, 2013) introduced the Word2Vec
080
      model, which utilizes a Skip-gram architecture to embed words into a high-dimensional vector space
      via neural networks, thereby capturing local contextual information within the text. Each word’s em-
081
      bedding vector carries rich semantic information, and the cosine distance between these vectors can
082
      effectively measure semantic similarity. Building on the Word2Vec model, researchers have pro-
083   posed various improved embedding models, such as GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014), Doc2Vec (Le
084   & Mikolov, 2014), and fastText (Xiong et al., 2021). These models enhance the understanding of
085   textual semantics through more complex structured representations.
086
      Unlike traditional word embedding models, FSFC employs a dynamic adjustment approach in
087
      its embedding layer. Traditional models are static and typically require pre-constructed corpora,
088
      demonstrating poor adaptability to new texts. In contrast, FSFC embedding layer continuously
089   adjusts embedding vectors during training based on the loss function, analogous to how the hu-
090   man brain refines its understanding of new information. Dynamic embedding method enhances the
091   model’s adaptability and reduces its reliance on pre-trained corpora.
092
093   2.2   S EQUENTIAL M ODELS FOR T EXT C LASSIFICATION
094
095   Neural networks based on GRU and LSTM architectures are mainly applied to learn multiple rich-
096   semantic sequential information in the relationships between words and their belonged documents
097   (Pham et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2016). (Kumar & S, 2022) proposed a hybrid model that combines
098   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) (LeCun et al., 1989) with Long Short-Term Memory net-
099
      works (LSTM) to improve short text classification performance. CNNs extract spatial features from
      the text, while LSTMs handle temporal sequence features, effectively capturing both local informa-
100
      tion and sequential dependencies in short texts. (Du et al., 2020) introduced an efficient recurrent
101
      neural network architecture based on Broad Learning System (BLS) (Chen & Liu, 2017), known as
102   R-BLS and G-BLS, which are similar to LSTM architectures. By incorporating BLS, this architec-
103   ture significantly accelerates training speed and mitigates common issues such as gradient vanishing
104   and explosion typically associated with RNNs and LSTMs. R-BLS addresses the limitations of
105   traditional BLS in processing sequential information and word importance, while G-BLS further
106   enhances information processing capabilities by introducing LSTM-like forget gates, enabling the
107   network to retain relevant information while discarding irrelevant data. (Behzadidoost et al., 2024)
      proposed a stacked BILSTM-SVM model that integrates Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      networks (BILSTM) (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997) with Support Vector Machines (SVM). This model
109   merges the two using a stacked approach to enhance text classification performance. The bidirec-
110   tional LSTM captures contextual information from both forward and backward directions, extracting
111   deep semantic features (Lu et al., 2023), while the SVM utilizes the high-dimensional semantic fea-
112   tures extracted by BILSTM for final classification.
113
      Although the impressive performance of LSTM and GRU based models in text classification tasks
114
      (Nithya et al., 2024), they still exhibit limitations when handling long texts. The gating mechanisms
115   of LSTM and GRU are primarily designed to capture long-term dependencies (Lu & Xu, 2023;
116   Fathnejat et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023). However, in text classification tasks, models often need
117   to focus only on key information relevant to the categories rather than all details within the text.
118   This reliance on long-term memory may result in models capturing a significant amount of irrel-
119   evant information during lengthy text processing, thus reducing training efficiency and increasing
120   computational overhead. Furthermore, these architectures still struggle to completely mitigate the
121   prevalent issues of gradient vanishing or explosion found in RNNs (Reusens et al., 2024). These
122   challenges suggest that relying solely on long-term memory RNN architectures may not be entirely
123
      suitable for text classification tasks.

125   3     M ETHODOLOGY
126
127
      To address the inefficiency caused by the processing of redundant information in traditional RNNs
128   for text classification tasks, we propose the Fast Salient Factor Concentration Recurrent Neural Net-
129   work (FSFC). This model focuses on short-term memory and is capable of dynamically aggregating
130   and compressing semantic information from the text, thereby reducing the computational load and
131   accelerating the classification process. FSFC is inspired by cognitive mechanisms in the human
132   brain, where essential task-related features are swiftly extracted in complex informational environ-
133   ments, while less relevant details are disregarded (Fonollosa et al., 2015).
134   FSFC consists of four stages: text mapping, semantic segmentation, clustering and compression,
135   and category classification. Our experiments demonstrate that FSFC not only achieves accuracy
136   comparable to traditional RNNs(LSTM, GRU) but also significantly improves training efficiency in
137   text classification tasks. Figure 1 illustrates the operational mechanism of FSFC.

160   the embedding layer is a weight matrix, where each row corresponds to the vector representation of
161   a word in the vocabulary. During training, the weights are updated according to the gradient of the
      loss function.

      3.2   C LUSTERING C OMPRESSION AND C ATEGORY C LASSIFICATION
205
206   With the reference vector Rf , we can quickly obtain a cosine similarity matrix S ∈ Rb×v , where b
207   represents the batch size and v represents the sequence length. The core of the clustering operation
208   is achieved through a masking mechanism. Based on predefined thresholds, the cosine similarity
209   is segmented into intervals, and the corresponding mask matrix is generated from S. Through the
210   weighted operation of the mask matrix, we can extract the embedding vectors that contain relevant
211   semantic information.
212   For the cosine similarity, we assume the following: for an n-class classification task, the similarity
213   can be divided into at most n + 1 segments. This means that for an n-class problem, the content
214   can be segmented into n + 1 parts, corresponding to the content relevant to each of the n classes
215   and the content unrelated to these n classes. In human learning, the process of classification often
      begins with detailed distinctions and gradually simplifies over time. Initially, due to insufficient


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      understanding of the classes, humans tend to divide the content into more detailed categories. How-
217   ever, with accumulated experience, the cognitive system evolves to adopt a more efficient strategy,
218   reducing the number of classes and retaining only the most important distinctions (Žauhar et al.,
219   2016; Constantinidis et al., 2023). We believe that under extreme conditions, complex tasks can
220   only be simplified to binary classification decisions at most. This simplification mechanism aligns
221   with Bayesian classification theory and the entropy minimization principle in information theory.
222   It is important to note that by ”extreme conditions,” we mean that not all multidimensional classi-
223   fication problems can be fully reduced to binary classification. In tasks involving highly complex
224   features, the simplification process may be constrained. And, (Wang et al., 2023a) demonstrated that
225   for non-linear RNNs to approximate stable non-linear sequential relationships, the memory structure
226
      must exhibit exponential decay. Based on the above theories, we designed a cognitive function for
      FSFC to dynamically adjust the granularity of classification. The cognitive function is expressed as
227
      shown in equation 6:
228
229
                                      Cn = C0 − (C0 − Cf ) × (1 − e−kn )                               (6)
230   Where Cn represents the complexity at the n-th training epoch, C0 is the initial complexity, and
231   Cf is the final complexity. n denotes the current training epoch, and k is the cognitive coefficient,
232   which controls the rate at which cognitive complexity decreases. The introduction of this cognitive
233   function enhances the model generalization ability.
234   Cosine similarity matrix not only helps the model perform effective semantic segmentation but can
235   also be used to generate a mask matrix for clustering. To improve computational efficiency, we
236   propose a method for generating the mask matrix by expanding the data dimensions and calculating
237   the mask matrix in parallel. Specifically, based on predefined thresholds, the embedding vectors
238   are divided into different similarity intervals, each corresponding to a mask matrix. All batches
239   of mask matrices can be generated in a single computation. The generated mask matrix has the
240
      structure (c, b, v, 1), where c denotes the number of classes, b denotes the batch size, and v denotes
      the sequence length. Each mask matrix corresponds to a class and marks the words that belong
241
      to that class. By generating the mask matrices in batches, we can achieve clustering for multiple
242
      classes in a single operation.

436   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
437
438   In this study, we propose a novel recurrent neural network component, FSFC, designed specifically
439   for text classification tasks, as a potential replacement for existing RNN components. FSFC ef-
440
      fectively reduces sequence length by performing semantic clustering and compression on the text,
      which helps mitigate issues such as gradient vanishing or explosion. Unlike traditional recurrent
441
      neural networks, FSFC focuses on leveraging the short-term memory capability of RNNs. Further-
442
      more, to comprehensively evaluate both the accuracy and training time of the network, we introduce
443   a new evaluation metric, E-score, which combines model accuracy with training time, providing a
444   more holistic measure of performance. Through the E-score, we are better able to assess the balance
445   between accuracy and computational efficiency across different networks, particularly in scenarios
446   where both model precision and time constraints must be considered.
447
      FSFC network demonstrates slightly lower accuracy compared to LSTM and GRU, primarily due to
448
      its omission of positional information between words. In future work, we plan to design a positional
449   encoder to incorporate word position information during the clustering and compression process,
450   thereby improving the accuracy of the FSFC network.
