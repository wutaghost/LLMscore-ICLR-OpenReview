009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual prompting (VP) has emerged as a popular method to repurpose pretrained
012            vision models for adaptation to downstream tasks. Unlike conventional model fine-
013            tuning techniques, VP introduces a universal perturbation directly into the input
014            data to facilitate task-specific fine-tuning rather than modifying model parameters.
015            However, there exists a noticeable performance gap between VP and conventional
016
               fine-tuning methods, highlighting an unexplored realm in theory and practice
               to understand and advance (input-level) VP to reduce its current performance
017
               gap. Towards this end, we introduce a generalized concept, termed activation
018
               prompt (AP), which extends the scope of (input-level) VP by enabling universal
019            perturbations to be applied to activation maps within the intermediate layers of the
020            model. By using AP to revisit the problem of VP and employing it as an analytical
021            tool, we demonstrate the intrinsic limitations of VP in both performance and
022            efficiency, revealing why input-level prompting may lack effectiveness compared to
023            AP, which exhibits a model-dependent layer preference. We show that AP is closely
024            related to normalization tuning in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and vision
025            transformers (ViTs), although each model type has distinct layer preferences for
026            prompting. We also theoretically elucidate the rationale behind such preference by
027
               analyzing global features across layers. Through extensive experiments across 29
               datasets and various model architectures, we provide a comprehensive performance
028
               analysis of AP, comparing it with VP and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
029
               baselines. Our results demonstrate AP’s superiority in both accuracy and efficiency,
030            considering factors such as time, parameters, memory usage, and throughput.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large pretrained models have emerged as fundamental components in deep learning (DL) (Brown
036   et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023a) in recent
037   years. Despite their exceptional performance, the substantial increase in computational demands,
038   as highlighted in recent studies (Frantar and Alistarh, 2023), has underlined the need for more
039
      economical and lightweight fine-tuning approaches. Thus, the pretraining-finetuning paradigm rises,
      allowing for quickly adapting a pretrained model to a plethora of downstream tasks (Jia et al., 2022;
040
      Hu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022a; Cai et al., 2020; Sung et al., 2022; Pfeiffer et al., 2020; Chen
041
      et al., 2023a). Among the various parameter-efficient finetuning (PEFT) methods (Hu et al., 2021;
042   Chen et al., 2022a; Pfeiffer et al., 2020; He et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023), prompting technique has
043   been gaining popularity in the vision domain (Liu et al., 2023; Li and Liang, 2021).
044
045
      Different from the model-centric PEFT techniques in computer vision (CV), the conventional visual
      prompting (VP) crafts specific input perturbations (known as ‘prompts’) to reprogram the pretrained
046
      model for a targeted task, without altering the model parameters. This offers a new data-centric
047
      viewpoint to analyze, understand, and harness the pretrained model (Chen et al., 2023a). However,
048   despite the recent advancement, the performance of state-of-the-art (SOTA) VP methods still lags
049   behind model-based fine-tuning methods (Chen et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2022). It appears that the
050   potential of VP has not been fully realized for vision models, particularly when considering its relative
051   progress compared to its counterpart in natural language processing (NLP) (Liu et al., 2023; Li and
052   Liang, 2021). In this work, we aim to provide a rigorous and comprehensive examination of VP and
053   explore its enhancement tailored for vision models, including convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
      and vision Transformers (ViTs). In particular, we ask:

054
          (Q) Is VP (visual prompting) truly beneficial for improving vision models and tasks, and under
055
          what conditions does it prove effective or ineffective?
056
057   To tackle question (Q), we present a generalized variant of VP termed activation prompt (AP), which
058   involves the incorporation of learnable perturbations into the activation maps of intermediate layers,
059   rather than focusing solely on the input layer. See Fig. 1 for an illustration. The introduction of AP
060   allows us to study the (in)effectiveness of (input-level) VP, as VP can be treated as a specific realization
061   of AP. By employing AP as both a bridge and an analytical tool, we show that the conventional
062   input-based VP might not be the most effective or efficient design. In fact, appropriately implemented
063   AP can outperform traditional VP significantly. To shed light on the underlying mechanism of AP, we
064
      present both empirical evidence and theoretical insights. It is also worth noting that, unlike VP, which
      can be applied in a black-box model setting (Tsai et al., 2020; Oh et al., 2023), AP requires modifying
065
      the parameters of intermediate activation maps and is only applicable in a white-box setting.

      Visual prompting. VP was first proposed in (Bahng et al., 2022a; Jia et al., 2022) to extend the
102   prompting technique in NLP. A similar idea with a different name, known as adversarial reprogram-
103   ming, was also proposed earlier in CV (Elsayed et al., 2018; Chen, 2022; Neekhara et al., 2018; 2022;
104   Chen et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2022b). It aims at re-purposing a fixed pretrained
105   model to adapt to a new task. Recent advancement focuses on improved label mapping (Chen et al.,
106   2021; Yang et al., 2023) and normalization strategy (Wu et al., 2022) to enhance VP. Other works fur-
107   ther extend VP to areas like adversarial defense (Chen et al., 2023b; Mao et al., 2022) and distribution
      shift (Huang et al., 2023a; Tsai et al., 2023), and vision-language models (Zhou et al., 2022).

      Theoretical study on prompt engineering. Existing theoretical works on prompt engineering include
109   the expressive power of the introduced parameter (Wei et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2023b; Akyürek et al.,
110   2022), the optimization process (Ding et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023), and the generalization
111   analysis (Xie et al., 2021; Oymak et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023a; Huang et al.,
112   2023b; Li et al., 2024a;b). Most studies concentrate on in-context learning, a tuning-free hard prompt
113   method. In contrast, for soft prompt tuning, Wei et al. (2021) show that prompting is powerful enough
114   to remove nonessential information for the downstream task. Ding et al. (2022) interpret prompt
115   tuning as a subspace optimization method for the solution or functional space. Notably, there is solely
116   one study (Oymak et al., 2023) on the generalization dynamics of gradient-based prompt tuning but
117
      relying on a single-layer Transformer architecture without the MLP layer, making it incapable of
      examining the impact of multiple layers.
118
119   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). PEFT demonstrates that only finetuning a small part of a
120   large pretrained model can achieve outstanding performance. In the domain of CV, besides prompting-
121   based methods, PEFT methods can be roughly classified into two categories. The former (Basu et al.,
122   2023; Xu et al., 2023) focuses on identifying a small ratio of parameters to update from the pretrained
123
      model, such as normalization tuning (Basu et al., 2023). The latter designs additional modules to the
      original network backbone to adapt to downstream tasks (Hu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022a; Pfeiffer
124
      et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021; Lian et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b; Luo
125
      et al., 2023). Examples include LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), adapter-based methods (Chen et al., 2022a;
126   Pfeiffer et al., 2020; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023), and FACT (Jie and Deng, 2023)
127   that tensorizes the ViT weights to a 3D tensor and reduces the tunable parameter ratio to less than
128   0.01%. We note that AP differentiates itself from the methods above by avoiding additional inference
129   overheads or any requirements on the model architectures.

      Preliminaries on classical VP. VP harnesses universal pixel-level perturbations applied to input
      images as a means of model adaptation (Bahng et al., 2022b). For example, VP enables the transfer
135
      learning of an ImageNet-trained source model to various downstream tasks without the need for fine-
136
      tuning the model weights. It has sparked significant interest in the recent research (Chen et al., 2023a;
137   Wu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a; Bahng et al., 2022b; Tsai et al., 2020). Concretely, let fθ denote
138   the pre-trained source model parameterized by θ, and D = {(x1 , y1 ), (x2 , y2 ), . . . , (xN , yN )}
139   denote the fine-tuning dataset for a downstream task, with x and y being the data feature and label,
140   respectively. The objective of VP is to obtain a perturbation vector, denoted as δVP , which is tailored
141   to a specific task but remains agnostic to the input data. This vector is then used to transform the input
142   data x through the function g(x, δVP ). Here g symbolizes the transformation template function that
143   molds the input image to fit the desired prompt pattern. Two prevalent templates include the addition
144   g(x, δVP ) = x + δVP (Zhang et al., 2022a; Bahng et al., 2022b), and the resize-and-concatenation
145
      g(x, δVP ) = [δVP , M (x)] (Chen et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2022a), where M is the resizing function.
      Unless specified otherwise, we consider the additive VP formulation.
146
147   Activation prompt (AP): Generalizing VP in feature space. The conventional VP approach
148   primarily focuses on making direct modifications to the input data. However, this direct manipulation
149   may have two limitations. First, raw input data typically contains an abundance of details, which can
150   introduce complications for tasks like prompt generation due to issues such as background clutter
151
      and semantic ambiguity (Yu et al., 2017). In contrast, intermediate features tend to encompass a
      broader range of local and global attributes, preserving more class-discriminative information for
152
      decision-making (Bau et al., 2017). Second, parameter updates in VP demand gradient propagation
153
      throughout the entire network. Consequently, even with a lower number of tunable parameters, the
154   training cost may increase.
155
156   Motivated by the above, we broaden the scope of VP into the feature domain and introduce the
157
      concept of activation prompting (AP), see Fig. 1 for an illustration. Given a neural network model
158
      with L layers, represented as θ = [θ (1) , θ (2) , . . . , θ (L) ], the output from the l-th layer is denoted
159   as z (l) = fθ(l) (z (l−1) ), where z (0) = x (i.e., the input date). Similar to VP, AP at the l-th layer is
160   defined by a perturbation vector δ (l) to the intermediate feature z (l) , leading to the ‘prompted’ feature
161   map g(z (l) , δ (l) ) = z (l) + δ (l) . We denote the output with the l-th-layer AP given θ as fθ (x, δ (l) ).
      The objective of AP is then to optimize δ (l) so as to facilitate the adaptation of the fixed source

      Drawing upon the analyses presented above and insights in Fig. 4, we hypothesize that AP exhibits a
271   preference for deep layers in CNNs and shallow layers in ViTs, which can be attributed to the models’
272   inclinations toward global features over local features. To bolster our hypothesis, we investigate how
273   global information is distributed across the layers of ViTs. We employ a methodology used in (Raghu
274   et al., 2021) and (Walmer et al., 2023) to compute the average attention distance between the position
275   of query tokens and the locations they attend to with the query within each self-attention head in ViTs.
276   This analysis unveils how each self-attention layer contributes to the balance between local and global
277   information in the overall representation. In Fig. 5 (B), we present the average attention distance
278   across 16 attention heads for with different layer indices of a pretrained ViT-Large/16. A general
279
      trend can be observed: the distribution of the sorted attention distance moves firstly downwards (layer
      index from 1 to layer 12). This implies that the ratio of the global features captured by attention in
280
      general decreases. When the layer index is larger than 15, the global feature ratio slightly increases.
281
      This trend roughly aligns well with the patterns observed in Fig. 4. These observations underscore
282   our claim that AP’s layer preference is influenced by the presence of global features. We provide
283   theoretical support in the following section to support the layer and architecture effect. In particular,
284   we focus on the more challenging part of ViTs, since the study on CNNs is abundant. Furthermore,

      Remark on the comparison of AP vs. VPT. While VPT (Jia et al., 2022) also suggests adding
287
      extra tokens (prompts) to all intermediate layers of a ViT, our approach differs fundamentally. AP
288   was motivated to introduce a broader framework for VP, where prompts are applied to intermediate
289   activations at any single layer, rather than across multiple or all layers as in VPT. This allows us to
290   rigorously explore optimal layer selection for effective prompting, where (input-level) VP is covered
291   as a special case. Unlike VPT, AP uncovers new insights into layer-specific effects, architectural
292   dependencies, and their explanations, supported by both empirical and theoretical analyses (as will
293   be evident later). Furthermore, our findings show that strategic layer selection in AP can match or
294   surpass the effectiveness of VPT’s multi-layer prompting (See Tab. 4 in Sec. 6).

      From a perspective of generalization, we focus on studying the layer and architecture effect for
299
      ViTs: To achieve the desired generalization performance (or test accuracy), will shallow-layer AP
300   tuning require less sample complexity than deep-layer ones for ViTs? If so, with the same sample
301   complexity, shallow-layer AP could achieve better performance than deep-layer ones. To show this,
302   we present the theoretical setups that satisfy the conditions of global features for ViTs, followed by
303   the generalization analysis with sample complexity bound in Theorem 1.

      of Transformers (Li et al., 2023b; Oymak et al., 2023; Tarzanagh et al., 2023), we derive theoretical
306   insights by considering a binary classification problem. We use a single-head, two-layer ViT (Huang
307   et al., 2023c; Tian et al., 2023; Nichani et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b) as the pretrained model,

      Limitations and discussions. We acknowledge a potential limitation of AP lies in its implicit reliance
520
      on the size of the pretrained model as a factor for achieving superior accuracy. For compact models
521   like ResNet-18 and ViT-Tiny, while AP enhances the performance of VP, it does not outperform
522   N ORM -T UNE. This observation suggests that AP may primarily utilize downstream data to guide or
523   “direct” the existing learned knowledge obtained during pretraining, rather than actively acquiring new
524   knowledge. However, we believe that this limitation does not prevent AP from future applications to
525   larger foundational vision models. We also note that, unlike VP, AP cannot be applied in black-box
526   settings where parameters are inaccessible. However, the primary motivation of this work is to
527   explore the conditions under which VP is effective or ineffective, using AP as an analytical tool to
528   study layer selection preferences for prompting. By doing so, AP broadens the scope of VP, providing
529
      deeper insights into its underlying mechanisms under different model settings.

535   connection with normalization tuning. Additionally, we investigate the layer preference of AP on
536   CNNs and ViTs both empirically and theoretically. Our experiments demonstrate the superiority of
537   AP over VP, highlighting its efficiency advantages, and showcasing comparable performance to the
