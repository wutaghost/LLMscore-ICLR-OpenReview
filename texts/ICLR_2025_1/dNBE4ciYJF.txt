009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities across var-
012               ious tasks, that are learned from massive amounts of text-based data. Although
013               LLMs can control output sequence length, particularly through instruction-based
014               settings, the internal mechanisms behind this control has been unexplored. In this
015               study, we provide empirical evidence on how output sequence length information
016
                  is encoded within the internal representations of LLMs. In particular, our find-
                  ings show that multi-head attention mechanisms are critical in determining output
017
                  sequence length, which can be adjusted in a disentangling manner. By scaling
018
                  specific hidden units within the model, we can control the output sequence length
019               without losing the informativeness of the generated text, thereby indicating that
020               length information is partially disentangled from semantic information. Moreover,
021               some hidden units become increasingly active as prompts become more length-
022               specific, thus reflecting the model’s internal awareness of this attribute. Our find-
023               ings suggest that LLMs have learned robust and adaptable internal mechanisms
024               for controlling output length without external controls.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large language models (LLMs) have gained considerable attention in recent years for their remark-
029   able task-solving capabilities (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Bubeck et al., 2023). LLMs
030   are trained to predict the next word in a sequence. They can produce coherent and informative
031   text, which demonstrates their implicit understanding of diverse linguistic structures (Tenney et al.,
032   2019; Niu et al., 2022; Beguš et al., 2023). Furthermore, they also learn when to stop generating
033   text to ensure that the output adheres to appropriate length constraints (Juseon-Do et al., 2024). In
034   LLMs, controlling output sequence length is crucial for real-world applications, such as summariza-
035   tion, where fitting content within specified length limits without losing informativeness is crucial.
036   Therefore, the number of studies attempting to improve length controllability has increased drasti-
037
      cally (Shen et al., 2023; Jie et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024).
038   Based on advancements in instruction-based LLMs, it is observed that injecting constraints into
039   prompts can further effectively control output length without requiring model modifications (Juseon-
040   Do et al., 2024). However, these prompt engineering methods mainly focus on external controls, and
041   it has not been explored how LLMs internally encode and constrain output sequence length. Users
042   of LLMs usually have a desired length for generated texts in applications, such as text summariza-
043
      tion (Liu et al., 2018; Makino et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023), machine transla-
      tion (Wu et al., 2016; Murray & Chiang, 2018; Zhuocheng et al., 2023), knowledge QA, or dialogue
044
      generation (Liu et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2021). Understanding these internal mechanisms is critical
045
      for achieving precise length control, while enhancing the interpretability and robustness of LLMs in
046   generation-based systems. Herein, we aim to investigate how output sequence length information
047   is encoded within the internal representations of general transformer architectures. Specifically, we
048   first investigate which components within LLM transformer layers contribute to length control. Our
049   findings reveal that the outputs from multi-head attention mechanisms in the lower layers play a key
050   role in determining and controlling output sequence length in a tunable and disentangled manner.
051
      We empirically demonstrate, based on human evaluations, that we can adjust output length during
052   generation without losing the coherence and informativeness of texts by scaling specific hidden

068   2       R ELATED W ORK
069
070   Large Language Models. In recent years, LLMs have achieved considerable success due to their
071   remarkable task-solving abilities, specifically in zero-shot settings (Radford et al., 2019; Brown
072   et al., 2020). LLMs have been broadly categorized into open and closed models. The open models,
073   such as the Llama or Phi family, offer flexible access to modify their architectures, while the closed
074   models, such as ChatGPT,2 have demonstrated remarkable reasoning abilities in various natural
075
      language processing tasks (Jiao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Laskar et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Xie
      et al., 2023; 2024; Juseon-Do et al., 2024). Recent studies have focused on finding better methods
076
      to prompt LLMs (Zhou et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023).
077
078   Interpretability. Due to increasing interest in investigating the internal mechanisms of deep neural
079   networks (Räuker et al., 2023), significant attempts have been made to understand LLMs with a
080   focus on models like BERT (Tenney et al., 2019; Rogers et al., 2020; Niu et al., 2022), GPT (Hanna
081
      et al., 2023), and even multimodal models (Goh et al., 2021). For instance, Gurnee & Tegmark
      (2024) showed that, when handling various prompts, LLMs learn linear representations of space
082
      and time across multiple scales that show robustness. They also showed that next token prediction
083
      can be changed simply by disentangling hidden units related to time. Heinzerling & Inui (2024)
084   introduced directions that encode numeric properties in an interpretable manner; hence, by disen-
085   tangling these representations, LLM prediction can change accordingly. Moreover, there have been
086   attempts to investigate how in-context learning with LLMs behaves similar to explicit fine-tuning
087   for better understanding them (Dai et al., 2023). Early efforts to investigate how neural networks
088   treat length information have focused on memory cell networks in LSTMs, as they recursively en-
089   code and decode sequences, though they failed to find single units related to length information (Shi
090   et al., 2016).
091   Length Controllable Summarization. Text summarization aims to produce a concise summary
092   from an original text by retaining informative contents (Liu et al., 2018; Takase & Okazaki, 2019;
093   Li et al., 2020; He et al., 2022). As the summarization often requires additional constraints such
094   as a desired summary length, previous studies have focused on learning length-specific parame-
095   ters (Kikuchi et al., 2016; Schumann et al., 2020; Ghalandari et al., 2022), injecting direct con-
096   straints (Takase & Okazaki, 2019; Makino et al., 2019), or splitting the training dataset into specific
097   length ranges (He et al., 2022). Recently, Juseon-Do et al. (2024) considered in-context learning and
098
      demonstrated that LLMs can control output sequence length through “length priming”. This method
      involves injecting more length-specific information into prompts, thereby allowing the model to ad-
099
      just output sequence length without modifying model architectures or learning parameters. Jie et al.
100
      (2024) considered length control types such as greater/smaller than a value with exhaustive model
101   modifications with reinforcement learning.
102
103   To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first attempt to interpret how length information is
104
      encoded in LLMs and demonstrate how length-specific information is partially disentangled from
      semantic information. Furthermore, by comparing various length-specific prompts, we investigate
105
      how in-context learning and fine-tuning can influence the internal representations of LLMs with

113
114
      Our goal is to understand whether and how length representations are encoded in LLMs when using
      various length-constraint prompts. For this, we extracted outputs from different components and
115
      layers of transformer architectures during text generation. We then applied linear regression to
116
      predict the generation time steps from these hidden states. We used the coefficient of determination,
117   R2 , to evaluate the extent to which internal states of transformer capture length representations.

      3.1      S UMMARIZATION DATASET
120
121   For our investigation, we used the Google sentence summarization dataset,3 given that summariza-
122   tion often requires additional constraints, such as a desired summary length (Takase & Okazaki,
123   2019; Schumann et al., 2020; Ghalandari et al., 2022). This dataset was automatically created by
124   considering the syntactic dependency tree structure from news headlines (Filippova & Altun, 2013).
125   The training, validation, and test datasets consist of 200,000, 1,000, and 1,000 pairs, respectively.
126
      The average gold compression ratio is 0.45 for the test dataset used in the evaluation.

      words to keep. We inject the length of ground-truth summaries for the length constraint instructions.

      3.2      M ODELS AND M ETHODS
144
145   Models. We performed our experiments using the Llama-2 family of pre-trained LLMs, which range
146   from 7B to 70B parameters (Touvron et al., 2023). These include the Llama-3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024)
147   and the Phi-3 family of Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct, as well as the Phi-3-small-8k-instruct (Abdin et al.,
148   2024).Additionally, we considered how 4- and 8-bit quantizations influence length representations
149   in LLMs.
150   To investigate how explicit fine-tuning with length constraint prompts affects length-related internal
151   representations within LLMs, we fine-tune a model on the Google sentence summarization dataset.
152   Following previous work, which includes length-constraint prompts to enhance model’s ability to
153   control the output sequence length (Juseon-Do et al., 2024), we utilized QLoRA, a technique that
154   can maintain the full 16-bit fine-tuning performance (Dettmers et al., 2023), for fine-tuning. QLoRA
155   extends the Low-Rank Adapters (LoRA) method (Hu et al., 2022), which is an advanced form of
156
      parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Mangrulkar et al., 2022) for LLMs. This approach integrates low-
      rank, trainable matrices with the frozen weights of each transformer layer. During training, we used
157
      8-bit quantization for QLoRA, while during inference, we employed 4-bit quantization. We used
158
      greedy decoding across all settings in our experiments to eliminate randomness in the generation
159   process. Appendix A provides further details of hyper-parameters and settings.

      4.1       L AYER - WISE A NALYSIS FOR L ENGTH R EPRESENTATIONS

      Figure 1 shows the variation of R2 for outputs from a transformer layer corresponding to Equations
247
      (1), (2), (3), and (4) using Llama-2-7B-Chat. It reveals an interesting pattern in the length represen-
248   tations within LLMs. In particular, in the second layer, the outputs of Equation (1), which indicates
249   the attention mechanism, show a stronger correlation with the length representations than the out-
250   puts from Equations (2), (3), and (4). While the outputs from the other equations also provide strong
251   correlations, the outputs from Equation (1) have consistently higher R2 scores for all prompts. We
252   also observed a gradual decrease in length representations as inputs are fed into subsequent LLM
253   layers, and tokens are produced in a step-by-step process. However, in the final layer, the length rep-
254   resentations begin to increase from the outputs of Equation (1). This result indicates that the LLM
255   captures length representations in the early stages, similar to how they capture semantic representa-
256
      tions (Niu et al., 2022). As such, the increase in length representations in the final layer indicates
      that the model may revisit this information to reinforce positional context.

      5.1   D O L ENGTH - SPECIFIC P ROMPTS A FFECT I NNER L ENGTH R EPRESENTATIONS ?
305
      We also trained separate regressions on each single hidden unit from the second layer of the attention
306   outputs in Llama-2-13B-Chat, which has a total of 5,120 hidden units. Table 4 shows the results of
307   the top-10 highest R2 scores based on prediction from individual hidden units to each time step.
308   Unlike all units that were used for prediction in Table 2, we observed distinct differences among the
309   constraints, including the length-specific prompting method (Priming).
310
      Compared to the No-constraint and Length prompting methods, length-related hidden units within
311
      the attention networks become more active in representing length information when we used more
312   length-specific prompts such as Priming. Moreover, in the zero-shot setting, the No-constraint and
313   Length prompts share nearly the same top-10 hidden units for representing length information. Con-
314   versely, when the Priming prompt was used, different hidden units become active, thus indicating a
315   shift in how length information is captured by the LLM with more length-specific prompting. Ad-
316   ditionally, more length-specific prompts of Priming cause each of the top-k hidden units to be more
317   highly activated than those in the No-constraint and Length prompt settings.
318   Furthermore, the hidden units for representing length information became nearly identical across
319   prompting methods when we fine-tuned LLMs, because the model learned the capability to precisely
320   control output sequence length. Interestingly, the same top-3 hidden units are activated with the
321   Priming prompt in the zero-shot and fine-tuning settings. This finding indicates that specific length-
322   related units are consistently activated during Priming, thus guiding LLMs to manage the generation
323   of output sequence length. These empirical results further demonstrate that LLMs understand in-
      context learning as a form of implicit fine-tuning (Dai et al., 2023).

364   5.2         D OES S CALING L ENGTH R EPRESENTATIONS A FFECT M ODEL -G ENERATED T EXT ?
365
366   To investigate the effect of scaling length representations on model-generated text, we disentangled
367   the top-k and smallest-k activated hidden units related to length representations in the second layer
368   from multi-head attention mechanisms. We multiplied these hidden units by negative or positive
369
      numeric values to adjust the output sequence length. This scaling was applied to all output token
      positions during the generation process, excluding the input token prompts. We aimed to empiri-
370
      cally demonstrate that such hidden units consider length representations in LLMs and are partially
371
      disentangled from semantic representations. We used Rouge-L (R-L) (Lin, 2004) to evaluate the
372   informativeness of the summarized sentences when we scaled length representations. R-L measures
373   the longest common subsequence between the generated and gold summaries. To evaluate the per-
374   formance with respect to length, we used ∆CR, which is an arithmetical difference between the
375   model-generated and gold compression ratios. Thus, ∆CR value close to zero indicates that the
376   model-generated summary has a compression ratio similar to that of the gold summary. A higher
377   ∆CR means the generated summary is longer than the gold summary, while a lower ∆CR means it
      is shorter.

515   6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this study, we investigated the process by which output sequence length information is encoded
518   within the internal representations of LLMs. We focused on identifying the specific components
519
      within transformer layers that contribute to length control during text generation tasks, particularly,
      sentence summarization. Our findings empirically demonstrated that the outputs from the second
520
      layer’s attention mechanisms showed a strong correlation with the generation time step, thus indi-
521
      cating that length representations were captured early in the process. We also found that this pattern
522   was consistent with different models with different sizes, such as the Llama and Phi families, and
523   continued to be robust even when 4- and 8-bit quantizations were applied.
524
525
      Furthermore, we analyzed individual hidden units from the second layer attention outputs and found
      that certain hidden units are highly activated and directly contributed to the process of representing
526
      length information. Moreover, these units became more active when length-specific prompts such
527
      as Priming were used. This finding indicates that LLMs adjust their internal representations based
528   on the input prompts. Furthermore, by scaling these length-related hidden units, we effectively
529   controlled the output sequence length without losing informativeness. While positive scaling factors
530   led to shorter summaries, negative scaling resulted in longer summaries. It indicates that length
531   information is partially disentangled from semantic representations within LLMs.
532
      Finally, our results revealed that fine-tuning further improved the LLMs’ capabilities by reinforcing
533
      reliance on the top-k length-related units. We also found the same activation of specific hidden units
534   in the Priming prompt are shared between zero-shot and fine-tuning settings, that indicates LLMs
535   have constructed robust internal mechanisms for controlling output sequence length, and in-context
536   learning performs similarly to implicit fine-tuning (Dai et al., 2023). Our findings have impor-
537   tant implications for the interpretability and controllability of LLMs in natural language generation
538   tasks. Understanding how length information is internally encoded allows for more precise length
539   control over generated outputs, which is crucial in applications, such as summarization and machine
      translation, where adhering to length constraints is often required.
