                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Mapping the conformational dynamics of proteins is crucial for elucidating their
013            functional mechanisms. While Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation enables de-
014            tailed time evolution of protein motion, its computational toll hinders its use in
015
               practice. To address this challenge, multiple deep learning models for reproducing
               and accelerating MD have been proposed drawing on transport-based generative
016
               methods. However, existing work focuses on generation through transport of sam-
017
               ples from prior distributions, that can often be distant from the data manifold. The
018            recently proposed framework of stochastic interpolants, instead, enables transport
019            between arbitrary distribution endpoints. Building upon this work, we introduce
020            EquiJump, a transferable SO(3)-equivariant model that bridges all-atom protein
021            dynamics simulation time steps directly. Our approach unifies diverse sampling
022            methods and is benchmarked against existing models on trajectory data of fast
023            folding proteins. EquiJump achieves state-of-the-art results on dynamics simula-
024            tion with a transferable model on all of the fast folding proteins.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Proteins are the workhorses of the cell, and simulating their dynamics is critical to biological dis-
031   covery and drug design (Karplus and Kuriyan, 2005). Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulation is an
032   important tool that leverages physics for time evolution, enabling precise exploration of the confor-
033   mational space of proteins (Hollingsworth and Dror, 2018). However, sampling with physically-
034   accurate molecular potentials requires small integration time steps, often making the simulation of
035
      phenomena at relevant biological timescales prohibitive (Lane et al., 2013).
036   To tackle this challenge, several studies have adopted deep learning models to capture surrogates of
037   MD potentials and dynamics (Noé et al., 2020; Durumeric et al., 2023; Arts et al., 2023). More recent
038   works (Schreiner et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Jing et al., 2024) have proposed to use deep learning-
039   based simulators trained on long-interval snapshots of MD trajectories to predict future states given
040   some starting configuration. These models draw from neural transport models (Ho et al., 2020;
041
      Lipman et al., 2022), learning a conditional or guided bridge between a prior distribution (⇢0 = N )
      and the target data manifold of simulation steps (⇢1 = ⇢data ). In contrast to this, the recent paradigm
042
      of Stochastic Interpolants (Albergo et al., 2023a; Albergo and Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) provides a
043
      method for directly bridging distinct arbitrary distributions.
044
045   In this work, we utilize this framework and introduce EquiJump, a Two-Sided Stochastic Inter-
046   polant model which bridges between long-interval timesteps of protein simulation directly (Figure
047
      1). EquiJump is SO(3)-equivariant and simulates all heavy atoms directly in 3D. We train a trans-
      ferable model on 12 fast-folding proteins (Majewski et al., 2023; Lindorff-Larsen et al., 2011) and
048
      successfully recover their dynamics.
049
050   Our main contributions are as follows:
051
052          • We extend the Two-Sided Stochastic Interpolants framework to simulate the dynamics of
053            three-dimensional representations. By training on trajectory data, our approach directly
               leverages the close relationship between consecutive timesteps.

054
             • We benchmark our model against existing generative frameworks for simulating the long-
055            time dynamics of a large protein, demonstrating the increased accuracy and parameter effi-
056            ciency of our approach.
057
             • We train transferable simulators for capturing the dynamics of 12 fast-folding proteins,
058
               comparing it to existing force-field model, ablating the impact of model complexity on
059
               simulation quality, and evaluating the trade-off between sampling speed and accuracy.

088   Recent advancements in protein modeling through deep learning have led to the development of sev-
089   eral models capable of replicating molecular dynamics (MD) trajectories. (Wang et al., 2019; Husic
090   et al., 2020) introduced supervised models trained through direct force matching, demonstrating
091   their ability to transfer simulations across different proteins. The most common machine learning
092   approach to atomistic MD are interatomic potentials trained on forces (Satorras et al., 2021; Batatia
093
      et al., 2022). Still, force matching methods require force information and in practice are often lim-
      ited to short time steps. Methods like (Du et al., 2024) can help sampling the transition path between
094
      two states, but rely on existing potentials. Several recent approaches reconstruct generic trajectories
095
      without forces. Many of them are based on an equivariant backbone: (Xu et al., 2023) and (Wu et al.,
096   2023) both directly predict the next configuration based on several previous steps, and have been ap-
097   plied to general tasks as well as MD simulations. The latter was tested on a small protein dataset
098   and shown to reconstruct deterministic motion. Other equivariant models use diffusion to match dis-
099   tributions: (Han et al., 2024) aims at modeling the whole trajectory and can reconstruct MD, while
100   (Luo et al., 2024) specializes on tasks such as structural relaxation. (Zhang et al., 2024) is based
101   on flow matching and predicts the next configuration from previous frames, but was not applied
102   to MD tasks. EquiJump is similar to these approaches, instead drawing on Two-Sided Stochastic
103   Interpolants and specialized for protein dynamics. In particular, we leverage the statistical nature of
104
      our approach to reproduce the stochastic nature of the task.
105   Drawing on coarse-graining and force matching, (Majewski et al., 2023) implements a unified trans-
106   ferable model for multiple proteins. (Fu et al., 2023) learns to predict accelerated, coarse grained
107   dynamics of polymers with GNNs. (Köhler et al., 2023) utilizes Normalizing Flows (Gabrié et al.,
      2022) for coarse grained force-matching, while (Arts et al., 2023) builds upon Denoising Diffusion

146   Neural transport methods have demonstrated outstanding performance in generative tasks (Ma et al.,
147   2024; Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2020). Stochastic Interpolants (Albergo et al.,
148   2023a; Albergo and Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) are a recently proposed class of generative models that
149   have reached state-of-the-art results in image generation (Ma et al., 2024; Albergo et al., 2023b).
150   One-sided stochastic interpolants, which generalize flow matching and denoising diffusion models,
151   transport samples from a prior distribution X0 ⇠ N to a target data distribution X1 ⇠ ⇢1 by utilizing
152   latent variables Z ⇠ N through the stochastic process {X⌧ }:
153                                         X⌧ = J(⌧, X1 ) + ↵(⌧ )Z                                      (1)
154   where ⌧ 2 [0, 1] is the time parameterization. The interpolant function J satisfies boundary condi-
155   tions J(0, X1 ) = 0 and J(1, X1 ) = X1 , and the noise schedule ↵ satisfies ↵(0) = 1 and ↵(1) = 0.
156

292   4.1   FAST-F OLDING P ROTEINS
293
      To evaluate the capability of our model in reproducing protein dynamics, we leverage the dataset
294
      of 12 fast-folding proteins produced by (Majewski et al., 2023), and originally investigated in
295
      (Lindorff-Larsen et al., 2011). The dataset consists of millions of snapshots of MD for 12 pro-
296   teins ranging from 10 to 80 residues, where in each trajectory snapshots are taken at the rate of
297   100 ps. The trajectories are made up of several NVT runs (20 to 100 ns) at T = 350 K from differ-
298   ent starting configurations sampling the phase space, for an aggregated simulation time of hundreds
299   to thousands of µs per protein. We refer to the original work and Appendix A.3 for more details.
300
      While sampling uniformly on the whole dataset is effective in producing configurations within the
301
      original phase space, it is not efficient for learning the potential energy surface. For the slowest
302   modes of the system, states are very high in free energy and heavily under-represented in our train-
303   ing dataset. To address this issue, taking inspiration by classical sampling methods such as umbrella
304   sampling (Torrie and Valleau, 1977) and metadynamics (Laio and Parrinello, 2002) we propose a
305   reweighing of the training set. Notably, since our model learns ⇢(Xt+1 | Xt ), the target distribu-
306   tion remains unchanged as long as we fix the endpoints (Xt , Xt+1 ): this sampling only varies the
307   speed at which different parts of the phase space are learned. To reweight our sampling towards
308   dynamically informative states, we first find relevant degrees of freedom through TICA analysis
309   (Pérez-Hernández et al., 2013), which offers a reduced dimensional space that highlights the slow
310
      macroscopic modes of the system. We then fit a small number of clusters (Figure 8) through k-
      means in this simplified space. Our enhanced dataset first samples a cluster, then from the cluster a
311
      configuration and its transition. Refer to Appendix A.4 for more details.
312
313
314   4.2   E QUILIBRATION OF M ODEL DYNAMICS
315
      To study the long-term dynamical behavior of our models, we estimate the stationary distribution of
316
      the learned dynamics and apply a correction to the density of sample observables. We leverage Time-
317
      lagged Independent Component Analysis (TICA) (Pérez-Hernández et al., 2013) and build Markov
318   State Models (MSM) on clusters over TIC components. We obtain reference TICA components
319   from the original trajectories by considering a similarity based on the Euclidean distance between
320   C↵ and a lagtime of 2ns. To estimate long-term probabilities after equilibration, we reweight the
321   density of sampled configurations. We first cluster the configuration using K-means in the first 4
322   TIC dimensions with 100 clusters. We then build a Markov State Model (MSM) on the basis of
323   these clusters by estimating the transition matrix at long time-lag (45 to 95ns). Finally, from the
      MSM largest eigenvectors we obtain the steady state probability of each cluster, which we use to


                                                               6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      reweight the distribution of observables for approximating their behavior at dynamical equilibrium.
325   Note that this reweighing is extremely sensitive to the correct description of the transition states, as
326   a wrong sampling of the transition probability will exponentially affect the relative probability of
327   different basins. As such, the correct description of these probability distributions is a good indicator
328   of the faithfulness of the long-term dynamics.

      5      C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we introduced EquiJump for learning the dynamics of 3D protein simulations. Equi-
527
      Jump extends Two-Sided Stochastic Interpolants for 3D dynamics through SO(3)-equivariant neural
528
      networks, and is implemented through a novel four-track architecture that handles all-atom struc-
529   tures. We validated our approach through a series of experiments on large-scale dataset of fast-
530   folding proteins, through which we compared generative frameworks and demonstrated a unified
531   model that accurately reproduces complex dynamics across different proteins. We ablated model
532   capacities and compared our model to existing approaches, outperforming state-of-the-art methods
533   in terms of accuracy and efficiency. Our results suggest EquiJump provides a stepping stone for
534   future research in modeling and accelerating protein dynamics simulation. Future work will focus
535   on architecture and parameter efficiency, and on transferability and generalization.

      A.1    B ROWNIAN DYNAMICS IN P ROTEIN -S OLVENT S YSTEMS AND C ONNECTION TO
705
             S TOCHASTIC I NTERPOLANTS
706
707   In the study of molecular dynamics of proteins immersed in solvents, it is crucial to account for
708   the interactions between the proteins and the surrounding fluid. Proteins in a solvent experience
709   random collisions with solvent molecules, leading to stochastic behavior that can be effectively
710   modeled using Brownian dynamics (Ermak and McCammon, 1978). This approach captures the
711   random motion arising from thermal fluctuations and solvent effects, providing a realistic depiction
712   of protein behavior in biological environments.
713   Generalized frictional interactions among the particles can be incorporated into the Langevin equa-
714   tion through a friction tensor R (Schlick, 2010). This tensor accounts for the action of the solvent
715   on the particles and modifies the Langevin equation to:
716
717
                                   MẌ(t) =      rE(X(t))       RẊ(t) + W(t),                             (10)
718
719
      where M is the mass matrix, X(t) represents the particle positions at time t, E(X(t)) is the potential
720
      energy, and W(t) is a random force representing thermal fluctuations from the solvent. The mean
721   and covariance of the random force W(t) are given by:
722
723
724                           hW(t)i = 0,      hW(t)W(t0 )T i = 2kB T R (t         t0 ),                   (11)
725
726   where kB is Boltzmann’s constant, T is the temperature, and (t t0 ) is the Dirac delta function.
727   This relation is based on the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, a fundamental result that connects the
728
      friction experienced by a particle to the fluctuations of the random force acting upon it, assuming
      the particle is undergoing random motion around thermal equilibrium.
729
730   This description ensures that the ensemble of trajectories generated from Eq. 10 is governed by the
731   Fokker-Planck equation, a partial differential equation that describes the evolution of the probability
732   density function of a particle’s position and momentum in phase space during diffusive motion.
733   In this context, the random force W(t) is modeled as white noise with no intrinsic timescale. The
734   inertial relaxation times, given by the inverses of the eigenvalues of the matrix M 1 R, define the
735   characteristic timescale of the thermal motion. When these inertial relaxation times are short com-
736   pared to the timescale of interest, it is appropriate to neglect inertia in the governing equation,
737   effectively discarding the acceleration term by assuming MẌ(t) ⇡ 0. Under this approximation,
738   Eq. 10 simplifies to the Brownian dynamics form:
739
740
                                     Ẋ(t) =    R 1 rE(X(t)) + R 1 W(t).                                   (12)
741
742
      This simplification reflects that solvent effects are sufficiently strong to render inertial forces negli-
743
      gible, resulting in motion that is predominantly Brownian and stochastic in nature. This description
744   is particularly effective for modeling very large, dense systems whose conformations in solution are
745   continuously and significantly altered by the fluid flow in their environment.
746
747
      To stably evolve Brownian dynamics eq. (12) over time, small integration steps are usually required
      due to the stiffness of the physical manifold. Molecular systems exhibit a wide range of timescales:
748
      fast atomic motions such as bond vibrations and thermal fluctuations occur on the order of femtosec-
749
      onds, while slower conformational shifts and larger-scale rearrangements may take place over much
750   longer periods. These necessitate small time steps to accurately capture the system’s rapid changes
751   without numerical instability. In contrast, Stochastic Interpolants eq. (9), while also following the
752   form of eq. (12), enable smoothing of the data manifold by convolution with small Gaussian pertur-
753   bations. This leads to a latent representation that is robust to noise, allowing for larger integration
754   steps. The smoother manifold helps overcome local energy barriers and navigate the broader confor-
755   mational landscape more efficiently, making it possible to simulate molecular dynamics on extended
      timescales without losing stability.

806   A.2.1    E QUIVARIANCE
807
808   The features used in EquiJump are irreducible representations (irreps) of SO(3). To prove that
809   EquiJump is equivariant, we demonstrate that all operations within the network preserve the trans-
      formation properties of the irreps under rotation.


                                                      15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Scalars, which are irreps of degree l = 0, remain invariant under rotation. For a scalar s 2 R and a
811   rotation R 2 SO(3), we have:
812                                                R · s = s.                                         (13)
813
      Applying functions such as MLPs to scalars preserves this invariance:
814
815                                               f (R · s) = f (s).                                        (14)
816
      For irreps with l > 0, equivariance depends on the nature of the operations. Linear operations
817
      combine irreps of the same degree using scalar weights. Let v and w be irreps and W a learnable
818   weight matrix. Under a rotation R,
819
820                                           R · (W v) = W (R · v),                                        (15)
821   where R · v represents the rotated vector. Since the weight matrix W does not interfere with the
822   transformation properties, linear operations are equivariant.
823
824
      EquiJump also employs tensor products, which combine two irreps v and w of degrees l1 and l2 ,
      respectively, to produce new irreps of degrees |l1 l2 |, . . . , (l1 + l2 ). The tensor product transforms
825
      under rotation as:
826
                                       R · (v ⌦ w) = (R · v) ⌦ (R · w),                                      (16)
827
828
      ensuring equivariance. In EquiJump, tensor products are used in two key cases: (1) between fea-
      tures and spherical harmonics Ylm (r) of relative positions, and (2) between features and themselves
829
      (tensor square). Spherical harmonics transform under rotation as:
830
                                                    X (l)
831                                  R · Ylm (r) =       Dmm0 (R)Ylm0 (r),                            (17)
832                                                    m0
833
               (l)
834   where Dmm0 (R) are elements of the Wigner-D matrix. Combining features with spherical harmon-
835   ics via tensor products preserves equivariance by construction.
836   The basic operation in EquiJump involves a tensor product followed by a linear transformation. Let
837   T represent this combined operation:
838
                                              T (v, w) = W (v ⌦ w),                                         (18)
839
840   where W is a learnable weight tensor. Under a rotation R,
841
                           R · T (v, w) = W (R · (v ⌦ w)) = W ((R · v) ⌦ (R · w)).                          (19)
842
843   This demonstrates that this basic operation is equivariant. Since scalar transformations, linear layers,
844   and tensor products all preserve equivariance, the entire EquiJump network is SO(3)-equivariant.
845   This ensures that outputs transform consistently with inputs under rotation, making EquiJump well-
846   suited for modeling the rotationally symmetric dynamics of protein structures in 3D space.

       A.11    S AMPLING PARAMETERS A BLATION

1352   We study the impact of changing sampling hyperparameters ✏(⌧ ) and d⌧ in the quality of long-term
1353   dynamics generation. For that, we train a model for simulating the dynamics of fast-folding protein
1354   Chignolin. We parameterize our model with H = 32 and train it for 120k steps with batch size 512.
1355   We consider scale of noising parameter ✏(⌧ ) = {0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 10.0} and number of
1356   steps in integration (1/d⌧ ) = {30, 50, 75, 100, 150, 200, 300}. For each, we sample 300 trajectories
1357   of 500 steps (50 ns). As above, we reweight metrics through MSM based on TICA-clusters to
1358
       estimate values at equilibrium.
1359   In Figure 15, we show the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JS) between ground truth and samples ob-
1360   tained at different parameterizations of ✏ and d⌧ . In Figure 16, we show the effects of hyperparamter
1361   variation on the (long-term reweighted) density of the first TIC components of samples. We observe
1362   that large variation (= 0.1, 10.0) on ✏ yields underperforming models. Notably, we observe that
1363   even with few steps (30, 50, 75) higher quality can be obtained through smaller ✏ (= 0.5, 0.75). Our
1364
       results suggest that further investigation about the noising schedule ✏(⌧ ) is a promising direction for
       increasing acceleration of high-quality simulation through fewer integration steps.
