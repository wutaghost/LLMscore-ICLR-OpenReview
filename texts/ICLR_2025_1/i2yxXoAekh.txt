009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spectral graph convolution, an important tool of data filtering on graphs, relies
012            on two essential decisions: selecting spectral bases for signal transformation and
013            parameterizing the kernel for frequency analysis. While recent techniques mainly
014            focus on standard Fourier transform and vector-valued spectral functions, they
015            fall short in flexibility to model signal distributions over large spatial ranges, and
016            capacity of spectral function. In this paper, we present a novel wavelet-based graph
017            convolution network, namely WaveGC, which integrates multi-resolution spectral
018            bases and a matrix-valued filter kernel. Theoretically, we establish that WaveGC
019
               can effectively capture and decouple short-range and long-range information,
               providing superior filtering flexibility, surpassing existing graph convolutional
020
               networks and graph Transformers (GTs). To instantiate WaveGC, we introduce
021
               a novel technique for learning general graph wavelets by separately combining
022            odd and even terms of Chebyshev polynomials. This approach strictly satisfies
023            wavelet admissibility criteria. Our numerical experiments showcase the capabilities
024            of the new network. By replacing the Transformer part in existing architectures
025            with WaveGC, we consistently observe improvements in both short-range and
026            long-range tasks. This underscores the effectiveness of the proposed model in
027            handling different scenarios. Our code is available at https://anonymous.
028            4open.science/r/WaveGC.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Spectral graph theory (SGT) (Chung, 1997), which enables analysis and learning on graph data,
034   has firmly established itself as a pivotal methodology in graph machine learning. A significant
035   milestone in SGT is the generalization of the convolution operation to graphs, as convolution for
036   grid-structured data, i.e. sequences and images, has demonstrated remarkable success (LeCun
037   et al., 1998; Hinton et al., 2012; Krizhevsky et al., 2012). Significant research interests in graph
038   convolution revolve around two key factors: (1) designing diverse bases for spectral transform, and
039
      (2) parameterizing powerful graph kernel. For (1), the commonly used graph Fourier basis, consisting
      of the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian (Shuman et al., 2013), stands as a prevalent choice.
040
      However, graph wavelets (Hammond et al., 2011) offer enhanced flexibility by constructing adaptable
041
      bases. For (2), classic approaches involve diagonalizing the kernel with fully free parameters (Bruna
042   et al., 2013) or employing various polynomial approximations such as Chebyshev (Defferrard et al.,
043   2016) and Cayley (Levie et al., 2018) polynomials. Additionally, convolution with a matrix-valued
044   kernel serves as the spectral function of Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) under the shift-invariant
045   condition (Li et al., 2021; Guibas et al., 2021).
046
      Despite the existence of techniques in each aspect, the integration of these two lines into a unified
047
      framework remains challenging, impeding the full potential of graph convolution. In an effort
048   to unravel this challenge, we introduce a novel operation — Wavelet-based Graph Convolution
049   (WaveGC), which seamlessly incorporates both spectral basis and kernel considerations. In terms
050   of spectral basis design, WaveGC is built upon graph wavelets, allowing it to capture information
051   across the entire graph through a multi-resolution approach from highly adaptive construction of
052   multiple graph wavelet bases. For filter parameterization, we opt for a matrix-valued spectral kernel
053   with weight-sharing. Beyond adjusting diagonal frequencies, the matrix-valued kernel offers greater
      flexibility to spectral filtering, thanks to its larger parameter space.

054
      To comprehensively explore WaveGC, we theoretically analyse and assess its information-capturing
055   capabilities. In contrast to the K-hop basic message-passing framework, WaveGC is demonstrated
056   to exhibit both significantly larger and smaller receptive fields concurrently, achieved through the
057   manipulation of scales. Previous graph wavelet theory (Hammond et al., 2011) only verifies the
058   localization in small scale limit. Instead, our proof is complete as it covers both extremely small
059   and large scales from the perspective of information mixing (Di Giovanni et al., 2023). Moreover,
060   our proof also implies that WaveGC is capable of simultaneously capturing both short-range and
061   long-range information for each node, akin to (graph) Transformers, which facilitate global node
062   interaction. Remarkably, WaveGC can distinguish information across diverse distances, thereby
063
      extending its flexibility beyond the scope of traditional Transformers.
064   To implement WaveGC, a critical step lies in constructing graph wavelet bases that satisfy two
065   fundamental criteria: (1) meeting the wavelet admissibility criteria (Mallat, 1999) and (2) showing
066   adaptability to different graphs. Existing designs of graph wavelets face limitations, with some
067   falling short in ensuring the criteria (Xu et al., 2019a; 2022), while others having fixed wavelet
068   forms, lacking adaptability (Zheng et al., 2021; Cho et al., 2023). To address these limitations,
069
      we propose an innovative and general implementation of graph wavelets. Our solution involves
      approximating scaling function basis and multiple wavelet bases using odd and even terms of
070
      Chebyshev polynomials, respectively. This approach is inspired by our observation that, after a certain
071
      transformation, even terms of Chebyshev polynomials strictly satisfy the admissibilitywe criteria,
072   while odd terms supplement direct current signals. Through the combination of these terms via
073   learnable coefficients, we aim to theoretically approximate scaling function and multiple wavelets
074   with arbitrary complexity and flexibility. Our contributions are:
075
076          • We derive a new Wavelet-based graph convolution (WaveGC), which integrates multi-
077            resolution bases and matrix-valued kernel, enhancing spectral convolution on large spatial
078
               ranges.
             • We theoretically prove that WaveGC can capture and distinguish the information from short
079
               and long ranges, surpassing conventional graph convolutions and GTs.
080
             • We pioneer a general implementation of learnable graph wavelets, employing odd terms and
081            even terms of Chebyshev polynomials individually. This implementation strictly satisfies
082            the wavelet admissibility criteria.
083          • Integrating WaveGC into three successful GTs as base models, our approach consistently
084            outperforms base methods on both short-range and long-range tasks, achieving up to 26.20%
085            improvement on CoraFull and 9.21% on VOC datasets.

132   3.1   G ENERAL SPECTRAL WAVELET VIA C HEBYSHEV DECOMPOSITION
133
      For the selection of the spectral transform F and its inverse F −1 , it can be tailored to the specific
134
      nature of data. For set data, the Dirac Delta function (Oppenheim et al., 1997) is employed, while the
135   fast Fourier Transform (FFT) proves efficient for both sequences (Li et al., 2021) and grids (Guibas
136   et al., 2021). In the context of graphs, the Fourier transform (F → U ⊤ ) emerges as one classical
137   candidate. However, some inherent flaws limit the capacity of Fourier bases. (1) Standard graph
138   Fourier bases, represented by one fixed matrix U ⊤ , maintain a constant resolution and fixed frequency
139   modes. (2) Fourier transform lacks the adaptability to be further optimized according to different
140   datasets and tasks. Therefore, multiple resolution and adaptability are two prerequisites for the design
141   of an advanced base.
142   Notably, wavelet base is able to conform the above two demands, and hence offers enhanced filtering
143   compared to Fourier base. For the resolution, the use of different scales sj allows wavelet to analyze
144   detailed components of a signal at different granularity. More importantly, due to its strong spatial
145   localization (Hammond et al., 2011), each wavelet corresponds to a signal diffused away from a
146   central node (Xu et al., 2019a). Therefore, these scales also control varying receptive fields in
147   spatial space, which enables the simultaneous fusion of short- and long-range information. For the
148   adaptability, graph wavelets offer the flexibility to adjust the shapes of wavelets and scaling function.
149
      These components can be collaboratively optimized for the alignment of basis characteristics with
      different datasets, potentially enhancing generalization performance.
150
151   Next, we need to determine the form of the scaling function basis Φ = U h(Λ)U ⊤ , the unit wavelet
152   basis Ψ = U g(Λ)U ⊤ , and the scales sj . The forms of h and g are expected to be powerful enough
153   and easily available. Concurrently, g should strictly satisfy the wavelet admissibility criteria, i.e.,
154   Eq. (1), and h should complementally provide DC signals. To achieve this target, we separately
155
      introduce odd terms and even terms from Chebyshev polynomials (Hammond et al., 2011) into the
      approximation of h and g. Please recall that the Chebyshev polynomial Tk (y) of order k may be
156
      computed by the stable recurrence relation Tk (y) = 2yTk−1 (y) − Tk−2 (y) with T0 = 1 and T1 = y.
157

158   expectations:
159                                   Tk (y) → 1/2 · (−Tk (y − 1) + 1).                                  (4)

204
205   Convolutional kernel F(κ) in Eq. (3) is usually parameterized in two ways: (1) Vector-valued
206   operator such as diag(θλ ) where diagonal elements form a parametrized function of the spectrum of
207   the graph G (Bruna et al., 2013; Defferrard et al., 2016; Levie et al., 2018), and (2) Matrix-valued

209   be view as the convolutional kernel of a shift-invariant Transformers (Guibas et al., 2021). Details
210   are given in Appendix B.

212   provides enough flexibility to adjust itself. Additionally, the experimental evidence in Section 6.3
213   shows the superiority of matrix-valued kernel against vector-valued one. The typical number of
214   parameters in M is N × d × d, which can be significant, particularly for large-scale graphs with
215   a large value of N . To efficiently model M, we adopt parameter sharing among all frequency
      modes, employing only one Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). This results in a significant reduction of

      or GNN network to learn the coefficients for Chebyshev bases. Comprehensively, the constructed

331   ranges cannot be precisely predicted and controlled. ChebNetII (He et al., 2022), reducing the Runge

334   multiple ranges information in spatial domain.

336
      these related studies. In addition, although both WaveGC and Transformers can handle various node
337   interaction ranges, the former adaptively learns which range must be emphasized, whereas the latter
338   mixes short- and long-range information together without distinction. Thus, WaveGC surpasses
339   Transformers in controlling distance based information.

      7     C ONCLUSION
481
482
      In this study, we proposed a novel graph convolution operation based on wavelets (WaveGC),
      establishing its theoretical capability to capture information at both short and long ranges through a
483
      multi-resolution approach.
484
485   Limitation. One potential limitation of WaveGC is the computational complexity. The main
      contribution of WaveGC is to address long-range interactions in graph convolution, so it inevitably


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      establishes connections between most of nodes. This results in the same O(N 2 ) complexity as
487   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and Specformer (Bo et al., 2023). A possible solution is to
488   decrease the number of considered frequency modes from N to ν. In this way, the complexity is
489   reduced to O(ν · N ). This operation makes WaveGC run on large-scale graph, i.e. ogbn-arxiv,
490   and the good performance supports this simplification. Moreover, the eigen-decomposition process
491   involves O(N 3 ) complexity. However, this decomposition is performed only once, prior to all
492   training experiments. To accelerate the decomposition, we may adopt randomized SVD Halko et al.
493   (2009) with complexity O(N 2 log K). Future work will focus on further simplification and scaling
494   up to larger graphs.

1171   In this paper, we adopt Eigenvalue Encoding (EE) Module (Bo et al., 2023) to encode eigenvalues.
1172   EE functions as a set-to-set spectral filter, enabling interactions between eigenvalues. In EE, both
1173
       magnitudes and relative differences of all eigenvalues are leveraged. Specifically, the authors use an
       eigenvalue encoding function to transform each λ from scalar R1 to a vector Rd :
1174
1175                   ρ(λ, 2i) = sin (ϵλ/100002i/d ),       ρ(λ, 2i + 1) = cos (ϵλ/100002i/d ),                (57)
1176
1177   where i is the dimension of the representations and ϵ is a hyper parameter. By encoding in this
1178   way, relative frequency shifts between eigenvalues are captured. Then, the raw representations of
1179   eigenvalues are the concatenation between eigenvalues and corresponding representation vectors:
1180
1181
                                  Zλ = [λ1 ||ρ(λ1 ), . . . , λN ||ρ(λN )]⊤ ∈ RN ×(d+1) .                        (58)

1183   skip-connection and feed forward network (FFN):
1184

1186

       injected into the learning of combination coefficients ã and b̃, and scales s̃.

1274   Upon examination of three kinds of wavelets (i.e., {SGWT, DEFT, GWNN}), those from SGWT

       Graph Transformer. Graph Transformer (GT) has attracted considerable attentions, where re-
       searchers mainly focus on two aspects, i.e., positional encoding and reduction of computational
1547
       complexity. Firstly, a suitable positional encoding (PE) can assist the Transformer to understand the
1548
       topology and complex relationships within the graph. GT (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020) proposes to
1549   employ Laplacian eigenvectors as PE with randomly flipping their signs. Graphormer (Ying et al.,
1550   2021) takes the distance of the shortest path between two nodes as spatial encoding, which is involved
1551   in attention calculation as a bias. SAN (Kreuzer et al., 2021) conducts a learned positional encoding
1552   architecture to address key limitations of previous GT analyzed in the paper. GraphGPS (Rampásek
1553   et al., 2022) provides different choices for PE, consisting of LapPE, RWSE, SignNet and Equiv-
1554   StableLapPE. GRIT (Ma et al., 2023) uses the proposed relative random walk probabilities (RRWP)
1555   initial PE to incorporate graph inductive biases. (Geisler et al., 2023) proposes two direction- and
1556   structure-aware PE for directed graphs, i.e., Magnetic Laplacian and directional random walk encod-
1557
       ing. Both GraphTrans (Wu et al., 2021) and SAT (Chen et al., 2022a) adopts a GNN cascaded with
       Transformer, where GNN can be viewed as an implicit PE to capture the local structure. Secondly,
1558
       because of the huge complexity of attention computation O(N 2 ), some studies endeavor to reduce it
1559
       to the linear complexity. ANS-GT (Zhang et al., 2022) proposes a hierarchical attention scheme with
1560   graph coarsening. DIFFORMER (Wu et al., 2023a) introduces an energy constrained diffusion model
1561   with a linear-complexity version. EXPHORMER (Shirzad et al., 2023) consists of a sparse attention
1562   mechanism based on virtual global nodes and expander graphs. NAGphormer (Chen et al., 2022b)
1563   can be trained in a mini-bath manner by aggregating neighbors from different hops with Hop2Token.
1564   NodeFormer (Wu et al., 2022) enables the efficient computation via kernerlized Gumbel-Softmax
1565   operator. SGFormer (Wu et al., 2023b) is empowered by a simple attention model that can efficiently
       propagate information among arbitrary nodes. Besides these two main aspects, Edgeformers (Jin


                                                               29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       et al., 2023) and EGT (Hussain et al., 2022) additionally explore the edges by injecting edge text
1567   information or designing residual edge channels respectively. Recently, Xing et al. (2024) are the
1568   first to reveal the over-globalizing problem in graph transformer, and propose CoBFormer to improve
1569   the GT capacity on local modeling with a theoretical guarantee. Furthermore, BRAIN NETWORK
1570   TRANSFORMER (Kan et al., 2022) and Grover (Rong et al., 2020) explore the applications of GT
1571   on human brains and molecular data.
1572
       Graph Wavelet Transform. Graph wavelet transform is a generalization of classical wavelet
1573   transform c(Mallat, 1999) into graph domain. SGWT (Hammond et al., 2011) defines the computing
1574   paradigm on weighted graph via spectral graph theory. Specifically, it defines scaling operation in
1575   time field as the scaling on eigenvalues. The authors also prove the localization properties of SGWT
1576   in the spatial domain in the limit of fine scales. To accelerate the computation on transform, they
1577   additionally present a fast Chebyshev polynomial approximation algorithm. Following SGWT, there
1578   have been some efforts on designing more powerful graph wavelet bases in spectral domain, whereas
1579   these methods have different flaws. GWNN (Xu et al., 2019a) chooses heat kernel as the filter to
1580   construct the bases. SGWF (Shen et al., 2021) proposes an end-to-end learned kernel function using
1581
       MLP. LGWNN (Xu et al., 2022) designs neural network-parameterized lifting structures, where the
       lifting operation is based on diffusion wavelets. FGT (Zheng et al., 2022) introduces a decimated
1582
       framelet for multiscale representation and constructs an up-down coarse-grained chain. SpGAT ??
1583
       introduces the attention mechanism in the spectral domain, using diffusion kernel as a basis. The
1584   graph wavelet bases learnt from these five methods are not guaranteed as band-pass filters in λ ∈ [0, 2]
1585   and thus violate admissibility condition (Mallat, 1999). UFGCONV (Zheng et al., 2021) defines a
1586   framelet-based graph convolution with Haar-type filters. Wave-GD (Cho et al., 2023) focuses on
1587   graph generation with score-based diffusion, and realizes multiple resolutions with graph wavelet.
1588   Furthermore, the authors set k(s) = sxe−sx as band-pass filter and k(s) = e−sx as low-pass filter.
1589   WaveNet (Yang et al., 2024) relies on Haar wavelets as bases, and uses the highest-order scaling
1590   function to approximate all the other wavelets and scaling functions. WGGP (Opolka et al., 2022)
1591   integrates Gaussian processes with Mexican Hat to represent varying levels of smoothness on the
1592
       graph. The above four methods fix the form of the constructed wavelets, extremely limiting the
       adaptivity to different datasets. In this paper, our WaveGC constructs band-pass filter and low-pass
1593
       filter purely depending on the even terms and odd terms of Chebyshev polynomials. In this case, the
1594
       admissibility condition is strictly guaranteed, and the constructed graph wavelets can be arbitrarily
1595   complex and flexible with the number of truncated terms increasing. In addition, there are also some
1596   papers exploring the applications of graph wavelets on multi-resolution matrix factorization (Hy &
1597   Kondor, 2022) and tensor decomposition (Leonardi & Van De Ville, 2013). SEA-GWNN (Deb et al.,
1598   2024) focuses on the second generation of wavelets, or lifting schemes, which is a different topic
1599   from ours.
