009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep networks are frequently tuned to novel tasks and continue learning from
012            ongoing data streams. Such sequential training requires consolidation of new
013            and past information, a challenge predominantly addressed by retaining the most
014            important data points - formally known as coresets. Traditionally, these coresets
015            consist of entire samples, such as images or sentences. However, recent transformer
016
               architectures operate on tokens, leading to the famous assertion that an image is
               worth 16x16 words. Intuitively, not all of these tokens are equally informative or
017
               memorable. Going beyond coresets, we thus propose to construct a deeper-level
018
               data summary on the level of tokens. Our respectively named core tokensets both
019            select the most informative data points and leverage feature attribution to store
020            only their most relevant features. We demonstrate that core tokensets yield signifi-
021            cant performance retention in incremental image classification, open-ended visual
022            question answering, and continual image captioning with significantly reduced
023            memory. In fact, we empirically find that a core tokenset of 1% of the data performs
024            comparably to at least a twice as large and up to 10 times larger coreset.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
      Deep learning models are rarely deployed in static environments and instead need to be continuously
029
      adjusted to their ever-changing surroundings. As distribution shifts occur over time, we require
030
      models to retain their performance on previous settings while accommodating new examples (Hadsell
031   et al., 2020; Kudithipudi et al., 2022; Mundt et al., 2023). Naively, re-training a model from scratch
032   for every change in the environment quickly becomes unfeasible for complex architectures with
033   billions of parameters (Touvron et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020). Consequently, research has focused
034   on identifying small, representative subsets of the training data (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017), which
035   can later be replayed continuously to avoid (catastrophic) forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989) of
036   previously acquired information (Graham et al., 2021; Hassani et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021). Formally,
037   these (weighted) subsets can be described as coresets; data summaries that approximate the original
038   loss function with a negligible loss in performance (Bachem et al., 2015; Blömer et al., 2016; Trapp
039
      et al., 2022). However, whereas multiple successful coreset selection techniques were proposed
      (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021a), we argue that modern architecture advances
040
      now allow for a yet to be leveraged level of data summarization. In particular, (vision) transformers
041
      now conveniently define each data input as an ordered set of tokens Vaswani et al. (2017); Dosovitskiy
042   et al. (2020). In turn, we posit that it is not only a subset of the data instances that allows effective
043   summarization but also only a handful of features within every data point that may be relevant to
044   generalizing the task at hand. We refer to the latter as core tokens and, respectively, the subset of
045   most meaningful tokens of important data instances as core tokensets.
046
      Core tokensets build on the general principle of coresets, where we aim to identify the tokens that
047
      approximate the loss within a negligible error margin of the full dataset. To this end, we demonstrate
048   that the attribution maps calculated using the attention scores across the transformer layers, as a
049   means to understand the relevance of each token, can serve as the basis for core token selection. In
050   practice, we thus explore different strategies to determine the token’s relevance, e.g. by exploiting
051   the gradients or perturbing the attention scores. Our first interesting insight is that naively retaining
052   only core tokens from each data sample at a specified rate results in a similar cost as an involved
053   coreset (that selects a subset of data instances) with an identical retention rate. However, and more
      importantly, we can couple techniques in a two-fold subset selection approach, i.e., our so-called

091   2    P RELIMINARIES AND R ELATED W ORK
092
093   Coresets: Conventional dataset summaries are constructed as instance-wise (weighted) subsets of the
094   entire training data. These coresets are comprised of the most informative instances with an aim to
095   approximate the full dataset with a negligible difference in the cost function. That is, we wish to find
096   the subset Cx of D, assuming an additively, into non-negative functions decomposable, cost function
097   for our given dataset D (Bachem et al., 2015; 2017), such that for any solution (parameters) Q:
098                     |cost(D, Q) − cost(Cx , Q)| ≤ ϵ · cost(D, Q), where ϵ > 0                                      (1)
099
      In practice, coresets were primarily designed to demonstrate dataset approximation capabilities for
100
      traditional machine learning models such as K-means(Blömer et al., 2016; Feldman et al., 2007;
101
      Har-Peled & Kushal, 2005), SVM (Dong et al., 2005), and logistic regression (Huggins et al., 2016),
102   with very few works aimed at deep neural networks (DNNs). For the latter, Killamsetty et al. (2021b)
103   resorts to bi-level optimization with coresets selection as an outer objective and the generalization of
104   model parameters as an inner objective. However, it requires several optimization steps, which can
105   be impractical for complex architectures. As an alternative to bi-level optimization, gradient-based
106   coreset approaches have thus emerged with an aim to solve an alternative to Eq. 1 that operates on
107   the gradients of the loss rather than approximating the loss directly. CRAIG (Mirzasoleiman et al.,
      2020) thereby, aims to identify the optimal coreset by converting the gradient matching problem to a


                                                                  2

108
      monotone submodular function optimization problem and solving it with a pre-defined error bound.
109   Similarly, GradMatch (Killamsetty et al., 2021a) employs full dataset gradients while additionally
110   employing L2 regularization to reduce dependency on any specific data sample.
111
112
      In sequential learning, coresets can effectively be used as replay buffers to interleave in continual
      training (Mundt et al., 2023) to avoid catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). However,
113
      prominent replay strategies rely on ad-hoc subset selection, such as reservoir or random sampling
114
      (Rolnick et al., 2019; Kumari et al., 2022; Tiwari et al., 2022), to retain previously acquired infor-
115   mation over the course of training. In our work, we evaluate the impact on transformers to solve
116   more complicated tasks beyond standard classification problems, such as sequential VQA & image
117   captioning and propose an advanced data summarization technique, referred to as core tokensets.
118
      Transformer preliminaries: Given a sample X, transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) make use of a
119
      sequence of tokens xp , where each token captures distinctive features of the data sample. Usually, for

      (F : number of feature channels). Subsequently, each patch is encoded into a single token embedding

      3    C ORE T OKENSETS : S UMMARIZING DATASETS WITH I NFORMATIVE T OKENS
129
130
      As transformers are now capable of processing data inputs as a sequence of multiple tokens, we now
      present a novel way to summarize datasets even more efficiently than traditional coresets.
131
132   Definition 3.1 (Core tokensets). A core tokenset Ct is a subset of tokens (⊆ T ) of a dataset D, such
133   that a solution Q found on the core tokenset approximates the cost of the full dataset within negligible
134
      error margin: |cost(T, Q) − cost(Ct , Q)| ≤ ϵ · cost(T, Q).
135
      This definition is a modification of core set equation 1. Instead of being based on data instances,
136
      it is now based on tokens and we can thus leverage the unique architectural properties of modern
137   transformers to process inputs at a sub-data point level. In order to identify the core tokens, we
138   associate each input token xtp with a binary importance variable v t :
139
140
                              Ct := {(xtp , t)|v t = 1, 0 <= t < |z0 |} s.t.|Ct | < T                      (3)
141
142
      Note that each stored core token remains associated with its position t, preserved in the form of
143
      integer indexes of the extracted tokens. This will later be used to preserve the original structure in
144   continued training. The importance variable will typically be regulated by the relevance of the token
145   to its current task, but we can also think of straightforward selection methods. For instance, a naive
146   (random) selection can be realized by sampling the importance variable from a Bernoulli distribution:
147   v t ∼ Bernoulli(r). Here, r naturally maps to our key hyperparameter, i.e. the retention rate that
148   reflects the percentage of tokens retained from T .
149
      Whereas there exist well-defined strategies to determine coresets, methods to select core tokens are
150   yet to be explored. Even though random sampling is a feasible baseline in forming memory Hayes
151   et al. (2021), randomly selecting core tokens can easily lead to storing redundant tokens. In particular,
152   in images, the object of interest often only occupies tiny regions of an input sample; storing the
153   background alone is unlikely to approximate the true loss well. As a remedy, we propose leveraging
154   attribution techniques and investigating the attention maps for token relevance to construct our set
155   of core tokens. First, each token is assigned a relevance score, determining its contribution towards
156   the prediction. We then draw inspiration from “explainability” techniques (XAI) (Chefer et al.,
157   2021b; Deiseroth et al., 2023) to elevate the efficacy of attribution maps further to calculate the token
158
      relevance score. For instance, we can perturb the attention scores across the blocks or calculate the
      gradients across the transformer blocks to quantify the contribution towards the final prediction.
159
160   Let us specifically outline the gradient-based approach to select core tokens. Given a transformer
161   model with B blocks, we first calculate the attention map (Ab ) for each block (b ∈ B) comprising
      h attention heads. We consider the Hadamard product of the gradients of the attention map (∇Ab )

473   6                     D ISCUSSION
474
475   We have thoroughly evaluated core tokensets across three different sequential tasks. Despite the
476   different nature of these tasks, our empirical findings provide consistent insights.
477
      The first crucial insight lies in the efficacy when comparing retention of data subsets, i.e., traditional
      coresets, vs. core tokens, i.e., storing the relevant subset of tokens of each data point. If these methods
478
      were to be viewed as separate competitors, then it appears safer to reside on the token level. Our
479
      perhaps obvious hypothesis here is that intuitively, one loses less information when throwing away an
480   informative token, in contrast to throwing away an entire data point. As an example, if the memory
481   size is chosen to be, e.g., 10 %, it is more likely that a high amount of relevant information is still
482   present in the top 10 % tokens of each data point, in contrast, to fully throwing away 90 % of the data
483   instances (as is done in a traditional core set). This should be particularly true if the dataset features
484   little redundancy and every data point carries some important information.
485   Our second crucial insight is that the natural selection of the most critical data points and most
      relevant core tokens for each data point should go hand in hand. We have demonstrated this through

504   that the large improvement in performance vs memory size obtained is a result of the cascaded nature
505   of selection. For example, consider a scenario where we are allowed a memory equivalent to 1% of
506
      the data. For a traditional core set, this implies discarding 99 % of all data instances. However, for a
      core tokenset, we can actually extract 10 % of the most meaningful data points and store only 10 %
507
      of their most relevant tokens (assuming all data points have equivalent dimensions, as is typically
508
      the case in benchmark data sets). If this respective 10 % carries the most relevant information, it is
509   clear why such a 1 % sized core tokensets performs comparably to a 10 % coreset. This fact has
510   consistently been observed throughout the various experiments in our paper.
511
512
      Finally, this leads us to discuss the limitations of our work and its prospective future. The first
      limitation is shared with the majority of coreset methods. Infact in its present form, the data retention
513
      rate (or memory buffer size) must be chosen in advance unless an additional optimization problem
514
      is to be involved. Second, we have deliberately chosen to treat the identification of essential data
515   points and their most informative tokens as two chained steps. This choice was motivated by
516   our desire to show the generality of the core tokenset principle and, respectively, to benchmark a
517   plethora of conceivable selection strategies. It could thus be shown that all core tokenset strategies
518   were beneficial in direct comparison to traditional core sets. However, some of the investigated
519   strategies are inherently amenable to direct combination into a single selection algorithm. In fact,
520   our empirically best-performing combination of GradMatch + GradLRP both rely on the gradient
521   signal. As such, it is conceivable that a joint gradient-based selection strategy can be formulated.
522   Although the computation cost of an additional backward pass is manageable, this would reduce two
523
      computations into a single step. More importantly, it opens up the avenue for future work to derive
      core tokenset guarantees and bounds for epsilon. Related to this, core tokens also share similarities
524
      with recent works on token pruning and merging (Meng et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
525
      2023). These methods utilize the notion of token importance to remove unnecessary overhead and
526
527
      improve efficiency during training and inference, but could prospectively also be leveraged to build
528
      core tokensets for sequential learning. We view these aspects as a promising future direction, along
      with further experimentation in other domains.

531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We have introduced core tokensets for data summarization tailored to the unique architectural
534   properties of transformers. Building on the notion of coresets, we have demonstrated that each data
535   sample can be sufficiently summarized with only a subset of core tokens. Consequently, we have
536   seen the applicability of feature attribution scores in determining the relevance of each input token
537   on the target task. Our empirical investigation in three distinct scenarios has demonstrated that core
538   tokens are a cost-effective way to effectively preserve the stability of a sequentially trained model.
539   About future work, we envision the development of single-stage core tokenset selection strategies,
      where the relevant set of samples and their informative tokens are identified concurrently.

      A.1    D ETAILS OF ATTRIBUTION STRATEGIES
759
760   In this section, we detail the different selection strategies that ultimately influence the informativeness
761   of the core tokensets and lay out the mathematical details behind each strategy.
762
      GradCam: Inspired by the idea proposed in the context of convolutional networks (Selvaraju et al.,
763
      2017), we implement a gradient-based feature attribution map for transformers. In particular, we
764
      only consider the last layer of the transformer right before the classification head to compute the
765   gradients of the attention heads. The corresponding feature map will obtain a shape of RT ×D where
766   T denotes the number of the tokens and D is the embedding dimension. As a note, GradCam is purely
767   a class-specific approach, where we only consider the gradients concerning the [CLS] token and set
768   the rest of the elements in the first dimension to zero.
769
      Rollout: Unlike Gradcam, this strategy is purely attention-based (A). The feature relevance map S is
770
      usually determined by multiplying the attention maps of all the blocks (b ∈ B) across the transformer
771   layers (Abnar & Zuidema, 2022). It is thereby:
772
773                                        S = A(1) · A(2) · A(3) · A(B)                                      (6)
774
775   GradLRP: GradLRP is the second gradient-based approach that amends some of GradCam’s
776   limitations. As the name suggests, it combines the gradients of the individual attention maps with the
777   layer-wise propagation score calculated from the target token to the input sample. Given a transformer
778   model with B blocks, we first calculate the attention map (Ab ) for each block (b ∈ B) comprising
779   h attention heads. We then consider the Hadamard product of the gradients of the attention map
780   (∇Ab ) and the layer-wise relevance score (LR ) (Bach et al., 2015) concerning a target class. The
781   final attribution map Āb is achieved by calculating the mean (Eh ) of the product across h attention
782
      heads in addition to an identity matrix to account for skip connections in transformers. The final
      influence matrix ST ×T for a set of tokens T is then defined by multiplying all the attention head
783
      scores, where each row consists of the relevance score of each token compared to the other token.
784
785                    Āb = I + Eh (∇Ab ⊙ LR ); S(T ×T ) = A¯(1) · A¯(2) · A¯(3) · A(B)
                                                                                     ¯                        (7)
786
787   Atman: This approach follows a different route, where the relevance of a token is determined by
788   leveraging the perturbation technique. In particular, we perturb the pre-softmax attention score H
789   (ϵRh×t×d ) for each token by a factor f and determine their influence over the final target loss. A
790   subtle advantage over its gradient counterpart is that perturbation-based approaches only require
791   forward passes without calculating or storing gradients. Across all heads h and the transformer blocks,
792   we can compute the modified pre-softmax attention scores Hh as:

867   Not surprisingly, all methods show similarly limited generalization capabilities to unseen tasks, as

      A.5     L ATENCY C ONSTRAINT OF C ORE T OKENSETS
948   We now discuss the latency for our core tokensets and single-stage selection strategies, such as coreset
949
      and core tokens. The latency incurred by core tokensets is primarily determined by the different core
950
      token strategies, considering that the computation of coresets remains consistent, irrespective of the
951
      choice of the subsequent subset selection strategy. Considering that we used only gradient-based
952
      coresets, it will incur a time complexity of O(D × (F P + BP )) where D represents the dataset, FP
953
      is a forward pass, and BP is a backward pass through the neural network. Meanwhile, core tokens such
954
      as GradLRP-based incur a complexity of O(D × H(F P + BP )) where H is the number of the at-
955
      tention heads. In contrast, Atman does not use backward passes at all, but iterates over all N tokens. It
956
      thus has a complexity of O(D × N (F P )). Consequently, for core tokensets, employing a GradMatch
957
      and Atman-based core tokenset will have a time complexity of O(D(F P + BP ) + C(N × F P )).
958
      Here, C is the subset selected by the coreset approach, as only this set is relevant for the determination
959
      of respective token relevance. The combination with other methods follows analogous reasoning.
