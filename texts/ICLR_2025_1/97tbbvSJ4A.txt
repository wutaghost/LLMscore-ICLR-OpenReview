011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we address the dual challenge of maintaining high accuracy and
014            ensuring fairness in differentially private (DP) deep learning models. The opti-
015
               mization process is inherently complicated by the necessity of injecting random
               noise and limiting training iterations, particularly for over-parameterized models.
016
               Moreover, DP mechanisms frequently exacerbate accuracy disparities across sub-
017
               populations, complicating the balance between privacy and fairness. To tackle
018            these challenges, we introduce a novel framework that systematically addresses
019            the trade-off between privacy and utility in DP deep learning. At the core of
020            our approach is the concept of instance-level smoothing, which enhances privacy
021            protections without compromising performance. Our theoretical contributions in-
022            clude deep insights into sample complexity, instance-level smoothing factors, and
023            error bounds required to achieve a given privacy budget. These insights provide a
024            robust foundation for optimizing the delicate balance between privacy and utility.
025            Our method demonstrates remarkable robustness, independent of iteration counts,
026
               model parameters, batch normalization processes, and subpopulation disparities.
               This flexibility enables an optimal balance between privacy preservation and util-
027
               ity, adaptable to a wide range of scenarios. Through extensive empirical studies
028
               on the large-scale medical imaging dataset CheXpert, we validate the effective-
029            ness of our approach. Our findings align with theoretical predictions, showing that
030            our method can effectively meet stringent privacy requirements while maintaining
031            high performance. By bridging the gap between formal privacy guarantees and
032            practical deep learning applications, our work lays the groundwork for future ad-
033            vancements in the field. This research empowers practitioners to protect sensitive
034            data during model training and ensures both data privacy and model generality,
035            paving the way for more secure and equitable AI systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Large neural networks with billions of parameters have achieved state-of-the-art performance across
040
      a wide range of machine learning tasks Brown & Mann (2020); Zhai et al. (2022). Despite their
041
      success, these models are known to memorize training data Zhang et al. (2021), leading to poten-
042   tial leakage of sensitive information. Privacy attacks capable of extracting memorized training data
043   have been demonstrated on various types of models, including language models Carlini et al. (2021),
044   diffusion models Carlini et al. (2023), and image classification models Balle et al. (2022). Addition-
045   ally, membership inference attacks, which can determine whether a particular data point was used to
046   train a model, have proven successful across multiple architectures and data modalities Carlini et al.
047   (2022). The implications of these vulnerabilities are profound, particularly in applications involving
048   private, confidential, or proprietary data, such as healthcare, finance, recommendation systems, and
049   mobility. To ensure the safe deployment of models trained on such data, it is crucial to address these
050
      privacy concerns effectively.
051   Differential Privacy (DP) Dwork et al. (2006) has emerged as the gold standard for protecting indi-
052   vidual privacy in data processing algorithms. However, achieving strong privacy protections with DP
053   is particularly challenging in deep learning, especially as model sizes and data dimensions increase.
      The most popular DP training technique in deep learning is Differentially Private Stochastic Gradi-

054
      ent Descent (DP-SGD) Abadi et al. (2016), which privatizes gradients by clipping and adding noise.
055   The strength of the privacy guarantee   depends on the noise scale, batch size, number of training
056   samples, and number of iterations. While DP-SGD is a near drop-in replacement for standard SGD,
057   it presents significant challenges that have hindered its widespread adoption.
058
      Achieving high accuracy with differentially private (DP) models faces two major challenges. First,
059
      injecting random noise and limiting training iterations complicates optimization, as noisy gradients
060
      and iteration constraints hinder effective hyperparameter tuning Anil et al. (2021). The noise’s Eu-
061   clidean norm scales with model size, degrading performance for larger models Yu et al. (2021). This
062   issue is particularly problematic given the prevalence of over-parameterized neural networks in AI.
063   Second, fairness issues arise in deep neural networks, exacerbated by DP mechanisms. Models can
064   show accuracy disparities across subpopulations, such as race, age, sex, or insurance type in medi-
065   cal tasks Seyyed-Kalantari et al. (2021). DP can worsen these disparities, increasing accuracy gaps
066   due to unbalanced subgroup data Bagdasaryan et al. (2019); Santos-Lozada et al. (2020). DP limits
067   information extraction from individual data points, conflicting with the accurate learning needed
068   for small or underrepresented subgroups, particularly in medical datasets Cummings et al. (2019);
069
      Suriyakumar et al. (2021).

086   Understanding the trade-off between privacy and utility in differentially private (DP) deep learning
087   models is crucial for both theoretical and practical advancements. This paper introduces a novel
088   framework that aims to bridge the performance gap between DP models and their non-private coun-
089   terparts by training an end-to-end DP network. Our central hypothesis is that samples with sparse
090   distributions in their feature space, based on pre-trained large models, are more prone to privacy
091   leakage. In contrast, classes with sufficient samples that are compactly clustered in the feature space
092
      will be well-represented, incurring minimal privacy sacrifice while maintaining high performance.
093   To illustrate the difference in feature density among classes in the CIFAR-10 dataset, we present
094   a graph depicting the mean distances within the 10 classes. This graph measures the proximity
095   of each sample to its 5 nearest neighbors. As shown in Figure 1, the mean sample distance for
096   car is 129.25, whereas for dog, it is 191.08. The observed trade-off between privacy and utility
097   Berrada et al. (August 2023) aligns well with these mean distances: the car class exhibits the best
098
      classification accuracy and the least privacy leakage, whereas the dog class suffers from significant
      privacy leakage and class disparity issues.
099
100   Given that each class has an equal number of 5000 training samples, the within-class compact-
101   ness and between-class distance largely determine the privacy-utility trade-off. In scenarios where
102   the sample numbers across classes are imbalanced, privacy preservation and class disparity issues
103   worsen. This observation motivates us to consider instance-level probability density function (PDF)
104
      estimation and instance-specific kernel smoothing to enhance privacy without degrading perfor-
      mance. We prefer the term instance smoothing over noise, as it better captures the essence of our
105
      approach in providing privacy protection alongside model generality.
106

      improved network architectures and pre-trained models, without additional constraints. This work

138   ematical definition of privacy known as differential privacy Dwork (2006), which ensures that any
139   individual training data point cannot be identified using the trained model and any additional side
140   information. More formally, we adopt the popular modern variant called zero-centered Concentrated

      Differential Privacy (zCDP), as defined below:

      We propose a novel approach that bypasses these issues. Our method adjusts the smoothing band-
163   widths of the PDF per instance, independent of iterations, enhancing the balance between privacy
164   and accuracy. It is robust to hyperparameter settings and avoids gradient clipping and noise addition,
165   reducing bias and variance. This allows for fine-tuning large pre-trained models in high-dimensional
166   spaces without affecting batch normalization. We also provide theoretical bounds on sample size and
167   classification errors, demonstrating its effectiveness.

      Overall, our method offers a practical and efficient alternative to DP-SGD, maintaining privacy while
169   improving model performance.

171   Adaptive Kernel Density Fine-tuning Our work diverges from the DP-SGD method by drawing
172   on adaptive kernel metric representation learning, particularly useful for tasks like anomaly detec-
173   tion. Kernel density-based learning captures the underlying probability density of data, using Gaus-
174   sian kernels to assess local density and identify anomalies, which are prone to privacy risks in sparse
175   regions Zhang et al. (2018). Kernel-based methods handle uncertainty and class-specific variances
176   well, as demonstrated by Non-isotropic von Mises-Fisher (nivMF) distributions that model complex
177   variances to enhance generalization Kirchhof et al. (2022).
178   We employ a projection network parameterized by W ∈ Rp×d , where d is the feature dimension
179   from the backbone network, and p is the projected dimension in the PDF space. This adaptive kernel
180   estimation-based approach emphasizes local data density, crucial for identifying and mitigating pri-
181   vacy risks in sparse regions. It allows fine-tuning of pre-trained models in high-dimensional spaces,
182   compatible with any backbone architecture.
183   Unlike DP-SGD, which requires per-sample gradient clipping and noise addition, our method ad-
184   justs the smoothing bandwidth at the instance level, leading to more efficient training. By focusing
185   on instance-level smoothing, we effectively balance privacy and accuracy, offering a practical and
186   efficient alternative to DP-SGD for differentially private learning.

194   smoothing bandwidth to each instance based on its relative distribution density.

791   A.4    L IMITATIONS
792
793   While our proposed framework demonstrates significant advancements in balancing privacy and
794   utility in differentially private (DP) deep learning models, several limitations warrant discussion:
795
            1. Generalization Across Domains: Although our empirical studies on the CheXpert dataset
796            show promising results, the generalization of our method to other domains and data types
797            remains to be thoroughly validated. Medical imaging datasets, like CheXpert, may possess
798            unique characteristics that are not representative of other datasets, potentially limiting the
799            applicability of our findings to different domains such as natural language processing or
800            other forms of visual data.
801         2. Scalability and Computational Overhead: Our instance-level smoothing mechanism,
802            while effective, introduces additional computational overhead that may not be scalable for
803            extremely large datasets or models with billions of parameters. The increased computa-
804            tional requirements could hinder the practical deployment of our framework in resource-
805            constrained environments.
806         3. Hyperparameter Sensitivity: The success of our method heavily relies on the careful tun-
807            ing of hyperparameters related to instance-level smoothing and noise injection. Identifying
808            the optimal settings for these parameters can be challenging and may require extensive
809            experimentation, which could limit the ease of adoption for practitioners with limited re-
               sources or expertise in hyperparameter optimization.


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
           4. Impact on Training Time: The necessity to balance privacy and utility often comes with a
811           trade-off in training time. Our method, which includes additional smoothing processes and
812           noise injection, may result in longer training times compared to non-DP models. This could
813           be a critical limitation for applications requiring rapid model development and deployment.
814
           5. Potential for Subpopulation Fairness: Despite our efforts to mitigate accuracy disparities
815           across subpopulations, the inherent complexity of ensuring fairness in DP models means
816           that some level of disparity may still persist. Future work is needed to develop more robust
817           methods for guaranteeing fairness across diverse and heterogeneous subpopulations.
818
           6. Theoretical Assumptions: Our theoretical insights and error bounds are based on certain
819           assumptions regarding sample complexity and instance-level smoothing factors. The prac-
820           tical validity of these assumptions in real-world scenarios needs further empirical validation
821           across a broader range of datasets and model architectures.
822
           7. Privacy Budget Allocation: The allocation of the privacy budget in DP models is a critical
823           factor that influences the overall performance. Our framework provides guidelines for op-
824           timizing this balance, but the practical implementation of these guidelines can be complex
825           and may not always achieve the desired outcomes in every context.
826
827   Addressing these limitations in future research will be crucial for advancing the practical applica-
828   bility and robustness of differentially private deep learning models, ensuring that they can meet the
829   dual challenges of high accuracy and fairness across a wide range of applications.
