009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained models have demonstrated impressive generalization capabilities, yet
012            they remain vulnerable to catastrophic forgetting when incrementally trained on
013            new tasks. Existing architecture-based strategies encounter two primary chal-
014            lenges: Firstly, integrating a pre-trained network with a trainable sub-network
015            complicates the delicate balance between learning plasticity and memory stability
016            across evolving tasks during learning. Secondly, the absence of robust intercon-
017            nections between pre-trained networks and various sub-networks limits the effec-
018
               tive retrieval of pertinent information during inference. In this study, we introduce
               the Artsy framework, inspired by the activation mechanisms of silent synapses via
019
               spike-timing-dependent plasticity observed in mature biological neural networks,
020
               to enhance the continual learning capabilities of pre-trained models. The Artsy
021            framework integrates two key components: 1) During training, the framework
022            mimics mature brain dynamics by maintaining memory stability for previously
023            learned knowledge within the pre-trained network while simultaneously promot-
024            ing learning plasticity in task-specific sub-networks. 2) During inference, arti-
025            ficial silent and functional synapses are utilized to establish precise connections
026            between the pre-synaptic neurons in the pre-trained network and the post-synaptic
027            neurons in the sub-networks, facilitated through synaptic consolidation, thereby
028            enabling effective extraction of relevant information from test samples. Compre-
029
               hensive experimental evaluations reveal that our model significantly outperforms
               conventional methods on class-incremental learning tasks, while also providing
030
               enhanced biological interpretability for architecture-based approaches. Moreover,
031
               we propose that the Artsy framework offers a promising avenue for simulating bi-
032            ological synaptic mechanisms, potentially advancing our understanding of neural
033            plasticity in both artificial and biological systems.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Pre-trained artificial neural networks have demonstrated notable generalization capabilities; how-
039   ever, they are prone to catastrophic forgetting when exposed to sequential training on new datasets,
040   as outlined in previous studies Wang et al. (2024). To overcome this limitation, it is imperative for
041   pre-trained models to employ continual learning (CL) strategies that enable them to assimilate new
042   tasks while preserving previously acquired knowledge. The process of training artificial networks
043   using various CL methodologies is depicted in Figure 1. Within the sequential learning paradigm,
044   both regularization-based Kirkpatrick et al. (2017) and optimization-based approaches Lopez-Paz &
045
      Ranzato (2017); Zeng et al. (2019) have been developed to mitigate memory degradation, attempting
      to maintain previous knowledge through weight regularization and gradient projection. Nonetheless,
046
      these methods frequently encounter difficulties in striking an optimal balance between learning plas-
047
      ticity for new tasks and maintaining stability of the established knowledge. In contrast, within the
048   joint learning framework, replay-based strategies Shin et al. (2017); Van de Ven et al. (2020) sidestep
049   the plasticity-stability trade-off by replaying experiences, utilizing generative models, and leverag-
050   ing features from previously learned data. However, this approach often incurs substantial energy
051   costs, particularly when training large-scale models on extensive raw datasets, as seen in models
052   like GPT-3. Alternatively, in the split learning paradigm, architecture-based methods Aljundi et al.
053   (2017); Yu et al. (2024) employ modular networks to facilitate learning from new data, offering a
      more energy-efficient solution.

071   Nevertheless, architecture-based approaches encounter two fundamental challenges. Firstly, dur-
072   ing the training phase, integrating a frozen pre-trained network with a trainable sub-network (e.g.,
073   Adaptformer Chen et al. (2022) or Mixture-of-Experts Jacobs et al. (1991)) necessitates optimizing
074   lightweight parameters within the sub-network Zhou et al. (2024a); Yu et al. (2024) to accommodate
075   a range of new tasks. This integration presents a considerable challenge in maintaining an optimal
076
      balance between learning plasticity for novel tasks and memory stability for previously acquired
      knowledge, akin to the issues faced in sequential learning paradigms. Secondly, in the testing phase,
077
      the model, comprising a static pre-trained network and several trained sub-networks that encapsulate
078
      specialized knowledge for various tasks Zhou et al. (2024b), lacks an effective mechanism for es-
079   tablishing connections between these networks. Consequently, the absence of learned inter-network
080   pathways hinders the model’s ability to effectively extract and leverage relevant information from
081   test samples, thereby limiting prediction accuracy.
082
      Biological neural networks exhibit remarkable proficiency in addressing CL tasks, a capability fun-
083
      damentally rooted in synaptic consolidation mechanisms. Synaptic plasticity plays a crucial role
084
      in achieving a balance between learning plasticity and memory stability, particularly in the devel-
085   oping, prepubescent brain. As young organisms (both animals and humans) acclimate to novel
086   environments, synaptic plasticity drives dynamic alterations in the synaptic connections between
087   presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons, facilitating adaptation to environmental changes Kerchner &
088   Nicoll (2008); Hanse et al. (2013). Silent synapses, in particular, contribute to this adaptive capacity
089   by enabling highly flexible connectivity between neurons Durand et al. (1996); Huang et al. (2015),
090   thereby supporting synaptic plasticity. Studies by Liao et al. (1995) and Isaac et al. (1995) have
091   identified the presence of silent synapses in the hippocampus, characterized by the mediation of N-
092   methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor responses in the absence of α-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-
093
      isoxazole propionic acid (AMPA) receptor-mediated activity. The structural configuration of these
      silent synapses is depicted in Figure 2. Moreover, the process of synaptic conversion—whereby
094
      silent synapses become functional (unsilencing) and vice versa—occurs frequently during brain de-
095
      velopment but is considered less common in mature neural networks Hanse et al. (2013). Despite
096   this, the adult brain retains the capacity for learning plasticity and memory stability, largely owing
097   to the ability of silent synapses located at filopodia to transform into functional synapses, thereby
098   forming new synaptic connections Vardalaki et al. (2022). Additionally, recent evidence Vardalaki
099   et al. (2022) suggests that the formation of new synaptic connections in the adult neocortex is instru-
100   mental not only in preserving existing memories via mature synapses at dendritic spines but also in
101   facilitating the rapid acquisition of new information through the dynamic activity of silent synapses
102   at filopodia.
103   In this study, we propose the Artsy framework, a novel approach to enhancing the CL capabilities
104   of pre-trained models by leveraging insights from the activation mechanisms of silent synapses via
105   spike-timing-dependent plasticity observed in biological neural networks. This biological process,
106   which enables the conversion of silent synapses into functional synapses within the adult neocortex
107   Vardalaki et al. (2022), serves as a foundation for expanding the learning potential of mature neural
      systems. The Artsy framework is designed around two key components: (1) It emulates the mature

152   We have implemented the Artsy framework on class-incremental learning tasks, and extensive ex-
153   perimental evaluations demonstrate that our model not only outperforms conventional methods but
154   also provides a more biologically interpretable approach compared to existing architecture-based
155   methods, which often lack clear biological explanations.

      2.2   S ILENT SYNAPSE IN BRAIN
190
      Synapses are fundamental units of information storage in the brain, connecting pre-synaptic and
191
      post-synaptic neurons to facilitate learning plasticity and memory stability. During development,
192   certain synapses are incapable of neurotransmission under basal conditions and remain in a reserve
193   state until activated by an appropriate trigger Hanse et al. (2013). Silent synapses, which are preva-
194   lent in the hippocampus of young mammalian brains, exhibit an excitatory postsynaptic current that
195   is absent at the resting membrane potential but becomes apparent upon depolarization. Functional
196   synapses require both AMPA and NMDA receptors to activate connections, whereas silent synapses
197   possess only NMDA receptors and lack AMPA receptors, rendering them inactive. Various studies
198   have demonstrated that presynaptic activity, mimicked by glutamate uncaging, leads to the insertion
199   of AMPA receptors in silent synapses. A current injection into the soma of the postsynaptic neu-
200
      ron produces a single action potential, thereby establishing new connections and converting silent
      synapses into functional synapses.
201
202   Silent synapses are abundant in the prepubescent brain, where they mediate circuit formation and
203   refinement, but are thought to be rare in adulthood. Recently, Vardalaki et al. (2022) provided evi-
204   dence that silent synapses on filopodia still occur in the adult brain. This discovery suggests a novel
205   mechanism for the flexible control of synaptic wiring, potentially expanding the learning capabili-
206
      ties of the mature brain. Pre-trained networks in machine learning exhibit strong zero-shot learning
      capabilities, but their adaptation to new environments still requires continual learning. In this study,
207
      insights from silent synapses in the mature brain are employed to extend the class-incremental learn-
208
      ing capabilities of pre-trained networks. We utilize artificial functional and silent synapses to create
209   connections between the pre-trained network (memory stability in the mature brain) and the initial-
210   ized sub-networks (learning plasticity in the mature brain).

220
221   The Artsy network architecture comprises four components: (1) a pre-trained network (analogous
222   to the mature brain network, including the pre-synaptic neuron on the spine); (2) initialized sub-
223   networks (analogous to the mature brain network, including the postsynaptic neuron on the filopo-
224   dia); (3) artificial synapses (analogous to silent and functional synapses); and (4) a classifier.
225   Pre-trained Network: Models pre-trained on large datasets exhibit strong generalization capabil-
226   ities. Similar to how young brains acquire extensive knowledge during development in dynamic
227   environments, neurons with synapses in the mature brain mediate circuit formation and refinement
228   to adapt to the real world. We posit that the pre-trained network is analogous to the adult brain. In
229   machine learning, the pre-trained network encodes the features of new data x, expressed as:

451   4.5   A BLATIONS ON A RTSY
452
      The Artsy framework comprises two primary components: (1) the network architecture, which in-
453
      cludes a pre-trained network and multiple sub-networks, and (2) the artificial synapse, which con-
454   nects the pre-trained network to the sub-networks. In our study, the sub-networks are implemented
455   using adapter modules. These adapters exhibit strong capabilities for downstream tasks and have
456   minimal impact on class-incremental learning (CIL) tasks Zhou et al. (2024a). Additionally, vari-
457   ants of pre-trained networks demonstrate strong zero-shot generalization capabilities for new tasks.
458   Therefore, we focus our investigation on the artificial synapse rather than the network architecture
459   in CIL tasks. The artificial synapse in Artsy is crucial for CIL tasks in this study. To effectively
460   extract relevant information from test samples during inference, a well-designed artificial synapse is
461   essential.
462   We focus on the ablation study of the artificial synapse, implemented as an MLP-based binary clas-
463   sifier. Different features extracted from new stimuli can influence the conversion of silent synapses
464   into functional synapses. We compare the impact of different features (good feature and bad feature)
465   from the pre-trained model on the activation of artificial silent synapses. As shown in Figure 4, using
466   bad feature for activating the artificial synapse results in lower Last and Average accuracy compared
467   to good feature. Bad feature cannot be effectively classified by the binary classifier, leading to in-
468
      correct connections between the networks and a failure to extract relevant information from the test
      samples. This experiment validates the importance of correct connections between the pre-trained
469
      network and sub-networks for CIL tasks. Furthermore, it highlights the necessity of selecting ap-
470
      propriate features for the artificial synapse and constructing an effective binary classifier to simulate
471   artificial synapses. Furthermore, these findings support the hypothesis that brain lesions causing
472   synaptic disconnections can lead to dementia by disrupting synaptic consolidation.

      5     R ELATED WORK
476
477
      Continual learning methods are categorized into fixed-architecture and dynamic-architecture strate-
      gies. Fixed-architecture approaches, such as sequential learning, incrementally acquire new tasks
478
      while minimizing forgetting through regularization terms in the loss function Aljundi et al. (2018);
479
      Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Li & Hoiem (2017); Ding et al. (2022), but may limit adaptability to novel
480   tasks. Joint learning, another fixed-architecture method, employs replay of stored data or features
481   from previous tasks Rebuffi et al. (2017); Hou et al. (2019); Buzzega et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2021);
482   Cha et al. (2021); Yan et al. (2021), balancing plasticity and stability but raising privacy concerns and
483   storage issues. In contrast, dynamic-architecture (expansion-based) methods dynamically modify
484   the network to incorporate new tasks while preserving prior knowledge Mallya & Lazebnik (2018);
485   Serra et al. (2018); Wang et al. (2020); Douillard et al. (2022). While effective against catastrophic
      forgetting, they increase memory consumption and face challenges in task-agnostic settings.

513   6   C ONCLUSION
514
515
      Our methodology draws inspiration from the dynamic conversion of silent synapses into functional
516   synapses, mirroring the adaptive learning mechanisms found in biological neural networks. This
517   biologically inspired process enables the Artsy framework to strike an optimal balance between
518   learning plasticity and memory stability, effectively addressing the challenge of catastrophic for-
519   getting commonly observed in continual learning scenarios. Our findings suggest that integrating
520   principles of synaptic plasticity into artificial neural networks can substantially improve their per-
521   formance and resilience in lifelong learning tasks. Ablation studies further underscore the critical
522   role of artificial synapses in maintaining both learning adaptability and memory stability. Specif-
523   ically, constructing artificial networks that emulate the behavior of silent synapses is essential for
524
      distinguishing between previously learned data and novel data. Utilizing out-of-distribution detec-
      tion methods Ran et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2024) can achieve this more effectively than employing
525
      an MLP-based binary classifier. Additionally, this approach offers potential avenues for simulating
526
      neurodegenerative conditions, particularly those marked by memory degradation due to synaptic
527   dysfunction Forner et al. (2017). We also propose that Artsy can emulate synaptic consolidation,
528   thereby opening new pathways for research into brain plasticity through the lens of artificial net-
529   works Albesa-González & Clopath (2024); Xu et al. (2024), with significant implications for both
530   artificial intelligence and neuroscience.
