011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) poses distinct challenges as agents
014            learn strategies through experiences. Gradient-based methods often fail to con-
015
               verge in MARL, and performances are highly sensitive to initial random seeds,
               contributing to what has been termed the MARL reproducibility crisis. Concur-
016
               rently, significant advances have been made in solving Variational Inequalities
017
               (VIs)—which include equilibrium-finding problems—particularly in addressing
018            the non-converging rotational dynamics that impede convergence of traditional
019            gradient-based optimization methods. This paper explores the potential of lever-
020            aging VI-based techniques to improve MARL training. Specifically, we study the
021            integration of VI methods—namely, Nested-Lookahead VI (nLA-VI) and Extragra-
022            dient (EG)—into the multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient (MADDPG)
023            algorithm. We present a VI reformulation of the actor-critic algorithm for both
024            single- and multi-agent settings. We introduce three algorithms that use nLA-VI,
025            EG, and a combination of both, named LA-MADDPG, EG-MADDPG, and LA-EG-
026
               MADDPG, respectively. Our empirical results show that these VI-based approaches
               yield significant performance improvements in benchmark environments, such as
027
               the zero-sum games: rock-paper-scissors and matching pennies, where equilibrium
028
               strategies can be quantitatively assessed, and the Multi-Agent Particle Environment:
029            Predator-prey benchmark, where VI-based methods also yield balanced participa-
030            tion of agents from the same team, further highlighting the substantial impact of
031            advanced optimization techniques on MARL performance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) is a powerful machine learning approach for solving
037   complex, multi-player problems in diverse domains. It has been applied to tasks such as coordinating
038   multi-robot and multi-drone systems for instance for search and warehouse automation, optimizing
039
      traffic flow and vehicle platooning in autonomous driving, managing energy distribution in smart
      grids, simulating financial markets and automated trading, improving patient management and drug
040
      discovery in healthcare, enhancing network performance in telecommunications, training intelligent
041
      agents in games (e.g., Omidshafiei et al., 2017; Vinyals et al., 2017; Spica et al., 2018; Zhou et al.,
042   2021; Bertsekas, 2021, among others). In MARL, a system of N agents seeks to jointly optimize a
043   shared objective, with each agent operating based on its own policy, derived from its observations of
044   the environment. Depending on their reward objectives, agents may exhibit cooperative, competitive,
045   or mixed behaviors. These interactions introduce complex learning dynamics, making MARL
046   significantly more challenging and distinct from single and actor-only reinforcement learning (RL).
047
      Despite the applicability of MARL to a wide range of problems, their deployment and research
048   development face significant challenges. Key issues include: (i) The iterative training process in
049   data-driven MARL is notoriously difficult, often failing to start to converge. This lack of conver-
050   gence remains a fundamental obstacle. (ii) Performance is highly sensitive to small changes in
051   hyperparameters or initial random seed variations, leading to unpredictable outcomes. This hinders
052   reproducibility. These challenges are also evident in single-agent reinforcement learning with
053   actor-critic structure (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999)–an instance of a two-player game. Such methods
      require meticulous hyperparameter tuning, and their outcomes can vary significantly based on the


                                                        1

054
      random seeds used for sampling and model initialization (Wang et al., 2022; Eimer et al., 2023).
055   This variability undermines reproducibility, complicating both research progress and real-world
056   deployment (Henderson et al., 2019; Lynnerup et al., 2019). In MARL, these challenges are even
057   more pronounced, contributing to what is often referred to as the reproducibility crisis (Bettini
058   et al., 2024). Small changes to hyperparameter values can drastically alter results, as demonstrated
059   by Gorsane et al. (2022). Their study reveals significant performance variability across different
060   seeds in popular MARL benchmarks such as the StarCraft multi-agent challenge (Samvelyan et al.,
061   2019). Additionally, gradient-based optimization methods in MARL face unique challenges, such as
062   difficulties in exploring the joint policy space of multiple agents (Li et al., 2023; Christianos et al.,
063
      2021), often leading to suboptimal solutions. Some MARL structures also exhibit inherent cycling
      effects (Zheng et al., 2021), further exacerbating the problem of convergence. In this work, we focus
064
      primarily on addressing challenge (i) by improving training stability. In doing so, we also aim to
065
      mitigate the high variability caused by random seed and hyperparameter sensitivity, thereby partially
066   addressing (ii).
067
068   A concurrent line of works focuses on the Variational Inequality (VI) problem, a general class of
069
      problems that encompasses both equilibria- and optima-finding problems; see Section 3 for definition.
      VIs generalize standard minimization problems. In this case, the operator F is a gradient field
070
      F ≡ ∇f . However, by allowing F to be a more general vector field, VIs also model problems such
071
      as finding equilibria in zero-sum and general-sum games (Cottle & Dantzig, 1968; Rockafellar, 1970).
072   It has been observed that standard optimization methods that perform well in minimization tasks
073   of the form minz f (z)—where f : Rd → R is a real-valued loss function—often fail to solve some
074   simple instances of VIs. This failure is due to the rotational component inherent in the gradient
075   dynamics of these settings, which causes the latest iterate to cycle around the solution, leading to
076   non-convergence (Mescheder et al., 2018; Balduzzi et al., 2018). More precisely, the Jacobian of the
077   associated vector field (see def. in Section 3) can be decomposed into a symmetric and antisymmetric
078   component (Balduzzi et al., 2018), where each behaves as a potential (Monderer & Shapley, 1996)
079   and a Hamiltonian (purely rotational) game, resp. For instance, the gradient descent method for the
080
      simple minz1 ∈Rd1 maxz2 ∈Rd2 z1 · z2 game, which simultaneously updates z1 , z1 rotates around
      the solution for infinitesimally small learning rates, and diverges away from it for practical choices
081
      of its value. As a result, all variation methods based on gradient descent, such as Adam (Kingma
082
      & Ba, 2015) have no hope of converging for some broad problem classes of VIs. This problematic
083   behavior is particularly pronounced when the separate sets of parameters are neural networks, as in
084   generative adversarial networks (GANs, Goodfellow et al., 2014). As a result, when GANs were first
085   introduced, substantial computational resources were required to fine-tune hyperparameters (Radford
086   et al., 2016). In addition, even for highly tuned hyperparameters, training with minimization methods
087   often (eventually) diverges away (Chavdarova et al., 2021), in sharp contrast to standard minimization.
088   The original GAN formulation (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and practical GAN implementations that are
089   not necessarily zero-sum, are all instances of VIs. The above training difficulties inspired numerous
090   recent research efforts to develop numerical methods to approximately solve variational inequalities
091
      (VIs) and to study how VI optimization differs from minimization. Various algorithms have been
      proposed and studied; reviewed in Sections 2 and 3 and Appendix A.1.1.
092
093   This paper hypothesizes that training instabilities in MARL and actor-critic RL primarily arise from
094   the rotational dynamics inherent in competitive learning objectives. This issue is compounded by
095   the prevalent reliance in practice on gradient descent-based methods, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba,
096   2015), which are known to induce similar issues in the Variational Inequality (VI) literature and have
097
      been observed to cause analogous challenges in other VI applications (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Gidel
      et al., 2019a; Chavdarova et al., 2021). Since VI optimization methods are specifically designed to
098
      address rotational vector fields, this paper raises the following question:
099
100
101   Can MARL algorithms benefit from the application of Variational Inequality optimization methods?
102
103
104
      To address this question, we focus on the multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient (MADDPG)
      method (Lowe et al., 2017) and integrate it with the (combination of) nested-Lookahead-VI (nLA-
105
      VI) (Chavdarova et al., 2021) and Extragradient (EG) (Korpelevich, 1976) methods for solving
106
      variational inequalities (VIs). Our main contributions are as follows:
107
      • Primerely, we present a VI perspective for multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) problems.

117   2    R ELATED W ORKS
118
119   Our work draws mainly from two lines of work that we review next.
120
121
122
      Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL). Various MARL algorithms have been devel-
      oped (Lowe et al., 2017; Iqbal & Sha, 2018; Ackermann et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2021), with some
123
      extending existing single-agent reinforcement learning (RL) methods (Rashid et al., 2018; Son et al.,
124
      2019; Yu et al., 2022; Kuba et al., 2022). Lowe et al. (2017) extend the actor-critic algorithm to the
125   MARL setting using the centralized training decentralized execution framework. In the proposed
126   algorithm, named multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient (MADDPG), each agent in the game
127   consists of two components: an actor and a critic. The actor is a policy network that has access
128   only to the local observations of the corresponding agent and is trained to output appropriate actions.
129   The critic is a value network that receives additional information about the policies of other agents
130   and learns to output the Q-value; see Section 3. After a phase of experience collection, a batch is
131   sampled from a replay buffer and used for training the agents. To our knowledge, all deep MARL
132   implementations rely on either stochastic gradient descent or Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015)
133
      to train all networks. Game theory and MARL share many foundational concepts, and several studies
      explore the relationships between the two fields (Yang & Wang, 2021; Fan, 2024), with some using
134
      game-theoretic approaches to model MARL problems (Zheng et al., 2021). This work proposes
135
      incorporating game-theoretic techniques into the optimization process of existing MARL methods to
136   determine if these techniques can enhance MARL optimization.
137
138
139   Variational Inequalities (VIs). VIs were first formulated to understand the equilibrium of a
140   dynamical system (Stampacchia, 1964). Since then, they have been studied extensively in mathematics
141   including operational research and network games (see Facchinei & Pang, 2003, and references
142
      therein). More recently, after the shown training difficulties of GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014)—
      which are an instance of VIs—an extensive line of works in machine learning studies the convergence
143
      of iterative gradient-based methods to solve VIs numerically. Since the last and average iterates
144
      can be far apart when solving VIs (see e.g., Chavdarova et al., 2019), these works primarily aimed
145   at obtaining last-iterate convergence for special cases of VIs that are important in applications,
146   including bilinear or strongly monotone games (e.g., Tseng, 1995; Malitsky, 2015; Facchinei &
147   Pang, 2003; Daskalakis et al., 2018; Liang & Stokes, 2019; Gidel et al., 2019b; Azizian et al., 2020;
148   Thekumparampil et al., 2022), VIs with cocoercive operators (Diakonikolas, 2020), or monotone
149   operators (Chavdarova et al., 2023; Gorbunov et al., 2022). Several works (i) exploit continuous-time
150   analyses (Ryu et al., 2019; Bot et al., 2020; Rosca et al., 2021; Chavdarova et al., 2023; Bot et al.,
151   2022), (ii) establish lower bounds for some VI classes (e.g., Golowich et al., 2020b;a), and (iii)
152   study the constrained setting (Daskalakis & Panageas, 2019; Cai et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023;
153
      Chavdarova et al., 2024), among other. Due to the computational complexities involved in training
      neural networks, iterative methods that rely solely on first-order derivative computation are the most
154
      commonly used approaches for solving variational inequalities (VIs). However, standard gradient
155
      descent and its momentum-based variants often fail to converge even on simple instances of VIs.
156   As a result, several alternative methods have been developed to address this issue. Some of the
157   most popular first-order methods for solving VIs include the extragradient method (Korpelevich,
158   1976), optimistic gradient method (Popov, 1980), Halpern method (Diakonikolas, 2020), and (nested)
159   Lookahead-VI method (Chavdarova et al., 2021); these are discussed in detail in Section 3 and
160   Appendix A.1.1. In this work, we primarily focus on the nested Lookahead-VI method, which has
161   achieved state-of-the-art results on the CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) benchmark for generative
      adversarial networks (Goodfellow et al., 2014).

164   Notation. Bold small letters denote vectors, and curly capital letters denote sets. Let Z be a convex
165   and compact set in the Euclidean space, with inner product ⟨·, ·⟩.

167   Setting: multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient. Markov Games (MGs) extend Markov
168   Decision Processes to the multi-agent setting. In a Markov Game, N agents interact within an
169   environment characterized by a set of states S. Agents receive observations oi , i = 1, . . . , N of the
170   current environment state s ∈ S. Based on their policies πi , each agent i chooses an action ai ∈ Ai
171   from predefined finite action sets Ai , i = 1, . . . , N . These actions, collectively represented as a, are
172   then applied to the environment, which transitions to a new state ŝ ∈ S according to a transition
173   function T : S → S. Each agent receives a reward ri , i = 1 . . . N , and a new observation ôi . In the
174
      MARL setting herein, each agent has its own Q-value that is, how much reward it expects to get from
      a state when joint action a is performed.
175
176   Multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient (MADDPG, Lowe et al., 2017), extends Deep
177   deterministic policy gradient (DDPG, Lillicrap et al., 2015) to multi-agent setting using the framework
178   of centralized training decentralized execution. Each agent i has (i) a critic network—Qi —which
179   acts as a centralized action-value function, (ii) a target critic network Q′i that is less frequently updated
180
      with the most recent Qi parameters for learning stability, (iii) an actor network µi which represents
      the policy to be updated, and (iv) a target actor network µ′i from which it selects its actions and is
181
      periodically updated with the learned policy µi . Both the Critic and Actor networks are modeled
182
      using feedforward networks, parameterized by w and θ respectively.
183

      LA-MADDPG. Algorithm 2 describes the LA-MADDPG method. Critically, the (nested)LA-VI
310
      method is used in the joint strategy space of all players. In this way, the averaging steps address the
311
      rotational component of the associated vector field defined by FMADDPG resulting from the adversarial
312   nature of the agents’ objectives. In particular, it is necessary not to use an agent whose parameters
313   have already been averaged at that iteration.
314
315
      The LA-MADDPG algorithm saves snapshots of the actor and critic networks for all agents, peri-
      odically averaging them with the current networks during training. While the MADDPG algorithm
316
      (Algorithm 4) runs normally using a base optimizer (e.g., Adam), at every interval k, a lookahead
317
      averaging step is performed between the current networks (denoted θ, w), and their saved snapshots
318   θs , ws , as detailed in Algorithm 1. This method updates both the current networks and snapshots with
319   the α-averaged values. Multiple nested lookahead levels can be applied, where each additional level
320   updates its snapshot after a longer interval; see Algorithm 1. We denote lookahead update intervals
321   (episodes) with k subscripted by s and a larger number of s in subscript implies outer lookahead level,
322   e.g., ks , kss , ksss for three levels. All agents undergo lookahead updates at the same step, applying
323   this to both the actor and critic parameters simultaneously. An extended version of the algorithm with

330   methods have convergence guarantees, that is EG-MADDPG (Korpelevich, 1976; Gorbunov et al.,
331   2022), LA-MADDPG (Pethick et al., 2023), and LA-EG-MADDPG (Chavdarova et al., 2021, Thm.
332   3) have convergence guarantees. Contrary to these, the standard gradient descent method does not
333   converge for this problem (Korpelevich, 1976).

346   2-player game: rock–paper–scissors. Rock–paper–scissors is a widely studied game in multi-agent
347   settings because, in addition to its analytically computable Nash equilibrium that allows for a precise
348
      performance measure, it demonstrates interesting cyclical behavior (Zhou, 2015; Wang et al., 2014).
      The game, with M = 3 actions, has a mixed Nash equilibrium where each action is played with equal
349
      probability. At equilibrium, each agent’s action distribution is ( 13 , 13 , 13 ). This equilibrium allows us
350
      to assess the alignment of learned policies with the optimal strategy. In our experiments version, N
351   players compete in an M -action game over t steps. At each step, players receive an observation of
352   their opponent’s last action. Once all players have selected their actions for the current step, rewards
353   are assigned to each player: as of −1 for a losing, 0 for tie and +1 for winning the game. we used
354   N = 2 players, M = 3 actions, and a time horizon of t = 25 steps.
355
      2-player game: matching pennies. The game has M = 2 actions, N = 2 players: even and odd
356
      that compete over t steps. At each step, the players must choose between two actions: Heads or
357   Tails. Even player wins with a reward of +1 if the players chose the same action and loses with a −1
358   otherwise, and vice versa. We used t = 25 steps. Similar to Rock–paper–scissors, this game also has
359   mixed Nash equilibrium where each action is played with equal probability. At equilibrium, each
360   agent’s action distribution is ( 12 , 12 ).

362
      rium for both rock–paper–scissors and matching pennies.

364   MPE: Predator-prey— from the Multi-Agent Particle Environments (MPE) benchmark (Lowe et al.,
365   2017). It consists of N good agents, L landmarks, and M adversary agents. The good agents
366
      are faster and receive negative rewards if caught by adversaries, while the slower adversary agents
      are rewarded for catching a good agent. All agents can observe the positions of other agents, and
367
      adversaries also observe the velocities of the good agents. Additionally, good agents are penalized
368
      for going out of bounds. This environment combines elements of both competition and collaboration.
369   While all adversaries are rewarded when one of them catches a good agent, their slower speed
370   typically requires them to collaborate, especially since there are usually more adversaries than good
371   agents. For our experiments, we set N = 1, M = 2, and L = 2.
372
      MPE: Physical deception, (Lowe et al., 2017). The game has N good agents, one adversary agent,
373
      and N landmarks, with one designated as the target. The adversary does not observe the target and
374
      must infer which of the N landmarks is the target one, aiming to get as close as possible and receiving
375   rewards based on its distance from the target. The good agents can observe the target and aim to

457   Summary. Overall, our results indicate the following. (i) With hyperparameter tuning, the proposed
458   VI-based methods achieve significant performance improvements; see Figure 1a. (ii) With informed
459
      guesses for hyperparameters (details in Appendix A.2.1), our VI-based methods consistently outper-
      form the baseline methods. (iii) Overall, the proposed VI methods do not yield worse performance
460
      than their respective baseline methods, as they effectively address the rotational dynamics.
461
462   Comparison among our VI methods & contrasting with GAN conclusions. The widely used
463   Extragradient (EG) method for solving VIs—known for its convergence for monotone VIs—overall
464   performs close to the baseline. EG only introduces a minor local adjustment compared to GD. As
465
      such, the results align with expectations: while EG occasionally outperforms GD (the baseline), its
      performance is often similar. In contrast, nested LA applies a significantly stronger contraction, with
466
      the degree of contraction increasing as the number of nested levels increases. This leads to substantial
467
      performance gains, particularly in terms of stability, as it prevents the last iterate from diverging.
468   However, if the number of nested levels is too high, the steps can become overly conservative or
469   slow. Based on our experiments, three levels of nested LA yielded the best results (see Fig. 1-a). The
470   results also confirm that the MARL vector field in these games is highly rotational. For scenarios
471   with highly competitive reward structures among agents, we recommend using VI methods with
472   higher contractiveness, such as employing multiple levels of nested LA.
473
      These observations are consistent with results from GANs settings (Chavdarova et al., 2021), while
474   EG offers slight improvements over the baseline, more contractive methods consistently achieve
475   better results.

      On the rewards as a metric in MARL. While saturating rewards are commonly used as a per-
      formance metric in MARL, our experiments suggest otherwise, consistent with observations made
478
      in some previous works such as (Bowling, 2004). In multi-agent games like Rock-paper-scissors,
479
      rewards may converge to a target value even with suboptimal policies, leading to misleading evalua-
480   tions. For instance, in Figure 3 (top row), agents repeatedly choose similar actions, resulting in ties
481   that yield the correct reward but fail to reach equilibrium—leaving them vulnerable to exploitation
482   by a more skilled opponent. Conversely, LA-MADDPG (bottom row) did not fully converge to
483   the maximum reward, but agents learned near-optimal policies by randomizing over their actions,
484   which is the desired equilibrium. This underscores the need for stronger evaluation metrics in multi-
485   agent reinforcement learning, particularly when the true equilibrium remains unknown. Refer to
      Appendix A.5 for additional discussion.

515   6    C ONCLUSION
516
517   This paper addresses the fundamental optimization challenges in multi-agent reinforcement learning
518   (MARL). By framing MARL as an instance of a Variational Inequality (VI) problem, we highlight its
519   inherent optimization difficulties, which resemble those encountered in solving VIs. These challenges
520   manifest in practice as notoriously difficult training, significant performance variability across random
521   seeds, and other issues that hinder MARL reproducibility, development, and deployment.
522
      To address these challenges, we leverage VI optimization techniques to enhance the convergence and
523   stability of MARL methods. We introduced the LA-MADDPG, EG-MADDPG and LA-EG-MADDPG
524   algorithms that combine the multi-agent deep deterministic policy gradient (MADDPG) method
525   with nested Lookahead-VI (Chavdarova et al., 2021), Extragradient (Korpelevich, 1976), and a
526   combination of both, respectively. Our experiments on the rock-paper-scissors, matching pennies and
527   two MPE environments (Lowe et al., 2017) consistently demonstrated the effectiveness of the VI
528   variants of MADDPG in improving performance and stabilizing training compared to the standard
529   baseline method. These findings point toward promising opportunities for further development of
530   VI-based methods in MARL, particularly in leveraging the structure of the MARL optimization
531
      landscape.

761
762   The following VI class is often referred to as the generalized class for VIs to that of convexity in
763   minimization.
764
765   Definition 1 (monotonicity) An operator F : Rd → Rd is monotone if ⟨z − z ′ , F (z) − F (z ′ )⟩ ≥
766
      0, ∀z, z ′ ∈ Rd . F is µ-strongly monotone if: ⟨z − z ′ , F (z) − F (z ′ )⟩ ≥ µ∥z − z ′ ∥2 for all
      z, z ′ ∈ Rd .

      In addition to those presented in the main part, we describe the following popular VI method.

771   Popov, 1980) is:
772                                zt+1 = zt − 2ηF (zt ) + ηF (zt−1 ) ,                      (OGD)
773   where η ∈ (0, 1) is the learning rate.

946   A.2.1    H YPERPARAMETER S ELECTION FOR N ESTED -L OOKAHEAD
947
948   In this section, we discuss and share guidelines for hyperparameter selection based on our experiments.
949
950   Summary.
951
952           • We observed two- or three-level of nested-Lookahead outperform single-level Lookahead.
953           • Each level has different k, denoted here with ks , kss , ksss as in the main part. These should
954             be selected as multiple of the selected k for the level before, that is, kss ≡ css ks , and
955             ksss ≡ csss kss , where css , csss are positive integers.
956           • We observed that for the innermost lookahead, small values for ks , such as smaller than 50,
957             perform better than using large values. For the outer kss , ksss large values work well, such
958             as in the range between 5 − 10 for the css , csss .
959           • We typically used α = 0.5, and we observed lower values, such as α = 0.3, give better
960             performances then α > 0.5.
961
962   Discussion.
963
964           • To give an intuition regarding the above-listed conclusions, small values for ks help because
965             the MARL setting is very noisy and the vector field is rotational. If large values are used
966             for ks , then the algorithm will diverge away. It is known that the combination of noise and
967             rotational vector field can cause methods to diverge away (Chavdarova et al., 2019).
968           • Relative to the analogous conclusions for GANs (Chavdarova et al., 2021), the differences
969             is that:
970               – The better-performing values for ks are of a similar range as for Lookahead with GD
971                 for GANs; however they are smaller than those used for Lookahead with EG for GANs.

1210   A.5   O N THE R EWARDS AS C ONVERGENCE M ETRIC
1211
       Based on our experiments and findings from the multi-agent literature (Bowling, 2004), we observe
1212
       that average rewards offer a weaker measure of convergence compared to policy convergence in
1213
       multi-agent games. This implies that rewards can reach a target value even when the underlying
1214   policy is suboptimal. For example, in the Rock–paper–scissors game, the Nash equilibrium policy
1215   leads to nearly equal wins for both players, resulting in a total reward of zero. However, this same
1216   reward can also be achieved if one player always wins while the other consistently loses, or if both
1217   players repeatedly select the same action, leading to a tie. As such, relying solely on rewards during
1218   training can be misleading.
1219
       Figure 3 (top row) depicts a case with the baseline where, despite rewards converging during training,
1220   the agents ultimately learned to play the same action repeatedly, resulting in ties. Although this
1221   matched the expected reward, it falls far short of equilibrium and leaves the agents vulnerable to
1222   exploitation by more skilled opponents. In contrast, the same figure shows results from LA-MADDPG
1223   under the same experimental conditions. Notably, while the rewards did not fully converge, the agents
1224   learned a near-optimal policy during evaluation, alternating between all three actions as expected.
1225   These results also align with the findings shown in Figure 1a.
1226   We explored the use of gradient norms as a potential metric in these scenarios but found them to
1227   be of limited utility, as they provided no clear indication of convergence for either method. We
1228   include those results in Figure 11, where we compare the gradient norms of Adam and LA across the
1229   networks of different players.
1230
       This work highlights the need for more robust evaluation metrics in multi-agent reinforcement
1231
       learning, a point also emphasized in (Lanctot et al., 2023), as reward-based metrics alone may be
1232   inadequate, particularly in situations where the true equilibrium is unknown.
