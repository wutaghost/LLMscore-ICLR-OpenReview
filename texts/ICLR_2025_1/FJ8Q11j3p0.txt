009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Despite the significant advancements in Deep Reinforcement Learning (RL) ob-
               served in the last decade, the amount of training experience necessary to learn
013
               effective policies remains one of the primary concerns both in simulated and real
014
               environments. Looking to solve this issue, previous work has shown that improved
015            training efficiency can be achieved by separately modeling agent and environment,
016            but usually requiring a supervisory agent mask. In contrast to RL, humans can
017            perfect a new skill from a very small number of trials and in most cases do so
018            without a supervisory signal, making neuroscientific studies of human development
019            a valuable source of inspiration for RL. In particular, we explore the idea of motor
020            prediction, which states that humans develop an internal model of themselves and
021            of the consequences that their motor commands have on the immediate sensory
022            inputs. Our insight is that the movement of the agent provides a cue that allows
023
               the duality between agent and environment to be learned. To instantiate this idea,
               we present Ego-Foresight, a self-supervised method for disentangling agent and
024
               environment based on motion and prediction. Our main finding is that visuomotor
025
               prediction of the agent provides good feature representations for the underlying
026            RL algorithm. To test our approach, we integrate Ego-Foresight with a model-free
027            RL algorithm to solve simulated robotic manipulation tasks, showing its ability to
028            improve efficiency and performance in different tasks while making strides towards
029            real-world RL applications, by removing the need for costly supervisory signals.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      While it usually goes unnoticed as we go about our daily lives, the human brain is constantly engaged
036   in predicting imminent future sensori inputs (Clark, 2015). This happens when we react to a friend
037   extending their arm for a handshake, when we notice a missing note in our favorite song, and when
038   we perceive movement in optical illusions (Watanabe et al., 2018). At a more fundamental level,
039   this process of predicting our sensations is seen by some as the driving force behind perception,
040   action, and learning (Friston, 2010). But while predicting external phenomena is a daunting task
041   for a brain, motor prediction (Wolpert & Flanagan, 2001) - i.e. predicting the sensori consequences
042   of one’s own movement - is remarkably more simple, yet equally important. Anyone who has ever
043   tried to self-tickle has experienced the brain in its predictive endeavors. In trying to predict external
044
      (and more critical) inputs, the brain is thought to suppress self-generated sensations, to increase the
      saliency of those coming from outside - making it hard to feel self-tickling (Blakemore et al., 2000).
045
046   In artificial systems, a significant effort has been devoted to learning World Models (Ha & Schmidhu-
047   ber (2018); Finn et al. (2016); Gumbsch et al. (2024)), which are designed to predict future states of
048   the whole environment and allow planning in the latent space. Despite encouraging deployments of
049   these models in real-world robotic learning (Wu et al., 2023), their application remains constrained to
050
      safe and simplified workspaces, with sample-efficient Deep being one of the main challenges.
051   Though comparatively less explored, the idea of separately modeling the agent and the environment
052   has also been investigated in RL, with previous work demonstrating improved sample-efficiency in
053   simulated robotic manipulation tasks (Gmelin et al., 2023). Additionally, this type of approach has
      been used to allow zero-shot policy transfer between different robots (Hu et al., 2022) and to improve

072   In our approach, which we name Ego-Foresight (EF), in alternative to some recent lines of re-
073   search (Padalkar et al., 2023), we place the agent’s embodiment as an intrinsic part of the learning
074   process, since it determines the visuomotor sensations that the agent can expect as it moves. Our
075   insight is that agent-environment disentanglement can be achieved by having the agent move while
076   trying to predict the visual changes to its body configuration; and that awareness and prediction of
077   the agent’s movements should improve its ability to solve complex tasks, as exemplified in Figure 1.
078   To implement this, we use an encoder-decoder model, that receives as input a limited amount of
079   context RGB frames and the future sequence of proprioceptive states (sense of self-position, e.g. joint
080   angles) that will result from the planned action sequence. The decoder outputs the RGB reconstruction
081   corresponding to the proprioceptive signal, with the agent in its future configuration. This framework
082   naturally lends itself to self-supervised training, by having the system predict future frames.
083   To test the quality of the learned representations, we combine our architecture with a model-free
084   RL algorithm and assess its performance on multiple simulated domains and on different robotic
085   manipulation and locomotion tasks, demonstrating that our approach can improve sample efficiency
086   and performance. We consider that the factors contributing to this result that the disentanglement
087   between agent and environment allows the RL algorithm to focus its capacity on learning the control
088   of the agent in the initial stages of training, and later on the external aspects and potential interactions
089   within the environment. Our approach combines concepts of model-based RL, in learning a model of
090   the agent, while i) avoiding the need to learn a transition function, and ii) requiring prediction only at
091
      training time, for feature learning. In summary, the key contributions of this paper are the following:

101   2       R ELATED W ORK AND BACKGROUND
102
103   Learning agent representations The notion of distinguishing self-generated sensations from those
104   caused by external factors has been studied and referred to under different terms, and with a broad
105   range of applications as motivation. Originating in psychology and neuroscience, with the study of
106   contingency awareness (Watson, 1966) and of sensorimotor learning (motor prediction) (Wolpert
107

108
      et al. (2011); Wolpert & Flanagan (2001)), in the last few years this concept has seen growing interest
109   in AI, as an auxiliary mechanism for learning.
110
111   In developmental robotics, Zhang & Nagai (2018) have focused on this problem from the standpoint
112
      of self-other distinction, by employing 8 NAO robots observing each other executing a set of motion
      primitives, and trying to differentiate self from the learned representations. Lanillos et al. (2020)
113
      note that to answer the question ”Is this my body?”, an agent should first learn to answer ”Am I
114
      generating those effects in the world”. Their robot learns the expected changes in the visual field as
115   it moves in front of a mirror or in front of a twin robot and classifies whether it is looking at itself
116   or not. Our approach is somewhat analogous to these works, in the sense that we identify as being
117   part of the agent that which can be visually predicted from the future proprioception states, while the
118   robot moves. In a related, but reverse direction, Wilkins & Stathis (2023) propose the act of doing
119   nothing as a means to distinguish self-generated from externally-generated sensations.
120
      Still in robotics, the idea of modeling the agent has connections with work in body perception and
121   visual imagination for goal-driven behaviour (Sancaktar et al., 2020) as well as self-recognition by
122   discovery of controllable points (Edsinger & Kemp (2006); Yang et al. (2020)). Another application
123   that has been explored is the learning of modular dynamic models, that decouple robot dynamics
124   from world dynamics, allowing the latter to be reused between robots with different morphologies.
125   Hu et al. (2022) propose a method for zero-shot policy transfer between robots, by taking advantage
126   of robot-specific information - such as the CAD model - to obtain a robot mask from which future
127   robot states can be predicted, given its dynamics. These future states are then used solve manipulation
128   tasks using model-based RL. Finally, this concept has also been used for ignoring changes in the
129
      robot as a means for measuring environment change, with the intention of incentivizing exploration
      in real household environments (Mendonca et al., 2023).
130
131   In machine learning (ML), the distinction between the agent and environment has been studied under
132   the umbrella of disentangled representations, a long-standing problem in ML (Bengio, 2013). While
133   most works take an information theoretic approach to disentangled representation learning (Higgins
134   et al. (2017); Kim & Mnih (2018); Chen et al. (2016)), some try to take advantage of known structural
135
      biases in the data, which is particularly relevant for sequential data, as it usually contains both time-
      variant and invariant features (Wiskott & Sejnowski, 2002). In video, this allows the disentanglement
136
      of content and motion (Villegas et al., 2017). Denton & Birodkar (2017) explore the insight that some
137
      factors are mostly constant throughout a video, while others remain consistent between videos but can
138   change over time to disentangle content and pose. The scene encoder of our method is based on the
139   model of Denton and Birodkar, but while they use an adversarial loss to model pose as a property that
140   doesn’t depend on the video, we take advantage of the notion of agency and use the proprioceptive
141   signal to model the visuomotor mapping with a simple reconstruction loss.
142
      Finally, agent-environment disentanglement can also be achieved through attention mechanisms (Choi
143
      et al., 2018) or from explicit supervision, as shown by Gmelin et al. (2023) with SEAR. In this
144   work, the authors demonstrate that learning this distinction using a supervisory mask of the agent
145   allows RL algorithms to achieve better sample-efficiency and performance, serving as the most direct
146   baseline for our work. Still, while in simulation this mask may be inexpensive to obtain, in real
147   world scenarios it requires training a dedicated segmentation model and manually labeling ground
148   truth images for supervision, for each new robot that is tested, reducing the real-world applicability
149   of SEAR. One final related line of research is the learning of representations that are invariant to
150   task-irrelevant information, either via Bisimulation Zhang et al. (2021) or by denoising distractors
151   using an explicit factorization of the representation (Fu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022)).
152
153   RL from Images RL problems are typically formulated as Markov Decision Problems, defined
154   as a tuple (S, A, T , R, γ, d0 ), where S is the state space, A is the action space, T (st+1 |st , at ) is
155   the transition function, R(st , at ) is the reward function, γ ∈ [0, 1] is a discount factor and d0 is
156   the distribution over initial states s0 . The objective in RL Pis to learn the policy π : S → A that
                                                                        ∞
157   maximizes the expected discounted cumulative reward Eπ [ t=0 γ t R(st , at )], with at ∼ π(·|st )
158
      and st+1 ∼ T (·|st , at ).
159   Over the last decade, work in Deep RL from images, where environment representations are learned
160   from high-dimensional inputs, has allowed RL agents to solve problems for which features cannot
161   be designed by experts. Since the first demonstration of human-level performance being achieved
      on arcade games (Mnih et al., 2013), numerous algorithmic improvements have been introduced.

195   Controllable video generation and prediction In learning to generate future frames conditioned

203
      model is significantly lighter, we believe the design principle of predicting the agent could be scaled

204
      higher capacity models. In appendix A.1 We show experiments on the BAIR dataset, with generation

      Environments We test our approach in 3 different environments, starting with the DeepMind
303
      Control Suite (DMC) (Tassa et al., 2018), a widely used benchmark for continuous control. Set in an
304
      empty and open world, it consists of a range of physical control tasks, such as locomotion, involving
305   a variety of agents, with different body configurations and number of degrees of freedom, allowing us
306   to test the generality of our approach to different embodiments and robots. The second environment
307   we test is the Distacting Control Suite (Stone et al., 2021), a variant of DMC which replaces the
308   background of each sequence with a randomly chosen image, allowing a better assessment of the
309   robustness of RL algorithms to the visual complexity of the real world, something the authors
310   demonstrate is a limitation of current methods. Finally, Meta-world is a simulated benchmark (Yu
311   et al., 2020) of robotic manipulation tasks with predefined reward functions. It presents a broad
312   distribution of tasks which require a diverse set of skills such as reaching, picking, placing, or
313
      inserting. We chose 10 that involve the manipulation of different objects. More details can be found
      in App. A.5. All environments are implemented using the Mujoco simulator (Todorov et al., 2012).
314

      Baselines We compare our model with three baselines: DrQ-v2 (Yarats et al., 2021a),
316
      SEAR (Gmelin et al., 2023), Dreamer-v3 (Hafner et al., 2023) and TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al.,
317
      2024), introduced in section 2. DrQ-v2 uses an encoder to embed the RGB frames into a feature
318   vector which models the full environment. SEAR expands on top of DrQ-v2, by splitting the feature
319   representation into agent and full environment information, which is achieved with a supervisory

321   viewed as an oracle baseline when compared to ours. In all experiments bellow, the supervisory
322   mask is obtained directly from the simulator. Finally, Dreamer-v3 is a model-based RL algorithm
323   which aims to improve sample-efficiency by learning from outcomes of actions which are imagined
      using a world model. The results of DrQ-v2 on DMC, and Dreamer-v3 and TD-MPC2 on DMC and

333   Architecture In our experiments, we chose an implementation of the scene encoder Es and the
334   decoder D based on the DCGAN (Radford et al., 2016), which consists of 5 convolutional layers,
335   each reducing (in Es ) or increasing (in D) the size of the feature map by half. To provide a sequence
336
      of context frames as input, we stack the frames along the channels dimension. Another change relative
      to DCGAN is including skip connections from the Es to the D, as done in the U-Net (Ronneberger
337
      et al., 2015). For the proprioception encoder, we use an LSTM preceded by 2 fully connected layers.
338
      More details can be found in A.3. We note that our choice of architecture is determined by the low
339   resolution of the RGB observations in the tested environments, which is at maximum 84 × 84 pixels.
340   Nevertheless, higher capacity architectures could be used to deal with higher dimensional inputs,
341   provided enough computational resources. We chose most hyperparameters to match those used in
342   SEAR (see A.5), including the size of the feature vectors. Additionally, for a fair comparison to the
343   baselines, we perform the motor-babbling stage (50k steps) from scratch for each task. In practice
344   however, it would suffice to go through this stage once per robot body (e.g. Meta-world’s Sawyer)
345   and then start learning the different tasks from a model pre-trained on babbling.
346
347   Metrics To compare our model against the baselines, we plot the mean episode reward for the
348   DMC and Distracting DMC benchmarks and the mean success rate for Metaworld, both as a function
349   of environment steps. Environment steps represent the number of times the environment is updated
350   according to an action. Because environment updates incur a significant computational cost, they
351
      are the preferred way of reporting sample-efficiency in the literature (Yarats et al. (2021a), Hafner
      et al. (2020)). Each curve presents the mean over 10 evaluation episodes, taken every 5k steps.
352
      Furthermore, in trying to reduce the instability of DDPG, we also average the performance over
353
      multiple random seeds and display the standard deviation in the shaded area of the plot.

357   DeepMind Control Suite We test our model on 4 different tasks of the DMC Suite (Figure 4),
358   using the Cartpole, Walker, Cheetah and Quadruped agents, which have 2, 7, 8 and 16 degrees of
359   freedom respectively. For every task we match or improve the performance of the baselines. We note
360   that the greater variability in our results is due to a lower number repetitions with different random
361   seeds. The results of EF in terms of sample-efficiency are also competitive with the baselines, with
362   the exception of the Walker task. Finally because during the babbling stage the agent is not acting
363   towards the maximization of the reward, the first 50k steps of the Cartpole task perform worse than
364   the baselines but, once this stage is over, the agent quickly solves the task. A noteworthy aspect of
365
      this experiment is the fact that the greater the complexity of the agent in terms of degrees of freedom,
      the more advantage our approach offers, with the Quadruped experiment being the one in which it

      5    D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
518
519
520
      In this work, we studied how motion can be used to disentangle agent and environment in a self-
      supervised manner. We integrate our approach with an RL algorithm and evaluate the effect of the
521
      learned feature representations on RL physical control and manipulation tasks solved in simulation.
522
      Our results show that in most tasks, our approach matches the performance of the best baselines,
523   and sometimes achieves better results. By removing the need for supervision while preserving
524   sample-efficiency, our method can be seen as a step towards model-free RL in real-world settings.
525   Some limitations of our work include the assumption that there are no big changes in the environment
526   inside the prediction horizon and the need for pixel-wise reconstruction of the scene, for which
527   different approaches such as a contrastive loss could be explored in the future. The fixed babbling
528   stage is another limitation, which should be made adaptive. Still, it opens up the possibility of reusing
529   a model pre-trained on babbling to improve sample-efficiency on multi-task learning. Finally, our
530   approach is comparable to SEAR in terms of wall-clock efficiency, but more expensive than DrQ-v2.
531
      However, we consider that for future work aiming at training RL methods directly on real-world
      robots, the main bottleneck lies in the number of episodes that can be obtained, whereas the learning
532
      algorithm only needs to run at least at the frequency of the robot’s control loop. For this reason we
533
      focus on sample-efficiency. Our results also indicate that one benefit of self-supervision is adaption
534   to a changing body-schema, in particular when the agent uses tools. In future research, we intend to
535   explore how fast this body-schema adaptation can be done, and whether performance on tasks that
536   require tools can benefit from it. Finally, we intend to apply our method to domains outside robotics,
537   such as autonomous driving, where the main difference is that the actions of the agent control the
538   optical flow of the observed world and not the configuration of the agent’s body. Here, our method
539   can additionally be used as a regularizing loss term for encouraging action that lead to predictable
      states, a beneficial feature in automated driving.

760   BAIR Dataset Commonly used in video prediction research, the BAIR dataset (Ebert et al., 2017)
761   consists of a Sawyer robot arm randomly moving and pushing a broad range of objects inside a
762   confined table. The set of objects and their disposition changes from video to video, as well as the
763   lighting conditions, providing some variability. It includes 43520 video samples, each 30 frames
764   long, with 64 × 64 resolution. each sequence includes the robot’s actions, defined as a 7D array of
765   Cartesian displacement and position of the end-effector and the gripper open/close state. The model
766   is trained by receiving 2 context frames and predicting the following 10. At test time, the prediction
767   horizon is extended to 28, with the context length staying the same. The proprioception is defined as
768
      the concatenation of the robot’s action and the gripper state.
769
770   Results In Figure 10, we show two sample predictions from EF on the BAIR dataset. The model
771
      succeeds in separating visual information that is part of the robot from the scene, predicting the
      trajectory of the robot’s arm according to its true motion, which shows that the model correctly
772
      learned the visuomotor map between proprioception and vision. The background, including moving
773
      objects, is reconstructed in their original position. Even so, our model still predicts that some change
774   should happen when the arm passes by an object, and it often blurs objects that predictably would
775   have moved. Nevertheless, it should be noted that the agent’s actions and object movement are
776   inherently correlated and therefore can’t be completely disentangled.
777
      To determine whether EF could be adapted to be used with model-based planning algorithms - that
778
      work by imagining the expected outcome of multiple different trajectories in parallel - we evaluate its
779
      ability to generalize to previously unseen trajectories. To achieve this, we train EF on BAIR dataset
780   using only the Cartesian position of the gripper as proprioception. This allows us to handcraft artificial
781   and previously unseen movements, as shown in Figure 11. While we display a single handcrafted
782   example, this simple experiment shows that, provided a policy function, the dynamics learned by EF
783   should manage to predict the outcome of sampled trajectories.

835   presented in Figure 13 show that even with the added proprioception state input, EF still remains
836   competitive or improves upon the baselines.

838   A.3    D ETAILED A RCHITECTURE
839
840   The scene encoder Es is a convolutional neural network with 5 layers. On all layers, we use kernel
841   size 4 and stride 2, except for layer 5 which has stride 1. Each layer uses Batch Normalization and
842   leaky ReLU activations. The number of filters in each layer is fixed at 32. The last layer has number
843   of filters corresponding to the size of hs and a hyperbolic tangent activation. The decoder is a mirror
844   of the encoder, with input size corresponding to the sum of the sizes of hs and hs . Additionally, skip
845
      connections are added between each Conv. layer of Es and the corresponding layer of the decoder.
      The output activation of the decoder is a sigmoid function. For the proprioception encoder we use a
846
      two layer fully connected neural network with leaky ReLU activations, followed by an LSTM, each
847
      with 256 units. After the LSTM and additional fully connected layer maps the size of the LSTM’s
848   output to the size of hp . The size of the feature vectors is chosen to be close to the baselines. Finally,
849   the list of hyperparameters used in the DMC, Distracting Control and Meta-world experiments is
850   presented in Table 2.

905
906   Meta-world is a simulated benchmark of robot manipulation tasks, designed for meta-learning and
907   multi-task learning, including more than 50 different tasks. As in BAIR, the agent is a Sawyer robot,
908
      but while for BAIR the we use an FPV camera angle, for Meta-world we opt to use a third-person
      view, as done in (Gmelin et al., 2023). While this means that the number of degrees of freedom that
909
      can be observed is higher (the full 9 DoF of the Sawyer), we verify that the model still manages
910
      to predict the agent’s movement. For the proprioceptive state, we use the 9D array of the robot’s
911   joint angles (7 arm joints and 2 fingers) and the 4D end-effector state, which includes the Cartesian
912   position of the gripper and the distance between the fingers. After each for episode the locations of
913   the targets are slightly varied.
