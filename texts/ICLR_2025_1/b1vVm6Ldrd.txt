011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In the social world, humans possess the capability to infer and reason about oth-
014            ers’ mental states (such as emotions, beliefs, and intentions), known as the Theory
015            of Mind (ToM). Simultaneously, humans’ own mental states evolve in response
016
               to social situations, a capability we refer to as socialization. Together, these ca-
               pabilities form the foundation of human social interaction. In the era of artifi-
017
               cial intelligence (AI), especially with the development of large language models
018
               (LLMs), we raise an intriguing question: How do LLMs perform in terms of ToM
019            and socialization capabilities? And more broadly, can these AI models truly en-
020            ter and navigate the real social world? Existing research evaluating LLMs’ ToM
021            and socialization capabilities by positioning LLMs as passive observers from a
022            third-person perspective, rather than as active participants. However, compared
023            to the third-person perspective, observing and understanding the world from an
024            ego-centric first-person perspective is a natural approach for both humans and AI
025            agents. The ToM and socialization capabilities of LLMs from a first-person per-
026            spective, a crucial attribute for advancing embodied AI agents, remain unexplored.
027
               To answer the aforementioned questions and bridge the research gap, we intro-
               duce EgoSocialArena, a novel framework designed to evaluate and investigate the
028
               ToM and socialization capabilities of LLMs from a first-person perspective. It
029
               encompasses two evaluation environments: static environment and interactive en-
030            vironment, with seven scenarios: Daily Life, Counterfactual, New World, Black-
031            jack, Number Guessing, and Limit Texas Hold’em, totaling 2,195 data entries.
032            With EgoSocialArena, we have conducted a comprehensive evaluation of nine ad-
033            vanced LLMs and observed some key insights regarding the future development
034            of LLMs as well as the capabilities levels of the most advanced LLMs currently
035            available.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040   In the complex social interactions of humans, two fundamental cognitive capabilities play crucial
041   roles: Theory of Mind (ToM) and Socialization. ToM is a fundamental psychological process,
042   defined as the capacity to reason about others’ mental states – beliefs, intents, desires, emotions,
043   knowledge, etc (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Ma et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Socialization
044
      refers to the capability for own mental state evolution in response to social situations. As illustrated
      in Figure 1(A), When the little boy receives a birthday gift or achieves good grades, he feels very
045
      happy. These intertwined capabilities are central to humans’ social life.
046
047   With the advent of the era of LLMs, powerful models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Claude
048   (Anthropic, 2024) have demonstrated remarkable competence in multiple tasks and domains. It
049   raises intriguing questions: How do LLMs perform in terms of ToM and socialization capabilities?
050
      And more broadly, can these AI models truly enter and navigate the real social world to achieve
      efficient human-computer interaction?
051

      2023), FanToM (Kim et al., 2023), HI-ToM (Wu et al., 2023), OpenToM (Xu et al., 2024), Negoti-

141   Existing ToM Benchmarks Previous evaluations for the ToM of LLMs primarily focus on testing
142   models using narrative stories, also referred to as reading comprehension scenarios. Specifically,
143   Le et al. (2019) proposed the ToMi benchmark based on the classic Sally-Anne test. Wu et al.
144   (2023) introduced the HI-ToM benchmark, which focuses on higher-order belief reasoning and sets
145   up scenarios where agents can communicate with each other. Gandhi et al. (2023) proposed Big-
146   ToM, which presents a framework for designing a ToM benchmark from synthetic templates for
147   evaluating different aspects of LLMs’ ToM capabilities (e.g., desire and belief). Xu et al. (2024)
148
      introduced OpenToM, which assigns personalities to agents in the stories and ensures that the story-
      lines are more reasonable and logical. Chen et al. (2024) proposed ToMBench, which systematically
149
      evaluates LLMs across all dimensions of ToM capabilities. Unlike the above methods that require
150
      LLMs to read stories and answer related questions, some studies evaluate LLMs’ performance by
151   inputting dialogues to them. Kim et al. (2023) proposed FanToM, which tests LLMs on their ability
152   to infer the mental states of characters in everyday conversations. Chan et al. (2024) introduced
153   NegotiationToM, which restricts the dialogue content to negotiation scenarios.
154
155
      Existing Socialization Benchmarks Sap et al. (2022) proposed SocialIQA and used it to evaluate
156   LLMs. SocialIQA contains many questions related to social commonsense. Ziems et al. (2023)
157   introduced NormBank, a large repository of social norms knowledge, which can be used to as-
158   sess social norm-related tasks. Li et al. (2024) reorganized and classified existing datasets related
159   to social intelligence. Xu et al. (2023) studied LLMs’ understanding of the world and explored
160   how different persuasion strategies could modify LLMs’ worldviews. Different from our work, the
161   aforementioned ToM and socialization benchmarks evaluate LLMs by placing them in a passive
      observer’s third-person perspective.

      Necessity of developing LLMs’ ToM and socialization capabilities Several studies (Gandhi
193
      et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023) have shown that LLMs have poor reasoning performance and ro-

      bustness on ToM and socialization tasks in a zero-shot setting, even with the current state-of-the-art

198   2023; Rabinowitz et al., 2018; Shu et al., 2021).

200   Ego-centric (First-person Perspective) Research In the fields of computer vision and robotics,
201   there has already been considerable research on a first-person perspective. For example, Cheng
202   et al. (2023) explored whether vision-language models can ”Think from a First-person Perspective?”
203   Huang et al. (2023) proposes the construction of embodied agents in a 3D world, which involves
204   acquiring and processing first-person perspective images. Huang et al. (2024) built a bridge between
205
      third-person and first-person perspectives at the action level, while Dou et al. (2024) proposed a
      method designed to transform exocentric video-language data for egocentric video representation
206
      learning. However, research on first-person perspectives in the field of natural language processing
207
      remains unexplored.

209
      LLMs for Interactive Scenarios Some work focuses on designing interaction strategies to en-
210   able LLMs to gain more benefits during interactions. For example, Zhang et al. (2024a) proposed
211   Agent-pro, Zhang et al. (2024b) introduced K-level reasoning, and Guo et al. (2023) put forward
212   the Suspicion-Agent. Additionally, Li et al. (2023) explored Multi-LLM collaboration by inform-
213   ing LLMs of task rules through prompts. Park et al. (2023) introduced generative agents that can
214   simulate human behavior. Bianchi et al. (2024) explored the social behavior of LLMs in negotiation
215   scenarios. Fu et al. (2023) show LLMs can improve each other in a negotiation scenario. Fan et al.
      (2024) examined the capability of LLMs to make rational decisions in game theoretic scenarios.

      EgoSocialArena provides a systematic approach to convert third-person perspective ToM bench-
219
      marks into first-person perspective, constructs Rule-based Agents at different cognitive levels and
220   Reinforcement Learning (RL) Agents, and includes various scenarios to evaluate LLMs’ ToM and
221   socialization capabilities from a first-person perspective.

      3.1    C ONVERTING E XISTING T HIRD - PERSON T O M B ENCHMARKS TO A F IRST- PERSON
224          P ERSPECTIVE
225
226   Foundation and Inspiration In LLM-based Agent applications, system message serves as a crit-
227   ical component, functioning to pre-set the model’s role and background. As illustrated in Figure
228   2(A), system message ”You are name and live in a town...” is used. Interestingly, in the domain of
229   LLM self-awareness research (Laine et al., 2024), a similar linguistic construct is employed. As
230   illustrated in Figure 2(B), researchers employ the pronoun ”you” to probe LLMs’ potential self-
231
      awareness. Inspired by and building upon studies in these two domains, we systematically modify
      system message, story, question, and answer options to transform third-person ToM benchmarks into
232
      a first-person perspective.
233
234
      Conversion Method As illustrated in Figure 2(C), unlike instructing LLMs in system message that
235
      ”you are a helpful assistant.”, we inform LLMs in system message that they have personally experi-
236
      enced certain social events, similar to deploy LLM-based Agent. As illustrated in Figure 2(D), we
237   employ the pronoun ”you” to replace specific characters in stories and questions, thereby situating
238   LLMs within particular roles. This approach enables the models to experience social events from
239   a first-person perspective. The framing of questions is akin to that employed in self-awareness re-
240   search. For modifications to answer options, consider LLMs answer from a first-person perspective,
241   substituting ’I’ for specific character in the options. Higher-order ToM questions from third-person
242   perspective benchmarks, after conversion, are still used to evaluate the ToM capabilities of LLMs
243   from a first-person perspective. In contrast, first-order ToM questions, after conversion, form an
244   assessment of LLMs’ mental states following social events, which we include in the scope of evalu-
245
      ating socialization capabilities.

247   3.2    I NTRIGUING AND D ISTINCTIVE S OCIAL S ITUATIONS
248
      As illustrated in Figure 3, we design three particularly interesting scenarios—Counterfactual, New
249
      World, and Blackjack—all used to evaluate the socialization capabilities of LLMs.
250
251
      Counterfactual In real social situations, the rules of Rock-Paper-Scissors are: rock beats scissors,
252
      scissors beat paper, and paper beats rock. An LLM can relatively easily establish a belief based on
253
      this situation. In contrast, we define a counterfactual situation for the Rock-Paper-Scissors game
254   (scissors beat rock, paper beats scissors, and rock beats paper) to explore whether an LLM can
255   establish a belief that matches this counterfactual situation.
256
257   New World We design stories that describe new social world scenarios that are significantly dif-
258   ferent from the current social world. We aim to investigate whether LLMs can demonstrate cognitive
259   flexibility and achieve substantive shifts in their understanding and reasoning about the social world.
260
261   Blackjack In the single-turn card game scenario, it is necessary to analyze the game situation,
262   i.e., analyze own cards in Self-belief and the opponent’s cards in External-belief. This forms a
263   comprehensive mental estimation of the current game situation. We experiment with the blackjack
264   card game, and its specific rules can be found in A.1.

350
      The conversion of the third-person perspective ToM benchmark to the first-person perspective is
351   achieved through GPT-4o, followed by manual verification and correction. The game hands for
352   Limit Texas Hold’em and Blackjack card games are generated by RLcard (Zha et al., 2019). Addi-
353   tionally, we manually construct scenarios for both the new world and counterfactual situations. After
354   the data collection, following Chen et al. (2024)’s method, we conduct two rounds of validation to
355   ensure the data’s correctness and quality. In 1st round, author A would first complete all samples
356   created by author B. For stories, questions, and answer options where there are disagreements, au-
357   thors A and B would discuss and modify them to reach a consensus as much as possible. In 2nd
358   round, for samples where consensus is still not reached, another author C would discuss with authors
359
      A and B to determine the final answer. After two rounds of discussion, the final average agreement
      reaches 97.6%.

      6     C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, considering the limitations of existing ToM and socialization benchmarks, the im-
525
      portance of first-person ToM and socialization capabilities in LLMs, and the natural approach of
526   observing and understanding the world from an ego-centric first-person perspective for both humans
527   and AI agents, we propose the EgoSocialArena framework. This framework is designed to compre-
528   hensively evaluate and probe the first-person ToM and socialization capabilities of LLMs in both
529   static and interactive environments, covering multiple scenarios. To avoid the ”babysitting” prob-
530   lem during the evaluation process and achieve fair and comprehensive assessments, we construct
531   rule-based agents at different cognitive levels and train RL agents. We collect a total of 2195 test
532   data entries and test multiple advanced and popular LLMs, revealing some interesting and key in-
533   sights, and highlighting a significant potential for enhancing the first-person ToM and socialization
534
      capabilities of LLMs, allowing them to truly enter the social world.

652   Blackjack Blackjack, also known as 21, is a popular card game that involves a dealer and a player.
653   Players must decide whether to hit or stand based on their own hand, the dealer’s face-up card, and
654   the dealer’s one hidden card. The objective is to beat the dealer without exceeding 21 points.
655
656   Limit Texas Hold’em The game commences with each player being dealt two private cards, which
657   belong exclusively to the player and remain hidden from the others. Five community cards are then
658   dealt face-up in a series of stages: a three-card Flop, followed by a single card on the Turn and
659
      another single card on the River. The player can choose from four actions: Fold, Check, Call, Raise.
      They aim to construct the best five-card poker hand possible using any combination of their private
660
      cards and community cards.
661
662
      Number Guessing Each player selects a number between 1 to 100. The objective is to select a
663
      number that is closest to 80% of the group’s average choice. The key idea is to guess how others
664   will estimate the average and thus decide the number to submit.
