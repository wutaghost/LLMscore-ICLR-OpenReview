                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Watermarking has recently emerged as an effective strategy for detecting the
010
               outputs of large language models (LLMs). Most existing schemes require white-
011            box access to the model’s next-token probability distribution, which is typically
012            not accessible to downstream users of an LLM API. In this work, we propose a
013            principled watermarking scheme that requires only the ability to sample sequences
014            from the LLM (i.e. black-box access), boasts a distortion-free property, and can be
015            chained or nested using multiple secret keys. We provide performance guarantees,
016            demonstrate how it can be leveraged when white-box access is available, and
017            show when it can outperform existing white-box schemes via comprehensive
018            experiments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
021
022
      It can be critical to understand whether a piece of text is generated by a large language model (LLM).
023   For instance, one often wants to know how trustworthy a piece of text is, and those written by an
024   LLM may be deemed untrustworthy as these models can hallucinate. This problem comes in different
025   flavors – one may want to detect whether it was generated by a specific model or by any model.
026   Furthermore, the detecting party may or may not have white-box access (e.g. an ability to compute
027   log-probabilities) to the generator they wish to test against. Typically, parties that have white-box
028   access are the owners of the model so we refer to this case as first-party detection and the counterpart
029   as third-party detection.
030   The goal of watermarking is to cleverly bias the generator so that first-party detection becomes
031   easier. Most proposed techniques do not modify the underlying LLM’s model weights or its training
032   procedure but rather inject the watermark during autoregressive decoding at inference time. They
033   require access to the next-token logits and inject the watermark every step of the sampling loop.
034   This required access prevents third-party users of an LLM from applying their own watermark as
035   proprietary APIs currently do not support this option. Supporting this functionality presents a security
036   risk in addition to significant engineering considerations. Concretely, Carlini et al. (2024) showed
037
      that parts of a production language model can be stolen from API access that exposes logits. In this
      work, we propose a watermarking scheme that gives power back to the people — third-party users
038
      can watermark a language model given nothing more than the ability to sample sequences from it.
039
      Our scheme is faithful to the underlying language model and it can outperform existing white-box
040   schemes.

      2    R ELATED W ORK

      We cover related work more extensively in the Appendix; we give a brief overview here. Watermarking
      in the context of generative language models is a relatively new field, building on prior work in
046
      linguistic steganography, where specific words in text are altered to encode information. Early
047
      schemes, such as Venugopal et al. (2011), focused on machine translation, but interest surged with
048   the works of Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a;b) and Aaronson (2023), which introduced watermarking
049   for LLMs. These schemes, while effective, can introduce some text distortion, though efforts like
050   Aaronson (2023) and Kuditipudi et al. (2023) seek to make watermarking distortion-free. Other
051   works, such as Lee et al. (2023) and Zhao et al. (2023), adapt these methods for specific tasks or
052   to improve resistance to adversarial attacks, while Fernandez et al. (2023) explores new detection
053   tests. Black-box watermarking methods like those by Yang et al. (2023) and Chang et al. (2024)
      attempt synonym substitution or word insertion but face challenges with text distortion. Paraphrasing

055   semantic-based approaches (Liu et al., 2023b; Hou et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023; Yoo et al., 2023).

064   High-level sketch. At a high level, our scheme operates autoregressively; each step, we sample
065   multiple generations from the LLM, score each with our secret key, and output the highest scoring
066   one. We do this repeatedly until our stopping condition (e.g. reaching the stop-token or the max
067   length) is met. To determine whether a piece of text was watermarked, we score it using our key — if
068   it’s high, it’s likely watermarked. We now describe the algorithm more formally.

149
      Theorem 4.1 tells us that sampling tokens using our proposed scheme is, from a probabilistic
150   perspective, indistinguishable from sampling from the underlying model, with the caveat that the
151   unique seed values are conditionally independent given the counts of sequences. If we dismiss hash
152   collisions as very low probability events, then since the key is fixed, this reduces to the assumption
153   that unique n-grams across the sampled sequences are independent. How strong of an assumption
154   this is depends on many factors such as m, the underlying distribution, and the counts (c1 , . . . , cj )
155   themselves. One can construct cases where the assumption is reasonable and others where it is
156   blatantly violated (e.g. if n-grams within a sequence are strongly correlated). One direction to making
157   the assumption more palatable is to draw a fresh keys i.i.d. for each hash call. This would obviously
158
      destroy detectability. As a trade-off, one can leverage a set of secret keys (i.e. by drawing keys
      uniformly at random from a key set), which may reduce distortion, but will hurt detection as each key
159
      in the set needs to be tested against.

253   5.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
254
      We evaluate performance using three criteria.
255
256   Detectability. How well can we discriminate between non-watermarked and watermarked text? We
257   choose non-watermarked text to be text generated by the same model, just without watermarking
258   applied during decoding. There are three reasons for choosing the negative class in this way. Firstly, it
259
      makes controlling for text length easier as we can generate as many tokens as we do for watermarked
      samples — in contrast, human responses are of varying lengths. Secondly, watermarked text has
260
      far more token / n-gram overlap with its non-watermarked counterpart than the human reference,
261
      which makes detection more challenging. Lastly, since one intended use case of our scheme is for
262   third-party users of a shared LLM service, users may want to distinguish between their watermarked
263   text and non-watermarked text generated by the same LLM service.
264
265
      Our primary one-number metric is ROC-AUC for this balanced binary classification task. Since
      performance at low FPR is often more useful in practice, we report the partial ROC-AUC (pAUC)

      Distortion. Our scheme, along with most of the baselines, boasts a distortion-free property. This
276
      property comes with assumptions that are often violated in practice, for example by reuse of the
277   secret key across watermarking calls. We quantify how faithful the watermarking procedure is to the
278   underlying generative model by computing both the perplexity and likelihood of watermarked text
279   under the generator (without watermarking). We include likelihood as the log-probabilities used in
280   calculating perplexity can over-emphasize outliers.
281
      Quality. Watermarking may distort the text per the model, but does the distortion tangibly affect
282
      the quality of the text? Quality can be challenging to define and measure — one proxy is likelihood
283   under a much larger model than the generator. Alternatively, one can run standard benchmark NLP
284   tasks and use classic metrics like exact match, etc. We instead opt for using Gemini-1.5-Pro as an
285   LLM judge and compute pairwise win rates for each watermark strategy against no watermarking
286   (greedy decoding). We do this in two ways for each scheme — (1) we compute win rates using a
287   single response for each prompt and (2) we first ask the LLM judge to pick the best of 3 responses for
288   each prompt and compute win rates using the best response. (2) represents the common practice of
289   sampling a few generations from the LLM and selecting the best one using some criterion. It captures
290   diversity, as methods that can express an answer in a few different good ways will have an advantage.
291
      A caveat with win rates is that they may not reflect the degree by which one method is better or worse.
      For instance, if one strategy’s output was always marginally worse than no watermarking, the win
292
      rate would be 0% — the same as if it were much worse.

295   5.3   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
296
297   An adversary in possession of watermarked text (but who lacks knowledge of the secret key) may
298   try to evade detection. We study how detectability degrades under two attack strategies —- random
299   token replacement and paraphrasing.
300
      Random token replacement. Here, we take the watermarked tokens and a random p-percent them
301   are corrupted by replacing their token with a random different one. p is taken to be [10, 20, 30, 40, 50].
302   This attack strategy is cheap for the adversary to carry out but will significantly degrade the quality of
303   the text.
304
      Paraphrasing. In this attack, the adversary attempts to evade detection by paraphrasing the water-
305
      marked text using the model. We use Gemini-1.5-Pro to paraphrase each non-truncated watermarked
306
      generation. Details are deferred to the Appendix.

      matched on perplexity, we achieve better detection. For example, δ = 0.5 achieves 3.39 PPL and

454
      with k = 1, m = 1024 outperforms Kuditipudi et al. (2023) and is on-par with Aaronson (2023) (see

      Appendix). Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) with δ = 0.5 gives 1.649 PPL and 61.6% AUC whereas
456   k = 1, m = 1024 gets us 1.610 PPL with 93.2% AUC and even 1.645 PPL with 89.7% AUC when
457   k = 50, m = 16 (black-box).

464   5.5.2    E FFECTS OF H YPERPARAMETERS
465
466   Increasing m improves perplexity but hurts diversity. Across k’s, we observe that perplexity
467   decreases as m increases, but that win rates, especially when best-of-3 generations are used, decrease.
468   For example, when k = 1, increasing m from 2 to 1024 decreases perplexity from 3.46 to 2.61 but
469   also drops the best-of-3 win rate from 66.4% to 62.2%. As remarked earlier, as m → ∞, ct /m → pt
470
      and our scheme becomes less diverse — deterministic conditioned on the prompt, like Aaronson’s.
      On the flip side, large m reduces sampling noise which drives down perplexity.
471
472   Increasing m improves detection but has diminishing returns. Across the board we see that
473   detection improves as m increases, but there are diminishing returns. For example, when k = 1,
474   our AUC increases from 90.2% to 95.8% as m goes from 2 to 4, but flattens out when m hits 16.
475   This corroborates our theoretical intuition from Theorem 4.2 which is further explored in Figure 4
476
      (Appendix).
477   For fixed m, increasing k hurts detection performance. For fixed m and target generation length
478   T , increasing k gives us fewer opportunities (fewer calls to WATERMARK S INGLE) to inject the
479   watermark signal, and detection consequently suffers. For example, when m = 32, AUC drops from
480   97.8% to 94.2% when k increases from 1 to 50.
481   U (0, 1) slightly outperforms alternative distributions. Flat distributions may offer better
482   robustness to attacks. In Table 4 (Appendix), we see that U (0, 1) fares comparably to N (0, 1)
483   and slightly outperforms χ22 both on detection and perplexity. For example, when k = 50, m = 2,
484   U (0, 1) and χ22 have AUCs of 69.6% and 68.1% respectively. Furthermore, we find evidence that
485   U (0, 1) offers better protection to attacks. For example, when k = 50, m = 32, the AUC for
      U (0, 1) (χ22 ) degrades from 94.2% (94.5%) to 85.5% (84.5%) in the presence of 10% random token

486
      corruption. We provide some intuition for why flat distributions like U (0, 1) may be more robust
487   than those with quickly decaying tails. Consider shaping the continuous F so it approaches Bern(p)
488   (i.e. f (x) ≈ (1 − p)δ(x) + pδ(x − 1)), where p is very small. Suppose k is large and m is small.
489   Then, the winning sequence Xi∗ will have extremely few (if any) of its Rj ’s equal to 1. If the text
490   is unmodified and these few n-grams are kept intact, we are fine, but if they are corrupted in an
491   attack, then the watermarking signal is effectively lost. In other words, flat distributions smear the
492   watermarking signal over more tokens than do sharper distributions, which localize the signal to
493   few lucky token positions. However, whereas scoring with FT = IrwinHall(T ) when F = U (0, 1)
494   involves computing T -fold convolutions or cardinal   B-splines,  when F = N (0, 1), FT is easier to
                                                               √
495   compute for very large T ; specifically, FT (x) = F x/ T .

497   5.5.3   O BSERVATIONS ON DETECTION
498
499   Length correction of Aaronson (2023) is crucial. Recall that the ROC-AUCs presented in Table 1
500   are computed over a pool of different lengths. Our p-value-based score for Aaronson (2023) improves
501
      detection significantly; for example, AUC goes from 71.7% to 97.9%. Table 7 (Appendix) shows
      that the sum-based p-value correction fares a bit worse, which was a little surprisingly given that this
502
      worked the best for our scheme, even for the k = 1 case.
503
504   Sum-based p-values outperform Fisher ones. In Table 2 (Appendix), we observe that replacing our
                                           PT
505   sum-based p-value (where H0 is that i ri ∼ FT ) by a Fisher combination of token-level p-values,
506   hurts detection performance. For example, when k = 1, m = 2, AUC degrades from 90.2% to 86.3%.
507   Note that when k = 1, this setting corresponds exactly to a stochastic version of Aaronson (2023).
508   Likelihood-ratio scoring does well for large k and small m, when its assumptions are more
509   realistic. In Table 3 (Appendix), we observe that likelihood-based scoring — both when the
510   distribution is Gamma and the exact likelihood ratio test (LRT) is used and under KDE with alternative
511   distributions — performs the best when the assumptions of no duplicate sequences or n-grams hold
512   better. This happens when the sequences are long (large k) and when fewer sequences are sampled
513   (small m). For example, when k = 1, AUC degrades monotonically from 78.1% to 55.4% as m
514   increases from 2 to 1024. In contrast, AUC under the p-value-based scoring increases monotonically
515
      with m, from 90.2% to 97.7%. Larger m increases the number of duplicate sequences sampled,
      increasing the importance of the latent exponent m/ci used in the scoring and deviating us further
516
      from the LRT assumptions. However, LRT has the potential to be an effective alternative when k
517
      is large. For example, when k = 50 and m = 32, Uniform KDE-based LRT gives AUC of 95.5%
518   compared to p-value’s 94.2%.
519
520   Detection performance improves sharply with test samples T . Figure 1 shows the effect of T on
521
      AUC. We see sharp improvements w.r.t. to T , even when k is large and m is small, highlighting the
      power of more test samples to counteract a weaker watermark signal.
522
523   Entropy improves detection performance. In Figure 1 we bucket prompts based on the entropy
524   of their non-watermarked response and then look at detection AUC on samples in each bucket. As
525   we expect, detection improves when the prompts confer more entropy in the response. This trend is
526   more stark for our method.
527   Paraphrasing can be extremely effective at destroying watermarks. We observe that paraphrasing
528   can effectively erase the watermark as detection performance for most methods is near random.
529   Kuditipudi et al. (2023) and Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) with large δ do better on AUC (but not so
530   much on pAUC). Furthermore, in Figures 1 and 2 (Appendix) we observe that large amounts of
531   random token corruption hurts our scheme and Aaronson (2023)’s more than it that of Kirchenbauer
532   et al. (2023a) or Kuditipudi et al. (2023).

534   6    C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this work, we present a framework for watermarking language models that requires nothing more
537   than a way to sample from them. Our framework is general and extensible, supporting various
538   real world use-cases, including the setting where the next-token probabilities are in fact available.
539   We study its various components and the trade-offs that arise, provide formal guarantees for the
      theoretically-inclined as well as concrete recommendations for the practitioner.

699   A.2    E XTENSIONS
700
701   We now discuss extensions of our method. At its crux, the scheme samples sequences of text from a
      service, divides each unique sequence into a bag of units (namely n-grams) where each unit is scored


                                                           13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      using a PRF and the scores are combined in an order-agnostic way. The strength of the watermark
703   depends on the number of distinct units across the candidate sequences and the robustness depends
704   on how many of the units are kept intact after the attack. Although any symmetric monotone function
705   can be used instead of the simple summation of the PRNs for each unit, we do not see any compelling
706   reason to make our algorithm more general in this way. However, we briefly highlight some other
707   possible extensions.
708
      Beam search. Rather than drawing i.i.d. samples from the model, one can apply our watermark
709   selection to the sequences that arise from beam search, with the caveat that this would violate our
710   distortion-free property.
711
712
      Semantic watermarking. Rather than use n-grams, the watermarker can extract a set of meaningful
      semantic units for each sampled text. Robustness may be improved as these units will largely remain
713
      intact under an attack like paraphrasing. On the other hand, many of the sampled sequences will have
714
      the same meaning, so there may be a lot of duplicate units across the candidate sequences, which
715   would degrade the watermark strength.
716
717   Paraphrasing. Thus far, we assumed the service provides m draws from the LLM. If m is large, this
718
      can be prohibitively expensive. The resource-constrained may consider the following alternative:
      draw one sample from the LLM and feed it to a much cheaper paraphrasing model to generate m
719
      paraphrases. The downside is that there may be a lot of duplicate n-grams across the candidate set.
720
721
722   A.3   F ULL R ELATED W ORK
723
724   Watermarking outside of the context of generative LLMs, which is sometimes referred to as lin-
725   guistic steganography, has a long history and typically involves editing specific words from an
726   non-watermarked text. Watermarking in the modern era of generative models is nascent — Venugopal
727   et al. (2011) devised a scheme for machine translation, but interest in the topic grew substantially
728   after the more recent seminal works of Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a;b) and Aaronson (2023). Many
729
      effective strategies employ some form of pseudorandom functions (PRFs) and cryptographic hashes
      on token n-grams in the input text. Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) proposes modifying the next-token
730
      probabilities every step of decoding such that a particular subset of the vocabulary, referred to as
731
      green list tokens, known only to those privy to the secret key, are made more probable. Watermarked
732   text then is expected to have more green tokens than non-watermarked text and can be reliably
733   detected with a statistical test. The scheme distorts the text, but with the right hyper-parameters a
734   strong watermark may be embedded with minimal degradation in text quality.
735
      Meanwhile, Aaronson (2023) proposes a clever distortion-free strategy which selects the token that is
736
      both highly probable and that achieves a high PRF value. Kuditipudi et al. (2023) applies a scheme
737
      similar in spirit to Aaronson (2023) but to improve robustness to attacks, pseudorandom numbers
738   (PRNs) are determined by cycling through a fixed, pre-determined sequence of values called the key,
739   rather than by n-grams. They compute a p-value using a permutation test to determine if the text was
740   watermarked with that specific key.
741
      Lee et al. (2023) adapts Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a)’s scheme for code-generation by applying the
742
      watermark only at decoding steps that have sufficient entropy. Zhao et al. (2023) investigates a special
743
      case of Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) where n = 0 for improved robustness to adversarial corruption.
744   Fernandez et al. (2023) tests various watermarking schemes on classical NLP benchmarks and also
745   introduces new statistical tests for detection — most notably, they suggest skipping duplicate n-grams
746   during testing.
747
      Yang et al. (2023) introduces a scheme that relies on black-box access to the LLM. Their method
748
      samples from the LLM and injects the watermark by replacing specific words with synonyms.
749
      Although their approach shares the assumption of black-box LLM access, as in our work, it has
750   limitations not present in ours: the watermarking process is restricted to words that can easily be
751   substituted with multiple synonyms, synonym generation is powered by a BERT model (Devlin,
752   2018), making it computationally expensive, and the scheme is not distortion-free. Chang et al.
753   (2024) presents P OST M ARK, a black-box watermarking method that uses semantic embeddings to
754   identify an input-dependent set of words. These words are then inserted into the text by an LLM after
755   decoding. However, this approach is also not distortion-free, as the insertion of words by the LLM
      often results in significantly longer watermarked text.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Given the weakness of many schemes to paraphrasing or word substitution attacks, some have
757   proposed watermarking based on semantics and other features that would remain intact for common
758   attack strategies (Liu et al., 2023b; Hou et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023; Yoo et al., 2023). Mean-
759   while, others have viewed the problem through the lens of cryptography and classical complexity
760   theory (Christ et al., 2023; Christ & Gunn, 2024). Lastly, Liu et al. (2023a) proposes an un-forgeable
761   publicly verifiable watermark algorithm that uses two different neural networks for watermark gener-
762   ation and detection. Huang et al. (2023) improves the statistical tests used for detection, providing
763   faster rates than prior work.
764   As the deployment of watermarks to LLMs is still early and also presumably secretive, the correct
765   threat model is still undetermined. Krishna et al. (2024) shows that paraphrasing can evade both
766   third-party and watermarking detectors alike. Some may posit that attacks like paraphrasing or
767   round-trip translation are unrealistic since either they are too expensive to conduct at scale or parties
768   in possession of a capable paraphrasing model have adequate resources to serve their own LLM.
769   Zhang et al. (2023) show that attackers with weaker computational capabilities can successfully
770   evade watermarks given access to a quality oracle that can evaluate whether a candidate output is
771
      a high-quality response to a prompt, and a perturbation oracle which can modify an output with
      a non-trivial probability of maintaining quality. Alarmingly, Gu et al. (2023) demonstrates that
772
      watermarks can be learned — an adversary can use a teacher model that employs decoder-based
773
      watermarking to train a student model to emulate the watermark. Thibaud et al. (2024) formulates
774   tests to determine whether a black-box language model is employing watermarking, and they do not
775   find strong evidence of watermarking among currently popular LLMs.
