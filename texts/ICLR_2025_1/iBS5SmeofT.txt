010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) models can generate high-quality samples from
013            a non-informative prior, but they can be slow, often needing hundreds of network
014            evaluations (NFE). To address this, we propose Implicit Dynamical Flow Fusion
015            (IDFF); IDFF learns a new vector field with an additional momentum term that
016
               enables taking longer steps during sample generation while maintaining the fidelity
               of the generated distribution. Consequently, IDFFs reduce the NFEs by a factor of
017
               ten (relative to CFMs) without sacrificing sample quality, enabling rapid sampling
018
               and efficient handling of image and time-series data generation tasks. We evaluate
019            IDFF on standard benchmarks such as CIFAR-10 and CelebA for image generation,
020            where we achieve likelihood and quality performance comparable to CFMs and
021            diffusion-based models with fewer NFEs. IDFF also shows superior performance
022            on time-series datasets modeling, including molecular simulation and sea surface
023            temperature (SST) datasets, highlighting its versatility and effectiveness across
024            different domains.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Diffusion models have emerged as powerful tools for modeling complex, high-dimensional data
030   by iteratively transforming random noise into meaningful information (Cachay et al., 2023; Myers
031   et al., 2022). These models have demonstrated remarkable success in various domains, including
032
      high-quality image generation (Song et al., 2020a) and text production (Liu et al., 2024; Kim et al.,
      2019). However, their training process remains computationally intensive, often requiring hundreds
033
      of function evaluations (NFEs) to achieve high-quality results (Ho et al., 2020). While techniques
034
      such as DPM-solver (Lu et al., 2022a;b) and Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIMs) (Song
035   et al., 2020b) have made strides in reducing NFEs for diffusion-based generative models, significant
036   computational challenges persist.
037
038
      In contrast to diffusion models, which parameterize the dynamics of noise-to-data transformation,
      Conditional Flow Matching (CFMs) directly parameterize vector fields governing this transformation
039
      (Liu et al., 2022; Albergo and Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Albergo et al., 2023). CFMs leverage the change
040
      of variable theory in statistics to define conditional probability paths, enabling faster convergence
041   during training compared to diffusion models (Lipman et al., 2022). However, generating high-quality
042   samples with CFMs still requires hundreds of NFEs, making the process computationally expensive
043   (Dao et al., 2023) and limiting the extension of CFM models to time-series data modeling, as NFEs
044   scale with sequence length.
045
      To address these challenges, we introduce a novel method to enhance the efficiency of CFMs, allowing
046
      for longer sampling steps without compromising the fidelity of the target distribution. Our approach
047   draws inspiration from Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) algorithms (Neal, 2012a; Neal et al., 2011),
048   which utilize the conservation properties of Hamiltonian dynamics to couple target and momentum
049   variables. The key contributions of our work are as follows:
050
051   1.   We propose Implicit Dynamical Flow Fusion (IDFF), which integrates a momentum term into
052        the vector field of conditional flow models. Similar to how HMC reduces the number of samples
053        required to reach the target distribution (Neal, 2012a; Neal et al., 2011), IDFF reduces the
           NFE by a factor of 10 compared to traditional CFMs while maintaining identical marginal

149   CFMs vs Diffusion Models. CFMs share similarities with diffusion models; both rely on defining
150   continuous probability paths over time. However, diffusion models use stochastic processes to
151
      transform data distributions, typically governed by an SDE. However, CFMs utilize a vector field to
      directly map an initial prior distribution to the target distribution via a deterministic process (Equation
152
      4). Diffusion models, such as denoising score matching models, rely on stochastic diffusion paths to
153
      approximate the data distribution. CFMs bypass this stochastic process by constructing a conditional
154   vector field and sampling from the ODE defined in Equation 3.

160   This approach seeks a mapping from p0 to p1 that minimizes the displacement cost between these
161   two distributions, resulting in flows that can be integrated accurately. This CFM variant is known

      3     I MPLICIT DYNAMICAL F LOW F USION M ODEL (IDFF)
222
      IDFF aims to improve sampling efficiency while enhancing sample quality of OT-CFMs through
223
      two key innovations: (1) the incorporation of a learnable Hamiltonian momentum term, ξt , into the
224
      conditional vector field, which enhances sampling efficiency; and (2) the proposal of a new objective
225   function that learns the IDFF’s vector field in input space.

230   learnable Hamiltonian momentum term, ξt , integrated with the original vector field vt (xt ). This
231   integration yields a new, more flexible vector field:

236
      is a hyperparameter that controls the overall influence of the momentum term. While the IDFF vector
237   field converges to OT-CFMs in either limit, outside of it, it enables the flow to leverage the momentum
238   variables to guide the flow based on the momentum variables. Our empirical results demonstrate that
239   this approach significantly improves sampling efficiency by substantially reducing the NFEs required.
240   Our approach also overcomes the limitations of uniform weighting across trajectories by prioritizing
241   samples near t = 0, 1, allowing finer attention to complex structures; see Figure 2.A.
242
      Designing momentum variables: The term ξt is designed to guide the new vector field toward the
243   correct probability flow for the next time step, ensuring its role in accurately modeling the distribution.
244   There are many potential choices to make here. Still, we take inspiration from Hamiltonian Monte
245   Carlo, which showcases how to leverage momenta to sample complex multi-modal posterior distribu-
246   tions rapidly Neal (2012b). A potential design for ξt is ξt = γ∇x log pt (xt ), which aligns with the
247   gradient of the log probability of the samples xt . If pt (xt | x1 ) is a Gaussian with variance σt2 , ξt

251   following Hamiltonian dynamics in an extended state space (x, p). In contrast, IDFF’s probability
252

      ξt . Both methods use auxiliary variables to enhance sampling efficiency and preserve the desired
253

254
      complex interactions in the flow. ξt = γ∇x log pt (xt ) drives the vector field toward regions of high

365
366   Recent advancements in CFMs include Schrödinger bridge-based methods (Richter et al., 2023)
367   and conjugate/splitting-based integrators (Pandey et al., 2023), both aimed at improving sampling
368
      efficiency. However, these methods still struggle to significantly reduce the NFEs, which remains a
      critical factor for computational efficiency.
369
370   In diffusion models, denoising diffusion implicit models (DDIMs) (Song et al., 2020b) were intro-
371   duced to address the issue of large NFEs by offering a more efficient sampling process. DDIMs
372   deterministically generate samples from latent variables, unlike DDPMs, which rely on Langevin
373   dynamics. By employing a variational approach, DDIMs accelerate sampling while preserving
374
      output quality. Other approaches to enhance sampling in diffusion-based generative models include
      distillation frameworks such as Flash (Kohler et al., 2024), which generate diverse samples in fewer
375
      steps by mitigating training-inference discrepancies.

      IDFF consists of a scoring model and an implicit flow component coupled with a parameter σ0 to
849
      generate flow w(.). We examine the effect of σ0 on sample quality for the CIFAR-10 and CelebA
850   datasets, with the NFE fixed at 10. As shown in Figure 4 (B), a value of σ0 = 0.6 achieves the
851   best balance between the diffusion and flow components regarding sample quality for both datasets.
852   This plot also demonstrates that sample quality is susceptible to σ0 , particularly for diffusion-based
853   models like DDPMs, compared to CFMs, especially at small NFEs, which is already noticed in some
854   research(Jolicoeur-Martineau et al., 2020; Jain et al., 2020).
