009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution recognition forms an important and well-studied problem in
012            deep learning, with the goal to filter out samples that do not belong to the dis-
013            tribution on which a network has been trained. The conclusion of this paper is
014            simple: a good hierarchical hyperbolic embedding is preferred for discriminating
015            in- and out-of-distribution samples. We introduce Balanced Hyperbolic Learning.
016            We outline a hyperbolic class embedding algorithm that jointly optimizes for hi-
017            erarchical distortion and balancing between shallow and wide subhierarchies. We
018
               can then use the class embeddings as hyperbolic prototypes for classification on
               in-distribution data. We outline how existing out-of-distribution scoring functions
019
               can be generalized to operate with hyperbolic prototypes. Empirical evaluations
020
               across 13 datasets and 13 scoring functions show that our hyperbolic embeddings
021            outperform existing out-of-distribution approaches when trained on the same data
022            with the same backbones. We also show that our hyperbolic embeddings outper-
023            form other hyperbolic approaches, can beat state-of-the-art contrastive methods,
024            and natively enable hierarchical out-of-distribution generalization.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Detecting out-of-distribution samples is crucial in real-world settings to make classification pre-
030   dictions reliable and ensure a safe deployment of trained models (Liu et al., 2021). These models
031   are typically trained on datasets with closed-world assumptions He et al. (2015), referred to as in-
032   distribution (ID) data, and testing samples that significantly deviate from training distribution are
033
      referred to as out-of-distribution (OOD) data. A wide range of works have proposed approaches to
      score the likelihood of a testing sample being OOD or not (Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b).
034
      Since OOD samples are unseen during training, the key approaches to determine OOD score for a
035
      model are based only on ID samples. Scoring functions to classify OOD samples are primarily based
036   on model’s confidence (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liang et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2022; Liu
037   et al., 2020b) or the feature distance from ID embeddings (Lee et al., 2018b; Sun et al., 2022)
038
039
      Recent literature has highlighted that scoring functions and optional training or outlier exposure are
      not the only considerations for effective out-of-distribution detection; the choice of embedding space
040
      directly influences out-of-distribution discrimination (Ming et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024). In this pa-
041
      per, we find that hyperbolic embeddings naturally help to discriminate in- and out-of-distribution
042   samples. We show this in Figure 1a. Different from the Euclidean classifier, the hyperbolic clas-
043   sifier provides strongly uniform distributions for samples near the origin and strongly peaked dis-
044   tributions for samples near the boundary. This observation matches directly with recent literature
045   on hyperbolic learning (Mettes et al., 2023). Hyperbolic geometry makes it possible to deal with
046   hierarchical distributions (Nickel & Kiela, 2017), spatial object boundaries (Ghadimi Atigh et al.,
047   2022), adversarial shifts (Guo et al., 2022), and uncertainty (Franco et al., 2023). All papers find
048   a direct link between the norm of representations in hyperbolic space and sample certainty, akin
049   to Figure 1a. We seek to take advantage of this natural property in hyperbolic learning to help
050
      discriminate out-of-distribution from in-distribution samples.
051   This paper introduces Balanced Hyperbolic Learning. We first represent classes as prototypes in
052   hyperbolic space based on their hierarchical relations. This naturally leads to a desirable ordering,
053   where in-distribution classes end up near the edge of the Poincaré ball and less specific (i.e. more
      general and uncertain) inner nodes end up closer to the origin as a function of their hierarchical

096

139   3.1   OVERVIEW OF THE PROPOSED METHOD
140
141   The hypothesis of this paper is that hyperbolic embeddings, accompanied by a hierarchical orga-
142   nization of in-distribution classes, are a natural match for out-of-distribution detection. The in-
143   distribution hierarchy is given as G = (V, E) with |V | > C denoting the C classes as leaf nodes
144
      with additional inner nodes leading to a root node. While an additional assumption, we find that
      such hierarchical information typically comes for free, for example by using large-scale knowledge
145
      graphs such as WordNet (Miller, 1995) or VerbNet (Schuler, 2005), or simply by prompting a large
146
      language model to provide a hierarchical decomposition of a set of classes (Liu et al., 2024).
147
148   The proposed method consists of two steps, (i) we first learn balanced hyperbolic embeddings for
149   class labels in the hyperbolic space, Bd , by optimizing for pairwise distances between class labels
150
      in the hyperbolic space to be equivalent to the graph distance defined by a given hierarchy of the
      classes. (ii) We then learn a network encoder fθ : X → Rd and project the embeddings to the
151
      hyperbolic space, Bd , with an exponential map. A distance-based loss between image features and
152
      class labels as prototypes in the hyperbolic space is used to shape the embedding space and enable
153   the learning of fθ , which will produce naturally discriminative embeddings for OOD detection. We
154   then show that we can use our resulting model with the plethora of existing scoring functions to
155   determine OOD scores.

      3.3   H YPERBOLIC OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION SCORING
227
      Scoring functions have been well-studied in out-of-distribution detection. We believe that adding
228   yet another does not fully hammer down our point that hyperbolic embeddings are powerful for out-
229   of-distribution detection in the broad sense. We will therefore focus on generalizing a wide range of
230   existing functions to operate on hyperbolic embeddings or prototypes. As we will show, this requires
231   minimal to no changes. We exclude functions that use additional outlier data, as our goal is to show
232   the effect of hyperbolic embeddings as is. We also exclude Mahalanobis-based functions, as each
233   explicitly assume features to be Euclidean. We perform evaluations on 13 different scoring functions
234   in total: MSP (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), Temperature Scaling (Guo et al., 2017), ODIN(Liang
235   et al., 2018), Energy(Liu et al., 2020b), Activation Shaping(ASH) (Djurisic et al., 2022), Generalized
236
      Entropy (GEN) (Liu et al., 2023) use logits to design their OOD score. Gram (Sastry & Oore, 2020),
      KNN (Sun et al., 2022), DICE (Sun & Li, 2022), RankFeat (Song et al., 2022), SHE(Zhang et al.,
237
      2022b), NNGuide (Park et al., 2023) and SCALE (Xu et al., 2023). All functions use features, logits,
238
      or probabilities at the intermediate or last layer.
239
240   MSP and Temp Scaling take the maximum of the softmax of the logits, fi as the score, and ODIN ad-
241   ditionally adds a noise perturbation to the input. This is directly applicable in our setup as well, with
242
      the only difference that the logits are now given by the negative of hyperbolic distances, −dB (zi , pi )
      for the hyperbolic embedding of zi of image xi and class prototype pi The energy score is defined
243                             PC
244
      as E(x, f ) = −T · log i efi (x)/T where fi is the logit corresponding to i-th label and T is the
      temperature hyperparameter. In our method, with z = expc0 (fi (xi )), this score is given by
245
246
247                                                         \label {eq:hyperbolic-energy} E(\bx ,f) = T \cdot \text {log}\sum _i^C e^{-d_{\mathbb {B}}(\mathbf {z}_i,p_i)/T}.                                                        (12)
248
249   Note that we no longer take the negative energy values because our logits are already given by the
250   negative of the prototype distance. Throughout the experiments, we use a T = 10 in the energy-
251   based scoring function for ours and T = 1 for the baseline, as these are the best performing settings
252   for both. All other scoring functions use features at the intermediate or last layer. We have investi-
253
      gated generalizing these functions to operate the exponential mapping and found no clear difference.
      Therefore, for scoring functions using features or intermediate layers, we compute scores on the eu-
254
      clidean features in our approach as well for direct comparison to Euclidean-trained counterparts.
255
      We note that the features have in our case been optimized to align with hyperbolic class prototypes,
256   hence these features still benefit from our approach.

314
315   We evaluate our method for OOD detection, benchmarking it against a baseline Euclidean network
316   across 13 scoring functions on various ID and OOD datasets. Additionally, we ablate the effects
317   of distortion and balancing, compare it with other hyperbolic approaches, and state-of-the-art OOD
318   methods. Finally, we provide a brief overview of the hierarchical OOD setting, with additional
319   analysis and details presented in Appendix C.

      Out-of-distribution detection. Recent methods like CIDER (Ming et al., 2022), PALM (Lu
499
      et al., 2024) show that training with hyperspherical prototypes makes the network robust to out-
500   of-distribution samples. where OOD samples lie between ID clusters on the hypersphere. Motivated
501   in a similar way, our method allows OOD samples to additionally lie between ID clusters and origin
502   by choosing hyperbolic geometry. There is some recent exploration into methods that do not just
503   rely on binary out-of-distribution detection. Lee et al. (2018a) introduce hierarchical novelty detec-
504   tion where they aim to find the closest super class for a novel class. This has also been investigated
505   in generalized open-set recognition (Geng et al., 2020; Dengxiong & Kong, 2023), using hierar-
506   chies and attributes. In our work, beyond conventional OOD detection, we introduce a fine-grained
507   evaluation approach that leverages hierarchies for improved detection.
508   Hyperbolic embeddings of hierarchies. The foundational work of Nickel and Kiela (Nickel &
509   Kiela, 2017) demonstrated that hyperbolic embeddings outperform Euclidean embeddings for hier-
510   archical data. Extensions include entailment cones for stricter hierarchical relations (Ganea et al.,
511   2018), combinatorial constructions (Sala et al., 2018), and effective applications of the Lorentz
512   model (Nickel & Kiela, 2018; Law et al., 2019).Recent unsupervised metric learning methods (Yan
513   et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023) were also effective to discover hierarchical information about data.
514
      We find that existing embedding algorithms assume balanced hierarchies, resulting in suboptimal
      embeddings of shallow subhierarchies. We introduce a distortion-based objective with explicit
515
      subhierarchy-balancing to avoid this limitation, which directly benefits out-of-distribution detection.
516
517   Hyperbolic learning of visual data. Hyperbolic learning has shown promise for OOD detec-
518   tion (Guo et al., 2022; van Spengler et al., 2023). Hyperbolic embeddings have been used for
519   generalized open-set recognition (Lee et al., 2018a; Dengxiong & Kong, 2023) and visual anomaly
520
      detection (Hong et al., 2023), where OOD samples are naturally positioned near the origin. A similar
      recent work from Zeng et al. (Zeng et al., 2023) show that learning hierarchies through tree distance
521
      regularization in euclidean space is beneficial for robustness. We take inspiration such works and
522
      strive to balance shallow and wide sub-hierarchies in our hyperbolic embeddings to avoid unwanted
523   biases to outperforms existing hyperbolic out-of-distribution detection approaches. Our approach is
524   general in nature and can be used with any out-of-distribution scoring function.

527   7           C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   Out-of-distribution detection is a difficult task. This work advocates for hierarchical hyperbolic em-
530   beddings to perform such a discrimination. We introduce an algorithm for positioning in-distribution
531
      classes as prototypes using their hierarchical relations through a balanced distortion-based objective.
      In turn, in-distribution learning becomes a hyperbolic sample-to-prototype optimization. Rather than
532
      adding yet another score, we show how the well-known existing functions effortlessly generalize to
533
      operate with hyperbolic prototypes. Experiments across a wide range of datasets and scoring func-
534   tions highlights the strong potential of hyperbolic embeddings for out-of-distribution detection. We
535   furthermore show that our approach leads to hierarchical out-of-distribution generalization without
536   any knowledge about out-of-distribution classes. We conclude that Balanced Hyperbolic Learning is
537   a powerful, general-purpose approach to enrich your out-of-distribution detection. Limitations. We
538   assume that a correct and known hierarchy is available. While it is possible to use LLM-generated
539   hierarchies Liu et al. (2024), verifying the correctness and usability is an exciting direction for future
      work.

      A    M OTIVATION FOR BALANCED H YPERBOLIC E MBEDDINGS
865
866
867   On bias towards deeper and wider subtrees. To better understand bias in existing methods to-
868   wards imbalances in hierarchies, we construct an imbalanced hierarchy over CIFAR-100 for 2,3
869   and 4 levels of granularity. This hierarchy deliberately incorporates subtrees of varying depths ( i.e.
870   levels of hierarchy) and widths (i.e. number of nodes), allowing us to systematically analyze how dif-
871   ferent approaches learn embeddings across uneven hierarchies. Specifically, we compare the learned
872   hierarchies from three methods: Poincaré embeddings (PE) Nickel & Kiela (2017), Hyperbolic en-
873   tailment cones (HEC) Ganea et al. (2018), and our proposed balanced hyperbolic embeddings. To
874
      analyze these methods, we plot the pairwise distances between nodes in the hierarchy, as shown in
      Figure 5. These pairwise distance plots help visualize the structural relationships within the learned
875
      embeddings, including the granularity and differentiation between hierarchical levels.
876
877   The visualizations reveal that existing methods such as PE and HEC exhibit a tendency to over-
878   prioritize narrower subtrees (those with fewer nodes) compared to wider subtrees, especially as
879   granularity increases. Moreover, these methods display limited differentiation between deeper lev-
880
      els of hierarchy, as evidenced by lower color gradient between leaf nodes (diagonal) and their cor-
      responding parent nodes in the pairwise distance plot. Our proposed approach, on the other hand,
881
      demonstrates a more balanced representation, effectively addressing these biases, providing a more
882
      accurate representation of the hierarchical structure.
883
884   Motivation for losses. The distortion loss ensures that all in-distribution classes are distributed in a
885   uniform hierarchical manner. The norm loss ensures that all nodes at the same hierarchical level are
886
      equally far away from the origin. This is highly preferred for OOD, especially when dealing with
      imbalanced trees, as OOD samples tend to be embedded closer to the origin. With our norm loss,
887
      we avoid a bias of OOD samples to shallow subtrees, leading to better ID/OOD discrimination. We
888
      visualize the variance of norms across all hierachical levels for a toy tree example to explain our
889   point. For a balanced tree with 3 levels and 5 nodes per level, we remove a percentage of nodes
890   randomly to introduce imbalance. In Figure 6, we plot the variance of norms as a function of the
891   percentage of nodes removed comparing our approach with distortion loss alone to the combination
892   of distortion and norm loss. The results clearly demonstrate that without the norm loss, the variance
893   of the norms increases significantly in imbalanced hierarchies, thereby underscoring the role of norm
894   loss in achieving balanced hierarchical representations.

       C.4       A BOUT H IERARCHICAL G ENERALIZATION
1149
       The setting for hierarchical generalization aims to evaluate how well our proposed model can handle
1150   OOD samples that belong to a closely related hierarchy. To this end, we adopt the CIFAR-100 OSR
1151   50/50 split setting from OpenOOD 1 Zhang et al. (2023b) and only use hierarchy information for
1152   the training data. For the evaluation of hierarchical metrics in Table 6, we use the whole hierarchy
1153   to measure the Lowest Common Ancestor (LCA) distances during evaluation. Below we give a
1154   detailed description of the hierarchical metrics used.
1155
       Hierarchical Distance (H-Dist). The H-Dist metric, as defined by Bertinetto et al. (2020), calcu-
1156   lates the mean height of the LCA between the ground truth and the predicted class when the input
1157   is misclassified. Here, we adapt this metric to consider H-dist as the mean height between LCA and
1158   the predicted ID class for an OOD sample.
1159
       Hierarchical Similarity Index (HSI). We adapt the HSI metric from Dengxiong & Kong (2023)
1160
       originally proposed for generalized open-set recognition(G-OSR), to fit our hierarchical OOD de-
1161
       tection setup. While G-OSR focuses on identifying the closest ancestor for unseen samples from
1162   ancestor nodes, our approach instead evaluates how closely the predicted ID class aligns with the
1163   true hierarchy of the OOD samples. The metrics are summarized as follows:
1164
1165
                                                      \text {HSI-}b_1 = \frac {1}{m} \sum _{l=1}^m \frac {1}{d(y_{gt1}^l, y_{\text {LCA}1}^l)}             (13)
1166
1167
1168
1169                                       \text {HSI-}b_2 = \frac {1}{m} \sum _{l=1}^m \frac {1}{ln(d(y_{gt2}^l, y_{\text {LCA}2}^l)+1) e}                (14)
1170
1171
        The hierarchical similarity index is defined by the Lowest Common Ancestor (LCA) distance be-
1172
       tween ground truth and the direct ances- tor of the predicted class. HSI-b1 is the inverse of distance
1173
       between direct ground truth ancestor and the lowest common ancestor and HSI-b2 is the inverse of
1174   the distance between ground truth class and lowest common ancestor. A lower distance represents
1175   better result.

1179   Out-of-distribution Detection. Conventional out-of-distribution detection is viewed as a binary
1180   task; a sample is either from the same distribution as the one used during training or not. It was
1181   addressed early on by Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016) which proposed a score based on softmax output
1182
       to detect such samples. Since then, numerous methods have been proposed to address this problem,
       aiming to utilize confidence and score-based (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Lee et al., 2018b; Liang
1183
       et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020b), distance-based (Lee et al., 2018b; Sehwag et al., 2021; Tao et al.,
1184
       2022; Sun et al., 2022) or generative-based (Ryu et al., 2018; Kong & Ramanan, 2021) methods to
1185   reliably classify whether a sample is out-of-distribution or not. Training-time methods additionally
1186   train with outlier data or have additional training strategies to make the network robust to outliers.
