011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Analog circuits are crucial in modern electronic systems, and automating their de-
014            sign has attracted significant research interest. One of major challenges is topol-
015
               ogy synthesis, which determines circuit components and their connections. Recent
               studies explore large language models (LLM) for topology synthesis. However,
016
               the scenarios addressed by these studies do not align well with practical applica-
017
               tions. Specifically, existing work uses vague design requirements as input and out-
018            puts an ideal model, but detailed structural requirements and device-level models
019            are more practical. Moreover, current approaches either formulate topology syn-
020            thesis as graph generation or Python code generation, whereas practical topology
021            design is a complex process that demands extensive design knowledge. In this
022            work, we propose AnalogXpert, a LLM-based agent aiming at solving practical
023            topology synthesis problem by incorporating circuit design expertise into LLMs.
024            First, we represent analog topology as SPICE code and introduce a subcircuit li-
025            brary to reduce the design space, in the same manner as experienced designers.
026
               Second, we decompose the problem into two sub-task (i.e., block selection and
               block connection) through the use of CoT and in-context learning techniques, to
027
               mimic the practical design process. Third, we introduce a proofreading strategy
028
               that allows LLMs to incrementally correct the errors in the initial design, akin to
029            human designers who iteratively check and adjust the initial topology design to
030            ensure accuracy. Finally, we construct a high-quality benchmark containing both
031            real data (30) and synthetic data (2k). AnalogXpert achieves 40% and 23% suc-
032            cess rates on the synthetic dataset and real dataset respectively, which is markedly
033            better than those of GPT-4o (3% on both the synthetic dataset and the real dataset).

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Analog circuits form the backbone of many modern electronic systems, playing a crucial role in
039
      processing continuous signals to achieve a variety of tasks in the devices that permeate our everyday
      lives. The significance of analog circuits lies in their ability to excel in situations where information
040
      must be captured and processed directly from natural sources, such as sound Zhu & Feng (2024),
041
      light Muramatsu et al. (2003), temperature Wang et al. (2013), or pressure Wang et al. (2017).
042   Analog circuit design usually can be divided into three stages: (1) Topology synthesis. Zhao &
043   Zhang (b) Topology synthesis determines the whole analog circuit topology. This stage will select
044   the basic devices (e.g. transistors, capacitors, resistors) and give the connection relationship among
045   these basic devices. (2) Circuit Sizing. Xiaohan Gao (2024); Wang et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2022);
046   Cao et al. (2022) The selected basic devices need to be applied with the appropriate parameters in
047   order to maximize the circuit performance for a given topology. (3) Layout synthesis. Chen et al.;
048   Zhang et al. (2024; 2023); Dhar et al. This stage performs the placement and routing for an analog
049   circuit to generate the final layout.
050   Analog topology synthesis is the foundation of the entire circuit design and determines the perfor-
051   mance of the circuit. Given the importance of analog topology synthesis, some research has emerged
052   and focused on the automation of it. Early work explores utilize graph neural networks (GNN) and
053   reinforcement learning (RL) (Zhao & Zhang, b;c; Dong et al.) for topology synthesis. Recently, as
      large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities across various fields, there has

079   In this work, we introduce AnalogXpert, designed to address more practical analog topology synthe-
080   sis problem while utilizing circuit design expertise for optimal performance. As shown in Figure 1,
081
      AnalogXpert takes detailed structure design requirements as input and output a real circuit topology
      design (rather than ideal models), which is represented as SPICE code. The intuition behind is that
082
      the SPICE code is widely used in practical design scenarios and exists on the internet which is very
083
      likely to have been seen in the pre-training of LLMs. Therefore, formulating analog topology as
084   SPICE code not only matches the practical scenarios but also facilitates understanding by LLMs.
085
086   Based on the above formulation, AnalogXpert presents a LLM-based agent for topology synthesis
087
      by incorporating circuit design expertise into LLMs (see Figure 2). First, a well-designed subcircuit
      library is proposed to reduce the design space and improve the generation success rate. With the
088
      customized text-format subcircuit library, AnalogXpert can generate the final topology from the
089
      subcircuit level rather than the device level which not only aligns with human design practices
090   but also greatly reduces the length of the model output. Second, the design task decomposition is
091   performed to make the design more logical and easy to check for errors. AnalogXpert leverages the
092   CoT to prompt LLMs to generate topology step by step. Specifically, the design task is decomposed
093   into block selection and block connection just as humans do in practical design scenarios. Third,
094   analog designers usually need to check the block types and connection relationships after they finish
095   the circuit design. This approach greatly improves the accuracy of analog topology generation.
096   AnalogXpert also takes this into account and proposes a proofreading strategy to check the generated
097   analog topologies and give revision messages back to LLMs for iterative refinement.
098   Since the task formulation of AnalogXpert is very different from previous works, we construct a new
099   benchmark including both real data (30) and synthetic data (2k). Previous studies only have one type
100   of data, either real data or synthetic data. Meanwhile, the number of real data is very limited (≤5),
101   except for the AnalogCoder Lai et al. (a) (24). Validated on the proposed benchmark, AnalogXpert
102   achieves 40% and 23% success rates in the synthetic dataset and real dataset respectively, which is
103   much better than the GPT-4o (3% in the synthetic dataset, 3% in the real dataset). The experimental
104
      results demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of the AnalogXpert.

106   ogy synthesis problem. We formulate it as a SPICE code generation problem and introduce a design
107   space reduction method based on an extensible subcircuit library. (2) We propose a CoT-based LLM

142
      Analog topology synthesis is the most challenging step in the analog design flow and thus has
143
      attracted extensive research interest. Before the introduction of LLMs, some research has al-
144
      ready attempted to automate analog topology synthesis with versatile AI methods. For example,
145   RLATS Zhao & Zhang (b;c) leverages reinforcement learning(RL) to build up the analog circuit
146   step by step. RLATS establishes a subcircuit library to simplify the topology synthesis problem so
147   that the RL agent is able to handle it. However, the design diversity of analog circuits makes it
148   difficult to transfer RL agents between different circuit types. CKTGNN Dong et al. builds up a 10k
149   dataset to train a graph neural network which achieves an impressive performance. The drawback
150   is that CKTGNN leverages the ideal model to reduce the design space. The ideal model can not
151   represent the entire legalized design space and can not be directly converted to the final topology.
152   With the introduction of LLM, more research works on the automation of topology synthesis have
153
      emerged. LADAC Liu et al. (a) and Artisan Zihao Chen (2024) leverage prompting and supervised
      fine-tuning(SFT) to enhance the analog design ability of LLM, respectively. However, they only
154
      validate the proposed framework on a few (≤5) real analog cases. This is not enough to demonstrate
155
      that LLM has adequate analog circuit design capabilities. LaMAGIC Chang et al. build up a 120k
156   synthetic dataset to support the SFT of LLM, but its design tasks are simpler than the actual analog
157   design tasks. LaMAGIC actually focuses on a kind of radio frequency circuit with limited basic
158   devices (≤ 6) including a capacitor(2 terminals), inductor(2 terminals), and switch(2 terminals).
159   Analog usually is made up of tens of basic devices (0∼50), such as transistors (4 terminals), capac-
160   itors (2 terminals), and resistors (2 terminals). AnalogCoderLai et al. (a) leverages a prompt-based
161   LLM to generate the analog topology and validate the framework on some real data. The disadvan-
      tage is that user requirements are very ambitious and can be handled directly by existing LLM agents

198   2.2         L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
199
200   Large language models with pre-trained parameters such as GPT, LLaMa Dubey et al. (2024); Tou-
201   vron et al. (2023), Claude, have demonstrated terrific ability in versatile tasks. Recent research on
202   prompting has further enhanced the LLM ability. For example, Chain-of-Thought(CoT) Wei et al.
203   (2023) is a technique that encourages language models to generate intermediate reasoning steps in
204   a step-by-step manner, rather than directly providing the final answer. This method improves the
205   model’s ability to solve complex tasks by making its reasoning process more transparent and struc-
206   tured. Tree-of-Thought(ToT) Yao et al. (2023) is a famous ToT evolution of CoT, which allows
207
      multiple possible solutions or reasoning paths to be explored simultaneously. This approach helps
      improve decision-making in complex tasks by evaluating different branches of thought before con-
208
      verging on a final answer. Some other researches focus on In-context learning Dong et al. (2024)
209
      which involves giving a language model examples of tasks or instructions within the same prompt
210   to improve the final performance. In addition to pre-generation promptings, post-generation feed-
211   back is equally important. Some feedback techniques such as self-reflection Renze & Guven (2024);
212   Madaan et al. (2023), can also improve the LLM performance. These techniques provide the foun-
213   dation for constructing agents with practical functions, such as gene-editing Huang et al., math Ahn
214   et al. (2024), and chip designs Liu et al. (b); Blocklove et al.; Lai et al. (b); He et al.. Based on these
215   techniques, we introduce the circuit design expertise, which allows LLM to really deal with analog
      topology synthesis problem.

      Method Overview. In this section, we elaborate on the detailed methods of AnalogXpert (as Fig-
219
      ure 2 shows), an efficient training-free analog design agent incorporating human design expertise.
220   AnalogXpert introduces a brand new task formulation of conditional analog topology synthesis
221   which can further demonstrate the design ability of LLM agents (Section 3.1). Leveraging a well-
222   designed subcircuit library, AnalogXpert constructs a novel and effective analog circuit represen-
223   tation which greatly helps LLM agents design analog circuits concisely (Section 3.2). A domain-
224   specific prompting design flow based on CoT is proposed and further enhanced by the in-context
225   learning (Section 3.3). Although the methods mentioned above can improve the design capability of
226   LLM agents, the single-round generation mode still struggles to handle complex tasks. Therefore,
227   AnalogXpert further introduces human experience-based proofreading to help LLM agents gradu-
228
      ally correct the mistakes and finally generate the correct topology in several rounds (Section 3.4).
      With the cooperation of these techniques, AnalogXpert has a decent performance in the conditional
229
      analog topology synthesis tasks.

      3.3              D ESIGN TASK D ECOMPOSITION
296
297   AnalogXpert mainly leverages CoT and in-context learning. The CoT has two major steps: (1)
298   Block Selection. Based on the previous subcircuit library, the analog topology synthesis task can be
299   performed like the human design process. In this step, LLM agents select the appropriate subcircuits
300   from the library according to the design requirements. (2) Block Connection. With the selected
301   subcircuits, LLM agents then determine the connection relationships and generate the final analog
302   topology. For in-context learning, AnalogXpert selects some design task examples as prompts. The
303
      principle of selection is the similarity of design requirements. For a given design requirement,
      AnalogXpert will give the most similar design task as an example. With the CoT and in-context
304
      learning, the basic generation functions can be achieved.

      3.4              H UMAN EXPERIENCE - BASED PROOFREADING
307
308   During the generation of analog circuit topology, we actually expect the LLM agent to follow some
309   design rules. A straightforward idea is that we summarize these rules as text and then use them as
310   prompts. However, when dealing with a series of rules, the prompt gets longer and longer, and it
311   becomes difficult for an LLM agent to fully understand these rules and follow them strictly. There-
312   fore, we propose human experience-based proofreading to get LLM agents out of this dilemma. The
313   basic idea of proofreading is depicted in Figure 4. In practice, the initial netlist does not provide
314   enough information for the rule-based checker. We annotate these netlists with the terminal types
315
      including current output (I/O), current input (I/I), and voltage (V). In this way, the previous sub-
      circuit library can be summarized into 10 types, making the error-checking process simpler (shown
316
      as the right-top part of Figure 4). After the annotation, a proofreading checker will detect the cor-
317
      responding errors, which are mainly categorized into block selection errors and block connection
318   errors. For the accuracy of checking, the proofreading checker is implemented with deterministic
319   programs rather than LLM agents. The detected errors as well as the violated rules make up the
320   final refinement prompt. The LLM agents take the refinement prompt to generate the refined ana-
321   log topology. AnalogXpert will repeat this process until the design meets the design requirements
322   or reaches the maximum iterations. It is worth noting that not only the current refinement tips are
323   provided, but also the generation and refinement history in order to avoid making similar mistakes
      as much as possible.

      4      E XPERIMENTS
347
348
      Baseline. We compare AnalogXpert with pure GPT-3.5 and GPT-4o, which are advanced models
349   with strong code generation ability and rich cross-disciplinary knowledge. For other work related to
350   topology synthesis, as they handle different problems with us, the comparison with them is infeasible
351   and thus is excluded. Specifically, AnalogXpert tackles concrete structure design requirement while
352   most related work explores ambiguous design specifications (Dong et al.; Zhao & Zhang, b; Liu
353   et al., a; Chang et al.; Zihao Chen, 2024; Lai et al., a). Besides, AnalogXpert directly works on
354   real-world device-level model and SPICE code while the related work focuses on ideal model (Dong
355   et al.; Chang et al.; Zihao Chen, 2024) and Python code-based format (Lai et al., a). If these methods
356   are used for our tasks, the results will be close to pure LLMs (e.g. GPT-4o, GPT-3.5) performance
357
      (as listed in Table 2) due to the different task formulation.
358   Benchmark. We construct two benchmarks for the final evaluation, including a real data benchmark
359   and a synthetic data benchmark. The real data benchmark is collected from a commercial tool named
360   AnalogDesignToolbox Anonymous. There are approximately 60 different analog design topologies,
361   and we select the most representative 30 analog topologies as the real data benchmark. The synthetic
362   data benchmark is built by a random generation Python code leveraging the subcircuit library. Each
363
      synthetic data consists of four parts, the stage number, the input blocks, other given blocks, and
      the maximum number of blocks. The generation task on synthetic data is selecting some subcircuit
364
      blocks based on the input blocks and other given blocks to form the final circuit design. The total
365
      number of used blocks should be less than the maximum number of blocks. The stage is set from
366   one to three, the input blocks and other blocks are randomly selected from the proposed subcircuit
367   library, and the maximum number of blocks is randomly selected from a range related to the stage
368   number(e.g. for one stage the range is 2-5). Finally, we generate 2k synthetic data with totally
369   different structure design requirements.
370
      Metrics. The metric of AnalogXpert is whether the LLM agent can generate the correct analog
371
      topology for the given design requirements in one trial. For real data, the correctness can only
372   be determined if all blocks and connections exactly match the design requirements. Such strict
373   correctness requires humans to check the analog topology results directly. For synthetic data, the
374   block selection and connection have some basic rules to follow. If a generated circuit topology
375   does not violate these rules, it will be determined as correct. Such correctness can be checked
376   by an automatic program. Tested on a certain number of cases, the correct ratio can be obtained.
377   The correct ratio can directly reflect the ability of the LLM agent to generate the required analog
      topology.

408   Main Results. In the main experiment, each method conducts seven different tasks including six
409   tasks on synthetic data and one task on the real data. The design requirements of Task 1-3 have
410   different input stage numbers (1-3) without any given blocks. The design requirements of Task 4-6
411
      have different input stage numbers (1-3) with an extra given block. The pure GPT-3.5 Turbo and
      GPT-4o are only tested on one hundred synthetic data because the automatic check program can
412
      not be performed without the subcircuit library-based representation. Thus, the results are checked
413
      by humans. On synthetic data Pure GPT-4o and pure GPT-3.5 Turbo can only achieve a correct
414   ratio of 3% and 1%, respectively. Such experimental results demonstrate the importance of the pro-
415   posed subcircuit library-based representation method. We can also observe that AnalogXpert(40%)
416   outperforms GPT-3.5Turbo+Ours(28%) on synthetic data. This result indicates that the proposed
417   framework needs models with sufficient comprehension to generate the topology more accurately.
418   On real data, only AnalogXpert achieves a 23% correct ratio, other methods have near-zero correct
419   ratios. The failure of GPT-3.5Turbo+Ours on real data is due to poor model comprehension and a
420   significant increase in task difficulty. The experimental results on both benchmarks demonstrate the
421   effectiveness of the proposed AnalogXpert in dealing with complex connection relationships and
422
      real-world topology synthesis problems.
423   Ablation Study. We perform the ablation study on each component of AnalogXpert except for the
424   subcircuit library-based representation. Subcircuit library-based representation is the foundation of
425   CoT and proofreading and the framework will become pure GPT-4o without it. The experimental
426   results indicate that CoT & In-context learning has less impact on performance compared to the
427   proofreading strategy. We also conduct the experiments with different proofreading rounds. The ex-
428
      perimental results are also consistent with the intuition that the correct ratio increases as the number
      of proofreading rounds increases, proving the effectiveness of this strategy.

464   5      C ONCLUSION
465
466   In this work, we propose AnalogXpert, a powerful analog topology synthesis tool based on training-
467   free LLMs. AnalogXpert leverages the subcircuit-based circuit representation, CoT&in-context
468   learning, and human experience-based proofreading to imitate the human design process and im-
469   prove the design accuracy. Both real data and synthetic data benchmarks are constructed on the
470   structural requirements tasks. The experimental results (40% and 23% correct ratio in real and
471   synthetic data) demonstrate the effectiveness of the AnalogXpert.
472   Limitation and Future Directions. Although this work has constructed both a real dataset and a
473   synthetic dataset, the size of the real dataset is still small. A larger real dataset may lead to a brand
474   new prompting method in the analog topology synthesis problem which can achieve a better correct
475   ratio. In the future, AnalogXpert can be used to generate sufficient synthetic data of the analog
476   circuit topologies in the SPICE code format. With high-quality synthetic data, some small models
477   can be fine-tuned to achieve a competitive performance. Having fine-tuned mini-models that can
478   be run locally ensures the security of design data, which is important for commercial circuit design
479
      companies.
