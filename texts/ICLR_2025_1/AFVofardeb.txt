009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Human vision flexibly extracts part-whole hierarchies from visual scenes. How-
012            ever, representing such hierarchical structures poses a key challenge for neural
013            networks. Most machine learning efforts addressing this issue have focused on
014            slot-based methods, which can be limiting due to their discrete nature and inability
015            to express uncertainty. Inspired by how neural syntax is organized in the brain, this
016
               paper presents a framework for representing hierarchical part-whole relationships
               through hierarchically nested neuronal coherence, characterized by its continu-
017
               ous and distributed nature. At the implementation level, we further developed a
018
               cortical-inspired hybrid model, Composer, which dynamically achieves emergent
019            nestedness when given images. To evaluate the emergent hierarchical structure,
020            we created four synthetic datasets and three quantitative metrics, demonstrating
021            its ability to parse a range of scenes of varying complexity. We believe this work,
022            from representation and implementation to evaluation, advances a new paradigm
023            for developing human-like vision in neural network models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      The representation of hierarchical structures is a critical challenge for neural networks (Riesenhuber
028
      & Dayan, 1996). While there is strong evidence from psychology that humans naturally parse objects
029
      into part-whole hierarchies (Hinton, 1979; Kahneman et al., 1992; Thompson, 1980), representing
030   these relationships in artificial neural networks remains a profound challenge (Hinton, 2021).
031
032   The challenge is illustrated by an example. Given a visual scene, as shown in Fig.1a, the mental
033
      interpretation of the parts and wholes can be uncertain—such as the number and grouping of objects,
      and so on. Therefore, the structure is dynamically inferred based on the "content" and is continuously
034
      constrained by the self-consistency of multiple sources of inconclusive information (Rogers &
035
      McClelland, 2014). The uncertain and content-sensitive nature of part-whole hierarchies challenges
036   solutions directly inspired by symbolic processing, which is discrete and lacks content sensitivity.
037
038   Most efforts to address this challenge in machine learning focus on slot-based methods (Greff et al.,
039
      2020), which can be viewed as a symbolic-inspired solution. These approaches divide their latent
      representations into predefined "slots," creating a discrete separation of object representations (Greff
040
      et al., 2020). These slots are easier to organize into compositional structures, such as graph neural
041
      networks (Han et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2017), generative models (Deng et al., 2021), attention-based
042   models (Sun et al., 2021; Fisher & Rao, 2022), or capsule networks (Hinton et al., 2018; Garau
043   et al., 2022). However, the predefined and discrete nature of slots conceptually limits their ability
044   to continuously infer content-sensitive structures from uncertainty. For instance, the existence and
045   maximum number of objects are predefined discretely when specifying the slots, independent of the
046   content.
047
      By contrast, discrete compositional structures, such as mental entities or their structural organization,
048   are hypothesized to be represented by continuously and dynamically correlated neuronal firings
049   (Buzsáki, 2010) (Fig.1d). This overall state is known as neuronal coherence, and each correlated
050   group of neurons is referred to as a cell assembly (Fig.1d), which can be organized into sequences
051   and hierarchical structures to form neural syntax (Buzsáki, 2010). Inspired by neuroscience, this
052   paper aims to develop a framework that represents part-whole hierarchies not with discrete slots, but
053   through continuous neuronal coherence in neural networks, thus bridging a key feature of human-like
      vision with machine learning models.

085   How does neuronal coherence emerges as a reflection of the parsing structure of various visual scenes?
086   At the implementation level, we adopt a hybrid approach that combines biological constraints and
087   machine learning, to develop a cortical-column-inspired neural network model, Composer (short for
088   COrtical-inspired eMergence of Part-whOle relationShip through nEuronal coheRence). Through
089   the iterative bottom-up and top-down processing within and across columns, nestedness gradually
090   emerges from randomness.
091   How can we explicitly and quantitatively evaluate the discrete hierarchical structure that is implicitly
092   represented as the continuous and uncertain correlation pattern among neuronal firings? To this
093   end, we developed four synthetic datasets of varying complexities and three quantitative metrics
094   to measure different aspects of part-whole representation. Both quantitative results and qualitative
095   visualizations confirm the validity of Composer and the plausibility of the framework. Finally, for
096   comparison, we show that Composer outperforms the state-of-the-art method, the Agglomerator
097
      (Garau et al., 2022). Our contributions are summarized as follows:
098   (1) We developed a brain-inspired representation framework to address the part-whole hierarchy in
099   neural networks through continuous neuronal coherence instead of discrete slots.
100
      (2) We introduced Composer, a brain-inspired hybrid model that integrates machine learning and
101   neuroscience, to demonstrate how the coherent state emerges to represent the part-whole relationship.
102
103   (3) We created datasets and quantitative metrics to explicitly interpret the hierarchical structure from
104
      uncertain and distributed representations in neural networks. Quantitative evaluation of hierarchical
      relationship is often missing in related works, yet it is essential for verifying or falsifying models.
105
106   Taken together, we are pioneering a systematic framework to represent the part-whole relationship in
107   neural networks, from representation, implementation, to evaluation, and we hope that the resulting
      insights will spark further innovations.

130   2    N EUROSCIENCE -M OTIVATED R EPRESENTATION H YPOTHESIS
131
132   A widely discussed hypothesis in neuroscience is that transiently active ensembles of neurons, known
133   as "cell assemblies," represent distinct cognitive entities (Hebb, 2005). These cell assemblies are
134   dynamically correlated and organized into sequences (Hebb, 2005) (Fig.1d), accompanied by gamma-
135   band rhythms (Buzsáki & Draguhn, 2004). It posits that the oscillatory firing patterns of neurons give
136   rise to two types of messages: (1) discharge frequency encodes the presence of features, and (2) the
137
      timing of spikes encodes feature "binding" (Singer, 2009; Malsburg, 1994). Consequently, temporally
      correlated spike firings "bind" distributed features (contents) into entities or neural words (structures).
138
      Furthermore, the hierarchical organization of assembly sequences may be regarded as a neural syntax,
139
      with the "nestedness" in hierarchical relationships represented as temporally nested structures of
140   cell assembly trajectories, accompanied by nested neural rhythms (Buzsáki, 2010) (Fig.1d). Such
141   nested states are of great interest in neuroscience and are argued to be prerequisites of consciousness
142   (Northoff & Zilio, 2022). Together, these hypotheses motivate a coherence-based representation
143   paradigm for part-whole hierarchy, introduced as follows.
144
      Spatial separation of levels: We firstly assume that given a visual scene (Fig.1a), the contents or
145
      features of visual objects are concurrently represented at different “part-whole levels” (Fig.1c). Each
146   level is supported by a set of “spiking neurons”, whose receptive fields are the corresponding features.
147
148   Correlation at different scales: Secondly, we assume that feature neurons at different levels tend
149
      to form correlation patterns of different spatial-temporal scales, or synchronized cell assemblies of
      different sizes, to “bind” features into parts or wholes. It is indicated by the colored box from large to
150
      small in Fig.1c.
151
152   Part-whole hierarchy as assembly sequence hierarchy: Thirdly, we assume that the longer life
153   time of each whole-level cell assembly covers the sequence of its part-level assemblies. As a result,
154   the nested structure in a part-whole relationship is represented as the nested temporal structure of cell
155
      assembly sequences (Fig.1d, right).
156
157   3    I NTUITION OF THE MECHANISM
158
159   How does a hierarchically structured assembly sequence emerge from randomness, with parts
160   temporally nested within wholes (Fig.1d)? This challenge comprises two subproblems: (1) how
161   assembly sequences at each level create neural words (parts/wholes), and (2) how assemblies at
      different levels nest to form neural syntax (hierarchical relationships).


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To form assembly sequences, we need to balance positive and negative feedback: First, a group of
163   neurons (cell assembly) is activated by positive feedback and then quickly inactivated by negative
164   feedback, allowing the next assembly to compete for emergence.
165
166
      From DAE to attractor network: To construct positive feedback, we explore the close relationship
      between denoising auto-encoder (DAE, (Vincent et al., 2008)) and attractor networks (Fig.2a), where
167
      denoising acts as a shared feature and a form of positive feedback. Specifically, by connecting the
168
      input and output layers of a DAE, we form a dynamical system: xt+1 = DAE(xt ), where the DAE
169   is trained on a large dataset. In this setup, each data sample serves as an attractor in the "bottom-up
170   / top-down" dynamics (Fig.2a), since the decoder output of the DAE recovers the noiseless data
171   sample from a perturbed input (Fig.2a middle). This denoising feedback from the DAE provides
172   positive feedback to stably activate a group of neurons based on "associative memories" (Krotov,
173   2023) parameterized within the DAE.
174
      From attractors to rhythmic activities: To establish negative feedback, we consider neuronal
175   adaptation, a universal biological constraint intrinsic to each neuron. Specifically, as shown in Fig.2b
176   left, a layer of spiking neurons with refractory periods connects the output and input of the DAE (also
177   receiving external input). The constraints imposed by the refractory period disrupt the stability of
178   the attractors in Fig.2a, forcing them to become transient. Additionally, to provide a time window
179   for switching between alternative transient attractors (Fig.2b middle), we explore another biological
180   constraint: coupling delay, which allows positive feedback to wait briefly. These biological constraints
181   transform the stable attractors in Fig.2a into a cyclic sequence of transient attractors (Fig.2b middle),
182   underpinning the rhythmic activity in the neuronal coherence state. See Zheng et al. (2022) for a
183
      theoretical proof.
184   From assembly sequence to assembly hierarchy: Inspired by neural syntax theory, we investigate
185   a third biological constraint: integration time window. Buzsáki (2010) proposed that a hierarchy
186   of time windows serves as the "syntax" for organizing cell assemblies, aligning with Mahjoory
187   et al. (2019)’s recent findings of a gradient of time scales along the cortical hierarchy. Specifically,
188   several columns in Fig.2b are stacked and interconnected (Fig.2c left). Higher-level columns have
189
      longer integration time windows to accommodate slower switching dynamics, while lower-level
      columns have shorter windows. To coordinate activities across levels, activated long-lived assemblies
190
      in higher-level columns constrain the dynamics of lower-level columns through gating, while the
191
      temporal integration of lower-level activities drives the dynamics of higher-level columns (Fig.2c
192   middle). These biological constraints intuitively promote the emergence of nested neuronal coherence
193   (Fig.2c middle). In the following section, we provide more details about the Composer.
194
195
196   4     M ODEL
197
198   In this work, we focus on a whole level and a part level, examining the neuronal coherence of
199   pixel-level features to simplify the problem. This framework can be generalized in future research
200   (see A.1). Overall, the Composer comprises two levels of columns interconnected by top-down
201   modulation and bottom-up integration (Fig.2c). Each column consists of a pixel-level spiking layer,
202
      referred to as the spike coding space (SCS, Fig.3c), and a denoising autoencoder (DAE). The term
      "pixel-level" indicates that the SCS at both levels matches the image dimensions (d). Within each
203
      column, the SCS and DAE are delay-coupled: the delayed feedback from the DAE modulates the
204
      firing rates of the spiking neurons through gating, consistent with dendritic computations of pyramidal
205   cells in the cortex (Sherman & Guillery, 1998). Additionally, spikes in the SCS are integrated within
206   a narrow time window before being fed to the DAE, acting as coincidence detectors (König et al.,
207   1996). In the following sections, we first introduce each column individually and then explain how
208   the columns are connected across levels.

      ground truth for images (Fig.5a). Due to the one-to-one correspondence between image pixels and

343   sively for evaluating representations, not for training purposes. The non-Euclidean Victor-Purpura
344   metric, Dvp (Victor & Purpura, 1996), evaluates distances among spike trains. Silhouette(a, b, L, D)
345
      is the well-known Silhouette score (Rousseeuw, 1987) that quantifies inner-cluster coherence for

346
      assigned a cluster label l ∈ L ) and metric D (measuring distances between samples in a and b). A
347
      higher Silhouette score signifies that data samples within the same cluster are closer together and
348   further apart from those in different clusters, reflecting more synchronous spike firings that encode
349   either parts or whole objects. This synchronization characterizes the degree of neuronal coherence
350   "within" each level.
351
      To assess the nested structure, we conceptualize nestedness as the average proximity of whole-level
352
      assemblies to all their constituent parts. It is crucial to consider s1 and s2 in conjunction to reflect
353
      cross-level nestedness. Accordingly, we generalize the Silhouette to capture cross-level coherence,
354   defining Nest Score ∝ Silhouette(s1 , s2 , GT2 , Dvp ), which quantifies the coherence between part-
355   level spike trains s1 and whole-level spike trains s2 based on the whole-level cluster assignment
356   GT2 (refer to A.4 for a comprehensive explanation). Notably, the pixel-wise correspondence among
357   s1 , s2 , GT1 , GT2 ensures comparability.
358
      To elucidate the scores’ intuition, we systematically introduce noise to a manually crafted ideal nested
359
      structure (Fig.5a) and monitor score changes. Perturbing only the part level leaves the Whole Score
360   unchanged, while the Part Score and Nest Score decline from 1 to 0 (Fig.5c); the reverse is true when
361   the whole level is perturbed (Fig.5d). Perturbing the relative order of part and whole assemblies to
362   disrupt nestedness significantly reduces the Nest Score, with the Part and Whole Scores remaining
363   stable (Fig.5e). Collectively, these three scores comprehensively reflect the structure of nested
364   neuronal coherence. More details are provided in A.4.5. Similar to the Silhouette Score, the optimal
365   coherence score is 1, with -1 indicating incoherence, and scores near 0 suggesting randomness.

425   of the DAE, in conjunction with the bottom-up integration of spike activity within SCS, facilitate the
426   concurrent emergence of both the attention sequence and neuronal coherence, as well as the rhythmic
427   population activity denoted as LF P1 (Fig.6e).

      effectively and flexibly represents the part-whole hierarchy of scenes with diverse complexities.

499   7    R ELATED WORK
500
501   Slot-based models represent a significant research direction for object-centric representation using
502   pre-defined discrete slots, as evidenced by works such as (Greff et al., 2015; 2016; 2017; 2019; 2020;
503   Locatello et al., 2020). This approach has also been extended to generate hierarchical structures in
504   graph neural networks (Han et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2017), generative models (Deng et al., 2021),
505
      attention-based models (Sun et al., 2021; Fisher & Rao, 2022) or capsule networks (Hinton et al.,
      2018). Nonetheless, the discrete and pre-defined nature of slots inherently restricts their capacity to
506
      infer representation structures continuously from uncertainty in a content-sensitive manner, such as
507
      determining the presence or number of objects. On the other hand, it is not clear how human brain
508   implements the boundary across slots and the weighting sharing required among slots (Greff et al.,
509   2020). Consequently, it poses a limitation in developing vision systems that closely mimic human
510   perception.
511
      In this paper, we investigate mechanisms that eschew discrete and predefined slots in favor of
512
      continuous, content-sensitive neuronal coherence. Although this concept has been sparingly explored
513
      in machine learning, there are several intriguing related studies that define coherence in various ways:
514   (1) through the phase of complex values (Löwe et al., 2022), (2) by the timing of spike firings (Zheng
515   et al., 2022), and (3) via the rotation of features (Lowe et al., 2023). However, these works primarily
516   focus on single-level objects, neglecting the part-whole relationship. The Composer extends the
517   concept of spike timing to accommodate the part-whole hierarchy, but it is not apparent how the
518   complex-valued network (Löwe et al., 2022) and feature rotation (Lowe et al., 2023) could be adapted
519   to represent different levels of objects. This is because both approaches heavily rely on a specific form
520   of activation function as a binding mechanism, which lacks free parameters to differentiate between
521   levels, unlike the time scale parameters in the Composer. Finally, the ideology of identical islands of
522
      vectors, as proposed by Hinton (2021) and implemented by Garau et al. (2022) holds promise for
      representing part-whole relationships through the coherent orientation of vectors. However, despite
523
      its ambitious goals, this approach currently lacks rigorous quantitative assessments to evaluate the
524
      hierarchical structures, which are crucial for validating or challenging the representation hypothesis.
525

527   8    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we look for solutions to continuously represent part-whole hierarchy by neuronal
530   coherence. To achieve this, we merge insights from neuroscience with machine learning techniques to
531   create the cortical-inspired Composer. This system dynamically build-up “neural syntax” of “neural
532   words” through iterative bottom-up coincidence detection and top-down predictive attention. We
533   anticipate that the Composer will pave the way for a new paradigm that unifies neuroscience theories
534   with machine learning methods, aiming to develop a more human-like visual processing system in
535
      the future. For a discussion on limitations and potential future directions, See A.1.

      A.1   L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
865
866
867
      In this section, we highlight several limitations that could be addressed in future works.
868   (1) Pixel-wise SCS as low-level feature layer and the Scalability
869
      In this work, the part-whole hierarchy is represented and evaluated based on the pixel-wise spike
870
      coding space (SCS), which has a one-to-one mapping to the image’s pixel space (topographical
871   mapping). Shared pixel-wise spatial map of s1 , s2 , GT1 , GT2 , x, γ1 , γ2 , Γ to the input image x
872   makes the spiking representation and the top-down attention much more interpretable so that the
873   part-whole hierarchy could be explicitly evaluated and visualized as Fig.6, Fig.7. Also different
874   object-level spiking pattern can be compared to compute the Nest Score. While topographical
875   mapping (a one-to-one spatial relation between internal representation to the external physical world)
876   is a common feature of the cortical representation, the representation (at each spatial point) could
877   be more abstract. For example, instead of a binary neuron associated with the “presence” of an
878   object occupying the location, a population of binary neurons can be assigned to each location, so
879
      that different aspects of features (associated with the object at the corresponding location) could be
      accounted for by the population vector. Assigning a population of neurons with locations is similar to
880
      the capsule idea in Capsule Network (Hinton et al., 2018) and the mini-column organization in Hinton
881
      (2021). This suggests a future direction to combine the neuronal synchrony (temporal coherence)
882   with identical islands of neurons (spatial coherence) in the GLOM architecture.
883
884   Besides, the object could be represented in the latent layer of the DAE instead of the pixel-level SCS
885
      in each column, similar to the Slot Attention(Locatello et al., 2020). Representing objects in the latent
      layer could enable transforms between levels as in GLOM, by replacing pixel-wise gating in this
886
      paper into a neural network that potentially parameterizes a coordinate transformation. It is notable
887
      that the underlining hypothesis is that all feature-levels and object-levels share a “topographical”
888   spatial map. The difference is what is being represented at each location (along feature level)
889   and how representations at different locations are correlated (along object-level). This is a natural
890   generalization of the representation hypothesis in Fig.1 in the Main Text.
891
      Besides, the input to the SCS of the lowest level is not restricted to be the pixel-level image but
892
      could be the output feature map of an encoder, which is called tokenization in Agglomerator (Garau
893
      et al., 2022). This kind of generalization has also been discussed in Hinton (2021). Notably, recent
894   work shows that the gap between object-centric representation models (previously focused on
895   toy datasets) and real-world images can be bridged by pre-trained DINO model(Seitzer et al.,
896   2022; Lowe et al., 2023). By applying the pretrained DINO model (Caron et al., 2021) as front-end,
897   original model only need to deal with much lower-dimensional latent representations, which have
898   more similar structure as synthetic datasets. Seitzer et al. (2022) and Lowe et al. (2023) provides the
899   insights that seemingly toy models can be scaled to account for real-world dataset by applying proper
900   front-end encoders. For Composer, a general-front end is also applicable and how to scale the model
901   to account for neural syntax of more complicated scenes is a line of promising future works.
902   In sum, the limitation of the part-whole hierarchy as a pixel-level relationship in pixel-level SCS
903   could potentially be generalized in three directions: (1) To allocate each location a column of spiking
904   neurons to form a “representation column” at each location. (2) replacing the simplified cross-level
905   interaction between pixel-wise SCSs as a proper neural network between latent layers. (3) the input to
906   the model could be generalized to the tokenized embeddings from the front-end encoder (e.g. DINO
907   (Seitzer et al., 2022; Lowe et al., 2023; Caron et al., 2021)). Notably, all these generalizations are
908
      compatible with the Composer and could be explored as future works.
909   (2) Coordination transformation
910
      As originally motivated in a cognitive science view point (Hinton, 1979) and restated in machine
911
      learning literature (Hinton, 2021), part-whole hierarchy contains two challenges: (1) the dynamical
912   emergence of the part-whole tree structure and (2) the implementation of a part-whole coordinate
913   transformation. The insight behind this paper is that the first challenge is the core challenge of the
914   problem while the latter one could be solved by implementing the transformation as a neural network.
915   In other words, the flexible forming of a symbolic tree structure (capable of capturing the basic nested
916   part-whole relationship) within a pure neural network is the hard problem that challenges the neural
917   network models. The second problem, implementing a coordinate transformation, is more compatible
      with the neural networks: such transformation could be realized as a (feedforward) neural network.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      In this work, we focus on how to represent the part-whole hierarchy within a pure neural network
919   model through emergent nested neuronal coherence. The coordinate of objects is assumed to be
920   already aligned in a pixel-wise manner between the whole-level and part-level so that the coordination
921   transformation is reduced to the inclusion mapping (similar to identity mapping). However, since the
922   parsing tree is realized within a pure neural network, the mechanism is compatible with more general
923   coordination transformation: it could be realized by replacing the pixel-wise gating with a trainable
924   neural network, which parameterizes the coordinate transformation.
925   (3) How many level are there?
926
927   In the representation hypothesis, we separate the entire representation space into discrete number of
928
      object levels. Therefore, different levels are explicitly distinguished before-hand. However, different
      from “discrete slots to represent object”, the discretization of representation into levels is a reasonable
929
      setting. The underlining hypothesis is that while object representation can be diverse and uncertain,
930
      human-vision only accounts for very limited finite number of levels at each instant. Although there
931   are many object levels in the external world (from galaxy to atom), only a limited fraction of these
932   levels are mapped to the internal representation space to form perception. For example, Hinton (2021)
933   argued that 5 level is enough to account for human vision. The part-level and whole-level is a relative
934   concept, so that a given object can be represented in either level based on the context. In other word,
935   the internal finite number of object levels can be flexibly reused to account for different external
936   object levels. This argument is the motivation to discretize the representation space explicitly into
937   object-levels. But at each object level, objects are represented in a distributed manner, by neuronal
938   coherence, instead of slots. As a result, the hierarchical relationship is also represented in a distributed
939
      manner.
940   In this paper, we show the part-whole hierarchy of two levels: whole and part. However, this minimal
941   structure could be naturally extended to account for more levels, since the form of interaction between
942   levels is mostly irrelevant to how many levels are there or which level it is in. All levels could share
943   a unified form of cross-level interaction and within-level interaction. Therefore, by stacking the
944   columns along the hierarchy, more levels are accounted for. As discussed in Hinton (2021), up to five
945
      levels are sufficient to realize human-like vision.
946   (4) Synthetic image
947
      In this paper, we use synthetic images to demonstrate how to represent the part-whole hierarchy. The
948
      benefit of using the synthetic image is that: (1) a common sense reasonable part-whole relationship
949   is known beforehand as ground truth, therefore it is more convenient to explicitly evaluate the
950   representation and test the capability. (2) The ground truth assignment of objects (part/whole) is
951   known, which could be utilized to evaluate the neuronal coherence. The weak side of a ground
952   truth is that such explicit assignment of part-whole ignores the ambiguity of parsing the scene: the
953   parsing could depend on many factors like prior knowledge, attention, goal, internal state, and so on.
954   Besides, parsing a real-world image without explicit part-whole hierarchy might be challenging for
955   other reasons (overlap, background, etc.). However, recent models (Hinton et al., 2018; Sun et al.,
956   2021; Garau et al., 2022) that claim to solve the part-whole problem actually resemble performing
957
      hierarchical feature extractions. Such confusion is partly due to the ambiguity of the part-whole
      relation, object definition and the complexity of features in the real-world images, which confuse
958
      the symbolic structure. Therefore, taking the present status of the problem1 and the challenges the
959
      problem implicates2 into account, one desirable roadmap is to focus on explicit evaluation based on
960   synthetic data first (so that it is easier to interpret whether the mechanism works) and then gradually
961   generalize the outcome to increasingly complex datasets in the future.
962
963

965   in the parameters of DAE’s weight, which in turn determines the dynamical property of the Composer.

967   given a visual scene of a face (Fig.1a), a human observer should have already had the concept of

       composed of spiking neurons without explicit supervision. The mechanism by principle is not limited
1003   to a certain modality or certain object type. Thus, it may help develop human-like perception systems.

1043   The Victor-Purpura metric (VP-metric) is a classical non-Euclidean metric to measure the distance
1044
       between arbitrary spike trains for evaluating the temporal coding in the visual cortex (Victor &

1045

1046

1047   treated as (binary) “Strings” and the distance from one spike train to the other can be defined as the
1048   number (cost) of “operations” to transform one spike train to the other (“Edit distance” of Strings).

1051

1052

1053   other. The Victor-Purpura distance is defined as the minimal cost to transform a spike train to the
1054   other (Fig.11):

1120   neuron belongs to which part or whole object), neuronal coherence (synchrony) is measured as the
1121   inner-cluster coherence of spike trains: the “inner-cluster” similarity and “inter-cluster” separability.
1122   Specifically, if we take (1) each neuron as a sample, (2) the spike train of each neuron as features,
1123   (3) the ground truth assignment as the clustering assignment (each pixel on ground truth correspond
1124   to a neuron in SCS), (4) the VP-metric as the distance measure, then, the inner-cluster coherence
1125   (Silhouette Score) of the ground-truth-induced cluster assignment is exactly the coherence measure
1126   of neuronal coherence. In other words, the high Silhouette Score indicates that the spike trains of
1127   neurons of the same cell assembly are closer to each other in terms of VP-metric, which can be

1688   The Composer is inspired by the neural syntax hypothesis(Buzsáki, 2010), which argues that the
1689   hierarchical organization of cell assemblies should be readout by hierarchical organization of time
1690   windows. In fact, it is likely in the Composer that the hierarchical organization of time window also
1691
       encourages the emergence of hierarchical cell assemblies. Motivated by the hypothesis, spikes in SCS
       are all integrated within a narrow time window (implemented as a integration function Ii of certain
1692
       time constant τi , i ∈ {1, 2, D, Γ}) before the downstream processing. The time scale parameters
1693
       related to the model are shown in Table.2, which has appeared in eq.1 to eq.7 in the main text. T is

1696   neurons and τδ2 is that of the whole-level spiking neurons. τr1 is the absolute refractory period of
1697   part-level spiking neurons and τr2 is that of the whole-level spiking neurons. τ1 is the integrative
1698   time window from part-level SCS to part-level DAE (eq.3) and τ2 is that from the whole-level SCS to
1699   whole-level DAE (eq.6). τD is the integrative time window from the part-level SCS to the whole-level
1700   SCS (eq.7, right). τΓ is the time window of the top-down feedback from the whole-level SCS to
1701   the part-level SCS (eq.7, left). τd′ is the coupling delay of the cross-level top-down feedback from
1702

1801   dataset, the number of assemblies increases. Given larger number of assemblies as tree nodes, the
1802   possible configurations of the parse tree (correct or incorrect) gets exponentially larger. Therefore, the
1803   searching becomes harder because the searching space enlarges exponentially. However, while hard
1804   refractory period forces the system to switch among different states (spike fires at wrong timings),

1806   the hard refractory period constraints the available next firing timing). Thus, introducing a relative
1807   refractory period can help the system jump out of the local minimum, once it ’finds’ much better
1808   states. It is likely that for this reason, enforcing g = 0 in Fig.9 in the main text slightly degrades the

       A.7    B IO - PLAUSIBILITY
2107
2108   In this section, we list and provide detailed discussion about the biological correlates of the design of
2109   the Composer in hope of inspiring future innovations.
2110
2111         1. Delay-coupled oscillatory neural network: In a recent work, Singer (2021) describes the
2112
                cerebral cortex as a delay-coupled recurrent oscillator network, which is very different from
                the architecture in the deep learning field. In the Composer, such architecture is captured,
2113
                integrated within the deep learning framework, and acting as an essential ingredient of
2114
                the mechanism (Section.A.5.3). The coupling delay provide a time window for alternative
2115            cell assemblies compete to emerge and disappear in order . In other words, the coupling
2116            delay makes the system non-Markovian and of infinite dimension (approximately, see
2117            Izhikevich (2006)), so that the coding scheme and the capability of associative memory is
2118            much enlarged11
2119         2. Feed-forward and feed-back pathway along the cortical hierarchy: In general, the cortex
2120            is organized into similar columns (Douglas & Martin, 2004), which is composed of six
2121            layers from layer I to layer VI (Fig.2d,g). Cortex are spatially organized corresponding
2122            to the spatial structure of the external physical world and hierarchically organized into
2123            levels. These basic features are captured in our model and act as essential elements for
2124            representation hypothesis: the representation of the part-whole hierarchy depends on such
2125            spatial and hierarchical organization. Notably, in a neurology paper (Markov et al., 2013),
2126
                the author also specifies the organization of the feedforward and feedback hierarchy. In
                detail, there are recognizable feedforward and feedback pathways between layer II/III of
2127
                higher and lower level columns. This corresponds to the cross-level interaction between the
2128
                part-level SCS and whole-level SCS in our model. Lastly, Markov et al. (2013) also shows
2129            that a long-distance feedforward path from lower level to high level exists in layer IIIb.
2130            These are realized as the skip connections from external driving input (x) to the whole level
2131            SCS layer in the Composer (eq.4). More generally, the feedback from higher levels contains
2132            signals originating from both layer II/III and layer V/VI (corresponding to the latent space
2133            in the Composer). It is left to future work to study the “cross-level” interaction between
2134            pixel-level SCS and the latent space of DAEs.
2135         3. Time scale hierarchy: Along the cortical hierarchy, there is a gradient of timescale hierarchy
2136            (Mahjoory et al., 2019)—’We found that the dominant peak frequency in a brain area
2137            decreases significantly, gradually and robustly along the posterior-anterior axis, following
2138            the global cortical hierarchy from early sensory to higher order areas’. Such time scale
2139            hierarchy is exploited in our model as the basis for representing hierarchical inclusion
2140            relationships among part-level cell assemblies and whole-level cell assemblies. However,
2141
                since the frequency spectra are not unlimited, the capability of the part-whole representation
                may be limited by the range of the total frequency bands. Here, we treat such limitation as a
2142
                shared weakness of our model and the brain, since the temporal resolution of cross-frequency
2143
                coupling has shown to be a constraint for the capability of working memory of humans
2144            (7 ± 2) (Nicola & Clopath, 2019). Indeed, Hinton (2021) argued that human also has a
2145            limited range of the hierarchy depth to represent instantaneously (∼ 5 levels). At least three
2146            frequency bands could be explored in the future: gamma band, alpha band, and theta band.
2147         4. Topographical mapping: As mentioned above, the spatial organization of the cortical
2148            column has a topographical correspondence to the physical world, called the topographical
2149            mapping (Eickhoff et al., 2017). Such location-wise representation is exploited in Hinton
2150            (2021) as a core basis to represent the part-whole hierarchy and is similarly essential for
2151            our model. The topographical relationship enables the representation of objects as a set of
2152            (grouped) spatial regions with correlated activation patterns (grouped pixels with similar
2153            spike trains in the Composer or grouped columns with identical vectors, so called “identical
2154            islands of vectors” in GLOM). Besides, the location-wise representation also helps to clarify
2155
                the inclusion relationship between whole and part across the hierarchy, both in our model
                and in GLOM. For example, the spatial region for part objects should also be spatially
2156
                covered by their whole objects. However, spatial organization itself is not sufficient to
2157
                represent part-whole hierarchy, because multiple co-activated features within the same level
2158
          11
2159         Each memory is realized as a trajectory instead of a single fixed point. The trajectory is the combination of
       transient fixed points so that the attractive states "in-principle" expand combinatorially or exponentially.


                                                               40
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2160
               can lead to ambiguity (the binding problem see Malsburg (1994)). As a result, we need
2161           similarity measure or coherence measure to specify “which is which”. This insight motivates
2162           the representation hypothesis of both spatial hierarchy in terms of topographical mapping
2163           and temporal hierarchy in terms of neuronal coherence.
2164
            5. Abstract away the cortical BU/TD processing as autoencoder: Predictive coding (Rao
2165
               & Ballard, 1999) was first proposed by Dana H. Ballard and Rajesh P. N. Rao to explain
2166
               the extra-classical receptive-field effects in primary visual cortex. Then, the predictive
2167           coding theory was mapped to the canonical circuit of cortical circuit (Bastos et al., 2012)
2168           and served as a unified theory of brain function (Friston, 2010). In the predictive coding
2169           model, the bottom-up and top-down feedback attention (BU/TD) is formalized as the
2170           autoencoder architecture, and the reconstruction error should be minimized to achieve
2171           minimal “prediction error” or “surprise”. Such architecture is exploited in our model to
2172           realize the inner-column bottom-up / top-down pathways (BU/TD) and reconstruction error
2173           is minimized as the objective function of training. Interestingly, such predictive feedback
2174           is also related to the temporal coherence both in the cortex (Engel et al., 2001) and in
2175
               the Composer. Interestingly, “abstracting away certain part of a dynamical system in
               neuroscience as a learnable neural network” has the advantage of building-up more flexible
2176
               dynamical models by training on large datasets. Therefore, it is plausible to generate new
2177
               hypothesis beyond the traditional ones (Eckstein et al., 2023; Peterson et al., 2021).
2178
2179        6. Sparse code and dense code: The dual coding scheme in the cortical circuits has been
2180           recognized when representing features: ultra-sparse coding in the superficial layer (layer
2181
               II/III) and dense coding in deeper layer (layer V/VI) (Tang et al., 2018; Wang, 2018). While
               the latter encodes the statistical aspects of features, the former might additionally encodes
2182
               the relationships. In this work, the dual coding scheme is realized as the sparse spike code
2183
               in SCS and real-valued dense vector code in the DAE’s latent space. The synchrony in the
2184           SCS additionally encodes the relationship among objects.
2185
2186        7. Relative refractory period: Strictly speaking, the absolute refractory period (ARP) refers to
2187
               the phase immediately after a spike initialization (∼ 2 ms). The later phase where a spike is
               harder to be triggered (though not impossible) is referred to as the relative refractory period
2188
               (RRP) (Dayan & Abbott, 2001). If we take 1-time step as 1 millisecond in real-world time,
2189
               then the absolute refractory period is around 10 milliseconds (Table.2) in the Composer,
2190           which is much longer than the strict absolute refractory period. Therefore, the picture
2191           should be clarified as follows: the excitability of spiking neurons after a spike increases
2192           gradually, in the form of 1 − e−t/τ . At the beginning phase, the excitability is low enough
2193           to prevent the neuron from firing a second spike given the conventional stimulus strength,
2194           but since the excitability increases rapidly during this phase, the relative period length is
2195           small compared to the whole refractory period. This beginning phase where the excitability
2196           is low enough compared to the stimulus strength but of fast increasing rate is treated as
2197           the absolute refractory period. In contrast, during the rest of the period, the excitability
2198           has recovered to the extent where neurons might generate a second spike but with a much
2199
               lower probability. Since the recovery is much slower during the second phase, the temporal
               range is much longer than that of the first phase. This slow recovery phase is modeled as
2200
               the relative refractory period in this work. The total refractory period can expand from tens
2201
               of milliseconds to much longer, depending on the channel type on the axon of the neuron
2202           (Gerstner et al., 2014). On the other hand, it is also conventional in numerical modeling
2203           that the absolute refractory period is modeled no less than 5 ms. In sum, the time scale of
2204           refractoriness fits the conventional setting of biological systems.
2205
            8. Dentritic computation of pyramidal cell: The driving signal and modulatory signal are
2206
               distinguished in the cortical circuit (Lee & Sherman, 2010), where the driving signal acts
2207
               on the proximal site of dendrites (near to the soma) and the modulatory signal acts on the
2208           distal sites (far from soma) (Spruston, 2008). The two types of inputs interact in a non-linear
2209           way. Such non-linear interaction between driving input and modulatory input is captured as
2210           the multiplication between the bottom-up integration and top-down modulation, realized as
2211           the pyramidal cells in the SCS (Fig.3ab in the main text). Such a gating effect inside the
2212           column is essential for the emergence of cell assemblies and the gating effect across levels
2213           is essential for the coordination of cell assemblies into nested temporal structure, so called
               nested neuronal coherence.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2214
            9. Coincidence detector: Abeles (1982) argued that cortical neurons in superficial layers
2215           are coincidence detectors, which detect sparse synchronous events within a narrow time
2216           window. In our model, the time constant of the integrative time window is small (τ1 ∼
2217           2ms, τ2 ∼ 5ms). As a result, the inner-level bottom-up integration of spiking activity in
2218           the superficial layer (pixel-wise SCS) is modeled as coincidence detectors. Such a narrow
2219           time window enables two things: (1) stochastic spikes fired at extremely adjacent time steps
2220           should be detected as a single event; (2) the temporal resolution of the synchronous event is
2221           kept within a small time-scale (∼ τ1 , τ2 ). Both are important to form a high-quality parse
2222           tree. Interestingly, a similar concept has also been developed in Hinton (2021), named as
2223
               ’coincidence filtering’.
2224       10. Meta-stability of cortical network: “...Single-trial analyses of ensemble activity in alert
2225           animals demonstrate that cortical circuit dynamics evolve through temporal sequences of
2226           metastable states. Metastability has been studied for its potential role in sensory coding,
2227           memory, and decision-making. Yet, very little is known about the network mechanisms
2228
               responsible for its genesis...” (Mazzucato et al., 2015). In this work, we build such a system
               of metastable states (Fig.2) by integrating the spiking neural network (SNN) and artificial
2229
               neural network (DAE as ANN) and further demonstrates its computational role in vision.
2230
2231       11. Neuronal assembly as code words: “A widely discussed hypothesis in neuroscience is that
2232           transiently active ensembles of neurons, known as “cell assemblies,” underlie numerous
2233
               operations of the brain, from encoding memories to reasoning. However, the mechanisms
               responsible for the formation and disbanding of cell assemblies and the temporal evolution
2234
               of cell assembly sequences are not well understood...I suggest that the hierarchical organi-
2235
               zation of cell assemblies may be regarded as a neural syntax...”(Buzsáki, 2010). Besides,
2236           assemblies are shown to be able to realize arbitrary computation function (Papadimitriou
2237           et al., 2019). By combining machine learning models and neuroscientific constraints, we
2238           show how cell assembly can be transiently formed and disbanded, and be organized into a
2239           sequence at each level, and be hierarchically organized into spatial-temporal nested structure
2240           to express the neural syntax. More generally, various features, even of a continuous nature
2241           are represented as neuronal assemblies in the brain (population binary code), this coding
2242           scheme provides the basis to enable the Composer to deal with continuous features (RGB
2243           color) in the future (Stockman, 2019). The reservoir of neuronal assemblies could be more
2244
               efficiently realized in neuromorphic devices (Pei et al., 2019) to account for larger range of
               features. From the viewpoint of a recent paper on assembly formation (Miehl et al., 2022),
2245
               our model generate the assemblies by DAE-induced symmetry-breaking, which is one of
2246
               the mechanism for assembly-generation.
2247
2248       12. Temporal binding theory and feature integration theory: Temporal binding theory
2249
               (Engel & Singer, 2001; Malsburg, 1994) and feature integration theory (Wolfe, 2020) are
               two mainstream theories to solve the binding problem: how distributed information is bound
2250
               together to form the whole. The former is based on time coding and neuronal synchrony
2251
               while the latter is based on top-down attention searching on a spatial map. The temporal
2252           synchrony, temporal coding, top-down attention, and spatial map are all captured in this
2253           model. Thus it is promising to explore whether it could serve as a canonical model to unify
2254           the two theories.
2255
           13. The role of sequential / spatial attention: The binding of distributed features or the
2256
               segmentation of objects are also argued to be related to spatially-shift attentions (Roelfsema,
2257           2023). In the Composer, such spatial / sequential attention co-emerges during the simulation
2258           (Fig.6e). Beyond theories that only highlight the role of attention, we further treat sequential
2259           attention and neuronal coherence as a “symbiotic couple”, and can be unified in a bigger
2260           picture of associative memory: both attention and neuronal coherence are partial description
2261           of certain-type associative memory (Fig.2). This bigger picture is consistent with recent
2262           findings (Ramsauer et al., 2020), which suggests that associative memory and attention is
2263           actually the same thing, but viewed from different angles.
2264       14. Temporal-spatial theory of consciousness: “We postulate four different neuronal mecha-
2265           nisms accounting for the different dimensions of consciousness: (i) “temporospatial nested-
2266           ness” of the spontaneous activity accounts for the level/state of consciousness as the neural
2267           predisposition of consciousness (NPC); (ii) “temporospatial alignment” of the pre-stimulus
               activity accounts for the content/form of consciousness as the neural prerequisite of con-

               sciousness (preNCC); (iii) “temporo-spatial expansion” of early stimulus-induced activity
2269           accounts for phenomenal consciousness as neural correlates of consciousness (NCC); (iv)
2270          “temporo-spatial globalization” of late stimulus-induced activity accounts for the cognitive
2271           features of consciousness as the neural consequence of consciousness (NCCcon).” (Northoff
2272           & Huang, 2017). In this work, the temporospatial nestedness emerges and indicates the
2273           perceptual awareness (eg. recognizing the part-whole relationship), and the temporospatial
2274           alignment clarifies the content/form of the scene. The underlining assumption is that: the
2275           perceptual awareness emerges only if a part-whole relationship (of a visual scene or of a
2276           cognitive concept or of a plan or of a solution of certain problems) is well-organized in the
2277
               internal representation of the brain (neural syntax). The quality of the representation reflect
               the “level of awareness” (eg. clear sight or dreamy sight). This assumption might be verified
2278
               or falsified in future works.
2279
2280       15. Gamma oscillation and perceptual awareness: “...One theory suggests that rhythmic
2281
               synchronization of neural discharges in the gamma band (around 40 Hz) may provide the
               necessary spatial and temporal links that bind together the processing in different brain
2282
               areas to build a coherent percept. In this article we propose that this mechanism could
2283
               also be used more generally for the construction of object representations that are driven
2284           by sensory input or internal, top-down processes...” (Tallon-Baudry & Bertrand, 1999). In
2285           this work, the spiking activity in the SCS approximately oscillates at the gamma band (tens
2286           of milliseconds if each time-step is regarded as 1 millisecond.) The gamma oscillation
2287           dynamically groups neurons into object representations (the representation hypothesis in the
2288           main text).
2289       16. Preconfigured brain: In a recent “inside-out” conceptual framework of the brain, as
2290           Gyorgy Buzaki put it—“...This is the organization I call the preformed or preconfigured
2291           brain: a preexisting dictionary of nonsense words combined with internally generated
2292           syntactical rules. The neuronal syntax with its hierarchically organized rhythms determines
2293           the lengths of neuronal messages and shapes their combinations. Thus, brain syntax preexists
2294           prior to meaningful content...“Preconfigured” usually means experience-independent. The
2295
               backbone of brain connectivity and its emerging dynamics are genetically defined. In a
               broader sense, the term “preconfigured” or “preexisting” is also often used to refer to a
2296
               brain with an existing knowledge base, ....In the preconfigured brain model, learning is
2297
               a matching process, in which preexisting neuronal patterns, initially nonsensical to the
2298           organism, acquire meaning with the help of experience...” (Buzsáki, 2019). Thus, the
2299           well-trained DAE in this paper could be treated as an essential preconfigured structure due
2300           to genetic codes or the life-long calibration of the sensory-action loop, which captures a
2301           range of object prototypes. Plasticity may only provide a secondary role to increase the
2302           precision of the ’good-enough’ model (Buzsáki, 2019). Overall, the Composer is highly
2303           motivated by the Buzaki’s insights.
2304       17. Plasticity: One of the designs that may depart from biology is that the connection weights
2305           are trained based on a gradient-based method instead of a correlation-based method, like
2306           Hebbian rule or spike timing plasticity (Gerstner et al., 2014). However, this could be
2307           explained from two points of view. First, as argued above, the well-trained DAE could
2308           be regarded as the preconfigured structure which is gradually searched from evolution
2309
               (amount to stochastic gradient-based search). Second, since the DAE structure in this
               model is relatively simple, the training objective (minimizing reconstruction error, the
2310
               difference between input and feedback) could be interpreted as increasing the correlation
2311
               between sensory neurons and modulatory neurons, so that the gradient-based training equals
2312           correlation-based plasticity. Indeed, Melchior & Wiskott (2019) shows that gradient-based
2313           learning and Hebbian plasticity can be unified in case of a simple autoencoder. Further,
2314           we could imagine that there is a two-stage learning algorithm, like the wake-sleep cycle:
2315           during the day, the system infers entities based on learned weight, during the night, the
2316           learned objects replay and the system efficiently updates the weight by association, which
2317           corresponds to the training phase of the DAE. Similar treatment has also been discussed in
2318           Hinton (2021).
2319       18. Inner-layer recurrent connection: Another design feature that may depart from the
2320           biological brain is that the spike coding space (SCS) itself is not recurrent in our model.
2321           However, this could also be explained from at least two points of view. First, the feedforward
               and recurrent connection usually have different functional roles in the cortical circuit,

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2322
               and have different levels of domination. For example, layer IV in the visual cortex are
2323           mainly feedforward and the recurrent effect are relatively weak. As a result, the inner-layer
2324           recurrence of SCS are treated as secondary compared to the recurrence of inner-column
2325           top-down feedback or inter-level top-down feedback. So that it is temporally ignored for
2326           simplicity. Further, the localized inner-level recurrence may play a secondary role (different
2327           from that of top-down feedback) to speed up the convergence by forming a grid frame (by
2328           spatially-organized connection) to encode the prior of the proximity property of objects
2329           (Gestalt principles(Wagemans et al., 2012)). Secondly, the entire two-layer column could
2330           be recognized as a single layer, with DAE parameterizing the recurrent connection weight
2331
               among spiking neurons, similar to Dmitry Krotov’s idea in Kozachkov et al. (2023). And the
               general mechanism still works. In other words, there is no restriction to view the two-level
2332
               system as a column or a layer. In either case, the models maintain their bio-plausibility.

2334       19. Polychronization refers to the generalization of absolute synchronization into structured
2335
               asynchrony. As argued in Izhikevich (2006), due to the heterogeneity and conduction delay
               of the neural system, polychrony is more plausible than absolute synchrony. While the
2336
               externally observed spike firing time is asynchronous, the arriving time of asynchronous
2337
               spikes to downstream readout neurons is (internally) synchronous. In other words, the shift in
2338           spiking time is balanced out by the shift in conduction delay. According to the Buzsaki’s idea
2339           (Buzsáki, 2010), the more rigorous definition of cell assemblies should be based on internal
2340           observation (downstream readout neurons) instead of external observation (human observer).
2341           Therefore, polychronous representation is in essence also synchronous representation. In
2342           Composer, due to the cross-level coupling-delay the part-level cell assemblies and whole-
2343           level cell assemblies seems to have a fixed temporal shift (Table.1). However, from a readout
2344           neuron perspective (eg. SCS spiking neurons), the spike arrival of whole level feedback
2345           (γ) is just in time to enslave the spike firing of part-level SCS neurons. For this reason, we
2346
               ignored the slight temporal shift when computing the score (Section.A.4.6). Besides, to
               make the visualization more intuitive, we compensate the temporal shift for visualization
2347
               in Fig.6, Fig.7 in the main text: synchronization is more intuitive to capture the nestedness
2348
               than polychronization, although they all indicates the coherence state.

2404   A.8.3    I NTRODUCING THE AGGLOMERATOR
2405
2406
       The Agglomerator (Garau et al., 2022) is a GLOM (Hinton, 2021) inspired implementation to deal
       with the part-whole hierarchy. The basic idea is to use similarity measures among vectors, which are
2407
       called columns, to dynamically group neural representation into "identical islands of vectors". The
2408
       spatial inclusion relationship among islands is interpreted as the part-whole hierarchy. We show the
2409   simplified architecture and representation scheme of the Agglomerator in Fig.21, in case the reader is
2410   not familiar with the model.
2411
2412
       More specifically, columns are organized into different levels. At each level, columns are spatially
       located on a grid mesh, like the topographical mapping. Different levels have different spatial scales,
2413
       reflected by different radii of horizontal connection among columns within the levels.
2414
2415   To put it in a nutshell, the Agglomerator also attempts to exploit (spatial) neuronal coherence
2416   to dynamically form the tree node at each level and to coordinate the nodes naturally by spatial
2417   nestedness. It is the comparability of the representation / architecture that we choose Agglomerator
2418
       as an appropriate benchmarks and recent SOTA. However, as shown in Table.7, they failed to form
       the node-level representation at all in the four explicit datasets, indicated by the Part Score and Whole
2419
       Score lower than 0. Since in GLOM, hierarchical coordination is not reflected by the cross-level
2420
       similarity among vectors (but only by spatial relationship of islands), the Nest Score does not reveal
2421   more insight except that representations between levels are not as coherent as in the Composer.

2561   A.8.7    T HE FAILURE OF THE AGGLOMERATOR
2562
2563   As shown in Table.7, the Agglomerator failed in all cases. As far as we know, in the original
2564   paper of the Agglomerator (Garau et al., 2022), the representation is not quantitatively evaluated in
2565
       terms of the hierarchical structure but for classification accuracy and object detection. Also, in the
       visualization, the representation across levels is more likely to extract features at different scales,
2566
       instead of forming an interpretable part-whole hierarchy. As far as we understand, although the
2567
       motivation and basic idea of the representation hypothesis are very promising, the representation
2568   hypothesis is not explicitly verified or falsified by quantitative metric. As shown in Table.7, it is
2569   worth re-evaluating the parsing ability of the Agglomerator on images with more ’explicit’ part-whole
2570   relationships and more appropriate ’quantitative metrics’ as we do.
2571
       Another explanation is the symmetry in our dataset. In the original paper, the seeming parsing of
2572
       different parts is likely due to the different colors associated with the parts. In other words, It is
2573
       observed that the Agglomerator only groups locations of similar color into islands (like feature
2574   extraction), instead of parsing the object based on knowledge of the object-centric representation
2575   (e.g. same object can have different colors and different objects can share the same color). To
2576   put it in another way, the grouping in the Agglomerator is due to the external asymmetry in the
2577   scene, e.g. different colors. However, in our dataset, all objects have the same color (black), and the
2578   symmetry-breaking process for grouping needs to occur internally. Therefore, the symmetry (shared
2579   color among objects) can challenge the Agglomerator to parse the scene.
2580   On the other hand, failure on our synthetic dataset and metrics neither excludes its potential validity
2581   in other cases nor excludes the possibility that it can be improved to solve the problems. Compared
2582   with our model, the Aggglomerator’s architecture is more flexible in dealing with real-world images,
2583   and training on larger real-world datasets can be very different from training on small-scale synthetic
2584   datasets. Therefore, the limitation of our dataset and metric is also notable. Besides, the downsampling
2585   of the ground truth is likely to magnify the failure of the Agglomerator since the Silhouette Score is
2586   more sensitive to incoherence when the sample number is lower (w × h < W × H). It is partially the
2587   reason why the score tends to be lower than 0. However, the results indeed show that SOTA models
2588
       can fail when explicitly evaluated.
2589   To put it in a nutshell, we highlight that the problem of representing part-whole hierarchy needs to be
2590   more explicitly evaluated to verify or falsify the proposed model. As far as we know, representing the
2591   part-whole hierarchy is far from being solved and combining real-world data and synthetic data is
       essential to evaluate models in the future.

2605   On the other hand, the Part Score and Nest Score undergo an initial descending period before going
2606   up. Here, we explain this phenomenon: Compared with the very starting phase, where spikes
2607   are randomly and densely fired, the emergence of whole-level squares around 500 ∼ 1000 time
2608   steps provide new conditions on the part level. While this has benefits in the long run, it could
2609

2610

2611

2612   the self-correcting / searching process, after the whole-level objects are recognized. Fortunately, after
2613   a short period of self-correcting, the Scores go up again and gradually converge to expected neuronal
2614   coherence as in other cases.
2615

2616
       one hand, the Composer needs to distinguish 6 wholes and 12 parts. On the other hand, 6 whole
2617
       objects and 12 part objects impose 61 2 potential combinations of part-whole relationships (each part
2618   can choose to belong to one of the six wholes). Therefore, it takes time to search for / sample the
2619   optimal configuration. Even if the parts/wholes are grouped by neuronal coherence, there is a high
2620   possibility that the parts and wholes are not well coordinated to form proper nested structure. Since
2621   the neural computation in the brain can also be regarded as sampling (Buesing et al., 2011), these
2622   challenges may also cause problems in perception like the binding problem (Engel & Singer, 2001;
2623   Von der Malsburg, 1999). Therefore, on the Ts dataset, the Nest Score lags behind the other scores.
2624   Convergence on Double-Digit MNIST is also challenging for the Composer because the objects are of
2625   much higher diversity. Therefore, it is harder for the Composer to clearly distinguish the objects and
2626   to form well-synchronized cell assemblies. Therefore, the Part Score is lower than other scores and
2627   the variance is higher than in other cases. However, it is surprising that the Composer still achieves
2628   good parsing, indicated by the convergent Nest Score, even though objects are less recognizable.

2888   A.9.4    M ULTI - STABILITY
2889
2890   In Fig.1 of the main text, we demonstrated one of the challenge of representing part-whole hierarchy
2891
       as the diverse interpretation of a given visual scene, based on self-consistency among contents. To
       showcase that Composer is capable of generating multiple part-whole relationship given an ambiguous
2892
       visual scene, we uses a new dataset, Teris dataset. Part objects are squares, and whole objects are
2893
       composed of 4 squares in various ways (Fig.30). So there are 15 possibilities.
2894
2895   We follow the same training protocol as introduced in A.6. Then we test the parsing result given an
2896   ambiguous image (Fig.30top-left), which is composed of two Teris objects. And there are alternative
2897
       interpretations, shown in Fig.30 middle and right respectively.
2898   To visualize the part-whole structure of spiking activity, we assign a color to each block in the image
2899   (Fig.30bottom-left), and color-code the neurons / spikes based on it. This process is similar to the
2900   main text. However, since the whole-level grouping is ambiguous, there is no "ground truth" at all
2901   and the coloring is only for visualization purpose.
2902   Surprisingly, we observed that two different type of part-whole hierarchy emerges, corresponding to
2903   different interpretation of the ambiguous image. This addiction result showcase the content-sensitive
2904   structure inference ability of Composer.

2909   principle, because the columns and cross-level pathways can be reused for multiple levels. Here, we
2910   provide an implementation of such generalization, where a three-level Composer are realized to parse
2911   samples in the SHOPs dataset. For SHOPs, We take the whole scene as the additional level: scene
2912
       level.
2913   As shown in Fig.31, hierarchically organized cell assemblies and hierarchical attention sequence
2914   emerges. Since the top level is the whole scene, there is no need to switch. So the scene assemblies
2915   slowly oscillate collectively and the top-level attention sustains. Here, for simplicity, we use whole-
       level ground truth to color the neuron / spikes at the scene-level.
