011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Mechanistic interpretability aims to understand the inner workings of large neural
014            networks by identifying circuits, or minimal subgraphs within the model that im-
015            plement algorithms responsible for performing specific tasks. These circuits are
016
               typically discovered and analyzed using a narrowly defined prompt format. How-
               ever, given the abilities of large language models (LLMs) to generalize across
017
               various prompt formats for the same task, it remains unclear how well these cir-
018
               cuits generalize. For instance, it is unclear whether the model’s generalization
019            results from reusing the same circuit components, the components behaving dif-
020            ferently, or the use of entirely different components. In this paper, we investigate
021            the generality of the indirect object identification (IOI) circuit in GPT-2 small,
022            which is well-studied and believed to implement a simple, interpretable algorithm.
023            We evaluate its performance on prompt variants that challenge the assumptions of
024            this algorithm. Our findings reveal that the circuit generalizes surprisingly well,
025            reusing all of its components and mechanisms while only adding additional input
026            edges. Notably, the circuit generalizes even to prompt variants where the original
027
               algorithm should fail; we discover a mechanism that explains this which we term
               S2 Hacking. Our findings indicate that circuits within LLMs may be more flexible
028
               and general than previously recognized, underscoring the importance of studying
029
               circuit generalization to better understand the broader capabilities of these models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Mechanistic interpretability (Elhage et al., 2021) is an increasingly prominent approach to under-
035
      standing the inner workings of large neural networks. Much work in this area is dedicated to discov-
036   ering circuits, which are subgraphs within the large model that faithfully represent how the model
037   solves a particular task of interest (Olah et al., 2020), by identifying attention heads and paths with
038   significant causal effects on the model output (Vig et al., 2020; Stolfo et al., 2023; Hanna et al.,
039   2023; Prakash et al., 2024). By studying these circuits, researchers aim to uncover simple, human-
040   interpretable algorithms that explain how the model solves the task.
041
      These circuits are typically analyzed only within the specific prompt format used to extract them.
042   However, modern LLMs can often solve the same task across various prompt formats. This raises
043   important questions about how a circuit generalizes when the prompt format is varied, especially
044   when the full model generalizes. For instance, it is unclear whether the model’s generalization
045   results from the reuse of the same circuit components, the components behaving differently, or the
046   use of entirely different components. Evaluating the generality of a circuit can provide a deeper
047   understanding of the circuit’s behavior and the range of scenarios in which the circuit serves as a
048   valid explanation for the full model’s performance.
049   The generality of circuits has significant implications for mechanistic interpretability. Since circuits
050   are typically extracted and evaluated using a specific set of prompts, there is no prior expectation for
051   them to generalize. In the worst case, a different prompt format might require a completely different
052   circuit. Ideally, however, the same circuit would solve the task across all prompt variants, providing
053   a general and reliable explanation for how the model performs the task. Figure 1 presents several
      hypotheses for how a circuit could generalize as the prompt format changes. Given that the goal of

      Indirect Object Identification (IOI) is one of the most well-studied tasks in the mechanistic inter-
075
      pretability literature. The model is given a prompt such as “When John and Mary went to the store,
076   John gave a drink to        ”, with the expected answer being “Mary”. Wang et al. (2023) discovered
077   a circuit in GPT-2 small that is said to implement a simple three-step algorithm: (1) identify all
078   previous names in the sentence (“Mary”, “John”, “John”); (2) remove all names that are duplicated
079   (“John”); and (3) output the remaining name (“Mary”). We refer to this as the IOI algorithm.
080
      This algorithm appears to be largely agnostic to prompt structure aside from the names, suggesting
081
      the circuit may generalize broadly. However, it should clearly fail on prompts where both names are
082   duplicated, since removing all duplicated names leaves no obviously correct answer. For example,
083   consider the prompt “When Mary and John went to the store, Mary was happy. John gave a drink to
084        ”, which breaks the IOI algorithm by adding three words. Yet, GPT-2 small returns the correct
085   answer with ∼ 89% confidence. If the model can be explained by a circuit that implements the IOI
086   algorithm, its ability to generalize to inputs where the algorithm itself predicts failure reveals a clear
087   discrepancy and raises critical questions about the generality of the IOI circuit.
088   In this paper, we investigate the generality of the IOI circuit. We study its performance and behavior
089   on two prompt variants, DoubleIO and TripleIO, which are designed to challenge the assumptions
090   of the IOI algorithm. Our key findings are:
091
092   (a) The IOI circuit vastly outperforms the full model on prompt variants where the IOI algorithm
093       would completely fail. Despite this, most of the attention heads in the circuit still retain their
094       functionalities as specified in Wang et al. (2023).
095
096   (b) We discover a mechanism in the IOI circuit, which we call S2 Hacking, that explains how the
097
          circuit is able to outperform the model on the prompt variants. However, S2 Hacking only
          appears in these variants and does not arise in the base IOI prompt format.
098
099
      (c) On discovering new circuits for the prompt variants, we find that they reuse all components of
100       the base IOI circuit, only adding edges from additional input tokens. The DoubleIO and TripleIO
101       circuits show 92% and 85% edge overlap respectively, and 100% node overlap in both cases.
102
103   These findings reveal that the base IOI circuit generalizes surprisingly well beyond its original task
104   design, reusing many of its heads and paths to handle prompt variants effectively. They also highlight
105   the value of studying circuit generalization, to assess the external validity of the algorithms they are
106   claimed to implement and unveil deeper underlying complexities. Overall, these results represent
107   a significant step towards understanding the more general capabilities of large neural networks and
      further demonstrate the promise of mechanistic interpretability in achieving this goal.

      2      BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      In this work, we evaluate the circuit for indirect object identification (IOI) in GPT-2 small (Wang
111   et al., 2023) using variants of the prompt format that was originally used to discover the circuit. We
112   refer to the original prompt format as base IOI. In the example from Figure 1, “John” and “Mary”
113   are referred to as the subject (S) and indirect object (IO) tokens respectively, and the IO token is
114   the expected answer. Each base IOI prompt contains one instance of the indirect object token (IO)
115   and two instances of the subject token (S1, S2). We later introduce variants of this prompt that can
116   include multiple instances of the indirect object token (IO1, IO2).
117   As in Wang et al. (2023), we measure the performance of circuits and the full model using the logit
118   difference metric, which is defined as the difference between the log probabilities of the IO token
119   and the S token. A larger positive logit difference indicates that the model predicts the correct token
120   (IO) with higher probability than the incorrect token (S).
121
      The base IOI circuit contains a set of distinct attention head types, each of which performs a specific
122
      function in the overall mechanism of the circuit. We include the base IOI circuit diagram in Figure
123   7, and further details can be found in Wang et al. (2023). We focus on the following head types:
124
125       • Name Mover heads are responsible for copying the correct token (IO) to the output. These
126         heads are active at the END token and attend to previous names in the sentence, copy the name
127         they attend to, and send it to the output.
128       • S-Inhibition heads ensure that the Name Mover heads focus on the IO token by modifying the
129         queries of the Name Mover heads and suppressing attention to the duplicated tokens (S1, S2).
130         They are active at the END token and attend to S2.
131       • Duplicate Token heads identify tokens that have appeared earlier in the sequence. These heads
132         are active at the duplicate instance of a token (S2), attend to its first occurrence (S1), and output
133         the position of the repeated token.
134       • Previous Token heads copy information from token S1 to the next token (S1+1), the token
135         facilitating the transfer of sequential token information.
136       • Induction heads serve a similar function to Duplicate Token Heads. They are active at the
137         position of the duplicated token (S2) and attend to the token that follows its previous instance
138         (S1+1). Their output is used as a pointer to S1, and to signal that S was duplicated.
139
      A small amount of prior work on circuit discovery has evaluated circuit generalization performance
140   using prompts outside of the format used for their discovery. However, these evaluations are often
141   limited and do not fully investigate how the circuit components and mechanisms behave under these
142   prompt changes. Wang et al. (2023) evaluated the base IOI circuit on adversarial examples similar to
143   the DoubleIO variant we use in this paper, and observed a drop in model performance. We build on
144   this finding by evaluating the circuit itself and explaining the mechanism behind the model’s lower
145   yet nontrivial performance. The Greater-Than (Hanna et al., 2023) and Arithmetic (Stolfo et al.,
146   2023) circuits were evaluated on prompt variants and included circuit overlap comparisons, though
147   these studies are mostly qualitative and not explored in further detail. The EAP-IG circuit discovery
148
      method (Hanna et al., 2024) was also evaluated using circuit overlap and cross-task faithfulness.

150   3      T ESTING G ENERALITY OF THE IOI C IRCUIT USING P ROMPT VARIANTS
151
152   Inferring a circuit from model behavior is a critical step, but it is not sufficient on its own—such a
153   circuit acts as a formal hypothesis about the problem-solving mechanisms of the full model, and this
154   hypothesis should be probed in various ways to determine the circumstances in which it holds. We
155   aim to assess the generality of the base IOI circuit and the range of circumstances in which its core
156   mechanisms persist. To this end, we introduce two variants of the base IOI prompt that, according
157   to the original description of the IOI algorithm, should be unsolvable by the base IOI circuit.
158   In this section, we present three key findings. First, we demonstrate that the unmodified base IOI
159   circuit is unexpectedly capable of sustaining its performance as we systematically vary the nature of
160   the task. Secondly, we demonstrate that the circuit significantly outperforms the model on the vari-
161   ants, showing consistently high logit difference scores while the model performance drops. Finally,
      we show that most of the attention heads in the circuit behave nearly identically to how they would

      on base IOI inputs. These findings suggest that the circuit effectively solves the task exactly as it
163   would on base IOI prompts, even though the necessary conditions for its success, as explained by
164   the IOI algorithm, are not met by the new prompts.

166   3.1   IOI P ROMPT VARIANTS
167
168   The IOI algorithm assumes that the subject (S) token is duplicated while the indirect object (IO)
169   token is not, and accordingly functions by detecting and suppressing the duplicated token. We can
170   test how much the performance of the base IOI circuit relies on this assumption by manipulating the
171   number of instances of the IO token. Our study includes the following range of prompts:
172
       Base IOI: When John and Mary went to the store, John gave a drink to  .
173
      DoubleIO: When John and Mary went to the store, Mary was happy. John gave a drink to   .
174
175    TripleIO: When John and Mary went to the store, Mary was happy. Mary sat on a bench. John
176
                 gave a drink to  .
177   In the DoubleIO variant, both the S and IO tokens are duplicated, making it unclear which one the
178   circuit should detect and suppress. In the TripleIO variant, the IO token appears three times and
179   the S token appears twice. Given the stated logic of the IOI algorithm, where the most frequently
180   duplicated token is suppressed, the circuit should return the S token since it now appears most
181   frequently in the prompt.

253   4   S2 H ACKING : P ERFORMANCE WITHOUT FAITHFULNESS
254
255   We observed in the previous section that the base IOI circuit significantly outperforms the full model
256   on the IOI prompt variants, with most of its components maintaining similar attention patterns to
257   base IOI inputs. In this section, we identify the source of the deviations in behavior and performance
258   between the base IOI circuit and the full model.
259   The basis of the discrepancy between the circuit and the model performance is a mechanism in the
260   base IOI circuit which we term S2 Hacking. In the base IOI circuit, the Induction and Duplicate
261   Token heads are primarily active at the S2 token, which is always the incorrect answer in each of the
262   IOI prompt variants. The outputs of these heads are then used by the S-Inhibition heads to suppress
263   the attention on the S1 and S2 tokens. This mechanism is a key factor in how the circuit is able to
264   return the correct (IO) token with high probability.
265   However, evaluating this circuit requires knocking out all paths that are not part of the circuit using
266   mean ablation (Wang et al., 2023). In the base IOI circuit, the S2 token is the only input token
267   that has a path from the input tokens to the END token that passes through the Duplicate Token and
268   Induction heads. This is because the paths from all other input tokens were shown to have a low
269   causal effect on the model output during the circuit discovery process. As a result, the paths from
      all other input tokens to these heads are knocked out.

291   We refer to this phenomenon as S2 Hacking. Note that this phenomenon only occurs in the base IOI
292   circuit, as it is a byproduct of the knockout procedure for evaluating the circuit and not actually how
293   the full model solves the task. This is evident from how much the circuit performance deviates from
294   the model performance. Additionally, S2 Hacking is not observed on base IOI inputs since they only
295   have one duplicated name (S), and was discovered by generalizing the prompt format. For the rest
296   of this section, we focus on the DoubleIO prompt variant and demonstrate how S2 Hacking occurs
297
      within the base IOI circuit. Additional details on all metrics and experiments are in Appendix A.

      5.1   A DDING PATHS FROM I NPUT T OKENS
379
380   The S2 Hacking mechanism suggests a starting point for discovering DoubleIO and TripleIO cir-
381   cuits. Recall that in the base IOI circuit, S2 is the only input token with outgoing paths to the
382   Duplicate heads, with all paths from the remaining input tokens being ablated out. For the variants,
383   we start with the base IOI circuit and restore some of these paths from other input tokens that were
384   originally ablated out, and see if any of them have a causal effect on the output of the model. The
385   results are shown in Figure 5.
386   For DoubleIO, we observe that adding paths from the IO2 token brings the circuit’s performance
387   closest to the full model, with a normalized faithfulness of 0.77. This is done by adding 10 edges
388   to the base IOI circuit: two for each of the three Duplicate heads and the two Previous Token heads
389   they depend on. In contrast, adding paths from just the IO1 token has little impact on the circuit’s
390   performance, while adding paths from other input tokens further degrades its performance. Hence
391   for every path from the S2 token to a Duplicate Token head, the corresponding path from the IO2
392
      token to the same head also has a causal effect on the model output.
393   A similar pattern emerges in TripleIO, where adding paths from the IO2 and IO3 tokens brings the
394   circuit’s performance closest to that of the full model, achieving a normalized faithfulness of 0.79.
395   This requires adding 20 edges to the base IOI circuit: the same 10 edges corresponding to the IO2
396   token that were added to the DoubleIO circuit, plus 10 edges corresponding to the IO3 token.

415   5.2   D ISCOVERING C IRCUIT R EUSE THROUGH PATH PATCHING
416
417   We use the methodology of Wang et al. (2023) to discover a circuit for the DoubleIO and TripleIO
418   variants, starting with the identification of Name Mover heads. For each attention head and relevant
419   input token, we compute the direct causal effect of the path that starts from the token and proceeds
420   through the head to the final logit at the END position. This type of token-level causal effect esti-
421   mation is important for variants like DoubleIO and TripleIO where multiple tokens are duplicated,
422
      since each of these duplicates can have paths to different heads.
423   Surprisingly, we do not observe any significant deviation in results compared to the base IOI circuit.
424   The Name Mover heads from the base IOI circuit are just as causally relevant for the DoubleIO and
425   TripleIO variants, and none of the other heads in the model have a substantial enough direct causal
426   effect score to suggest the addition of a new Name Mover head to the circuit. These results indicate
427   that the Name Mover heads from the base IOI circuit are being reused for both variants.
428   We observe the same pattern in the S-Inhibition heads, which we refer to more generally as Inhibition
429   heads since they could inhibit either of the duplicated names in the prompt. To identify these heads,
430   we compute the direct causal effects of every head in the model on the queries of the Name Mover
431   heads. We find that all of the S-Inhibition heads from the base IOI circuit have the most significant
      causal effects, indicating that the DoubleIO and TripleIO circuits are also reusing these heads.

500
      The circuit in Figure 7 demonstrates that Inhibition heads now receive information from both the
501
      IO2 and S2 duplicate tokens in the DoubleIO prompt. This raises a natural question: How does the
502
      model decide which duplicate to suppress to produce the correct answer? To answer this, we sought
503   to find decision points: heads that are most responsible for choosing between the duplicates.
504
505   Surprisingly, we find that the order in which names appear in the prompt significantly affects the
506
      performance of both the full model and the DoubleIO circuit, with higher performance when the IO
      token comes first. Figure 8 (left) shows the logit differences stratified by whether S or IO comes
507
      first, and the overall logit difference appears to be an average of the two. This suggests that one or
508
      more attention heads respond differently depending on the order of the names in the prompt.
509
510   We find this behavior in head 2.2, a Previous Token head. This head appears to implement a “first
511   come, first serve” mechanism, where it primarily attends to the name that appears first in the prompt,
512
      thereby serving as a key decision point in the circuit. As shown in Figure 8 (right), the head fre-
      quently assigns high attention to one of the name tokens (S, IO), based on which appeared first. In
513
      prompts where IO appeared first, the head attended far more to the IO+1 token than it did its S+1
514
      counterpart, and vice versa when S appeared first. The full set of experiments is in Appendix B, and
515   a more detailed study of the duplicate suppression mechanism is left to future work.

533
534   Our investigation reveals that the IOI circuit in GPT-2 small generalizes more effectively than pre-
535   viously understood, reusing its core components and mechanisms while adapting through minimal
536   structural changes, such as adding input edges. Although the base IOI circuit behaved unexpectedly
537   on the DoubleIO and TripleIO prompt variants, as demonstrated by S2 Hacking, the functionality
538   of its attention heads remained intact. This study of circuit generalization and evaluation on prompt
539   variants ultimately deepened our understanding of how GPT-2 small solves the IOI task, while of-
      fering critical insights into the broader capabilities of large neural networks.

      A     S2 H ACKING
576
      In this section, we provide further detail on how each of the attention heads in the base IOI circuit
577
      behave under S2 Hacking. The Name Mover heads in the circuit are responsible for returning the
578
      output, ideally the IO token, and we find that all of these heads attend primarily to the IO token. To
579   understand how this occurs, we consider all of the heads that have an incoming edge from the S2
580   token: the S-Inhibition, Induction, Duplicate, and Previous Token heads.
581
582
      A.1   M ETRICS
583
584   Each head type is defined by a query position (“active” token) and a key position (“attended” token).
585   For DoubleIO prompts, most heads have a decision to focus on tokens leading to the prediction of
586   either S or IO. For each head type, Wang et al. (2023) specify which tokens to primarily attend to
587   in order to produce the correct answer. We refer to these as the “correct” and “incorrect” tokens for
588   that head type, and they are used in the definition of the confidence ratio in Section 4.1. We specify
589   these “correct” and “incorrect” tokens for each head type below.
590   For Inhibition heads, which are active at END, attending to S2 suppresses the prediction of the sub-
591   ject token and increases the probability of the Name Mover heads returning the IO token, which is
592   the “correct” output. Attending to IO2, which suppresses the prediction of the IO token, is con-
593   sidered “incorrect”. We refer to the attention probability of S2 at END position as the “correct”
      probability, while the incorrect probability is the attention probability of IO2 at END. Similarly, for


                                                        11
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      Induction heads we refer to the attention probability of S1+1 at S2 as “correct”, and the attention
595   probability of IO1+1 at IO2 as “incorrect”. For Duplicate Token heads, we refer to the attention
596   probability of S1 at S1+1 as “correct”, and the attention probability of IO1 at IO1+1 as “incor-
597   rect”. For Previous Token heads, we refer to the attention probability of S1 at S1+1 as “correct”,
598   and the attention probability of IO1 at IO1+1 as “incorrect”.
599
600   A.2    S-I NHIBITION H EADS
601
602   The ideal behavior of the S-Inhibition heads on the base IOI prompt is to focus on the duplicated
603   token S2 at the END position. These heads influence the queries of the Name Mover heads, guiding
604   them to reduce their attention on the S2 token. This enables them to focus on the IO token, which
605
      is the correct answer. However, since there are two duplicates in the DoubleIO prompt variant, S2
      and IO2, it is possible for these heads to attend to both duplicates. The optimal behavior of these
606
      heads is to place high attention on the S2 token while maintaining low attention on the IO2 token
607
      at the END position.
608
609   We see an example of S2 Hacking in Head 8.6, which exhibits a higher confidence ratio in the circuit
610   compared to the model. The model confidence ratio is close to 1 and the circuit confidence ratio is
611
      greater than 2, indicating that the head attends roughly equally to the S2 and IO2 tokens in the
      model but significantly favors the S2 token in the circuit. This enables it to effectively direct the
612
      Name Mover heads to focus much less on the S2 token and thereby avoid the incorrect answer.
613
614   However, not all S-Inhibition heads rely on S2 Hacking. The confidence ratios for Head 8.10 are
615   very similar in the model and the circuit, in both cases the head attends more to the S2 token. While
616   Heads 7.3 and 7.9 exhibit significantly higher confidence ratios in the circuit compared to the model,
617
      the model confidence ratio for these heads are already very high, indicating that they predominantly
      attend to the S2 tokens regardless. These heads seem to suggest how the model still shows nontrivial
618
      performance on the DoubleIO prompt variant, even if not as high as the circuit.
619
620
      A.3    I NDUCTION H EADS
621
622   When an Induction head encounters a duplicate instance of the subject token S2 in the prompt, it
623   attends primarily to the word immediately following the previous instance of the subject (S1+1).
624   There are two duplicates in the DoubleIO prompt variant, so the circuit performance would increase
625   if the induction head placed high attention on the S1+1 token at the S2 position and low attention
626   on the IO1+1 token at the IO2 position. This would ensure that the head continues to prioritize the
627   S2 token.
628   S2 Hacking is evident in Heads 5.5 and 5.9. Their confidence ratios are close to 1 in the full model,
629   indicating roughly equal attention assigned to both the S2 and IO2 tokens. However, the circuit
630   confidence ratios are significantly higher, suggesting a stronger preference for the S2 token. Addi-
631   tionally, the functional faithfulness scores show higher values for the S2 token compared to the IO2
632   token, indicating that attention on the S2 token deviates further from the behavior of the full model.
633
      In contrast, Head 6.9 does not appear to rely on S2 Hacking since it already favors the S2 token
634   heavily, as shown by the high confidence ratios on both the model and the circuit and the functional
635   faithfulness scores on the S2 and IO2 tokens are very similar. This is similar to the pattern we
636   observed in the S-Inhibition Heads 7.3 and 7.9. Interestingly, Head 5.8 does not seem to benefit
637   from S2 Hacking and still favors the IO2 token.
638
639   A.4    D UPLICATE H EADS
640
641   The function of the Duplicate heads is to focus on the first instance of a name in the prompt when a
642   duplicate instance is encountered. Since the Duplicate heads influence the queries of the Induction
643   heads, it is advantageous for them to attend highly to S1 at the S2 token position while avoiding
644
      attending to IO1 at the IO2 token position.
645   Heads 0.1 and 0.10 demonstrate nearly identical performance between the circuit and the full model,
646   with both attending equally to IO1 and S1. This is evident from the confidence and functional
647   faithfulness scores all being close to 1. In contrast, Head 3.0 shows strong signs of S2 Hacking;
      while its confidence ratio is close to 1 for the full model, it significantly increases for the circuit. The


                                                          12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      functional faithfulness scores further show that the behavior of this head on the S2 token diverges
649   from the full model much more than on the IO2 token. This head shows signs of a decision point
650   where S2 Hacking begins, with its effects cascading down to the Induction and S-Inhibition heads.
651
652   A.5    P REVIOUS T OKEN H EADS
653
654   The purpose of a Previous Token head at a given token position is to attend to the token that imme-
655   diately precedes it in the prompt. In the DoubleIO prompt variant, it would be beneficial for these
656   heads to maintain this functionality at the S1+1 position while minimizing attention on the IO1 to-
657   ken at the IO1+1 position. This is important because the Previous Token heads influence the values
658   of the Induction heads in the base IOI circuit, and we have previously seen how the Induction heads
659
      benefit from focusing on the subject token.
660   Unlike the other head types above, the Previous Token heads appear not to rely very much on the
661   S2 Hacking mechanism. Head 4.11 functions almost identically in both the circuit and the model,
662   with confidence and functional faithfulness scores consistently close to 1. While Head 2.2 slightly
663   benefits from S2 Hacking, the circuit deviates very little from the full model. In both cases, these
664   heads attend more to the S1 token at position S1+1 than they do to the IO1 token at the IO1+1
665
      position, so this head appears to favor the S1 token regardless.
