011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Fairness in predictions is of direct importance in practice due to legal, ethical, and
014            societal reasons. This is often accomplished through counterfactual fairness, which
015
               ensures that the prediction for an individual is the same as that in a counterfactual
               world under a different sensitive attribute. However, achieving counterfactual
016
               fairness is challenging as counterfactuals are unobservable. Existing baselines
017
               for counterfactual fairness do not have theoretical guarantees. In this paper, we
018            propose a novel counterfactual fairness predictor for making predictions under
019            counterfactual fairness. Here, we follow the standard counterfactual fairness setting
020            and directly learn the counterfactual distribution of the descendants of the sensitive
021            attribute via tailored neural networks, which we then use to enforce fair predictions
022            through a novel counterfactual mediator regularization. Unique to our work is
023            that we provide theoretical guarantees that our method is effective in ensuring
024            the notion of counterfactual fairness. We further compare the performance across
025            various datasets, where our method achieves state-of-the-art performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Fairness in machine learning is mandated for a large number of practical applications due to legal,
030   ethical, and societal reasons (Barocas & Selbst, 2016; Kleinberg et al., 2019; Feuerriegel et al., 2020;
031   Angwin et al., 2016; De Arteaga et al., 2022; von Zahn et al., 2022). Examples are predictions in
032   credit lending or recidivism prediction, where fairness is mandated by anti-discrimination laws.
033
      In this paper, we focus on the notion of counterfactual fairness (Kusner et al., 2017). The notion
034
      of counterfactual fairness has recently received significant attention (e.g., Kusner et al., 2017; Garg
035
      et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Chiappa, 2019; Kim et al., 2021; Abroshan et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2019;
036   Zuo et al., 2022; Grari et al., 2023; Rosenblatt & Witter, 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Zuo et al., 2023;
037   Anthis & Veitch, 2024; Silva, 2024). One reason is that counterfactual fairness directly relates to legal
038   terminology in that a prediction is fair towards an individual if the prediction does not change had the
039   individual belonged to a different demographic group defined by some sensitive attribute (e.g., gender,
040   race). However, ensuring counterfactual fairness is challenging as, in practice, counterfactuals are
041   generally unobservable.
042   Prior works have developed methods for achieving counterfactual fairness in predictive tasks (see
043   Sec. 2). Originally, Kusner et al. (2017) described a conceptual algorithm to achieve counterfactual
044   fairness. Therein, the idea is to first estimate a set of latent (background) variables and then train
045   a prediction model without using the sensitive attribute or its descendants. More recently, many
046   works have extended the conceptual algorithm through neural methods (Pfohl et al., 2019; Kim et al.,
047   2021; Grari et al., 2023). However, these methods have a key shortcoming: they have no theoretical
048   guarantees for achieving counterfactual fairness. Our proposed method is the first to ensure consistent
049   estimation of the counterfactual distribution by leveraging the identifiability of counterfactual theory,
050
      and further offers theoretical guarantees in achieving counterfactual fairness.
051   In this paper, we present a novel deep neural network called Generative Counterfactual Fairness
052   Network (GCFN) for making predictions under counterfactual fairness. For this, we build upon
053   Standard Fairness Model (Plecko & Bareinboim, 2022). Our method leverages tailored generative
      adversarial networks to directly learn the counterfactual distribution of the descendants of the

055   novel counterfactual mediator regularization. We further provide theoretical guarantees that, if the

      Overall, our main contributions are as follows:1 (1) We propose a novel deep neural network for

      2.1    FAIRNESS NOTIONS FOR PREDICTIONS
068   Over the past years, the machine learning community has developed an extensive series of fairness
069   notions for predictive tasks so that one can train unbiased machine learning models; see Appendix B
070   for a detailed overview. In this paper, we focus on counterfactual fairness (Kusner et al., 2017), due
071   to its relevance in practice (Barocas & Selbst, 2016; De Arteaga et al., 2022).
072
073   2.2    P REDICTIONS UNDER COUNTERFACTUAL FAIRNESS
074
075   Originally, Kusner et al. (2017) introduced a conceptual algorithm to achieve predictions under
076   counterfactual fairness. The idea is to first infer a set of latent background variables and subsequently
077   train a prediction model using these inferred latent variables and non-descendants of sensitive
078
      attributes. Kusner et al. (2017) provided only a conceptual algorithm, while later works offered actual
      instantiations. The conceptual algorithm can not directly achieve fairness prediction from data and
079
      a causal graph; instead, it requires the specification of structural equations to ensure identifiability,
080
      which makes it impractical. We provide detailed comparison to Kusner et al. (2017) in Appendix D.1.
081
082   State-of-the-art approaches build upon the above idea but integrate neural learning techniques,
083   typically by using VAEs. These are mCEVAE (Pfohl et al., 2019), DCEVAE (Kim et al., 2021),
084
      and ADVAE (Grari et al., 2023). In general, these methods proceed by first computing the posterior
      distribution of the latent variables, given the observational data and a prior on latent variables. Based
085
      on that, they compute the implied counterfactual distributions, which can either be utilized directly
086
      for predictive purposes or can act as a constraint incorporated within the training loss. In sum, the
087   methods in Pfohl et al. (2019); Kim et al. (2021); Grari et al. (2023) are our main baselines. We
088   provide a detailed comparison of these papers and ours in the Appendix D.2. However, none of these
089   methods have shown the identifiability of the latent variables, which implies non-identifiability of the
090   counterfactual queries, thus can lead to unfair prediction. Further details of the benefits over these
091   latent variable baselines are in Appendix D.3.
092
      Why existing methods are problematic: Prior works for counterfactual fairness prediction all act as
093   heuristics that may return estimates but these estimates do not correspond to the true value. (e.g.,
094   (Pfohl et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021; Grari et al., 2023; Zuo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
095   2024)). In other words, predictions can be generated that can be unfair. The reason is of theoretical
096   nature: existing baselines did not consider identifiability of counterfactuals and were still unclear
097   under which scenarios counterfactual fairness can be achieved in the first place.
098
      The core of our contribution are solving the following two shortcomings: 1 The learned latent
099   variables in existing methods are not identifiable.2 The lack of identifiability can lead to that the
100   true counterfactual distributions are not learned. This can thus lead to an overall low prediction
101   performance but, importantly, may even undermine fairness objectives. 2 : The existing methods

182   4        G ENERATIVE C OUNTERFACTUAL FAIRNESS N ETWORK
183
184   Overview: Here, we introduce our proposed method called Generative Counterfactual Fairness
185   Network (GCFN). An overview of our method is in Fig. 2. GCFN proceeds in two steps: Step 1 uses
186   a significantly modified GAN to learn the counterfactual distribution of the mediator. Step 2 uses
187   the generated counterfactual mediators from the first step together with our counterfactual mediator
188   regularization to enforce counterfactual fairness. The pseudocode is in Appendix G.
189
      Why do we need counterfactuals of the mediator? Different from existing methods for causal effect
190   estimation (Yoon et al., 2018; Bica et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), we are not interested in obtaining
191   counterfactuals of the target Y (=   b ladder 2 in Pearl’s causality ladder). Instead, we are interested
192   in counterfactuals for the mediator M , which captures the entire influence of the sensitive attribute
193   and its descendants on the target (=    b ladder 3). Thus, by training the prediction model with our
194   counterfactual mediator regularization, we remove the information from the sensitive attribute
195   to ensure fairness while keeping the rest useful information of data to maintain high prediction
196   performance. What is the advantage of using a GAN in our method? The GAN in our method enables
197   us to directly learn transformations of factual mediators to counterfactuals without the intermediate
198
      step of inferring latent variables. As a result, we eliminate the need for the abduction-action-prediction
      procedure (Pearl, 2009) and avoid the complexities and potential inaccuracies of inferring and then
199
      using latent variables for prediction. Further detailed discussion of the benefits over inferring latent
200
      variable baselines in Appendix D.3.

360   by reducing (ii) the second term Rcm for all the generators through minimizing our training loss in
361   Eq. 9, we can effectively enforce the predictor to learn counterfactual fair predictions.4

      Performance metrics: Methods for causal fairness aim at both: (i) achieve high accuracy while
      (ii) ensuring causal fairness, which essentially yields a multi-criteria decision-making problem. To this
383
      end, we follow standard procedures and reformulate the multi-criteria decision-making problem using
384

385   fairness from Sec. 4.2. We define the utility function as Uγ (accuracy, CF ) = accuracy − γ × CF
386   with a given utility weight γ. A larger utility Uγ is better. The weight γ depends on the application and
387   is set by the decision-maker; here, we report results for a wide range of weights γ ∈ {0.1, . . . , 1.0}.

389
      GANs and consider the worst-case counterfactual fairness, i.e., we take the maximum value of
390
      counterfactual mediator regularization maxsj=1 Rcm (h, Gj ). We implement our GCFN in PyTorch.

392   batch normalization in the generator for stability, and train all the GANs for 300 epochs with 256
393   batch size. The prediction model is a multilayer perceptron, which we train for 30 epochs at a 0.005
394   learning rate. Since the utility function considers two metrics, the weight λ is set to 0.5 to get a good
395   balance. More implementation details and hyperparameter tuning are in Appendix J.

429   5.2.2    A DDITIONAL INSIGHTS
430
431   Computational efficiency: Our proposed framework used multiple GANs primarily to meet the
      mathematical assumptions and thus ensure theoretical guarantees. However, having multiple GANs is


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      not necessary in practice. For this, we provide experimental results in Appendix K.3 to demonstrate
433   that a single GAN is sufficient for state-of-the-art performance.
434
435   Why does our method not simply reproduce the factual mediator? Intuitively, one may think that
436
      the GAN would simply copy the factual mediators because the counterfactual mediators can not be
      observed during training. However, this is not the case due to the adversarial training process of the
437
      generator. By training the discriminator to differentiate between factual and generated counterfactual
438
      mediators, the generator is guided to learn the correct counterfactual distribution. It could be seen as
439   a form of teacher forcing.
440
441   We conduct experiments to empirically verify that our method does not simply reproduce the factual
442
      mediators, but actually learns the counterfactual mediators. Experimental results and details are in
      Appendix K.2. We observe that the generated counterfactual mediator is similar to the ground-truth
443
      counterfactual mediator, while the factual and the generated counterfactual mediators are highly
444
      dissimilar. In other words, our model can correctly learn the counterfactual mediators. We additionally
445   give three important arguments for the reason why it works in Appendix K.2.

813
814   Recent literature has extensively explored different fairness notions (e.g., Dwork et al., 2012; Feldman
815   et al., 2015; Grgic.Hlaca et al., 2016; Hardt et al., 2016; Joseph et al., 2016; Zafar et al., 2017;

818   overview, we refer to Makhlouf et al. (2020) and Plecko & Bareinboim (2022). There have been also
819
      theoretical advances (e.g., Fawkes et al., 2022; Rosenblatt & Witter, 2023) but these are orthogonal
      to ours.
820
821   Existing fairness notions can be loosely classified into notions for group- and individual-level fairness,
822   as well as causal notions, some aim at path-specific fairness (e.g., Nabi & Shpitser, 2018; Chiappa,
823   2019). We adopt the definition of counterfactual fairness from Kusner et al. (2017).
824   Counterfactual fairness definition (Kusner et al., 2017): Given a predictive problem with fairness
825   considerations, where A, X and Y represent the sensitive attributes, remaining attributes, and output
826   of interest respectively, for a causal model M = ⟨V = {A, X, Y }, U, F, P(U)⟩, prediction model
827   Ŷ = h(X, A, U) is counterfactual fair, if under any context X = x and A = a,
828
829                       P Ŷa (U) | X = x, A = a = P Ŷa′ (U) | X = x, A = a ,                       (12)
830
831   for any value a′ attainable by A. This is equivalent to the following formulation:
832             P (h(Xa (U), a, U) | X = x, A = a) = P (h(Xa′ (U), a′ , U) | X = x, A = a) .               (13)
833
834   Our paper adapts the later formulation by doing the following. First, we make the prediction model
835   independent of the sensitive attributes A, as they could only make the predictive model unfairer.
836
      Second, given the general non-identifiability of the posterior distribution of the exogenous noise, i.e.,
      P (U | X = x, A = a), we consider only the prediction models dependent on the observed covariates.
837
      Third, we split observed covariates X on pre-treatment covariates (confounders) and post-treatment
838
      covariates (mediators). Thus, we yield our definition of a fair predictor in Eq. 1.

      C     I DENTIFICATION OF COUNTERFACTUALS
865
866
      C.1   I MPORTANCE OF THEORETICAL GUARANTEES FOR IDENTIFAIBILITY
867
868   In causal inference, “identifiability” refers to a mathematical condition that permits a causal quantity
869   to be measured from observed data (Pearl, 2009). Importantly, identification is different from
870   estimatability because methods that act as heuristics may return estimates but they do not correspond
871   to the true value. For the latter, we refer to D’Amour (2019) where the authors point out several
872   technical issues that, if a latent variable is not unique, it is possible to have local minima, which leads
873
      to unsafe results in causal inference. As a result, the lack of identifiability can lead to that the true
      counterfactual distributions is not being learned (but some other distribution). This can thus cause an
874
      overall low prediction performance but, more importantly, may even undermine fairness objectives.
875
876   Needless to say, identification of counterfactuals is a very challenging, and current literature on this
877   direction requires assumptions to fulfill it (Xia et al., 2022; Melnychuk et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
878   2022; Nasr-Esfahany et al., 2023). We are aligned and thus make similar assumptions. Thereby, we
879
      can – for the first time – show in which scenarios counterfactual fairness can be fulfilled (and in
      which scenarios not). This broadens our understanding of how counterfactual fairness operates.
880
881
882   C.2   D ISCUSSION ABOUT BGM ASSUMPTION
883
      The bijective generation mechanism (BGM) (Nasr-Esfahany et al., 2023) is required to ensure
884   the identifiability of our method. The BGM assumption is crucial for the identification of the
885   counterfactuals. It includes many popular identifiable SCMs as special cases, e.g., ANM (Peters et al.,
886   2014), LSNM and (Immer et al., 2023), and PNL (Zhang & Hyvärinen, 2010). Thus, this assumption
887   can be seen as one of the most general assumptions that lead to point identifiability.
888
      It is hard to argue whether real-world datasets usually satisfy the BGM assumption. Rather, this
889
      assumption provides a guideline for which datasets it is – in principle – possible to provide answers
890   to the counterfactual questions and for which not. As discovered by (Melnychuk et al., 2023), the
891   relaxation of the BGM assumption not only immediately leads to point non-identifiability but also to
892   non-informative partial identification bounds. Still, it can be intuitively re-formulated (Melnychuk
893   et al., 2023) as follows: In fM , the sensitive attribute, A, is assumed to interact only with the observed
894   covariates, X, and not with the exogenous noise, UM . Many real-world data-generation mechanisms /
895   phenomena, if studied closely, can be said to satisfy this assumption (e.g., simulators in physics and
896   medicine but also neuroscience and behavioral processes).
897   Current literature on counterfactual fairness does not even have any theoretical guarantees on the
898   identifiability of their methods or provide no guarantees of the correctness of counterfactual fairness
899   achieved on either synthetic datasets or real-world datasets. We believe having some assumptions to
900   make our method with theoretical guarantees is better than methods with no correctness guarantee at
901   all and thus helps us to understand where and when counterfactual fairness can be fulfilled from a
902   theoretical point of view.

      D     R ELATED WORKS

      D.1    K USNER COUNTERFACTUAL FAIRNESS
921
922   Originally, Kusner et al. (2017) introduced a conceptual algorithm to achieve predictions under
923   counterfactual fairness. The idea is to first infer a set of latent background variables and subsequently
924   train a prediction model using these inferred latent variables and non-descendants of sensitive
925   attributes. Kusner et al. (2017) provided only a conceptual algorithm, while only later works
926   proceeded by offering actual instantiations. In particular, Kusner et al. (2017) did not clarify how to
927   learn latent variables in practice and did not prove the identifiability of the inferred latent variables by
928   a model. As such, the conceptual algorithm does not provide any theoretical guarantees for achieving
929   counterfactual fairness in the final prediction models. Furthermore, the conceptual algorithm is
930
      not able to directly learn fairness prediction from a given dataset and a given causal graph, but
      instead it requires the specification of additional structural equations and thus requires further domain
931
      knowledge. Without knowing the ground-truth structural causal model, Kusner et al. (2017) can not
932
      have identifiability and can not achieve counterfactual fairness. In sum, this makes the conceptual
933   algorithm – and any other instantiation building upon it – impractical.
934
935
      D.2    D ISCUSSION ABOUT THEORETICAL GUARANTEES IN COUNTERFACTUAL FAIRNESS
936
             PREDICTION
937
938   Current works related to counterfactual fairness prediction often state that their proposed methods
939   satisfy counterfactual fairness under certain conditions (Pfohl et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021; Grari
940   et al., 2023; Zuo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). However, none of these papers
941   consider or discuss the identifiability of counterfactuals. These papers either require some implied
942   strong assumptions about the identifiability of counterfactuals (which they do not specify) or fully
943   ignore the identifiability at all, and hope their method can somehow manage to learn the correct
944   counterfactuals. As we discuss below, this is often not true because of which these methods may
945
      learn predictions that are unfair.
946   Zuo et al. (2023) claims that they form the counterfactual prediction by drawing from the counterfac-
947   tual distribution. However, it does not clarify how they exactly managed to learn this counterfactual
948   distribution, and neither does the paper prove why the learned counterfactual distribution by this
949   model should be identifiable. They just give a conceptual algorithm, yet which may learn an unfair
950   objective.
951   Wang et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024) involve the step of learning the latent variables and later
952   training a prediction model using these inferred latent variables. These papers do not clarify how they
953   managed to learn the latent variables and do not have any proper identification guarantees for learned
954   latent variables. For example, they can use, for example, a VAE to estimate the latent variable but it is
955   not guaranteed that it can be correctly identified. Again, this leads to predictions that can be actually
956   unfair.
957   Methods that act as heuristics as those above may return estimates but these estimates do not
958   correspond to the true value. So, theoretically, these methods can converge against predictions that
959   are not fair but unfair.
960
961   D.3    B ENEFITS OVER LATENT VARIABLE BASELINES
962
963   Importantly, the latent variable baselines for counterfactual fairness (e.g., mCEVAE (Pfohl et al.,
964   2019), DCEVAE (Kim et al., 2021), and ADVAE (Grari et al., 2023)) are far from being easy as they
965   do not rely on off-the-shelf methods. Rather, they also learn a latent variable in non-trivial ways.
966   The inferred latent variable U should be independent of the sensitive attribute A while representing
967   all other useful information from the observation data. However, there are two main challenges:
968
      (1) The latent variable U is not identifiable. (2) It is very hard to learn such U to satisfy the above
      independence requirement, especially for high-dimensional or other more complicated settings.
969
      Hence, we argue that baselines based on some custom latent variables are highly challenging.
970
971   Because of (1) and (2), there are no theoretical guarantees for the VAE-based methods. Hence, it
      is mathematically unclear whether they actually learn the correct counterfactual fair predictions.

1093
       E.2.1     A RROW FROM X TO A
1094
1095   Below, we clarify that the arrow from X to A is not a limitation but a more general setting.
1096
       In causal inference, having an arrow means there is no additional assumption, while the absence of
1097
       an arrow means that there is an assumption. So in our causal graph, the presence of an arrow from
1098
       X to A indicates the allowance of a causal relationship between variables, that is X can be a direct
1099   cause of A, this is permissible/allowed in our framework, but the direct causal relationship X → A
1100   is not a necessity. If there is no arrow from X to A, it is actually a stronger assumption, because it
1101   forbids X to be the cause of A.
1102
1103   E.2.2     M EDIATOR SELECTION
1104
1105   In this section, we clarify that having the mediator in the causal graph is a standard setting (Schröder
1106
       et al., 2023). Below, we give a detailed introduction to the mediator selection and further offer a
       step-by-step process on how practitioners can adapt their settings to our graph.
1107
1108
               1. Knowledge of M is required for any theoretically grounded method that aims to iden-
1109              tify/estimate counterfactual effects. It is well-known in the causal inference literature that
1110              knowledge of pre-treatment and post-treatment variables is required to identify a causal
1111              effect (even in Layer 2 of Pearl’s causal hierarchy) (Pearl, 2009). Hence, such knowledge
1112              must be available to identify stronger fairness notions such as counterfactual fairness, which
1113              lie on layer 3 of Pearl’s hierarchy. Works that do not distinguish between post- and pre-
1114              treatment variables have no hope of achieving identifiability on the interventional level,
1115              and also no hope for the (harder) counterfactual level. The assumption is consistent with
1116              established literature on causal fairness and the corresponding model is even called ”the
1117
                  Standard Fairness Model” in the literature (Plecko & Bareinboim, 2022). Mediators are part
                  of a standard causal model, particularly in the context of fairness where sensitive attributes
1118
                  are often involved due to some discrimination-related issues. Mediators naturally occur in
1119
                  practical applications where there are sensitive attributes. Knowledge of M is given in many
1120              practical scenarios where our method could be applied.
1121
               2. Knowledge of mediators is often realistic as they are usually available mediators in observed
1122
                  data (De Arteaga et al., 2022). The reason is located in the definition of sensitive attributes.
1123
                  Sensitive attributes are of concern to (dis)advantage certain groups of society because the
1124              sensitive attribute is known to influence other variables, which may act as proxies that drive
1125              some outcome. For example, consider a loan application and take gender as a sensitive
1126              attribute. Obviously, gender is sensitive in loan applications as it is known to not only be
1127              responsible for discrimination but it naturally affects many other secondary outcomes (e.g.,
1128              income) which make it so important to mitigate discrimination with respect to the sensitive
1129              attribute in the first place. If gender was not affecting outcomes broadly, one would not
1130              necessarily see the importance of mitigating discrimination with regard to it and one would
1131              thus not consider it as a sensitive attribute.
1132           3. Our assumptions regarding M are weaker than in some existing literature: it is important to
1133              clarify that we have a more general setting about mediators. Our method does not require
                  complete knowledge of the entire causal graph. In some previous works, such as (Kim et al.,


                                                           21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
                2021), detailed knowledge of the causal relationships among mediators is required, while
1135            our methodology does not need to specify the causal relationship among them. This makes
1136            our framework more flexible and broadly applicable.
1137
             4. What if the mediator M is not correctly identified? In this case, one could still employ
1138
                a ”worst-case” approach by including all variables we are unsure about into M , which
1139            gives us a heuristic worst-case approach, This is similar to other methods that do not
1140            distinguish between X and M (Xu et al., 2019). However, those methods that use such
1141            heuristics (including baselines) have no theoretical grounding. In summary, we would like
1142            to emphasize that the ability of our method to incorporate knowledge of M is an advantage
1143            rather than a disadvantage of our method. If M can be correctly identified, then our method
1144            provides strong performance (accuracy) with theoretical guarantees on identifiability of
1145            counterfactual fairness. If not, the worst-case approach is still possible for applying our
1146            method.
1147
       In summary, it is important to clarify that we have a more general setting about mediators. Our
1148
       method does not require complete knowledge of the entire causal graph. Our primary objective
1149
       is to identify whether variables are pre- or post-treatment, in other words, determining if they are
1150   descendants of the sensitive attribute. Variables potentially affected by the sensitive attribute are
1151   classified as mediators. In some previous works, detailed knowledge of the causal relationships
1152   among mediators is required, while our methodology does not. This makes our framework more
1153   flexible and broadly applicable.

       J.1   I MPLEMENTATION OF OUR METHOD
1677
1678   To ensure our GCFN to achieve counterfactual fairness, we train s = 10 GANs and consider the
1679   worst-case counterfactual fairness, i.e., we take the maximum value of counterfactual mediator
1680   regularization maxsj=1 Rcm (h, Gj ). Our GCFN is implemented in PyTorch. Both the generator and
1681   the discriminator in the GAN model are designed as deep neural networks, each with a hidden layer
1682   of dimension 64. LeakyReLU is employed as the activation function and batch normalization is
1683
       applied in the generator to enhance training stability. The GAN training procedure is performed for
       300 epochs with a batch size of 256 at each iteration. We set the learning rate to 0.0005. Following
1684
       the GAN training, the prediction model, structured as a multilayer perceptron (MLP), is trained
1685
       separately. This classifier can incorporate spectral normalization in its linear layers to ensure Lipschitz
1686   continuously. It is trained for 30 epochs, with the same learning rate of 0.005 applied. The training
1687   time of our GCFN on (semi-) synthetic dataset is comparable to or smaller than the baselines.
1688

1747   K.2   A DDITIONAL INSIGHTS : WHY OUR METHOD DOES NOT SIMPLY REPRODUCE THE
1748         FACTUAL MEDIATORS BUT ACTUALLY LEARNS THE COUNTERFACTUAL MEDIATORS ?
1749
       As an additional analysis, we now provide further insights into how our GCFN operates. Specifically,
1750
       one may think that our GCFN simply learns to reproduce factual mediators in the GAN rather than
1751
       actually learning the counterfactual mediators. However, this is not the case. To show this, we
1752   compare the (1) the factual mediator M , (2) the ground-truth counterfactual mediator MA′ , and
1753   (3) the generated counterfactual mediator M̂A′ . The normalized mean squared error (MSE) between
1754   them is in Table 2 (D1 , D2 , D3 refer to synthetic, semi-syn (sigmoid) and semi-syn (sin) dataset
1755   in Appendix I, respectively. We find: (1) The factual mediator and the generated counterfactual
1756   mediator are highly dissimilar. This is shown by a normalized MSE(M, M̂A′ ) of ≈ 1. (2) The
1757   ground-truth counterfactual mediator and our generated counterfactual mediator are highly similar.
1758   This shown by a normalized MSE(MA′ , M̂A′ ) of close to zero. In sum, our GCFN is effective in
1759   learning counterfactual mediators (and does not reproduce the factual data).
1760
       We further give explanations for why our GAN does not copy the factual values but learns the
1761
       counterfactual values (due to its custom design!). It is true that during training, we cannot directly
1762
       learn the counterfactual mediators in a supervised way as they are unobservable. Instead, we can
1763   only leverage the reconstruction loss on the factual mediators. The reason why we can still learn
1764   the correct counterfactual mediators is due to the adversarial training process of the generator. By
1765   training the discriminator to differentiate between factual and generated counterfactual mediators,
1766   the generator is guided to learn the correct counterfactual distribution. It could be seen as a form of
1767   teacher forcing.
1768
       Importantly, there are three important arguments for why our method does not simply reproduce the
1769   factual mediators but actually learns the counterfactual mediators even though it is not observed.
1770
1771   Intuitively: The input of the discriminator D contains the counterfactual and the factual and the
1772
       order of them is intentionally randomized. Suppose, hypothetically, that the factual always comes
       in the first place, then it is easy to distinguish. However, in the design of our framework, this is not
1773
       the case. In our framework, the data are intentionally shuffled, so that factual and counterfactual
1774
       positions are random.
1775
1776   Technical reason: If the generator G would just copy the factual values of the mediator M and
1777   output a trivial solution, it would be super hard for the discriminator D to distinguish, implying that
1778
       the loss of D would be super large. We would observe mode collapse during training, yet which we
       did not observe and thus provides support for our argument.
1779
1780   Theoretical reason: We provide theoretical proof in our Lemma 1. Therein, we show theoretically
1781   that our generator consistently estimates the counterfactual distribution of the mediator P(Ma′ | X =
       x, A = a, M = m). For each specific input data (X = x, A = a, M = m), we then generate the


                                                                                    33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       counterfactual mediator from the distribution P(Ma′ | X = x, A = a, M = m). Hence, we offer
1783   theoretical proof that we learn counterfactual fairness correctly.
