009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks that can produce accurate, input-conditional uncertainty represen-
012            tations are critical for real-world applications. Recent progress on heteroscedastic
013            continuous regression has shown great promise for calibrated uncertainty quan-
014            tification on complex tasks, like image regression. However, when these methods
015
               are applied to discrete regression tasks, such as crowd counting, ratings predic-
               tion, or inventory estimation, they tend to produce predictive distributions with
016
               numerous pathologies. Moreover, discrete models based on the Generalized Linear
017
               Model (GLM) framework either cannot process complex input or are not fully
018            heterosedastic. To address these issues we propose the Deep Double Poisson Net-
019            work (DDPN). In contrast to networks trained to minimize Gaussian negative log
020            likelihood (NLL), discrete network parameterizations (i.e., Poisson, Negative bino-
021            mial), and GLMs, DDPN can produce discrete predictive distributions of arbitrary
022            flexibility. Additionally, we propose a technique to tune the prioritization of mean
023            fit and probabilistic calibration during training. We show DDPN 1) vastly outper-
024            forms existing discrete models; 2) meets or exceeds the accuracy and flexibility of
025            networks trained with Gaussian NLL; 3) produces proper predictive distributions
026
               over discrete counts; and 4) exhibits superior out-of-distribution detection. DDPN
               can easily be applied to a variety of count regression datasets including tabular,
027
               image, point cloud, and text data.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The pursuit of neural networks capable of learning accurate and reliable uncertainty representations
033   has gained significant traction in recent years (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Kendall & Gal, 2017;
034   Gawlikowski et al., 2023; Dheur & Taieb, 2023). Input-dependent uncertainty is useful for detecting
035   out-of-distribution data (Amini et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Kang et al., 2023), active learning (Settles,
036
      2009; Ziatdinov, 2024), reinforcement learning (Yu et al., 2020; Jenkins et al., 2022), and real-world
      decision-making under uncertainty (Abdar et al., 2021). While uncertainty quantification applied
037
      to regression on continuous outputs is well-studied, training neural networks to make probabilistic
038
      predictions over discrete counts has traditionally received less attention, despite multiple relevant
039   applications. In recent years, neural networks have been trained to predict the size of crowds (Zhang
040   et al., 2016; Lian et al., 2019; Zhang & Chan, 2020; Zou et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Lin & Chan,
041   2023), the number of cars in a parking lot (Hsieh et al., 2017), traffic flow (Lv et al., 2014; Liu
042   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020), agricultural yields (You et al., 2017), inventory of product on shelves
043   (Jenkins et al., 2023), and bacteria in microscopic images (Marsden et al., 2018). In this paper, we are
044   interested in training neural networks to output a flexible, calibrated, and properly specified predictive
045   distribution over discrete counts (Figure 1).
046   A common approach to uncertainty representation in complex regression tasks has been to apply
047   the generalized linear model (GLM) framework, but to replace the linear predictor with a neural
048   network. The network is then trained to output the mean and variance of a Gaussian distribution,
                  T
049    µ̂i , σ̂i2 = fΘ (xi ) (Nix & Weigend, 1994), while minimizing Gaussian negative log likelihood
050   (NLL) loss via gradient-based optimization. This form of input-conditional predictive variance is
051   known as heteroscedastic regression. Recent work has improved the performance of heteroscedastic
052   regression by mediating the influence of σ̂ 2 on the gradient of the mean, which can cause instability
053   during training, miscalibrated predictive variance, or a poor mean fit (Immer et al., 2024; Seitzer
      et al., 2022; Stirn et al., 2023).

      However, when each of these methods is applied to count regression, the model is trained to output
073
      an input-dependent probability density function, p(y|fΘ (x)); y ∈ R, over a discrete output space,
074   i.e. y ∈ Z≥0 . Applying a continuous density function to a discrete domain creates three critical
075   pathologies. First, the continuous predictive distribution will assign non-zero probability mass
076   to infeasible real values that fall in between valid integers. Second, the predictive intervals are
077   unbounded and can assign non-zero probability to negative values when the predicted mean is small.
078   Third, the boundaries of the predictive intervals (i.e., high density interval or 95% credible interval)
079   are likely to fall between two valid integers, diminishing their interpretability and utility. To overcome
080   these limitations, we desire a properly specified probability mass function, conditional on the input
081   features: p(y|fΘ (x)); y ∈ Z≥0 .
082   Discrete regression has historically been treated similarly to the Gaussian case. For example, previous
083   work trains a network to predict the λ parameter of a Poisson distribution and minimize its NLL
084   (Fallah et al., 2009). However, the Poisson parameterization of the neural network suffers from the
085   equi-dispersion assumption: predictive mean and variance of the Poisson distribution are the same
086   (λ̂ = µ̂ = σ̂ 2 ). Therefore, the model is not flexible enough to produce separate input-dependent mean
087   and variance predictions. Another common alternative is to train the network to minimize Negative
088   Binomial (NB) NLL (Xie, 2022). The Negative Binomial breaks equi-dispersion by introducing
089   another parameter to the PMF. This helps disentangle the mean and variance, but suffers from the
090
      over-dispersion assumption: σ̂ 2 ≥ µ̂. Consequently, this model is not flexible enough to assign
      uncertainty less than its mean prediction for a given input. Meanwhile, discrete GLMs fit without a
091
      neural network feature extractor lack representational capacity to process complex input and are also
092
      not fully heteroscedastic (Efron, 1986; Murphy, 2023).
093
094
095   Our Contributions To address these issues, we introduce the Deep Double Poisson Network
096   (DDPN), a novel discrete neural regression model (See Figure 1). In contrast to Gaussian-based
097   heteroscedastic regressors, DDPN is a neural network trained to output the parameters of the Double
098   Poisson Distribution (Efron, 1986), which represents a highly flexible, discrete predictive distribution,
099
      p(y|fΘ (x)); y ∈ Z≥0 . DDPN is fully heteroscedastic such that the predicted mean and dispersion
      are independent, conditioned on the input. Additionally, we demonstrate that DDPN is subject
100
      to similar dynamics between mean and dispersion during training as Gaussian-based techniques
101
      (Immer et al., 2024; Seitzer et al., 2022; Stirn et al., 2023), and propose a β modification to the
102   NLL to temper this relationship and achieve ‘tunable mean fit’ (Figure 3). Compared to existing
103   discrete regression models, DDPN is flexible enough to handle over-, under- and equi-dispersion,
104   making it a superior choice to the Poisson and Negative Binomial deep networks for discrete
105   predictive uncertainty quantification. Our experiments show that DDPN can learn accurate and
106   reliable uncertainty representations on both tabular and complex data (image, point cloud, and text).
107   DDPN matches or exceeds the performance and calibration of Gaussian-based alternatives and offers
      superior out-of-distribution detection compared with existing techniques.

      2     M ODELING P REDICTIVE U NCERTAINTY WITH N EURAL N ETWORKS

119
      Predictive uncertainty can be decomposed into two types: epistemic (uncertainty of the model
      weights) and aleatoric uncertainty (observation noise) (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Der Kiureghian &
120
      Ditlevsen, 2009).
121
122
      2.1     E PISTEMIC U NCERTAINTY
123
124   Epistemic uncertainty refers to uncertainty due to model misspecification. Modern neural networks
125   tend to be significantly underspecified by the data, which introduces a high degree of uncertainty
126   (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020). In general, this type of uncertainty can be reduced through additional data
127   acquisition. A variety of techniques have been proposed to explicitly represent epistemic uncertainty
128   including Bayesian inference (Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Chen et al., 2014; Hoffman et al., 2014),
129   variational inference (Graves, 2011), and Laplace approximation (Daxberger et al., 2021). Recently,
130   deep ensembles have emerged as a simple and popular alternative (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017;
131
      D’Angelo & Fortuin, 2021). Other work connects Bayesian inference and ensembles by arguing the
      latter can viewed as a Bayesian model average where the posterior is sampled at multiple local modes
132
      (Fort et al., 2019; Wilson & Izmailov, 2020). This approach has a number of attractive properties:
133
      1) it generally improves predictive performance (Dietterich, 2000); 2) it can model more complex
134   predictive distributions; and 3) it effectively represents uncertainty over learned weights, which leads
135   to better probabilistic calibration.
136
137   2.2     H ETEROSCEDASTIC R EGRESSION FOR A LEATORIC U NCERTAINTY
138
139   Aleatoric uncertainty quantifies observation noise and generally cannot be reduced with more data
140   (Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009; Kendall & Gal, 2017). In practice, this uncertainty can be
141   introduced by low resolution sensors, blurry images, or the intrinsic noise of a signal. Aleatoric noise
142   is commonly modeled in machine learning by fitting the parameters of a distribution over the output,
143   rather than a point prediction. Uncertainty is often represented by a dispersion parameter, σ, that is
144
      learned from the training data. When dispersion varies for each input, σi , we get a heteroscedastic
      model. Below, we detail how aleatoric uncertainty is modeled in both the GLM and deep learning
145
      literature.
146
147

      2.2.2          G ENERALIZED R EGRESSION WITH D EEP L EARNING
190
191   GLMs are limited in their predictive power and cannot fit complex data. To address this, similar
192   theoretical principles have been applied to specify deep neural networks, which are much more flexible
193   than GLMs and map to a larger number of data modalities (Fallah et al., 2009; Xie, 2022; Qi et al.,
194   2020; Barron, 2019; Fan et al., 2019). These works adjust the natural parameter mapping as follows:
195   let z = gΘ1:L−1 (xi ) denote the features extracted by the first L − 1 layers of a neural network. Both
196   the mean and dispersion are outputs of the network: l(µi ) = ηi = wTη z(xi ), σi = wTσ z. One specific
197   example of this approach is detailed in both Nix & Weigend (1994) and Kendall & Gal (2017), where
                                                                                                  T
198   the network is trained to output the mean and log variance of a Gaussian, [µ̂i , log σ̂i ] = fΘ (xi )
199   with the objective of minimizing Gaussian NLL. Recent work has identified issues with this training
200
      strategy due to the the influence of σ̂ on the mean, µ̂. Immer et al. (2024) reparameterize the neural
      network to output the natural parameters of the Gaussian distribution. Seitzer et al. (2022) propose a
201
      modified loss function and introduce a hyperparameter, β ∈ [0, 1], which tempers the impact of σ̂ 2 on
202
      the gradient of the mean. Stirn et al. (2023) re-scale the gradient of µ̂ and modify the architecture of
203   the underlying network to include separate sub-networks for µ̂ and σ̂ 2 , along with the stop gradient
204   operation to prevent the gradient of σ̂ 2 from impacting the µ̂(x) sub-network.
205
206
      For count regression, one can specify a neural network that outputs the parameters of a discrete
      distribution. For example, Fallah et al. (2009) train a neural network to predict the mean and variance
207
      parameter, λ, of a Poisson distribution, while Xie (2022) applies this idea to the Negative Binomial
208
      distribution. As discussed previously, these approaches suffer from the equi- and over-dispersion
209   assumptions. In contrast, DDPN produces a fully heteroscedastic, discrete predictive distribution,
210   and offers tunable mean fit through a likelihood β modification.

      Each regression method is evaluated in terms of two criteria. First, Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
330
      measures the predictive accuracy and mean fit; lower values imply higher accuracy. Second, Negative
331   Log Likelihood (NLL) measures the calibration, or quality, of the predictive distribution (Can-
332   dela et al., 2005); lower values imply greater agreement between the predictive distribution p and
333   the observed label yi . We choose to omit the commonly-used ECE (Kuleshov et al., 2018) as a
334   measure of calibration due to its recently identified shortcomings when evaluating discrete prob-
335   ability distributions (Young & Jenkins, 2024). To facilitate comparison between NLL obtained
336   from continuous and discrete models, we use the continuity correction to convert Gaussian den-
337   sities into probabilities. Given a predicted Gaussian CDF F̂i for some input-output pair (xi , yi ),
338   we take P (Y = yi |F̂i ) ≈ F̂i (yi + 12 ) − F̂i (yi − 21 ). We then compute NLL as the average of
339   −log P (Y = yi |F̂i ) across the evaluation set. For each technique, we train and evaluate 5 models
340   and report the empirical mean and standard deviation (in parentheses). To form ensembles, these
341   same 5 models are combined.

352   proper distribution over discrete counts. It achieves slightly better mean fit (lower MAE) and roughly
353
      equivalent calibration (NLL). Conversely, the Poisson and Negative Binomial DNNs lack the capacity
      to recover the heteroscedastic variance pattern of the data. For another simulated demonstration
354
      of DDPN’s flexibility, see Appendix A.2, where we show DDPN can recover the ground-truth
355
      conditional dependencies in the data even when explicitly misspecified.

358

361   bike rentals between the years 2011 and 2012 in the Capital bikeshare system. The features are the

367
      output the parameters of each benchmarked distribution. See Table 2 for results.

371   likely performs well because the provided features are not sufficient for concentrated predictions
372
      and the data are naturally equi- to over-dispersed. On the other hand, both DDPN and β1.0 -DDPN
      outperform all methods in terms of probabilistic fit (NLL). In Collision, we see that β0.5 -DDPN
373
      and β1.0 -DDPN top the baselines in terms of mean fit while maintaining competitive NLL with the
374
      DP GLM. DDPN also performs well on these two dimensions and is close to Seitzer and NB DNN in
375   terms of mean fit. In both cases, modeling epistemic uncertainty via ensembling provides significant

453   4.4                      O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION B EHAVIOR
454
      In this section, we compare the out-of-distribution (OOD) behavior of DDPNs to existing methods.
455   To assess OOD behavior, for each model that has been trained on Reviews, we feed it verses from
456   the King James Version of the Holy Bible, and compute the entropy (Shannon, 1948) of each of the
457   resultant predictive distributions; we call these OOD entropy values. We do the same with the test
458   split of Reviews, and call them in-distribution (ID) entropy values. We then compare the empirical
459   distributions of these entropy values (Amini et al., 2020) by performing a one-sided permutation test
460   (Good, 2013) on the difference of means. This procedure outputs a test statistic, ∆ = x̄OOD − x̄ID ,
461   and a p-value (for more details see Appendix B.5). Higher entropy indicates higher uncertainty in a
462   model’s predictive distributions. Thus, we expect that the models most able to distinguish between
463
      ID / OOD will have the larger ∆ since their mean entropy should be higher for OOD inputs than ID
      inputs.
464
465   The results of our experiment are displayed in Figure 4. With statistical significance, DDPN shows
466   the greatest ability of all benchmarked regression models to differentiate between ID and OOD inputs,
467   as demonstrated by the largest ∆  ¯ (the average ∆ across trials). Existing count regression techniques
468   (NB DNN, Poisson DNN) fail to exhibit any separation between predictive entropy on ID and OOD
469
      data. We note that only half of Gaussian regression approaches benchmarked (Immer et al., 2024;
      Seitzer et al., 2022) achieve a significant gap between ID and OOD entropies. For a similar analysis
470
      showing the supremacy of DDPN ensemble methods in terms of OOD behavior, see Figure 8 in the
471
      Appendix. We provide a case study of OOD detection in Appendix C.2. In particular, Figure 12
472   highlights the effective OOD behavior of DDPN.
473
474   In Section 3.2 we discussed the motivation for β-DDPN as a mechanism to prioritize mean accuracy
475
      over probabilistic calibration. Empirically, this hypothesis is generally supported by our experiments.
      The β modification that is used to enhance mean fit appears to hurt a model’s recognition of OOD.
476
      From all experiments, our general conclusion is the virtue of β-DDPN is highly accurate mean
477
      prediction, while the advantage of standard DDPN is reliable calibration and effective OOD detection.

      5                 C ONCLUSION
528
529
      Overall, we conclude that DDPNs are well-suited for complicated count regression tasks. Our
530
      main findings are that DDPNs 1) vastly outperform existing deep learning methods with discrete
531   predictive distributions; 2) match or exceed the performance of state-of-the-art heteroscedastic
532   regression techniques; 3) address pathologies with Gaussian-based heteroscedastic regressors applied
533   to discrete counts; and 4) provide superior out-of-distribution detection, compared to existing methods.
534   Moreover, DDPNs are general and can be applied to a variety of tasks and data modalities.

      A.1     L IMITATIONS
830
831   DDPNs are general, easy to implement, and can be applied to a variety of datasets. However, some
832   limitations do exist. One limitation that might arise is on count regression problems of very high
833   frequency (i.e., on the order of thousands or millions). In this paper, we don’t study the behavior
834   of DDPN relative to existing benchmarks on high counts. In this scenario, it is possible that the
835   choice of a Gaussian as the predictive distribution may offer a good approximation, even though the
836   regression targets are discrete.
837   We also note that the general approximations E[Z] ≈ µ and Var[Z] ≈ µϕ for some Z ∼ DP(µ, ϕ) we
838   employ in this work have not been extensively studied. It is possible that there are edge cases where
839   these estimates diverge from the true moments of the distribution.
840
      One difficulty that can sometimes arise when training a DDPN is poor convergence of the model
841
      weights. In preliminary experiments for this research, we had trouble obtaining consistently high-
842   performing solutions with the SGD (Kiefer & Wolfowitz, 1952) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014)
843   optimizers, thus AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) was used instead. Future researchers using the
844   DDPN technique should be wary of this behavior.
845
      In this paper, we performed a single out-of-distribution (OOD) experiment on Amazon Reviews.
846
      This experiment provided encouraging evidence of the efficacy of DDPN for OOD detection. However,
847
      the conclusions drawn from this experiment may be somewhat limited in scope since the experiment
848   was performed on a single dataset and task. Future work should seek to build off of these results to
849   more fully explore the OOD properties of DDPN on other count regression tasks.
850

854   data are drawn from a non-double poisson distribution. We simulate a dataset as follows: Let
855   X ∼ Uniform[−3, 3] and Y |X ∼ NegBinom(X 2 , 0.5). We train a NegBinom DNN (Xie, 2022), a
856   DDPN, a β0.5 -DDPN, and a β1.0 -DDPN each with the same MLP backbone (see Section B.1 of the
857   Appendix for specific architecture details). A depiction of the learned distributions can be seen in

      Figure 6, with the MAE and NLL indicated in each panel. These results suggest that even when the
      data-generating process is not strictly Double Poisson, the flexibility of DDPN allows it to recover

      the ground-truth distribution anyway.

1063   In certain special cases of count regression where the targets are assumed to live on a bounded
1064   subset of Z≥0 , it is possible to model the data via a multi-class neural network (trained with cross
1065
       entropy) as opposed to an unbounded discrete probability distribution. Two of the complex datasets
       we benchmark in this paper can be seen as falling into this category: Inventory (since we expect
1066
       a finite number of products to be on a given shelf) and Reviews (since ratings live on a fixed scale
1067
       from 1 to 5). We provide metrics for a multi-class NN on these datasets in Table 4. The results
1068   are somewhat nuanced. on Inventory, it appears that treating a finite, discrete response as a
1069   count regression problem has clear advantages, as all models benchmarked in the main body of the
1070   paper (see Table 3) achieve superior mean fit when compared to the multi-class NN, with many also
1071   exhibiting better calibration. Meanwhile, on Reviews, we find the multi-class NN performs well,
1072   though this is also somewhat of a mixed bag: since the cross-entropy approach does not account for
1073   ordering, it yields occasional multi-modal pathologies wherein the model places high joint probability
1074   on extreme values (i.e., 1 and 5). See Figure 9 for examples.
1075   In general, we favor treating discrete count responses via the natural probabilistic interpretation with
1076   an integer-valued random variable. Even in the case where the response is assumed to be finite, we
1077   find that a well-fit model learns to decay probabilities for values past the lower and upper bounds (see
1078   the case studies in C.2 for a practical example). Additionally, a multi-class model requires the set of
1079   labels at train and test time to remain constant. However, in many counting tasks it is plausible that
       the training data does not cover all possible values.
