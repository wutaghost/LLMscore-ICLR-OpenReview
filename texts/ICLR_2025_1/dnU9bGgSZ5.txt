009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Macro placement is a crucial and complex issue in chip design. In recent stud-
012            ies, reinforcement learning (RL) has demonstrated outstanding performance in
013            optimizing chip wirelength, but this leads to thermally inefficient design. Addi-
014            tionally, due to the specialized expertise necessary for creating chip benchmarks
015            and the constraints imposed by confidentiality agreements, there exists a scarcity
016            of publicly available chip thermal placement benchmarks. This work introduces
017            a reinforcement learning-based thermal placement model that can optimize both
018
               wirelength and max temperatures. We also strictly followed the chip design pro-
               cess and established a macro thermal placement benchmark. This significantly
019
               reduces the entry barriers for researchers, facilitating benchmarking and result
020
               replication. Compared to other models, our model notably diminishes the chip’s
021            max temperature of the chip while slightly extending wirelength on smaller-scale
022            chips. On large-scale chips, our model can further reduce wirelength while de-
023            creasing the chip’s max temperature. Our code and benchmarks will be open
024            sourced soon.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   With the development of large-scale integrated circuits (IC), placement is a crucial task that directly
030   affects chip performance, such as speed and energy costGarg & Shukla (2016). In the placement
031   task, macros (more than 100) and standard cells (more than 10k) are placed in appropriate locations
032   to meet the design metrics such as wirelength, routability, timing, power, max temperature, and
033
      manufacturabilityQiu et al. (2023). As chip sizes continue to increase, manual design struggles
      to meet various design metrics simultaneously. Therefore, finding an automatic and efficient chip
034
      placement method becomes crucial.
035
036   Recent chip placement approachesChen et al. (2008); Lu et al. (2014); Lin et al. (2019); Cheng et al.
037   (2018); Viswanathan et al. (2007) have shown significant advantages in wirelength optimization.
038   However, As shown in Figure 1(d), shorter wirelength often results in an aggregation of components
039
      that increases max temperatures of chip then impacting chip performance. Max temperatures and
      temperature gradients have a definite effect on the reliability and performance of integrated circuits.
040
      For example, large temperature gradients increase clock skew in clock distribution networksXia et al.
041
      (2017), and device overheating due thermal runaway can occur in semiconductor devices due to the
042   positive feedback between high temperature and increasing leakage currentMolter et al. (2023); Li
043   et al. (2005).
044
045
      Recent methods rarely prioritize heat optimization as a primary objective in chip placement. Existing
      approaches that simultaneously optimize wirelength and max temperature mainly focus on chiplet
046
      placement, and relatively few research focuses on macro placement. These works primarily suffer
047
      from the following three shortcomings: First, Two-Stage Optimization Leads to Suboptimal. As
048   showen in Figure1(a) ,almost all thermal placement methods Ma et al. (2021); Chiou et al. (2023)
049   divide the optimization process into two steps: first optimizing wirelength, and then optimizing max
050   temperatures. This approach significantly restricts the model’s exploration space during heat opti-
051   mization, as component placements are nearly fixed, leading to local optima. Second, These heat
052   optimization methods are less effective for scenarios with high-density components. Thermal
053   placement methods typically use techniques like translation, rotation, and swapping after placing
      all components on the chip canvas to reduce max temperature. However, as shown in Figure 1(b),

      when component density is too high, it greatly limits the space available for translation and rotation,
055   significantly impacting optimization results. Third, there is a lack of macro thermal placement
056   benchmarks. Thermal placement benchmarks are primarily focused on chiplet placement, with
057   relatively fewer benchmarks addressing macro thermal placement. Generating these benchmarks
058   requires running Electronic Design Automation (EDA) flows, which necessitate expertise in chip
059   design—making data generation costly Jiang et al. (2024). Additionally, non-disclosure agreements
060   (NDAs) for manufacturing techniques and EDA tools limit the release of raw data. As a result, most

125   2.1   C HIP P LACEMENT M ETHODS
126
127   There are two main optimization indicators commonly used in chip placement: using wirelength as
128   an optimization metricChen et al. (2008); Lu et al. (2014); Lin et al. (2019); Cheng et al. (2018);
129   Viswanathan et al. (2007); Kim et al. (2012); Kim & Markov (2012); Spindler et al. (2008); Chan
130
      et al. (2006); Lai et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2024); Mirhoseini et al. (2021) and optimizing both max
      temperature and wirelength simultaneouslyMa et al. (2021); Chiou et al. (2023).
131
132   Using wirelength as an optimization metric. The optimization of wirelength is primarily catego-
133   rized into classic methods (e.g., analytical methods)Chen et al. (2008); Lu et al. (2014); Lin et al.
134   (2019); Cheng et al. (2018); Viswanathan et al. (2007); Kim et al. (2012); Kim & Markov (2012);
135   Spindler et al. (2008); Chan et al. (2006); Shi et al. (2024) and learning-based methods (e.g.,RL)Lai
136
      et al. (2022); Mirhoseini et al. (2021). Those methods typically utilize min W irelength(s, H)
      as the objective function, with some incorporating additional objectives such as congestion and
137
      overlap. For example, DREAMPlaceLin et al. (2019) utilizes analytical methods to optimize wire-
138
      length and density, convert the placement task into min W A(s, H) + λDensity(s, H). W A de-
139   notes the smoothed weighted average wirelength used to approximate Half Perimeter Wire Length
140   (HPWL), Density denotes the differentiable density measure used to penalize overlap, and λ is
141   the trade-off factor. The problem is then solved numerically using classical mathematical optimiza-
142   tion techniques, such as gradient descent, to rapidly generate a high-quality complete placement.
143   MaskPlaceLai et al. (2022) utilizes reinforcement learning to optimize wirelength. Reinforcement
144   learning views the placement process as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). In each step t, a com-
145   ponent is placed on the chip canvas. Set the reward as rt = HP W Lt−1 − HP W Lt and train the
146   model to maximize the reward in order to achieve the min W irelength. Those methods can achieve
147
      placement results that surpass humans in terms of wirelength optimization, but they overlook the
      impact of max temperatures on the chip, leading to a reduction in the chip’s thermal performance.
148
149   Optimizing both thermal and wirelength. Some methods consider the chip’s thermal distri-
150   bution during placement and optimize wirelength and max temperature simultaneously. How-
151   ever, these methods primarily concentrate on 2.5D chiplet placement and relatively less on macro
152   placement. TAP-2.5DMa et al. (2021) employs simulated annealing to discover a placement re-
153
      sult with improved wirelength and max temperature by moving the chiplet through translation
      and rotation from the initial layout generated byChen & Chang (2006). SA cost function is
154
      cost = αT +(1−α)W ,where T and W are temperature and wirelength respectively. α is the balance
155
      coefficient. α = 0 when T ≤ 85. Then, α incrementally rises with T until it peaks at a maximum
156   value of 0.9. This means that heat optimization is withheld during wirelength optimization until the
157   temperature surpasses 85 degrees Celsius, at which point heat optimization is initiated. Chiou et al.
158   (2023) utilizes an SP-based tree to achieve wirelength-focused placement. After the placement is
159   completed, perform post-placement with thermal considerations. However, these approaches prior-
160   itize wirelength optimization initially and subsequently address heat optimization once wirelength
161   optimization reaches a certain level. If the interplay between the wirelength and thermal parame-
      ters is not considered initially, the system will converge to a locally optimal solution. Furthermore,

166   2.2   C HIP DATASET AND B ENCHMARK
167
168   Datasets and benchmarks are crucial for the development of research. This facilitates benchmarking
169   and result reproducibility, while also reducing the barriers to entry for new researchers. Researchers
170   have developed various datasets tailored for specific tasks to foster advancements in chip design.
171   In the prediction task, CircuitNet and CircuitNet 2.0Jiang et al. (2024); Chai et al. (2023) collected
172   over 10,000 data points from CPU, GPU, and AI chips, and conducted multi-model prediction tasks
173   such as timing, routing feasibility, and IR-drop prediction. In macro placement tasks, generally used
174   public benchmarks include ISPDNam et al. (2005; 2006), IBM benchmarkAlpert (1998), and the
175
      Ariane RISC-V CPU designZaruba & Benini (2019). These benchmarks mainly include the length
      and width and pin position (the components are interconnected through pins.) of each component,
176
      and the topological relationships (Netlist) of components. Due to confidentiality reasons, those
177
      benchmarks do not disclose specific power of individual components. Due to the absence of specific
178   power for each component, thermal field for macro placement cannot be conducted. While the power
179   density of each macro can be constrained within the range of 105 −107 W/m2 based on statistical
180   principlesCong et al. (2004), the power density of components varies across different manufacturing
181   processes and component librariesBorkar (1999); Wrzecionko et al. (2009); Ku et al. (2007); Hanson
182   et al. (2003); Li et al. (2005); Kim et al. (2005). Additionally, the power of each macro is also
183   influenced by voltage fluctuations and frequencies. In other words, the power of macros is influenced
184   by various factors and requires precise calculations to obtain more accurate results.

192

193   while satisfying certain constraints. In macro placement, common optimization metrics mainly
194   include wirelength and max temperature, aiming for the chip to have the shortest possible wire-
195   length and the lowest temperature. Consider with the challenges associated with directly calculating
196   wirelengths, recent work primarily relies on Half Perimeter WireLength (HPWL) to approximate
197   wirelength which is computed by accumulating all the half-perimeters of bounding rectangle of all
198   the nets from the chip netlist. As the chip power is predominantly generated in macros, we calculate
199   the power density of each macro based on its power and area. Subsequently, we obtain the chip’s
200   thermal field through finite element analysis, extracting the max temperature from it.
201   Placement constraints mainly include: overlap, which avoid overlapping between each macro, In

203   each position’s routing in the chip canvas should be less than a fixed threshold. Therefore, the entire

514   7   C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we developed a reinforcement learning-based macro placement model that optimizes
517   both wirelength and thermal field, thus achieving a balance between wirelength and max temper-
518   ature. Furthermore, we established 15 open-source macro thermal placement benchmarks through
519   a comprehensive EDA process. We obtained gate-level netlists and detailed power information for
520
      each macro through logic synthesis. From the experiments, it is evident that our model can re-
      duce the chip’s max temperature while slightly increasing the wirelength on smaller-scale chips.
521
      On larger-scale chips, our model disperses the initial component placement through heat rewards,
522
      providing ample space for subsequent macros and reducing wirelength to a certain extent. This also
523   demonstrates the importance of early heat optimization in large-scale chip placement. We aim for
524   our benchmark to promote research in chip thermal placement, thereby enabling chips to achieve
525   optimal performance.
