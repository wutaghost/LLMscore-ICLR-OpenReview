009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This article makes discrete masked models for the generative modeling of discrete
012            data controllable. The goal is to generate samples of a discrete random variable
013            that adheres to a posterior distribution, satisfies specific constraints, or optimizes
014            a reward function. This methodological development enables broad applications
015            across downstream tasks such as class-specific image generation and protein de-
016
               sign. Existing approaches for controllable generation of masked models typically
               rely on task-specific fine-tuning or additional modifications, which can be ineffi-
017
               cient and resource-intensive. To overcome these limitations, we propose a novel
018
               plug-and-play framework based on importance sampling that bypasses the need
019            for training a conditional score. Our framework is agnostic to the choice of con-
020            trol criteria, requires no gradient information, and is well-suited for tasks such
021            as posterior sampling, Bayesian inverse problems, and constrained generation.
022            We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through extensive experiments,
023            showcasing its versatility across multiple domains, including protein design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Modeling complex discrete probability distributions in high-dimensional spaces is a crucial chal-
028
      lenge across multiple domains in generative AI, including language, vision, audio, and biology.
029
      Among the various approaches, discrete masked models have emerged as powerful tools, offering
030   robust solutions for generating and understanding discrete data. Notable examples include BERT
031   for language modeling (Devlin et al., 2019), MaskGIT for image synthesis (Chang et al., 2022),
032   DNABERT for DNA modeling (Ji et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023), the ESM series for protein gener-
033   ation (Rives et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024), and the more recent masked discrete
034   diffusion models (see, e.g., Lou et al. (2024); Ou et al. (2024); Sahoo et al. (2024); Shi et al. (2024);
035   Zheng et al. (2024)). These models typically learn the conditional distribution of each masked po-
036   sition given a partially masked data sequence, allowing for iterative decoding to generate a full
037   sequence during inference.
038   In many practical applications of masked models, the objective extends beyond generating realis-
039   tic samples from the data distribution to doing to in a controlled manner. This involves generating
040   samples that align with specific constraints, conditions, or prompts, often by sampling from a condi-
041   tional data distribution or maximizing a reward function (Zhang et al., 2023). Controlled generation
042   is crucial in tasks such as (1) posterior sampling, where samples are drawn from a posterior distribu-
043   tion conditioned on observed data; (2) constrained generation, where the aim is to produce samples
044
      that meet predefined constraints. These applications highlight the growing need for generative mod-
      els that not only capture the underlying data distribution, but also allow for flexible control over the
045
      generated outputs.
046
047   For controllable generation, it is desirable to establish a framework of plug-and-play samplers that
048   allows for efficient sampling from the desired controlled distribution without requiring further train-
049   ing or fine-tuning of the underlying pre-trained model, given any suitable control criterion. This
050
      approach is computationally advantageous, as it avoids the costly and time-consuming process of
      retraining the model for each new task, making it a highly scalable and adaptable solution for real-
051
      world applications.
052
053   However, existing work on plug-and-play controllable generation primarily focused on the continu-
      ous domain, such as continuous diffusion models (Chung et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Huang et al.,

      In this paper, we address this gap by developing a plug-and-play framework for controllable genera-
077
      tion using discrete masked models, eliminating the need for task-specific fine-tuning. Our algorithm
078
      operates through iterative unmasking and remasking. At each step, given the unmasked positions, we
079   apply a mean-field approximation to estimate the conditional distribution of the masked positions,
080   sample from it via Monte Carlo, and employ importance sampling to filter the most likely samples.
081   We then remask a portion of the newly generated positions, and repeat this unmasking-remasking
082   process for several times until all positions are unmasked. Since the complexity of querying the
083   masked model to obtain conditional probabilities is typically much higher than querying the reward
084   function in most of the real-world applications, the Monte Carlo estimation introduces minimal com-
085   putational overhead, keeping the sampling and filtering process efficient. In our experiments, high-
086   quality samples can be obtained with approximately 10 queries to the masked model and around
087
      1000 Monte Carlo samples, demonstrating the effectiveness of our proposed algorithm.
088   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
089
090           • We tackle the problem of plug-and-play controllable generation for discrete masked mod-
091             els, introducing an efficient and economical paradigm for sampling from these models.
092
093           • We propose a novel framework based on mean-field approximation of multi-dimensional
094             discrete distributions and iterative masking-unmasking. This fine-tuning-free approach en-
095             ables sample generation that satisfies control criteria for any (potentially non-differentiable)
096             reward functions. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first plug-and-play controllable
097             sampler for discrete masked models.
098
099           • We demonstrate the versatility of our method through multiple experiments, including sam-
100             pling sequence of integers with equality constraint and designing protein sequences, high-
101             lighting its adaptability and effectiveness across diverse tasks.

      2.1   D ISCRETE M ASKED M ODELS
111
112   Discrete masked models (or simply masked models) are designed to learn the distribution of se-
113   quential discrete data. Let {1, . . . , N } represent a vocabulary of N tokens, and consider a random
114   sequence X = (X 1 , . . . , X D ) ∈ {1, . . . , N }D of length D. For example, X can be a sentence
115   with D words, a protein composed of D amino acids, or a discrete latent representation of images
116   tokenized by a vector quantized variational autoencoder (VQ-VAE) (van den Oord et al., 2017). To
117   learn the probability distribution p of X given i.i.d. samples of X ∼ p, masked models are trained
118
      by randomly replacing certain positions in the sequence with a masked token M, and learning the
      probability of the masked positions conditional on the unmasked portion of the sequence.
119
120   Throughout this paper, for a partially observed sequence x ∈ {1, . . . , N, M}D , we use Ω := {d :
121   xd ̸= M} and M := {d : xd = M} to denote the unmasked and masked positions, respectively.
122   Formally, a masked model pb takes such a sequence as input and output a matrix pb(x) ∈ RD×N
                                                                                               ≥0   that
123   approximates the following probability distribution:

      There are several equivalent formulation of masked models. First, as demonstrated in the sampling
151
      procedure outlined above, they can be interpreted as any-order autoregressive models (Hoogeboom
152   et al., 2022; Shih et al., 2022), where the joint distribution is factorized using any arbitrary order σ,
153   and the model learns the conditional distributions p(X σ(d) |X σ(<d) ), d ∈ {1, . . . , D}. Second, re-
154   cent research (Ou et al., 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) has shown that
155   masked models are equivalent to the masked discrete diffusion model, which involves reversing a
156   continuous-time Markov chain that transform any data distribution into the distribution concentrated
157   on the sequence with all masked states.

184
185   In this section, we present our framework for plug-and-play controllable generation of masked mod-
186   els. We begin by drawing insights from the plug-and-play conditional generation for continuous
187   diffusion models in Section 3.1, and then propose our novel algorithms tailored specifically for dis-
188
      crete masked models in Section 3.2.
189
190   3.1   E XISTING P LUG - AND - PLAY S AMPLERS FOR C ONTINUOUS D IFFUSION M ODELS
191
      In continuous diffusion model, the data distribution is X0 ∼ p(x0 ), and a diffusion      process (Xt ∼
192
      pt )t∈[0,T ] transforms data to noise following Xt |X0 = x0 ∼ N x0 , σt2 I . Leveraging the data
193
      samples and the diffusion process, one can learn the score function ∇xt log pt (xt ) for all t > 0.
194
      Given a condition variable Y with distribution p(y|x0 ) conditional on X0 = x0 , the task of sampling
195
      from the posterior distribution p(x0 |y) boils down to estimating ∇xt log pt (y|xt ) for all t > 0, where
196   pt (y|xt ) is the predicted distribution of Y given noisy sample Xt = xt .
197
198   To estimate this quantity in a training-free way, Chung et al. (2023) observed that pt (y|xt ) =
199
      Ep(x0 |xt ) p(y|x0 ), and proposed to approximate the unknown distribution p(x0 |xt ) by the point
200
      mass at x b0 (xt ) := E(X0 |Xt = xt ) = xt + σt2 ∇xt log pt (xt ), resulting in pt (y|xt ) ≈ p(y|bx0 (xt )).
      Song et al. (2023) later proposed a Gaussian approximation centered at x     b0 (xt ) with a suitable vari-
201
      ance, estimating the expectation by the empirical mean over i.i.d. Gaussian samples. Finally, one
202
      step of backward propagation yields the gradient of log p(x0 |xt ) with respect to xt . Throughout
203   this process, only one query to the score model ∇xt log pt (xt ) is required, while the conditional
204   probability p(y|x0 ) is generally easy to obtain, minimizing computational overhead.
205
206
      To sum up, the key ingredient of this approach is the approximation of the distribution p(x0 |xt ),
207   which involves predicting the clean data X0 given a noisy sample Xt = xt .

209   3.2   C ONTROLLABLE GENERATION FOR M ASKED M ODELS

      general controllable generation problems.
212
213   In the discrete case, unlike the Gaussian noise Xt |X0 = x0 ∼ N x0 , σt2 I , we now have a mask-
214   ing process Xt |X0 = x0 obtained by independently masking each position with a probability that
215   depends on t. As the required entity is not the score function here, we cannot directly apply the con-
      tinuous diffusion model’s strategy. Moreover, the discrete state space lacks a Gaussian distribution,

           Conditional generation based on guidance. As an important task in controllable generation, con-
317
           ditional generation aims to generate a random variable X ∼ p(x) (e.g., image) given another random
318        variable Y known as the condition (e.g., the text description of an image). A popular approach is
319        guidance: for example, in continuous diffusion model, the classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol,
320        2021) learns a classifier p(y|x) that predicts the condition Y given a noisy sample of X and lever-
321        ages its information to generate X conditional on Y = y. Classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans,
322        2022) trains a score model that approximates both the conditional and unconditional score functions,
323        using a combination of them for conditional generation. These approaches can be extended to the
           discrete diffusion model (see Nisonoff et al. (2024)).

      Controllable generation for discrete generative models. The study of controllable generation is
325   an emerging area in language modeling (see, e.g., Zhang et al. (2023) for a review). A notable work,
326   Dathathri et al. (2020), proposed applying gradient updates to the key-value cache in transformers,
327   a task-agnostic approach but requiring fine-tuning during inference. For diffusion models, a recent
328   work Li et al. (2024) introduced soft value-based decoding, a derivative-free algorithm that requires
329   pre-sampled trajectories x0 , x1 , . . . , xT of discrete diffusion model to estimate a conditional expec-
330   tation. This method does not exploit the special properties of the masking process. To the best of
331   our knowledge, there are no fine-tuning-free samplers for controllable generation in discrete masked
332   models.
