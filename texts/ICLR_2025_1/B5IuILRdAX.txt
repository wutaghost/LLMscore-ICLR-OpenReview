                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               In the realm of Artificial Intelligence Generated Content (AIGC), flow-matching
               models have emerged as a powerhouse, achieving success due to their robust theo-
011
               retical underpinnings and solid ability for large-scale generative modeling. These
012
               models have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance, but their brilliance comes
013            at a cost. The process of sampling from these models is notoriously demanding
014            on computational resources, as it necessitates the use of multi-step numerical or-
015            dinary differential equations (ODEs). Against this backdrop, this paper presents a
016            novel solution with theoretical guarantees in the form of Flow Generator Match-
017            ing (FGM), an innovative approach designed to accelerate the sampling of flow-
018            matching models into a one-step generation, while maintaining the original per-
019            formance. On the CIFAR10 unconditional generation benchmark, our one-step
020            FGM model achieves a new record Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) score of
021
               3.08 among few-step flow-matching-based models, outperforming original 50-
               step flow-matching models. Furthermore, we use the FGM to distill the Stable
022
               Diffusion 3, a leading text-to-image flow-matching model based on the MM-DiT
023
               architecture. The resulting MM-DiT-FGM one-step text-to-image model demon-
024            strates outstanding industry-level performance. When evaluated on the GenEval
025            benchmark, MM-DiT-FGM has delivered remarkable generating qualities, rival-
026            ing other multi-step models in light of the efficiency of a single generation step.

029   1   I NTRODUCTIONS
030
031   Over the past decade, deep generative models have achieved remarkable advancements across var-
032   ious applications including data generation (Karras et al., 2020b; 2022; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021;
033   Oord et al., 2016; Ho et al., 2022; Poole et al., 2022; Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022),
034   density estimation (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018; Chen et al., 2019), and image editing (Meng et al.,
035   2021; Couairon et al., 2022). These models have notably excelled in producing high-resolution,
036   text-driven data such as images (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022;
037   2021; Luo, 2024), videos (Ho et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2024), audios (Evans et al., 2024), and
038
      others (Zhang et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2023; Luo & Zhang, 2024; Luo et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
      2023; Feng et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024c; Geng et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024;
039
      Pokle et al., 2022), pushing the boundaries of Artificial Intelligence Generated Content (AIGC).
040
041   Among the spectrum of deep generative models, flow-matching models (FMs) have emerged as par-
042   ticularly potent, showcasing robust performance in applications like likelihood computation (Grath-
043   wohl et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018) and text-conditional image synthesis(Esser et al., 2024; Liu
044
      et al., 2023). Flow models utilize neural networks to parametrize a continuous-time transportation
      field, establishing a bijective mapping between real data and random prior noises. They are trained
045
      to learn conditional vector fields using flow-matching methods (Lipman et al., 2022b; Albergo &
046
      Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Neklyudov et al., 2023). The flexible parametrization and
047   relative ease of training make FMs versatile across various datasets and applications.
048
049   However, despite their strengths, FMs still have severe drawbacks. Primarily, sampling from FMs in-
050
      volves multiple evaluations of the deep neural network, leading to computational inefficiencies. This
      limitation restricts their broader application, especially in scenarios where efficiency is paramount.
051
      Therefore fast sampling from flow models is important though challenging.
052
053   Step-wise distillation has emerged as a viable strategy to mitigate the computational inefficiencies
      associated with iterative sampling processes in deep generative models, particularly for accelerating

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Diffusion Distillation. Diffusion distillation (Luo, 2023) is an active research line aiming to ac-
111   celerate diffusion model sampling using distillation techniques. There are mainly three lines of
112   approaches to distill pre-trained diffusion models to obtain solid few-step models. The first line is
113   the distribution matching method. Luo et al. (2024a) first explore diffusion distillation by minimiz-
114   ing the Integral KL divergence. Yin et al. (2024b) extended this concept by incorporating a data
115   regression loss to enhance performance. Zhou et al. (2024) investigated distillation by focusing on
116   minimizing the Fisher divergence, while Luo et al. (2024b) applied a general score-based divergence
117   to the distillation process. Many other approaches have also studied distribution matching distilla-
118
      tion (Nguyen & Tran, 2024; Yuda Song, 2024; Heek et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2021;
      Xu et al., 2024). In this paper, our approach is related to distribution matching distillation. However,
119
      how to properly apply distribution matching distillation in the regime of flow models is technically
120
      difficult. The second line is the so-called trajectory distillation, which aims to use few-step models
121   to learn the diffusion model’s trajectory (Luhman & Luhman, 2021; Salimans & Ho, 2022; Geng
122   et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2022). Other works use the self-consistency of the diffusion model’s tra-
123   jectory to learn few-step models (Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2023; Liu
124   et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023b; Geng et al., 2024b; Salimans et al., 2024).
125
126   Acceleration of Flow Matching Models. In recent years, there have been efforts to accelerate the
127   sampling process of flow-matching models, most current work focuses on straightening the trajec-
128   tories of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). ReFlow (Liu et al., 2022) replaces the arbitrary
129   coupling of noise and data originally used for training flow matching with a deterministic coupling
130
      generated by a teacher model, enabling the model to learn a rectified flow from the data. CFM
      (Yang et al., 2024) shares a similar concept with consistency models but differs by applying consis-
131
      tency constraints to the velocity field space instead of the sample space. This approach also serves
132
      as a form of regularization aimed at straightening the trajectories of ODEs. Though these works
133   have demonstrated decent accelerations, they are essentially different from our proposed FGM. The
134   FGM is built upon a probabilistic perspective that guarantees the generator distribution matches the
135   teacher FM by minimizing the flow-matching objective. Besides, as we show in Section 5.1, the
136   FGM outperforms the mentioned methods with significant margins.

      4     F LOW G ENERATOR M ATCHING
178
      In this section, we introduce Flow Generator Matching (FGM), a general method tailored for the one-
179
      step distillation of flow-matching models. We begin by defining problem setup and notations. Then
180
      we introduce our matching objective function and how FGM minimizes this objective. Finally, we
181   compare FGM with existing flow distillation approaches, highlighting the empirical and theoretical
182   advantages of our methods.
183

186   Problem Formulation. Our framework is built upon a pre-trained flow-matching model that ac-
187   curately approximates the marginal vector field ut (xt ). The flow ut (xt ) bridges the noise and data
188   distribution. We also know the conditional transition qt (xt |x0 ) which implies ut (xt |x0 ). Assume
189   the pre-trained flow matching model provides a sufficiently good approximation of data distribution,
190
      i.e., q0 is the ground truth data distribution.
191   Our goal is to train a one-step generator model gθ , which directly transports a random noise z ∼ pz
192   to obtain a sample x0 = gθ (z). Let pθ,0 denote the distribution of the student model over the
193   generated sample x, and pθ,t denote the marginal probability path transitioned with qt (xt |x0 ), i.e.,

310   Differences From Diffusion Distillations The FGM gets inspiration from one-step diffusion dis-
311   tillation by minimizing the distribution divergences (Luo et al., 2024a; Zhou et al., 2024; Luo et al.,
312   2024b), however, the resulting theory is essentially different from those of one-step diffusion distil-
313
      lation. The most significant difference between FGM and one-step diffusion distillation is that the
      flow matching does not imply explicit modeling of either the probability density as the diffusion
314
      models do. Therefore, the definitions of distribution divergences can not be applied to flow models
315
      as well as its distillation. However, the FGM overcomes such an issue by directly working with the
316   flow-matching objective instead of distribution divergence. The main insight is that our proposed
317   explicit-implicit gradient equivalent theory bypasses the intractable flow-matching objective, result-
318   ing in strong practical algorithms with theoretical guarantees. We think Theorem 4.2 may also bring
319   novel contributions to other future studies on flow-matching models.
320
321   Comparison with Other Flow Distillation Methods There are few existing works that try to
322   accelerate flow models to single-step or few-step generative models. The consistency flow matching
323   (CFM) (Yang et al., 2024) is a most recent work that distills pre-trained flow models into one or
      two-step models. Though CFM has shown decent results, it is different from our FGM in both

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      theoretical and practical aspects. First, the theory behind CFM is built on the trajectory consistency
325   of flow models, which is directly generalized from consistency models(Song et al., 2023; Song
326   & Dhariwal, 2023; Geng et al., 2024b). On the contrary, our FGM is motivated by starting from
327   flow-matching objectives, trying to train the one-step generator’s implicit flow with the ground truth
328   teacher flow, with theoretical guarantees. On the practical aspects, on CIFAR10 generation, we show
329   that our trained one-step FGM models archive a new SoTA FID of 3.08 among flow-based models,
330   outperforming CFM’s best 2-step generation result with an FID of 5.34. Such strong empirical
331   performance marks the FGM as a solid solution for accelerating flow-matching models on standard
332   benchmarks. Besides the toyish CIFAR10 generation, in Section 5.2 we also use FGM to distill
333
      leading large-scale text-to-image flow models, obtaining a very robust one-step text-to-image model
      with almost no performance declines.

      5.1   O NE - STEP CIFAR10 G ENERATION

      Experiment Settings. We first evaluated the effectiveness of FGM on the CIFAR10 dataset
346   (Krizhevsky et al., 2014), the standard testbed for generative model performances. We pre-train
347   flow matching models on CIFAR10 conditional and unconditional generation using ReFlow objec-
348   tive (3.6). We refer to the neural network architecture used for EDM model(Karras et al., 2022).
349   We train both conditional and unconditional models with a batch size of 512 for 20000k images,
350   the resulting in-house-trained flow model shows a CIFAR10 unconditional FID of 2.52 with 300
351   generation steps, which is slightly worse than the original ReFlow model (Liu et al., 2022) which
352   has an FID of 2.58 using 127 generation steps. However, in Table 1, we find such a slightly worse
353   model does not influence the distillation of a strong one-step generator.
354   These flow models serve as the teacher models for flow generator matching (FGM). Then we apply
355   FGM to distill one-step generators from flow models. We assess the quality of generated images via
356   Frechet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017). Lower FID scores indicate higher sample
357   quality and diversity.
358   Notice that loss (4.11) and loss (4.12) together composite a full parameter gradient of the FGM loss.
359   We find two losses works great for toyish 2D dataset generations using only Multi-layer percep-
360   tions. In practice, we find that using loss (4.11) on CIFAR10 models leads to instability, which is
361   a similar observation as Poole et al. (2022) that the condition number of its Jacobian term might be
362   ill-posed. Therefore we do not use loss (4.11) when training and observing good performances. The
363   experiments conducted w and w/o regression loss (4.11) can be found in the Appendix C.2. Training
364   details and hyperparameters are shown in Appendix B.1.

525   6   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we introduce flow-generator matching (FGM), a strong probabilistic one-step distil-
528   lation approach for flow-matching models. We establish the theoretical foundations of FGM. We
529   also validate the strong empirical performances of FGM on both one-step CIFAR10 generation and
530
      large-scale one-step text-to-image generation.
531   Though FGM has a solid theoretical foundation as well as strong empirical performances, it still has
532   limitations. The first limitation is that currently the FGM still requires an additional flow model that
533   is used for approximating the generator-induced flow vectors. This requirement asks for additional
534   memory costs for distillation and potentially brings challenges when pre-trained flow models and the
535   generators are of large model sizes. Secondly, the FGM is a purely image-data-free approach, which
536
      means that it does not need real image data when distilling. However, as a well-known argument,
      consistently incorporating high-quality image data is important to improve the performances of text-
537
      to-image generative models. We hope that future works will explore how to integrate data into the
538
      distillation process.

       B.2   T EXT- TO -I MAGE
973
974    Hyper-parameters We detail the hyperparameters used in the distillation of our text-to-image
975    models, specifically for both the one-step generator and the online flow model. Both models are
976    trained in BF16 precision using the Adam optimizer with the following settings: β1 = 0, β2 =
977    0.999, ϵ = 1.0 × 10−6 , and a learning rate of 5.0 × 10−6 . For both the FGM loss and the flow
978    matching loss, we sample timestep t ∈ [0, 1], following the Esser et al. (2024) using a logit-normal
979    distribution as the timestep density function. The FGM loss employs π(2.4, 1.0), while the flow
980
       matching loss uses π(−1.0, 2.0). During the generator training phase, the GAN loss weight is set to
       1 × 10−2 , whereas for the discriminator training, it is set to 5 × 10−2 . Additionally, we apply a loss
981
       scaling factor of 100 for the generator, and the entire model is trained with a batch size of 192.
982
983
       Training Details During the training of the generator, we employed classifier-free guidance for
984    inference on the teacher model when calculating L2 (θ). To prevent artifacts in the output caused by
985    an excessively high guidance scale, we opted for a more stable guidance scale of 4.0. To further re-
986    duce memory consumption, we pre-encoded the prompts dataset into embeddings. For the negative
987    prompts used in classifier-free guidance, we used empty text for encoding and storage. Additionally,
988    by applying Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) across the teacher model, online flow model, and
989    generator, we achieved a batch size of 4 with a gradient accumulation of 6, ultimately allowing us
990    to reach a batch size of 192 on 8xH800-80G.
991
992    Discriminator Design For the design of the discriminator’s network architecture, we drew on pre-
993
       vious work (Yin et al., 2024a), using the online flow model itself as a feature extractor for images,
       supplemented by a lightweight convolutional network as the classification head to differentiate be-
994
       tween the distributions of noisy real data and generated data. However, unlike Yin et al. (2024a), the
995
       teacher model we chose does not have an explicit encoder structure. As a result, we output the hidden
996    states from different layers of the transformer and found that the shallow features, specifically those
997    from layer 2, better reflect the content of the image compared to deeper layers. Thus, we empirically
998    selected this layer’s features as the input for the subsequent classification head. Additionally, as
999    mentioned earlier, we discovered that GAN can perform well in certain noise ranges where FGM
1000   is inefficient. Therefore, another distinction from Yin et al. (2024a) is our different design for the
1001   noise schedules used for FGM and GAN loss. The former primarily samples in high-noise ranges,
1002   while the latter focuses on sampling in lower-noise ranges. GAN training is conducted on a syn-
1003   thetic dataset containing approximately 500K high-quality images at a resolution of 1024px. Texts
1004
       and images have also been pre-encoded and stored to reduce computational load during training.
1005
       Model Parameterization The standard flow-matching model for generating data from noise can
1006
       be represented in EDM formulation as follows:
1007
1008                           x0 = cskip · z − cout · vθ (cin · z, t), z ∼ N (0, I)                   (B.1)
                                                                            ∗                       ∗
1009   Generally, the conventional choices for one-step generator are t = t = 1, cskip = 1, cout = t , cin =
1010   1. However, in practice, we identified two empirical modifications to these parameters that can
1011   further enhance the model’s generation performance.
1012   First, regarding the choice of t∗ , since we need to inherit weights from the teacher model, selecting
1013   t∗ effectively means choosing a specific vθ,t∗ from a family of models with shared parameters vθ,t .
1014   To optimize our initialization weights, we can select the model that performs best for one-step
1015   generation within this family. Given a simple hyperparameter search, we noticed that t∗ = 0.97
1016   is a good choice.
1017   Second, we examined the input scaling factor cin . While the standard choice is cin = 1, we noticed
1018   during our training that the generated results consistently contained some small noise and blurriness
1019   that were difficult to eliminate. After multiple tuning attempts, we suspected that the variance of the
1020   model input was too large. We decided to slightly reduce the input variance and chose cin = 0.8.
1021   Consequently, we derived our final model parameterization:
1022                              x0 = z − 0.97 · vθ (0.8 · z, 0.97)), z ∼ N (0, I)                     (B.2)
