011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Training a precise binary classifier with limited supervision in weakly supervised
014            learning scenarios holds considerable research significance in practical settings.
015            Leveraging pairwise unlabeled data with confidence differences has been demon-
016
               strated to outperform learning from pointwise unlabeled data. We theoretically
               analyze the various supervisory signals reflected by confidence differences in con-
017
               fidence difference (ConfDiff) classification and identify challenges arising from
018
               noisy signals when confidence differences are small. To address this, we parti-
019            tion the dataset into two subsets with distinct supervisory signals and propose a
020            consistency regularization-based risk estimator to encourage similar outputs for
021            similar instances, mitigating the impact of noisy supervision. We further derive
022            and analyze its estimation error bounds theoretically. Extensive experiments on
023            benchmark and UCI datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. Addi-
024            tionally, to effectively capture the influence of real-world noise on the confidence
025            difference, we artificially perturb the confidence difference distribution and demon-
026            strate the robustness of our method under noisy conditions through comprehensive
027
               experiments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Weakly supervised learning is an essential research field in machine learning, focusing on training
033
      accurate predictive models under conditions of low supervision or imprecise labeling. Due to the
      difficulty of obtaining precise supervision in real-world scenarios, weakly supervised learning holds
034
      significant research value and significance for effectively leveraging limited available supervision
035
      information. Consequently, the field of weakly supervised learning has increasingly attracted attention
036   from experts and scholars in recent years, leading to the emergence of many typical weakly supervised
037   learning methods, such as multi-instance learning [32; 30; 24; 19], positive and unlabeled (PU)
038   learning [10; 5; 31; 16; 23], and others.
039
      A prevalent idea in weakly supervised classification involves maximizing the utilization of pointwise
040
      weakly supervised information [4], thereby prompting the development of various techniques based
041
      on soft labels [18; 26], mixup [28; 21; 27; 9; 7; 13], and others. Nevertheless, it is undeniable that
042   annotating pointwise information in real-world classification problems is a complex and laborious
043   task, further compounded by the personal biases of annotators which frequently exacerbate the
044   probability of inaccuracies. In such scenarios, pairwise comparison information between data points
045   may be more readily obtainable in real-world settings than pointwise information, and it often exhibits
046   greater resistance to biases compared to pointwise semi-supervised information [1]. For instance,
047   in medical diagnosis, accurately determining whether a patient has a disease solely based on their
048   presented symptoms is challenging. However, comparing the symptoms of this patient with those of
049   others provides more accessible information and reduces the probability of misdiagnosis. Extensive
050
      research has been conducted on pairwise analysis in numerous binary classification problems, leading
      to the development of risk minimization functions capable of inducing binary classifiers across
051
      various combinations of pairwise similarities, dissimilarities, and unlabeled data [1; 20; 14; 15; 22].
052
053   In recent work, pairwise comparison (Pcomp) classification has shown that in tackling difficult
      point labeling tasks, people can more easily gather comparative information between two examples,

054
      constituting a form of weakly supervised information [4]. However, in real-world application scenar-
055   ios, individuals may not only distinguish which of two examples is more likely to be classified as
056   positive over the other but also gauge the extent of the disparity in their confidence levels regarding
057   positivity. In light of this framework, Wang et al. introduced a new pairwise weakly supervised
058   classification problem called confidence-difference (ConfDiff) classification, and proposed the cor-
059   responding ConfDiff method [22]. To establish confidence difference, the ConfDiff method first
060   utilizes binary-labeled data to train a probability classifier. Subsequently, unlabeled data pairs are fed
061   into the classifier to generate posterior probabilities, from which confidence difference are computed
062   based on the differences between these posterior probabilities. However, through the analysis of the
063
      various supervised signals in the ConfDiff method, we identify that ConfDiff method encourages
      unlabeled data pairs to predict opposite classes from both experimental and theoretical perspectives.
064
      This prediction direction is valid when the confidence difference is large. However, when the con-
065
      fidence difference is small, the instances may belong to the same or different classes, and such a
066   predictive tendency may lead to samples from the same class being incorrectly classified as belonging
067   to different classes, thereby introducing noisy supervisory signals.
068
069
      To handle this problem, in this paper, we concentrate on mitigating the impact of inaccurate predictions
      when confidence differences are small. Specifically, we analyze the different supervised signals
070
      induced by varying confidence differences in the ConfDiff method. We find that pairwise instances
071
      with small confidence differences tend to introduce noisy supervised signals, while those with larger
072   confidence differences provide more reliable supervision. Based on this observation, we propose
073   a ConfDiff classification method that incorporates consistency regularization. By partitioning the
074   dataset based on the accuracy of predictive information, we introduce a consistency regularization term
075   for the subset with relatively precise predictions, encouraging the model to produce similar outputs
076   for pairs with small confidence differences. Meanwhile, for the subset with relatively imprecise
077   predictions, we preserve the benefit of reliable supervised signals. Experimental results demonstrate
078   that our method outperforms existing baselines in most cases and exhibits strong robustness even
079   under artificial noise interference.
080   In summary, this paper’s key contributions can be outlined as follows:
081
082          • We introduce a method for ConfDiff classification which aims to enhance the accuracy of
083            weakly supervised classification by constructing risk estimator through Consistency Risk
084
               and Consistency Regularization (CRCR).
085          • We theoretically analyze various supervised signals reflected by different confidence differ-
086            ences in ConfDiff classification. Additionally, we theoretically estimate the error bounds of
087
               our proposed method.
088          • We validate the effectiveness of our method through experiments by comparing it with
089            existing baselines on datasets of varying scales. In addition, the robustness of our method is
090
               further validated under the influence of artificially added noise.

      5     C ONCLUSION
527
      In this paper, we propose a novel ConfDiff classification method based on consistency risk and con-
528
      sistency regularization to address the challenge of noisy supervised signals in ConfDiff classification.
529
      We conduct a theoretical analysis of various supervised signals associated with different confidence
530   differences. Based on this analysis, the ConfDiff dataset is partitioned into two subsets according
531   to the reliability of the supervised information. For the subset with more reliable supervision, we
532   employ a consistency risk to preserve precise supervised information. Conversely, for the subset with
533   less reliable supervision, we leverage consistency regularization to mitigate the impact of erroneous
534   predictions. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that the proposed CRCR method outperforms
535   state-of-the-art baselines and exhibits strong robustness, even under artificially induced noise.

      C    L IMITATIONS
811
812
      The noise generation method we proposed primarily utilizes a Gaussian distribution to perturb confi-
813   dence difference distributions originally concentrated around specific values, aiming to approximate
814   the confidence difference distributions that may manifest in the real world. Consequently, artificial
815   datasets are utilized. In the future, we may consider annotating pairwise confidence difference
816   datasets derived from real-world scenarios. It would allow for experiments using authentic datasets
817   rather than artificially constructed ones, offering substantial practical significance.
818
      Additionally, the datasets used are actually multi-label datasets although we focus on binary clas-
819   sification problems in weakly supervised learning. Then the labels of these multi-label datasets
820   are partitioned into two disjoint subsets, each serving as positive and negative classes, respectively,
821   thereby converting them into binary classification datasets. In the future, we will consider expanding
822   the problem scenario to multi-label classification.
823
824
      D    B ROADER I MPACTS
825
826
      The noise confidence difference classification proposed in this paper stands to notably improve
827
      decision accuracy in real-world settings. It addresses potential noise impacts present in real-world data
828   and holds substantial practical significance as a plausible scenario in weakly supervised domains. Its
829   applicability can be extended to various fields including medical diagnosis, rehabilitation assessment,
830   and financial risk management.
831
      However, it’s important to acknowledge that the confidence difference utilized in our method within
832
      weakly supervised settings might be influenced by potential data biases inherent in the real world.
833
      Furthermore, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in weakly supervised scenarios,
834   there’s a risk of excessive dependence on algorithms for decision-making, potentially overlooking the
835   cultivation of individual decision-making capabilities and autonomy.
