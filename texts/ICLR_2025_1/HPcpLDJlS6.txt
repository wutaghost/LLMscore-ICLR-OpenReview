009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose stated causal language modeling (stated-CLM), a novel method to en-
012            hance the memory capacity of large language models (LLMs) without modifying
013            their architecture or parameters. Unlike existing context segmentation and sliding
014            methods that discard low-weight tokens, stated-CLM compresses adjacent tokens,
015            significantly reducing context information loss. We utilize the classic network
016
               pruning techniques with second-order derivatives to optimize the compressed to-
               ken in the differentiable key-value space. Experiments on LLaMA, Mistral, and
017
               Gemma demonstrate that stated-CLM outperforms baselines on the LongBench
018
               benchmark by an average of 6.12% (LLaMA3.1-8B) and 5.97% (Mistral-v0.3-
019            7B). On TopicRet, stated-CLM achieves accuracy levels comparable to full con-
020            text models, while the baselines’ accuracy is close to zero.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Memorizing certain knowledge is a critical requirement for large-scale language models (LLMs).
026   For example, a company’s enterprise chatbot needs to memorize necessary facts about its products.
027
      Existing LLMs with sequence-to-sequence and attention mechanisms have inherent memorization
      limitations: they rely on context to represent memory, meaning that this relevant knowledge must be
028
      included within the context. This approach faces two main challenges: a rigid limitation on context
029
      length and the quadratic growth in computational complexity.
030
031   Our goal is to enable LLMs to remember more without excessively occupying context length. A
032   typical approach is to add a differentiable external memory module to existing models (Weston et al.,
033
      2015)(Wu et al., 2022)(Munkhdalai et al., 2024)(He et al., 2024)(Tworkowski et al., 2024). The
      additional memory module encodes contexts, enhancing the memorization ability by retrieving the
034
      module when predicting the next token. However, this memory enhancement requires architecture
035
      modification and adaptive fine-tuning of the original LLMs, which increases the additional training
036   overhead. The fine-tuning data may be biased and compromise the generalizability of the original
037   model.
038
039
      To retain the generalization abilities of LLMs, we aim to enhance the memory capacity of orig-
      inal LLMs without architecture or parameter modification. This means that the same length of
040
      contextual tokens need to memorize more information for upcoming token predictions. Recently
041
      emerging context segmentation and sliding methods have been exploring this route, including
042   StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023), LazyLLM (Fu et al., 2024), and LongCache (Liu et al., 2024b).
043   The core strategy of these methods is to retain only the critical tokens in the context (e.g., tokens
044   with the highest attention scores) and discard other tokens. Although this route does not disrupt the
045   original LLMs, the context is compromised. A large number of low-weight tokens that may still
046   have an impact on the token predictions are directly discarded, resulting in context information loss.
047
      In this paper, we compress rather than discard low-weight tokens while keeping the architecture and
048   parameters in the original LLMs. This reduces context information loss while inheriting the general-
049   ization ability of the original LLMs. Our intuition lies in the fact that language can naturally be short-
050   ened. A simple example is that Alice went to the library. The library was
051   quiet. can be shortened to Alice went to the library. It was quiet. LLMs
052   have the ability to make consistent subsequent predictions for both contexts. From a memory per-
053   spective, LLMs have the ability to memorize more information with fewer tokens (i.e., using “It” to
      memorize “The library”).

      This inspires us to propose the stated causal language modeling (stated-CLM), aimed at enhancing
055   the context-memorization capabilities of LLMs. We extend the iterative next token prediction in
056   the existing CLM and propose an iterative next token prediction and adjacent token compression
057   method. The core idea relies on a simple yet powerful operation - adjacent token compression —
058   to achieve atomic memorization operations. More specifically, after generating each next token, we
059   select a pair of adjacent tokens in the context and compress them into one token, thereby maintaining
060   the context at a desired length. Compared to existing context segmentation methods, the compressed
061   token inherits the information of two original tokens, thus reducing information loss.
062   We compare stated-CLM with context segmentation methods in Figure 1. StreamingLLM and Long-
063   Cache discard a large number of low-weight tokens, resulting in a loss of context information. In
064   contrast, stated-CLM preserves context information to the greatest extent possible while reducing
065   token volumes. Furthermore, since each atomic compression operation only involves two adjacent
066   tokens, stated-CLM incurs lower compression loss per iteration compared to traditional blockwise
067   encoding and compression methods (Xiao et al., 2024)(Munkhdalai et al., 2024). This makes it
068   more feasible for the iterative token-by-token generation process. The stated-CLM can be adapted
069
      to existing CLMs without any fine-tuning.

084   To implement adjacent token compression without fine-tuning model parameters, our goal is to
085   find an optimal replacement token for a pair of tokens (for example, using “It” to replace “The
086   library”). First, to make the optimization differentiable, we perform compression in the key-value
087
      space instead of the token space. We refer to network pruning techniques (LeCun et al., 1989), using
      second derivatives to optimize and compute the optimal compressed token encoding, and employ the
088
      Fisher Information Matrix to enhance computational efficiency. We also ensure that in the attention
089
      mechanism, once the key-values are replaced with multiple identical tokens, any new query can
090   produce consistent outputs. To this end, we extend the original attention mechanism to accurately
091   restore the outputs before compression.
092
093
      We conducted extensive experiments over LLaMA (Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral, and Gemma (Team
      et al., 2024). The results demonstrate that stated-CLM significantly enhances the memory capacity
094
      of the original LLMs. On the LongBench benchmark, the stated-CLM based on LLaMA3.1-8B
095
      outperformed the competitors by an average of 6.12%, while the Mistral-v0.3-7B-based version
096   showed an average improvement of 5.97%. In the historical information retrieval task TopicRet,
097   where competitors’ accuracy was nearly zero, stated-CLM achieved accuracy levels comparable to
098   those obtained using full context.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Enhancing the memorization capabilities of language models has emerged as a critical problem.
104
      Various methods have been proposed for long-context LLMs. We discuss three techniques that are
      more related to our study below.
105
106   Sparse Attention Mechanisms The original Transformer has O(n2 ) complexity for a sequence
107   with n tokens, due to the dense attention access. Therefore, a simple way is to reduce the density
      of attention to O(n). Typical approaches include Sparse Transformer (Child et al., 2019), Long-


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      former (Beltagy et al., 2020), and Big Bird (Zaheer et al., 2020). These methods leverage some prior
109   dependency assumptions to optimize the attention patterns, significantly reducing computational
110   complexity and enabling the model to handle longer sequences. However, such prior assumptions
111   may not always hold in practice, and the resulting sparsity may not be effective in capturing all
112   relevant dependencies. Besides, adapting existing LLMs with dense attention to sparse attention is
113   nontrivial and leads to generalization capability loss.
114
      External Memory Modules To expand the memory capacity of models, researchers have incor-
115   porated external knowledge storage modules (Weston et al., 2015)(Wu et al., 2022)(Munkhdalai
116   et al., 2024)(He et al., 2024)(Tworkowski et al., 2024). Memorizing Transformers shows that related
117   key-value pairs can be retrieved through a kNN lookup. Focused Transformer (Tworkowski et al.,
118   2024) uses contrastive learning to help identify relevant and irrelevant tokens. LongMem (Wang
119   et al., 2024) and CAMELoT (He et al., 2024) uses decoupled LLMs and memory modules. During
120   training the memory modules, the parameters of the original LLMs are frozen. Although additional
121   memory modules can compress context, adjustments to the model architecture require retraining and
122   biases in the training data will be introduced into the model.
123   Context Segmentation and Sliding Researchers have developed various context strategies that op-
124   timize the contexts for memorization, instead of optimizing the models (Ge et al., 2024) (Mu et al.,
125   2024). Typical methods include StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2023), LazyLLM (Fu et al., 2024), and
126   LongCache (Liu et al., 2024b). These strategies dynamically select and retain key tokens, thereby
127   reducing the computational and storage burden when processing long sequences. StreamingLLM
128   ensures that the model focuses on the initial (sink) tokens and the recent tokens. LazyLLM employs
129
      a dynamic pruning strategy that selects different subsets of tokens in different generation steps,
      although they might be pruned in previous steps. LongCache intelligently manages the cache by
130
      expanding StreamingLLM to keep critical middle tokens. However, these compression strategies
131
      essentially involve directly discarding tokens with lower weights, which results in significant infor-
132   mation loss during the process.

      3.1   M EMORIZING ON NATIVE C ASUAL L ANGUAGE M ODELING
138
      Traditional causal language modeling (CLM) adopts the next-word prediction: iteratively predicting
139   the next token based on historical tokens. This paradigm demonstrates strong inference capabilities,
140   but has limitations in memory. It is formulated below:
141
                                          PCLM (wn+1 | w1 , . . . , wn )                                 (1)
142
143   where wn+1 denotes the current token, and w1 , . . . , wn represent the historical tokens. As we stated
144   in the introduction, the memorization capacity of CLMs is limited by the number of tokens n.
145   To enhance the memorization, we introduce the stated causal language modeling (stated-CLM) on
146   the native CLM. In stated-CLM, each token can be either an original input token or represent the
147   memory of a span of continuous tokens. To keep a desired number of tokens in the context, the next
148   token prediction is expanded to token generation and memorization.
149
      Generation-stage: Predicts the next token using existing tokens:
150
151                                     PCLM (wn+1 | w̃1 , w̃2 , . . . , w̃n )                           (2)
152   where w̃i represents a mixed token, which can be either an original token or reprsenting the memory
153   of a context span. Note that we do not manuplate the probabilistic model PCLM , the existing CLM
154   architecture and its pre-trained parameters are retained to pursue model generalization ability.
155   Memorization-stage: Compresses the long context into a shorter one while maintaining the consis-
156   tency of the subsequent predictions. This is achieved by an atomic operation: compress an adjacent
157   token pair from the context into a single token, so that the context length is reduced from n + 1 to n:
158
                                       compress(em , em+1 ) : R2×d → Rd                                  (3)
159
160   Here, em and em+1 are the embeddings of adjacent tokens wm and wm+1 , respectively.
161   compress(em , em+1 ) encodes these two adjacent tokens into a single token, thus achieving basic
      context compression functionality.

      m, m + 1 to be compressed, the CLM loss function before compression is LossCLM (e1 , e2 , . . . , en ).
191
      After compression, the loss function becomes:
192
193                       LossCLM (e1 , . . . , em−1 , compress(em , em+1 ), em+2 , . . . , en )              (6)
194   Our objective is to find the optimal compressed embedding that minimizes the disturbance.
195

196

197   Therefore, we the optimal compressed token embedding via the following two steps:
198
199           1. Convert Adjacent Tokens into Identical Embeddings. Replace (em , em+1 ) with two
200              identical vectors (e∗ , e∗ ), thus maintaining the dimensionality of the input.
201           2. Expand the Attention Mechanism to Support Identical Embeddings. Expand the at-
202              tention mechanism to efficiently handle tokens with multiple identical embeddings. The
203              outputs need to be consistent with the uncompressed tokens for any query embeddings.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduced stated causal language modeling, a novel approach to enhance the
489
      context memorization capabilities of large language models without modifying their architecture or
490   parameters. By leveraging the natural compressibility of language, our method compresses adjacent
491   tokens in the context rather than discarding them, significantly reducing information loss compared
492   to existing context segmentation methods.
493
      Our experiments across multiple LLM architectures, including LLaMA, Mistral, and Gemma,
494
      demonstrate the effectiveness of stated-CLM in improving memory capacity. On the LongBench
495
      benchmark, stated-CLM outperformed baselines by an average of 6.12% with LLaMA3.1-8B and
496   5.97% with Mistral-v0.3-7B. In the challenging historical information retrieval task TopicRet,
497   stated-CLM achieved accuracy levels comparable to full context use, where competitors’ perfor-
498   mance was near zero.
