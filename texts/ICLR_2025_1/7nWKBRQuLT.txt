009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding 3D scenes with point cloud data in tasks such as object referenc-
012            ing, question-answering, and captioning poses significant challenges to vision lan-
013            guage models (VLMs), due to the complexity of integrating both linguistic and
014            spatial information. While existing methods have mapped point cloud features
015            into LLM space to enable 3D scene comprehension, they often overlook view-
016            point information and the relative spatial distance between objects, this can lead
017            to confusion in interpreting spatial descriptions and grounding objects. This pa-
018
               per presents a geometry-enhanced vision LM (GeVLM) to address these chal-
               lenges. Specifically, we propose viewpoint-consistent position encoding (VCPE)
019
               and distance-aware cross-entropy (DACE) loss, which enhance the model’s abil-
020
               ity to interpret relative spatial relationships agnostic to camera viewpoint and in-
021            corporate distance information in the label space. We additionally introduce the
022            DetailedScanRefer dataset, which provides identifiers and spatial annotation for
023            each object mentioned in the referencing description to further emphasize spatial
024            relationships. GeVLM demonstrates significant improvements over the Chat-3D
025            v2 baseline, particularly with 4.0% and 2.7% absolute increase in Acc@0.25 and
026            Acc@0.50 respectively on the ScanRefer benchmark.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rapid advancement of Multimodal Large Language Models (LLMs) has greatly enhanced their
032   capabilities in addressing a wide range of tasks involving complex input modalities, such as audio
033
      (Tang et al., 2024a; Chu et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2024), images (Liu et al., 2024c;b; Li et al.,
      2023; Bai et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b) and videos (Zhang et al., 2023a; Cheng
034
      et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024). Recent studies have focused on extending the application of LLMs
035
      to the understanding of realistic 3D scenes represented by point clouds(Han et al., 2023; Hong
036   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024b;a), enabling these models
037   to perform tasks such as question-answering, object referencing, and captioning for real-world 3D
038   scenes. Specifically, the task of 3D referencing (Chen et al., 2020) requires LLMs to comprehend
039   detailed object descriptions while simultaneously understanding complex 3D scenes to accurately
040   identify the object being referenced. This task presents significant challenges due to the need for a
041   comprehensive understanding of both linguistic and spatial information.
042   Previous work in this area has successfully grounded LLMs on 3D point clouds, demonstrating scene
043   comprehension abilities (Hong et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023). Chat-3D (Wang et al., 2023b) maps 3D
044   features into the LLM space and uses a relation module to capture spatial relationships, showcasing
045   strong conversational abilities within 3D environments. Further advancements (Huang et al., 2023b)
046   enhance 3D object referencing by integrating unique identifiers with detailed scene annotations.
047   Nevertheless, these approaches overlook the importance of viewpoint consistency across different
048   examples by using 3D world coordinates as input. Moreover, the training objectives are simply
049   cross-entropy (CE) which penalizes other objects equally regardless of whether they are close or
050
      far to the target. These along with suboptimal positional encodings limit performance in scene
      understanding and object grounding.
051
052   To address the aforementioned deficiencies, this paper proposes a geometry-enhanced visual lan-
053   guage model (GeVLM) to improve 3D object grounding performance from perspectives including
      model structure, training criteria and dataset annotations. Specifically, we propose the viewpoint-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      consistent position encoding (VCPE) which allows relative spatial relationships, e.g. left/right, to be
055   correctly referred to under arbitrary camera viewpoint. Besides, we propose distance-aware cross-
056   entropy (DACE) loss which incorporates relative distance information into the label space so that
057   non-target object tokens receive different levels of penalization depending on their spatial affinity
058   to the target. To further boost the 3D grounding performance, we propose the DetailedScanRefer
059   dataset which includes the object identifier and the location for each object mentioned in the descrip-
060   tion. As a result, GeVLM showed consistent improvements over the Chat-3D-v2 on a range of 3D
061   scene understanding tasks. Specifically, GeVLM achieved 4.0% and 2.7% absolute improvements
062   in Acc@0.25 and Acc@0.50 on ScanRefer respectively.
063   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
064
065          • This paper presents GeVLM, a geometry-enhanced VLM for 3D object referencing and
066            understanding, leveraging easy-to-refer object identifiers. To the best of our knowledge,
067            GeVLM is the first visual LLM that formally investigates and incorporates 3D viewpoint
068
               information and relative 3D spatial distance in visual LLMs.
069          • We propose VCPE to ensure position encoding consistency of point cloud coordinates
070            across different viewpoints. In addition, we propose DACE to inject distance information

075            in Acc@0.25 and 2.7% in Acc@0.50 on the ScanRefer benchmark.

079   3D Grounding using Language Models Recent research has explored the integration of Large
080   Language Models (LLMs) with 3D object understanding for various applications. LLM-Grounder
081   (Yang et al., 2024) utilizes LLMs to decompose complex queries and evaluate spatial relations for
082   zero-shot 3D visual grounding. Grounded 3D-LLM (Chen et al., 2024b) introduces scene referent
083   tokens and contrastive language-scene pre-training to unify various 3D vision tasks within a gen-
084
      erative framework. Transcrib3D (Fang et al., 2024) brings together 3D detection methods and the
      emergent reasoning capabilities of large language models (LLMs). Cube-LLM (Cho et al., 2024),
085
      a multi-modal large language model, can ground and reason about 3D objects in images without
086
      3D-specific architectural design or training.
087
088
      Language-Driven 3D Scene Understanding There has been growing interest in using natural
089   language to enhance how computers interpret and interact with 3D environments. This approach,
090   known as “3D-language scene understanding,” involves training models to understand 3D scenes
091   based on verbal instructions. This method is applied across several tasks. Specifically, 3D Visual
092   Grounding (Chen et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Hsu et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
093   2024; Unal et al., 2024) involves models identifying a specified object within a 3D scene according
094   to a language query.
095
096   3D Large Multi-modal Models Through the usage of large scale 3D object datasets (Yu et al.,
097   2022; Xue et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), 3D Object-level Large Multi-modal Models (LMMs)(Xu
098   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Qi et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024b) have managed to bridge the gap
099
      between 3D modality and texts. However, these models fall short when complex spatial reasoning
      is needed for 3D scenes. Therefore, multiple models (Ziyu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang
100
      et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024b) have been proposed as scene-level LLMs. 3D-LLM (Hong et al.,
101
      2023) uses point clouds and their features as input and can handle various 3D-related tasks. The
102   model attempts to improve the understanding of complex spatial relationships among objects by
103   using positional embeddings and learning location tokens. However, the model projects 3D features
104   into the 2D feature space of a pretrained vision-language model, posing significant challenges to
105   capture the 3D spatial structure and complex relationships among objects. Chat-3D (Wang et al.,
106   2023b) and Chat-Scene (Huang et al., 2023a) directly utilizes 3D scene-text data to align the 3D
107   scene with large language model (Llama). However, Chat-3D could only handle one target object per
      conversation. To overcome this limitation, Chat-3D-v2 (Huang et al., 2023b), as our baseline model,

112   3     G EOMETRY-E NHANCED V ISUAL LM
113
114
      In this section, we introduce a novel geometry-enhanced vision-language model (GeVLM) designed
      to address 3D object grounding tasks. Our approach builds on the principles of Chat-3D, leveraging
115
      object identifiers for efficient reference within a 3D point cloud. GeVLM integrates a 3D segmenter,
116
      a 3D feature encoder, a 3D position encoder, and a pretrained language model (LLM). Notably, the
117   3D segmenter and feature encoder remain frozen during training. The primary goal is to fine-tune
118   the pretrained LLM to interpret language referring expressions by incorporating 3D geometric cues.
119
120
      These geometric cues are considered in two key aspects. First, we propose a viewpoint-consistent
      position encoding (VCPE) to account for camera perspective in 3D scene understanding, as detailed
121
      in Section 3.1. Second, we introduce a distance-aware cross-entropy (DACE) loss, discussed in
122
      Section 3.2, to highlight the importance of spatial affinity in the grounding task. Additionally, in
123   Section 4, we present a densely annotated grounding dataset, curated with assistance from GPT-4o.

146   3.1   V IEWPOINT-C ONSISTENT P OSITION E NCODING
147
      Imagine you are inside a room and refer to the chair in front of you. The success of the referring
148
      depends on the viewpoint of the observer. In other words, ambiguities will arise if the viewpoint is
149
      not known. This is supported by the fact that the annotation from ScanRefer includes the camera
150   pose information. However, existing methods (Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023b) overlook
151   the viewpoint information, hence refer to the same 3D point cloud when querying different referring
152   descriptions. We notice that the incorrect grounding outcomes are mainly due to the rotation of
153   the camera viewpoint, which makes relative spatial descriptions such as left/right and front/back
154   confusing to LLMs. For example, in the 3D scenein Fig.2 with 4 different viewpoints, the description
155   ”the shelf is to the right of the bed” only makes sense when observing the scene from a consistent
156   viewpoint, e.g. 1 and 4. Nevertheless, methods like Chat-3D and 3D-LLM ignore camera viewpoint,
157   and directly utilize world coordinates as input for object grounding. This inevitably introduces
158
      viewpoint inconsistency to the model training and leads to sub-optimal performance.
159   In GeVLM, we carefully transform 3D point cloud to ensure viewpoint consistency across referring
160   expressions. Based on the transformed coordinates, we propose a position encoding module, VCPE,
161   to effectively learn the relative spatial relationship for downstream 3D tasks. Specifically, to achieve
      viewpoint consistency, we apply a 3D transformation using the rotation matrix R ∈ R3×3 from

      3.2   D ISTANCE -AWARE C ROSS -E NTROPY L OSS
203
204   Most 3D VLMs commonly rely on language loss to fine-tune 3D tasks due to its simplicity. Efforts
205   have been made to unify multimodal tasks under a single language-based objective. However, we
206   argue that applying standard cross-entropy (CE) loss to 3D grounding tasks is inadequate. Specifi-
207   cally, when training a model with CE to predict the token for a referred object, it penalizes all other
208   object tokens equally. This contrasts with 3D object detection and segmentation, where the goal is
209   to minimize the distance between the ground truth and predictions.
210   Building on this insight, we propose DACE to incorporate geometric distance between objects into
211   the loss computation. This approach allows spatial relationships to be considered during training.
212   We categorize tokens into regular tokens and object tokens. We append 100 object tokens to rep-
213   resent scene objects with token IDs ranging from 32,000 to 32,099. For instance, object token
214   <OBJ000> will be indexed by 32,000. The DACE loss differentiates regular tokens and object to-
215   ken: standard CE loss is applied to the regular tokens, while a soft label is used for the object token
      predictions. We further detail the DACE loss next.

      Visibility and Object Annotation The need to do a visibility check during object annotation
296   arises from annotation noise. Simply projecting an object’s center onto the image can lead to incor-
297   rect annotations, as hidden or partially visible objects may be mistakenly included. This becomes
298   particularly problematic in later stages, such as querying GPT4o for high-quality responses, where
299   accurately labeling only visible objects is crucial. To address this issue, we project the 3D instance
300   segmentation mask to image space and compare it with the scene’s depth map. We project 3D points
301   onto the 2D image plane using the camera parameters as follows:
302
                                                  fx Xc                      fy Yc
303                                          u=         + cx ,        v=           + cy
                                                   Zc                         Zc
304
305   where (Xc , Yc , Zc ) represent the 3D point in the camera coordinate system, and fx , fy and cx , cy
306   denote the focal lengths and principal points of the camera, respectively. Visibility is confirmed
307   by comparing the estimated depth Zc with the depth map Ddepth , using the condition visible =
308   |Zc − Ddepth (u, v)| ≤ δ, where δ=0.1 meter accounts for minor discrepancies due to sensor noise.

331   Training Data We follow exactly the same training data setup as Chat3D-v2 (Wang et al., 2023b)
332   so that our results are directly comparable. The training datasets include ScanRefer (Chen et al.,
333   2020), Scan2Cap (Chen et al., 2021), ScanQA (Azuma et al., 2022), SQA3D (Ma et al., 2023),
334   Multi3DRef (Zhang et al., 2023b), and NR3D (Achlioptas et al., 2020). We also use ObjAlign,
335   which is a dataset for aligning object IDs with objects2 . Among these datasets, only ScanRefer
336   and Scan2Cap tasks use viewpoint information, as they are the only datasets providing it. The
337   proposed DetailedScanRefer, with about 16,000 samples in total, is also used where specified. For
338   validation, we use ScanRefer, Scan2Cap, ScanQA, SQA3D, and Multi3DRef to select the best model
339
      checkpoint. The Scan2Cap dataset is modified by associating a camera pose with each caption. We
      refer to Appendix B for details.

      Model and Training Specifications The proposed GeLVM uses the Vicuna-7B-v1.5 as the LLM
342
      backbone which is fine-tuned using the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022). To ex-
343   tract object features, we utilize the pretrained Uni3D (Chen et al., 2023a) as the 3D encoder which
344   is frozen during training. The segmentation model Mask3D (Schult et al., 2023) is employed for
345   consistent and accurate segmentation of the 3D data, which is also frozen during training. We have
346   used code from OpenIns3D (Huang et al., 2024b) to assist with visibility checks and to develop
347   visualization tools for pixel-level masks. The VCPE module uses 256-dim final positional embed-
348   ding, and 128-dim Fourier features. The positional embedding projection layer and the multi-head
349   attention module are also trainable components. There are 100 object proposals for each scene.
350   The entire training process using Adam optimizer and cosine learning rate scheduler for 3 epochs
351
      requires approximately 11 hours on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Our training settings are as follows.

462   6   C ONCLUSION
463
464   This paper introduces a geometry-enhanced visual language model (GeVLM) to improve 3D object
465   grounding and scene understanding. By integrating viewpoint-consistent position encoding (VCPE)
466   and distance-aware cross-entropy (DACE) loss, GeVLM achieves improved interpretation of spatial
467   relationships, while effectively incorporating distance information into the label space. Additionally,
468   the DetailedScanRefer dataset is proposed to offer dense object identifiers that complement object
469
      reference descriptions, enhancing the model’s spatial reasoning capabilities. GeVLM achieves sig-
      nificant performance gains over the Chat3D-v2 baseline, with notable improvements of 4.0% in
470
      Acc@0.25 and 2.7% in Acc@0.50 on the ScanRefer benchmark.
471
472
473   7   L IMITATIONS
474
475   Our work has several limitations, including the need for further refinement of the camera pose-
476   matching algorithm to achieve optimal performance. Our approach is specifically tailored to tasks
477   that rely heavily on viewpoint information, making accurate camera pose information essential to
478
      fully leverage the model’s capabilities. Many existing datasets lack this type of annotation, limiting
      the applicability of our method. Furthermore, the prompt design for photo annotation could be
479
      improved to enhance both efficiency and precision. Lastly, while this paper uses the Chat-3D-v2 as
480
      the baseline and follows the exact model and training configuration for direct comparability, we also
481   notice that, as a fast-evolving field, the latest work, such as Chat-Scene (Huang et al., 2023a), has
482   proposed foundation models that surpass the performance of Chat-3D-v2 baseline by clear margins.
483   However, our proposed methods are orthogonal to these advancements and, in theory, could be
484   applied to achieve further improvements. Exploring these opportunities will be important future
485   work when additional resources and time are available.

916   In this appendix, we provide a detailed explanation of the modifications made to the Scan2Cap
917   dataset for our viewpoint-aware captioning task. We also discuss the implications for evaluation and
      how these changes affect comparability with existing models.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Scan2Cap (Chen et al., 2021) is a captioning dataset generated based on ScanRefer (Chen et al.,
919   2020), which provides natural language descriptions of objects within 3D indoor scenes from the
920   ScanNet dataset (Dai et al., 2017).
921
922
      Each description in ScanRefer is associated with a specific camera pose. By reusing these camera
      poses, we reconstruct viewpoints for the Scan2Cap dataset, making it viewpoint-aware. This ap-
923
      proach enhances the dataset by incorporating spatial context and specific viewpoints, providing a
924
      more comprehensive captioning task.
925
926   The Scan2Cap dataset utilizes a set of predefined prompts to guide the captioning task. Notably,
927   these prompts are used in the original Chat-3D-v2 task (Huang et al., 2023b). The prompts are
928
      designed to elicit detailed descriptions of objects and their spatial relationships within a scene. The
      prompts include:
929
930
           1. “Begin by detailing the visual aspects of the <id> before delving into its spatial context
931           among other elements within the scene.”
932
           2. “First, depict the physical characteristics of the <id>, followed by its placement and inter-
933
              actions within the surrounding environment.”
934
935
           3. “Describe the appearance of the <id>, then elaborate on its positioning relative to other
              objects in the scene.”
936
937        4. “Paint a picture of the visual attributes of <id>, then explore how it relates spatially to
938           other elements in the scene.”
939        5. “Start by articulating the outward features of the <id>, then transition into its spatial align-
940           ment within the broader scene.”
941        6. “Provide a detailed description of the appearance of <id> before analyzing its spatial
942           connections with other elements in the scene.”
943        7. “Capture the essence of the appearance of <id>, then analyze its spatial relationships
944           within the scene’s context.”
945
           8. “Detail the physical characteristics of the <id> and subsequently examine its spatial dy-
946           namics amidst other objects in the scene.”
947
           9. “Describe the visual traits of <id> first, then elucidate its spatial arrangements in relation
948
              to neighboring elements.”
949
950
          10. “Begin by outlining the appearance of <id>, then proceed to illustrate its spatial orienta-
              tion within the scene alongside other objects.”
951
