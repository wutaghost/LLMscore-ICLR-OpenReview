011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Emergence, the phenomenon of a rapid performance increase once the model scale
014            reaches a threshold, has achieved widespread attention recently. The literature has
015
               observed that monosemantic neurons in neural networks gradually diminish as
               the model scale increases. Subsequently, Learning From Emergence is proposed
016
               to actively inhibit monosemantic neurons in relatively small neural networks (e.g.,
017
               BERT and Swin-Transformer) for promoting model performance with fine-tuning.
018            However, to ultimately achieve emergence, it is demanding to support the monose-
019            mantic neuron inhibition in the pretraining phase of large-scale models. Thus,
020            this work further pushes the boundary of this research direction to be Learning
021            Towards Emergence (L2E) and enables the training and validating of the impact
022            of inhibiting monosemantic neurons on larger pre-trained neural networks (e.g.,
023            Pythia-70M, 410M, and 2.8B). More specifically, to bridge the gap in current re-
024            search, we first conduct experiments on models of various scales (up to 6.9B) to
025            validate the monosemantic ideas. Then, we present a novel method L2E to ad-
026
               dress the inefficient monosemantic neuron retrieval and ineffective monosemantic
               neuron inhibition when existing methods are applied in the pretraining phase of
027
               large-scale models. It employs an adjustable thresholding technique for efficient
028
               neuron retrieval, incorporates a False Killing Rate metric to assess inhibition ef-
029            fects, and proposes a regularization-style inhibition approach, which addresses
030            the limitations of previous approaches in both efficiency and effectiveness. Ex-
031            perimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of L2E’s monosemantic neuron
032            inhibition and its efficiency in implementation with large-scale models.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The success of large-scale pretraining models, such as GPT-3.5 (Ouyang et al., 2022), has drawn
038   widespread attention in understanding their dynamics across different scales. Studies on Scaling
039
      Laws (Henighan et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020) have analyzed the relationship between scale and
      performance, which typically follows a mild power law. However, recent research has observed dra-
040
      matic performance improvements that defy these scaling laws when the model scales reach certain
041
      thresholds—a phenomenon termed Emergence (Wei et al., 2022). The resulting emergent abili-
042   ties of these models are somehow recognized as a key factor in their success, prompting numerous
043   follow-up investigations (Hu et al., 2024). Some studies suggest that the impressive emergence
044   phenomenon may simply caused by deficiencies in observing and evaluating the accumulation of
045   abilities (Schaeffer et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024). But such deficiencies may persist for a long time
046   because the commonly used unsupervised losses and weakly labeled datasets. Subsequently, a series
047   of studies try to predict (Hu et al., 2024) and induce (Wang et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024) emergence.
048   In earlier years, researchers propose the concept of monosemantic neurons to interpret model func-
049   tionality (Bau et al., 2020; Elhage et al., 2022). These neurons form 1-to-1 mappings with human-
050   friendly features (such as “dog” in images (Olah et al., 2020) or “Python” in code languages as
051   shown in Figure 1(a)). In contrast, polysemantic neurons are activated for multiple features (Goh
052   et al., 2021; Bricken et al., 2023) (see Figure 1(b)). The discovery of monosemantic neurons, es-
053   pecially when visualizing impressively (Olah et al., 2020), greatly excite researchers when neural
      networks are considered as black-box. However, following the favor of monosemantic neurons in

097

      and capture consistent patterns for guidance across scales. Finally, we propose a regularization-style
098
      inhibition approach, which addresses the ineffectiveness of existing work when applied to pretrain-
099
      ing tasks. Experiments conducted on various tasks and scales using Pythia models (Biderman et al.,
100   2023) (from 70M to 2.8B), validating the effectiveness and efficiency of L2E.

283   4.1              R ECALL OF ME ME L
284
285   As discussed in Section 2, Wang et al. (2024) introduces the MEmeL method to first detect monose-
286   mantic neurons and retrieve them, then inhibit them to promote the relatively small neural networks.
287
      However, the current method is limited by following 3 limitations. First, monosemantic neuron
      detection is an O(1) operation based on MS. Values of neurons z in a layer are ranked by their
288
      monosemantic scores (ϕ(z)). Then, the top-k neurons are selected for inhibition, where k is a
289
      relatively small number (≤ 100). But this setting of k lacks solid justification. A deeper insight
290   into the dynamics of monosemanticity is needed for high-quality detection. Secondly, top-k neu-
291   rons retrieval becomes inefficient when the number of neurons in a layer is large and k increases.
292   Specifically, the time complexity O(kN ) of comparing N neurons in the layer increases to sorting
293   with O(N log N ). This creates a significant bottleneck as the scale increases, necessitating a more
294   efficient method. Thirdly, after obtaining the neurons to deactivate, MEmeL proposed Reverse De-
295   activation to inhibit the selected neurons with theoretical guarantees. However, this method assumes
296   that neuron activation is a well-trained result rather than a mistake due to insufficient training. This
297   assumption is not always valid, especially when applied at the beginning of pretraining. Therefore,
298
      we propose L2E to address the 3 limitations of existing works on inhibiting monosemantic neurons.
299
300   4.2              L2E: L EARNING T OWARDS E MERGENCE
301
      False Killing Rate: Determines How Many Neurons to Inhibit. Recall that the previous method
302
      majorly tried to validate the assumption of the influence of monosemanticity, which only inhibited
303
      no more than 100 most monosemantic neurons (Wang et al., 2024). However, large models has
304   a great number of neurons in a single layer, e.g., 5,242,880 for the 2.8B pythia model, where the
305   influence of inhibiting dozens of neurons is neglegable.
306
307
      Here we discuss the intuition behind the setting of k. From the perspective of memorization and
      reasoning, monosemantic and polysemantic neurons are assumed to play different roles. To prevent
308
      the model from overfitting-style rote memory, we aim to keep the polysemantic neurons and inhibit
309
      the monosemantic ones. This is achieved by filtering out the neurons with top-k MS values. A small
310   k will lead to weak inhibition, while a large k may also inhibit polysemantic neurons, which impairs
311   functionality. To quantify the impairment, we propose the False Killing Rate (FKR) to measure
312   the proportion of unexpected inhibitions where the inputs are not from monosemantic features. To
313   be more specific, given a dataset with n input instances X = {x[i] }ni=1 and a layer of N neurons
314   z = {zj }Nj=1 , the FKR is defined as:

      Efficient Neuron Retrieval. When the inhibition level comes to several percentages (e.g., 2%)
349
      of neurons in a layer, efficiency becomes a bottleneck. Originally, finding the largest MS value
350   costs Θ(N ) times for a layer of N neurons, which will be extended to Θ(kN ) ∼ Θ(N 2 ) for the
351   top-k inhibition. Using special data structures such as heap can reduce the cost to O(N log(k)) ∼
352   O(N log(N )), which is still expensive for large scale models.
353
      Here, we design a moving threshold to circumvent the calculation of precise top-k value. Being
354
      inspired while conducting monosemanticity analysis, we found that the inhibition should be better
355
      as a global percentage rather than an in-batch ranking. For example, a single batch of inputs may
356   activate fewer monosemantic neurons, which should be given a lower level of inhibition. Therefore,
357   we maintain a moving threshold to inhibit the most monosemantic neurons globally. The detailed
358   implementation, primarily an engineering design, is provided in Appendix A. Our design involves
359   only an element-wise comparison per batch with an O(1) update to converge the threshold to the
360   global value of the 2%-th highest MS, achieving efficient inhibition.
361
      Efficient Neuron Inhibition. Given the identified monosemantic neurons, the previous method
362   developed Reverse Deactivation to inhibit them (Wang et al., 2024). This approach makes use of
363   the model’s reliance on monosemanticity to naturally deactivate neurons. In short, it assumes that a
364   monosemantic neuron is well-trained and effective, such that reducing its activation would lead to an
365   increase in loss. However, the assumption is more valid for a well-trained model but less prevalent
366   in the pretraining stage, particularly at its very beginning. To handle the problem, we propose a
367   regularization-style method to encourage the inhibition. Specifically, we introduce a new term to the
368   loss function to penalize the MS values of the monosemantic neurons directly, that is, to minimize

      5.4   L IMITATIONS
461
462   There is still work to be done to fully validate the effectiveness of L2E, especially its potential to
463   induce Emergence. Current experiments are limited to Pythia models, with the largest size being
464   2.8B and training steps at 10% of full pretraining. This limitation is an unavoidable trade-off at this
465   trial stage, where detailed analysis is required with our resources fully utilized.
466
      Additionally, monosemantic analysis relies heavily on feature datasets, which significantly influence
467
      the results. Our analyses show particular consistency with the Natural Language feature dataset, a
468   high-quality set where different languages are clearly distinguished. For each language, identifying
469   its context neurons is straightforward, with an inference accuracy of 100% (Gurnee et al., 2023).
470   However, there’s no consensus on how to properly define feature datasets, which hinders compre-
471   hensive monosemanticity analysis. To validate the universality of our findings, one need to conduct
472   analysis using more high-quality datasets. See Appendix B.1 for more results and discussions.

      6     C ONCLUSION
476
477
      Our paper addresses the challenges in understanding and improving large-scale pretraining models
      through the lens of monosemanticity. We experimentally validate the metric Monosemanticity Scale
478
      for quantifying monosemantic levels, which further enables a comprehensive analysis of monose-
479
      manticity dynamics across different model scales. Our main contribution, L2E (Learning Towards
480   Emergence), offers a novel approach to inhibiting monosemantic neurons in large-scale pretrain-
481   ing models. By incorporating the False Killing Rate metric, employing an adjustable thresholding
482   technique, and proposing a regularization-style inhibition approach,L2E addresses the limitations
483   of previous methods in both efficiency and effectiveness. Experimental results on Pythia models
484   across various tasks and scales demonstrate the potential of L2E in enhancing model performance
485   during pretraining. This work contributes to the ongoing research on understanding and inducing
      emergence in large language models, paving the way for future advancements in the field.

      C.1    R ELATIONSHIP WITH G ROKKING
953
954   While monosemanticity functions like rote memorization in neural networks, a phenomenon called
955   “grokking” also explores the negative impact of neuron memorization. The initial study by Power
956   et al. (2022) observed grokking in very small models, where the test loss dramatically decreased
957   after the training loss had converged for a long time. The authors highlighted an under-explored
958   area: how neural networks generalize beyond mere memorization? Unlike studies on emergence,
959   their research meticulously examined very small models (with only 2 layers). Interestingly, their
960   analysis on algorithmic datasets aligns with our findings, hypothesizing that memorization hinders
961   more complex reasoning.
962   To investigate the reason of grokking, Liu et al. (2022) examines multiple datasets using models
963   with fixed L2 norm of weight wc . They find that a large wc leads to poor representation and impairs
964   generalization in grokking. On the other hand, Nanda et al. (2023) focuses on the modular addi-
965   tion task and reverse engineering the model weights. They summarize the training process leading
966   to grokking in 3 stages: memorization, circuit formation, and memorization component removal.
967   Notably, both papers highlight grokking’s potential to aid in understanding emergence. However,
968
      unlike our work, their studies were limited to very small models.
969   Subsequent research has conducted case studies to explore the benefits of grokking (Xu et al., 2024)
970   and gain deeper understanding (Rubin et al., 2024; Levi et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024). Notably,
971   Levi et al. (2024) raises doubts about whether the occurrence of grokking might be due to the accu-
      racy measure used, a concern similar to that raised about Emergence (Schaeffer et al., 2023).


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       To conclude, complementing our research, grokking-related studies provide highly detailed analyses
973    of small models (Liu et al., 2022; Doshi et al., 2024; Pearce et al., 2023), similar to observing particle
974    motion through a microscope. Hopefully, these two research directions could yield discoveries
975    from different perspectives and potentially combine to further enhance model performance, such
976    as interchanging techniques for inducing grokking and emergence (Wang et al., 2024; Lyu et al.,
977    2024). Besides, we highlight an interesting example from their experiments, where an extremely
978    small model (with only 5 neurons) is incapable of even memorizing (Pearce et al., 2023). This raises
979    a question: for current challenging tasks, where do our models stand? Are they at the stage of partial
980    memorization awaiting generalization, or have they not even reached the point of memorization?
981
982    C.2    R ELATIONSHIP WITH S PARSITY
983
       When we introduce the idea of inhibiting monosemanticity, many readers express concern about its
984
       influence on sparsity, which is useful for efficient computation and model compression, especially
985
       in large models. In this section, we provide a concise review of sparsity and its potential conflicts
986    and cooperation with our proposition.
987
988    As models grow larger, researchers have observed that many weights become near-zero or insignif-
989
       icant, forming sparse connections between neurons (Han et al., 2015b; Li et al., 2017; Han et al.,
       2015a). Leveraging this sparsity, various pruning strategies for weights and neurons have been de-
990
       veloped to compress models while maintaining performance. Although weight-based pruning is
991
       theoretically efficient (Frankle & Carbin, 2019), it presents practical challenges (Sun et al., 2021),
992    often resulting in irregular networks that are difficult to deploy. To address this, new GPU architec-
993    tures have been proposed to support sparse computation (NVIDIA, 2020), alongside methodological
994    improvements (Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023).
995
       In addition to weight sparsity, researchers also focus on activation sparsity in neural networks (Kurtz
996
       et al., 2020; Akiva-Hochman et al., 2022). As discussed in Section 2, when using the ReLU acti-
997
       vation function, neuron outputs ≤ 0 are naturally considered inactive, creating a form of sparsity.
998    Researchers leverage this sparsity to reduce computation and enhance it further by setting values
999    below a higher threshold to 0 (Kurtz et al., 2020). Combining both weight and activation sparsity
1000   can improve model efficiency (Akiva-Hochman et al., 2022). Unlike static weight sparsity, utiliz-
1001   ing activation sparsity requires monitoring dynamic flow (i.e., each input has a different sparsity
1002   pattern), which is more challenging and functionally similar to our L2E.
1003
       In recent years, as large language models have grown, high-level designs supporting activation spar-
1004   sity have emerged, such as the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) layer (Fedus et al., 2022). While pruning
1005   based on activation values induces sparsity at irregular positions, MoE activates only one expert for
1006   each input, creating physical continuity on the GPU. This approach is more hardware-friendly and
1007   thus preferred in industrial pipelines.
1008
       The main conflict between sparsity and our method lies in the functional assumption of activation.
1009
       Monosemanticity forms a 1-to-1 correlation, which is considered similar to rote memorization and
1010   may hinder complex reasoning abilities. However, this 1-to-1 mapping is favored in sparsity studies.
1011   When we reduce monosemanticity, a single input would activate multiple neurons, rendering pruning
1012   based on sparsity ineffective. If both approaches are proved valid, balancing L2E and sparsity will
1013   become a trade-off between effectiveness and efficiency.
1014                                                                      Other activated neurons
       Fortunately, our current studies focus on the in-
