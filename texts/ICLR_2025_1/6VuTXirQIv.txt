009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphical modelling for structured data analysis has gained prominence across nu-
012            merous domains. A significant computational challenge lies in efficiently capturing
013            complex relationships within large-scale graph structures. Graph coarsening, which
014            reduces graph size by merging nodes and edges into supernodes and superedges,
015            enhances scalability and is crucial for graph neural networks (GNNs). However,
016
               current methods either construct graphs from large-scale attribute data or assume
               a pre-existing graph before coarsening, limiting their applicability, especially in
017
               domains like healthcare and finance where graph structure is often unavailable. In
018
               this paper, we present a novel framework that directly learns a coarsened graph
019            from attribute information, reducing computational complexity and enhancing
020            robustness against adversarial attacks, which commonly target vulnerabilities in
021            graph structures. By integrating label information, our framework also enables
022            semi-supervised learning, leading to improved performance on downstream tasks.
023            Extensive experiments show that our method outperforms state-of-the-art coarsen-
024            ing techniques in both accuracy and computational efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Graph-based methods are powerful tools for representing relationships between entities and are
029
      widely used across domains such as biology, finance, sociology, and engineering. In some cases,
030   relationships are explicit and directly observable, such as friendships in social networks [1], co-
031   authorships in academic publications [2], or neighbouring nodes in sensor networks [3]. However,
032   in many scenarios, such as gene regulation [4], stock trading behavior [4], or drug interactions [5],
033   relationships are latent and must be inferred. Inferring these relationships requires sophisticated
034   domain-specific approaches to construct high-quality graph representations tailored to specific tasks
035   [6; 7; 8; 9].
036   The increasing scale and diversity of modern datasets [10; 11; 12] pose significant challenges to
037   traditional graph-based methods. Chief among these is the high computational cost of existing
038   algorithms, which becomes prohibitive for large-scale datasets. Techniques such as graph coarsening,
039   condensation, and summarization have been introduced to address scalability by reducing the size
040   of graph structures [13; 14; 15; 16; 17; 18; 19]. However, these approaches rely on the existence
041   of an explicit graph structure, which is not always available. In such cases, a graph must first be
042   inferred from raw data, a computationally intensive process that exacerbates scalability and memory
043   constraints, particularly for large datasets.
044   To overcome the challenge of learning a graph structure prior to the coarsening process, this work
045   introduces a novel framework that directly learns a coarsened graph from the data itself. By eliminat-
046   ing the need for full graph construction, the proposed method effectively addresses key limitations of
047   traditional approaches, enabling more efficient handling of large-scale datasets and reducing both
048   computational and memory overhead.
049   We propose a novel optimization-based framework, Coarsened Graph Learning (CGL), which directly
050   learns a coarsened graph from feature data alone. CGL simultaneously resolves the challenges of
051   scalability and the need for an initial graph structure. While GNNs [20; 21; 22] have demonstrated
052   outstanding graph modeling abilities, they are vulnerable to noisy edge graphs, which can signifi-
053   cantly degrade their performance [23; 24]. Adversarial edges, often linking nodes with dissimilar
      labels or attributes, can contaminate node neighborhoods, propagating noise and corrupting node

054
      representations. This issue is particularly prevalent in real-world graphs, such as social networks,
055   where bots create links with regular users to spread misinformation. Considerable research has
056   focused on developing robust GNNs [25; 26], and our method also offers robustness by learning a
057   coarsened graph independently of the graph structure, making it impervious to adversarial attacks on

059
      The proposed CGL framework is a multi-block, non-convex optimization problem that can be

064   datasets. We also assess the time required for graph learning and coarsening, comparing traditional
065   methods like [6] with our direct coarsened graph learning approach via CGL. Finally, we demonstrate
066   the robustness of CGL by highlighting its resilience to adversarial attacks on graph structure, where
067   traditional coarsening methods falter.
068   Additionally, in practical applications, data points often come with some node label information. We
069   integrate this label information into our objective function, significantly enhancing downstream task
070   performance. Extensive experiments demonstrate that incorporating label information into the CGL
071   objective substantially improves its overall effectiveness. The key contributions of this work are:
072
073         1. A pipeline for performing downstream tasks using GNNs by utilizing only the features.
074         2. A novel optimization-based framework that “learns a coarsened graph directly from fea-
075            tures" or raw data for scaling of GNNs.
076         3. A label informed “semi-supervised coarsened graph learning" framework which enhances
077            the downstream task performance using GNNs.
078
            4. Through experimentation demonstrating the efficacy of proposed frameworks in terms of
079
               node classification performance and computational efficiency.

      2.1   G RAPH L EARNING FROM DATA X
101
      How to learn a p nodes graph with p data points X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xp ]⊤ ∈ Rp×n is well understood
102   [28; 29; 30; 6; 31]. The goal here is to infer the connectivity relationships between pair of datapoints
103   xi and xj in form of a graph matrix which is generally done by solving the following class of
104   optimization problem:
105                                      min f (Θ) + g(X, Θ) + αh(Θ)                                       (2)
                                        Θ∈SΘ
106
107   In this context, f (Θ) is a function that ensures the connectivity of the learned graph. For example,
      f (Θ) = − log det(Θ + J) has been used in [32], and f (Θ) = −1T log(A1), where A = D − Θ


                                                         2

      is the adjacency matrix and D is the degree matrix, has been employed in [6]. These functions are
109   designed to ensure connectivity in the graph. Next, the term g(X, Θ) is a graph fitting term. One
110   common choice is g(X, Θ) = tr(X ⊤ ΘX) [33; 6; 32], known as the smoothness or Dirichlet energy
111   of the graph. Minimizing this term implies that the learned graph is smooth, meaning that nodes
112   with similar features are connected through stronger weights. Lastly, h(Θ) acts as a regularizer to
113   impose additional desirable properties on the graph. For instance, h(Θ) = ∥Θ∥2F is often used to
114   ensure sparsity in the learned graph.

120
      mechanisms where each node aggregates information from its neighbors to update its representation

      iteratively. One of the common architecture of GNN is graph convolution network(GCN). Let
122   htv represent the feature vector of node v at iteration t. The representation ht+1  is computed by
                                                                                      v
123   aggregating the representation vectors htu of neighboring nodes u, resulting in a more stable and
124   informative representation. These enhanced representation vectors are then input into a task-specific
125   multi-layer perceptron (MLP) for final predictions.

133   2.3   G RAPH D IMENSIONALITY R EDUCTION
134
135   The size of the dataset is increasing day by day, leading to significant computational costs and memory
136
      requirements to learn the graph. Graph condensation and coarsening [13; 14; 15; 16; 17; 18; 19] are
      techniques to reduce graph complexity while preserving key information. In graph condensation [15],
137
      a smaller synthetic graph Gc = (Vc , Ec , Xc , Yc ) is generated to ensure that a graph neural network
138
      (GNN) trained on Gc performs comparably to one trained on the original G, using a loss function
139   L(GN NθGc (Gc ), GN NθG (G)). Graph coarsening [13; 14; 19], on the other hand, maps G = (V, E)
140   to a coarser graph Gc = (Vc , Ec ), with |Vc | < |V|, often through surjective mappings P , where
141   each non zero entry of mapping matrix P i.e. Pij indicates the j−th node of the original graph got
142   mapped to the i-th supernode of the coarsened graph. While condensation focuses on preserving GNN
143   performance, coarsening emphasizes maintaining structural properties. However, these techniques
144   assume the graph is available, which is not the case in domains like gene regulation or financial stock
145   analysis. In such cases, graph learning is required as a preliminary step, which is computationally
146   intensive for large datasets, and performing coarsening afterward further increases complexity. To
147   address this, we have developed a coarsened graph learning technique that directly learns a coarsened
148
      graph from raw data, eliminating the need for separate graph learning and coarsening steps, thereby
      significantly reducing computational overhead.
149
      Goal: Given p data points X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xp ]⊤ the goal is to efficiently learn a good quality
150
      coarsened graph Gc (Θc , Xc ) with k nodes, where k ≪ p.
151

290   In general, datasets consist of features X and label information Y , where Y ∈ {0, 1}n×l . If node v i
291   is labeled, yi represents the corresponding one-hot indicator vector; otherwise, yi = 0 for unlabeled
292   data. Incorporating this label information while learning a coarsened graph may lead to improve
293   performance in downstream tasks. Consider a toy example with 10 data points and 3 labels: {red,
294   blue, green}. We aim to learn a coarsened graph with 4 nodes. Various structures for the coarsened
295   graph are possible. Here, we illustrate two possible coarsened graphs, Gc1 and Gc2 . Training a GNN
296   on Gc2 results in a better-trained model compared to Gc1 . This is because, in Gc2 , data points with
297   similar labels are grouped into the same supernode, leading to a sparser [P Y ] matrix, which enhances
298
      the training efficiency and effectiveness of the GNN.

      5.3   T IME C OMPLEXITY
466
467   The per-iteration time complexity of the proposed CGL and SCGL algorithms is O(kpn + k 2 p),
468   where p represents the number of original data points, k is the number of nodes in the coarsened
469   graph, and n is the dimension of each data point xi . In comparison, the time complexity of existing
470   state-of-the-art graph coarsening techniques varies significantly. The GCOND algorithm, for instance,
471   has a time complexity of O(k 2 d2 + rL pd2 + Lk 2 d + Lkd), where r is the number of sampled
472   neighbors per node, L is the number of MLP layers, and d is the number of hidden units for all layers.
473   On the other hand, the FGC algorithm has a time complexity of O(7p2 k + 5k 2 p + 5kpn + k 3 ), with
474
      p representing the number of features, k the number of clusters, and n the number of nodes. It is
      evident from Table 6 that proposed CGL algorithm is much faster than existing state of the art graph
475
      coarsening techniques.

532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper introduces a novel Coarsened Graph Learning (CGL) framework that directly learns a
535   coarsened graph from raw data or features, eliminating the need for a graph structure. The framework
536   also includes a pipeline for GNNs that utilizes only feature data. By incorporating label information,
537   CGL enhances performance in node classification tasks. Extensive experiments show that CGL
538   outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy and efficiency. Additionally,
539   CGL demonstrates robustness against adversarial attacks and generalizability across various GNN
      architectures, maintaining high node classification performance.
