009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Agile locomotion of legged robots, characterized by high momentum and frequent
012            contact changes, is a challenging task that demands precise motor control. There-
013            fore, the training process for such skills often relies on additional techniques, such
014            as reward engineering, expert demonstrations, and curriculum learning. However,
015            these requirements hinder the generalizability of methods because we may lack
016            sufficient prior knowledge or demonstration datasets for some tasks. In this work,
017
               we consider the problem of automated learning agile motions using its intrinsic
               motivation, which can greatly reduce the effort of a human engineer. Inspired by
018
               unsupervised skill discovery, our learning framework encourages the agent to ex-
019
               plore various skills to maximize the given task reward. Finally, we train a param-
020            eter to balance the two distinct rewards through a bi-level optimization process.
021            We demonstrate that our method can train quadrupeds to perform highly agile mo-
022            tions, ranging from crawling, jumping, and leaping to complex maneuvers such as
023            jumping off a perpendicular wall.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Agile motor skills are challenging for both humans and robots to learn because they require com-
044   plex planning of full-body movements and precise motor control. For example, mastering advanced
045   gymnastics skills involves carefully coordinating the teaching and practice phases. Some unintu-
046   itive motor skills, such as the Fosbury flop in high jump or the Eurostep in basketball, took athletes
047   decades to discover. Similarly, developing controllers for agile skills remains one of the most diffi-
048   cult tasks in robotics, which makes an algorithm easily get stuck in local minima.
049   In recent years, legged robot locomotion, when combined with deep reinforcement learning (RL),
050   has reached a high level of agility (Tan et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2020b; Hwangbo et al., 2019; Xie
051   et al., 2021; Song et al., 2020; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Smith et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024). However,
052   those algorithms often require additional techniques to learn challenging skills, such as reward en-
053   gineering based on domain expertise (Zhuang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b),
      demonstration datasets (Bogdanovic et al., 2022; Kilinc & Montana, 2022; Li et al., 2023a; He

067   to develop an automated learning algorithm that reduces manual engineering effort, which can learn
068   highly agile skills such as the wall-jump shown in Figure 1. However, developing an automated
069   learning framework for agile locomotion is not straightforward because the high momentum and
070   contact changes make an optimization ill-conditioned with multiple local minima.
071
      In this work, we aim to design a learning algorithm to achieve the given difficult task by exploring a
072
      diverse set of possible approaches. Consider a locomotion task with two types of obstacles, as shown
073   in Figure 2: the robot must jump over the obstacle in the left figure and crawl under the box in the
074   right figure. We want the robot to overcome both tasks based on a simple task description, such as
075   “moving forward.” However, the robot would struggle to solve these tasks because this description
076   does not provide enough incentive to explore different base heights. Therefore, our algorithm must
077   intrinsically motivate the robot to examine various gaits, especially when learning with the given
078   task reward becomes saturated.
079   In detail, our approach combines two objectives: solving the given task and finding diverse solutions.
080   Solving the task is represented by maximizing the task reward. The task reward should be kept
081   simple, such as following forward velocity commands to move toward task completion. On the
082   other hand, exploring diverse behaviors is achieved by maximizing a diversity reward, which is
083   derived from skill discovery methods. This encourages the agent to try various approaches to find
084   the desired height, orientation, velocity, or angular velocity needed to solve the task. However,
085   balancing two distinct objectives is not straightfoward and one may overpower the other. If the task
086   reward dominates, agents may not sufficiently explore diverse behaviors. Conversely, if the diversity
087
      reward dominates, agents may spend too much time exploring, failing to solve the task. This is
      analogous to the exploration-exploitation trade-off in RL (Sutton, 2018). To address this problem,
088
      we introduce a learnable parameter λ to balance the two objectives. We train λ to automatically
089
      adjust the weight of the diversity reward to maximize the task reward. Details of training λ will
090   be covered in Section 3.2. This approach enables the agent to effectively balance exploration and
091   exploitation.
092
093
      In summary, our approach aims to adopt skill discovery methods to enhance the task-specific re-
      ward by incorporating human priors. The primary contributions of this work are as follows: (1)
094
      We propose a novel framework that combines RL and unsupervised skill discovery algorithms to
095
      automatically learn agile locomotion skills. (2) We provide a thorough derivation of bi-level opti-
096   mization framework for training the balancing parameter λ. We also demonstrate that our approach
097   of adapting λ robustly finds the optimal value for a given task. (3) We evaluate our method on three
098   challenging locomotion tasks: jumping, leaping, and crawling. In these environments, we compare
099   our approach against exploration-based methods for utilizing human priors, showing that our method
100   outperforms the baselines. (4) We demonstrate that our method can train unprecedented levels of
101   agile behavior, such as accomplishing a wall-jump.

106   Unsupervised Skill Discovery. To establish an association between the skill z and the resulting
107   policy π(a|s, z), DIAYN (Rudin et al., 2022a) proposes maximizing the mutual information between
      skills and states, I(z; s). However, a limitation of DIAYN is that its objective can be fully optimized


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      with only minor differences between states, as long as the discriminator can distinguish between the
109   skills, even if these differences are minimal.
110
111   To address this issue, LSD (Park et al., 2022) suggests an alternative objective that provides more
112
      incentive to increase state differences. However, LSD measures state differences using Euclidean
      distance, which leads to a focus on “easy change” within existing state dimensions. For example, in
113
      manipulation tasks, changing the robot arm’s end-effector position is considered an easy-to-change
114
      state, whereas altering the target object’s position is more challenging. To tackle this challenge,
115   CSD (Park et al., 2023a) introduced a different distance metric called “controllability-aware dis-
116   tance”. This metric assigns higher values to state transitions that are less likely to occur, thereby
117   encouraging the learning process to focus more on state changes that are rare.
118
      It is worth noting that DIAYN, LSD, and CSD primarily address low-dimensional state spaces. ME-
119
      TRA (Park et al., 2023b), on the other hand, tackles the skill discovery problem in high-dimensional
120
      image inputs. METRA also incorporates the Wasserstein Dependency Measure, IW DM (Ozair et al.,
121   2019), between skill z and states, encouraging the agent to visit maximally different states for dif-
122   ferent skills, based on the given distance metric. We utilized METRA as our base skill discovery
123   algorithm.
124
125   Learning Agile Locomotion. Recently, learning-based methods have demonstrated highly agile
126   locomotion capabilities such as high-speed running (Margolis et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2021), jumping
127   (Li et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023a), and climbing (Rudin et al., 2022a; Lee et al., 2020a). Our
128   work aims to cover not only jumping, running, and leaping, but also wall-jumping, which involves a
129   parkour-style motion combining flipping and jumping using walls.
130   The work most related to ours is that of Zhuang et al. (2023), which used a manually designed
131   reward that penalizes the overlap between the robot and imaginary obstacles. They trained agents to
132   minimize these overlaps, resulting in the learning of agile behaviors. In contrast, we aim to train a
133   similar set of tasks without the need for extensive reward designs. Instead, we allow an unsupervised
134   reinforcement learning (RL) method to discover the skills required to solve these tasks.

388   Selecting the appropriate value for λ is crucial, as the scale of both the task reward and diversity

      algorithm effectively adjusts λ during training. We compare our adaptive approach to fixed values of

      tasks from the previous section, with each method trained using three different random seeds. We

      measured performance based on the number of obstacles passed.

      forms all fixed-value experiments. Training with fixed λ values of 0.01, 0.1, and 10 failed to pass

402   sults indicate that some tasks require a gradual increase in λ, while others benefit from maintaining
403   a steady value in the range of [0.2, 0.4].
404
      It is also important to note that our method does not correspond to a single fixed λ value through-
405
      out training. In other words, there may not exist a single value of λ that could yield an identical
406   training curve. Our approach adjusts λ dynamically, resulting in different values at different stages
407   of training, which allows the agent to achieve an appropriate balance of diversity and task reward
408   throughout the learning process.

      4.3   C ONVERGENCE OF DIFFERENT SKILLS INTO A NARROW SOLUTION SPACE
411
412
      One potential challenge of incorporating a skill discovery module into the learning process is the
413
      difficulty of selecting the exact skill that solves the task after training, especially if only a small
414   portion of the skill space is effective. However, we observed that as training progresses, a growing
415   number of skill vectors z ∼ N (0, I) become capable of solving the task. To demonstrate this,
416   we selected model checkpoints at various stages of training and measured the success rate using
417   100 randomly sampled skills. This experiment was repeated ten times to determine the standard
418   deviation.
419
      The results are presented in Table 1. For the leap task, initially, only 30% of the skills were success-
420   ful, but this number eventually approached nearly 100%. Similarly, for the climb and crawl tasks,
421   the proportion of successful skills increased steadily. This suggests that once a viable solution is
422   discovered, different skill vectors converge into similar behaviors with the solution, especially when
423   the solution space is narrow for the given task. This contrasts with a typical skill discovery scenario
424   where only a small subset of skills solves the task. Instead, in our case, the proportion of successful
425   skills increased significantly over time.
426   This indicates that the resulting behavior of π(a|s, z) can converge to a similar behavior despite us-
427   ing different skill vectors z. Intuitively, the training process involves an initial phase of exploration,
428   followed by convergence to a solution. We observe that this phenomenon of later convergence is
429   facilitated by task rewards: when a skill finds a successful solution, the corresponding trajectory
430   receives higher rewards, which results in the increased probability of the actions taken. Because all
431   skills share the same policy network, this learning propagates to other skill-conditioned behaviors,
      leading to what we term a “positive collapse” of skills. Initially diverse behaviors converge to a

470   Lastly, we pushed our method to its limits. We introduced a new task named wall-jump, which re-
471   quires the robot to perform a sequence of highly agile motions, including running, jumping, flipping,
472   and landing in a specific order. To make this feasible, we devised a guideline-based reward that is
473   widely adopted in robotics(Tang et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2023). The reward encourages the agent to
474   follow the guideline specified by a user. We used this reward as rtask . More details about the reward
475   design can be found in Appendix C. The exact guideline used is shown in Figure 7(a). Note that the
476
      guideline only provides the target trajectory for the root position while not offering any information
      about orientation.
477
478   However, providing the guideline alone was not sufficient for the agent to successfully perform the
479   wall-jump. Figure 7(b) shows the resulting behavior of the agent trained solely with rtask . The robot
480   was able to follow the guideline up until it reached the perpendicular wall, but then crashed its back
481   against the wall. The cumulative reward for this episode was about 5.0, as shown by the blue curve
482
      in Figure 7(e). We observe that the robot needs to acquire a specific orientation to kick off the wall
      and land safely.
483
484   Therefore, we provided the robot’s base’s roll, pitch, and yaw as input to the skill discovery
485   algorithm, allowing our method to explore and learn diverse orientations of the robot when needed.
      Figures 7(c) and (d) show the resulting behavior. Our method was able to acquire the specific

      orientation needed to kick off the wall. As a result, our approach achieved a much higher task
487   return, with a value of 9.5 as indicated by the green curve in Figure 7(e).

      increased from 0 to 1 during the interval from 0.5k to 3k steps. In this experiment, λ was capped

      5   C ONCLUSION
498
499
      In this work, we presented a novel framework that integrates unsupervised skill discovery with task-
      specific reinforcement learning to enable legged robots to learn highly agile locomotion behaviors
500
      with minimal manual intervention. By balancing exploration and task rewards through a bi-level
501
      optimization process, our method allows robots to discover diverse strategies and refine them to
502   achieve complex tasks such as crawling, jumping, leaping, and performing agile maneuvers like
503   wall-jumping.
504
505
      We demonstrated that the incorporation of skill discovery methods not only facilitates the explo-
      ration of diverse behaviors but also enhances sample efficiency compared to traditional exploration-
506
      based techniques. We also showed that our method outperforms pure exploration-based baselines in
507
      various tasks, and the learned skills consistently converge to an optimal solution, ensuring the ro-
508   bustness and reproducibility of the learned behaviors. Furthermore, we pushed the boundary of agile
509   locomotion learning with the successful implementation of the challenging wall-jump task, show-
510   casing the potential of our method to handle even the most demanding dynamic behaviors. Future
511   work could extend this approach to more diverse environments, exploring its potential in real-world
512   robotic applications.

      C    D ETAILS OF THE GUIDELINE FOLLOWING REWARD
727
      For the wall-jump task, we defined a special task reward, rtask , based on a guideline provided by a
728
      human. The guideline consists of a sequence of n points:
729
730                                             gi=0,1,...,n−1 ∈ R3
731
      Let the robot’s base position in global 3D space be denoted as x ∈ R3 . At each time step, the robot
732
      has a target point gi , starting with g0 . When the robot reaches the current target, it moves on to the
733
      next target, gi+1 . A target is considered reached when the distance between x and gi falls below a
734   threshold h ∈ R, i.e., ||x − gi ||2 < h.
735
736   Then, the reward can be defined as follows:
737                                              rt = e−||x−gi ||2
738
739   This term has the desirable property of being bounded between 0 and 1. It approaches 0 when the
740
      robot is infinitely far from the current target and becomes 1 when the robot exactly reaches the target.
      This property contributes to stability during the learning process. We optimized this reward using
741
      reinforcement learning (RL) to train the agent to follow the given guideline.
