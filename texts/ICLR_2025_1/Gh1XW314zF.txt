011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Multimodal healthcare research is crucial for improving clinical decision-making
014            by integrating diverse data types, such as clinical notes, lab results, and imag-
015            ing. Large Language Models (LLMs) are widely recognized for their exceptional
016
               text-based reasoning capabilities, making them effective in processing complex
               clinical narratives. However, they struggle to incorporate multimodal data, lim-
017
               iting their broader applicability in healthcare analysis. In this work, we pro-
018
               pose MG-LLM (Multimodal Graph-LLM), a novel framework that leverages the
019            strengths of LLMs while enhancing them with multimodal alignment and data
020            integration through Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). GNNs propagate informa-
021            tion across similar patients, model temporal relationships between visits, and align
022            information from different modalities, creating enriched multimodal context vec-
023            tors. These context vectors are then injected into the intermediate layers of the
024            LLM, allowing it to harness both textual reasoning and multimodal data for more
025            accurate predictions. We evaluate MG-LLM on the MIMIC-IV and MIMIC-CXR
026            datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in clinical prediction tasks com-
027
               pared to baseline models. Our results showcase the potential of combining the text
               reasoning power of LLMs with GNN-driven multimodal alignment for robust,
028
               comprehensive healthcare analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Electronic Health Records (EHR) data provide a multimodal representation of a patient’s health,
034
      encompassing medical images, unstructured data such as clinical notes, codified data such as ICD-
035
      9/10/11 codes, and structured data such as labs or vitals as shown in Figure 1 (Hoerbst and Ammen-
036   werth, 2010). The integration of these diverse data types offers great promises in enhancing clinical
037   predictions in terms of more accurate and holistic patient assessments (Huang et al., 2020; Kim,
038   2022; Zhang et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Moshawrab et al., 2023). However,
039   the complexity and heterogeneity of EHR data present unique analytical challenges, particularly in
040   effective representation and integration of patient information across heterogeneous data modalities.
041
      Large language models (LLMs) have the potential to make a transformative impact on healthcare
042   due to their exceptional capabilities in processing and reasoning over text data. These models excel
043   in tasks such as summarizing clinical notes, extracting medical conditions, and generating clinical
044   recommendations(Nazi and Peng, 2024a). Despite these advancements, LLMs face limitations when
045   applied to complex, heterogeneous multimodal healthcare data. Two major challenges arise when
046   considering the use of LLMs for clinical predictions.
047
      Limitations of LLMs with Multimodal EHR Data. LLMs
048   have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in processing tex-
049   tual data, but they face significant challenges when dealing
050   with multimodal EHR data (Nazi and Peng, 2024a; Kim,
051   2022; Zhou et al., 2024). These challenges arise from the
052   inherent design of LLMs, which are primarily optimized for
053   handling sequential text data. This focus on text restricts
      their ability to seamlessly incorporate non-textual data such     Figure 1: Multimodality in EHR.

054
      as medical images, lab results, and structured codes like ICD codes, resulting in incomplete or frag-
055   mented patient representations and underutilization of the rich, multimodal information available
056   in EHRs (Nazi and Peng, 2024b; Yildirim et al., 2024). Although a few multimodal LLMs have
057   been introduced (He et al., 2024; Song et al., 2023), they often require additional frameworks or
058   specialized architectures to align and contextualize information from other modalities, such as med-
059   ical images or labs and vitals. This approach does not fully leverage the integrated reasoning and
060   language capabilities inherent to LLMs (Mumtaz et al., 2024) and has not yet been actively explored
061   in EHR data (Huang et al., 2023).
062   Another limitation is the LLMs’ inability to perform context-dependent interpretation of medical
063   data. For instance, they may struggle to determine the clinical significance of a slight elevation in
064   a lab value for different patients with varying health contexts. In real-world diagnostic tasks, these
065   limitations lead to a performance gap between LLMs and human physicians. LLMs often underper-
066   form in synthesizing diagnostic information, following treatment guidelines, and integrating diverse
067   data sources that are crucial for accurate clinical predictions (Hager et al., 2024). Despite these
068   challenges, the potential of LLMs in healthcare remains significant, underscoring the need for con-
069
      tinued development to enhance their ability to process diverse data types and maintain the contextual
      understanding necessary for effective clinical decision-making.
070
071   Limitations of LLMs in Leveraging Patient Similarity. Learning from the experiences of similar
072   patients is a critical aspect of clinical decision-making, as healthcare professionals often draw upon
073   patterns observed in patients with comparable conditions to inform diagnoses and treatment plans
074   (Zhang et al., 2022a). For example, when assessing a patient’s symptoms, physicians may consider
075
      how patients with similar medical histories responded to certain treatments or exhibited particular
      disease progressions.
076
077   In contrast, LLMs process each patient case as an isolated input during inference. They rely on
078   the generalized knowledge encoded in their training data to generate responses, without explicitly
079   referencing individual patient cases or directly accessing a repository of similar patients’ informa-
080   tion. This is because LLMs do not maintain a dynamic, context-aware memory of specific cases but
081
      instead generate responses based on probabilistic patterns learned from a broad dataset (Bommasani
      et al., 2022). While this approach allows LLMs to generalize across various scenarios and generate
082
      plausible responses, it may miss critical, nuanced similarities that are often essential in healthcare
083
      settings. Moreover, healthcare decision-making often involves evaluating complex, multifaceted
084   patterns across time, considering not only the current symptoms but also how these symptoms have
085   evolved in relation to past medical events (Ageno et al., 2023). The temporal nature of clinical data,
086   along with the need to consider information from similar patients, requires a more sophisticated
087   approach than the static, generalized knowledge offered by current LLMs.
088
      To address these limitations, we propose MG-LLM ((Multimodal Graph-LLM), which leverages the
089
      language-processing and reasoning capabilities of LLMs while seamlessly integrating multimodal
090   patient data. Our approach aims to enhance patient representation, improve multimodal data inte-
091   gration, and enable better contextual reasoning. The key contributions of our work are as follows.
092
093          ⋆ Dynamic Patient Similarity Integration: A graph-based approach that explicitly models
094            patient similarities and temporal relationships, enabling LLMs to make more informed,
095            context-aware decisions.
096
             ⋆ Multimodal Data Integration: Align multimodal data into a unified representation that
097            can be seamlessly injested and utilized by LLMs.
098
099          ⋆ Improved Contextual Reasoning: Preserve the LLM’s strong text-based reasoning abili-
100
               ties while augmenting it with multimodal information.

103
104   2.1   M ULTIMODAL A PPROACH
105
106   Existing multimodal LLM approaches typically involve the use of modality-specific encoders that
107   transform different data types, such as images, time-series data, and structured clinical data, into
      a shared representation space compatible with LLMs. Some methods use encoders to transform


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      high-dimensional health data, such as spirograms and lab values, into the LLM’s token embedding
109   space, allowing the model to process these inputs along with text data (Belyaeva et al., 2023). Sim-
110   ilarly, other approaches integrate clinical notes with structured data by encoding lab results into
111   text-like embeddings to facilitate integration with LLMs (Ding et al., 2024). However, this approach
112   may limit the LLM’s ability to fully leverage its strengths in text-based reasoning. Other meth-
113   ods directly convert clinical data into textual descriptions (e.g., representing lab values as text), but
114   this limits their ability to handle non-textual modalities like medical images effectively (Rezk et al.,
115   2024). Despite their innovative designs, these approaches face common issues. They often treat each
116   modality separately before concatenating the resulting embeddings, which can lead to fragmented
117
      representations and reduce the LLM’s ability to leverage its full language-based reasoning capabil-
      ities. These challenges highlight the need for more cohesive frameworks that can more effectively
118
      integrate and reason over diverse healthcare modalities.
119
120   Various non-LLM multimodal frameworks have also been developed to tackle the challenges of in-
121   tegrating diverse medical data types. Graph-based approaches have been particularly effective in
122   modeling the complex relationships within EHR data, enabling the flexible integration of different
123
      data modalities (Gao et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022a). Some methods focus on addressing the com-
      mon issue of missing data in medical records by enhancing the robustness of multimodal analysis
124
      through sophisticated data imputation techniques (Zhang et al., 2022a). Temporal aspects of pa-
125
      tient data have also been considered, with several models designed to handle sequential multimodal
126   information, thereby providing a more comprehensive view of patient health over time (Wu et al.,
127   2024). Additionally, other approaches have concentrated on integrating specific modalities prevalent
128   in healthcare, such as combining deep learning models for medical image analysis with architectures
129   designed to handle structured data, thereby offering a holistic perspective on patient health (Zhang
130   et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). These advancements reflect the growing emphasis on comprehensive
131   data integration to support more informed and accurate clinical decision-making. While these mod-
132   els have made significant strides in handling different types of healthcare data, they often lack the
133   deep language understanding and contextual reasoning capabilities that LLMs possess.
134
135   2.2   G RAPHS AND LLM S
136
137   Recent work has made notable progress in combining LLMs with graph-based methods to leverage
138
      the structured knowledge encoded in graphs alongside the language understanding capabilities of
      LLMs. Techniques like Graph Neural Prompting and the LLaGA framework integrate LLMs with
139
      graph neural networks (GNNs) to encode and align graph data, significantly enhancing the LLMs’
140
      performance on tasks such as question answering and knowledge retrieval (Tian et al., 2023; Chen
141   et al., 2024). However, these approaches mainly address single-modality graphs or text data, with
142   limited exploration of multimodal graphs that incorporate diverse data types like images, clinical
143   notes, and structured clinical data. While models like LLM4GraphGen (Yao et al., 2024) and Talk
144   Like a Graph(Fatemi et al., 2023) have advanced graph generation and encoding for LLMs, they
145   largely focus on single-modality settings, highlighting a need for research on integrating LLMs with
146   multimodal graphs to fully utilize the rich, heterogeneous data available in fields such as healthcare.

      3     O UR A PPROACH : MG-LLM
149
150
      In this section, we introduce MG-LLM, a framework specifically designed to leverage multimodal
151
      data with LLMs. The process begins by constructing graphs for each modality, such as codified
152   data, lab results, and imaging data. We then apply GNNs to propagate information across these
153   graphs. Next, we align the updated embeddings across modalities to generate a comprehensive
154   context vector for each patient. This context vector is injected into the intermediate layers of the
155   LLM, which is simultaneously prompted with clinical notes to predict clinical outcome of interest,
156   such as 1-year mortality risk. An illustration of MG-LLM is provided in Figure 2.
157
158   3.1   I NFORMATION P ROPAGATION
159
160   We generate embeddings for each modality using pre-trained encoders. For codified data and clin-
161   ical text, embeddings are obtained using BioClinicalBERT, a model specifically trained on clinical
      data to capture the nuances of medical language (Alsentzer et al., 2019). For the imaging data,

189   corresponding embeddings (He et al., 2015). Other pre-trained encoders can also be used.
190   Once the embeddings are obtained for each data point, we construct a separate graph for each modal-
191   ity. Each graph represents a distinct data modality, with nodes corresponding to data points (i.e. pa-
192   tients)and edges representing relationships between these nodes. We introduce two types of edges:
193   temporal edges and similarity edges. Temporal edges connect nodes that correspond to the same
194   patient across different time points, thereby capturing the longitudinal nature of patient care. This
195   allows the model to track changes in a patient’s health over time, ensuring that any temporal trends
196
      or patterns are incorporated into the model’s predictions.
197   In addition to temporal edges, similarity edges are established by calculating the cosine similarity
198   between the embeddings of different data points. These similarity edges link nodes based on shared
199   characteristics, even if the nodes represent different patients. This enables the graph structure to
200   propagate information not only within the same patient (across visits) but also across different pa-
201   tients who exhibit similar patterns. By doing so, the model can learn from the experiences of similar
202
      patients, improving its predictive power.
203   The combination of temporal and similarity edges in the graph structure enables efficient informa-
204   tion propagation through a GNN. The GNN allows information to flow through these connections,
205   effectively transferring information both within a single patient over time and across different pa-
206   tients. As a result, the model can capture both the temporal dynamics of a patient’s health and
207   broader population-level patterns, providing a more comprehensive representation of the data and
208
      improving the accuracy of downstream predictions, such as mortality risk or disease progression.

210   3.2   M ULTIMODAL A LIGNMENT
211
212   To align the embeddings across different modalities, we draw inspiration from the ImageBind frame-
213   work (Girdhar et al., 2023). ImageBind is a state-of-the-art framework that creates a shared embed-
214   ding space for multiple modalities—such as images, audio, and text—by using images as the central
215   modality for alignment. This approach facilitates cross-modal retrieval and understanding by learn-
      ing a shared representation that captures relationships between these diverse data types. ImageBind’s

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      strength lies in its ability to bridge these modalities into a common space, enabling effective cross-
217   modal interactions.
218
219   We adapt this idea to the healthcare domain by using clinical text as the primary modality for align-
220
      ment instead of images. The choice of “text modality” as central modality is motivated by its ability
      to provide a comprehensive narrative of a patient’s conditions, capturing nuances that are often not
221
      fully reflected in “non-text modalities” such as codified data (e.g., diagnosis codes) and medical
222
      images. Furthermore, utilizing clinical text allows us to leverage the powerful language processing
223   capabilities of LLMs, which excel in understanding and generating insights from complex text data.
224
225   To achieve effective multimodal alignment, we first obtain updated embeddings from the modality-
226
      specific graphs. Through contrastive learning, we map the non-text modality embeddings (codified
      data, labs and vitals, medical images) and the text modality embeddings into a shared projection
227
      space. This alignment process ensures that embeddings from different modalities are represented in
228
      a common latent space, enabling more effective cross-modal interactions and information sharing.
229   The shared projection space is optimized using an InfoNCE loss (Parulekar et al., 2023). Given text
230   data Ti and another modality data Mi , we compute the embeddings ti and mi and the loss as:

      3.3   I NCORPORATING M ULTIMODAL C ONTEXT IN LLM S
246   Once we generate a multimodal context vector that encapsulates information from various data
247   sources, the next step is to integrate this context into the LLM in a way that enriches its predic-
248   tions without altering its fundamental architecture. This is achieved by injecting the multimodal
249   context vector into the LLM’s residual streams, which would allow the model to incorporate exter-
250   nal information at various layers while preserving its inherent structure and reasoning capabilities
251   (Li et al., 2024). The rationale behind this approach is to maintain the LLM’s core strengths in
252   natural language processing while enhancing its ability to process non-textual data, such as med-
253   ical images or lab results, which are crucial for clinical decision-making. By incorporating this
254
      multimodal information in the form of a context vector, we enable the LLM to use structured and
      unstructured medical data to make more informed and contextually rich predictions, particularly in
255
      scenarios such as predicting a patient’s mortality risk.
256
257   The integration of the context vector into the LLM occurs at multiple points within its internal
258   architecture. Let rlt represent the residual stream at layer l and token position t. Normally, the
259   LLM adds the output activations from the Multi-Head Attention (MHA) and Multi-Layer Perceptron
260
      (MLP) modules to the residual streams at each layer. However, in our approach, we enhance this
      mechanism by introducing a weighted linear combination of the patient’s context vector with the
261
      MHA and MLP activations. This step is expressed as:
262
263                        rtl = rtl−1 + λmha · v + βmha · alt + λmlp · v + βmlp · mlt                   (2)
264   where λmha and λmlp are scaling factors for the context vector integration into the MHA and MLP
265   layers. βmha and βmlp are scaling factors for the original MHA and MLP outputs. We apply this
266   process to all residual streams using the forward hook. By injecting the context vector into the resid-
267   ual streams in this manner, the LLM can leverage external context effectively and efficiently while
268   preserving its inherent architecture and text-based reasoning capabilities. This approach allows us to
269   integrate non-text modalities into the model while only providing text as input to the LLM, ensuring
      that we retain the LLM’s core strength in text-based reasoning.

      For clinical prediction, our approach is as follows using mortality prediction as an example. When
271   prompting the LLM, we task it with estimating the patient’s probability of experiencing mortality
272   within a year. The model is provided with the relevant clinical text as input, while the multimodal
273   context vector is injected into its layers to support the decision-making process. This framework
274   ensures that the LLM has access to a wealth of external context beyond the text alone, enabling
275   it to provide more accurate and nuanced predictions. By integrating text and non-text modalities
276   seamlessly within the LLM, we retain the model’s strengths in natural language processing and text
277   reasoning while enhancing its capability to incorporate complex, multimodal medical data and hence
278   more comprehensive patient medical history.

285   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
286
287   MIMIC-IV Data. MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV) is a large, publicly
288   available dataset containing de-identified health-related data associated with patients who stayed in
289   critical care units at the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (Johnson et al., 2023). This dataset
290   includes structured data such as demographics, vital signs, laboratory measurements, medications,
291   and procedures and also unstructured data in the form of clinical notes. MIMIC-CXR is an open-
292
      access database of de-identified chest X-ray images paired with radiology reports that can be linked
      to the MIMIC-IV dataset (Johnson et al., 2019). For the purposes of this experiment, we extract
293
      labs and vital values, codes for prescriptions and diagnoses, clinical text, and imaging data. Our
294
      prediction task is a binary classification of one-year-mortality for the patient. For patient A with
295   visits v1 , v2 , ..., vn , we will use information from all of the visits to predict risk of mortality within
296   one year timeframe after vn . More details about dataset processing is in Appendix.
297
298
      Baseline Models. For existing state-of-the-art models, we include multimodal LLM baselines and
      other state-of-the-art frameworks designed for clinical prediction tasks. Multimodal LLM frame-
299
      works, such as HeLM, LLMM, and GPT-4-based methods, explore various approaches to integrat-
300
      ing multimodal data with LLMs (Belyaeva et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024; Rezk et al., 2024). HeLM
301   encodes non-text modalities into the same token embedding space using modality-specific encoders
302   and then inputs them to the LLM (Belyaeva et al., 2023). LLMM combines laboratory test data with
303   text embeddings through an attention mechanism (Ding et al., 2024). Rezk et al. transform clinical
304   data into text format for input into the LLM (Rezk et al., 2024). For non-LLM based frameworks, the
305   HAIM framework leverages the integration of multiple data modalities, such as clinical notes, struc-
306   tured data, and medical images, to improve clinical predictions through a unified model architecture
307   (Soenksen et al., 2022). M3Care focuses on learning with incomplete modalities in multimodal
308   healthcare data by employing neural networks and patient similarity measures (Zhang et al., 2022a).
309
      MUSE incorporates flexible bipartite graphs and contrastive learning loss to generate multimodal
      patient representations (Wu et al., 2024). mmFormer introduces a transformer-based approach to
310
      multimodal fusion, using attention mechanisms to align and integrate information from multiple
311
      modalities, thereby capturing complex relationships in the data (Zhang et al., 2022b). Collectively,
312   these frameworks provide comprehensive baselines for evaluating the performance of our proposed
313   method, given their strong track records in handling diverse and complex multimodal healthcare
314   data.
315
      Implementation Details. The dataset was split into 70% training and 30% testing. For MG-LLM ,
316
      we trained the entire framework end to end. During training, we used a combination of two losses: a
317
      weighted binary cross-entropy loss and the alignment loss, which was scaled by 0.01 to appropriately
318   adjust its contribution within the overall task. To handle any non-valid outputs generated by the
319   LLM, we imposed penalties on outputs that fall outside the expected prediction range, ensuring the
320   model remains within valid boundaries. MG-LLM uses Llama 3-8B as its LLM backbone. For the
321   baseline experiments, we used the best hyperparameter setting provided in the original papers. If the
322   hyperparameters were not available, we tuned the learning rate in 1e-3, 1e-4, 1e-5 and batch size in
323   8, 16, 64. All experiments were conducted using NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Each experiment was run
      three times with different seeds to ensure reproducibility, and we averaged the results.

367
      tion provided by each modality. This allows it to make better overall predictions, particularly when
368
      dealing with complex medical datasets, where multimodal input is crucial for accurate classification.

      4.5   E XPLAINABILITY
473
474   A key advantage of LLMs over other multimodal frameworks is their ability to not only generate
475   predictions but also provide natural language explanations for the decisions that they make. This
476   capability is particularly appealing and valuable in clinical settings, where transparency and under-
477
      standing the reasoning behind predictions are essential for building trust in health professionals, pa-
      tients and other stakeholders. As shown in Figure 4, MG-LLM generates detailed, context-specific
478
      explanations for both positive and negative mortality predictions. For instance, when predicting
479
      high mortality risk, the model provides a comprehensive explanation citing the patient’s history of
480   coronary artery disease, multiple myocardial infarctions, and other clinical details that justify the
481   prediction. In contrast, for a negative mortality prediction, the model explains how the patient’s
482   stable condition, well-managed comorbidities, and absence of immediate life-threatening complica-
483   tions contribute to the outcome. This level of detail in the explanations enhances interpretability and
484   allows clinicians to better understand and verify the model’s reasoning. In comparison, non-LLM
485   baseline models lack this ability to provide transparent, context-specific reasoning, underscoring the
      distinct advantage of using LLMs in high-stake decision making in areas such as healthcare.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      Multimodal healthcare data holds a wealth of valuable information that, which, when integrated
489
      effectively, can enhance clinical decision-making. However, existing approaches that do not uti-
490   lize LLMs fail to capitalize on their powerful reasoning capabilities, while current LLM-powered
491   approaches often overlook key modalities like medical images. Our work introduces MG-LLM , a
492   flexible framework that bridges these gaps by propagating information across patients and injecting
493   multimodal context into LLMs. By incorporating clinical notes, lab results, codified data, and im-
494   ages into LLMs, MG-LLM retains the model’s strengths in text-based reasoning while enhancing
495   it by utilizaing other data modalities to create a more comprehensive context. The demonstrated
496   improvements over other methods in our experiements show MG-LLM ’s effectiveness in leverag-
497   ing multimodal data. Future research may include more comprehensive assessment of explanations
498
      generated by MG-LLM and in additional EHRs datasets such as those from primary care settings.
