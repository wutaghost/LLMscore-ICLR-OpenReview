009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The significant advancements of Large Language Models (LLMs) in generative
012            tasks have led to a growing body of work exploring LLM-based embedding mod-
013            els. While these models, employing different pooling and attention strategies,
014            have achieved state-of-the-art performance on public embedding benchmarks,
015            questions still arise about what constitutes an effective design for LLM-based em-
016            bedding models. However, these models are often trained on different datasets,
017            using different LLM base models or training settings. Moreover, evaluations on
018
               public embedding benchmarks often fail to report statistical significance, mak-
               ing it difficult to determine which designs truly contribute to final performance.
019
               This complicates the process for practitioners seeking optimal training recipes for
020
               LLM-based embedding models. In this study, we conduct a large-scale experiment
021            by training a series of LLM-based embedding models using the same training data
022            and base model but differing in their pooling and attention strategies. The results
023            show that there is no one-size-fits-all solution: while bidirectional attention and
024            an additional trainable pooling layer outperform in text similarity and informa-
025            tion retrieval tasks, they do not significantly surpass simpler designs like EOS-last
026            token pooling and default causal attention in clustering and classification tasks.
027            Furthermore, we propose a new pooling strategy, Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling,
028            which transforms the outputs of all hidden layers, rather than just the last layer,
029
               using a cross-attention network. This method proves to be statistically superior in
               text similarity and retrieval tasks compared to existing pooling methods. Overall,
030
               this paper sheds light on effective training strategies for LLM-based embedding
031
               models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   A text embedding is a high-dimensional representation that captures the semantic information of text
037   and is crucial for many tasks, such as information retrieval and semantic textual similarity. For ex-
038   ample, text embedding models, which convert input text into embeddings, are essential components
039   in semantic search and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) retrieval-augmented generation sys-
040   tems (RAGs) (Gao et al., 2023; Hu and Lu, 2024). Companies such as OpenAI and Cohere provide
041   embeddings as services via APIs.
042   Previous studies primarily employ encoder-only models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and
043   Sentence-BERT (Reimers, 2019), as embedding models. Recently, with the significant advance-
044   ments in LLMs, the community has started to explore using LLMs as base models, fine-tuning them
045   accordingly to serve as embedding models (Wang et al., 2023; Springer et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024).
046   On embedding model benchmarks such as Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) (Muen-
047   nighoff et al., 2022), LLM-based embedding models show promising performance and dominate the
048   leaderboard compared to previous encoder-only models.
049   Pooling and attention are two main designs involved in converting an LLM into an embedding
050   model. Pooling strategies are used to obtain a fixed-size dense vector representing the input se-
051   quence. For example, E5-mistral-7b-instruct (Wang et al., 2023) and SRF-Embedding-Mistral
052   (Meng et al., 2024) use EOS-last token pooling as their pooling strategy. NV-Embed (Lee et al.,
053   2024) uses a trainable pooling layer to obtain the final embeddings. The attention strategy con-
      strains the direction in which tokens can attend to others. By default, an LLM is pre-trained with

057   tional attention so that every token in the sequence can attend to every other token (BehnamGhader
058   et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024).

071   raises the question: what makes an embedding model perform better? Is it the higher quality of
072   the fine-tuning dataset, the greater capability of the base LLM, or the use of different pooling and
073   attention strategies that makes the embedding model more effective? Unfortunately, most exist-
074   ing LLM-based embedding models are trained using different datasets with different base models,
075
      making it difficult to draw conclusions regarding the contribution of each design choice.
076   In this paper, we conduct large-scale experiments to empirically evaluate pooling and attention
077   strategies for LLM-based embedding models. To ensure a fair comparison between different strate-
078   gies, we fine-tune the same base LLM models (Mistral-7B and Qwen2-0.5B) using different com-
079   binations of pooling and attention strategies commonly employed in existing models. We conduct
080   statistical testing to rigorously compare the performance of these models. Interestingly, we find that
081
      there is no one-size-fits-all solution. For example, LLMs with bidirectional attention and an addi-
      tional trainable pooling layer demonstrate superior performance in semantic textual similarity (STS)
082
      and information retrieval tasks but underperform in clustering and classification tasks.
083
084   In addition to empirically testing existing pooling strategies, we also propose a new pooling strategy,
085   Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling, which leverages LLM hidden states across multiple internal layers
086   and transforms them using a trainable network. This strategy is motivated by the observation that
087
      different internal layers in LLM may encode orthogonal information that is not captured in the final
      layer but could be relevant for certain downstream tasks. Empirical experiments show that this
088
      new pooling strategy, which pools information from multiple layers, outperforms existing methods
089
      that use only the last layer. Overall, we hope that these large-scale training experiments and the
090   proposed pooling strategy can collectively enhance the community’s efforts to improve LLM-based
091   embedding model performance. We will release the implementation of the proposed pooling method
092   and the series of fine-tuned embedding models for replication.

100
101   A pooling strategy focuses on obtaining a fixed-size embedding from the LLM hidden states for an
102   input sequence. We denote the LLM hidden states as a matrix H ∈ Rl×n×d , where l is the hidden
103   layer, n is the sequence length, and d is the hidden size. Three commonly used pooling strategies
104   for matrix H are widely used.
105
      EOS-Last Token Pooling: heos = H[−1,n,:] Since the next-word prediction is often the training
106   objective for LLM, the last token of the whole input sequence, therefore, captures all the information

      2.2   ATTENTION S TRATEGY
122
      LLMs are mostly pre-trained using a causal attention mask (Radford et al., 2018), a unidirectional
123   attention mechanism that allows the current token to only attend to preceding tokens. However,
124   several recent studies have demonstrated the limitations of unidirectional attention and have adapted
125   the attention mask of the LLM to be bidirectional during the fine-tuning process, allowing each
126   token to access the bidirectional context in the sequence (BehnamGhader et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
127   2024; Springer et al., 2024). Subsequently, we denote these two strategies as Causal attention and
128   Bidirectional attention.
129
130   2.3   F INE -T UNING LLM S AS E MBEDDING M ODELS
131
132   While existing LLM-based embedding models differ in their pooling and attention strategies, the
133
      training process is largely similar. To fine-tune an LLM as an embedding model, the contrastive
      learning method is often used (Henderson et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). In
134
      short, contrastive learning encourages the embedding of a focal example to be similar to that of a
135
      positive example while being distant from its negative example, thus enabling a base LLM to adapt
136   to embedding-related tasks. All the works listed in Table 1 use contrastive learning to fine-tune the
137   base LLM. However, they use different training data, so the final performance may be confounded
138   by the dataset.

      3     M ULTI -L AYERS T RAINABLE P OOLING : A N EW P OOLING S TRATEGY THAT
142         O BTAINS E MBEDDING F ROM M ULTIPLE L AYERS
143
144   As shown in Table 1, state-of-the-art LLM-based embedding models use pooling strategies that
145
      obtain embeddings from the last layer of the LLM’s hidden states, regardless of whether they employ
      EOS-last token pooling, mean pooling, or trainable pooling. But can we achieve better results by
146
      using hidden states from other layers? Prior work has shown that different layers of language models,
147
      such as encoder-only BERT or decoder-only LLMs, encode different semantic information (Clark,
148   2019; Oh et al., 2022; Ju et al., 2024). Therefore, we hypothesize that the other layers may contain
149   relevant information that complements the last layer. In this section, we first verify that each layer’s
150   hidden states encode distinct information and that the intermediate layers may also contain relevant
151   information beneficial to downstream tasks. We then propose a new pooling method, termed Multi-
152   Layers Trainable Pooling, which consists of a trainable pooling layer that uses hidden states from
153   all layers.

      3.2   M ULTI -L AYERS T RAINABLE P OOLING
237
      Motivated by the above findings, we propose a new pooling strategy termed Multi-Layers Trainable
238   Pooling, which utilizes an additional trainable layer to capture semantic information from all layers
239   in an LLM. The method is shown in Figure 3. Let H ∈ Rl×n×d represent the LLM hidden state
240   matrix of an input sequence, where l is the number of hidden layers, n is the sequence length,
241   and d is the hidden size. The high-level idea of Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling is to introduce a
242   trainable layer that learns to pool hidden states from different layers. Specifically, there are three
243   main components in this pooling method:
244   Input: LLM Hidden States Across All Layers. The first step involves selecting the LLM output
245   H as the input for the pooling operation. For causal attention LLMs, we use the EOS token hidden
246   states of all LLM layers, denoted as hcausal = H[:,−1,:] , as the input to the subsequent trainable
247   pooling layer. This approach is chosen because, in causal attention LLMs, earlier tokens may in-
248   troduce bias, subsequently affecting the final embedding (Springer et al., 2024). For bidirectional
249   attention LLMs, we  Pnconsider the mean embedding across the token length dimension, expressed as
250   hbi-directional = n1 i=1 H[:,i,:] . The resulting vector serves as the input to the subsequent trainable
251   pooling layer.
252   Layer Weight Matrix. Different layers may have varying importance to the final embedding de-
253   pending on the task. To account for this, we introduce a trainable layer weights matrix that captures
254   the significance of each layer. Specifically, we combine the LLM output with a trainable layer
255   weight matrix, W ∈ Rl×d . The combined layer matrix is computed as hlayers = h + W, where h is
256   the input (either hcausal or hbi-directional ).
257   Cross Attention Matrix. The combined layer matrix hlayers is then multiplied by the parameter
258   matrices WK and WV and produce the key matrix K and value matrix V accordingly. The K and V
                                                                        ′
259   matrix is then combined with a trainable query matrix Q ∈ Rr×d , with d′ being the inner dimension
260   of the cross-attention block and r being the number of latent dimensions which is the same with
261   LLM hidden dimension. Note that the K and V are both derived from the linear transformation of
262   input hlayers , while Q is a trainable parameter matrix. This cross-attention network is similar to the
263   method used in Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), which maps varying-size video frames to fixed-size
264   visual embeddings, similar to obtaining embeddings from multiple layers.
265   The cross-attention network computes attention between fixed, trainable queries and keys/values de-
266   rived from the input to capture and encode the most relevant information from hlayers into a semantic
267   latent space. The output of this cross-attention network is then passed through a multi-layer per-
268   ceptron (MLP) to produce the final output embedding. Using a trainable query matrix (Q) instead
269   of deriving it directly from the input data (hlayers ) allows the cross-attention mechanism to more
      effectively filter semantic information from multi-layer hidden states through the trainable Q.

      Compared to NV-embed (Lee et al., 2024), which pioneered the use of a trainable pooling layer, our
271   proposed pooling method introduces three key innovations. First, we utilize hidden states from all
272   layers rather than just the last layer. Second, we include a trainable layer weight matrix to account
273   for the varying importance of different layers across tasks. Third, while NV-embed transforms the
274   last layer’s hidden states to the query matrix Q, our method combines the hidden state matrix with
275   the layer weight matrix and transforms it into key matrix K and value matrix V.

      4     P OOLING AND ATTENTION E XPERIMENTS
278
279
      As shown in Table 1, state-of-the-art embedding models are often trained using different pooling and
280
      attention strategies. However, two factors obscure our understanding of the most effective designs
281   for LLM-based embedding models. First, they are trained using different datasets, which is a key
282   confounding factor that significantly influences final performance on the MTEB benchmarks. Sec-
283   ond, they primarily report results without statistical testing, making it unclear whether the observed
284   performance improvements are statistically meaningful. To address these, we aim to empirically
285   assess the effectiveness of different pooling and attention strategies through a fair comparison using
286   the same dataset and training protocol.
287
288   4.1   P OOLING AND ATTENTION C OMBINATIONS
289
290   In the experiment, we consider the following five design combinations:
291   Model 1: EOS-Last Token Pooling + Causal Attention
292
      Model 2: Last-Layer Trainable Pooling + Causal Attention
293
294   Model 3: Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling+ Causal Attention
295
      Model 4: Last-Layer Trainable Pooling + Bidirectional Attention
296
297   Model 5: Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling+ Bidirectional attention
298   These five combinations allow us to conduct pairwise comparisons between different pooling and
299   attention strategies by controlling for other potential confounding factors. For example, by compar-
300   ing Model 1, Model 2, and Model 3, we can assess the effectiveness of different pooling strategies
301   under the same attention method. Similarly, by comparing Model 3 and Model 5, we can evaluate
302   how different attention methods affect embedding performance when using a multi-layer trainable
303   pooling strategy.
304   Why Mean Pooling Is Not Considered in Our Setting? First, prior work has demonstrated that
305   employing mean pooling in a causal attention LLM-based embedding model introduces a bias to-
306   wards the earlier tokens (Springer et al., 2024), leading to poor performance (Wang et al., 2023;
307   BehnamGhader et al., 2024). Second, for LLMs that use bidirectional attention, NV-embed (Lee
308   et al., 2024) has shown that an additional trainable pooling layer can outperform mean pooling.
309   Thus, mean pooling does not appear to be a viable choice for either attention strategy. To keep our
310   experiment manageable in scope, we have therefore excluded mean pooling from consideration.
311   Why EOS-Last Token Pooling + Bidirectional Attention Is Not Considered in Our Setting?
312   Intuitively, when bidirectional attention is used, the EOS-Last token is no longer meaningful as the
313   input embedding. In fact, existing LLM-based embedding models that use bidirectional attention
314   typically employ pooling techniques such as mean pooling or trainable pooling layers (Lee et al.,
315   2024; BehnamGhader et al., 2024; Muennighoff et al., 2024). Therefore, in our experiment, we do
316   not include this combination of pooling and attention.

      7     R ELATED W ORKS
499
500   7.1    E NCODER - BASED E MBEDDING M ODELS
501
502   Text embedding has evolved with the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). Encoder
503   models, particularly BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and the T5 Encoder (Raffel et al., 2020), have
504
      been widely used in tasks like text similarity by capturing sentence-level semantics. Building upon
      this foundation, Sentence-BERT (Reimers, 2019) uses Siamese networks for fixed-size embeddings
505
      to efficiently retrieve semantically similar sentences. Furthermore, the INSTRUCTOR model (Su
506
      et al., 2023) leverages the T5 Encoder to incorporate various instructional prompts, allowing it to
507   adapt to a wide range of downstream tasks. The BGE-M3 model (Chen et al., 2024), based on XLM-
508   RoBERTa (Conneau et al., 2020), integrates dense and sparse retrieval to support multi-granularity
509   in the retrieval process.
510
511
512   7.2    LLM- BASED E MBEDDING M ODELS
513
      The success of LLMs in text generation tasks has sparked increasing interest in exploring LLM-
514
      based embedding models. RepLLama (Ma et al., 2024) pioneered this promising direction by
515
      finetuning an LLM as a dense retriever, demonstrating the potential of LLMs in embedding tasks.
516   Building upon this foundation, subsequent research has explored various techniques to enhance
517   LLM-based embedding models. E5-mistral-7b-instruct (Wang et al., 2023) investigated the usage of
518   synthetic data in the training process. Recognizing the significance of capturing bidirectional con-
519   text in embedding tasks, GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2024) and LLM2Vec (BehnamGhader et al.,
520   2024) employ bidirectional attention mechanisms in their LLM architectures. By attending to both
521   past and future tokens, these models can generate more contextually informed embeddings, poten-
522   tially leading to improved performance in tasks such as text similarity and retrieval. Furthermore,
523   NV-Embed (Lee et al., 2024) introduces a novel latent attention layer to obtain pooled embeddings
524
      for a sequence of tokens. These advancements showcase the ongoing efforts to harness the power
      of LLMs for embedding tasks. However, existing LLM-based embedding models are often trained
525
      on different datasets, leading to mixed conclusions regarding the effectiveness of pooling and atten-
526
      tion strategies. Our work aims to empirically evaluate and deepen our understanding of the training
527   design choices for LLM-based embeddings.
528
529
530   8     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this study, we investigate LLM-based embedding models, focusing on two key design elements:
533
      pooling and attention. We conduct a large-scale experiment by fine-tuning five LLM-based embed-
534   ding models on the same training data using different pooling and attention strategies. Our findings
535   highlight that fine-tuning LLMs with bidirectional attention and an additional trainable pooling layer
536   demonstrates superior performance in semantic textual similarity and information retrieval tasks, but
537   underperforms in clustering and classification tasks. Furthermore, we introduce a new and effective
538   pooling method, Multi-Layers Trainable Pooling, which leverages all layers rather than just the last
539   hidden layer to capture broader and potentially more relevant semantic information. We hope this
      work sheds light on training LLM-based embedding models.
