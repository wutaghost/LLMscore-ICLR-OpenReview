009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            DETR has made notable performance improvements in object detection tasks by
012            leveraging the long-range modeling capabilities of Transformers, but encoding
013            all tokens indiscriminately significantly escalates computational cost and leads to
014            slow convergence. Recent sparsification strategies effectively reduce computa-
015            tional cost through sparse encoders. However, these methods rely heavily on a
016
               fixed sparse ratio, which overlooks the coherence of feature representation across
               levels, leading to performance degradation in complex scenes. To address this
017
               issue, we propose a novel object detection approach aimed at constructing con-
018
               sistent representations of multi-level features. The approach composes two steps:
019            First, we introduce a perspective proposal module that leverages the spatial infor-
020            mation of high-level foreground features to guide the sparse sampling of low-level
021            features, ensuring both integrity and coherence of multi-scale feature information.
022            Furthermore, we integrated semantic probability to perform hierarchical and dy-
023            namic adjustments to the saliency of queries, thereby refining the semantic inter-
024            action among foreground queries. Experimental results demonstrate that on the
025            challenging task-specific VisDrone dataset, our pDETR method enhances AP by
026            1.8% compared to DINO. On the COCO 2017 dataset, the performance improve-
027
               ment of pDETR is even more apparent, achieving a +2.5% increase in AP: under
               the 1× schedule, pDETR attains an AP of 51.5%, and under the 2× schedule, the
028
               AP further increases to 52.0%. Moreover, it exhibits faster convergence, exceed-
029
               ing 40% AP in just 2 training epochs while reducing computational cost by 13%
030            in terms of FLOPs, indicating superior detection capability.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Object detection is one of the most challenging and influential tasks in the field of computer vi-
036   sion, with widespread applications in autonomous driving, video surveillance, and robotic naviga-
037   tion. In recent years, traditional object detection methods based on convolutional neural networks
038   (CNNs)(Cai & Vasconcelos, 2018; Redmon, 2016) have made significant advancements, thanks to
039
      the rapid development of deep learning technologies(He et al., 2016). However, these methods still
      rely on hand-crafted designs(Ren et al., 2015) for preprocessing, candidate region generation, and
040
      post-processing steps, which limit their flexibility and scalability. But the introduction of DETR (De-
041
      tection Transformer)(Carion et al., 2020) eliminates the dependence on manually designed convolu-
042   tional detectors. As an end-to-end object detection framework, DETR incorporates the Transformer
043   architecture, harnessing its capacity for long-range modeling, leading to substantial improvements
044   in object detection.(Arnab et al., 2021)
045
      Despite DETR’s strong performance on large-scale general-purpose datasets like COCO(Lin et al.,
046
      2014), its effectiveness is highly dependent on the scale and quality of the training data, and it suf-
047
      fers from slow convergence(Zhu et al., 2020; Hou et al., 2024b). The core issue lies in the conflict
048   between the sparse distribution of foreground objects and the Transformer’s uniform processing of
049   all tokens, which results in negative predictions dominating the training process, thereby requiring
050   more samples to achieve effective convergence. The Transformer was initially designed for natural
051   language processing (NLP)(Vaswani, 2017), where language consists of highly abstract sequences of
052   symbols containing rich syntactic and semantic information. It operates without imposing structural
053   bias on the input sequence, treating all tokens uniformly, and captures long-range dependencies be-
      tween tokens through the self-attention mechanism. However, in images, many pixels are redundant,

086

087
      rupts information continuity between layers and leads to semantic misalignment. Here, we further
088   conduct a visual analysis of the semantic misalignment issues caused by relying on a static sparsity
089   ratio. As shown in Figure 1, the baseline that depends on a static sparsity ratio exhibits an imbalance
090   in the selection across different feature levels, leading to the omission of some key object tokens.
091
      To address these challenges, we propose the pDETR model, which innovatively integrates a Per-
092
      spective Proposal Module and a Semantic-Aware Module, aiming to comprehensively enhance the
093
      performance of the DETR framework. First, the Perspective Proposal Module uses high-level fore-
094   ground features to guide sparse sampling in lower layers, maintaining feature transmission across
095   scales and focusing on key features during processing. Secondly, we developed the Semantic-Aware
096   Module, which integrates semantic probabilities to dynamically adjust the saliency of each token at
097   a fine-grained level. This enhances the interaction between semantic information and object queries,
098   thereby improving the model’s object detection performance in complex scenes.
099

102          • We propose a Perspective Proposal Module that leverages the spatial position information

104            the integrity and continuity of multi-scale feature information.
105
106          • We design a Semantic-Aware Module that uses semantic probability to perform hierar-
107            chical and dynamic adjustments to the saliency of queries, thereby refining the semantic
               interaction among foreground queries.

118   Efficient computation in detection transformer.
119
      As is well known, DETR suffers from high computational complexity and memory usage(Carion
120
      et al., 2020). In efforts to reduce redundant computations, several advancements have been made.
121   Deformable DETR(Zhu et al., 2020) enhances convergence speed by limiting its attention mech-
122   anism to a small set of key sampling points around reference points, thus reducing unnecessary
123   calculations. Efficient-DETR(Neubeck & Van Gool, 2006) simplifies the model by initializing ob-
124   ject containers with dense priors, which allows for reducing the number of layers in both the encoder
125   and decoder while maintaining detection performance. Lite DETR(Li et al., 2022) introduces an in-
126   terleaved update mechanism between high-level and low-level features, optimizing the flow of infor-
127   mation. PnP-DETR(Wang et al., 2021a) abstracts image feature maps into fine-grained foreground
128   object feature vectors and coarse background context vectors, effectively balancing detail and con-
129
      text. Additionally, recent approaches have adopted sparse encoding strategies to limit the number
      of queries involved in self-attention within the encoder. Sparse DETR(Roh et al., 2021) refines
130
      only the top-ρ% of tokens across all encoder layers based on Decoder cross-Attention Map results.
131
      Focus DETR(Zheng et al., 2023) and Salience DETR(Hou et al., 2024a) introduce token scoring
132   mechanisms within the encoder to prioritize more informative tokens for attention. However, these
133   methods still rely on static sparsity ratios to select foreground tokens, implemented by performing
134   hierarchical top-k selection at each feature level, which fails to fully leverage the complementary
135   nature of features across different layers. Independently performing sparse query encoding at each
136   feature level leads to semantic mismatches and spatial inconsistencies between tokens in different
137   layers. This issue becomes particularly evident when dealing with complex datasets. To address this
138   problem, we propose a method that uses the spatial position information of high-level foreground
139   tokens as priors to guide sparse encoding sampling of lower-level features, ensuring the consistency
140
      of semantic and spatial information of the foreground tokens.
141   Multi-Scale Features for Object Detection
142
      In the field of object detection, effectively representing and handling objects at different scales has
143
      always been a core challenge. Traditional CNN-based detectors widely employ multi-scale fea-
144   ture extraction techniques such as Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN)(Lin et al., 2017), Bidirectional
145   Feature Pyramid Networks (BiFPN)(Chen et al., 2021), Path Aggregation Networks (PANet)(Wang
146   et al., 2019), and NAS-FPN(Ghiasi et al., 2019). These methods achieve effective multi-scale ob-
147   ject detection by fusing features at different scales, significantly improving model efficiency and
148   performance. However, DETR faces significant challenges when processing multi-scale features.
149
      First, handling multi-scale features directly in DETR leads to substantial computational overhead.
150   Since DETR relies on the self-attention mechanism, it processes all tokens indiscriminately, lacking
151   a specialized mechanism to handle objects at different scales(Roh et al., 2021). This global pro-
152   cessing not only fails to capture the details of small objects effectively but also wastes considerable
153   computational resources on irrelevant background areas. This issue becomes particularly prominent
154   when processing high-resolution images, where redundant computations are more pronounced. As
155   a result, DETR performs worse in multi-scale scenarios compared to CNN-based detectors, particu-
156   larly in detecting small objects(Yang et al., 2024).
157   To address this problem, we propose pDETR. Specifically, we first use spatial information from
158   high-level foreground features to guide the sparse sampling of lower-level features, enabling the
159   model to focus on foreground features while significantly reducing computational costs. Secondly,
160   we utilize semantic probabilities to further refine the focus on foreground areas in a fine-grained
161   manner, preventing distraction by irrelevant background regions. pDETR strikes a balance between
      accuracy and efficiency, providing a superior solution for multi-scale object detection.

197   DETR
198   We first review the basic workflow of the DETR(Carion et al., 2020) detector. Initially, it extracts
199   a 2D feature map from the backbone network, denoted as F ∈ RH×W ×D , where H and W are
200   the height and width of the feature map, and D is the feature dimension. The feature map is then
201   flattened into a sequence Fflat ∈ RN ×D , where N = H × W represents the number of tokens.
202   These tokens are processed by the Transformer encoder, generating enhanced memory Z ∈ RN ×D .
203   In the decoding stage, DETR introduces a set of learnable object queries Q ∈ RM ×D , where M
204   denotes the number of queries. The decoder interacts with the output from the encoder and the
205   object queries, predicting a class probability sm ∈ RC and a bounding box bm ∈ R4 for each
206
      query qm , where C is the number of object classes. The final detection results consist of the set
      {(sm , bm )}M
                  m=1 , representing the predictions for the objects in the image.

208   Sparse DETR-like Detectors
209
      To mitigate the high computational cost associated with the self-attention mechanism in DETR,
210   sparse DETR-like detectors introduced sparsification strategies focused on foreground feature se-
211   lection. These approaches aim to reduce the number of tokens participating in self-attention by se-
212   lecting only the most relevant foreground tokens, thus improving computational efficiency. Sparse
213   DETR employs the Decoder cross-Attention Map from the decoder as a supervision signal, dynami-
214   cally selecting the top-ρ% tokens in the encoder layers. Building on this, Focus DETR uses a binary
215   supervision signal (foreground or background) derived from ground truth labels, where foreground
      tokens are marked as 1 and background tokens as 0.

277   3.3   S EMANTIC AWARE M ODULE
278
279   To enhance semantic interaction among foreground queries, we developed a Semantic Aware Mod-
280   ule. This module further refines the localization of target categories within the foreground areas
281   identified by the Perspective Proposal while effectively suppressing background noise. In the DINO
282
      model, it was observed that using unrefined content queries as object query initialization could intro-
      duce noise that misleads the decoder. Based on this insight and to maintain simplicity in the encoder
283
      design, our Semantic Aware Module does not rely on label information but dynamically adjusts the
284
      weight of each token in the query using semantic probability information. This approach allows for
285   more precise localization of target categories while effectively suppressing background noise.
286

339   Implementation Details
340   The implementation details of pDETR are consistent with other detectors similar to DETR. We used
341   the AdamW optimizer to train the model on four NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPUs (24GB each), with
342   weight decay set to 1 × 10−4 . The initial learning rate was set to 1 × 10−5 for the backbone and

346
      VisDrone dataset, given the smaller object sizes and more complex scenes, we performed 36 epochs

      A.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS O F THE P ERSPECTIVE P ROPOSAL
597
598   The Perspective Proposal Module is designed to resolve spatial and semantic inconsistencies be-
599   tween feature layers by constructing proposals progressively. The process starts with the top feature
600   layer, which contains the richest semantic information, and proceeds downward, ensuring that the
601   higher-layer semantics effectively guide the spatial details in lower layers. For the topmost layer,
602   the original saliency mask is directly used as foreground tokens, determined by saliency scores from
603   Salience DETR (as shown in equation 1). These scores are calculated based on the distance between
604   each token’s position in the original image and the ground truth bounding box, ensuring the accuracy
605   of the selected tokens.
606   In subsequent layers, the foreground token positions from the previous layer are upsampled using
607   nearest-neighbor interpolation to match the resolution of the current layer. This upsampling ensures
608   that the high-level tokens are correctly projected onto the lower-resolution feature maps, maintaining
609   semantic consistency across layers. After upsampling, we refine the foreground token selection by
610   applying a 3x3 convolution to the mask, expanding each token’s influence to its neighboring pixels.
611   Tokens with activation values below a set threshold are considered isolated and removed. This
612
      process helps ensure that the remaining foreground tokens are semantically relevant and spatially
      consistent.
613
614   These operations are intended to prevent isolated tokens from negatively affecting the next layer’s
615   Perspective Proposal. Without removal, these tokens may lead to suboptimal proposals in later
616   layers, as they may lack sufficient information. By iteratively applying this refinement across layers,
617   the Perspective Proposal Module ensures the semantic information from higher layers aligns with
618
      the spatial structure in lower layers, improving proposal quality and avoiding the negative effects of
      isolated tokens.
