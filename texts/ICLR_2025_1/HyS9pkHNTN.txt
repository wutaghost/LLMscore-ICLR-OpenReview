011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            World-model (WM) is a highly promising approach for training AI agents. How-
014            ever, in complex learning systems such as autonomous driving, AI agents interact
015            with others in a dynamic environment and face significant challenges such as
016
               partial observability and non-stationarity. Inspired by how humans naturally solve
               complex tasks hierarchically and how human drivers share their intentions (e.g.,
017
               using turn signals), we introduce HANSOME, a WM-based hierarchical planning
018
               with semantic communications framework. In HANSOME, semantic information,
019            particularly text and compressed visual data, is generated and shared to improve
020            two-level planning. HANSOME incorporates two important designs: 1) A hi-
021            erarchical planning strategy, where the higher-level policy generates semantic
022            intentions, and semantic alignment is devised to ensure that the lower-level policy
023            determines specific controls to execute these intentions. 2) A cross-modal encoder-
024            decoder to fuse and utilize shared semantic information and enhance planning
025            through multi-modal understanding. A key advantage of HANSOME is that the
026            generated intentions not only enhance the lower-level policy but also can be shared
027
               and understood by both humans and other AVs to improve their planning. Further-
               more, we devise AdaSMO, an entropy-controlled adaptive scalarization method, to
028
               tackle multi-objective optimization problem in hierarchical learning. Extensive ex-
029
               periments show that HANSOME outperforms state-of-the-art WM-based methods
030            in challenging driving tasks, enhancing overall traffic safety and efficiency.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION

      An ambitious goal of embodied AI is to develop cognitive agents capable of dynamically and
036   adaptively planning to perform tasks in complex, high-dimensional environments. World-model
037   (WM)-based reinforcement learning (RL), an end-to-end learning approach, has demonstrated signifi-
038   cant potential. In WM, a latent dynamics model of the environment is first learned and then leveraged
039   to train policies. However, applying WM to real-world applications, such as autonomous driving in
040   traffic networks, presents numerous challenges. These environments involve heterogeneous agents
041   interacting in environments with intertwined system dynamics. A key obstacle in such complex set-
042   tings is insufficient information available to the ego agent, which operates under partial observability
043   and must plan in non-stationary environments.
044   A promising solution to the above challenge is to enable agents to share information (Zhu et al.,
045   2022) . Recent research has explored sharing different types of information, such as (encoded)
046   partial observations (Jiang & Lu, 2018), hidden states (Sukhbaatar et al., 2016), policy and value
047   networks (Peng et al., 2017), and (encoded) action intentions (Kim et al., 2020; Qi & Zhu, 2018).
048   Intention sharing between vehicles has been demonstrated to be a practical and promising approach
049   to improving safety and efficiency in real-world vehicle-to-vehicle (V2V) applications (Wang et al.,
050
      2023a; 2024; 2023b; Xie et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022).
051   However, it is nontrivial to ensure that the shared information can be understood and utilized by agents
052   of interest, which is challenging in real-world applications, such as in mixed traffic where human
053   drivers and different types of autonomous vehicles (AVs) co-exist. AVs may share sensor data (Yu
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2022a) or detection results (Xu et al., 2021), whereas human drivers tend to

054
      share and interpret turn signals, text messages, or voice prompts from navigation apps. Moreover,
055   human information sharing often takes place at the intention level, improving the communication
056   efficiency. This also aligns well with the hierarchical nature of human thought processes. If AVs
057   can understand and generate intentions like turn signals, texts, or voices, human drivers and AVs
058   can communicate with ease. The end-to-end “black-box” approach, which maps observation inputs
059   directly to actions such as steering and acceleration, impedes the sharing of interpretable intentions.
060   In this work, we attempt to address this issue and answer the following question for WM-based RL
061   for autonomous driving: “How to generate interpretable information for semantic communications
062   and utilize such information to improve planning among heterogeneous agents?”
063   To address this question, we develop HANSOME, based on the key insight that humans can naturally
064   solve complex tasks quickly by leveraging hierarchical thinking and decision-making (Wang et al.,
065   2023c), an essential component of human intelligence. Specifically, the human brain possesses a
066   structured architecture capable of not only controlling specific muscular patterns but also of planning
067   more abstract goals (Turella et al., 2020). This approach further provides an avenue for efficient
068   information sharing as discussed earlier.

      A key challenge in training hierarchical planners is non-stationarity, as both policies evolve simul-
101   taneously. For example, the higher-level policy observes different transitions and rewards because
102   the lower-level policy constantly changes, even in the same state with the same higher-level goal.
103   This is a known challenge in hierarchical RL (Pateria et al., 2021; Hutsebaut-Buysse et al., 2022).
104   Prior works usually mitigate the issue by updating transition data through relabelling and hindsight
105   replay (Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2019). Instead of relying on additional
106   relabelling processes, we devise AdaSMO, an entropy-controlled adaptive scalarization technique, to
107   train hierarchical planners. We view two-level training as a multi-objective optimization problem,
      which in general has a set of Pareto optimal points forming a Pareto frontier (as illustrated in Fig-

109   oscillate across Pareto optimal points. With this insight, AdaSMO dynamically adapts the relative

111   policy exploration, ultimately guiding them to converge on the desired policy.

      Our main contributions are as follows:
113
114    • HANSOME Design. We introduce HANSOME, a WM-based hierarchical planning with seman-
115      tic communications framework, to enable interpretable information sharing among heterogeneous
116      agents. HANSOME has a hierarchical planning strategy where the higher-level policy generates
117      and shares semantic intentions in the form of text to guide the lower-level policy which in turn
118      decides specific controls. A cross-modal encoder-decoder is devised to fuse and understand the
119      shared semantic information. Since information such as vehicle location or speed is not accessible
120      in the WM’s latent representation, we propose translating intentions into waypoints to enforce
121
         semantic alignment between higher-level intentions and lower-level controls. The reward function
         is meticulously designed to balance the objectives of intention generation and waypoint following.
122
123    • Adaptive Scalarization in Multi-objective Optimization (AdaSMO) for HANSOME. We
124
         view hierarchical training as multi-objective optimization and devise AdaSMO to dynamically
         balance learning of two-level policies to address non-stationarity. As the lower-level policy
125
         becomes more skilled, the higher-level policy progressively reduces its exploration by controlling
126
         action entropy, while gradually increasing the complexity of the lower-level subtasks.
127
       • Extensive Experiments on Complex Urban Driving Tasks. We present extensive empirical
128
         results in Section 4 to demonstrate the capability of HANSOME on a variety of challenging
129
         urban driving tasks involving communications with other agents. Ablation studies are used to
130      demonstrate the necessity of HANSOME’s semantic communications and hierarchical planning
131      in solving tasks where current state-of-the-art WM-based RL methods may fail, and show
132      AdaSMO’s effectiveness in training a good hierarchical planning strategy. Unlike prior WM-
133      based RL works, HANSOME enables semantic communications across agents, and learns to
134      generate and understand messages within WMs’ imagination.

      World Models for Autonomous Driving. WM studies in the field of autonomous driving can be
139   grouped into two categories (Guan et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). The first category leverages WMs as
140   neural driving simulators to synthesize realistic driving videos (Yang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Kim
141   et al., 2021). For instance, GAIA-1 (Hu et al., 2023) generates driving scenarios from videos, texts,
142   and actions. DriveDreamer (Wang et al., 2023d) and DriveDreamer-2 (Zhao et al., 2024) enhance
143   scenario generation with high-definition maps and 3D bounding boxes, and integrate large language
144   models for user-friendly interaction, respectively. ADriver-1 (Jia et al., 2023) advances this approach
145   by eliminating the need for extensive prior information and achieving sustained driving through
146   continuous scenario and action prediction. The second category utilizes WMs to train and evaluate
147
      agent policies within simulated environments. MILE (Hu et al., 2022) employs a Dreamer-style WM
      for imitation learning, utilizing road map and camera inputs to predict transitions in future BEVs.
148
      Prior works also explored Dreamer-style models for online RL. SEM2 (Gao et al., 2022) utilizes
149
      DreamerV2 and decodes camera and LiDAR data into semantic BEVs. Think2Drive (Li et al., 2024)
150   trains DreamerV3 with BEV inputs on CARLA Leaderboard scenarios. Notably, MILE, SEM2,
151   and Think2Drive use pre-determined routes provided by CARLA map topology to guide the ego
152   agent. While HANSOME agents can determine their own routes using intentions generated by the
153   higher-level policy; moreover, HANSOME utilizes semantic communications to improve planning.
154
      Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL). HRL decomposes long-horizon tasks into simpler
155
      subtasks (Parr & Russell, 1997; Dayan & Hinton, 1992; Sutton et al., 1999), by learning a higher-level

157   primitive actions to achieve them. Many prior works determine subgoal spaces through subtask
158   discovery (Pateria et al., 2021; Hamed et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2019). For instance, Director (Hafner
159   et al., 2022) learns sub-goal spaces directly from high-dimensional image space. HIRO (Nachum et al.,
160   2018) and HAC (Levy et al., 2017) mitigate non-stationarity in hierarchical training by employing
161   relabelling techniques and hindsight replay. However, the discovered higher-level goals are often
      not interpretable by heterogeneous agents, limiting their applicability in real-world multi-agent

      World Model. We adopt the Dreamer-style WM paradigm to learn environment representations and
237
      dynamics through interaction (Hafner et al., 2023). The hierarchical policy is trained from scratch
238   within the WM’s imagination. The WM maintains an internal state ht using a Recurrent State Space
239   Model (RSSM) (Hafner et al., 2020; 2023), which compresses the observations and actions from
240   the past t − 1 steps. Let ϕ denote the combined parameter vector of the WM. At each time step t,
241   given the hidden state ht , an encoder processes the observation ot ( (e.g., BEVs, destination, shared
242   intentions) into a latent representation zt , such that zt ∼ pϕ (zt |ht , ot ). Additionally, a dynamics
243   predictor estimates zt without relying on ot , i.e., zˆt ∼ pϕ (zˆt |ht ). The model state is then defined
244   as xt = [ht , zt ]. From xt , the WM decodes an observation o′t ∼ pϕ (o′t |xt ), predicts a reward
245   rt ∼ pϕ (rt |xt ), and estimates a discount factor γt ∼ pϕ (γt |xt ), which represents the terminal
246
      probability. An actor is trained to generate actions at conditioned on xt . The RSSM then updates
      the internal state for the next time step as ht+1 = fϕ (xt , at ). HANSOME employs the Dreamer
247
      paradigm in its experiments; however, its design is not restricted to this specific structure and can
248
      easily incorporate future advancements in WMs.

253   3.1   K EY D ESIGN C OMPONENTS IN HANSOME
254
255   Hierarchical Planning Aided by Semantics. Human drivers naturally decompose driving maneuvers
256   into subgoals. Thus inspired, HANSOME breaks down complex driving tasks into a series of
257
      semantic intentions that can be described using texts understandable by human drivers. The semantic
      information not only improves traffic safety and efficiency by informing other agents, but also guides
258
      the lower-level policy to achieve long horizon planning.
259
260   For the higher-level policy, we define a set of semantic intentions that align with human driving
261   behaviors. This set, denoted as the intention space I, includes texts such as “Lane Follow”, “Right
262   Lane Change”, and “Left Lane Change”. We also denote the learned higher-level policy to be πθH .
263   Every T time steps, a new intention it ∼ πθH (·|xt ) ∈ I is selected by the policy, conditioned on the
264
      current model state xt . In this way, the complex task is decomposed into a sequence of subgoals in
      the form of semantic intentions.
265
266   Given a higher-level intention it , the lower-level policy πθL maps the current model state xt to a
267   control command ct (acceleration and steering), conditioned on it , i.e., ct ∼ πθL (·|xt , it ). Then the
268   joint action can be represented as at = [it , ct ], which is fed into the sequence model of the WM to
269   predict the next frame. Theoretically, the dynamics of the environment depend only on ct , but we
      include it in the action to enforce semantic alignment for the lower-level policy, as elaborated below.

      Semantic Alignment for Hierarchical Planning. Learning a lower-level policy that can effectively
271   “understand” text-based intentions and align with their semantics is highly non-trivial, particularly
272   in WM settings. This challenge arises because information such as vehicle location or speed is
273   inaccessible in the WM’s latent representation, making it impossible to directly evaluate alignment
274   from the latent space. Since only the WM understands its own latent representations and can predict
275   future rewards based on them, alignment can be reinforced through reward signals. Rewarding
276   engineering has been the main challenge in RL, especially for complex task domains like autonomous
277   driving (Kiran et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Designing a separate reward for each intention is
278   cumbersome and impractical, as it does not scale with new intentions.
279   Therefore, we propose to visualize intentions to waypoints for semantic alignment. The semantics of
280   the higher-level intention it is “translated” into a sequence of waypoints wt = {wt,i }ni=1 that can be
281   rendered on the BEV. The lower-level policy’s objective is now to follow these waypoints on BEVs
282   which implicitly aligns with the semantics. Since the translated waypoints exist in the observation
283   space, the higher-level intentions must be included in the action space for the WM to accurately
284   predict these waypoints. We emphasize in HANSOME, waypoints are planned by HANSOME itself,
285
      instead of being pre-determined by simulator as in Gao et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024).
286   Reward Design for Hierarchical Planning. Designing reward functions is known to be challenging
287   in general. In HANSOME, reward design needs to take into account the signals at both levels, namely
288   (1) generating intentions and (2) following waypoints. Our extensive experiments reveal that directly
289   using a weighted sum of the two yields poor results. If the weight for (1) is too small, the higher-level
290   policy may fail to learn the desired behavior. Conversely, a larger weight for (1) may overwhelm
291
      and disrupt the reward signal for learning the lower-level policy, thus significantly slowing down the
      training process. To overcome this challenge, we propose combining these components by dividing
292
      the waypoint-following reward by a factor proportional to the deviation extent of the intention from
293
      the overall destination. In this way, we can effectively amplify the impact of (1), while still providing
294   a proportionate reward for the lower-level policy to follow the waypoints. Specifically, the reward for
295   following the waypoints is given by

            O PTIMIZATION V IEW

      Learning multiple levels of policies simultaneously is highly non-trivial due to the challenge of
331   non-stationarity (Pateria et al., 2021). This is because the lower-level policy is non-stationary during
332   training—even when given the same subgoal—so the trajectory it produces varies over time. This
333   complicates the higher-level policy’s learning process, as it observes inconsistent trajectories for the
334   same subgoals. A classic approach to address non-stationarity is subgoal relabeling and hindsight
335   replay (Andrychowicz et al., 2017; Levy et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2019), where achieved states are
336   used to relabel subgoals in transition data. Instead of relabeling transition data, we propose AdaSMO
337   to dynamically adjust the higher-level policy exploration, thus balancing two-level learning, and
338   mitigating non-stationarity without the need for data relabeling.
339   We view hierarchical policy learning as a multi-objective optimization problem with the two objectives
340   of maximizing the reward of the higher-level and lower-level policy. For multi-objective optimization
341   problems, there are a set of Pareto optimal points forming a frontier, as illustrated in Figure 1. The
342   desired point on this frontier would enable the higher-level policy to plan a suitable route and the
343   lower-level policy to execute it successfully. However, a naive scalarization of the two objectives
344   may yield poor returns. Since there is no universal global optimum, the learned policy may oscillate
345
      between extreme points or converge to an undesired one. In our empirical studies, we observe that the
      higher-level policy converges much faster than the lower-level policy. When the lower-level policy
346
      is inadequate, the higher-level policy attempts to maximize rewards by generating overly simplistic
347
      plans, such as straight lines. As a result, the lower-level policy becomes fixated on these basic tasks
348   and is unable to handle more complex ones.
349
350   Entropy-Controlled Adaptive Scalarization. To resolve these issues, we propose an entropy-
351
      controlled adaptive scalarization technique for multi-objective optimization (AdaSMO) to balance the
      training of higher-level and lower-level policies. The entropy of the higher-level policy is dynamically
352
      adjusted to embody different weights in the scalarization of the two objectives. A large entropy
353
      generates a nearly uniform distribution over the output intentions, in which case the primary goal is
354   to enable the lower-level policy to learn to follow each individual intention. As the entropy decays,
355   the higher-level policy reduces its exploration and begins to converge at a controllable rate toward
356   the desired Pareto optimal. AdaSMO, therefore, echoes the human learning process, which begins
357   with mastering basic skills before progressively integrating them into more complex tasks. Naturally,
358   reward signals are used as a measure of policy quality to guide this adaptation. In practice, the entropy
359   is adjusted by dividing the output of the higher-level policy’s MLP head by a scaling factor S before
360   applying the softmax layer, i.e.,

396   Benchmark Settings. Dreamer-style and online RL works typically evaluate models in a highly
397   realistic simulator, CARLA, with customized scenarios (Gao et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022; Xie et al.,
398
      2021) or Leaderboard (Li et al., 2024), given the interactions with environments needed by online
      RL and its closed-loop nature. See Appendix A for how prior works customize tasks to their needs.
399
      Therefore, to examine the benefits of semantic communications in HANSOME and the baselines,
400
      as well as the advantages of the hierarchical planner, we develop four challenging tasks to evaluate
401   various model capabilities, including 1) DenseTraffic, a task featuring dense traffic with 300
402   randomly spawned vehicles in CARLA Town04. The ego agent needs to navigate through traffic
403   flows, change lanes, and avoid collisions to reach destinations. 2) LeftTurn and 3) RightTurn
404   are tasks performed at intersections, where the agent has to merge into dense traffic at the proper time
405   when other vehicles randomly divert from the flow. Notably, the background vehicles are set to be
406   aggressive such that they do not actively avoid collisions, mimicking irrational human drivers. 4)
407   ObstacleBypass, where the agent is asked to go straight on a lane but with an obstacle ahead. It
408   tests the agent’s flexibility to deviate from the pre-determined route destination and return later.
409   Baseline Settings. Our baselines include state-of-the-art WMs, DreamerV2 (Hafner et al., 2020),
410   Director (Hafner et al., 2022), and DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023). Director generates images
411   as higher-level goals but does not present actionable text semantics; DreamerV2 and DreamerV3
412   are single-level planning frameworks. Prior applications of WM-based agents in CARLA (Gao
413   et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024) typically adopt Dreamer-style single-level planning.

415
      2022) modifies DreamerV2 decoder to output BEV masks. Both models are Dreamer-based and not
      open-sourced; therefore, we use DreamerV2 and DreamerV3 to represent them. These baselines
416
      do not take into account multi-agent interactions. There is not yet a hierarchical or communicative
417
      WM-based RL algorithm as our baselines, nor closed-loop autonomous driving benchmark with
418   communications as benchmarks. For fair comparison, we assume enhanced observability via semantic
419   communications across all baselines. To highlight this, we denote baselines with a “-C” suffix (see
420   Table 1) meaning semantic communications are enabled for these baselines.

      5     D ISCUSSION
520
521   We propose HANSOME, a WM-based hierarchical planning framework that mirrors the human
522   approach of decomposing driving behaviors into different levels of abstraction and using turn signals
523   to inform other drivers. HANSOME seamlessly integrates hierarchical planning with semantic
524   communication using visual and textual information. The higher-level policy generates text-based
525   intentions to guide the lower-level policy and to communicate with other agents. HANSOME
526   employs a novel adaptive approach, AdaSMO, to tackle the challenging multi-objective optimization
527   in hierarchical planning. Through WM-based RL, HANSOME learns both higher-level and lower-
528   level policies from scratch within the WM’s imagination, mastering complex driving tasks and
529
      effectively navigating dense traffic. Extensive experiments demonstrate that HANSOME outperforms
      state-of-the-art WM-based RL algorithms and enhances traffic safety and efficiency through its
530
      hierarchical planning and semantic communication capabilities.
531
532   We further discuss our ego-centric training algorithm in Appendix B. Multi-agent RL is notoriously
533   difficult to train due to intertwined dynamics (e.g., when poor initial policies send inconsistent
534   messages that misaligned with the agents’ behaviors), making it difficult for others to learn effec-
535
      tively from them. However, by utilizing ego-centric learning with semantic communications, our
      experiments show that HANSOME successfully learns during training and generalizes to multi-agent
536
      execution to some extent. We observe that multiple HANSOME agents interact and negotiate with
537
      other HANSOME agents or rule-based agents through semantic communications in mixed-agent
538   high-dimensional environments. While HANSOME demonstrates promising potential in multi-agent
539   tasks, future studies are needed to investigate the multi-agent RL training.

869   Customized CARLA Benchmarks. It is a common practice for WM-based online RL to customize
870
      CARLA scenarios to test their algorithms. SEM2 (Gao et al., 2022) and Iso-Dream (Pan et al., 2022)
      uses a task in Town03 to let the agent maximize rewards within 1000 steps and avoid collisions along
871
      the way. SEM2 uses 100 vehicles, and Iso-Dream uses 20 vehicles for training and 10 vehicles for
872
      testing. LILI (Xie et al., 2021) customizes a task where ego vehicle has to avoid an aggressive vehicle
873   when moving forward to verify its opponent modelling.
874
875
876
877   Comparison with Multi-Agent RL. Multi-agent RL (MARL) with communication has been
878
      extensively studied (Zhu et al., 2022), enabling RL agents to share past observations (Sukhbaatar
      et al., 2016), actions (Peng et al., 2017), or intentions (Kim et al., 2020). However, MARL often
879
      struggles with tasks such as autonomous driving, which take place in high-dimensional environments
880
      with complex dynamics. Additionally, MARL faces challenges due to the co-evolution of policies,
881   which causes non-stationarity and hinders effective learning when agents interact with one another.
882   To avoid these problems, we adopt ego-centric learning to enable policy learning with lightweight
883   communication in multi-agent systems. We discuss details of ego-centric learning in Appendix B.
884   Furthermore, our experiments demonstrate that, although HANSOME learns in an ego-centric manner,
885   it generalizes to multi-agent scenarios that include a mix of HANSOME agents and rule-based agents
886   to some extend.
887
888
889   Comparison with Large Language Models (LLMs) for Autonomous Driving. Recent studies
890   have explored the application of LLMs in autonomous driving (Yang et al., 2023), such as Driv-
891   eLM (Sima et al., 2023), DriveVLM (Tian et al., 2024), Dilu (Wen et al., 2023), GPT-Driver (Mao
892   et al., 2023), and DriveGPT4 (Xu et al., 2024). For instance, DriveLM and DriveVLM optimize
893   natural language processing metrics by comparing scene descriptions and analyses with ground-truth
894   annotations, such as driving captions or visual question answering, utilizing GPT-based models. Their
895   “hierarchical planning” involves generating action descriptions from text prompts and converting
896
      these descriptions into waypoint tokens. However, this waypoint tokenization relies on trajectory
      statistics from training data, and there is no actual control to execute the plan in closed-loop settings.
897
      In contrast, LMDrive (Shao et al., 2024) is a closed-loop approach, where the higher-level instruc-
898
      tions are provided as inputs for the vehicle to follow. PlanAgent (Zheng et al., 2024) introduces
899   a chain-of-thought module to understand scenes and plan routes with text prompts. HANSOME’s
900   models are significantly more lightweight, comprising approximately 30 million parameters, en-
901   hancing its practicality for real-time inference. HANSOME learns both higher-level and lower-level
902   policies from scratch within the WM’s imagination, and evaluate policies in a closed-loop manner.
903   Its higher-level policy generates semantic intentions without depending on prior knowledge from
904   LLMs or trajectory statistics in datasets. Furthermore, LLM-based approaches typically focus on
905   processing and understanding natural language inputs to reason and inform driving decisions, which
906   may not encompass the full spectrum of data required for autonomous driving. HANSOME, however,
907
      integrates image inputs directly into its learning and decision-making process without intermediate
      text prompts and outputs, enabling a more holistic understanding of the driving environment and
908
      enhancing its ability to make informed decisions.
909
910
      End-to-End (E2E) Autonomous Driving and Benchmarks. Autonomous driving has witnessed
911
      rapid growth recently thanks to the advacement of E2E approaches (Chen et al., 2023). Unlike
912   conventional approaches that employ a modular design and separate perception, prediction, planning
913   modules, E2E approaches aim at producing driving plans or actions directly from raw sensor data
914   inputs. Prior studies can be roughly categorized into two folds: imitation learning (IL) (Chen et al.,
915   2020; Prakash et al., 2020; Zhang & Cho, 2017; Shao et al., 2023; Chitta et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2018),
916   and reinforcement learning (RL) (Li et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Chekroun et al.,
917   2023; Toromanoff et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022) methods. Several open-loop benchmarks were
      developed to test E2E approaches, including CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017), nuScenes (Caesar


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      et al., 2020), Argoverse (Chang et al., 2019), Waymo (Schwall et al., 2020), and nuPlan (Caesar
919   et al., 2021). Recently, closed-loop benchmarks like CARLA have become more recommended for
920   research (Chen et al., 2023), as there is no strong evidence to suggest that good open-loop results
921   correlate with good closed-loop performance. Dreamer-style works (Gao et al., 2024; Li et al.,
922   2024; Pan et al., 2022), due to their interactive demands for online RL, often use CARLA as a
923   closed-loop benchmark. CARLA allows flexible control over environments and background traffic,
924   which is essential for evaluating HANSOME, as it requires multi-agent interactions and semantic
925   communications in complex, dense traffic scenarios.
926
927   Comparison with Hierarchical Planner in Embodied AI. Our related work discussion on hierar-
928   chical planning focuses on reinforcement learning, as it aligns with our approach. We want to further
929   discuss the advancements in embodied AI community that enables the agent to plan over language
930
      abstractions, typically through Large Language Models (LLMs). HiP (Ajay et al., 2024) uses LLMs
      to construct symbolic plans, and trains a visual model, an action model, jointly to solve long-horizon
931
      tasks. VLP (Du et al., 2023) uses vision-language models as both policies and value functions; a
932
      text-to-video model is trained to generate video plans that illustrate how to complete the final task.
933   Unlike these embodied AI works, HANSOME considers environments where heterogeneous agents
934   communicate for better planning. Moreover, HANSOME is a lightweight online RL framework that
935   does not rely on offline data or expert demonstrations. It has 30 million parameters, significantly
936   fewer than large vision or language models in embodied AI research, which can be critical to fulfill
937   low-latency demands of AVs.
938
939   An Example of Multi-Objective Optimization View for HRL In principle, the training of
940   hierarchical planning can be viewed as a multi-objective optimization problem, with two objectives
941   being to maximize the reward of the higher-level policy and the lower-level policy, and there are trade-
942   offs between the two objectives in general. For instance, the higher-level policy may plan sophisticated
943   routes involving frequent lane changes and overtaking maneuvers to reach the destination faster,
944
      without considering whether the lower-level policy can realistically execute such complex maneuvers,
      leading to poor lower-level performance. On the other extreme, a poor higher-level policy may adhere
945
      to simplistic plans like a straight-line path, so that the lower-level policy can achieve nearly perfect
946
      performance in following just a straight line.

949   B    E GO - CENTRIC L EARNING
950
951   A challenge to address for HANSOME is the source of shared intentions during training. A straight-
952   forward approach is to spawn multiple agents in the environment, each independently controlled by
953   the hierarchical policy, and allow them to communicate with each other. However, the main drawback
954
      of this approach is that these agents will initially not follow their generated intentions due to the
      lack of a good lower-level policy. Since the WM needs to predict the trajectories of background
955
      vehicles based on their shared intentions, any misalignment between the agents’ behavior and their
956
      intentions will mislead the WM in understanding these intentions. Only when the lower-level policy
957   is sufficiently good at following intentions can the WM begin to learn the correct interpretation of
958   each intention, enabling the agent to use this information for better planning.
959
960
      To mitigate this issue and accelerate the training process, we use a distributed learning method to train
      HANSOME. In particular, the background vehicles that the agent interacts with are controlled by
961
      CARLA’s autopilot (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017), a rule-based autonomous driving algorithm. Although
962
      not perfect, the autopilot can primarily follow a randomly generated route. Their intentions and
963   planned routes can be extracted from CARLA’s traffic manager and shared with our agents. In this
964   way, the agent can simultaneously learn to follow the higher-level intentions it generates and utilize
965   the shared intentions from other vehicles for better planning. Despite being trained in a distributed
966   manner, our experiments demonstrate that the agents generalize well in multi-agent environments
967   (see Figure 6).

970   et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2021) train and evaluate the ego agent in environments where the background
971   vehicles are controlled by rule-based CARLA autopilots that have privileged information to CARLA
      environments.

1172   AdaSMO Warm-Up We introduce the concept of warm-up, through which the lower-level policy
1173
       is prioritized during training while the higher-level policy remains random exploration. It is critical
       to specify a proper timing to terminate the warm-up. We heuristically use the extrinsic reward to
1174
       threshold the warm-up.
1175
1176   Specifically, we experimented with thresholds 80 and 100 to terminate the warm-up at 30K (red
1177   curve) and 70K (blue curve) steps, respectively. We notice that adjusting the warm-up termination
1178   timing has a significant influence on AdaSMO training speed. During warm-up, the higher-level
1179
       policy keeps a high degree of exploration. This enables lower-level policy being trained to follow
       instructions from higher-level. If the warm-up is terminated too early, the lower-level policy has not
1180
       been well trained for lane-following. Thus, the higher-level policy, whose training largely depends on
1181
       lower-level policy performance, cannot improve immediately. The significant delay after warm-up
1182   termination and before reward curve rises up can be observed on red curve in Figure 12. The warm-up
1183   terminates at 30K steps while the reward grows only after 150K steps. In the contrast, a proper
1184   warm-up termination timing results in well trained lower-level policy. The higher-level policy can be
1185   trained based on a stable lower-level lane-following policy and improved rapidly, as the rapid growth
1186   at 70K steps shown on the blue curve. However, in both situations, the reward converges to the same
1187   level, which means our method is robust to the hyper-parameters, while a good set of parameters can
       significantly speed up training.

       D.2    ACTION S PACE
1227
       We include the action space settings in Table 6. Here is a detailed explanation of the action space
1228
       design.
1229
1230
       Intentions. In CARLA map topology, there are six driving commands: three for movement on lanes
1231
       (follow lanes, change to left lane, or change to right lane) and three for movements at intersections
1232   (go straight, turn left, or turn right). HANSOME uses a set of driving primitives in three directions as
1233   the higher-level intention space I = Straight, Lef t, Right. These intentions cover possible driving
1234   behaviors on both lanes and intersections. This intuitive setting also aligns with how human drivers
1235   use turn signals with three states (left . It is also a common practice in the AV community; for example,
1236   VAD (Jiang et al., 2023) uses going straight, left, or right as the 3-dimensional high-level action
1237   space. The high-level action of VAD is obtained through pre-determined routes, while HANSOME
1238   can generate intentions on its own.
1239
1240   Vehicle Controls. The vehicle controls given by the lower-level policy use a 5 × 3 dimensional
1241   one-hot encoding, where there are 5 discrete steering values and 3 acceleration values. This effectively
       reduces the search space while allowing the agent to perform various tasks.

1255   D.3    C OMMUNICATION S ETTINGS
1256
1257   Given our action space discussed in Appendix D.2, the intention messages are formatted in one-
1258   hot encoding for each sender. When multiple agents send messages, the message space can grow
1259   exponentially with the number of agents communicating with the ego vehicle. Therefore, it is
1260   crucial to introduce a strategy to communicate with the most relevant agents. Determining ”whom to
1261   communicate with” in multi-agent environments is a highly non-trivial problem (Zhu et al., 2022). A
1262
       common approach is to select nearby agents (Yun et al., 2021). In our work, we adopt this strategy by
       selecting the three nearest vehicles for all tasks. This approach is intuitive in human driving scenarios
1263
       and performs reasonably well in our experiments. Investigating more complex communication
1264
       protocols can be explored in future research. For different higher-level intentions, the ego agent may
1265   be interested in agents from different directions. For example, when the intention is to change lanes,
1266   the ego vehicle primarily focuses on the vehicle ahead or the nearest vehicles in neighboring lanes.
