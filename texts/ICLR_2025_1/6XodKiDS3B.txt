009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Sequential learning in deep models often suffers from challenges such as catas-
012            trophic forgetting and loss of plasticity, largely due to the permutation dependence
013            of gradient-based algorithms, where the order of training data impacts the learning
014            outcome. In this work, we introduce a novel approximately permutation-invariant
015            learning framework based on high-dimensional particle filters. We theoretically
016            demonstrate that particle filters are invariant to the sequential ordering of train-
017            ing minibatches or tasks, offering a principled solution to mitigate catastrophic
018
               forgetting and loss-of-plasticity. We develop an efficient particle filter for opti-
               mizing high-dimensional models, combining the strengths of Bayesian methods
019
               with gradient-based optimization. Through extensive experiments on continual
020
               supervised and reinforcement learning benchmarks, including SplitMNIST, SplitCI-
021            FAR100, and ProcGen, we empirically show that our method consistently improves
022            performance, while reducing variance compared to standard baselines.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   What is the optimal order for training data? This question is fundamental to understanding how
028   the sequencing of training data impacts machine learning model performance. In sequential learning
029   settings, such as continual learning and lifelong learning, the sequencing of training data plays a
030   crucial role in determining model performance. When models are trained on ordered minibatches of
031   data, poor ordering—referred to as ”poor permutations”—can result in catastrophic forgetting and
032
      loss of plasticity (Wang et al., 2024; Abel et al., 2023).
033   In continual learning, models process tasks in a specific sequence. Unlike conventional training,
034   where minibatch data is randomized, continual learning often relies on a strict sequence, making
035   models prone to overfitting on newer tasks while losing performance on older tasks. This is known
036   as catastrophic forgetting, where new information erases prior knowledge, severely degrading
037   performance on earlier tasks (Kim & Han, 2023; van de Ven et al., 2022).
038   Similarly, in lifelong reinforcement learning (LRL), agents must adapt to new tasks sequentially. The
039   order in which these tasks are presented can lead to loss of plasticity, limiting the agent’s ability to
040   adapt to new environments (Muppidi et al., 2024; Lyle et al., 2022; Abbas et al., 2023; Sokar et al.,
041   2023). This poor ordering can further manifest as negative transfer or primacy bias, where learning
042   earlier tasks biases the agent towards those tasks, impeding adaptation to new tasks (Nikishin et al.,
043   2022; Ahn et al., 2024).
044   To address these challenges, we propose a shift in perspective, viewing the problem through the lens
045   of permutation invariance. By developing learning algorithms that are invariant to the order of data
046   presentation, we can mitigate catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity. Our key insight is the use
047   of particle filters, a probabilistic tool widely used in state estimation, to achieve this goal.
048   Particle filters excel at dynamically estimating system states from noisy data and are grounded in
049   Bayesian inference (Thrun et al., 2005; Doucet et al., 2001b; Jonschkowski et al., 2018; Karkus et al.,
050   2021; Corenflos et al., 2021; Pulido & van Leeuwen, 2019; Maken et al., 2022; Boopathy et al., 2024).
051   However, their application to modern machine learning has been limited due to scalability issues
052   in high-dimensional settings. In contrast, gradient-based optimization techniques such as gradient
053   descent efficiently handle high-dimensional spaces but lack the probabilistic framework offered by
      particle filters.

      In this work, we bridge this gap by proposing a novel particle filter designed specifically for high-
055   dimensional learning. We show that by adapting particle filters to high-dimensional learning problems,
056   we can achieve more robust permutation-invariant learning. Our approach provides a new perspective
057   on training in sequential settings and also addresses the core challenges of catastrophic forgetting and
058   loss of plasticity in a principled manner.

078   2.1   PARTICLE F ILTERS
079
      Particle filters, or sequential Monte Carlo methods, are widely used for state estimation in non-
080
      linear and non-Gaussian settings. They represent probability distributions through a set of samples
081   (particles), providing flexibility in capturing complex dynamics (Doucet et al., 2001a). In fields such
082   as robotics, particle filters have been applied successfully to localization and mapping problems,
083   where they handle uncertainty and non-linearities effectively (Thrun, 2002). However, a key limitation
084   is their scalability: as the dimensionality of the problem increases, the number of particles needed
085   grows exponentially, making them less practical in high-dimensional spaces like those in machine
086   learning (Bengtsson et al., 2008). Recent efforts have focused on improving particle filter scalability
087   through adaptive resampling and dimensionality reduction techniques (Li et al., 2015), but these
088   approaches have not fully bridged the gap for large-scale machine learning applications. Our work
089
      addresses this gap by proposing a high-dimensional particle filter that is computationally efficient
      and well-suited for machine learning tasks.
090
091
092   2.2   BAYESIAN M ODEL AVERAGING
093
      Bayesian model averaging (BMA) is a powerful technique for integrating uncertainty into model
094
      predictions by averaging across multiple models (Hoeting et al., 1999; Wasserman, 2000). By
095
      weighting model predictions based on their posterior probabilities, BMA can provide more robust
096   predictions and better capture model uncertainty compared to single-model approaches (Raftery
097   et al., 2005). In modern machine learning, BMA has been employed to enhance performance and
098   uncertainty estimation, notably in ensemble techniques (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Wortsman
099   et al., 2022). In particular, some works consider ensembling in the context of continual learning
100   (Rypeść et al., 2024; Carta et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2020): they notice that ensembled models achieve
101   better performance and stability in continual learning. Nevertheless, the ensembling approach of these
102   methods lacks a Bayesian formalism, thereby losing both theoretical guarantees and applicability to
103   permutation-invariant learning. Overall, BMA has not been extensively explored in the context of
104
      continual or permutation-invariant learning, where uncertainty over tasks and sequential data plays a
      crucial role.
105
106   In this work, we demonstrate the benefits of particle filters in high-dimensional machine learning
107   settings. We then describe a particular particle filter that functions as a BMA technique, and
      demonstrate its advantages empirically.

183

188

189
      tend to be highly dependent on the ordering of data points. Permutation-invariance enables learning
190
      algorithms with less stochastic outputs: in a perfectly permutation-invariant particle filter, the only
191   potential sources of randomness are the initial selection of particles and the randomness in the particle
192   filter updates themselves.

194   3.3   AVOIDING CATASTROPHIC FORGETTING AND LOSS OF PLASTICITY
195
196   Now, we demonstrate that particle filters naturally avoid catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity.

362   4    E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
363
364   In this section, we empirically validate the permutation-invariance of our gradient-based weighted
365   particle filter (hereafter referred to as the weighted particle filter or WPF) and demonstrate its
366   effectiveness in mitigating catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity across continual and lifelong
367   learning benchmarks.
368
369   Continual Learning Experiments: We evaluate our weighted particle filter on continual learning
370   benchmarks SplitMNIST (LeCun & Cortes, 2010), SplitCIFAR100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), and ProcGen
371   (Cobbe et al., 2020). SplitMNIST is divided into 5 ”super class” splits, and SplitCIFAR100 into
372   20, both for class-incremental learning. For ProcGen, we use image-action trajectory datasets from
373   the games Starpilot, Fruitbot, and Dodgeball, partitioned into 15 levels sampled using the hard
374

439
      Performance Against Other Filters: Tables 1 and 2 provide a summary of the performance
440
      comparison between our weighted particle filter and the baseline particle filters in both continual
441
      learning and lifelong RL experiments. Our weighted particle filter consistently achieves higher mean
442   accuracy (averaged over classes and permutations) on SplitMNIST, SplitCIFAR100, and ProcGen
443   Behavior Cloning datasets compared to the baseline particle filter, averaging particles, and traditional
444   gradient descent (single particle).
445
      Furthermore, Figure 3 demonstrates that our weighted particle filter is more resistant to loss of
446
      plasticity in LRL experiments compared to PPO using gradient descent. This underscores the
447
      advantage of incorporating particle weighting into the training process. This effect may align with
448   the conclusions of Lyle et al. (2023); Sokar et al. (2023); Muppidi et al. (2024); Kumar et al. (2023),
449   suggesting that when a model is not overly specialized to a specific task is better able to adapt to new
450   tasks. In our approach, maintaining multiple particles—some tuned to domain-specific tasks and
451   others oriented towards different sequential tasks—enables the agent to switch to well-performing
452   particles when adapting to new environments, thereby preserving plasticity. While theoretically, the
453   baseline particle filter has the advantages of a Bayesian approach, because of the lack of gradient-
454   based optimization, it fails. This lack of gradient-based optimization in high dimensions means it is
455   essentially making random guesses, leading to performances that are close to what would be expected
456
      by chance. While gradient descent might show improved results in the latest epoch, it typically does
      so at the expense of previous epochs’ performance. Therefore, when the performance is averaged
457
      across all epochs, the result is diminished, approaching close to random chance levels.
458

461   results presented in Table 1 indicate that our weighted particle filter not only successfully avoids
462   catastrophic forgetting but also outperforms all other methods in the SplitMNIST setting. In the

      SplitCIFAR100 dataset, our model closely competes with the top-performing continual learning
      model, LWF.

468   across all games. Similar to the continual learning experiments, we observe that combining the
469   weighted particle filter with PPO + EWC or PPO + TRAC results in an increase in average normalized
470   return, demonstrating the effectiveness of these combined approaches.

510   Permutation Invariance A distinctive feature of our gradient-based weighted particle filter is its
511   approximate permutation invariance. To validate this property, we evaluated the average normalized
512   variances over classes or levels across 10 permutation runs for each experiment and each method in
513   both the continual learning and lifelong reinforcement learning setups. Each run involved training
514   on a different order of class datasets for SplitMNIST and SplitCIFAR100, or on a different order of
515   levels for the ProcGen games.
516   Tables 1 and 2 show that, in all experiments, our Weighted Particle Filter exhibited lower variance
517   compared to gradient descent. Additionally, when comparing continual learning or lifelong RL
518   methods with and without the particle filter, we observe that the Weighted Particle Filter consistently
519   increased performance/return and reduced variance. Figure 2 effectively illustrates this relationship
520   in the SplitMNIST and SplitCIFAR100 experiments. The bottom right region of each plot represents
521   the ideal scenario of high accuracy and low task variance. It is evident from both plots that this
522
      optimal region is dominated by either the Weighted Particle Filter alone or continual learning methods
      combined with the Weighted Particle Filter, demonstrating the advantages of our approach.

525   5      D ISCUSSION
526
527
      Poor permutations of training data, such as strictly ordered minibatches, can lead to catastrophic
528   forgetting and loss of plasticity. To overcome this challenge, we theoretically demonstrated that
529   particle filters can be permutation-invariant, allowing them to mitigate the issues associated with
530   poor ordering of training data. This permutation invariance offers a principled solution to avoiding
531   catastrophic forgetting and preserving plasticity throughout learning.
532
      We proposed a simple, gradient-based weighted particle filter that was effective in continual, lifelong,
533
      and permutation-invariant learning. While effective, our particle filter requires memory scaling with
534   the number of particles, which can be computationally costly. Moreover, our particle filter alone
535   is often not as effective as in combination with other approaches, limiting its use as a stand-alone
536   algorithm. Nevertheless, our approach has a number of advantages: it is highly parallelizable,
537   requiring minimal interaction between particles. It is also domain-agnostic: our approach resists
538   catastrophic forgetting and loss of plasticity in both lifelong reinforcement learning and supervised
539   continual learning settings. Finally, it is easily combined with existing algorithms. These advantages
      suggest that particle filter methods may offer many other benefits to modern machine learning.

      Lifelong RL Setup: For the lifelong reinforcement learning setup, we followed the same experi-
870
      mental protocol as Muppidi et al. (2024). In the ProcGen experiments, individual game levels were
871   generated using a seed value as the start level parameter, which was incremented sequentially to
872   create new levels. Every 2 million steps, a new level was introduced to the agent using the hard
873   distribution mode. To assess permutation invariance, the sequence of start-level seeds was permuted
874   10 times, providing a diverse set of training orders for evaluation.

876   Model details: Our Gradient-based particle filter uses 100 particles. Particles are initialized
877   randomly from PyTorch’s nn.module network parameters. A small amount of noise is injected into
878   these parameters in the beginning of training to increase exploration of the solution space. Our
879   Gradient-descent implementation uses the same code, except we initialize the particle filter with only
880   one particle. The averaging particle filter simply takes the average of the accuracies of all of the
881
      particles. The baseline particle filter does the following:
882
            1. Resamples particles from the existing pool with probabilities proportional to the exponential
883            of the negative loss associated with each particle. .
884
            2. Applies perturbations to particles, enabling the exploration of the solution space.
885
886         3. Updates the weights of the particles based on the new loss.

889
      as prescribed in the (van de Ven et al., 2022) code implementation. These three models used a
