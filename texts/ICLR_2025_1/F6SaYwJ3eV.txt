009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Posterior sampling in high-dimensional spaces using generative models holds
012            significant promise for various applications, including but not limited to inverse
013            problems and guided generation tasks. Despite many recent developments, gener-
014            ating diverse posterior samples remains a challenge, as existing methods require
015            restarting the entire generative process for each new sample, making the procedure
016            computationally expensive. In this work, we propose efficient posterior sampling
017            by simulating Langevin dynamics in the noise space of a pre-trained generative
018
               model. By exploiting the mapping between the noise and data spaces which can be
               provided by distilled flows or consistency models, our method enables seamless
019
               exploration of the posterior without the need to re-run the full sampling chain,
020
               drastically reducing computational overhead. Theoretically, we prove a guarantee
021            for the proposed noise-space Langevin dynamics to approximate the posterior,
022            assuming that the generative model sufficiently approximates the prior distribution.
023            Our framework is experimentally validated on image restoration tasks involving
024            noisy linear and nonlinear forward operators applied to LSUN-Bedroom (256 x
025            256) and ImageNet (64 x 64) datasets. The results demonstrate that our approach
026            generates high-fidelity samples with enhanced semantic diversity even under a lim-
027            ited number of function evaluations, offering superior efficiency and performance
028            compared to existing diffusion-based posterior sampling techniques.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Generative models that approximate complex data priors have been leveraged for a range of guided
      generation tasks in recent years (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Chung et al., 2023). Early works focused
034
      on conditional synthesis using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014;
035
      Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Brock et al., 2019; Karras et al., 2019; 2020). However, diffusion models
036   have recently surpassed GANs as the state of the art in generative modeling (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
037   2021a), demonstrating superior performance in guided generation tasks (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
038   Choi et al., 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2021). Posterior sampling, as a guided generation framework, has
039   garnered significant interest (Kawar et al., 2021; 2022; Chung et al., 2023), particularly for providing
040   candidate solutions to noisy inverse problems.
041
      Solving noisy inverse problems involves reconstructing an unknown signal x from noisy measure-
042   ments y, where the forward model is characterized by the measurement likelihood p(y | x). The
043   objective is to sample from the posterior distribution p(x | y) ∝ p(y | x)p(x). Such posteriors are of-
044   ten intractable in practical applications due to the complexity of the prior distribution p(x). However,
045   learned generative models that approximate complex data priors can enable approximate sampling
046   from the posterior p(x | y). Early approaches for solving inverse problems using diffusion models to
047   approximate p(x | y) relied on problem-specific architectures (Saharia et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022;
048   Lugmayr et al., 2022) and required training dedicated generative models for each task (Saharia et al.,
049   2022a; Shi et al., 2022). In contrast, methods that utilize pre-trained diffusion models as priors for
050
      posterior sampling offer greater flexibility and are training-free (Kawar et al., 2021; 2022; Chung
      et al., 2022a;b; Wang et al., 2023), with recent extensions targeting nonlinear inverse problems (Chung
051
      et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023a;b; He et al., 2024).
052

      they yield a point estimate or multiple estimates. Existing approaches for posterior sampling focus

      2   BACKGROUND
120   Diffusion models. Sampling from diffusion models (DMs) is accomplished via simulation of the
121   reverse process corresponding to the forward-time, noising stochastic differential equation (SDE)
122   dxt = µ(xt , t)dt + β(t)dWt (Song et al., 2021b), where Wt is the standard Brownian motion in Rd
123   and t ∈ [0, 1]. Initialized with data from a data-generating distribution pdata , diffusion is typically
124   parameterized such that the terminal distribution of the forward-time SDE is a tractable Gaussian
125   distribution γ. This SDE shares marginal densities pt with the probability flow- (PF-)ODE:
126
                                                       1    2
127                                dxt = µ(xt , t) − β(t) ∇ log pt (xt ) dt.                               (1)
                                                       2
128
129   Score-based generative models are a class of DM which approximate ∇ log pt (xt ) with a neural
130   network score model. Given such a model, (1) can be solved in reverse time using numerical ODE
131   integration techniques (Song et al., 2021a; Karras et al., 2022).
132   Deterministic diffusion solvers. In contrast to stochastic DM samplers based on Markov chains (Ho
133   et al., 2020) and SDEs (Song et al., 2021b), deterministic DM solvers primarily focus on simulating
134   the PF-ODE (1). Song et al. (2021a) presented DDIM, an implicit modeling technique yielding a
135   deterministic mapping between noise and data samples. Subsequent works considered alternate,
136   higher-order solvers for the PF-ODE (Karras et al., 2022), yielding high-quality samples in fewer
137   function evaluations.
138   Flow models. Continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) represent another class of ODE-based genera-
139   tive models, using neural networks to approximate the dynamics of a continuous mapping between
140   noise and data (Chen et al., 2018). Recent extensions focus on learning more direct trajectories (Liu
141   et al., 2023b) and simulation-free training (Lipman et al., 2023). As with deterministic diffusion
142
      solvers corresponding to the PF-ODE, sampling via these methods requires numerical simulation of
      an ODE whose dynamics are defined by the neural network model.
143
144   Consistency models. Efficient ODE simulation is of particular interest for efficient sampling from
145   DMs (Song et al., 2021a; Karras et al., 2022) and CNFs (Lipman et al., 2023). However, fast
146
      numerical ODE solvers still require tens of steps to produce high-fidelity samples (Lu et al., 2022;
      Dockhorn et al., 2022). As a result, score model distillation techniques have arisen to yield fast,
147
      effective samplers from the PF-ODE. Consistency models (CMs) are a prominent class of distilled
148
      DMs that enable single- and few-step sampling (Song et al., 2023c). CMs learn a mapping fθ
149   (parameterized by θ) between a point xt along the PF-ODE trajectory to the initial state:
150
151                                        x0 = fθ (xt , t) for t ∈ [0, 1],                               (2)
152
      where x0 is a sample from pdata . Therefore, single-step sampling can be achieved by sampling
153   x1 ∼ γ and evaluating the CM at x1 . Multi-step sampling can be achieved by alternating denoising
154   (via evaluation of the CM) and partial noising, trading off efficiency for fidelity.

      mapping Φ.

      Choice of initial value and warm-start. A natural choice for the initial noise space value z 0
295
      can be a generic sample z 0 ∼ γ (the noise space prior). However, while this will correspond to a
296   high-likelihood sample according to the data prior (given a well-trained generative model), it may
297   be far from the data posterior. As such, one may warm-start the sampler by optimizing Ly (x0 )
298   with respect to x1 using standard optimization techniques such as gradient descent or Adam. In all
299   experiments, we warm start sampling using K steps of Adam optimization, initializing EM sampling
300   with z 0 being the optimization output. See Appendix B.1 for further detail.
301   Computational requirements. The main computational burden is with respect to the computation
302   of the loss gradient ∇x1 Ly (x0 ), which requires differentiating through the mapping Φ. This can
303   be alleviated by choosing a Φ which consists of a small number of function evaluations (NFEs).
304   Additional computational burden is due to burn-in/warm start to yield z 0 , the initial value of EM
305   simulation. Therefore, the total NFEs to simulate N steps of EM (i.e., to yield N samples) is
306
      η · (K + N ), where η is the NFEs required to evaluate Φ. However, this burden is amortized over EM
      sampling, as progressive EM simulation yields increasingly fewer overall NFEs per sample, which
307
      asymptotically approaches η (the NFEs required to compute Φ). Therefore, we represent Φ using
308
      CM sampling, which can be accomplished for η = 1 or 2. While multi-step (η > 1) CM sampling
309   is typically stochastic (Song et al., 2023c), we fix the noise in each step to result in a deterministic
310   mapping. See Appendix B.1 for details.
311
      Role of EM step size τ . The step size of EM, τ , controls the time scales over which the Langevin
312   dynamics are simulated with respect to the number of EM steps. Larger τ results in more rapid
313   exploration of the posterior, potentially leading to more diverse samples over shorter timescales.
314   However, τ must also be kept small enough to ensure the stability of EM sampling. Therefore, this
315   hyper-parameter provides a degree of control over the diversity of samples provided by the proposed
316   algorithm. Choosing large τ while maintaining stability can yield diverse samples, potentially
317   revealing particularly uncertain semantic features within the posterior.

495   7      R ELATED WORKS
496
      Posterior sampling with generative models. Diffusion-based inverse problem solvers consist of
497
      task-specific frameworks (Saharia et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022; Lugmayr et al., 2022), optimized
498
      approaches (Saharia et al., 2022a; Shi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a), and training-free techniques
499   leveraging pre-trained diffusion priors (Kawar et al., 2021; 2022; Chung et al., 2022a;b; Wang et al.,
500   2023; Chung et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023a;b; He et al., 2024; Dou & Song, 2024). Early training-free
501   methods for solving inverse problems utilize measurement-space projection (Song et al., 2021a; Choi
502   et al., 2021), while others addressed noisy problems via consistency in the spectral domain (Kawar
503   et al., 2021; 2022; Wang et al., 2023) or using manifold constraints (Chung et al., 2022b; He et al.,
504   2024). Recent works consider general noisy and nonlinear inverse problems using an approximation
505   of the measurement likelihood in each generation step (Chung et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023a;b).
506   An emerging area of interest focuses on developing diffusion posterior sampling techniques with
507
      provable guarantees (Xu & Chi, 2024; Bruna & Han, 2024). For instance, Xu & Chi (2024) develop
      an alternating measurement projection/guided diffusion approach for which they provide asymptotic
508
      convergence guarantees, while Bruna & Han (2024) utilize tilted transport in linear inverse problems
509
      which provably samples the posterior under certain conditions. Diffusion-base posterior sampling
510   works can also be adapted to flow-based models, e.g., Pokle et al. (2023) adapt ΠGDM (Song et al.,
511   2023a) to CNFs. These existing works modify the sampling trajectory of generative priors, requiring
512   repeated simulation of the entire sampling process to produce multiple posterior samples, hindering
513   scalability to many samples. The proposed sampling in the noise space of one- or few-step mappings
514   enables the efficient generation of many posterior samples.
515   Guided generation via noise space iteration. For generative models that provide deterministic
516   mappings between a latent noise space and data, such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), flows (Chen
517   et al., 2018), and CMs (Song et al., 2023c), optimization of noise can guide generation towards
518   conditional information (Bojanowski et al., 2018; Galatolo. et al., 2021; Patashnik et al., 2021; Asim
519   et al., 2020; Whang et al., 2021; Ben-Hamu et al., 2024). In the GAN literature, this is primarily
520   addressed using text-to-image guided synthesis (Galatolo. et al., 2021; Patashnik et al., 2021) or
521
      task-specific objectives (Bojanowski et al., 2018). This type of approach has also been used to solve
      inverse problems using flow-based models (Asim et al., 2020; Whang et al., 2021); for instance,
522
      D-Flow (Ben-Hamu et al., 2024) optimizes with respect to the noise input to CNFs. Our method
523
      also iterates in the noise space, simulating Langevin dynamics for posterior sampling instead of
524   optimizing to yield a point estimate. Computing gradients through CNFs is expensive (Chen et al.,
525   2018), requiring at least tens of function evaluation per ODE solution (Lu et al., 2022; Dockhorn
526   et al., 2022). The use of CMs in our approach facilitates computation of the gradient in as few as
527   one call to the neural network, enabling the progressive accumulation of posterior samples during
528   Langevin dynamics simulation.
529
530
      8      D ISCUSSION
531
532
      We have outlined a novel approach for posterior sampling via Langevin dynamics in the noise space
533
      of a generative model. Using a CM mapping from noise to data, our posterior sampling provides
534   solutions to general noisy image inverse problems, demonstrating superior reconstruction fidelity
535   to other CM methods and competitiveness with diffusion baselines. A primary limitation of our
536   approach is the low visual quality in some posterior samples. Fidelity drawbacks can be attributed to
537   a relatively poor approximation of the prior by CMs. Future work will focus on improving fidelity
538   of diverse samples, perhaps by using more accurate prior models and adaptive simulation of the
539   SDE. Regardless, our method produces highly diverse samples, representing meaningful semantic
      uncertainty of data features within the posterior.

747   Consistency model generative process. To represent the map Φ from noise space to data space,
748   we utilize the pre-trained CMs of Song et al. (2023c) with a 1- or 2-step sampler. For the 2-step
749   sampler, we use standard multistep consistency sampling (Algorithm 1, Song et al. (2023c)), i.e.,
750                                                       p
751                                x0 = fθ fθ (xT , T ) + t2 − ϵ2 z, t ,
752
753   where fθ is the pre-trained CM, xT ← x1 , T = 80, ϵ = 2 × 10−3 is a small noise offset , and t is an
754   intermediate "time step" along the PF-ODE trajectory (the "halfway" point). In Song et al. (2023c), z
755   is sampled from the standard Gaussian for each call to Φ. In this work, we sample z once and fix it
      for all future calls to Φ, which we observe to empirically improve performance.

          Warm-start initialization and sampling. The posterior sampling process begins with a warm-start
757       initialization consisting of K steps of Adam optimization with learning rate, β1 , and β2 for each
758       experiment outlined in Tables A.1, A.2, and A.3. This is followed by N steps of Langevin dynamics
759       simulation (via EM discretization in the main-text experiments) using step size τ . The NFEs per
760       sample can be computed as η(K + N )/N , where η is the number of steps used for CM generation.
761       All experiments are implemented in PyTorch and are run on a system with NVIDIA A100 GPUs.

799       B.2        D ETAILS OF THE BASELINES
800
          The baseline methods conduct t = 1, . . . , N Euler steps for sampling. All methods require a
801
          denoiser to provide x0 ≈ x̂0 (xt ) at each sampling step t, which is achieved using either a pre-trained
802       EDM (Karras et al., 2022) or CM (Song et al., 2023c), both obtained from Song et al. (2023c) for
803       each dataset.
804
805       Diffusion Posterior Sampling (DPS). DPS (Chung et al., 2023) utilizes the denoiser corresponding
806       to a pre-trained DM to approximate the measurement likelihood gradient at each step of DM sampling.
807       At each state xt along the diffusion sampling trajectory, a score-base diffusion model can provide a
808       predicted x̂0 (xt ), which can be used to compute ∇xt p(y|x̂0 ) via differentiation through the score-
809       based model. In DPS, each step of diffusion sampling is adjusted by this gradient with weight ζ, i.e.,
          xt−1 ← xt−1 − ζ∇xt p(y|x̂0 ).


                                                                                                    15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Manifold Preserving Guided Diffusion (MPGD). MPGD (He et al., 2023) computes the gradient
811   of the measurement likelihood in the denoised space rather than with respect to xt at each step,
812   taking a gradient step in x̂0 before updating the diffusion iterate. That is, MPGD conducts the update
813   x̂0 ← x̂0 (xt ) − ζ∇x̂0 p(y|x̂0 (xt )), which can then be use to yield xt−1 at each step. MPGD also
814   provides an optional manifold projection step which utilizes pre-trained autoencoders to ensure x̂0
815   remains on the data manifold. For a fair comparison, we only consider MPGD without manifold
816   projection in this work.
817
818   Loss Guided Diffusion (LGD). LGD (Song et al., 2023b) aims to improve the approximation
819   of p(y|x0 ) at each step along the sampling trajectory via a Monte Carlo approach. Viewing
820   p(y|x̂0 ) in DPS as a delta distribution approximation of p(y|x0 ) about x̂0 , LGD instead com-
                                                   (m)
821   putes the log-mean-exponential of p(y|x̂0 ) for p     m = 1, . . . , M perturbed copies of x̂0 . That

                          0P t t    r   I), where r  = β
                                                   t    t /   1 + βt2 . The weighted (by ζ) Monte Carlo

826   B.3   D EGRADATIONS AND FORWARD OPERATORS
827
828
      In all experiments, pixel values are scaled from [-1, 1] (as in Song et al. (2023c)) before application of
      forward operators. The details of the measurement likelihoods corresponding to each forward operator
829
      are outlined below. All methods use σ = 0.1, except for phase retrieval, which uses σ = 0.05.
830
831
      Super-resolution. The super-resolution task is defined by the following measurement likelihood:
832
833                                       y ∼ N (y|AvgPoolf (x), σ 2 I),
834
835   where AvgPool represents 2D average pooling by a factor f .
836
837   Gaussian deblur. Gaussian blur is defined by a block Hankel matrix C ψ representing convolution
838   of x with kernel ψ:
839                                       y ∼ N (y|C ψ x, σ 2 I).
840   We consider a 61 x 61 Gaussian kernel with standard deviation of 3.0, as in Chung et al. (2023).
841
842
      Inpainting. The measurement likelihood corresponding to p% inpainting is a function of a mask P
843   with (1-p)% uniformly random 0 values:
844
845                                             y ∼ N (y|P x, σ 2 I).
846
847   Nonlinear deblur. Following Chung et al. (2023), the forward nonlinear blur operator is a pre-
848   trained neural network Fϕ to approximate the integration of non-blurry images over a short time
849   frame given a single sharp image (Tran et al., 2021). Therefore, the measurement likelihood is as
850   follows:
851                                       y ∼ N (y|Fϕ (x), σ 2 I).
852
853   Phase retrieval. The forward operator of the phase retrieval task takes the absolute value of the
854   2D Discrete Fourier Transform F applied to x: |F x|. However, since this task is known to be
855   highly ill-posed (Hayes, 1982; Chung et al., 2023), an oversampling matrix P is also applied (with
856   oversampling ratio 1 in this work):
857                                           y ∼ N (y||F P x|, σ 2 I).
858
859
      High dynamic range reconstruction. In the HDR forward model, pixel values are scaled by a
860
      factor of 2 before truncation back to the range [-1, 1]. Therefore, the measurement likelihood is as
861   follows:
862
                                        y ∼ N (y|clip(2x, −1, 1), σ 2 I),
863
      where clip(·, −1, 1) truncates all input values to the range [-1, 1].
