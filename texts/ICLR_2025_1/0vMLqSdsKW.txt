009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As recommender systems become widely deployed in different domains, they
012            increasingly influence their users’ beliefs and preferences. Auditing recommender
013            systems is crucial as it not only ensures the improvement of recommendation algo-
014            rithms but also provides ways to assess and address ethical concerns surrounding
015            them. In this work, we view recommender system auditing from a causal lens and
016            provide a general recipe for defining auditing metrics. Under this general causal
017            auditing framework, we categorize existing auditing metrics and identify gaps
018
               in them—notably, the lack of metrics for auditing user agency while accounting
               for the multi-step dynamics of the recommendation process. We leverage our
019
               framework and propose two classes of such metrics: future- and past-reachability
020
               and stability, that measure the ability of a user to influence their own and other
021            users’ recommendations, respectively. We provide both a gradient-based and a
022            black-box approach for computing these metrics, allowing the auditor to compute
023            them under different levels of access to the recommender system. Empirically,
024            we demonstrate the efficacy of methods for computing the proposed metrics and
025            inspect the design of recommender systems through these proposed metrics.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recommender systems actively shape people’s online experiences by determining which information
031   (e.g., social media posts and job postings) is most visible to them. These socio-technical systems
032
      have the potential to influence individuals’ choices and beliefs as well as public opinion at large
      which can have tangible ethical impact (Milano et al., 2020a; Burki, 2019; Rafailidis & Nanopoulos,
033
      2016). Monitoring recommender systems for potential harmful effects is a difficult task, requiring
034
      careful design of evaluation metrics and auditing frameworks.
035
036   Traditional metrics for evaluating recommender systems are often correlational in nature, focusing
037   on the system’s ability to recommend content perceived as relevant by the users based on their past
038
      behavior. However, these metrics tend to overlook critical ethical concerns, particularly how these
      systems interact with and shape user beliefs and preferences over time, raising concerns about user
039
      agency and the extent to which users control their own online experiences.
040
041   Metrics for measuring user agency are inherently causal because they involve answering what-if
042   questions. For example, the question “Do users have agency over their recommendations?” requires
043   causal reasoning on the behavior of the recommender system under user behavior shift (Dean et al.,
044
      2020; Akp, 2022). Instead of pure prediction, we are interested in the effect of interventions. While
      some causal auditing metrics have been proposed (Dean et al., 2020; Curmei et al., 2021), a principled
045
      framework for reasoning about them in recommender system audits is still lacking.
046
047   We present a causal graphical model to capture the dynamics of general recommender systems
048   including matrix factorization and neural network-based models (Section 3.1). This causal model
049   allows us to provide a general recipe for defining causal metrics for auditing recommender systems,
050
      and categorize existing auditing metrics based on interventions and outcomes of interest (Section 3.2).
051   Addressing the lack of metrics for measuring user agency, we use the unified causal framework to
052   formalize two classes of metrics of user agency: past- and future-reachability (Section 4.1), which
053   extends the one defined in Dean et al. (2020) as well as past- and future-stability (Section 4.2) Reach-
      ability measures a user’s ability to be recommended (or reach) a desired item under modifications to


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      their future or historical ratings. Stability measures how sensitive a user’s recommendations are to
055   edits made by adversarial users to their own ratings.
056
057   Next, we provide both a gradient-based and a black-box approach for computing these user-agency
058
      metrics. When the recommender system is learned through matrix factorization, under mild as-
      sumptions, we show that the corresponding optimization objectives for these metrics have a special
059
      structure that can be leveraged to obtain the optimum efficiently (Section 5). Using the proposed
060
      metrics, we inspect how the stochasticity of recommendations and the nature of the recommender
061   system (sequence-dependent versus not) may influence user agency. We find, on average, an increase
062   in the stability of a user’s recommendations but smaller upgrades in reachability as the stochasticity
063   of the system decreases; and that a standard matrix factorization based recommender system is less
064   stable but facilitates better reachability for users as compared to a deep recurrent recommender
065   network (Section 6). We summarize our contributions as follows:
066
      • We provide a general causal framework for defining new causal metrics and categorizing existing
067     metrics for auditing recommender systems in a principled manner (Section 3);
068   • Using our proposed framework, we develop two classes of metrics for measuring user agency while
069     accounting for the dynamics of the recommendation process: past- and future-reachability and
070     stability (Section 4). We provide effective ways to compute the metrics, allowing the auditor to
071     have different levels of access to the systems (Section 5).
072   • Empirically, we investigate two common classes of recommender systems in terms of our proposed
073     user agency metrics and found that higher stochasticity in a recommender system will help with
074     stability but harm reachability (Section 6).

      2    R ELATED W ORK
077
078
      Prior works on ethical issues in recommender systems have identified several areas of concerns
079
      including fairness, inappropriate content, privacy, autonomy and personal identity, opacity, and wider
080
      social effects (Milano et al., 2020b). A variety of metrics have been proposed to address measures of
081   fairness (Patro et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020), moral appropriateness of content (Tang & Winoto,
082   2015), stability (Adomavicius & Zhang, 2012), and diversity (Nguyen et al., 2014; Silveira et al.,
083   2019; Parapar & Radlinski, 2021). Most of these metrics rely on observational quantities under a fixed
084   recommendation policy and neglect the effects of users’ behaviors, preferences, or the recommender
085   system itself. Studying user agency requires answering causal questions pertaining to how a
086   recommender would respond to interventions made in the user’s behavior over time, which cannot be
087   answered by association-based metrics alone. Therefore, the metrics we formalize are interventional
088   and counterfactual. Interventional metrics quantify the effect of hypothetical changes to specific
089
      parts of the recommender system. Counterfactual metrics evaluate how the system would behave if
      interventions were performed in hindsight while taking into account what actually happened.
090
091   Recently there have been works taking a step towards defining interventional metrics (e.g., (Dean
092   et al., 2020; Curmei et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2020)) by considering the effect of specific interventions
093   on the recommender system’s behavior. Dean et al. (2020); Curmei et al. (2021) take the intervention
094   to be users’ own feedback and the outcome of interest to be the recommendation they receive,
095
      allowing their proposed metric to answer questions concerning user agency. However, existing causal
      metrics are often limited in the kind of interventions they consider, with few works tapping into
096
      new types of interventions and ethical concerns. In addition, most are one-step metrics, ignoring the
097
      recommender-user interaction dynamics over time. Separately, another line of work integrates causal
098   approaches into recommender systems, often proposing new causality-based models focusing on
099   estimating the direct effect of recommendations on user engagement (Schnabel et al., 2016; Sharma
100   et al., 2015; Sato et al., 2020). These metrics fall under the interventional layer of Pearl’s causal
101   hierarchy, and choose the intervention to be the recommendations themselves, and the outcome of
102   interest in this case is commonly the user feedback (Chen et al., 2020).

      When defining interventional and counterfactual metrics, one needs to be explicit about the in-
163   tervention to apply, the outcome one cares about, and the distributions involved in obtaining the
164   metric. More specifically, to define a metric of causal nature, one needs to specify the following three
165   quantities of interests: (i) the intervention; (ii) the (random) outcome of interest; and (iii) a metric (or
166   in other words, a functional) that maps the random outcome of interest to a real value. This process
167   allows us to categorize existing causal metrics used for auditing recommender systems based on the
168   nature of the intervention and outcome of interest:
169   • Recommendation Ai as intervention: In this case, the auditor interrogates the effect of changing the
170     recommendation algorithm. Often, the outcome of interest is user ratings Oi , and the corresponding
171     interventional distribution is P(Oi |do(A)) (e.g., exposure bias (Chen et al., 2023)). This quantity is
172     often at the core of a recommendation algorithm and is studied the most, as recommender systems
173     use predicted user preferences to make recommendations, through optimizing the recommendations
174     against user interests, e.g., maxai P(Oi |do(A = ai ))† .
175   • Other user’s data D−i as intervention: Here, the auditor may want to investigate under the current
176     recommender system, what would happen to a particular user (or a particular group of users) if
177
        other users have changed their behavior. For example, in measuring conformity bias (i.e., how
        other users’ ratings may influence the user’s reaction to the same item) (Chen et al., 2023), the
178
        auditor inspects whether the probability of the user’s own rating change as one intervenes on other
179
        user’s historical ratings/data. The corresponding interventional distribution is P(Oi |do(D−i )).
180   • User’s own reaction Oi (or Hi ) as intervention: The auditor may be interested in inspecting the
181     amount of change a user’s own reaction may cause in their recommendations. In this case, The
182     outcome of interest, as we will discuss in Section 4, can be the user’s recommendation (Dean et al.,
183     2020), i.e., the distribution of interest is P(Ai |do(Oi )).
184
      With this perspective and the proposed three-step recipe for defining new metrics, an auditor can
185
      identify gaps in existing metrics and define new ones. As an example, in Section 4, we define a
186   suite of user-centric causal metrics using this framework. Finally, there is often less discussion on
187   counterfactual metrics in auditing recommender systems, since it involves inspecting quantities that
188   are hard (if not impossible) to obtain in practice. We provide more discussion on this in Section 4.
189   We also note that the aforementioned metrics are only example metrics that one can categorize using
190   this framework. There are other metrics that fit in this framework (e.g., selection bias and position
191   bias (Chen et al., 2023)) that we do not go into details.

      4    U SER -C ENTRIC C AUSAL M ETRICS FOR AUDITING U SER AGENCY
194
195
      While much attention has been given to auditing various ethical concerns of recommender systems,
196   there has been comparatively little work conducted on measuring user agency. This gap is partic-
197   ularly notable given the rich line of qualitative work emphasizing the importance of user agency
198   (Milano et al., 2020a). User agency is a user’s power over their own recommendations compared to
199   recommendations being driven by external forces like other users’ behaviors or algorithmic profiling
200   (de Vries, 2010). It can be compromised in various ways, several of which we target with the metrics
201   we propose. First, recommender systems can enforce filter bubbles that restrict users from diverse
202   content feeds and amplify biases (Milano et al., 2020b). Second, recommendation algorithms can be
203   vulnerable to strategic behavior and adversarial attacks that alter recommendations for unrelated users
204
      (Milano et al., 2020b). For example, consider content duplication attacks on e-commerce platforms
      (Fröbe et al., 2020). In this setting, providers game the recommendation system by duplicating item
205
      listings with little to no changes to maximize the probability of recommendation. Maintaining user
206
      agency over their own recommendations in this scenario requires recommendation stability. The
207   user-centric causal metrics we define in this section are designed with the intent of quantifying user
208   agency from these two separate viewpoints: reachability measures the extent to which a user can
209   escape filter bubbles (Section 4.1), while stability assesses the influence that other strategic users can
210   have on this user’s recommendations (Section 4.2).
211
      When defining these metrics, we take the interventions as user’s feedback Oi or other user’s feedback
212
      O−i , and the outcome of interests as their recommendations Ai . We call these metrics user-centric
213   since the interventions are user feedback (actions that users can take), and the outcome of interest is

302   While both interventional and counterfactual metrics measures different aspects of user agency, there
303   is a distinction in their scope:
304
      • Past-/counterfactual metrics focus on how user behavior (e.g., the items a user chose to rate)
305
        contributes to the recommendations they receive in the present. For example, consider a social
306     media user who primarily receives recommendations for cat videos in the present. Counterfactual
307     metrics help us understand whether the narrowness in the recommendations can be attributed to the
308     recommendation system, or the user’s behavior in the past, which imply vastly different conclusions
309     in terms of user agency. If engaging with cat videos unfavorably in the past would have led to more
310     diverse recommendations in the present, the observed narrow recommendations do not imply a
311     violation of user agency.
312   • Future-/interventional metrics focus on the user’s radius of influence on recommendations in the
313     future. Assume the user who likes cat videos adjusts their preferences and now shows interest in
314
        dog videos. Interventional metrics help us understand the ability of changed user behavior to steer
        upcoming recommendations. If alterations in behavior (engaging with more dog videos) do not
315
        lead to diversification of recommendations away from only cat content, we can conclude that there
316
        is a violation of user agency for their own recommendations.
317
318   Though both reachability and stability are measures of user agency, the two metrics focus on different
319   aspects of it: stability focuses on the influences of behavior changes of other users, while reachability
320
      focuses on influences of the individual at hand. Empirically, in our experiments with embedding-
      based recommender systems, we observe a trade-off between these two metrics (Section 6). Further
321
      investigation into this trade-off is an interesting direction for future work.

      When computing the user-centric causal metrics, we need to consider two aspects: (i) whether the
327
      auditor can intervene on the recommender system, i.e., if they have access to the interventional
328   distribution; and (ii) whether they can obtain gradients of the objective functions in equation 1-
329   equation 4. By obtaining the gradients, the metrics can be computed through methods like gradient
330   descent. Similar to Dean et al. (2020), we consider cases where the auditor can apply intervention
331   on the recommender system, thus addressing concerns around (i). We provide methods that allow
332   auditors to have different levels of access to the internals of the system (e.g., they may not directly
333   have access to the required gradients). Before delving into our methods, we first provide a high-level

      Conclusion. This paper presents a causal framework to formalize interventional and counterfactual
532
      metrics to audit recommender systems in a principled manner. Addressing the problem of user
533
      agency, we present the metrics of reachability and stability to quantify a user’s agency over their
534   recommendations under edits to their own or a different user’s history. In our experiments, we
535   find that with more stochasticity, users’ agency towards their own recommendation increases as
536   indicated by the increase of reachability. However, higher stochasticity also allows other users’ to
537   potentially have more control over the systems, indicated by lower stability of the system. Comparing
538   recommendation algorithms, we observed that MF has higher reachability than the RNN, suggesting
539   that more complex models may allow less user agency in some aspects, which could be attributed to
      the fact that small changes to a dataset have low influence on large-scale deep learning systems.

      Ethics Statement: Our paper develops a causal framework for auditing recommender systems
541   for ethical concerns, primarily related to user agency. Because recommender systems control the
542   information users see to a large extent, they have the potential to influence their ideologies and
543   behavior in the long run. Users can get caught in filter bubbles that only serve to reinforce their
544   biases and insulate them from opposing viewpoints. Additionally, users may be vulnerable to their
545   recommendations being manipulated by third parties. For instance, a third party looking to propagate
546   their viewpoint might coordinate their interactions with the recommender system in a manner that
547   induces certain items/posts to be rank highly in the recommendation lists of some target set of users.
548   As detailed in the paper, both these scenarios imply a lack of user agency and the metrics we propose
549
      quantify this so that these issues can potentially be identified.

      A     S TRUCTURAL C AUSAL M ODELS
703
704   In this work, we use the SCM framework (Pearl, 2009) to model causal relationships. An SCM is
705   a tuple M = (U, V, F, P(U)), where U and V are the sets of exogenous and endogenous nodes.
706   An SCM implies a directed acyclic graph (DAG) G over the nodes V with Pai denoting the parents
707   of node Vi ∈ V. Each Vi ∈ V is generated using the structural equation Vi := fi (Pai , Ui ), where
708   Pai ⊂ V, Ui ∈ U, and fi ∈ F. The joint distribution P(U) induces the observational distribution
709   P(V). An interventional distribution is generated by the (modified) SCM Mx = (U, V, Fx , P(U)),
710   where Fx is the set of functions obtained by replacing the structural equation for X ∈ V with X := x.
711   Informally, it represents the state of the system after intervening on X and setting it to x, denoted by
712
      do(X = x). SCMs also allow us to compute counterfactual quantities that express what would have
      happened, had we set the node X to x, given that the (factual) event E occurred. Counterfactuals
713
      distributions are generated using the SCM Mcf := (U, V, Fx , P(U|E)).

895

896

897
               |V | is the size of the item vocabulary. The (k · m + t)th parameter represents the user’s
898            action if they were to be recommended item m at time t0 + t.
899
             • Past Stability: Details provided in 3. We use a model trained up to time t0 − k, and then
900
               aim to optimize the adversary’s ratings for the factual next k items with the objective of
901            maximally changing the user’s recommended list, with the same stochastic recommendation
902            policy mentioned above. We use Hellinger Distance as our distance metric. There are k
903            parameters of optimization, where k is the time horizon, corresponding to the adversary’s
904            action at each time step.
905          • Future Stability: Details provided in 4. We use a model trained up to time t0 and the
906            adversary follows the recommendations given by a deterministic recommender system that
907            returns the top-1 item that the user hasn’t already rated based on predicted score, with the
908            same objective mentioned above. There are k × |V | parameters of optimization here, where
909            |V | is the size of the item vocabulary. The (k · m + t)th parameter represents the adversary’s
910            action if they were to be recommended item m at time t0 + t.

       In our experiments, we set the user to always select the top item recommended by the system. While
1442
       sampling items from a user’s preference distribution (e.g., using Gumbel-softmax) is possible, it
1443
       introduces more variability as user’s now interact with a more diverse variety of item sequences,
1444   which necessitates additional parameter updates since our parameter space consists of a separate
1445   parameter for every (item, timestep) tuple. Additionally, we demonstrate below how top-1 selection
1446   affects gradient computation, with the subgradient of the item selection term reducing to 0, which is
1447   not the case for Gumbel-softmax selection. Example of 2-step gradient computation with respect to
