009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The urban environment is characterized by complex spatio-temporal dynamics
012            arising from diverse human activities and interactions. Effectively modeling these
013            dynamics is essential for understanding and optimizing urban systems. In this
014            work, we introduce UrbanDiT, a foundation model for open-world urban spatio-
015            temporal learning that successfully scale up diffusion transformers in this field.
016            UrbanDiT pioneers a unified model that integrates diverse spatio-temporal data
017            sources and types while learning universal spatio-temporal patterns across dif-
018
               ferent cities and scenarios. This allows the model to unify both multi-data and
               multi-task learning, and effectively support a wide range of spatio-temporal ap-
019
               plications. Its key innovation lies in the elaborated prompt learning framework,
020
               which adaptively generates both data-driven and task-specific prompts, guiding
021            the model to deliver superior performance across various urban applications.
022
               UrbanDiT offers three primary advantages: 1) It unifies diverse data types, such
023            as grid-based and graph-based data, into a sequential format, allowing to cap-
024            ture spatio-temporal dynamics across diverse scenarios of different cities; 2) With
025            masking strategies and task-specific prompts, it supports a wide range of tasks,
026            including bi-directional spatio-temporal prediction, temporal interpolation, spa-
027            tial extrapolation, and spatio-temporal imputation; and 3) It generalizes effec-
028            tively to open-world scenarios, with its powerful zero-shot capabilities outper-
029            forming nearly all baselines with training data. These features allow UrbanDiT to
030            achieves state-of-the-art performance in different domains such as transportation
031
               traffic, crowd flows, taxi demand, bike usage, and cellular traffic, across multi-
               ple cities and tasks. UrbanDiT sets up a new benchmark for foundation models
032
               in the urban spatio-temporal domain. Code and datasets are publicly available at
033
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/UrbanDiT.

      In the context of urban spatio-temporal modeling, recent advancements such as GPD (Yuan et al.,
      2024b), UrbanGPT (Li et al., 2024), and UniST (Yuan et al., 2024a) have opened exciting avenues
070
      for understanding complex urban dynamics. As compared in Table 1, these models either utilize
071
      LLMs (Li et al., 2024) or develop unified models from scratch (Yuan et al., 2024a;b) tailored for ur-
072   ban spatio-temporal predictions. By training on multiple datasets, they have showcased impressive
073   generalization capabilities. However, their focus remains largely on prediction tasks, and they are
074   often restricted to specific data types—such as grid-based data (Li et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024a)
075   or graph-based traffic data (Yuan et al., 2024b). Thus, realizing the full potential of foundation mod-
076   els capable of seamlessly handling diverse data types, sources, and tasks in open-world scenarios
077   remains an open and largely unexplored area of research.
078   Urban spatio-temporal data is typically defined by diverse properties, including varying spatial res-
079   olutions, temporal dynamics, and complex interactions among entities. Building an effective foun-
080   dation model requires a scalable architecture capable of accommodating these complexities. More-
081   over, the intricate nature of urban spatio-temporal dynamics necessitates a model that can learn from
082   complex data distributions. Diffusion Transformers, exemplified by models like Sora (Brooks et al.,
083   2024), offer a compelling solution for this purpose. By combining the generative power of diffu-
084   sion processes with the scalability and flexibility of transformer architectures, diffusion transformers
085   present a promising backbone.
086   In this work, we introduce UrbanDiT, which unifies training across diverse urban scenarios and
087   tasks, effectively scaling up diffusion transformers for comprehensive urban spatio-temporal learn-
088   ing. It offers three appealing benefits: 1) It unifies diverse data types into a sequential format, allow-
089   ing it to capture spatio-temporal patterns across various cities and domains, guided by data-driven
090   prompts that highlight critical patterns. 2) It supports a wide range of tasks with a single model,
091   using masking strategies and task-specific prompts, without the need for re-training across different
092
      tasks. 3) It generalizes well to open-world scenarios, exhibiting powerful zero-shot performance. To
      achieve this, we first unify different input data by converting it into the sequential format. We build
093
      the denoising network using transformer blocks, equipped with both temporal and spatial attention
094
      modules. To integrate diverse data types and tasks, we propose a unified prompt learning framework
095   that enhances the denoising process. This framework maintains memory pools to capture learned
096   spatio-temporal patterns and generate data-driven prompts, while also create task-specific prompts
097   for various spatio-temporal tasks. These prompts are concatenated into the unified sequential input
098   before being fed into the transformer modules. The design of prompt learning serves as a flexible
099   intermediary, adaptable to a wide range of scenarios.
100
      UrbanDiT, built on the DiT backbone with a prompt learning framework, is a pioneering open-
101   world foundation model. It excels at handling diverse urban spatio-temporal data and a wide range
102   of tasks, including bi-directional spatio-temporal prediction, temporal interpolation, spatial extrapo-
103   lation, and spatio-temporal imputation. This makes UrbanDiT a powerful and universal solution for
104   various urban spatio-temporal applications. We summarize our contributions as follows:

      2.1   U RBAN S PATIO -T EMPORAL L EARNING
119
120   Urban spatio-temporal learning encompass a variety of tasks such as prediction (Tan et al., 2023b;
121   Bai et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2023; Li et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2017), interpolation (Aumond et al.,
122   2018; Gräler et al., 2016), extrapolation (Miller et al., 2004; Ma et al., 2019), and imputation (Tashiro
123   et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023), addressing how urban systems evolve across space and time. Deep
124   learning has achieved significant progress in these areas, with techniques ranging from CNNs (Li
125   et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2017), RNNs (Wang et al., 2017; 2018; Lin et al., 2020), MLPs (Shao
126   et al., 2022a), GNNs (Bai et al., 2020; Geng et al., 2019), and Transformers (Chen et al., 2022;
127   Jiang et al., 2023), to the more recent use of diffusion models (Yuan et al., 2023; 2024b; Tashiro
128
      et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2023). Each of these approaches has been employed to model complicated
      spatio-temporal relationships inherent to urban environments. However, most existing models are
129
      tailored to specific datasets and tasks. In contrast, our approach is designed to handle multiple tasks
130
      and generalize across diverse urban scenarios without the need for re-training on new datasets.
131
132
      2.2   U RBAN F OUNDATION M ODELS
133
134   Foundation models have made significant progress in language models (Touvron et al., 2023; Brown
135   et al., 2020) and image generation (Brooks et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Esser et al., 2024). Re-
136   cently, researchers have extended the concept of foundation models to urban environments, aiming
137   to address unique challenges of urban spatio-temporal data. Some representative works in this area
138   include UrbanGPT (Li et al., 2024), UniST (Yuan et al., 2024a), and CityGPT (Feng et al., 2024b).
139   UrbanGPT introduces LLMs designed for spatio-temporal predictions within urban contexts. UniST
140
      develops a foundation model from scratch specifically for urban prediction tasks, demonstrating
      zero-shot capabilities that allow the model to generalize to new scenarios without additional train-
141
      ing. CityGPT, on the other hand, focuses on enhancing the LLM’s ability to comprehend and solve
142
      urban tasks by improving its understanding of urban spaces. Table 1 provides a comparison of key
143   abilities across existing urban foundation models and UrbanDiT. As shown, UrbanDiT is trained
144   from scratch, allowing it to fully leverage data diversity while offering flexibility across a wide
145   range of tasks. Additionally, it demonstrates emergent zero-shot capabilities. Compared to previous
146   efforts, UrbanDiT represents a significant advancement in developing urban foundation models.
147
148   2.3   D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR S PATIO -T EMPORAL DATA
149
150   Diffusion models, originally popularized in image generation, have recently gained attention in han-
151   dling spatio-temporal data and time series. They iteratively add and remove noise from data, allow-
152   ing them to capture complex patterns across both temporal and spatial dimensions (Yang et al., 2024;
153
      Yuan et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023; Rasul et al., 2021). In the context of time series,
      diffusion models have been applied to tasks such as forecasting (Kollovieh et al., 2024; Rasul et al.,
154
      2021) and imputation (Xiao et al., 2023; Tashiro et al., 2021), outperforming traditional methods
155
      by generating more accurate and coherent sequences. For spatio-temporal data, diffusion models
156   have proven useful in a variety of tasks, including traffic prediction (Wen et al., 2023), environ-
157   mental monitoring (Yuan et al., 2023), and human mobility generation (Zhu et al., 2024; 2023). By
158   effectively modeling spatio-temporal dependencies, these models can capture both the spatial corre-
159   lations and temporal dynamics inherent in urban systems. UrbanDiT leverages the generative power
160   of diffusion models to capture complex urban spatio-temporal patterns, while its flexible condition-
161   ing mechanisms allow it to address a wide range of spatio-temporal tasks. This makes UrbanDiT a
      significant advancement in applying diffusion models to urban spatio-temporal challenges.

188   3.1      P RELIMINARY
189
190   Urban Spatio-Temporal Data. Urban spatio-temporal data typically falls into two categories: grid-
191   based and graph-based data. Grid-based data is structured in a uniform grid layout. Graph-based
192   data, on the other hand, highlights connectivity, capturing the relationships between various urban
193
      entities like streets and intersections. For both different spatial organizations, the temporal dimen-
      sion is characterized as time series data. The data can be denoted as X N ×T , where N denotes the
194
      number of spatial partitions. For graph-based data, N corresponds to the number of nodes, while for
195
      grid-based data, it is defined as the product of the height and width of the grid (N = H × W ). This
196   enables a unified representation of urban spatio-temporal data with different spatial organizations.
197
198   Urban Spatio-Temporal Tasks. In addition to the commonly recognized (1) forward prediction
199
      task, urban spatio-temporal analysis encompasses several other critical tasks. (2) Backward Predic-
      tion involves estimating past states based on current or future data. It is essential for understanding
200
      historical trends and validating predictive models. (3) Temporal Interpolation aims to estimate val-
201
      ues at unobserved time points within a known temporal range. (4) Spatial Extrapolation involves
202   predicting values beyond the observed spatial domain. It is important for assessing potential changes
203   in urban environments and planning for future developments. (5) Spatio-Temporal Imputation refers
204   to the process of filling in missing values in spatio-temporal datasets.
205

      The training process alternates between multiple datasets and tasks. In each iteration, we randomly
      select a dataset and a corresponding task to perform gradient descent training. This approach en-
312
      hances the model’s robustness by exposing it to diverse scenarios and helps prevent overfitting by
313
      ensuring the model learns from a wide range of inputs and objectives. Let D = {D1 , D2 , . . . , Dm }
314   represent the set of datasets, and T = {T1 , T2 , . . . , Tk } denote the set of tasks. Let L(di , ti ) be the
315   loss function for the chosen dataset di and task ti , with the model parameters denoted as θ. Overall,
316   the training process can be summarized as follows:
317
            For i = 1 to N :    di ∼ Uniform(D),         ti ∼ Uniform(T )           ⇒   θ ← θ − η∇L(di , ti ; θ)
318
319   where N is the total number of training iterations and η is the learning rate.
320   For the training of the UrbanDiT model, we adopt a novel diffusion training approach proposed
321   by the InstaFlow (Liu et al., 2023a), which significantly improves the efficiency of spatio-temporal
322   data generation. By employing rectified flow, it is an ordinary differential equation (ODE)-based
323   framework that aligns the noise and data distributions through a straightened trajectory, as opposed
      to the curved paths often seen in traditional models.

476   4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES .
477
478   Prompt. Unified prompt learning is a key design in UrbanDiT. To investigate the contribution of
479   each prompt to the final performance, we conduct ablation studies by systematically removing each
480   type of prompt. Specifically, we identify four types of prompts: F for frequency-domain prompt,
481   T for time-domain prompt, S for spatial prompt, and M for task-specific prompt. We denote the
482
      removal of a prompt as w/o {F, T, S, M } and indicate the absence of any prompt as w/o P .
483   Figure 6 presents the results of ablation studies. The findings reveal that removing any single prompt
484   significantly degrades the model’s performance. In the absence of prompt design altogether, the
485   model exhibits the poorest performance. Among the four types of prompts, the removal of the
      frequency-domain prompt has the most pronounced negative impact on the overall performance.

773   We provide a detailed overview of the datasets utilized in our study to support future research in
774   the field of urban spatio-temporal modeling. The datasets are categorized into two distinct types:
775   grid-based and graph-based spatio-temporal data. Each type of data reflects different spatial orga-
776   nizations and dynamics, enabling a comprehensive evaluation of model performance across varied
777
      urban scenarios.
778   Grid-based data represent spatial information in a structured, uniform grid layout, where each grid
779   cell corresponds to a specific geographical area. Table 5 outlines the essential details and statistics
780   for the grid-based datasets, including spatial resolution, temporal resolution, temporal period, and
781   the size of each dataset.
782   Graph-based data, on the other hand, capture urban spatial relationships through a network of nodes
783   and edges, where nodes typically represent points of interest (e.g., intersections or key locations),
784   and edges represent the connections between them (e.g., roads or transit lines). This type of data
785   is well-suited for modeling scenarios that involve irregular spatial structures, such as transporta-
786   tion networks. Table 6 provides a comprehensive summary of the graph-based datasets, including
787   information on the number of nodes, edges, temporal resolution, temporal period, and dataset size.

791   B.1   S EQUENTIAL F ORMAT OF I NPUT DATA
792
793   We provide a detailed description of the data unification process for both grid-based and graph-based
794   spatio-temporal data. The key goal is to transform the data into a unified sequential format suitable
795   for the transformer’s input.
796   Grid-based data is structured in a uniform grid layout, typically represented in a three-dimensional
797   form Xgrid ∈ RT ×H×W with two spatial dimensions (height H and width W ) and one temporal
798   dimension T . To process this data, we utilize 3D Convolutional Neural Networks (3D CNN), which
799   are widely used for capturing both spatial and temporal dependencies in spatio-temporal tasks. The
800   process is formulated as follows:
801
802
803                                  X ′ = C ONV 3D(Xgrid , kernel size = (pt , ps , ps ))
804                                 Xp = R ESHAPE(X ′ , [N ])
805
806
      where N = pTt × pHs × W     ps represents the total number of spatio-temporal partitions, effectively
807
      converting the data into a one-dimensional sequence for further processing by the transformer model.
808
809   Graph-based data is inherently non-Euclidean, capturing relationships between urban entities (e.g.,
      streets and intersections). The spatial dimension is represented by a graph structure with nodes


                                                                  15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      and edges, and the temporal dimension is still captured as a time series at each node. The graph-
811   based data can be represented as a tensor Xgraph ∈ RN ×T , where N is the number of nodes in
812   the graph, and T is the number of time steps. To handle the temporal dimension, we first apply a
813   1D convolutional network (1D CNN) along the time axis to capture local temporal dependencies.
814   Next, to capture spatial relationships, we apply a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Zhang
815   et al., 2019) on the graph structure. For each temporal patch, the GCN aggregates information
816   from neighboring nodes using the graph’s adjacency matrix A ∈ RN ×N . Finally, we reshape the
817   graph-based data into a sequential format. The operations are formulated as follows:
818
819
820                                X ′ = C ONV 1D(Xgraph , kernel size = pt )
821                                X ′ = GCN(X ′ , A, W )
822                               Xp = R ESHAPE(X ′ , [M ])
823
824
      where M represents the number of spatio-temporal patches, ensuring that the graph-based data is
825   transformed into a one-dimensional sequence, similar to the grid-based data. This unified sequential
826   representation allows both data types to be processed consistently by the transformer model.

904     two parts: within-frame and between-frames, and employs differential divergence regularization
