009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language model (LLM) agents have demonstrated substantial potential
012            across various tasks, particularly in multi-agent systems. Among these, Diverge-
013            to-Converge (D2C) frameworks stand out for their ability to iteratively diversify
014            and converge intermediate thoughts to improve problem-solving. In this paper,
015            we conduct a comprehensive study on the prejudice and fidelity of typical D2C
016            frameworks, including both model-level and society-level frameworks. ❶ In the
017            prejudice section, we uncover an inherent confirmation bias in D2C systems, which
018
               not only leads to suboptimal performance, but also amplifies social biases, such
               as gender discrimination and political partisanship. Surprisingly, we find that by
019
               reframing open-ended problems into controlled initialized problems, this bias can
020
               be leveraged to foster more equitable and effective agent interactions, ultimately
021            improving performance. ❷ In the fidelity section, we explore the scaling laws of
022            D2C frameworks at different granularities, revealing that increasing the number of
023            agents enhances performance only when the system is not yet saturated—such as in
024            complex tasks or with weaker agents. In saturated scenarios, however, adding more
025            agents can degrade performance. To facilitate further study, we develop APF-Bench,
026            a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate such inherent weaknesses of D2C
027            frameworks. We hope our findings offer instructional insights into the strengths
028            and limitations of D2C multi-agent systems, offering guidance for developing more
029
               robust and effective collaborative AI systems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language model (LLM) agents have emerged as powerful tools for a wide range of tasks,
034
      leveraging their ability to generate human-like text and make decisions across diverse applica-
035
      tions (Paranjape et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2024). Multi-agent systems, in particular,
036   have shown great promise by enabling LLMs to collaborate, compete, and communicate to solve
037   complex problems (Guo et al., 2024a). Among these systems, Diverge-to-Converge (D2C) frame-
038   works (Wang et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023) stand out for their iterative approach:
039   agents independently explore different solutions before converging on an optimized outcome.
040
      However, while D2C frameworks offer significant advantages in enhancing problem-solving capabili-
041
      ties, our work reveals that the iterative nature of these frameworks can introduce inherent challenges
042   that affect both system performance and fairness. In this work, we explore two key challenges:
043   prejudice and fidelity in D2C multi-agent systems. We study both model-level frameworks, such
044   as Self-Consistency (Wang et al., 2022), Debate (Liang et al., 2023) and Consultancy (Kenton
045   et al., 2024); and society-level frameworks, including CAMEL (Li et al., 2023), LangChain (Pandya &
046   Holia, 2023), and AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023).
047

048   cally, we find that for various D2C frameworks, the initial stance of the first agent disproportionately

050   performance but also amplifies existing social biases in LLMs, such as gender discrimination (Wan
051   et al., 2023) and political partisanship (Rettenberger et al., 2024). Interestingly, we find that reframing
052   open-ended problems as controlled initialized problems by leveraging the potential solution space
053   of a given problem can mitigate this bias, leading to more equitable and effective agent interactions.

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
      LLM Agents. LLMs have been widely adopted for single-agent systems, where their capabilities
096   in planning, memory, and tool use are leveraged to tackle complex tasks (Guo et al., 2024a; Weng,
097   2023; Xi et al., 2023). These systems have demonstrated proficiency in decomposing tasks, retaining
098   context, and integrating external tools (Khot et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023). However,
099   single-agent systems are limited in complex scenarios requiring collaboration or competition, which
100   has led to the rise of multi-agent systems. Recently, Multi-agent systems enable LLMs to interact in
101   complex environments, allowing for collaborative problem-solving and specialization (Guo et al.,
102   2024b). Notably, diverge-to-converge style frameworks (Wang et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Kenton
103   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023), where agents initially explore diverse
104
      solutions before converging on a final outcome, have shown promise in applications such as society
      simulation (Park et al., 2023), software development (Hong et al., 2023), and game simulation (Xu
105
      et al., 2024). This work aims to uncover the inferent prejudice and fidelity in multi-agent systems.
106
107   Evaluation of LLM Agents. Researchers have developed various benchmarks to comprehensively
      evaluate the efficacy of LLM agents across different tasks. For instance, the RICO dataset (Deka


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2017) is frequently used with Screen2Vec (Li et al., 2021) for evaluating agent performance
109   in mobile interfaces. Benchmarks like the PPTC benchmark (Guo et al., 2023a) assess LLM agents
110   on multi-round dialogue tasks for specific applications, while others such as ToolBench (Qin et al.,
111   2023) and GameBench (Costarelli et al., 2024) evaluate agents’ tool use and strategic reasoning
112   abilities, respectively. SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2024) challenges agents to resolve real-world
113   GitHub issues, emphasizing programming proficiency. While these benchmarks are valuable for
114   assessing the general abilities of LLM agents, they often overlook the structural weaknesses inherent
115   in multi-agent systems, particularly those following the diverge-to-converge paradigm. To address
116   this gap, we introduce APF-Bench, a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the weaknesses
117
      inherent in both model-level and society-level multi-agent frameworks.
118   Prejudice in LLMs. LLMs are known to exhibit and amplify biases present in their training data,
119   including societal biases such as gender (Wan et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024), race (Zhou, 2024;
120   Wang et al., 2024), and political ideology (Rettenberger et al., 2024). These biases can manifest
121   in various applications, from language generation to decision-making, leading to unfair or harmful
122   outcomes. Prior work has explored the ways in which LLMs reinforce stereotypes (Kotek et al.,
123
      2023). Approaches like adversarial training (Ernst et al., 2023), data augmentation (Raza et al.,
      2024), and prompt engineering (Kamruzzaman & Kim, 2024) have been proposed to mitigate these
124
      biases. Our work builds on this by focusing on how bias influences multi-agent interactions within
125
      diverge-to-converge frameworks, providing insights into the amplification and mitigation of biases.

128   3     P RELIMINARIES : D IVERGE - TO -C ONVERGE F RAMEWORKS
129
130   In this section, we formally define the two levels of multi-agent frameworks evaluated in APF-Bench:
131   model-level and society-level multi-agent systems. Diverge-to-converge (D2C) frameworks are
132
      characterized by an iterative process where agents initially diverge by exploring different potential
      solutions and later converge by synthesizing these diverse outputs into a final solution. These
133
      frameworks rely on agents either collaborating or competing to improve decision-making through this
134
      cyclical divergence and convergence. To quantify this process, we introduce a parameter C, which
135   tracks the total number of calls made to any LLM during task execution. Illustrative examples of
136   these frameworks are provided in Figure 2.
137
138   3.1   M ODEL - LEVEL M ULTI -AGENT S YSTEM
139
140   In model-level frameworks different agents operate at the model level to collaborate or compete toward
141   a consensus or solution. We describe three specific D2C model-level frameworks: self-consistency,
142   debate, and consultancy, with C denoting the total number of LLM calls.
143   ▷ Self-Consistency. In self-consistency, an agent A generates multiple independent solutions
144   {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn } for a given problem P . These solutions are then aggregated, typically by majority
145   voting or averaging, to produce a final consistent output s∗ . Divergence occurs when the agent
146   explores different possible solutions, while convergence happens when the system aggregates them.
147   The total number of LLM calls is C = n, where n solutions are generated and processed. Formally,
148   this is defined as: s∗ = Aggregate({A(P, θ1 ), A(P, θ2 ), . . . , A(P, θn )}), where θi represents the
149   different configurations of the agent.
150   ▷ Debate. In the debate framework, two agents Aaff (Affirmative) and Aneg (Negative) argue over
151   the correctness of their respective solutions to problem P in up to t rounds. Divergence occurs as
152   both agents present different arguments in each round, and convergence is achieved when a judger J
153   (another LLM) decides on the final outcome. The judger can terminate the debate early if a conclusive
154   decision is reached before round t. The total number of LLM calls is C = 3t′ , where t′ ≤ t represents
155   the number of completed rounds. Mathematically, the debate is expressed as:

186
187   In society-level D2C frameworks, multiple autonomous agents operate independently but collaborate
188   to solve a larger task. Divergence occurs as each agent is assigned a specific role ri and tackles a
189   different sub-task, and convergence happens when their outputs are combined into a final solution.

                              Pm for a specific sub-task Pi of the overall task P , and the total number of LLM

      calls is given by C = i=1 Ci , where Ci is the number of calls made by agent Ari . Formally, the

192
      the strategy for integrating sub-task solutions into the overall task solution.
193
194   Examples of society-level frameworks include AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023), CAMEL (Li et al., 2023),

196   collaboration. AutoGen allows for dynamic agent interactions across diverse domains, while CAMEL
197
      focuses on role-based cooperation with minimal human guidance. LangChain structures complex
      task dependencies through graph-based modeling. In all these systems, the efficiency of collaboration
198
      is crucially impacted by the total number of LLM calls, which directly affects computational cost.

201   4        D EFINITIONS OF P REJUDICE AND F IDELITY
202
203   In this section, we formally define the key concepts of Prejudice and Fidelity as they apply to Diverge-
204   to-Converge (D2C) multi-agent systems. These definitions allow us to systematically analyze the
205   effects of agent interactions and scaling behavior within D2C frameworks.
206
207   4.1         P REJUDICE IN D2C F RAMEWORKS
208
209   Definition 1. Prejudice in D2C frameworks refers to the inherent biases introduced by the initial
210   conditions of the system, which disproportionately affect the final outcomes.
211
212
      This concept of prejudice aligns with the notion of confirmation bias, where the initial stance or role
      of an agent skews the final decision-making process.
213
214   Specifically, in D2C frameworks, prejudice menifest as follows: the initial stance of the first agent
215   proposer significantly influences the final outcome. For instance, in a debate framework, the initial
      argument presented by the affirmative agent can disproportionately sway the debate’s conclusion,


                                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      especially if subsequent rounds reinforce the initial stance rather than challenge it. Formally, let Ainit
217   be the initial agent’s stance, and s∗ be the final solution. The influence of Ainit on s∗ is defined as:
218
219                                                              ∂s∗
                                                Prejudice =
220                                                             ∂Ainit
221   A high value of Prejudice indicates that the initial agent’s stance heavily influences the final outcome,
222   suggesting confirmation bias.
223
224
      In practice, we use the performance variation under changed conditions to indicate the prejudice.
      Prejudice not only affects the performance of D2C systems but also amplifies underlying social biases
225
      embedded in large language models (LLMs), such as gender discrimination (Wan et al., 2023) and
226
      political partisanship (Rettenberger et al., 2024). These biases, if unchecked, can propagate through
227   agent interactions, leading to unfair or skewed outcomes.
228
229
      4.2   F IDELITY IN D2C F RAMEWORKS
230
231   Definition 2. Fidelity in D2C frameworks refers to the system’s ability to enhance performance as
232   the number of agents, computational resources, or data scales.
233
234   Fidelity examines how well the system’s architecture supports effective collaboration and decision-
235   making without performance degradation. It encompasses four main dimensions:
236   1. Agent Count. The number of agents, which is characterized by the number of LLM calls C, in a
237   D2C framework plays a critical role in performance. Let P (C) represent the system’s performance.
238   Fidelity is depicted by how P (C) scales as C increases.
239
      2. Total Token Usage. The total number of tokens processed by the LLMs during multi-agent
240   interactions impacts the computational cost. Let Ttotal denote the total tokens used by all agents:
241
                                                              C
242                                                           X
243                                                Ttotal =         Ti
                                                              i=1
244
245   where Ti is the number of tokens processed by agent Ai . High fidelity in this dimension implies that
246   the system can handle increased token usage without incurring significant computational overhead or
247   loss in performance.
248   3. Monetary Cost. The financial cost of running D2C systems scales with the number of tokens
249   processed and the number of agents involved. Let Mmonetary represent the monetary cost, which is
250   proportional to Ttotal and the number of agents: Mmonetary = m · Ttotal , where m is the cost per token.
251   High fidelity in this dimension means that the system maintains performance at a reasonable cost as
252   resources scale.
253
      By defining prejudice and fidelity in this formal manner, we provide a framework for evaluating
254   both the inherent biases and the scaling behavior of D2C multi-agent systems. These metrics help
255   identify the conditions under which D2C frameworks succeed or fail, offering insights for optimizing
256   performance and fairness.

      Evaluation Datasets. We employ several datasets that evaluate a broad range of reasoning and
261
      problem-solving abilities under the impact of prejudice and fidelity. Each dataset focuses on a

      specific task type, and we use unique metrics associated with these datasets to evaluate the models
263   comprehensively. We propose to refine the datasets by reducing the trivial samples as detailed in
264   Section 5. We list the sample numbers for the datasets here.
265
266   • PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020): A dataset for two-choice physical interaction question answering, designed
267     to test commonsense physical reasoning i.e., given a question either “statement-1” or “statement-2”
268     is correct which essentially forms a binary solution space. The metric used is Accuracy, measuring
269     how often the model selects the correct answer. The original dataset contains 1837 samples, and
        the refined version has 304 samples.

      • StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021): A dataset for binary yes or no questions that requires implicit
271     reasoning. The evaluation metric is Yes/No Accuracy, which assesses the proportion of correct
272     answers based on implicit strategies derived by the model. The original dataset contains 2290
273     samples, and the refined version has 297 samples.
274
      • GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021): A dataset for grade-school-level arithmetic and mathematical
275     reasoning, where models solve math word problems with a non-binary solution space i.e. lies in R

278   • Chess Move Validity (Srivastava et al., 2022): This dataset assesses strategic reasoning through
279     chess move predictions, with performance measured by the Pawn Score (evaluated by Stockfish),

289     not apply refinement since the complexity of the dataset is already high.
290
      These datasets cover diverse reasoning tasks and enable a comprehensive evaluation of the models
291
      across domains like commonsense reasoning, debate, factual reasoning, and strategy.
292
293   Dataset Problem Reframing. To explicitly evaluate the confirmation bias, we propose a problem
294   reframing technique to transform open-ended questions into controlled initialized questions by
295   leveraging the potential solution space of a given problem. This reframing approach allows us to
296
      explicitly evaluate the confirmation bias on how initial conditions of the D2C systems affect the
      final outcomes. For example, the original open-ended questions require models to generate specific
297
      answers,
298
299               What is the answer to 3 + 4?
300
301   We transform this into a controlled initialized question format, which asks whether a proposed value
302   {x} is the correct answer to the problem:
303
304
                  For the question “What is the answer to 3 + 4”, is {x} the correct answer?
305
      Here, x can either be the ground-truth answer (in this case, 7) or an incorrect answer (e.g., 9). We
306
      refer to this as the control reframing. We further distinguish between two conditions of control
307
      reframing as follows:
308
309
      • Control (Right) Reframing: This occurs when x is the ground-truth answer. In our example, x = 7,
        the question asks if 7 is the correct answer to 3 + 4. This framing allows the model to directly
310
        confirm whether the correct answer is valid.
311
      • Control (Wrong) Reframing: This occurs when x is an incorrect answer. For instance, if x = 9,
312
        the model is asked whether 9 is the correct answer to 3 + 4. This framing introduces a deliberate
313     mismatch, testing the model’s ability to identify incorrect answers and adjust accordingly.
314
315
      By transforming open-ended problems into controlled initialized questions, we gain a more controlled
      environment to assess how models respond under different conditions. This reframing technique
316
      enables us to observe how D2C systems handle biases in problem formulation and whether they
317
      can maintain robust performance when faced with diverse options, as in the controlled cases. In
318   Appendix A, we detail the proposed controlled initialization framework for the datasets.
319
320   Dataset Refinement. The original datasets used in our evaluation contain many simple questions
321
      that result in high accuracy across all models, making it difficult to distinguish the performance of
      different models and frameworks. To address this issue, we refine the datasets by selecting samples
322
      where not all models make correct predictions, focusing on more challenging examples that are likely
323
      to expose meaningful performance differences between multi-agent systems. The dataset refinement
      process follows these steps:

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      • Correctness Evaluation: We first evaluate each model on every sample in the dataset. For each
325     sample, we count how many models make the correct prediction. Samples where all models
326     provide correct predictions are discarded.
327   • Framework-Control Sampling: From the pool of framework-control combinations (e.g., Debate
328     with control wrong), we randomly select three combinations. These combinations are applied to
329     the refined dataset to further evaluate model performance under different conditions.
330   • Final Sampling: After applying the framework-control settings, we sort the refined dataset by the
331     number of correct answers (i.e., from the hardest samples, where the fewest models were correct,
332     to the easiest). We then select the top 300 samples with the fewest correct answers to ensure we
333     focus on the most challenging and informative cases.

405   6.1.1    C ONFIRMATION B IAS
406
      We evaluate confirmation bias by performing condition-control evaluations, fixing the initial stance of
407
      the first agent, and measuring its influence on the outcome. Table 1 presents the results for model-level
408   frameworks. Results for society-level frameworks are given in Table 2 in Appendix D due to space
409   limitation. Our key findings are as follows.
410
411
      ▷ LLM and Framework Variability: Different LLMs excel at different tasks. For example, Gemini
      models perform better in mathematical reasoning (e.g., Gemini-Pro achieves 94% accuracy on
412
      GSM8K), while GPT models outperform in chess (e.g., GPT-4o achieves 76.4% pawn advantage).
413
      Larger models, such as GPT-4o and Gemini-Pro, consistently perform better than their smaller
414   versions (e.g., GPT-4o-mini and Gemini-Flash).
415
416   ▷ Controlled Initialization via Problem Reframing: One of the key insights from the experiments
417
      is that D2C frameworks using the proposed controlled initialization through problem reframing
      consistently outperform those using open-ended approaches. The performance difference is partic-
418
      ularly evident for less capable or smaller LLMs, such as GPT-4o-mini and Gemini-Flash, whose
419
      performances are less saturated. For example, in the Chess Move Validity dataset, GPT-4o-mini
420   shows a 6.2% improvement with control (right) reframing compared to open-ended initialization.
421   This improvement is more modest for larger models like GPT-4o, where the performance gains are
422   smaller but still present. The results suggest that the controlled initialization method helps smaller
423   models converge to better solutions by narrowing their search space.
424
      ▷ Surprising Effectiveness of Control (Wrong) Reframing: We speculate that this occurs because
425
      D2C frameworks encourage exploration and favor an outcome that diverges from the initial stance.
426   This shows that such confirmation bias can be leveraged to promote model performance. This is
427   particularly evident in frameworks like Debate and Consultancy, where agents tend to adjust their
428   final decisions away from the initial proposition. For example, in the Chess-Moving dataset, Control
429   (Wrong) Reframing leads to a 3.3% improvement over Control (Right) Reframing for smaller models
430   like Gemini-Flash. This finding is beneficial for practical applications, as reframing an open-ended
431   task into a controlled question with a random wrong answer can lead to near-optimal performance,
      approaching the results seen in Control (Wrong) Reframing.

533   7                                       C ONCLUSION
534   We investigate the challenges of prejudice and fidelity in Diverge-to-Converge (D2C) multi-agent
535   frameworks. Our findings revealed inherent confirmation bias that affects performance and amplifies
536   social biases. We demonstrated that reframing open-ended problems into controlled (right or wrong)
537   initialized questions can utilize these biases to enhance performance. Furthermore, we explored the
538   scaling laws of D2C systems, showing that increasing agent numbers improves performance only in
539   unsaturated scenarios, while saturated systems suffer from over-convergence and redundancy.

      A     C ONTROLLED I NITIALIZATION
760
      Here, we present controlled initialization through problem reframing for multi-agent systems by
761
      leveraging the potential solution space of a given problem.
762
763   Let, Q denote a question, SR the correct answer space, and SW the wrong answer space. Controlled
764   initialization is structured as follows:
765
      • For controlled wrong initialization, the prompt is: “Is SW the correct answer to the question?”
766
767   • For controlled right initialization, the prompt is: “Is SR the correct answer to the question?”
768
      As discussed earlier, PIQA and StrategyQA have a binary solution space whereas GSM8K and
769
      Chess Move Validity datasets exhibit a non-binary solution space. So for binary tasks like PIQA
770   and StrategyQA, where SW =∼ SR , the solution space is straightforward. However, for non-binary
771   tasks:
772
773   • GSM8K: SR ⊂ R (correct numerical solution), and SW = R \ SR .
774   • Chess Move Validity: SR is the set of valid answers out of 64 possible moves, and SW encompasses
775     the complement of valid moves.
776
777   Broader Applicability and Frameworks: These controlled initializations are initiated on the affirma-
778
      tive side during the starting round for both Multi-label frameworks (e.g., Self-consistency, Debate,
      Consultancy) and Society-label frameworks (e.g., CAMEL, LangChain, AutoGen). By explicitly
779
      tailoring the initialization to the solution space, we maintain flexibility to address task-specific
780
      complexity.
781

796   consistent evaluation during multi-agent coordination and convergence processes.

      Comparison with Other LLM Debias Directions. The biases studied in this work are identified
856
      and addressed at the agent system level, focusing on the interactions within multi-agent systems
857
      (MAS) rather than on individual large language models (LLMs). While prior research has extensively
858   examined biases at the data and model levels, our approach provides a complementary perspective by
859   investigating how biases can propagate or be mitigated through agent interactions. This distinction
860   shows the importance of considering biases beyond the traditional data and model-focused frame-
861   works. Our findings are orthogonal to, yet supportive of, existing efforts to reduce bias in AI systems.
862   By addressing bias within the context of MAS frameworks, we provide new insights that contribute
863   to the broader goal of developing fair and unbiased AI systems. A detailed clarification of this
      perspective is included in the appendix for further reference.


                                                                 16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Future Work. While this study focuses on biases within MAS frameworks, there is significant potential
865   to expand this research by integrating insights from data and model-level bias studies. Future work
866   could explore the interplay between agent system-level biases and those originating from datasets or
867   individual LLMs, aiming to create a unified framework for bias detection and mitigation across these
868   dimensions. Additionally, we aim to investigate systematic alignment strategies for bias mitigation at
869   different levels of the AI pipeline. Further exploration of how varying multi-agent configurations
870   affect the propagation or mitigation of biases will also enhance our understanding. These directions
871   promise to broaden the applicability of our work and contribute to the development of more robust
872   and fair MAS frameworks.
