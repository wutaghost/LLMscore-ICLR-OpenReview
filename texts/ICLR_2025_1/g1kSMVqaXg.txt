011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding how training samples affect models improves model interpretabil-
014            ity, optimization strategies, and anomaly detection. However, existing methods
015            for estimating sample influence provide only static assessments, rely on restric-
016
               tive assumptions, and require high computational costs. We propose Dynamic
               Influence Tracker (DIT), a novel method to estimate time-varying sample influ-
017
               ence in models trained with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). DIT enables
018
               fine-grained analysis of sample influence within arbitrary time windows during
019            training through a two-phase algorithm. The training phase efficiently captures
020            and stores necessary information about the SGD trajectory, while the inference
021            phase computes the influence of samples on the model within a specified time
022            window. We provide a theoretical error bound for our estimator without assuming
023            convexity, showing its reliability across various learning scenarios. Our exper-
024            imental results reveal the evolution of sample influence throughout the training
025            process, enhancing understanding of learning dynamics. We show DIT‚Äôs effec-
026            tiveness in improving model performance through anomalous sample detection
027
               and its potential for advancing curriculum learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep neural networks, optimized via Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) (Bottou, 2010), have
033
      achieved remarkable success across various domains. Despite these achievements, it is challenging
      to estimate the dynamic influence of training samples on the learning process. This understanding
034
      is key to enhancing model interpretability, improving optimization strategies, designing effective
035
      curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009), and enabling early anomaly detection (Chandola et al.,
036   2009).
037
038   Existing methods for estimating sample influence, such as influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2017)
039
      and SGD-influence (Hara et al., 2019), provide foundational insights but face several limitations:
      First, existing methods can only estimate the overall impact of samples on the final model. They
040
      provide a single, static estimate of influence for the entire training process, failing to capture how
041
      the influence evolves throughout different stages of the training process. Second, they often rely
042   on strong assumptions about loss convergence, convexity or model optimality. These conditions are
043   rarely met in modern deep learning environments featuring complex, non-convex loss landscapes.
044   This can lead to inaccurate influence assessments. Third, most methods involve computationally in-
045   tensive operations, such as retraining the model multiple times (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019) or inverting
046   the Hessian matrix (Koh & Liang, 2017). The high computational costs limit practical applicability
047   and make real-time influence analysis infeasible. Collectively, these limitations obscure the time-
048   varying nature of sample influence, thus restricting the utility and applicability of existing models in
049   real-world scenarios.
050   To address these challenges, we propose the Dynamic Influence Tracker (DIT) to estimate the time-
051   varying influence of training samples on models trained using SGD. Our method enables to estimate
052   sample influence within arbitrary time windows through a two-phase algorithm. The training phase,
053   executed only once, captures and stores necessary information about the SGD process, particularly
      focusing on the evolution of the model‚Äôs parameters over time. The inference phase utilizes the

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      stored information to compute the influence of selected samples within specified time windows,
055   enabling efficient and flexible analysis.
056
057   Compared with existing works, DIT offers the following advantages:
058
            1) Real-Time and Dynamic Influence Tracking. DIT provides granular real-time sample in-
059            fluence estimates within arbitrary time windows during model training, capturing dynamic
060            influence fluctuations. Our experiments show that DIT can identify important samples
061            early in training, optimizing the process and enhancing performance in applications such
062            as mislabeled sample detection.
063         2) Robustness to Non-Convergence and Non-Convexity. DIT handles non-convex loss
064            landscapes effectively by utilizing gradient analysis and Hessian-vector approximations
065            without assuming convergence or global optimality. We provide theoretical guarantees on
066            the accuracy of our estimates, showing that estimation errors grow controllably with the
067            training interval, ensuring reliable results even in non-convex settings.
068         3) Query-Based Multifaceted Influence Measure. Our query-based algorithm enables mul-
069            tifaceted analysis of model behavior by projecting parameter changes to specific directions.
070            This approach allows for targeted estimation of how training samples impact loss gradients,
071            predictions, and other model properties, providing a comprehensive understanding of sam-
072            ple influence.

326   While existing methods typically provide a static estimate of sample influence for the entire training
327   process, our study shows that sample influence on model performance is dynamic and evolves over
328   time. To uncover this, we conducted a preliminary exploration using LOO, as it provides a ground
329   truth assessment of each sample‚Äôs influence on model performance. Our methodology involved
330   randomly selecting 256 training samples and using LOO to evaluate their loss change at each epoch
331   during model training. For each sample and each epoch, we temporarily removed the sample from
332
      the training set, retrained the model for that epoch, and recorded the resulting change in loss. This
      process generated a time series of sample influences, allowing us to track how the importance of
333
      each sample evolved throughout the training process.

336   identify patterns of sample influence rather than relative influences, we normalized the values within
337   each epoch using StandardScaler. We then used linear regression to analyze trends in influence

      changes. Figure 1 shows four distinct influence evolution patterns, displaying centroid values for

472

473

478

482   ing stages generally yield better detection accuracy, particularly for complex models. As models
483   converge, the influence of mislabeled samples becomes more distinguishable relative to correctly

499   6      R ELATED W ORKS
500
501   Estimating the influence of individual training samples on machine learning models is important
502   for optimization and interpretability. While the Leave-One-Out (LOO) method is straightforward,
503   it‚Äôs computationally prohibitive for large datasets or complex models. Influence functions (Koh &
504   Liang, 2017) offer a more feasible alternative, estimating the impact of removing a single training
505
      sample on model performance at convergence. However, their effectiveness is limited in non-convex
      scenarios common in deep learning (Basu et al., 2021). Recent extensions (Guo et al., 2021;
506
      Schioppa et al., 2022; Choe et al., 2024) still provide static, full-process influence measures, failing
507
      to capture dynamic sample influence during training.
508
509   Shapley Value-based approaches (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019) provide a robust, equitable valuation of
510   individual sample contributions by considering all possible subsets of training data. Efficient approx-
511
      imation algorithms (Jia et al., 2019; 2021; Xu et al., 2021) and domain-specific extensions (Schoch
      et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2022) have improved scalability, but remain computationally
512
      expensive for large-scale problems.
513
514   Data cleansing and pruning focus on removing noisy or irrelevant data. SGD-influence (Hara et al.,
515   2019) analyzes the gradient descent process and estimates sample influence across the entire training
516   trajectory. Our proposed DIT extends this approach, enabling influence estimation within arbitrary
517
      time windows during training, providing more flexible error bound analysis and detailed experimen-
      tal evaluation. Forgetting events (Toneva et al., 2018) and early-training scores (Paul et al., 2021)
518
      enable efficient data pruning. MOSO (Tan et al., 2024) identifies less informative samples via
519
      gradient deviations, and YOCO (He et al., 2023) enables flexible resizing of condensed datasets.
520
521   Despite these advancements, analyzing sample influence within arbitrary time windows during train-
522   ing remains a challenge. DIT addresses this gap by providing a flexible, computationally efficient
523
      method for fine-grained influence tracking without relying on strong convexity assumptions. It
      enables multidimensional influence measurement with a single training process, offering a compre-
524
      hensive understanding of sample importance throughout the learning trajectory.
525
526
527   7      C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper introduces Dynamic Influence Tracker (DIT), a novel approach for fine-grained estima-
530   tion of individual training sample influence within arbitrary time windows in SGD-trained models.
531   Our method‚Äôs query-based design enables multifaceted analysis of sample influence on various as-
532
      pects of model performance effectively. Our theoretical analysis provides error bounds without
      assuming convexity. Extensive experimental results reveal patterns in influence dynamics and show
533
      that DIT consistently outperforms existing methods in influence estimation accuracy, particularly
534
      for complex models and datasets.

732   A.2.2      A PPLICATIONS IN M ODERN D EEP L EARNING
733
734
      There are two particularly promising applications of DIT: large language model fine-tuning and
      curriculum learning.
735
736   In the context of large pre-trained models, DIT addresses several fundamental challenges that have
737   previously limited influence analysis methods. The method‚Äôs assumption-free nature makes it partic-
738   ularly suitable for fine-tuning scenarios, as it requires no constraints on global optimality or conver-
739   gence. By treating the pre-trained model‚Äôs parameters as the initial state őł0 , DIT naturally integrates
740
      with existing fine-tuning workflows without requiring complete retraining cycles.
741   Moreover, the scalability of DIT‚Äôs model-agnostic design presents a significant advantage for large-
742   scale applications. Whether applied to full model fine-tuning or specific architectural components
743   like adapters, the query-based approach efficiently tracks parameter influence while maintaining
744   computational feasibility. This scalability is further enhanced by DIT‚Äôs shown ability to identify
745   influential samples in early training stages, enabling effective analysis of large language models
746
      while minimizing storage requirements through targeted early-stage tracking.
747   DIT also offers novel approaches to curriculum learning by providing data-driven methods for sam-
748   ple ordering and difficulty assessment. The identification of distinct influence patterns naturally in-
749   forms curriculum design: Early Influencers serve as optimal starting points for initial training stages,
750   while Late Bloomers naturally align with curriculum progression. Stable Influencers provide con-
751   sistent anchoring points across different training phases, enabling automatic difficulty assessment
752
      without relying on manual labeling techniques.
753   This approach to curriculum learning is supported by our empirical findings on cross-stage influence
754   correlations. The strong correlation between early and full training influence enables reliable early
755   identification of important samples. Simultaneously, the observed low correlation between early and
      late stages provides empirical support for the necessity of progressive learning approaches. These

1384   A.6.2    S AMPLE I NFLUENCE DYNAMICS M ETHODOLOGY
1385
       The methodology for analyzing sample influence dynamics consists of several key steps.
1386
1387        1) Sampling and Influence Tracking: We randomly select 256 training points and track their
1388           influence, measured as loss change via LOO, over 20 epochs of training. This fine-grained
1389           sampling provides detailed influence trajectories for each point.
1390
            2) Standardization and Trend Analysis: We standardize the influence values using Stan-
1391           dardScaler to normalize the value across different epochs. For each sample, a linear re-
1392           gression is performed on its standardized influence values over time. The slope of this
1393           regression line indicates the overall trend direction (increasing or decreasing influence).
1394           The p-value of the regression determines whether this trend is statistically significant.
1395        3) Adaptive Pattern Categorization: Each sample is categorized based on its statistical prop-
1396           erties, including a) Trend significance (determined by the p-value) b) Trend direction (pos-
1397           itive or negative slope) c) Standard deviation of influence values (a measure of fluctuation).
1398
            4) Pattern Analysis: We calculate the proportion of samples in each category and compute
1399           the centroid of each category by averaging the standardized influence values of all points
1400           within that category.
