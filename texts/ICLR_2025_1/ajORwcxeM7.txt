009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating conditional average treatment effects (CATE) from observational data is
012            an important problem and is of high practical relevance for many domains. Despite
013            the great efforts of recent studies to accurately estimate CATE, most methods
014            require complete observation of all covariates of an individual. However, in real-
015            world scenarios, the acquisition of covariate information is usually done in a active
016            manner, which motivates us to develop methods to minimize the total measurement
017            cost by actively selecting the most appropriate covariates to measure while guaran-
018
               teeing the CATE estimation accuracy. To this end, in this paper, we first extend the
               existing methods for estimating CATE to allow accurate estimation in the presence
019
               of unmeasured covariates. Next, we theoretically show the advantage of dynami-
020
               cally adjusting the sampling strategy based on an evolving understanding of the
021            information measured in the covariates. Then, we formulate the dynamic sampling
022            strategy learning as a partially observed Markov decision process (POMDP) and
023            further develop a policy gradient method to solve the optimal dynamic policy.
024            Extensive experiments conducted on three real-world datasets demonstrate the
025            effectiveness of our proposed methods.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Treatment effect estimation using observational data plays a crucial role in a broad range of domains
031   such as precision medicine (Alaa & Van Der Schaar, 2017), digital markering (Chernozhukov et al.,
032   2013), and policy making (Athey, 2015). For example, in healthcare, a doctor could use covariate
033
      information about a patient to estimate the conditional average treatment effect (CATE), defined as
      the difference between the patient’s expected potential outcomes under different treatment conditions,
034
      which can be used to help determine which treatment leads to a more desired outcome. The basic
035
      challenge for accurately estimating CATE is that, since each individual can be only assigned one
036   treatment, we always observe the corresponding potential outcome, but not both, which is also known
037   as the fundamental problem of causal inference (Holland, 1986).
038
039
      Many methods have been proposed to accurately estimate CATE. Specifically, most methods strive to
      balance covariates to estimate CATE accurately, such as matching, stratification, outcome regression,
040
      weighting, and doubly robust methods (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Rosenbaum, 1987; Hainmueller,
041
      2012; Li et al., 2016). Benefiting from recent advances in deep learning, representation learning
042   methods propose to learn a covariate representation that is independent of the treatment to overcome
043   the covariate shift between the treatment and control groups (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al.,
044   2017b), which can be further enhanced by exploiting the local similarity presevation (Yao et al.,
045   2018), simultaneously modeling the propensity and the potential outcomes (van der Laan & Rose,
046   2011; Shi et al., 2019), and disentangling the covariates Hassanpour & Greiner (2020). In addition,
047   by exploiting the generative models, CEVAE (Louizos et al., 2017) and GANITE (Yoon et al., 2018)
048   generate counterfactual outcomes for CATE estimation.
049   Despite the great efforts of recent studies to accurately estimate CATE, most methods require
050   complete observation of all covariates of an individual. However, we believe that this is not practical
051   in real-world scenarios–instead, the measurement of covariates should be active and well-designed.
052   Moving back to the illustrated example in healthcare, when a doctor initially meets a new patient,
053   it is hardly possible for the doctor to have all the covariate information about the patient. Instead,
      the doctor might ask the patient to take some medical tests, such as drawing blood or taking medical

      images. By taking such medical tests, the doctor can collect more, but not all, of the patient’s covariate
055   information. Based on the collected covariate information, the doctor can either (1) collect more
056   covariates by letting the patient take more medical tests or (2) stop collecting extra covariates by
057   recognizing that the already collected covariates are sufficient for accurately estimating CATE and
058   making the treatment decisions for that patient. In addition, it is reasonable to assume that there
059   is a cost associated with the measurement of each covariate. Therefore, it is necessary to develop
060   methods to minimize the total measurement cost by actively selecting the most appropriate covariates
061   to measure while guaranteeing the CATE estimation accuracy.
062   To fill this gap, this paper studies the active covariate measurement for treatment effect estimation.
063   The main contributions are summarized below:
064
      • We extend the existing representation learning methods for estimating CATE to allow accurate
065
      estimation in the presence of unmeasured covariates. Specifically, we introduce a learnable embed-
066
      ding lookup table for each covariate, and design a uniform sampling approach to make the CATE
067   estimation robust to different numbers of measured covariates as inputs.
068   • We consider how to select the covariates to be observed, making it possible to maximize the estima-
069   tion accuracy while minimizing the associated costs. Through theoretical analysis, we demonstrate
070   the superiority of dynamic policy over static policy, where the latter employs a fixed set of covariates
071   for all instances, whereas the former dynamically adjusts its sampling strategy based on an evolving
072   understanding of the information measured in the covariates.
073   • We formulate the dynamic sampling strategy learning as a Partially Observed Markov Decision
074   Process (POMDP) and develop a policy gradient method to solve the optimal dynamic policy. This
075
      allows us to model the dynamic sampling process as a sequence of decisions made under uncertainty,
      with the aim of maximizing the expected sum of rewards over time.
076
      • Experiments on real-world datasets show our method can effectively achieve active covariate
077
      measurement, ensuring the accuracy of CATE estimation while minimizing the measurement cost.

080   2    R ELATED W ORK
081
      Dynamic Sampling. Dynamic sampling strategy adaptively collects measurements based on informa-
082
      tion from previous measurements. Recent works have effectively framed these challenges as Markov
083   decision processes (MDPs) and have approached solutions using reinforcement learning (RL) (Li,
084   2017). Examples abound in various domains, including the Travelling Salesman Problem (Bello et al.,
085   2016), Vehicle Routing Problem (Kool et al., 2018), and Influence Maximization (Manchanda et al.,
086   2019). These studies have consistently demonstrated that RL-based policies can outperform static or
087   greedy approaches in terms of efficiency and effectiveness. In our work, by formulating the active
088   covariate measurement problem as a POMDP (Sondik, 1971; Kaelbling et al., 1998), we extend the
089   above methods to minimize the total measurement cost by actively selecting the most appropriate
090   covariates to measure while guaranteeing the CATE estimation accuracy.
091   CATE Estimation. Benefiting from recent advances in machine learning, many methods have been
092   proposed for estimating CATE, including matching methods (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Schwab
093   et al., 2018; Yao et al., 2018), tree-based methods (Chipman et al., 2010; Wager & Athey, 2018),
094   representation learning methods (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017b; Shi et al., 2019; Wu
095   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), and generative methods (Louizos et al., 2017; Yoon et al., 2018; Wu
096   & Fukumizu, 2021). Unlike the existing work devoted to estimating CATE with complete observation
097
      of all covariates of an individual, our work focuses on a more practical setting, in which the covariates
      is measured from a active manner with varying costs.

102   We consider the case of binary treatment. Suppose a simple random sampling of n units from
103   a super population P, for each unit i, the covariates and the assigned treatment are denoted as
104   Xi = (Xi,1 , . . . , Xi,p ) ∈ Rm and Wi ∈ {0, 1}, respectively, where Wi = 1 and Wi = 0 means
105   receiving and not receiving the treatment, respectively. Let Yi ∈ R be the outcome of interest. To
106   study CATE estimation, we adopt the potential outcome framework (Rubin, 1974; Neyman, 1990)
107   in causal inference. Specifically, let Yi (0) and Yi (1) be the outcome of unit i had this unit receive
      treatment Wi = 0 and Wi = 1, respectively. Since each unit can be only assigned with one treatment,

109   the well-known fundamental problem of causal inference (Holland, 1986; Morgan & Winship, 2015).
110
111   For unit i, the individual treatment effect (ITE) is defined as ITEi = Yi (1) − Yi (0), which indicates
112
      that whether the treatment Wi = 1 is beneficial for individual i. The conditional average treatment
      effect (CATE) is defined as

115   which is the difference in the conditional mean of potential outcomes given fully measured covariates.
116   To identify CATE, we assume that the observation for unit i is Yi = (1 − Wi )Yi (0) + Wi Yi (1). In
117   other words, the observed outcome is the potential outcome corresponding to the assigned treatment,
118   which is also known as the consistency assumption in the causal literature. We assume that the stable
119   unit treatment value assumption (STUVA) assumption holds, i.e., there should be no alternative
120   form of treatment and interference between units. Furthermore, we require the strong ignorability
121   assumption (Yi (0), Yi (1)) ⊥
                                  ⊥ Wi |Xi and the positivity assumption η < P(Wi = 1|Xi = x) < 1 − η,
122   where η is a constant between 0 and 1/2.

190

      5.2    BASELINE S AMPLING P OLICES .
418
419   We compare our dynamic sampling policy with the following four baseline sampling policies.
420   Random, a non-data-driven sampling policy that randomly chooses to stop or continue sampling,
421   and if to continue, randomly measures a covariate in each acquisition step.
422   Static, a data-driven sampling policy that uses a fixed mask vector M ∗ to act on each data without
423   randomness. In practice, we get M ∗ by solving Equation (4) via grid search.
424   Greedy, a data-driven and adaptive sampling policy. In practice, we find out the k-nearest neighbor-
425   hood of the measured covariates in the training dataset and then select the unmeasured covariate that
426   leads to the greatest average Total decreasing within these k neighbors. We stop when no unmeasured
427   covariate will lead to the average Total decrease.
428   Oracle Greedy, a theoretical benchmark sampling policy that provides a lower bound on the Total.
429   In practice, We select the unmeasured covariate that leads to the greatest Total decrease in each
430   acquisition step for each test data. We stop when no unmeasured covariate will lead to the Total
431   decrease. It’s important to reiterate that this policy is not a feasible method in practical scenarios due
      to its reliance on foresight, and also unfair to compare it with other policies.

477   6     C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this work, we discuss a novel treatment effect estimation problem, i.e., how to balance measurement
480
      cost and accuracy in the case of incomplete observation of all covariates. We extend previous
      methodologies for estimating treatment effects, introducing the capability to handle scenarios where
481
      covariates are partially observed. Then, we introduce the dynamic covariate measurement policy
482
      which adaptively decides which covariate to measure or stop sampling at each acquisition step
483   according to the observed covariates. We further show that our dynamic sampling policy is superior
484   to other baseline policies theoretically and empirically.

      I MPACT S TATEMENT
649
650
      This paper introduces the Active Causal Learning (ACL) framework for heterogeneous treatment
651   effect estimation, a significant step forward in the application of machine learning for causal inference.
652   The technique is particularly beneficial in the healthcare sector, where it can aid in discerning the
653   causal determinants of diseases from sub-sampled series data, facilitating the creation of more
654   effective treatment plans. The PCMP allows for a dynamic and systematic approach to modeling,
655   which can lead to better-informed medical decisions and personalized patient care. However, its
656   effectiveness is contingent upon the quality and structure of the data; if the underlying data fails to
657   capture the true complexity of causal relationships or exhibits inconsistencies, the model’s capacity to
658   accurately identify these relationships may be compromised. Despite these limitations, the potential
659
      impact of this research in improving the balance of measurement cost and health outcomes through
      more nuanced data analysis is substantial.
660

662   A     M ORE E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS

664   A.1    O UR M ETHODS
665
666
      The Estimation Model is designed for causal inference, processing input data through a customizable
      data preprocessing layer followed by a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) architecture. The data
667
      preprocessing layer, which can be selected from multiple versions, handles both numerical and
668
      categorical data, incorporating embeddings for categorical variables and accounting for missing data.
669   The processed data is then fed into two separate MLPs based on the treatment variable. Each MLP,
670   consisting of an input layer, a hidden layer with LeakyReLU activation and dropout, and an output
671   layer, predicts potential outcomes y(0) or y(1) for the respective subsets of the data. This architecture
672   allows for efficient and specialized processing of data for causal inference applications.
673
      We train the estimation model by minimizing the MSE loss via Adam optimizer. The learning rate is
674
      set to 0.001, the weight decay is set to 0.00001, and the embedding size is set to 2. We train is for 50
675   epochs. The choice of mask vector is quite tricky. We provide the mask vector sampling strategy
676   called uniform, which indicates that we first uniformly sample the number of 1 in the mask vector,
677   and then uniformly sample a mask vector M that M ⊤ · 1 equals to the number.
678
      The Policy Model in this setup utilizes the data preprocessing layer from the Estimation Model
679
      for feature extraction. This extractor processes input observations and passes them through a fully
680
      connected neural network, which consists of three layers with LeakyReLU activations behind the
681   beginning two linear layers, to generate features for policy decisions. Additionally, the Policy Model
682   includes an Action Network that modifies these features based on the presence of missing data,
683   indicated by a mask. This network emphasizes relevant features and diminishes the impact of missing
684   data, ultimately producing an output that informs policy decisions in a context-sensitive manner.
685
      Inspired by the PPO algorithm, we solve the dynamic sampling optimization problem based on our

      formulated POMDP via policy gradient. Specifically, given a parameterized policy πθ , its value

748   A.2.2    O PTIMAL S TATIC
749
750   The Static Policy is also a baseline strategy for variable selection in a deterministic, data-driven, and
751   non-adaptive manner. It is a two-stage approach designed for variable selection, focusing on the
752
      generation and application of an optimal mask vector. This method diverges from dynamic selection
      strategies by employing a static, uniform approach to variable sampling across all test instances.
753
754   The first stage involves training on a given dataset to derive an optimal mask vector by solving
755   Equation (4). This vector represents a fixed pattern of variable selection, determined based on the
      dataset’s characteristics and the target objective.

      Since the objective function of Equation (4) is non-differentiable to M , we leverage the grid search
757   algorithm, a derivative-free optimization method to solve the optimization problem. This is achieved
758   by iteratively testing different combinations of variables, represented by a mask vector, and evaluating
759   their performance based on a Total computed by the environment. The Total is calculated for each
760   possible mask vector by turning one of the dimensions on or off in the mask and observing the effect
761   on the model’s output. The search for the optimal mask involves computing Totals for all variables
762   and updating the current mask based on which variable leads to the minimum Total. If the addition of
763   a new mask vector does not improve the minimum Total by a significant margin, the search terminates,
764   and the current mask is considered optimal.
765   The second stage is the inference stage. Once the optimal mask vector is obtained, it is applied
766   uniformly across all test data instances. This means that every test instance is evaluated using the
767   same set of variables, as dictated by the mask vector.

793   A.2.3    G REEDY P OLICY
794
      The Greedy Policy is designed as a heuristic approach for variable selection in a data-driven manner.
795
      It selects variables by integrating the k-nearest neighbors (KNN) approach with a greedy selection
796
      mechanism.
797
798   Initially, for each test instance, the algorithm identifies its k-nearest neighbors within the training
799   dataset. In particular, for quantitative variables, we first standardize the value of the dimension in the
800
      data set to [0, 1], and then define the distance as the absolute value of the difference; for categorical
      variables, we define the distance of different categories as 1 and the distance of the same category as
801
      0. This identification is based on the Euclidean distance metric. The number of neighbors, denoted as
802
      k, is a critical parameter and its optimal value is determined through experimental tuning.
803
804   Once the nearest neighbors are identified, the algorithm enters an iterative covariate evaluation phase.
805   In each iteration, it assesses the impact of each unmeasured covariate on the predictive model’s
806
      performance. This is achieved by temporarily including each covariate in the model and calculating
      the resulting average Total change across the k-nearest neighbors.
807
808   The core of the Greedy Policy lies in its selection criterion. In every iteration, the algorithm selects
809   the unmeasured covariate that yields the highest average Total decrease. This covariate is then
      permanently added to the set of selected variables, and the sampling pattern is updated accordingly.

811   algorithm re-evaluates the remaining unmeasured covariates, as the inclusion of a new covariate
812   can change the dynamics of the model’s performance. The algorithm halts when there are no more
813   covariates that significantly improve the model’s Total, indicating that the addition of further variables
814   would likely not provide substantial benefits.

841   A.2.4    O RACLE G REEDY
842
      The Oracle Greedy Policy is an idealized, theoretical approach to variable selection that assumes ac-
843
      cess to perfect, omniscient knowledge about the impact of each covariate on the model’s performance.
844
      Unlike other methods, which relies on data-driven estimations and heuristics, the Oracle Greedy uses
845   its ’all-knowing’ perspective to make the most optimal choices at each step.
846
847   At each iteration of the variable selection process, the Oracle reviews all the unmeasured covariates.
848
      With its perfect foresight, the Oracle predicts the exact change in the score – as defined by the
      objective function of Equation (3) – that would result from the inclusion of each covariate. Similar to
849
      the selection criterion of Greedy Policy, it then selects the covariate that offers the most significant
850
      decrement in the score. What’s more, the Oracle reviews can also achieve the ground truth value of
851   y(0) and y(1), so the score is calculated by the ground truth values, instead of the estimated values
852   via some causal methods.
853
854
      The process continues iteratively, with the Oracle selecting the most impactful covariate at each
      step. The termination of the algorithm occurs when adding any of the remaining covariates ceases to
855
      significantly improve the model’s score. However, this stopping criterion, like the selection process
856
      itself, is based on the Oracle’s perfect knowledge rather than on empirical data analysis or a significant
857   improvement threshold.
858
859   Notation. It’s important to reiterate that the Oracle Greedy Policy is not a feasible method in practical
860
      scenarios due to its reliance on an unrealistic level of foresight. It serves as a theoretical benchmark,
      providing an upper bound on the efficacy of variable selection strategies. This conceptual tool allows
861
      researchers to gauge the potential limits of their data-driven methods and to understand the gap
862
      between practical algorithms and the idealized ‘perfect’ selection strategy.
863
