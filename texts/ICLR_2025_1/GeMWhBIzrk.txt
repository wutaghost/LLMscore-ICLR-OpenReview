011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural networks, especially deep learning, have achieved revolutionary advances
014
               in several domains, including image and speech recognition, with excellent re-
               sults. However, their reliance on labeled data, lack of interpretability, and incon-
015
               sistency with physical principles limit their applicability in groundwater seepage
016
               prediction and other scientific disciplines. Physics-Informed Neural Networks
017            (PINNs) significantly improve these issues by integrating physical knowledge
018            with neural networks. This study focuses on modeling the groundwater flow
019            field and proposes a physics-informed river-canal groundwater seepage model
020            (PI-RGSM). This model enables self-supervised learning by incorporating hard
021            constraints of boundary and initial conditions, utilizing hydrogeological param-
022            eters and boundary conditions as direct inputs, thus diminishing dependence on
023            observable data. Compared to the baseline PINNs, the PI-RGSM adapts to and ac-
024            curately predicts diverse seepage situations with just one training session, achiev-
025
               ing a mean coefficient of determination of 0.978. To further enhance applicability
               in complex dynamic groundwater seepage situations, we propose PI-RGSM-K,
026
               which builds upon PI-RGSM. This model simulates heterogeneous groundwa-
027
               ter seepage fields and improves performance in complex seepage environments
028            through parameterized hydraulic conductivity field K(x, y) and fine-adjusted
029            model architecture, attaining a mean coefficient of determination of 0.982. The
030            physics-informed neural network models proposed in this study demonstrate ex-
031            ceptional efficacy in precisely forecasting groundwater seepage behavior.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Groundwater is a crucial source of fresh water for the global population, essential for residential
036   life, agricultural irrigation, industrial production, and ecosystem protection (Massuel et al., 2018;
037   Xie et al., 2023; Mishra, 2023). It is imperative to manage this rich resource sustainably to en-
038   sure its long-term availability. Therefore, it is very important to understand groundwater dynamics.
039
      Field-based observations, theoretical analysis, hydrogeological modeling are essential ways to study
      groundwater dynamics (Li et al., 2021; Su et al., 2023). However, although some field-based obser-
040
      vations (e.g., pump tests, tracer Tests, monitoring wells, etc.) are inevitable, large amount of field-
041
      based observations for analyzing groundwater dynamics are time-consuming and labor-intensive;
042   although theoretical analysis is effective in providing deep understanding of the basic principles and
043   flexibility in exploring simplified complex systems, the oversimplification, heavy dependence on as-
044   sumptions and lack of specificity limit the further application. Hydrogeological modeling is widely
045   used to simulate groundwater dynamics for its relatively easy and cheap of implementing, ability
046   of complex system representation, scenario analyzing, results visualization (Chen et al., 2020; Jing
047   et al., 2023).
048   With the evolution of scientific paradigms (Caı́no-Lores et al., 2020), researches on natural science
049   has mitigated from empirical and theoretical approaches to computational and data-intensive scien-
050   tific paradigms driven by computer simulations and big data (Babovi & Bajat, 2023; Li et al., 2023).
051   In the field of computational fluid dynamics, numerical models have achieved accurate simulations
052   of physical processes (refer to Appendix A). Important issues in those spatial discretization meth-
053   ods for solving the partial differential equations (PDEs) of groundwater are the computationally
      intensive. Continuous advancements in data-intensive scientific paradigms have empowered deep

054
      neural networks (DNN) to achieve significant breakthroughs in many fields (refer to Appendix A).
055   However, the ”black box” nature of DNN exhibiting a lack of transparency in their decision-making
056   processes and the significant dependence on extensive training data, limit their use in groundwa-
057   ter research. The physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) which combine the physical laws and
058   neural networks have improved the interpretability of models and diminished reliance on extensive
059   datasets. Wang et al. (2020) proposed a theoretically guided neural network model (TgNN) based on
060   the loss function of groundwater seepage differential equations, which improved the generalization
061   performance of the groundwater seepage model with limited observational data. Additionally, the
062   team demonstrated that the physics-informed model could effectively use physical information to
063
      predict groundwater seepage responses beyond the training set through groundwater seepage trans-
      fer learning tasks. Wang et al. (2021a) proposed a neural network constrained by geostatistical infor-
064
      mation and a physics-guided autoencoder based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Wang
065
      et al., 2021b), applying the physical guidance method to the parameter inversion of groundwater
066   hydraulic conductivity fields. The model’s accuracy and practicality were further enhanced. The
067   study by (Pashaei Kalajahi et al., 2022) demonstrated that, even with limited data, physics-guided
068   neural networks could accurately estimate key parameters such as hydraulic conductivity and poros-
069   ity of the medium, showcasing their strong ability to combine data-driven approaches with physical
070   guidance. Daolun et al. (2021) established a new specialized neuron model incorporating pressure
071   gradient information and proposed an algorithm called signpost neural network (SNN), which signif-
072   icantly improves the accuracy of solving unsteady seepage partial differential equations. Although
073   significant progress has been made in improving groundwater seepage models using PINNs, current
074
      models still heavily depend on the adequacy and quality of observed data. Moreover, after the model
      has been trained, its applicability is generally limited to specific hydrogeological parameter settings,
075
      making it difficult to generalize to broader or unforeseen hydrogeological scenarios, thus restricting
076
      the model’s versatility and practicality.
077
078   Contributions: In this paper, a self-supervised deep learning method for groundwater seepage pre-
079   diction, PI-RGSM, is proposed to address the limitations of baseline PINNs, which depends on ob-
080
      served data and have limited flexibility. Strategies of incorporating hard constraints combined with
      boundary condition inputs into the baseline PINNs and utilizing hydrogeological parameters and
081
      boundary conditions as direct inputs are adopted. These allow the model to adapt new hydrogeolog-
082
      ical environments without retraining. Additionally, an extended version of the model, PI-RGSM-K,
083   is developed to simulate more complex heterogeneous seepage scenarios by parameterizing hy-
084   draulic conductivity field K(x, y). This enhances the precision and adaptability of groundwater
085   seepage prediction, effectively overcoming the limitations of baseline models in heterogeneous seep-
086   age fields. Several representative river-canal groundwater seepage cases are designed to evaluate the
087   accuracy and efficiency of the proposed method.
088
      Paper Organization: The theoretical background is summarized in Section 2, along with a dis-
089   cussion of the improvement method for baseline PINNs. Section 3 introduces the construction and
090   experimental setup of the PI-RGSM and PI-RGSM-K models. A series of experiments verifying the
091   predictive capability of PI-RGSM and its extended model, PI-RGSM-K, under various hydrogeolog-
092   ical conditions (including variations in precipitation, hydraulic conductivity, boundary conditions,
093   and heterogeneous hydraulic conductivity field) are presented in Section 4. Finally, the summary
094   and conclusions are provided in Section 5.

      2.3   C ONSTRAINTS FROM BOUNDARY AND INITIAL CONDITION ( HARD CONSTRAINTS )
183
184   The optimization process of the neural network relies on the physics-informed loss function, which
185   is centered around the groundwater seepage PDE, as discussed in Section 2.2. The physics-informed
186
      loss function involves the interaction between multiple loss function terms, and if multiple losses
      are solved iteratively without additional processing, the solution mode is referred to as a “soft con-
187
      straint”. In soft constraint, the direction of the neural network solution may deviate due to different
188
      solution gradients of tasks, particularly when the boundaries of the objective function or initial con-
189   ditions are unconventional. Thus, assigning weights to the loss function for each task is a relative
190   compromise; however, these loss weights are usually manually adjusted in a tedious trial-and-error
191   process which also increases the computational cost. To address this issue, we introduce the “hard
192   constraints” by constructing the neural network output in the form of an elementary function that
193   satisfies Dirichlet boundary conditions, along with the boundary condition equation. Thus, avoiding
194   directly constructing the residual terms RESBC and RESIC for the boundary and initial condi-
195   tions, significantly reducing the number of residuals, alleviating the pressure on the model when
196   faced with multiple optimization problems, and facilitating the manual adjustment of training hy-
197
      perparameters λi . Simultaneously, since the hydraulic head processed with hard constraints satisfies
      the constraints of boundary and initial conditions, the model does not need to find a specific solution
198
      under uncertain boundary conditions during the optimization stage, thus enhancing the overall con-
199
      vergence effect and eliminating the need for labeled data to provide specific solution information.

      3     E XPERIMENT
251   The PI-RGSM model proposed in this study consists of fully connected neural networks (FCNN),
252   hard constraints, and loss function design. As shown in Figure 2 (ignoring the red dashed line in the
253   input feature section), the input features of the model include the spatial-temporal coordinates of the
254   river-canals seepage (x, y, t), adjustable water levels of the river-canals (Ha , Hb ), and hydrogeolog-
255   ical parameters (e.g., the source/sink term W and hydraulic conductivity K). These multidimen-
256   sional input data are processed through the neural network to output the initial predicted hydraulic
257   head H. The model introduces boundary condition function C(X) and condition function of definite
258   solution φ(X) as hard constraints to constrain the model to predict the hydraulic head (ĤC ). Au-
259   tomatic differentiation (AD) is then employed to compute the partial derivatives of hydraulic head.
260
      The source/sink term (W ) and hydrogeological parameters (e.g., hydraulic conductivity) are incor-
      porated into the PDE constraint (LOSSP HY ), along with the engineering control loss (LOSSEC ),
261
      to form a system of PDEs (Ketkar et al., 2021). These two loss terms are balanced to optimize
262
      the overall training by adjusting the parameters λ1 and λ2 . The model demonstrates flexibility by
263   outputting the corresponding hydraulic head for different boundary conditions and hydrogeological
264   parameters without retraining.

323   To consider the heterogeneity of the hydraulic conductivity field, we replace the homogeneous hy-

334
      better reflect the real geological environment, we can build a separate neural network to take (x, y)
335
      as the input of the neural network, and K as the output. Using the powerful function approximation
336   ability and automatic differential characteristics of the neural network, we can obtain the gradient
337   characteristics of K about x and y, which is the direction we need to improve in the future.

340
      4.1   PI-RGSM
341
342   The major advantage of PI-RGSM is the self-supervised learning scheme. To verify whether the hard
343   constraint method enables PI-RGSM to achieve self-supervised learning and reduce the dependence
344
      on observations, experiments were conducted between PI-RGSM and baseline PINNs and their
      extended models. The experiment did not use observations but instead relied solely on the physics-
345
      informed loss function for model training. Since the baseline PINNs and their extended models
346
      cannot modify the predefined hydrogeological parameters after training, the experiment was set
347   to fixed conditions: precipitation W =0.004m/d, homogeneous hydraulic conductivity K=0.7m/d,
348   and canal heights of 2m. The prediction results were compared with reference data generated by
349   MODFLOW. For the baseline PINNs, the loss term weights were set as λP DE = 100, λBC = 1,
350   λIC = 1, λEC = 1, and λOB = 1 based on empirical experience to balance their contributions
351   to the overall loss function. The network structure of baseline PINNs was determined through hy-
352   perparameter tuning, with 7 layers and 50 neurons per layer identified as the optimal configuration.
353   Detailed tuning processes and results are provided in the appendix F.
354   The performance metrics of models are calculated and shown in Table 2. Results show that the
355   baseline PINNs fail to capture the variation trend of phreatic head, with high errors. Extensions
356   like RCNN and GW-PINN demonstrate significantly better performance compared to the baseline,
357   achieving R2 values of 0.95862 and 0.96246, respectively, but they are limited to a single hydrogeo-
358   logical environment. However, PI-RGSM further outperforms them under the same test conditions,
359   achieving an R2 accuracy exceeding 0.98. This result demonstrates that the PI-RGSM successfully
360   eliminates dependence on observations and realizes self-supervised learning.

666   A    R ELATED WORKS
667
668   With the evolution of scientific paradigms (Caı́no-Lores et al., 2020), researches on natural science
669   has mitigated from empirical and theoretical approaches to computational and data-intensive sci-
670   entific paradigms driven by computer simulations and big data (Babovi & Bajat, 2023; Li et al.,
671   2023). In the field of computational fluid dynamics, numerical models including FLUENT (Galeev
672   et al., 2013), MODFLOW (Brunner et al., 2010; Harbaugh, 2005; Hariharan & Shankar, 2017), and
673   COMSOL Multiphysics (Multiphysics, 1998), have achieved accurate simulations of physical pro-
674   cesses like complex multiphase flow, solute transport, and interactions between surface and soil wa-
675   ter within groundwater systems. Important issues in those spatial discretization methods for solving
676
      the partial differential equations (PDEs) of groundwater are the computationally intensive. More-
      over, the implementation of these models is typically challenging for personnel at water resource
677
      management agencies, as highly specialized knowledge and extensive experience in numerical mod-
678
      eling are required. Continuous advancements in data-intensive scientific paradigms have empowered
679   deep neural networks to achieve significant breakthroughs in image recognition (Krizhevsky et al.,
680   2017), meteorological modeling (Wan et al., 2018), material informatics (Niu et al., 2020), hydrol-
681   ogy (Maxwell et al., 2021), and mathematics(Choudhary et al., 2020), due to the powerful feature
682   extraction (Yu et al., 2013; Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006) and complex function approximation
683   abilities (Cybenko, 1989). Nonetheless, as ”black box” models, neural networks exhibit a lack of
684   transparency in their decision-making processes and depend significantly on extensive training data,
685   limiting their use in groundwater research. Recently, by combining physical laws with neural net-
686   works, the physics-informed neural network (PINNs) method has improved the interpretability of
687
      models and diminished reliance on extensive datasets, providing an efficient and accurate approach
      for groundwater system modeling, and thus promoting the scientific and reliability of groundwater
688
      prediction and management decisions.
689
690   The concept of PINNs was first proposed by Raissi et al. (2019). In their approach, the neural net-
691   work was trained by constructing physical constraints, and optimized by using the residuals based
692   on initial conditions, boundary conditions, and governing equations as loss functions. Neural net-
693
      work derivatives were computed through automatic differentiation (AD). This method effectively
      solves both continuous and discrete PDEs, marking a new era of integration of physical knowl-
694
      edge and deep learning. The introduction of PINNs has significantly expanded the scope of neural
695
      networks in scientific research and engineering applications, while markedly enhancing the predic-
696   tion accuracy, generalization ability, and interpretability of models. Since then, researchers have
697   continued to explore and innovate within the PINNs framework, applying it to various fields such
698   as fluid mechanics (Haghighat et al., 2021), structural dynamics (Moradi et al., 2023), acoustics
699   (Song et al., 2021), and solid mechanics (Wang et al., 2023). In hydrological research, PINNs of-
700   fer a new perspective for more convenient and efficient prediction of groundwater flow with their
701   unique physical guidance characteristics. Wang et al. (2020) proposed a theoretically guided neural
      network model (TgNN) based on the loss function of groundwater seepage differential equations,


                                                       13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      which improved the generalization performance of the groundwater seepage model with limited
703   observational data. Additionally, the team demonstrated that the physics-informed model could ef-
704   fectively use physical information to predict groundwater seepage responses beyond the training set
705   through groundwater seepage transfer learning tasks. Cho & Kim (2022) used an LSTM network
706   to regress the residual differences between a neural network model and the Weather Research and
707   Forecasting model-Hydro (WRF-Hydro). They developed a hybrid model (WRF-Hydro-LSTM) to
708   predict groundwater levels, and experimental results indicated that this hybrid model achieved higher
709   prediction accuracy, less sensitivity to training datasets, and better generalization performance than
710   the LSTM network or WRF-Hydro model alone. Wang et al. (2021a) proposed a neural network
711
      constrained by geostatistical information and a physics-guided autoencoder based on convolutional
      neural networks (CNNs) (Wang et al., 2021b), applying the physical guidance method to the pa-
712
      rameter inversion of groundwater hydraulic conductivity fields. The model’s accuracy and practi-
713
      cality were further enhanced. The study by (Pashaei Kalajahi et al., 2022) demonstrated that, even
714   with limited data, physics-guided neural networks could accurately estimate key parameters such
715   as hydraulic conductivity and porosity of the medium, showcasing their strong ability to combine
716   data-driven approaches with physical guidance. Daolun et al. (2021) established a new specialized
717   neuron model incorporating pressure gradient information and proposed an algorithm called sign-
718   post neural network (SNN), which significantly improves the accuracy of solving unsteady seepage
719   partial differential equations. Although significant progress has been made in improving groundwa-
720   ter seepage models using PINNs, current models still heavily depend on the adequacy and quality
721   of observed data and remain sensitive to outliers. If the training datasets are insufficient or contain
722
      corrupted data, the simulation results may exhibit considerable bias. Moreover, after the model has
      been trained, its applicability is generally limited to specific hydrogeological parameter settings,
723
      making it difficult to generalize to broader or unforeseen hydrogeological scenarios, thus restricting
724
      the model’s versatility and practicality.

797   F    H YPERPARAMETER TUNING PROCEDURE OF BASELINE PINN S
798
799   The hyperparameter settings of the neural network, including the learning rate, layers, and neurons,
800   directly influence the predictive accuracy of baseline PINNs. Figure 7 illustrates the effects of these
801   factors on the L2 relative error. As the initial learning rate decreases, the error drops significantly
802   and stabilizes below 1%, reaching a minimum at a learning rate of 0.001. However, further reducing
803   the learning rate introduces additional errors. The results show that increasing the number of layers
804   generally improves prediction accuracy, but the improvement becomes negligible beyond 5 layers.
805   Similarly, increasing the number of neurons per layer reduces the error, with the most significant
806
      reduction observed up to 30 neurons per layer. After this point, further increases in neurons yield
      diminishing returns.
807
808   The optimal network structure identified in this study consists of 7 layers with 50 neurons per layer,
809   achieving an L2 error of 2.83%. While deeper or wider networks may further improve accuracy
      slightly, the additional computational cost outweighs the marginal benefits due to the relatively low
