009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In-context learning (ICL), as an emergent behavior of large language models
012            (LLMs), has exhibited impressive capability in solving previously unseen tasks
013            based on the observations of the given samples without extra training. However,
014            recent works find that LLMs irregularly obtain unexpected fragmented decision
015            boundaries in simple discriminative tasks, such as binary linear classification. Our
016            observations on the output of Llama-3-8B for the reasoning process of label predic-
017            tions reveal that LLMs tend to leverage the existing machine learning algorithms to
018            perform discriminative tasks. Specifically, LLMs tend first to select a strategy for
019
               the given task and then predict the labels of query data by executing the selected
               strategy. Based on the observation, in this paper, we propose to dive into such a
020
               behavior of LLMs for a deeper understanding of the discriminative capability of
021
               LLMs. We conduct a series of analyses on Llama-3-8B to determine the behaviors
022            adopted by LLMs in the discriminative tasks, including probing the label predic-
023            tions of query data and the corresponding confidence of LLMs under different
024            prompt settings. Moreover, we also probe the preference of LLMs for strategy se-
025            lection and then simulate the behavior of LLMs performing classification based on
026            the preference. The analysis and simulation results provide important observations
027            and insights into the properties of LLMs in performing discriminative tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs), which are equipped with billions of parameters and pre-trained
      on huge amounts of corpora, have exhibited impressive capability in solving various kinds of tasks,
034
      such as reasoning commonsense and arithmetic problems (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
035
      2022; Kojima et al., 2022; Suzgun et al., 2022). A significant ability derived from these large-scale
036   transformer-based models is in-context learning (ICL) (Brown, 2020). In in-context learning, with
037   elaborately designed prompts, LLMs can be adapted to previously unseen tasks conditioning on the
038   given in-context samples and instructions (Wei et al., 2022) without having to be explicitly trained.
039
      An essential problem in in-context learning is determining the underlying mechanism of in-context
040
      learning for a comprehensive understanding of such a powerful paradigm. Several works have been
041
      done from both theoretical and empirical perspectives (Von Oswald et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Shi
042   et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Webson & Pavlick, 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Agarwal
043   et al., 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024; Garg et al., 2022; Nguyen & Grover, 2022). Recently, an interesting
044   paradigm (Zhao et al., 2024) for understanding in-context learning is qualitatively analyzing the
045   decision boundaries generated by LLMs on discriminative tasks, such as binary classification, which
046   is widely adopted in conventional machine learning topics, under the in-context learning setting.
047
      Although great success has been achieved in complex problems (Achiam et al., 2023; Lampinen et al.,
048   2022; Suzgun et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024), current works (Zhao et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024)
049   find that LLMs perform poorly on simple discriminative tasks. In detail, compared to conventional
050   machine learning algorithms (e.g., SVM and MLP), which can generate smooth and continuous deci-
051   sion boundaries, LLMs irregularly obtain unexpected fragmented decision boundaries on the simple
052   linearly separate data (cf. Fig. 1). Although a series of analyses, ranging from the hyperparameter
053   setting (e.g., the numbers of model parameters and in-context samples) to prior information (e.g.,
      the order of in-context samples and the names of labels) have been done by Zhao et al. (2024), the

068   In this paper, we first examine Llama-3-8B’s outputs of the label predictions for query data. Specif-
069
      ically, we output the complete responses of the first 10 query data in a linear classification task.
      According to these outputs, we observe a phenomenon that Llama-3-8B consistently performs clas-
070
      sification by leveraging existing algorithms, including machine learning methods (e.g., Decision
071
      Tree or KNN) and other statistical methods (e.g., measuring the mean and standard deviation of
072   in-context data). This indicates that LLMs tend to solve discriminative tasks in a two-step paradigm.
073   Specifically, given a classification task, LLMs first select a strategy from a strategy space and then
074   execute the strategy to predict the labels of query data based on the observations of in-context data.
075
      The observation above provides two perspectives to study the discriminative capability of LLMs:
076
      strategy selection and strategy execution. For the strategy selection, we examine the decision
077
      boundaries derived from Llama-3-8B with different prompts. Specifically, we prompt Llama-3-8B
078   to perform classification with specialized machine learning algorithms to determine whether LLMs
079   can take full advantage of machine learning tools to achieve similar boundaries to those obtained
080   from the true machine learning algorithms in scikit-learn package (Pedregosa et al., 2011). For the
081   strategy execution, we ask the LLM to output its reasoning process of the prediction and examine
082   the execution of the strategy. Both of these analyses reveal that LLMs can successfully execute the
083   strategy but fail to complete the calculations correctly. Moreover, we also probe the preference of
084   Llama-3-8B for the strategies and find that the modification of prompts can significantly influence
085   the preference of the LLM for the strategies. Further, based on the obtained preference, we try to
086
      simulate the behavior of the LLM with the algorithms in the scikit-learn package. The simulation
      implies that LLMs perform classification tasks via observing data features in most cases, though it
087
      claims to follow the instructions to utilize the specialized method. Further, we also find that it is the
088
      randomness in the feature selection that results in fragmented decision boundaries.
089
090   Our contribution of this paper can be summarized as follows:
091
092
             • We observe that LLMs tend to perform discriminative tasks in a two-step paradigm, where
               LLMs first randomly sample a strategy for the query data point and then predict the label
093
               based on the execution of the selected strategy on the given in-context samples in Section 3.
094
             • We study the decision boundaries derived from Llama-3-8B with different prompts in which
095
               learning strategies are specialized. Both qualitative and quantitative results show that LLMs
096
               cannot effectively take full advantage of machine learning algorithms. Our observations
097            imply that the overconfidence and the poor ability in math may be the reason for failure to
098            obtain smooth decision boundaries in Section 4.
099          • We probe the preference of Llama-3-8B for strategies in classification tasks. Based on the
100            preference, we simulate the behavior of LLMs and find that Llama-3-8B tends to perform
101            classification by observing the features of the given data. Further, we also find that the
102            fragmented boundaries may result from the randomness of feature selection in Section 5.

      3    A R EVIEW ON D ISCRIMINATIVE C APABILITY OF LLM S
135
      In this section, we follow Zhao et al. (2024) and conduct analyses on the decision boundaries on
136
      classification tasks to explore the discriminative capability of LLMs. Specifically, we first reproduce
137
      the decision boundaries of both conventional machine learning algorithms and Llama-3-8B (Touvron
138   et al., 2023) on the typical binary linear classification tasks. Different from Zhao et al. (2024), in
139   addition to the prediction results, we also care about the behavior of LLMs in discriminative tasks.
140
141
      In in-context learning, LLMs perform the classification by inferring the labels of query data based
      on the observation of a few labeled in-context data. In this work, the LLM will be exposed to a set
142
      of data points belonging to 2 classes to infer the labels of query data in the same plane. To make it
143
      easier to visualize, we follow Zhao et al. (2024) and perform the classification on 2-dimension data.

181

182
      the predictions of labels based on the execution of the selected algorithm. Thus, the behavior of the
183
      LLM in solving discriminative tasks can be seen as a two-step paradigm, in which a strategy is first
184   sampled from a strategy space and then the selected strategy is executed to predict the labels of query
185   data. Meanwhile, according to all reasoning outputs (cf. Appendix D) from Llama-3-8B, we can also
186   observe that LLMs leverage the machine learning and other statistical methods in a hybrid way.

205   Based on our observation on the behavior of Llama-3-8B above, two questions are raised: (1) Do
206   LLMs take full advantage of existing machine learning algorithms to perform classification? Since
207   the visualization results in Fig. 1 demonstrate that these classical methods can perfectly solve the
208
      classification task, we are curious about whether LLMs benefit from these tools. (2) How do LLMs
      select the algorithm to solve each task? According to our observation on the reasoning output of
209
      Llama-3-8B, LLMs solve classification tasks by leveraging the existing methods in a hybrid way.
210
      However, the property of such a behavior remains unclear. Thus, in order to have a comprehensive
211   understanding, we propose to dive into the behavior of LLMs in performing classification tasks.

311   According to the figures, we find that Llama-3-8B with the standard prompt are more confident in
312   the prediction of query data which are close to Class 1 in the upper right area (cf. Fig. 3(a)). The
313   phenomenon reflects Llama-3-8B’s preference for Class 1. In fact, the difference in confidence shown
314   in Fig. 3(a), to some extent, is consistent with the decision boundary in Fig. 1(f). Intuitively, we
315
      can observe that both the upper right and lower left areas should be predicted with high confidence
      since they are evidently closer to Class 1 and Class 0 respectively. However, Llama-3-8B only shows
316
      high confidence for Class 1. This implies that Llama-3-8B tends to select a class as the “anchor”
317
      for classification. Specifically, Class 1 is selected as the “anchor” in this case, if the given query
318   sample reveals the same features as the “anchor” class (e.g., Class 1), it will be assigned to Class
319   1. Otherwise, the given query sample will be assigned to Class 0. In other words, Llama-3-8B
320   tends to be more cautious to assign a query sample to Class 1 than to Class 0. However, when the
321   learning strategy is specialized in the prompts, the LLM becomes confident in almost all query data

509   6    R ELATED W ORK
510
511   With the development of deep learning models, the sizes of models and data have been significantly
512   scaled (Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023). Along with the increased scale,
513   the capability of these foundation models is also evidently improved. One of these impressive abilities
514   is in-context learning. The key idea of in-context learning is learning to perform tasks with only
515   a few samples in the form of demonstration. Currently, in-context learning is mainly performed
516   via prompts (Liu et al., 2023). Specifically, by elaborately designing the instructions of the tasks,
517
      LLMs can follow these contents to complete the complex tasks, such as reasoning (Wei et al., 2022).
      However, why ICL can achieve such impressive performance remains an open problem. Several
518
      works have been done from both theoretical and empirical perspectives (Von Oswald et al., 2023;
519
      Dai et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Webson & Pavlick, 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Reid
520   et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024; Garg et al., 2022; Nguyen & Grover, 2022).
521   Recently, Zhao et al. (2024) proposes to understand in-context learning via discriminative tasks (e.g.,
522   binary linear, circle, and moon classification tasks) (Shi et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024). Specifically,
523   given two classes of data, LLMs are required to predict the labels of query data in the same plane. In
524   this work, LLMs are found to be irregularly incompetent in achieving smooth decision boundaries as
525   done by conventional machine learning methods if the models are not fine-tuned in an appropriate
526   way. Our work is inspired by Zhao et al. (2024). In this paper, we propose to dive into the behavior of
527   LLMs in discriminative tasks to figure out the reasons for the failure in the simple classification tasks.
528
529   7    C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we mainly dive into the behavior of LLMs in classification tasks to explore the
532   reasons for the fragmented decision boundaries derived from LLMs and investigate the discriminative
533   capability of LLMs in in-context learning via a series of analyses. According to the empirical
534   results, we find that LLMs tend to resort to existing methods to perform classification. However,
535   in fact, LLMs cannot effectively leverage the existing machine learning algorithm and tend to be
536   overconfident in predictions. Further, we propose to simulate the behavior of LLMs in classification
537
      tasks by probing the preference for machine learning algorithms. The simulation results reveal that
      LLMs implicitly leverage the mechanism of Decision Tree to perform classification, though it claims
538
      to follow the instructions to use the specialized method. Moreover, the results also indicate the reason
539
      for the fragmented decision boundaries may be the randomness in feature branches in Decision Tree.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      In-context learning is an important capability derived from pertaining large language models on huge
759   amounts of corpora. Abundant works have been done to explore this powerful paradigm.
760
761   In-context Learning. With the development of deep learning models, the sizes of models and data
762
      have been significantly scaled (Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023). Along
      with the increased scale, the capability of these foundation models is also evidently improved. One of
763
      these impressive abilities is in-context learning. The key idea of in-context learning is learning to
764
      perform tasks with only a few samples in the form of demonstration. This resembles the decision-
765   making process of human beings, where the common features are extracted from the demonstrations
766   and applied to the analogical new tasks (Winston, 1980).
767
768
      Currently, in-context learning is mainly performed via prompts (Liu et al., 2023). Specifically, by
      elaborately designing the instructions and the demonstrations of the tasks, LLMs can follow these
769
      contents and mimic the behavior to complete complex tasks, such as reasoning (Wei et al., 2022).
770
      However, why in-context learning achieves such impressive performance remains an open problem.
771
772   Some previous works try to build a connection between in-context learning and gradient-based meta-
773   learning (Finn et al., 2017; Finn & Levine, 2017; Gordon et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Zhou et al.,
774
      2019; Rajeswaran et al., 2019). For example, Von Oswald et al. (2023) demonstrates that the linear
      self-attention and the gradient descent on linear regression are equivalent in construction. Meanwhile,
775
      Dai et al. (2023) demonstrates that the calculation of attention can be treated as a dual form of
776
      gradient descent. In such a case, the transformer models can thus be viewed as meta-optimizers. In
777   addition, in-context learning can also be explained from the perspective of gradient descent. For
778   instance, Ahn et al. (2024) observe that the transformer performs preconditioned gradient descent
779   when the parameters are trained to converge. Zhang et al. (2024) demonstrates that the transformer is
780   able to achieve competitive prediction error with the best linear prediction on a new prediction task.
781
      In addition, some other works also try to explore in-context learning from a practical perspective.
782
      For example, Wei et al. (2023) studies large language models with respect to the size of models. In
783   this work, prior knowledge, such as labels, is demonstrated to be essential to the performance of
784   in-context learning. Lampinen et al. (2022) demonstrates that plugging explanations in in-context
785   samples can significantly improve the performance of LLMs on the tasks.
786
      Discriminative Tasks with LLMs. LLMs have been demonstrated to be powerful on generation
787
      tasks, such as reasoning (Wei et al., 2022) and Q&A (Achiam et al., 2023). However, the capability on
788
      discriminative tasks is not well explored. Recently, Shi et al. (2023) looks into the discriminative ca-
789   pability of LLMs by transferring the discriminative tasks into language descriptions and demonstrates
790   that the performance of LLMs is closely related to the contents in prompts. Specifically, if irrelevant
791   information is contained in the contents, the performance will be significantly damaged. Besides,
792   Xiao et al. (2024) proposes to perform discriminative tasks by optimizing the LLM with LLMs.
793   Specifically, in this work, two LLMs are adopted respectively as the learner and the optimizer. The
794   prompts, which are used in the learner, are treated as some kind of “parameters” and are optimized
795   by the optimizer LLM with specific hyperparameters, such as learning rate. The results show that
796   the performance on discriminative tasks can be improved after a few learning steps. In order to
797
      examine the discriminative capability of LLMs, Zhao et al. (2024) proposes to make LLMs perform
      conventional classification tasks and probe the decision boundaries. In this work, LLMs are found
798
      to be irregularly incompetent and fail to achieve smooth decision boundaries if the model is not
799
      fine-tuned in an appropriate way. Our work is inspired by Zhao et al. (2024). In this paper, we
800   propose to dive into the behavior of LLMs in discriminative tasks to figure out the reasons for the
801   failure in the simple classification tasks.

809   The classification tasks adopted in this paper include linear classification, circle classification, and
      moon classification. In the main contents of the paper, we mainly consider linear classification tasks.

      The classification tasks are generated with the existing functions make classification,
811   make circles, and make moons in scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011). In this paper, we
812   mainly consider binary classification tasks. In linear classification, a set of linear separated data is
813   generated around a hypercube. In circle classification, two circles of data, where the smaller circle is
814   in the larger one, are generated. In moon classification, two interleaving half circles are generated.
815
      By default, in each class, the number of classes is 2, and the number of samples in each class is
816
      set to 64. The class sep parameter in linear classification is randomly sampled from the range
817   [1.5, 2.0]; the factor parameter in circle classification is randomly sampled from [0.1, 0.4]; and
818   the noise parameter in moon classification is randomly sampled from [0.05, 0.1]. In particular, in
819   circle classification tasks, the parameter noise is set to 0.03.

       E    E XAMPLE R ESPONSES OF L LAMA -3-8B WITH ML METHODS
1081
1082
1083   In Setion 4, we conduct an analysis on the case that the Llama-3-8B is prompted to perform
1084   classification with specialized machine learning methods. According to the decision boundaries
1085   visualized in Fig. 2, Llama-3-8B fails to achieve as smooth decision boundaries as those obtained
1086   from the conventional machine learning methods, such as Decision Tree, KNN, SVM, and MLP.
1087
       According to the visualized decision boundaries, we can observe that LLMs tend to be overconfident
1088   in label prediction of query data. Our analysis on confidence (cf. Fig. 3) further reveals that the
1089   confidence in the case, where machine learning methods are specialized, is enhanced significantly.
1090   Specifically, in the standard case, LLMs are allowed to leverage arbitrary methods and the high
1091   confidence is only observed in the upper right area. In contrast, in the ML-only case, where only
1092   machine learning methods are availabel, the confidence of all query data increases. From our
1093   perspective, we conjecture that such a phenomenon may be derived from the explicit reasoning
1094   process. In detail, given the specialized machine learning method, the LLM tends to list the steps of
1095   the selected algorithm and executes these steps. Such a behavior resembles the chain of thoughts
1096
       (CoT, (Wei et al., 2022)), where a complex problem is divided into several simple sub-tasks.
1097   Moreover, according to the example reasoning output mentioned in Section 4.2, we also notice
1098   that LLMs do poorly in math, especially calculation. Specifically, according to the example output,
1099   although KNN algorithm is executed, almost all calculations of Euclidean distance are wrong.
1100   With all the results above taken into consideration, two conclusions can be summarized. On the one
1101   hand, Llama-3-8B does not successfully perform the specialized machine learning method. On the
1102   other hand, Llama-3-8B is not competent enough for the calculation tasks, and such a drawback, to
1103   some extent, limits Llama-3-8B’s discriminative capability in classification tasks.
1104
       To further examine the reasoning process above, we check the responses that are generated with
1105
       prompts where other machine learning algorithms are specialized. The responses are presented as
1106   follows. According to these responses, we can observe that Llama-3-8B is able to list the concrete
1107   steps of the specialized algorithms and execute them in inference.
1108
1109
