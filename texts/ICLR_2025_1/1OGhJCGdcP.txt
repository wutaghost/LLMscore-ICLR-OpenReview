011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The integration of graphs with Goal-conditioned Hierarchical Reinforcement
014            Learning (GCHRL) has recently gained attention, as the intermediate goals (sub-
015            goals) can be effectively sampled from graphs that naturally represent the overall
016
               task structure in most RL tasks. However, some existing approaches often rely on
               domain-specific knowledge to construct these graphs, limiting their applicability
017
               to new tasks. Other graph-based approaches create graphs dynamically during ex-
018
               ploration but struggle to fully utilize them because they have problems passing the
019            information in the graphs to newly visited states. Additionally, current GCHRL
020            methods face challenges such as sample inefficiency and poor subgoal representa-
021            tions. In this paper, we present a solution to these issues through the development
022            of a graph encoder-decoder that can evaluate unseen states. Our proposed method,
023            Graph-Guided sub-Goal representation Generation RL (G4RL), can be incorpo-
024            rated into any existing GCHRL method to enhance performance. We show that the
025            graph encoder-decoder can be effectively implemented using a network trained
026            on the state graph generated during exploration. Empirical results indicate that
027
               leveraging high and low-level intrinsic rewards from the graph encoder-decoder
               significantly enhances the performance of state-of-the-art GCHRL approaches in
028
               both dense and sparse reward environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Traditional reinforcement learning methods face great challenges when learning policies in envi-
034
      ronments with long time horizons and sparse rewards. To address these challenges, Hierarchical
035
      Reinforcement Learning (HRL) methods have been proposed to break problems into smaller, more
036   manageable scales suitable for efficient learning. Previous works (Sutton et al., 1999; Kulkarni et al.,
037   2016; Vezhnevets et al., 2017; Levy et al., 2017) have shown its capability in large, sparse-reward
038   environments. Among the HRL methods, Goal-Conditioned Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning
039   (GCHRL) has attracted much attention because of its well-defined paradigm (Nachum et al., 2018b)
040   and resemblance to the human thinking process. Although GCHRL has shown superior performance
041   compared to non-hierarchical methods in certain scenarios, questions such as how to find better sub-
042   goal representations and explore the state space more efficiently (Nachum et al., 2018a; Guo et al.,
043   2021) remain unanswered.
044   GCHRL methods typically utilize two levels of agents as stated in Nachum et al. (2018b). A high-
045   level agent chooses the next target state based on its current state, while a low-level agent decides
046   how to reach the target state specified by its high-level instructor. Both agents face their own chal-
047   lenges. The high-level agent suffers from sample inefficiency in environments with a large state-
048   action space, a problem also encountered by non-hierarchical methods, while the low-level agent
049   is trained solely by the reward signals derived from the distances between the current state and
050
      subgoals in the representation space, making the reward signals highly susceptible to bad subgoal
      representations.
051
052   Previous works have tried to either enhance the efficiency of high-level exploration (Huang et al.,
053   2019; Zhang et al., 2022) or find a good subgoal space to boost GCHRL’s performance in complex
      environments (Wang et al., 2024). To the best of our knowledge, while these methods have made


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      progress in their respective areas, no prior work has attempted to address them in combination. This
055   integration is crucial, as uniting an effective high-level agent with an accurate low-level agent within
056   a cohesive framework can yield performance improvements that surpass simple additive effects.
057
058
      Combining graph theory with RL has recently become a trend in the community (Lee et al., 2022;
      Gieselmann & Pokorny, 2021) as graphs are inherently well-suited for representing the environment
059
      and task structure.
060
061   Previous work has focused on areas such as decision-making through graph search or traversal (Wan
062   et al., 2021; Shang et al., 2019; Eysenbach et al., 2019), and as well as using graphs as world models
063   (Zhang et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2019).
064   Yet, many previous works rely on pre-crafted graphs which hinders the generalization of the meth-
065   ods. Additionally, most existing works use graphs constructed directly from the original state space,
066   which cannot provide meaningful guidance when the agent encounters a state that is not represented
067   in those graphs.
068   To utilize graph representations when a new state (node) is encountered, switching to these repre-
069   sentations (Hamilton, 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Khoshraftar & An, 2024) through graph learning is a
070   viable option. Recent studies have shown that employing various types of graph representations for
071   learning can benefit the underlying RL problem (Klissarov & Precup, 2020; Klissarov & Machado,
072   2023).
073
      In this paper, we propose a novel approach that simultaneously addresses all of the aforementioned
074   problems. Specifically, we keep track of visited states, construct a state graph based on them until
075   the number of nodes reaches a threshold, and then update the graph by adding and dropping nodes
076   as new states are visited, all while building a subgoal space through graph learning based on state
077   representation and spatial connection information provided by the graph. By generating subgoal
078   representations through graph learning, these representations will reflect their relative positions in
079   the decision chain, thereby creating a more effective subgoal space. In estimating the distance
080   between the current state and the intended subgoal in the original space, we use the distance between
081   their corresponding representations in the subgoal space. This distance is then used to calculate the
082
      intrinsic reward for curiosity-driven exploration, aiming to improve sample efficiency.
083   The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
084
085          • We propose a novel architecture that employs a graph encoder-decoder to summarize spatial
086
               information into subgoal representations, enabling the evaluation of newly visited states.
               This architecture can be integrated into any existing GCHRL algorithm to enhance perfor-
087
               mance.
088
089          • We present a method for online construction of the state graph as a world model (Ha &
090
               Schmidhuber, 2018; Zhang et al., 2021) for the agent by sampling from trajectories.
091          • We use novelty-based auxiliary rewards (Simsek & Barto, 2006; Nehmzow et al., 2013)
092            derived from subgoal representations to improve sample efficiency for high-level and low-
093            level agents.
094
      We tested our approach on several real-world application environments (Todorov et al., 2012) to
095   assess the significance of our experimental results. The findings indicate that our method can signif-
096   icantly enhance the performance of the underlying HRL approach in terms of both sample efficiency
097   and success rate/cumulative reward.

      2.2   G OAL - CONDITIONED H IERARCHICAL RL (GCHRL)
116
117   Goal-Conditioned Reinforcement Learning (GCRL) trains agents to achieve specific goals, which
118   are the target states. The agent receives an additional goal input along with the state input and
119   learns a policy to achieve this goal. Goals are represented explicitly in the input to the policy,
120   guiding the agent’s actions towards achieving specific outcomes, and the reward function is often
121   goal-dependent, providing positive feedback when the agent reaches the desired goal state.
122   To deal with large and complicated environments, Goal-Conditioned Hierarchical Reinforcement
123   Learning (GCHRL) (Nachum et al., 2018b; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) decomposes the
124   learning task into a hierarchy of smaller, more manageable sub-tasks. Typically there are two levels
125   of agents. At time step t, the high-level agent chooses a subgoal gt which is the state representation
126   of a target state that the high-level agent wants the low-level agent to achieve as a part of the overall
127   task. This choice is made by sampling gt from the high-level policy πh (gt |ϕ(st )), where ϕ : s 7→ Rd
128   is the state representation function which gives a condensed representation of the state.
129   Each state st can be mapped to its subgoal feature g(st ) by a subgoal feature extractor. Note that
130   g(st ) is not the same as gt . The former, g(st ), is the learned subgoal feature of the current state st ,
131   while the latter, gt , is the target state we aim to reach from the state st in one step or multiple steps.
132
      Given the subgoal gt sampled from the high-level policy πh (gt |ϕ(st )) for the current time step
133   t and the state representation vector ϕ(st ), a low-level agent executes action at based on the
134   low-level policy πl (at |ϕ(st ), gt ). The low-level agent is trained using the intrinsic reward signal
135   rint (st , gt , at , st+1 ) = −∥ϕ(st+1 ) − gt ∥2 to encourage it to achieve the subgoal.
136
      Both agents can be implemented by any policy-based methods, including those introduced in previ-
137
      ous works on policy gradients such as Fujimoto et al. (2018); Haarnoja et al. (2018) and Schulman
138
      et al. (2017).

      2.3   G RAPH AND MDP
141
142   Graph is a generic data structure, which can model complex relations among objects in many real-
143   world problems. A graph is defined as G = (V, E), where V = {1, 2, . . . , N } is the set of nodes
144   and E = {eij } is the set of edges without self-loops. The adjacency matrix of G is denoted by
145   A = (Ai,j ) ∈ RN ×N with Ai,j = 1 if there is an edge between nodes i and j, otherwise Ai,j = 0.
146   The adjacency matrix can be extended to a weighted adjacency matrix, where Ai,j is a weight of the
147   edge eij .
148
      In MDP, a node can represent a state, while the edge weights can model the transition probabilities
149
      or reachability statistics between states.

152   3     M ETHODS
153
154   This section presents our framework, Graph-Guided subGoal representation Generation (G4RL).
155   Our method reshapes the subgoal space utilizing a state graph to incorporate the relative spatial
156   information of visited states.
157   One drawback of previous hierarchical reinforcement learning algorithms (Nachum et al., 2018b;
158   Kim et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2024) is the Euclidean distance calculated in the
159   original state representation space between the current state and the intended goal does not reflect
160   the true progress of the low-level agent, as there is rarely a straight path in the space from the current
161   state to the subgoal. A low-level agent trained with such information may receive an inaccurate
      reward signal, thus impairing its performance. Another issue is that the high-level agent may propose


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a subgoal that is too difficult to reach if its output is not constrained, wasting exploration steps
163   on trying to reach those unreasonable subgoals (Zhang et al., 2022). Our proposed method aims
164   to mitigate both problems by calculating the distance in a subgoal representation space between
165   subgoal representations given by a graph encoder-decoder. This graph encoder-decoder takes into
166   account the actual connectivity between states, ensuring that the generated subgoal representations
167   respect adjacency information. This approach provides more accurate reward signals and leads to
168   more reasonable target subgoals.

170   3.1     S TATE GRAPH
171
172   To record the visited states and their connections, we maintain a state graph G = (V, E) with a fixed
173   number N of nodes1 . This graph is built and updated during training, with no pre-training using
174   expert data or handcrafted process involved in its construction.
175   Each node is labelled by the corresponding state and for each node st , the corresponding state
176   representation vector ϕ(st ), which is also referred to as the node feature, is stored. Edges in the
177   graph represent connectivity between states. The graph is constantly updated during exploration.

      3.3   A DAPTIVE TRAINING SCHEDULE OF THE GRAPH ENCODER - DECODER
242
243   The data stored in the graph, which comprises state representations as node features and connection
244   information in the weighted adjacency matrix A, is constantly updated. Due to the nature of online
245   training, the update rate varies across episodes. If we train the graph encoder-decoder at regular
246   intervals, this may lead to high variance and data loss in earlier episodes, as well as over-training in
247   later episodes. To avoid this, we propose an adaptive training schedule for the graph encoder-decoder
248   in the following paragraph.
249   There are two types of data changing in the graph: edge update, as shown in equation (4), and node
250   replacement. We introduce a variable c to track the weighted number of data changes. Since the
251   replacement of nodes has a much higher impact on the data than the edge update, we add N − 1 to
252   c if a node replacement occurs, and add 1 to c if an edge update happens:

260   3.4   H IERARCHICAL AGENT WITH GRAPH ENCODER - DECODER
261
      Our proposed method involves traditional goal-conditioned settings and a subgoal representation
262
      extractor implemented by a graph encoder-decoder.
263
264   The high-level policy πh (gt |ϕ(st )) nominates a subgoal every K steps and is trained using environ-
265   mental reward rext . The policy can be implemented by any policy-based RL algorithm that takes
266   transition tuples (st , gt , at , rt , st+1 , gt+1 ) as input. To encourage it to propose a subgoal that is not
267
      too difficult to reach from the current state st for more efficient exploration, we add an intrinsic term
      to the high-level reward, considering the distance between the subgoal features of st and gt in the
268
      subgoal space:
269
                        rh (st , gt , st+1 ) = rext + rint = rext + αh · D(E(ϕ(st )), E(gt )),                   (9)

453   5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
454
455   We present a novel approach using a graph encoder-decoder to mitigate the issues of poor subgoal
456
      representations and sample inefficiency in GCHRL. The architecture is designed to efficiently eval-
      uate unseen states by operating in the graph representation space. Our proposed architecture is easy
457
      to implement and can be integrated into any prevailing GCHRL algorithms for better performance.
458
      Additionally, we provide a comprehensive explanation of why the proposed architecture works, sup-
459   ported by experiments on both sparse and non-sparse control tasks that demonstrate the effectiveness
460   and robustness of our method.
461
462
      Generalizing knowledge learnt in one environment to other similar environments remains to be
      solved in reinforcement learning. In the future, we will extend our work by exploring how to gen-
463
      erate subgoals with more interpretable representations for knowledge transfer with the help of other
464
      kinds of graph representations (e.g. Graph Laplacian). Another direction is to transfer the knowl-
465   edge stored in the graph to other tasks by analyzing graph structures and mapping nodes of their
466   state graphs.

571   A.1   E NVIRONMENT DETAILS
572
573   AntMaze This environment is a part of the Gymnasium-Robotics libraries. The size of the environ-
574   ment is 24 × 24. Both of the state space and the action space are continuous, with a state dimension
575   of 31 and an action dimension of 8. The reward of each step is defined by its negative Euclidean
576
      distance from the current location to the target position. At evaluation time, the goal is set to (0, 16)
      and an episode is recognized as successful if the agent is within an Euclidean distance of 5 from the
577
      goal.
578
579   AntMaze Sparse This environment is a variant of Antmaze. The size of the environment is 20 × 20.
580   The state and action spaces are the same as those in AntMaze. The reward of each step is 1 if and
581   only if the agent reaches within an Euclidean distance of 1 from the goal, which is set to (2, 9).
582   AntGather This environment is described in Duan et al. (2016). The size of the environment is
583   20 × 20. The state and action spaces are continuous. The task involves gathering apples to the
584   designated place. The agent will be awarded +1 for each apple gathered and −1 for each bomb
585   gathered. Apples and bombs are randomly placed in the 20 × 20 world.

587   A.2   N ETWORK ARCHITECTURE DETAILS
588
589   Our network architecture for the HRL agents is the same as described in Nachum et al. (2018b) and
590
      in Zhang et al. (2022). For HIRO and HRAC, both the high-level and low-level agents use the TD3
      algorithm. The size of each hidden layer in actor and critic networks in TD3 is 300.
591
592   For the graph encoder-decoder, we use a four-layer fully connected network with the hidden size of
593   128. The activation function used is ReLU. The decoder is a dot product of the two input subgoal
      representations.
