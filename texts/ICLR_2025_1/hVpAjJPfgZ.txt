011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) aims to predict future trends based on
014            historical data. While longer lookback windows theoretically provide more com-
015
               prehensive insights, current Transformer-based models face the Lookback Win-
               dow Limitation (LWL). On one hand, longer windows introduce redundant in-
016
               formation, which can hinder model learning. On the other hand, Transformers
017
               tend to overfit temporal noise rather than extract meaningful temporal information
018            when dealing with longer sequences, compounded by their quadratic complexity.
019            In this paper, we aim to overcome LWL, enabling models to leverage more histor-
020            ical information for improved performance. Specifically, to mitigate information
021            redundancy, we introduce the Information Bottleneck Filter (IBF), which applies
022            information bottleneck theory to extract essential subsequences from the input.
023            Additionally, to address the limitations of the Transformer architecture in han-
024            dling long sequences, we propose the Hybrid-Transformer-Mamba (HTM), which
025            combines the linear complexity and long-range modeling capabilities of Mamba
026
               with the Transformer’s strength in modeling short sequences. We integrate these
               two model-agnostic modules into various existing methods and conduct experi-
027
               ments on seven datasets. The results demonstrate that incorporating these modules
028
               effectively overcomes the lookback window limitations. Notably, by combining
029            them with the Patch strategy, we design the PIH (Patch-IBF-HTM), successfully
030            extending the window length to 1024—a significantly larger window than previ-
031            ously achieved—and achieving state-of-the-art results, highlighting the potential
032            of exploring even longer windows.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) (Lim & Zohren, 2020) holds significant importance
038   across various domains such as traffic management, energy optimization, and financial analysis.
039
      Transformer-base methods (Vaswani et al., 2017), known for their attention mechanisms that facili-
      tate the automatic learning of sequential dependencies, have emerged as promising tools for LTSF.
040
      Notable models like Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), and PatchTST (Nie
041
      et al., 2023) have demonstrated successful applications of Transformers in this domain. To enhance
042   the forecasting capability of the model, extending the lookback window is a natural choice. A longer
043   window enables the model to capture long-term trends more accurately, improving its ability to pre-
044   dict seasonal variations, cyclical patterns, and overall trends. For example, as shown in Fig. 1 (a),
045   when using a longer window L2 , the model successfully captures the cyclical trend in the high-
046   lighted elliptical region, whereas using a shorter window L1 results in failure. In theory, as the
047   window length L increases, the model’s performance should gradually improve. However, current
048   Transformer-based models encounter a Lookback Window Limitation (LWL) (Zeng et al., 2022).
049   This limitation implies that after reaching the optimal performance at a certain window length L,
050
      further increasing the window does not yield better results. A natural question then arises: How can
      we break through LWL and enable the model to perform better with longer windows?
051
052   We analyse this issue from both an information-theoretic perspective and a model architecture per-
053   spective. From the information perspective, time series naturally possess redundancy, and longer
      windows tend to have higher redundancy (Prichard & Theiler, 1994a;b). As shown in Fig. 1 (b), after

079   The Patch strategy is one approach to overcome LWL by treating consecutive time steps as a single
080   patch (Nie et al., 2023; Zhang & Yan, 2023). This reduces sequence redundancy and significantly
081   decreases the effective sequence length for the Transformer. However, the Patch method is heuristic
082
      and lacks adaptability. It can only reduce redundancy at the local level, failing to address redundancy
      at the global level. Moreover, it does not mitigate the quadratic complexity inherent in Transformers.

      patches increases, the computational demands increase dramatically.
085
086   In this paper, we propose two model-agnostic modules to address the issues of information redun-
087   dancy and architectural limitations, respectively. To alleviate information redundany, we intro-
088
      duce the Information Bottleneck Filter (IBF) module based on information bottleneck (IB) the-
      ory. The IBF module aims to identify informative subsequences while minimizing redundancy and
089
      noise (Alemi et al., 2016), enabling the model to prioritize significant subsequences within the se-
090
      quence. Directly optimizing the IB objective for sequences proves challenging owing to their dis-
091   crete nature (Yu et al., 2021b;a), often resulting in training instability and degraded outcomes. Here,
092   we propose the adoption of a probabilistic framework for sequence selection, alongside the intro-
093   duction of a noise injection strategy. Initially, noise is injected into sequence elements with a certain
094   probability, thereby disrupting the flow of information from the input sequence to the perturbed
095   sequence. Subsequently, we incentivize the perturbed sequence to retain its informative proper-
096   ties in relation to the labels. The fundamental concept underlying this approach is that important
097   subsequences should have a low probability of noise injection, whereas injecting larger noise into
098   redundant sequences does not significantly impact predictions. By tailoring a noise prior for each
099
      input, the IB objective can yield a manageable variational upper bound. To address the difficulties
      that Transformers face in handling long sequences, we introduce Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), a re-
100
      cently proposed State Space Model (SSM) characterized by linear complexity. Mamba has garnered
101
      attention for its efficacy and efficiency in modeling extensive dependencies within sequential data
102   (Ma et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024), rendering it particularly suitable for temporal
103   data analysis. However, this does not imply a complete replacement of Transformers with Mamba.
104   On one hand, while Mamba theoretically demonstrates linear complexity, Transformers incur lower
105   computational overheads for shorter sequences owing to efficient hardware optimizations (see Ap-
106   pendix A.6). On the other hand, in short sequence modeling, we observe discernible performance
107   differences between Transformers and Mamba across various datasets, potentially stemming from
      their distinct capabilities in encoding diverse sequence patterns. To harness the strengths of both ar-

108
      chitectures simultaneously, we propose Hybrid-Transformer-Mamba (HTM). Specifically, rooted in
109   the unique characteristics of time series data where temporal relationships persist even after down-
110   sampling, we partition lengthy sequences into shorter subsequences. Then, we employ Mamba to
111   capture long-term information from the input long sequence, while utilizing Transformer to capture
112   short-term information from the short subsequences.
113
      We integrated the two aforementioned model-agnostic modules into multiple Transformer-based
114
      models and conducted detailed experiments on seven datasets. The results demonstrate that these
115   modules can effectively assist Transformer-based models in overcoming the LWL, enabling better
116   performance with larger windows while reducing computational costs by 2 to 3 times. Notably,
117   by incorporating these modules into the PatchTST model, we developed the PIH model (Patch-
118   IBF-HTM), where the window length was extended to 1024—a significantly larger setting than in
119   previous studies. The PIH model achieved state-of-the-art results, proving the effectiveness of using
120   longer lookback windows. Our work can inspire future research to explore even longer window
121   sizes. (Recent time series large models (Liu et al., 2024a; Jin et al., 2024) have adopted window
122   sizes greater than L = 1024, which we will discuss in Appendix A.1 in relation to our approach.)
123   In summary, our primary contributions are as follows: First, while previous work has identified
124   the existence of the LWL in Transformer-based methods, we focus on overcoming this limitation.
125   Secondly, we introduce IBF and HTM, two model-agnostic modules designed from the perspectives
126   of the information bottleneck and model architecture, respectively, to address the LWL. Thirdly,
127   by integrating these modules into multiple existing models, we observe substantial performance
128   improvements across seven datasets. Notably, the PIH model, which combines these modules with
129
      the Patch strategy, achieved state-of-the-art results, demonstrating the effectiveness and versatility
      of our proposed modules.

134   2.1   T RANSFORMER - BASED M ODELS
135
136   Due to the attention mechanism’s capability to capture long-range dependencies, Transformer-based
137   models have found widespread application in language and vision tasks. Early attempts (Song et al.,
138
      2018; Ma et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019) at directly applying vanilla Transformers to time series data
      failed in long sequence forecasting tasks, as the self-attention operation scales quadratically with the
139
      input sequence length. Existing approaches primarily address this challenge through two avenues.
140
      Patch-based methods, exemplified by PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) and CrossFormer (Zhang & Yan,
141   2023), conceptualize consecutive time steps as patches, reducing the number of input tokens and
142   augmenting local semantics to mitigate redundancy. However, patch-based methods impose con-
143   straints on the input data format, and computational expenses persist even at the patch level when
144   the window is large. Another approach focuses on sparse attention mechanisms. Models such as
145   Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2022b), and
146   FEDformer (Zhou et al., 2022) adapt the self-attention mechanism to achieve complexities of O(L)
147   or O(L log(L)). These models rely on specific designs and often sacrifice representational capac-
148   ity, thereby compromising performance. Our work is independent of these approaches and can be
149
      effectively integrated into them.

      Recently, several approaches have emerged to incorporate Mamba into time series modeling, each
      introducing unique innovations to enhance the capture of temporal dynamics. Bi-Mamba+ (Liang
154
      et al., 2024) introduces a novel Mamba+ block by incorporating a forget gate within Mamba. This
155
      modification enables the selective combination of new features with historical ones in a comple-

157   enhance feature interactions among time series elements, Bi-Mamba+ applies this approach in both
158   forward and backward directions. S-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024) adopts a different approach by
159   autonomously tokenizing time points of each variate using a linear layer. The method employs a
160   bidirectional Mamba layer to extract inter-variate correlations and a Feed-Forward Network to learn
161   temporal dependencies. Ultimately, S-Mamba generates forecasting results through a linear map-
      ping layer, highlighting its structured yet flexible approach to capturing temporal patterns. TimeMa-

187   2.3   I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK (IB)
188
189   The essence of the IB principle lies in distilling a compact yet predictive code from the input sig-
190   nal (Tishby et al., 2000). Pioneering work by (Alemi et al., 2016) introduced the concept of vari-
191   ational information bottleneck (VIB), thereby enriching deep learning methodologies. Presently,
192
      IB and VIB find extensive applications in deep learning, predominantly in representation learning
      and feature selection domains. In representation learning, the focus is on training deterministic or
193
      stochastic encoders to derive condensed yet semantically rich representations of input data. These
194
      representations serve as valuable inputs for a plethora of downstream tasks spanning computer vi-
195   sion (Luo et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2019), reinforcement learning (Goyal et al., 2019; Igl et al., 2019),
196   natural language processing (Wang et al., 2020), and node representation learning (Wu et al., 2020).
197   Meanwhile, in the realm of feature selection, IB is used to select a subset of input features such as
198   pixels in images or dimensions in vectors, which are maximally predictive to the label of input data.
199   Strategies such as injecting noise into intermediate representations of pre-trained networks and sub-
200   sequently selecting regions with optimal information per dimension have been explored (Achille &
201   Soatto, 2018; Schulz & et al., 2020). Additionally, techniques like learning drop rates for individual
202   dimensions of vector-structured features have been proposed (Kim et al., 2021).

307   short sequences is a promising paradigm (Mehta et al., 2023; Pilault et al., 2023; Lieber et al.,

      split methods capable of retaining semantic information: interval split and block split, denoted as:

315

316

317

319   the even and odd elements, our interval split extends this approach to partitioning patch sequence

321   preserves the global characteristics of the sequence. Additionally, we propose the block split, where

323   periodicity of time series, where one period (or multiples of a period) is considered as a block, thus

515   5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
516
517   Conclusion. In this paper, we focus on addressing the LWL by analyzing it from both model archi-
518   tecture and information-theoretic perspectives, proposing the HTM and IBF modules. We combine
519   these with the patch strategy to design the PIH model, which can handle longer windows than previ-
520   ous works and achieves state-of-the-art results, demonstrating the potential of exploring longer win-
521   dows. Additionally, we integrate these two modules into other Transformer-based models, enabling
522   them to overcome window limitations and achieve improved performance with longer windows.
523
524   Limitations and Future Work. First, our experiments demonstrate that extending the window
525   length to L = 1024 still yields performance improvements, suggesting that further exploration of
526
      longer windows is a promising direction. Secondly, we found that longer lookback windows are not
      always beneficial for all datasets. Therefore, identifying which types of data are suitable for very
527
      long windows is another important area for future research. Thirdly, the interval split and block
528
      split methods proposed in this paper are heuristic. Designing an adaptive, end-to-end segmentation
529   method tailored to each training dataset may lead to better results. Lastly, while recent large time-
530   series models have adopted much longer windows, we claim that our approach is orthogonal to
531   theirs. It is worth exploring whether our method can be integrated into these large models to further
532   extend their window sizes.

      A.5     E XPERIMENTS SETTINGS
845
846   PIH is built upon the PatchTST framework and thus incorporates all hyperparameters from
847   PatchTST. To ensure a fair comparison, we adhered strictly to the settings of PatchTST for these
848   shared hyperparameters, with the exception of the learning rate. We conducted a hyperparameter
849   search only for those introduced by the HTM and IBF modules, as this was necessary. The only ex-
850   ception is the learning rate. Given the introduction of the Mamba and IBF modules, the default learn-
851   ing rate of lr = 0.0001 in PatchTST is suboptimal. Consequently, we set the search space for the
852   PIH learning rate to lr = {0.001, 0.0005, 0.0001}. To ensure a fair comparison, we also performed
853   a hyperparameter search for the learning rate in PatchTST, using lr = {0.001, 0.0005, 0.0001},
854
      and selected the optimal results. The resulting mean Absolute Error (MAE) values were 0.310 and
      0.335, which are almost unchanged compared to the default learning rate (lr = 0.0001), yielding
855
      0.310 and 0.336. Thus, this does not affect our result analysis.
856
857   Our model incorporates several crucial hyperparameters, including K, which determines the number
858   of partitions; β, which governs the balance between prediction and compression in the information
859   bottleneck (IB) objective; and the temperature factor τ , which influences subsequence sampling.
860
      We set K ∈ {2, 4}, β ∈ {0.0001, 0.001, 0.1, 1}, and τ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1, 2}. We selected the optimal
      hyperparameters based on the results from the validation set.
861
862   Additionally, we analyzed the effects of these hyperparameters. The results indicate that the choice
863   of K does not significantly impact performance. In contrast, both τ and β exhibit considerable
      influence on performance, likely due to variations in the redundancy levels across different datasets.

      A.6   M AMBA VS T RANSFORMER
865
866   We analyze our model from both performance and computational overhead perspectives and find
867   that the hybrid architecture has distinct advantages over using only Mamba or Transformer.
868
869   From a performance perspective , the ablation experiments presented in Fig. 6 indicate that re-
870   moving the Transformer results in slightly worse performance, highlighting the significant advantage
871   of the combined Transformer and Mamba architecture. This finding is further supported by recent
872   works such as Mamba-2-Hybrid (Waleffe et al., 2024), Dimba (Fei et al., 2024), and Jamba (Lieber
873
      et al., 2024).
874
875
      Considering computational overhead , our framework employs the Transformer solely to pro-
      cess the partitioned short subsequences, which generally mitigates concerns about the costs associ-
876
      ated with the Transformer. To validate this, we compared the computation time and GPU memory
877
      usage between using a single layer of Mamba and a single layer of Transformer under various look-
878   back window settings (with nearly identical parameter counts). As shown in Fig. 4, when L ≤ 336,
879   the computational overhead of the Transformer is even lower than that of Mamba; however, at
880   L = 1024, the computational cost of the Transformer is nearly twice that of Mamba. In our ex-
881   periments, K is typically set to 4, resulting in a subsequence length of L/K = 1024/4 < 336.
882   Consequently, the addition of the Transformer module incurs less overhead compared to using only
883   Mamba.
884   In summary, we conclude that retaining the Transformer module is essential for enhancing perfor-
885   mance while managing computational costs effectively.
