009                                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                                Training reinforcement learning (RL) agents on robotic tasks typically requires a
012                                large number of training samples. This is because training data often consists of
013                                noisy trajectories, whether from exploration or human-collected demonstrations,
014                                making it difficult to learn value functions that understand the effect of taking each
015                                action. On the other hand, recent behavior-cloning (BC) approaches have shown
016
                                   that predicting a sequence of actions enables policies to effectively approximate
                                   noisy, multi-modal distributions of expert demonstrations. Can we use a similar idea
017
                                   for improving RL on robotic tasks? In this paper, we introduce a novel RL algorithm
018
                                   that learns a critic network that outputs Q-values over a sequence of actions. By
019                                explicitly training the value functions to learn the consequence of executing a
020                                series of current and future actions, our algorithm allows for learning useful value
021                                functions from noisy trajectories. We study our algorithm across various setups
022                                with sparse and dense rewards, and with or without demonstrations, spanning
023                                mobile bi-manual manipulation, whole-body control, and tabletop manipulation
024                                tasks from BiGym, HumanoidBench, and RLBench. We find that, by learning the
025                                critic network with action sequences, our algorithm outperforms various RL and
026                                BC baselines, in particular on challenging humanoid control tasks.

      1                    I NTRODUCTION
046
047
      Reinforcement learning (RL) holds the promise of continually improving policies through online trial-
048   and-error experiences (Sutton & Barto, 2018), making it an ideal choice for developing robots that
049   can adapt to various environments. However, despite this promise, training RL agents on robotic tasks
050   typically requires a prohibitively large number of training samples (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Herzog
051   et al., 2023), which becomes problematic as deploying robots often incurs a huge cost. Therefore
052   many of the recent successful approaches on robot learning have been based on behavior-cloning
053   (BC; Pomerleau 1988), which can learn strong policies from offline expert demonstrations (Brohan
      et al., 2023b;a; Zhao et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024a).

072   One cause for the poor data-efficiency of RL algorithms on robotic tasks is that training data consists
073   of noisy trajectories. When collecting data for training RL agents, we typically inject some noise into
074
      actions for exploration (Sehnke et al., 2010; Lillicrap et al., 2016) that may induce trajectories with
      jerky motions. Moreover, we often initialize training with human-collected demonstrations that can
075
      consist of noisy multi-modal trajectories (Chernyadev et al., 2024). Such noisy data distributions
076
      make it difficult to learn value functions that should understand the consequence of taking each
077   action. We indeed find that prior RL algorithms perform much worse than the BC baseline on mobile
078   bi-manual manipulation tasks with human-collected demonstrations when compared to a tabletop
079   manipulation setup with synthetic demonstrations collected via motion-planning (see Figure 1).
080
      On the other hand, recent BC approaches have shown that predicting a sequence of actions enables
081
      policies to effectively approximate the noisy, multi-modal distribution of expert demonstrations (Zhao
082
      et al., 2023; Chi et al., 2023). Inspired by this, in this paper, we investigate how to use a similar
083   idea for improving the data-efficiency of RL algorithms on robotic tasks. In particular, we present
084   a novel RL algorithm that learns a critic network that outputs Q-values over a sequence of actions
085   (see Figure 2). By training the critic network to explicitly learn the consequence of taking a series
086   of current and future actions, our algorithm enables the RL agents to effectively learn useful value
087   functions from noisy trajectories. We build this algorithm upon a recent value-based RL algorithm
088   that learns RL agents to zoom-into continuous action space in a coarse-to-fine manner (Seo et al.,
089   2024), thus we refer to our algorithm as Coarse-to-fine Q-Network with Action Sequence (CQN-AS).
090   To evaluate the generality and capabilities of CQN-AS, we study CQN-AS on various setups with
091   sparse and dense rewards, and with or without demonstrations. In BiGym benchmark (Chernyadev
092   et al., 2024), which provides human-collected demonstrations for mobile bi-manual manipulation
093   tasks, CQN-AS outperforms various model-free RL and BC baselines (Yarats et al., 2022; Zhao
094   et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2024). Moreover, in HumanoidBench (Sferrazza et al., 2024), which consists
095   of densely-rewarded humanoid control tasks, we show that CQN-AS can also be effective without
096   demonstrations, outperforming prior model-free RL baselines (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Seo et al., 2024).
097
      Finally, in RLBench (James et al., 2020), which provides synthetic demonstrations generated via
      motion-planning, CQN-AS achieves similar performance as model-free RL and BC baselines on
098
      most tasks, but significantly better performance on several long-horizon manipulation tasks.

103   Problem setup We mainly consider a robotic control problem which we formulate as a partially
104   observable Markov decision process (Kaelbling et al., 1998; Sutton & Barto, 2018). At each time
105   step t, an RL agent encounters an observation ot , executes an action at , receives a reward rt+1 ,
106   and encounters a new observation ot+1 from the environment. Because the observation ot does not
107   contain full information about the internal state of the environment, in this work, we use a stack of
      past observations as inputs to the RL agent by following the common practice in Mnih et al. (2015).

148   3    C OARSE - TO - FINE Q-N ETWORK WITH ACTION S EQUENCE
149
150   We present Coarse-to-fine Q-Network with Action Sequence (CQN-AS), a value-based RL algorithm
151   that learns a critic network that outputs Q-values for a sequence of actions at:t+K = {at , ..., at+K−1 }
152
      for a given observation ot . Our main motivation for this design comes from one of the key ideas
      in recent behavior-cloning (BC) approaches, i.e., predicting action sequences, which helps resolve
153
      ambiguity when approximating noisy, multi-modal distributions of expert demonstrations (Zhao et al.,
154
      2023; Chi et al., 2023). Similarly, by explicitly learning Q-values of both current and future actions
155   from the given state, our approach aims to mitigate the challenge of learning Q-values with noisy
156   trajectories from exploratory behaviors or human-collected demonstrations.
157
158
      This section describes how we design our critic network with action sequence (see Section 3.1) and
      how we utilize action sequence outputs to control robots at each time step (see Section 3.2). The
159
      overview of our algorithm is available in Figure 2.

193   3.2    ACTION E XECUTION AND T RAINING D ETAILS
194
      While the idea of using action sequence is simple, there are two important yet small details for
195   effectively training RL agents with action sequence: (i) how we execute actions at each time step to
196   control robots and (ii) how we store training data and sample batches for training.
197
198   Executing action with temporal ensemble With the policy that outputs an action sequence at:t+K ,
199   one important question is how to execute actions at time step i ∈ {t, ..., t + K − 1}. For this, we use
200   the idea of Zhao et al. (2023) that utilizes temporal ensemble,
                                                                P which computes
                                                                           P          at:t+K every time step,
201   saves it to a buffer, and executes a weighted average i wi at−i / wi where wi = exp(−m ∗ i)
202   denotes a weight that assigns higher value to more recent actions. We find this scheme outperforms
203   the alternative of computing at:t+K every K steps and executing each action for subsequent K steps
204
      on most tasks we considered, except on several tasks that need reactive control.
205
      Storing training data from environment interaction When storing samples from online envi-
206
      ronment interaction, we store a transition (ot , ât , rt+1 , ot+1 ) where ât denotes an action executed at
207
      time step t. For instance, if we use temporal ensemble for action execution, ât is a weighted average
208   of action outputs obtained from previous K time steps.
209
210   Sampling training data from a replay buffer When sampling training data from the replay buffer,
211   we sample a transition with action sequence, i.e., (ot , ât:t+K , rt+1 , ot+1 ). If we sample time step t
212   near the end of episode so that we do not have enough data to construct a full action sequence, we fill
213   the action sequence with null actions. In particular, in position control where we specify the position
214   of joints or end effectors, we repeat the action from the last step so that the agent learns not to change
215   the position. On the other hand, in torque control where we specify the force to apply to joints, we
      set the action after the last step to zero so that agent learns to not to apply force.

229   4       E XPERIMENT
230
231   We study CQN-AS on 53 robotic tasks spanning mobile bi-manual manipulation, whole-body control,
232   and tabletop manipulation tasks from BiGym (Chernyadev et al., 2024), HumanoidBench (Sferrazza
233
      et al., 2024), and RLBench (James et al., 2020) environments (see Figure 3 for examples of robotic
      tasks). These tasks with sparse and dense rewards, with or without vision sensors, and with or without
234
      demonstrations, allow for evaluating the capabilities and limitations of our algorithm. In particular,
235
      our experiments are designed to investigate the following questions:
236
237             • Can CQN-AS quickly match the performance of a recent BC algorithm (Zhao et al., 2023)
238               and surpass it through online learning? How does CQN-AS compare to previous model-free
239               RL algorithms (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Yarats et al., 2022; Seo et al., 2024)?
240             • What is the contribution of each component in CQN-AS?
241             • Under which conditions is CQN-AS effective? When does CQN-AS fail?
242
243   Baselines for fine-grained control tasks with demonstrations For tasks that need high-precision
244   control, e.g., manipulation tasks from BiGym and RLBench, we consider model-free RL baselines
245   that aim to learn deterministic policies, as we find that stochastic policies struggle to solve such
246   fine-grained control tasks. Specifically, we consider (i) Coarse-to-fine Q-Network (CQN; Seo et al.
247   2024), a value-based RL algorithm that learns to zoom-into continuous action space in a coarse-to-fine
248   manner, and (ii) DrQ-v2+, an optimized demo-driven variant of a model-free actor-critic algorithm
249   DrQ-v2 (Yarats et al., 2022) that uses a deterministic policy algorithm and data augmentation. We
250   further consider (iii) Action Chunking Transformer (ACT; Zhao et al. 2023), a BC algorithm that
251
      trains a transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) policy to predict action sequence and utilizes temporal
      ensemble for executing actions, as our highly-optimized BC baseline.
252
253
      Baselines for whole-body control tasks with dense reward For locomotion tasks with dense
254
      reward, we consider (i) Soft Actor-Critic (SAC; Haarnoja et al. 2018), a model-free actor-critic RL
255   algorithm that maximizes action entropy, and (ii) Coarse-to-fine Q-Network (CQN; Seo et al. 2024).
256   Moreover, although it is not the goal of this paper to compare against model-based RL algorithms,
257   we also consider two model-based baselines: (iii) DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023), a model-based
258   RL algorithm that learns a latent dynamics model and a policy using imagined trajectories and (iv)
259   TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al., 2024), a model-based RL algorithm that learns a latent dynamics model
260   and utilizes local trajectory optimization in imagined latent trajectories.
261
262   Implementation details For training with expert demonstrations, we follow the setup of Seo et al.
263   (2024). Specifically, we keep a separate replay buffer that stores demonstrations and sample half
264
      of training data from demonstrations. We also relabel successful online episodes as demonstrations
      and store them in the demonstration replay buffer. For CQN-AS, we use an auxiliary BC loss from
265
      Seo et al. (2024) based on large margin loss (Hester et al., 2018). For actor-critic baselines, we
266
      use an auxiliary BC loss that minimizes L2 loss between the policy outputs and expert actions. We
267   implement CQN-AS based on a publicly available source code of CQN2 based on PyTorch (Paszke
268   et al., 2019). We will release the full source code upon publication.

306    4.1                        B I G YM E XPERIMENTS
307
308   We study CQN-AS on mobile bi-manual manipulation tasks from BiGym (Chernyadev et al., 2024).
309   BiGym’s human-collected demonstrations are often noisy and multi-modal, posing challenges to RL
310
      algorithms which should effectively leverage demonstrations for solving sparsely-rewarded tasks.
311
312   Setup Because we find that not all demonstrations from BiGym benchmark can be successfully
313   replayed3 , we replay all the demonstrations and only use the successful ones as demonstrations.
314
      We do not discard ones that fail to be replayed, but we use them as training data with zero reward
      because they can still be useful as failure experiences. To avoid training with too few demon-
315

456
457    Effect of action sequence length Figure 7a shows the performance of CQN-AS with different
458    action sequence lengths on two BiGym tasks. We find that training the critic network with longer
459    action sequences improves and stabilizes performance.
460
461   RL objective is crucial for strong performance Figure 7b shows the performance of CQN-AS
462   without RL objective that trains the model only with BC objective on successful demonstrations. We
463   find this baseline significantly underperforms CQN-AS, which shows that RL objective is indeed
464   enabling the agent to learn from online trial-and-error experiences.
465
466    Effect of temporal ensemble Figure 7c shows that performance largely degrades without temporal
467
       ensemble on Saucepan To Hop as temporal ensemble induces a smooth motion and thus improves
       performance in fine-grained control tasks. But we also find that temporal ensemble can be harmful
468
       on Reach Target Single. We hypothesize this is because temporal ensemble often makes it difficult
469
       to refine behaviors based on recent visual observations. Nonetheless, we use temporal ensemble for
470    all the tasks as we find it helps on most tasks and we aim to use the same set of hyperparameters.
471
472    Failure case: Torque control Figure 7d shows that CQN-AS underperforms CQN on locomotion
473    tasks with torque control. We hypothesize this is because a sequence of joint positions usually has a
474    semantic meaning in joint spaces, making it easier to learn with, when compared to learning how to
475    apply a sequence of torques. Addressing this failure case is an interesting future direction.

479
      Behavior cloning with action sequence Recent behavior cloning approaches have shown that
480   predicting a sequence of actions enables the policy to effectively imitate noisy expert trajectories and
481   helps in dealing with idle actions from human pauses during data collection (Zhao et al., 2023; Chi
482   et al., 2023). In particular, Zhao et al. (2023) train a transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017) that
483   predicts action sequence and Chi et al. (2023) train a denoising diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) that
484   approximates the action distributions. This idea has been extended to multi-task setup (Bharadhwaj
485   et al., 2024), mobile manipulation (Fu et al., 2024b) and humanoid control (Fu et al., 2024a). Our
      work is inspired by this line of work and proposed to learn RL agents with action sequence.


                                                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Reinforcement learning with action sequence In the context of reinforcement learning, Medini &
487   Shrivastava (2019) proposed to pre-compute frequent action sequences from expert demonstrations
488   and augment the action space with these sequences. However, this idea introduces additional
489   complexity and is not scalable to setups without demonstrations. One recent work relevant to ours
490   is Saanum et al. (2024) that encourages a sequence of actions from RL agents to be predictable
491   and smooth. But this differs from our work in that it uses the concept of action sequence only for
492   computing the penalty term. Recently, Ankile et al. (2024) point out that RL with action sequence
493   is challenging and instead proposes to use RL for learning a single-step policy that corrects action
494   sequence predictions from BC. In contrast, our work shows that training RL agents with action
495
      sequence is feasible and leads to improved performance compared to prior RL algorithms.
496
497   6    C ONCLUSION
498
499   We presented Coarse-to-fine Q-Network with Action Sequence (CQN-AS), a value-based RL algo-
500   rithm that trains a critic network that outputs Q-values over action sequences. Extensive experiments
501   in benchmarks with various setups show that our idea not only improves the performance of the base
502   algorithm but also allows for solving complex tasks where prior RL algorithms completely fail.
503
      We believe our work will be strong evidence that shows RL can realize its promise to develop robots
504   that can continually improve through online trial-and-error experiences, surpassing the performance
505   of BC approaches. We are excited about future directions, including real-world RL with humanoid
506   robots, incorporating advanced critic architectures (Kapturowski et al., 2023; Chebotar et al., 2023;
507   Springenberg et al., 2024), bootstrapping RL agents from imitation learning (Hu et al., 2023; Xing
508   et al., 2024) or offline RL (Nair et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021), extending the idea to recent model-based
509   RL approaches (Hafner et al., 2023; Hansen et al., 2024), to name but a few.

811   GTX 1080Ti, NVIDIA Titan XP, and NVIDIA RTX 2080Ti with 11 or 12GB VRAM. With 2080Ti
