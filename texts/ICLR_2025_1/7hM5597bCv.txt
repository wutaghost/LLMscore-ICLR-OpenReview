                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011
                  We propose a novel offline reinforcement learning (offline RL) approach, intro-
012
                  ducing the Diffusion-model-guided Implicit Q-learning with Adaptive Revalua-
013               tion (DIAR) framework. We address two key challenges in offline RL: out-of-
014               distribution samples and long-horizon problems. We leverage diffusion models to
015               learn state-action sequence distributions and incorporate value functions for more
016               balanced and adaptive decision-making. DIAR introduces an Adaptive Revalua-
017               tion mechanism that dynamically adjusts decision lengths by comparing current
018               and future state values, enabling flexible long-term decision-making. Furthermore,
019               we address Q-value overestimation by combining Q-network learning with a value
020               function guided by a diffusion model. The diffusion model generates diverse latent
021
                  trajectories, enhancing policy robustness and generalization. As demonstrated in
                  tasks like Maze2D, AntMaze, and Kitchen, DIAR consistently outperforms state-
022
                  of-the-art algorithms in long-horizon, sparse-reward environments.

      Offline reinforcement learning (offline RL) is a type of reinforcement learning where an agent learns
042   a policy using pre-collected datasets instead of gathering data through direct interactions with the en-
043   vironment (Fujimoto et al., 2019). By avoiding real-world interactions, offline RL eliminates safety
044   concerns. Furthermore, it makes efficient use of the collected data, which is especially beneficial
045   when gathering new data is costly or time-consuming. However, offline RL depends on the dataset,
046   meaning the policy it learns may perform poorly if the data is low quality or biased. Moreover, a
047   distributional shift can occur during the process of learning from offline data (Levine et al., 2020),
048   leading to degraded performance in the real environment.
049   To overcome the limitations of offline RL, existing research have been made to address these is-
050   sues by leveraging diffusion models, a type of generative model (Janner et al., 2022). Incorporating
051   diffusion models allows for learning the overall distribution of the state and action spaces, allow-
052   ing decisions to be made based on this knowledge. Methods such as Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022)
053   and Decision Diffuser (DD) (Ajay et al., 2023) use diffusion models to predict decisions not au-
      toregressively one step at a time, but instead by inferring the entire decision for the length of the


                                                               1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      horizon at once, achieving strong performance in long-horizon tasks. Additionally, methods like
055   LDCQ (Venkatraman et al., 2024) propose using latent diffusion models to learn the Q-function,
056   allowing the Q-function to make more appropriate predictions for out-of-distribution state-actions.
057
058
      Recent studies of diffusion-based offline RL methods, often bypass the use of the Q-function or rely
      on other offline Q-learning methods (Janner et al., 2022; Ajay et al., 2023). However, recent research
059
      has proposed a novel approach that does not avoid the Q-function but instead leverages diffusion
060
      models to assist in Q-learning (Wang et al., 2023; Venkatraman et al., 2024). This approach enables
061   handling a wide range of Q-values for diverse states and actions. We found that using samples
062   generated by diffusion models can improve the agent’s performance.
063
064
      Therefore, we propose Diffusion-model-guided Implicit Q-learning with Adaptive Revaluation
      (DIAR), which integrates the value function and data sampled from the diffusion model into the
065
      training and decision process. This approach provides more objective assessment of the current
066
      state, enabling the Q-function to achieve a balance between long-horizon decision-making and step-
067   by-step refinement. In the training process, the Q-function and value function alternates between
068   learning from the dataset and samples generated by the diffusion model, allowing it to adapt to a
069   wide variety of scenarios. Additionally, value function also helps to reevaluate the current decision
070   to explore new action sequences and select a more optimal path.
071
      DIAR consistently outperforms existing offline RL algorithms, especially in environments that in-
072
      volve complex route planning and long-horizon state-action pairs like Figure 2. Additionally, as
073   shown in Figure 1, DIAR achieves state-of-the-art performance in environments such as Maze2D,
074   AntMaze, and Kitchen (Fu et al., 2020). This research highlights the potential of diffusion models
075   to enhance both policy abstraction and adaptability in offline RL, with significant implications for
076   real-world applications in robotics and autonomous systems.

      2.1   O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
096
097   Offline reinforcement learning (offline RL), also referred to as batch reinforcement learning, has
098   gained significant attention in recent years due to its potential in learning effective policies from
099   pre-collected datasets without further interaction with the environment. This paradigm is particularly
100   useful in real-world applications where exploration can be costly or dangerous, such as healthcare,
101   robotics (Kalashnikov et al., 2018), and autonomous driving.
102   One of the primary challenges in offline RL is the issue of out-of-distribution actions (Kumar et al.,
103   2019), where a learned policy selects actions not well represented in the offline dataset. To address
104   this, several works have introduced behavior regularization techniques that constrain the policy to
105   remain close to the behavior policy seen in the offline data. Among these, Conservative Q-learning
106   (CQL) introduces a conservative Q-function that underestimates the value of out-of-distribution ac-
107   tions, reducing the likelihood of the learned policy selecting potentially harmful actions (Kumar
      et al., 2020). By minimizing the overestimation of value functions, CQL facilitates more reliable


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      policy learning from offline data. Another notable approach is Implicit Q-learning (IQL), which im-
109   plicitly regularizes the learned Q-function by keeping it close to the empirical value of the actions
110   observed in the dataset (Kostrikov et al., 2022). This prevents the over-optimization of Q-values
111   for actions that are rarely or never observed in the offline dataset. Additionally, Batch-Constrained
112   Q-learning (BCQ) imposes direct limitations on the learned policy to prevent deviations from the
113   actions observed in the offline dataset (Fujimoto et al., 2019). BCQ introduces a constraint that en-
114   sures the learned policy selects actions similar to the behavior policy, thus avoiding the exploitation
115   of inaccurate Q-value estimates for unseen actions.

117   2.2    D IFFUSION - BASED P LANNING IN O FFLINE RL
118
119
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) have shown remarkable performance
      in fields such as image inpainting (Lugmayr et al., 2022) and image generation (Ramesh et al., 2022;
120
      Saharia et al., 2022). Recent research has extended the application of diffusion models beyond image
121
      domains to address classical trajectory optimization challenges in offline RL. One prominent model,
122   Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022), directly learns trajectory distributions and generates tailored trajec-
123   tories based on situational demands. By prioritizing trajectory accuracy over single-step precision,
124   Diffuser mitigates compounding errors and adapts to novel tasks or goals unseen during training.
125   Additionally, Decision Diffuser (DD) was introduced, which predicts the next state using a state
126   diffusion model and leverages inverse dynamics for decision-making (Ajay et al., 2023). Further-
127   more, a method called Latent Diffusion-Constrained Q-learning (LDCQ) has been proposed, which
128   combines latent diffusion models with Q-learning to reduce extrapolation errors (Venkatraman et al.,
129   2024). Emerging methods also focus on learning interpretable skills from visual and language inputs
130
      and applying conditional planning via diffusion models (Liang et al., 2024). Approaches that gener-
      ate goal-divergent trajectories using Gaussian noise and facilitate reverse training through denoising
131
      processes have also been explored (Jain & Ravanbakhsh, 2023).

      4     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
193
      Using diffusion models to address long-horizon tasks typically involves training over the full tra-
194
      jectory length (Janner et al., 2022). This approach differs from autoregressive methods that focus
195
      on selecting the best action at each step, as it learns the entire action sequence over the horizon.
196   This allows the model to learn long sequences of decisions at once and generate a large number of
197   actions in a single pass. However, predicting decisions across the entire horizon may not always lead
198   to optimal outcomes, as the primary goal is to generate a sequence of decisions corresponding to the
199   sequence length.
200
      Additionally, there is a well-known problem of overestimating the Q-value when training a Q-
201
      network (Hasselt et al., 2016; 2018; Fu et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2020).
202   This occurs when certain actions, appearing intermittently, are assigned a high Q(s, a) value. In
203   these cases, the state may not actually hold high value, but the Q-value becomes “lucky” and in-
204   flated. Therefore, it is essential to ensure that the Q-network does not overestimate and can correctly
205   assess the value based on the current state.
206
      To resolve both of these issues, we propose Diffusion-model-guided Implicit Q-learning with Adap-
207
      tive Revaluation (DIAR), introducing a value function to assess the value of each situation. Unlike
208   the Q-network, which learns the value of both state and action, the state-value function learns only
209   the value of the state. By introducing constraints from the value function, we can train a more bal-
210   anced Q-network and, during the decision-making phase, make more optimal predictions with the
211   help of the value function.

           4.2     A DAPTIVE R EVALUATION IN P OLICY E XECUTION
312
313        DIAR method reforms a decision if the value of the current state is higher than the value of the state
314        after making a decision over the horizon length H. We refer to this process as Adaptive Revaluation.
315        Using the value-network Vη , if the current state’s value V (st ) is greater than V (st+H ), the value
316        after making a decision for H steps, the method generates a new latent vector zt from the current
317        state st and continues the decision-making process. When predicting over the horizon length, there
318        may be cases where taking a different action midway through the horizon would be more optimal.
319        In such cases, the value-network Vη checks this, and if the condition is met, a new latent vector is
320
           generated.
321        Adaptive Revaluation uses the difference in value to examine whether the agent’s predicted decision
322        is optimal. Since the current state st can be obtained directly from the environment, it is easy to
323        compute the value V (st ) of the current state st . Whether the current trajectory is optimal can be
           determined using a state decoder fθ (st+H |st , zt ). By inputting the current state st and latent vector

523   6       C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this study, we proposed Diffusion-model-guided Implicit Q-learning with Adaptive Revaluation
526   (DIAR), which leverages diffusion models to improve abstraction capabilities and train more adap-
527   tive agents in offline RL. First, we introduced an Adaptive Revaluation algorithm based on the value
528   function, which allows for long-horizon predictions while enabling the agent to flexibly revise its
529   decisions to discover more optimal ones. Second, we propose an Diffusion-model-guided Implicit
530   Q-learning. Offline RL faces the limitation of difficulty in evaluating out-of-distribution state-action
531   pairs, as it learns from a fixed dataset. By leveraging the diffusion model, a generative model, we
532   balance the learning of the value function and Q-function to cover a broader range of cases. By com-
533   bining these two methods, we achieved state-of-the-art performance in long-horizon sparse reward
534
      tasks such as Maze2D, AntMaze, and Kitchen. Our approach is particularly strong in long-horizon
      sparse reward situations, where it is challenging to assess the current value. Additionally, a key ad-
535
      vantage of our method is that it performs well without requiring extensive hyper-parameter tuning
536
      for each task. We believe that the latent diffusion model holds significant strengths in offline RL and
537   has high potential for applications in various fields such as robotics.

      A     E XPERIMENTS D ETAILS
649
650
      DIAR consists of three main components: the β-VAE for learning latent skills, the latent diffusion
651   model for learning distributions through latent vectors, and the Q-function, which learns the value of
652   state-latent vector pairs and selects the best latent. These three models are trained sequentially, and
653   when learning the same task, the earlier models can be reused. Detailed model settings and hyperpa-
654   rameters are discussed in the next section. For more detailed code implementation and process, you
655   can refer directly to the code on GitHub.
656
657   A.1    β-VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER
658
659   The β-VAE consists of an encoder, policy decoder, state prior, and state decoder. The encoder uses
660   two stacked bidirectional GRUs. The output of the GRU is used to compute the mean and standard
661
      deviation. Each GRU output is passed through an MLP to calculate the mean and standard deviation,
      which are then used to compute the latent vector. This latent vector is used by the state prior, state
662
      decoder, and policy decoder. The policy decoder takes the latent vector and the current state as input
663
      to predict the current action. The state decoder takes the latent vector and the current state to predict
664   the future state. Lastly, the state prior learns the distribution of the latent vector for the current state,
665   ensuring that the latent vector generated by the encoder is trained similarly through KL divergence.
666
667
      In Maze2D, H = 30 is used; in AntMaze and Kitchen, H = 20 is used. The diffusion model for the
      diffusion prior used in β-VAE training employs a transformer architecture. This model differs from
668
      the latent diffusion model discussed in the next section, and they are trained independently. Training
669
      the β-VAE for too many epochs can lead to overfitting of the latent vector, which can negatively
670   impact the next stage.
