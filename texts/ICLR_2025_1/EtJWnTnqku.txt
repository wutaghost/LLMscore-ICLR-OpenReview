009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This study presents Medical Vision Generalist (MVG), the first foundation model
012            capable of handling various medical imaging tasks—such as cross-modal synthesis,
013            image segmentation, denoising, and inpainting—within a unified image-to-image
014            generation framework. Specifically, MVG employs an in-context generation strat-
015            egy that standardizes the handling of inputs and outputs as images. By treating
016            these tasks as an image generation process conditioned on prompt image-label
017            pairs and input images, this approach enables a flexible unification of various
018
               tasks, even those spanning different modalities and datasets. To capitalize on both
               local and global context, we design a hybrid method combining masked image
019
               modeling with autoregressive training for conditional image generation. This
020
               hybrid approach yields the most robust performance across all involved medical
021            imaging tasks. To rigorously evaluate MVG’s capabilities, we curated the first
022            comprehensive generalist medical vision benchmark, comprising 13 datasets and
023            spanning four imaging modalities (CT, MRI, X-ray, and micro-ultrasound). Our
024            results consistently establish MVG’s superior performance, outperforming existing
025            vision generalists, such as Painter and LVM. Furthermore, MVG exhibits strong
026            scalability, with its performance demonstrably improving when trained on a more
027            diverse set of tasks, and can be effectively adapted to unseen datasets with only
028            minimal task-specific samples. The code will be available soon.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The precise interpretation of medical images is imperative for timely disease detection, diagnosis, and
      treatment Cheng et al. (2022b); De Fauw et al. (2018). Deep-learning based models have emerged
034
      as powerful tools in medical image analysis, tackling various challenges spanning from segmenting
035
      specific anatomical structures Ji et al. (2022); Luo et al. (2021); Fu et al. (2021), localizing single
036   organ diseases Zhu et al. (2019); Zhao et al. (2021); Huo et al. (2020); Cheng et al. (2022a); Ardila
037   et al. (2019); Kim et al. (2022); Heller et al. (2021), to cross-modality image synthesis on brain
038   MRI Xie et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Dayarathna et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023). However, these
039   models, often referred to as specialist models, are typically customized for specific tasks, modalities,
040   or anatomical regions. While this specialization often results in exceptional performance in certain
041   contexts, it can lead to a severe performance drop when applied to new tasks or when tasked with
042   training multi-domain data.
043   To address this challenge, recently, there has been a partial shift of research focus in developing
044   generalist medical AI models Moor et al. (2023); Tu et al. (2023), which necessitate only a single
045   training phase but are capable of wide application across a diverse array of medical tasks. Specifically,
046   these generalist frameworks unify input and output spaces, allowing straightforward adaptation to
047   various tasks through user-provided prompts. While existing generalist medical AI models like
048   MedSAM have demonstrated impressive performance Ma et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2023); Butoi
049   et al. (2023), their applicability in medical visual tasks remains limited (e.g., to segmentation tasks
050
      only). A unified, truly generalist vision model capable of addressing a vast array of medical imaging
      tasks remains a critical missing piece in the current medical research landscape.
051
052   Motivated by the remarkable success of in-context learning in natural language processing Brown
053   et al. (2020a); OpenAI (2023) and computer vision Bai et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b;a), we hereby
      propose Medical Vision Generalist (MVG), the first generalist model in the medical imaging domain.

072   Specifically, MVG leverages an in-context learning framework to unify a set of medical imaging
073   tasks, including cross-modal synthesis, denoising, segmentation, and inpainting across modalities
074   like CT, MRI, X-ray, and Micro-ultrasound. In contrast to prior task- and data-specific medical
075   AI models, MVG offers adaptability to new data with minimal labeled samples, eliminating the
076   need for retraining. To achieve this, MVG first standardizes the input/output space using in-context
077   coloring, which maps various tasks into a single-channel coloring scheme. This removes the need for
078
      task-specific heads, thus regulating the model to learn exclusively from prompts. Subsequently, tasks
      are unified through conditional image generation, where MVG generates the output conditioned on
079
      both the task prompt and a sample image.
080
081   To capture both local and global context, we devise a hybrid strategy that combines masked im-
082   age modeling and autoregressive training for conditional image generation. The former involves
083   concatenating prompt images, labels, task inputs, and labels, followed by random masking; the
084
      latter constructs prompt image-label pairs, task inputs, and labels as long visual sentences. During
      inference, MVG conditions predictions on the prompts selected from locations closely matching the
085
      task images, ensuring contextual relevance and guidance that enhances output quality and consistency.
086
087   Furthermore, we have curated the first unified medical imaging benchmark, encompassing 13 datasets
088   spanning a range of human anatomies (e.g., abdomen, pelvis, brain, chest) and modalities (e.g., CT,
089   MRI, X-ray, micro-ultrasound). This new benchmark enables a comprehensive assessment of our
090
      MVG models. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our MVG in performing various
      medical vision tasks with only one model. As illustrated in Figure 2, our MVG outperforms the
091
      previous generalist models by a large margin. For instance, our MVG achieves 0.735 mIoU on all
092
      segmentation tasks and outperforms the previous best vision generalist by 0.123 mIoU. Furthermore,
093   our MVG demonstrates two intriguing properties: 1) it scales well with multiple tasks and datasets,
094   suggesting its potential to excel further as diverse datasets continue to emerge; and 2) it can efficiently
095   generalize to new datasets, with only a few specific examples needed for each task.

      Universal Models and In-Context Learning. The advent of the universal Transformer architecture
133   and its success in generative pretraining has inspired the development of universal models that
134   tackle a wide range of computer vision tasks Chen et al. (2021b; 2022); Lu et al. (2022); Wang
135   et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a). In-context learning is a novel few-shot learning
136   paradigm that emerged in large language models and was first proposed by GPT-3 Brown et al.
137   (2020b). Specifically, in-context learning enables one model to perform different tasks with only
138   in-context examples as prompts. While the prompts for language models are mostly defined as a
139   few sentences, in-context learning in other domains is still in an early exploration stage. As one of
140   the earliest works, Flamingo Alayrac et al. (2022) extends the modality of in-context learning with
141
      language instructions and sequences of images and videos. Perceiver-IO Jaegle et al. (2021) uses the
      Transformer architecture for a general-purpose model that handles data from arbitrary settings like
142
      natural language, visual understanding, multi-modal reasoning, and StarCraft II. AD Laskin et al.
143
      (2022) introduces in-context learning to reinforcement learning with algorithm distillation. DPT Lee
144   et al. (2024) provides a sample-efficient RL algorithm with strong in-context decision-making. In this
145   paper, we use a sequence of paired medical images to build a vision model with in-context learning
146   ability, unifying 13 medical tasks as a generation task.

149   3     M ETHOD
150
151   Unlike previous medical AI models, which are specific to one or a few predefined imaging tasks and
152   produce a predetermined set of outputs, the proposed MVG aims to offer unprecedented flexibility
153   across tasks, modalities, and datasets. The key idea is to unify medical imaging tasks, such as
154   cross-modal synthesis, image segmentation, denoising, and inpainting, within an image-to-image
155   generation framework.
156
157   3.1   TASKS
158
159   Our MVG is designed to address various medical imaging tasks, with a particular emphasis in
160   this study on segmentation, cross-modal synthesis, inpainting, and denoising tasks for which well-
161   represented public datasets are available. However, it is crucial to note that its design should be
      widely applicable to any image-to-image generation task.

162
      Segmentation. Medical image segmentation, including CT, MRI, X-ray, and Micro-ultrasound
163   segmentation, involves dividing an image obtained from these modalities into distinct segments to
164   isolate regions of interest, such as organs or abnormalities. The input space for these tasks typically
165   consists of images from CT, MRI, X-ray, or Micro-ultrasound scans. The output space is represented
166   by a mask, where each value (excluding the background) in the mask corresponds to a different class
167   or type of object, such as a liver or kidney.
168
      Cross-modal synthesis. Cross-modal synthesis aims to generate images in one modality from images
169   of another modality for the same subject, aiding in visualization and facilitating multi-modal medical
170   image analysis. The input space and output space are different medical imaging modalities.
171
172
      Brain image inpainting. In the context of brain image processing, inpainting refers to the process of
      synthesizing healthy brain tissue in regions affected by glioma, a type of brain tumor Kofler et al.
173
      (2023). Inpainting allows professionals to effectively utilize non-standard imaging protocols and
174
      directly apply brain parcellation tools to facilitate treatment planning. The input space is the corrupted
175   brain MRI and the output space is the corresponding brain MRI restoring the affected regions to a
176   normal state.
177
178
      Denoising. Denoising aims to reconstruct full-dose CT images from low-dose CT images, allowing
      for reduced radiation doses during CT scans while preserving diagnostic image quality. The input
179
      space is the scanned CT image with low-dose radiation, while the output space is the corresponding
180
      image with full-dose radiation.

      Assume an input image is denoted as x ∈ RH×W , the output could be a segmentation map, a
185
      synthesized brain image in the target modality, a restored normal brain MRI, or a full-dose CT image
186
      of the same size. To unify the output space of images across tasks, our MVG adopts a strategy beyond
187   task-specific heads: mapping different tasks into a single-channel coloring scheme, inspired by Wang
188   et al. (2023a;b). Specifically, we explore three different in-context coloring methods for segmentation
189   that circumvent reliance on label values, including binary, pre-defined, and random colorization.
190
      Binary colorization. We break down the problem of segmenting multiple classes into individual
191
      binary segmentation tasks, each focusing on separating one class from the background. Specifically,
192
      if a segmentation mask contains Nk foreground classes, we simply split it to Nk binary masks.
193   However, this requires multiple inferences when an image contains more than one foreground class.
194
195   Pre-defined colorization. In this approach, we allocate a predetermined unique color to each
196
      segmentation mask derived from diverse datasets. Suppose there are K segmentation datasets, with
      each dataset containing Nk classes. Consequently, the nth class of the kth dataset is assigned the
197            k−1
               P
198   value of     i ∗ Ni + n. Note that different tasks may involve classes with identical semantics; for
199            i=1
      example, both the AMOS segmentation dataset Ji et al. (2022) and the Synapse dataset Landman
200
      et al. (2015) include the class "Liver". However, distinct colors are assigned to the same class across
201
      different tasks.
202
203   Random colorization. The use of pre-defined colors may restrict the adaptability and efficacy of
204   MVG, as they can cause the model to focus on learning tasks based on the color of the prompt rather
205
      than the contextual information Wang et al. (2023b). To address this limitation, we build a set of
      colors and randomly sample colors for different semantics in one iteration but the same semantic in
206
      the prompt label and task label share the same color.
207
208   Except for medical image segmentation, the outputs of all other tasks in this study do not involve
209   categorical values that need to be predicted. Therefore, we do not apply coloring for these tasks.

      5      C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we present MVG, a versatile model capable of handling various medical imaging tasks,
529   including cross-modal synthesis, segmentation, denoising, and inpainting, within a unified image-
530   to-image generation framework. MVG employs an in-context generation strategy to standardize
531
      inputs and outputs as images, allowing flexible task unification across various modalities and datasets.
      A hybrid approach combining masked image modeling and autoregressive training proved the
532
      most effective. To thoroughly assess MVG’s potential and limitations, we also curate the first
533
      comprehensive generalist medical vision benchmark consisting of 13 datasets across 4 imaging
534   modalities, including CT, MRI, X-ray, and Micro-ultrasound. Experiment results demonstrate that
535   MVG consistently outperforms existing vision generalists. Benefiting from the in-context learning
536   scheme, MVG demonstrates exceptional flexibility, scalability, and potential for generalization to
537   unseen datasets with minimal samples. We will make our code and benchmark publicly available to
538   encourage future research in medical AI generalists. We believe MVG can serve as a stepstone to
539   make medical imaging tools more accessible to clinical researchers, other scientists, or beyond, by
      lowering the bar of machine learning expertise, computational resources, and human labor.
