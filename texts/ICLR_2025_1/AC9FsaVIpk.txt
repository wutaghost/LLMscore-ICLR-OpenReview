                                                      Abstract
010
                 Linear attention methods provide a strong alternative to softmax attention as they
011              allow for efficient recurrent decoding. Recent research has focused on enhancing
012              standard linear attention by incorporating gating while retaining its computational
013              benefits. Such Gated Linear Attention (GLA) architectures include highly competi-
                 tive models such as Mamba and RWKV. In this work, we examine the in-context
014              learning capabilities of the GLA model and make the following contributions. We
015              show that a multilayer GLA can implement a general class of Weighted Precondi-
016              tioned Gradient Descent (WPGD) algorithms with data-dependent weights. These
017
                 weights are induced by the gating and allows the model to control the contribution
                 of individual tokens to prediction. To further understand the mechanics of weight-
018              ing, we introduce a novel data model with multitask prompts and characterize the
019              optimization landscape of the problem of learning a WPGD algorithm. We identify
020              mild conditions under which there is a unique (global) minimum up to scaling
                 invariance, and the associated WPGD algorithm is unique as well. Finally, we
021              translate these findings to explore the optimization landscape of GLA and shed
022              light on how gating facilitates context-aware learning and when it is provably better
023              than vanilla linear attention.

025   1     Introduction
026
027   The Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017) has become the de facto standard for language
028   modeling tasks. The key component of the Transformer is the self-attention mechanism, which
029
      computes softmax-based similarities between all token pairs. Despite its success, the self-attention
      mechanism has quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length, making it computationally
030   expensive for long sequences. To address this issue, a growing body of work has proposed near-linear
031   time approaches to sequence modeling. The initial approaches included linear attention and state-
032   space models, both achieving O(1) inference complexity per generated token, thanks to their recurrent
      form. While these initial architectures typically do not match softmax-attention in performance, recent
033
      recurrent models such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), mLSTM (Beck et al., 2024),
034   GLA Transformer (Yang et al., 2023), and RWKV-6 (Peng et al., 2024) achieve highly competitive
035   results with the softmax Transformer. Notably, as highlighted in Yang et al. (2023), these architectures
036   can be viewed as variants of gated linear attention (GLA), which incorporates a gating mechanism
      within the recurrence of linear attention.
037

044
      The central objective of this work is to enhance the mathematical understanding of the GLA mecha-
045   nism. In-context learning (ICL), one of the most remarkable features of modern sequence models,
046   provides a powerful framework to achieve this aim. ICL refers to the ability of a sequence model
047   to implicitly infer functional relationships from the demonstrations provided in its context window
      (Brown, 2020; Min et al., 2022). It is inherently related to the modelвАЩs ability to emulate learning
048
      algorithms. Notably, ICL has been a major topic of empirical and theoretical interest in recent years.
049   More specifically, a series of works have examined the approximation and optimization characteris-
050   tics of linear attention, and have provably connected linear attention to the preconditioned gradient
051   descent algorithm (Von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Given that the
      GLA recurrence in (1) has a richer design space, this leads us to ask:
052
053              Q: What are the ICL capabilities of the GLA mechanism? What learning algorithm
                 does it emulate when presented with an ICL task?

      1.1   Related work
087
088   We discuss prior literature under two topics.
089
      Efficient sequence models. Recent sequence model proposals вАУ such as RetNet (Sun et al., 2023),
090   Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), xLSTM (Beck et al., 2024), GLA Transformer (Yang et al., 2023), RWKV-
091   6 (Peng et al., 2024) вАУ admit efficient recurrent forms while being increasingly competitive with
092   the transformer architecture with softmax-attention. However, we have a rather limited theoretical
      understanding of these architectures, especially, when it comes to their optimization landscape and
093   ICL capabilities. Park et al. (2024); Grazzi et al. (2024) demonstrate that Mamba is effective in
094   competitive with a transformer of similar size in various ICL tasks whereas Arora et al. (2024);
095   Jelassi et al. (2024) establish theoretical and empirical shortcomings of recurrent models for solving
      recall tasks. It is worth mentioning that, GLA models also connect to state-space models and linear
096
      RNNs (De et al., 2024; Orvieto et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022), as they could be
097   viewed as time-varying SSMs (Dao & Gu, 2024; Sieber et al., 2024). Finally, GLA models are also
098   closely related to implicit self-attention frameworks. For example, the work by Zimerman et al.
099   (2024) on unified implicit attention highlights how models such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) and
      RWKV (Peng et al., 2023) can be viewed under a shared attention mechanism. Additionally, Zong
100   et al. (2024) leverage gated cross-attention for robust multimodal fusion, demonstrating another
101   practical application of gated mechanisms. Both approaches align with GLAвАЩs data-dependent gating,
102   suggesting its potential for explainability and stable fusion tasks.
103   Theory of in-context learning. The theoretical aspects of ICL has been studied by a growing body
104   of works during the past few years (Xie et al.; von Oswald et al., 2023; Gatmiry et al.; Li et al., 2023;
105
      Collins et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Fu et al.; Lin & Lee, 2024; Aky√Љrek et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
      2023). A subset of these follow the setting of Garg et al. (2022) which investigates the ICL ability of
106   transformers by focusing on prompts where each example is labeled by a task function from a specific
107   function class, such as linear models. Aky√Љrek et al. (2023) focuses on linear regression and provide a
      transformer construction that can perform a single step of GD based on in-context examples. Similarly,

      Von Oswald et al. (2023) provide a construction of weights in linear attention-only transformers that
109   can replicate GD steps for a linear regression task on in-context examples. Notably, they observe
110   similarities between their constructed networks and those resulting from training on ICL prompts for
111   linear regression tasks. Building on these, Zhang et al. (2024); Mahankali et al. (2023); Ahn et al.

      that their suboptimal convergence dynamics closely resemble those of online gradient descent with
116   non-decaying step sizes. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) analyzes the landscape of the H3 architecture,
117   an SSM, under the same dataset model. They show that H3 can implement WPGD thanks to its
118   convolutional/SSM filter. However, their WPGD theory is restricted to the trivial setting of equal
      weights, relying on the standard prompt model with IID examples and shared tasks. In contrast, we
119   propose novel multitask datasets and prompt models where nontrivial weighting is provably optimal.
120   This allows us to characterize the loss landscape of WPGD and explore advanced GLA models,
121   linking them to data-dependent WPGD algorithms.

529   6   Discussion
530
531   To summarize, this work offers a fresh theoretical perspective on gated linear attention models
      through in-context learning by showing that they can emulate data-dependent weighted preconditioned
532   gradient descent (WPGD) algorithms. Our work also reveals how gating is crucial for achieving ICL
533   with stronger data/context adaptivity by demonstrating clear separations between linear attention,
534   scalar-valued gating, and vector-valued gating. We study the optimization landscape of GLA through
      a connection to the WPGD formulation (3). We have advocated that (3) is a problem of fundamental
535
      mathematical interest in its own right, developed the first characterization of its optimization landscape,
536   and showed that it enjoys unique global minima and no other stationary point under mild conditions.
537
      Limitations and Future Work. Our analysis is currently limited to characterizing the landscape of
538   scalar gating in GLA models. Extending this framework to vector-valued gating and exploring when
539   delimiters are necessary for learning, as well as investigating the GLA landscape where gates depend
      on input features, are promising directions for future research.
