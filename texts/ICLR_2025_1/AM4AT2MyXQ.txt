009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate the novel optimizer Lion (Evolved Sign Momen-
012            tum), which demonstrates superior performance compared to the well-established
013            Adam in a wide range of tasks. Lion is a combination of Sign Gradient Descent
014            (SignGD) and momentum, utilizing a fixed step size and adjusting the gradient
015            direction via a sign operation. Despite its promising results, Lion currently lacks
016
               comprehensive theoretical justification. We also discuss Normalized Gradient De-
               scent methods, characterized by a fixed step size, which predate Lion. We show
017
               that both Lion and NormGD have notable disadvantages, and to address these is-
018
               sues, we propose a new method SepNorm, which normalizes gradients across dif-
019            ferent parameter groups. SepNorm generalizes both Lion and NormGD, offering
020            a more adaptable and stable optimization approach. Our theoretical analysis on
021            quadratic functions reveals mechanisms of convergence behind the methods and
022            allows us to formulate implicit bias criteria for them. Additionally, we introduce
023            OrtSepNorm, an extension of SepNorm that makes update direction orthogonal
024            to the weights, and we demonstrate that OrtSepNorm converges to a fixed weight
025            norm, thereby making the training process more stable. Empirical evaluations re-
026            veal that SepNorm and OrtSepNorm outperform both Lion and Adam in a range
027
               of computer vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP) tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The overparameterization property of modern neural networks is a key factor contributing to their
032   high performance across a wide range of tasks. Numerous studies have demonstrated the impact of
033   overparameterization property on the training process and loss landscape. However, it also signif-
034   icantly slows down training, making it computationally expensive. Therefore, the development of
035   new optimization schemes that leverage the advantages of overparameterization while speeding up
036   training is an important and actual problem.
037   Many optimization methods have been introduced specifically for neural network training. One such
038   class of methods involves fixed step size schemes. The first proposed method was Norm (S)GD
039   (Mandic, 2004), the key idea of which was the normalization of the gradient to the unit sphere.
040   Another fixed step size method was Sign (S)GD (Moulay et al., 2019), which applies an element-
041   wise sign operation to the gradient. Additionally, generalized approaches like the Block Normalized
042   Gradient method (Yu et al., 2017) have been proposed, although they have not been deeply studied.
043   The authors of the paper (Chen et al., 2024) proposed a novel optimizer, Lion (Evolved Sign Mo-
044   mentum), discovered through program search in the symbolic representation of optimizer space.
045   Lion combines various modifications of traditional methods with Sign (S)GD scheme: exponential
046   smoothing with momentum, decoupled weight decay, and sign operation. Similar to Sign (S)GD,
047   this method has a fixed step size since the norm of the output of the sign function equals the number
048   of nonzero elements. The authors demonstrate that Lion outperforms widely used method AdamW
049   across a various range of tasks, including language modeling and image generation with diffusion
050
      models.
051   However, due to the novelty of Lion, there is a lack of theoretical justification for its performance.
052   In our paper, we investigate the mechanisms behind its effectiveness. We analyze the behavior of the
053   method on quadratic functions and demonstrate that the sign operation establishes the lower bound
      on the possible loss that is dependent on the learning rate and the sharpness of the minima.

054
      Furthermore, we introduce a new method called SepNorm, which renormalizes each group of pa-
055   rameters separately, similar to Block Normalized GD. This is a more general approach because Lion
056   can be considered as SepNorm where each weight forms a separate group. For SepNorm, we also
057   demonstrate inductive bias properties for quadratic functions. Additionally, we show that SepNorm
058   overcomes a disadvantage of Lion, which we term ”Momentum Tracing”.
059
      We also propose the OrtSepNorm method, a modification of SepNorm that projects the momentum
060
      onto a direction orthogonal to weight decay. For scale-invariant networks, we show that OrtSepNorm
061   allows the model to first converge to a fixed weight norm, the value of which can be computed a
062   priori, and then find better global minimum of the training loss using its implicit bias as an external
063   criterion.
064
      We conduct a range of experiments across different architectures and tasks, demonstrating better
065
      or comparable performance of the proposed methods over Lion and AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017).
066
      We also perform experiments in a Grokking setup (Liu et al., 2022b), confirming our theoretical
067   hypotheses about the positive impact of equalizing convergence speed across different groups of
068   parameters.
069
070
      Thus, the main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
071
             • We identify the existing drawbacks of Lion and Norm (S)GD methods and provide experi-
072            mental evidence of their negative effects on the training process.
073
             • We introduce SepNorm, a new optimization method that normalizes each group of param-
074
               eters separately, providing a more general approach than Lion.
075
076
             • We investigate the behavior of Lion and SepNorm on quadratic functions, demonstrating
               their inductive bias properties.
077
078          • We develop OrtSepNorm, a modification of SepNorm that projects momentum orthogo-
079            nally to weight decay, enhancing convergence stability for scale-invariant networks.
080          • We conduct extensive experiments across different architectures and tasks, showing that
081            SepNorm achieves better or comparable performance to state-of-the-art optimizers like
082            AdamW and Lion.

084   2   R ELATED WORKS
085
086   Overparameterization is a property of modern neural networks where the number of parameters is
087   significantly greater than the number of training samples. Many studies explore the benefits of over-
088   parameterization (Belkin et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022a; Schaeffer et al., 2023; Neyshabur et al.,
089   2018). Another property of modern models is scale-invariance. Scale-invariance means that all or
090   part of the parameters are invariant to multiplication by a positive constant. This property arises due
091   to the use of normalization layers in combination with ReLU activations and has been extensively
092   studied (Cho & Lee, 2017; Van Laarhoven, 2017; Zhang et al., 2018; Li & Arora, 2019; Li et al.,
093
      2020). In (Kodryan et al., 2022), the authors investigate the behavior of the training process of scale-
      invariant models with fixed weight norms and discover a strong connection between learning rate
094
      and generalization. Enhancing generalization ability is one of the core problems in deep learning.
095
      Many works try to use the sharpness of minima on the training loss as a measure of generalization
096   (Keskar et al., 2016; Andriushchenko et al., 2023). Methods based on implicit sharpness minimiza-
097   tion demonstrate notable improvements in performance on various tasks (Foret et al., 2020; Kwon
098   et al., 2021; Zhuang et al., 2022).
099
      Various optimization methods have been proposed to improve the training of neural networks. The
100
      most popular is the Adam method (Kingma, 2014), which adds to traditional SGD (Nesterov, 1983)
101
      a momentum mechanism and element-wise gradient adjustment with running statistics of gradient
102   squares. This works as a preconditioning, allowing the model to adapt the step size independently
103   for each component and descend down the slopes of the loss function (Cohen et al., 2022). Its
104   modification, AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017), uses decoupled weight decay, which provides classic
105   theoretical regularization on the weight norm. Besides, element-wise adjustments, many methods
106   use other techniques. The Norm (S)GD method normalizes the gradient to the unit sphere, which
107   leads to a fixed step size. Many properties of this method have been studied in (Murray et al., 2019;
      Mandic, 2004; Zhao et al., 2021). Another method with a fixed step size is Sign GD (Moulay et al.,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019; Li et al., 2023; Safaryan & Richtárik, 2021). This method applies the sign operation to the
109   update vector, which can be considered as Adam with specific relationship between moving averages
110   between first and second order statistics. In paper (Balles et al., 2020), the authors provide deep
111   theoretical research of Sign GD methods in terms of separable smoothness. They demonstrate that
112   the method is sensitive to the diagonal concentration property of hessian and provide experimental
113   evidence on quadratic functions.
114
      The more general method Block Normalized GD, which was introduced in (Yu et al., 2017), uses
115   block-wise gradient normalization, adding flexibility in choosing blocks for gradient adjustment.
116
117   The novel method Lion (Chen et al., 2024) was developed using program search in the symbolic
118
      representation of the optimizer space. This method can be considered as Sign GD with momentum,
      gradient enhancement (which can be seen as Nesterov Momentum), and decoupled weight decay.
119
      The authors demonstrate its superiority in comparison to AdamW on a wide range of tasks.
120
121   In paper (Chen et al., 2023), the authors analyze the continuous variant of Lion and introduce the
122   family of Lion-K methods. Since the sign function is not differentiable, the authors use the con-
123   cept of a subgradient and prove that optimization by the Lion method corresponds to conditional
124
      optimization under constraint on the weight norm:

      Many works try to provide theoretical justifications for the existing methods in application to neural
      networks. In paper Barrett & Dherin (2020), it was demonstrated that gradient descent implements
132
      an implicit regularization of gradient norm, which is a good sharpness measure. This also applies
133
      to stochastic methods and even to methods with adaptive steps such as Adam Smith et al. (2021),
134   Cattaneo et al. (2023). Another interesting property strictly connected with implicit bias is the Edge
135   of Stability Cohen et al. (2021), Cohen et al. (2022). This phenomenon relates to the behavior of
136   the training process near the zero-loss manifold in which the model exhibits a drift according to
137   implicit regularization flow. Research of this phenomenon for Norm (S)GD on quadratic functions
138   demonstrates that the method has an implicit bias which regularizes the spectral norm of the Hessian
139   Murray et al. (2019), another widely used sharpness measure.

152   Lion uses the sign operation applied to momentum adjusted gradient. Since the norm of the sign     √
153   operation equals the number of nonzero elements, this often results in a fixed step size equal to η d,
154   where d is the dimension of the network. The sign operation affects the update direction. Therefore,
155   without weight decay and learning rate annealing, Lion can be considered to optimize the function
156   on a predefined rectangular grid, significantly differing from traditional approaches.
157   For fixed step size methods, a more natural approach is to normalize the gradient or momentum up-
158   date to its norm. This method, known as Norm (S)GD, was introduced in a paper prior to Lion and
159   Sign (S)GD. Without the limitation of optimization on rectangular grid, this method should be more
160   flexible in terms of update directions. However, Lion has an important advantage over Norm (S)GD.
161   Due to the vanishing gradient problem, some weights may receive small gradient components, es-
      pecially those corresponding to first layers. After the sign operation, Lion equalizes updates based

197   However, using the sign operation in Lion also introduces a drawback. Most modern neural networks
198   incorporate normalization layers, such as Batch Normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) and Layer
199   Normalization (Ba et al., 2016), followed by ReLU or similar activations. When the preactivation
200
      feature map components are negative, ReLU outputs zero, resulting in no backpropagation signal
      through those components. Consequently, the corresponding components of the bias term in the
201
      normalization layer receive zero gradient.
202
203   After the gradient becomes zero, these components may continue updating in the direction of mo-
204   mentum for an extended period until the gradient becomes non-zero again or the momentum value
205   diminishes to numerical precision limits. If the momentum components associated with the bias
206
      term of the normalization layers are negative, a positive feedback loop can occur. In this scenario,
      the bias components shift further into negative values, reducing the preactivation values even more,
207
      which in turn maintains zero gradients.
208
209   When the momentum values have already become very small and a gradient signal suddenly reap-
210   pears, the weight values might have moved into suboptimal regions, causing abrupt changes that
211   destabilize the training process. We term this effect ”momentum tracing”. With both convolutional
212
      and linear layers multiple parameters lose the gradient signal when the corresponding BatchNorm
      activation outputs zero. The experimental evidence of the effect is illustrated in 2.
213
214   In contrast to ReLU, LeakyReLU activation preserve backpropagation signal even for negative val-
215   ues of preactivation components, which eliminates the effect. Experiments with LeakyReLU acti-
      vaions presented in Appendix C.

      7   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we introduce and analyze SepNorm and OrtSepNorm, new optimization methods de-
489   signed to address the shortcomings of Lion and Norm GD. Our theoretical and empirical evaluations
490   have revealed certain drawbacks of Lion. By examining the Lion alongside another fixed step size
491   method, Norm GD, we outline the major properties of both methods, which led us to develop the
492   SepNorm scheme. Specifically, the combination of a sign operation with momentum in Lion led to
493   instabilities due to a phenomenon we termed ”Momentum Tracing.” Furthermore, we analyze the
494   application of our methods to scale-invariant networks and demonstrate that they can converge to
495   a fixed weight norm, thereby stabilizing the training process. Additionally, we provide a theoreti-
496   cal investigation into the behavior of these methods on quadratic functions, which helps us define
497
      their implicit bias properties. Empirical evaluations across various practical setups show that the
      proposed SepNorm methods outperform both Adam and Lion.
498
499
500
      Limitations. OrtSepNorm showed superior performance only in NLP tasks. Experiments on CV
      tasks with ResNet architectures demonstrate suboptimal performance for the method due to the use
501
      of a scheduler that causes the weight norm to become too low values, which in turn introduces
502
      instabilities to the training process. to the training process. Also, we provide theoretical results only
503   for quadratic functions, which may be insufficient to generalize our theoretical findings to neural
504   networks.
