009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep generative models have made significant strides in continuous data generation,
012            such as producing realistic images and 3D protein conformations. However, due
013            to the sensitivity of topological graphs to noise and the constraints of long-range
014            discrete relationships, the generation of purely discrete data—such as topolog-
015            ical graphs—remains a long-standing challenge, with property control proving
016            even more elusive. In this paper, we propose a novel molecular graph genera-
017            tive framework, called CtrlMol, to learn the topological graphs of molecules in
018
               a differentiable parameter space. Unlike diffusion models that iteratively refine
               samples, CtrlMol optimizes distribution parameters at different noise levels through
019
               a pre-defined Bayesian flow. At each sampling step, we leverage a property-guided
020
               output distribution to have fine-grained control of topological graphs toward the
021            given property. Experimental results demonstrate that CtrlMol outperforms all the
022            competing baselines in generating natural molecule graphs. In addition, CtrlMol
023            advances the state of the art in producing the molecules with the desired properties.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The molecular graph generation aims to generate valid and realistic molecules with desired properties,
029   which is a fundamental task in drug discovery. However, this problem is very challenging. On
030   the one hand, the sample space is discrete by nature, where the data distribution is rugged and
031   not easy to learn based on continuous assumptions. On the other hand, the molecular graphs are
032   primarily multi-modality, including the nodes (i.e., atoms) and the edges (i.e., bonds), which are
033
      jointly dependent but follow disparate distributions. Moreover, the edges in the molecular graph are
      not only discrete and sparse but also obey multiple constraints in chemical valence. These distinct
034
      characteristics of the different modalities further lift the complexity of molecular graph generation.
035
036   Traditional generative approaches transform the topological molecular structures into sequences
037   (e.g., SMILES) and formulate molecular graph generation as the sequence generation. In this way,
038   sequential models such as LSTM and Transformer are used to produce the molecule graph in an
039
      auto-regressive manner. For example, Gómez-Bombarelli et al. (2018) learn the molecular SMILES
      strings with a variational auto-encoder. But the lack of explicitly syntactic constraints for molecular
040
      graphs makes them inclined to generate invalid sequences. Dai et al. (2018) and Kusner et al. (2017)
041
      impose task-specific grammars into the sequence decoder to enhance the validity of the generated
042   sequences. As a cost of introducing the syntactic constraints, the learning of inconsecutive sequences
043   becomes challenging.
044
045
      As another alternative, graph generative networks directly output molecule graphs by iteratively
      adding atoms and chemical bonds. In particular, GraphAF defines a feed-forward neural network
046
      from molecular graph structures to the base distribution and generates the nodes and edges based on
047
      existing sub-graphs (Shi et al., 2020). GraphDF generates molecular graphs by sequentially sampling
048   discrete latent variables and mapping them to new nodes and edges (Luo et al., 2021). Although
049   graph generative models achieve impressive advancement, the graph generative process is more
050   complicated than the sequential one due to the high degree of freedom in generative ordering. With
051   the success of the diffusion model (DM) in producing realistic continuous images, DM is also applied
052   to molecule generation by denoising the token embeddings in the form of molecular sequences.
053   Runcie & Mey (2023) present a selective iterative latent variable refinement method for conditioning
      an existing pre-trained equivariant diffusion model toward the molecule graph generation. However,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the simplified continuity assumptions of data distribution in DM (e.g., Gaussian distribution) do not
055   always hold in discrete data, which deepens the difficulties of multi-modality issues.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce a novel framework, CtrlMol to address the multi-modality issue in
058
      molecular graph generation. The key idea is to establish a conditional Bayes flow network with
      different base distributions for the sampling of the multi-modality. Concretely, CtrlMol adopts a
059
      unified probabilistic modeling formulation for different modalities in the molecular graph, including
060
      the atom identity (i.e., nodes) and the chemical bonds (i.e., edges). Second, we introduce a topo-
061   logical complete edge sampling strategy to address the huge complexity O(N 2 ) in edge generation.
062   Further, CtrlMol employs the property-guided output distribution to have fine-grained control of
063   the topological structures. Finally, CtrlMol optimizes distribution parameters by minimizing the
064   discrepancy between the output distribution and the true data distribution.
065
      We evaluate CtrlMol on both unconditional and conditional molecule generation tasks. Experimental
066
      results demonstrate that CtrlMol outperforms all competing baselines in generating realistic molecular
067   graphs. For conditional generation, CtrlMol achieves an improvement of 26% and 64% over the
068   previous best generators in terms of QED and LogP properties, respectively. Moreover, CtrlMol
069   could sample with any number of steps by virtue of the continuous parameter sampling space, which
070   leads to a 100× speedup with SOTA performance on conditional molecule generation.

      2     R ELATED WORK
074
075
      Early methods typically adopt SMILES/SMARTS strings as a text-based representation for atom-by-
      atom molecule generation. By decoding the molecular strings token by token, the generative model
076
      can learn prior knowledge that encapsulates the grammar and syntax of valid SMILES (Jensen, 2019;
077
      Segler et al., 2018; Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018). With the development of graph neural networks
078   (GNNs), topological graphs emerge as a more prevailing representation of molecules. For example,
079   JTVAE (Jin et al., 2018b), GCPN (You et al., 2018), GraphAF (Shi et al., 2020), and GraphDF (Luo
080   et al., 2021) proposed several autoregressive models to generate new nodes and edges based on the
081   generated molecular graph.
082
      Apart from the molecular strings (1D) and topological graphs (2D), the molecule can be also
083
      represented as the atom types and the corresponding coordinates (3D). For the generation of molecular
084   3D structures, diffusion models have been widely adopted. DiffSBDD (Schneuing et al., 2022) and
085   TargetDiff (Guan et al., 2022) learn the distribution of atom types and positions from a standard
086   Gaussian prior based via the diffusion process. DecompDiff (Guan et al., 2023) decomposes the
087   ligand molecule and the prior into arms and scaffolds and then leverages the reverse process of
088   diffusion to generate molecules. In fact, these diffusion-based generative methods leverage the
089   continuous 3D coordinates to avoid the generation of the chemical bonds of atoms, which are sparse
090   and discrete. Considering that the 3D structures of molecules are usually not with high-quality (e.g.,
091   approximated by RDKit library) and available, the generation of atoms and coordinates can hardly
092
      serve as a universal molecular graph generator, especially in the scenarios lack of 3D structures (e.g.,
      generation toward in vivo and in vitro properties).
093
094   Recently, Bayes flow networks (BFNs) have been introduced to generate discrete structures in
095   continuous parameter space (Qu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2023). Similar to diffusion generators,
096   GeoBFN decomposes the molecular structure into atoms and the coordinates. Then GeoBFN adopts
097   BFN to generate both of them and transforms the atom coordinates to build the final molecular
098
      graphs (Song et al., 2023). Different from GeoBFN, Our approach focuses on the 2D molecular
      graph and proposes several novel components (such as the topological complete edge sampler and
099
      property-guided output distribution) to address the multi-modality and edge sparsity issues.

104   3.1   P ROBLEM DEFINITION
105
106   We focus on the 2D molecular graphs due to the abundance of available data. The geometry of a
107   2D molecular graph is denoted as M = {(V , E)}, where V ∈ R|V |×KV is the set of node (i.e.,
      atoms) features and E ∈ R|V |×|V |×KE is the set of edge (i.e., bonds) features. Each node and edge


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      has categorical attributes corresponding to their types. The i-th node is represented by a one-hot
109   vector v i ∈ V with KV dimension, where KV represents the total number of atomic types. Similarly,
110   the j-th edge ej ∈ E is represented as a one-hot vector of dimension KE , with KE denoting
111   the total number of bond types. Without loss of generality, there is an edge type to stand for no
112   connection between two nodes. The primary goal of conditional molecular generation is to produce
113   valid molecular structures M that satisfy specific properties c (e.g., logP, QED (Bickerton et al.,
114   2012), etc.). By conditioning the generation process on these properties, we aim to explore the vast
115   chemical space effectively, facilitating the discovery of new compounds with desired characteristics.

117   3.2    T HE OVERVIEW OF BAYESIAN FLOW NETOWRKS
118
      To effectively address the discrete nature of molecular topology, our CtrlMol model generates
119
      molecules by sampling in a continuous parameter space using the Bayesian Flow Networks (BFN)
120
      framework. The key distinction between BFN and diffusion models is that BFN refines the parameters
121   of the data distribution rather than operating on noisy data as diffusion models do.
122
123   As illustrated in Figure 1, the generative process can be conceptualized as a message exchange
124

      referred to as the “input distribution” (pI (M | θ)), where θ is the parameters of the prior distribution
125
126
      for the data M. Additionally, the receiver can estimate an “output distribution” (pO (M̂ | Φ(θ, t)))
      based on the parameters θ of the input distribution along with the processing time t, using a neural
127

      network to predict the data M̂.

231   Topological complete edge sampling strategy. As demonstrated in Theorem 1, the connections of
232   a K-regular graph can cover any arbitrary graph with the node degree at most K, regardless of the
233   node identity. In other words, if we want to generate a given topological graph with Nv nodes and
234   the degree at most K, the K-regular graph with Nv nodes is complete to be the initial topological
235   graph for sampling. Considering that the node in molecular graphs with the maximum connections
236
      is the carbon atom, we set the maximum number of the edge connections in the initial graph as
      K = 4. Formally speaking, we will randomly add several edges with "no connection" type into
237
      the given molecular graph M = {(V , E)} to satisfy that the degree equals K and then build the
238
      input distribution of edges. Note that the above topological edge sampling strategy is guaranteed by
239   Theorem 1 and largely reduces the computational complexity to O(KNv ), where K is a constant.

241   Theorem 1. For arbitrary positive integer N and D (D < N ), undirected graph G =< V, E > that
242
      has |V| = N and deg(v) = D, v ∈ V, undirected graph H =< V H , E H > that has |V H | = N and
      deg(v) ≤ D, v ∈ V H , there exists at least one subgraph G ′ ⊆ G such that H is isomorphic to G ′ .
243
      (Function deg(v) denotes the degree of the vertex v.)
244
245   Proof. Our goal is to find a subgraph G ′ ⊆ G that is isomorphic to H. We employ a combinatorial
246   argument based on the properties of regular graphs and the degrees of vertices in H. We will take
247   the following steps to construct a subgraph G of G using a greedy algorithm: (1) Start with an empty
248
      subgraph G ′ . (2) Iterate through the vertices of H. For each vertex vi in H, we will select deg(vi )
      neighbors from G that are not yet included in G ′ and add them to G ′ . (3) For each vertex vi in H:
249
      Identify deg(vi ) vertices from G that are not already included in G ′ and that can serve as neighbors.
250
      This selection is possible because G is K-regular. As long as the vertices chosen are distinct and do
251   not exceed K in degree, we can ensure that the selections are valid. (4) To ensure that the resulting
252   subgraph G ′ is isomorphic to H, we need to maintain the structure of H. The selection of neighbors
253   in G should respect the edges present in H. By the property of regular graphs, since G has sufficient
254   edges to accommodate the degree constraints of H (as G has K edges available per vertex), we can
255   map the edges of H to G ′ .
256
      After selecting neighbors for all vertices of H, we have ensured that G ′ has n vertices. The edges
257   selected match the edges of H in terms of structure. Thus, we have constructed a subgraph G ′ of G
258   such that G ′ is isomorphic to H.

      4.3   M ETRICS
359
360   We use several widely used metrics to evaluate our model on both conditional and unconditional
361   molecular graph generation. For unconditional molecule generation, we focus on three metrics:
362   validity, uniqueness, and novelty. Validity measures the percentage of molecules that comply with
363   chemical valency rules. In addition, since some methods check the valency during the generation
364   process, we also report Validity w/o check, which reflects the validity percentage when this valency
365   correction is disabled. Uniqueness is defined as the percentage of unique molecules among the
366   generated valid molecules. Novelty represents the percentage of the generated molecules that do not
367
      appear in the training data. For conditional molecule generation, we evaluate the performance of each
      model by comparing the mean absolute error (MAE) between the target properties and those of the
368
      generated molecules. All the metrics are calculated based on 10000 generated molecules.

      4.4   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
371
372   Our CtrlMol uses a graph attention network to model the interdependence of the different variables
373   and produce the output distribution. The network consists of 9 graph attention layers with 8 attention
374   heads. Both the hidden dimension and the output dimension are 256. The node and edge features
375   output by the graph network are then mapped to the dimensions corresponding to the number of atom
376   types KV = 9 and edge types KE = 5 to generate the output distribution. The model is trained
377   through the Adam optimizer for 1000 epochs, where the batch size is 256 and the learning rate is
      0.0005.

      Unconditional molecule generation We evaluate the ability of the CtrlMol to generate realistic
      molecules by calculating metrics validity, uniqueness, and novelty. Table 1 shows that our model
411
      outperforms the previous methods across these metrics. While several models utilize valency checks
412
      during each sampling step to ensure the validity of generated molecules, this checking process relies
413   on external chemical rules that the model itself does not learn, which does not accurately reflect the
414   model’s true capabilities. For instance, GCPN may achieve 100% validity through valency checking,
415   yet it fails to capture the underlying distribution of molecules (20% validity without check).
416
      Therefore, we focus on the validity without check, which can truly reflect the model’s understanding of
417
      molecular characteristics. CtrlMol achieves the best results in validity without checking, highlighting
418
      the superiority of our model in generating realistic molecules through a one-shot sampling process
419   without re-sampling by valency check. Additionally, CtrlMol demonstrates 100% uniqueness and
420   novelty, highlighting its capability to produce new molecular structures that do not appear in the
421   training data. This indicates that the model has genuinely captured the underlying distribution
422   characteristics of the molecules, rather than merely overfitting to the training data.

      5   C ONCLUSION
475
476
477
      Deep generative models have achieved remarkable progress in generating continuous data, such as
      lifelike images and 3D protein structures. Nonetheless, purely discrete data like topological molecular
478
      graphs surfer the serious issues of the multi-modality and the connection sparsity, making molecular
479
      graph generation a challenging task for the existing methods. In this paper, we introduce CtrlMol,
480   an innovative framework for generating molecular graphs within a differentiable parameter space.
481   In contrast to diffusion models that enhance samples through iterative refinement, CtrlMol adjusts
482   distribution parameters at different noise levels via a non-parametric Bayesian flow. CtrlMol also
483   integrates a topological complete edge sampling strategy and property-guided output distribution to
484   address the above generative issue. The experimental results indicate that CtrlMol outperforms all
485   current benchmarks in creating realistic molecular graphs, also establishing a new benchmark for
      generating molecules with pre-defined characteristics.
