011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Energy based models (EBMs) are appealing for their generality and simplicity
014            in data likelihood modeling, but have conventionally been difficult to train due
015
               to the unstable and time-consuming implicit MCMC sampling during contrastive
               divergence training. In this paper, we present a novel energy-based generative
016
               framework, Variational Potential Flow (VAPO), that entirely dispenses with im-
017
               plicit MCMC sampling and does not rely on complementary latent models or
018            cooperative training. The VAPO framework aims to learn a potential energy func-
019            tion whose gradient (flow) guides the prior samples, so that their density evolution
020            closely follows an approximate data likelihood homotopy. An energy loss function
021            is then formulated to minimize the Kullback-Leibler divergence between density
022            evolution of the flow-driven prior and the data likelihood homotopy. Images can
023            be generated after training the potential energy, by initializing the samples from
024            Gaussian prior and solving the SDE governing the potential flow. Experiment
025            results show that the proposed VAPO framework is capable of generating realistic
026
               images on various image datasets. In particular, our proposed framework achieves
               competitive FID scores for unconditional image generation on the CIFAR-10 and
027
               CelebA datasets.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, deep generative modeling has garnered significant attention for unsupervised learning
033   of complex, high-dimensional data distributions Bond-Taylor et al. (2022). In particular, probabilistic
034   generative models such as variational autoencoders Kingma & Welling (2022), normalizing flows
035   Rezende & Mohamed (2015), score matching or diffusion models Song et al. (2021); Kim et al.
036   (2021); Karras et al. (2022). Poisson flow Xu et al. (2022; 2023), and energy-based models (EBMs)
037   Du & Mordatch (2019); Grathwohl et al. (2020a) aim to maximize the likelihood (probability density)
038   underlying the data. By design, these probabilistic frameworks enhance training stability, accelerate
039
      model convergence, and reduce mode collapse compared to generative adversarial networks Srivastava
      et al. (2017), albeit at the cost of a slow sampling procedure and poor model scalability Grathwohl
040
      et al. (2020b). Among these frameworks, EBMs have emerged as a flexible and expressive class of
041
      probabilistic generative models Du & Mordatch (2019); Grathwohl et al. (2020b); Du et al. (2021);
042   Nijkamp et al. (2019); Gao et al. (2020; 2021); Zhu et al. (2024); Grathwohl et al. (2020a); Yang et al.
043   (2023). EBMs model high-dimensional data space with a network-parameterized energy potential
044   function that assigns data regions with energy that is directly (or inversely) proportional to the
045   unnormalized data likelihood Song & Kingma (2021). This provides a natural interpretation of the
046   network model in the form of an energy landscape, endowing EBMs with inherent interpretability.
047
      Deep EBMs are particularly appealing since they impose no restrictions on the network architecture,
048   potentially resulting in high expressiveness Bond-Taylor et al. (2022). Moreover, they are more robust
049   and generalize well to out-of-distribution samples Du & Mordatch (2019); Grathwohl et al. (2020a)
050   as regions with high probability under the model but low probability under the data distribution are
051   explicitly penalized during training. Additionally, EBMs, which trace back to Boltzmann machines
052   Hinton (2002), have strong ties to physics models and can thus borrow insights and techniques
053   from statistical physics for their development and analysis Feinauer & Lucibello (2021). On these
      grounds, EBMs have been applied across a diverse array of applications apart from image modelling,

054
      including text generation Deng et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2022), point cloud synthesis Xie et al. (2021a),
055   scene graph generation Suhail et al. (2021), anomaly detection Vu et al. (2017); Yoon et al. (2023),
056   earth observation Castillo-Navarro et al. (2022), robot learning Du et al. (2020); Sodhi et al. (2022),
057   trajectory prediction Pang et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023), and molecular design Liu et al. (2021);
058   Sun et al. (2021).
059
      Despite a number of desirable properties, deep EBMs require implicit Langevin Markov Chain Monte
060
      Carlo (MCMC) sampling during the contrastive divergence training. MCMC sampling in a high-
061   dimensional setting, however, has shown to be challenging due to poor mode mixing and excessively
062   long mixing time Bond-Taylor et al. (2022); Du & Mordatch (2019); Nijkamp et al. (2019); Gao et al.
063   (2020); Grathwohl et al. (2020a); Nijkamp et al. (2022). As result, energy potential functions learned
064   with non-convergent MCMC do not have valid steady-states, in the sense that MCMC samples can
065   differ greatly from data samples Grathwohl et al. (2020b). Current deep EBMs are thus plagued by
066   high variance training and high computational complexity due to MCMC sampling. In view of this,
067   recent works have explored learning complementary latent model to amortize away the challenging
068   MCMC sampling Xiao et al. (2021); Grathwohl et al. (2021); Hill et al. (2022); Pang et al. (2020);
069
      Yu et al. (2023), or cooperative learning where model-generated samples serve as initial points for
      subsequent MCMC revision in the latent space Xie et al. (2020); Cui & Han (2023). While such
070
      approaches alleviate the burden of MCMC sampling, it comes at the expense of the inherent flexibility
071
      and composability of EBMs Du et al. (2021).
072
073   In this paper, we introduce Variational Potential Flow (VAPO), a novel energy-based generative
074   framework that eliminates the need for implicit MCMC sampling and complementary models. At the
075
      core of VAPO lies the construction of a homotopy (smooth path) that bridges the prior distribution
      with the data likelihood. Subsequently, a potential flow with model-parameterized potential energy
076
      function is designed to guide the evolution of prior sample densities along this approximate data
077
      likelihood homotopy. Applying a variational approach to this path-matching strategy ultimately
078   yields a probabilistic Poisson’s equation, where the weak solution corresponds to minimizing the
079   energy loss function of our proposed VAPO.
080
081
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
082
             • We introduce VAPO, a novel energy-based generative framework that entirely dispenses
083
               with the unstable and inefficient implicit MCMC sampling. Our proposed framework learns
084            a potential energy function whose gradient (flow) guides the prior samples, ensuring that
085            their density evolution path closely follows the approximate data likelihood homotopy.
086
             • We derive an energy loss function for VAPO by constructing a variational formulation of
087
               the intractable homotopy path-matching problem. Solving this energy loss objective is
088
               equivalent to minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between density evolution of the
089            flow-driven prior and the approximate data likelihood homotopy.
090
091
             • To assess the effectiveness of our proposed VAPO for image generation, we conduct
               experiments on the CIFAR-10 and CelebA datasets and benchmark the performances against
092
               state-of-the-art generative models. Our proposed framework achieves competitive FID
093
               scores for unconditional image generation.

      2.2   PARTICLE F LOW
116
      Particle flow, initially introduced by the series of papers Daum & Huang (2007), is a class of nonlinear
117   Bayesian filtering (sequential inference) methods that aim to approximate the posterior distribution
118   p(xt |x̄0:t ) of the state of system given the observations. While particle flow methods are closely
119   related to normalizing flows Rezende & Mohamed (2015) and neural ordinary differential equations
120   (ODEs) Chen et al. (2018), these latter frameworks do not explicitly accommodate a Bayes update.
121   In particular, particle flow performs the Bayes update p(xt |x̄0:t ) ∝ p(xt |x̄0:t−1 ) p(x̄t |xt , x̄0:t−1 )
122   by subjecting prior samples xt ∼ p(xt |x̄0:t−1 ) to a series of infinitesimal transformations through
123   an ODE dx   dτ = v(x, τ ) parameterized by a flow velocity (field) function v(x, τ ), in a pseudo-time
124   interval τ ∈ [0, 1] in between sampling time steps. The flow velocity is designed such that the driven
125   Kolmogorov forward path evolution (Fokker–Planck dynamics, see (12)) of the sample particles,
126   coincides with a data log-homotopy (smooth path) that inherently perform the Bayes update. Despite
127
      its efficacy in time-series inference Pal et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2019b); Yang et al. (2014) and
      resilience to the curse of dimensionality Surace et al. (2019), particle flow has yet to be explored in
128
      generative modelling for high-dimensional data.

      3     VARIATIONAL E NERGY-BASED P OTENTIAL F LOW
153
154
      In this section, we introduce a novel generative modelling framework, Variational Energy-Based
155
      Potential Flow (VAPO), drawing inspiration from both particle flow and the calculus of variations.
156   First, we establish a homotopy that transforms a prior to the data likelihood and derive the evolution
157   of the prior in time. Then, we design an energy-generated potential flow and a weighted Poisson’s
158   equation that aligns the evolving density distribution of transported particles with the homotopy-
159   driven prior. Subsequently, we formulate a variational loss function where its optimization with
160   respect to the flow-generating potential energy is equivalent to solving the Poisson’s equation. Finally,
161   we describe the model architecture that is used to parameterize the potential energy function and the
      backward SDE integration for generative sampling.

403
404   In this section, we show that VAPO is an effective generative model for images. In particular, Section
405   4.1 demonstrates that VAPO is capable of generating realistic unconditional images on the CIFAR-
406   10 and CelebA datasets. Section 4.2 demonstrates that VAPO is capable of performing smooth
407   interpolation between two generated samples. Apart from that, we also show that VAPO exhibits
408   extensive mode coverage and robustness to anomalous data, and generalizes well to unseen test
409   data. Specifically, Appendix D.1 evaluates model over-fitting and generalization based on the energy
410   histogram of CIFAR-10 train and test sets and the nearest neighbors of generated samples. Appendix
411
      D.2 examines robustness to anomalous data by assessing its performance on out-of-distribution
      (OOD) detection on various image datasets. Appendix E evaluates the convergence of the flow-driven
412
      approximate likelihood density and its image samples on long-run sampling. Appendix F accesses
413
      the capability of VAPO for compositional generation on the CelebA dataset.
414
415   Here, we include two model variants, namely VAPO-A and VAPO-T. Specifically, VAPO-A considers
416   an autonomous (independent of time) energy Φ(x(t)) and VAPO-T considers the more general time-
417
      varying energy Φ(x(t), t). Here, we parameterize VAPO-A using the WideResNet architecture of
      Xiao et al. (2021) and VAPO-T using the Unet architecture of Du et al. (2023). The performance
418
      of VAPO-T are evaluated only on image generation and compositional generation. Implementation
419
      details, including model architecture, training, numerical SDE solver, datasets and FID evaluation are
420   provided in Appendix C.

472   5     C ONCLUSION
473
474   We propose VAPO, a novel energy-based generative modelling framework without the need for
475   expensive and unstable MCMC runs amidst training. Despite the improvement over the majority of
476   existing EBMs, there is still a large performance gap between VAPO and the state-of-the-art score-
477   based (or diffusion) and Poisson flow models Song et al. (2021); Kim et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022).
478   To close this gap, diffusion recovery likelihood Gao et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2024), which is shown
479   to be more tractable than marginal likelihood, can be incorporated into the VAPO framework for a
480
      more controlled diffusion-guided energy optimization. The dimensionality augmentation technique
      of Xu et al. (2022; 2023) can also be integrated given that fundamentally, both Poisson flow and
481
      VAPO aim to model potential field governed by a Poisson’s equation. On top of that, the scalability
482
      of VAPO to higher resolution images and its generalizability to other data modalities have yet to be
483   validated. In addition, the current VAPO framework does not allow for class-conditional generation.
484   Moreover, the model training requires a large number of iterations to converge and thus warrants
485   improvement. These important aspects are earmarked for future extensions of our work.

791   A    R ELATED W ORKS
792
793   Flow-based and diffusion generative models have been pivotal in recent advancements in generative
794   modeling, with a variety of innovative approaches enhancing their effectiveness and applicability.
795
      Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) Kim et al. (2021) and Score-Based Generative
      Models (NCSN++) Song et al. (2021) have demonstrated the efficacy of stochastic differential
796
      equations (SDEs) in generating high-quality samples through the progressive scaling and refinement
797
      of noise. Building upon these foundational techniques, Rectified Flow Liu et al. (2023b) enhances
798   the efficiency of data generation by learning rectified paths to streamlining the diffusion process for
799   rapid generative sampling.
800
801
      In parallel, Flow Matching Lipman et al. (2023) focuses on learning the evolution of time-dependent
      diffeomorphic mappings that transport a prior density to a target density, effectively generalizing
802
      beyond the probability paths constructed by simple diffusion through the leverage of optimal transport.
803
      Stochastic Interpolants Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden (2023) enhance conventional normalizing flows
804   Rezende & Mohamed (2015); Chen et al. (2018) by incorporating stochastic processes that interpolate
805   between prior and target densities, thereby increasing their expressiveness and enabling the handling
806   of more complex data geometries. Schrödinger Bridge Matching Shi et al. (2023) integrates optimal
807   transport theory with diffusion processes through the Schrödinger bridge framework, employing
808   iterative Markovian fitting to construct a probabilistic model that aligns densities, while regularizing
809   entropy for a smoother density transition. Inspired by electrostatics, the Poisson Flow Generative
      Model (PFGM) Xu et al. (2022) extends flow-based generative models by integrating the Poisson


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      equation for learning an invertible Poisson flow within an augmented space that includes an additional
811   dimension.
812
813   Additionally, Action Matching Neklyudov et al. (2023) introduces a novel paradigm by learning the
814
      dynamics of the generative process through the optimization of the potential action (energy) that
      governs the underlying gradient field. This perspective enables a more physically grounded approach
815
      to modeling stochastic dynamics within generative frameworks. On the other hand, approaches such
816
      as Diffusion Recovery Likelihood (DRL) Gao et al. (2021) and Denoising Diffusion Adversarial
817   Energy-Based Models (DDAEBM) Geng et al. (2024) harness diffusion processes to enhance the
818   sampling efficiency and training stability of conventional energy-based models (EBMs). Together,
819   these models represent significant strides in integrating diffusion, flow-based, optimal transport, and
820   energy-based methodologies for generative modeling.
821
      Nevertheless, existing generative models have not incorporated log-homotopy transformations or a
822
      variational (Deep Ritz) formulation that aligns the Fokker–Planck (or continuity) equation directly
823   with the evolution of a time-dependent density homotopy. Furthermore, our proposed VAPO loss
824   is formulated in terms of potential energy, with energy learning primarily governed by the bilinear
825   covariance operator. This formulation offers direct control over the energy training, a characteristic
826   inherent to the contrastive divergence loss Hinton (2002) of EBMs but absent in score matching
827   Vincent (2011). Such control enables potential applications in safe image generation Schramowski
828   et al. (2023), eliminating the need for post hoc guidance techniques.

       Our network architectures for the model variants VAPO-A and VAPO-T are based on the WideResNet

1128   norm of the convolutional layer in WideResNet. Also, we remove the final scale-by-sigma operation
1129   Kim et al. (2021); Song et al. (2021) and replace it with a L2 norm 12 ∥x(t) − fθ (x(t))∥2 between
1130   the input x(t) and the output of the Unet fθ (x(t)). Here, we replace LeakyReLU activations with
1131   Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU) activations Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017) for both WideResNet
1132   and Unet, which we found improves training stability and convergence. Additionally, we apply weight
1133   normalization with data-dependent initialization Salimans & Kingma (2016) on the convolutional
       layers of WideResNet to regularize the output energy.
