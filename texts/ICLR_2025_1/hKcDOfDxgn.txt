009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Replay is a powerful strategy to promote learning in artificial intelligence and the
012            brain. However, the conditions to generate it and its functional advantages have
013            not been fully recognized. In this study, we develop a modular reinforcement
014            learning model that could generate replay. We prove that replay generated in this
015            way helps complete the task. We also analyze the information contained in the
016
               representation and provide a mechanism for how replay makes a difference. Our
               design avoids complex assumptions and enables replay to emerge naturally within
017
               a task-optimized paradigm. Our model also reproduces key phenomena observed
018
               in biological agents. This research explores the structural biases in modular ANN
019            to generate replay and its potential utility in developing efficient RL.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Experience replay (Lin, 1992) is an important technique for online reinforcement learning (RL). By
025   storing past experiences in a circular buffer and training the agent on these experiences repeatedly,
026   experience replay improves sample efficiency and training stability (Schaul et al., 2015), and suc-
027
      cessfully improves the performance of RL algorithms (Mnih, 2013; Mnih et al., 2015). There are
      several works on how to design more sophisticated experience replay mechanisms, like hyperpa-
028
      rameter tuning (Fedus et al., 2020), prioritizing replay with higher temporal difference (TD) error
029
      (Schaul et al., 2015), or developing a generative replay model (Van de Ven & Tolias, 2018). How-
030   ever, given the significant efficiency gap between biological and RL agents, we explore whether
031   replay algorithms can be further improved by drawing inspiration from brain structures.
032
033
      In neuroscience, replay is the reactivation of a sequence of place cells that encode recently experi-
      enced locations. It has been observed in many brain areas during rest or sleep, from the hippocampus
034
      (Buzsáki, 1986; Nádasdy et al., 1999) to the neocortex, including the prefrontal cortex (Peyrache
035
      et al., 2009; Kaefer et al., 2020), visual cortex (Ji & Wilson, 2007), and motor cortex (Eichenlaub
036   et al., 2020). Some people believe it represents the ”virtual trajectory” in the brain that primar-
037   ily serves two functions: memory consolidation (Foster & Wilson, 2006; O’Neill et al., 2010) and
038   planning (Pfeiffer & Foster, 2013; Widloski & Foster, 2022). Its dynamic properties and functional
039   diversity have attracted significant attention in neuroscience research.
040
      There are currently two focuses in this field: the emerging condition of replay and the function of
041
      replay. Many models have been proposed to explain these two aspects. Some biophysical models
042   suggest that replay arises from the combination of past experiences and specific neuronal network
043   connectivity rules, such as asymmetrical synaptic connections (Tsodyks et al., 1996) and short-term
044   depression (Romani & Tsodyks, 2015). In addition to connectivity rules, task optimization provides
045   another way to understand the emergence of replay. (Krishna et al., 2024) proved that denoising
046   dynamics under task optimization could lead to offline reactivation of past experiences. Levenstein
047   et al. (2024) verified that only the network trained on multi-step predictive learning with recur-
048   rent connection and head-direction input can generate offline trajectories resembling replay. These
049   models are illustrative in that they connect conditions like circuit structures, task optimization or pre-
050
      dictive learning to the generation of replay. However, they fail to explain the functional importance
      of replay in the task.
051
052   On the other hand, experience replay provides good evidence that replay does have some importance
053   and helps learning. However, this approach is not ideal for exploring the conditions under which
      replay emerges, as all replay of stored experiences is hard-coded and predefined. The model never

054
      finds a way to organize experiences and perform efficient replay without force. The gap between
055   these two research fields leads to the natural question of whether we could find some conditions un-
056   der which a model could naturally generate adaptive replay sequences with functional importance.
057   Identifying such conditions could provide important insights on how to design new reinforcement
058   learning agents that quickly adapt to the environment, given the present low efficiency of RL algo-
059   rithms and the unique advantage of biological agents in learning new tasks.
060
      Recently, some studies have attempted to approach this problem within the RL framework. For
061   example, Mattar & Daw (2018) propose that a special variable called the expected value of backup
062   (EVB) determines the replay priority of different experiences, and the replay is accompanied by
063   Bellman updates which are essential for learning the value function and policy for the task. However,
064   because we cannot know EVB until we perform value iteration for the whole environment, and after
065   value iteration it seems unnecessary to use replay at all, the generating condition in there are still
066   some hard-coded constraints in this method.
067   In this article, we propose a model to circumvent hard-coded settings, provide some conditions as
068   a natural explanation for the emergence of replay, and then show the functional importance of our
069   generated replay sequences. In summary,
070
071         1. Based on two straightforward conditions, we develop a model capable of generating offline
072            replay activation.
073         2. Throughout the entire learning process of the flexible navigation task, the evolution of
074            replay spatial distribution of our model resembles that observed in real animals.
075         3. Through ablation experiments, we verify the functionality of replay by showing that the
076            exploration efficiency of our model surpasses that of models without replay.
077
            4. We analyze the information that replay flow carries, and explain how replay can promote
078
               efficient learning.

081   2   M ETHODS
082
083   Conditions to generate instrumental replay What could these conditions possibly be? The main
084
      areas where replay occurs are the prefrontal cortex (PFC) and hippocampal formation (HF), usually
      modeled as policy network and world model respectively due to their unique functions. Replay in
085
      these two individual areas is often observed to be coordinated (”phase-lock”) when a new environ-
086
      ment learning task is presented (Place et al., 2016). We hypothesize that communication between
087   these two areas is crucial for the generation of instrumental replay. Therefore, we propose two
088   conditions that could be implemented in RL agents:
089
090             Condition 1: Replay serves for reward maximization.
                                                                                                         (1)
091             Condition 2: Replay is accompanied by communication between PFC and HF.
092
      To achieve Condition 1, we employ the end-to-end RL framework without hard-coded design. For
093
      Condition 2, our RL agent has a world model and a policy model, representing HF and PFC re-
094   spectively, and we add an additional information passage between these two models that is trained
095   alongside the policy network. This setting allows the agent to adjust the information communication
096   flow based on its influence on actions and expected rewards. Discussions about the plausibility of
097   these settings can be found in section 4.1.
098
099   Principle of designing different modules We preface the introduction of model structure with
100   key biological biases that we incorporate into our design.
101
      Biologically, HF has abundant recurrent connections in its CA3 area (Le Duigou et al., 2014), which
102   is also thought to be a major area generating replay. Thus we implement a gated recurrent unit
103   (GRU) for HF. Besides, there are two main functions for HF. First, the HF is good at encoding the
104   environment structure (i.e. cognitive map) which can serve as a basis for path integration and flexible
105   navigation (Whittington et al., 2018). In our setting, the HF module learns to do this by predicting
106   the next location (path integration) and reward, based on history (hidden state) and environmental
107   information (input) (Figure 1A). The HF is also thought to store episodic memory (Whittington
      et al., 2018; Battaglia et al., 2011), and in our model, it learns to memorize past reward history.

146   To handle high-dimensional visual inputs, we use an ancillary Encoder module modeled by a con-

148   establish an information passage between HF and PFC. Because replay is only detected when the
149
      subject receives a reward and stops to consume it (Igata et al., 2021), the information passage re-

150
      During the agent’s movement, the PFC decides the agent’s next action (i.e., up, down, left, or right)
151
      based on the input of environmental representation processed by the Encoder module. At this time,
152   it does not communicate with the HF module. Meanwhile, the HF maintains its own dynamics for
153   correct place cell activation and recent episodic memory (Figure 1B). When the agent receives a
154   reward, the two modules interact for several steps and influence each other through the information
155   passage. The reactivation of place cells in the HF is then probed and analyzed as replay.

196   3.1   B IOLOGICALLY SIMILAR REPLAY SEQUENCES EMERGE AFTER TRAINING
197
198   Task setting We use the animal experiment from (Igata et al., 2021) as a reference, in which a
199   rodent was trying to navigate to a dynamically changing reward location in an open area (Figure 1D).
200   First, the animal learned to run from the starting point S to checkpoint 1 (C1) to get a small reward,
201   and then run from C1 to G to get a large reward. Violation of the order would cause no return
202   at all (Figure 1D). When the stage of pre-learning was finished, the small reward was relocated to
203
      checkpoint 2 (C2, Figure 2B), requiring the animal to learn a new optimal path (S-C2-G). This was
      the beginning of the learning stage. Initially, the animal continued to take the path S-C1-G followed
204
      by G-C2-G after failing to receive a reward at G. It then realized the existence of a shortcut (S-C2)
205
      and gradually settled on it. The aim was to investigate changes in the relative amount of replay
206   sequences representing different parts of the room (”replay distribution” in this paper) during the
207   learning stage when the animal was trying to adapt to the change of reward location. They found
208   that the amount of replay of the original trajectory (S-C1, C1-G) decayed rapidly, while the shortcut
209   (S-C2) increased even before the agent adopted it (Figure 2C). This result could be evidence that
210   replay supports flexible navigation by exploring possible paths and strengthening better ones.
211
212   Simulation result We create a similar simulation environment as in (Igata et al., 2021). The
213   animal is replaced by an RL agent moving in a 5 × 5 open arena. As in the animal experiment, we
214   put the small reward at C1 and then relocate it to C2. Without adjusting the network parameters, and
215   simply by modifying the hidden states of the RNNs, the RL agent successfully identifies the optimal
      path to the new checkpoint. (Figure 3A).

258   be random noise or an all-zero vector. Because the information exchange between HF and PFC is

439   4     D ISCUSSION
440
441   In this article, we have demonstrated that biologically similar replay can emerge from the interaction
442   between the HF module and PFC module within a task-optimization framework. We show that
443   replay in our model is actually a form of multi-step information emission from HF to PFC. Then
444   we show that during replay, the context is updated both in HF and PFC, and the cognitive map and
445   future plan are updated in PFC. Therefore we describe how replay helps learning. At last, we plot
446   the manifold to give a more intuitive understanding. See Appendix A.3 for reproducibility results.

      4.1   P LAUSIBILITY
449
450
      Conditions Our two sufficient conditions (1) are inspired by neuroscientific research. For Con-
451   dition (1), studies have shown that blocking replay impairs spatial learning (Jadhav et al., 2012;
452   Girardeau et al., 2009; Ego-Stengel & Wilson, 2010). The presence of task-focused replay can pre-
453   dict the accuracy of following decisions (Ólafsdóttir et al., 2017). Replay is modulated by reward
454   (Ambrose et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2021; Michon et al., 2019), and is associated with reward-guided
455   updates of internal models based on past experiences (Carey et al., 2019).
456
      For condition (2), the interaction between HF and PFC during replay is supported by a lot of evi-
457
      dence. For example, replay is accompanied by sharp-wave ripples that synchronize the wave phases
458   of HF and PFC (Tang et al., 2017). Replay is thought to be the substrate of the continuous memory
459   transition from the hippocampus to the neocortex, namely memory consolidation(Kumaran et al.,
460   2016). Recent computational models also reveal that the communication between the world model
461   and policy network could help decision-making (Jensen et al., 2024).
462
      However, there is some evidence that does not support this hypothesis as a generating condition
463
      for replay. For example, Kaefer et al. (2020) reported PFC replay that is strongly associated with
464   rule-switching performance but is independent of HF replay. As we do not measure PFC replay, it
465   may be that not replay but other PFC activities in our model. Future work could design experiments
466   to make this clear.
467
468
469
      The segregation of HF and PFC during movement The notion that the HF and PFC are entirely
      segregated during movement may seem somewhat implausible. We acknowledge that this highlights
470
      a limitation of our model’s fidelity to realistic biological settings. However, we focus on replay
471
      which occurs only at rest. During mobility, the HF and PFC could communicate with each other
472   not by replay but by theta waves, which are thought to be mainly driven by external signals and
473   involve only nearby place cells’ firing, rather than a global sequence. Allowing the two modules
474   to communicate during movement might add unnecessary details less relevant to the problem we
475   want to analyze. However, it would also be exciting to see how adding biological details could still
476   produce the phenomena we want and how different factors interact with each other.
477
478
      The difference of PFC from the striatum Previous RL theories for animal behaviors also em-
479
      phasize the striatum as the center for model-free learning. The dorsal lateral striatum (DLS) and the
480   ventral lateral striatum (VLS) correspond to the actor and critic parts in the classical Actor-Critic
481   algorithm. The dopamine serving as TD signal is calculated in the ventral tegmental area (VTA)
482   and drives synaptic plasticity in the whole network (Montague, 1999). Does this role of striatum
483   overlap with PFC? Wang et al. (2018) proposes a meta-reinforcement learning model where PFC is
484   the inner-loop ”fast” learner and the dopamine system is the ”slow” learner. We think it’s also useful
485   to consider the training process involved weight changes as a kind of dopamine learning, and later
      context identifying and following decision-making involved hidden state changes as PFC learning.

      4.2   H OW REPLAY PROMOTES LEARNING
487
488   The classical view posits that replay aids value-based reinforcement learning (Liu et al., 2021; Foster
489   & Wilson, 2006; Mattar & Daw, 2018; Ambrose et al., 2016), and our model provides a concrete
490   implementation supporting this view. In our model, cognitive map modulation in the PFC changes
491   as a result of replay and then stabilizes (Figure 4). A value-modulated cognitive map in the PFC
492   is reminiscent of value and task structure representations in the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) (?Zhou
493   et al., 2019; Padoa-Schioppa & Assad, 2006), a part of PFC. Our work suggests these representations
494
      could combine as a value function (V function) to guide decision-making. Our PFC cognitive map
      is formed with the help of HF, which also aligns with previous findings (Mızrak et al., 2021).
495
496   Despite those model-free components, our model should still be classified as model-based. The
497   main difference from AI model-based methods is that they sample from the distribution learned by
498   the world model and use these samples to improve the policy. The advantage is that it could provide
499   gradients for the sampling process and have the gradients of the reward loss directly back-propagated
500
      to the policy network. Our method, however, doesn’t design how the world model should help the
      policy network. The replay in our model is similar to a sampling process and maybe gradients are
501
      propagated through this process but future work needs to make this clearer. Recently Jensen et al.
502
      (2024) explores the model-based explanation of replay, but they also have an explicit design that the
503   samples from the world model are directly taken as input to the policy network.

670
      Replay is defined as the sequential activation of place cells. Therefore, a replay trajectory would
671
      represent a path in the room. We determine the attribution of replay by calculating the overlapping of
672   our replay with different parts of the room (Figure 6 left). Equal overlapping would cause exclusion
673   of that trajectory. Finally, the numbers of replay trajectories representing each part of the room are
674   normalized to get the spatial distribution of replay, namely ”replay distribution”.
675
      The rule we use is a little more complicated. Each part of the room is divided into two segments
676
      with scores that equal to 1 and 0.5 respectively. subsection A.1 provides a concrete example. The
677
      red trajectory is one replay.
