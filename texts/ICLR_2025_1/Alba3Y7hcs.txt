009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               While large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities across
012
               a wide range of domains, they still encounter significant challenges in reason-
013
               ing tasks that require gathering evidence over multiple turns and drawing logical
014            conclusions from this evidence. These challenges present significant obstacles
015            for LLM chat user interfaces, which rely on multi-turn interactions to facilitate
016            effective collaboration. This limitation leads to real-world issues; for example,
017            service chatbots must gather necessary information from customers over multiple
018            turns to diagnose and resolve problems effectively. Despite the multi-turn nature
019            of many real-world LLM use cases, most existing benchmarks rely on carefully
020            curated single-turn tests, which often blur the line between memorization and gen-
021            uine reasoning. To address this, we introduce the Wason Inductive Logic Test
022
               (WILT), a simple yet challenging multi-turn reasoning benchmark designed to
               resist memorization. WILT is inspired by the Wason 2-4-6 task (Wason, 1960),
023
               where participants must infer a basic boolean function involving three variables
024
               (e.g., x < y < z) by proposing test cases (such as (2, 4, 6)). In WILT, each test
025            starts from a clean slate, with only the initial instructions provided, preventing
026            models from relying on pre-learned responses. Over several turns, models must
027            interact with the environment by suggesting test cases to narrow the possible hy-
028            potheses and ultimately infer the hidden function based on the outcomes. Our
029            findings reveal that LLMs struggle with this task, exhibiting distinct strengths and
030            weaknesses: some are better at narrowing down the hypothesis space by propos-
031            ing valuable test cases, while others are more adept at deducing the hidden func-
032            tion from observed cases. Despite these variations, the best-performing model
033
               achieves only 28% accuracy, highlighting a significant gap in LLM performance
               on complex multi-turn reasoning tasks.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large language models (LLMs) powered by the transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017) have en-
040
      abled a new computing paradigm driven by natural language. These models are increasingly in-
041
      tegrated into day-to-day life beyond the machine learning research space, where they help many
042   people with common tasks. These models interact with users through multi-turn conversations, a
043   capability of next-token-prediction models bolstered via instruction-tuning (Mishra et al., 2021) and
044   alignment post-training phases (Ouyang et al., 2022). Despite their impressive single-turn perfor-
045   mance, LLMs often falter in scenarios requiring multi-turn reasoning and evidence gathering.
046
      The reasoning capability of LLMs, particularly in multi-turn scenarios, is of substantial interest. A
047
      commonly reported failure pattern for LLMs is the “doom loop”, where after an initially unsatis-
048   factory response, the model repeatedly responds with a near-identical message to one of its earlier
049   messages, providing minimal utility. For example, in code generation tasks like the example shown
050   in Figure 1, a model may repeatedly reply with code blocks identical to ones it has already seen or
051   produced, which impairs its subsequent usefulness. Strong multi-turn performance can be thought
052   of as collecting evidence over multiple steps to reduce the hypothesis space of the model’s possible
053   responses to only include useful ones: an important capability which remains critical beyond the
      first turn even in everyday LLM use cases.

      Measuring the performance of LLMs has been challenging for the research community. Existing
055   benchmarks overwhelmingly focus on single-turn interactions (Hendrycks et al., 2020; 2021), and
056   are often subject to memorization or overfitting (Goodhart, 1984; Recht et al., 2019). As a result, dif-
057   ficult benchmarks often keep a held-out, publicly unavailable test set to accurately evaluate models
058   (Chollet, 2019). While some multi-turn benchmarks have emerged to attempt to measure phenom-
059   ena like the “doom loop” (McLaughlin, 2024), none so far have done so for multi-turn, multi-step
060   inductive reasoning.
061   To bridge this gap, we introduce the Wason Inductive Logic Test (WILT), a multi-turn benchmark
062   designed to assess LLMs’ ability to gather evidence and reason across multiple turns. Inspired by the
063   Wason 2-4-6 task (Wason, 1960), WILT requires models to propose test cases (e.g. (2, 4, 6)), observe
064   the results (True or False), and iteratively narrow down the hypothesis space in order to deduce a
065   hidden rule (e.g. x < y < z). Since the task always begins from an identical starting state, it cannot
066   simply be solved by memorizing the answer to a prompt and forces the model to use multiple turns
067   to gather evidence. Therefore, WILT explicitly tests for two important capabilities in an LLM: (1)
068   whether the model can efficiently narrow down the hypothesis space of possible solutions by posing
069
      the right test cases over multiple turns, and (2) whether the model can recommend a simple and
      meaningful solution to the problem after accumulating evidence. While we find that LLMs struggle
070
      on this task overall, we find that some of them are better on one of aforementioned sub-tasks than
071
      the other. This knowledge can be useful for practitioners when choosing which models to deploy
072   for real world use cases.
073
074   In this work, we make the following contributions:
075
               1. Introducing WILT - We introduce a multi-turn inductive logic benchmark that cannot be
076
                  solved in a single turn. This requires a model to gather information, reflect upon what it
077               observes, and make decisions when sufficient evidence is gathered, mirroring real-world
078               tasks like debugging code or reasoning over time.
079
               2. Evaluating LLMs - We show that state-of-the-art LLMs struggle significantly on this task,
080
                  with the best model achieving only 28% accuracy. This highlights a significant deficiency
081               in multi-turn performance of top-performing LLMs, suggesting that strong single-turn per-
082               formance may not always transfer to multi-turn settings.
083
               3. Further Analysis - We perform several experiments analyzing hypothesis space reduction,
084
                  response complexity, and the performance of models when provided test cases from a dif-
085               ferent model. We demonstrate that models have varied strengths and weaknesses on this
086               task, and that combining models together in a multi-turn setting may increase performance
087               compared to using a single strong model.

      2       WILT
091
      The Wason Inductive Logic Test (WILT) is a benchmark for LLM reasoning inspired by the Wason
092
      2-4-6 task (Wason, 1960). Models begin with the instruction that they must uncover the hidden rule,
093   and may pose up to 30 test cases of that rule. For example, they can pose the tuple (2, 4, 6) and the
094   test will respond with “True, 29 Attempts Remaining.”
095
096
      All hidden rules take three numbers and return a boolean. These rules are simple and non-stochastic,
      so there is no additional value to posing the same test multiple times. Valid inputs include any float
097
      or integer that can be typed in three or fewer characters, excluding signs and the decimal point (e.g.
098
      -999, 1.23, 5). The hidden rules are written as Python lambda functions. After a maximum of thirty
099   tries (or any turn before then), the model may make one attempt to guess the function, after which
100   the test will terminate. The model must return a Python lambda function that is the same as or
101   equivalent1 to the hidden rule in order to receive full points.
102
      WILT is conceptually simple, but very challenging. Humans are able to identify simple rules despite
103
      the infinitely large hypothesis space, the unbounded difficulty of a hidden function, and the impos-
104
      sibility of verifying the correctness of your response (Tweney et al., 1980; Tukey, 1986). Without
105   effective priors for what constitutes a “simple” or “reasonable” answer, it becomes trivial to con-
106   struct an arbitrary counterexample to a current hypothesis. For example, consider the canonical

      The WILT benchmark therefore tests a few high-value behaviors of interest:
152
153        1. Multi-Turn Capability: Participants that fall into doom loops are punished by virtue of
154           having less useful information with which to infer the hidden rule.
155
           2. Hypothesis Space Reduction: Participants are rewarded for proposing test cases that ef-
156
              fectively narrow down the possible rules, despite that hypothesis space being infinitely
157
              large.
158
159
           3. Susceptibility to Confirmation Bias: Participants who are more prone to “confirming
              their hypothesis” rather than seeking falsification will perform poorly upon this task.
160
161        4. Inductive Reasoning: Proposing good test cases is a useful test of inductive reasoning and
              the ability to generalize from a number of specific examples.

162
                5. Deductive Reasoning: Proposing sensible functions after observing many test cases is
163                a useful test of deductive reasoning. Success in this task involves identifying a specific
164                function that accurately fits a set of examples.
165
                6. Occam’s Razor: Participants are rewarded for finding the simplest explanation fitting the
166
                   examples.

174   3       R ELATED W ORK
175
176   Compared to other reasoning benchmarks, WILT stands out as both highly multi-turn focused and
177   unusually robust to memorization. In contrast to other benchmarks, WILT requires models to in-
178   teract with an environment by proposing their own test cases to uncover a hidden function without
179   relying on pre-provided examples. This setup reduces the risk of overfitting, as each test begins with
180   the same initial instructions, and the model must generate and interpret its own data.
181
182   3.1       R EASONING B ENCHMARKS
183
184   There are a wide variety of reasoning benchmarks used to evaluate large language models. Some
185
      very notable among these are MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), Com-
      monsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2018), StrategyQA (Geva et al., 2021), BIG-BENCH (Srivastava et al.,
186
      2022), SciBench (Wang et al., 2023b), SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021), ARC-AGI (Chollet, 2019),
187
      MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), GPQA (Rein et al., 2023), and HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021).
188   These benchmarks are the standard for measuring LLM reasoning capabilities, but are overwhelm-
189   ingly carefully chosen single-turn problems which aim to meaningfully separate the performance
190   of different models on reasoning-like outputs such as math, code, or logic puzzles. However, these
191   benchmarks are subject to train-on-test leakage, even if efforts are made to decontaminate the dataset
192   (Yang et al., 2023), and the majority are explicitly single-turn tests. Our benchmark directly mea-
193   sures the model’s ability to navigate multi-turn scenarios, and does not require careful hiding of a
194   test set to prevent misleading results.
195   With respect to reasoning about simple functions, a benchmark that stands out as similar to ours
196   is CRUXEval (Gu et al., 2024), which assembles a list of 800 simple Python functions and input-
197   output pairs, and evaluates language models on their ability to predict input from output and output
198   from input. Our work could be seen as a multi-turn, more difficult extension of this work – one
199   where the function is replaced with a black box, where helpful and informative input-output pairs
200   are not provided but instead need to be searched for by the language model, and where the objective
201   is to infer the hidden function rather than the input or output.
202
203   3.2       M ULTI -T URN B ENCHMARKS
204
      There are a handful of multi-turn benchmarks used to evaluate LLMs. PlanBench (Valmeekam et al.,
205
      2022) is one prominent benchmark which attempts to measure the ability of LLMs to navigate plan-
206
      ning problems. This is a class of problems which are solved easily by classical planning algorithms
207   such as STRIPS (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971), and like our benchmark poses a significant challenge to
208   LLMs. PlanBench is a primarily multi-step, single-turn benchmark with a multi-turn component
209   (i.e. replanning based on unexpected events), which contrasts with our benchmark’s more direct
210   multi-turn focus. This can be observed in the o1 models performing comparatively well on Plan-
211   Bench (Valmeekam et al., 2024), since scaling inference time compute within a single turn would
212   be expected to improve performance substantially.
213   Closest to ours is Aidan-bench (McLaughlin, 2024), which directly measures the cosine similar-
214   ity between multi-turn responses. This represents a more pure measurement of the doom loop

249
      Hypothesis space representation is a commonly used framing in inductive logic tasks for LLMs. In

250
      propose hypotheses in natural language and then implement these hypotheses as Python programs.
251

252   approach could be used along with chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022) for WILT as well.

427   4.4   R ESPONSE C OMPLEXITY
428
429   To determine how well the models employ Occam’s Razor, we explore different metrics to gauge
430   whether the models find the simplest rule that covers the examples. From existing Bayesian models
431   of cognition (Tenenbaum, 1999; Tenenbaum & Griffiths, 2001), the size principle uses hypothesis
      size as a measure of simplicity. In these Bayesian models, hypothesis size is calculated as the number


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      of values that match the hypothesis. Calculating hypothesis size in this manner is only possible when
433   the test values are within a limited countable range. In our case, the possible test values are infinite,
434   requiring some alternative metrics to gauge hypothesis size. We use three metrics:
435
436         1. Number of Operators: We count the number of operators used in the rule expression.
437         2. Response Length: We calculate the string length of the rule expression. The longer the
438            length, the more complex it is likely to be. As longer outputs tend to be arbitrarily preferred
439            by automatic evaluators (Dubois et al., 2024), it is particularly important to measure the
440            brevity of the response for cases where simplicity is desired.
441         3. Set Inclusion: We generate a grid of integer-float tuples and apply them to guessed and
442            actual rules to generate sets of tuples returning “True”. If the set of the guessed rule is a
443            subset or superset of the actual rule, we then calculate their set size ratio. A ratio of 1 is
444            ideal, > 1 suggests a less complex guess, and < 1 a more complex one.
445
446   Table 2 shows the complexity metrics of the LLMs. Many LLMs with high accuracy such as Claude
447
      3.5 Sonnet, chatgpt-4o-latest, and Mistral Large have long response lengths. However, o1-preview
      has a short response length and few operators, despite its high performance on the task. The differ-
448
      ences in response length and number of operators is most clearly seen in the incorrect answers. For
449
      example, if the correct rule is lambda x, y, z: (x * y * z)% 2 == 1, Claude 3.5 Sonnet’s
450   guess is lambda x,y,z: all(n > 0 and int(n)== n and (n & (n-1)== 0)and (n %
451   3 == 0 or n == 1)for n in [x,y,z]), which is more convoluted than o1-preview’s guess
452   of lambda x, y, z: abs(x)== 1 and abs(y)== 1 and abs(z)== 1. Combined with its
453   low number of average guesses made before making the final guess, o1-preview appears to follow
454   Occam’s Razor very closely compared to most of the other high-performing models with longer
455   response lengths. For the set inclusion ratio, the best models tend to cluster around an intermediate
456   value of 2-4.

      5   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we highlight the importance of the multi-turn setting for understanding the performance
526   of large language models in everyday use cases. We introduced a new benchmark which attempts to
527   measure performance in this common setting, and show its difficulty even for state-of-the-art LLMs.
528   With the models exhibiting non-overlapping strengths, the resulting performance of these models
529   on the WILT task provides useful context for what types of tasks they would be useful for, beyond
530
      simply suggesting the top-performing model is the best one to use for all applications.
531   Our benchmark identifies a critical way that typical benchmarks differ from day-to-day use of LLMs:
532   whereas effective multi-turn collaboration often requires asking clarifying questions, performing
533   experiments, and gathering new evidence, typical benchmarks are overwhelmingly designed to be
534   solved immediately. This may, in turn, create an implicit incentive for models to end conversations
535   as quickly as possible, even where that may be ineffective or inappropriate.
536   With this work, we aim to provide a benchmark that measures a model’s capacity for exploring an
537   environment and reasoning based on its own decisions across multiple turns. We believe that this
538   paradigm offers a more direct measurement of reasoning capability compared to other benchmarks.
539   Likewise, we believe an explicit focus on multi-turn reasoning capability more directly shines a light
      upon the most common uses of LLMs.

      A.2     T EST C ASE N OVELTY
744
745   Test case novelty is an interesting second order metric for success upon the WILT task. Broadly
746   speaking, models that reuse fewer tests are rewarded with more information for which to solve the
747   task. Models that very rarely re-propose a test tend to perform very well upon WILT, but the inverse
748   is not necessarily true – models that loop tests often still arrive at the right answer.
749
      Repeated tests are useful for bifurcating the types of failures on WILT – one being the doom loop
750   phenomenon, and the other being reasoning capability conditioned upon some available evidence.
751   One hypothesis for the observed behavior is that certain models are primarily oriented towards
752   single-turn scenarios, and that one type of failure need not imply the other. DeepSeek Chat v2.5, for
753   example, demonstrates strong initial hypothesis space reduction compared to other models, which
754   allows it notably better performance on WILT compared to other models with similar repeat counts
755   (e.g. open-mistral-nemo). Strong single-turn performance and deductive reasoning capabilities can
      help salvage performance from a model that demonstrates difficulty with multi-turn inductive logic.
