009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011             Molecular Relational Learning (MRL) is a rapidly growing field that focuses
012             on understanding the interaction dynamics between molecules, which is cru-
013             cial for applications ranging from catalyst engineering to drug discovery. De-
014             spite recent progress, earlier MRL approaches are limited to using only the 2D
015             topological structure of molecules, as obtaining the 3D interaction geometry re-
016             mains prohibitively expensive. This paper introduces a novel 3D geometric pre-
017
                training strategy for MRL (3DMRL) that incorporates a 3D virtual interaction
                environment, overcoming the limitations of costly traditional quantum mechani-
018
                cal calculation methods. With the constructed 3D virtual interaction environment,
019
                3DMRL trains 2D MRL model to learn the overall 3D geometric information of
020             molecular interaction through contrastive learning. Moreover, fine-grained inter-
021             action between molecules is learned through force prediction loss, which is crucial
022             in understanding the wide range of molecular interaction processes. Extensive ex-
023             periments on various tasks using real-world datasets, including out-of-distribution
024             and extrapolation scenarios, demonstrate the effectiveness of 3DMRL, showing
025             up to a 24.93% improvement in performance across 40 tasks. Our code is publicly
026             available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/3DMRL-F973.

054
      However, acquiring stereochemical structures of molecules is often very costly, resulting in limited
055   availability of such 3D geometric information for downstream tasks (Liu et al., 2021). Consequently,
056   in the domain of molecular property prediction (MPP), there has been substantial progress in inject-
057   ing 3D geometric information to 2D molecular graph encoders during the pre-training phase, while
058   utilizing only the 2D molecular graph encoder for downstream tasks (Stärk et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
059   2023). In contrast, compared to the MPP, pre-training and fine-tuning strategies for MRL have been
060   surprisingly underexplored, primarily due to the following two distinct challenges associated with
061   modeling complex molecular interaction environments.
062   Firstly, interactions between molecules occur through complex geometry as they are chaotically
063   distributed in space as shown in Figure 1 (b). Therefore, it is essential to consider not only each
064   molecule’s independent geometry but also their relative positions and orientations in space. This re-
065   quirement further complicates the acquisition of geometric information, making it more challenging
066   to obtain detailed 3D geometry of molecular interaction environments. Consequently, it is essential
067   to model an interaction environment that can simulate molecular interactions based solely on the 3D
068
      geometry of the individual molecules.
069   Secondly, in addition to the complexity of the interaction environment, the forces between molecules
070   during interactions are vital in MRL, as they are key to understanding a wide range of physical,
071   chemical, and biological processes. For instance, in solute-solvent interactions, polar solutes dis-
072   solve in polar solvents because of dipole-dipole interactions or hydrogen bonding. These forces
073
      allow solute molecules to form favorable interactions with solvent molecules, promoting solvation
      and enhancing solubility (Atkins et al., 2023). Thus, it is essential to develop pre-training strategies
074
      that effectively capture the forces between molecules within their interaction geometry.
075
076   To address these challenges, we introduce a novel 3D geometric pre-training strategy that is ap-
077   plicable to various MRL models by incorporating the 3D geometry of the interaction environment
078   for molecules (3DMRL). Specifically, instead of relying on costly traditional quantum mechanical
      calculation methods to obtain interaction environments, we first propose a virtual interaction en-
079
      vironment involving multiple molecules designed to simulate real molecular interactions. Then,
080
      during the pre-training stage, a 2D MRL model is trained to produce representations that are aligned
081   with those of the 3D virtual interaction environment via contrastive learning. Additionally, the
082   2D MRL model is trained to predict the forces between molecules within this virtual interaction
083   environment, allowing the model to effectively learn fine-grained atom-level interactions between
084   molecules. These two pre-training strategies enable the 2D MRL model to be pre-trained to un-
085   derstand the nature of molecular interactions, facilitating positive transfer to a wide range of down-
086   stream MRL tasks. In this paper, we make the following contributions:
087
       • Rather than relying on costly traditional quantum mechanical calculation methods to obtain in-
088
         teraction geometry, we propose a virtual interaction geometry made up of multiple molecules to
089
         mimic the molecular interaction environment observed in real-world conditions (Section 4.1).
090
091
       • We propose pre-training strategies that enable the 2D MRL model to learn representations
         aligned with the 3D virtual interaction environment and capture the intermolecular forces be-
092
         tween interacting molecules within the environment (Section 4.2).
093
094
       • We conduct extensive experiments across various MRL models pre-trained with 3DMRL on a
         range of MRL tasks, including out-of-distribution and extrapolation scenarios. These exper-
095
         iments demonstrate improvements of up to 24.93% compared to MRL methods trained from
096
         scratch, underscoring the versatility of 3DMRL (Section 5).
097
098   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper proposing pre-training strategies specifically
099   designed for molecular relational learning.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   2.1   M OLECULAR R ELATIONAL L EARNING (MRL)
104
105   Molecular Relational Learning (MRL) focuses on understanding the interaction dynamics between
106   paired molecules. Delfos (Lim & Jung, 2019) employs recurrent neural networks combined with
107   attention mechanisms to predict solvation-free energy, a key factor influencing the solubility of
      chemical substances, using SMILES string as input. Similarly, CIGIN (Pathak et al., 2020) utilizes

                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      message-passing neural networks (Gilmer et al., 2017) along with a cross-attention mechanism to
109   capture atomic representations for solvation-free energy prediction. In a different context, Joung
110   et al. (2021) use graph convolutional networks (Kipf & Welling, 2016) to generate representations
111   of chromophores and solvents, which are then used to predict various optical and photophysical
112   properties of chromophores, essential for developing new materials with vibrant colors. Meanwhile,
113   MHCADDI (Deac et al., 2019) introduces a co-attentive message passing network (Veličković et al.,
114   2017) designed for predicting drug-drug interactions (DDI), which aggregates information from
115   all atoms within a pair of molecules, not just within individual molecules. Recently, CGIB (Lee
116
      et al., 2023a) and CMRL (Lee et al., 2023b) have introduced a comprehensive framework for MRL
      tasks, such as predicting solvation-free energy, chromophore-solute interactions, and drug-drug in-
117
      teractions. These models achieve this by identifying core functional groups involved in molecular
118
      interactions using information bottleneck and causal theory, respectively. However, prior studies
119   have largely ignored molecules’ 3D geometric information despite its well-established importance
120   in comprehending various molecular properties.
121
122   2.2   3D P RE - TRAINING FOR M OLECULAR P ROPERTY P REDICTION (MPP)
123
124   Recently, the molecular science community has shown increasing interest in pre-training machine
125   learning models with unlabeled data, primarily due to the scarcity of labeled data for downstream
126   tasks (Lee et al., 2023b; Velez-Arce et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). A promising approach in this
127   area leverages molecules’ inherent nature, which can be effectively represented as both 2D topo-
128   logical graphs and 3D geometric graphs. For instance, 3D Infomax (Stärk et al., 2022) aims to
      enhance mutual information between 2D and 3D molecular representations using contrastive learn-
129
      ing. GraphMVP (Liu et al., 2021) extends this concept by introducing a generative pre-training
130
      framework alongside contrastive learning. More recently, Noisy Nodes (Zaidi et al., 2022) and
131   MoleculeSDE (Liu et al., 2023) have introduced methods to learn the 3D geometric distribution
132   of molecules using a denoising framework, thereby uncovering the connection between the score
133   function and the force field of molecules. Although the 3D structure of molecules has been effec-
134   tively leveraged in pre-training for predicting single molecular properties, it remains surprisingly
135   underexplored in the context of molecular relational learning (MRL).

      4.1   V IRTUAL I NTERACTION G EOMETRY C ONSTRUCTION
186   While the 3D geometry of molecules plays a significant role in predicting molecular properties,
187   acquiring this information involves a trade-off between cost and accuracy. For example, RDKit’s
188   ETKDG algorithm (Landrum, 2013) is fast but less accurate. In contrast, the widely adopted meta-
189   dynamics method, CREST (Grimme, 2019), achieves a more balanced compromise between speed
190   and accuracy, yet still requires around 6 hours to process a drug-like molecule. This challenge is
191   even more pronounced in MRL, which necessitates not just the geometry of individual molecules but
192   also the relative spatial arrangements between multiple molecules (Durrant & McCammon, 2011;
193   Sosso et al., 2016). Therefore, this study aims to develop a virtual interaction geometry consist-
      ing of multiple molecules to mimic real-world molecular interactions utilizing the 3D geometry of
194
      individual molecules. However, it is not trivial to model the environment of real-world molecular
195
      interaction environments due to its chaotic nature as shown in Figure 1 (b).
196

285   4.2.2   I NTERMOLECULAR F ORCE P REDICTION
286
      Beyond the overall geometry of interaction, it is essential to learn about the intermolecular forces
287   between molecules during molecular interactions, as these forces govern how molecules behave,
288   interact, and react in various environments. Inspired by scientific knowledge, we propose a pre-
289   training strategy to predict the direction of forces acting between molecules based on the assumption
290   that forces are exerted between molecules during their interactions (London, 1937). That is, we aim
291   to pre-train the 2D MRL model to predict forces in the constructed virtual interaction geometry.
292   However, predicting forces from a 2D representation is challenging because the prediction must
293   adhere to the physical properties of forces, specifically being equivariant to rotations and transitions
294
      in 3D Euclidean space, also known as SE(3)-equivariance (Duval et al., 2023). To address this, we
      propose predicting the force between molecules by utilizing local frame (Du et al., 2022), which
295
      allows for flexible conversion between invariant and equivariant features.

397   Evaluation metrics. For regression tasks, we use Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) to measure the
398
      difference between the predicted and the ground truth values. For classification tasks, we measure
      the model performance using the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (AUROC).
399
400   Evaluation protocol. Following Pathak et al. (2020), for the molecular interaction prediction task,
401   we evaluate the models under a 5-fold cross-validation scheme. The dataset is randomly split into 5
402   subsets and one of the subsets is used as the test set, while the remaining subsets are used to train
403   the model. A subset of the test set is selected as the validation set for hyperparameter selection and
404
      early stopping. We repeat 5-fold cross-validation three times (i.e., 15 runs in total) and report the
      accuracy and standard deviation of the repeats.
405
406   For the DDI prediction task (Lee et al., 2023a), we conduct experiments on two different out-of-
407   distribution scenarios, namely molecule split and scaffold split. For the molecule split, the perfor-
408   mance is evaluated when the models are presented with new molecules not included in the training
409
      dataset. Specifically, let G denote the total set of molecules in the dataset. Given G, we split G
      into Gold and Gnew , so that Gold contains the set of molecules that have been seen in the training
410
      phase, and Gnew contains the set of molecules that have not been seen in the training phase. Then,
411
      the new split of dataset consists of Dtrain = {(G 1 , G 2 ) ∈ D|G 1 ∈ Gold ∧ G 2 ∈ Gold } and Dtest =
412   {(G 1 , G 2 ) ∈ D|(G 1 ∈ Gnew ∧G 2 ∈ Gnew )∨(G 1 ∈ Gnew ∧G 2 ∈ Gold )∨(G 1 ∈ Gold ∧G 2 ∈ Gnew )}.
413   We use a subset of Dtest as the validation set in inductive setting. In the scaffold split setting (Huang
414   et al., 2021), just like in the molecule split, molecules corresponding to scaffolds that were not seen
415   during training will be used for testing. For both splits, we repeat 5 independent experiments with
416   different random seeds on split data, and report the accuracy and the standard deviation of the re-
417   peats. In both scenarios, we split the data into training, validation, and test sets with a ratio of
418   60/20/20%. We provide details on model implementation and training in Section C. Our code is
419
      publicly available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/3DMRL-F973.

475
476   Ablation Studies. To further understand our model, we conduct an ablation study to investigate
477   the impact of two key components on the final performance. Specifically, as shown in Equation 7,
478
      the objective function contains two terms: (i) contrastive learning-based loss and (ii) intermolecular
      force prediction loss; we curate two variants that involve only (i) (denoted only cont.) and only
479
      (ii) (denoted only force) in Figure 3 (a). As shown in Figure 3 (a), the contrastive learning-based
480   loss plays a particularly critical role. Removing it from 3DMRL results in a significant performance
481   drop, even falling below MPP pre-training strategies such as 3D Infomax and GraphMVP. This
482   is because the contrastive loss allows the model to capture the overall interaction geometry at the
483   molecular level, while the force prediction loss focuses on learning more fine-grained, atom-level
484   interactions. However, combining both losses, as in 3DMRL, yields the best results, demonstrating
485   the importance of leveraging the strengths of both levels of granularity. We provide further detailed
      results of ablation studies in Appendix D.3.

      Environment analysis. While we propose assigning a single small molecule to each target atom
498   during molecular interaction in Section 4.1, we also investigate the impact of varying the number
499   of assigned small molecules per atom in the larger molecule. As illustrated in Figure 3 (b), we ob-

501   a fixed number of target atoms n. This suggests that modeling interactions between multiple small
502   molecules and a single atom in a larger molecule can degrade model performance. This is consis-
503   tent with scientific understanding that, although hydrogen bonding can occasionally allow multiple
504   molecules to interact with a single atom simultaneously, steric and electronic hindrances frequently
505
      impede such interactions. Thus, we contend that our proposed virtual interaction geometry appro-
      priately reflects the real-world physics in molecular interactions.

508   effect of the number of target atoms n, which determines the number of small molecules in a virtual
509   interaction geometry. To do so, we examine the Chromophore dataset, where the larger molecules
510   primarily consist of 34 atoms each. In Figure 3 (c), we observe that the model achieves optimal
511
      performance when using five small molecules to construct the virtual interaction geometry. More
      specifically, using too few small molecules (n = 2) results in poorer performance, as it fails to
512
      adequately simulate real-world interaction environments. On the other hand, the model performance
513   also declines as the number of small molecules increases, likely due to the 3D geometry encoder
514   overfitting to the small molecules with an excessive count. Furthermore, we observe that as the
515   number of target atoms increases, more extensive computational resources are required to encode
516   the 3D interaction geometry during pre-training. Hence, selecting an appropriate number of target
517   atoms is crucial for both model performance and computational efficiency. We provide additional
518   analyses on different datasets in Appendix D.4.

520   force prediction loss, in Equation 7. In Figure 3 (d), the model’s performance declines as α increases
521   from 0.1, primarily because it overly emphasizes atom-level interactions between the molecules in-
522   stead of considering the overall interaction geometry. Conversely, we also notice a drop in perfor-
523   mance when force prediction loss is not utilized (i.e., α = 0.0), as this causes the model to lose
524   ability in learning fine-grained atom-level interactions. It is important to note that while we set
525   n = 5 and α = 0.1 across all datasets during pre-training, models pre-trained with varying n and α
526
      consistently outperform those trained from scratch, demonstrating the robustness of 3DMRL.

528   6      C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we propose 3DMRL, a novel pre-training framework that effectively integrates 3D
531   geometric information into molecular relational learning (MRL). By constructing a virtual interac-
532   tion geometry and employing contrastive learning and intermolecular force prediction, our approach
533   successfully injects complex 3D geometry information of molecular interactions into 2D MRL mod-
534
      els. Experimental results demonstrate that 3DMRL significantly enhances the performance of 2D
      MRL models across various downstream tasks and neural architectures, validating the importance
535
      of incorporating 3D geometric data.
536
537   Future work will extend the current research to (1) drug-target binding affinity prediction, which is
538   a fundamental task in drug discovery, where the larger molecule is a protein target, involving more
539
      complex protein structures, and (2) organic-inorganic interaction prediction tasks mimicking the
      dissociation process, focusing on accurately modeling the behavior of organic-inorganic complexes.

759
760   We utilize three distinct datasets, i.e., Chromophore, CombiSolv, and DDI, to pre-train 3DMRL for
761   each downstream task as described in Section 5. Specifically, we use the Chromophore dataset for
762   downstream tasks involving the optical properties of chromophores, the CombiSolv dataset for tasks
763   related to the solvation free energy of solutes, and the DDI dataset, which we created for the drug-
764   drug interaction task.
765
       • The Chromophore dataset (Joung et al., 2020) consists of 20,236 combinations derived from
766
         6,815 chromophores and 1,336 solvents, provided in SMILES string format. For pre-training,
767      we initially convert chromophores and solvents into their respective 3D structures via rdkit,
768      resulting in 6,524 3D structures for chromophores and 1,255 for solvents. These 6,524 unique
769      chromophores are then randomly paired with the 1,255 solvents to generate a sufficient number
770      of pairs. Out of the possible 8,187,620 chromophore-solvent combinations, we randomly sample
771      1%, which corresponds to 81,876 pairs, for pre-training.
772    • The CombiSolv dataset (Vermeire & Green, 2021) contains 10,145 combinations derived from
773      1,368 solutes and 291 solvents, provided in SMILES string format. Similar to our approach
774      with the Chromophore dataset, we first convert solutes and solvents into their corresponding 3D
775      structures, yielding 1,368 3D structures for solutes and 290 for solvents. From the potential
776      random combinations, we select 79,344 solute-solvent pairs, representing 20% of all possible
777      pairs.
778    • For the DDI dataset, we compile drug-drug pairs from the ZhangDDI (Zhang et al., 2017b),
779      ChChMiner (Zitnik et al., 2018), and DeepDDI (Ryu et al., 2018) datasets. From a total of
780      235,547 positive pairs, we randomly sample 40% (i.e., 94,218 pairs) for use as the pre-training
781      dataset. While chromophores and solutes act as the larger molecule g 1 in molecular interaction
782      tasks, in the DDI dataset, we designate the drug with the larger radius as the larger molecule.

936   For the 2D MRL model, following a previous work (Pathak et al., 2020), we use 3-layer MPNNs

938   vent for the molecular interaction prediction, while we use a GIN (Xu et al., 2018) to encode both

940
      ReLU activation function for both. For the 3D virtual environment encoder f3D , we utilize SchNet
941
      (Schütt et al., 2017), which guarantees an SE(3)-invariant representation of the environment. For
942   both molecular interaction and drug-drug interaction tasks, we configure SchNet with 128 hidden
943   channels, 128 filters, 6 interaction layers, and a cutoff distance of 5.0.

948   learning rate of 0.0005 for the chromophore task, 0.0001 for the solvation free energy task, and
949   0.0005 for the DDI tasks. The model is optimized over 100 epochs during pre-training.
950   In the downstream tasks, the learning rate was reduced by a factor of 10−1 after 20 epochs of no
951   improvement in model performance in validation set, following the approach in a previous work
952   (Pathak et al., 2020), with the initial learning rate of 0.005 for the chromophore task, 0.001 for the
953   solvation free energy task, and 0.0005 for the DDI tasks.
