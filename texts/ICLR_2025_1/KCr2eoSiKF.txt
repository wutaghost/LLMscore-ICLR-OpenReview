010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            The widespread use of machine learning models in high-stakes domains can have a
013            major negative impact, especially on individuals who receive undesirable outcomes.
014            Algorithmic recourse provides such individuals with suggestions of minimum-cost
015            improvements they can make to achieve a desirable outcome in the future. However,
016
               machine learning models often get updated over time and this can cause a recourse
               to become invalid (i.e., not lead to the desirable outcome). The robust recourse
017
               literature aims to choose recourses less sensitive, even against adversarial model
018
               changes, but this comes at a higher cost. To overcome this obstacle, we initiate
019            the study of algorithmic recourse through the learning-augmented framework and
020            evaluate the extent to which a designer equipped with a prediction regarding future
021            model changes can reduce the cost of recourse when the prediction is accurate
022            (consistency) while also limiting the cost even when the prediction is inaccurate
023            (robustness). We propose a novel algorithm for this problem, study the robustness-
024            consistency trade-off, and analyze how prediction accuracy affects performance.
025

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Machine learning models are nowadays widely deployed even in sensitive domains such as lending
030   or hiring. For example, financial institutions use these models to determine whether someone should
031   receive a loan. Given the major impact that such decisions can have on people’s lives, a plethora
032   of recent work in responsible machine learning aims to make these models fair (Berk et al., 2021;
033
      Barocas et al., 2019; Zafar et al., 2017; Hardt et al., 2016), transparent (Lakkaraju et al., 2016; Rudin,
      2019), and explainable (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Smilkov et al., 2017). A notable
034
      line of work along this direction called algorithmic recourse (Wachter et al., 2018; Ustun et al., 2019;
035
      Karimi et al., 2020a), provides each individual who was given an undesirable label (e.g., one whose
036   loan request was denied) with a minimum cost improvement suggestion to achieve the desired label.
037
038   One important weakness of much of the work on algorithmic recourse is the assumption that models
039
      are fixed and do not change (Wachter et al., 2018; Ustun et al., 2019). In practice, many models are
      periodically updated to reflect the changes in data distribution or the environment, which can cause the
040
      recourse to become invalid, i.e., following it may not lead to a desirable outcome (Dominguez-Olmedo
041
      et al., 2022). To alleviate this problem, Upadhyay et al. (2021) proposed a recourse framework that is
042   robust to adversarial changes to the model parameters and provided an algorithm called ROAR to
043   compute robust recourses. Subsequently, Nguyen et al. (2022) proposed another algorithm (RBR for
044   short) to improve ROAR’s performance for non-linear models. While both of these works computer
045   recourses less sensitive to adversarial model changes, this comes at the price of higher cost.
046
      To overcome this issue, we revisit the algorithmic recourse problem through the lens of the learning-
047
      augmented framework (Mitzenmacher & Vassilvitskii, 2020) which has been used in a surge of recent
048   work to overcome the limitations of adversarial (i.e., worst-case) analysis. Specifically, rather than
049   assuming that the designer has no information regarding how the model can change, we assume the
050   designer can formulate predictions regarding what these changes may be. However, crucially, these
051   predictions are unreliable and can be arbitrarily inaccurate. Using the learning-augmented approach,
052   our goal is to optimize the validity-cost trade-off by computing recourses that perform near-optimally
053   when the predictions are accurate (consistency) while maintaining good performance even in the
      worst-case, i.e., even when the predictions are arbitrarily inaccurate (robustness).

054
      Our Results In Section 3, we adapt the learning-augmented framework to algorithmic recourse,
055   and our first result (Section 3.1) is a computationally efficient algorithm that computes a recourse
056   with optimal robustness for generalized linear models. This is a non-convex problem and, to the
057   best of our knowledge, this is the first optimal algorithm for any robust recourse problem. For
058   non-linear models, we first approximate the model with a local linear model and then utilize our
059   algorithm to provide recourse for the approximate model. In Section 4.1, we empirically study the
060   combinations of robustness and consistency that are achievable across different datasets and models.
061   These results indicate that the trade-off between robustness and consistency is domain-dependent and
062   can vary greatly across different datasets and models. Furthermore, apart from the extreme measures
063
      of consistency and robustness, in Section 4.1, we also study how the quality of recourse solutions
      returned by our algorithm degrades as a function of the prediction error. Finally, in Section 4.2 we
064
      compare our recourses to those computed by ROAR and RBR, and we observe that our recourses have
065
      higher validity than these two baselines. Furthermore, for any fixed level of validity, our recourses
066   generally have lower costs compared to ROAR and RBR.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
069
070   The most closely related works are by Upadhyay et al. (2021) and Nguyen et al. (2022). We compare
071   our approach to both papers in Section 4.2. The RoCourseNet algorithm (Guo et al., 2023) also
072   provides robust recourse though a direct comparison with this algorithm is not possible, as it is an
073   end-to-end approach, i.e., it simultaneously optimizes for the learned model and robust recourse while
074   the initial model is fixed in our approach, just like in (Upadhyay et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022).
075   The learning-augmented framework has been applied to a wide variety of settings, aiming to provide
076   a refined understanding of the performance guarantees that are achievable beyond the worst case.
077   One of the main application domains is the design of algorithms (e.g., online algorithms (Lykouris
078   & Vassilvitskii, 2018; Purohit et al., 2018)), but it has also been used toward the design of data
079   structures (Kraska et al., 2018), mechanisms interacting with strategic agents (Agrawal et al., 2022;
080   Xu & Lu, 2022), and privacy-preserving methods for processing sensitive data (Khodak et al., 2023).
081
      This is already a vast and rapidly growing literature; see (Lindermayr & Megow, 2024) for a frequently
      updated and organized list of related papers. Additional related work is provided in Appendix A.

084   2     P RELIMINARIES
085
086   Consider a predictive model fθ : X → Y, parameterized by θ ∈ Θ ⊆ Rd , which maps instances
087   (e.g., loan applicants) from a feature space X ⊆ Rd to an outcome space Y = {0, 1}. The values of
088   0 and 1 represent undesirable and desirable outcomes (e.g., loan denial or approval), respectively. If a
089
      model fθ0 yields an undesirable outcome for some instance x0 ∈ X , i.e., fθ0 (x0 ) = 0, the objective
      in recourse is to suggest the least costly way to modify x0 (e.g., how an applicant should strengthen
090
      their application) so that the resulting instance x′ would achieve the desirable outcome under fθ0 .
091
      Given a cost function c : X × X → R+ that quantifies the cost of this transformation from x0 to x′ ,
092   the recourse is defined with the following optimization problem (Upadhyay et al., 2021):
093
094
                                         min ℓ (fθ0 (x′ ), 1) + λ · c (x′ , x0 ) ,                             (1)
                                         x′ ∈X
095
      where ℓ : R × R → R+ is a loss function (such as binary cross entropy or squared loss) that captures
096   the extent to which the condition fθ0 (x′ ) = 1 is violated and λ ≥ 0 is a regularizer that balances the
097   degree of violation from the desirable outcome and the cost of modifying x0 to x′ . The regularizer λ
098   can be decreased gradually until the desired outcome is reached. In this work, following the approach
099   of Upadhyay et al. (2021); Rawal & Lakkaraju (2020), we assume the cost function is the L1 distance
100   i.e. c(x, x′ ) = ∥x − x′ ∥1 . This implies that all the features are manipulable, they can be changed
101   independently, and the cost of manipulating each feature is the same.1 We also assume the loss
102   function ℓ is convex and decreasing in its first argument, which is satisfied by many commonly used
103   loss functions such as binary-cross entropy or squared loss.
104   We denote the total cost of a given recourse x′ for a given model θ using
105
                                   J(x0 , x′ , θ, λ) = ℓ (fθ (x′ ), 1) + λ · c(x′ , x0 ).                      (2)

110   The objective defined in Equation (1) assumes that the parameters of the model remain the same
111   over time, but this does not capture the fact that, in practice, predictive models may be periodically
112
      retrained and updated (Upadhyay et al., 2021). These updates can cause a recourse that is valid in the
      original model (i.e., one that would lead to the desirable outcome in that model) to become invalid in
113
      the updated model (Dutta et al., 2022; Black et al., 2022). It is, therefore, natural to require a recourse
114
      solution whose validity is robust to (slight) changes in the model parameters.
115
116   In line with prior work on robust recourse (Upadhyay et al., 2021; Black et al., 2022), we assume
117   that the parameters of the updated model can be any θ′ ∈ Θα , where Θα ⊆ Θ is a “neighborhood”
118
      around the parameters, θ0 , of the original model. Specifically, this neighborhood is defined using the
      L∞ distance and a parameter α, so that a. Given θ0 and Θα , the robust solution would be to choose a
119
      recourse xr that minimizes the total cost assuming the parameters of the updated model θ′ ∈ Θα are

      3    L EARNING -AUGMENTED F RAMEWORK FOR ROBUST R ECOURSE
126
127
      Choosing the robust recourse xr according to (3) optimizes the total cost against an adversarially
128   chosen θ ∈ Θα , but this total cost may be much higher than the optimal total cost in hindsight, i.e.,
129   if we knew the new model parameters, θ′ . This is due to the overly pessimistic assumption that the
130   designer has no information regarding what the realized θ′ ∈ Θα may be. Aiming to overcome
131   similarly pessimistic results in a variety of other domains, a surge of recent work has used the learning-
132   augmented framework (Mitzenmacher & Vassilvitskii, 2020) to provide a more refined and practical
133   analysis. This framework assumes the designer is equipped with some unreliable (machine-learned)
134   prediction and then seeks to achieve near-optimal performance whenever the prediction is accurate
135   while simultaneously maintaining some robustness even if the prediction is arbitrarily inaccurate.
136   We adapt the learning-augmented framework to the algorithmic recourse problem and assume that the
137   designer can generate (or is provided with) an unreliable prediction θ̂ ∈ Θα regarding the model’s
138   parameters after the model change. For example, in the loan approval setting, a prediction can be
139   inferred by any information regarding whether the lender would be tightening or loosening its policy
140   over time, or even conveying more information about the changes in the form of an exact prediction
141   for the future model. If the designer trusts the accuracy of this prediction, then an optimal solution
142   would be to choose a recourse xc that is consistent with this prediction, θ̂, i.e.,
143
                                              xc ∈ arg min J(x′ , θ̂).                                       (4)
144                                                    ′ x ∈X
145
      However, since this prediction is unreliable, following it blindly could lead to very poor robustness.
146   To evaluate the performance of a recourse x′ based on the learning-augmented framework we use the
147   robustness and consistency measures, defined below.
148
      Definition 3.1 (Robustness). Given a parameter α, the robustness of a recourse x′ ∈ X is
149
150                               R(x′ , α) = max

                                                  J(x′ , θ′ ) − max

                                                                    J(xr , θ′ ),                             (5)
                                               θ ∈Θα                θ ∈Θα

153
154
      The robustness measure evaluates the worst-case total cost of x′ against an adversarial change of
      the model and then compares it to the corresponding total cost of xr . The robustness is always at
155
      least zero (since xr could always be chosen as the proposed recourse x′ ) and lower robustness values
156
      are more desirable. Note that Upadhyay et al. (2021) measure robustness in absolute terms, but we
157   evaluate it relative to xr to enable a more direct comparison between robustness and consistency.
158
159
      Definition 3.2 (Consistency). Given a prediction θ̂ ∈ Θα , the consistency of a recourse x′ ∈ X is
160                                       C(x′ , θ̂) = J(x′ , θ̂) − J(xc , θ̂),                              (6)

      where xc is defined in Equation (4).

      The consistency is also always at least zero (zero consistency can be achieved simply by using
163   x′ = xc , i.e., by trusting the prediction) and lower consistency values are more desirable.

165   Therefore, choosing xr guarantees an optimal robustness of zero, but it can lead to poor consistency,
166
      and choosing xc guarantees an optimal consistency of zero, but it can lead to poor robustness. One of
      our main goals in this paper is to study the achievable trade-off between robustness and consistency,
167
      and we present our experimental evaluation of this trade-off in Section 4.1.

170   3.1    C OMPUTING ROBUST AND C ONSISTENT R ECOURSES
171
      We next propose an algorithm to compute xr , i.e., a robust recourse that provides an optimal solution
172
      to the optimization problem of Equation (3) when the function fθ is a generalized linear model. This
173   algorithm can also be used to compute the consistent solution xc of Equation (4), by setting α = 0.
174   A model is generalized linear if fθ can be written as fθ (x) := g ◦ hθ (x), i.e., a composition of two
175   functions, where hθ : X → R is a linear function mapping inputs to scores and g : R → [0, 1] is a
176   non-decreasing function mapping scores to probabilities of favorable outcome (which is the label 1 in
177   our setting). For example, setting g to be the sigmoid function will recover the logistic regression.
178
      Note that even for generalized linear functions, the objective function in (4) is non-convex (see Ap-
179   pendix B). Hence, gradient-based approaches such as RObust Algorithmic Recourse (ROAR) (Upad-
180   hyay et al., 2021) or Robust Bayesian Recourse (RBR) (Nguyen et al., 2022) can only converge to a
181   locally optimal recourse, as opposed to our algorithm which guarantees globally optimal recourse.
182   We empirically compare the performance of our algorithm to these algorithms in Section 4.2.
      We introduce a few additional notations. We use sgn to denote the function sgn(s) = 1[s ≥ 0]
183
184
      where 1 is the indicator function. When applied to a vector, the sgn is applied element-wise to
185   each dimension of the vector. For an integer n ∈ N, [n] := {1, . . . , n}. We also use ei to denote a
186   d-dimensional unit vector with all zeros except for i-th coordinate which has a value of one.
187
188
      ALGORITHM 1: Optimal Robust Recourse
189
      Input : x0 , θ0 , ℓ, c, α
190
      Output: x′
191    1: Initialize x′ ← x0
192    2: Initialize ACTIVE=[d]                                                          ▷ Set of coordinates to update
193    3: for i ∈ [d] do
194    4:    if x0 [i] ̸= 0 then
195    5:       Initialize θ′ [i] ← θ0 [i] − α · sgn(x0 [i])              ▷ Initialization for θ′ (the worst-case model)
196    6:    else
197
       7:       if |θ0 [i]| > α then
       8:           Initialize θ′ [i] ← θ0 [i] − α · sgn(θ0 [i])
198    9:       else
199   10:           ACTIVE ← ACTIVE \{i}                               ▷ Remove the coordinate that cannot improve J
200
      11: while ACTIVE ̸= ∅ do
201   12:    i ← arg maxj∈ACTIVE |θ′ [j]|                                                 ▷ Next coordinate to update
202   13:    ∆ ← arg min∆ J(x′ + ∆ei , θ′ ) − J(x′ , θ′ ) ▷ Compute the best update for the selected coordinate
203   14:    if sgn(x′ [i] + ∆) = sgn(x′ [i]) then
204   15:        x′ [i] ← x′ [i] + ∆                                                 ▷ Apply the update and terminate
205   16:        break
      17:    else

207   19:        if sgn(θ0 [i]) = sgn(θ0 [i] + α · sgn(x0 [i])) then
208   20:            θ′ [i] ← θ0 [i] + α · sgn(x0 [i])                                        ▷ Modify θ′ accordingly
209   21:        else
210   22:            ACTIVE ← ACTIVE \{i}

213   Algorithm 1 starts by computing the worst-case model θ′ for the “default” recourse of x0 ; see
214   Lemma B.2 for why the for-loop of Algorithm 1 (lines 3-10) achieves that. Then, facing θ′ , the
215   algorithm greedily modifies x0 into xr while simultaneously updating θ′ to ensure that it remains
      the worst-case model for the current recourse x′ . In each iteration of the while loop, the algorithm

      identifies the dimension i of θ′ that has the largest absolute value (line 12) and then computes the
217   optimal change of x′ [i] if we were to keep θ′ fixed (line 13). If this change does not cause x′ [i] to
218   flip its sign, the adversary does indeed remain fixed, so x′ is the optimal recourse and the algorithm
219   terminates. On the other hand, if the recommended change would flip the sign of x′ [i], this would
220   cause the adversarial response to change as well (see Lemma B.2). The algorithm instead applies this
221   change all the way up to x′ [i] = 0, it updates θ′ accordingly, and repeats. If, during this process, for
222   some dimension i we have x′ [i] = 0 and an update of the adversarial model θ′ [i] could cause its sign
223   to flip, then no further change in this dimension is allowed (i is removed from the ACTIVE set).
224   We next describe the optimality guarantee of Algorithm 1. We defer the full proof to Appendix B.
225
      Theorem 3.3. If fθ is a generalized linear model, then Algorithm 1 returns a robust recourse
226
      x′ ∈ arg minx∈X maxθ′ ∈Θα J(x, θ′ ) in polynomial time.

228   While Algorithm 1 is designed for generalized linear models, it can be extended to non-linear models
229   by first approximating fθ locally. This idea has also been used in prior work (Upadhyay et al., 2021;
230   Ustun et al., 2019; Rawal & Lakkaraju, 2020). See Section 4 for more details. Moreover, in many
231   settings, there are constraints on the space of feasible recourses (e.g., the recourse cannot decrease
232   age if it is a feature), or the data contains categorical features. While Algorithm 1 cannot handle
233   such cases, similar to prior work (Upadhyay et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023), the
234   recourse of Algorithm 1 can be post-processed (e.g., by projection) to guarantee feasibility.

236   4    E XPERIMENTS
237
238   In this section, we provide experimental results with real and synthetic datasets. We first describe the
239   datasets and implementation details, and then present our findings in Sections 4.1 and 4.2.
240
241   Datasets We experiment on both synthetic and real-world data. For the synthetic dataset, we follow
242   a process similar to Upadhyay et al. (2021). We generate 1000 data points in two dimensions. For
243   each data point, we first sample a label y uniformly at random from Y = {0, 1}. We then sample the
244   instance corresponding to this label from a Gaussian distribution N (µy , Σy ). We set µ0 = [−2, −2],
245   µ1 = [+2, +2], and Σ0 = Σ1 = 0.5I (see Figure 1(a) in (Upadhyay et al., 2021)). We also use
246
      two real datasets. The first dataset is the German Credit dataset (Hofmann, 1994) which consists of
      1000 data points each with 7 features containing information about a loan applicant (such as age,
247
      marital status, income, and credit duration), and binary labels good (1) or bad (0) determines the
248
      creditworthiness. The second dataset is the Small Business Administration dataset (Min Li & Taylor,
249   2018) which contains the small business loans approved by the State of California from 1989 to 2004.
250   The dataset includes 1159 data points each with 28 features containing information about the business
251   (such as business category, zip code, and number of jobs created by the business) and the binary
252   labels indicate whether the small business has defaulted on the loan (0) or not (1). For real-world
253   data, we normalize the features. We use the datasets to learn the initial model θ0 . Experimental result
254   for a larger dataset is reported in Appendix C.4.
255
256   Implementation Details We use 5-fold cross-validation in our experiments. We use 4 folds from
257   the data to train the initial model θ0 and use the remaining fold to compute the recourse. The recourse
258   is only computed for instances that receive an undesirable label (0) under θ0 . We report average values
259   (over folds and test instances) in all our experiments. We used logistic regression as our linear model
260
      and trained it using Scikit-Learn. As our non-linear model, we used a 3-level neural network with 50,
      100, and 200 nodes in each successive layer. The neural network uses ReLU activation functions,
261
      binary cross-entropy loss, and Adam optimizer, and is trained for 100 epochs using PyTorch. The
262
      selected architecture is identical to prior work (Upadhyay et al., 2021). See Appendix C.1.
263
264   To generate robust or consistent recourses for linear models we implemented Algorithm 1. We used
265   the code from (Upadhyay et al., 2021) and (Nguyen et al., 2022) for ROAR and RBR’s implementation
266
      as our baselines. Similar to (Upadhyay et al., 2021), when the model is non-linear we first approximate
      it locally with LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and use the local model to generate recourse with either
267
      Algorithm 1 or ROAR, resulting in potentially different parameters θ0 for different instances.
268
269   We next describe our choices for parameters for Algorithm 1 and ROAR. We use binary cross-entropy
      as the loss function ℓ and L1 distance as the cost function c. We use L∞ norm to measure the closeness

271   2021) for selecting α and λ. We fix an α and greedily search for λ that maximizes the recourse validity
272   under the original model θ0 . We study the effect of varying α and λ in Section 4.2 and Appendix C.3.

      Robustness-Consistency Trade-off To study the trade-off between consistency and robustness,
325   we generated 5 predictions. For logistic regression models, we generated 4 perturbations of the
326   original model by adding or subtracting α in each dimension. For neural network models, we
327   added the perturbation to the LIME approximation of the initial model θ0 . Along with θ0 , these
328   form the 5 model parameters we used as predictions. We use α = 0.5 for the trade-off results (see
329   Appendix C.3 for different values of α). To compute the trade-off, for each given prediction θ̂, we

331   optimization problem, we used a variant of Algorithm 1 (see Appendix C.2). Once, we compute the
332   solution to this optimization problem, we can compute the robustness and consistency of the solution
333   using Equations (5) and (6).

      Smoothness We also study how errors in the prediction can affect the quality of the recourse
354
      solution. In particular, for each dataset and model pair, we compute a correct prediction regarding
355   the future model following the approach of (Upadhyay et al., 2021): this future model is derived
356   by either shifting the data or as the result of temporal changes in data collection (see Appendix C.1
357   for more details). We then create additional, incorrect, predictions by adding perturbations to
358   each coordinate of the correct prediction. We use four different values for the amount of added
359   perturbations: {+ϵ, −ϵ, +2ϵ, −2ϵ}. The difference in the magnitude of perturbations allows us to
360   generate predictions with different distances from the correct prediction: The ϵ values depend on both
361   the dataset and the trained model and are chosen to ensure that all the predictions are within α = 1
362   distance of the original model. See Appendix C.1 for details.
363   Given a prediction θ̂, a learner can utilize this prediction to generate a recourse. We assume the
364
      learner solves the optimization arg minx′ maxθ′ ∈Θα β · J(x′ , θ′ ) + (1 − β) · J(x′ , θ̂) to generate a
365   recourse. By varying β from 0 to 1, we can simulate a diverse set of strategies for the learner: β = 1
366   corresponds to a learner that ignores the prediction and returns the robust recourse, β = 0 corresponds
367   to a learner that fully trusts the prediction and returns the consistent solution and β ∈ (0, 1) simulate
368   learners which lie in between the two extremes.

      4.2                      F INDINGS : C OMPARISON WITH ROAR AND RBR
481
482   In Section 4.1, we showed that Algorithm 1 computes recourses with significantly smaller robustness
483   costs compared to ROAR. In this section, we perform a more detailed comparison of our algorithm
484   with ROAR and RBR (Nguyen et al., 2022) which are two main prior approaches to computing robust
485   recourse. We first compute the robust recourse with our algorithm and these baselines. To perform a
      similar comparison as in prior work, we then break down the total robustness to understand the effect


                                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of each of the terms in Equation 1. The first term, ℓ (fθ′ (x′ ), 1), is a proxy for worst-case validity
487   and the second term, c (x′ , x0 ), is the cost of modifying x0 .
488
489   More formally, focusing on instances with undesirable labels under the original model fθ0 , worst-case
490
      validity is defined as the fraction of these instances labeled with the desirable label post recourse.
      The labels of these instances are determined using the worst-case model within α distance of fθ0 .
491
      This worst-case model is the one that minimizes the fraction of instances that achieve the desirable
492
      label post-recourse. We highlight that as opposed to computing a possibly different worst-case model
493   for each instance, as is done in Sections 3 and 4.1, we compute a single worst-case model to be
494   consistent with how validity is defined in prior work (Upadhyay et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022).
495   We use projected gradient ascent to compute this worst-case model. See Appendix C.1.
496
      Figure 3 depicts the Pareto frontier of the trade-off between the worst-case validity and cost of
497
      recourse for all datasets (rows) and models (columns). The Pareto frontier for RBR is obtained by
498
      varying the parameters of RBR exactly as is done in (Nguyen et al., 2022). In particular, we set
499   the ambiguity sizes ϵ1 and ϵ0 to ϵ0 , ϵ1 ∈ [0, 1] with increments of 0.5, and the maximum recourse
500   cost δ = ∥x0 − xr ∥1 + δ+ to δ+ ∈ [0, 1] with increments of 0.2. The Pareto frontier for Algorithm
501   1 and ROAR is computed by varying α ∈ [0.02, 0.2] in increments of 0.02. For ROAR and our
502   algorithm, we used three different λs: 0.05, 0.1, and 0.2, and the trade-off for each choice is plotted
503   with a different color. To avoid overcrowding, we only included the results of λ = 0.05 for ROAR.
504   Increasing λ to 0.1 does not change the trade-off and λ = 0.2 degrades the validity even further.
505   For logistic regression models (left panel in Figure 3), where the optimal worst-case model can be
506   computed efficiently, our algorithm (regardless of choice of λ) almost always dominates RBR and
507   ROAR in terms of both cost and worst-case validity. The validity of RBR and ROAR remains low
508   over all datasets specifically in real-world datasets. On the other hand, our algorithm displays a wide
509   range for validity: low for λ = 0.2 to almost 1 for λ = 0.05. Consistent with prior work (Pawelczyk
510   et al., 2023a), the cost of the recourse increases significantly for validity values that reach 1.
511   For neural network models (right panel in Figure 3), gradient ascent is not guaranteed to find the
512   optimal worst-case model. Perhaps due to this, we observe that the worst-case validity of RBR and
513   ROAR are improved for neural network models compared to the logistic regression models. However,
514   the validity is still generally lower compared to ours (except for Figure 3b). The worst-case validity
515   of our algorithm for neural network models improves for high λs but degrades slightly for low λs,
516   compared to logistic models. However, the cost of recourse is generally lower for all algorithms
517   perhaps again due to the challenge of computing the optimal worst-case model (Guo et al., 2023).

520   5    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
521
522
523   We initiated the study of the algorithmic recourse problem through the learning-augmented framework.
524   One limitation of our work is the assumption that the cost of modifying features is the same for all
525
      inputs and measured using the L1 norm. While our framework can handle customizable weights
      for different inputs, using any norm as the cost function implies that the features can be modified
526
      independently and does not consider the causal relationship and dependencies between different
527
      features. Some prior work on algorithmic recourse aims to understand the actionability of the recourse
528   solution as well as considering these casual relationships (Karimi et al., 2020a; Joshi et al., 2019;
529   Pawelczyk et al., 2020). We leave the study of these issues as future work (see Appendix A).
530
531
      Our notion of robustness and consistency measures the performance of the algorithm against the
      optimal robust or consistent solution in an additive manner (similar to regret in online learning (Cesa-
532
      Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006)). This comparison can also be done multiplicatively, similar to the competi-
533
      tive ratio for online algorithms (Mitzenmacher & Vassilvitskii, 2020). We leave the computation of
534   robust and consistent recourses under a multiplicative comparison benchmark as well as the study
535   of their trade-off as future work. Moreover, studying a weaker notions of model change such as
536   measuring the model change by L1 or L2 norm (Upadhyay et al., 2021) or studying alternative ways
537   of formalizing model change (see e.g., (Hamman et al., 2023)) is an interesting direction for future
538   work. Finally, we assumed the prediction about the updated model is explicitly given. In practice,
539   the feedback about the updated model might be “weaker" or even noisy (Bechavod et al., 2022).
      Incorporating such feedback into our framework is an exciting future work direction.

738
739   The emerging literature on the interpretability and explainability of machine learning systems mainly
740   advocates for two main approaches. The first approach aims to build inherently simple or interpretable
741
      models such as decision lists (Lakkaraju et al., 2016) or generalized additive models (Wang et al.,
      2017; Yang et al., 2017). These approaches provide global explanations for the deployed models.
742
      The second approach attempts to explain the decisions of complex black-box models (such as deep
743
      neural networks) only on specific inputs (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Smilkov et al.,
744   2017; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Agarwal et al., 2021; Koh & Liang, 2017;
745   Barshan et al., 2020; Ehyaei et al., 2024). These approaches provide a local explanation of the model
746   and are sometimes referred to as post-hoc explanations.
747
      Recourse is a post-hoc counterfactual explanation that aims to provide the lowest cost modification
748
      that changes the prediction for a given input with an undesirable prediction under the current
749
      model (Wachter et al., 2018; Ustun et al., 2019; Looveren & Klaise, 2021; Rawal & Lakkaraju,
750   2020; Slack et al., 2021; Pawelczyk et al., 2023a). Since its introduction, different formulations
751   have been used to model the optimization problem in recourse (see (Verma et al., 2020) for an
752   overview). Wachter et al. (2018) and Pawelczyk et al. (2023a) considered score-based classifiers
753   and defined modifications to help instances achieve the desired scores. On the other hand, for binary
754   classifiers, Ustun et al. (2019) required the modification to result in the desired label. Roughly
755   speaking, the first setting can be viewed as a relaxation of the second setting and we follow the second
      formulation in our work.

      The follow-up works on the problem study several other aspects such as focusing on specific models
757   such as linear models (Ustun et al., 2019) or decision-trees (Kanamori et al., 2024; Bewley et al.,
758   2024), understanding the setting and its implicit assumptions and implications (Barocas et al., 2020;
759   Venkatasubramanian & Alfano, 2020; Fokkema et al., 2024; Gao & Lakkaraju, 2023), attainability
760   or actionability (Joshi et al., 2019; Pawelczyk et al., 2020; Karimi et al., 2020a; Ustun et al., 2019),
761   imperfect causal knowledge (Karimi et al., 2020b), fairness in terms of cost of implementation for
762   different subgroups (Guldogan et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2019; Heidari et al., 2019) and repeated
763   dynamics (Fonseca et al., 2023; Bell et al., 2024; Ehyaei et al., 2024). Extending our work to account
764   for these different aspects of recourse is left for future work.
765   In addition to the closest related work mentioned in Section 1.1, Pawelczyk et al. (2023b) studies how
766   model updates due to the “right to be forgotten" can affect recourse validity. Dutta et al. (2022) studied
767   the robustness in recourse for tree-based ensembles. Dominguez-Olmedo et al. (2022) showed that

      change and provide recourse with theoretical guarantees on the validity of the recourse.

962     achieved by moving x∗ [i] further away from x0 [i] than xr [i] did was worth the cost suffered by this
963     increase. Since the cost of x∗ is greater than the cost of xr , it must be the case that its inner product
964     is greater. Therefore, line 13 of the algorithm determined that moving xr [i] further away from x0 [i]
965     would not lead to an improvement of the objective even for a smaller inner product. Once again,
966
        the convexity of J(·) with respect to the inner product combined with the aforementioned facts
        implies that this increase must have hurt x∗ as well, leading to a contradiction.
