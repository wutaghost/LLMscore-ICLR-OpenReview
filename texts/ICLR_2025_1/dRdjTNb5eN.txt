009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Despite the strong performance of large language models (LLMs) across a wide
012                range of tasks, they still have reliability issues. Previous studies indicate that
013                strong LLMs like GPT-4-turbo excel in evaluating the reliability of responses
014                from LLMs, but face efficiency and local deployment issues. Thus, to enable
015                weak LLMs to effectively assess the reliability of LLM responses, we propose a
016                novel cross-query-comparison-based method called Meta Ranking (MR). Unlike
017
                   previous few-shot methods that solely based on in-context learning capabilities in
                   LLMs, MR assesses reliability by pairwise ranking the target query-response pair
018
                   with multiple reference query-response pairs. We found that MR is highly effec-
019
                   tive in error detection for LLM responses, that MR with weaker LLMs, which
020                have lower task performance, results in higher judgement precision against base-
021                lines with the same or even stronger models. Moreover, the method requires as
022                few as five reference samples and significantly improving efficiency. We further
023                demonstrate that MR can enhance strong LLMs’ performance in two practical
024                applications: model cascading and instruction tuning. In model cascading, we
025                combine open- and closed-source LLMs to achieve performance comparable to
026                GPT-4-turbo with lower costs. In instruction tuning, we use MR for iterative
027                training data filtering, significantly reducing data processing time and enabling
028
                   LLaMA-7B and Phi-2 to surpass 13B models with fewer training tokens. These
                   results underscore the high potential of MR in both efficiency and effectiveness.1

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance in various tasks (OpenAI,
035   2023; Touvron et al., 2023b; Du et al., 2022). However, they still face reliability challenges. For
036
      example, these models often produce responses that seem plausible but are factually incorrect, a
      phenomenon known as “hallucination” (Huang et al., 2023). Fine-tuning and alignment techniques
037
      have been extensively studied to mitigate this issue (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Rafailov
038
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024). Recent studies sadly demonstrate that halluci-
039   nation is inevitable (Xu et al., 2024). Consequently, instead of resolving it directly, we focus on
040   developing techniques to discriminate the reliability of responses from LLMs.
041
042
      Recent research has highlighted the potential of strong LLMs in evaluating response reliabil-
      ity (Zheng et al., 2023a). Highly capable models, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), have shown
043
      effective in assessing the quality of LLM responses through few-shot in-context learning (ICL) (Yin
044
      et al., 2023). However, these models are often prohibitively large, resulting in high computational
045   and monetary costs. Also, most of these models are closed-source, which limits their deployment
046   in local environments. On the other hand, weak models, are often better choices for efficiency and
047   local setup. However, their performance is usually lower, probably due to the inherent low capacity
048   in ICL (Figure 1 (a)). This raises a critical question: Is it possible to enable weak LLMs to effectively
049   judge the reliability of LLM responses despite the low task performance?
050
      To address this question, we propose a novel method named Meta Ranking (MR). Inspired from the
051   idea of pairwise ranking on responses to the same query (Wang et al., 2024b; Ke et al., 2024; Zhu
052   et al., 2023a), we raised a core hypothesis of MR, that the reliability of a response can be discerned

      the target query-response pair with multiple reference pairs (Figure 1 (b)). Specifically, MR utilizes
082

091   idation: (1) enhancing LLM inference through model cascading between open- and closed-source
092   LLMs, where queries are routed to the appropriate LLM based on reliability assessments. It de-
093   mands high efficiency of the judgement process. With MR, the model cascading achieves perfor-
094   mance comparable to GPT-4-turbo while consuming less than half API tokens. And (2) iteratively
095   filtering training datasets to improve instruction tuning, which prefers local deployment and also, the
096   efficiency. With a light-weight judge model, MR leads to advancements over existing data selection
097   methods on the Alpaca-52k dataset (Taori et al., 2023), in terms of effectiveness and efficiency.
098   In summary, our contributions are threefold:
099
100         1. We introduce Meta Ranking (MR), a novel method for assessing the reliability of LLM
101            responses through cross-query comparison with reference query-response pairs.
102
            2. We demonstrate that MR enables weak LLMs to judge the reliability of LLM responses,
103            surpassing previous uncertainty estimation and prompting methods even with some strong
104            LLMs, on effectiveness and efficiency.
105
106         3. Additionally, we explore two practical applications of MR, improving strong LLMs in both
107
               inference and training, respectively. These results underscore the considerable potential of
               our proposed method in both efficiency and effectiveness.

      3         M AIN E XPERIMENT: E RROR D ETECTION WITH M ETA R ANKING
191
192   In the following section, we empirically demonstrate that Meta Ranking can effectively judge the
193   reliability of LLM responses concerning correctness. We leverage error detection on responses
194   from LLMs in reasoning tasks for validation. Our findings indicate that the MR approach achieves
195   high judgement precision despite the low task performance, response accuracies, and languages.
196
197   3.1       S ETTINGS
198
199   The error detection task requires identifying whether a response from an LLM is incorrect given the
200
      query. The settings are as follows, and implementation details are in Appendix C.1:
201   Datasets: To validate the effectiveness of methods on diverse tasks, we conduct experiments on 14
202   reasoning datasets, including subjects of “STEM”, “Arithmetics”, “Humanities”, “Social Science”,
203   “Chinese Culture”, etc. Specifically, we selected 8 test sets in MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b;a),
204   5 test sets in CMMLU (Li et al., 2024a), and the test set from GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). The
205   MMLU and CMMLU datasets are multi-choice and the GSM8K dataset contains open-ended math
206
      queries. We select 5 few-shot samples for each dataset from its development or training set.
207   Response Generation: For different model accuracies, we chose LLaMA-2-chat-7B (Touvron
208   et al., 2023b) and OpenChat-3.5 (Wang et al., 2024a) to zero-shot generate responses for English
209   queries (MMLU and GSM8K), and ChatGLM-2-6B (Zeng et al., 2023) and Yi-6B-Chat (01.AI,
210   2023) for Chinese ones (CMMLU). For each multi-choice query, we first generate the reasoning path
211   for judgement, and then extract the choice by calling the generator model again.
212   MR Settings: We prompted LLaMA-2, ChatGLM-2, OpenChat-3.5, GPT-3.5-turbo, and Phi-2 to
213   judge on different query-response pairs with Meta Ranking. We also tested an LLM-as-a-Judge-
214   tuned model JudgeLM-7B-v1 (Zhu et al., 2023a) in MR to see if the fine-tuning for the evaluation
215   of responses to the same query helps. By setting each label Si with the value ±1, where +1 and −1
      denote True and False, we apply MR on this task to identify incorrect responses.

247   3.2   D ISCUSSION
248   Meta Ranking is Effective across Different LLM Backbones The effectiveness of MR might at-
249   tribute to the position agnosticism to reference and judgement without over-confidence. In Figure 3,
250   we report results of all baselines and MR in error detection on LLaMA-2-generated responses, and
251   the actual performance of LLMs on MMLU. Impressively, we found that MR with Phi-2 notably
252   exceeds all baseline methods, except for P(T) with GPT-4-turbo, reaching a precision score of 0.77,
253   more than double the performance of P(T) with Phi-2 and reaching 88% GPT-4-turbo performance.
254   With LLaMA-2 and ChatGLM-2, MR exceeds P(T) significantly. However, JudgeLM performs not
255
      as well as other pretrained or general aligned LLMs in the MR results and fails to generalize for
      the P(T) method. It might be the fine-tuning process limits its generalization. Overall, MR consis-
256
      tently outperforms the random baseline to a greater margin than P(T), and has a greater gap between
257
      judgement precision and task performance. However, GPT-4-turbo detect the error most accurately
258   even with P(T), probably relying on its strong reasoning capabilities and generalizability (OpenAI,
259   2023). We also depict F1 scores in Appendix C.1 to indicate MR is not biased to identify most
260   responses as incorrect ones, which could result in 1.00 judgement precision but extremely low F1.

      4     A PPLICATIONS OF M ETA R ANKING
301   In this section, we present two practical applications to further validate the effectiveness of Meta
302   Ranking beyond mere correctness, as shown in Figure 5. Each application is implemented by
303   collecting reference query-response pairs and setting reference reliability scores. (a) With the
304   assessment of the reliability of responses from open-source LLMs to the given queries, we identify
305   and route unsolved queries to stronger closed-source LLMs. It could achieve better efficiency and
306   remaining performance of closed-source models. However, it demands the model used for judge-
307
      ment is weaker than the open-source LLM in deployment, otherwise it is better in place to respond
      to queries. (b) By evaluating the quality of instruction data, we can refine the supervised fine-
308
      tuning (SFT) for LLM instruction tuning, whose key factor is the quality of training data (Liu et al.,
309
      2024). By filtering low-quality data after each epoch and further introducing post-SFT training with
310   mere instruction data, we achieve significant improvement against state-of-the-art SFT data selec-
311   tion methods. For this data-related application, the method is better to be locally deployed and with
312   high efficiency. Thus, we decide MR with weak LLMs is suitable for judgement for these.
313
314   4.1     M ODEL C ASCADING
315
      Since LLMs exhibit varying performance across various tasks, we propose using MR within a model
316
      cascading system. As depicted in Figure 5 (a), this system employs MR to assess the reliability of
317
      generated responses from open-source LLMs. Queries with responses deemed unreliable by MR are
318   routed to more powerful, but also more costly, closed-source LLMs for better answers. This system
319   aims to achieve performance similar to closed-source models with improved efficiency.

458
459   4.2.1   I MPLEMENTATION
460
461   The application contains two stages (Figure 5 (b)): (1) SFT with MR guided data selection and (2)
462   post-SFT training with both estimated low- and high-quality data from the last epoch at stage 1.
463   For the first stage, besides regular SFT, we extract a small set of queries from the training set and,
464   after each epoch, ask the tuned LLM to respond to those queries. With the generated responses
465   and the queries as reference, the MR method could judge whether each sample in the original
466   training dataset matches the quality of the reference. For simplicity in MR, we set the reliability
467   score of all reference pairs to 1. Thus, we could filter training data samples that fail the judgment,
468   i.e. unreliable, improving training efficiency and, potentially, LLM performance.
469   For the second stage, we want to utilize both the filtered low-quality and the high-quality data
470   to further train the LLM. Since post-SFT training methods (e.g., PPO (Ouyang et al., 2022),
471   DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023)) require multiple responses of diverse human preferences or quality
472   to the same query, they are not compatible. Recently, Ethayarajh et al. (2024) proposed Kahneman-
473   Tversky Optimization (KTO) to align LLMs towards desired and away from undesired query-
474   response pairs contrastively. However, we are aware that their objective is misaligned with our
475   requirement because the low-quality data is derived from the SFT dataset, which is not completely
476
      negative. Therefore, in order to incorporate both high- and low-quality data as positive and partially
      positive samples, we propose positive-KTO (pKTO). Intuitively, pKTO differs from KTO only in
477
      dealing with low-quality data, where pKTO regulates the reward of these data with MSE loss instead
478
      of decreasing it unlimitedly. Specifically, we use equal amount of low-quality and high-quality data
479   samples from the last epoach in the first stage to fine-tune the LLM with the pKTO objective. Please
480   refer to Appendix D.2 for the detailed implementation and comparisons with DPO and KTO.

508   5   R ELATED W ORK
509
510   Evaluation of LLM Responses Extensive research has been conducted to evaluate responses from
511   LLMs. Studies have focused on estimating uncertainty to gauge the potential reliability of LLM
512   responses (Kuhn et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2023). Furthermore, LLMs are capable of providing
513   uncertainty scores from itself by fine-tuning (Chen et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024), which usu-
514   ally requires an amount of training data, or black-box measurements (Lin et al., 2024). However,
515   these methods often require an amount of labeled data for calibration to determine a threshold (Han
516   et al., 2024). Additionally, the LLM-as-a-judge approach effectively assesses the accuracy of LLM
517   responses from strong LLMs (Zheng et al., 2023a) with manual prompting rules. Contrarily, our
518
      Meta Ranking method leverages weak LLMs and a training-free judgment based on cross-query
      comparisons with much fewer examples.
519
520   Model Cascading with LLMs Recent studies on model cascading focus on how LLMs can selec-
521   tively call tools or stronger models only in difficult situations for better efficiency. Tool calls or
522   another trial happens on external feedback from environment (Lin et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023).
523   For tasks with explicit criteria, e.g., coding, LLMs can call stronger models after their failure (Zhang
524
      et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024). Selection can also be achieved through fine-tuning (Erbacher et al.,
      2024) or uncertainty estimation (Han et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024). In our approach, MR route
525
      queries on complicated open-ended tasks, and empirical results are demonstrated in Section 4.1.2.
526
527   Data-Efficient Training for LLMs Coresets (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) are used in machine learn-
528   ing for a long period. For LLMs, several data selection methods are developed for SFT (Liu et al.,
529   2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024) and post-SFT stages (Gulcehre et al.,
530
      2023; Aksitov et al., 2024). Inspired by the latter, we introduce iteratively filtering SFT data based
      on MR results after each epoch, and used in post-SFT training.

533   6   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We present Meta Ranking (MR), a novel method effectively enabling weak LLMs to judge the re-
536   liability of LLM responses. By comparing a target query-response pair with a small number of
537   reference pairs, MR outperforms strong baselines in error detection without fine-tuning. Further-
538   more, the method significantly enhances strong LLMs’ performance and efficiency in two practical
539   application scenarios, model cascading and instruction tuning. These findings highlight the potential
      of MR for broader inference- and training-time applications with LLMs.

899   A    L IMITATIONS
900
901   There are several limitations to our work that we would like to acknowledge:
902
      First, we have not explored deep in the compatibility between the model training process and the
903
      Meta Ranking (MR) method. Different model training strategies may affect the effectiveness of MR.
904
      It is an interesting direction for future work to study how to better integrate MR with the alignment
905   process (e.g., SFT and post-SFT training) on LLMs.
906
907   Also, we have not focused on finding potential applications of the Meta Ranking method for strong
908
      models. Our current experiments focus on enabling weak LLMs to judge the reliability of LLM
      responses due to its superior efficiency and effectiveness. It remains an open question of what
909
      practical usage could incorporate MR with strong models like GPT-4-turbo. Exploring the potential
910
      applications of MR for strong models is also a direction for future work.
911
912   Last but not least, inaccurate judgments in sensitive domains like healthcare could lead to erroneous
913   advice or misdiagnosis, endangering patient’s health. In finance, inaccurate judgments could result
914
      in flawed financial advice or risk assessments, leading to substantial economic losses.
915   In conclusion, while our proposed Meta Ranking method has shown promising results in enabling
916   weak LLMs to judge the reliability of LLM responses and enhancing LLM performance in practical
917   applications, there are still spaces to be explored. We hope that future research can address these
      limitations and further improve the method.

      The Meta Ranking (MR) method presented in this paper has the potential to significantly influence
921   the field of LLMs and their applications. Here, we discuss the broader impact of our work in several
922   key areas:
923
924
      Data and LLM Response Curation: Meta Ranking enables the use of smaller, less resource-
      intensive models for response judgement, which previously required large, expensive models. Thus,
925
      MR is more cost-efficient and practical for landing in real-world scenarios. Also, MR does not
926
      inherently incorporate risks in the methodology, while the language model MR utilizes could con-
927   tain potential risks from its pre-train data. The application of MR, including model cascading and
928   instruction tuning, may result in risky results due to the nature of the application. However, MR
929   could actually be used in risk mitigation for LLMs by identifying and filtering LLM responses with
930   potential risks.
931
      LLM Inference and Training: The MR method can improve the efficiency and effectiveness of
932
      LLM inference and training. By routing queries to the most appropriate LLMs based on reliabil-
933   ity assessments, MR can save computational resources and improve response times, making LLMs
934   more practical for real-world applications. Additionally, the iterative training data refinement en-
935   abled by MR can lead to more accurate and reliable LLMs, which is crucial for maintaining public
936   trust in AI systems.
937
      In conclusion, the Meta Ranking method not only enhances the capabilities of weak LLMs but also
938
      has the potential to transform how we develop, deploy, and interact with AI systems, leading to a
939   more reliable, efficient, and equitable integration of AI in various aspects of our lives.

1340   matching, which serves as an explicit criterion for correctness judgement. In MR (Algorithm 1), the
1341   label of a query-response pair is defined as the match rate of generated and source functions across
