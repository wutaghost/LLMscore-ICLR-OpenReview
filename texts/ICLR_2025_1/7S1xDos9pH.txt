011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Conventional uncertainty-aware temporal difference (TD) learning methods often
014            rely on simplistic assumptions, typically including a zero-mean Gaussian distribu-
015            tion for TD errors. Such oversimplification can lead to inaccurate error representa-
016            tions and compromised uncertainty estimation. In this paper, we introduce a novel
017            framework for generalized Gaussian error modeling in deep reinforcement learn-
018            ing, applicable to both discrete and continuous control settings. Our framework
019
               enhances the flexibility of error distribution modeling by incorporating additional
               higher-order moment, particularly kurtosis, thereby improving the estimation and
020
               mitigation of data-dependent noise, i.e., aleatoric uncertainty. We examine the
021
               influence of the shape parameter of the generalized Gaussian distribution (GGD)
022            on aleatoric uncertainty and provide a closed-form expression that demonstrates
023            an inverse relationship between uncertainty and the shape parameter. Addition-
024            ally, we propose a theoretically grounded weighting scheme to fully leverage the
025            GGD. To address epistemic uncertainty, we enhance the batch inverse variance
026            weighting by incorporating bias reduction and kurtosis considerations, resulting
027            in improved robustness. Extensive experimental evaluations using policy gradient
028            algorithms demonstrate the consistent efficacy of our method, showcasing signif-
029            icant performance improvements.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Deep reinforcement learning (RL) has demonstrated promising potential across various real-world
035
      applications, e.g., finance (Moody & Saffell, 1998; Byun et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023), and au-
036   tonomous driving (Kahn et al., 2017; Emamifar & Ghoreishi, 2023; Hoel et al., 2023). One crit-
037   ical avenue for improving the performance and robustness of RL agents in these complex, high-
038   dimensional environments is the quantification and integration of uncertainty associated with the
039   decisions made by agents or the environment (Lockwood & Si, 2022). Effective management of
040   uncertainty promotes the agents to make more informed decisions leading to enhanced sample effi-
041   ciency in RL context, which is particularly beneficial in unseen or ambiguous situations.
042   Temporal difference (TD) learning is a fundamental component of many RL algorithms, facilitating
043   value function estimation and policy derivation through iterative updates (Sutton, 1988). Tradi-
044   tionally, these TD updates are typically grounded in L2 loss, corresponding to maximum likelihood
045   estimation (MLE) under the assumption of Gaussian error. Such simplification may be overly restric-
046   tive, especially considering the noisy nature of TD errors, which are based on constantly changing
047   estimates of value functions and policies. This assumption compromises sample efficiency, neces-
048   sitating the incorporation of additional distributional parameters for flexible but computationally
049   efficient TD error modeling.
050   In statistics and probability theory, distributions are typically characterized by their central tendency,
051   variability, and shape (DeCarlo, 1997; Milton et al., 2017). Traditional deep RL methods, however,
052   effectively exploit on the variance of the error distribution through the scale parameter, yet they often
053   disregard its shape. This oversight hinders these methods from fully capturing the true underlying
      uncertainty. The kurtosis, the scale-independent moment, does significantly influence both infer-

055   estimation.
056
057   Therefore, it is essential to incorporate the shape of the error distribution into TD learning to better

      reflect uncertainties present in RL environments, enabling more robust and reliable decision-making
      processes in dynamic and complex scenarios.

126   2.1   U NCERTAINTY
127
128   Uncertainty in neural networks is commonly decomposed into two sources: aleatoric and epistemic
129   (Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009; Kendall & Gal, 2017; Depeweg et al., 2018; Valdenegro-Toro &
130
      Mori, 2022). Epistemic uncertainty arises from limitations within the neural network and can poten-
      tially be reduced through further learning or model improvements. In contrast, aleatoric uncertainty
131
      stems from the inherent stochasticity of the environment or the dynamics of the agent-environment
132
      interactions and is fundamentally irreducible.
133
134   This distinction is crucial in the context of RL, where areas with high epistemic uncertainty need to
135   be explored, whereas exploring areas with high aleatoric uncertainty may lead to ineffective training,
136
      since the agent might have adequate knowledge but insufficient information for decisive actions.
      Quantifying aleatoric uncertainty is known to facilitate learning dynamics of stochastic processes
137
      and enables risk-sensitive decision making (Dabney et al., 2018; Vlastelica et al., 2021; Seitzer
138
      et al., 2022).
139
140   To address aleatoric uncertainty, variance networks are frequently employed. These networks, de-
141   noted as Qσ , are used in conjunction with value approximation networks Qµ (Bishop, 1994; Nix
142
      & Weigend, 1994; Kendall & Gal, 2017; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Mai et al., 2022; Mavor-
      Parker et al., 2022). The estimated variance from Qσ is utilized for heteroscedastic Gaussian error
143
      modeling, which captures aleatoric uncertainty (Seitzer et al., 2022). Specifically, the TD errors
144
      are modeled under a Gaussian distribution, i.e., δt ∼ N (Qµt , Qσt 2 ), and the network minimizes the
145
      Gaussian negative log-likelihood (NLL). This formulation penalizes errors proportionally to their
146   predicted variance. Larger Qσt values, indicating greater aleatoric uncertainty, reduce the penalty for
147   large errors, effectively addressing their impact.
148
149

      posed in Mai et al. (2022), is applied. This approach scales error contributions inversely to their
150

151

152   bled value heads Qµt , and ξ is either a hyperparameter or numerically computed to ensure a sufficient
153   effective batch size. Integrating this into the overall loss results in the BIV loss:

170

172

      understanding of learning dynamics cannot be overlooked. This is especially relevant for MLE base
173
      on the normality assumption, which is commonly applied in variance network frameworks. Focusing
174
      solely on averages or even deviations is proven to be inadequate in the presence of outlier samples
175   (David, 1979; Gather & Kale, 1988).
176
177   For instance, consider the impact of non-normal samples on the estimate of the variance, as described
178
      in Proposition 1 with proof presented in Appendix B.1.

201   that while Gumbel modeling initially aligns with the error distribution propagated through the chain
202
      of max operations, its Gumbel-like attribute may diminish over the course of training (Garg et al.,

203
      the tail behavior of diverse distributions. This method is adaptable to wider range of MDPs, even
204
      those without max operators.

      3.1.1    E MPIRICAL EVIDENCE
252   Figure 2 presents empirical findings that reveal a significant deviation from Gaussian distribution in
253   TD errors, evidenced by well-fitted GGDs and pronounced differences in the shape of distributions.
254   This non-normality becomes particularly apparent when contrasting initial and final evaluations,
255   suggesting an increasing prominence of tailedness throughout the training process.
256
      We hypothesize that such departure from normality stems from the exploratory nature of agent be-
257
      havior. During exploration, agents frequently encounter unexpected states and rewards, i.e., regions
258   of the state-action space or reward function that are rarely visited. This leads to a higher frequency of
259   outlier errors, leading to a broader spectrum of TD errors than those seen in purely exploitative sce-
260   narios. This increased variability likely contributes to the emergence of non-normal distributions,
261   characterized by heavier tails.
262
      Furthermore, the observed evolution in the tails of TD errors underscores the shifting interplay be-
263
      tween aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties. As training advances, epistemic uncertainty typically
264   diminishes, which inherently has Gaussian-like characteristics as it is measured by the variance of
265   the ensembled estimates (Kendall & Gal, 2017). Aleatoric uncertainty, on the other hand, arises
266   from irreducible noise inherent in the environment, e.g., stochasticity in rewards or transitions, and
267   becomes more pronounced as the agent explores new states and actions. Such dynamics potentially
268   result in non-normally distributed errors with heavier tails. Notably, the diminishing property of
269   Gumbel-like attribute, discussed in Section 2.2, is also reflected in the evolving TD error distribu-
      tions.

      3.1.2   T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS
299
300   Given the empirical suitability of GGD for modeling TD errors, we conduct an in-depth theoretical
301   examination of GGD. We begin by demonstrating the well-definedness of GGD regression, with a
302   specific emphasis on the positive-definiteness of its PDF under certain conditions.
303   Theorem 1 (Positive-definiteness (Bochner, 1937; Ushakov, 2011; Dytso et al., 2018)). The NLL of
304   GGD is well-defined for β ∈ (0, 2].
305
306   This theorem guarantees the well-definedness of the NLL of the GGD. It ensures that the PDF is
307
      guaranteed to be positive everywhere under highly probable conditions of β, thus affirming the suit-
      ability of the NLL as a loss function for a valid probability distribution. In fact, it is known that
308
      parameter estimation for GGD can also be numerically accomplished by minimizing the NLL func-
309
      tion, with asymptotic normality, consistency, and efficiency of the estimates ensured under suitable
310   regularity conditions (Agro, 1995). This positive-definiteness not only implies a theoretical property
311   but also has practical implications in deep RL, where ensuring stability and convergence is crucial.
312   The positive-definiteness of the PDF of GGD shown in the proof, elaborated in Appendix B.2, also
313   assures that the function integrates to one.
314
      The shape parameter β also introduces a desirable property of risk-aversion to GGD, which can be
315
      mathematically formulated by stochastic dominance (Levy, 1992; Martin et al., 2020). Stochastic
316   dominance, a concept assessing random variables via a stochastic order, reflects the shared prefer-
317   ences of rational decision-makers.

319   ables X1 ∼ GGD(0, α, β1 ) and X2 ∼ GGD(0, α, β2 ), where α > 0 and β1 , β2 > 0. If β1 ≤ β2 ,
320   then X2 exhibits second-order stochastic dominance over X1 . This dominance implies, for all x,

      The above theorem, with its proof detailed in Appendix B.3, suggests second-order stochastic dom-

      Remark 3. The training of the critic is more directly influenced by aleatoric uncertainty, since only
335   the critic loss is a direct function of the state, action, and reward, with the actor being downstream
336   of the critic in uncertainty propagation. GGD error modeling enables us to quantify aleatoric un-
337   certainty as a closed form, i.e., σ 2 = α2 Γ(3/β)/Γ(1/β) (Upadhyay et al., 2021). Remarkably,
338   aleatoric uncertainty exhibits a negative proportionality to the shape parameter β on an exponential
339   scale, with a constant scale parameter α = 1 adopted in our implementation employing a beta head
340   exclusively. Building on this, risk-averse weighting mitigates the negative impacts of noisy super-
341   vision by assigning higher weights to errors with lower aleatoric uncertainty for the loss attenuation
342   term.

417   To underscore the versatility and robustness of our approach, we deliberately choose baseline algo-
418   rithms that cover a wide spectrum of RL paradigms. Specifically, we employ soft actor-critic (SAC)
419   (Haarnoja et al., 2018), an off-policy Q-based policy gradient algorithm, and proximal policy op-

      Gumbel error modeling methods. This constraint is intended to focus on computationally efficient
      algorithms that only incorporate an additional layer, referred to as a head.

427   variants, we employ five ensembled critics. The parameter ξ from Equation (4) is computed with a

      minimum batch size of 16, and the regularizing temperature λ is set to 0.1. Additional experimental
      details are provided in Appendix C.

474   5   D ISCUSSION
475
476
      In this paper, we advocate for and substantiate the integration of GGD modeling for TD error anal-
477   ysis. Our main contribution is the introduction of a novel framework that enables robust train-
478   ing methodologies by leveraging the distribution’s shape. This approach accounts for both data-
479   dependent noise, i.e., aleatoric uncertainty, and the uncertainty of value estimation, i.e., epistemic
480   uncertainty, ultimately enhancing the model’s stability and accuracy.
481
482   Further investigation An imperative avenue for further investigation is the application of GGD
483   within the context of maximum entropy RL. Similar to how the Gaussian distribution maximizes en-
484   tropy with constraints up to the second moment, the GGD maximizes entropy subject to a constraint
485   on the p-th absolute moment (MacKay, 2003). Exploring higher moments of the distribution could
      provide new insights into maximum entropy RL frameworks.

510   The absence of a comprehensive regret analysis in our current study also presents an opportunity for
511   future work. Considering that aleatoric uncertainty in TD learning predominantly arises from reward
512   dynamics, conducting a regret analysis on heavy-tailed TD error is warranted. This is particularly
513   relevant as previous research has extensively studied regret in the context of heavy-tailed rewards.
514   While our experiments focus on policy gradient methods, the implications of TD error tailedness
515   extend to the Q-learning family of algorithms. We provide empirical findings on this extension in
516   Appendix E, which demonstrate the broader applicability of our approach. Additionally, explor-
517   ing the generalized extreme value distribution could further enrich our understanding of tailedness
518   phenomena due to its close association with extreme value theory.
519
520   Relevant applications The implications of tailedness in TD error distributions extend into various
521   domains, notably robust RL and risk-sensitive RL. The focus in robust RL lies on developing algo-
522   rithms that are less sensitive to noise and outliers within the reward signal. Recognizing potential
523
      deviations from normality in TD error distributions is critical for designing such algorithms. Our
      work emphasizes the importance of considering non-normal error distributions, especially the tail
524
      behaviors, to enhance the robustness of RL algorithms.
525
526   Another significant direction is risk-sensitive RL, which seeks to assess and mitigate the risks as-
527   sociated with different action choices. In noisy and outlier-prone environments, capturing the risk
528   profile using a Gaussian assumption for TD errors might be inadequate. By considering the GGD,
529
      which better models the tail behaviors of error distributions, we can develop more accurate and
      reliable risk-sensitive RL algorithms.
530
531   In summary, our exploration into GGD modeling of TD errors opens several promising research
532   directions and applications, emphasizing the need to consider non-normal error distributions for
533   enhancing the robustness and risk-sensitivity of RL algorithms.

1162   As discussed in Section 5, we expand our empirical analysis to include Q-learning, with particular
1163   attention to DQN (Mnih et al., 2015). Figure 16 demonstrates that TD errors generated by DQN
1164   exhibit deviations from a Gaussian distribution and closely follow the GGD.
1165   The performance of DQN in noisy discrete control environments, as depicted in Figure 17, high-

       Since our implementation of DQN utilizes a greedy action policy, the approximated value func-
1170
       tion inherently represents the policy. Consequently, employing risk-averse weighting might impede
1171   exploration. Conversely, in policy gradient algorithms featuring separate policy networks, such risk-
1172   aversion serves to mitigate noisy supervision, aligning with its intended purpose. The adverse effects
1173   of risk-aversion are evident in Figure 18, where the model trained with the original NLL exhibits
1174   slightly superior sample efficiency compared to the proposed method.
