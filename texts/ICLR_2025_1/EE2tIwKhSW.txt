009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Membership inference attacks (MIAs) on diffusion models have emerged as poten-
012            tial evidence of unauthorized data usage in training pre-trained diffusion models.
013            These attacks aim to detect the presence of specific images in training datasets of
014            diffusion models. Our study delves into the evaluation of state-of-the-art MIAs on
015            diffusion models and reveals critical flaws and overly optimistic performance esti-
016
               mates in existing MIA evaluation. We introduce CopyMark, a more realistic MIA
               benchmark that distinguishes itself through the support for pre-trained diffusion
017
               models, unbiased datasets, and fair evaluation pipelines. Through extensive experi-
018
               ments, we demonstrate that the effectiveness of current MIA methods significantly
019            degrades under these more practical conditions. Based on our results, we alert that
020            MIA, in its current state, is not a reliable approach for identifying unauthorized
021            data usage in pre-trained diffusion models. To the best of our knowledge, we are
022            the first to discover the performance overestimation of MIAs on diffusion models
023            and present a unified benchmark for more realistic evaluation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION AND R ELATED W ORKS
027
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al.,
028
      2020b) have revolutionized the field of image synthesis. A notable advantage of these models is
029
      their ability to train stably on vast web-sourced datasets containing billions of images (Schuhmann
030   et al., 2021; 2022). This capability has paved the way for large-scale pre-trained models in image
031   synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024). However,
032   these pre-trained models have raised concerns regarding unauthorized data usage (Samuelson, 2023;
033   Sag, 2023), as their training datasets often include numerous copyrighted images without proper
034   authorization. In response, copyright owners have initiated a series of lawsuits against producers
035   of pre-trained diffusion models (Andersen, 2023; Zhang, 2024). Within this context, membership
036   inference attacks (MIAs) on diffusion models (Duan et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023;
037   2024; Tang et al., 2023) have emerged. MIAs aim to separate members (data used for training) and
038
      non-members (data not used for training). Their results help determine whether specific images were
      included in the training dataset of diffusion models, thus being considered as potential evidence of
039
      unauthorized data usage in AI copyright lawsuits related to diffusion models.
040
041   However, recent research suggests that MIAs on Large Language Models (LLMs) may perform
042   successfully because they are evaluated under defective setups with distribution shift (Das et al., 2024;
043   Maini et al., 2024). Their performance is confounded by evaluating on non-members belonging to a
044
      different distribution from the members (Maini et al., 2024). This finding raises concern on the true
      effectiveness of MIAs, including those on diffusion models.
045
046   Inspired by the above idea, we investigate the current evaluation of MIAs on diffusion models.
047   Unfortunately, we find that there are similar defects in the evaluation of MIAs on diffusion models.
048   Specifically, the evaluation is based on 1) over-trained models (Duan et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023;
049   Fu et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2023) and 2) member datasets and non-member datasets with distribution
050
      shifts (Duan et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023). Both setups make the task of MIA easier than the
      real-world scenario, with pre-trained diffusion models and unshifted members and non-members.
051
      This defective evaluation leaves unknown the true performance of MIAs on diffusion models.
052
053   To fill the blank of real-world evaluation, we build CopyMark, the first unified benchmark for
      membership inference attacks on diffusion models. CopyMark gathers 1) all pre-trained diffusion

      2.2   M EMBERSHIP INFERENCE ATTACKS ON DIFFUSION MODELS
109
110   Membership Inferences Attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017; Hayes et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020;
111   Carlini et al., 2023) determine whether a datapoint is part of the training dataset of certain diffusion
112   models. We give the formal problem statement of MIAs as follows:
113
      Membership Inference Attacks Given the training dataset Dmember (members) of a model θ and
114   a hold-out dataset Dnon (non-member), membership inference attacks aim at designing a function
115   f (x, θ), that f (x, θ) = 1 for x ∈ Dmember and f (x, θ) = 0 for x ∈ Dnon . Here, x is an image.
116
117
      Currently, they are two types of MIA methods on diffusion models:
118
             • Loss-based MIAs (Duan et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023): Loss-based MIAs
119            are built on the general hypothesis that the training loss of members is smaller than that of
120            non-members. They therefore calculate a function R(x, θ) ∈ R for data point x based on
121            the training loss of diffusion models and find an optimal threshold τ to discriminate R(x, θ)
122            of members and non-members.
                                                 f (x, θ) := 1[R(x, θ) < τ ]
123
                                                                                                            (3)
124
125          • Classifier-based MIAs (Pang et al., 2023): Classifier-based MIAs believe that members
126            and non-members have different features gθ (x) in diffusion models. The features could be
127            gradients and neural representations. These features can be used to train a neural network F
128            to classify members and non-members.
129                                                 f (x, θ) := F (gθ (x))                                  (4)
130
131   Membership inference attacks of diffusion models are evaluated by two metrics: true positive rate
132   at a low false positive rate (Carlini et al., 2023) and AUC. Here, true positive rate (TPR) means the
133   percentage of predicting members as members correctly, while false positive rate (FPR) means the
134   percentage of predicting non-members as members falsely. We are interested in this TPR at X% FPR
135
      because we only care about whether some members could be identified without errors in practice.
      Take detecting unauthorized data usage as an example. With only one copyright image is determined
136
      as the member, we can then prove the existence of unauthorized data usage. We also include AUC as
137
      our metric, which is a classical measurement on the discrimination of MIA methods. In practice, we
138   calculate TPRs and FPRs on the dataset. Then, we search for the optimal threshold and calculate
139   AUC on the same dataset.
140
141
      Notably, the evaluation of MIAs need to follow the MI security game protocol (Carlini et al., 2023;
      Hu & Pang, 2023), that the member Dmember and the non-member Dnon should come from the same
142
      data distribution. This is because we do not know the distribution of members and non-members in
143
      real-world scenarios of MIAs, for example, detecting unauthorized data usage in pre-trained diffusion
144   models. We then need to assume the worst case when the member and the non-member Dnon come
145   from the same data distribution so that we cannot simply distinguish them without the help of model
146   θ. However, we will show in the rest of this paper that this protocol is not well followed in existing
147   MIAs on diffusion models.

151
      In this section, we reveal the fundamental defect within current evaluation of MIAs on diffusion
152
      models. We start from explaining two choices in the evaluation setup of MIAs:
153
154          • over-training v.s. pre-training: Over-training refers to overly training a model for large
155            epochs on a small training dataset, e.g. 2048 epochs on CIFAR10. What distinguishes
156            over-training from traditional over-fitting is that over-training improves the generation perfor-
157            mance of small diffusion models and is considered as a default setup for this training (Song
158            & Ermon, 2020). That explains why most existing diffusion MIA benchmarks have the
159            problem of over-training. Pre-training, in contrast, only trains diffusion models on a very
160
               large dataset, for example, for 1 or 2 epochs.
161          • shifted datasets v.s. unshifted datasets: Dataset shift means that the member dataset and the
               non-member dataset do no come from the same data distribution. On the contrary, unshifted

185   Over-training and shifted datasets are the unrealistic choice. Specifically,
186
             • over-training easily gives rise to over-fitting, since the model is trained for hundreds of
187
               steps on each data point from a limited dataset. This over-fitting markably lowers the
188
               training loss of members and even causes memorization (Gu et al., 2023), making it easier to
189            distinguish members from non-members based on training losses. However, recent progress
190            in pre-training diffusion models shows the potential to train photorealistic diffusion models
191            for only 1 epoch on large-scale text-image datasets (Rombach et al., 2022; Gokaslan et al.,
192            2023), which does not make the training loss of members much lower than that of non-
193            members (Wen et al., 2024). Since these pre-trained models are the real-world interests of
194            MIAs on diffusion models, evaluting MIAs on over-trained diffusion models are unrealistic.
195          • dataset shift makes it possible to distinguish members from non-members without accessing
196            the model. Hence, MIA methods succeeding on shifted datasets are probably dataset
197            classifiers (Liu & He, 2024) that only captures the difference in image semantics, rather
198            than real membership inference attacks. Such dataset classifiers will fail on correctly
199            discriminating members and non-members that come from the same data distribution.

203

205   Kong et al., 2023; Pang et al., 2023), and LDM + CelebA (member) & FFHQ (non-member) (Fu et al.,
206   2023) over-train diffusion models for at least 300 iterations on each of the data point. On the other
207   hand, although SD1.5 + LAION (member) & MS-COCO (non-member) (Duan et al., 2023; Kong
208   et al., 2023) exploits a pre-trained diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022), it picks non-members
209   from MSCOCO, whose distribution is markably different from that of LAION. With over-training
210   and dataset shift, it is unknown whether the success of MIA methods depends on the over-fitting or a
211   non-member dataset whose distribution differs from that of the member dataset. This is a fatal defect
212
      of current evaluation of MIAs on diffusion models.
213   While avoiding over-training is simple by using pre-trained diffusion models for benchmarks, quan-
214   tifying and preventing dataset shifts are not trivial. We will then conduct experiments to measure
215   dataset shifts and show whether our benchmark eliminates dataset shifts (Section 5.1). We investigate
      the impact of different levels of dataset shifts on MIA performance (Section 5.3).

      To overcome the defect in previous evaluation and investigate the real-world performance of MIAs
229   on diffusion models, we design and implement CopyMark, the first unified benchmark for MIAs on
230   diffusion models. CopyMark distinguishes itself from previous evaluation from the following three
231   aspects: 1) CopyMark is built on pre-trained diffusion models with no over-training and member and
232   non-member datasets without dataset shift, which overcome these two defects of previous evaluation

234   dataset used to find the threshold or train the classifier, which examines how MIAs perform on a blind
235   test (Section 4.2); 3) CopyMark is implemented on diffusers, the state-of-the-art inference framework
236   for diffusion models, making it flexible to generalize to new diffusion models (Section 4.3)

279
280   MIA methods on diffusion models use one dataset to find the optimal threshold or train the classifier
281   (see Section 2.2 for explanation). However, previous evaluation of MIA methods also uses the same
282   dataset for evaluation. This raises doubts on the generalizability of these MIA methods. We propose
283   to complement this drawback by introducing an extra dataset called test dataset.
284

285   non-members from the source. We denote one by the validation dataset and the other by the test
286   dataset. Our evaluation pipeline has two stages. The first stage is the same as previous evaluation,
287   that we use the validation dataset to search for the optimal threshold to calculate TPR at X% FPR
288   and AUC or train the classifier. The second stage is different, that we test the optimal threshold or the
289   trained classifier on the test dataset. Since the test dataset does not involve in searching the optimal
290   threshold or training the classifier, the second stage can be viewed as a blind test to the threshold or

315   4.3   I MPLEMENTATION
316
317   Previously, there is no unified benchmark for MIAs on diffusion models. To fill this blank, we
318   implement all state-of-the-art baseline methods of MIAs on diffusion models in CopyMark. We base
319   our implementation on diffusers (von Platen et al., 2022), the state-of-the-art inference framework for
320
      diffusion models. We discuss the details of our implemention by points:
321   diffusers diffusers provides a unified API for running different diffusion models. We implement
322   MIA methods as pipeline objects in diffusers. This enables MIA methods to generalize swiftly to
323   different diffusion models. While diffusers is updated to support newly released diffusion models,
      CopyMark can benefit from the update and provide straight-forward generalization of MIAs to new

      diffusion models. This is the main advantage of implementing CopyMark on diffusers. We omit other
325   implementation details to Appendix A.1.
326
327   Evaluation Metrics Following Duan et al. (2023), we randomlyy pick 2500 images as members and
328
      2500 images as non-members. We repeat this picking twice to produce one validation dataset and
      one test dataset. For the validation dataset, we follow Carlini et al. (2023) post TPR at 1% FPR and
329
      0.1% together with the AUC (Algorithm 1). For the test dataset, we post the TPR and FPR for two
330
      optimal thresholds (the threshold at 1% FPR and that at 0.1% FPR) obtained from the validation set
331   (Algorithm 2). The random seed of all evaluation is fixed for full reproducibility.
332
333   Baselines Duan et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2023) show that general MIA methods do not work well
334
      in diffusion models. Hence, our baselines consist of MIA methods on diffusion models, including

      In this section, we use experiments to demonstrate the failure of current diffusion MIA methods on
      real-world benchmarks and validate our attribution in Section 3. We first quantify dataset shifts of all
346
      benchmark setups in CopyMark and validate that there are no or only minor dataset shifts (Section
347
      5.1). Then, we post the performance of MIA methods on differrent setups of CopyMark (Section 5.2),
348   which reproduces their success on defective benchmarks and reveals their failure on our real-world
349   benchmarks. To further investigate the impact of dataset shifts on the MIA performance, we apply
350   current MIA methods on a series of non-member datasets with different proportions of shifted and
351   non-shifted data, and find that dataset shifts and current MIA performance have strong correlation
352   (Section 5.3). This validates our claim about the hallucination of success of diffusion MIA.

      5.1   Q UANTIFYING DATASET S HIFTS
356
357
      We first validate the existence of dataset shifts between member datasets and non-member datasets
358
      by quantifying them. We use CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to extract the representation of images in
359
      members & non-members and take them as the base of our quantification. Figure 2 demonstrates
360   the visualization of semantic representations. Then, we exploit two strategies to quantifying dataset
361   shifts: 1) Explicitly, we calculate three distance metrics between the representation distribution
362   of members and that of non-members: normalized Wasserstein distance (by the internal variance),
363   Frechet distance, and Mahalanobis distance. 2) Implicitly, we train a linear classifier to classify the
364   representations of members & non-members.
365
      Table 3 shows the result of 1). Distances between members and non-members are distinctly larger in
366   two defective setups (a) and (b), compared to our three improved setups (c), (d), and (e). Similar
367   observations appear in the result of 2), posted in Table 4. Linear classifiers can easily separate
368   representations of members and those of non-members in two defective setups, which cannot be done
369   in our three improved setups. Notably, both distance metrics and the classifier shows that there are
370   more dataset shifts in our setup (d) than setup (c) and (e), although these shifts are minor. Specifically,
371   three distances are consistently larger for setup (d), and the true positive rate of the linear classifier is
372   little higher. This validates the description in Section 3 that there are minor dataset shifts in setup (d).
373   Comparing Table 3 and Table 4, we have two conclusions: First, among three distance metrics,
374   Frechet distance (FD) can better measure the dataset shifts, for it shows the best consistency with
375   the performance of the linear classifier. This is also supported by the fact that Frechet Inception
376   Distance (FID), a Frechet distance using Inception representations, is the most widely-used metrics
377   for the shift between real data and data generated by generative models. Second, a golden FD for an
      excellent diffusion MIA benchmark should be around than 0.05, while only our setup (e) meets the
      requirement. In Section 5.2, we will see that setup (e) disables all current diffusion MIA.

409   MIAs perform consistently with results in the original papers We compare our results on setup

507   5.3   H OW D O DATASET S HIFTS IMPACT MIA P ERFORMANCE ?
508
      We further investigate how different levels of dataset shifts impact the performance of diffusion MIA.
509
      To this end, we focus on Stable Diffusion 1.5, because it has two different non-member datasets in
510   our benchmark, where LAION-Non-member does not have dataset shifts and COCO2017-Val does
511   (the reference member dataset is our LAION dataset). This provides possibilities to construct a series
512   of non-member datasets with different levels of dataset shifts by interpolation. We then construct
513   five non-member datasets by mixing data from above two non-member datasets with proportions
514   of 0%:100%, 25%:75%, 50%:50%, 75%:25%, and 100%:0%. We calculate their Frechet distance
515   and then evaluate two loss-based MIA methods, SecMI and PIA, on these non-member datasets and
516   the fixed LAION member dataset. Figure 1 demonstrates the result. The Frechet distance increases
517   with the increase of the proportion of COCO2017-Val, the non-member dataset with dataset shifts.
518
      Consistent with our expectation, the performance of two MIA methods have monotonously positive
      correlation with the the dataset shifts on different levels of dataset shifts. In other words, they perform
519
      better when there is a more serious dataset shift.
520
521   Notably, the setup of 100%:0% is the most widely used MIA benchmark (setup b). We show that
522   one can manipulate the result of MIA evaluation easily by only changing non-members, which is
523   supposed to be irrelevant to the result. This indicates that current MIA evaluation is unreliable.

532   7     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we reveal two defects in the previous evaluation of membership inference attacks
535   (MIAs) on diffusion models: over-training and dataset shifts, which result in overestimate of MIAs’
536   performance. To overcome these defects, we propose CopyMark, the first unified benchmark for
537   MIAs on diffusion models. Our choice of models and datasets keeps CopyMark away from over-
538   training and dataset shifts. We evaluate existing MIA methods with CopyMark and find that current
539   MIAs on diffusion models fail in real-world scenarios of MIA. We alert that MIAs on diffusion
      models are not trustworthy tool to provide evidence for unauthorized data usage in diffusion models.

      diffusers diffusers abstracts inference workflows of diffusion models as pipelines. A pipeline loads
      and manages Modules of diffusion models. Then, it takes inputs and returns outputs. For example,
743
      StableDiffusionImage2ImagePipeline takes images and text prompts as inputs and uses modules
744
      of Stable Diffusion to sample output images with SDEdit (Meng et al., 2021). Usually, state-of-
745   the-art diffusion models consist of three modules: U-Net (UNet2DModel), VAE (AutoencoderKL),
746   and text encoder (CLIPTextModel (Radford et al., 2021)). One diffusion model only have one
747   set of modules. However, it may have several different pipelines. For example, StableDiffusion-
748   Img2ImgPipeline (Meng et al., 2021), StableDiffusionInstructPix2PixPipeline (Brooks et al., 2023),
749   and StableDiffusionGLIGENPipeline (Li et al., 2023) are all pipelines of Stable Diffusion v1.5.
750
      Baselines We first introduce the idea of our baselines as follows:
751
752          • SecMI (Duan et al., 2023): A loss-based method. SecMI uses a parameterized forward step
753            pθ (xt |xt−1 , x0 ) to predict xt from xt−1 . Then, it applies a reverse step pθ (xt |xt−1 , x0 ) to
754            predict xet−1 . The score is given by the l2 distance between xt−1 and x     et−1 , that diffusion
755            models should better predict xt−1 for member images. SecMI use the distance at t = 50,
               while we also try a variant that uses the distance at different t, termed by SecMI++.


                                                          14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
                 • PIA (Kong et al., 2023): A loss-based method. PIA distinguishes member data by checking
757                how diffusion models denoise the xt with the same noise ϵ0 . The score is given by the loss
758                computed over different timesteps t.
759
                 • PFAMI (Fu et al., 2023): A loss-based method. PFAMI exploits the loss fluctuation in
760
                   the image neighborhood. The neighborhood of image x refers to images that share similar
761                contents with x and is constructed by cropping x. It compares the loss of x with that of its
762                neighborhood. It assumes that the loss of non-member images approximates those of their
763                neighborhood images, while the loss of member images should be a distinct local minimum
764                among those of the neighborhood images.
765              • GSA (Pang et al., 2023): The first gradient-based method. GSA aggregates gradients
766                on modules in diffusion models over different timesteps t and uses the l2 norm of these
767                gradients as features. Then, it trains an XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) binary classifier
768                to discriminate features from member data and those from non-member data. The score is
769                given by the classifier. GSA has two variants, termed by GSA1 and GSA2 .
770
771
      All baselines are implemented based on their official open-sourced implementation. For SecMI, we
      use DDIM (Song et al., 2020a) as the sampling method with η = 0 and pick the score at t = 50
772
      as advised by the official implementation 3 . For PIA, we follow the official implementation 4 to

      compute losses at t ∈ {0, 10, 20, ..., 480}. For PFAMI, we follow the original setup in the official
774   implementation 5 to set the neighbor number N as 10, the attacking number M as 1, and the
775   interval of perturbation strengths as [0.75, 0.9]. For the above methods, we separate the threshold
776   interval [τmin , τmax ] in Algorithm 1 into 10,000 sub-intervals and pick the corresponding 10,000

778   compute losses (GSA1 ) or gradients (GSA2 ) over different modules at t ∈ {0, 50, 100, ..., 1000}.

781   GSA2 is fixed to 0.5 because the XGBoost classifier outputs binary scores of {0, 1}. For all methods,

837
838
      A.3.1    W HY DO CURRENT DIFFUSION MIA METHODS FAIL ?
839   Two kinds of MIAs, loss-based MIAs (Duan et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023) and
840   classifier-based MIAs (Pang et al., 2023), fail for different reasons:
841
842
      Loss-based MIAs: These methods are built on the hypothesis that training losses of members are
      lower than those of non-members. However, pre-trained diffusion models were trained on one data
843
      point for one iteration. Hence, the difference between member training losses and non-member
844
      training losses are smaller. Also, the training loss depends on the Gaussian noise added to the clean
845   data and the time step. While current loss-based MIA simply use randomly sampled noise and time
846   steps to calculate losses, it is difficult to accurately locate the noise and the time step where the loss is
847   minimized. That is the reason why the calculated loss is not a good measure for membership. To
848   overcome this, we should take into consideration the loss dependency on noise and time step. This
849   will make us better locate the exact loss being optimized and then distinguish members.
850
      Classifier-based MIAs: Classifier-based MIA methods are built on the assumption that image
851   representations of members and non-members in the diffusion models are distinguishable. However,
852   they suffer the same problem with loss-based methods, that the image representation is also time-
853   step-dependent and noise-dependent. This means that classifier-based MIA methods also need to
854   detect the exact noise and time step used in that one training epoch for the specific data point.
855
      Generally, both current loss-based methods and classifier-based methods neglect the fact that any
856
      features in diffusion models have high dependency on the time step and the noise. Without modeling
857   this dependency, it would be always difficult to determine the membership.
858
859
      A.3.2    A RE MIA S POTENTIAL EVIDENCE OF UNAUTHORIZED DATA USAGE IN AI LAWSUITS ?
860
861   Recent progress in AI copyright lawsuits (Andersen, 2023; Zhang, 2024) indicates the necessity of
862   evidence of unauthorized data usage in pre-trained diffusion models. Specifically, the plaintiff claims
863   that pre-trained diffusion models copy their copyright images without authorization and expects
      to get evidence of this copying by MIAs on diffusion models. According to copyright laws (U.S.


                                                           16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Copyright Office, 2021; European Parliament and Council, 2001), however, the proof of copying
865   requires showing substantial similarity between the defendant’s work and original elements of the
866   plaintiff’s work. In the context of AI copyright lawsuits, this means that the plaintiff must provide
867   images generated by pre-trained diffusion models with content similarities to their copyright images.
868   Unfortunately, today’s MIAs could only give binary membership indicators as outputs. Moreover,
869   as shown in this paper, current MIAs on diffusion models are not reliable tools to even indicate
870   membership. Hence, it is non-realistic to make use of MIAs as evidence in AI copyright lawsuits.
