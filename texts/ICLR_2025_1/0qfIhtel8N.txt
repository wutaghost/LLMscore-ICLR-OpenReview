009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In the realm of advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) and autonomous driv-
012            ing, the accurate classification of driver emotions, behaviors and contextual envi-
013            ronments is critical for enhancing vehicle safety and user experience. This study
014            investigates the performance of various neural network architectures across four
015            distinct classification tasks: Emotion Recognition, Driver Behavior Recognition,
016            Scene-Centric Context Recognition and Vehicle-Based Context Recognition, all
017            of which incorporate visual information captured through cameras. By utilizing
018            camera-based data, we aim to evaluate how different neural architectures han-
019            dle visual inputs in these diverse contexts, thereby exploring the robustness and
020
               generalization of each model to different real-world scenarios. We compare the
               performance of several state-of-the-art models and introduce a novel contribution
021
               that significantly improve classification accuracies in all areas. Our results demon-
022
               strate that the proposed Liquid Dino architecture achieves an overall average ac-
023            curacy of 83.79%, outperforming other models in recognizing driver emotions,
024            behaviors and contextual scenarios. These enhancements underscore the potential
025            of our proposed methods in contributing to the development of more reliable and
026            responsive ADAS.

030
031
032   With the rapid advancement of intelligent transportation systems, the necessity for highly sophisti-
033
      cated driver monitoring systems has become increasingly paramount. Accurate detection and clas-
      sification of driver emotions (Naqvi et al. (2020); Zepf et al. (2020)), behaviors (Chatziloizos et al.
034
      (2024); Shahverdy et al. (2020)) and surrounding and vehicle contexts (Worrall et al. (2012)) are
035
      essential for the development of robust Advanced Driver-Assistance Systems (ADAS) and fully au-
036   tonomous vehicles. These systems not only play a critical role in enhancing road safety but also
037   contribute significantly to improving the overall driving experience by preemptively identifying and
038   mitigating potential hazards.
039
      However, the intricate nature of human emotions and behaviors, combined with the ever-changing
040
      dynamics of driving environments, presents substantial challenges for conventional machine learn-
041
      ing models. Traditional approaches, such as ResNet (He et al., 2015), VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman,
042   2015) and MobileNet (Howard et al., 2017a), often struggle to capture these nuanced variations with
043   the precision required for real-world applications. This limitation underscores the need for more ad-
044   vanced neural network architectures capable of addressing the complex demands of modern driver
045   monitoring systems.
046
      In this study, we aim to overcome these challenges by introducing Liquid Dino and evaluating the
047
      performance of several state-of-the-art neural networks across four critical classification tasks: Emo-
048   tion Recognition, Driver Behavior Recognition, Scene-Centric Context Recognition and Vehicle-
049   Based Context Recognition. We used the AIDE dataset (Yang et al., 2023), from which we used the
050   frames from each video sequence in order to classify simultaneously all the previous tasks. Also,
051   we benchmark these tasks using both established models and our novel contributions, which in-
052   clude a combination of CNN with Closed-form Continuous-time Neural Networks (CFC) (Hasani
053   et al., 2022), DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) and a specialized CNN configuration applied in driver’s
      behaviour identification before (Chatziloizos et al., 2024). These models incorporate cutting-edge

      2   R ELATED W ORK
071
072
073   In earlier efforts to develop driver monitoring systems, traditional methods relied heavily on hand-
074   crafted features and rule-based algorithms to detect driver behaviors. These systems often utilized
075   basic computer vision techniques such as edge detection, skin tone analysis, or head pose estimation
076
      to identify potential distractions (O’Mahony et al., 2020; Dalal & Triggs, 2005; Murphy-Chutorian
      et al., 2007; Veeraraghavan et al., 2007). For example, detecting eye movements and blink rates was
077
      a common approach for determining driver fatigue (Jiao et al., 2014). However, these rule-based
078
      methods struggled to generalize across diverse environments, lighting conditions and driver behav-
079   iors. Additionally, they lacked the adaptability and accuracy needed for complex driving scenar-
080   ios, leading to the emergence of deep learning approaches, which have since become the dominant
081   framework for addressing these challenges.
082
      In the context of driver monitoring systems, network architectures are typically designed with the
083
      practical constraints of deployment in on-road vehicles. Leveraging advances in deep learning,
084
      many approaches have adopted classical models that are well-established in the field. These include
085   widely accepted architectures such as the AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), GoogleNet (Szegedy
086   et al., 2014), PP-Res18 (Zhou et al., 2017), VGG and ResNet families. In parallel, the need for
087   resource-efficient solutions has led to the adoption of lightweight models like MobileNet V1/V2
088   (Howard et al., 2017b; Sandler et al., 2018) and ShuffleNet V1/V2 (Zhang et al., 2018; Ma et al.,
089   2018), which are particularly advantageous for real-time applications in vehicles. For tasks involv-
090   ing video-based data, 3D-CNN models such as C3D (Tran et al., 2015), I3D (Carreira & Zisserman,
091   2017), SlowFast (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019), TimeSFormer (Bertasius et al., 2021) and 3D-ResNet
092   (Hara et al., 2018) have been implemented to capture spatio-temporal features effectively.Also in
093
      previous methodologies, the ST-GCN (Yan et al., 2018) was employed to deal with the skeleton
      sequences via multi-level spatio-temporal graphs. Additionally, specialized network structures have
094
      been developed to address the unique patterns in driving-related data. In our work, we extensively
095
      utilize a combination of classical, lightweight and state-of-the-art (SOTA) baseline models to con-
096   duct a comprehensive set of experiments across various learning paradigms. This diverse array
097   of models and input streams provides critical insights into selecting the most appropriate network
098   structures for driving-aware systems.
099
      To accommodate the complex requirements of multi-stream and multi-modal inputs in driving per-
100
      ception tasks, various fusion strategies have been developed. These strategies are generally catego-
101
      rized into data-level, feature-level and decision-level fusion. For instance, data-level fusion methods,
102   such as those proposed by Ortega et al. (2020), merge infrared and depth frames through pixel-
103   wise correlation, resulting in enhanced perception performance compared to unimodal approaches.
104   Feature-level fusion commonly involves techniques like feature summation or concatenation to inte-
105   grate diverse data streams. Furthermore, decision-level fusion approaches, such as those by Kopuklu
106   et al. (2021), involve training separate models for each driver view and then combining the outputs
107   based on similarity scores. In our research, we introduce the fusion of interior and exterior cameras
      in a form of mosaic, thereby improving the overall performance of the driving perception system.

111   3.1   DATASET D ESCRIPTION
112
113   The AssIstive Driving pErception (AIDE) dataset (Yang et al., 2023) is a novel and comprehensive
114   resource designed to enhance the capabilities of vision-driven driver monitoring systems (DMS).
115   The dataset was meticulously collected under real-world driving conditions, ensuring the authentic-
116   ity and diversity of the data. While most datasets, either offer only the interior view of the cabin
117   or just the outer envirnment, AIDE stands out due to its multi-view, multi-modal and multi-task de-
118
      sign, offering a rich dataset that captures both the internal state of the driver and the external driving
      environment.
119
120   The dataset includes four distinct camera views to provide a wide range of visual information. Three
121   external cameras are mounted on the vehicle to capture the left view, right view and front view, thus
122   covering the traffic context. A fourth camera is installed inside the vehicle to monitor the driver’s
123   state. Each camera records video at a resolution of 1920x1080 pixels, with a frame rate of 15 frames
124
      per second (fps) and a dynamic range of 120 dB. These cameras are synchronized to ensure temporal
      alignment across all views, enabling a cohesive analysis of the driving scenario and driver behavior.
125
126   The dataset is extensively annotated, offering detailed labels for the driver’s face, body posture and
127   gestures, as well as the external traffic environment. Annotations span various categories which are
128   the four tasks that we are going to classify, including:
129
130          • Driver Emotions (DER) : Labels include states such as anxiety, peace and weariness.
131
132          • Driver Behaviors (DBR): Actions like smoking, talking and dozing off are annotated.
133
134          • Scene-Centric or Traffic Context (TCR): Situations such as traffic jams or smooth traffic
135            are documented.
136
137
             • Vehicle Conditions (VCR): The state of the vehicle, such as parking and lane position, is
138            recorded.
139
140
      This multi-task framework allows for a holistic analysis of the driving experience, integrating data
141
      on the driver’s behavior and emotional state with external traffic conditions.
142
143   The data collection was conducted during naturalistic driving sessions, where participants were
144   unaware of the specific data collection objectives to capture authentic driving behavior. Data was
145
      collected from multiple drivers with varied driving styles across different times and days, ensuring
      the dataset encompasses a wide range of driving scenarios and conditions. Overall, the AIDE dataset
146
      is a versatile and detailed dataset that can serve as a foundation for the next generation of driver
147
      monitoring systems.

153
      In our preprocessing pipeline as shown in Figure 1, we began by handling the individual images
      extracted from the videos of the AIDE dataset, carefully aligning and processing them for further
154
      analysis. Specifically, we merged four of these images into a single composite image arranged in a
155
      2x2 grid format. This merging process not only streamlined the data but also allowed us to main-
156   tain the spatial relationships and contextual integrity of the original images. After forming these
157   composite images, we resized them to smaller dimensions to facilitate faster processing and reduce
158   computational load, while ensuring that the essential visual information was preserved. This resizing
159   step was critical in balancing the trade-off between image clarity and the efficiency of subsequent
160   model training and analysis. The preprocessing steps we implemented significantly optimized the
161   dataset, making it more suitable for the demands of machine learning tasks, particularly those in-
      volving complex vision-based driver monitoring systems.

212   4.1               CNN
213
214   The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture presented in (Chatziloizos et al., 2024) was
215   initially designed for the classification of images into 10 distinct categories, identifying the be-
      haviour of the driver. The network is constructed using a series of MixNet-inspired blocks (Tan


                                                                                                                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      & Le, 2019), each comprising convolutional layers, depthwise separable convolutions and residual
217   connections. The initial block applies a convolutional layer with 60 channels using a 5x5 kernel,
218   followed by batch normalization and a depthwise separable convolution with a 3x3 kernel, main-
219   taining the same padding. Additionally, this block incorporates a Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) (Hu
220   et al., 2019) mechanism to bolster the network’s capacity for capturing detailed and intricate pat-
221   terns. This structure is iteratively refined in the subsequent blocks, where the depthwise convolutions
222   are progressively expanded to use 5x5 and 7x7 kernels. To optimize performance, the Swish (Ra-
223   machandran et al., 2017) activation function is employed, having shown superior results compared
224   to ReLU (Nair & Hinton, 2010) and GeLU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2023) in preliminary tests. The
225
      network also includes dropout layers to mitigate the risk of overfitting during training. Following the
      sequence of MixNet-style blocks, a final convolutional block is introduced, featuring convolutional
226
      layers with Swish activation, batch normalization and max-pooling, which collectively contribute to
227
      establishing the network’s spatial hierarchy. The output layer, equipped with a softmax activation
228   function, assigns probabilities to classify the input images into one of the specified classes. The
229   Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017) is utilized to fine-tune the model’s parameters, aiming to
230   maximize classification accuracy while keeping the model’s complexity and parameter count to a
231   minimum.
232
233   4.2   DINOV 2
234
235   DINOv2 (Distillation with No Labels version 2) (Oquab et al., 2023) is an advanced self-supervised
236   learning method designed for computer vision tasks, particularly in scenarios where labeled data
237   is scarce or unavailable. Building on the success of its predecessor, DINOv2 leverages the power
238   of self-distillation and Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2014) to learn rich visual
239
      representations from large-scale, unlabeled image datasets. The core innovation of DINOv2 lies in
      its ability to distill knowledge from a teacher model to a student model without requiring manual
240
      annotations, allowing the model to learn meaningful patterns and features directly from the data.
241
      This approach not only enhances the robustness and generalization capabilities of the model but also
242   reduces the dependency on labeled datasets, making it highly versatile for a wide range of computer
243   vision applications, including image classification, object detection and segmentation. DINOv2 has
244   been shown to produce state-of-the-art results, demonstrating the effectiveness of self-supervised
245   learning in unlocking the potential of large-scale image data.
246
247   4.3   C LOSED - FORM C ONTINUOUS - TIME L IQUID N EURAL N ETWORKS (CFC)
248
249   The hidden state of a Liquid Time-Constant (LTC) network (Hasani et al., 2022; 2020) is given by
250   the following initial value problem (IVP), which models the system’s dynamics as:
251
                                      \frac {dx}{dt} = -\left (w_\tau + f(x, I, \theta )\right )x(t) + A f(x, I, \theta )
252                                                                                                                                    (1)
253
254   where x(t) represents the hidden states, I(t) is the input to the system, wτ is a time-constant param-
255   eter vector, A is a bias vector and f is a neural network parameterized by θ.
256   In the context of an LTC system determined by this IVP and constructed by a single cell receiving
257   a one-dimensional time-series input I(t) with no self-connections, the system’s behavior can be
258   approximated by the following closed-form solution:
259
260                              x(t) = \left (x_0 - A\right ) e^{-\left [w_\tau + f(I(t), \theta )\right ]t} f(-I(t), \theta ) + A    (2)
261
262   Recent advancements in continuous-time neural networks have led to a significant breakthrough
263   with the development of such closed-form solutions, particularly within the framework of LTC net-
264   works. Traditionally, the expressive power of continuous-time models was limited by the reliance
265   on numerical differential equation solvers. This dependency not only constrained scalability but also
266
      slowed progress in understanding complex natural phenomena, such as neural dynamics.
267   The introduction of tightly bounded approximations of previously unsolved integrals within the
268   liquid time-constant dynamics has enabled the derivation of closed-form solutions, thereby circum-
269   venting the need for complex numerical solvers. This innovation has dramatically accelerated both
      training and inference processes by several orders of magnitude. Moreover, these advancements

270
      allow continuous-time and continuous-depth neural models to scale more efficiently than their dif-
271   ferential equation-based counterparts. From our experimental results, when the CFC module was
272   used after a backbone it improved each results by around 1%.
273
274
      Consequently, these closed-form models, rooted in liquid networks, have demonstrated superior
      performance in time-series modeling. They offer a robust alternative to advanced recurrent neu-
275
      ral networks, marking a significant leap forward in the design of spatiotemporal decision-making
276
      systems.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
510
511   This study demonstrates significant advancements in the classification of driver emotions, behaviors
512   and contextual environments using state-of-the-art neural network architectures. The novel Liq-
513
      uid Dino model, along with the DINOv2 and CNN configurations, show substantial improvements
      over traditional models, achieving average accuracies that surpass previous bibliography at least by
514
      5%. The Liquid Dino architecture achieved an overall average accuracy of 83.79%, marking a clear
515
      improvement over the alternative models tested. Specifically, it excelled in Traffic Context Recog-
516   nition, with an accuracy of 95.03% and Vehicle Condition Recognition, achieving 84.76%. These
517   results emphasize the model’s ability to generalize well to complex, real-world scenarios and that the
518   combination of liquid networks, which are nonlinear state-space models (SSMs), with transformer
519   architectures like DINOv2 shows promising potential for future work.
520
      A key finding from this research is that the Liquid Dino model, despite its marginally slower infer-
521
      ence time compared to other configurations, operates well within the thresholds required for real-
522   time applications, making it a viable option for integration into resource-constrained systems, such
523   as those in autonomous vehicles. The model’s ability to maintain high accuracy without compro-
524   mising operational efficiency highlights its potential for real-world deployment in advanced driver-
525   assistance systems (ADAS). Additionally, the Liquid Dino model processes input frame by frame,
526   eliminating the need to wait for a sequence of frames before making a decision, as is the case with
527   some previous 3D-based methodologies. This capability not only reduces latency but also allows for
528   more immediate and continuous monitoring, which is crucial for applications like driver behavior
529   analysis and safety interventions in real-time systems.
530   Furthermore, the improvements achieved in classification accuracy are particularly impactful for
531   the development of intelligent driver monitoring systems. By improving the ability to accurately
532   recognize driver states, such as emotions and behaviors, as well as traffic and vehicle conditions,
533   this model contributes directly to the enhancement of road safety. These advancements are pivotal
534   in reducing driver distractions and ensuring timely interventions, ultimately creating a safer driving
535   environment.
536   In conclusion, this research demonstrates the superiority of the Liquid Dino architecture in address-
537   ing the challenges posed by multi-task learning in autonomous driving scenarios. The findings
538   suggest promising avenues for further research, particularly in refining hybrid architectures and
539   optimizing real-time implementation strategies, paving the way for more efficient and reliable au-
      tonomous driving systems.
