011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research on optimization using large language models (LLMs) typically
014            involves either iterative next-step solution seeking or directly prompting LLMs
015            to generate critical optimization codes. However, these methods often suffer
016
               from low computational efficiency, high sensitivity to prompt design, and a lack
               of domain-specific knowledge. We introduce LLaMoCo, the first instruction-
017
               tuning framework designed to adapt LLMs for solving optimization problems in
018
               a code-to-code manner. LLaMoCo features a comprehensive instruction set that
019            includes code-style problem descriptions as input prompts and robust optimization
020            codes from expert optimizers as target outputs. We then develop a novel two-
021            phase learning strategy with a contrastive learning-based warm-up to enhance
022            convergence during instruction tuning. Extensive experiments demonstrate that a
023            CodeGen (350M) model tuned by our LLaMoCo yields a powerful domain-specific
024            model for generating expert-level optimizers, achieving superior performance
025            compared to GPT-4 Turbo and other competitors on both synthetic and realistic
026            problem sets. The trained model and the usage instructions are available online.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Nowadays, Large Language Models (LLMs) are posing a profound impact on human society (Floridi
032   and Chiriatti, 2020; Lund and Wang, 2023) through their remarkable natural language understanding
033
      and ability to solve complex tasks (Biswas, 2023a; Lund and Wang, 2023; Biswas, 2023b). Op-
      timization is very related to LLMs. Optimization is everywhere yet can not be easily solved by
034
      everyone (AhmadiTeshnizi et al., 2023). For now, most optimization problems from scientific or
035
      industrial scenarios are solved by hand, requiring expert-level knowledge to 1) formulate the problem,
036   2) solve the problem with the desired optimizer. This in turn hinders the widespread of optimization
037   techniques. This raises a key research question: Can LLMs tackle challenging optimization problems
038   that are difficult for humans to address? This question drives the core of our study in this paper.
039
      In the literature, several works have explored the possibilities of using LLMs to solve optimization
040
      problems. A common and straightforward way is to iteratively prompt LLMs to generate better
041
      solutions through a multi-turn conversation process (Yang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023a; Liu et al.,
042   2023a), sometimes incorporating the concept of in-context learning. This solution-to-solution process
043   involves prompting the LLMs with initial or current best-so-far solutions and iteratively requesting
044   improved solutions. While showing certain effectiveness, they can have several limitations: 1)
045   the scale of target optimization tasks (e.g., in terms of the number of decision variables, historical
046   solutions and newly generated solutions) is constrained by the context window length of LLMs;
047   2) the iterative process typically involves hundreds rounds of conversations, consuming significant
048   resources or API callings; and 3) due to LLMs’ sensitivity to prompt design, it is nontrivial to provide
049   consistent prompts that ensure ideal outputs.
050   An alternative way involves directly prompting LLMs to generate optimization programs, either
051   through reusing existing optimization toolboxes (AhmadiTeshnizi et al., 2023) or combining multiple
052   optimizers to create novel ones (Pluhacek et al., 2023). It can be more efficient than the solution-to-
053   solution methods for two reasons: 1) only a few rounds of conversation are needed to generate codes;
      and 2) the prompts and generated codes do not include solution information, making it compatible

074   For example, OptiMUS (AhmadiTeshnizi et al., 2023) integrates hints about the target optimizer
075   into the prompts, requiring a deep understanding of optimization domain knowledge. Additionally,
076   LLMs pre-trained on a broad corpus often fail to generate customized optimizers tailored to a specific

080   In this paper, we propose LLaMoCo, a novel framework that adapts general-purpose Large Language
081   Models for optimization Code generation. Unlike methods based solely on prompt engineering,
082   LLaMoCo learns a domain-specific model to generate expert-level optimizers tailored at the instance
083   level. It leverages a well-formatted instruction set comprising code-to-code pairs of problem de-
084
      scriptions in Python or LaTeX and their corresponding high-performing optimizer implementations,
      refined through extensive benchmarking and hyper-parameter tuning. Once trained with our cus-
085
      tomized instruction set that contains diverse examples of effective optimizers for various problem
086
      instances with distinct landscapes, the model can generalize well to unseen optimization problems.
087   To accelerate the training convergence, we introduce two-phase learning: first enhancing the latent
088   representation through contrastive learning (Hadsell et al., 2006), followed by sequence-to-sequence
089   instruction tuning with a designed loss function. LLaMoCo offers several unique advantages: 1) It
090   generates optimization programs in a single round, making it more efficient for large-scale problems;
091   2) We standardize problem description prompts to structured codes, reducing prompt design effort
092   and enhancing underlying pattern learning for superior optimization performance and zero-shot
093   generalization; 3) LLaMoCo can generate more robust, expert-level optimizers compared to existing
094   general LLMs. Figure 1 depicts the difference between our LLaMoCo and existing approaches.
095   Extensive experiments reveal the remarkably robust performance of our LLaMoCo, surpassing exist-
096   ing methods. Notably, we show that instruction tuning a small LLM like CodeGen-350M (Nijkamp
097   et al., 2023) on domain-specific tasks significantly outperforms even very large models like GPT-
098   4 (Achiam et al., 2023). Moreover, we also provide in-depth analyses of the two-phase adaptation
099   strategy, training data sensitivity, and zero-shot generalization performance. Our contributions
100   are four folds: 1) we introduce LLaMoCo, the first framework for adapting LLMs to generate
101
      expert-level optimizers; 2) we establish a code-to-code instruction set tailored for the optimization
      domain; 3) we propose a two-phase training strategy based on contrastive warm-up training; and 4)
102
      our LLaMoCo exhibits superior zero-shot generalization, efficiency, and robustness.

106
107   Fine-tuning LLMs. Pre-trained LLMs can be refined by parameter updates on specific tasks
      through a fine-tuning process. We introduce two prominent fine-tuning strategies: Instruction

108
      Tuning (IT) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Alignment Tuning (AT) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler
109   et al., 2019), each serving distinct purposes. Generally, IT involves fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs
110   using a moderate collection of formatted task instances (Wei et al., 2022a). The fine-tuning process
111   typically includes two steps: 1) prepare instruction-formatted training examples by associating a task
112   description with each task instance, which aids LLMs in understanding tasks (Sanh et al., 2022); and
113   2) leverage the prepared instruction set to fine-tune LLMs using a sequence-to-sequence supervised
114   loss (Gupta et al., 2023). By incorporating a well-established task-specific instruction set, IT can
115   effectively inject domain-specific knowledge into general LLMs. It enables the transfer of LLMs
116   to specific experts in domains like medicine (Singhal et al., 2023), law (Huang et al., 2023) and
117
      finance (Zhang et al., 2023a). Differently, AT aims to correct unexpected behaviors of LLMs by
      aligning the models with human values and preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2019). A
118
      practical algorithm for AT is the Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler
119
      et al., 2019), which firstly estimates a reward model on a human-preference data collection via
120   maximum likelihood. It then uses the learned reward model to provide feedback and post-trains
121   the LLMs through Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). A recent work
122   named Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) first reparameterizes the reward
123   function based on the parameters of the pre-trained LLMs, saving the modelling and training of the
124   reward function. DPO is mathematically equivalent to RLHF but is even more efficient, which is
125   widely adopted in the latest LLMs such as Mistral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024). Our LLaMoCo is the first
126   instruction-tuning framework for adapting general LLMs as an efficient and effective optimization
127   tool, which addresses the unsatisfactory optimization performance due to the limited domain-specific
128
      knowledge of general LLMs.
129   LLMs for code generation. Generating code from natural language descriptions is exciting and com-
130   plex (Zan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021). Although general-purpose LLMs such as GPT (Brown et al.,
131   2020), Llama 2 (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2024) show competitive performance
132   on the widely used LLM benchmarks including HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), MBPP (Austin et al.,
133   2021) and DS-1000 (Lai et al., 2023), their performance on a particular task may still be limited.
134
      Recent efforts have focused on developing LLMs specifically tailored for code generation. These
      models can be trained exclusively on code, such as AlphaCode (Li et al., 2022) and StarCoder (Li
135
      et al., 2023), fine-tuned from general LLMs, like Codex (Chen et al., 2021) and Code Llama (Roziere
136
      et al., 2023), or prompted from pre-trained LLMs such as FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024).
137   Notably, Codex shows that a 12B LLM can solve 72.31% of complex programming tasks posed by
138   humans. This success has led to the emergence of various Code LLMs, such as CodeGen (Nijkamp
139   et al., 2023) that factorizes a potentially long specification into multiple steps to enhance program
140   synthesis, and Code Llama that extends Llama 2 models through a cascade of fine-tuning steps. Other
141   models such as Phi-2 (Javaheripi et al., 2023), InCoder (Fried et al., 2023) and CodeGeeX (Zheng
142   et al., 2023) have also gained great attention. Nevertheless, the target scenario in our LLaMoCo is the
143   optimization domain, where the optimization code generation is more challenging than normal code
144   generation task due to the intricate semantic alignment issue and data imbalance issue. We in this
145
      paper propose a novel two-stage instruction tuning to address these challenges.
146   LLMs as optimizers. Optimization is crucial in numerous science and engineering fields but
147   poses significant challenges. Unlike tasks such as language understanding, optimization problems
148   are difficult for humans to solve without efficient algorithms, challenging LLM’s reasoning and
149   generalization abilities. Recent research has explored prompting LLMs for solutions of the
150   given numerical optimization (Yang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b), optimizer
151
      discovery (Pluhacek et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b), or prompt optimization (Guo
      et al., 2023b) scenarios, which is based on prompt engineering and in-context learning (Min et al.,
152
      2022). Typically, these methods involve a set of candidate solutions for improvement, where LLMs
153
      receive prompts with these solutions and their objective values and then generate improved solutions
154   iteratively until a termination condition is met. However, such a paradigm challenges the expertise of
155   the general LLMs for optimization, which is less developed during their pre-training. To address this,
156   the latest studies innovatively proposed prompting LLMs to behave like black-box optimizers, that is,
157   instructing LLMs to perform mutation, crossover operations and elitism strategy (Liu et al., 2023a;
158   Lehman et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Brahmachary et al., 2024) on the candidate solutions. An
159   eye-catching work of this line is EvoPrompting (Chen et al., 2023), which is surprisingly capable of
160   finding neural network architectures with state-of-the-art performance. However, these approaches
161   have limitations in efficiency due to the need for extensive iterations.

      In contrast, several studies share a similar ambition with our LLaMoCo: prompting LLMs directly
163   for pieces of executable optimization programs. An emerging research area is LLMs for Algorithm
164   Design (Liu et al., 2024) in the combinatorial optimization domain, that is, applying LLMs at the
165   algorithm design level to search and refine algorithm programs in an evolution fashion. By evolving
166   the programs of constructive methods, the finally obtained optimization program could beat certain
167   human-crafted heuristics. OptiMUS (AhmadiTeshnizi et al., 2023), on the other hand, integrates
168   hints about the target optimizer into the prompts to create and test new optimizers for numerical
169   optimization problems. Differently, our LLaMoCo facilitate a novel paradigm: fine-tuning LLMs
170   to generate advanced optimizer programs for numerical optimization in one round conversation at
171
      the instance level. To this end, we propose a stipulated optimization problem description format, a
      novel automated synthetic problem generation procedure and a large-scale benchmarking process
172
      to construct a comprehensive instruction set. To the best of our knowledge, all the aforementioned
173
      works focus on prompt engineering of pre-trained LLMs, and the area of fine-tuning general LLMs
174   with optimization-domain knowledge remains unexplored.

177   3     LL A M O C O
178
179   LLaMoCo is the first instruction tuning framework for adapting general-purpose LLMs to generate
180
      instance-level optimizers. Operating on a code-to-code basis, it takes an optimization problem written
      with Python or LaTeX and generates a code implementation of a customized optimizer (as illustrated
181
      in Appendix F). Achieving such code-to-code flexibility encounters several unique challenges. First,
182
      we propose a novel synthesizing procedure which could automatically generate sufficient and diverse
183   synthetic functions with/without constraints for instruction-tuning (Section 3.1). Next, to construct
184   high-quality prompt-answer data for the instruction tuning, we propose an innovative and automated
185   large-scale benchmarking process to facilitate effective answer labeling (Section 3.1). At last, based
186   on the semantic alignment issue and data imbalance issue in the constructed instruction set, we design
187   a novel two-phase tuning strategy to fine-tune general LLMs, as detailed in Section 3.2
188

      Knowledge gathering. In our study, the term ‘knowledge’ refers to expertise in handling optimization
223   problems, including selecting a well-performing optimizer and configuring its hyperparameters. To
224   this end, we conduct exhaustive benchmarking to determine one effective optimizer for each instance
225   p ∈ P . Concretely, we filter a wide range of optimizers from the literature (Stork et al., 2022;
226   Zhan et al., 2022), competitions (Wu et al., 2017; Mohamed et al., 2021; Turner et al., 2021), and
227   benchmarks (R.Turner and D.Eriksson, 2019; Guo et al., 2023c), selecting 23 optimizers that span
228   various algorithm families, including Evolutionary Algorithms (e.g., GA (Holland, 1992; Clune et al.,
229   2008; Wang et al., 2023), DE (Storn and Price, 1997; Xu et al., 2020; Biswas et al., 2021; Ye et al.,
230   2023), PSO (Kennedy and Eberhart, 1995; Gong et al., 2015; Wu and Wang, 2022; Lu et al., 2023)
231
      and ES (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008; Hansen, 2009; He et al., 2020)),
      Bayesian Optimization (Snoek et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2020), Local Search strategies (Kirkpatrick
232
      et al., 1983; Van Laarhoven et al., 1987; Xiang et al., 1997; Fontes et al., 2023), and Numerical
233
      Optimization methods (Kraft, 1988; Conn et al., 2000; Powell, 2007; Bollapragada et al., 2018). To
234   determine the most effective optimizer for each instance p, we employ a two-step process. Firstly, we
235   perform a grid search to identify the best configuration for each optimizer on p (conducted multiple
236   runs to reduce the impact of variance). Subsequently, we select the optimizer that yields the best
237   performance among all the configured optimizers. The selected optimizer and its configuration are
238   implemented as a piece of Python code, denoted as ap , serving as the knowledge of the desired
239   optimizer’s implementation for instance p. Refer to Appendix A and Appendix E.4 for details and
240   correctness of the benchmarking process respectively.
241   Instruction set construction. So far we have obtained a problem set P of 6000 optimization
242   instances and their best-performing optimizer code. However, how to describe each instance p ∈ P in
243   a programming language has not been resolved. Common sense is that we human beings describe an
244   optimization problem’s mathematical formulation in computers by programming languages such as
245   Python or LaTeX. However, even with such universal languages, different users created diverse code
246   pieces to formulate the given problem due to their programming habits. This motivates us to augment
247   each instance in P to diverse problem descriptions written in either Python or LaTeX. Training on
248
      such augmented data could help LLaMoCo output consistent optimization code even if its inputs are
      different description versions of the same problem instance. To achieve this, we have conducted a
249
      survey among computer science students, and let them independently write problem descriptions
250
      for instances in P by both Python and LaTeX. We analysed the collected scripts and found several
251   major programming patterns. By using these patterns, we create 4 ∼ 6 Python or LaTeX problem
252   descriptions for each instance. Due to the space limitation, we provide details about the found
253   patterns in Appendix B. After the augmentation, for each problem instance p ∈ P , we first insert
254   each description of it into the prompt template to attain 4 ∼ 6 text prompts {q1,p , q2,p , ...}. We then
255   insert the selected best-performing optimization code for p into the answer template to attain a text
256   answer ap . At last, for each p, we construct 4 ∼ 6 prompt-answer pairs {(q1,p , ap ), (q2,p , ap ), ...}. By
257   repeatedly constructing prompt-answer pairs for all instances in P , we finally construct an instruction
258   tuning set I comprising 32570 prompt-answer pairs.

262   Contrastive warm-up. Given the constructed instruction set I, although we could naively ap-
263   ply regular LM loss to train an LLM to fit prompt-answer pairs in I, the training convergence
264
      and stability suffer from two cases: a) there are 6000 problem instances involved, and they can

265
      on each instance. This means that there is a chance two different problems hold very similar
266

269   line. However, the answer for the one without constraint should be the DE algorithm and for

338   Baselines. We include two solution-to-solution approaches, OPRO (Yang et al., 2023) and LMEA (Liu
339
      et al., 2023a), which prompt pre-trained LLMs (e.g., GPT-4 Turbo) repeatedly to generate and improve
      solutions for the given problems. Compared to OPRO, LMEA additionally engineered its prompt
340
      with an explicit indication of using some evolutionary operators to let LLMs act as an evolutionary
341
      optimizer for performance boost. We also include six general LLMs for code generation, namely
342   CodeGen-Mono-350M (Nijkamp et al., 2023), Phi-2-2.7B (Javaheripi et al., 2023), Code Llama-
343   7B (Roziere et al., 2023), Llama 2-70B (Touvron et al., 2023), DeepSeedMath-Instruct-7B (Shao
344   et al., 2024) and GPT-4 Turbo (Achiam et al., 2023). We prompt these three general LLMs with
345   the same format as in our instruction set I to generate an optimizer for each problem instance. Note
346   that we do not include the LLMs for algorithm design works such as EoH (Liu et al., 2024) into
347   the comparison, since works in this line primarily address the combinatorial optimization problems
348   but our LLaMoCo revolves around numerical optimization problems. The configurations of the
349   baselines are set by default according to the corresponding references, and listed in Appendix C. Note
350
      that we do not include Algorithm Selection methods (Kerschke et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2024) for
      comparison since LLaMoCo generates complete optimizer source codes that not only specify the
351
      selected algorithm but also include the necessary implementation details, which is beyond the scope
352
      of standard algorithm selection. Besides, LLaMoCo performs hyper-parameter tuning as part of the
353   optimization code generation process, providing a level of configurability that algorithm selection
354   methods cannot achieve.
355
356
      Performance metrics. When evaluating the performance of LLMs for optimization, we consider
      four metrics: 1) the code error rate, which indicates the proportion of problems for which the LLMs
357
      generate optimization codes with bugs (lower values are preferable); 2) the code recovery cost,
358
      which measures the proportion of lines of code that need to be corrected in order to fix the bugs
359   in the erroneous codes (lower values are preferable); 3) the average optimization performance on
360   the test problems (higher values are preferable), which is a min-max normalized term indicating
361   the optimization results over the tested problem set; and 4) the average computational overhead for
362   solving a problem, which is determined by the number of tokens used for both the input and output
363   of LLMs (lower values are preferable). These four metrics could provide a comprehensive evaluation
364   of existing baselines and our LLaMoCo in aspects of code generation robustness, optimization
365   performance and runtime complexity. The detailed calculations can be found in Appendix D.

      4.3   A BLATION STUDY
467
468   Contrastive warm-up. The contrastive warm-up phase in our proposed two-phase instruction
469   tuning strategy (see Section 3.2) aims to reduce the cross-modal ambiguity by aligning the latent
470   representations of different prompts that share the same desired optimizer (vice versa). We illustrate
471   the performance gain curves on Ieval with or without the contrastive warm-up during the instruction
472   tuning in the left of Figure 3, where LLaMoCo-S is applied as a showcase and the error bars indicate
473   the variance across 5 training runs. The results show that incorporating such a contrastive warm-up
474   strategy aids in accelerating the convergence of the subsequent instruction tuning. Furthermore, it is
475   advantageous for the LLMs to generate accurate codes and enhance the overall performance. We
476
      refer to Appendix E.5 for all results on different LLaMoCo models and test problem sets.
477   Diversity enhancement. To improve the generalization of the fine-tuned LLMs in LLaMoCo, we
478   enrich the task descriptions for each problem instance by augmenting the description of its objective
479   function and constraints with Python or LaTeX codes of different writing styles. We illustrate the
480   effect of this procedure in the middle of Figure 3 by showing the optimization performance of six
481   LLaMoCo-S models trained on pure Python, pure LaTeX and Python+LaTeX data, with or without
482
      the diversity enhancement by rephrasing. The results show that providing multi-lingual descriptions
      of optimization problems significantly boosts the generalization performance, while rephrasing each
483
      description with multiple writing styles further enhances the final training results.
484
485   Balanced data sampling. In LLaMoCo, we address the imbalanced data distribution (caused by
      dominate optimizers) through performing example-proportional sampling on Itrain . To investigate its


                                                                                                                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      effectiveness, we train two LLaMoCo-S models on Itrain , with or without the data balancing strategy,
487   respectively. The optimization performance of the two models is presented in the right of Figure 3, by
488   separately considering the majority instances (which request the dominating optimizers), the minority
489   instances (which request the others), and the overall instances of Ieval . The results consistently show
490   that keeping a balanced training data distribution significantly boosts performance.

492   4.4   O PEN -E NDED D ISCUSSION : I S GPT-4 A T RUE O PTIMIZATION E XPERT ?
493
494   Considering the competitive performance of GPT-4, as shown in Table 1, we delve into whether
495   GPT-4 can be deemed as a genuine optimization expert. Upon viewing the optimization codes
496
      generated by GPT-4 for both test and realistic problem sets, a noteworthy pattern emerges. GPT-4
      consistently leans towards generating a specific numerical optimizer, SLSQP (Kraft, 1988), for almost
497
      all tested problems. While SLSQP is a classical solver for convex quadratic programming and is
498
      included in our chosen advanced optimizers, our benchmarking results identify that on a proportion
499   of tested problems, it underperforms the others such as the Vanilla DE (Storn and Price, 1997).
500
501   To investigate further, we have conducted testing on a series of latest GPT models: GPT-4o, GPT
502
      o1-mini and GPT o1-preview. In particular, we also add a GPT-4 vector search baseline (OpenAI,
      2023) which uses a vector search method to provide in-context enhancement for the GPT-4 model.
503
      We present the performance comparision in Table 4. From the results, we can observe that: a) o1 v.s.
504
      GPT-4o: indeed, o1 models achieve significantly lower coding errors than 4o model, demonstrating
505   their robust coding enhancement. b) o1-mini v.s. LLaMoCo: on the one hand, the error rate of
506   o1-mini is lower than our LLaMoCo, which originates from the black-box training of o1-mini on
507   extremely large coding tasks. On the other hand, LLaMoCo, trained on a very small model, could
508   achieve more optimization performance gain, with fewer tokens consumed. Besides, we look into
509   the source codes generated by o1-mini as we have done for GPT-4 Turbo model in Section 4.4. It
510   turns out that o1-mini also leans toward generating a particular optimizer, DE algorithm, for almost
511   all tested problems. This further underscores the core motivation of LLaMoCo, which is exploring
512   how to inject domain-specific knowledge into LLMs to adapt them for specific tasks. c) By providing
513
      GPT-4 Turbo with an example prompt-answer pair which is similar to the tested prompt, the error
      rate of the generated optimizer code significantly declines. d) However, such a prompting strategy
514
      consumes doubled tokens than directly prompting GPT-4 Turbo, which is inefficient considering our
515
      LLaMoCo only requires 2.4k tokens to achieve superior optimization performance. This underscores
516   the importance of our LLaMoCo for adapting LLMs to solve optimization problems.

518   5     C ONCLUSION
519
520
      We introduce LLaMoCo, the first instruction-tuning framework to adapt general LLMs to function
      as expert-level systems to solve optimization problems. To achieve this, we meticulously construct
521
      an instruction set with more than 30k demonstration examples and then employ a novel two-phase
522
      instruction tuning strategy to fine-tune a series of LLMs. The results show that our models consistently
523   outperform existing approaches. Notably, we observe that a relatively small LLM is sufficient to
524   be tuned as an expert-level optimization code generator superior to GPT-4. As a pioneering work,
525   LLaMoCo holds certain limitations. On the one hand, The current LLaMoCo has been fine-tuned
526   on a dataset comprising 30k single-objective optimization problems, serving as a proof of concept
527   for the framework’s potential on larger-scale training. Hence, these models may not yet be fully
528   equipped to handle all out-of-distribution problem types. Exploring LLaMoCo’s applicability to other
529   additional problem domains represents an exciting avenue for future research. On the other hand,
530   beyond the current reliance on a labeled dataset for fine-tuning, we can see significant potential in
531
      further enhancing LLaMoCo’s efficiency and generalization capabilities by integrating Alignment
      Tuning (AT), which not only promises to refine the model’s performance with expert optimization
532
      knowledge but also ensures that the solutions it generates are more interpretable and aligned with
533
      human knowledge. Other future research directions include but not limited to: a) the potential
534   application of the proposed contrastive warmup strategy to other domains outlines an interesting and
535   open-ended future work. b) the design of the instruction dataset such as employing CoT (Wei et al.,
536   2022b) to enhance the reasoning ability of the fine-tuned model, is very promising. c) incorporate
537   LLaMoCo with effective multi-turn code optimization framework might be a promising way to further
538   improve both the code correctness and the optimization performance of LLaMoCo. In a word, we
539   propose LLaMoCo with the hope that this preliminary exploratory research could appeal to more
      researchers to explore the potential of LLMs as Optimizers.

1136
       Training settings. For generating the task set P , the problem dimension D for each pi is randomly
1137   chosen from [2, 50], and the number of components K is randomly chosen from [1, 5]. We randomly
1138   split the instruction set I into a training set Itrain with 30k input-output pairs and a test set Ieval with
1139   the rest examples. We leave other details in Appendix. For our two-phase instruction tuning, we
1140   deploy 5 epochs of contrastive warm-up and 20 epochs of instruction tuning for all fundamental
1141   models. Specifically, we first apply SGD (Amari, 1993) with a fixed learning rate 5 × 10−4 in the
1142   contrastive warm-up phase, alongside φ = 0.3. Then, we apply AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter,
1143   2019) to optimize the LLMs in the instruction tuning phase. During the initial 1k iterations, the
1144   learning rate gradually increases from 0 to 5 × 10−4 in a linear manner. Subsequently, it decreases to
1145
       0 according to a cosine schedule. The batch size in both phases is set to 4. Note that we fine-tune
       the CodeGen-Mono (350M) with full parameters, but apply LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) to fine-tune the
1146
       larger Phi-2 (2.7B) and Code Llama (7B) models, with the rank r = 8, scaling factor α = 32, and a
1147
       dropout rate of 0.05. All experiments are performed on a platform with an Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold
1148   6348 CPU, 504GB RAM and a Nvidia A800 (80GB) GPU. Upon the settings, the training duration
1149   for CodeGen is one day, whereas Phi-2 and Code Llama require 2.5 days and 4 days, respectively.
1150
1151
       Settings of the baseline. The general LLM baselines we considered in the comparison are configured
       by their default settings . In particular, we adopt a temperature of 0, a maximum token length of 2048,
1152
       and the basemodel codellama − 7b − instruct for Code Llama-7B, which follows the reported
1153
       settings for their pass@1 evaluation on MBPP and HumanEval benchmark (see Table 2 in their paper
1154   Roziere et al. (2023)). Besides, the configurations for Llama2-70B baseline is with a temperature of
1155   0.1, a maximum token length of 2048, and the basemodel llama − 70b − chat, which aligns with the
1156   settings for MBPP and HumanEval benchmark (Table 21 in their paper (Touvron et al., 2023)). To the
1157   best of our knowledge, the MBPP and the HumanEval benchmark are the most related code generation
1158   tasks to the optimization program generation task of LLaMoCo. We directly call the corresponding
1159   APIs (see https://docs.llama-api.com/essentials/chat) for the evaluation in our
1160   paper. As for GPT-4 Turbo, since there are limited literature discussing the code generation ability of
1161   GPT-4 Trubo, we adopt the default settings with a temperature of 1, a maximum token length of 2048,
1162
       and the basemodel gpt − 4 − 1106 − preview of the OpenAI API (see https://platform.
       openai.com/docs/guides/text-generation/chat-completions-api).
1163
1164   We would also clarify the configurations for OPRO and LMEA. Following the comparison settings
1165   stated at the LMEA paper (Liu et al., 2023a), the population size N is set to 16, the maximum
1166   generation number G is set to 250, for both OPRO and LMEA, with the backbone LLM as gpt −
1167   4 − 1106 − preview version of GPT-4 Turbo. The major difference of these two approaches lies
1168
       on the concrete temperature settings. While OPRO reported 1.0 as the best performing temperature
       (see Section 5.1 of their paper (Yang et al., 2023)), LMEA (Liu et al., 2023a) adaptively adjust the
1169
       temperature along the optimization horizon: with initial temperature as 1.0, which will increase by
1170
       0.1 if it fails to find a better solution for every 20 generations.
1171
1172   Homogeneous batch sampling. We further apply a homogeneous batch sampling strategy at the
1173   instruction tuning phase to reinforce the alignment of the different rephrasing version prompts for
1174
       a problem p ∈ P . Concretely, we force the LLMs to sample data pairs which come from the same
       problem instances in a mini-batch. We observe consistent boosts in the training of LLaMoCo-S,
1175
       LLaMoCo-M and LLaMoCo-L. By presenting the LLMs with a batch of homogeneous samples, they
1176
       can learn patterns specific to these cross-modal prompts data more effectively.
1177
1178   Batch size. We would clarify that due to the resource limitation, all of our experiments are run on an

1184   D    C ALCULATION OF E XPERIMENTAL S TATISTICS
1185
1186   To provide a thorough evaluation on the LLMs fine-tuned by our LLaMoCo and the other approaches,
1187   for a group of Np problem instances, we first leverage the optimization programs generated by each
       LLM to optimize them, for 5 independent runs. Then we calculate the average error rate, recovery


                                                           22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       cost, optimization performance and computational cost of an approach as the performance metrics of
1189   overall performance. The calculation details of these four performance metrics in our experimental
1190   results are as follows:
1191
1192
       Error rate (Err.) The robustness of the generated optimization program is a very important index for
       quality of service (QoS). We measure the robustness by the proportion of error programs generated
1193
       by an LLM, named as error rate. For each instance, we use the optimization program generated by an
1194
       LLM (ours or the others) to optimize that instance for 5 independent runs. We count the number of
1195   the generated programs which encounter compilation error or runtime error when being executed,
1196   denoted as Nerr (every single run on each instance is counted). Then the error rate of an approach on
1197                                         Nerr
       the tested instances is calculated as 5×Np
                                                  .
1198
1199   Recovery cost (Rec.) While an optimization program may encounter compilation error or runtime
1200   error, we observe from our experiments that a certain proportion of the error programs could be
1201   repaired and recovered. We provide a metric named recovery cost to measure the efforts required to
1202
       repair the generated programs. Concretely, during the test time, if the optimization program generated
       by an LLM was non-functional, we would give the tested LLM an additional turn of conversation
1203
       to refine the errors of the generated optimization code. Concretely, we construct a prompt: for
1204
