008                                                A BSTRACT
009
               We present Causal Relation Networks (CausalRNs), the first all-MLP sequence
010
               modeling architecture with linear-time parallel training. To enable autoregressive
011
               modeling, we made Relation Networks (RNs) equivariant and causal through
012            relaxation and masking. Contrary to the earlier belief that RNs are quadratic-time,
013            we show that when using exp(x) as the activation function, any RN is linear-
014            time, fully parallelizable, and numerically stable. Our derivation spontaneously
015            gave rise to familiar design choices adopted by state-of-the-art architectures, e.g.
016            exponential gating and state expansion. Such duality provided a new perspective,
017            from which we not only validated popular design choices, but also discovered
018            new design considerations. Experiments on autoregressive language modeling and
019            image classification showed CausalRNs to be comparable to Linear Transformers.
020
               The quadratic variant of CausalRNs achieved perfect retrieval on the copying task,
               which was previously only possible with Transformers.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   One of the mysteries in the field of sequence modeling is how the Transformer architecture has
026   remained largely the same since its inception in 2017 (Vaswani et al., 2017). While the field has
027   seen tremendous progress—from successfully scaling up these architectures in language modeling
028   (Radford et al., 2018; Achiam et al., 2023) to realizing that most machine learning tasks can be
029   formulated as instances of sequence modeling (Carion et al., 2020; Sharir et al., 2021; Achiam et al.,
030   2023)—our theoretical understanding of Transformers remained lacking.
031   Contrasting this limited understanding of Transformers is our increasing knowledge of Multi-Layer
032   Perceptrons (MLPs) (Rosenbaltt, 1957), the fundamental building blocks of deep learning. Over
033   the years, we have gained increased insights into MLPs, from the theory of Neural Tangent Kernels
034   (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018) to the empirical and theoretical guarantees of global convergence in the
035   over-parameterized regime (Barboni et al., 2022). MLPs’ lack of strong inductive biases has also
036   led to the emergence of all-MLP architectures that offer interesting insights. For example, the MLP-
037   Mixers (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) showed that MLPs can be powerful image classifiers, and directly
038
      scaling MLPs (Bachmann et al., 2024) demonstrated their efficiency and scalability. However, there
      has not yet been a Transformer-style all-MLP architecture that supports autoregressive modeling.
039
040   In this paper, we propose a novel all-MLP (Rosenbaltt, 1957) architecture called the Causal Relation
041   Network (CausalRN) (see Figure 1). The original Relation Networks (RNs) were proposed to model
042   the complex pairwise relationship within a set of feature vectors, but had limited scalability, flexibil-
043   ity, and computational efficiency (Santoro et al., 2017). In fact, training an RN requires computing
044
      a quadratic number of shared-weight MLPs, with respect to the number of feature vectors. RNs are
      also incompatible with autoregressive modeling.
045
046   To make RNs compatible with autoregressive sequence modeling(Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford
047   et al., 2018), we made two key modifications. First, we relaxed one of their summation steps.
048   This makes RNs equivariant like Transformers and enables stacking of RN blocks to create deeper
049   networks. Second, we causally masked the summation step (see Figure 1 (d)), which is a requirement
050
      for models to train and predict autoregressively (Radford et al., 2018).
051   Each CausalRN block computes a quadratic number of MLPs, which can be computationally heavy.
052   We discovered that the input and output layers can be pre-computed in linear time and reused. In
053   addition, we show that CausalRNs can be fully linearized simply by switching the activation function
      from ReLU (Nair & Hinton, 2010) to the exponential function, exp(x). This linearization is exact,

079   A surprising discovery emerged when we force CausalRNs to become quadratic. By using pre-
080   activation normalization, we recover an infinitely growing matrix-valued state that is irreducible to
081   a single vector. We hypothesize that this allows for efficient in-context retrieval, similar to the KV
082   cache of a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). Our results suggest matrix-valued states are critical
083   for designing future architectures that might try to match or surpass Transformers.
084   This work is a scientific investigation in machine learning (Nakkiran & Belkin, 2022). Our main
085   contribution lies in revealing novel theoretical results and surprising phenomena. In the process, we
086   raise new research questions and propose new research directions. We do not position the CausalRN
087   as a replacement for Transformers or State Space Models (SSMs).

095   Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) Multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) (Rosenbaltt, 1957) are the
096   simplest form of a neural network. MLPs were originally introduced as a method to fit non-linear
097   functions for data with vector inputs and outputs. They emerged as powerful architectures in ma-
098   chine learning due to their ability to approximate any continuous function (Hornik et al., 1989).
099   The single-hidden-layer MLP is a foundational building block for more complex neural network
100   architectures (Zaheer et al., 2017; Santoro et al., 2017; Vaswani et al., 2017).

      3.3    P OST- REDUCTION NORMALIZATION
236
237   In its original definition, a Relation Network (Santoro et al., 2017) uses summation to aggregate
238   information from feature vectors (see Eq. 3). To ensure the resultant vector has a stable variance, we
239   propose post-reduction normalization (see Figure 2). This is achieved by applying Layer Normal-
240   ization (Ba et al., 2016) right after the summation step. We test post-reduction normalization and its
241   effect in Section 4.2.
242   As added benefits, post-reduction normalization allows for better interpretability. We visualize an
243   attention map extracted from a vision BiRN in Section 4.4.
244
245   3.4    P RE - ACTIVATION NORMALIZATION
246
247   We showed that exponentially-activated CausalRNs are reducible to linear time and space complex-
248   ity. This is beneficial from an engineering point-of-view, but it could hurt in-context retrieval ability
249   (Oren et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024). This is because a linearized CausalRN has a fixed vector-valued
250   memory. In contrast, a Transformer has an infinitely growing matrix-valued memory (Oren et al.,
251   2024), commonly known as a KV cache (Vaswani et al., 2017).
252   Different from RNNs (Peng et al., 2023) and SSMs (Gu & Dao, 2023), CausalRNs offer an elegant
253   way to induce an infinite matrix-valued memory. By applying pre-activation normalization (see
254   Fig. 2), exponentially-activated CausalRNs become irreducible. This is because the exponential
255   property no longer holds for exp (µ(x + y)), where µ is a normalization operator. They also take
256   quadratic time to train, so we introduce an approximation, exp (µ(x) + µ(y)), which preserves the
257   exponential property and linearizability. We call this the approximate pre-activation normalization.
258   We hypothesize that, while a generic CausalRN maintains an infinitely growing matrix-valued mem-
259   ory, it is degenerate and cannot hold as much information. Pre-activation normalization induces a
260   non-degenerate infinite matrix-valued memory that enables effective in-context retrieval, similar to
261   a KV cache (Vaswani et al., 2017). We test this hypothesis in Section 4.4.

429   plication, due to come clear limitations: (1) CausalRNs do not have a multi-head scheme; (2) the
430   current implementation, while linear-time and parallel-trainable, is not I/O-aware with wide hidden
431   layers; (3) CausalRNs do not fully utilize tensor cores. Nonetheless, CausalRNs are valuable for
      theoretical explorations, and is very computationally practical.

596   It is straightforward to implement Bidirectional Relation Networks (BiRNs) and Causal Relation
597   Networks (CausalRNs) in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). In Figure 8, we share code snippets
598   for the key computation of of both modules. Variable a and b each has shape (batch size,
599   num tokens, emb size). Variable a corresponds to the set of vectors outputted by a linear
600   layer, and variable b corresponds to the set of vectors outputted by an affine layer. The key insight is
601   to use logsumexp or logcumsumexp to perform the reduction step before subtracting maximum
602   values along certain axes. This stability trick preserves collinearity. Therefore, as long as we apply
603   post-reduction normalization, our stability trick is exact. The full code will be release on github.

      B    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
615
616   Architecture We compared our CausalRNs with Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), Linear
617   Transformers(Katharopoulos et al., 2020), and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). There are two scale vari-
618   ants: base and tiny. For base models, we set de = 768 and dh = 3072. The number of layers is 12.
619   The number of heads is 12, if applicable. Under the base setting, both CausalRNs and Transformers
620   have 85.6M parameters, and the Mamba architecture has 45.8M parameters. For tiny models, to
621   match the scale of the copying task, we set de = 192, which was 150% of 128 and 75% of 256.
622   128 and 256 are the length of the random sequence that we are copying. Under the tiny setting, for
623   both linear and quadratic CausalRNs, we chose dh = de = 192. The number of (Linear) CausalRN
624
      blocks is 12, totaling 1.34 million parameters. CausalRNs are homogeneous architectures, meaning
      they exclusively use CausalRN layers without mixing MLP or self-attention layers. Residual con-
625
      nections and pre-normalization are used, following the Transformer architecture pattern. For both
626
      linear and quadratic Transformers, we chose dh = 4de = 768. We used 12 (Linear) Transformer
627   blocks, totaling 5.33 million parameters. For both Transformers, a sweep for the attention heads
628   were performed in {1, 2, 4, 8}. We found using a single head gave optimal performance under our
629   choice of de = 192.
630
631   Initialization Following common practices, we initialized all biases to zero and all weights ran-
632   domly following N (0, 0.02), except for output layers, which were further divided by the square root
633   of the number of residual connections. Token embeddings and positional embeddings were trainable
634   and randomly initialized following N (0, 1).
635
636   Optimization For optimization, we used Adam with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. We linearly
637
      warmed up the learning rate within the first 50 iterations. Without data leakage, we performed a
      learning rate sweep in {1 × 10−5 , 5 × 10−5 , 1 × 10−4 , 5 × 10−4 , 1 × 10−3 }, and found 5 × 10−4
638
      to be optimal for Transformers and 1 × 10−3 to be optimal for CausalRNs. To ensure a meaningful
639
      comparison of convergence speed, we chose 5 × 10−4 since this was the minimum of both. We note
640   that this choice was optimal for Transformers and suboptimal for CausalRNs.
641
642   Training We trained all models on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU using a batch size of 320 for a
643   maximum of 2000 iterations. Each training run can be completed within one and a half hours.
644
645   Evaluation For each iteration step, we calculated the cross-entropy loss and average accuracy
646   from 320 online samples to evaluate the models. The accuracy was computed in parallel, not through
647   autoregressive decoding. We note that a 100% parallel accuracy necessarily implies a 100% autore-
      gressive accuracy. Both CausalRNs and Transformers eventually achieves 100% parallel accuracy.
