009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in domain adaptation have shown promise in transferring knowl-
012            edge across domains characterized by a continuous value or vector, such as varying
013            patient ages, where “age” serves as a continuous index. However, these approaches
014            often fail when spurious features shift continuously along with the domain index.
015            This paper introduces the first method designed to withstand the continuous shift-
016            ing of spurious features during domain adaptation. Our method enhances domain
017            adaptation performance by aligning causally transportable encodings across con-
018
               tinuously indexed domains. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that our approach
               more effectively ensures causal transportability across different domains. Empirical
019
               results, from both semi-synthetic and real-world medical datasets, indicate that our
020
               method outperforms state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Machine learning typically presumes that the training and test data comes from identical distribu-
026
      tions, hoping that the trained model effectively generalizes to the test environment. However, this
027   presumption breaks when the training and testing occur across different domains (e.g., with distinct
028   source and target domains). Domain adaptation (DA) effectively addresses this challenge by utilizing
029   labeled data from the source domain along with either unlabeled or minimally labeled data from the
030   target domain, thereby improving model performance (Ben-David et al., 2010; Ganin et al., 2016;
031   Tzeng et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019).
032
      Continuously indexed domain adaptation (CIDA) (Wang et al., 2020) generalizes typical DA, which
033   focuses on discrete domains (transferring from dataset A to B), to DA across continuously indexed
034   domains, where domain shift is characterized by a continuous index such as time and location. For
035   instance, in healthcare, CIDA can be instrumental in transferring knowledge across patient data
036   that varies with age. As patients age, their physiological parameters and response to treatments can
037   change subtly; in response, CIDA aims to train a model that can adapt to these continuous shifts.
038   Unfortunately, previous DA methods (Ganin et al., 2016; Tzeng et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019),
039   including CIDA (Wang et al., 2020), often fail when spurious features are continuously shifting.
040   Example 1 (Continuously Shifting Spurious Features in Sleep Studies). Suppose one trains a
041   model to take as input a time series of breathing signal x ∈ RT to predict the corresponding sleep
042   stage as y pred ∈ {‘Awake’, ‘Light Sleep’, ‘Deep Sleep’, ‘REM Sleep’}. Here ‘respiratory rate’ is a
043   typical spurious feature. If one trains the model using data from young subjects (with age 20 ∼ 30),
044   it will learn that ‘higher respiratory rates’ often correspond to ‘REM Sleep’. However, as subjects
045
      continuously age, they may have a slower respiratory rate in breathing patterns. Therefore the
      spurious feature ‘respiratory rate’ no longer works when predicting sleep stages for older subjects,
046
      and the older the subject is, the lower the model accuracy.
047
048   Our analysis shows that either alignment-based methods (Ganin et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2020)
049   or causality-inspired methods (Mao et al., 2022) alone do not solve the problem. Alignment-
050   based methods tend to align spurious features rather than causal features, therefore often fail to
051   generalize across continuously indexed domains with continuously shifting spurious features. On the
052   other hand, causality-inspired methods aiming to learn causal features (encodings) may collapse to
053   non-causal (association-based) methods (see Sec. 3 for detailed analysis), therefore also failing to
      generalize. Motivated by such analysis, we then propose to jointly (1) infer causally transportable

078   2     R ELATED W ORK
079
080   Typical Domain Adaptation. Domain Adaptation has long been studied (Farahani et al., 2021;
081   Csurka, 2017; Ben-David et al., 2010; Peng et al., 2019; Prabhu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Xu
082   et al., 2022) to promote model’s generalization ability on unseen domains, with unlabeled or a
083   limited amount of labeled data. Traditional methods include importance weighting (Shimodaira,
084   2000; Gretton et al., 2009; Lipton et al., 2018), self-training (Zou et al., 2018; Kumar et al., 2020;
085   Prabhu et al., 2021), distribution matching (Pan et al., 2010; Tzeng et al., 2014; Sun & Saenko, 2016;
086   Peng et al., 2019; Nguyen-Meidine et al., 2021) and adversarial-based training (Ganin et al., 2016;
087
      Zhang et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023). Typically, these methods
      focus on categorical domains, where an one-hot vector indicates which domain a data point comes
088
      from. In contrast, CIDA (Wang et al., 2020) extends to continuous domains, with a continuous
089
      variable specifying each data point’s domain. CIDA seeks to align encodings of different domain
090   based on this continuous identification. However, during the alignment, CIDA struggles to eliminate
091   the influence of continuously shifting spurious features, leading to poor performance (see empirical
092   results in Sec. 5). In contrast, our method successfully mitigates the impact of such spurious features
093   by inferring and aligning causally transportable representations.
094
      Causality-Inspired Domain Adaptation. Causal inference is a powerful method for modeling
095
      knowledge transfer (Bareinboim & Pearl, 2014; 2013; 2016; Bühlmann, 2020; Correa & Bareinboim,
096   2019; 2020; Magliacane et al., 2018; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018) and ensuring structure invariance
097   (Pearl et al., 2000). Various studies have explored its application to eliminate spurious features and
098   improve domain adaptation performance (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Mahajan et al., 2021; Mao et al.,
099   2021; Yue et al., 2021). For instance, VOOD (Mao et al., 2022) aims to estimate invariant causal
100   effect across different environments, hoping to improve domain generalization (DG) performance.
101   However, these causal methods (1) are still subject to covariate shift, often compromising the causal
102   model’s identifiability (Pearl et al., 2000), (2) can easily degenerate to association-based method, and
103   (3) are not designed for continuously indexed domains. In contrast, our CADA effectively addresses
104
      these problem via joint latent-space distribution alignment and causal inference.
105   Comparison of Representative Methods. Table 1 summarizes the differences between our CADA
106   and representative previous methods in terms of whether they (1) are designed for continuously
107   indexed domains, (2) are causal/robust against spurious features, (3) can naturally handle multiple
      source/target domains, (4) are robust against covariate shift, and (5) are designed for DA or DG.

      3.1    A C AUSAL V IEW ON C ONTINUOUSLY I NDEXED D OMAIN A DAPTATION

129   Structural Causal Model. We use the Structural Causal Model (SCM) in Fig. 1 to describe the
130   underlying generative process of the random variables the data X and their labels Y . In addition to
131   the observed variables X and Y , this SCM also involves the following variables:
132
          • R, which is a vector encoding both causal and spurious features of the input data X,
133
          • V , which is the input’s causal factors extracted from the representation R,
134
          • U , which is the unobserved noise and spurious features from external sources, and
135       • K, which is the continuous domain index.
136
137   U and K together affect both the generation of input X and the extraction of causal factors V from
138
      R; in other words, U and K guide the process of eliminating spurious features from R. Note that in
      the context of causal inference, K can be considered as a continuous version of the transportability
139
      node (Pearl & Bareinboim, 2011); it points to variables whose generation process differs for different
140
      domains.
141
142   Following Example 1, below we provide an example of using the SCM in Fig. 1 for sleep studies
143   across continuously indexed domains (patients of different ages).
144    Example 2 (Causal Model for Sleep Studies). In sleep studies (Zhao et al., 2017), one typical
145    task is to estimate the sleep stage Y (e.g., “Awake” and “Deep Sleep”) from a patient’s breathing
146    signal X; here “age” is a domain index K; patients of different ages belong to different domains.
147    One robust way to predict Y is to use breathing patterns such as periodicity (periodic breathing
148    signals usually indicate “Deep Sleep”); these patterns are encoded as causal representations V
149
       in our CADA. The unobserved spurious features U in sleep studies may include ‘respiratory rate’
       mentioned in Example 1. Breathing signals X from older patients, i.e., larger K, may have a lower
150
      ‘respiratory rate’ due to weakened respiratory muscles; therefore it is not a reliable feature for
151
       predicting Y . A neural network can extract the compact representation R given the raw breathing
152    signal X as input, but R still inherits the spurious features U from X; therefore directly using R
153    to predict Y would not generalize across domains (different K), as in Example 1. To address this
154    problem, we would like to remove the spurious features U from R according to K, thereby obtaining
155    the causal factors/representations V . We can then train a generalizable (and causally transportable)
156    model to predict sleep stages Y using the causal representations V .

308   5    E XPERIMENTS
309
310   We evaluated our method, CADA, using a semi-synthetic image dataset, Continuous Colored-
311   MNIST (C2MNIST), along with two real-world medical datasets, Sleep Heart Health Study (SHHS)
312   (Quan et al., 1998) and Multi-Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA) (Chen et al., 2014), where
313   continuously shifting spurious features are introduced. These empirical studies corroborate the
314   theoretical discoveries outlined earlier and demonstrate the following:
315          • Using categorical domain adaptation to align continuously indexed domains with continu-
316            ously shifting spurious features leads to suboptimal alignment and performance.
317          • Continuously indexed domain adaptation methods alone tend to align shifting spurious
318            features rather than causal features, and are therefore not effective for domain adaptation
319            with continuously shifting spurious features.
320          • Causality-based domain adaptation methods alone suffer from covariant shift across domains
321            and are not effective in adaptation across continuously indexed domains.
322          • Our CADA successfully infer and align causal representations from continuously indexed
323
               domains even in the presence of continuously shifting spurious features, thereby significantly
               improving performance.

357   5.1   BASELINES AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
358
      We compare CADA with state-of-the-art methods in domain adaptation and causal transportation
359
      baselines, including Continuous Unsupervised Adaptation (CUA) (Bobu et al., 2018), Adversarial Dis-
360   criminative Domain Adaptation (ADDA) (Tzeng et al., 2017), Domain Adversarial Neural Network
361   (DANN) (Ganin et al., 2016), Conditional Domain Adversarial Neural Network (CDANN) (Zhao
362   et al., 2017), Margin Disparity Discrepancy (MDD) (Zhang et al., 2019), Continuously Indexed
363   Domain Adaptation (CIDA) (Wang et al., 2020), and Visual Out-of-Distribution Generalization
364   (VOOD) (Mao et al., 2022).
365
      Since the majority of the baselines are not designed for continuously indexed domains, we made
366   slight generalizations to accommodate these baselines. Specifically, for ADDA, MDD and VOOD,
367   data with different domain indices are merged into one source and one target domain; for DANN,
368   CDANN and CUA, the continuous domain spectrum is discretized into multiple disjoint domains,
369   enabling adaptation between multiple source and target domains. In the case of CUA, the model
370   adapts from the source domains to each target domain individually, progressing from the closest
371   target to the farthest one. All methods are implemented in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) with the
372   same neural network architectures and inputs (x and domain index k) for fair comparison.

      5.3   H EALTHCARE DATASETS
477
478
      Dataset Description. We use two medical datasets, SHHS and MESA to evaluate different methods.
479
      Both datasets contain records of subjects’ full-night breathing signals and corresponding sleep stage
480   labels for every 30-second segment. Sleep stage labels include “Awake”, “Light Sleep N1”, “Light
481   Sleep N2”, “Deep Sleep”, and “Rapid Eye Movement (REM)”. Given a breathing signal segment x,
482   a common task in sleep studies is to predict the sleep stage label y. While prior studies may consider
483   ’Light Sleep N1’ and ’Light Sleep N2’ to be a single stage (Zhao et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020),
484   we maintain a 5-class classification task in this paper. Among all the subjects’ information, age can
485   serve as a natural domain index. The age range of subjects used is [44, 90] and [54, 92] for SHHS and
      MESA, respectively. Our SHHS and MESA datasets contain continuously shifting spurious noise to


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      simulate the potential increase in noise due to age-related lower-quality sleeping, breathing disorder
487   (e.g., sleep apnea), etc.
488
489   Accuracy. Table 3 and Table 4 show the accuracy for different methods on SHHS and MESA,
490
      respectively. The target domains of both datasets are divided into 7 parts for evaluation based on the
      distance from the source domains. One observation is that VOOD, as a causal tranporation method,
491
      shows negative improvement compared to Source-Only (e.g., training the model on source domains
492
      and directly use it on target domains without adaptation). While methods that directly use categorical
493   domain adaptation perform poorly in the normal continuously indexed domain adaptation setting,
494   CUA shows better performance than other baselines in our setting. On the other hand, our CADA
495   avoids the influence of continuously shifting spurious correlation, demonstrating stable and good
496   performance in both datasets.

      6   C ONCLUSION
499
500
      In this paper, we identify the problem of continuous spurious shift and propose continuously trans-
501
      portable domain adaptation (CADA) as the first general DA method to address this problem. Our
502   theoretical analysis shows that our CADA can better ensure causal transportability across continu-
503   ously indexed domains. Empirical results on both semi-synthetic and real-world medical datasets
504   show that our method outperforms the state-of-the-art DA methods in the face of continuous spurious
505   shift. Interesting future work includes extending the proposed method to multi-dimensional domain
506   indices, more complex shifting spurious features, and other applications beyond healthcare.
