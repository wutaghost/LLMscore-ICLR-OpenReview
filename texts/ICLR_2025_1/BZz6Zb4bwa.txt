009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) plays a key role in training deep learning mod-
012            els, yet its ability to implicitly regularize and enhance generalization remains an
013            open theoretical question. We apply Large Deviation Theory (LDT) to analyze why
014            SGD selects models with strong generalization properties. We show that the gener-
015
               alization error jointly depends on the level of concentration of its empirical loss
               around its expected value and the abnormality of the random deviations stemming
016
               from the stochastic nature of the training data observation process. Our analysis
017
               reveals that SGD gradients are inherently biased toward models exhibiting more
018            concentrated losses and less abnormal and smaller random deviations. These theo-
019            retical insights are empirically validated using deep convolutional neural networks,
020            confirming that mini-batch training acts as a natural regularizer by preventing
021            convergence to models with high generalization errors.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) has become a crucial tool in modern deep learning, driving the
027
      training of models that power today’s AI applications (Bottou, 2010). In addition to being an efficient
      optimization algorithm, SGD plays a vital role in shaping the generalization performance of models,
028
      particularly in overparameterized systems where many solutions can perfectly fit the training data
029
      (Zhang et al., 2017). Remarkably, SGD exhibits an implicit bias toward solutions that generalize well
030   to unseen data, an intriguing phenomenon that has captured the attention of researchers.
031
032   A key reason for this implicit bias is the inherent noise from the stochastic nature of gradient updates
033
      in SGD (Neyshabur et al., 2015b; Zou et al., 2021). This noise directs the optimization process
      toward flat minima —solutions that are less sensitive to data perturbations— resulting in models with
034
      robust generalization (Keskar et al., 2016). Researchers have linked this behavior to a preference for
035
      simpler, lower-complexity solutions, indicating that SGD’s stochasticity acts as an implicit regularizer
036   (Neyshabur et al., 2015b; Hardt et al., 2016; Zou et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2023). Recent studies show
037   that, even in simple models like linear regression, SGD’s implicit regularization can outperform
038   explicit methods like ridge regression, especially in overparameterized settings (Zou et al., 2021).
039   These insights underscore the importance of algorithmic regularization in deep learning, yet there is
040   still a pressing need for new perspectives and explanations to unravel the relationship between SGD,
041   noise, and generalization. The exact nature of this phenomenon remains one of the most compelling
042   open questions in theoretical machine learning (Ghorbani et al., 2019).
043   We present a novel theoretical analysis of SGD’s implicit bias using principles from Large Deviation
044   Theory (LDT) (Ellis, 2006; Touchette, 2009). We introduce a new decomposition of the generalization
045   error based on the rate function, showing that it depends on the concentration of empirical loss around
046   its expected value and the magnitude of random deviations from the stochastic training data. This
047   decomposition breaks the gradient of the training loss into three terms: (i) biases the algorithm
048   toward models with lower expected loss, (ii) favors less concentrated empirical losses, and (iii)
049   promotes models with larger random deviations. We show that small mini-batches prevent SGD from
050
      converging to models with large generalization error.
051   These findings provide a new perspective on SGD and suggest ways to improve optimization.
052   Specifically, we show that SGD does not need to follow every mini-batch’s gradients to achieve low
053   generalization error. By discarding mini-batches that contribute little information, we can achieve
      more efficient solutions with better generalization, paving the way for enhanced performance in SGD.

      2    P RELIMINARIES

056   In this work, we build on the idea that the empirical loss of each model in a given class behaves
057   as a random variable with a distinct mean and varying concentration around that mean. A dataset
058   induces a realization of this random variable. Formally, let D represent a training dataset of size
059   n > 0, generated i.i.d. from an unknown distribution ν(y, x). The model class is parameterized by
060   θ ∈ Θ, and for each model θ, its loss function ℓ(y, x, θ) is assumed to be positive.P  The expected loss
                                                                                              n
061   is L(θ) = Eν [ℓ(y, x, θ)], while the empirical loss on dataset D is L̂(D, θ) = n1 i=1 ℓ(y i , xi , θ).
062   The empirical loss L̂(D, θ) behaves as a random variable L̂n (θ), as it is derived from the randomly
063   sampled dataset D ∼ ν n . The realized value of L̂n (θ) when the dataset D is observed is denoted as
064   L̂(D, θ). Each model’s empirical loss L̂n (θ) has mean equal to L(θ), but the degree of concentration
065   around this mean varies. Figure 1 (left) illustrates this with histograms for three InceptionV3 models
066   (Szegedy et al., 2016), using datasets of size n = 50, produced using methods from Masegosa and
067
      Ortega (2024). The histograms show that concentration varies: the Initial model (using Kaiming or
      He initialization (He et al., 2015)) is highly concentrated around its mean L(θ) = ln 10, while the
068
      ℓ2 -regularized model also has greater concentration compared to the Standard model.
069
070   Empirical risk minimization seeks to find a model θ that minimizes the realized empirical loss,
071   minθ L̂(D, θ). The main challenge is to choose models whose empirical loss is close to the expected
072   loss L(θ), ensuring a small generalization error, defined as the difference between L̂(D, θ) and L(θ).
073   This work demonstrates that generalization error is influenced by two key factors: (i) the level of
074   concentration of the random variable L̂n (θ); a small empirical loss L̂(D, θ) could result from a
075
      model with a high expected loss L(θ) but poor concentration, which is undesirable since such models
076
      generalize poorly. It could also come from a model with a well-concentrated L̂n (θ) and a lower mean
      L(θ), the desired outcome. (ii) the level of abnormality of the generalization error, which refers to
077
      the possibility that a small empirical loss may be due to an unlikely, abnormal occurrence from the
078
      left tail of L̂n (θ), irrespective of the concentration level.
079

140   In this section, we introduce a novel decomposition of a model’s generalization error, formalize
141   the concept of abnormality in the generalization error, and demonstrate how (full-batch) Gradient
142   Descent (GD) is biased toward finding models with poorly concentrated empirical losses and whose

300   4        T HE I MPLICIT B IAS OF S TOCHASTIC G RADIENT D ESCENT (SGD)
301
302   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) seeks to minimize L̂(D, θ) by following the gradients of
303   L̂(Bt , θ t ), where Bt represents the mini-batch processed by SGD at iteration t. When batch sizes
304   are large, the gradients of L̂(D, θ) and L̂(Bt , θ t ) are closely aligned. However, as the batch size
305   decreases, this alignment can deteriorate significantly. This effect is empirically illustrated by the
306   green dots of Figure 3, these green dots display the cosine similarity between ∇θ L̂(Bt , θ t ) and
307   ∇θ L̂(D, θ t ) computed for trained InceptionV3 models with different batch sizes. With large batch
308   sizes, the gradients of L̂(D, θ t ) and L̂(Bt , θ t ) are strongly aligned. However, for smaller batch sizes
309   (those typically used in machine learning), the misalignment is much higher.
310
      This misalignment between the gradients of L̂(D, θ t ) and L̂(Bt , θ t ) is the effect of the so-called
311   gradient noise introduced by SGD (Keskar et al., 2017; Jastrzebski et al., 2017). This gradient noise
312   is known to be the key factor behind the superior generalization performance of models trained
313   with SGD compared to those trained with GD. Although both SGD and GD converge to neural
314   networks that minimize and interpolate the training data (i.e., L̂(D, θ) ≈ 0), the minima found by
315   SGD typically result in better generalization error (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997). Figure 2
316   (left) illustrates this widely recognized effect in the literature.

      To further validate our results, we conducted an experiment in Figure 5 where we applied SGD
474   optimization but discarded batches with α(Bt , θ t ) values deemed large. We used Theorem 4

479   is likely to be poor, as the term KL (ν | γ), which controls this similarity, would be large according to
480   Theorem 7. Therefore, in such cases, it’s more effective to skip the batch, avoid following its gradient,
481   wait for the next batch and repeats the procedure.
482   Figure 5 supports our theoretical analysis. The reduction in the level of abnormality leads to effects
483   analogous to those seen when reducing the batch size, as when transitioning from GD to SGD. This
484   corroborates the idea that by reducing the level of abnormality, we can biased the optimizer towards
485   models with smaller generalization error. This experiment should not be interpreted as a novel
      training approach because to compute α(Bt , θ t ), we are using the test set to approximate ν.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      The generalization capabilities of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) have been extensively studied,
489   with various theories proposed to explain why SGD often outperforms deterministic optimization
490   methods in terms of generalization. A prominent line of research attributes this phenomenon to the
491   tendency of SGD to converge to flat minima in the loss landscape. Hochreiter and Schmidhuber
492   (1997) first introduced the concept of flat minima, suggesting that solutions located in wide, flat
493   regions of the loss surface generalize better to unseen data. This idea has been further explored by
494   many other works (Keskar et al., 2016), who observed that small-batch SGD gravitates towards flatter
495   minima, while large-batch training tends to find sharp minima associated with poorer generalization.
496   Although our work focuses on the concentration properties of the empirical loss rather than the
497
      geometry of the loss landscape, our main hypothesis is that the connection between these two lines of
      research is that flatter minima correspond to models which are more concentrated and/or with less
498
      abnormal generalization error.
499
500   Another perspective considers the implicit regularization effect of SGD. Neyshabur et al. (2015a)
501   proposed that SGD biases models towards solutions with smaller norms, aligning with capacity
502   control theories that relate model complexity to generalization. This implicit norm minimization
503
      effect has been linked to the generalization performance of deep neural networks, as networks
      with smaller weights are thought to be less prone to overfitting (Bartlett et al., 2017). The work
504
      of Masegosa and Ortega (2024) would establish a link between these works and our work, as it
505
      establishes that models with smaller norms exhibit greater concentration in their empirical losses.
506
507   Works using concentration bounds to understand SGD build on the same conceptualization by treating
508   the empirical loss of each model as a random variable (Kawaguchi et al., 2017; Bartlett et al., 2017;
509
      Neyshabur et al., 2017; Golowich et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2019). However, these works typically
      rely on upper bounds, which are often known to be vacuous or overly loose in deep neural networks
510
      (Nagarajan and Kolter, 2019; Gastpar et al., 2024). Moreover, they generally do not account for the
511
      individual concentration properties of each model in the hypothesis space, potentially overlooking
512   critical nuances in how different models generalize (Casado et al., 2024). In contrast, our work
513   leverages a fundamental equality that directly decomposes the training loss into distinct components,
514   providing a more nuanced and detailed analysis that goes beyond the limitations of traditional
515   concentration bounds.
516
517
      6    C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
518
519
      In this work, we have presented a novel theoretical analysis of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
520
      using principles from Large Deviation Theory (LDT). Our findings reveal that the generalization
521   error in SGD can be decomposed into components influenced by the expected loss, the concentration
522   of the empirical loss, and the level of abnormal deviations from the expected value.
523
524
      Our analysis reveals that the primary effect of gradient noise in SGD is to limit the exploration of
      models where the empirical loss deviates substantially from the expected loss. We show that this
525
      effect ensures that SGD tends to visit models where the empirical gradients closely align with the
526
      expected gradients, resulting in a more effective reduction of the expected loss and, consequently,
527   leading to models with lower generalization error.
528
529   While this work offers valuable theoretical insights into the implicit regularization effects of SGD,
530
      there are several limitations that need to be addressed. Firstly, although we introduced the concept of
      SKIP-SGD, we did not provide a fully developed alternative to standard SGD. We believe, however,
531
      that this approach could be transformed into a viable optimization technique by using an independent
532
      validation dataset to compute α(Bt , θ t ), potentially paving the way for entirely new variations of
533   SGD. Secondly, our empirical findings were limited to a specific set of models, and it is crucial to
534   validate these results across a broader range of architectures and tasks to ensure their generalizability.
535   Lastly, the role of explicit regularization methods, such as ℓ2 regularization or the use of invariant
536   architectures, should be investigated within this framework to better understand how they interact
537   with and influence the implicit biases of SGD.
