011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) simultaneously facilitate the generation and de-
014            tection of harmful text. Leading LLM developers, such as OpenAI, Meta, and
015
               Google, are driving a paradigm shift in the detection of harmful text, moving
               from conventional detectors to fine-tuned LLMs. However, these newly released
016
               models, which require substantial computational and data resources, have not yet
017
               been thoroughly investigated for their effectiveness in this new paradigm. In this
018            work, we propose JudgeRail, a novel and generic framework that guides open-
019            source LLMs to adhere to judicial principles during text moderation.Additionally,
020            we introduce a new logit rectification method that can extract an LLM’s classi-
021            fication intent, effectively controls its output format, and accelerates detection.
022            By integrating several top-performing open-source LLMs into JudgeRail with-
023            out any fine-tuning and evaluating them against OpenAI Moderation API, Lla-
024            maGuard3, ShieldGemma, and other conventional moderation solutions across
025            various datasets, including those specifically designed for jailbreaking LLMs, we
026
               demonstrate that JudgeRail can adapt these LLMs to be competitive with fine-
               tuned moderation models and significantly outperform conventional solutions.
027
               Moreover, we evaluate all models for detection latency, a critical yet rarely ex-
028
               amined practical aspect, and show that LLMs with JudgeRail require only 46% to
029            55% of the time needed by LlamaGuard3 and ShieldGemma. The generic nature
030            and competitive performance of JudgeRail highlight its potential for promoting
031            the practicality of LLM-based harmful text detectors. Warning: some text ex-
032            amples presented in this paper may be offensive to some readers.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Harmful text exhibits the inherent flexibility of natural language, making its detection an enduring
038   research challenge and a critical practical concern. As large language models (LLMs)(Llama Team,
039
      2024; Team et al., 2024; GLM et al., 2024) are rapidly evolving, they can be exploited to generate
      a wide array of harmful text, including discriminatory, obscene, and hateful content(Lees et al.,
040
      2022). Beyond these harmful categories, jailbreaking LLMs has expanded the scope of harmful text
041
      to encompass more severe categories such as crime planning, self-harm, and defamation(Inan et al.,
042   2023; Meta, 2024). Furthermore, the jailbreak prompts used to tame LLMs for generating harmful
043   content, are themselves emerging as a distinct and concerning category of harmful text.
044
045
      These threats have not been adequately addressed by conventional text moderation solutions(Lees
      et al., 2022; Hartvigsen et al., 2022). For example, we have investigated Perspective API, a commer-
046
      cial tool primarily designed for detecting harmful text, and have listed the harmful content it covers
047
      in Table 1. Some aforementioned newly identified harmful text clearly fall outside these categories.
048   On the other hand, as detailed in the bottom of Table 1, the latest OpenAI Moderation API moderates
049   a broader range of harmful text, with a particular focus on malicious instructions designed to jail-
050   break LLMs. LlamaGuard models (Inan et al., 2023) further expand detection capabilities to include
051   a more fine-grained taxonomy of harmful content. However, according to public reports(Lees et al.,
052   2022; Markov et al., 2023), Perspective API requires a large-scale proprietary corpus to train its
053   toxic content classifiers. Similarly, the OpenAI Moderation API takes approximately 220K training
      samples, along with sophisticated data augmentation and label quality control mechanisms. Given

065   the scarcity of newly identified harmful text across various categories and the daily emergence of
066   unknown risks, it has become increasingly evident that training and updating specific moderation
067   models to generalize across implicit and novel threats is unsustainable.
068   Recently, leading developers of LLMs, such as Meta and Google, have released moderation models
069   supported by their respective LLMs. Meta’s LlamaGuard series (Llama Team, 2024) and Google’s
070   ShieldGemma models (Zeng et al., 2024) have demonstrated improved capabilities for detecting
071   both common toxic speech and jailbreak prompts. This signals a paradigm shift towards detecting
072   harmful text using fine-tuned LLMs. Nevertheless, it does not alleviate the demand for large-scale
073   training data and substantial computational resources. For example, building ShieldGemma (Zeng
074
      et al., 2024) requires more than 130K samples. Additionally, fine-tuning even a moderately sized 7B
      model takes four A100 GPUs and approximately five hours of compute time (Han et al., 2024). This
075
      implies that the landscape of text moderation is increasingly dominated by organizations with sub-
076
      stantial computation and data resources. Consequently, it can be challenging for users to customize
077   these tools to prioritize certain harmful categories, such as discrimination or politically sensitive
078   content, without relying on the tool providers to adjust their moderation capabilities.
079
080
      Given that general-purpose LLMs are both the source and target of text content risks, they inherently
      possess the capability to recognize harmful text. More importantly, LLMs can infer the harmful cat-
081
      egory to which a piece of text belongs, making them well-suited for moderation when provided
082
      with a well-designed harmful taxonomy. Using these ideas, we propose JudgeRail, a generic and
083   efficient framework that leverages top-performing open-source LLMs to act as a judge, adhering to
084   the presumption of innocence principle in moderating harmful text. However, the outputs of LLMs
085   are often difficult to control, typically requiring complex data parsing or even multi-round process-
086   ing. This significantly increases detection latency and diminishes their practicality as detectors.
087   To efficiently extract valid outputs that align with a pre-determined taxonomy, we have designed a
088   logit rectification method that enables effective control over the LLM’s output while simultaneously
089   accelerating the detection process in JudgeRail. Notably, detection latency, a critical aspect for prac-
090   tical text moderation, has been rarely discussed in existing literature, yet it is essential for building
091
      effective LLM guardrails (Rebedea et al., 2023; Guardrail, 2024).
092   We evaluate JudgeRail with various open-source LLMs on diverse harmful text detection datasets
093   comprising over 34K samples, and compare LLMs equipped with JudgeRail to different BERT-
094   based detection models, commercial moderation tools, and specialized moderation LLMs. The re-
095   sults show that LLMs with JudgeRail significantly outperform conventional moderation solutions
096   while remaining on par with LLM-based moderation tools. Moreover, LLMs with JudgeRail re-
097
      quire only 46% to 55% of the time needed by LlamaGuard3 and ShieldGemma. We also evaluated
      4-bit versions of all LLMs, for the first time, revealing that these models consume significantly less
098
      memory with negligible performance degradation. These findings contrast with studies Li et al.
099
      (2024); Gong et al. (2024) that have demonstrated the crucial role of model precision in generative
100   tasks. This differing impact leads us to wonder that decision-making moderation tasks may have
101   distinct requirements for model precision. Our results shed light on a new paradigm for efficiently
102   developing practical text moderation tools that can evolve along with open-source LLMs.
103
      We summarize our contributions below.
104
105   • We design a simple yet effective JudgeRail framework for adapting general LLMs to detect harm-
106     ful text. We thoroughly investigate the impact of different safety taxonomies on detection perfor-
107     mance, emphasizing the importance of well-defined label systems and validated labels, as well as
        the utility of in-context few-shot calibration.

      • We propose a novel logit rectification method that ensures valid output and significantly reduces
109     detection latency. This method can be readily adapted to various classification tasks using LLMs.
110   • We comprehensively evaluate and compare various LLMs using JudgeRail with a wide spectrum
111     of moderation solutions, including BERT-based detectors, commercial tools like Perspective API
112     and OpenAI Moderation API, and specialized LLMs such as LlamaGuard and ShieldGemma.
113     Our results show that open-source LLMs equipped with JudgeRail achieve highly competitive

115     4-bit LLMs exhibit trivial performance degradation but increased latency, highlighting the need

      2.1   LLM- BASED M ODERATION M ODELS
122
      Recently, LLMs have been increasingly utilized for content moderation. For example, SplineLLM
123
      (Balestriero et al., 2024) introduces a method to extract a small set of latent features from LLMs that
124   characterize users’ prompts, which can then be used for detecting harmful text. RigorLLM (Yuan
125   et al., 2024) is a framework that combines an optimized safe suffix with a fine-tuned LLM and a
126   K-Nearest Neighbor algorithm. Leading LLM providers, such as Google and Meta, supported by a
127   substantial amount of meticulously constructed data, and significant computational resources, have
128   released their own LLM-based moderation tools, including ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024) and
129   the LlamaGuard series (Llama Team, 2024). These recent developments illustrate a trend where
130   LLMs are increasingly becoming the foundational models in the content moderation domain.
131
132   2.2   C ONVENTIONAL M ODERATION M ODELS
133
134   Prior to the widespread adoption of LLMs, the prevalent approaches to detecting harmful text in-
135   volved using extensive datasets to train classifiers based on pre-trained models such as BERT (Devlin
136   et al., 2019) and Transformer (Vaswani, 2017). For example, ToxRoberta (Hartvigsen et al., 2022)
137   has demonstrated effectiveness in detecting both explicit and implicit toxic language. It is fine-tuned
138
      from ToxDectRoBERTa (Zhou et al., 2021) using the TOXIGEN dataset. As an effective toxicity
      classifier, S-nlp(Logacheva et al., 2022) is a fine-tuned RoBERTa(Liu, 2019) model, trained on the
139
      English samples from three datasets provided by Jigsaw(cjadams et al., 2017a; 2019; Kivlichan et al.,
140
      2020). In addition, several commercial moderation APIs have been developed, including Perspective
141   API and OpenAI Moderation API. Specifically, Perspective API (Lees et al., 2022) utilizes a sin-
142   gle compact pre-trained Charformer-based Transformer (Tay et al., 2022) to identify six categories
143   of toxic speech. OpenAI Moderation API (Markov et al., 2023) is built on a sophisticated system
144   designed to capture rare harmful content, incorporating a holistic approach that includes content
145   taxonomies, labeling instructions, data quality control, and an active learning pipeline. Moreover,
146   Some studies have attempted to design a court-like jury mechanism to assist with content modera-
147   tion, aiming to enhance the fairness and reliability of the evaluation process, including Jury Learning
148   (Gordon et al., 2022) and Digital Juries (Fan & Zhang, 2020). Jury Learning integrates dissenting
149
      voices by modeling individual annotators and allowing practitioners to define the jury composition.
      Digital Juries proposes a civics-oriented approach for adjudicating content moderation cases. De-
150
      spite their effectiveness in identifying explicit toxic text, these specialized models and tools have
151
      faced challenges in moderating more implicit and previously unseen forms of harmful content, such
152   as diverse jailbreak prompts (Shen et al., 2023).
153
154
155   3     M ETHODOLOGY
156
157   The role-play capability of LLMs can be viewed as a double-edged sword, as it can be exploited for
158   conducting jailbreak attacks (Shen et al., 2024), it also be harnessed for detecting harmful text and
159   countering jailbreak instructions. In the JudgeRail framework, we assign a judge role to integrated
160   LLMs, prompting them to adhere to the presumption of innocence judicial principle, thereby making
161   precise and fair judgments. In conjunction with this framework, we introduce a logit rectification
      method to extract valid detection results from the potentially random generated content.

174   3.1   J UDGE R AIL P ROMPTING F RAMEWORK
175
176   The JudgeRail prompting framework comprises three key components: character description, label
177   system, and calibration examples, as shown in Figure 1.
178
179   Character Description An LLM is assigned a judge character to determine whether an input text
180   is harmful and to classify it into specific harmful categories. Similar to real-world judicial practice,
181   we instruct the LLM to adhere to the presumption of innocence principle, which can be considered
182
      common knowledge, implying that there is no need for specific fine-tuning of the model to compre-
      hend and comply with this principle. Moreover, we employ the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) technique
183
      to guide the LLM in identifying explicit and concrete indicators of harmful content before classi-
184
      fying the text. This structured reasoning process ensures that the model makes informed decisions
185   based on clear evidence of harmful content. The complete prompt is provided in the appendix.
186
187
      Label System A label system with semantically distinct harmful categories is crucial for enabling
188   an LLM to make more precise detection. Unlike conventional supervised detection models, which
189   learn the correlation between input data and output labels, an LLM makes decisions by identifying
190   the semantic relationships between the input text and the output categories. We follow the label
191   systems of existing commercial moderation tools to design our own. Specifically, for toxic text, we
192   adopt the label system from either Perspective API (1st row in Table 1) or OpenAI Moderation API
193   (2nd row in Table 1). For jailbreak prompts, we have selected the label system from LlamaGuard3
194   (3rd row in Table 1), as the LlamaGuard model series have been shown to be effective in detecting
195   jailbreak prompts (Inan et al., 2023). We assign a unique character symbol to each category, includ-
196
      ing both harmful and non-harmful ones. This allows for the design of a simple character-matching
      mechanism, which guides the LLM to act similarly to a classifier and facilitates the parsing of out-
197
      put results. For example, we assign numerical labels such as 0, 1, 2, and so forth, to categories
198
      “Not Harmful”, “Toxicity”, “Severe Toxicity”, etc., as defined by Perspective API. The label system
199   serves as a “soft” guide to encourage LLMs to adhere to the desired symbolic output format.
200
201   Calibration Examples The in-context learning capabilities of LLMs enable a significant boost
202   in their generation quality with just a few relevant examples. During our initial exploration, we
203   observed that most LLMs tend to produce abnormally high false positive rates. To mitigate this
204   issue, we incorporate in-context examples of falsely classified text into our prompting framework,
205   thereby reminding the model to avoid overly strict classifications.
206
207   3.2   L OGIT R ECTIFICATION
208
209   The generative nature of LLMs and their conversational interaction style, reinforced by instruc-
210   tion tuning, make their output inherently prone to deviate from the symbolic labels specified in
211   the prompting framework. For example, in our experiments with the Llama3-8B-Instruct model
212
      (Llama Team, 2024), it frequently failed to directly generate classified symbols and instead pro-
      duced responses explicitly designed to reject harmful content. This behavior is likely a consequence
213
      of conservative safety alignment.
214
215   To extract valid classification symbols from an LLM’s potentially random output, we propose to
      rectify its output logits by nullifying out-of-domain logits. More formally, given an LLM M that


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      generates a sequence tokens x = [x1 , x2 , . . . , x|x| ] conditioned on a provided prompt p, the gen-
217
      eration process can be denoted as x ∼ M (· | p). Each token is selected by sampling from its
218   logit distribution. For example, selecting token x1 corresponds to x1 ∼ P (Softmax(l)), where
219   l = {l1 , l2 , . . . , l|V | } ∈ RV , and V denotes the vocabulary size of M . We only consider logit values
220   from a set S of indices of pre-determined symbolic tokens which corresponds to the selected label
221   system as shown in Table 4.3. This corresponds to applying a multi-dimensional rectification layer
222   N (·) to l, and outputting the selected logits as N (l) = {li∈S }, for i = 1, . . . , V . Thus, only the
223   logits in l that correspond to the tokens in S have their value preserved, while the rest are nullified
224   to zero. The remaining logits {li∈S } are then normalized to determine the final classification result.
225   This method is inspired by our hypothesis that an LLM following an instruction may have its incli-
226   nation toward a particular output embedded in the logits associated with its first output token. To
227   validate this hypothesis, we implemented a simplified prompt, where the model is asked to judge
228   whether a piece of text is harmful using an open-ended prompt such as “Give me your judgment
229   result” without specifying the output format. We randomly sampled 100 samples, consisting of 50
230   harmful and 50 harmless ones, from the dataset published by Zheng et al. (Zheng et al., 2024). We
231   then used the logits of the first output tokens of Gemma2-9B-IT (Team et al., 2024) across these 100
232
      samples to determine their harmfulness, following our logit rectification method. By comparing the
      results from the logit rectification with the ground truth labels, we found that this method resulted
233
      in only four false positives and achieved an accuracy rate of 96%. Similar results with 500 and 1000
234
      samples are presented in the appendix.
235
236   It is important to note that the benefits of adopting logit rectification are multi-dimensional. First,
237   it simplifies output processing, as the detection results are transformed to be deterministic. Second,
238
      since the processing latency of an LLM is directly proportional to the number of tokens it generates,
      logit rectification minimizes the token count, thereby accelerating the process. Last but not least, we
239
      consider this logit rectification method to be a generic approach for unveiling the implicit intentions
240
      of an LLM. It can be readily adapted to a variety of LLM-based classification tasks.

      5        L IMITATION
518
519
      To ensure fast detection, we use a relatively simple in-context learning mechanism, which may
520
      limit performance improvement. In future work, we will explore more complex mechanisms, such
521   as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Moreover, we have shown that existing label systems
522   have some degree of semantic ambiguity, which limits detection performance. This motivates our
523   future work on designing a more refined label system with better-separated semantic representations.
524   Additionally, note that the detection capabilities of JudgeRail essentially depend on its underlying
525   LLM. This indicates that one needs to perform model selection instead of using arbitrary LLMs.
526   However, JudgeRail also benefits from this as the underlying LLM evolves in its capability.

      6        C ONCLUSION
529
530
      This paper introduces the JudgeRail framework, which effectively and efficiently adapts open-source
531
      LLMs into harmful text detectors. We have thoroughly investigated the influence of the label system,
532
      in-context few-shot calibration examples, and a novel logit rectification method. We have evaluated
533   three open-source LLMs equipped with JudgeRail, as well as several LLM-based and conventional
534   moderation tools, on five datasets that encompass toxic speech data and jailbreak prompts. Our ex-
535   periments demonstrate that LLM-based models achieve significantly better performance than con-
536   ventional detectors, while LLMs with JudgeRail are competitive with fine-tuned moderation LLMs.
537   The results also show that LLMs with JudgeRail require approximately half the time needed by
538   LlamaGuard3 and ShieldGemma to process a sample on average.
