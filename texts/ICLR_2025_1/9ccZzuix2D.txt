                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            With the increasing size of datasets used for training neural networks, data pruning
010            has gained traction in recent years. However, most current data pruning algorithms
011            are limited in their ability to preserve accuracy compared to models trained on
012            the full data, especially in high pruning regimes. In this paper we explore the
013            application of data pruning while incorporating knowledge distillation (KD) when
014            training on a pruned subset. That is, rather than relying solely on ground-truth
015            labels, we also use the soft predictions from a teacher network pre-trained on
016            the complete data. By integrating KD into training, we demonstrate significant
017
               improvement across datasets, pruning methods, and on all pruning fractions. We
               first establish a theoretical motivation for employing self-distillation to improve
018
               training on pruned data. Then, we empirically make a compelling and highly
019
               practical observation: using KD, simple random pruning is comparable or superior
020            to sophisticated pruning methods across all pruning regimes. On ImageNet for
021            example, we achieve superior accuracy despite training on a random subset of only
022            50% of the data. Additionally, we demonstrate a crucial connection between the
023            pruning factor and the optimal knowledge distillation weight. This helps mitigate
024            the impact of samples with noisy labels and low-quality images retained by typical
025            pruning algorithms. Finally, we make an intriguing observation: when using lower
026            pruning fractions, larger teachers lead to accuracy degradation, while surprisingly,
027            employing teachers with a smaller capacity than the student’s may improve results.
028
               Our code will be made available.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recently, data pruning has gained increased interest in the literature due to the growing size of
033   datasets used for training neural networks. Algorithms for data pruning aim to retain the most
034   representative samples of a given dataset and enable the conservation of memory and reduction of
035   computational costs by allowing training on a compact and small subset of the original data. For
036   instance, data pruning can be useful for accelerating hyper-parameter optimization or neural archi-
037   tecture search (NAS) efforts. It may also be used in continual learning or active learning applications.
038   Existing methods for data pruning have shown remarkable success in achieving good accuracy while
039   retaining only a fraction, f < 1, of the original data; see for example (Toneva et al., 2018; Paul
040   et al., 2021; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Meding et al., 2021) and the overview in (Guo et al., 2022).
041   However, those approaches are still limited in their ability to match the accuracy levels obtained by
042   models trained on the complete dataset, especially in high compression regimes (low f ).
043   Score-based data pruning algorithms typically rely on the entire data to train neural networks for
044   selecting the most representative samples. The ‘forgetting’ method (Toneva et al., 2018) counts for
045   each sample the number of instances during training where the network’s prediction for that sample
046   shifts from “correct” to “misclassified”. Samples with high rates of forgetting events are assigned
047   higher scores as they are considered harder and more valuable for the training. The GraNd and
048   EL2N methods (Paul et al., 2021) compute a score for each sample based on the gradient norm
049   (GraNd) or the error L2-norm (EL2N) between the network’s prediction and the ground-truth label,
050
      respectively. The scores are computed and averaged over an ensemble of models trained on the full
      dataset. For each method, we note that once the sample scores are calculated, the models trained on
051
      the full dataset are discarded and are no longer in use.
052
053   In this paper, we explore the benefit of using a model trained on a complete dataset to enhance train-
      ing on a pruned subset of the data using knowledge distillation (KD). The motivation behind this

092   We experimentally demonstrate that incorporating the (soft) predictions provided by the teacher
093
      throughout the training process on the pruned data significantly and consistently improves accuracy
      across multiple datasets, various pruning algorithms, and all pruning fractions (see Fig. 1b for ex-
094
      ample). In particular, using KD, we can achieve comparable or even higher accuracy with only a
095
      small portion of the data (e.g., retaining 50% and 10% of the data for CIFAR-100 and SVHN, re-
096   spectively). Moreover, a dramatic improvement is achieved especially for small pruning fractions
097   (low f ). For example, on CIFAR-100 with pruning factor f = 0.1, accuracy improves by 17% (from
098   39.8% to 56.8%) using random pruning. On ImageNet with f = 0.1, the Top-5 accuracy increases
099   by 5% (from 82.37% to 87.19%) using random pruning, and by 20% (from 62.47% to 82.47%)
100   using EL2N. To explain these improvements, we provide theoretical motivation for integrating SD
101   when training on pruned data. Specifically, we show that using a teacher trained on the entire data
102   reduces the bias of the student’s estimation error.
103   In addition, we present several empirical key observations. First, our results demonstrate that simple
104   random pruning outperforms other sophisticated pruning algorithms in high pruning regimes (low
105   f ), both with and without knowledge distillation. Notably, prior research demonstrated this phe-
106   nomenon in the absence of KD (Sorscher et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022). Second, we demonstrate
107   a useful connection between the pruning factor f and the optimal weight of the KD loss. Gener-
      ally, utilizing data pruning algorithms to select high-scoring samples amplifies sensitivity to samples

      with noisy labels or low quality. This is because keeping the hardest samples increases the portion
109   of these samples as we retain a smaller data fraction. Based on this observation, we propose to
110   adapt the weight of the KD loss according to the pruning factor. That is, for low pruning factors,
111   we should increase the contribution of the KD term as the teacher’s soft predictions reflect possible

115   pruning regimes (low f ), the optimization becomes significantly more sensitive to the capacity gap
116   between the teacher and the student model. This relates to the well known capacity gap problem
117   (Mirzadeh et al., 2019). Interestingly, we find that for small pruning fractions, the student benefits
118   more from teachers with equal or even smaller capacities than its own, see Fig. 1c.

120
121          • Utilizing KD in data pruning, we find that training is robust to the choice of pruning mech-
122            anism at high pruning fractions. Notably, random pruning with KD achieves comparable
123            or superior accuracy compared to other sophisticated methods across all pruning regimes.
124          • We theoretically show, for the case of linear regression, that using a teacher trained on the
125            entire data reduces the bias of the student’s estimation error.
126
             • We demonstrate that by appropriately choosing the KD weight, one can mitigate the impact
127
               of label noise and low-quality samples that are retained by common pruning algorithms.
128
129
             • We make the striking observation that, for small pruning fractions, increasing the teacher
               size degrades accuracy, while, intriguingly, using teachers with smaller capacities than the
130
               student’s improves results.

133   2    R ELATED WORK
134
135   Data pruning. Data pruning, also known as coreset selection (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2019; Huggins
136   et al., 2016; Tolochinsky & Feldman, 2018), refers to methods aiming to reduce the dataset size for
137   training neural networks. Recent approaches have shown significant progress in retaining less data
138   while maintaining high classification accuracy (Toneva et al., 2018; Paul et al., 2021; Feldman &
139   Zhang, 2020; Meding et al., 2021; Chitta et al., 2019; Sorscher et al., 2022). In (Sorscher et al.,
140   2022), the authors showed theoretically and empirically that data pruning can improve the power
141
      law scaling of the dataset size by choosing an optimal pruning fraction as a function of the initial
      dataset size. Additionally, studies in (Sorscher et al., 2022; Ayed & Hayou, 2023) have demonstrated
142
      that existing pruning algorithms often underperform when compared to random pruning methods,
143
      especially in high pruning regimes. In (Zheng et al., 2022), the authors suggested a theoretical
144   explanation to this accuracy drop, and proposed a coverage-centric pruning approach which better
145   handles the data coverage. Also, in (Yang et al., 2022), the authors proposed to model the sample
146   selection procedure as a constrained discrete optimization problem. Recently, (Tan et al., 2023)
147   introduced an alternative pruning technique to the costly leave-one-out procedure, leveraging a first-
148   order approximation. This approach assigns higher scores to samples whose gradients consistently
149   align with the gradient expectations across all training stages.
150   Data pruning proves valuable at reducing memory and computational cost in various applications,
151   including tasks such as hyper-parameter search (Coleman et al., 2019), NAS (Dai et al., 2020),
152   continual and incremental learning (Lange et al., 2019), as well as active learning (Mirzasoleiman
153   et al., 2019; Chitta et al., 2019).
154
      Other related fields are dataset distillation and data-free knowledge distillation (DFKD). Dataset
155
      distillation approaches (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2023) aim to compress a given
156   dataset by synthesizing a small number of samples from the original data. The goal of DFKD is to
157   employ model compression in scenarios where the original dataset is inaccessible, for example, due
158   to privacy concerns. Common approaches for DFKD involve generating synthetic samples suitable
159   for KD (Luo et al., 2020; Yoo et al., 2019) or inverting the teacher’s information to reconstruct
160   synthetic inputs (Nayak et al., 2019; Yin et al., 2019). Recently, the works in (Cui et al., 2022; Yin
161   et al., 2023), utilized pseudo labels in training with dataset distillation. Unlike dataset distillation and
      DFKD, which include synthetic data generation, our work focuses on enhancing models trained on


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      pruned datasets created through sample selection, using KD. Moreover, this paper presents practical
163   and innovative findings for applying KD in data pruning.
164
165   Knowledge distillation. Knowledge distillation is a popular method aiming at distilling the knowl-
166
      edge from one network to another. It is often used to improve the accuracy of a small model using
      the guidance of a large teacher network (Bucila et al., 2006; Hinton et al., 2015). In recent years,
167
      numerous variants and extensions of KD have been developed. For example, (Zagoruyko & Ko-
168
      modakis, 2016; Romero et al., 2014) utilized feature activations from intermediate layers to transfer
169   knowledge across different representation levels. Other methods have proposed variants of KD crite-
170   ria (Yim et al., 2017; Huang & Wang, 2017; Kim et al., 2018; Ahn et al., 2019), as well as designing
171   objectives for representation distillation, as demonstrated in (Tian et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020).
172   More recently, several approaches have been introduced (Zhu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Huang
173   et al., 2022), pushing the boundaries of KD. Self-distillation (SD) refers to the case where the teacher
174   and student have identical architectures. It has been demonstrated that accuracy improvement can
175   be achieved using SD (Furlanello et al., 2018). Recently, theoretical findings were introduced for
176   self-distillation in the presence of label noise (Das & Sanghavi, 2023).
177   In our paper, we explore the process of distilling knowledge from a model trained on a large dataset
178   to a model trained on a pruned subset of the original data. We focus on self-distillation and present
179   several striking observations that emerge when integrating SD for data pruning.

183   Given a dataset D with N labeled samples {xi , yi }N i=1 , a data pruning algorithm A aims at selecting
184   a subset P ⊂ D of the most representative samples for training. We denote by f the pruning factor,
185   which represents the fraction of data to retain, calculated as f = Nf /N where Nf is the size of
186   the pruned dataset. Note that 0 < f < 1. Score-based algorithms assign a score to each sample,
187   representing its importance in the learning process. Let si be the score corresponding to a sample xi ,
188   sorting them in a descending order sk1 > sk2 , ..., > skN , following the sorting indices {k1 , ..., kN },
189   we obtain the pruned dataset by retaining the highest scoring samples, P = {xk1 , ..., xkNf }. Usu-
190   ally, score-based algorithms retain hard samples while excluding the easy ones. Note that in random
191   pruning, we simply sample the indices k1 , ..., kN uniformly. In this paper, given a pruning algorithm
192   A, our objective is to train a model on the pruned dataset P while maximizing accuracy.
193

196

200   the pruned data P. In this work, we investigate a training scheme which incorporates the soft
201   predictions of a teacher network, pre-trained on the full dataset, throughout training on the pruned

255   using knowledge distillation we can train a student model on the remaining available data while
256   maintaining a high level of accuracy.

      3.2   M ITIGATING NOISY SAMPLES IN PRUNED DATASETS
259
260   In general, hard samples are essential for the optimization process as they are located close to the
261   classification boundaries. However, retaining the hardest samples while excluding moderate and
262   easy ones increases the proportion of samples with noisy and ambiguous labels, or images with poor
263   quality. For example, in Fig. 3, we present the highest scoring images selected by the ‘forgetting’
264   pruning algorithm for CIFAR-100 and SVHN. As can be seen, in the majority of the images deter-
265   mining the class is non-trivial due to the complexity of the category (e.g., fine-grained classes) or
266
      due to poor quality. By using knowledge distillation the student can learn such label ambiguity and
      mitigate noisy labels.
267
268   In a recent work (Das & Sanghavi, 2023) it was demonstrated that the benefit of using a teacher’s
269   predictions increases with the degree of label noise. Consequently, it was found that more weight
      should be assigned to the KD term as the noise variance increases. Similarly, in our work we

365   4    E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS
366
367   In this section we provide empirical evidence for our method through extensive experimentation
368
      over a variety of datasets, an assortment of data pruning methods and across a wide range of pruning
      levels. Then, we also investigate how the KD weight, the teacher size and the KD method affect
369
      student performance under different pruning regimes.
370
371   Datasets. We perform experiments on four classification datasets: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
372   a) with 10 classes, consists of 50,000 training samples and 10,000 testing samples; SVHN (Netzer
373   et al., 2011) with 10 classes, consists of 73,257 training samples and 26,032 testing samples; CIFAR-
374
      100 (Krizhevsky et al., b) with 100 classes, consists of 50,000 training samples and 10,000 testing
      samples; and ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) with 1,000 classes, consists of 1.2M training
375
      samples and 50K testing samples.

397   4.1   T RAINING ON PRUNED DATA WITH KD
398
399   To demonstrate the advantage of incorporating KD-based supervision when training on pruned data,
400   we utilize the aforementioned data pruning methods on each dataset using a wide range of pruning
401
      factors. Then, we train models on the produced data subsets with and without KD. We note that in
      the presence of KD the respective teachers that are utilized are trained on the full datasets.
402
403   As can be observed in Figs. 4 and 5, the incorporation of KD into the training process consistently
404   enhances model accuracy across all of the tested scenarios, regardless of the tested dataset, pruning
405   method or pruning level. For example, compared to baseline models trained on the full datasets
406   without KD, utilizing KD can lead to comparable accuracy levels by retaining only small portions
407
      of the original datasets (e.g., 10%, 30%, 50% on SVHN, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, respectively,
      using ‘forgetting’). In fact, even on a large scale dataset as ImageNet, comparable accuracy can be
408
      achieved by randomly retaining just 30% of the data, while training on larger subsets remarkably
409
      results in superior accuracy to the baseline (e.g., +1.6% using a random subset of 70%).
410
411   Moreover, we note that the accuracy gains due to KD are most significant in high-compression
412   scenarios. For instance, on CIFAR-100 with f = 0.1, KD contributes to absolute accuracy improve-
413
      ments of 17%, 22.4%, 21%, and 19.7% across the random, ‘forgetting’, GraNd, and EL2N pruning
      methods, respectively. Similarly, on SVHN, which permits even stronger compression, improve-
414
      ments of the same order of magnitude can be observed at a lower pruning factor (f = 0.01).
415
416   These findings support the idea that the soft-predictions produced by a well-informed teacher contain
417   rich and valuable information that can greatly benefit a student in a limited-data setting. This ‘dark
418   knowledge’, notably absent in conventional one-hot labels, allows the student to deduce stronger
419
      generalizations from each available data sample, which in turn translates to better performance given
      the same training data.
420
421   Finally, two additional interesting patterns emerge from our experiments. First, in high-compression
422   scenarios (e.g., f ≤ 0.4 in CIFAR-100, f ≤ 0.08 in SVHN), it is evident that random pruning
423   surpasses all other methods in effectiveness, both with and without KD. This aligns with the notion
424   that aggressive pruning via score-based techniques retains larger concentrations of low quality or
425
      noisy samples due to mistaking them for challenging cases. This phenomenon was previously noted
      without KD in (Sorscher et al., 2022). Second, under low-compression conditions (e.g., f ≥ 0.5
426
      in CIFAR-100, f ≥ 0.2 in SVHN), we observe that KD renders the student model robust to the
427
      pruning technique used. This finding is significant as it suggests that it may be possible to forgo
428   state-of-the-art pruning techniques in favor of basic random pruning in the presence of KD.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we investigated the application of knowledge distillation for training models on pruned
533
      data. We demonstrated the significant benefits of incorporating the teacher’s soft predictions into the
534   training of the student across all pruning fractions, various pruning algorithms and multiple datasets.
535   We empirically found that incorporating KD while using simple random pruning can achieve com-
536   parable or superior accuracy compared to sophisticated pruning approaches. We also demonstrated
537   a useful connection between the pruning factor and the KD weight, and propose to adapt α ac-
538   cordingly. Finally, for small pruning fractions, we made the surprising observation that the student
539   benefits more from teachers with equal or even smaller capacities than that of its own, over teachers
      with larger capacities.

769   A.1    O BTAINING THE PRUNING SCORES

771   We utilize the default pruning recipes offered by the DeepCore framework (Guo et al., 2022) in order
