009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce Hydra-MDP++ , a novel end-to-end autonomous driving framework
012            that integrates rule-based and neural planners by learning from human demonstra-
013            tions and distilling knowledge from rule-based experts. We propose a teacher-
014            student knowledge distillation framework with a multi-head student decoder that
015            integrates feedback from rule-based expert teachers. The student model achieves
016            state-of-the-art performance on the NAVSIM benchmark with a tiny image en-
017            coder. Moreover, to address limitations in existing evaluation metrics, we expand
018
               the teacher model to include traffic light compliance, lane-keeping ability, and
               extended comfort. This is intended to ensure a more robust decision synthesis
019
               in driving. Hydra-MDP++ demonstrates robust and efficient performance across
020
               diverse driving scenarios, achieving a 91.0% drive score on NAVSIM by simply
021            scaling the image encoder. Our work contributes to developing more reliable and
022            adaptable autonomous driving systems that combine the strengths of rule-based
023            and neural planning approaches.

026   1    INTRODUCTION
027
028   Developing reliable and robust motion planning systems remains a critical challenge in the rapidly
029   evolving field of autonomous driving. Traditional rule-based planners (Treiber et al., 2000; Thrun
030   et al., 2006; Bacha et al., 2008; Leonard et al., 2008; Fan et al., 2018; Dauner et al., 2023) have long
031   been a cornerstone of autonomous driving systems. These planners rely on predefined rules and
032   heuristics to make decisions, offering transparency and interpretability. They excel in handling
033
      well-defined scenarios and can be easily adjusted to comply with traffic regulations. However, they
      often struggle with the unpredictability and complexity of real-world driving situations.
034
035   On the other hand, there is a well-established history of using neural networks for vision-based
036   steering control and autonomous driving (Pomerleau, 1988; Lecun et al., 2004; Bojarski et al., 2016).
037   Neural planners (Bansal et al., 2018; Qureshi et al., 2019) and their applications in end-to-end
038   autonomous driving (Codevilla et al., 2018; Zeng et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022;
039
      2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024) have gained significant attention in
      recent years. These data-driven approaches can learn from vast amounts of driving data, potentially
040
      capturing nuances of driving behavior that are difficult to encode in rule-based systems. End-to-end
041
      autonomous driving are noteworthy for their ability to process all available image features directly
042   through to the planning stage, enabling them to capture and respond to subtle cues and complex
043   interactions in driving scenarios. This capability allows them to potentially make more nuanced
044   and context-aware decisions, enhancing the overall performance and safety of autonomous vehicles.
045   While these planners promise adaptability to more diverse scenarios, they can sometimes be opaque
046   in their decision-making process.
047
      Rule-based and neural planners have long been viewed as occupying the opposite ends of the au-
048   tonomous driving spectrum. However, we argue that this perceived dichotomy may be overstated.
049   The gap between these approaches can be bridged by expanding the capabilities of neural planners
050   beyond mere imitation of human demonstrations to knowledge distillation from interpretable
051   rule-based experts. While imitation learning excels at replicating human actions in specific scenar-
052   ios, it often overlooks critical safety considerations (Dauner et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). In contrast,
053   those rule-based experts focus on the key components essential to safe and efficient driving: adher-
      ence to traffic rules, collision avoidance, and driving comfort.

      rate both human demonstrations and expert-guided decision analysis, namely Hydra-MDP++ . This
      framework utilizes a teacher-student knowledge distillation (KD) architecture to capture the essence

073
      teacher models, derived from human demonstrations and rule-based systems, validate these propos-
074   als based on various aspects of expert driving knowledge. We implement this multi-target validation
075   using a multi-head decoder, enabling the integration of feedback from specialized teachers that rep-
076   resent different components of safe and efficient driving, namely Hydra-Distillation. We compare
077   our proposed approach, the previous neural planners, and rule-base planners in Fig. 1.
078
      We evaluate our approach on NAVSIM, a data-driven non-reactive autonomous vehicle simulation
079
      and benchmarking tool. NAVSIM provides a middle ground between open-loop and closed-loop
080   evaluations using large datasets combined with a non-reactive simulator. It gathers simulation-based
081   metrics such as progress and time-to-collision by unrolling bird’s eye view abstractions of test scenes
082   for a short simulation horizon. This non-reactive simulation allows for efficient, open-loop metric
083   computation while aligning better with closed-loop evaluations than traditional displacement errors.
084   As an extension of the NAVSIM challenge-winning solution Hydra-MDP (Li et al., 2024), Hydra-
085   MDP++ leverages a simple encoder-decoder architecture. For the encoder, it simply uses classic
086   pretrained vision encoders, such as ResNet-34 (He et al., 2016) or VoVNet-99 (Lee & Park, 2020).
087   The decoder employs a lightweight and simple transformer network. Hydra-MDP++ achieved state-
088
      of-the-art performance on NAVSIM using only a lightweight ResNet-34 network without additional
      complex components. Hydra-MDP++ can easily outperform and achieve a 91.0% PDM Score by
089
      simply scaling the image encoder.
090
091   Moreover, we found that the NAVSIM-derived teachers do not sufficiently capture the full spectrum
092   of driving decision-making, potentially leading to unsafe behaviors. To address this, we expand
093   the original teacher by incorporating traffic light compliance (TL), lane-keeping ability (LK), and
094
      extended comfort (EC).

      2     RELATED WORK
109
110
      2.1   E ND - TO -E ND AUTONOMOUS DRIVING
111
112   End-to-end autonomous driving streamlines the entire stack from perception to planning into a single
113   optimizable network. This eliminates the need for manually designing intermediate representations.
114   Following pioneering work (Lyu et al., 2019; Bojarski et al., 2016; Kendall et al., 2019), a diverse
115   landscape of end-to-end models has emerged. For example, many end-to-end approaches focus on
116   closed-loop simulators that use single-frame cameras, LiDAR point clouds, or a combination of both
117   to mimic expert behaviour in autonomous driving, namely imitation learning (IL). Expert behaviour
118   typically takes two forms, trajectories and control actions. Transfuser and its variants (Prakash et al.,
119   2021; Chitta et al., 2022) use a simple GRU to auto-regressively predict waypoints for autonomous
120
      driving. LAV (Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022) adopts a temporal GRU module to further refine the tra-
      jectory. UniAD (Hu et al., 2023) first integrates perception, prediction, and planning into a unified
121
      transfomer network. VAD (Jiang et al., 2023) models the driving scene using vectorized represen-
122
      tations for efficiency. The recent work PARA-Drive (Weng et al., 2024) implements a fully parallel
123   end-to-end autonomous driving architecture, surpassing the performance of VAD and tripling the
124   processing speed. While they all perform impressively on the NAVSIM benchmark (Contributors,
125   2024; Dauner et al., 2024), we found that these methods still do not fully mimic human behaviour
126   and score low on certain metrics. This suggests that imitation learning still has limitations.
127
128
      2.2   RULE - BASED AUTONOMOUS DRIVING
129
130   Rule-based planners offer a structured, interpretable decision-making framework (Treiber et al.,
131   2000; Thrun et al., 2006; Bacha et al., 2008; Leonard et al., 2008; Fan et al., 2018; Dauner et al.,
132   2023). They have been foundational in ensuring safety and predictability by encoding explicit traf-
133   fic rules and heuristics into the driving system (e.g., apply a hard brake when an object is straight
134   ahead). One widely used framework is the Intelligent Driver Model (IDM (Treiber et al., 2000)),
135   which governs vehicle acceleration and braking behavior based on relative distances and speeds, en-
136
      abling safe and efficient car-following in various traffic conditions. Extensions of IDM (Fan et al.,
      2018) further build on rule-based principles by integrating explicit traffic laws and driving heuristics
137
      to guide decision-making in complex environments like urban traffic, leveraging a modular archi-
138
      tecture for tasks such as lane-changing and intersection handling. The recent study (Dauner et al.,
139   2023) proposes a rule-based planner named PDM-Planner. It assesses the current state of closed-
140   loop planning in the field, revealing the limitations of learning-based methods in complex real-world
141   scenarios and the value of simple rule-based priors such as centerline-following and collision avoid-
142   ance.
143
144
      2.3   C LOSED -L OOP B ENCHMARKING WITH S IMULATION
145
146   Closed-loop benchmarking with simulation is essential for evaluating autonomous driving systems
147   by measuring key aspects like safety, rule compliance, and comfort. Tools such as CARLA (Doso-
148   vitskiy et al., 2017) and Metadrive (Li et al., 2022) focus on sensor-based simulations, mimicking the
149   real world through virtual cameras and LiDAR. In contrast, platforms like nuPlan (Karnchanachari
150   et al., 2024) and Waymax (Gulino et al., 2024) utilize data-driven approaches to simulate urban en-
151   vironments with real-world data, providing a more dynamic and realistic evaluation of autonomous
152   agents. Despite advancements, replicating accurate traffic behaviors and realistic sensor data re-
153
      mains challenging. Achieving high fidelity in environmental interactions and vehicle dynamics is
      crucial for ensuring reliability in autonomous systems. Existing data-driven sensor simulations at-
154
      tempt to bridge this gap by adapting real sensor data for new driving scenarios, but they still fall
155
      short in rendering quality. For instance, many simulations depend on graphical-based rendering to
156   replicate sensor input like LiDAR, often resulting in discrepancies when compared to real-world
157   conditions. Simulators integrating more realistic sensor behavior, like CARLA and Metadrive, still
158   face hurdles in domain gaps related to visual fidelity and sensor precision. The latest NAVSIM
159   benchmark (Contributors, 2024; Dauner et al., 2024) addresses some of these challenges by eval-
160   uating planners with extended metrics, such as driving consistency and safety, that better reflect
161   real-world driving performance. However, it assumes a non-reactive environment, where agents do
      not influence their surroundings over a short horizon, simplifying real-world dynamics in its assess-

294   3.4   E XTENDED RULE - BASED TEACHERS
295
296   Hydra-MDP++ exhibits strong performance on the NAVSIM benchmark. Nevertheless, we observe
297   certain issues in planned trajectories of the model, which are not perfectly covered by existing met-
298   rics used by NAVSIM. These issues include traffic rule violations, deviation from the centerline,
299   and inconsistent predictions between consecutive frames, which can result in oscillation. In light of
300   these observations, we expand the original teacher by incorporating Traffic Lights Compliance (TL),
301
      Lane Keeping Ability (LK), and Extended Comfort (EC). Furthermore, our framework is capable of
      integrating additional rule-based teachers in the event that new rules are designed.
302
303   Traffic Lights Compliance. It is essential for all vehicles to follow traffic signals, represented
304   by the metric S T L . This metric evaluates whether a vehicle runs a red light. Specifically, for the
305   upcoming four seconds, if the vehicle crosses a crosswalk while the light is red, it will be flagged
306   for running the red light. In such an event, S T L is set to 0. However, if the vehicle complies and
307
      avoids crossing during the red light, S T L is assigned a value of 1.
308   Driving Direction Compliance. The S DDC metric is employed to determine whether the trajectory
309   of the vehicle between two consecutive time steps remains aligned with the centreline’s positive
310   direction, within an allowable distance deviation of τD . In the context of time steps i and i + 1, the
311   vehicle’s positions are defined as (xi , yi ) and (xi+1 , yi+1 ), respectively. The closest lane segment,
312   vj , is then identified, and the projections of these two positions onto the positive direction of vj
313
      are calculated. The distance between the two projected points is subsequently defined as dpi . The
314
      subscore S DDC = 1 if, for every time steps i, the condition: dpi ≤ τD holds.
315   Lane Keeping Ability. The lane keeping subscore S LK assesses a vehicle’s ability to stay within
316   a lateral deviation limit τD from the lane. This subscore reflects how effectively the vehicle can
317   maintain its intended path during navigation. At each time step i, we calculate the minimum per-
318   pendicular distance di between the ego vehicle (xi , yi ) and nearby lane segments vj :
319

      5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We present Hydra-MDP++ , a state-of-the-art end-to-end motion planner designed to synergize the
529   strengths of rule-based and neural planning methodologies. By learning from extensive human driv-
530   ing demonstrations and the insights provided by rule-based experts, Hydra-MDP++ can navigate
531
      complex environments more effectively. Our expert-guided hydra-distillation paradigm aligns the
      planner with simulation-based metrics in NAVSIM, enhancing its reliability and performance. To
532
      address the shortcomings of existing evaluation metrics, we have expanded the teacher model to
533
      include crucial aspects such as Traffic Lights Compliance, Lane Keeping Ability, and Extended
534   Comfort. This comprehensive approach ensures that the decision-making process in driving sce-
535   narios is robust, adaptable, and adheres to safety standards. The evaluation of Hydra-MDP++ us-
536   ing the public NAVSIM dataset reveals its superior performance compared to traditional methods,
537   demonstrating that it achieves high levels of accuracy and compliance with fewer parameters, which
538   highlights its efficiency and effectiveness in real-world applications. This advancement not only
539   improves driving performance but also contributes to the ongoing development of safer and more
      intelligent autonomous vehicles.
