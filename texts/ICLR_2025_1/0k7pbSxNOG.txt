011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Predicting the tensor properties of crystalline materials is a fundamental task
014            in materials science. Unlike single-value property prediction, which is inher-
015
               ently invariant, tensor property prediction requires maintaining O(3) group tensor
               equivariance. This equivariance constraint often introduces tremendous compu-
016
               tational costs, necessitating specialized designs for effective and efficient predic-
017
               tions. To address this limitation, we propose a general O(3)-equivariant frame-
018            work for fast crystal tensor property prediction, called GoeCTP. Our framework
019            is efficient as it does not need to impose equivalence constraints onto the net-
020            work architecture. Instead, GoeCTP captures the tensor equivariance with a sim-
021            ple external rotation and reflection (R&R) module based on polar decomposition.
022            The crafted external R&R module can rotate and reflect the crystal into an in-
023            variant standardized crystal position in space without introducing extra compu-
024            tational cost. We show that GoeCTP is general as it is a plug-and-play mod-
025            ule that can be smoothly integrated with any existing single-value property pre-
026
               diction framework for predicting tensor properties. Experimental results indi-
               cate that GoeCTP achieves higher prediction performance and runs 13× faster
027
               compared to existing state-of-the-art methods in elastic benchmarking datasets,
028
               underscoring its effectiveness and efficiency. Our code is publicly available at
029            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GoeCTP-FC98/.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The tensor properties of crystalline materials can capture intricate material responses through high-
035   order tensors, with wide-ranging applications in fields such as physics, electronics, and engineer-
036   ing (Yan et al., 2024b). Compared to single-value property prediction, predicting tensor properties
037   of crystalline materials is substantially more complex. This complexity arises from the fact that
038   tensor properties describe how crystals respond to external physical fields, such as electric fields or
039
      mechanical stress (Nye, 1985; Resta, 1994; Yan et al., 2024b). Consequently, tensor property pre-
      diction modeling necessitates preserving consistency with the crystal’s spatial position, exhibiting
040
      “special” O(3) equivariance (Yan et al., 2024b). Furthermore, high-order tensor property predic-
041
      tion is computationally intensive due to its high dimensionality and large data volume. Therefore,
042   providing fast and accurate predictions of tensor properties across various materials is challenging.
043
044   Thus far, several works have been dedicated to crystal tensor property prediction. One prominent
045
      category of approaches involves ab initio physical simulation techniques, such as density functional
      theory (DFT) (Petousis et al., 2016). These classical simulation techniques can accurately calculate
046
      various material properties, including electronic structures, phonon spectra, and tensor properties.
047
      However, they necessitate extensive computational resources due to the complexity of handling a
048   vast number of atoms and electrons in crystal systems (Yan et al., 2024b), hindering their applica-
049   bility in practice. Alternatively, machine learning (ML) models have been proposed to facilitate the
050   process of crystalline material property prediction. These methods typically leverage high-precision
051   datasets deriving from ab initio simulations and utilize crystal graph construction techniques along
052   with graph neural networks (GNNs) (Chen et al., 2019; Louis et al., 2020; Choudhary & DeCost,
053   2021; Xie & Grossman, 2018) or transformers (Yan et al., 2024a; Taniai et al., 2024; Yan et al.,
      2022; Lee et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a).

054
      Existing ML methods have primarily focused on predicting single-value properties of crystals. They
055   exhibit promising prediction performance by enforcing the periodic invariance constraint into the
056   model design. However, these methods cannot be directly applied to tensor property prediction as
057   they overlook the inherent anisotropy of the crystal systems, failing to achieve “special” O(3) equiv-
058   ariance (Yan et al., 2024a; Taniai et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a). Some recent
059   works attempt to ensure equivariance through specialized designs of network architectures (Mao
060   et al., 2024; Lou & Ganose, 2024; Heilman et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024; Pakornchote et al., 2023;
061   Yan et al., 2024b; Zhong et al., 2023). These methods generally employ harmonic decomposition
062   to achieve equivariance, where the tensor space is decomposed into the direct sum of irreducible
063
      representations of the rotation group. In this regard, numerous computationally intensive opera-
      tions, such as tensor products and the merging of irreducible representations, are required. These
064
      operations incur large amounts of computation overhead, particularly when processing high-order
065
      data.
066
067   To address the above challenges, we propose a novel O(3)-equivariant framework for fast crystal
068   tensor property prediction dubbed GoeCTP. In particular, instead of enforcing the equivariance into
069
      the model design, we craft a simple yet effective “rotation and reflection” (R&R) module based on
      polar decomposition. Our designed R&R module rotates and reflects the input crystal with differ-
070
      ent positions into a standardized invariant position in space. This standardized crystal position is
071
      subsequently passed into the property prediction network to obtain an invariant tensor value. Mean-
072   while, the orthogonal matrix obtained from the R&R module is used to achieve equivariant tensor
073   properties prediction. Our approach is plug-and-play as it can be readily integrated with any ex-
074   isting single-value property prediction network for predicting tensor properties without incurring
075   additional computational costs. Compared to the previous state-of-the-art work Yan et al. (2024b),
076   the GoeCTP method achieves higher quality prediction results and runs more than 13× faster in the
077   elastic benchmarking dataset.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES
082
083   The structure of crystalline materials consists of a periodic arrangement of atoms in 3D space, with
084   a repeating unit called a unit cell. An entire crystal typically can be characterized by describing the
085   parameters of a single unit cell, such as the types and coordinates of the atoms within it, as well as
086   the lattice parameters (Yan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b; Jiao et al., 2024). General methods for
087   describing crystals can be divided into the Cartesian coordinate system and the fractional coordinate
088   system, as described below.
089   Cartesian Coordinate System. A crystal can be mathematically represented as M = (A, X, L),
090   where A = [a1 , a2 , · · · , an ]T ∈ Rn×da denotes atom features for n atoms within a unit cell.
091   Each ai ∈ Rda is a da -dimensional feature vector characterizing an individual atom. The matrix
092   X = [x1 , x2 , · · · , xn ]T ∈ Rn×3 contains the 3D Cartesian coordinates of n atoms in the unit
093   cell. The lattice maxtrix L = [l1 , l2 , l3 ] ∈ R3×3 consists of the lattice vectors l1 , l2 , and l3 ,
094   which form the basis of the 3D Euclidean space. A complete crystal is therefore represented as
095   (Â, X̂) = {(âi , x̂i )|x̂i = xi + k1 l1 + k2 l2 + k3 l3 , âi = ai , k1 , k2 , k3 ∈ Z, i ∈ Z, 1 ≤ i ≤ n}. In
096   this representation, the integers ki and li denote all possible atomic positions in the periodic lattice.
097   Fractional Coordinate System. Instead of using the standard orthogonal basis, fractional coor-
098   dinate system utilizes the lattice matrix L = [l1 , l2 , l3 ] ∈ R3×3 as the basis vectors for atomic
099   positions. With this representation, the position of an atom is given by a fractional coordinate vector
100   denoted as fi = [f1 , f2 , f3 ]T ∈ [0, 1)3 . The corresponding Cartesian coordinate vector can then be
101   expressed as xi = fi li . Therefore, for a crystal M, it can be represented as M = (A, F, L), where
102   F = [f1 , · · · , fn ]T ∈ [0, 1)n×3 represents the fractional coordinates of all atoms in the unit cell.

198
      positive semi-definite matrix represents a stretching or scaling. When applied to crystal structures,

199
      orthogonal matrix, allowing us to directly transfer equivariance without the need for intricate model
200
      design. Furthermore, polar decomposition is only performed during the data preprocessing stage and
201   does not occur during network training, thereby introducing virtually no additional computational
202   burden.

      3.2       O UR P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK : GoeCTP
205
206   In what follows, we will first introduce the core rotation and reflection (R&R) module of the pro-
207   posed GoeCTP, focusing on how to obtain a standardized crystal position for a crystal with arbitrary
208   spatial direction using polar decomposition. Then, we will describe how the input crystal data is
209   processed and introduce the property prediction network of GoeCTP. Finally, we will explain how
210   proposed GoeCTP achieves equivariant property predictions. An overview of the GoeCTP frame-
211   work is illustrated in Fig. 2.
212   R&R Module. The primary function of the R&R Module is to rotate and reflect the crystal to a
213   standardized crystal position in space. In the fractional coordinate system, the O(3) group trans-
214   formations applied to a crystal affect only the lattice matrix L, while fractional coordinates remain
215   invariant, making it convenient for the R&R module to perform rotation and reflection operations
      on the crystal data. Therefore, R&R module adopts the fractional coordinate system to represent the

310   4   R ELATED WORK
311
312   GNN-Based Methods. CGCNN is a pioneering GNN model specifically designed for handling
313   crystal structures (Xie & Grossman, 2018). This model proposed to represent crystal structures as
314   multi-edge crystal graphs. It was applied to predict various single-value properties such as forma-
315
      tion energy and band gap. Since then, several GNN methods have been developed to improve upon
      CGCNN through exploring various network designs or leveraging prior knowledge (Chen et al.,
316
      2019; Louis et al., 2020; Choudhary & DeCost, 2021; Das et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023). These
317
      GNN methods are primarily designed for single-value property prediction and do not address the
318   prediction of high-order tensor properties, such as dielectric or elastic tensors. Furthermore, they
319   lack the ability to preserve the equivariance required for accurate high-order tensor property predic-
320   tion. In contrast, recent studies attempted to ensure equivariance through specialized network archi-
321   tectures (Mao et al., 2024; Lou & Ganose, 2024; Heilman et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024; Pakornchote
322   et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024b; Zhong et al., 2023). These approaches generally employ harmonic
323   decomposition to achieve equivariance for tensor properties. Within these network architectures,
      many operations are required, such as tensor products and combining irreducible representations.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      These processes significantly increase computational costs, especially when handling higher-order
325   data.
326
327   Transformer-Based Methods. Transformers, with their self-attention mechanism and parallel pro-
328
      cessing capabilities, are particularly well-suited for predicting crystal material properties. Mat-
      former (Yan et al., 2022), one of the earliest Transformer-based networks used for crystal material
329
      property prediction, encoded crystal periodic patterns by using the geometric distances between
330
      the same atoms in neighboring unit cells. This addressed the issue in earlier GNN-based meth-
331   ods, including CGCNN, MEGNet, GATGNN, and others, which neglected the periodic patterns of
332   infinite crystal structures. Subsequently, more advanced Transformer-based approaches were pro-
333   posed. Some methods, such as ComFormer (Yan et al., 2024a), CrystalFormer (Wang et al., 2024a),
334   and CrystalFormer (Taniai et al., 2024), typically incorporate either enhanced graph construction
335   techniques or physical priors, exhibiting impressive results in the prediction of single-value proper-
336   ties. Furthermore, DOSTransformer (Lee et al., 2024) is tailored for the density of states prediction,
337   utilizing prompt-guided multi-modal transformer architecture to achieve super performance. Never-
338   theless, these models are not directly applicable for accurate high-order tensor property prediction
339
      due to their inability to capture the necessary equivariance.

372

373
      the predicted tensor and the ground truth tensor to the Fnorm of the ground truth tensor. This ratio
374
      can be expressed as ||ypred − ylabel ||F /||ylabel ||F , where || · ||F is Fnorm, and ylabel and ypred
375   represent the ground truth and predicted values, respectively. For instance, EwT 25% indicates that
376   the proportion of predicted samples with this ratio is below 25%. Higher values of EwT signify

      6     C ONCLUSION , LIMITATIONS , AND FUTURE WORKS
526
527   In this work, we propose a novel O(3)-equivariant framework GoeCTP for fast crystal tensor pre-
528   diction, which is a plug-and-play framework that can be integrated with any existing single-value
529   property prediction network, enabling them to predict tensor properties with almost no additional
530   computational cost. Based on predicting invariant tensor properties from standardized crystal posi-
531
      tions and using an external module to ensure tensor equivariance, this approach has achieved state-
      of-the-art performance and the highest efficiency in different widely used crystal tensor property
532
      benchmark tests. The limitations of our current GoeCTP include (1) The performance of GoeCTP
533
      is inherently dependent on the performance of the Prediction module. (2) Currently, GoeCTP is
534   specifically tailored for the prediction of tensor properties in crystalline materials and has not yet
535   been adapted for other material types. In future work, we intend to explore the following directions:
536   for (1), we aim to improve performance by integrating prior knowledge related to the independent
537   components of tensor properties across different crystal systems in Appendix A.3 (Further discus-
538   sion on the utilization of space group constraints on tensor properties can be found in Appendix
539   A.6); for (2), we plan to extend the framework to other domains, such as 3D point cloud research,
      broadening its applicability. Seeing Appendix A.5 for more extensibility and limitations details.

      A.2    O(3) EQUIVARIANCE FOR CRYSTAL TENSOR PROPERTIES
742
      The O(3) equivariance required for crystal tensor property prediction tasks differs from the O(3)
743
      equivariance typically encountered in general molecular studies (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
744   2024; Song et al., 2024). In molecular studies, for a function f : (A, F, L) → y ∈ Rn , if it is
745   O(3) equivariant, then it satisfies f (A, F, QL) = Qf (A, F, L), where Q ∈ Rn×n is an arbitrary
746   orthogonal matrix. Here, the function f can be seen as the model.
747
      Dielectric tensor. However, in the prediction of crystal tensor properties, such as the dielectric
748
      tensor ε ∈ R3×3 , the requirements for O(3) equivariance differ. For a function f : (A, F, L) → ε,
749
      if it is O(3) equivariant, it must satisfy f (A, F, QL) = Qf (A, F, L)QT . The specific reason for
750   this difference can be referenced in the previous work (Yan et al., 2024b), and is rooted in the fact
751   that the dielectric tensor characterizes a material’s polarization response to an external electric field,
752   describing the relationship between the electric displacement D ∈ R3 and the applied electric field

754   we have D′ = ε′ E′ , where D′ = QD and E′ = QE. This results in the transformation of the
755   dielectric tensor under O(3) group transformation as ε′ = QεQT . This transformation principle
      extends similarly to other crystal tensors, as follows.

       Extensibility. For the extensibility of our proposed method, a natural extension would be to adapt
       it for use with 3D point cloud systems, such as 3D molecular systems. The ability of our method
999
       to directly transmit rotational information renders it particularly effective for specialized equivariant
1000
       tasks. For instance, in equivariant 3D molecular generation tasks, our approach can directly transfer
1001   equivariant information from time step 0 to time step T , bypassing multiple denoising networks
1002   (Hoogeboom et al., 2022). This is previous equivariant techniques such as equivariant network
1003   (Satorras et al., 2021) and frame averaging (Puny et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024) cannot achieve.
1004   Specifically, a molecule in 3D space can be represented as M = (A, X), where A ∈ Rda ×n
1005   denotes da -dimensional atom features for n atoms in the molecular, and X ∈ R3×n contains the 3D
1006   coordinates of these n atoms. To extend our method, polar decomposition would be applied to the
1007   coordinate matrix, such that X = Q exp(S). Then, the rotational information Q can be transferred
1008   to the output of the network to complete the equivariant task. As demonstrated in Higham (1986), if
1009
       the coordinate matrix is full-rank, the polar decomposition is unique, enabling a seamless extension
       of our method to 3D molecular systems. However, there are special cases where this approach may
1010
       fail. For example, in a molecule composed of three atoms all lying within the same plane, the
1011
       coordinate matrix X may not be full-rank (e.g., when all atomic coordinates lie on a plane passing
1012   through the origin). This is similar to the scenario where our method is applied to 2D crystals,
1013   where directly applying polar decomposition may not yield a unique 3 × 3 orthogonal matrix Q
1014   and a unique standardized position H. Additionally, since proposed framework operates externally
1015   to the network and does not participate in actual training, it may potentially assist large language
1016   models in achieving equivariant prediction tasks.
