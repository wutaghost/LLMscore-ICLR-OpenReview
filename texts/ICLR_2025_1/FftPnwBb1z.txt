009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Designing drugs that can restore a diseased cell to its healthy state is an emerg-
012            ing approach in systems pharmacology to address medical needs that conventional
013            target-based drug discovery paradigms have failed to meet. Single-cell transcrip-
014            tomics can comprehensively map the differences between diseased and healthy
015            cellular states, making it a valuable technique for systems pharmacology. How-
016
               ever, single-cell omics data is noisy, heterogeneous, scarce, and high-dimensional.
               As a result, no machine learning methods currently exist to use single-cell omics
017
               data to design new drug molecules. We have developed a new deep generative
018
               framework named MolGene-E that can tackle this challenge. MolGene-E com-
019            bines two novel models: 1) a cross-modal model that can harmonize and denoise
020            chemical-perturbed bulk and single-cell transcriptomics data, and 2) a contrastive
021            learning-based generative model that can generate new molecules based on the
022            transcriptomics data. MolGene-E consistently outperforms baseline methods in
023            generating high-quality, hit-like molecules from gene expression profiles obtained
024            from single-cell datasets and gene expressions induced by knocking out targets
025            using CRISPR. This superior performance is demonstrated across diverse de novo
026            molecule generation metrics, which makes MolGene-E a potentially powerful new
027
               tool for drug discovery.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Capitalizing on the success of deep learning across various domains such as natural language, im-
032   ages, and videos, deep generative models have been extensively applied to the generation of small
033   organic compounds targeting a specific disease gene for drug discovery Zeng et al. (2022). How-
034   ever, this one-drug-one-target paradigm has had limited success in tackling polygenic, multifactorial
035   diseases. Due to the high costs, prolonged development timelines, and low success rates associated
036   with target-based drug discovery, there has been a resurgence of interest in phenotypic drug dis-
037   covery. As a matter of fact, approximately 90% of approved drugs have been discovered through
038   a phenotype-driven approach Vincent et al. (2022). Therefore, phenotype-based molecular genera-
039
      tion, also known as inverse molecule design, holds promise for the discovery of novel therapeutics
      aimed at addressing medical needs that conventional target-based drug discovery paradigms have
040
      failed to meet.
041
042   The effectiveness of phenotype-based drug discovery relies upon the careful selection of an appro-
043   priate phenotype readout. Chemical-induced transcriptomics has been embraced as a comprehensive
044   systematic measurement for phenotype drug discovery. The transcriptomic change resulting from
045
      chemical exposure can function as a chemical signature for predicting drug responses as well as
      aid in the elucidation of drug targets and the inference of drug-modulated pathways. This approach
046
      has demonstrated successful applications in phenotype drug repurposing Salame et al. (2022) Pham
047
      et al. (2021). Several deep learning methods have been proposed to leverage chemical-induced bulk
048   gene expression data for inverse molecule design. Notably, MolGAN Méndez-Lucio et al. (2020)
049   generates molecules conditioning a generative adversarial network with bulk transcriptomics data.
050   Although it shows promising results, GANs are susceptible to scalability, as we show that their per-
051   formance drops significantly when trained on higher dimensional data. Furthermore, GANs have
052   a black-box nature, and inferring the relation between the condition (gene expression) and genera-
053   tion (molecules) is quite cumbersome. Another recent work is the GxVAE Li & Yamanishi (2024),
      which employs two joint variational autoencoders (VAEs) to facilitate the extraction of latent gene

      The abundance of single-cell omics data provides new opportunities for phenotype-based drug dis-
089
      covery. Single-cell transcriptomics data offer new insights into disease heterogeneity within and
090   across species, illuminating the complexity of pathological processes. An effective therapy often
091   needs to modulate disease etiology at the single-cell level Han et al. (2022). Furthermore, pre-
092   cise characterization of single-cell chemical transcriptomics is crucial to bridge translational gaps
093   between disease models (e.g., organoids and animals) and human patients, a critical bottleneck in
094   drug discovery Van de Sande et al. (2023). Nonetheless, there remains a scarcity of methods for
095   leveraging single-cell transcriptomics data in inverse molecule design.
096   Compared with protein structures that exhibit a relatively clean nature, omics data is plagued by its
097   high-dimensionality and susceptibility to noise, stemming from biological stochasticity and techni-
098   cal artifacts. These complexities pose hurdles for single-cell inverse molecule design, exacerbated by
099   the limited availability of chemical-perturbed single-cell transcriptomics data. LINCS1000 Subra-
100   manian et al. (2017) serves as a comprehensive chemical transcriptomics database, profiling 19,811
101   chemicals across 77 cell lines. However, this database profiles only 978 landmark genes. Moreover,
102   the gene expression data in LINCS1000 is obtained using a specific imaging technique, leading to
103   significant distributional discrepancies from RNA-seq data. Due to these challenges, no methods
104
      exist for inverse molecule design based on single-cell omics data.
105   To address these challenges, we introduce MolGene-E, a deep learning framework for single-cell
106   molecule generation. The key contributions of MolGene-E are twofold: First, we develop a do-
107   main adaptation model that is capable of harmonizing and denoising L1000toRNAseq and Sciplex-3
      single-cell chemical transcriptomics data. Second, we design a generative algorithm that leverages

109   these components, MolGene-E facilitates the generation of novel molecules with specific phenotypic

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
      To leverage chemical-induced bulk gene expression data for inverse molecule design, MolGAN
116
      Méndez-Lucio et al. (2020) exploited gene expression profiles to generate hit-like molecules. How-
117
      ever, the conditioning network in MolGAN makes use of real and fake conditions, where conditions
118   outside of the training batch are assumed to be fake, which could introduce potential inaccuracies
119   due to the presence of multiple replicates with distinct gene expressions in the L1000 dataset. A
120   more recent work, GxVAE Li & Yamanishi (2024), proposed using joint VAEs to extract latent
121   features from gene expressions and using them as conditions for generating molecules utilizing a
122   second VAE for encoding molecules. These approaches result in poorly structured latent space, con-
123   sequently affecting the model’s ability to generate chemically valid molecules. Additionally, these
124   models do not address the incumbent challenge of leveraging highly sparse and out-of-distribution
125   single-cell datasets to generate molecules. To address these challenges, we developed a denoising
126
      gene expression encoder, which reconstructs the median gene expression in the case of replicates
      chemical samples, essentially acting as a denoising gene expression autoencoder. This approach
127
      ensures a more meaningful and robust representation of gene expressions, contributing to a better-
128
      structured latent space and enhancing the generation of valid and diverse molecular structures.
129

131   3     M ETHODS
132
133   MolGene-E is a framework designed for the de novo generation of molecules with desired biological
134   properties. As shown in Figure 1, the framework involves a five-step process that integrates diverse
135
      data representations and deep learning modules to align chemical and gene expression profile infor-
      mation effectively.
136
137
138   3.1   D ENOISING VAE FOR G ENE E XPRESSION P ROFILES
139
      In chemical-induced bulk gene expression data, multiple distinct gene expression profiles perturbed
140   by replicate chemicals can exist. In order to manage and interpret the complex data from multiple
141   replicates, MolGene-E employs a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). The VAE is trained with the
142   objective of reconstructing the median gene expression profile from the gene expression profiles
143   corresponding to replicate chemical perturbations in a batch (Figure 1a). This approach ensures
144   that the VAE captures the most representative gene expression profile, smoothing out anomalies and
145   focusing on the core response to chemical perturbations. This process enhances the reliability of the
146   gene expression data used in further steps.
147
148   3.2   SELFIES VAE FOR C HEMICALS
149
150   For representing the chemical structures in perturbations, MolGene-E leverages SELFIES (Self-
151
      Referencing Embedded Strings) Krenn et al. (2020) due to its guaranteed 100% validity in contrast
      to using SMILES strings to represent molecules for molecule design Méndez-Lucio et al. (2020).
152
      These SELFIES strings are encoded using a VAE model pretrained on ZINC dataset Gao et al.
153
      (2022) (Figure 1b). The use of SELFIES allows for a comprehensive and error-resistant encoding
154   of molecular structures, facilitating seamless integration with machine learning models.
155
156

161   and SupCon loss Khosla et al. (2020). The objective of this module is to align the embeddings of
      phenotypes (gene expression profiles) with the embeddings of the SELFIES representations of the

191   3.4   M APPING G ENE E XPRESSIONS TO C HEMICAL E MBEDDINGS
192
193   To complete the alignment process, MolGene-E employs a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)-based
194   prior model (Figure 1d). This model is trained to map the embeddings of gene expression profiles
195   to the embeddings of their corresponding chemical counterparts. The MLP-based prior effectively
196
      bridges the gap between biological responses and chemical structures, enabling the generation of
      novel molecules that can induce desired gene expression changes.
197
198
199   3.5   G ENERATION OF D RUG C ANDIDATES
200
      After training the prior model, gene expressions corresponding to chemical perturbations can be
201   used for inference to generate drug candidates that might result in similar perturbation effects. The
202   gene expression embeddings z gene are extracted using the pretrained gene expression encoder in
203   the contrastive learning module and subsequently input to the prior model to compute chemical
204   embeddings z chem . z chem are then decoded via the SELFIES VAE model to obtain potential drug
205   candidates in the form of novel molecular structures.

247   Model Settings. For the denoising VAE, we used hidden layers of sizes [1024, 512, 256] with
248
      layernorm and a dropout rate of 0.3. We used a latent dimension of 128. The weight for KL-term of
      loss was increased linearly from the first to the last epoch. We trained the model for 400 epochs to
249
      achieve good performance on the validation set.
250
251   For the SELFIES VAE, it maximizes a lower bound of the likelihood (evidence lower bound
252   (ELBO)) instead of estimating the likelihood directly. We used the pretrained model and archi-
253   tecture identical to the one implemented in MOSES Polykovskiy et al. (2020) to model SELFIES
254
      strings. The architecture used a bidirectional Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) with a linear output layer
      as an encoder. The decoder was a 3-layer GRU of 512 hidden dimensions with intermediate dropout
255
      layers and a dropout rate of 0.2. Training was done with a batch size of 128, utilizing a gradient
256
      clipping of 50, KL-term weight linearly increased from 0 to 1 during training. We optimized the
257   model using Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 3e-4.
258
259   For the prior model, it is a feed forward MLP architecture with hidden sizes [1024, 512, 256] and a
260
      latent dimension of size 128. The model was trained to minimize a mean squared error loss for the
      reconstruction of chemical embedding space utilizing the aligned spaces from both modalities. The
261
      model used a learning rate of 1e-3 and a batch size of 128.
262
263   For the training of MolGene-E, we minimize the contrastive learning objective L (Equation 3) with
264   an approach similar to Radford et al. (2021). MolGene-E was trained for 600 epochs with a batch
265   size of 128 and a learning rate of 1e-4. A projection network with MLP hidden layers [128, 128]
266
      was further added to the gene expression encoder. When generating drug candidates, 400 unique
      chemical candidates are generated by sampling from the latent space for each gene expression pro-
267
      file.

298   4.2   M OL G ENE -E I MPROVES THE S UCCESS R ATE OF I NVERSE M OLECULE D ESIGN
299
300   We use a challenge task to evaluate the performance of the molecular generation from gene expres-
301   sions. If a drug can correctly revert gene expressions from a disease state to a healthy state, the drug
302
      could interact with disease-causing genes, i.e., drug targets. In other words, the gene expression
      changes that are caused by the target gene knock-out or knock-down should be similar to those that
303
      result from the chemical perturbation targeting the knock-out/down gene. In our experiments, ref-
304
      erence molecules from the test-split of L1000toRNAseq dataset were considered which had single
305   target knock-outs in the CRISPR gene perturbation dataset for the MCF7 cell line. Gene expres-
306   sion profiles for these targets were used for the inference of drug candidates. As shown in Figure
307   2, MolGene-E outperforms both baseline models, MolGAN and GxVAE, in terms of average Tani-
308   moto similarities computed using the MACCS keys between the generated and reference molecules.
309   For further evaluation of quality of generated molecules, other metrics such as uniqueness, validity,
310   novelty, diversity and synthesizability (SA) were used as listed in Table 1. MolGene-E performs the
311   best overall by consistently achieving higher scores in validity and novelty while maintaining a high
312   level of uniqueness, diversity, and SA. This indicates that MolGene-E not only generates molecules
313
      that closely resemble the reference compounds in terms of structural similarity but also proposes
      novel and diverse chemical scaffolds that are synthetically feasible.

371   5    C ONCLUSION
372
373   In this paper, we developed a deep generative model that utilizes phenotypic properties from single-
374   cell omics data to generate high-quality lead candidates for drug discovery. MolGene-E consistently
375   outperforms baseline methods in generating high-quality, hit-like molecules from gene expression
376   profiles obtained from single-cell datasets and gene expressions induced by CRISPR-based knockout
377   targets. This superior performance is demonstrated across de novo molecule generation metrics,
      including novelty, diversity, uniqueness, and synthesizability.
