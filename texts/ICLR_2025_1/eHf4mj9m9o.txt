009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Characterizing and understanding the dynamics of stochastic gradient descent
012            (SGD) around saddle points remains an open problem in neural network optimiza-
013            tion. We identify two distinct types of saddle points, demonstrating that Type-II
014            saddles pose a significant challenge due to vanishing gradient noise, which makes
015            them particularly difficult for SGD to escape. We show that the dynamics around
016
               these saddles can be effectively modeled by a random matrix product process, al-
               lowing us to apply concepts from probabilistic stability and Lyapunov exponents.
017
               By leveraging ergodic theory, we establish that saddle points can be either at-
018
               tractive or repulsive for SGD, leading to a classification of four distinct dynamic
019            phases based on the gradient’s signal-to-noise ratio near the saddle. We apply the
020            theory to the training at the initial stage of neural networks, explaining an intrigu-
021            ing phenomenon that neural networks are prone to be stuck at the initialization
022            point at a larger learning rate. Our results offer a novel theoretical framework for
023            understanding the intricate behavior of SGD around saddle points, with implica-
024            tions for improving optimization strategies in deep learning.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Understanding the optimization landscape is fundamental to unlocking the full potential of deep
029   learning. Despite the remarkable successes of neural networks, their training often involves navigat-
030
      ing complex loss surfaces riddled with saddle points, which significantly influence the behavior and
      efficiency of stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Recent research suggests that the trajectory of SGD
031
      predominantly moves from one saddle point to another during training (Jacot et al., 2021; Abbe
032
      et al., 2023), making the ability to characterize and understand these dynamics an urgent priority for
033   the deep learning community.
034
035   In this work, we delve into this critical problem by introducing a classification of saddle points into
036
      two distinct types: (1) Type-I, where gradient noise persists at the saddle, and (2) Type-II, where the
      gradient noise vanishes entirely (Def. 1). Our primary focus is on the Type-II saddles, which pose a
037
      greater challenge due to their tendency to trap SGD, even in the presence of a negative eigenvalue
038
      in the Hessian matrix.
039
040   Our main contributions are:
041   1. Classification of saddles: we propose to classify saddle points in neural networks into two
042      types, Type-I where the noises of the gradient do not vanish in the escape directions, and Type-II
043      saddles where the gradient noises vanish in the escape directions;
044   2. Probabilistic Stability: we propose using the probabilistic stability and Lyapunov exponents
045      to study the attractivity of Type-II saddle points of neural networks, bridging the conventional
046      study of dynamical systems in control theory and ergodic theory to study the linear dynamics of
047      SGD close stationary points;
048   3. Phases and Difficulty of Network Initialization: we use the probabilistic stability to show that
049      close to a Type-II saddle point, SGD has at least four different phases of learning, which we
050
         apply to understand the difficulty of using a large learning rate during the initialization of neural
         nets.
051
052   This work is organized as follows. Section 2 discusses the most relevant works. Section 3 discusses
053   the difference between the two types of saddle points encountered in training neural networks. Sec-
      tion 4 studies the probabilistic stability and Lyapunov exponent of SGD around these fixed points.

      Dynamical stability. Dynamical stability centers around studying the attractivity and repulsiveness
059
      of fixed points. From an optimization perspective, stability can be seen as a worst-case guarantee
060   for optimization because the training algorithm cannot converge to a fixed point that is unstable.
061   Conceptually, the work closest to ours is Wu et al. (2018), which uses the variance of SGD to
062   characterize the set of solutions that are preferred by SGD, namely the solutions that are flat and have
063   a rather weak noise. Wang & Jacot (2023) extends the discussion to explore the rank of solutions at
064   local minima. However, as our result shows, moment-based notions of stability cannot be applied
065   to saddle points. Instead, we propose to study this problem with the tools of probabilistic stability,
066   which has been studied extensively in control theory (Khas’ minskii, 1967; Eckmann & Ruelle,
067   1985; Teel et al., 2014). The connection between Lyapunov exponents and probabilistic stability for
068
      the random matrix product problem is well known (Diaconis & Freedman, 1999), but its relevance
      for studying saddle points in deep learning is unclear. Prior works have used similar notions to study
069
      the convergence to a local minimum (Gurbuzbalaban et al., 2021; Hodgkinson & Mahoney, 2021).
070
      In contrast, our focus is on the attractivity of saddle points. Recently, Chen et al. (2024) introduced
071   the concept of stochasitc collapse, which can be seen as a manifestation of the probabilistic stability
072   when the dynamics is in continuous time.

525   6    D ISCUSSION
526
527   We have shown that special attention should be paid to the study of Type-II saddle points in neu-
528   ral networks. To this purpose, we demonstrate that the probabilistic stability serves as an essential
529   notion for understanding the attractivity of Type-II saddle points as no moment-based notions of
530   stability can be used to study when a saddle point becomes attractive. These effects are only present
531
      for SGD and not for GD, implying that the algorithmic regularization due to SGD is qualitatively
      different from that of GD. Our result also sheds light on how a solution in the loss landscape is
532
      chosen by SGD. In the probabilistic-stability perspective, SGD performs a selection between con-
533
      verging to saddles and to local minima, not between sharp minima and flat ones. A main limitation
534   of our work is that we only focused on the local dynamics close to stationary points, and it would be
535   important to link it to the global learning dynamics. Depending on how one views the problem of
536   Type-II saddles, there are two interesting future directions. If we regard the Type-II saddles as major
537   obstacles to optimization, an important future problem is to design algorithms to better escape from
538   them or to remove them from the loss landscape. Alternatively, the Type-II saddles can function as
539   effective capacity constraints on the models, and one might also be interested in leveraging them to
      better regularize neural networks.
