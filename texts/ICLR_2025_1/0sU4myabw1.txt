009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Accelerating molecular docking – the process of predicting how molecules bind
012            to protein targets – could boost small-molecule drug discovery and revolution-
013            ize medicine. Unfortunately, current molecular docking tools are too slow to
014            screen potential drugs against all relevant proteins, which often results in missed
015            drug candidates or unexpected side effects occurring in clinical trials. To ad-
016
               dress this gap, we introduce R APID D OCK, an efficient transformer-based model
               for blind molecular docking. R APID D OCK achieves at least a 100× speed advan-
017
               tage over existing methods without compromising accuracy. On the Posebusters
018
               and DockGen benchmarks, our method achieves 52.1% and 44.0% success rates
019            (RMSD <2Å), respectively. The average inference time is 0.04 seconds on a sin-
020            gle GPU, highlighting R APID D OCK’s potential for large-scale docking studies.
021            We examine the key features of R APID D OCK that enable leveraging the trans-
022            former architecture for molecular docking, including the use of relative distance
023            embeddings of 3D structures in attention matrices, pre-training on protein folding,
024            and a custom loss function invariant to molecular symmetries. We make the model
025            code and weights publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Accelerating the drug discovery process could revolutionize medicine. As most novel drugs are
030   small molecules (Kinch et al., 2024), various deep learning methods have been proposed to stream-
031   line the process of docking such molecules to druggable protein targets (Abramson et al., 2024;
032   Corso et al., 2024; 2022; Qiao et al., 2024). While impressive, none of these methods is both accu-
033   rate and fast.
034
      In fact, to obtain a comprehensive view of its effects, a molecule needs to be screened against
035
      thousands of proteins (Sjöstedt et al., 2020). However, state-of-the-art methods (Corso et al., 2024;
036   2022; Abramson et al., 2024; Qiao et al., 2024) report run-times on the scale of seconds per protein
037   on a single GPU. Consequently, screening a relatively small database of one million molecules (Yu
038   et al., 2023) against the proteome, i.e., all proteins in the human body, would take years, even with
039   hundreds of GPUs. Such time-frames are unacceptable in the drug development process.
040
      To address this challenge, we introduce R APID D OCK, a transformer-based model that performs
041
      molecular docking in a single forward pass, in hundredths of a second on a single GPU. R APID -
042   D OCK performs blind docking, using unbound, possibly computationally folded proteins, so it can
043   be applied to unexplored protein targets. Given the 3D structure of a protein and a molecule, our
044   method predicts all pairwise distances in the resulting protein-molecule complex, including the
045   molecule’s atom-atom distances and atom-amino acid distances.
046
      R APID D OCK achieves success rates, i.e., RMSD < 2Å, of 52.1% on the Posebusters benchmark
047
      (Buttenschoen et al., 2024) and 44.0% on the DockGen benchmark (Corso et al., 2024), with 0.03
048   and 0.05 seconds average runtimes, respectively. Our method significantly outperforms the re-
049   cent DiffDock-L (Corso et al., 2024) (22.6% success rate, 35.4 seconds on DockGen) or the Neu-
050   ralPLexer (Qiao et al., 2024) (27.4% success rate, 3.77 seconds on Posebusters). Because of its
051   accuracy and speed, R APID D OCK can enable novel use cases and research directions. For example,
052   with R APID D OCK, docking ten million molecules to all human proteins on a cluster with 512 GPUs
053   would take nine days, compared to about 20 years with DiffDock-L or even 200 years required with
      a computationally-intensive method like AlphaFold-3 (Abramson et al., 2024).

      2     M ETHOD
109
110
      R APID D OCK, illustrated in Figure 1, is based on the transformer encoder architecture (Vaswani,
111
      2017) and returns the positions of atoms in the docked molecule. R APID D OCK represents the protein
112   and molecule spatial structures by distance matrices whose embeddings are added to the attention
113   matrices. Because R APID D OCK operates on distances only, it is invariant to translations, reflections,
114   or rotations, which we found crucial for accurate docking. Below we present the details.
115
116
      2.1   M OLECULE AND PROTEIN REPRESENTATION
117
118   The input to R APID D OCK consists of a sequence of tokens corresponding to molecule atoms and
119   protein amino acids. The 3D structures of the protein and molecule are represented by learnable
120   embeddings of their respective distance matrices. Additionally, we include the information on the
121   molecule’s atom charges because of the need to model the electrostatic interactions governing the
122   docking process. We also utilize amino acid embeddings obtained from the protein language model
123   ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023).
124
125   Atom and amino acid dictionary We have 26 amino acid tokens (20 standard amino acids and 6
126   tokens for non-standard or unknown residues) and 56 atom tokens (all atoms likely to be found in
127
      drug molecules, i.e., elements lighter than uranium, excluding noble gases or reactive alkali metals).
128
129   Ligand atom charges To account for electrostatic interactions, we include information about atom
130   charges in the molecules. For each atom we include its partial charge (Gasteiger charges), which is
131
      the distribution of the molecule electron density spread over the ligand atoms. The values of charges
      are embedded (see below) and added to the atom embeddings.
132
133
134
      Protein language embeddings To improve the protein representation, we use embeddings from
      the 650M ESM-2 model Lin et al. (2023), one embedding per amino acid. These embeddings are
135
      added to the amino acid embeddings (to match the dimensionality, we use a linear projection layer).
136
137
138
      Molecule rigid distance matrix The ligand spatial structure is represented by the distance matrix
      D l between its heavy atom coordinates xl ∈ RL×3 , where L is the number of heavy atoms in the lig-
139
      and. Only the fixed distances across the molecule’s possible conformations are recorded, and others
140
      are denoted by a special value of −1, which indicates that the corresponding attention matrix entries
141   should not be modified, see below. The moleule’s geometric representation thus distinguishes the
142   rigid and moving parts of the molecule, which is important for modeling its allowable movements.
143   To obtain the matrix, we generate 96 molecule conformations in total, using two methods: 64 from
144   RDKit’s built-in algorithm based on MMFF optimization and 32 from the torsion-based algorithm
145   described in (Zhou et al., 2023). The distances are averaged across all conformations and only
146   elements with a standard deviation below 0.3 Å are recorded.
147
148   Protein distance matrix To represent the protein’s 3D structure, we extract the positions of alpha-
149   carbon atom for each amino acid xp ∈ RP ×3 , where P is the number of amino acids in the protein,
150   and calculate the distance matrix D p = ∥xpi − xpj ∥ 1≤i,j≤P.
151

227   In case of molecules with permutation symmetries, such as benzene, a single docked pose might be
228   represented by several sets of atom positions, and thus different distance matrices. In such a case,
229
      the loss function may not assign the smallest value to the correct conformation. To alleviate this

230

231

232   the Permutation Loss introduced in (Zhu et al., 2022), with our approach applying permutations to
233   the ground truth (labels) instead of the predictions.

      3     E XPERIMENTS SETUP
249
      3.1   DATASETS
250
251   Training and test datasets Following (Corso et al., 2024), R APID D OCK is trained on PDBBind
252   (Liu et al., 2017) and BindingMOAD (Hu et al., 2005) and tested on Posebusters (Buttenschoen
253   et al., 2024) and DockGen (Corso et al., 2024) datasets. In addition, we augment the training dataset
254   by computationally generated apostructures – unbound protein structures in the absence of a ligand –
255   for about 30% of the training examples using AlphaFold-2 server, so that the model can see both the
256   unbound and ground-truth bound proteins (also called holostructures). The apostructures are only
257   used for defining the embeddings of the input distance matrices, while the loss is always computed
258
      based on the holostructures. This way, we allow the model to learn to deform the protein during the
      docking process.
259
260
      Ligands preparation The 3D coordinates of ligands are read from their PDB files and their graph
261
      structure is matched with their CCD reference. Entries for which such matching fails are filtered
262   out. Ligands with more than 128 heavy atoms are filtered out. Whenever calculating the ligand rigid
263   distance matrix is impossible because of a failure in calculating conformations or force fields, such
264   entry is removed.
265
266   3.2   T RAINING P ROCEDURE
267
268   R APID D OCK’s transformer architecture has 16 layers, each with 4 attention heads and a hidden size
269   of 512, resulting in approximately 60 million parameters. We found that such "deep and narrow"
      architecture performed better than shallower-but-wider models with the same number of parameters.

270
      Since similar physico-chemical principles govern both protein folding and ligand binding, we pre-
271   trained the model on a protein folding task with the hope that it would improve the docking perfor-
272   mance. We used approximately 440k structures generated by AlphaFold-2 on the SwissProt protein
273   database (Varadi et al., 2024; Gasteiger et al., 2001). More precisely, the model was trained to pre-
274   dict distances between amino acids, with a masking factor of 97% applied to the input distances,
275   which effectively simulates the protein folding task.
276
      Following pre-training, the model was fine-tuned on the molecular docking task on our training
277   dataset. In total, the training process took about three days on a machine with eight A100 GPUs.
278   The details are described in A.4

282
283   We evaluate R APID D OCK on two recent challenging benchmarks that were not part of the training
284
      dataset: the DockGen benchmark (Corso et al., 2024), and the Posebusters dataset (Buttenschoen
      et al., 2024). These benchmarks cover a wide range of structures across various protein domains.
285
      We calculate the heavy-atom Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) between the predicted ligand
286
      and the crystal (ground-truth) ligand atoms. We report both the median RMSD and the percent of
287
      examples with RMSD < 2Å (denoted % RMSD < 2Å), the latter being a widely-used threshold for
288   considering a molecule to be correctly docked (Bell & Zhang, 2019). A more detailed definition of
289   RMSD is described in A.2.

291   4.1   M AIN RESULTS
292
293   Accuracy and speed We compare R APID D OCK, both in terms of accuracy and runtimes, to a
294   classical docking tool SMINA (Koes et al., 2013) and three recent state-of-the-art blind docking
295   methods: AlphaFold-3 (Abramson et al., 2024), NeuralPLexer (Qiao et al., 2024), and DiffDock-L
296   (Corso et al., 2024), see Table 1. R APID D OCK is more accurate than DiffDock-L and about three
297   orders of magnitude faster. While the speed advantage over NeuralPLexer is less pronounced (about
298
      100× faster), R APID D OCK has an even larger accuracy advantage over that model. Finally, R APID -
      D OCK is a staggering four orders of magnitude faster than AlphaFold-3. Although AlphaFold-3
299
      achieves the highest accuracy, it is computationally intensive and therefore less practical for high-
300
      throughput screening studies with thousands of compounds.
301
302   Overall, the experiments show outstanding performance of R APID D OCK, which offers excellent
303   accuracy at unmatched speeds. The backbone transformer architecture is efficient and allows for
304
      performing the docking in a single pass. The model is also well-suited for applications to down-
      stream tasks such as predicting the binding strength, e.g., by direct fine-tuning.
305
306
      Unlocking Proteome-wide docking To validate R APID D OCK’s potential for proteome-wide
307
      docking studies, we used our model to run inference for twelve well-studied drugs and tox-
308
      ins, and entire human proteome consisting of 19222 proteins from The Human Protein Atlas
309   (https://proteinatlas.org/) (Uhlén et al., 2015). The whole process took on average
310   74 seconds per ligand on a machine with eight A100 GPUs.

371   5     R ELATED W ORK
372
373   Traditionally, blind docking consisted of two stages: pocket finding and molecule pose search.
374   Pocket-finding involves identifying cavities in the protein (Le Guilloux et al., 2009; Krivák & Hok-
375   sza, 2018; Yan et al., 2022) while molecule pose search aims to find the optimal molecule confor-
376   mation in the pocket based on scoring functions (Koes et al., 2013; McNutt et al., 2021). While still
377   useful, traditional docking approaches are being gradually replaced by deep learning tools (Corso
      et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Clyde et al., 2023).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      First successful deep learning models for blind molecular docking applied geometric graph neural
379   networks (Stärk et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). However, their RMSD-minimizing
380   objective has been considered inadequate for modeling the molecule’s position. Instead, DiffDock
381   (Corso et al., 2022) first formulated molecular docking as generative modeling of the ligand pose
382   distribution, with subsequent approaches adapting that paradigm (Lu et al., 2024; Nakata et al.,
383   2023; Corso et al., 2024). However, inference involves costly diffusions, making these approaches
384   two-to-three orders of magnitude slower than non-generative methods such as R APID D OCK.
385   Recently, a different approach to docking has emerged with RoseTTAFold All-Atom (Krishna
386   et al., 2024) and AlphaFold-3 (Abramson et al., 2024). These tools treat the protein-molecule
387   complex as a single structure to be reconstructed from the protein’s amino acid sequence and the
388   molecule. AlphaFold-3 claims state-of-the-art results in terms of accuracy (76.9% of examples with
389   RMSD < 2Å on PoseBusters). However, the docking-while-folding framework is extremely com-
390   putationally expensive. Consequently, AlphaFold-3’s reported inference runtimes are about four
391   orders of magnitude longer than those of R APID D OCK run on the same hardware. Although in-
392   crementally faster docking-while-folding approaches have been published (Qiao et al., 2024), their
393   speed is still orders of magnitude too slow for allowing large-scale studies envisioned in this paper.
394   To the best of our knowledge, R APID D OCK is the first end-to-end transformer-based model for blind
395   molecular docking. There are, however, notable recent efforts utilizing transformers or transformer-
396   like modules in docking tools. FeatureDock (Xue et al., 2024) is a transformer-based model for
397   pocket-based docking based on a grid of features around the pocket. ETDock (Yi et al., 2023) is
398   composed of several custom modules utilizing the attention mechanism. Our approach also shares
399   several ideas with other existing methods. The equivariance of 3D structures representations is
400
      a common feature (Stärk et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2024). Obtaining the molecule
      position based on predicted distances is done similarly in TANKBind (Lu et al., 2022) or ArtiDock
401
      (Voitsitskyi et al., 2024). Finally, (Gao et al., 2024) most recently proposed a symmetry-invariant
402
      loss function similar to ours.

405   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORK
406
407
      R APID D OCK achieves excellent accuracy and has a significant speed advantage over other methods.
408   There are several aspects, however, that need further work.
409
410   First, we plan to develop a confidence score for R APID D OCK’s predictions which is important for
411
      decision making. While this can be done by training another external light model, ideally we would
      like to extend R APID D OCK to so it can return its own confidence score.
412
413   Second, we plan to fine-tune R APID D OCK on ligand-protein binding strength prediction, including
414   to identify non-binding ligands. R APID D OCK’s speed would then allow for efficient identification
415   of all potentially toxic interactions of a drug across the human proteome (∼20k proteins), which is
416   not computationally feasible today and would greatly accelerate the drug discovery process.
417   Third, we plan to perform more detailed studies of the scaling properties and train larger models
418   on bigger datasets. Among others, we plan to extend pre-training to over 200M protein structures
419   predicted by AlphaFold 2 Varadi et al. (2024), and use the PLINDER (Durairaj et al., 2024) protein-
420   ligand datasets for the main training. This will allow us to extend R APID D OCK to tasks such as
421   protein folding or docking without requiring any input protein structure.

425
426   R APID D OCK is a truly fast and accurate molecular docking model that opens new possibilities for
427   in silico drug design. Trained on both protein folding and molecular docking, the model achieves
428
      accurate results in molecular docking both on holo- and apostructures.
429   R APID D OCK can already be used for target fishing, that is, screening existing drugs for potential
430   novel targets. We hope that, in the near future, R APID D OCK will provide drug designers with a
431   comprehensive view of potential drug interactions across the human proteome. This will unlock
      important research avenues in biology and machine learning.

671   A.6     O PTIONAL POST- PROCESSING OF THE RECONSTRUCTED LIGAND
672
      The positions of atoms obtained by minimizing the objective in Equation (2) may not correspond to
673
      a chemically valid molecule conformation. We found that the following post-processing step can be
674
      employed if such a conformation is desired. The step involves solving an additional optimization
675   problem with an objective function composed of three terms
676
677                                       Lpost = Lr + Ls + Lb + Lh ,
678
      where
679
680           • Lr is the same as in Equation (2).
681           • Ls is defined as the L1 loss between the non-negative entries in the molecule rigid distance
682             matrix described in Section 2.1 and the corresponding predicted distances.
683           • Lb is the bond-angle loss defined as the mean value of 1 − cos(∆α) where ∆α is the
684             difference between the predicted and expected bond angle, with expected angles defined
685             as those that are constant across the RDKit conformations. More precisely, the angles are
686             computed based on the molecule rigid distance matrix where only the non-negative entries
687             corresponding to pairs of atoms sharing a bond are considered.
688           • Lh is defined in an analogous way for dihedral angles.
689
690   The initial guess for the post-processing step is the output of the ligand reconstruction described
691   in Section 2.4. We found that after post-processing, 85% of predictions with RMSD < 2Å on the
692   Posebusters benchmark are also physically valid as defined in Buttenschoen et al. (2024).
