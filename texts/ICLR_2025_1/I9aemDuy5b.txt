                                                       A BSTRACT
011
                In this work, we analyze stochastic ℓp steepest descent for non-convex problems.
012
                Specifically, for p > 2, we establish ϵ-approximate stationarity (in expectation)
013                                                 ∗
                with respect to the dual norm ∥·∥pp∗ at a rate of O(ϵ−4 ), thereby generalizing the
014
                previous guarantees for signSGD (p = ∞). In addition, inspired by techniques
015             for the convex setting, we present a new accelerated ℓp descent method, called
016             S TACEY, based on interpolated primal-dual iterate sequences that are designed for
017             non-Euclidean smooth optimization settings. We compare our algorithm against
018             popular methods such as SGD, Adam, AdamW, and Lion on image classification
019             and pretraining language modeling tasks, and our results demonstrate the poten-
020             tial for both faster convergence and achieving higher accuracy. We further evaluate
021             our algorithm for different values of p across various models and datasets, high-
022             lighting the importance and efficiency of non-Euclidean methods as compared to
023
                standard Euclidean-based approaches.1

025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Stochastic first-order methods have proven essential for efficiently training modern deep learning
028   models. In addition to the basic stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm (Robbins & Monro,
029   1951)—along with its momentum-based variants (Nesterov, 1983; Polyak, 1964)—other methods,
030   such as AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011), Adam (Kingma, 2014), and AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter,
031   2019), incorporate second moment gradient information to provide per-coordinate scaling, and the
032   use of these adaptive techniques has since become standard for optimizing deep neural networks.
033   A related approach involves updating the parameters based on the sign of the (stochastic) gradi-
034   ent (Balles et al., 2020; Bernstein et al., 2018; Riedmiller & Braun, 1992). For example, the
035   Lion method (Chen et al., 2023)—discovered symbolically through a program search—combines
036   the sign-based step with a certain momentum scheme (which differs from that of the Signum
037   method (Bernstein et al., 2018)), and more recently, the Lion-K method (Chen et al., 2024) estab-
038   lishes a family of methods—for which Lion is a special case—defined in terms of a general convex
039   function K(·). These algorithms have been shown to be competitive with—and in some cases even
040
      outperform—popular adaptive methods, particularly for large language models.
041
      Guarantees for non-convex optimization. Given the empirical success of sign-based methods,
042
      we may then naturally ask why they perform as well as they do.2 Although globally optimizing
043
      non-convex problems is NP-hard in general, one may nevertheless instead consider the relaxed goal
044   of reaching approximate stationary points—sometimes strengthened to that of finding approximate
045   local minima (Agarwal et al., 2017; Carmon et al., 2018; Ge et al., 2015)—for both determinis-
046   tic (Carmon et al., 2017) and stochastic (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013) first-order methods. However, cru-
047   cial to these guarantees (and their limitations) are the assumptions we make, notable among them
048   being that the function is smooth, and there additionally lies behind these notions of stationarity (and
049   smoothness) a particular choice of norm.

      For example, in the case of SGD, Ghadimi & Lan (2013) establish approximate stationarity guar-
055   antees of the form E[∥∇f (x̂)∥2 ] ≤ ϵ (where ∥·∥2 denotes the standard Euclidean norm) under a
056   smoothness assumption similarly defined with respect to ∥·∥2 . On the other hand, Bernstein et al.
057   (2018) show how signSGD—which we may also view as (unscaled) stochastic steepest descent
058   w.r.t. ∥·∥∞ —can guarantee that E[∥∇f (x̂)∥1 ] ≤ ϵ, under a particular ℓ2 majorization assumption
059   (which, as we discuss further in Appendix B, implies smoothness w.r.t. ∥·∥∞ (Balles et al., 2020)).
060
061   Stochastic ℓp descent. Taken together, these two examples—albeit from opposite ends of the
062   (norm) spectrum—suggest a fundamental interplay between the (primal) norm that is the basis of
063   the steepest descent iteration (paired with smoothness defined in terms of the same norm) and the
064   (dual) norm used to measure approximate stationarity. Previous works, however, have focused on
065   either the case of stochastic steepest descent w.r.t. ∥·∥p for p = 2 (SGD) or p = ∞ (signSGD),
066   or else depend on unconventional noise assumptions (Carlson et al., 2015), thus leaving open the
067   question—which we address in Section 3—of extending these results to all 2 < p < ∞ under
068
      standard variance assumptions.
069   While at first glance this may appear to be a straightforward extension, in fact several technical
070   challenges arise when generalizing the analysis under ℓp smoothness assumptions, among them the
071   fact that the stochastic coordinate-wise scaled step is not an unbiased estimator of the (deterministic)
072   steepest descent direction (as for p = 2), nor is the magnitude the same across all coordinates of each
073   step (as for p = ∞).3 Indeed, extensions of this sort, in terms of general ℓp norms, for minimizing
074
      the dual norm of the gradient have been addressed in the deterministic, convex setting (Diakoniko-
      las & Guzmán, 2024) (as have related questions for minimizing the optimality gap (Guzmán &
075
      Nemirovski, 2015; Nemirovskii & Nesterov, 1985)), and so our results provide a natural counterpart
076
      for the stochastic, non-convex setting.
077
078   Even so, one may reasonably ask: why should we ever be concerned with any p other than 2 or ∞?
079
080   Problem geometry and acceleration. In fact, we believe a key observation here lies in determin-
081   ing the appropriate geometry for the problem at hand, most clearly reflected in not only the choice
082   of norm used for measuring smoothness, but also the magnitude of the smoothness parameter it-
083   self.4 (This is naturally to be balanced against the different dual norms—e.g., ∥∇f (x̂)∥2 for p = 2
084   vs. ∥∇f (x̂)∥1 for p = ∞—used to define approximate stationarity.) Unfortunately, it can be dif-
085   ficult to determine the precise smoothness parameters w.r.t. general ℓp norms (Balles et al., 2020);
086
      nevertheless, there is ample evidence (e.g., Adolphs et al. (2019); Becker et al. (1988); Cohen et al.
      (2021a); Ghorbani et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2024); Li et al. (2020); Li & Zhang (2024); Papyan
087
      (2018))—including empirical results of our own, as we later present in Section 5—to suggest that a
088
      different choice of p (outside of 2 or ∞) could allow for better adapting to the structure of certain
089   (deep learning) objectives.
090

092
      second lens through which we observe the potential for general p, namely that of acceleration (Allen-
      Zhu & Orecchia, 2017; Bai & Bullins, 2024; Nemirovskii & Nesterov, 1985; Nesterov, 1983; 2005).

152   ness assumptions, whereby we establish, for 2 < p < ∞, convergence guarantees of the form

      Building on the unaccelerated stochastic ℓp descent for non-convex settings, we present acceler-
300   ated versions of the method through the interpolation of two sequences in primal and dual spaces.
301   Indeed, this type of interpolation is the basis of the linear coupling framework (Allen-Zhu & Orec-
302   chia, 2017), wherein a steepest descent step is carefully coupled with a mirror descent step. Similar
303   “coupling” can also be found in Nesterov’s generalization of standard AGD to non-Euclidean set-
304   tings (Nesterov, 2005) and recent acceleration for ℓp descent in the deterministic convex setting
305   (Bai & Bullins, 2024). Inspired by these previous examples (and their successes, e.g., (Bullins,
306   2020; Jambulapati et al., 2019; Sherman, 2017; Sidford & Tian, 2018)), we introduce a practical
307   acceleration scheme called S TACEY, which is specifically designed for non-Euclidean methods. As
308
      presented in Algorithm 2, the algorithm takes the steepest descent step with respect to the ℓp -norm
      in line 4 and then a gradient step in line 5. The update on the variable θ is an interpolation between
309
      the two, controlled by the parameter τ . The algorithm generalizes linear coupling (Allen-Zhu &
310
      Orecchia, 2017) with non-Euclidean steepest descent while taking the mirror descent step with the
311   distance generating function chosen as 12 ∥·∥22 . We further specify the name as S TACEY(p,2) to clarify
312   the norms in which the steepest descent and mirror descent steps are taken.

514      5.4                              D ISCUSSION
515
516     As we observe throughout the experiments, S TACEY demonstrates superior performance over SGD,
517     which showcases its ability to adapt to a broader range of non-Euclidean geometries. This adaptabil-
518     ity verifies S TACEY’s convergence for general ℓp -norms, making it a better choice for optimization
519     tasks that present complex geometries and extend beyond the conventional Euclidean frameworks.
520     Compared with Adam and AdamW, S TACEY confirms that the introduced acceleration technique is
521     well-aligned with the principles of non-Euclidean optimization. The superior results validate that
522     S TACEY’s acceleration mechanism, which is purposefully designed for non-Euclidean spaces, out-
523
        performs the traditional adaptive methods that rely on Euclidean-centric assumptions. Furthermore,
        S TACEY’s improved performance over Lion highlights the effectiveness of interpolating primal and
524
        dual sequences as an acceleration strategy, in contrast to simply incorporating momentum. The
525
        primal-dual interpolation ensures a more balanced and stable progression towards optimality, lever-
526     aging information from both primal and dual sequences. This strategy allows S TACEY to achieve
527     faster convergence, even in challenging settings and complex tasks like large-scale image classifica-
528     tion and pretraining LLMs.

534     This paper investigates the steepest descent algorithm in ℓp norm for stochastic non-convex op-
535     timization. We establish for the stochastic ℓp descent algorithm an O(ϵ−4 ) convergence rate in
                                                                             ∗
536     expectation to a stationary point with respect to the dual norm ∥·∥pp∗ . Building on these techniques,
537     we further proposed an acceleration scheme for non-Euclidean methods, incorporated stochastic ℓp
538     descent with mirror descent, and presented an accelerated algorithm called S TACEY. We evaluated
539     the performance of S TACEY on large-scale image classification and pretraining language modeling
        tasks and achieved both faster convergence and higher accuracy compared to other methods.
