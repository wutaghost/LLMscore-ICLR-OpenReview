009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Block Floating Point (BFP) quantization offers a hardware-efficient numerical
012            range trade-off. Previous studies have quantized weights and activations to an
013            extremely low precision using the BFP arithmetic. However, as the precision
014            of weights and activations diminishes, we identify that accumulation becomes
015            a hardware bottleneck in the BFP MAC. Nevertheless, existing attempts to de-
016
               crease the precision of accumulation in matrix multiplication generally preserve
               model performance through training with a pre-selected, fixed accumulation pre-
017
               cision. Nonetheless, selecting an unduly low precision leads to notable perfor-
018
               mance degradation, and these studies lack an effective approach to establish the
019            lower precision limit, potentially incurring considerable training costs. Hence,
020            we propose a statistical method to analyze the impact of reduced accumulation
021            precision on the inference of deep learning applications. Due to the presence of
022            fixed-point accumulation and floating-point accumulation in BFP matrix multi-
023            plication, we formulate a set of equations to relate the data range of fixed-point
024            multiply-accumulate operations and the effects of floating-point swamping to the
025            parameters of BFP quantization, the length of accumulation, model weights, and
026            the minimum number of bits required for accumulation, thereby determining the
027
               appropriate accumulation precision. Applied to MMLU Llama2-7B, SQuAD-v1.1
               BERT-Large and BERT-Base and CIFAR-10 ResNet-50, our precision settings
028
               yield performance close to the FP32 baseline. Meanwhile, further precision re-
029
               duction degrades performance, indicating our approach’s proximity to precision
030            limits. Guided by our equations, the hardware exhibits a 13.7%-28.7% enhance-
031            ment in area and power efficiency over high-precision accumulation under identi-
032            cal quantization configuration, and it demonstrated a 10.3× area reduction and an
033            11.0× power reduction compared to traditional BFP implementations.

038   Deep learning technology has achieved significant success in a wide range of applications through
039
      the training of large-scale deep models with extensive datasets. Concurrently, this approach has im-
      posed substantial storage and computational burdens. Quantization emerges as a promising method
040
      to reduce the cost of deep learning by diminishing the bit-width of data flow within models, thereby
041
      reducing storage and computational overhead (Deng et al., 2020). As an effective numerical sys-
042   tem for deep learning, Block Floating Point (BFP) strikes a favorable balance between dynamic
043   range and hardware cost (Drumond et al., 2018). Specifically, previous studies have demonstrated
044   that low-precision BFP formats can achieve accuracy comparable to FP32 under various deep learn-
045   ing workloads (Darvish Rouhani et al., 2020; Drumond et al., 2018; Soloveychik et al., 2022; Köster
046   et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2022). However, it is observed that as the quantization precision decreases,
047   accumulation becomes a hardware bottleneck in BFP MAC. As illustrated in Figure 1(b), the area
048   occupied by the accumulation component accounts for 17.8%, 33.7%, and 64.4% for BFP16, BFP8,
049   and BFP4, respectively. Therefore, reducing accumulation precision can further enhance hardware
050
      efficiency on top of lowering quantization precision.

      2023; Ni et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022). Nevertheless, methods to mitigate the impact

103   2.1           R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Our work endeavors to establish a theoretical framework for determining the boundary of accumu-
106   lator bit-width for the BFP format. Although this topic has not been previously discussed, there
107   has been extensive exploration of fixed-point accumulator bit-width and floating-point accumulator
      bit-width.


                                                                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Fixed-point accumulator bit-width WrapNet (Ni et al., 2020) leverages the cyclic nature of in-
109   teger computer arithmetic by inserting a differentiable cyclic activation function, rendering neural
110   networks robust to integer overflow. This allows for the selection of ultra-low-precision fixed-point
111   accumulator bit-width. However, they also note that high overflow rates can lead to training insta-
112   bility. A2Q (Colbert et al., 2023) adheres to the principle of avoiding overflow and approach the
113   determination of fixed-point accumulator bit-width boundaries from both the data type and weight
114   perspectives. Xie et al. introduce a quantization range mapping factor α to maximize data repre-
115   sentation capabilities while avoiding overflow under a specified accumulator bit-width (Xie et al.,
116   2020). While their training method can ensure model accuracy at an appropriate accumulator bit-
117
      width, they do not provide an efficient approach to determine the boundary of the accumulator
      bit-width.
118
119   Floating-point accumulation bit-width Wang et al. illustrate that the phenomenon of swamping
120   significantly limits the potential for reducing accumulation precision (Wang et al., 2018). To address
121   this issue, they propose two novel techniques: chunk-based accumulation and floating-point stochas-
122   tic rounding. These methods allow for the training of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) even when
123
      the accumulation bit-width is decreased to FP16, thereby circumventing the constraints imposed by
      swamping. Additionally, Sakr et al. establish a connection between the decrease in accumulation
124
      precision and the training efficiency of DNNs by examining how the exacerbation of swamping
125
      phenomena, due to reducing accumulation precision, affects the variance of matrix multiplication
126   outcomes (Sakr et al., 2019). Based on this analysis, they select an appropriate accumulation bit-
127   width.
128
129   2.2   BFP F ORMAT, BFP Q UANTIZATION AND BFP MAC
130
131   BFP format is a numerical representation method wherein a group of data shares one exponent.
132   Quantization methods that adhere to this data format can be classified as fixed-point uniform quan-
133   tization (Jacob et al., 2018). Fixed-point uniform quantization can be categorized into multiple
134   levels of methods based on the granularity of quantization. Quantization granularity varies, with
135
      per-tensor being the coarsest, using a single scaling factor for the entire matrix. Finer granularity
      is achieved through per-channel or per-token scaling. Block-wise quantization further refines this
136
      by dividing channels or tokens into blocks with a step size, yielding BFP quantization as a distinct
137
      variant with scaling factors as powers of two. Therefore, BFP quantization (Rouhani et al., 2023;
138   Darvish Rouhani et al., 2023) can be expressed as:
139
                                              X                  |X|
140                                   Xq = ⌈ s ⌋, s = max(⌊log2 ⌋) − N + 1                                (1)
                                              2
141
      where ⌈·⌋ is the rounding function, X is the object to be quantized, Xq is the corresponding quantized
142
      result, s is the scaling factor obtained through quantization, and N is the number of bits used for the
143
      low-precision representation.
144
145   The BFP multiplier-accumulator architecture is bifurcated into two primary modules: the INT-MAC
146   (Integer Multiply and Accumulate) and the FP-ACC (Floating Point Accumulate). The INT-MAC
147
      comprises a set of signed fixed-point multipliers, an addition tree, and an exponent summing adder,
      corresponding to the fixed-point multiplication and accumulation within the BFP inner product. This
148
      phase is termed intra-block computation. Conversely, the FP-ACC module includes normalization,
149
      an exponent alignment unit, an adder, and a fixed-to-floating-point conversion block, handling the
150   floating-point accumulation of the BFP inner product. This stage is identified as inter-block com-
151   putation. In Figure 1(d), we elucidate the implications of BFP format, intra-block and inter-block
152   operations using a straightforward example. SE denotes the shared exponent, A and B represent
153   the two matrices involved in the matrix multiplication computation, respectively, C denotes the re-
154   sulting matrix, INT signifies the fixed-point result after intra-block fixed-point accumulation, and F
155   indicates the number that has been normalized and is ready for floating-point accumulation.

170   3.2    M EAN , VARIANCE AND THE F ROBENIUS NORM
171
172   Accumulation overflow is a critical issue to be addressed in the context of fixed-point quantization,
173
      which can have a significant impact on model performance. As shown in the Table 1, we observe
      that minor overflow rates cause slight performance decline, but increased rates lead to significant
174
      degradation in model performance. In the design of the MAC unit, it is common practice to calculate
175
      the theoretical maximum data range that the partial sums can reach based on the input data format
176   to prevent overflow. Equation 2 is a formula for calculating the maximum bit width required for the
177   partial sums based on the input data format. Here, both A and W are signed numbers.
178
179       K(2min(Awidth −1,Wwidth −1) − 2Awidth +Wwidth −2 ) ≤ P artial Sum ≤ K2Awidth +Wwidth −2 (2)
180   In deep learning models, partial sums rarely reach the theoretical extreme values because it is nearly
181   impossible for all input tensors to be quantized to the extreme values. Consequently, the range
182   derived from Equation 2 typically exceeds the actual data distribution. By the 3σ principle, the vast
183   majority of data falls within (µ − 3σ, µ + 3σ). Thus, bounding the partial sums by their mean and
184   variance can mitigate data range wastage.
185   In the inference phase of deep learning models, the FP32 precision matrix multiplication is regarded
186   as the benchmark for state-of-the-art performance. The inference quality is inferred to be supe-
187   rior when the outcomes of matrix multiplications using alternative precisions are closer to the FP32
188   results. Consequently, the challenge of correlating data precision with model accuracy can be re-
189   framed as one of determining the proximity between the reduced-precision result matrix and the
190   FP32 precision result matrix. For this purpose, we focus on numerical approximation and employ
191   the Frobenius norm (Suh et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2020) as the metric for comparison.

524   6     C ONCLUSION
525
526   We present an analytical approach to predict the optimal accumulation precision for BFP GEMM
527   operations in deep learning inference, balancing performance with precision. Our experiments con-
528   firm that this precision is near the limit while maintaining comparable performance to the base-
529   line. Additionally, we demonstrate the effectiveness of segmented accumulation in further reducing
530   floating-point precision. An interesting phenomenon is observed, where the decline in model per-
531   formance with decreasing accumulation precision varies under different quantization configurations.
532
      Notably, highly quantized models exhibit a lower robustness and are more susceptible to reaching
      the precision boundary. Therefore, incorporating the impact of quantization on model robustness
533
      into our theoretical analysis could further improve our theoretical framework. We believe that our
534
      work provides theoretical support for the design of MAC units in deep learning inference.

785   E    A PPLYING T HEOREM TO T RAINING TASKS
786
787   We endeavor to apply our theoretical framework to training tasks. As illustrated in the Figure 6,
788   we trained ResNet-18 on the CIFAR-10 image classification task with a block size of 128 under
789   BFP8 quantization configuration for 90 epochs with a learning rate of 0.1. Given that the maxi-
790   mum accumulation lengths for ResNet-18 in forward, backward, and gradient computation matrix
791   multiplications are 4608, 4608, and 131072, respectively, our theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) de-
792   duces that the corresponding floating-point accumulation mantissa widths for these three types of
793
      matrix multiplications are 4, 4, and 8 bits. We used the training results with FP32 accumulation as a
      baseline and conducted ablation studies on the forward floating-point accumulation mantissa width,
794
      backward floating-point accumulation mantissa width, and gradient computation floating-point ac-
795
      cumulation precision mantissa width by controlling variables. The experimental results are depicted
796   in the figure. Based on these results, we observed that reducing accumulation precision within an
797   appropriate range does not affect the convergence of model training. Specifically, the accumulation
798   precision for backward and gradient computation has a minimal impact on model convergence, while
799   the forward accumulation precision has a relatively greater influence. The forward results serve as
800   the foundation for gradient computation and backward propagation, demanding higher precision.
801   Therefore, when intolerable loss occurs due to an overly small accumulation bit width, the model
802   struggles to converge to a satisfactory local optimum. In summary, our experiment reveals that the
803   data precision requirement for the forward process is higher than that for backward and gradient
804
      computation, thus validating the applicability of our theory in selecting accumulation precision for
      training tasks.
