                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Reward learning plays a pivotal role in Reinforcement Learning from Human
010
               Feedback (RLHF), ensuring the alignment of language models. The Bradley-
011
               Terry (BT) model stands as the prevalent choice for capturing human preferences
012            from datasets containing pairs of chosen and rejected responses. In preference
013            modeling, the focus is not on absolute values but rather on the reward difference
014            between chosen and rejected responses, referred to as preference strength. Thus,
015            precise evaluation of preference strength holds paramount importance in prefer-
016            ence modeling. However, an easily overlooked factor significantly affecting pref-
017            erence strength measurement is that human attitudes towards two responses may
018            not solely indicate a preference for one over the other and ties are also a common
019            occurrence. To address this, we propose the adoption of the generalized Bradley-
020
               Terry model – the Bradley-Terry model with ties (BTT) – to accommodate tied
               preferences, thus leveraging additional information. We prove that even with the
021
               access to the true distributions of prompt and response, disregarding ties can lead
022
               to a notable bias in preference strength measurement. Comprehensive experi-
023            ments further validate the advantages of incorporating ties in preference model-
024            ing. Notably, fine-tuning with BTT significantly outperforms fine-tuning with BT
025            on synthetic preference datasets with ties, labeled by state-of-the-art open-source
026            LLMs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019;
032   Ouyang et al., 2022) has played a pivotal role in aligning large language models (LLMs) (Kenton
033
      et al., 2021), enhancing specific capabilities of LLMs in various fields, such as summarization (Sti-
      ennon et al., 2020), coding (Gao et al., 2023), and medical assistance (Moor et al., 2023). A crucial
034
      component of the RLHF process is the reward model, which serves as the primary mechanism for
035
      integrating human preferences and feedback into the learning process (Wang et al., 2024). The re-
036   ward model guides the optimization procedure of RLHF towards objectives aligned with human
037   preferences (Kaufmann et al., 2023). Therefore, the accuracy of the reward model greatly affects
038   or even determines the effectiveness of alignment with human preferences. Moreover, the direct
039   preference optimization (DPO) method (Rafailov et al., 2024) utilizes LLMs to implicitly represent
040   the reward model through mathematical transformations, bypassing the complex RL optimization
041   phase and focusing solely on the reward modeling phase. As a simplified alternative to RLHF, DPO
042   has demonstrated computational efficiency and competitive performance compared to RLHF.
043   To learn a reward model from human preferences, obtaining high-quality human preference data
044   is crucial (Wang et al., 2024), typically achieved by having human labelers annotate previously
045   collected data consisting of a prompt and a pair of responses (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022).
046   Conventional approaches (Rafailov et al., 2024; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020) often
047   assume that the latent human preference model follows the Bradley-Terry (BT) (Bradley & Terry,
048   1952) model, where the preference distribution can be expressed as:

092   In this paper, we propose to model human preferences with the Bradley-Terry model with ties (BTT)
093   (Rao & Kupper, 1967). We demonstrate that if we blindly adopt BT as our preference model (with
094   the true latent preference model being BTT) can lead to significant bias in measuring preference
095   strength due to model mismatch. To illustrate the model mismatch problem occurring in conven-
096   tional preference datasets lacking ties when simply assuming the latent preference model is BT, we
097   first introduce a simulated preference generation procedure. Specifically, we generate two prefer-
098   ence datasets—one with ties and one without ties. By analyzing the maximum likelihood estimates
099
      (MLE) based on BTT and BT preference models on these datasets respectively, we quantify the
      bias in measuring preference strength due to the model mismatch. Furthermore, we show that al-
100
      though the bias term is bounded, it can still have a substantial impact. Since most conventional
101
      preference datasets lack ties, we propose a novel method to address the preference model mismatch
102   problem, which subtracts the bias term from the MLE loss function to recover the true preference
103   strength measurement. This method can be viewed as a variant of adaptive margin (Touvron et al.,
104   2023) when training the reward model and a variant of DPO with offset (ODPO) when training
105   DPO (Amini et al., 2024). To further demonstrate the benefit of incorporating ties in preference
106   modeling, we use state-of-the-art open-source LLMs to simulate human judgment and label ties in
107   a conventional preference dataset without ties, and then evaluate the fine-tuned models on this syn-
      thetic preference dataset with ties. It is important to note that the main limitation of this paper is

      the inability to conduct experiments on real human-labeled preference datasets with ties, due to the
109   scarcity of such datasets and the high cost of manual annotation and evaluation. Addressing this
110   limitation could be considered for future work.

      Main Contributions. Our contributions can be outlined as follows:
113
             • We advocate for the inclusion of tie options when labeling preference data, aligning with
114            human preference habits. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose the use
115            of BTT to model human preference.
116
             • We derive the bias in measuring preference strength caused by model mismatch when
117

               LLMs, and evaluate fine-tuning with BTT and BT on this dataset. The results show that
122
               fine-tuning with BTT consistently outperforms fine-tuning with BT.

127   The reward model plays a crucial role in RLHF, guiding LLMs towards objectives aligned with hu-
128   man preferences (Christiano et al., 2017; Kaufmann et al., 2023). Recent related work has addressed
129   various aspects of reward modeling. Wang et al. (2024) conducted a comprehensive study on reward
130   models, proposing a method to measure the strength of preferences within the data and introduc-
131
      ing contrastive learning to enhance the ability of reward models to distinguish between chosen and
      rejected responses. Zhu et al. (2024) analyzed reward overfitting and overoptimization problems
132
      in RLHF, proposing to mitigate them using an iterative data smoothing method. Dai et al. (2023)
133
      proposed training a cost model in addition to the reward model to decouple human preferences
134   regarding helpfulness and harmlessness.
135
136   As a simplified alternative to RLHF, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) has achieved significant success and
137
      impact. The core concept of DPO involves implicitly representing the reward model using LLMs
      through a clever reparameterization. Recently, there has been extensive research focused on enhanc-
138
      ing and broadening the scope of DPO. Amini et al. (2024) propose DPO with an offset (ODPO),
139
      where the likelihood difference between the preferred and dispreferred response must exceed an
140   offset value. Zhou et al. (2023) extend DPO for multiple alignment objectives by training LMs as
141   implicit collective reward models, combining all objectives with specific weightings. Chowdhury
142   et al. (2024) propose robust DPO methods to mitigate the bias introduced by noise in preference
143   data on average.
144
      The preference model serves as the foundation for reflecting human feedback, with the Bradley-
145
      Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) being the most commonly used preference model in
146   RLHF. Indeed, various generalized models based on the BT model have been proposed to address
147   different scenarios, such as handling home advantage (Agresti, 2012), ties (Rao & Kupper, 1967),
148   multiple comparisons (Plackett, 1975; Luce, 2005), and team comparisons (Huang et al., 2006). In
149   particular, the Plackett-Luce (PL) model, a popular extension for handling multiple comparisons,
150   has also found application in RLHF (Zhu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024).

155

      First, we conduct a simulation experiment to show that, when the ground truth reward function

356   trained with the BT model exhibits a stronger preference strength bias compared to the one trained

      models. We follow Rafailov et al. (2024), fine-tuning on Anthropic’s HH-RLHF dataset (Bai et al.,

471   6   D ISCUSSION
472
473   In this paper, we introduced the concept of incorporating ties into preference modeling. Specifically,
474   we applied the generalized Bradley-Terry model—the Bradley-Terry model with ties—to more ac-
475
      curately capture human preferences. Additionally, we analyzed the bias in measuring preference
      strength due to model mismatch and proposed a novel method to mitigate this bias. Extensive ex-
476
      periments demonstrate the benefits of considering ties in preference modeling. A limitation of this
477
      work is the absence of real human-annotated preference datasets with ties, as collecting such data is
478   both expensive and time-consuming. Future work involving human-labeled preference datasets with
479   ties could significantly improve the effectiveness of preference modeling.
