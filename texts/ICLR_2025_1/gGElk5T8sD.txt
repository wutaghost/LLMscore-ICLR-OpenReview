009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly en-
012
               hanced their ability to process long contexts, yet a notable gap remains in gen-
013            erating long, aligned outputs. This limitation stems from a training gap where
014            pre-training lacks effective instructions for long-text generation, and post-training
015            data primarily consists of short query-response pairs. Current approaches, such as
016            instruction backtranslation and behavior imitation, face challenges including data
017            quality, copyright issues, and constraints on proprietary model usage. In this pa-
018            per, we introduce an innovative iterative training framework called Self-Lengthen
019            that leverages only the intrinsic knowledge and skills of LLMs without the need
020            for auxiliary data or proprietary models. The framework consists of two roles: the
021
               Generator and the Extender. The Generator produces the initial response, which
               is then split and expanded by the Extender. This process results in a new, longer
022
               response, which is used to train both the Generator and the Extender iteratively.
023
               Through this process, the models are progressively trained to handle increasingly
024            longer responses. Experiments on benchmarks and human evaluations show that
025            Self-Lengthen outperforms existing methods in long-text generation, when ap-
026            plied to top open-source LLMs such as Qwen2 and LLaMA3.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recently we have witnessed significant breakthroughs in Large Language Models (LLMs), accom-
035   panied by extensive research aimed at improving their alignment with human demands (Cao et al.,
036   2024; Gao et al., 2024; Quan, 2024a;b). One key research area is enhancing LLMs’ abilities to
037   manage increasingly long contexts (Han et al., 2023; Pawar et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023). While
038   much of the existing research on long context LLM alignment emphasizes processing lengthy in-
039
      puts, and several open-source models like Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024) and Llama3 (Dubey et al.,
      2024) herds of models already demonstrate impressive capabilities in long context understanding
040
      tasks, such as Needle in a Haystack (Kamradt, 2023), a notable gap remains in the ability of LLMs
041
      to generate long aligned outputs effectively, as illustrated in Figure 1, and this issue cannot be easily
042   addressed by manipulating decoding strategies. We analyze that this is due to a training gap: during
043   the pre-training stage, despite having access to a vast array of long text sources, there is a lack of
044   effective instructions to cultivate this capability. Conversely, in the post-training stage, the majority
045   of human-conducted or AI-augmented query-response pairs are short, which leads to the trained
046   LLMs facing challenges in generating long outputs.
047
      To address this issue, current efforts employ two strategies: instruction backtranslation (Li et al.,
048   2023) and behavior imitation (Xu et al., 2024), to construct post-training data with long responses.
049   Instruction backtranslation leverages existing long-form, high-quality human-written texts, such as
050   those found in magazines or books, as responses, and uses them to generate corresponding queries.
051   However, obtaining high-quality data that spans various long-generation tasks and domains is chal-
052   lenging, and much of the available data presents risks of copyright infringement (Maini et al., 2024).
053   On the other hand, behavior imitation aims to cost-effectively leverage proprietary models like GPT-
      4 to generate lengthy responses. This approach, which assumes the existence of a more proficient

081
          In this paper, for the first time, we demonstrate how we can stimulate the long-generation ability
          using only the LLM’s intrinsic knowledge and skills, without the need for any auxiliary data or
082
          proprietary models. At the core of our approach is an iterative training framework called Self-
083
          Lengthen, which consists of two roles: the Generator and the Extender. The Generator is responsible
084       for generating the initial response, while the Extender’s task is to lengthen the response. Specifically,
085       at the beginning, both the Generator and the Extender are initialized using an existing instruct model.
086       Given a query, the Generator first outputs a response. Then, we split the response into two parts
087       based on punctuation. The Extender is prompted to expand the first part, after which it combines the
088       expanded version of the first part with the original response to revise and expand the second part.
089       This procedure results in a new response that is approximately twice as long as the original. Next, the
090       Generator is trained to directly produce this longer response based on the query, while the Extender
091       is fine-tuned to extend the original response into this longer one. This results in updated versions
092
          of the Generator and Extender after each round of training. By iteratively applying this process, the
          Generator and Extender are progressively trained to handle increasingly longer responses. Once the
093
          Generator is able to produce outputs of the desired length, it can be used to generate long query-
094
          response pairs, effectively creating the post-training data needed for long-form tasks.
095
096       Experiments conducted on both benchmarks and human evaluations demonstrate that Self-Lengthen
097       consistently achieves better long-text generation capabilities compared to instruction backtranslation
098
          and behavior imitation, even without the need for additional long-form text data or proprietary mod-
          els. A more detailed analysis reveals that with each iteration of Self-Lengthen training, both the
099
          Generator and the Extender are able to produce content that is approximately twice as long as the
100
          output from the previous round, while maintaining the same level of quality.

191   4     M ETHODOLOGY: S ELF -L ENGTHEN
192
193   Based on user instructions, our algorithm empowers the seed instruct model to autonomously iterate
194   to enhance the ability to produce longer outputs. This involves a process of gradually expanding
195   the model’s output while training it in reverse, effectively increasing the length and content of its
196   generated output step by step. We show our high-level methodology in Figure 2.
197
198   4.1   I NDUCTIVELY EXTENDING LONG OUTPUT ABILITY
199
200   The workflow of our Self-Lengthen can be split into four essential steps: 1) instruction augmen-
201   tation, 2) initial response generation, 3) response extension, and 4) fine-tuning the next iteration’s
202
      models. These four steps will be iteratively conducted and inductively extend the LLM’s output
      length.
203
204
      Instruction Augmentation We employ self-instructing to bootstrap our instruction set. Specif-
205
      ically, we maintain an instruction pool and randomly select two instructions at a time as few-shot
206
      learning examples to generate a new instruction. This approach fosters deep exploration and a
207   diverse array of instructions. Additionally, we employ the seed LLM to validate the generated in-
208   structions, filtering out any unsuitable instructions for producing long responses.
209
210   Initial Response Generation In the i-th iteration, we employ the Generator Geni , which has
211   been fine-tuned from the previous iteration, to produce initial responses y based on the augmented
212   instructions x. As the Generator’s ability to generate long outputs improves with each iteration, the
213   length of these initial responses will also grow progressively.
214
215   Response Extension After generating the initial response y, the next step is to extend its length
      to create a longer response y + . The vanilla extension method, namely directly using a prompt to


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      generate an extended response, is limited to the model’s upper length constraint and cannot yield
217   continuous increases during iterations. To overcome this, we propose a two-stage extension method
218   that allows us to reach and exceed the model’s length limits.
219
220   • In Stage 1, we use the first half of the initial response, denoted as y[: 21 ] , as input for the extension
221     process, Exti . This yields an extended version y1+ for the first half of the content. Because the
222     input is shorter, the model can produce a significantly longer output than the input—potentially
223     expanding y[: 12 ] to almost twice its original length, matching about len(y).
224
      • In Stage 2, we provide the entire initial response y to Exti for further expansion. Here, y1+ serves
225
        as the existing extended content for in-context learning (ICL), illustrating the structure and content
226     of the extension. Since y1 is derived from the first half of y, we anticipate that this approach will
227     facilitate further expansion of the subsequent content in a similar manner.
228
229   A key technique used in this process is the removal of the last third of y1+ , denoted as y1+ [: 2 ] . This

267   of the following issues:

291   4.2   F INAL A LIGNMENT
292
293   Our goal isn’t just to acquire a specific Generator or Extender; we aim to enhance the long text
294   generation capabilities of any seed model while maintaining its overall performance. To accomplish
295   this, after multiple iterations of our method, we will gather query-response pairs and implement
296
      length controls in the queries to create SFT data.
297
298
      Data Collecting We will gather data from the initial responses y and extended responses y + , along
      with their corresponding prompts x, in each iteration to create a more extensive dataset. This dataset
299
      will encompass a variety of prompt lengths and types. Additionally, we will use the Generators in
300
      each iteration to generate more data, further enriching our dataset.
301
302
      Query Rephrasing for Length Control In our earlier steps, we did not sufficiently highlight the
303
      importance of length control in the input instructions. As a result, the Generators produce responses
304   of varying lengths for the same instruction across different iterations. To develop a general model,
305   we will now incorporate length constraints in the input instructions. Specifically, we will first count
306   the length of each response, denoted as len(y), and then utilize our seed LLM to integrate this
307   constraint into the input instructions. We will strategically design prompts to create a diverse range
308   of output instructions that encompass various types of length restrictions.

      In our evaluation, we begin by testing the performance of several widely-used open-source and

421   and LongWriter datasets either lack appropriate length requirements in their queries or have unsuit-
422   able length constraints, resulting in results that fall significantly short of expectations. To ensure a
423   fair evaluation, we introduce length constraints by applying the same workflow and prompts to their
424   original queries. Finally, we compare the improvements achieved against the backbone LLM.
425
      The results are presented in Table 2. We observe that most existing open-source and proprietary
426   LLMs struggle to generate outputs longer than 4,000 words, consistent with our preliminary study.
427   However, after fine-tuning on our sourced dataset, we achieved a significant increase in output length
428   compared to general models. This improvement is also advantageous when compared to backtrans-
429   lation and the plan-and-write approaches. Furthermore, we find that the quality of the outputs out-
430   performs the other two methods overall and surpasses that of the backbone model by a considerable
431   margin. The only aspect that is reduced is clarity, which is understandable, as longer outputs tend to
      increase the read complexity, especially when the content is much richer.

500   6   C ONCLUSION
501
502   In this work, we introduced Self-Lengthen, which demonstrates for the first time how to elicit high-
503
      quality, length-following responses from existing LLMs without relying on additional data or pro-
      prietary models. Self-Lengthen alternately trains a Generator and an Extender, where the Extender
504
      incrementally expands the Generator’s responses in segments, serving as a training objective to en-
505
      hance both the length of the Generator’s subsequent response and the Extender’s subsequent exten-
506   sion. Through three macro iterations, we can produce responses that are about eight times longer,
507   thereby constructing high-quality (query, long response) training data. Our method has been rig-
508   orously evaluated using both benchmark metrics and human assessments, which have consistently
509   confirmed the quality of the training data produced by Self-Lengthenand the effectiveness of the
510   trained models.
