009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) robustness is a desired property of computer vision
012            models. Improving model robustness requires high-quality signals from robust-
013            ness benchmarks to quantify progress. While various benchmark datasets such
014            as ImageNet-C were proposed in the ImageNet era, most ImageNet-C corruption
015            types are no longer OOD relative to today’s large datasets scraped from the web,
016            which already contain common corruptions such as blur or JPEG compression
017            artifacts. Consequently, these standard benchmarks are no longer well-suited for
018
               evaluating OOD robustness in the era of web-scale datasets. Indeed, recent mod-
               els show saturating scores on ImageNet-era OOD benchmarks, indicating that it
019
               is unclear whether models trained on web-scale datasets truly become better at
020
               OOD generalization or whether they have simply been exposed to the test distor-
021            tions during training. To address this, we here introduce LAION-C as a bench-
022            mark alternative for ImageNet-C. LAION-C consists of six novel distortion types
023            across five severity levels designed to be OOD, even for web-scale datasets such
024            as LAION. In a comprehensive evaluation of state-of-the-art models, we find that
025            the LAION-C dataset poses significant challenges to contemporary models. We
026            additionally conducted a psychophysical experiment to evaluate the difficulty of
027            our proposed corruptions for human observers, enabling a comparison of models
028            to lab-quality human robustness data. We observe a paradigm shift in OOD gener-
029
               alization: from humans outperforming models, to the best models now matching
               or outperforming the best human observers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Vision models have been a cornerstone of modern machine learning, driving breakthroughs in di-
035
      verse applications. In recent years, large-scale vision models such as vision transformers (Doso-
036   vitskiy et al., 2021) and ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022), trained on expansive web-scale datasets like
037   LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022), have pushed the boundaries of performance on standard bench-
038   marks. However, the continued advancement and reliable evaluation of these models depends on the
039   availability of datasets that effectively challenge model robustness and generalization capabilities.
040
      ImageNet-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) has long stood as the de facto standard for OOD eval-
041
      uation, particularly for models trained on ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015). It contains images
042   that are systematically different from those in ImageNet, meaning that models trained on ImageNet
043   must robustly generalize to perform well on ImageNet-C. Previous work (e.g., Hendrycks & Diet-
044   terich, 2019) found that OOD generalization is not trivial to achieve: Many vision models do indeed
045   struggle with OOD datasets like ImageNet-C even if they perform well on ImageNet. Hence, these
046   types of unfamiliar inputs are crucial for evaluating the robustness of machine learning models since
047   they are indicative of performance on unexpected input; a challenge that many deployed models
048   face. Modern models trained on much larger web-scale datasets, e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021),
049   exhibit much better performance on classic OOD datasets than IN-trained models, potentially sug-
050
      gesting that they have learned robust representations which better generalize to unseen data.
051   However, as modern training datasets are scaled well beyond ImageNet, existing OOD bench-
052   marks might not be truly OOD with respect to web-scale datasets anymore. OOD datasets such
053   as ImageNet-C were explicitly created to be OOD with respect to the most popular dataset at that
      time: ImageNet. ImageNet-C contains images with corruptions potentially relevant for practical ap-

      Given the importance of OOD generalization in practice, there is a pressing need for a new bench-

094
      OOD dataset for the era of web-scale vision models. Our contributions are as follows:
095
096   1. Given that existing OOD datasets are often no longer OOD for models trained on web-scale
097      datasets, we introduce LAION-C, a novel benchmark dataset with six manually designed cor-
098      ruption types and 16 superclasses to evaluate the robustness of web-scale vision models.
099   2. We conduct a comprehensive performance analysis of various models on LAION-C and report a
100      robust human OOD generalization baseline obtained through psychophysical experiments with
101      19 participants, collecting 11,400 trials in a highly controlled laboratory environment.
102
      3. The resulting data serves as an OOD benchmark for current and future models, enabling not
103      only an assessment of their generalization ability on truly OOD data but also providing insights
104      into the discrepancies between human and machine perception, observing a paradigm shift in
105      OOD generalization: from humans outperforming models to the best models now matching or
106      outperforming the best human observers.

138   2         M ETHODS
139
140   2.1                 C ONSTRUCTING N EW OOD D ISTORTIONS
141
142   As described in the introduction and depicted in Fig. 1, ImageNet-C is not OOD for models trained
143   on large-scale datasets. Given the limitations of existing benchmarks like ImageNet-C, we develop
144   a novel dataset specifically designed to challenge these foundation models more rigorously. Our
145
      dataset introduces six carefully designed, fully synthetic distortions that models have not encoun-
      tered during training. These distortions are designed to be OOD even for web-scale datasets (as
146
      supported by quantitative evidence presented later). Hence, models truly need to generalize beyond
147
      their training distributions to perform well on this benchmark which we term LAION-C.
148
149
150   Distortions The core idea behind our distortions is to intentionally disrupt visual consistency and
151   perceptual cues that models rely on for image classification, such as texture (Geirhos et al., 2019).
152
      Following ImageNet-C, each distortion consists of five different intensity levels. The distortions
      capture a range of visual challenges ranging from disruptions of local image information to more
153
      global structural alterations, as described below and illustrated in Fig. 2.
154
155
      • Mosaic: The original image is broken down into smaller tiles, each replaced by a chromatically
156
        similar picture. This patchwork creates a mosaic effect that disrupts edges and textures while
157
        introducing contextually irrelevant information.
158
159   • Glitched: The original image undergoes an artistic digital corruption with horizontal lines over-
160     laying shifted image segments and color channel shifts. This dislocates the global contextual
161     structure of the image. While the concept of such glitchy images has been explored in earlier
        work (Kaufmann et al., 2019), our transformation introduces a more intense corruption.


                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      • Vertical Lines: The original image is deconstructed into bent vertical line segments. This distor-
163     tion retains the original colors but strips away local information, disrupting the contours and edges
164     of the image and introducing visual discontinuity.
165
      • Geometric Shapes: The original image is overlaid with overlapping geometric figures such as
166
        squares, circles, and stars. This visual clutter introduces local noise that obscures the main object,
167
        like the Kaleidoscope corruption from Kaufmann et al. (2019).
168
169
      • Stickers: The original image is augmented with assorted image patches. This addition of visual
        elements masks features of the primary object.
170
171   • Luminance Checkerboard: The original image is divided into a grid, with the luminance of
172     each cell altered in a checkerboard pattern. The stark luminance contrast between adjacent tiles
173
        and artificial grid boundaries makes this distortion challenging.
174
      We intend to build a challenging dataset that has the potential to guide the future development of
175
      vision models. Our dataset incorporates these tougher and less common distortions to simulate the
176
      difficulty of OOD scenarios that models might encounter in real-world applications. We tune the
177   intensity levels of each distortion such that either humans or a contemporary vision model (ViT-B)
178   achieve chance performance on the highest intensity level, i.e. no model is expected to perform well
179   on the hardest levels. The other intensity levels are chosen so that we can observe a gradual decline
180   in accuracy, ensuring that the distortions are sufficiently challenging.
181
      These distortions are then applied to a carefully curated subset of images from the ImageNet vali-
182
      dation dataset. To contextualize model performance, we later want to compare it to human perfor-
183   mance. As human evaluations on datasets with hundreds of classes cannot be scaled to sufficiently
184   many participants, we follow previous work (Geirhos et al., 2018) and simplify the task to a 16-class
185   classification problem. We extract 285 ImageNet-classes to form 16 superclasses, namely ball, bird,
186   boat, bottle, butterfly, car & truck, cat, chair, dog, fish, fruit, instrument, primate, snake, timekeep-
187   ing, and tool. For robust statistical analysis, our dataset comprises 273 images for each superclass.
188   This data size selection allows us to ensure that a 3% difference in the performance between mod-
189   els is statistically significant. Our dataset serves as a proxy for the unforeseen OOD environments
190   future models must handle, advancing the state of robustness evaluation. Additionally, we manually
191
      filter the dataset to ensure that none of the images in one superclass contain objects from another
      class or require specific cultural knowledge for classification. This process helps to avoid ambiguous
192
      ground-truth labels.

      2.2   M EASURING M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
195
196   We use the generated datasets to evaluate the performance of a suite of 58 vision models. Our se-
197   lection includes models trained on large-scale web datasets and fine-tuned on ImageNet-1k, such as
198   Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022), and EVA (Fang
199   et al., 2023; 2024). For comparison, we also evaluate the performance of smaller-scale model fami-
200   lies such as ResNet (He et al., 2016) and MobileNet (Howard, 2017) and large-scale models trained
201   only on ImageNet-1k. Additionally, we also evaluate GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and Gemini 1.5 Pro
202   (Team et al., 2024) on a representative subset of LAION images. See Tab. 6 for a complete list of
203   all models we evaluate. To address the imbalance caused by distinct numbers of subclasses within
204
      each superclass, we compute the average probability values across subclasses for each superclass, a
      method first suggested by Geirhos et al. (2018). This adjustment mitigates biases introduced by the
205
      varying subclass distributions, ensuring a more accurate model performance evaluation.

      2.3   C OLLECTING H UMAN P ERFORMANCE VIA L AB E XPERIMENTS
209
      To explore the discrepancies between human and machine perception, we design a psychophysical
210   experiment to gather human classification data on the augmented images. This experiment builds
211   on previous paradigms (Geirhos et al., 2018; 2021) to ensure consistency and comparability. In the
212   experiment, 19 human subjects are briefly presented with a distorted image and are asked to classify
213   it into one of 16 classes, reminiscent of how a DNN might be evaluated on a classification task (in
214   contrast to e.g. open response paradigms, where participants could give arbitrary textual responses).
215   Participants were recruited from the university student body, and screened for normal or corrected-
      to-normal vision and absence of color blindness. The experiment was conducted in a controlled


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      dark environment using a 22” VIEWPixx 3D light LCD monitor, with stimuli presented centrally
217   at a fixed viewing distance to ensure foveal presentation. The classification task was divided into
218   two warm-up blocks and ten main experiment blocks, with each block containing images from 16
219   superclasses. Participants were given 2.5 s to view each image, followed by a 2 s response window
220   to classify the image by clicking on a set of icons. To motivate high performance, a monetary bonus
221   was awarded for surpassing fixed, pre-determined performance thresholds for each block. Further
222   methodological details are provided in Appx. A.2.
223
224   2.4   Q UANTIFYING H UMAN -M ACHINE A LIGNMENT VIA E RROR C ONSISTENCY.
225
226   To quantify the alignment between human and machine visual perception, we adopt the error consis-
227   tency metric proposed in Geirhos et al. (2020b). Error consistency, denoted as κ ∈ [−1, 1], measures
228   the degree of agreement between the classification mistakes of two observers. In brief, κ takes on
229   a value of 1.0 if two observers are perfectly consistent, i.e. if they make classification mistakes on
230   exactly the same images. Two independent binomial observers that agree no more than expected by
231   chance will result in a κ of 0, while two maximally inconsistent observers will have a κ of -1. See
232   Appx. A.3 or Geirhos et al. (2020b) for a detailed explanation of the metric.

297
298   In Fig. 4, we compare model performance on ImageNet-C against performance on LAION-C. Evi-
299   dently, the average model performance on ImageNet-C stays above or close to 60%, indicating that

484
485   Summary. Given that existing OOD benchmarks are often no longer OOD for models trained on
      web-scale datasets like LAION since distortions like blur and digital corruptions are commonplace


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      on the web, we here introduce LAION-C. LAION-C is a benchmark designed to evaluate the ro-
487   bustness and generalization capabilities of modern vision models trained on web-scale datasets. Our
488   empirical results demonstrate that LAION-C is more challenging for a representative suite of vision
489   models than the existing ImageNet-C benchmark, particularly at higher distortion intensity levels.
490   Our thorough human evaluation in a highly controlled psychophysical laboratory totaling 11,400
491   trials shows that the best models often outperform even the best human observers. While they do not
492   always follow similar strategies (as indicated by error consistency analysis), this reassuring finding
493   indicates that the best models have indeed substantially progressed in their ability to handle unex-
494   pected input and are not just getting better on in-distribution distortions. Given that the LAION-C
495
      benchmark dataset, by virtue of its construction, serves as a better proxy for a model’s ability to
      recognize objects despite an unexpected distortion, we recommend it as an OOD benchmark for
496
      current and future web-scale vision models.
497
498
      Limitations. While we have shown that LAION-C can effectively reveal shortcomings in model
499
      robustness, we have not yet fully explored why certain models underperform on specific distortions.
500
      Although our empirical results provide valuable insights, further investigation is required to clarify
501   which visual cues the models rely on under different conditions. Such an analysis could inform the
502   development of new inductive biases or architectural improvements, since a better understanding of
503   these mechanisms could lead to improvements in both model interpretability and robustness. Given
504   our current focus on introducing the dataset, this was not fully addressed here, but could be an area
505   for future exploration. Furthermore, it is an open question what the performance limit on LAION-C
506   looks like. Since fine-tuning models on LAION-C results in significant performance gains, particu-
507   larly at higher distortion levels, there clearly is potential for optimization through advanced training
508   techniques. However, how to further improve generalization across OOD scenarios, especially to
509
      enhance the models’ ability to handle the novel distortions presented by LAION-C, remains an open
      question that warrants further exploration. To retain its value as an OOD benchmark, LAION-C
510
      should not be used as a training or fine-tuning dataset (except for analysis purposes).
511
512
      Conclusion and outlook. Just a few years ago, early investigations into generalization abilities of
513
      deep neural networks showed humans vastly outperforming the best models (Geirhos et al., 2018;
514
      Dodge & Karam, 2019). Fast-forwarding to today, LAION-C shows that the best models either
515   match or outperform human performance on challenging OOD distortions. This finding is reassuring
516   in the light of growing concerns about the quality of existing evaluation datasets, including the
517   concern that OOD datasets like ImageNet-C may no longer serve their original purpose in the era
518   of web-scale training datasets. Our findings indicate that the often super-human performance of
519   modern models is achieved through super-human strategies: Models use a variety of image cues—
520   including, in all likelihood, background pixels to perform well on some of our challenging datasets.
521   Given their high performance across the board, they no longer rely on a single strategy that fails
522   when faced with a test case that distorts a particular image cue. This marks a paradigm shift in OOD
523
      generalization: From humans outperforming models to models outperforming humans, from relying
      on a single strategy to a diverse set of multiple robust strategies, and from OOD benchmarking
524
      measuring progress towards human-like object recognition to better performance now indicating
525
      super-human (in other words, less human-like) vision models.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORK
705
706   OOD generalization ability of vision models. As deep learning has advanced to the point where
707   models can reliably generalize to data that matches their training distribution or even exceed the
708   quality of the original labels (Beyer et al., 2020), OOD-robustness, as measured by specifically
709   designed OOD test sets, has moved to the center stage of computer vision research. In partic-
710   ular, ImageNet-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019), a dataset containing images from the test-set
711   of ImageNet to which various fairly natural corruptions such as blurring and pixelation were ap-
712   plied, has long been the gold standard for assessing OOD-performance, to the point where data
713
      augmentations proposed to increase OOD robustness were found to only work well because they are
      similar to the ImageNet-C corruptions (Mintun et al., 2021). In contrast, ImageNet-R (Hendrycks
714
      et al., 2021a) instead provides a more complex distribution shift by collecting different renditions
715
      of the target classes such as sculptures and paintings, instead of photos. A more subtle distribu-
716   tion shift which still caused considerable drops in model performance for ImageNet-trained models,
717   was proposed by Recht et al. (2019). They collected ImageNetV2, a new test set for ImageNet
718   that should theoretically not differ from the ImageNet test set at all, because it was collected with a
719   very similar methodology, but revealed that models do perform slightly worse on ImageNetV2 than
720   on the original test set. Hendrycks et al. (2021b) proposed two other OOD-test sets which do not
721   rely on synthetic image manipulations but instead consist of natural images that are in some sense
722   OOD relative to ImageNet, either by virtue of displaying object classes not present in ImageNet
723   (ImageNet-O) or by showing an object of an ImageNet-class in a scene that is weird enough to fool
724
      most models (ImageNet-A). What all of these datasets have in common is that, by design, they pro-
      vide distribution shifts relative to ImageNet. But with the rapid evolution of deep learning, these
725
      traditional benchmarks have become less challenging for state-of-the-art vision models trained on
726
      expansive web-scale datasets (Radford et al., 2021). While it is to some degree possible to predict a
727   model’s OOD generalization from its training set performance (Taori et al., 2020), the only reliable
728   measurements of this capability stem from empirical evaluations of models on OOD datasets. Our
729   work addresses this need by introducing LAION-C, a dataset that incorporates novel and complex
730   synthetic distortions tailored to challenge even advanced vision systems.
731
732   Advancement in visual foundation models The rise of visual foundation models such as Vi-
733   sion Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) and BeiT (Bao
734   et al., 2022) has redefined what constitutes standard performance across many visual tasks. These
735   improvements in performance partially stem from architectural innovations and parameter optimiza-
736   tion, but were mostly powered by the effective leveraging of unprecedented dataset sizes (Zhai et al.,
737   2022). However, because visual foundation models were trained on web-scale datasets, the extent
738   of their generalization capability remains underexplored.
739
740   Comparing human vs. machine perception. Deep Neural Networks were originally conceived
741   as models of human vision (Fukushima, 1975) and were found to be the best available models for
742
      neuronal activity in the primate visual cortex (Yamins et al., 2014), even if not trained for this task.
      Today, there is a growing body of research dedicated to evaluating the adequacy of neural networks
743
      as behavioral models of human core object recognition (Doerig et al., 2023; Schrimpf et al., 2018;
744
      Wichmann & Geirhos, 2023; Muttenthaler et al., 2023). Building upon the findings of Geirhos
745   et al. (2021), who illustrate the narrowing of the behavioral gap between humans and machines
746   in terms of their error consistency, our study further explores this dynamic utilizing LAION-C. We
747   conducted a systematic analysis of differences in perception between human and machine observers,
748   and assessed if the behavioral gap is closing further, as well as highlighting the persistent cognitive
749   differences between humans and machines.

      A.3     E RROR C ONSISTENCY
811
812   Here, we provide a more detailed explanation of error consistency (EC), summarizing Geirhos et al.
813   (2020b). The EC between two observers which both classified a sequence of samples is obtained
814   by first using the necessary ground-truth labels to assess which images each observer classified cor-
815   rectly. A trial increases the agreement if both observers solved it correctly, or if they both failed
816   (and decreases it if only one of them got the trial correct while the other one failed). One then calcu-
817   lates how much more agreement was observed between the two observers relative to the agreement
818
      expected by chance. This is done by calculating Cohen’s Kappa (Cohen, 1960), which is defined
                o −pe
      as κ = p1−p     , where po is the (empirically measured) proportion of agreement-trials and pe is the
819                e
      (theoretical) expected agreement when modeling both observers as independent binomial observers.
820
      κ takes on values between −1 and 1, with a higher κ signifying higher levels of agreement, and a
821
      κ of 0 indicating that a pair of observers does not agree more frequently than one would expect by
822   chance, given their marginal correctness probabilities.
823
824   In this work, we calculate the error consistency between model responses and human classification
825
      data. To do this, we first collect all human responses. Since each human participant saw a fresh set
      of stimuli, we thus obtain exactly one human response per image. We then calculate each model’s
826
      EC to this list of human responses.

831     ImageNet validation set whose average color best matches that of the tile. The values of n per
832     intensity level are:
833         – Level 1: n = 4
834         – Level 2: n = 6

836         – Level 4: n = 16

840
        and color channel offsets are applied to random regions as follows:
841         – Level 1: Shift up to 8% of image width in 4 regions, ±4 pixel channel offset.
842         – Level 2: Shift up to 32% of image width in 8 regions, ±8 pixel channel offset.
843         – Level 3: Shift up to 50% of image width in 10 regions, ±10 pixel channel offset.
844         – Level 4: Shift up to 128% of image width in 16 regions, ±16 pixel channel offset.
845         – Level 5: Shift up to 200% of image width in 20 regions, ±20 pixel channel offset.
846
        The implementation is inspired by T (2020)

849

850
        color of that section. The parameters for each intensity level are:
851
852         – Level 1: 224 vertical sections, with 1-pixel steps along the y-axis.
853         – Level 2: 178 vertical sections, with 2-pixel steps along the y-axis.
854         – Level 3: 112 vertical sections, with 4-pixel steps along the y-axis.
855         – Level 4: 84 vertical sections, with 6-pixel steps along the y-axis.
856         – Level 5: 60 vertical sections, with 8-pixel steps along the y-axis.

951   A.5   ACCURACY
952
953   To demonstrate the value of LAION-C as a benchmark for evaluating model robustness, we analyze
954   how model performance on LAION-C correlates with that on ImageNet-C. Grounding our compari-
955   son in models that have demonstrated a baseline level of robustness on well-established benchmarks,
956
      we apply a threshold to include 40 models that achieved at least 60% accuracy on ImageNet-C.
      As shown in Fig. 9, the majority of data points lie above the identity line representing performance
957
      alignment on LAION-C and ImageNet-C. The gradual slope of the data points, combined with their
958
      positioning, indicates that models generally perform better on ImageNet-C, while their performance
959   on LAION-C is more dispersed and often substantially lower.
960   This broader distribution of performance highlights that LAION-C introduces more challenging dis-
961   tortions, prompting models to exhibit greater variability in robustness. The moderate Kendall’s tau
962   coefficient (τ = 0.66) between the models’ performances on LAION-C and ImageNet-C further un-
963   derscores this, indicating notable pairwise differences in how models rank across these two bench-
964   marks, unearthing vulnerabilities that are less pronounced on ImageNet-C. These results demon-
965   strate the necessity of LAION-C as a complementary benchmark for a more comprehensive evalua-
966   tion of model robustness.
