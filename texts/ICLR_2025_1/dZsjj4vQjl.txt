009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) excel in vision–language tasks by
012            pre-training solely on coarse-grained concept annotations (e.g., image captions).
013            We hypothesize that integrating fine-grained concept annotations (e.g., object labels
014            and object regions) will further improve performance, as both data granularities
015            complement each other in terms of breadth and depth in concept representation.
016            We introduce a new dataset featuring Multimodal Multi-Grained Concept annota-
017            tions (MMG I C) for MLLMs. In constructing MMG I C, we explore the impact of
018            different data recipes on multimodal comprehension and generation. Our analyses
019            reveal that multi-grained concept annotations integrate and complement each other,
020            under our structured template and autoregressive discrete framework.
021            We definitively show that multi-grained concepts do facilitate MLLMs to better
022            locate and learn concepts, aligning vision and language at multiple granularities.
023
               We further validate our hypothesis by investigating the comparison and collabora-
               tion between MMG I C and image–caption data on 12 multimodal comprehension
024
               and generation benchmarks, e.g., their appropriate combination achieve 3.95% and
025
               2.34% accuracy improvements on POPE and SEED-Bench. Code, data and models
026            will be made openly available.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the rapid development of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al.,
032   2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) and Visual Foundation Models (VFMs) (Radford et al., 2021; Rombach
033   et al., 2022; Dehghani et al., 2023), researchers have started to explore the potential of unifying them
034   into Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to perform various Vision–Language (VL) tasks,
035   such as image captioning, visual question answering, and text-to-image generation. MLLMs, such
036   as Emu (Sun et al., 2023c), SEED-LLaMA (Ge et al., 2023b) and LaVIT (Jin et al., 2023), follow
037   a similar framework that integrates the capabilities of LLMs and VFMs under an autoregressive
038   objective of predicting the next visual or textual token, showing impressive performance on VL tasks.
039   Despite their success, existing MLLMs typically do not make full use of concepts in VL learning,
040   relying on coarse-grained concept annotations (e.g., image captions) but ignoring fine-grained con-
041   cept annotations (e.g., object labels and object regions). This leads to superficial and incomplete
042   understanding of concepts, limiting VL alignment. Specifically, by “concept” we mean an abstraction
043   and generalization of a group of things having common characteristics (Goguen, 1969; Carey, 2000;
044   Blouw et al., 2016). Concepts can be categorized into concrete and abstract concepts by whether they
045
      can be sensed by five human senses (Shevade et al., 2005; Connell et al., 2018). Concrete concepts,
      such as objects, attributes, and relationships, are not only easy to collect and annotate, but also semanti-
046
      cally consistent when expressed across modalities (Chen et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2020).
047
      Hence, many traditional Vision–Language Models (VLMs) combine coarse- and fine-grained concept
048   annotations to better learn cross-modal consistent concrete concepts, thus improving VL alignment (Li
049   et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2022; Menon & Vondrick, 2023). However, they rely
050   on additional components and loss functions to leverage different-grained concept annotations (e.g.,
051   bounding box prediction), and optimize the multimodal comprehension ability of VLMs at different
052   granularities separately through multitask learning. Moreover, they seldom explore the potential of
053   multi-grained concept annotations in multimodal generation tasks, such as text-to-image generation,
      let alone exploring both multimodal comprehension and generation tasks under the same framework.

054
      As prior work demonstrate that concepts are crucial for VL alignment and combining coarse- and fine-
055   grained concept annotations can better learn concepts, we argue that existing MLLMs should make bet-
056   ter use of concepts by incorporating multi-grained concept annotations into their training. To this end,
057   we first merge four public, object detection datasets to construct a new multimodal dataset, MMG I C.
058   Our goal is not to replace or surpass existing image–caption datasets, but to address the lack of datasets
059   with multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs and explore their potential. Different from pre-
060   vious VLM work, we 1) provide multimodal annotations for images, including both textual forms
061   (caption, labels and label descriptions) and visual form (object regions), to enrich multi-grained con-
062   cept annotations; 2) design a structured template to integrate multimodal multi-grained concept anno-
063
      tations into image–text interleaved documents, to leverage the complex context processing capability
      of MLLMs; 3) instead of additional components or loss functions used in VLMs, train MLLMs with
064
      an autoregressive discrete framework and predict the next visual or textual token in a multimodal dis-
065
      crete token sequence, to improve multimodal comprehension and generation ability across multiple
066   granularities simultaneously. This can reuse existing LLM training regimes and ensure the generality
067   and applicability of MMG I C to different MLLM frameworks. The key contributions are as follows:
068
069   • We introduce MMG I C, a new dataset with multimodal multi-grained concept annotations (Sec. 2).
070     Under a general MLLM framework (Sec. 3), we show that MMG I C can help MLLMs better locate
071
        and learn concepts, thus aligning vision and language across multiple granularities (Sec. 4).
072   • We explore different data recipes for multi-grained concept annotations (Sec. 4.1 & 4.4). Our
073     analyses show that multi-grained annotations can integrate and complement each other to help
074
        MLLMs ground concepts in the textual annotations to corresponding regions in images, thus
        improving the ability to understand and generate concepts.
075
076   • Through the evaluation of 12 benchmarks for multimodal comprehension and generation in both
077
        pre-training (Sec. 4.2) and supervised fine-tuning stages (Sec. 4.3), we explore the comparison and
        collaboration between MMG I C and coarse-grained image–caption data. We find that they each have
078
        their own strengths in depth and breadth of concept representation, and that appropriate curriculum
079
        learning strategies can effectively combine their strengths to further improve performance.

082   2     MMG I C DATASET
083
084   To be clear, our goal is not to supplant existing image–caption datasets, but to build a multimodal
085
      dataset with multi-grained concept annotations to address the lack of such datasets, and then explore its
      potential in MLLMs. We now introduce its collection, pre-processing, complement and construction.
086
087
      2.1   C OLLECTION AND P RE - PROCESSING
088
089   In this work, we focus on concrete concepts, especially objects, attributes of objects, and relationships
090   between objects. They are fundamental elements in VL learning and widely annotated in object detec-
091   tion datasets. Therefore, we collect four public large-scale human-annotated object detection datasets,
092   including Open Images (Kuznetsova et al., 2020), Objects365 (Shao et al., 2019), V3Det (Wang
093   et al., 2023a), and Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017). Images in these datasets are uploaded to
094   Flickr by real-world users and collected by dataset providers. They typically show complex scenes
095   with multiple objects, and are annotated with fine-grained category labels and object bounding boxes.
096
      Comparing with widely-used coarse-grained image–caption datasets, fine-grained object annotations
      provided in these datasets can help MLLMs locate and learn concepts in images.
097
098
      Object Annotation Pre-processing. Fine-grained object annotations includes category labels and
099
      bounding box coordinates for each object region. To accommodate varying aspect ratios of bounding
100   boxes and the requirement for a square input image, we crop a new larger square region Si that
101   contains the original object region Ri , Ri ⊆ Si , with their centers aligned as closely as possible. We
102   then update the annotations of Si by integrating the category label of Ri with the category labels
103   of surrounding object regions. Notably, instead of transforming bounding box coordinates into
104   tokens in the text (Chen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023c; Peng et al., 2023) or visual markers in the
105   image (Shtedritski et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024), for each object, MMG I C directly
106   provides visual tokens of the cropped region Si and textual tokens of fine-grained category labels and
107   location descriptions (Figure 1 3 ). Fine-grained cropped regions can help MLLMs locate and align
      concepts in images and in annotations at a detailed level.

      2.2      M ULTI -G RAINED C ONCEPT A NNOTATION C OMPLEMENT
129
130   We follow LAION-COCO (Schuhmann et al., 2023) to automatically synthesize captions for all
131   images (Figure 1 1 ) by BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b) and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), since partial
132   images are not annotated with captions. We do not use or synthesize captions for object regions
133   to avoid introducing hallucinations.
134
135   Label Description Generation. Label descriptions are corresponding concept descriptions of con-
136   crete concepts in the image, which convey understanding about a concept by visually observed details
137   and relevant knowledge. Previous works (Shen et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Menon & Vondrick, 2023)
138   successfully improve concept understanding by introducing label descriptions from WordNet (Miller,
139   1992) and LLMs (Brown et al., 2020). Inspired by them, we design prompt templates and several
140   human-annotated examples for each type of category labels (object, object attribute, and relationship
141   between objects), and then generate label-description pairs (Figure 1 2 ) by GPT-4 (Achiam et al.,
142
      2023). We manually check them to ensure quality. Moreover, for better differentiation of polysemous
      category labels, we manually check them based on the definitions in WordNet, and update them based
143
      on the specific data samples, e.g., “batter” → “batter (ballplayer)” or “batter (cooking)”.

      2.3      C ONSTRUCTION
147
      Above steps transform four object detection datasets into MMG I C, a multimodal dataset with multi-
148   grained concept annotations. Furthermore, we carefully design a structured template to integrate
149   multi-grained concept annotations into an image–text interleaved document. As shown in Figure 1
150   (Left), the structured template consists of: 1 coarse-grained image-annotation part: each image is
151   annotated with a short and general description of the whole image; 2 fine-grained image-annotation
152   part: concrete concepts (objects, attributes and relationships) present in the image are annotated
153   with corresponding category labels and label descriptions; 3 fine-grained object-annotation part:
154   each object in the image is annotated with a cropped object region, object labels in the region, and a
155   location description. A data example of MMG I C is shown in Figure 1 (Right).
156   Different from previous VLM work that provide multiple isolated annotations for each image,
157   MMG I C provides richer multimodal multi-grained concept annotations in both textual forms (caption,
158   labels and label descriptions) and visual form (object regions) for each image, and integrates them into
159   an image–text interleaved document by our structured template. This can leverage MLLMs’ complex
160   context processing capability to facilitate VL alignment across multiple granularities simultaneously
161   under our MLLM framework. In a nutshell, MMG I C fills the gap in the MLLM field for datasets
      with multi-grained concept annotations. It contains 3.5M unique images, 23.9M unique object

168
169   We introduce a general MLLM framework and its two training stages. Our goal is not to develop new
170
      frameworks, training objectives or benchmark SOTAs, but to explore the potential of multi-grained
      concept annotations for MLLMs under the general framework.

205
      and ensures the generality and applicability of MMG I C to different MLLM frameworks. More impor-
206

207   MMG I C and MLLMs’ complex context processing capability. In all training stages, we follow com-
208   mon practice (Ge et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023a) to freeze most of the parameters and only tune partial
209   parameters of the LLM: a VL vocabulary, additional LoRA modules (Hu et al., 2021), norm layers and
210   a language modeling head layer, to greatly improve efficiency. We pre-tokenize images into discrete
211   visual token sequences and do not load all visual modules during training since they are frozen.

214
215   Similar to LLMs that tokenize text-only documents into discrete textual token sequences, for each
      data sample in MMG I C shown in Fig. 1 (Right) or an image–caption pair, our framework tokenize it

      into a discrete token sequence consisting of interleaved visual and textual token sequences. To help
217   the LLM distinguish between two types of sequences, following LaVIT, we add two special tokens
218   [IMG] and [/IMG] to the vocabulary, and insert them before and after each visual token sequence,

222   3.3    T RAINING S TAGE 2: S UPERVISED F INE - TUNING (SFT)
223
224   To align pre-trained MLLMs with natural language instructions, following previous works (Sun et al.,
225   2023c;a; Liu et al., 2023c;b; 2024a; Zhu et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2024a), we collect 1.21M samples
226
      from several public datasets for supervised fine-tuning, including multimodal instruction datasets (Liu
      et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023c; Zhang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; LAION, 2024b; Brooks et al.,
227
      2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and text-only instruction datasets (Taori et al., 2023; Steven Tey, 2023),
228
      and 1M samples from an aesthetic image–caption dataset (LAION, 2024a). We also play back 1M
229   samples from MMG I C to avoid forgetting the knowledge learned in the pre-training stage. Following
230   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c;b; 2024a), all datasets are transformed into a unified format, which consists
231   of a general system message and multiple instruction–answer pairs. Only answer tokens are accounted
232   in loss calculation. Details of instruction data are provided in Appendix H.

247

248
      coarse-grained image captions (C), fine-grained category labels (L), label descriptions (D) and object
249
      regions (R). Hence, we design four data recipes to investigate the importance of each component on
250   zero-shot image captioning and image generation tasks and find the best recipe. As shown in Table 1:
251
252       • {0, 1, 2}-th rows: simply appending category labels to image captions does not help and even
253
            hurts the performance on both tasks. MLLMs may struggle to understand the association
            between images, captions and category labels, leading to confusion and treating category labels
254
            as additional noise. Whereas, label descriptions can strengthen the association between images
255
            and annotations, to help MLLMs align the concepts represented by labels with the concepts
256         in the image, thus mitigating the performance drop. As shown in Figure 3 (Top), MMG I C(C)
257         incorrectly identifies “accordion” as “electronic keyboard”. While for MMG I C(CLD), label
258         descriptions can help MLLMs better understand labels by visual details “pleated bellows and
259         keyboard, box-like” and relevant knowledge “portable”.
260       • {2, 3}-th rows: object regions can further complement other annotations to help MLLMs better
261         locate and align concepts in images and in annotations at a fine-grained level, thus significantly
262         improving the performance on both tasks. As shown in Figure 3 (Bottom), MMG I C(CLD)
263         incorrectly identifies “laying” as “sitting” and “on top of a toilet” as “on the floor next to a toilet”.
264         While for MMG I C(CLDR), object regions can help MLLMs ground concepts in the textual
265         annotations to corresponding regions in the image, especially in terms of instance interaction
266
            and spatial relationship here, which is also consistent with meso analyses in Section 4.4.

451
452   To delve into the potential of multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs, we conduct meso
453   analyses on SEED-Bench (Li et al., 2023a), a large-scale multi-choice benchmark for multimodal
454   comprehension. We follow common practice (Ge et al., 2023b) to select 9 image evaluation
455   dimensions, i.e., about 14K questions, and denote it as SEED-Bench-IMG. We ignore the “Text
456   Recognition” dimension due to noisy data and small scale (only 85), leading to result fluctuations.
457
      “Scene Understanding, Visual Reasoning” dimensions focus on the holistic understanding and
      cross-modal reasoning of the image, while the other 6 dimensions focus on in-depth understanding
458
      of concrete concepts in the image. “Coarse-grained” (CG) means that only image captions from
459
      MMG I C are used. “Fine-grained” (FG) uses category labels, label descriptions and object regions
460   from MMG I C. “Multi-grained” (MG) means that both above are used.
461
462
463   Quantitative Analysis. We first explore the impact of different-grained concept annotations from
464
      MMG I C on each evaluation dimension of SEED-Bench-IMG in Figure 5 (Left). For overall accuracy,
      FG significantly improves performance over CG by 1.39 points, and multi-grained can further
465
      improve by 1.4 points. For each evaluation dimension:
466
467       • FG provides deeper understanding of concepts than CG. Compared with coarse-grained image
468         captions, fine-grained concept annotations can help MLLMs better understand and locate con-
469         cepts in images, and recognize relationships between concepts, especially on the “Instance Iden-
470
            tity, Spatial Relation, Instance Counting, Instance Interaction, Visual Reasoning” dimensions.
471       • MG can facilitate collaboration between concept annotations of different granularities, thus
472         fully integrating each other’s strengths and achieving further improvements in all dimensions,
473         especially on the “Scene Understanding, Instance Attribute, Instance Counting, Instance
474
            Interaction, Visual Reasoning” dimensions. This demonstrates that our structured template for
            MMG I C can help MLLMs better utilize multi-grained concept annotations to learn concepts
475
            and thus promote vision–language alignment across multiple granularities simultaneously.
476
477
478   Qualitative Analysis. To better analyse the advantages of different-grained concept annotations in
479   MMG I C for MLLMs, we provide corresponding qualitative analysis in Figure 5 (Right). More case
480   studies are provided in Appendix C.4.
481       • Example 1 “Instance Identity”: FG provides deeper understanding of concepts than CG. While

503   5    R ELATED W ORK
504
505   MLLMs have emerged recently including ones (Sun et al., 2023c; Ge et al., 2023b; Jin et al., 2023;
506   Zhu et al., 2023a; Dong et al., 2024) that propose multimodal generalists capable of both multimodal
507   comprehension and generation. However, existing MLLMs often overlook the importance of concepts
508   and only utilize coarse-grained concept annotations, e.g., image captions, which may limit vision–
509   language alignment. Factually, many traditional VLMs recognized the importance of concepts in
510   vision–language learning. To better utilize concepts in vision–language learning and improve perfor-
511
      mance, they incorporated fine-grained concept annotations into coarse-grained image captions. For ex-
      ample, object labels (Li et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), label descriptions (Shen et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
512
      2022; Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Li et al., 2024b), region descriptions (Zeng et al., 2021) and object
513
      regions (Zeng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022b). Especially, Oscar (Li et al., 2020) appends fine-grained ob-
514   ject labels detected in the image after the coarse-grained image caption to simplify semantic alignment
515   between vision and language. X-VLM (Zeng et al., 2021) proposes to align visual concepts (images
516   and object regions) with coarse-grained image captions and fine-grained object labels and object re-
517   gion descriptions in multi-granularity. In this paper, different from previous VLM work, we collect and
518   construct rich multimodal multi-grained concept annotations in MMG I C dataset. Without additional
519   components or loss functions, we design a structured template to leverage the advantages of MLLMs
520   under an autoregressive discrete framework. Through evaluation of 12 multimodal comprehension and
521   generation benchmarks, as well as the comparison, combination, and analysis of MMG I C and image–
522
      caption data, for the first time, we explore and demonstrate the potential of MMG I C in MLLMs.
523
524   6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   We introduce a new multimodal dataset, MMG I C, providing multi-grained concept annotations
527   in both textual and visual form. MMG I C allows us to measure the potential of appropriate use of
528   concepts. With our structured template and autoregressive discrete framework, multi-grained concept
529
      annotations can integrate and complement each other to help MLLMs better locate and learn concepts,
      thereby aligning vision and language across multiple granularities simultaneously. Furthermore,
530
      MMG I C and coarse-grained image–caption data each have their own strengths in depth and breadth
531
      of concept representation, and appropriately combining them can effectively integrate their strengths
532   to further improve performance. We hope to inspire future research to further explore the potential
533   of multi-grained concept annotations by incorporating more different types of annotations, scaling
534   up data by automatic synthesis, scaling up (concrete and even abstract) concepts, and other VL tasks.

       A     B ROADER I MPACT
1050
1051
       In this paper, we introduce MMG I C, a new dataset with multimodal multi-grained concept annotations
       for MLLMs. We first collect, pre-process and complement four public large-scale human-annotated
1052
       object detection datasets. With a well-designed structured template, we transform these datasets
1053
       into our MMG I C dataset to address the lack of such datasets in the MLLM field and support our
1054   exploration. Under an autoregressive discrete framework, we explore the potential of MMG I C for
1055   MLLMs by evaluations and analyses on various downstream vision–language benchmarks. We
1056   demonstrate that MMG I C can provide multi-grained concept annotations to help MLLMs better
1057   locate and learn concepts, thus facilitating vision–language alignment across multiple granularities
1058   simultaneously. We will open-source the models and the code of the complete pipeline from data
1059   processing, model training to evaluation for facilitating more reproducible research. We hope that our
1060   work can inspire more research on multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs.
1061   We do not anticipate a specific negative impact, but, as with any multimodal large language model
1062   (MLLM), we recommend to exercise caution. MMG I C dataset is constructed from four public
1063   widely-used object detection datasets and will not bring any potential ethical or societal issues. We
1064   will follow the usage rules and copyright of original datasets. Newly generated data parts will follow
1065   the usage rules and copyrights of the corresponding open-source or closed-source models. We will
1066   require researchers who use our code and models to follow the principles of positive AI research to
1067   avoid model abuse and negative societal impact.

1070
1071   B.1    AUTOMATIC A NNOTATION S YNTHESIS
1072
1073   Nowadays, both LLMs and MLLMs can continuously improve the performance and generalization
1074   ability by scaling up training data. Although our MMG I C dataset, based on several human-annotated
1075   object detection datasets and our well-designed data pipeline, can provide high-quality multi-grained
1076
       concept annotations, it is still limited by the scale of the original datasets. A natural idea is to
       automatically synthesize a variety of different types of annotations for any image. This is a promising
1077
       direction for future research, which can scale up to more concepts and continuously improve MLLMs.
1078
1079   Recent works (Peng et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Rasheed et al., 2023; Li
       et al., 2023d; Yang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c) have proposed to automatically synthesize various


                                                        20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       annotations for large-scale web images with external open/close source models and complicated
1081   pipelines. KOSMOS -2 (Peng et al., 2023) extracted noun phrases from image captions and detected
1082   bounding boxes from images to synthesize Grounded image–text pairs (G R IT) to improve the
1083   grounding capability of MLLMs. KOSMOS -G (Pan et al., 2023) synthesized image captions and
1084   segment object regions for images to construct compositional image generation instruction tuning data
1085   to improve the zero-shot subject-driven image generation capability of MLLMs. All-Seeing (Wang
1086   et al., 2023b) and GLaMM (Rasheed et al., 2023) designed complicated pipelines to combine multiple
1087   external models to synthesize various annotations for large-scale web images from SA-1B (Kirillov
1088   et al., 2023), including detailed image captions, object labels, object regions, question-answer pairs
1089
       about objects in the image, scene graphs, and so on, to improve the image/region-level captioning
       and grounding capability of MLLMs. SoM (Yang et al., 2023) synthesized semantic segmentation
1090
       masks for images to improve the image segmentation and grounding capability of MLLMs.
1091
1092   Although these works have shown that automatically synthesized annotations can improve the
1093   performance of MLLMs, cascading multiple external models and complicated pipelines may introduce
1094   noise and bias to the synthesized annotations. Existing works introduced complex data pipelines
1095
       and costly manual reviews (especially for pre-training data exceeding 1M) to reduce noise, but
       their released data still contains quite a bit of hallucinations, noise, and redundancy. Moreover,
1096
       these works mainly focus on exploring the potential of automatically synthesized annotations in
1097
       the multimodal comprehension or generation capability of MLLMs, and have not explored both
1098   under a unified framework at the same time. Among them, All-Seeing and GLaMM are similar to
1099   MMG I C proposed in this paper. Both of them synthesize various annotations for images, including
1100   image captions, category labels and object regions provided in MMG I C. They don’t provide label
1101   descriptions but provide visual question–answering pairs or scene graphs, or other annotations.
1102   However, the final data forms of them are similar to the related works in traditional VLMs, where
1103   each image is paired with isolated different types of annotations. They need to introduce additional
1104   components and loss functions to utilize different annotations, such as object region annotations, to
1105   optimize the model’s comprehension capability at different granularities separately. Besides, their
1106
       experiments mainly explore the object recognition and segmentation, image/region-level captioning
       and grounding capability of MLLMs, which are actually downstream tasks that obviously benefit
1107
       from their synthesized/annotated different types of annotations.
1108
1109   In contrast, in this paper, from the perspective of concept annotations, we explore the potential of
1110   multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs for the first time, in both multimodal comprehension
1111   and multimodal generation. We mainly focus on general multimodal benchmarks instead of spe-
1112
       cific benchmarks that fine-grained annotations are good at (e.g., object recognition and grounding
       benchmarks). We want to evaluate and analyze the potential of multi-grained concept annotations
1113
       for MLLMs in a more comprehensive, general and fair way, by comparing and collaborating with
1114
       widely-used coarse-grained image–caption data.
1115
1116   We believe that the automatic synthesis of multi-grained concept annotations for any image is a
1117   promising direction for future research. In addition, incorporating more different types of annotations,
1118
       including not only various annotations found in existing works, but also annotations for text-rich
       images and table/chart images, would be valuable for future research. We have explored the potential
1119
       of multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs in both multimodal comprehension and generation,
1120
       including image captioning, text-to-image generation, visual question answering, multi-choice
1121   benchmarks, and image editing. It is interesting to explore more benchmarks on object recognition
1122   and segmentation, grounding capability and other vision–language tasks in the future. Finally, in
1123   this work, we focus on concrete concepts, especially objects, attributes of objects, and relationships
1124   between objects. It is very interesting to explore more concrete concepts and also abstract concepts,
1125   such as emotions, events, and so on, in the future.

1172   Parameter-Efficient Training. In this work, we standardize a generative vision–language frame-
1173   work based on existing MLLMs, which consists of a LLM and several visual modules. We follow
1174   previous works (Ge et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023a) to freeze all visual modules and most of the LLM

1182   found that the latter strategy can better capture high-level semantics instead of low-level image details
1183   in the former strategy, which is more effective for multimodal comprehension. As the concurrent
1184   work of SEED, LaVIT also found that high-level semantics matters for downstream tasks, and further
1185   supported dynamic sequence length varying from the image. Compare SEED and LaVIT, we take
1186   the visual tokenizer from LaVIT, which can better preserve image details and semantic information.

1279
       To further investigate how MMG I C can better help MLLMs utilize concepts to align vision and lan-
1280
       guage and improve the capability of generating concepts we conduct both qualitative and quantitative
1281   analyses on image editing task.
1282
1283   Although MMG I C does not contain any samples about image editing, with our structured template,
1284
       each sample of MMG I C can be seen as an image–text interleaved document with an image, multiple
       cropped regions from the image, textual annotations, and also template instruction text. Such design
1285
       can help MLLMs better understand each region in the image, as well as the relationships within and
1286
       between regions, thus better learning and generating concepts, and aligning images and instructions.
1287   We hypothesize that this may help MLLMs learn more diverse image generation abilities.
1288
1289

       D.1    D IFFERENCE BETWEEN O UR F RAMEWORK WITH E XISTING MLLM F RAMEWORKS
1641
1642   This paper mainly focuses on MLLMs that are multimodal generalists capable of both multimodal
1643   comprehension and generation. Existing MLLMs (Sun et al., 2023c; Jin et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023b;
1644   Zhu et al., 2023a; Sun et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2022a; 2024a) are typically trained with
1645   an autoregressive objective, i.e., generate the prediction of the next token in a multimodal sequence
1646   containing discrete textual tokens, and discrete visual tokens or continuous visual embeddings.
1647   MLLMs process images in different ways. For example, EMU (Sun et al., 2023c;a) and VL-
1648
       GPT (Zhu et al., 2023a) transform images into continuous visual embeddings by visual encoders,
       and then adopt a separate regression head to predict visual embeddings. In contrast, CM3Leon (Yu
1649
       et al., 2023a), Unified-IO (Lu et al., 2022a; 2024a) and SEED-LLaMA (Ge et al., 2023b) tokenize
1650
       images into discrete visual tokens by visual tokenizers, and then predict visual tokens by the extended
1651   vocabulary with the unified language model head. Technically, different training datasets, training
1652   settings (e.g., full- or partial-param), framework, evaluation settings (e.g., image resolution), etc., lead
1653   to non-comparable and unfair comparisons of our baselines with existing MLLMs. Their computation
1654   (more training data and fully fine-tune the LLM params) and data resources are extremely expensive
1655   and large (well over 10 times that of our work). Our autoregressive discrete framework for MLLMs
1656   is based on SEED-LLaMA and LaVIT, which consists of several visual modules and a LLM with
1657   an extended vision–language vocabulary, as shown in Figure 2. Next, we describe the differences
1658   between our framework and these two MLLMs.
1659       • Visual Modules: our visual modules are inherited from LaVIT (Jin et al., 2023), which consists
1660         of a visual encoder, a visual tokenizer, a visual decoder and a diffusion model. The differences
1661         between the two MLLMs mainly lie in the design of the latter three modules. The visual tokenizer
1662         of SEED-LLaMA uses the Casual Q-Former from BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b) to compress visual
1663         representations into a fixed-length sequence of visual embeddings, while LaVIT is more flexible
1664         and designs a dynamic visual tokenizer with token selector and token merger, which can obtain
1665
             a more flexible and effective sequence of visual embeddings. For the discrete visual token
             sequence predicted by the LLM, SEED-LLaMA uses an MLP as the visual decoder to compress
1666
             and reconstruct it into the image embedding (1 token) and directly generate images through
1667
             the frozen unCLIP-SD, while LaVIT uses multiple transformer blocks as the visual decoder to
1668         reconstruct the discrete visual token sequence into a continuous feature map by learned queries
1669         (256 tokens), and then uses the conditional denoising U-Net to progressively recover image
1670         pixels from a Gaussian noise with the feature map as the condition, which can retain more image
1671         information generated by the LLM, thus achieving better image generation results. We freeze
1672         all visual modules and pre-tokenize images into discrete visual token sequences, and only load
1673         vision modules to generate actual images when evaluate on downstream image generation tasks.
             This can greatly reduce the training cost and improve efficiency. Notably, the vision modules in


                                                           31
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1674
           our framework, though inherits from LaVIT, only performs "Stage 1 Tokenizer Training," while
1675       LaVIT’s vision module also performs "Stage 2: Unified Vision–Language Pre-training."
1676
         • Extended VL Vocabulary: as we stated in Section 3.1, SEED-LLaMA and LaVIT directly learn
1677       new visual token embeddings and initialize them with the distribution of original textual token
1678       embeddings. Visual tokens in the VL vocabulary correspond one-to-one with visual latent codes
1679       in the visual codebook. Considering the large difference in embedding dimensions between
1680       visual token embeddings in the VL vocabulary (4096) and visual latent codes in the visual
1681       codebook (32), we follow the factorized embedding parameterization in ALBERT (Lan et al.,
1682       2020) to replace visual token embeddings |V| × 4096 with two smaller embedding matrices
1683       |V| × 32, 32 × 4096, where |V| is the number of visual tokens in the VL vocabulary. The former
1684       matrix is directly initialized with visual latent codes in the visual codebook. In our preliminary
1685
           experiments, we found that our initialization method of the VL vocabulary can significantly
           improve the performance of multimodal comprehension tasks, and has similar performance on
1686
           multimodal generation tasks.
1687
         • Training Objective: SEED-LLaMA and our framework both treat the multimodal sequence as
1688
           a discrete sequence of image–text interleaved tokens, and train the LLM with an autoregressive
1689
           objective to generate predictions of the next token. To mitigate the loss of detailed information
1690       caused by vector quantization, LaVIT designs two different training objectives for image-to-text
1691       and text-to-image cases. For image-to-text, LaVIT is similar to EMU and uses the continuous
1692       visual features of images as the condition to predict all discrete textual tokens and calculate
1693       loss for textual tokens only; for text-to-image, LaVIT is similar to SEED-LLaMA and our
1694       framework, which tokenizes images into discrete visual token sequences and calculates loss for
1695       both textual and visual tokens. To pursue simplicity, efficiency, and scalability, our framework
1696       is similar to SEED-LLaMA, which tokenizes images into discrete visual token sequences, so
1697       that different types of multimodal training data can be converted into discrete sequences of
1698
           image–text interleaved tokens, and trained with a unified autoregressive objective to fully learn
           the extended vision–language vocabulary of the LLM.
1699
1700
         • Training Data and Settings: For our framework, we use MMG I C with 4M samples and IC
           with 52M image–caption pairs for pre-training; we use 1.21M multimodal instruction samples,
1701
           1M aesthetic image–caption pairs and 1M samples from MMG I C for supervised fine-tuning;
1702
           during pre-training and SFT stages, our framework freezes all parameters of vision modules
1703       and most parameters of the LLM, and only tunes partial parameters of the LLM (<8%): the
1704       extended VL vocabulary, additional LoRA modules, norm layers and a language model head
1705       layer. Existing MLLMs typically use more pre-training and fine-tuning data, and fully fine-tune
1706       all LLM parameters and partial vision module parameters. Take SEED-LLaMA as an example,
1707       uses more pre-training data (∼770M vs <60M), SFT data (∼145M vs <4M), and full-param
1708       fine-tuning. Its computation and data resources are extremely expensive and large (well over 10
1709       times that of our work). As shown in Table 3 and 4, compared with SEED-LLaMA, our baseline
1710       MLLM-MMG I C & IC requires significantly less data (∼1/15) and partial-param fine-tuning
1711
           (<8%), achieving significant advantages on most benchmarks and comparable performance
           on COCO and VizWiz. Comparing with LaVIT, even though our framework does not convert
1712
           images into continuous visual features to trade off lower loss of image representation and better
1713
           multimodal comprehension performance, we still achieve significant advantages on three VQA
1714       benchmarks, and comparable performance on other benchmarks.
1715
1716   It should be emphasized that it is difficult to compare our baselines with existing MLLMs fairly, and
1717
       the purpose of this paper is not to develop new frameworks, training objectives or benchmark SOTAs,
       but to explore the potential of multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs. To this end, we explore
1718
       different data recipes for multi-grained concept annotations and investigate the comparison and
1719
       collaboration between MMG I C with widely-used coarse-grained image–caption data. Evaluations
1720   and in-depth analyses on 12 benchmarks for multimodal comprehension and generation are conducted
1721   in both pre-training and supervised fine-tuning stages.
1722
1723   Comparison with Grounding MLLMs. Fine-grained object region annotations are crucial for
1724   grounding VLMs/MLLMs. They usually convert bounding box coordinates into discrete tokens in
1725   text or visual markers in images. Take KOSMOS -2 (Peng et al., 2023) as an example, it falls into
1726   the former category, utilizing object bounding box coordinates through special discrete position
1727   tokens placed after corresponding texts in the caption. Unlike KOSMOS -2, MMG I C converts object
       bounding box coordinates into corresponding object regions (cropped from images), transforming

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       pure textual object-level annotations into multimodal annotations. Structured templates integrate
1729   different granularities into unified image–text interleaved documents, enhancing MLLM’s ability to
1730   locate concepts in textual annotations to corresponding regions in images. With the unique complex
1731   context processing capabilities of MLLMs and the LM autoregressive loss, our framework can
1732   explicitly utilize multi-grained annotations in MMG I C to optimize multimodal alignment across

1735   D.2   A PPLICABILITY OF MMG I C TO E XISTING MLLM S
1736
1737   Our framework combines the advantages of LaVIT and SEED-LLaMA to allow efficient exploration
1738   on various multimodal tasks, using only LM autoregressive loss without extra modules and loss
1739   functions for MMG I C, maintaining the framework’s generality and efficiency. Compared to the
1740   current image–text pairs or image–text interleaved documents used by MLLMs, MMG I C can be seen
1741   as image–text interleaved documents with textual (caption, label, label description) and visual (object
1742   region) multi-grained concept annotations. They are well-integrated through our carefully designed
1743
       structured templates, forming image–text interleaved documents that can be directly used by various
       MLLMs. Therefore, both our MMG I C dataset and our framework exhibit good generality. MMG I C
1744
       do not impose any requirements or limitations on the model architecture or loss function of MLLMs.
1745
       It can be directly applied to various MLLMs, whether they process images as continuous vectors
1746   (e.g., EMU, LaVIT, VL-GPT) or discrete tokens (e.g., CM3Leon, SEED-LLaMA, Chameleon). Our
1747   experiments on 12 multimodal comprehension and generation benchmarks also ensure the reliability
1748   and credibility of our conclusions, making sure that MMG I C can be quickly applied to other MLLMs’
1749   training. Overall, our model framework and training loss follow CM3Leon and SEED-LLaMA’s spirit
1750   of unified discrete sequence modeling, offering simplicity, efficiency, and scalability. We did not
1751   make any special architectural or loss design modifications for MLLMs, ensuring good generalization
1752   and reliability of our framework, training loss, dataset, and experimental explorations.
1753   Moreover, our focus is to explore the potential of multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs.
1754   Directly fine-tuning existing SOTA MLLMs cannot fully demonstrate this point. Thus, we explore
1755   comparison and collaboration between MMG I C and IC starting from the multimodal pre-training
1756   phase. Unfortunately, all SOTA MLLMs use the LAION-5B dataset (and its subsets) for pre-training.
1757   However, the official data download channels were closed by the LAION team due to safety reviews
1758   (from December 19, 2023). This prevents us from retraining existing SOTA MLLMs from the
1759   pre-training stage in a controlled environment to directly prove MMG I C’s superiority over IC.
1760
       Therefore, we collected ∼250M image–caption pairs from other widely used and recognized datasets
       and selected ∼52M as the IC dataset for our experiments. Through the evaluation of three baselines
1761
       across 12 multimodal understanding and generation benchmarks, we demonstrate the potential of
1762
       MMG I C for MLLMs. Combining MMG I C and IC can leverage their respective advantages in terms
1763   of depth and breadth of concept representation, further improving MLLM performance.

1766   D.3   A NNOTATION S YNTHESIS IN MMG I C
1767
       MMG I C is constructed from several public human-annotated object detection datasets, which covers
1768
       many common concepts but currently cannot scale up to cover more concepts in the real world. We
1769
       only automatically synthesize image captions for all images with the de-facto captioning model BLIP-
1770   2 (Li et al., 2023b) and ranking model CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and synthesize label descriptions
1771   for all labels with the strong GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). For synthesized captions, we randomly
1772   sample 1000 captions to manually check the quality, and find that the quality is acceptable. For
1773   synthesized label descriptions, we carefully design prompt templates and human-annotated in-context
1774   examples, and manually check all synthesized label descriptions to ensure the quality. Besides, with
1775   the help of WordNet, we check category labels, and update the polysemous labels based on the specific
1776   data samples for better differentiation, e.g., “batter” → “batter (ballplayer)” or “batter (cooking)”.
1777   In this paper, we want to use and check existing human-annotated annotations as much as possible,
1778   and minimize the automatic generation of annotations, thus reducing noise that interferes with our
1779   exploration of multi-grained concept annotations for MLLMs. Hence, we take the above strategies
1780   to ensure the quality of synthesized annotations (captions and label descriptions). Moreover, in
1781   MMG I C, fine-grained attribute and relationship labels are only available in partial images from the
       original datasets. Although we try to automatically generate fine-grained attributes and relationship


                                                         33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       annotations with external open-source models based on image captions (and images), after careful
1783   manual checks, the synthesized fine-grained annotations are found to be of low quality, with non-
1784   negligible hallucinations and noise, which are very likely to negatively impact MLLMs. Thus, only
1785   high-quality manually annotated fine-grained annotations were used in MMG I C to avoid unnecessary
1786   hallucinations and noise. We focus our limited resources and effort on carefully handling existing
1787   data with meticulous manual checks and in-depth experimental exploration to ensure the quality of
1788   MMG I C and the reliability of our exploration.
1789   To further ensure that such partially missing fine-grained annotations do not introduce additional
1790   bias, we compare MMG I C with only object labels and label descriptions, as well as MMG I C with
1791   all category labels and label descriptions, i.e., MMG I C(CLD). We find that the performance is still
1792   improved by the partially available fine-grained attribute and relationship labels. More importantly,
1793   as shown in Section 4.4 and Figure 5 (Left), compared to MMG I C with only image captions, multi-
1794   grained concept annotations in MMG I C can still help MLLMs achieve significant improvements of
1795   2.15 and 10.31 points in the corresponding "Instance Attribute" and "Instance Interaction" dimensions,

2088
       category labels for each object. To accommodate varying aspect ratios of object bounding boxes
2089   and the requirement for a square input image, following KOSMOS -G (Pan et al., 2023), we crop
2090   a new larger square object region Si that contains the original object region Ri , Ri ⊆ Si , with
2091   their centers aligned as closely as possible. Since Si may include not only the object region Ri
2092   but also the surrounding object regions, we design a simple but effective strategy to update the
2093   object label annotations of Si by merging the object label annotations of Ri with the object label
2094   annotations of surrounding object regions, to improve the quality of object annotations. For each Ri
2095   and its corresponding new region Si , we calculate the intersection-over-area (IoA) between Si and
                                           Area(Si ∩Rj )
2096   all Rj(j̸=i) , i.e., IoA(Si , Rj ) = Area(R  j)
                                                         . If IoA(Si , Rj ) ≥ 0.8, we consider Rj as a part of Si
2097   and update the annotations of Rj to the annotations of Si . This strategy can help to include more
2098   relevant object regions in the region Si and improve the quality of object annotations. After that, we
2099   remove Si with duplicate annotations and keep the unique ones.
2100   Since each visual token in our framework corresponds to a 14 × 14 pixel region, we remove Si
2101   with edge length less than 28 pixels or greater than 182 pixels to ensure that the object region is not
2102   too small or too large. We also remove all the images that don’t have any bounding box and resize
2103   all images to 224 × 224 pixels. Finally, for objects in the image, MMG I C provides visual tokens
2104   of cropped regions and textual tokens of corresponding fine-grained category labels and location
2105   descriptions. The pre-processed object regions in the images are ready to be tokenized and fill in the
       template in Section F.6.
