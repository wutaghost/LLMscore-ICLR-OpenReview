009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Ribonucleic acid (RNA) plays a crucial role in various biological functions, and
012            designing sequences that reliably fold into specified structures remains a signifi-
013            cant challenge in computational biology. Existing methods often struggle with ef-
014            ficiency and scalability, as they require extensive search or optimization to tackle
015            this complex combinatorial problem. In this paper, we propose C OCO RNA,
016
               a COllective RNA design method using COoperative multi-agent reinforcement
               learning, for the RNA secondary structure design problem. C OCO RNA decom-
017
               poses the RNA design task into multiple sub-tasks, which are assigned to multi-
018
               ple agents to solve collaboratively, alleviating the challenges of the curse of di-
019            mensionality as well as the issues of sparse and delayed rewards.By employing a
020            centralized Critic network and leveraging global information during training, we
021            promote cooperation among agents, enabling the distributed policies to coopera-
022            tively optimize the joint objective, thereby resulting in a high-quality collective
023            RNA design policy. The trained model is capable of completing RNA secondary
024            structure design with less time and fewer steps, without requiring further training
025            or search on new tasks. We evaluate C OCO RNA on the Rfam dataset and the
026            Eterna100 benchmark. Experimental results demonstrate that C OCO RNA outper-
027
               forms existing algorithms in terms of design time and success rate, highlighting
               its practicality and effectiveness.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   RNA plays diverse roles in biological systems, including encoding proteins, regulating gene expres-
033   sion, and acting as a catalyst in biochemical reactions (Dykstra et al., 2022). Since the function of
034   RNA is closely tied to its structure, designing RNA molecules that can fold into desired structures
035   has become a critical problem. We focus on the RNA secondary structure design problem, which
036   involves creating RNA sequences that can fold into a desired secondary structure. This is a complex
037   combinatorial optimization problem and is computationally challenging due to the vastness of the
038
      sequence space and the intricate base-pairing interactions.
039   Most existing RNA design algorithms rely on online search or optimization techniques. Meth-
040   ods such as RNAinverse (Hofacker et al., 1994), RNA-SSD (Andronescu et al., 2004), INFO-
041   RNA (Busch & Backofen, 2006), and antaRNA (Kleinkauf et al., 2015) use stochastic search strate-
042   gies to find sequences that fold into the target structure. However, for longer sequences, the high
043   dimensionality of the search space severely affects efficiency and scalability.
044   Some learning-based methods (Eastman et al., 2018; Runge et al., 2019) aim to reduce reliance on
045   random search but still require extensive search or optimization times. However, existing methods
046   also face the curse of dimensionality: the design space grows exponentially with sequence length,
047   leading to extremely high computational costs. This limitation restricts the applicability of some
048   methods; for instance, RNAinformer (Patil et al., 2024) is a generative model-based design method
049   but is only suitable for short sequences.
050   Reinforcement learning (RL) is a promising approach to addressing biological sequence design
051   problems. RL enables agents to learn decision-making policies through trial-and-error and explo-
052   ration without relying on labeled data. Additionally, RL allows for flexible optimization guidance
053   through the use of reward functions, which can be tailored to encourage desired design outcomes.
      Another potential advantage of RL-based methods is the ability to offload time-consuming online


                                                        1

054
      optimization to offline training, enabling zero-shot learning on new tasks. However, RL-based meth-
055   ods face challenges in the context of RNA design. The vast design space inherent to biological
056   sequences increases the difficulty of policy exploration and learning, adversely affecting policy per-
057   formance. Moreover, longer sequences exacerbate the issues of delayed and sparse rewards (Ried-
058   miller et al., 2018), which makes it difficult for RL agents to receive sufficient feedback. As a
059   result, existing RL-based methods either require long search times (Eastman et al., 2018) or require
060   online optimization for each target structure during the design phase (Runge et al., 2019). While
061   these methods show promise, they do not fully exploit the potential of learning-based approaches to
062   generalize across different target structures.
063   To address the above challenges, we propose a collective RNA design method based on multi-
064   agent reinforcement learning (MARL). By decomposing the problem and learning cooperatively, we
065   alleviate the curse of dimensionality and mitigate sparse rewards, thereby improving the efficiency
066   and quality of RNA design. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
067
068          • We formulate the RNA secondary structure design as a collective design problem, where
069
               multiple agents cooperate to design RNA sequences. By dividing the task among multiple
               agents—each focusing on a specific part of the sequence or structural elements—we ef-
070
               fectively reduce the complexity faced by individual agents, enabling more efficient policy
071
               learning and exploration.
072
073          • We propose C OCO RNA, a MARL method based on the Centralized Training with De-
074
               centralized Execution (CTDE) framework to learn collective RNA design policies. Addi-
               tionally, we introduce a search-augmented experience replay method to improve learning
075
               efficiency. Once trained, C OCO RNA can efficiently design RNA sequences within just one
076
               or a few episodes, requiring minimal time and steps without the need for further training or
077            search on new tasks.
078
             • We conduct experiments on the Rfam dataset and the Eterna100-v2 benchmark. The results
079
               demonstrate that C OCO RNA surpasses existing methods in both design time and success
080
               rate. Moreover, across both datasets, C OCO RNA solves more sequences within shorter
081            time limits, demonstrating superior generalization performance.

086   2.1   RNA S ECONDARY S TRUCTURE D ESIGN
087
      Optimization methods. Intelligent optimization algorithms have made significant progress in solv-
088
      ing the RNA secondary structure design problem (Churkin et al., 2018). Methods such as INFO-
089
      RNA (Busch & Backofen, 2006) combine dynamic programming with stochastic local search for
090   RNA inverse folding. MODENA (Taneda, 2010) utilizes multi-objective genetic algorithms, and
091   antaRNA (Kleinkauf et al., 2015) employs ant colony optimization techniques. These methods face
092   major challenges when dealing with longer sequences, as the cost of performing stochastic search
093   increases exponentially with sequence length. Additionally, evaluating designed RNA sequences
094   requires repeatedly applying folding algorithms like ViennaRNA (Lorenz et al., 2011), which are
095   based on dynamic programming and have computation times that grow significantly with sequence
096   length.
097   Learning-based methods. Recently, learning-based methods have partially alleviated this problem.
098   Reinforcement learning approaches (Eastman et al., 2018; Angermueller et al., 2019; Runge et al.,
099   2019; 2024b) have been proposed for RNA design and other biological sequence design problems.
100   For instance, Eastman et al. (2018) used RL to perform local search in the RNA sequence space.
101   Runge et al. (2019) combined RL with neural architecture search to improve RNA design policies.
102   Additionally, generative model-based methods like RNAinformer (Patil et al., 2024) have been de-
103   veloped. While RNAinformer show promise, it require substantial computational resources and is
104
      thus only applicable to sequences shorter than 100 nucleotides.
105   Unlike traditional optimization or search-based methods, RL has the potential to enable agents to
106   adaptively make decisions without additional training or optimization, thereby achieving few-shot or
107   even zero-shot design when encountering new instances. However, previous RL-based works have
      not truly achieved zero-shot design. For example, LEARNA (Runge et al., 2019) requires hundreds


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      or even thousands of learning episodes when facing new RNA structures. The vast search space of
109   the RNA design problem increases the difficulty of policy exploration and improvement, making it
110   challenging to obtain policies with strong generalization capabilities.
111
112
      Our work addresses these challenges by introducing an MARL framework that decomposes the
      RNA design problem into sub-tasks handled by multiple agents. Similarly, RNA-SSD (Andronescu
113
      et al., 2004) reduces the size of the search space by splitting the full sequence into shorter subse-
114
      quences and optimizing each subsequence independently. However, combining solutions to local
115   problems often does not yield a solution to the global problem, and RNA-SSD is unable to incor-
116   porate global knowledge. In contrast, our method fully leverages global information, promoting
117   cooperation among multiple agents and guiding joint optimization. Another work that adopts a sim-
118   ilar idea is G AME O PT (Bal et al., 2023), which establishes a game between different optimization
119   variables to decouple the combinatorial decision space into individual decision sets. Although both
120   methods are based on problem decomposition, our approach fundamentally differs from G AME O PT.
121   Our goal is to train a set of policies that can cooperatively make dynamic decisions to complete the
122   design task, rather than searching for an optimal solution consisting of variables.
123
124   2.2   M ULTI -AGENT R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
125
      MARL methods address decision-making problems involving multiple agents by employing RL
126
      techniques (Gronauer & Diepold, 2022; Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023). In MARL, agents learn
127   to cooperate or compete by interacting with the environment and optimizing their policies based on
128   a reward function that defines the task objectives. This approach allows agents to autonomously
129   learn policies without human intervention. Algorithms such as COMA (Foerster et al., 2018),
130   QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018) and MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022) have demonstrated significant poten-
131   tial on complex MARL benchmarks like the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (Samvelyan et al.,
132   2019; Ellis et al., 2024). Recently, MARL has been widely applied to various real-world multi-agent
133   tasks, including multi-robot systems (Chen et al., 2017; Willemsen et al., 2021; Paul et al., 2022),
134   production scheduling (Johnson et al., 2022), and autonomous driving (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2016;
135
      Candela et al., 2022).
136   However, to the best of our knowledge, no prior work has modeled biological sequence design as a
137   multi-agent decision-making problem and applied MARL to solve it. Our work is the first to explore
138   the potential of MARL in RNA sequence design, leveraging the cooperative capabilities of multiple
139   agents to tackle the high complexity of the RNA design problem.

143   3.1   RNA D ESIGN P ROBLEM
144
145   We consider the RNA secondary structure design problem: given a target secondary structure, design
146   an RNA sequence that most stably folds into that structure. RNA secondary structures are commonly
147   represented using dot-bracket notation, where unpaired nucleotides are denoted by dots (.), and base
148
      pairs are represented by matching parentheses: an opening parenthesis (() indicates the 5’ end of a
      base pair, and a closing parenthesis ()) indicates the 3’ end. For example, the notation ((...))
149
      represents a hairpin loop structure with base pairs at the ends and unpaired nucleotides in the loop.
150
151   At each position in the RNA sequence, there are four possible nucleotides: adenine (A), uracil (U),
152   guanine (G), and cytosine (C). Designing an RNA sequence that folds into a specific secondary
153   structure is a complex combinatorial optimization problem. The size of the search space grows
154
      exponentially with the length of the RNA sequence. For a sequence of length l, there are 4l possible
      nucleotide combinations, making exhaustive search computationally infeasible for large l.

181   3.3   C ENTRALIZED T RAINING WITH D ECENTRALIZED E XECUTION
182
183   The Centralized Training with Decentralized Execution (CTDE) (Kraemer & Banerjee, 2016) is a
184   paradigm in MARL that leverages the advantages of both centralized and decentralized approaches.
185   During the training phase, agents utilize global information to evaluate and improve their local
186
      policies. In the execution phase, agents select actions based on their local observations and policy
      functions. CTDE mitigates the non-stationarity problem in multi-agent environments by allowing
187
      agents to consider the joint dynamics during training, promoting interaction and cooperation among
188
      agents. At the same time, decentralized execution based on local observations alleviates the curse
189   of dimensionality and enhances system scalability, reducing the dependency on global information
190   and communication capabilities.
191
192
      Actor-Critic Methods with CTDE: In the standard Actor-Critic framework (Barto et al., 1983), the
      Critic network learns the value function to evaluate the current policy, while the Actor network learns
193
      the policy function and adjusts the policy parameters based on the Critic’s estimation. The Actor-
194
      Critic architecture can be naturally extended to multi-agent systems under the CTDE paradigm,
195   resulting in centralized Critics and decentralized Actors (Lowe et al., 2017; Iqbal & Sha, 2019).
196   During training, the global information is used to train the centralized Critic networks, and the
197   outputs of the Critic are used to improve each agent’s Actor network. During execution, agents make
198   decisions independently based on their own policies, relying solely on their local observations.

335   4.4   S EARCH -AUGMENTED E XPERIENCE R EPLAY
336
337
      Due to the vastness of the RNA design space, learning-based methods may face a cold-start problem:
      initial random policies perform poorly, leading to a lack of high-quality experience data for effective
338
      learning. Inspired by the Hindsight Experience Replay (HER) (Andrychowicz et al., 2017), we in-
339
      troduce a Search-Augmented Experience Replay (SAER) method, using a limited amount of greedy
340   search during the early stages of training to improve data quality.
341
342   Specifically, after an agent selects an action a according to its policy π and receives a reward r, we
343
      perform a local search to check if there exists a better action a′ that yields a higher reward r′ . If
      such an action is found, we replace the original action with the better one and modify the experience
344
      tuple (o, a, r) to (o, a′ , r′ ). By doing so, we enhance the quality of the experience data, which in
345
      turn improves learning efficiency.
346
347   This approach helps alleviate the cold-start problem by providing the agents with higher-quality
348   training data in the early stages. To prevent the local greedy search from causing the policy to
349
      converge prematurely to suboptimal solutions, we gradually reduce and eventually remove this op-
      eration as training progresses.
350
351   It is important to note that the replay buffer used here differs from those in off-policy RL meth-
352   ods (Mnih et al., 2015; Lillicrap et al., 2016). In our method, experiences are collected over multiple
353   steps using the current policy until a fixed horizon is reached. At that point, the buffer is used to
354   perform multiple gradient-based updates to both the Actor and Critic networks. Once the updates
355
      are completed, the buffer is cleared, and new experiences are sampled based on the updated policy.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we proposed C OCO RNA, a collective RNA design method using cooperative multi-
530   agent reinforcement learning to address the challenges in RNA secondary structure design. By
531   formulating the RNA design task as a collective problem and decomposing it into multiple sub-tasks
532   assigned to individual agents, the complexity faced by each agent was effectively reduced. Further-
533   more, the implementation of the CTDE framework combined with the introduction of the SAER
534   method fostered multi-agent cooperation, enhanced policy exploration, and improved learning effi-
535   ciency. Experiments conducted on the Rfam dataset and the Eterna100-v2 benchmark demonstrated
536
      both the effectiveness and efficiency of C OCO RNA.
537   We believe that the success of C OCO RNA showcases the potential of cooperative multi-agent re-
538   inforcement learning in biological sequence design tasks and complex combinatorial optimization
539   problems. We anticipate that this method can be extended to more complex application scenarios,
      such as RNA tertiary structure design and protein design.

844   B.2   L IMITATION AND D ISCUSSION
845
      In theory, gradient-based deep learning and reinforcement learning methods generally guarantee
846
      convergence to local optima rather than global optima due to the non-convex nature of the optimiza-
847
      tion landscape. However, in practice, the ability of reinforcement learning algorithms to go beyond
848   local optima and approach global optima depends significantly on how well they balance exploration
849   and exploitation.
850
851
      Specifically, CocoRNA is built upon PPO, which includes an entropy bonus in the objective function,
      which explicitly encourages the policy to maintain high entropy. This prevents the policy from
852
      becoming too deterministic too quickly, avoiding premature convergence to suboptimal policies and
853
      promoting continued exploration.
854
855   In the context of the RNA design task, the multi-agent framework of CocoRNA further aids in
856   overcoming local optima. Unlike single-agent methods that mutate one nucleotide at one step, our
857
      method allows multiple agents to act simultaneously on different parts of the RNA sequence. This
      introduces greater diversity and increases the exploration of the state space, thereby increasing the
858
      opportunities to discover better policies.

936
      D.1   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURES
937
938   Each agent in the C OCO RNA framework is equipped with its own Actor and Critic networks.
939   Although the Critic network leverages shared global information, the network parameters are not
940   shared across agents. The Actor network takes local observations as input and outputs a discrete
941   action (the nucleotide to be placed in the RNA sequence). The Critic network, on the other hand,
942   processes the global state, which includes the full RNA sequence and secondary structure, and out-
943   puts a scalar value representing the estimated cumulative future reward for the entire system.
944
945         • Actor Network: The Actor network consists of several fully connected layers followed by
946           a softmax output layer. Each agent’s observation is passed through the network to compute
947           a probability distribution over the action space {A, U, G, C}.
948         • Critic Network: The Critic network takes the entire RNA sequence and secondary struc-
949           ture as input. This input is processed through two separate CNN modules: one for the
950           sequence and one for the structure. The outputs of these modules are concatenated and
951           passed through a fully connected network to produce a value estimate for the entire system.
952
953
      D.2   T RAINING AND O PTIMIZATION
954
955   The training procedure involves collecting experiences over a fixed horizon, storing them in a replay
956   buffer, and periodically updating the Actor and Critic networks using these stored experiences. The
957   PPO algorithm is used to update the networks by maximizing the clipped objective, ensuring stable
958   training. We also apply the Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) technique to compute the
959   advantage function, which helps reduce variance during training.

1207
       In the intersection of machine learning and biology, data leakage is a significant concern (Bernett
       et al., 2024). For supervised learning methods, if the test set contains data that are highly similar to
1208
       those in the training set, it may not accurately assess the algorithm’s performance on unseen data.
1209
       However, since reinforcement learning is not a supervised learning method and the dataset does not
1210   contain explicit labels, data leakage is usually not an issue.
1211
1212   To further demonstrate C OCO RNA’s generalization capability across different RNA structures, we
1213
       re-split the dataset based on structural similarity rather than using a random split. Specifically, we
       first calculated the edit distance between each pair of RNA structures in the dataset, which consists
1214
       of 65, 000 RNA structures. This resulted in a 65, 000 × 65, 000 similarity matrix that quantifies the
1215
       pairwise differences between all RNA structures in the dataset. Next, we applied the MiniBatch K-
1216   Means clustering algorithm to partition the dataset into 100 distinct clusters based on the calculated
1217   distances. We then selected one cluster at random to serve as the test set, consisting of 650 RNA
1218   structures. We filtered the remaining data by removing RNA structures that had high similarity to
1219   the test set, and retained 60, 000 RNA structures as the final training set. The minimum edit distance
1220   between any structure in the test set and those in the training set is 111, and the average edit distance
1221   is 188.42.
1222   Table 13 presents the results of training and testing on the re-split dataset. It can be observed
1223   that the differences in results across different datasets are minimal; different splitting methods do

1392
1393
1394
       While C OCO RNA demonstrates promising results in RNA secondary structure design, there are
       several limitations.
1395
1396   First, it is non-trivial to design a accurate and reliable reward model given the complexity of biolog-
1397   ical systems. We may resort to large-scale pretrained models, e.g., RNA/protein language models.
1398   However, these models are often too large and thus are computationally expensive. One potential
1399   solution is to go with a model-based RL, where an explicit model of the environment is learned and
1400
       used to predict future states and rewards more efficiently.
1401   Another potential limitation is the decomposition method considered in the current CocoRNA. Given
1402   the high-dimensional nucleotide design space, it will be more promising to study an adaptive decom-
1403   position mechanism. This might be achieved by designing hierarchical policies, where high-level
       agents make decisions about task decomposition and low-level agents focus on specific sub-tasks.

1404
       Last but not least, this paper chooses the RNA inverse design problem as a proof-of-concept study.
1405   However, we believe CocoRNA or even MARL will be a potent method for solving such highly
1406   complex structured space problems. As part of our next step, we will explore other biological
1407   sequence design problems such as protein.
