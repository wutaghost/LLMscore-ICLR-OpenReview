009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Iterative refinement has emerged as an effective paradigm for enhancing the capa-
012            bilities of large language models (LLMs) on complex tasks. However, existing ap-
013            proaches typically implement iterative refinement at the application or prompting
014            level, relying on autoregressive (AR) modeling. The sequential token generation
015            in AR models can lead to high inference latency. To overcome these challenges,
016
               we propose Context-Wise Order-Agnostic Language Modeling (COrAL), which
               incorporates iterative refinement directly into the LLM architecture while main-
017
               taining computational efficiency. Our approach models multiple token dependen-
018
               cies within manageable context windows, enabling the model to perform iterative
019            refinement internally during the generation process. Leveraging the order-agnostic
020            nature of COrAL, we introduce sliding blockwise order-agnostic decoding, which
021            performs multi-token forward prediction and backward reconstruction within con-
022            text windows. This allows the model to iteratively refine its outputs in parallel
023            in the sliding block, effectively capturing diverse dependencies without the high
024            inference cost of sequential generation. Empirical evaluations on reasoning tasks
025            demonstrate that COrAL improves performance and inference speed, respectively,
026            achieving absolute accuracy gains of 4.6% on GSM8K and 4.0% on LogiQA,
027
               along with inference speedups of up to 3.9× over next-token baselines. Prelimi-
               nary results on code generation indicate a drop in pass rates due to inconsistencies
028
               in order-agnostic outputs, highlighting the inherent quality–speed trade-off.

      2.2   O BJECTIVE : C ONTEXT-W ISE O RDER -AGNOSTIC AUTOREGRESSIVE M ODELING
163
164   To address the above limitations in AR language modeling, we propose Context-Wise Order-
165   Agnostic Language Modeling (COrAL), unifying token-level dependency modeling and sequence-
166   level denoising to advance the capabilities of current LLMs. Previous order-agnostic modeling
167   works attempt to capture various factorization orders involving long dependencies that are difficult
168   to model and fit. In contrast, COrAL learns the orderless relationships within predetermined context
169   windows. Built on the AR foundation, our COrAL framework leverages the superior capability of
170
      sequential language modeling in LLMs.
171   COrAL tackles the problem of generative language modeling by combining forward multi-token
172   prediction with backward denoising in a context-wise and order-agnostic framework. Denoting the
173   context window size as k 2 , we model the conditional probability distribution of each target token by
174   considering an ensemble of dependencies over all possible positions in the context:

      ment. The conventional AR modeling, in comparison, becomes a specific case where only the
      forward prediction with k=1, conditioned on previous tokens in the target sequence y, is modeled.

      Forward Prediction and Backward Reconstruction. As shown in Figure 3, we decompose the
182
      order-agnostic objective into forward prediction and backward reconstruction. In forward pre-
183   diction, COrAL learns to predict multiple future tokens simultaneously given past tokens in the
184   ground-truth sequence. For backward reconstruction, we randomly corrupt tokens in the input se-
185   quence to create the intermediate states y (l) in Eq. 3. Similar to BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), we
186   compute the loss only on the corrupted tokens. During training, we use the original data for predic-
187   tion and the corrupted data for reconstruction. This decomposition disentangles the self-refinement
188   capability from forward prediction, leveraging all data points to enhance sequence modeling.
189
190   Corruption Strategy. Our corruption and reconstruction process is a form of denoising autoen-
191   coding (Vincent et al., 2008) in language modeling. However, instead of representation learning, we
192   aim to endow the model with the self-refinement capability to revise the generated content. Inspired
193   by masked autoencoders (He et al., 2022), we divide the output sequence into non-overlapping
194
      patches and randomly sample a subset for corruption. Each patch is a fragment of text containing
      one or multiple consecutive tokens in the sequence. Specifically, we corrupt a patch by either (i)
195
      replacing it with a random patch sampled from the current sequence or (ii) repeating the first token
196
      to replace the other tokens in the patch. This design draws on insight from Ye et al. (2024) that
197   model performance can be significantly improved by simply enhancing consistency across steps.

199   2.3   A RCHITECTURE : TARGET-AWARE Q UERY R EPRESENTATION FOR S ELF -ATTENTION
200
201   We build our framework by adapting the standard architecture of LLMs using decoder-only Trans-
202   formers (Brown et al., 2020). Unlike prior NAR works employing encoder–decoder architec-
203   tures (Lee et al., 2018; Kasai et al., 2020), the conventional AR foundation predicts the same dis-
204   tribution given the current context regardless of the target token position. While this demonstrates
205   advanced capabilities of sequence modeling and generation, the typical parameterization of next-
206
      token distribution constrains its generalizability to the order-agnostic objective in Eq. 3.
207   Previous works on order-agnostic modeling have explored various ways to incorporate positional
208   information, including scaling up the dimensionality of the final projection layer (Stern et al., 2018)
209   and adding look-ahead tokens (Monea et al., 2023) or extra decoding heads (Cai et al., 2024;
210   Gloeckle et al., 2024). Despite their promising performance, these methods introduce the over-
211   head of additional self-attention network calls and new parameters for multi-position prediction.
212
      Instead, we propose a seamless adjustment without adding extra model parameters. Specifically, we
      apply a generalized Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) at the final layer of the
213
      decoder-only Transformers to integrate target-aware information into the query representations.
214

      Target-Aware RoPE. RoPE encodes positional information into query and key representations,
217   ensuring that their inner product inherently contains relative position information in self-attention:
218   f (qm , m)⊤ f (kn , n) = g(qm , kn , m−n), where f is the positional encoding function applied to the
219   query and key embeddings at m-th and n-th positions, respectively. Conventional RoPE integrates
220   positional information of the current token to form the query representation. While this effectively
221   enhances the position-aware representation of the input token in intermediate hidden states, it intro-
222   duces inherent misalignment with the target token position when using the learned representation
223   for output prediction. To avoid this problem, we propose Target-Aware RoPE (Figure 3), which
224   modifies the positional encoding function at the final layer by considering the target token position
225
      in the query representation:
226                      f (qm , µ)⊤ f (kn , n) = g(qm , kn , µ − n), µ ∈ [m − k, m + k]                   (4)
227   The rationale behind this modification is that the position encoding in RoPE can adapt the represen-
228   tation of the current token to be tailored for the target position. This simple yet effective adjustment
229   endows the model with the target-aware capability, allowing it to predict tokens at various positions
230   without the overhead of additional entire network calls.

232   3   S LIDING B LOCKWISE O RDER -AGNOSTIC D ECODING
233
234   Leveraging the order-agnostic capabilities of COrAL, we propose Sliding Blockwise Order-
235   Agnostic Decoding, a parallel decoding strategy to enable efficient iterative refinement.
236
      High inference latency significantly hinders the broader application of AR-LLMs. Recent studies
237   have tackled this bottleneck from various angles to accelerate inference. For instance, speculative
238   decoding employs a smaller, faster draft model to propose multiple continuations, which the larger
239   target model then verifies and accepts (Leviathan et al., 2023; Miao et al., 2024). Blockwise parallel
240   decoding directly leverages the large model to generate multiple tokens simutaneously (Stern et al.,
241   2018; Cai et al., 2024). However, these studies increase memory consumption, which thus limits
242   the scalability and impedes distributional deployment. Another promising line of work breaks the
243   sequential dependency by adopting Jacobi decoding (Santilli et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024) for iterative
244   refinement without architectural add-ons. Kou et al. (2024) propose consistency LLMs to further
245
      improve the performance of Jacobi decoding inspired by consistency models (Song et al., 2023).
246   While these existing approaches improve inference efficiency, they rely on the conventional left-to-
247   right AR foundation with monotonic dependencies. In this work, we leverage the order-agnostic
248   nature of COrAL to perform backward sequence-level refinement and forward multi-token predic-
249   tion simultaneously, significantly accelerating inference. At each step, we ensemble the output
250   distributions based on multiple possible dependencies and construct a candidate set to fill a block
251
      of the output sequence. Furthermore, this process facilitates self-refinement by modifying previous
      generations at a higher-level horizon, enhancing output quality with advanced inference capabili-
252
      ties. Next, we detail the ensemble strategy in decoding for candidate construction and verification,
253
      corresponding to the “Collect” and “Verify and Slide” parts in Algorithm 1, respectively.

344   soning, we filter LogiCoT (Liu et al., 2023b) with deduplication and reformulation, obtaining 313K
345   training samples. We assess logical reasoning performance with multiple-choice reading compre-
346   hension tasks that test interpretation and decision-making skills: LogiQA (Liu et al., 2023a), based
347   on the Chinese Civil Service Examination, and ReClor (Yu et al., 2020), sourced from Law School
348   Admission Council exams. For code generation, we train on Magicoder-Eval-Instruct-110K (Wei
349   et al., 2023) and evaluate using programming tasks from HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021).
350
351   Experimental Protocol. To address the discrepancy between the next-token-based pre-trained
352   model and the target order-agnostic model, we adopt a two-stage training strategy (Kumar et al.,
353
      2022) to progressively enhance order-agnostic modeling. We begin with a domain-specific super-
      vised fine-tuned (SFT) model. In the first stage, we perform order-agnostic training exclusively
354
      on the last target-aware layer, while freezing the other layers to preserve the output quality. In the
355
      second stage, we train the entire model by focusing on the previously frozen layers first and then
356   unlocking the last layer to train together. We use Mistral-7B-v0.3 and DeepSeek-Coder-6.7B-base
357   as the base models for reasoning and code generation tasks, respectively. During inference, we
358   explore the effect of the verification stage and ablate the values of decoding context window size and
359   block size. Given the order-agnostic training tax resulting from the discrepancy between pretraining
360   and fine-tuning objectives, we use next-token prediction with the same model as the baseline to
361   ensure a fair comparison. We detail our hyperparameter settings in Section 4.2 and Appendix D.

      How does COrAL model order-agnostic dependencies? We compare the model capabilities
500
      across different positions using token-wise losses and accuracies in Figure 7. Generally, COrAL
501   performs better on backward reconstruction than forward prediction, as shown in the lower losses
502   and higher accuracies on backward dependencies. Notably, we see better generalizability of back-
503   ward reconstruction. For example, given the backward context window size k = 8 and forward
504   context window size k = 4, we find that the loss and accuracy of backward reconstruction with
505   dependencies longer than the training context window size, such as positions |−9| > |−8|, are also
506   at the same level as other backward dependencies. Differently, we observe a dramatic increase in
507   loss and a drop in accuracy from positions 4 to 5 on longer dependencies in forward prediction. This
508   explains how backward refinement benefits from more information in sequence-level generation to
509
      improve performance. We observe decreased performance for forward prediction as the dependency
      gets longer, especially when it exceeds the forward context window size in training. However, we
510
      can mitigate this issue by aggregating multiple predictions for each position. As shown in Figure 7b,
511
      while forward positions with longer dependencies obtain lower accuracies on tghe first prediction,
512   the accumulated accuracies of their non-first predictions are generally higher than those from other
513   dependencies. This illustrates how COrAL can benefit from the tree construction and verification
514   stage in decoding (Section 3) by considering multiple candidates for each position.
515
516   Computation Overhead. One concern regarding order-agnostic modeling is the potential compu-
517   tation overhead to accommodate more dependencies in the context windows. As target-aware RoPE
518   is only applied on the last layer, this overhead scales relatively slower as we increase the number
519   of positions to predict. For example, with forward and backward context window sizes each set as
520   k = 4, each forward pass of COrAL costs 5.48 TFLOPS, compared with 2.81 TFLOPS of next-
521   token prediction. In other words, COrAL predicts 8× number of tokens with less than 2× overhead
522
      in computational cost. This indicates the efficiency of COrAL in leveraging available computation
      resources to accelerate and enhance inference. Furthermore, we can adjust the forward and back-
523
      ward context window sizes to determine the number of tokens to predict in parallel, demonstrating
524
      the flexibility and generalizability of COrAL with target-aware RoPE.

527   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   By unifying denoising with context-wise order-agnostic language modeling and introducing target-
530   aware positional encoding, COrAL incorporates iterative refinement directly into the language gen-
531
      eration process while keeping inference costs low. This approach offers a promising direction for
      developing more efficient and capable large language models by effectively capturing local depen-
532
      dencies within context windows and reducing inference latency.
533
534   The effectiveness and efficiency of COrAL underscores the promise of order-agnostic strategies as a
535   generalized architecture to facilitate generative language modeling and text generation. Specifically,
536   it suggests new opportunities to unify: (i) the sequence modeling and varying-length generation
537
      abilities of autoregressive modeling, (ii) the multi-dependency modeling and multi-token prediction
      mechanisms in order-agnostic modeling, and (iii) the efficient way of iterative refinement in denois-
538
      ing techniques. We hope our work will motivate future research to explore order-agnostic modeling
539
      and denoising in various tasks and other domains beyond sequence modeling.

      L IMITATIONS
919
920
      This work proposes an approach to integrate iterative denoising with order-agnostic language mod-
921
      eling to enhance both the effectiveness and efficiency of LLM inference. While it offers a promising
922   paradigm for mitigating issues related to monotonic dependencies and inference latency in conven-
923   tional autoregressive models, several directions remain for further exploration, including designing
924   corruption and decoding strategies to tailor the model to specific tasks, optimizing the training pro-
925   cess to overcome the order-agnostic training tax, and probing the generalizability and scalability of
926   COrAL across different context sizes, model scales, and tasks.
927
      Specifically, order-agnostic language modeling can struggle with tasks that demand specific output
928   formats or syntax due to inconsistencies in the multi-token predictions. This indicates the impor-
929   tance of a task-specific design of the acceptance scheme in order-agnostic decoding. For instance,
930   the performance of the verification policy in Eq. 6 may vary by language and domain. Addition-
931   ally, applying semantic-aware weights to different dependencies could further enhance task-specific
932   features in the generated outputs. Future work can further explore the potential of incorporating
933   different evaluation heuristics to guide the inference process.
934   Furthermore, incorporating corrupted data may introduce discrepancies between training- and
935   inference-time objectives. For example, our experiments explore rule-based context-wise corruption
936   strategies to create noisy data. Future work could focus on diversifying the types of corruption and
937   scaling the difficulty level and proportion to better understand their impacts on model capabilities.
938
      Finally, due to the computation constraint, we explore the model capabilities in order-agnostic mod-
939
      eling with fixed context window sizes during the SFT stage only. Future work may investigate the
940   effect of scaling context window sizes in both forward and backward directions. Moreover, increas-
941   ing the context window sizes may exacerbate the discrepancy between autoregressive pre-training
942   and order-agnostic fine-tuning. We thus anticipate future work to extend COrAL to the pre-training
943   stage to further enhance model capabilities.

974
       Order-Agnostic Language Modeling. Order-agnostic architectures have been explored to over-
975
       come the limitations of sequential generation in autoregressive models. Uria et al. (2014) propose
976    permutation-based autoregressive models to learn different data orderings for density estimation.
977    In language modeling, Yang et al. (2019) further explore the idea of order-agnostic autoregressive
978    modeling as a generalized pretraining method. Welleck et al. (2019) explore the possibility of non-
979    monotonic text generation in a tree-structure manner and achieve competitive performance with the
980    conventional left-to-right sequential generation. To avoid the high latency in autoregressive decod-
981    ing, Gu et al. (2018) introduce non-autoregressive machine translation by breaking the sequentially
982    causal dependency across time into conditionally independent per-step distributions with latent vari-
983    ables as intermediate steps. Lee et al. (2018) adopt iterative refinement to interpret the latent variable
984
       model, inspired by the design of denoising autoencoders (Alain & Bengio, 2014). Follow-up works
       on non-autoregressive machine translation show promising performance of the iterative refinement
985
       process of mask-predict (Ghazvininejad et al., 2019; Kasai et al., 2020). Our work explores the
986
       potential of unifying the strengths of order-agnostic modeling and denoising to advance sequential
987    modeling in LLMs, demonstrating an efficient way to conduct iterative refinement internally.
988
989    LLM Self-Refinement. Self-refinement in LLMs focuses on various feedback mechanisms to
990    improve the model performance dynamically. Existing works utilize the feedback mainly in two
991    directions. The first one relates to prompting-based frameworks such as instance-level refine-
992    ment (Madaan et al., 2023), step-level guided search (Yao et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023), and
993    principle-driven reasoning (Zheng et al., 2024). Another line of work adapts the feedback as training
994    signals to further enhance the performance of LLMs, including rationale-augmented refinement (Ze-
995    likman et al., 2022), hindsight-driven alignment (Zhang et al., 2023; 2024c), and search-enhanced
996    preference learning (Xie et al., 2024). Unlike existing works relying on the AR foundation in con-
997
       ventional LLMs, we leverage the order-agnostic modeling ability of COrAL to conduct the iterative
       refinement internally while foregoing the computation overhead in AR-LLMs to maintain efficiency.
998
999
1000
       Parallel Decoding. Parallel decoding methods aim to accelerate LLM inference by generating
       multiple tokens simultaneously rather than sequentially. Non-autoregressive models (Gu et al.,
1001
       2018) and blockwise decoding approaches (Stern et al., 2018; Monea et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024)
1002
       have enabled faster generation but often struggle with output inconsistencies. Speculative decod-
1003   ing techniques (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Miao et al., 2024) adopts a faster draft
1004   model to speedup inference while struggling with the deficiency in scalability. Self-speculative de-
1005   coding (Zhang et al., 2024a) uses the same model for drafting by selectively skipping certain inter-
1006   mediate layers. Look-ahead (Santilli et al., 2023) and Jacobi (Fu et al., 2024) decoding, on the other
1007   hand, directly utilize the AR LLMs to enhance performance iteratively. Consistency LLMs (Kou
1008   et al., 2024) further reduces this iteration time drawing inspiration from consistency models (Song
1009   et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2024). In this work, we realize parallel decoding leveraging the
1010   multi-token generation ability of COrAL. Instead of decoding toward the forward direction only, we
1011
       support backward refinement simultaneously to enhance the generation quality further.
1012
1013   Iterative Refinement. Prompt engineering approaches (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023)
1014
       exploit incorrect attempts in historical data to improve the performance of a frozen LLM. In con-
       trast, our method enables the model to directly correct generated mistakes via backward refinement.
1015
       Verifier-based methods (Cobbe et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2024) train separate models to re-rank
1016
       outputs. These strategies are orthogonal to our method, which could further enhance COrAL by pro-
1017   viding stronger verification mechanisms. An et al. (2024) demonstrate that the mistake reasoning
1018   data can be directly utilized through a standard fine-tuning approach. However, this approach relies
1019   on AR-LLMs and sequential prediction, whereas COrAL introduces a fundamentally new paradigm
1020   by enabling mistake correction through backward dependencies.
1021
1022   Scheduled Sampling. Scheduled sampling (Bengio et al., 2015) aims to mitigate the discrepancy
1023   between training and inference, it gradually transitions from teacher-forcing to self-generated inputs
1024   using curriculum learning. In contrast, COrAL decomposes the order-agnostic training into two
1025   separate objectives: forward prediction with ground-truth input and backward reconstruction with
       corrupted input. Inspired by scheduled sampling, future iterations of COrAL could explore cur-

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       riculum strategies to gradually increase corrupted input ratios, enhancing robustness and stability.
1027   Furthermore, scheduled sampling is designed for sequential decoding at inference, while COrAL
1028   employs blockwise order-agnostic decoding, enabling multi-token forward prediction for speedup
1029   and backward refinement for quality improvement.

       Effect of Two-Stage Training. As discussed in Section 4.2, a high corruption ratio can cause a
1180
       collapse in model performance as the noisy data contains corrupted information in a format that the
1181
       model has not seen in pretraining. Furthermore, we are also faced with the order-agnostic train-
1182   ing tax to endow an AR-based LLM with denoising and multi-token prediction abilities. In this
1183   section, we elaborate on the two-stage training we designed to mitigate this issue. Following Cai
1184   et al. (2024), we first tune the last layer where we apply target-aware RoPE. This adapts the pre-
1185   vious parameterization on next-token prediction to target-aware multi-position prediction. Due to
1186   the discrepancy of training objectives in pretraining and fine-tuning, full fine-tuning is still essential
1187   to ensure better performance on multi-token prediction. To stabilize the training process, we then
       freeze the last layer and gradually unlock it through the second training stage of full fine-tuning.

       Empirically, we find this strategy effective for stabilizing the autoregressive loss changes in forward

1191   This performance degradation possibly comes from two aspects: the difference in training objectives
1192   and the incorporation of corrupted data in fine-tuning. We leave it to future work to further explore
1193   the effect of applying our order-agnostic framework to the pretraining stage.
