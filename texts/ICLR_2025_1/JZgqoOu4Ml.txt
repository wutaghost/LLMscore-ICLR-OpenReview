009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Many inverse problems are ill-posed and need to be complemented by prior in-
012            formation that restricts the class of admissible models. Bayesian approaches en-
013            code this information as prior distributions that impose generic properties on the
014            model such as sparsity, non-negativity or smoothness. However, in case of com-
015            plex structured models such as images, graphs or three-dimensional (3D) objects,
016            generic prior distributions tend to favor models that differ largely from those ob-
017
               served in the real world. Here we explore the use of diffusion models as priors
               that are combined with experimental data within a Bayesian framework. We use
018
               3D point clouds to represent 3D objects such as household items or biomolecu-
019
               lar complexes formed from proteins and nucleic acids. We train diffusion models
020            that generate coarse-grained 3D structures at a medium resolution and integrate
021            these with incomplete and noisy experimental data. To demonstrate the power of
022            our approach, we focus on the reconstruction of biomolecular assemblies from
023            cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) images, which is an important inverse prob-
024            lem in structural biology. We find that posterior sampling with diffusion model
025            priors allows for 3D reconstruction from very sparse, low-resolution and partial
026            observations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Inverse problems are encountered in many different scientific fields. The basic setting is that we
031
      observe noisy and incomplete data y and seek to find a model x that predicts mock data via a
032   forward model A such that y ≈ A(x). An important subclass are linear models where A is a linear
033   operator. Well-known inverse problems are deconvolution or tomography.
034
035
      The challenge in solving inverse problems stems from the fact that they tend to be ill-posed mean-
      ing that many models can produce highly similar data and/or the reconstructed model can be very
036
      sensitive to noise. The remedy is to combine a reconstruction loss with a regularizer. Well-studied
037
      regularizers are Tikhonov regularization (aka ridge regression), sparsity, and non-negativity.
038
039   Bayesian inference offers a powerful framework to tackle inverse problems. The conditional prob-
040   ability p(y | x), the likelihood, relates the data y to the mock data A(x) via a noise model. A
041
      common assumption is independent Gaussian noise resulting in the likelihood

042                                                       ∥y − A(x)∥2

055   two-dimensional (2D) projections. Cryo-EM images are typically very noisy with signal-to-noise
056   ratios (SNR) far below one. On the other hand, a large body of knowledge has been accumulated

063
      and use generic priors enforcing non-negativity or penalizing high-frequency contributions. If one
      were to sample volumes from the corresponding prior, the sampled structures would not resemble
064
      any of the known biomolecular structures. Here we try to encode the rich knowledge available in the
065
      PDB as a diffusion model prior. We test 3D reconstruction from sparse, low-resolution and partial
066   measurements by posterior sampling with diffusion models as priors.

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTION
070
      Our contributions are as follows: We propose a method to reconstruct 3D structures from 2D pro-
071   jections that utilizes diffusion models as priors. Using diffusion priors has previously not been
072   explored to solve the 3D reconstruction problem in cryo-EM. The combination of the diffusion
073   model prior with a likelihood allows us to reconstruct 3D structures from very sparse observations
074   such as 2D projections, low-resolution structures and known structures of subunits. This is achieved
075   with diffusion-based posterior sampling (DPS) (Chung et al., 2023) a method that has not yet been
076   investigated in the context of 3D data. We combine DPS with optimized diffusion schedules and
077   second-order correction steps with adaptable noise injection (Karras et al., 2022) to improve sample
078   quality and runtime. We demonstrate the fidelity and flexibility of our method on highly complex
079
      and diverse datasets of 3D point clouds from ShapeNet and the PDB.
080   We emphasize that the reconstruction problem which we solve differs from the problem of recon-
081   structing a 3D surface from a 2D surface color image, which is tackled by, for example, Point-E
082   (Nichol et al., 2022), Shape-E (Jun & Nichol, 2023), PC2 (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2023), One-2-3-45
083   (Liu et al., 2023) and BDM (Xu et al., 2024). The main difference is that in our work the 2D ob-
084   servations are projections that provide information about the density across the full volume rather
085   than only information about the surface. In addition, our approach is also capable of conditioning
086
      on coarse-grained or partial observations of the 3D structure. Moreover, the reconstruction problem
      in cryo-EM aims to reconstruct the internal structure not only the surface.

089   2     BACKGROUND ON D IFFUSION M ODELS
090
091   Diffusion models have gained wide recognition in the field of generative modeling (Sohl-Dickstein
092   et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021), particularly in image synthesis,
093   where diffusion models have demonstrated their capability by surpassing former leading models in
094   key metrics (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) and continue to set new records (Karras et al., 2024). In
095   generative modeling, the main goal is to learn a sampler for an unknown distribution p0 from i.i.d.
096   samples x(0)i ∼ p0 that serve as training data. A diffusion model tries to achieve this goal by
097   approximating a probability flow from a latent Gaussian distribution pT to the unknown target p0 .
098   For this purpose, a forward process from the target distribution p0 to the latent distribution pT is
099   defined in terms of a stochastic differential equations (SDE) of the form
100
101                                       dx = f (x, t) dt + g(t) dwt ,                                  (3)
102
103   where wt is a Wiener process, f (·, t) : Rd → Rd is the drift of x(t) and g : R → R is the diffusion
104
      coefficient (Song et al., 2021). Starting at time t = 0 with samples x(0) ∼ p0 from the target
      distribution, process (3) is designed such that it gradually destroys the information content of the
105
      samples x(0) by transforming them into samples x(T ) from an isotropic Gaussian.
106
107   A diffusion model aims to represent the reverse process from pT to p0 such that we can draw noise
      from a Gaussian distribution and slowly transform it into samples from the data distribution p0 .

129   2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS IN 3D
130
131
      Apart from the 2D image domain, diffusion models have been employed to estimate the distribution
      of 3D objects. Various representations have been used including point clouds (Luo & Hu, 2021; Vah-
132
      dat et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021), meshes and implicit neural representations
133
      (Jain et al., 2021; Jun & Nichol, 2023; Erkoç et al., 2023) such as neural radiance fields (Mildenhall
134   et al., 2021). Here, we employ a point cloud representation and adopt the point transformer archi-
135   tecture from Nichol et al. (2022). This representation allows us to model 3D volume densities such
136   as cryo-EM maps efficiently, unlike meshes, which only model the surface. Furthermore, the point
137   cloud representation simplifies the process of developing likelihoods for the cryo-EM reconstruction
138   problem. In addition, we avoid any kind of latent diffusion (as, for example, proposed by Vahdat
139   et al. (2022)) for which likelihood guidance is more difficult (Song et al., 2024).
140

144

149   pt (y | x(t)) and use its score ∇x(t) log pt (y | x(t)) during inference for classifier guidance (Dhari-

153   Another line of work attempts to avoid task-specific training and instead uses the known forward
154   model to guide the generation process (Chung et al., 2023; 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Lugmayr et al.,

464   We designed various benchmarks based on a held-out set of 100 structures from Cryo2StructDataset

466   2D and/or 3D information. Again, as a baseline we used ML models obtained by maximizing
467

      the Supplementary Material, Sections A.4.1 to A.4.7). We generated ten models with and without
468
      diffusion model per reconstruction task. To assess the accuracy of the model structures, we compare
469
      them against the atomic structure deposited in the PDB and the point cloud obtained by fitting a
470   1024-component mixture of Gaussians used for the generation of the input measurements. The 3D
471   points generated by ML and DPS tend to concentrate in [−1, 1]3 . Before a meaningful comparison
472   between the ground truth and model structures can be made, we first need to scale the model points
473   so as to match the physical units of the coordinates in the PDB file (which are in Å). We achieve this
474   by matching the radii of gyration. However, there could still be a mismatch between the ground truth
475   and the scaled model resulting from a relative rotation and translation (rigid transformation) between
476   the two point clouds. We estimate the optimal alignment of both point clouds by maximizing the
477   kernel correlation (Tsin & Kanade, 2004).
478   After scaling and superposition, we can meaningfully compare model point clouds against the
479   atomic and coarse-grained ground truth structures. We assess the accuracy of the models with
480   the root mean square deviation (RMSD) which is commonly used to compare biomolecular struc-
481   tures. Since there is no one-to-one correspondence between the points in the cloud representing the
482   ground truth (all heavy atoms in the PDB file or component means of the Gaussian mixture) and
                                                                          PN
483   the models computed with ML or DPS, we compute RMSD = ( N1 n=1 ∥xn − x′ℓn ∥2 )1/2 where
484   ℓn ∈ {1, . . . , 1024} encodes the correspondence between points xn representing the ground truth
485   and points x′m representing the model (where m ∈ {1, . . . , 1024}). In case the ground truth is rep-
      resented by all heavy atoms, we set ℓn = argmin∥xn − x′m ∥ (where ”argmin” runs over all m) and

487   largely in the number of heavy atoms from N = 2178 to N = 110541. In case the ground truth is
488   represented by the 1024 component means of the Gaussian mixture (also referred to as ”subsampled
489   structure” in the following), we compute ℓn by solving the linear assignment problem that matches

      4.4   L IMITATIONS
521
522
523
      A major limitation of the proposed method concerns its runtime. In each approximate DPS step
      with correction, we have to evaluate the gradient of the energy from our forward model twice.
524
      Overall, this means that we need 2 × #timesteps − 1 network function evaluations and have to solve
525
      (2×#timesteps−1)×#observations linear assignment problems to obtain a single 3D reconstruction.
526   However, the time to reconstruct a 3D structure in the case of 6 input projections and 40 timesteps
527   within a batch of 10 still takes ≈ 1.2 min per sample on a A100 GPU in combination with an Intel
528   Xeon Platinum 8360Y 2.40 GHz CPU.

      5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We propose a Bayesian approach for 3D reconstruction from sparse measurements such as 2D pro-
535   jections, coarse-grained structures, and/or substructures, using diffusion models as priors. Diffusion
536   models are capable of encoding rich prior information about 3D structures and enable us to re-
537   construct meaningful 3D models from very sparse input data via approximate diffusion posterior
538   sampling. Diffusion priors can distill rich data sources and thereby complement existing regulariza-
539   tion techniques whenever such training data are available. The goal of future research is to improve
      the resolution of the 3D reconstructions.
