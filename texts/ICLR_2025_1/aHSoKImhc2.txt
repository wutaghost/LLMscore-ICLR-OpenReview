009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have become dominant in large-scale deep learning tasks across var-
012            ious domains, including text, 2D and 3D vision. However, the quadratic com-
013            plexity of their attention mechanism limits their efficiency as the sequence length
014            increases, particularly in high-resolution 3D data such as point clouds. Recently,
015            state space models (SSMs) like Mamba have emerged as promising alternatives,
016            offering linear complexity, scalability, and high performance in long-sequence
017            tasks. The key challenge in the application of SSMs in this domain lies in reconcil-
018            ing the non-sequential structure of point clouds with the inherently directional (or
019
               bi-directional) order-dependent processing of recurrent models like Mamba. To
               achieve this, previous research proposed reorganizing point clouds along multiple
020
               directions or predetermined paths in 3D space, concatenating the results to pro-
021
               duce a single 1D sequence capturing different views. In our work we introduce a
022            method to convert point clouds into 1D sequences that maintains 3D spatial struc-
023            ture with no need for data replication, allowing Mamba’s sequential processing
024            to be applied effectively in an almost permutation-invariant manner. In contrast
025            to other works, we found that our method does not require positional embed-
026            dings, and allows for shorter sequence lengths while still achieving state-of-the-art
027            results in ModelNet40 and ScanObjectNN datasets and surpassing Transformer-
028            based models in both accuracy and efficiency.

      Today, the transformer architecture is the most common technology powering large-scale deep learn-
      ing systems. Since their introduction by Vaswani et al. (2017), transformers have been widely
034

037   In 3D vision and particularly point cloud analysis, transformers achieve state-of-the-art results (Guo
038   et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Pang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024), often surpassing convolution-based
039   approaches (Wu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018) at scale.

045   2021; Chen et al., 2021; Wortsman et al., 2023; Arora et al., 2024; Ramapuram et al., 2024), as
046   well as the development more efficient GPU implementations of softmax attention (Dao et al., 2022;
047   Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024). However, the most relevant leap forward on this issue arguably came
048   in recently and coincided with the design of state-space models such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023)
049   as well as other parallelizable token mixers (Poli et al., 2023; De et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023;

072
073            How shall we apply a sequential model to non-sequential data, e.g. a point cloud?
074
075   Addressing this question is scientifically intriguing, timely, and crucial for fully harnessing the po-
076   tential of new efficient attention variants in the 3D domain. While inspecting the constantly growing
077   literature on Mamba applications in 3D vision, we can see two recurring patterns3 .
078
079    (A) 3D point cloud data has to be converted into an ordered sequence before Mamba can be ap-
080        plied. This has been achieved with different strategies such as reordering the points along
081
           axis and replicating the sequence (Liang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a) or scanning it from
           different directions (Liu et al., 2024b).
082
083    (B) Much like in transformers, positional embeddings are used. This information is conceptually
084        redundant since (1) positional information is contained in the feature themselves, and (2) the
085        ordering of patches along the constructed sequence is already used by Mamba implicitly. Note
086
           that in text, Mamba is often applied without positional embeddings (Waleffe et al., 2024).
087
      While the performance of Mamba in 3D data already shows promise, often surpassing transformers
088

092
093    1. We draw attention to the problem of sequence construction when applying Mamba to 2D or 3D
094       data. We complement our discussion with both theoretical considerations on invariances and

102
          principled ordering along a point cloud can improve performance of Mamba models in this
          setting.

127   2   R ELATED W ORK
128
129   Point Cloud Transformers. Transformers, initially designed for NLP, have proven to be highly
130   effective in point cloud analysis due to their global attention mechanisms and permutation invariance
131
      properties. Early models like Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) demonstrated that
      transformers could outperform CNNs in classification by applying attention directly to patches. This
132
      success inspired their application to point clouds, where global feature modeling is crucial.
133
      Point-BERT (Yu et al., 2022) applied BERT’s masked modeling to 3D data, using a discrete tok-
134   enizer to convert point patches into tokens and self-supervised pre-training to recover masked points.
135   Point-MAE (Pang et al., 2022) further advanced this with masked autoencoding, learning latent rep-
136   resentations by reconstructing missing parts from masked inputs. These methods outperformed
137   traditional models by leveraging large unlabeled datasets, but their self-attention’s quadratic com-
138   plexity limits scalability. OctFormer (Wang, 2023) addressed this by using octree-based attention,
139   reducing computational costs through local window partitioning while maintaining performance for
140   large-scale tasks.PointGPT (Chen et al., 2023) uses an autoregressive framework inspired by GPT,
141   treating point patches as sequential data to predict the next patch. This pre-training strategy shows
142
      strong generalization in few-shot and downstream tasks, enhancing transformers’ flexibility in point
      cloud processing. Similarly, PCT (Guo et al., 2021) leverages transformer architecture with permuta-
143
      tion invariance to process unordered point sequences. By using farthest point sampling and nearest
144
      neighbor search, PCT captures local context effectively, achieving state-of-the-art performance in
145   tasks like shape classification and part segmentation.
146
147
      Point Cloud State Space Models. The use of SSMs in point cloud analysis has recently gained at-
      tention as a promising approach to address the computational limitations of transformer-based archi-
148
      tectures. Although transformers effectively capture global dependencies, their quadratic complexity
149
      impedes scaling to high-resolution point clouds. In contrast, SSMs like the Mamba architecture of-
150   fer linear complexity and efficient long-range modeling. Yet a primary challenge in applying SSMs
151   to point clouds is the unordered nature of the data, which does not align well with the sequential
152   processing of SSMs. To address this, researchers have already proposed methods to convert point
153   clouds into sequences.
154
      One common strategy among recent works is to design ordering methods that preserve the spa-
155
      tial relationships within point clouds when converting them into sequences. For example, Point-
156   Mamba (Liang et al., 2024) and Point Cloud Mamba (PCM) (Zhang et al., 2024a) introduce axis-
157   wise reordering techniques and sequence replication to improve SSMs’ ability to capture both local
158   and global structures. Other studies use hierarchical data structures to reflect the spatial hierar-
159   chy, such as octree-based ordering in OctreeMamba (Liu et al., 2024b), which organizes points in
160   a z-order sequence, maintaining spatial relationships while capturing features at multiple scales.
161   While preserving spatial relationships during serialization is crucial, enhancing local feature extrac-
      tion within SSMs is equally important for point cloud analysis. Although SSMs model long-range


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      dependencies efficiently, capturing fine-grained local details remains important. Mamba3D (Han
163   et al., 2024) addresses this by introducing a Local Norm Pooling block to improve local geometric
164   representation. It also employs a bidirectional SSM operating on both tokens and feature channels,
165   balancing local and global structure modeling without increasing computational complexity.
166
      Following (Waleffe et al., 2024; Dao & Gu, 2024a), Another line of research combines the strengths
167
      of Transformers and SSMs for better performance and efficiency. PoinTramba (Wang et al., 2024)
168
      integrates Transformers to capture detailed dependencies within point groups, while Mamba mod-
169   els relationships between groups using a bidirectional importance-aware ordering strategy. By re-
170   ordering group embeddings based on importance scores, this approach improves performance and
171   addresses random ordering issues in SSMs. SSMs are also applied to point cloud completion and
172   filtering. 3DMambaIPF (Zhou et al., 2024) uses Mamba’s selection mechanism with HyperPoint
173   modules to reconstruct point clouds from incomplete inputs, preserving local details often lost in
174   Transformers. For filtering, it combines SSMs with differentiable rendering to reduce noise in large-
175   scale point clouds, improving alignment with real-world structures and handling datasets with hun-
176   dreds of thousands of points where other methods struggle.
177   These works show that SSMs effectively address key challenges in point cloud analysis, offering
178   efficient and scalable solutions for various tasks. With advances in serialization, feature extraction,
179   and hybrid architectures, SSMs have become a valuable approach for advancing 3D vision applica-
180   tions. In this work, our goal is to further strengthen these results by offering a simple, principled
181   and robust solution for constructing input sequences input of Mamba-like models.

191   3.1   BASIC S TRATEGIES IN P OINT C LOUD A NALYSIS
192
193
      We outline the typical pipeline used for point cloud analysis in recent deep models. These are not
      specific to our model, but will allow us to make connections and simplify the discussion.
194
195   Preprocessing. The goal of the preprocessing phase is to reduce the cardinality of the point cloud
196   while preserving the structure of the data, allowing for more efficient computation in subsequent
197
      stages. Formally, let P = {pi | pi ∈ R3 , i = 1, . . . , N } represent the point cloud, where N is
      the total number of points, and pi = (xi , yi , zi ) denotes the 3D coordinates of each point. After
198
      normalizing the points, a 2-step process is often followed:
199
200   1. Center Selection: nc points are selected using the Farthest Point Sampling (FPS) algorithm. FPS
201
         iteratively selects points that are farthest from each other, ensuring that the sample is represen-
         tative of the original point cloud. These points are referred to as ”centers” {Ci }ni=1
                                                                                              c
                                                                                                 , providing
202
         global information about the object.
203
204   2. Patch Creation: For each center, np nearest points are selected using the k-Nearest Neighbors
205
         (kNN) algorithm. This results in a set of patches {Pi }ni=1   c
                                                                         , each centered around one of the
         chosen centers, capturing more localized information about the object.
206
207   The values of nc and np are hyperparameters of this preprocessing stage. In our experiments, we
208   followed the procedure of previous work in the literature that can be found in Appendix A.
209   Patch Embedding. Each patch Pi is embedded into a fixed-dimensional vector pi through a se-
210   quence of expansions, convolutions, and linear projections. This embedding process is a pointwise
211   transformation from RBS×np ×3 → RBS×np ×de . Here BS is the batch size, np is the number of points
212   per patch, and de is the embedding dimension. This embedding captures local geometric information
213
      within each patch, which is crucial for understanding the finer details of the object structure.
214   Center Embedding. Each center Ci is embedded into a fixed-dimensional vector ci to capture
215   global positional information and provide context for the relationships between different patches.
      This embedding process is a pointwise transformation from RBS×nc ×3 → RBS×nc ×de .

      This result is rooted in more general statements regarding Turing Completeness of RNNs (Siegel-

300   2D and 3D applications, the notion of “position” cannot be easily captured by 1D ordering in a
301   sequence: when processing data X where each Xi relates to a precise position in space, the out-
302
      put Y crucially depends on the chosen order the Xi s are arranged into – in contrast with softmax
      attention (see propositions above). In a point cloud, we might order along the principal axis in 3D
303
      space and feed point clusters one at a time along these axes (Liang et al., 2024) or along an octree-
304
      determined path (Liu et al., 2024b). The output of S6, in this case, still depends on the processing
305   order, regardless of the inclusion of additional positional embeddings in X and despite the potential
306   bidirectional application of such models.

      In this paper, our goal is to work towards a principled strategy for processing point clouds with in-
      herently sequential models such as Mamba. We first describe in-depth one existing approach (Liang
309
      et al., 2024) in Sec. 3.3.1 and then propose a patch reordering strategy that is able to match or
310
      improve performance compared to existing approaches, without requiring positional embedding but
311   relying solely on the sequential patch ordering we introduce. This both reveals sensitivity to Mamba
312   in sequence construction and potential for future developments using our strategy.

       • Centers that are close in the 3D space may not be adjacent in the sequence, which can affect the
         model’s robustness and ability to capture spatial relationships effectively.
340
341    • As we show in Sec. 4, this method is highly sensitive to the presence of positional embedding
342      information. While this is common in standard attention-based architectures, it is less natural in
343      Mamba-based models. In addition, it increases number of parameters and introduces additional
344      redundancy.
345
346   3.3.2       NIMBA S TRATEGY
347
348   To overcome the limitations of the PointMamba strategy, we propose the NIMBA approach, designed
349   to better maintain geometric relationships by ensuring that consecutive centers in the 1D sequence
350
      fed to the model are close in 3D space. The NIMBA strategy is based on the concept of local
      proximity preservation:
351
352
      1. Initial Axis-Wise Ordering: Initially, centers are flattened by ordering along the y-axis. Any
353
         initial order could work, but we chose the y-axis as empirical results indicated that this ordering
354
         reduces the computational cost of the following phase.
355
356   2. Proximity Check: We scan the sequence obtained previously and we iteratively check the dis-
357      tance between the current and the next center. If the distance exceeds a predefined threshold r,
358      we look for a center along the sequence that is near enough to the starting center and place it
359
         next to it. If no suitable center is found within the threshold, the sequence proceeds to the next
         center without modification. In this way, we ensure that consecutive centers in the sequence have
360
         a distance less than r.
361
362
      Mathematically, the proximity check can be described as ∥Ci −Ci+1 ∥ < r, where ∥·∥ represents√the
363
      Euclidean distance in 3D space. The choice of r is crucial: a high threshold value (e.g., r ≥ 2 3,
364
      the diagonal of a unit cube) means that the sequence remains similar to the initial order since the
365
      requirement will always be satisfied. Instead, using a low threshold (e.g., r = 0) is computationally
366   expensive since each center would be compared to all the others in the sequence, and will result in
367   an ordering identical to the initial axis-wise order, as no centers will be considered close enough
368   to trigger reordering. In our experiment we found r = 0.8 to be a good balance between quality
369   of reordered sequence and computational cost. Other than efficiency reasons, the choice of the
370   threshold r is related to the nature of point cloud datasets. Indeed, in ModelNet and Scanobject
371   datasets the objects are contained in a [−1, 1]3 cube. The literature confirms that this comes from
372   the nomalization step, which is a standard procedure in similar works and datasets. The choice of r
373   can be interpreted as a portion of the distance between the center of the scene and the border of the
374
      scene, which should be 40%. Figure 2 show the complete pipeline of NIMBA
375   Figure 1 show the sequence created from the two strategies. When comparing to the PointMamba
376   approach, NIMBA does not rely on positional embeddings and doesn’t replicate the sequence: the
377   reordering strategy proposed leverages the spatial relationships of points, allowing the model to rely
      only on the patch embedding, thus enhancing accuracy and stability.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
      In this paper, we introduced NIMBA , a robust and principled approach for point cloud processing
526
      using state space models (SSMs). When using such causal models, a key challenge is to effectively
527
      convert a 3D set of data into a 1D sequence for proper analysis. We addressed this by proposing a
528   spatially-aware reordering strategy that preserves spatial relationships between points. Differently
529   from others, our method eliminates the need for positional embeddings and sequence replication,
530   enhancing both efficiency and performance. Our experimental results demonstrate that NIMBA out-
531   performs or matches transformer-based and other Mamba-based models on benchmark datasets such
532   as ModelNet, ScanObject, and ShapeNetPart in classification and segmentation tasks.
533
      Limitations and Future Work. While NIMBA successfully addresses several challenges in point
534   cloud analysis with SSMs, certain limitations remain. From an optimization standpoint, the model
535   shows limited improvement when scaled. Additionally, when replacing the Mamba block with
536   Mamba2 or integrating NIMBA into hybrid architectures, we observed performance declines, sug-
537   gesting potential integration issues. We encourage further investigation into optimizing SSMs and
538   leveraging their integration with transformer architecture for point cloud analysis. We believe this
539   work offers a new perspective on applying non-transformer models in domains beyond natural lan-
      guage processing, highlighting the potential of SSMs in 3D vision applications.

      A.1   DATASETS
813
814   In our experiments, we evaluate the performance of our model using three publicly available 3D
815   datasets: ModelNet40, ScanObjectNN and ShapeNetPart.
816
      ModelNet40 (Wu et al., 2015): It is a widely used benchmark synthetic dataset used to evaluate 3D
817   object classification models. It consists of 12,311 CAD models across 40 categories, representing
818   clean and noise-free 3D shapes, such as airplanes, chairs, and cars, offering a diverse set of 3D
819   shapes.
820
      ScanObjectNN (Uy et al., 2019): It presents a more challenging real-world scenario by offering
821
      around 15,000 objects across 15 categories, scanned from real-world indoor scenes. Unlike the
822
      controlled environment of CAD datasets, the objects in ScanObjectNN are captured in cluttered
823   and noisy environments, introducing additional complexity due to background noise, occlusion, and
824   deformation. The diversity of real-world settings in this dataset makes it particularly suited for
825   evaluating the robustness of models in practical object classification tasks. The dataset comes in
826   three variants with different degree of difficulty:
827
828          • OBJ-ONLY: the easiest variant in which there is only the object in the scene. This is the
829            most similar variant to a CAD analogous.
830          • OBJ-BG: an intermediate variant in which there is also the background. We focused mostly
831            on this variant since it is the most similar to the real world.
832          • PB-T50-RS: the hardest version that adds some perturbations to the objects and can be used
833            as a benchmark to test the robustness on the classification task
834
835   ShapeNetPart (Yi et al., 2016) It is a widely recognized benchmark for 3D shape segmentation
836   tasks. This dataset is a subset of the larger ShapeNet repository and includes 31,693 3D CAD
837
      models categorized into 16 common object classes such as chairs, planes, and tables. Each model
      is richly annotated with detailed geometric and semantic labels, providing valuable information for
838
      training and evaluating segmentation algorithms.
