009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Real-world datasets follow an imbalanced distribution, which poses significant
012            challenges in rare-category object detection. Recent studies tackle this problem
013            by developing re-weighting and re-sampling methods, that utilise the class fre-
014            quencies of the dataset. However, these techniques focus solely on the frequency
015            statistics and ignore the distribution of the classes in image space, missing im-
016            portant information. In contrast to them, we propose Fractal CALibration (FRA-
017            CAL): a novel post-calibration method for long-tailed object detection. FRACAL
018
               devises a logit adjustment method that utilises the fractal dimension to estimate
               how uniformly classes are distributed in image space. During inference, it uses the
019
               fractal dimension to inversely downweight the probabilities of uniformly spaced
020
               class predictions achieving balance in two axes: between frequent and rare cate-
021            gories, and between uniformly spaced and sparsely spaced classes. FRACAL is a
022            post-processing method and it does not require any training, also it can be com-
023            bined with many off-the-shelf models such as one-stage sigmoid detectors and
024            two-stage instance segmentation models. FRACAL boosts the rare class perfor-
025            mance by up to 8.6% and surpasses all previous methods on LVIS dataset, while
026            showing good generalisation to other datasets such as COCO, V3Det and Open-
027            Images. We provide the code in the Appendix.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, there have been astonishing developments in the field of object detection Carion
033
      et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2022); Lyu et al. (2022). Most of these works utilise vast, balanced,
      curated datasets such as ImageNet1k Deng et al. (2009), or MS-COCO Lin et al. (2014) to learn
034
      efficient image representations. However, in the real world, data are rarely balanced, in fact, they
035
      follow a long-tailed distribution Liu et al. (2019). When models are trained with long-tailed data,
036   they perform well for the frequent classes of the distribution but they perform inadequately for the
037   rare classes Wang et al. (2020); Ren et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020). This problem poses significant
038   challenges to the safe deployment of detection and instance segmentation models in real-world safe-
039   critical applications such as autonomous vehicles, medical applications, and industrial applications,
040   scenarios where rare class detection is paramount.
041
      Many approaches address the long-tailed detection problem by employing adaptive re-weighting or
042   data resampling techniques to handle imbalanced distributions Wang et al. (2021a;b); Zang et al.
043   (2021). However all these methods require training. In contrast, in long-tailed image classification,
044   alternative methods focus on mitigating class imbalance during inference through a post-calibrated
045   softmax adjustment (PCSA) Alexandridis et al. (2023); Ren et al. (2020); Hong et al. (2021). PCSA
046   boasts strong performance, good compatibility with many methods like data augmentation, masked
047   image modeling, contrastive learning, and does not necessitate specialized loss function optimiza-
048   tion, making it more user friendly Xu et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2022). However,
049   current PCSA methods utilise solely the train set’s class frequency ps (y) as shown in Figure 1-left
050
      and they overlook the significance of the classes’ dependence on the location distribution ps (y, u).
      This is a significant limitation of previous PCSA methods because the location information is a
051
      critical indicator considering the correlation between classes y and their respective locations u.
052
053   Motivated by the class-location dependence Kayhan & Gemert (2020), in this work, we investigate a
      novel way to incorporate location information into post-calibration for imbalanced object detection

073   Our method comes with several advantages. First, it performs an effective class calibration, suit-

075   class-location dependency Kayhan & Gemert (2020), using the fractal dimension, and it fuses this
076

      technique that complements the frequency-dependent class calibration method and achieves higher
077
      overall performance compared to previous PCSA techniques.
078

080   Sigmoid-based models, various instance segmentation architectures, various backbones, sampling
081   strategies, and largely increase the performance during the inference step. FRACAL significantly
082
      advances the performance on the challenging LVISv1 benchmark Gupta et al. (2019) with no train-
      ing, or additional inference cost by 8.6% rare mask average precision (APrm ).

084   Our contributions are as follows:
085
086          • For the first time, we show the importance of the class-location dependence in post-
087
               calibration for long-tailed object detection.
088          • We capture the location-class dependence via a space-aware long-tailed object detection

090          • Our method performs remarkably on various detectors and backbones, on both heavily
091            imbalanced datasets such as LVIS and less imbalanced datasets such as COCO, V3DET
092            and OpenImages, outperforming the state-of-the-art by up to 8.6%.

350   3.4   L OCALISED C ALIBRATION
351
352   By putting Eq. 6 and Eq. 9 together, we get the final FRActal CALibration (FRACAL) as:
353
                                                                        \text {FRACAL}(z_y) = \frac {\text {S}(\text {C}(z_y))}{\sum _{j=1}^{C+1} \text {S}(\text {C}(z_j))} . \label {eq:local}                                                        (10)
354
355
      Our proposed method tackles the classification imbalance using additional space statistics. On the
356   classification axis, we use the class priors ps (y) and perform logit adjustments. On the space axis,
357   we use the fractal dimension Φ(y) to perform a space-aware calibration that accounts for the object’s
358   location distribution ps (y, u). In Eq. 10, we renormalise both foreground and background logits to
359   preserve a probabilistic prediction after the space calibration in Eq. 9.
360
      Extending to binary classifiers. In long-tailed object detection there are many works that use only
361
      binary classifiers Alexandridis et al. (2022); Tan et al. (2020; 2021); Li et al. (2022a); Wang et al.
362   (2021b); Hyun Cho & Krähenbühl (2022); Hsieh et al. (2021). In this case, the logit zi performs two
363   tasks simultaneously: It discriminates among the foreground classes and performs background-to-
364   foreground classification. Thus, to correctly apply foreground calibration, we first need to decouple
365   the foreground and background predictions. To do so, we filter out the background proposals using
366   the model’s predictions as follows:
367
368                     \text {FRACAL}_{b}(z_i) = \eta (\text {C}(z_i) -\log _{\beta }(\frac {\Phi (y)^\lambda }{\sum _i^C \Phi (i)^\lambda }) +\log _{\beta }(\frac {1}{C})) \cdot \eta (z_i), \label {eq:lcd_calib2}                                  (11)
369
370   where η(zi ) is the sigmoid activation function that acts as a filter for low-scoring proposals. Com-
371
      pared to Eq. 10, Eq. 11 performs class calibration and space calibration in logit space, lowering the
      false-positive detection rate.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose FRACAL, a novel post-calibration method for long-tailed object detection. Our method
534   performs a space-aware logit adjustment, that utilises the fractal dimension and incorporates space
535   information during calibration. FRACAL majorly boosts the performance of the detectors and makes
536   more rare class predictions that are evenly spread inside the image. We show that FRACAL can be
537   easily combined with both one-stage Sigmoid detectors and two-stage Softmax segmentation mod-
538   els. Our method boosts the performance of detectors by up to 8.6% without training or additional
539   inference cost, surpassing the SOTA in the LVIS benchmark and showing good generalisation to
      COCO, V3Det and OpenImages.
