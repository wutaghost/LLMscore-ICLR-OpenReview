009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multi-modality learning has become a crucial technique in enhancing the per-
012
               formance of machine learning applications across various domains, including
013
               autonomous driving, robotics, and perception systems. Existing frameworks,
014            such as Auxiliary Modality Learning (AML), effectively utilize multiple data
015            sources during training and enable inference with reduced modalities, but they
016            primarily operate in a single-agent context. This limitation is particularly crit-
017            ical in dynamic environments, such as connected autonomous vehicles (CAV),
018            where incomplete data coverage can result in decision-making blind spots. To
019            address these challenges, we introduce Collaborative Auxiliary Modality Learn-
020            ing (CAML), a novel extension of the AML framework for multi-agent systems.
021            CAML facilitates collaboration among agents by allowing them to share multi-
022
               modal data during training. During inference, each agent operates effectively with
               fewer modalities, ensuring robustness in performance even with missing data. We
023
               analyze the effectiveness of CAML from the perspective of uncertainty reduction
024
               and data coverage, providing a theoretical support to understand and explain why
025            CAML works better than AML. We then validate CAML through experiments in
026            collaborative decision-making for CAV in accident-prone scenarios. Experimen-
027            tal results show that CAML outperforms AML across all tested scenarios, achiev-
028            ing up to a 58.3% improvement in accident detection. Additionally, we validate
029            our approach on real-world data from aerial-ground vehicles for collaborative se-
030            mantic segmentation, achieving up to 10.8% improvement in mIoU compared to
031            AML.

033   1 I NTRODUCTION
034
      Multi-modality learning has become an essential approach in a wide range of machine learning ap-
035
      plications, particularly in areas such as autonomous driving (El Madawi et al., 2019; Xiao et al.,
036   2020; Gao et al., 2018) , robotics (Noda et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2020), and perception systems
037   (Zhuang et al., 2021; Bayoudh et al., 2022), where the availability of multiple data sources (e.g.,
038   RGB images, LiDAR, radar, etc.) improves model performance by providing complementary infor-
039   mation. However, these multi-modality systems often suffer from increased computational complex-
040   ity and latency at inference time. Moreover, some modalities may not be consistently available or
041   reliable in real-world conditions, necessitating strategies that can compensate for missing modalities
042   during inference.
043   Recent work on machine learning (Hoffman et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018; Garcia et al., 2018; 2019;
044   Piasco et al., 2021) aims to address these problems by allowing models to leverage additional modal-
045   ities during training while enabling inference using fewer or even a single modality. For example, a
046   model might be trained using both RGB and LiDAR data, but during deployment, it only requires
047   RGB data to operate. These approaches reduces the computational burden and accommodates real-
048   world conditions where certain sensors may be unavailable. Shen et al. (2023) formalized these
049   learning tasks as Auxiliary Modality Learning (AML). The AML framework successfully reduces
050
      the dependency on expensive or unreliable modalities, but it focuses on the single-agent setting,
      where an individual model is trained to handle reduced modalities during inference.
051
052   Despite the benefits of AML, several gaps remain. First, a major limitation in the current AML
053   framework is the inability to exploit collaboration between agents, particularly in dynamic envi-
      ronments such as connected autonomous vehicles (CAV). In such scenarios, data coverage from a

054
      single agent is often incomplete because of occlusion or limited sensor range, leading to blind spots
055   or increased uncertainty in decision-making. Second, the information from multiple modalities can
056   complement each other across agents, especially in multi-agent settings such as vehicle-to-vehicle
057   (V2V) communication or collaborative robotics. Different agents may have access to complemen-
058   tary sensory information, which could be shared to have agents make more informed and safer
059   decisions, notably in accident-prone scenarios. However, current AML approaches do not exploit
060   this potential for collaboration.
061   To bridge these gaps, inspired by AML (Shen et al., 2023), we propose Collaborative Auxiliary
062   Modality Learning (CAML), a significant extension of the AML framework to multi-agent systems,
063   which is much more genreal than AML. CAML allows agents to collaborate and share multimodal
064   data during training, but enables inference with reduced or fewer modalities per agent. As suggested
065   by Shen et al. (2023), CAML leverages knowledge distillation (Hinton, 2015), transferring knowl-
066   edge from a teacher model into a student model. This enables the student to operate with missing
067   modalities during inference. For instance, in autonomous driving, multiple vehicles can share sensor
068   information such as LiDAR and RGB images during training to build more robust representations,
069
      while during runtime testing, each vehicle performs inference using only RGB images.
070   CAML addresses two key challenges: First, it reduces uncertainty and enhances data coverage in
071   dynamic environments by leveraging complementary information from multiple agents. Second, it
072   maintains efficient, modality-reduced inference during testing. Unlike previous work that either fo-
073   cuses on multi-agent collaboration but without addressing modality reduction at test time, or tackles
074   multi-modality learning in single-agent settings, CAML unifies these concepts. Through collabora-
075
      tion, CAML enables agents to compensate for each other’s blind spots, resulting in more informed
      prediction or decision-making even when some modalities are unavailable at deployment.
076
077   In summary, our work offers the following key contributions:
078
      • We introduce CAML, a novel framework that extends Auxiliary Modality Learning (AML) to
079
        multi-agent systems. CAML allows agents to share multimodal data during training, while per-
080
        forming efficient, reduced-modality inference during testing. By shifting AML from a single-
081     agent paradigm to a collaborative framework, CAML leverages the strengths of multi-agent col-
082     laboration, reducing estimation uncertainty and offering complementary information to capture a
083     broader and more detailed data representation.
084   • We systematically analyze the effectiveness of CAML from the perspective of uncertainty re-
085     duction and data coverage, providing a theoretical support to understand and explain why
086     CAML works better than AML.
087   • We validate CAML through experiments in collaborative decision-making for connected au-
088     tonomous driving in accident-prone scenarios and collaborative semantic segmentation for real-
089     world data of aerial-ground vehicles. CAML demonstrates up to 58.3% improvement in accident
090     detection compared to AML in decision-making for autonomous driving, significantly enhancing
091     driving safety, and up to 10.8% improvement in mIoU for semantic segmentation.

094
      Multi-Agent Collaboration. Collaboration in multi-agent systems has been widely studied across
095
      fields such as autonomous driving and robotics. In autonomous driving, prior research has explored
096   various strategies, including spatio-temporal graph neural networks (Gao et al., 2024), LiDAR-based
097   end-to-end systems (Cui et al., 2022), decentralized cooperative lane-changing (Nie et al., 2016) and
098   game-theoretic models (Hang et al., 2021). In robotics, Mandi et al. (2024) presented a hierarchical
099   multi-robot collaboration approach using large language models, while Zhou et al. (2022) proposed
100   a perception framework for multi-robot systems built on graph neural networks. A review of multi-
101   robot systems in search and rescue operations was provided by Queralta et al. (2020), and Bae et al.
102   (2019) developed a reinforcement learning (RL) method for multi-robot path planning. Additionally,
103   various communication mechanisms, such as Who2com (Liu et al., 2020b), When2com (Liu et al.,
104
      2020a), and Where2comm (Hu et al., 2022), have been created to optimize agent interactions.
105   Despite these advancements, existing multi-agent collaboration frameworks remain limited by their
106   focus on specific tasks and the assumption that agents will have consistent access to the same data
107   modalities during both training and testing, an assumption that may not hold in real-world applica-
      tions. To address these gaps, our framework, CAML, enables agents to collaborate during training

      by sharing multimodal data, but at test time, each agent performs inference using reduced modality.
109   This reduces the dependency on certain modalities for deployment, while still allowing agents to
110   leverage additional data during training to enhance overall performance and robustness.
111
112   Auxiliary Modality Learning. Auxiliary Modality Learning (AML) (Shen et al., 2023) has
113   emerged as an effective solution to reduce computational costs and the amount of input data re-
114
      quired for inference. By utilizing auxiliary modalities during training, AML minimizes reliance on
      those modalities at inference time. For example, Hoffman et al. (2016) introduced a method that in-
115
      corporates depth images during training to enhance test-time RGB-only detection models. Similarly,
116
      Wang et al. (2018) proposed PM-GANs to learn a full-modal representation using data from partial
117   modalities, while Garcia et al. (2018; 2019) developed approaches that use depth and RGB videos
118   during training but rely solely on RGB data for testing. Piasco et al. (2021) created a localization
119   system that predicts depth maps from RGB query images at test time. Building on these works,
120   Shen et al. (2023) formalized the AML framework, systematically classifying auxiliary modality
121   types and AML architectures.
122
      However, existing AML frameworks are typically designed for single-agent settings, failing to
123   exploit the potential benefits of multi-agent collaboration for improving multimodal learning.
124   CAML extends AML to multi-agent environments, allowing agents to collaboratively learn richer
125   multimodal representations during training. This approach mitigates the loss of information when
126   modalities are reduced during inference, as the learned features are reinforced by data shared across
127   agents.
128
129
      Knowledge Distillation. Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton, 2015) is a widely used technique
      in many domains to reduce computation by transferring knowledge from a large, complex model
130
      (teacher) to a simpler model (student). In computer vision, (Gou et al., 2021) provided a compre-
131
      hensive survey of KD applications, while Beyer et al. (2022) conducted an empirical investigation
132   to develop a robust and effective recipe for making SOTA large-scale models more practical. Ad-
133   ditionally, Tung & Mori (2019) introduced a KD loss function that aligns the training of a student
134   network with input pairs producing similar activation in the teacher network. In natural language
135   processing, Xu et al. (2024) reviewed the applications of KD in LLMs, while Sun et al. (2019) pro-
136   posed a Patient KD method to compress larger models into lightweight counterparts that maintain
137   effectiveness. Hahn & Choi (2019) also suggested a KD approach that leverages the soft target prob-
138   abilities of the training model to train other neural networks. In autonomous driving, Lan & Tian
139   (2022) presented an approach for visual detection, Cho et al. (2023); Sautier et al. (2022) used KD
140
      for 3D object detection.
141   Notice that existing KD mostly distills knowledge from a larger model to a smaller one to reduce
142   computation, Shen et al. (2023) aimed to design a cross-modality learning approach using KD to
143   utilize the hidden information from auxiliary modalities within the AML framework. But AML is
144   limited by the scope of a single-agent paradigm, missing opportunities for collaborative knowledge
145   sharing across agents. In contrast, we leverage KD within multi-agent settings, where the teacher
146
      models are trained with access to shared multimodal data (e.g., RGB and LidAR) from multiple
      agents. By distilling this collaborative knowledge into each agent’s reduced modality (e.g., RGB),
147
      CAML enables robust inference during deployment, even with fewer modalities. This collaborative
148
      distillation process enhances each agent’s performance by providing richer, complementary knowl-
149   edge from the collaborative training phase.

152   3    C OLLABORATIVE AUXILIARY M ODALITY L EARNING
153   In AML (Shen et al., 2023), which operates in a single-agent framework, the missing modalities
154   during testing are referred to as auxiliary modalities, while those that remain available are called
155   the main modality. In contrast, our framework CAML is more general for multi-agent collabora-
156   tion. Each agent can process a different number of modalities during training, different agents in
157   CAML can have different main modalities and auxiliary modalities. There is no correlation between
158
      the number of agents and the number of modalities.
159   We define our problem in both training and testing phases. In the training phase, we consider a
160   multi-agent system with N agents collaboratively completing a task. The set of agents is denoted as
161   Atrain = {A1 , A2 , . . . , AN }. The observations of all agents are denoted as X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN },
      where xi is the observation acquired by the i-th agent Ai ∈ Atrain . The ground truth label is

163   autonomous vehicle). The set of modalities is denoted as Itrain = {I1 , I2 , . . . , IK }, such as RGB,
164   LiDAR, Depth, etc, where K is the number of modalities avaiable during training. During training,
165   each agent has access to all these K modalities.
166
      In the testing phase, we assume there are M agents. The set of test agents is denoted as Atest =
167
      {A1 , A2 , . . . , AM }. In addition, the set of modalities is denoted as Itest , which is a subset of Itrain .
168
      The number of modalities available during testing is denoted as L, where L ≤ K. The set of agents

173   Given the problem definition, we aim to estimate the posterior distribution P (y|X) of the ground
174   truth label y given all agents’ observations X. During training, we train both a teacher model where
175   each agent has access to all modalities in Itrain and a student model where each agent has access
176   to partial modalities in Itest . We employ Knowledge Distillation (KD) to transfer the knowledge
177   derived from the teacher model to the student model, enabling the student to benefit from additional
178
      information, as illustrated in Figure 1. At test time, we perform inference using the student model,
      which relies on the test modality observations X test .
179
180   Specifically, in the teacher model, each agent has access to all multimodal observations and indepen-
181   dently processes its local observations to produce embeddings. These embeddings are then shared
182   among agents based on whether the system operates in a centralized or decentralized manner. If
183   the system is centralized, all collaborative agents share their embeddings with one designated ego
184
      agent for centralized processing. If the system is decentralized, each agent shares the embeddings
      with other agents. Subsequently, the shared embeddings corresponding to the same modality are
185
      aggregated together. Then we fuse (e.g., via concatenation) the aggregated embeddings of differ-
186
      ent modalities to create a comprehensive multimodal embedding. This multimodal embedding is
187   then passed through a prediction module to produce the teacher model’s final prediction. The stu-
188   dent model follows a similar network architecture as the teacher. However, instead of processing
189   all modalities, each agent processes only a single or a subset of modalities, which can vary across
190   agents. By sharing these embeddings among agents, the student model also constructs a multimodal
191   embedding, leveraging the different modalities observed by various agents. This multimodal em-
192   bedding is then used to generate the student model’s prediction. Thus, our approach enables the
193   student model to maintain strong predictive performance despite missing modalities during testing,
194   significantly enhancing its robustness and generalizability.

      4    A NALYSIS
198   To compare whether CAML outperforms AML with a single agent theoretically, we analyze from
199   two key perspectives: uncertainty reduction and data coverage enhancement. Data coverage can
200   be further discussed from two dimensions: complementary information and information gain. We
201   aim to address three major questions: (a) Uncertainty Reduction: Does the collaboration among
202   multiple agents help reduce the variance of the posterior distribution, resulting in more confident
203   estimates? (b) Complementary Information: Does the collaboration of multiple agents provide
204
      complementary information that increases data coverage? Specifically, does combining observations
      from each agent lead to a more accurate and comprehensive prediction compared to using a single
205
      agent? (c) Information Gain: Does the collaboration increase the mutual information between the
206
      observations and the true label?
207
208

209
      typically assumed to be Gaussian: P (y) = N (y|µ0 , σ02 ), where µ0 is the prior mean and σ02 is the
210
      prior variance.

      Complementary Information. To address question (b) about complementary information, we
      study data coverage and information provided by each agent in a multi-agent system. Let the entire
256
      data space be denoted as D, which consists of various subsets. Each agent Ai in the system covers
257
      a subset of this data space: Ci ⊆ D. The overall coverage by the system is given by the union of
258   all subsets covered by individual agents: Cmulti = ∪N  i=1 Ci . This ensures that |Cmulti | ≥ max |Ci |.
259   If only a single agent is available, it can only observe a portion of the data space, leaving parts of
260   the space unobserved, which leads to incomplete information for estimating the true label y. We
261   show an qualitative example of multi-agent collaboration provides complementary information to
262   enhance data coverage in Fig . 6 in the Appendix.
263
      From a probabilistic perspective, when multi-agent collaboration is in place, the combined likeli-
264   hood P (X|y) is modeled as a multivariate distribution (as discussed in Section 4). This approach
265   provides a broader and more accurate representation of the data space by integrating information
266   from all agents and modeling the dependencies and correlations between them. Compared to a uni-
267   variate distribution P (xi |y) for a single agent Ai , the multivariate distribution covers a larger portion
268   of the data space D, thus enhancing data coverage. This allows the exploration of more complex
269   patterns, relationships, and complementary information from different agents. By capturing a richer
      set of interactions and correlations among the agents’ observations, the multivariate distribution sup-

270
      ports more informed decision-making. The model’s predictions are based on a comprehensive view
271   of the environment, thus leading to more accurate outcomes.
272
      Information Gain. To address question (c) about information gain, we analyze using information
273
      theory. Let I(y; xi ) represent the mutual information between the true label y and agent Ai ’s ob-
274
      servation xi , which quantifies how much information xi provides about the estimation of y. The
275   mutual information between y and the set of all observations X is I(y; X).
276
277   In the context of multi-agent collaboration, the joint observations X from multiple agents typically
278
      provide more comprehensive information about the true label y compared to the observation of
      any single agent. Therefore, the mutual information I(y; X) is always greater than or equal to the
279
      mutual information from a single agent: I(y; X) ≥ I(y; xi ). Thus, the combined observations from
280
      multi-agent collaboration provide more information about y than a single observation, improving
281   the overall estimate. By leveraging the combined knowledge from multiple agents, the prediction
282   of y becomes more accurate, reflecting the added value of collaboration. Multi-agent systems are
283   generally more informative, as the interaction and joint information between agents can reduce
284   uncertainty about the target variable, as discussed in Section 4.

288   5.1   C OLLABORATIVE D ECISION -M AKING IN C ONNECTED AUTONOMOUS D RIVING

      To evaluate our approach, we first focus on collaborative decision-making in connected autonomous
      driving (CAV). This involves making critical decisions for the ego vehicle in accident-prone scenar-
291
      ios, such as determining whether or not to take a braking action.
292
293   Data Collection. We utilize a connected autonomous driving simulation that integrates CARLA
294   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) with AutoCast (Qiu et al., 2021). Following prior research (Cui et al.,
295
      2022; Gao et al., 2024), we focus on three complex traffic scenarios prone to accidents due to limited
      sensor coverage or obstructed views, as illustrated in Fig. 2: (1) Overtaking: A sedan is blocked
296
      by a truck on a narrow, two-way road with a dashed centerline. The truck also obscures the sedan’s
297
      view of oncoming traffic. The ego vehicle must decide when and how to safely pass the truck. (2)
298   Left Turn: The ego vehicle attempts a left turn at a yield sign. Its view is partially blocked by a
299   truck waiting in the opposite left-turn lane, reducing visibility of vehicles coming from the opposite
300   direction. (3) Red Light Violation: As the ego vehicle crosses an intersection, another vehicle runs
301   a red light. Due to nearby vehicles waiting to turn left, the ego vehicle’s sensors are unable to detect
302   the violator.
303
      For each scenario, we collect 24 trials, dividing them into 12 trials for training through behavior
304   cloning (BC) and 12 trials for testing. Each trial includes RGB images and LiDAR point clouds
305   captured by a variable number of connected vehicles, along with the ground truth actions of the
306   ego vehicle. At each timestamp, the ego vehicle has a maximum of three collaborative vehicles,
307   provided their distance is within a threshold of 150 meters (Gao et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2022). For
308   each vehicle, both RGB and LiDAR data are used during training, while only RGB data is used
309   during testing in CAML.

      decision-making, with RGBD data used for both training and testing.

461   To further evaluate our approach, we focus on collaborative perception by conducting experiments
462   with real-world data from aerial and ground vehicles for collaborative semantic segmentation. We
463   use the dataset CoPeD (Zhou et al., 2024), with one aerial vehicle and one ground vehicle, in two
464   different real-world scenarios of the indoor NYUARPL and the outdoor HOUSEA. For more details
465   about the dataset, please refer to Zhou et al. (2024). Additionally, we introduce noise to the RGBD
466   data collected by the ground vehicle. For both aerial and ground vehicles, RGB and depth data are
467
      used during training, while only RGB data is used during testing in CAML.
468   Experimental Setup. We adopt the FCN (Long et al., 2015) architecture as the backbone for
469   semantic segmentation. We resize the input RGB and depth images to 224 × 224. To process
470   RGB and depth data locally for each vehicle, we use ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) as the encoder
471   to extract feature maps of size 7 × 7. The RGB features from both vehicles are shared and fused
472   through channel-wise concatenation, and the depth features are processed similarly. Then we apply
473   1 × 1 convolution to reduce the fused feature maps to the original channel dimensions for RGB and
474   depth, respectively. We subsequently apply cross-attention to fuse the RGB and depth feature maps
475   to generate multi-agent multi-modal feature aggregations. These aggregated features are passed
476
      through the decoder and upsampled to produce an output map matching the input image size.
477   We first train a teacher model offline for semantic segmentation with aerial-ground vehicles collab-
478   oration using cross-entropy loss, where each vehicle has both RGB and depth data. Then we train a
479   student model to mimic the behavior of the teacher model with only RGB data for both aerial and
480   ground vehicles through Knowledge Distillation (KD). The KD process is similar to that of the col-
481   laborative decision-making in CAV, please refer to Appendix A.4, but here we use a cross-entropy
482
      loss as the student task loss. For the detailed training settings, please see Appendix A.3.1.
483   We evaluate performance using the Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) metric, which quantifies
484   the average overlap between predicted segmentation outputs and ground truth across all classes. We
485   compare the performance of CAML with AML (Shen et al., 2023) and FCN (Long et al., 2015).
      In the AML approach, only the ground vehicle operates, with RGB and depth data available during

508   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
509   In conclusion, we propose Collaborative Auxiliary Modality Learning (CAML), a novel framework
510   that extends the Auxiliary Modality Learning (AML) paradigm to multi-agent systems. Unlike pre-
511   vious approaches that either concentrate on multi-agent collaboration without addressing modality
512   reduction or handle multi-modality learning in single-agent settings, CAML unifies these concepts.
513
      It allows agents to collaborate in learning from shared modalities during training but enables ef-
      ficient, modality-reduced inference. This approach not only reduces computational cost and data
514
      requirements at test time but also improves the accuracy of predictions by leveraging multi-agent
515
      collaboration. Furthermore, we systematically analyze the effectiveness of CAML from the perspec-
516   tive of uncertainty reduction and data coverage, providing a theoretical support to understand and
517   explain why CAML works better than AML. We validate CAML on collaborative decision-making
518   tasks for connected autonomous driving in complex and accident-prone scenarios, CAML achieves
519   an improvement up to 58.3% in accident detection compared to AML. Additionally, we validate our
520   approach on real-world data from aerial-ground vehicles for collaborative semantic segmentation,
521   achieving up to 10.8% improvement in mIoU compared to AML. These significant enhancements
522   in driving safety and semantic estimation underscore the practical implications of our framework.
523   Even though the advancements of CAML, there are some limitations and failure cases. One failure
524   case is that if the modalities are misaligned, the model may struggle to perform effective fusion,
525   leading to incorrect predictions. For instance, if modalities such as RGB and depth are captured at
526   different time intervals or from non-overlapping fields of view, combining them effectively can be
527   difficult. The auxiliary modalities or views from collaborative agents may become noise, useless
528   or even degrading performance. Another limitation is the increasing system complexity. As the
529   number of agents increases, the complexity of the system grows. The fusion of multi-agent and
530
      multi-modal data introduces challenges related to coordination overhead, which may lead to delays
      in the collaborative learning process.
531
532   Future work can focus on addressing these limitations and failure cases, and further exploring the
533   applicability of CAML in other domains where multi-agent collaboration with multi-modal data is
534   essential, and investigating additional strategies to enhance performance during testing with fewer
535   modalities.

      A.2.2    A BLATION S TUDIES
757
758   In the ablation studies, we explore another variant of CAML called Pre-fusion CAML, applied
759   to the experiment of aerial-ground vehicles collaborative semantic segmentation. However, it is
760   important to note that this variant can be applied to other domains and experiments as well. In
761   this variant, each vehicle first locally extract feature maps of size 7 × 7 for both RGB and depth
762   modalities. Instead of separately fusing the RGB and depth features between the vehicles, we first
763
      fuse the feature maps of RGB and depth within each single vehicle using cross-attention. Then
      we share and merge the fused RGBD features between vehicles via concatenation. We also apply
764
      1 × 1 convolution to reduce the feature maps to the original channel dimensions. The multi-agent,
765
      multi-modal feature aggregations then pass through the decoder. Finally, we obtain the output map
766   by upsampling to match the input image size. The mIoU of the Pre-fusion CAML is similar to that
767   of CAML, achieving 59.16% and 65.78% for indoor and outdoor environments, respectively. By
768   comparison, CAML achieves 60.05% and 66.83% in the same settings. Although the fusion order is
769   different, both versions benefit from robust feature aggregation and multi-agent collaboration, which
770   ultimately results in better segmentation performance.
771
      Both CAML and its variant Pre-fusion CAML have their advantages, CAML fuses the same modal-
772   ities across different agents, which provides better alignment because it ensures consistency in fea-
773   ture representation. And this approach is particularly beneficial when individual agent views are
774   limited, as CAML effectively leverages diverse viewpoints to provide complementary information,
775   enhancing overall data coverage. On the other hand, Pre-fusion CAML allows agent-specific con-
776   textual understanding by fusing different modalities locally within each agent. Furthermore, the
777   system avoids redundant communication between agents by transmitting multi-modal aggregated
778   features rather than modality-specific features separately. CAML can easily shift to Pre-fusion
779   CAML because of the flexibility of our framework, depending on application scenarios.
