009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we study the adversarial robustness of deep neural networks for
012            classification tasks. We look at the smallest magnitude of possible additive per-
013            turbations that can change the output of a classification algorithm. We provide
014            a matrix-theoretic explanation of the adversarial fragility of deep neural network
015
               for classification. In particular, our theoretical results show that neural network’s
               adversarial robustness can degrade as the input dimension d increases. Analytically
                                                                                           √
016
               we show that neural networks’ adversarial robustness can be only 1/ d of the
017
               best possible adversarial robustness. Our matrix-theoretic explanation is consistent
018
               with an earlier information-theoretic feature-compression-based explanation for
019            the adversarial fragility of neural networks.

      1    Introduction
023   Deep learning or neural network based classifiers are known to offer high classification accuracy in
024   many classification tasks. However, it is also observed that deep learning based classifiers often suffer
025   from adversarial fragility and have low robustness under adversarial perturbations Szegedy et al.
026   (2014); Goodfellow et al. (2014). For example, when a small amount of adversarial noise is added to
027   the signal input of a deep learning classifier, its output can dramatically change from an accurate label
028   to an inaccurate label, even though the input signal is barely changed according to human perceptions.
029
      The reason why the deep learning classifier is often fragile has remained a mystery, even though there
      have been various theories explaining this phenomenon, see e.g. Akhtar & Mian (2018); Yuan et al.
030
      (2017); Huang et al. (2018); Wu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023) for surveys.
031
032   These studies, however, have not yet resulted in a consensus on the important question: a theoretical
033   explanation for adversarial fragility. Instead, we currently have multiple competing theoretical
034   explanations, which include (a) quasi-linearity/smoothness of the decision function in AI classifiers
035   Goodfellow et al. (2014); Li & Spratling (2023); Kanai et al. (2023); Eustratiadis et al. (2022), (b)
036   high curvature of the decision boundary Fawzi et al. (2016); Reza et al. (2023); Singla et al. (2021),
037
      (c) closeness of the classification boundary to the data sub-manifold Tanay & Griffin (2016); Zeng
      et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2022), and (d) information-theoretic feature compression hypothesis Xie
038
      et al. (2019). In Ilyas et al. (2019), the authors argued that the adversarial fragility of neural network
039
      possibly came from the neural network utilizing non-robust features for classification. However, there
040   are recent works, for example Li et al. (2023), which show that non-robust features might not be able
041   to fully explain the adversarial fragility of neural network based classifiers.
042
043   Besides these works, there are results trying to use high dimensional statistical analysis tools to
044   theoretically understand the adversarial robustness of classification models. An asymptotically exact
045
      formula given in Hassani & Javanmard (2022) shows that higher overparametrization leads to a worse
      robust generalization error for the adversarially-trained models. The performance of high-dimensional
046
      linear models is studied in Donhauser et al. (2021) and Javanmard et al. (2020) which showed that
047
      the robust generalization error of adversarially-trained models becomes worse as the models become
048   more overparameterized. The analysis in Taheri et al. (2021) is for the adversarially-trained linear
049   model in the high-dimensional regime where the dimension of data grows with the size of the training
050   data-set at a constant ratio for binary classification. In Taheri et al. (2021), the authors precisely
051   analyzed the performance of adversarial training with ℓ2 and ℓ∞ -norm bounded perturbations in
052   binary classification for Gaussian mixture and generalized linear models. It was shown in Tsipras
053   et al. (2019) that there exists a trade-off between the standard accuracy of a model and its robustness
      to adversarial perturbations. It is also observed that using more data can improve this trade-off

054
      Carmon et al. (2022); Min et al. (2021); Najafi et al. (2019); Raghunathan et al. (2019); Rebuffi et al.
055   (2021).
056
057   Despite these efforts, there is still not clear consensus or theoretical understanding of the fundamental
058
      reason for the adversarial fragility of neural network based classifiers Li et al. (2023). It might be
      tempting to explain the adversarial fragility of neural network based classifiers purely as the gap
059
      between the average-case performance (the performance of the classifier under random average-
060
      case noise) and the worst-case performance (the performance of the classifier under well-crafted
061   worst-case perturbation), for example through the linearity of the model Goodfellow et al. (2014).
062   However, we argue that this average-case-versus-worst-case gap cannot explain the dramatic fragility
063   of deep learning based classifiers. Firstly, it is common that there is a gap between average-case and
064   worst-case performance: it exists for almost every classifier (even including theoretically optimal
065   classifiers), and is not particularly tied to neural network based classifiers. Secondly, we can show
066   that there exists good-performing classifiers whose worst-case performance are provably orders
067   of dimension better than the worst-case performance of deep learning based classifiers. So there
068   are deeper reasons for the adversarial fragility of neural network based classifiers than just the
069
      worst-case-versus-average-case degradation.
070   In this paper, we study the adversarial robustness of deep neural networks for classification tasks from
071   a different perspective than the current literature. We focus on comparing the worst-case performance
072   of neural network based classifiers and optimal classifiers. We look at the smallest magnitude of
073   possible additive perturbations that can change the output of the classification algorithm. We provide
074   a matrix-theoretic explanation of the adversarial fragility of deep neural network. In particular,
075
      our theoretical results show that neural network’s adversarial robustness can degrade as the input
076
      dimension√ d increases. Analytically we show that neural networks’ adversarial robustness can be
077
      only 1/ d of the best possible adversarial robustness.

      In particular, in this paper, through concrete classification examples and matrix-theoretic derivations,
081
      we show that the adversarial fragility of neural network based classifiers comes from the fact that
082
      very often neural network only uses a subset (or compressed features) of all the features to perform
083   the classification tasks. Thus in adversarial attacks, one just needs to add perturbations to change the
084   small subsets of features used by the neural networks. This conclusion from matrix-theoretic analysis
085   is consistent with the earlier information-theoretic feature-compression-based hypothesis that neural
086   network based classifier’s fragility comes from its utilizing compressed features for final classification
087   decisions Xie et al. (2019). Different from Xie et al. (2019) which gave a higher-level explanation
088   based on the feature compression hypothesis and high-dimensional geometric analysis, this paper
089   gives the analysis of adversarial fragility building on concrete neural network architectures and
090   classification examples. Our results are derived for linear and non-linear, for two-layer and general
091
      multiple-layer neural networks with different assumptions on network weights, and for different
      classification tasks. As a byproduct, we developed a characterization of the distribution of the QR
092
      decomposition of the products of random Gaussian matrices in Lemma 3.

      7    C ONCLUSIONS
526
527   We study the adversarial robustness of deep neural networks for classification tasks. The adversarial
528   robustness of a classifier is defined as the smallest possible additive perturbations that can change the
529   classification output. We provide a matrix-theoretic explanation of the adversarial fragility of deep
530   neural network. Our theoretical results show that neural network’s adversarial robustness can degrade
531
      as the input dimension
                      √       d increases. Analytically we show that neural networks’ adversarial robustness
532
      can be only 1/ d of the best possible adversarial robustness. Our matrix-theoretic explanation is
      consistent with an earlier information-theoretic feature-compression-based explanation. Limitations
533
      of this paper include the need to extend detailed theoretical analysis and numerical experiments to
534
      more general data distributions, neural network architectures, and the need to further explore the
535   relationship between adversarial robustness and network parameters such as number of layers.

766
767   Proof. Suppose that we add a perturbation q to the input x + ϵx1 , namely the input becomes
768   x + ϵx1 + q. Then
769   f1 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f1 (x + ϵx1 ) + ∇f1 (x)T q   and   f2 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f2 (x + ϵx1 ) + ∇f2 (x)T q
770
771   We want to pick a q such that
772               f1 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f1 (x + ϵx2 ) and      f2 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f2 (x + ϵx2 ).
773
774   Apparently, we can take q = ϵ(x2 − x1 ) to make this happen. However, we claim we can potentially
775   take a perturbation of a much smaller size to achieve this goal. We note that
776
777                        f1 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f1 (x) + ϵ∇f1 (x)T x1 + ∇f1 (x)T q
778   and
779                        f2 (x + ϵx1 + q) ≈ f2 (x) + ϵ∇f2 (x)T x1 + ∇f2 (x)T q.
780
781   We want
782                     f1 (x) + ϵ∇f1 (x)T x1 + ∇f1 (x)T q = f1 (x) + ϵ∇f1 (x)T x2 ,
783   and
784                     f2 (x) + ϵ∇f2 (x)T x1 + ∇f2 (x)T q = f2 (x) + ϵ∇f2 (x)T x2 .
785
786   Namely, we want
787
      ϵ∇f1 (x)T x1 + ∇f1 (x)T q = ϵ∇f1 (x)T x2 ,      and   ϵ∇f2 (x)T x1 + ∇f2 (x)T q = ϵ∇f2 (x)T x2 .
788
789
      So
790             ∇f1 (x)T q = ϵ∇f1 (x)T (x2 − x1 ),    and   ∇f2 (x)T q = ϵ∇f2 (x)T (x2 − x1 ).
791
792   Then we can just let q be the projection of ϵ(x2 − x1 ) onto the subspace spanned by ∇f1 (x) and
793   ∇f2 (x).
794
      If ∇f1 (x), ∇f2 (x), and x2 − x1 all have independent standard Gaussian random variables as their
795   elements, then the square of the magnitude (in ℓ2 norm ) of that projection of x2 − x1 will follow a
796   chi-squared distribution of degree 2. At the same time, the square of the magnitude of x2 − x1 will
797   follow the chi-squared distribution with degree d. Moreover, as d → ∞, the square of the magnitude
798   of x2 − x1 is Θ(d) with high probability. Thus changing from x + ϵx1 to x + ϵx2 will be O(d) times
799   more difficult than changing the classifier’s label using an adversarial attack.
