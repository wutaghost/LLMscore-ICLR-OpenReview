010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Modern machine learning pipelines leverage large amounts of public data, making
013            it infeasible to guarantee data quality and leaving models open to poisoning and
014
               backdoor attacks. Provably bounding model behavior under such attacks remains
               an open problem. In this work, we address this challenge by developing the first
015
               framework providing provable guarantees on the behavior of models trained with
016
               potentially manipulated data without modifying the model or learning algorithm.
017            In particular, our framework certifies robustness against untargeted and targeted
018            poisoning, as well as backdoor attacks, for bounded and unbounded manipulations
019            of the training inputs and labels. Our method leverages convex relaxations to
020            over-approximate the set of all possible parameter updates for a given poisoning
021            threat model, allowing us to bound the set of all reachable parameters for any
022            gradient-based learning algorithm. Given this set of parameters, we provide bounds
023            on worst-case behavior, including model performance and backdoor success rate.
024            We demonstrate our approach on multiple real-world datasets from applications
025
               including energy consumption, medical imaging, and autonomous driving.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      To achieve state-of-the-art performance, modern machine learning pipelines involve pre-training on
030   massive, uncurated datasets; subsequently, models are fine-tuned with task-specific data to maximize
031   downstream performance (Han et al., 2021). Unfortunately, the datasets used in both steps are
032   potentially untrustworthy and of such scale that rigorous quality checks become impractical.
033
      Data Poisoning. Yet, adversarial manipulation, i.e., poisoning attacks, affecting even a small
034
      proportion of data used for either pre-training or fine-tuning can lead to catastrophic model failures
035
      (Carlini et al., 2023). For instance, Yang et al. (2017) show how popular recommender systems
036   on sites such as YouTube, Ebay, and Yelp can be easily manipulated by poisoning. Likewise, Zhu
037   et al. (2019) show that poisoning even 1% of training data can lead models to misclassify targeted
038   examples, and Han et al. (2022) use poisoning to selectively trigger backdoor vulnerabilities in lane
039   detection systems to force critical errors.
040
      Poisoning Defenses. Despite the gravity of the failure modes induced by poisoning attacks, counter-
041
      measures are generally attack-specific and only defend against known attack methods (Tian et al.,
042   2022). The result of attack-specific defenses is an effective “arms race” between attackers trying
043   to circumvent the latest defenses and counter-measures being developed for the new attacks. In
044   effect, even best practices, i.e., using the latest defenses, provide no guarantees of protection against
045   poisoning attacks. To date, relatively few approaches have sought provable guarantees against
046   poisoning attacks. These methods are often limited in scope, e.g., applying only to linear models
047   (Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Steinhardt et al., 2017) or only providing approximate guarantees for a limited
048   set of poisoning settings (Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022). Other approaches partition datasets
049   into hundreds or thousands of disjoint shards and then aggregate predictions such that the effects
050
      of poisoning is provably limited (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Wang et al., 2022). In contrast, our goal in
      this work is not to produce a robust learning algorithm, but to efficiently analyze the sensitivity of
051
      (un-modified) algorithms. Further discussion of related works is provided in Appendix A.
052
053   This Work: General Certificates of Poisoning Robustness. We present an approach for computing
      sound and general certificates of robustness to poisoning attacks for any model trained with first-order

054
      optimization methods, e.g., stochastic gradient descent or Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014). The proposed
055   strategy begins by treating various poisoning attacks as constraints over an adversary’s perturbation
056   ‘budget’ in input and label spaces. Following the comprehensive taxonomy by Tian et al. (2022),
057   we view the objective of each poisoning attack as an optimization problem. We consider three
058   objectives: (i) untargeted attacks: reducing model test performance to cause denial-of-service, (ii)
059   targeted attacks: compromising model performance on certain types of inputs, and (iii) backdoor
060   attacks: leaving the model performance stable, but introducing a trigger pattern that causes errors
061   at deployment time. Our approach then leverages convex relaxations of both the training problem
062   and the constraint sets defining the threat model to compute a sound (but incomplete) certificate that
063
      bounds the impact of the poisoning attack.
064   Paper Outline. The paper is organized as follows. We first provide a general framework for poisoning
065   attacks, describing how our formulation captures the settings studied in prior works (related works
066   are reviewed in Appendix A). We then present abstract gradient training (AGT), our technique
067   for over-approximating the effect of a given poisoning attack and discuss implementation details,
068   including a novel, explicit formulation of CROWN-like bounds (Zhang et al., 2018) on the weight
069
      gradients. We conclude with extensive ablation experiments on datasets from household energy
      consumption, medical image classification, and autonomous vehicle driving. In summary, this paper
070
      makes the following key contributions:
071
072   • A framework, including a novel bound propagation strategy, for computing sound bounds on the
073     influence of a poisoning adversary on any model trained with gradient-based methods.
074
      • Based on the above, a series of formal proofs that allow us to bound the effect of poisoning attacks
075
        that seek to corrupt the system with targeted, untargeted, or backdoor attacks.
076
077   • An extensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of our approach.

098   This section defines the capabilities of the poisoning attack adversaries that we seek to certify against.
099   We consider two distinct threat models: (1) ℓp -norm bounded adversaries, which are commonly
100   assumed in backdoor attack models (Saha et al., 2020; Weber et al., 2023); (2) unbounded adversaries,
101   which are more general and have the capability to inject arbitrary data into the training set. In both
102   cases, we consider adversaries able to modify both features and labels simultaneously.

104   training data in both the feature and label space, where the magnitude of the perturbation is bounded

106   a distance of up to ϵ in the ℓp -norm. Similarly, we define the label-space poisoning capability as
107   modifying at most m labels by magnitude at most ν in an ℓq -norm. Note that label-space poisoning
      encompasses both classification and (multivariate) regression settings. Common label-flipping attacks

      This section develops a novel approach for certifying robustness to poisoning attacks starting by
165   assuming we have access to the set of all reachable trained models in the form of intervals over model
166   parameters. We first show how these intervals can be used to certify robustness to the above attacks.
167   We then formulate a general algorithm for bounding the effect of adversaries on model parameters,
168   producing an interval containing all reachable training parameters. We then instantiate it using a novel
169   formulation of CROWN-style bounds which can soundly compute the quantities required within.

310
      i.e., A := [AL , AU ] ⊂ Rn1 ×n2 and interval matrix multiplication as ⊗, meaning AB ∈ A ⊗ B
311

314   interval arithmetic techniques. We denote interval vectors as a := [aL , aU ] with analogous operations.

      Forward Pass Bounds Mirroring developments in robustness certification of neural networks, we
317
      provide a novel, explicit extension of the CROWN algorithm (Zhang et al., 2018) to account for
318   interval-bounded weights. The standard CROWN algorithm bounds the outputs of the m-th layer of a
319   neural network by back-propagating linear bounds over each intermediate activation function to the

368
      Figure 1 visualizes the resulting worst-case decision boundaries for a simple binary classifier con-
369
      sisting of a neural network with a hidden layer of 128 neurons. In this classification setting, label
370
      poisoning results in looser bounds than feature space poisoning, with m = 5 producing bounds of
371   approximately the same width as n = 50. This is due to the relatively large interval introduced by a
372   label flipping attack y (i) ∈ {0, 1}, compared to an interval of width ϵ introduced in a feature-space
373   attack. We also emphasise that Algorithm 1 assumes at most m, n poisoned points per batch, rather
374   than per dataset. In regression settings, label poisoning is relatively weaker than feature poisoning
375   for a given strength ϵ = ν, since the feature-space interval propagates through both the forward and
376   backward training passes, while the label only participates in the backward pass. This effect is partic-
377   ularly pronounced in deep networks, since interval/CROWN bounds tend to weaken exponentially
      with depth (Mao et al., 2023; Sosnin & Tsay, 2024).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Comparison to Interval Bound Propagation. The CROWN algorithm in § 3.3 is not strictly tighter
379   than interval bound propagation (IBP). Specifically, the non-associativity of double-interval matrix
380   multiplication leads to significantly different interval sizes depending on the order in which the
381   multiplications are performed: IBP performs interval matrix multiplications in a ‘forwards’ ordering,
382   while CROWN uses a ‘backwards’ ordering. Empirically, we observe that CROWN tends to be tighter
383   for deeper networks, while IBP may outperform CROWN for smaller networks. In our numerical
384   experiments, we compute both CROWN and IBP bounds and take the element-wise tightest bound.
385   Combined Forward and Backward Pass Bounds. The CROWN algorithm can be applied to any
386   composition of functions that can be upper- and lower-bounded by linear equations. Therefore, it is
387   possible to consider both the forwards and backwards passes in a single combined CROWN pass for
388   many loss functions. However, linear bounds on the gradient of the loss function tend to be relatively
389   loose, e.g., linear bounds on the softmax function may be orders-of-magnitude looser than constant
390   [0, 1] bounds (Wei et al., 2023). As a result, we found that the tightest bounds were obtained using
391   IBP/CROWN on the forward pass and IBP on the backward pass.
392   Computational Complexity. The computational complexity of Algorithm 1 depends on the method
393   used to bound on the gradients. In the simplest case, IBP can be used to compute bounds on the
394   gradients in 4× the cost of a standard forward and backward pass (see Appendix C). Likewise, our
395   CROWN bounds admit a cost of at most 4 times the cost of the original CROWN algorithm. For an
396   m layer network with n neurons per layer and n outputs, the time complexity of the original CROWN
397   algorithm is O(m2 n3 ) (Zhang et al., 2018). We further note that the SEMin / SEMax operations
398   required by Theorems B.1 and 3.3 can be computed in O(b) for each index and in practice can be
399
      efficiently parallelized using GPU-based implementations. In summary, Abstract Gradient Training
      using IBP has time complexity equivalent to standard neural network training (O(bmn2 ) for each
400
      batch of size b), but with our tighter, CROWN-based, bounds the complexity is O(bm2 n3 ) per batch.
401
402   Limitations. While Algorithm 1 is able to obtain valid-parameter space bounds for any gradient-based
403   training algorithm, the tightness of these bounds depends on the exact architecture, hyperparameters
404   and training procedure used. In particular, bound-propagation between successive iterations of the
405
      algorithm assumes the worst-case poisoning at each parameter index, which may not be achievable
      by realistic poisoning attacks. Therefore, obtaining non-vacuous guarantees with our algorithm
406
      often requires training with larger batch-sizes and/or for fewer epochs than is typical. Additionally,
407
      certain loss functions, such as multi-class cross entropy, have particularly loose interval relaxations.
408   Therefore, AGT obtains relatively weaker guarantees for multi-class problems when compared to
409   regression or binary classification settings. We hope that tighter bound-propagation approaches, such
410   as those based on more expressive abstract domains, may overcome this limitation in future works.
411
      Computing Certificates of Poisoning Robustness. Algorithm 1 returns valid parameter-space
412
      bounds [θL , θU ] for a given poisoning adversary. To provide certificates of poisoning robustness for
413
      a specific query at a point x, we first bound the model output f θ (x)∀θ ∈ [θL , θU ] using the bound-
414   propagation procedure described above. In classification settings, the robustness of the prediction can
415   then be certified by checking if the lower bound on the output logit for the target class is greater than
416   the upper bounds of all other classes (i.e. [fjθ (x)]L ≥ [fiθ (x)]U ∀i ̸= j). If this condition is satisfied,
417   then the model always predicts class j at the point x for all parameters within our parameter-space
418   bounds, and thus this prediction is certifiably robust to poisoning. Details on computing bounds on
419   other poisoning adversary objectives (3), (4), and (5) can be found in Appendix G.

533       5                C ONCLUSIONS
534
535       We proposed a mathematical framework for computing sound parameter-space bounds on the influ-
536       ence of a poisoning attack for gradient-based training. Our framework defines generic constraint sets
537       to represent general poisoning attacks and propagates them through the forward and backward passes
538       of model training. Based on the resulting parameter-space bounds, we provided rigorous bounds
539       on the effects of various poisoning attacks. Finally, we demonstrated our proposed approach to be
          effective on tasks including autonomous driving and the classification of medical images.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
705   Data Poisoning. Poisoning attacks have existed for nearly two decades and are a serious security
706
      concern (Biggio & Roli, 2018; Biggio et al., 2014; Newsome et al., 2006). In Muñoz-González
      et al. (2017) the authors formulate a general gradient-based attack that generates poisoned samples
707
      that corrupt model performance when introduced into the dataset (now termed, untargeted attack).
708
      Backdoor attacks manipulate a small proportion of the data such that, when a specific pattern is
709   seen at test-time, the model returns a specific, erroneous prediction Chen et al. (2017); Gu et al.
710   (2017); Han et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2019). Popular defenses are attack specific, e.g., generating
711   datasets using known attack strategies to classify and reject potentially poisoned inputs (Li et al.,
712   2020). Alternative strategies apply noise or clipping to mitigate certain attacks (Hong et al., 2020).
713
714
715   Poisoning Defenses. General defenses to poisoning attacks seek to provide upper-bounds on the
716
      effectiveness of any attack strategy. In this area, Steinhardt et al. (2017) provide such upper-bounds
      for linear models trained with gradient descent. Rosenfeld et al. (2020) present a statistical upper-
717
      bound on the effectiveness of ℓ2 perturbations on training labels for linear models using randomized
718
      smoothing. Xie et al. (2022) observe that differential privacy, which usually covers addition or
719   removal of data points, can also provide statistical guarantees in some limited poisoning settings.
720
721
722   Certified Poisoning Robustness Relative to inference-time adversarial robustness (also referred to
723   as evasion attacks), less attention has been devoted to provable guarantees against data poisoning
724   adversaries. Existing methods for deterministic certification of robustness to poisoning adversaries
725
      involve design of a learning process with careful partitioning and ensembling such that the resulting
      model has poisoning robustness guarantees (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Rezaei et al.,
726
      2023). We refer to these methods as "aggregation" methods. In contrast, our approach is a method
727
      for analysis and certification of standard, unmodified machine learning algorithms. Aggregation
728   approaches have been shown to offer strong guarantees against poisoning adversaries albeit at a
729   substantial computational cost including: storing and training thousands of models on (potentially
730   disjoint) subsets of the dataset and the requirement to evaluate each of the potentially thousands of
731   models for each prediction; additionally, these methods require that one have potentially thousands
732   of times more data than is necessary for training a single classifier. By designing algorithms to be
733   robust to poisoning adversaries aggregation based approaches are able to scale to larger models than
734   are considered in this work (Levine & Feizi, 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Rezaei et al., 2023). Yet,
735   the computational cost of our approach, in the simplest case, is only four times that of standard
736
      training and inference and we do not require that one has access to enough data to train multiple
      well-performing models. Furthermore, our approach enables reasoning about backdoor attacks, where
737
      these partitioning approaches cannot. We finally highlight that the method presented in this paper
738
      is orthogonal/complementary to the partitioning approach, and thus future works may be able to
739   combine the two effectively.
740
741
742   Certified Adversarial Robustness. Sound algorithms (i.e., no false positives) for upper-bounding
743   the effectiveness of inference-time adversaries are well-studied for trained models (Gehr et al., 2018)
744   and training models for robustness (Gowal et al., 2018; Müller et al., 2022). These approaches
745
      typically utilize ideas from formal methods (Katz et al., 2017; Wicker et al., 2018) or optimization
      (Botoeva et al., 2020; Bunel et al., 2018; Huchette et al., 2023). Most related to this work are strategies
746
      that consider intervals over both model inputs and parameters (Wicker et al., 2020), as well as some
747
      preliminary work on robust explanations that bound the input gradients of a model Wicker et al.
748   (2022). Despite these methodological relationships, none of these methods directly apply to the
749   general training setting studied here.

781   In this appendix, we provide a basic introduction to interval matrix arithmetic, which forms the basic
782   building block of our CROWN-style bounds. We denote intervals over matrices as A := [AL , AU ] ⊆
783   Rn×m such that for all A ∈ A, AL ≤ A ≤ AU .
784   Definition 2 (Interval Matrix Arithmetic). Let A = [AL , AU ] and B = [BL , BU ] be intervals over
785   matrices. Let ⊕, ⊗, ⊙ represent interval matrix addition, matrix multiplication and elementwise
786   multiplication, such that
787                                  A + B ∈ [A ⊕ B] ∀A ∈ A, B ∈ B,
788                                  A × B ∈ [A ⊗ B] ∀A ∈ A, B ∈ B,
789
                                     A ◦ B ∈ [A ⊙ B] ∀A ∈ A, B ∈ B.
790
791   These operations can be computed using standard interval arithmetic techniques in at most 4× the
792   cost of a standard matrix operation. For example, interval matrix multiplication can be computed
793   using the following procedure.
794   Definition 3 (Interval Matrix Multiplication). Given element-wise intervals over matrices [AL , AU ]
795   where AL , AU ∈ Rn×m and [BL , BU ] where BL , BU ∈ Rm×k , define the matrices Aµ = (AU +
796   AL )/2 and Ar = (AU − AL )/2. Allow Bµ and Br to be defined analogously, then computing using
797   Rump’s algorithm (Rump, 1999),
798
                                   CL = Aµ Bµ − |Aµ |Br − Ar |Bµ | − Ar Br
799
                                   CU = Aµ Bµ + |Aµ |Br + Ar |Bµ | + Ar Br ,
800
801   we have that CLi,j ≤ [A′ B ′ ]i,j ≤ CU i,j ∀A′ ∈ [AL , AU ], B ′ ∈ [BL , BU ]. Nguyen (2012) showed
802   that the above bounds have a worst-case overestimation factor of 1.5.
803
      Interval arithmetic is commonly applied as a basic verification or adversarial training technique by
804
      propagating intervals through the intermediate layers of a neural network (Gowal et al., 2018).

1359   J.1        C OMPARISON WITH D EEP PARTITION AGGREGATION
1360
1361   As discussed in Appendix A, existing approaches seeking certifiable robustness to data poisoning rely
1362   on partitioning the dataset and training large ensemble models. Figure 9 compares the guarantees
1363   provided by Abstract Gradient Training to those of a popular ensemble method, (Self-Supervised)-
1364
       Deep Partition Aggregation (SS-DPA) (Levine & Feizi, 2020).
1365   SS-DPA demonstrates both higher nominal and certified accuracies than AGT on the MNIST dataset.
1366   However, the ensemble approach is specifically designed to be robust to data poisoning attacks. On
1367   the other hand, AGT focuses on certifying an existing training algorithm by analyzing the sensitivity
1368   of standard training pipelines to data poisoning.
1369   Additionally, our experiments show that AGT incurs a run-time cost of approximately 2-4 times
1370   standard training. In contrast, SS-DPA requires training an ensemble of thousands of classifiers,
1371   demanding significant data and computational costs. Finally, we highlight once again that AGT is
1372   complementary to ensemble approaches, and future works may seek to combine the two.
