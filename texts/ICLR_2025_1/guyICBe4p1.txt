009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent work has demonstrated that the latent spaces of large language models
012            (LLMs) contain directions predictive of the truth of sentences. Multiple meth-
013            ods recover such directions and build probes that are described as uncovering
014            a model’s “knowledge” or “beliefs”. We investigate this phenomenon, looking
015            closely at the impact of context on the probes. Our experiments establish where
016            in the LLM the probe’s predictions are (most) sensitive to the presence of related
017            sentences, and how to best characterize this kind of sensitivity. We do so by
018
               measuring different types of consistency errors that occur after probing an LLM
               whose inputs consist of hypotheses preceded by (negated) supporting and contra-
019
               dicting sentences. We also perform a causal intervention experiment, investigat-
020
               ing whether moving the representation of a premise along these belief directions
021            influences the position of an entailed or contradicted sentence along that same di-
022            rection. We find that the probes we test are generally context sensitive, but that
023            contexts which should not affect the truth often still impact the probe outputs.
024            Our experiments show that the type of errors depend on the layer, the model, and
025            the kind of data. Finally, our results suggest that belief directions are (one of the)
026            causal mediators in the inference process that incorporates in-context information.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As Large Language Models (LLMs) enjoy increasing mainstream adoption, it becomes more im-
032   portant to understand why they fail in some cases, while excelling in others. Hallucination is a
033
      type of failure where the LLM produces grammatical but inaccurate text. Recent work shows that
      LLM latent spaces contain directions that are predictive of the truth of sentences (Burns et al., 2023;
034
      Marks & Tegmark, 2023), and that this enables mitigating hallucination without additional training
035
      (Li et al., 2023). The presence of such directions suggests that the model represents sentences as
036   more or less (likely to be) true, resembling a kind of (occurrent) belief. By projecting hidden acti-
037   vations on such belief directions we obtain belief probes. Such probes accurately identify if a model
038   represents a sentence as true, even in misleading contexts where prompting fails Burns et al. (2023).
039
      Research into belief directions has already shown how they can be used to mitigate factual errors
040
      Li et al. (2023), a type of hallucination that can occur independently of context. Another type of
041
      hallucination is characterized by inconsistency (Huang et al., 2023). Working towards the mitigation
042   of this type of hallucination requires understanding the impact of context on belief probes. Our
043   experiments investigate the behaviour of belief probes on sentences that appear in contexts with
044   other related sentences. This enables us to determine how inferential contexts influence an LLM’s
045   assessment of truth-values.
046
      LLMs perform well on tasks which are commonly held to require reasoning (Suzgun et al., 2022).
047
      Thus, we expect LLMs’ degrees of belief to be sensitive to relevant information provided in context.
048   For example, given a premise Q followed by a hypothesis H, such as “December is during the
049   winter for New York” and “In New York, days are shortest in December”. We might expect a model
050   to represent (potentially at different points) different degrees of belief for these sentences, such as:
051   1) prior beliefs (not paying attention to the context at all); 2) conditional beliefs where the context
052   is assumed to be truthful; or 3) beliefs that incorporate the model’s own assessment of the context’s
053   truth. Our experiments vary the truth of premises and hypotheses to determine to what extent each
      of these are happening.

073   We also investigate if the belief directions causally mediate truth-value judgment, or if they only

080
      In summary, our contributions are: (1) experiments demonstrating the context sensitivity of belief
081   probes and the consistency with which they incorporate it; we quantify both across layers, model
082   sizes (7 and 13 billion), type of training (pretrained-only vs. instruction-tuned); and (2) an experi-
083   ment demonstrating that belief directions causally mediate natural language inference. We also pro-
084   pose a new variant of CCS (Burns et al., 2023) for which convergence is more stable, and otherwise
085   behaves and performs similarly. All our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
086   science/r/lcb2-AF5D.

089   2                              R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Probing LLM representations for the truth of sentences has recently received much interest. Burns
092   et al. (2023) introduce Contrast Consistent Search (CCS), an unsupervised probing methods based
093   on the representations of contrasting sentence pairs. Their probes often outperform a (zero-shot)
094   prompting approach, even when applied to misleading prompts. Li et al. (2023) shift model activa-
095   tions in the ‘truth direction’ at inference time, mitigating hallucination. Their interventions use 1)
096   directions from probes trained with logistic regression (LR) and CCS; and 2) a new method (Mass
097   Mean Shift), which finds the direction as the difference between the means of the true and false
098
      sentence representations. Marks & Tegmark (2023) use Mass Mean Shift directions and turn them
      into probes (Mass-Mean Probing, MMP). They show that all probes (based on LR, CCS, and MMP)
099
      generalize well between datasets, with MMP performing the best. In their causal intervention exper-
100
      iment, the representations are moved in the identified directions, and MMP is shown to be the best
101   mediator, causing the highest increase in probability of the model calling a false statement true.
102
103   Most of the preceding methods used data consisting of single facts. While Burns et al. (2023) did
104
      include datasets with context, including various NLI datasets, they did not study the impact of that
      context on their probes. We specifically study the in-context behaviour of these probes, analysing
105
      their consistency, and what this means for the way LLMs incorporate contextual information.
106
107   Like Marks & Tegmark (2023), we also investigate the causal implication of directions in LLM
      latent space. However, rather than investigate what causes the greatest change in token predictions,


                                                                                                      2
108
      we investigate which direction to move a premise in, such that it causes the correct change in the
109   probability of a related hypothesis, as evaluated by the same direction.
110
111   Herrmann & Levinstein (2024) have recently formulated four requirements for a representation to
112
      count as belief-like. One of those requirements is coherence, which requires that belief probes be
      logically consistent. Our method measures specific kinds of coherence: two error scores measure the
113
      extent to which probed beliefs depend on semantically irrelevant factors, and the two others measure
114
      if beliefs are consistent with either kind of context
115
116   Recent work has also criticised this type of probing. Belief probes might identify sentence properties
117   that correlate with truth in the model’s training data (Levinstein & Herrmann, 2024), especially
118
      when truth is not the most salient feature (Farquhar et al., 2023). We argue that arbitrary spurious
      correlations are unlikely to be coherent, and will perform poorly with our error scores. However,
119
      another concern might be that the direction found does encode beliefs, but a more subjective one.
120
      Our prompt design does ensure that beliefs are not those held by a person or character that the input
121   text explicitly mentions, as in Farquhar et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2024). But, future work will have
122   to determine if probed beliefs are best ascribed to: (i) the LLM itself; (ii) a text-producing process
123   simulated by the LLM; (iii) the general public (“commonly-held belief”, Levinstein & Herrmann,
124   2024); or something else entirely. We believe that our method is useful in any case.

      3       M ETHODOLOGY
127
128
      We describe our method in three parts: in 3.1 we cover belief probes, the necessary assumptions,
129   notation, and methods to construct them; in 3.2 we describe how to construct samples suitable to
130   probe for truth-value judgment; and in 3.3 we introduce error scores based on which we evaluate to
131   what extent proper truth-value judgment is taking place.

133   3.1       B ELIEF P ROBING
134
135   We use several belief probing methods in our experiments. These methods use datasets of sentences,
136   consisting of both true and false statements. We can turn any true statement into a false statement
137   (and vice versa) by negating it. We use X + and X − to denote the affirmed (positive) and negated
138
      (negative) case as X − , and their LLM vector representations are given as x+ , x− (see section 4
      for how we negate sentences and for how vector representations are extracted). Thus, the dataset
139
      used to train probes consist of pairs of hidden states extracted for the positive and negative variants
140
      of statements (x+ , x− , y + , y − ) ∈ D, and their labels indicating which of the two is true (with
141   y + = 1 − y − ). When we refer to X or x without polarity, the polarity could be positive or negative.
142
143   When using belief probes, we assume that the truth of sentences is latently modelled by LLMs.
144
      We characterize this latent model as a probability distribution Pλ (X).1 The belief probes p(x) are
      assumed to (approximately) recover this distribution. We use Pλ (x) as a shorthand for Pλ (X = 1).
145
146   We consider only belief probes of the form: p(x) = σ(x · θ), where θ is the belief direction.
147

187   3.2   P ROBING FOR TRUTH - VALUE JUDGMENT
188
189   Truth-value judgment (TVJ) tasks are used in language acquisition research to assess children’s lin-
190   guistic competencies. Subjects are “asked to make a bipolar judgment about whether a statement
191   accurately describes a particular situation alluded to in some context or preamble” (Gordon, 1996).
192
      TVJ tasks assume the subject has “some conception of the notion of truth in the sense of a correspon-
      dence between what is said and the situation referred to” (Gordon, 1996). Using this assumption, the
193
      subjects are then asked questions to probe their understanding of various grammatical constructions.
194
195   We use TVJ tasks to explore if LLMs have similar notions of truth, specifically, if LLMs represent
196   the truth of sentences in a way that is sensitive to context. The task could be posed the same way it
197   would be posed to a child, asking questions and making inferences about the LLM’s competencies
198
      based on its answers. However, by using belief probes, we can infer its “answer” directly from the
      way it represents the input and learn how it changes throughout its layers. To do this, we have a
199
      setup as displayed in Figure 1, where the context or preamble consists of a premise Q and the a
200
      hypothesis H, which serves the role of the ‘question’. In this setup we probe the LLM to see if it
201   represents H as true or as false (instead of asking a question). The bracketed parts in Q and H are
202   omitted or included to produce affirmed and negated variants of the sentences (Q+ , Q− , H + , H − ).
203
204
      The setup is similar to a natural language inference task. However, we do not directly evaluate a
      model on its ability to classify sentence pairs by their meaning relation: R ∈ {e, c, n} (entailment,
205
      contradiction or neutral). Instead, we measure if the model’s truth-value judgments (as measured by
206
      the belief probes) are consistent with it being able to differentiate between the meaning relations.
207
208   In the introduction, we mentioned different ways in which beliefs can interact with the context of a
209   statement. We define three kinds of beliefs in the following way:
210   • prior beliefs, independent of the context, given by Pλ (H);
211   • conditional beliefs, specifically where the context is assumed to be truthful, given by Pλ (H|q);
212
      • marginal beliefs, where the truth of theP

      3.3   E VALUATION
235
236
      To evaluate LLMs on their ability to do truth-value judgment, we include a number of error scores,
237
      each indicating the extent to which probe outputs indicate a violation of some desirable behaviour.
238   Table 1 shows the error scores and the (in)equalities on which they are based.
239
240   We first define the premise effect (PE ) as the difference in probability assigned to the hypothesis
241
      when preceded with an affirmed premise and probability assigned to the hypothesis on its own:
      PE = p(h; q + ) − p(h). We call the mean absolute premise effect that a method obtains when
242
      evaluated its premise sensitivity. This metric can help us differentiate between prior beliefs on the
243
      one hand, and conditional or marginal beliefs on the other.
244
245   The effect of adding the in-context premise can differ in magnitude, depending on which belief
246   probing method we use. In order to make the error scores of different methods comparable to each
247
      other, we express the magnitude of the errors in multiples of the premise effect PE . This makes the
      error scores independent of the overall premise sensitivity of the belief probing method.
248
249   The first two error scores, E1 and E2 (see Table 1) are based on the fact that we expect the probabili-
250   ties to only depend on factors actually capable of influencing the truth value of the hypothesis. Thus,
251   these error scores are proportional to the absolute change in probability that occurs after having the
252   hypothesis preceded by either: 1) a corrupted premise q̃, or 2) an unrelated premise q ′ . The truth
253
      value of both corrupted and unrelated premises are independent of the truth value of the hypothesis,
      which is why we should expect the equalities for E1 and E2 in Table 1 to hold.
254
255   E3 and E4 measure when probes fail to behave like conditional and marginal beliefs, respectively.
256
      For E3, we assume the model treats the context as truthful, and thus should consider the premise
257   false when negated (and true when affirmed). If the premise is false, the original meaning relation
258   either switches (between entailment and contradiction), or becomes neutral. When the relationship
259   switches the premise effect should be opposite as well (from increasing the probability, to decreas-
260   ing, and vice versa). But, if negation creates a neutral relationship, then the probability should be the
261   same as when there is no premise. Together, this gives us the inclusive inequalities in the left column
                                                                                        −
262   of Table 1. For the error score, we have: (p(h; q − ) − p(h)) · PE −1 = p(h;q        )−p(h)
                                                                                   p(h;q + )−p(h) . By taking the
263   max{·, 0} of this fraction, we can isolate those cases where the numerator and denominator have
264   the same sign, which are the errors we want to capture in the score.
265
266
      For E4, if the language model bases itself on its own evaluation of the premise, then it should ignore
      whether the premise is affirmed or negated. In that case, the probability assigned to the hypothesis
267
      should be equal regardless of the polarity of the premise assertion.
268

      impossible to have a score of zero for both simultaneously. See Appendix A for additional details.

      4    E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      To answer our research questions, we make use of datasets with samples of related sentences, whose
273   truth values depend on each other. We use samples from these datasets by creating prompts where
274   the sentences are either affirmed or negated.
275
276   T RAINING & EVALUATING PROBES
277
278   We train probes in two settings: no-prem and pos-prem. For no-prem, the premise Q is
279   left out, and for pos-prem the premise appears in the positive (or affirmed) variant. We include
280   these settings, because they allow us to understand more about how beliefs are represented. A
281   belief direction found in the no-prem setting, that separates true from false (on held out data
282
      from the same distribution), is a direction that represents prior belief. If that direction also shows
      context-sensitivity (when evaluated with premises in-context), that would be evidence that the model
283
      does not represent the prior and contextual beliefs in orthogonal directions. For pos-prem, the
284
      direction(s) that separate true from false in the training distribution can also be influenced by what
285   appears in context. If the directions found for pos-prem and no-prem are different, it suggests
286   there are separate (but possibly related) directions used to represent context-sensitive truth.
287
288
      The probe inputs h are the mean-normalized representations of the answer tokens (‘cor-
      rect’/‘incorrect’) of the sample, extracted for each layer. To make the results from different probing
289
      methods comparable, we calibrate the probes such that their predictions for the p(h) case have the
290
      same variance. We train probes on the following LLMs: Llama2-7b, Llama2-13b (Touvron et al.,
291   2023), and OLMo-7b with and without instruction tuning (Groeneveld et al., 2024).
292
293   To measure the premise effect, and error scores described in subsection 3.3, we include the following
294
      evaluation cases: p(h), p(h; q + ), p(h; q − ), p(h; q ′ ), p(h; q̃). We evaluate both the no-prem and
      pos-prem in all of these cases. The first two cases are ‘in distribution’ for the no-prem and
295
      pos-prem settings, respectively. The other combinations are out of distribution. When evaluating
296
      the probes we use: p(h) = 21 (1 − p(h− ) + p(h+ )).

      DATA
299
300   We use two existing datasets in our experiments. The first dataset (SNLI, Bowman et al., 2015)
301   contains statements that describe images (to which an LLM has no access). The second dataset
302   (EntailmentBank, Dalvi et al., 2021) contains hypotheses that are sentences with general world
303   knowledge. These are facts the LLM may have encountered during training and for which it could
304   already have a strong prior belief.
305   For both datasets, the polarity of the premises and hypothesis is determined by the inclusion or omis-
306   sion of the ‘in’ that appears in square brackets. This style of negation sidesteps potential problems
307   with choosing how to negate a sentence, which can sometimes be difficult.4 The corrupted sentences
308   are created by replacing the characters in each word of the base sentence with random characters.
309
310   EntailmentBank This dataset is similar in structure to SNLI, consisting of premises and hypothe-
311   ses, but it contains only entailments. The subject of the statements are also different since Entail-
312   mentBank was derived from ARC (Clark et al., 2018), which consists of grade-school level science
313   questions. We combine the premises of EntailmentBank with the questions and answers from ARC
314
      on which they were based. In order to create contradictions we combine the premises of Entailment-
      Bank with an incorrectly answered question. For example:

344   This makes for an interesting scenario when it comes to belief probing. A belief probing method
345   might identify a direction that only encapsulates a statistical pattern, rather than the model’s belief
346   direction. Although, it is also possible that the statistical pattern is represented the same way as
347   other reasons to believe a sentence, in the model’s belief direction. After the addition of a premise,
348
      we do not expect a representation should move (coherently) in a direction which merely encodes
      a statistical pattern. Thus, if a probe trained only on hypotheses does respond coherently to the
349
      presence of a premise at test time, it suggests that we have found a belief direction.

362   the hypothesis is entailed, the probabilities are higher, when the hypothesis is contradicted they are
363

475   5     C ONCLUSION
476
477   We have investigated LLM truth-value judgment, which requires correctly incorporating context
478   when determining the truth value of a sentence. Based on our expectations of how the probability of
479   a sentence should or should not change in a supporting, contradicting, or neutral context, we created
480   four error scores. In our experiments, we used several probing methods on four language models,
481   and quantified how they assign probabilities to hypotheses in different contexts.
482   From our results it is clear that LLMs do incorporate context when representing sentences as more
483   or less (likely to be) true. However, we also observe that contexts which should have no bearing on
484   truth values still have a sizeable impact on a sentence’s position along the belief direction revealed
485   by the probes. Our intervention experiment shows that the positioning of premises along belief
      directions (partially) determines the positioning of related hypotheses along the same direction.


                                                            9
486
      We believe that our work is a first step to better understanding and addressing inconsistencies in
487   LLM generated text. Fully understanding the in-context behaviour of belief-probing methods will
488   help to ascertain exactly why inconsistent generations arise, for example whether: the model has
489   represented part of the context as false; the model fails to accurately represent the meaning relation
490   between the context and possible generations; or both. Finally, the causal connection between truth
491   values of related sentences might be part of a mechanism that, when fully uncovered, could explain
492   how LLMs do well on reasoning tasks.
493
494   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
495
      In our experiments we have investigated one direction at a time. Recently, Bürger et al. (2024) have
496
      shown that beliefs in LLMs use a two-dimensional subspace: one direction consistently points from
497
      true to false, and another is polarity-sensitive and points from false to true for negated statements.
498   It is possible that marginal and conditional beliefs also occupy independent directions, but finding
499   them requires data where the ‘being entailed / contradicted by context’ and ‘being true / false’
500   features can be varied completely independently. We leave this for future work.
501
      We would also like to dive deeper into the representations of meaning-relations in LLMs, and the
502
      exact mechanisms responsible for incorporating that information into the belief directions. For
503
      example, by investigating the construction of probes that reveal if a model represents two sentences
504   as having a particular meaning relation. Then, we can detect when the model disagrees with the gold
505   standard meaning relation provided by the dataset. With probabilities for all three relevant variables:
506   H, Q, R, an even more precise evaluation would become possible.
507
      In our experiments we have only investigated models with 7 or 13 billion parameters. To fully
508
      investigate the interaction of our error scores with model size, additional experiments are needed.
