010
011
                          Mathematical reasoning presents a significant challenge for Large Language Mod-
012                       els (LLMs) due to the extensive and precise chain of reasoning required for accuracy.
013                       Ensuring the correctness of each reasoning step is critical. To address this, we
014                       aim to enhance the robustness and factuality of LLMs by learning from human
015                       feedback. However, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has shown limited
016                       benefits for long-chain mathematical reasoning, as models employing DPO strug-
017                       gle to identify detailed errors in incorrect answers. This limitation stems from
018                       a lack of fine-grained process supervision. We propose a simple, effective, and
019                       data-efficient method called Step-DPO, which treats individual reasoning steps as
020
                          units for preference optimization rather than evaluating answers holistically. Addi-
                          tionally, we have developed a data construction pipeline for Step-DPO, enabling
021
                          the creation of a high-quality dataset containing 10K step-wise preference pairs.
022
                          We also observe that in DPO, the data generated by the policy model is more effec-
023                       tive than that produced by humans or GPT-4, due to the former’s in-distribution
024                       nature. Our findings demonstrate that as few as 10K preference data pairs and
025                       fewer than 500 Step-DPO training steps can yield a nearly 3% gain in accuracy on
026                       MATH for models with over 70B parameters. Notably, Step-DPO, when applied
027                       to Qwen2-72B-Instruct, achieves scores of 70.8% and 94.0% on the test sets of
028                       MATH and GSM8K, respectively, surpassing a series of closed-source models,
029                       including GPT-4-1106, Claude-3-Opus, and Gemini-1.5-Pro.

      Mathematical reasoning is recognized as a critical long-chain reasoning ability in Large Language
076
      Models (LLMs). This task is particularly challenging due to the often extensive chain of thought
077
      required, which can include numerous reasoning steps. Any error in these steps can lead to an
078   incorrect final answer.
079
080   Numerous studies (Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Liu & Yao, 2024; Lu et al.,
081
      2024; Li et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) have
      proposed various data augmentation techniques during the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage to
082
      enhance alignment. However, models in the SFT process are prone to hallucinations, resulting in
083
      saturated performance. A potential reason for this, as highlighted in Hong et al. (2024), is that as
084   the probability of preferred outputs increases, so does the probability of undesirable ones. This
085   phenomenon makes the model more likely to make errors in long-chain reasoning. Therefore, it is
086   essential to develop methods to suppress the likelihood of undesirable outputs.
087
      Recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) has been proposed for
088
      alignment using pair-wise preference data and is popular due to its simplicity. Despite its effectiveness
089
      in chat benchmarks (Tunstall et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024), DPO offers minimal benefits for long-
090   chain mathematical reasoning. As shown in Fig. 2 (left), models using vanilla DPO perform poorly
091   in distinguishing between preferred and undesirable outputs, failing to identify errors in rejected
092   answers. Additionally, Fig. 2 (right) shows that the reward margin (i.e., the gap between the rewards
093   of preferred and undesirable outputs) is limited for models using vanilla DPO and plateaus with
094   further training. These findings indicate that models fine-tuned with vanilla DPO cannot pinpoint
095   detailed errors in incorrect answers, hindering the improvement of reasoning abilities.
096   In this work, we introduce Step-DPO, where each intermediate reasoning step is treated as the basic
097   unit for preference optimization. As illustrated in Fig. 3, unlike vanilla DPO, which only considers
098   preference optimization between complete answers (i.e., p(ywin |x) and p(ylose |x)), Step-DPO
099   examines the step-by-step answer (i.e., y = [s1 , ..., sn ]) and specifically targets the first erroneous
100   reasoning step. Step-DPO aims to select a correct reasoning step and reject an incorrect one, given a
101   math problem and several initial correct reasoning steps (i.e., maximize p(swin |x; s1 , s2 , ..., sk−1 )
102   and minimize p(slose |x; s1 , s2 , ..., sk−1 )). This transition allows the model to easily locate erroneous
103   tokens for effective optimization, significantly enhancing long-chain reasoning.
104   Moreover, we present an effective and economical pipeline to collect pair-wise preference data,
105   resulting in a high-quality dataset for Step-DPO. This dataset contains approximately 10K samples,
106   each consisting of: 1) a mathematical problem, 2) prior reasoning steps, 3) the chosen step, and 4)
107   the rejected step. Our three-step pipeline for dataset construction includes: 1) Error collection, 2)
      Step localization, and 3) Rectification. Notably, the chosen reasoning step is generated by the policy

129   2.1       M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
130
131
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited substantial reasoning capabilities, primarily due
      to their auto-regressive nature, which allows them to predict the next token based on contextual
132
      information. However, these models still struggle with long-chain reasoning tasks, particularly in
133
      mathematical contexts. Several prior studies (Yao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Yoran et al., 2023;
134   Li et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022) have attempted to enhance the
135   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) inference framework (Wei et al., 2022) to address this issue. While these
136   efforts have led to significant improvements in certain tasks, they have not fully mitigated common
137   hallucinations and have limited generalizability across all reasoning tasks.
138
      Another research direction (Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Liu & Yao, 2024; Lu
139
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Liu
140   et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Mitra et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023)
141   focuses on various data augmentation techniques, such as rephrasing, extension, and evolution, for
142   supervised fine-tuning (SFT). These methods have significantly enhanced the reasoning abilities of
143   LLMs, but their performance plateaus once the data reaches a certain volume. Additionally, methods
144   like those proposed by Wang et al. (2023a); Liao et al. (2024); Toshniwal et al. (2024); Gou et al.
145   (2023) employ external tools, such as Python, to substantially reduce calculation errors.
146   Other approaches (Azerbayev et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2024; Wang
147   et al., 2023c) involve continued pre-training on extensive, high-quality math-related datasets, which
148   markedly improve mathematical reasoning capabilities. Recent studies (Xu et al., 2024; Ying et al.,
149   2024) have explored reinforcement learning to mitigate hallucinations in mathematical reasoning.
150   Works like Lightman et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023b); Jiao et al. (2024); Hwang
151   et al. (2024) emphasize the importance of step-by-step verification in reinforcement learning for
152   mathematical problems. However, these methods still rely on the quality of the reward model and
153   require the complex training pipelines of RLHF. Building on this line of research, we propose
154
      Step-DPO, a simpler, more effective, and more efficient method.
155
156   2.2       R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
157
      Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) can align models with human preferences. However, as the probability
158
      of preferred outputs increases, so does the likelihood of undesirable ones, leading to hallucinations. To
159   generate more reliable outputs, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano
160   et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) has been introduced for LLM alignment. This approach involves
161

      training a reward model with comparison data and then using this reward model to optimize the policy
163   model. The final performance heavily depends on the quality of the reward model, and the training
164   pipeline is quite complex.
165
166
      To simplify this process, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) was proposed,
      which directly uses pair-wise preference data for model optimization. This transition significantly
167
      streamlines the training pipeline. While DPO has proven effective in chat benchmarks, it offers
168
      only marginal benefits for mathematical reasoning. Inheriting the principles of DPO, Step-DPO is
169   specifically designed for long-chain reasoning and has shown significant performance improvements
170   in solving math word problems.

173   3     S TEP -DPO
174
175   In this section, we elaborate on the proposed Step-DPO. First, we present step-wise formulation
176   in Sec. 3.1, a novel approach designed to enhance long-chain reasoning abilities by building on
177
      DPO. Next, in Sec. 3.2, we illustrate a pipeline for constructing the step-wise preference dataset for
      Step-DPO. Both components are essential for achieving the desired performance improvements.

179
180   3.1   S TEP - WISE F ORMULATION
181
      Preliminary. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) is
182
      an effective approach for enhancing the robustness, factuality, and safety of LLMs (Ouyang et al.,
183   2022). RLHF consists of two training phases: 1) reward model training, and 2) policy model training.
184   However, the final performance of RLHF is highly sensitive to various hyperparameters in both
185   phases, necessitating meticulous tuning.
186
      To avoid this complex training pipeline, Rafailov et al. (2024) proposed Direct Preference Opti-
187
      mization (DPO), which directly uses pair-wise preference data to optimize the policy model with an
188
      equivalent optimization objective. Specifically, given an input prompt x, and a preference data pair
189   (ywin , ylose ), DPO aims to maximize the probability of the preferred output ywin and minimize that
190   of the undesirable output ylose . The optimization objective is formulated as:
191
192                                                            πθ (ywin |x)           πθ (ylose |x)
              LDP O (θ) = −E(x,ywin ,ylose )∼D [log σ(β log                  − β log                 )],     (1)
193                                                           πref (ywin |x)         πref (ylose |x)
194
      where D is the pair-wise preference dataset, σ is the sigmoid function, πθ (·|x) is the policy model to be
195
      optimized, πref (·|x) is the reference model kept unchanged during training, and the hyperparameter
196
      β controls the distance from the reference model.
197
198
      Our Solution. While DPO has proven effective in chat benchmarks, it brings only marginal
199   improvements for long-chain reasoning tasks such as mathematical problems, as shown in Fig. 2
200   and Table 3. This limitation arises because most undesirable answers in these tasks may not contain
201   errors initially; the first error often appears midway through the reasoning process. Rejecting an
202   entire undesirable answer in DPO may also discard preceding correct reasoning steps, introducing
203   significant noise and negatively impacting training.
204
      Analogous to how teachers correct students by pinpointing specific errors rather than dismissing
205   entire answers, our proposed Step-DPO provides more detailed supervision by identifying the specific
206   erroneous reasoning step. This granular focus allows the model to swiftly locate, rectify, and further
207   avoid erroneous steps.
208

211   the correct next reasoning step swin and minimize the probability of the incorrect one slose . This
212   objective can be formulated as:

256   3.2      I N - DISTRIBUTION T RAINING DATA C ONSTRUCTION
257
258
      According to the optimization target of Step-DPO, we need to create a corresponding high-quality
      pair-wise preference dataset. Each data sample should comprise four entries: 1) prompt x; 2) initial
259
      reasoning steps s1∼k−1 = [s1 , . . . , sk−1 ]; 3) preferred reasoning step swin ; 4) undesirable reasoning
260
      step slose , as shown in Fig. 5. To obtain a high-quality dataset, we propose a data construction
261   pipeline illustrated in Fig. 4, which includes the following three steps.
262
263
      Error collection. First, we collect a set D0 = {(x, ŷ)} of mathematical problems x with ground-
264   truth answers ŷ. Each mathematical problem x is then used as a prompt to infer answers using
265   the initial model πref . Before inference, we add the step-wise Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prefix for
266   prompting, i.e., "Let’s think step by step. Step 1:". This ensures that the model’s
267   inference results are structured into multiple reasoning steps, with each step explicitly starting with
268   "Step i:". Upon completion of inference, we obtain the model answers y for each mathematical
269   problem x. We then select instances where the final answer y differs from the ground truth ŷ, resulting
      in a dataset of erroneous inference results, denoted as D1 = {(x, ŷ, y)|x ∈ D0 }.


                                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Step localization. Given that each erroneous inference result is explicitly presented as a sequence
271   of reasoning steps y = [s1 , s2 , . . . , sn ], we proceed to verify the correctness of each reasoning step
272   until we find the first error and record its step number k. This process can be done manually or using
273   GPT-4. We select sk as the erroneous reasoning step slose , resulting in a dataset that contains the
274   erroneous steps, denoted as D2 = {(x, ŷ, s1∼k−1 , slose )|x ∈ D1 }.
275
276   Rectification. To obtain the corresponding correct reasoning step for each sample in D2 , we need
277   to sample multiple outputs ycont by inferring the model πref with the prompt x and the preceding
278   correct reasoning steps s1∼k−1 . This process is formulated as:
279
                                            ycont ∼ πref (y|x; s1∼k−1 ).                                      (3)
280
281   We retain those outputs where the final answer matches the ground truth. Among the remaining
282   outputs, we select the first reasoning step in ycont as swin , resulting in the final dataset D =
283   {(x, s1∼k−1 , slose , swin )|x ∈ D2 }. An example of a resulting data sample is shown in Fig. 5.
284   Notably, some cases may have correct final answers but erroneous intermediate reasoning steps.
285   Therefore, we may need to further filter out samples where swin is incorrect, which can be done
286   manually or by GPT-4. We omit this process in the notations for simplicity, and more details are
287   provided in Appendix A.1.
288
      It is important to note that the data pipeline is user-friendly. In this data pipeline, humans or GPT-4
289
      are only required to locate errors and verify the correctness of reasoning steps, and they do not need
290   to write answers or rectifications by themselves.
291
292   We also note that the use of in-distribution data is crucial. When selecting swin , we use outputs
293
      generated by the model πref rather than answers rectified by humans or GPT-4. Since human or GPT-
294
      4 rectified answers sood
                            win are out-of-distribution (OOD) regarding the model πref , the log-probability
295   of outputting sood
                      win  (i.e., log πref (sood

                        id

298
      self-generated in-distribution data as the preferred answer proves to be a more effective way of
      aligning with human preferences.

529   5         C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we proposed a simple, effective, and data-efficient method called Step-DPO. Unlike DPO,
532   which compares preferences between holistic answers, Step-DPO uses a single reasoning step as the
533   fundamental unit for preference comparison. This transition enables fine-grained process supervision
534   for LLMs, facilitating the quick localization of errors within incorrect answers. Additionally, we
535
      introduced a data construction pipeline for Step-DPO, creating a dataset with 10K preference data
      pairs. Our results demonstrate the significant improvements achieved by Step-DPO and the 10K
536
      dataset, particularly for large models. We hope that Step-DPO will provide new insights into model
537
      alignment for long-chain reasoning problems.
