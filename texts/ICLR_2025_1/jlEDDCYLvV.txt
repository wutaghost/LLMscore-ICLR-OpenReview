009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Developing intelligent agents that can effectively coordinate with diverse human
012            partners is a fundamental goal of artificial general intelligence. Previous approaches
013            typically generate a variety of partners to cover human policies, and then either
014            train a single universal agent or maintain multiple best-response (BR) policies
015            for different partners. However, the first direction struggles with the stochastic
016
               and multimodal nature of human behaviors, and the second relies on costly few-
               shot adaptations during policy deployment, which is unbearable in real-world
017
               applications such as healthcare and autonomous driving. Recognizing that human
018
               partners can easily articulate their preferences or behavioral styles through natural
019            languages and make conventions beforehand, we propose a framework for Human-
020            AI Coordination via Policy Generation from Language-guided Diffusion, referred to
021            as Haland. Haland first trains BR policies for various partners using reinforcement
022            learning, and then compresses policy parameters into a single latent diffusion
023            model, conditioned on task-relevant language derived from their behaviors. Finally,
024            the alignment between task-relevant and natural languages is achieved to facilitate
025            efficient human-AI coordination. Empirical evaluations across diverse cooperative
026            environments demonstrate that Haland generates agents with significantly enhanced
027
               zero-shot coordination performance, utilizing only natural language instructions
               from various partners, and outperforms existing methods by approximately 89.64%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   One of the primary objectives of artificial general intelligence is to develop intelligent agents capable
033   of effectively coordinating with humans to achieve shared goals, known as human-AI coordina-
034   tion (Carroll et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b;a). This holds significant potential in
035   applications such as industrial assembly system (Nourmohammadi et al., 2022), healthcare (Gleichauf
036   et al., 2022), video games (Siu et al., 2021), etc. Despite the impressive progess made by cooperative
037   multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) in enabling agents to collaborate towards common goals
038   across various domains (Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023), some researches apply MARL to promote
039
      human-AI coordination, but it is challenging for MARL agents to effectively coordinate with human
      partners (Mirsky et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023b). The difficulty arises because agents trained by
040
      traditional MARL find it challenging to understand the intentions and preferences of different human
041
      collaborators and fail to adapt their behaviors accordingly (Ji et al., 2023).
042
043   A canonical approach to developing the cooperative agent, often referred to as the ego agent, entails
044   mimicking human behavior using real human data via behavioral cloning (BC) and training the best
045
      response (BR) to the fixed BC policy through reinforcement learning (RL) (Hu et al., 2022; Lou
      et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024). However, this method necessitates the laborious and costly task of
046
      collecting extensive human data. Alternatively, some approaches train the ego agent without relying
047
      on human data by creating diverse partner agents beforehand, with the expectation that they can
048   cover diverse human policies. These approaches can be broadly classified into two main directions
049   for downstream deployment. One direction aims to train a single universal ego agent capable of
050   effectively cooperating with various human players. Among them, self-play (SP) and other-play (OP)
051   methods train the ego agent by repeatedly playing against a single partner, but they may become
052   entrenched in the specific cooperative pattern (Silver et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2020). Furthermore,
053   population-based training (PBT) methods (Long et al., 2023) first generate a diverse pool of partners
      by maximizing the divergence of trajectory distribution (Lupu et al., 2021), population (Zhao et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2023), or minimizing cross-play rewards (Charakorn et al., 2022), etc. Subsequently, they train a
055   common best response ego agent to adapt to different partners. In contrast to learning an universal ego
056   agent, the other direction trains a group of ego agents or augments the ego agent policy with auxiliary
057   partner identifier, and selects the appropriate one through techniques such as few-shot adaptation
058   when faced with unknown human partners. For instance, Maze coevolves two populations of ego
059   agents and partners and selects the most suitable policy during testing (Xue et al., 2022a), while
060   Macop develops high-compatibility cooperative training paradigms by continuously expanding policy
061   heads (Yuan et al., 2023a), showing stronger coordination ability in complex scenarios.
062   However, existing methods in these two directions have certain limitations. Firstly, developing an
063   universal policy requires meticulous design to ensure efficient training and suffers from stochastic and
064   multimodal human behaviors due to limited model capacity (Wang et al., 2024b). Secondly, the few-
065   shot adaptation process for partner identification or policy selection typically requires to run multiple
066   episodes beforehand, which can be costly and even unachievable in real-world scenarios, such as
067   medical application (Coronato et al., 2020) and automatic driving (Yan et al., 2022). These limitations
068   hinders the development of efficient human-AI coordination. Besides, neither direction showcases
069
      explainability towards human preference explicitly. Note that humans often reach conventions (Shih
      et al., 2021; Guan et al., 2023) before coordination by expressing their own behavior styles or
070
      preferences with language. Therefore, a natural question arises: Can we achieve efficient human-AI
071
      coordination with language instructions only?
072
073   To tackle the above issues, we propose Haland, an efficient human-AI coordination framework via
074   policy generation from language-guided diffusion. Concretely, given a set of diverse partners, we first
075
      train corresponding BR policies to each partner via reinforcement learning. Inspired by the powerful
      expressiveness and generation capability of Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022),
076
      we compress the parameters of these BR policies into a single generative model conditioning on task-
077
      relevant language derived from their behaviors, so as to deal with stochastic and multimodal human
078   behavior. Afterwards, we achieve alignment between the task-relevant and natural languages by
079   introducing a tailored language translator. During deployment, a human partner provides the language
080   instruction with respect to preference and it will be translated into corresponding task-relevant
081   language, Haland then generates the ego agent policy that can effectively coordinate with the human
082   partner through the conditional denoising process. We demonstrate Haland’s superior collaborative
083   capabilities across various human-AI coordination environments, including both single-task and
084   multi-task settings with diverse partners.

087   2   R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Human-AI Coordination endeavors to empower AI systems with the capabilities of effectively
090   coordinating with diverse human partners (Dafoe et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b).
091   One direction is to model human behaviors from real human data via behavioral cloning (BC). How-
092   ever, high-quality human data is costly to collect in real-world scenarios beforehand. Alternatively,
093
      existing works on human-AI coordination without human data can be broadly categorized into two
      main directions. They both create diverse partners in the hope that human policies during testing
094
      can be covered. The first direction is to train a single universal ego agent to coordinate with diverse
095
      partners. Among the plethora of methods, self-play (SP) approaches (Tesauro, 1994; Silver et al.,
096   2017) involve training ego agents by coordinating against themselves, while other-play (Hu et al.,
097   2020) introduces diversity into coordination patterns to disrupt the symmetry of self-play policies.
098   Population-based training methods have emerged as prevalent approaches to enhance policy diversity.
099   For instance, FCP (Strouse et al., 2021) introduces diversity by employing different random seeds
100   and checkpoints at various training stages. MEP (Zhao et al., 2023) and TrajeDi (Lupu et al., 2021)
101   optimize population-level entropy objectives alongside coordination returns to achieve a diverse
102   population. The other direction trains a group of ego agents or augments one ego agent with auxiliary
103   partner identifier. For instance, Maze coevolves two populations of ego agents and partners through
104
      evolution (Xue et al., 2022a). However, these methods require the human partner to coordinate with
      probing policies for a few episodes, to select the proper ego agent or attain the correct identifier.
105
106   Language-guided Reinforcement Learning involves training agents to perform tasks based on
107   Natural Language (NL) instructions (Luketina et al., 2019). Previous methods focus on training
      instruction-following agents by exposing NL instructions to RL policies directly. For instance, litera-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ture (Hill et al., 2020) encodes NL instructions using a pre-trained language model and incorporates
109   the NL embedding into the policy. Literature (Chaplot et al., 2018) combines human instructions with
110   agent observations using a multiplication-based mechanism and pre-trains the instruction-following
111   policy through behavior cloning (Pomerleau, 1991). Alternatively, literature (Akakzia et al., 2021) en-
112   codes NL instructions into a manually-designed binary vector where each element represents specific
113   semantics. The concept of instruction-following policies also has connections with Hierarchical RL
114   (Barto & Mahadevan, 2003), where NL instructions naturally serve as task abstractions for low-level
115   policies (Blukis et al., 2021). HAL (Jiang et al., 2019) leverages the compositional structure of NL
116   to make decisions directly at the NL level for solving long-term, complex RL tasks. Furthermore,
117
      TALAR (Pang et al., 2023) introduces task-related task languages as a unique representation of NL
      instructions that is easily interpretable by the policy. Instead of directly exposing NL instructions to
118
      policies, Haland reconstructs cooperative policies through guided diffusion generation with translated
119
      NL instructions, more related work are discussed in App. A.

      Two-player Cooperative Markov Game Most human-AI coordination problems can be mod-
      eled as a two-player cooperative Markov Game (Littman, 1994), which is described by a tuple
125
      ⟨S, A1 , A2 , T , R⟩. S is the set of states, A1 and A2 are the action spaces of the two agents, respec-
126
      tively, which can be different in a heterogeneous setting. T : S ×A1 ×A2 ×S → [0, 1] is the transition
127   function, and the joint action of two agents result in a shared reward given by R : S × A1 × A2 → R.
128   At each time step, agents receive the state st and output actions a1t ∈ A1 , a2t ∈ A2 . The joint action
129   leads to the next state st+1 ∼ T (·|st , a1t , a2t ) and a global reward R(st , a1t , a2t ).

      4.3   L ANGUAGE A LIGNMENT FOR ROBUST G ENERATION
247
248   Although language-guided diffusion enables the generation of cooperative policies aligned with
249   partner preferences, the variability and redundancy inherent in natural language present several
250   challenges for the training and deployment of diffusion model. First, natural language exhibits
251   variability and humans may have different linguistic conventions, which means a specific instruction
252   can be conveyed using different expressions. Consequently, if the generator is trained using only a
253   limited set of expressions, it may struggle to effectively generate cooperative policies when confronted
254   with unfamiliar expressions during real-world deployment. Second, natural language instructions
255
      often contain syntactic components irrelevant to specific tasks. These redundant syntactic components,
      combined with the variability of natural language, pose challenges for the diffusion model in aligning
256
      various natural language instructions with corresponding policy representations.
257
258   To address the challenges posed by the variability and redundancy of natural language, we develope a
259   suite of task-relevant language to accurately capture task-specific information that can also accurately
260   reflect the behavior styles of policies during training. Specifically, in our developed task language
261   (TL), each policy is associated with a unique TL embedding LjT which can be learnable or event-
262
      based embeddings, facilitating clear differentiation between different policies by the diffusion model.
      Subsequently, we construct a translator to map the variable natural language (NL) instructions
263

510   5.5       A BLATION S TUDY
511
512   Haland is composed of multiple components, and we conducted ablation studies in the Diverse
513   Coordination layout to investigate their impacts. First, to highlight the outstanding conditional
514   generation capability of the latent diffusion model M, we replaced it with a Conditional Adversarial
515   Generative Model (CGAN) (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) conditioning on the partner labels. We
516
      implemented two variations of CGAN: one using an MLP network and another utilizing the same
      UNet structure as the diffusion model, denoted as W/o Diff-MLP and W/o Diff-UNet, respectively.
517
      Next, to emphasize the importance of the task language and the translator for language alignment,
518
      we removed the translator and directly train the diffusion model conditioning on NL instructions,
519   denoted as W/o Translator. Finally, for policy compression and reconstruction, we derived W/o VAE
520   by removing the VAE f = (fE , fD ) and attempted to directly distill best response policies using a
521   diffusion model. As shown in Tab. 1, CGAN fails to model all best response policies effectively.
522   The replacement of MLP with UNet also fails to enhance the generation capability of the CGAN.
523   Furthermore, after removing the translator for language alignment, the diffusion model collapses
524   to generating only two best response policies due to the high similarity between NL instructions.
525   After removing the VAE in the latent diffusion model, directly modeling the distribution of policy
526   parameters is challenging for diffusion model as W/o VAE shows in the table.

531
      This paper tackles the challenge of zero-shot human-AI coordination during deployment, harnessing
      the intuitive nature of human expression through natural language instructions. We introduce a novel
532
      framework, named Human-AI Coordination via Policy Generation from Language-guided Diffusion
533
      (Haland), which compresses diverse best response policies into a single diffusion-based generator.
534   Empirical evaluations conducted across diverse cooperative environments validate the effectiveness
535   of Haland. Haland enhances policy deployment through language instruction, moving away from
536   few-shot adaptation. In the future, it could be extended to address policy shifts using techniques
537   like detection and adaptation in open machine learning settings (Zhou, 2022), where teammates may
538   experience policy changes within a single episode (Zhang et al., 2023). Additionally, combining this
539   approach with stronger language models such as T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) for real-world embodied
      tasks (Liu et al., 2024b) holds significant potential.

794   A    D ETAILED R ELATED W ORK
795
796    Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) (Albrecht et al., 2023) involves a team of
797   agents learning a joint policy to tackle tasks through interactions with the environment, optimizing
798   their policies through reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Compared to traditional
799   methods, MARL offers advantages in handling environmental uncertainty and learning to solve
800   unknown tasks without excessive domain knowledge. However, MARL introduces new challenges
801
      distinct from single-agent settings. On one hand, in environments where multiple agents coexist,
      observations are often partially observable, limiting individual agents’ access to global information
802
      and hindering optimal decision-making (Zhu et al., 2022). On the other hand, as multiple agents
803
      learn simultaneously, policies dynamically change, rendering the environment non-stationary from an
804   individual agent’s perspective, which may impede convergence (Papoudakis et al., 2019). Moreover,
805   in cooperative MARL scenarios where agents share common goals, the challenge of accurately
806   assigning rewards to facilitate efficient cooperation learning, known as credit assignment, becomes
807   crucial (Yuan et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2021a). Additionally, as the number of agents in a Multi-
808   Agent System (MAS) (Dorri et al., 2018) increases, the search space for solving RL problems
809   exponentially expands, posing scalability issues and making policy learning and search extremely
      challenging (Zhang, 2011; Christianos et al., 2021). In recent years, the fusion of deep learning


                                                      15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      and MARL has yielded significant advancements, with various algorithms proposed and applied to
811   address complex tasks. These include policy gradient-based methods like MADDPG (Lowe et al.,
812   2017) and MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022), value-based methods such as VDN (Sunehag et al., 2018)
813   and QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018), and approaches leveraging transformer architectures to enhance
814   coordination capabilities, like MAT (Wen et al., 2022). Meanwhile, MARL has garnered widespread
815   attention and showcased significant progress across various fields (Zhang et al., 2021), demonstrating
816   promising applications in path planning (Chung et al., 2024), autonomous driving (Zhang et al.,
817   2024b), active voltage control (Wang et al., 2021b), and dynamic algorithm configuration (Xue et al.,
818   2022b).
819
820
      Human-AI Coordination endeavors to empower AI systems with the capabilities needed for
      cooperation and to nurture collaboration (Dafoe et al., 2020; 2021). Considerable attention has
821
      been directed toward the development of AI systems or agents adept at effectively coordinating
822
      with diverse human collaborators in recent years. A prevalent approach involves techniques such as
823   modeling (Albrecht & Stone, 2018) to understand others’ intentions or behavior, or constructing an
824   effective behavior model over human data and planning with this model (Sheridan, 2016). However,
825   these methods often entail an expensive and time-consuming data-collection process. Building on
826   the achievements of cooperative MARL (Yuan et al., 2023b), numerous related approaches like
827   ad-hoc teamwork (AHT) (Mirsky et al., 2022), few-shot teamwork (FST) (Fosong et al., 2022), and
828   zero-shot coordination (ZSC) (Treutlein et al., 2021) have emerged. AHT tackles the challenge of
829   designing agents capable of coordinating with new teammates without prior coordination (Stone
830   et al., 2010). In FST settings (Fosong et al., 2022), agents trained within a team to accomplish one
831
      task are combined with agents from different tasks, requiring them to adapt collectively to an unseen
      but related task. In ZSC settings, ego agents are trained to interact with various partners during
832
      the training phase, enabling successful coordination with novel partners or human collaborators,
833
      garnering significant attention across different domains. Among the plethora of methods, self-play
834   (SP) approaches (Tesauro, 1994; Silver et al., 2017) involve training ego agents by competing
835   against themselves, while other-play (Hu et al., 2020) introduces diversity into coordination patterns
836   by training agents with another agent, disrupting the symmetry of self-play policies. Population-
837   based methods have emerged as prevalent approaches to enhance policy diversity. For instance,
838   FCP (Strouse et al., 2021) introduces diversity by employing different random seeds and checkpoints
839   at various training stages. MEP (Zhao et al., 2023) and TrajeDi (Lupu et al., 2021) optimize
840   population-level entropy objectives alongside coordination returns to achieve a diverse population.
841   These methods operate under the premise that exposing the ego agent to training partners with diverse
842
      skills, preferences, and behavioral styles enhances its robustness and enables collaboration with
      novel partners. However, they often yield agent populations with only low-level or policy-level
843
      diversity, overlooking the multimodal challenge associated with adapting a single ego agent to
844
      partners with diverse high-level behavioral styles, preferences, and skills. Alternatively, MAZE (Xue
845   et al., 2022a) maintains separate ego agent and partner populations and trains both simultaneously
846   through coevolution, while ensemble approaches are necessary to determine the optimal cooperative
847   action during deployment. Macop develops high-compatibility cooperative training paradigms by
848   continuously expanding policy heads (Yuan et al., 2023a). These methods represent a further step
849   toward effective coordination with diverse and multimodal teammates. Others focus on open-ended
850   coordination (Li et al., 2023b), biased human (Yu et al., 2023), open ad hoc teamwork (Wang et al.,
851   2024a), human-AI coordination evaluation (Wang et al., 2024b), combining with LLM (Li et al.,
852   2023a; Liu et al., 2024a), etc.
853
854   Language-guided Reinforcement Learning involves training agents to perform tasks based on
855
      Natural Language (NL) instructions (Luketina et al., 2019). Previous methods focus on training
      instruction-following agents by exposing NL instructions to RL policies directly. For instance, Litera-
856
      ture (Hill et al., 2020) encodes NL instructions using a pre-trained language model and incorporates
857
      the NL encoding into the policy. Literature (Chaplot et al., 2018) combines human instructions with
858   agent observations using a multiplication-based mechanism and pre-trains the instruction-following
859   policy through behavior cloning (Pomerleau, 1991). Alternatively, Literature (Akakzia et al., 2021)
860   encodes NL instructions into a manually-designed binary vector where each element represents spe-
861   cific semantics. The concept of instruction-following policies has connections with Hierarchical RL
862   (Barto & Mahadevan, 2003), where NL instructions naturally serve as task abstractions for low-level
863   policies (Blukis et al., 2021). HAL (Jiang et al., 2019) leverages the compositional structure of NL
      to make decisions directly at the NL level for solving long-term, complex RL tasks. Furthermore,


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      TALAR (Pang et al., 2023) introduces a task-related task language as a unique representation of NL
865   instructions that is easily interpretable by the policy. Instead of directly exposing NL instructions
866   to policies, Haland reconstructs cooperative policies aligned with the requirements specified in NL
867   instructions through language-guided diffusion. In the Human-AI setting, Literature (Hu & Sadigh,
868   2023) develops InstructQ and InstructPPO that enables humans to specify what kind of strategies
869   they expect from their AI partners through natural language instructions. Proagent (Zhang et al.,
870   2024a)harnesses large language models (LLMs) to create proactive agents capable of dynamically
871   adapting their behavior to enhance cooperation with teammates. SAMA (Li et al., 2023a) proposes a
872   novel “disentangled” decisionmaking method, Semantically Aligned task decomposition in MARL
873
      (SAMA), that prompts pre-trained language models with chain-of-thought that can suggest potential
      goal for efficient coordination. HAPLAN (Guan et al., 2023) ask humans to give their preferences to
874
      the LLM and review the proposed conventions, ensuring an effective human-AI coordination with a
875
      better alignment to human biases. One recent work HLA (Liu et al., 2024a) also employs LLM to
876   facilitate human-AI coordination. However, its main idea is to build an instruction-following agent
877   with LLM, requiring a continuous stream of natural language instructions from human. This places
878   the whole burden on human and can lead to inefficient coordination.

      B       OVERALL A RCHITECTURE OF HALAN
882
883
      Fig. 1 illustrates the overall architecture of our approach, Haland, which comprises three primary
      stages: data preparation, training, and deployment. During the data preparation phase, human data,
884
      including behavioral or preference data, along with corresponding NL descriptions or instructions, are
885
      collected. These collected human data are then utilized to construct human proxies through techniques
886   such as imitation learning behavioral cloning (BC). During the subsequent training phase, we begin
887   by training the best response (BR) policies using the constructed human proxies. Then, the modules
888   of Haland, which include the VAE for policy compression and reconstruction, the diffusion model
889   for policy generation, and the translator for language alignment, are trained. During the deployment
890   phase, NL instructions are converted into TL embeddings using the trained translator. Subsequently,
891   the latent diffusion model generates appropriate policy representations conditioning on these TL
892   embeddings. Not that during the training phase, zN is produced via the diffusion process, which
893   involves iteratively adding noise to the policy latent representation z. Whereas, in the deployment
894
      phase, zN is directly obtained through sampling from the Gaussian distribution.

954   Center Pots In the Center Pots layout, two cooking pots are located in the center of the kitchen,
955   surrounded by a ring-shaped passage. We obtain agent pairs with diverse coordination patterns by
956   limiting the working space of the partner agent. These constraints include working only on the
957   upper/lower side, only on the left/right side, or only in a specific corner, like the upper left side of the
958   kitchen. (1) When the partner agent works only on the left side, the best coordination strategy for the
959   ego agent is to work on the right side independently. (2) When the partner agent works only on the

962
      works only on the lower side, the roles of the ego agent and the partner agent switch, symmetric to

964   left side, focusing on tasks related to onions and only working with the left cooking pot, the best
965   coordination strategy for the ego agent is to focus on complementary tasks for the same cooking pot.

968   carefully adapt their behaviors to each other. We obtain agent pairs with diverse coordination patterns

970   agent works only with cooking pots on the upper/lower side and delivery locations on the left/right
971   side, the optimal coordination strategy for the ego agent is to work with cooking pots and delivery

994    C.3     BASELINES
995
996    We leverage Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) from Stable-Baselines3
997    (SB3) (Raffin et al., 2021) as the training algorithm for both ego agents and partner agents. In
998
       particular, we utilize recurrent value and policy networks 2 comprising Long Short-Term Memory
       (LSTM) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) units with a hidden size of 256 to enhance the adaptability
999
       of universal egos. Conversely, the policy networks for partner agents consist of simple Multi-Layer
1000
       Perceptrons (MLPs).
1001
1002
       Oracle Ego agents trained alongside the diverse partners are approximations of the best responses
1003   and serve as the Oracle policies specific to each partner.
1004
1005   General Ego This approach involves training a single ego agent with a diverse population of
1006   partners, expecting it to accommodate different partners based solely on observation.
1007
1008   Instruction-Following (Instructed) Ego This approach is similar to the General Ego, with the
1009   distinction that during training, the policy input comprises one-hot labels indicating different partners
1010   for collaboration. The Instruction-Following Ego’s policy integrates an instruction-embedding
1011   module, implemented as MLP, to process the partner labels.
1012
1013   Adaptive Ego This approach is similar to the Instruction-Following Ego, yet it differs in incorporat-
1014   ing the partners’ one-hot actions during collaboration, rather than partner labels, as part of the policy
1015   input. By taking the partners’ actions as input, Adaptive Ego implicitly performs teammate modeling.

1113   E             I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF HALAN
1114
1115   Haland involves a Variational AutoEncoder for policy compression, a Latent Diffusion Model for
1116   language-guided generation and a Translator for language alignment.
1117
1118   E.1               VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER
1119
1120   The Variational Autoencoder (VAE) comprises an encoder designed to compress policy parameters
1121   into low-dimensional representations, coupled with a conditional Graph Hypernetwork (GHN)
1122   responsible for estimating the policy parameters based on these representations. The encoder
1123
       incorporates M Conv1D blocks to process the weight matrices Wm and an MLP to process the
       bias vectors bm , where m = 1, · · · , M and M is the number of layers in the policy network.
1124
       Following this, a final fully connected layer processes the concatenated features and generates the
1125
       latent representations. The architecture of the encoder is derived from the structure proposed in
1126   literature (Hegde et al., 2023), while the GHN implementation is adapted from literature (Hegde &
1127   Sukhatme, 2023).
1128

       intermediate features and language embeddings. Further details regarding the Residual Block and

1313   H    H UMAN -AI E XPERIMENTS
1314
1315   To evaluate how effectively the Haland and baseline agents collaborate with real human players, we
1316   recruited eight participants for a human-AI collaboration study designed within a human-in-the-loop
1317   framework in the Diverse Orders layout. Participants began by reading the game instructions and
1318
       viewing gameplay demonstrations. They were then tasked with collaborating as effectively as possible
       with the AI partner to complete as many orders as they could. Following this, participants interacted
1319
       with the AI partners through a web-based interface using keyboard controls, as shown in Fig. 13.
1320
       Before each round, participants provided a natural language description to specify their behavioral
1321   preferences, which is used for the selection of Oracle policy and the policy generation of Haland.
1322
1323   After completing each game episode (400 timesteps), participants rated their satisfaction with the AI
1324
       partner on a five-point Likert-like scale, ranging from 1 (very dissatisfied) to 5 (very satisfied). Upon
       finishing all games involving human players and AI agents, both the collaborative performance and
1325
       the human ratings for the different agents were statistically analyzed. Fig. 7(b) shows the collaborative
1326
       performance of AI agents with real human players. The pairwise differences in average ratings were
1327   calculated as the human preference values, as shown in Fig. 7(c).
1328
1329   Additionally, human proxies exhibiting different behavioral styles were constructed through behavior
1330
       cloning from human play trajectories collected using the aforementioned human-AI interaction
       interface in the human-in-the-loop experiments. The collaborative performance with these human
1331
       proxies is presented in Figure 7(a). The primary distinction between real human players and human
1332
       proxies is that human players can actively adapt to the AI agents, while the behavior of human proxies
1333   is relatively fixed. This lack of adaptability leads to a decline in performance in the Proxy-AI results
1334   compared to the results obtained with real human players.
