011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We consider deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) in the context of rein-
014            forcement learning with sparse rewards. To enhance exploration, we introduce
015            a search procedure, ϵt-greedy, which generates exploratory options for exploring
016
               less-visited states. We prove that search using ϵt-greedy has polynomial sam-
               ple complexity under mild MDP assumptions. To more efficiently use the infor-
017
               mation provided by rewarded transitions, we develop a new dual experience re-
018
               play buffer framework, GDRB, and implement longest n-step returns. The result-
019            ing algorithm, ETGL-DDPG, integrates all three techniques: ϵt-greedy, GDRB,
020            and Longest n-step, into DDPG. We evaluate ETGL-DDPG on standard bench-
021            marks and demonstrate that it outperforms DDPG, as well as other state-of-the-art
022            methods, across all tested sparse-reward continuous environments. Ablation stud-
023            ies further highlight how each strategy individually enhances the performance of
024            DDPG in this setting.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al., 2015) is one of the representative al-
030   gorithms for reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018), alongside other prominent ap-
031   proaches (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Fujimoto et al., 2018; Andrychowicz et al., 2017). The method has
032   been extensively used for continuous control environments with dense reward signals (Duan et al.,
033
      2016). However, its performance degrades significantly when the reward signals are sparse and are
      only observed upon reaching the goal (Matheron et al., 2019).
034
035   In sparse-reward environments where success depends on reaching a goal state, DDPG’s deficiency
036   can be explained from three perspectives. The first one is its lack of directional exploration. Like
037   other off-policy RL algorithms, DDPG employs a behavior policy for exploring the environment.
038   The standard choices are either an ϵ-greedy behavior policy that samples a random action with prob-
039
      ability ϵ (e.g., 0.1), or the main policy with artificial noise. As argued in (Dabney et al., 2020), these
      one-step noise augmented greedy strategies are ineffective for exploring large sparse-reward state
040
      spaces due to the lack of temporal abstraction. To improve ϵ-greedy, Dabney et al. (2020) propose a
041
      temporally extended ϵz-greedy policy that expands exploration into multiple steps, controlled by a
042   distribution z. ϵz-greedy represents an advancement from the option framework for reinforcement
043   learning (Sutton et al., 1999). Theoretically, an option O is defined as a tuple O = ⟨I, π, β⟩, where I
044   is the set of states where an option can begin, π is the option policy that determines which actions to
045   take while executing the option, and β is the termination condition. In ϵz-greedy, each option repeats
046   a primitive action for a specific number of time steps which is sampled from a distribution z (e.g., a
047   uniform distribution). The option can begin at any state with probability ϵ and terminates whenever
048   their length reaches a limit that is decided by z. While ϵz-greedy improves over ϵ-greedy, it is also
049   directionless: for exploratory action, the agent does not use any information from its experience for
050
      more informed exploration.
051   The second drawback of DDPG is its uniform treatment of zero and non-zero rewards in the re-
052   play buffer. For most off-policy RL algorithms, a replay buffer is used to store and sample transi-
053   tions of the agent’s interactions with the environment. By default, DDPG uses a uniform sampling
      strategy that assigns an equal probability of being chosen to all transitions in the buffer. In sparse-

082
      its slow information propagation when updating its learning policy. Since only the last transition in
      a successful episode (i.e., goal reached) gets rewarded, in standard DDPG, the agent must achieve
083
      the goal many times to make sure that the reward is eventually propagated backward to early states.
084
      It is known that one way to achieve this is to provide intermediate rewards with reward shaping
085   methods (Laud, 2004). However, effective reward shaping is usually problem-specific and does not
086   generalize to a wide range of tasks.

088
      In this paper, we enhance DDPG (Lillicrap et al., 2015) to address all three aforementioned prob-
      lems. Our first contribution is ϵt-greedy, a new temporally version of ϵ-greedy that utilizes a light-
089
      weight search procedure, similar to Laud (2004), to enable more directional exploration based on the
090
      agent’s previous experience data. We show that similar to ϵz-greedy, ϵt-greedy has polynomial sam-
091   ple complexity in related parameters of the MDP. Our second contribution is a new goal-conditioned
092   dual replay buffer (GDRB), that uses two replay buffers along with an adaptive sampling strategy
093   to differentiate goal-reached and goal-not-reached experience data. These two buffers differ in re-
094   tention policy, size, and the transitions they store. Our third enhancement is to replace the one-step
095   update in DDPG with the longest n-step return for all transitions in an episode. Figure 1 illus-
096   trates the innovations of ETGL-DDPG. In Section 4, we evaluate the performance of ETGL-DDPG
097   through extensive experiments on 2D and 3D continuous control benchmarks. We show that each
098   of the three strategies individually improves the performance of DDPG. Furthermore, ETGL-DDPG
099
      outperforms current state-of-the-art methods across all tested environments.

103   We consider a Markov decision process (MDP) defined by the tuple (S, A, T , r, γ, ρ). S is the set of
104   states, A is the set of actions, T (s′ |s, a) is the transition distribution, r : S×A×S → R is the reward
105   function, γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount factor, and ρ(s0 , sg ) is the distribution from which initial and goal
106   states are sampled for each episode. Every episode starts with sampling a new pair of initial and goal
107   states. At each time-step t, the agent chooses an action using its policy and considering the current
      state and the goal state at = π(st , sg ) resulting in reward rt = (st , at , sg ). The next state is sampled

      2.1   D EEP D ETERMINISTIC P OLICY G RADIENT (DDPG)
115
116   To ease presentation, we adopt our notation with explicit reference to the goal state for both the
117   critic and the actor networks in DDPG. DDPG maintains an actor µ(s, sg ) and a critic Q(s, a, sg ).
118   The agent explores the environment through a stochastic policy a ∼ µ(s, sg ) + w, where w is a
119   noise sampled from a normal distribution or an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process (Uhlenbeck & Ornstein,
120   1930). To update both actor and critic, transition tuples are sampled from a replay buffer to perform
121   a mini-batch gradient descent. The critic is updated by a loss L,
122
123                                         L = E[Q(st , at , sg ) − yt ]2                                   (1)
124
125   where yt = rt + γQ′ (st+1 , µ′ (st+1 , sg ), sg ). Q′ and µ′ are the target critic and actor, respectively;
126   their weights are soft-updated to the current weights of the main critic and actor, respectively. The
127   actor is updated by the deterministic policy gradient algorithm (Silver et al., 2014) to maximize the
128   estimated Q-values of the critic using loss −Es [Q(s, µ(s, sg ), sg )].

130   2.2   L OCALITY-S ENSITIVE H ASHING
131
132   Our approach discretizes the state space with a hash function ϕ : S → M, that maps states to buckets
133   in M. When we encounter a state s, we increment the visit count for ϕ(s). We use n(ϕ(s)) as the visit
134   counts of all states that map to the same bucket ϕ(s). Clearly, the granularity of the discretization
135
      significantly impacts our exploration method. The goal for the granularity is that “distant” states are
      in separate buckets while “similar” states are grouped into one.
136
137   We use Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH) as our hashing function, a popular class of hash functions
138   for querying nearest neighbors based on a similarity metric (Bloom, 1970). SimHash (Charikar,
139   2002) is a computationally efficient LSH method that calculates similarity based on angular distance.
140   SimHash retrieves a binary code of state s ∈ S as
141
142                                      ϕ(s) = sgn(Af (s)) ∈ {−1, 1}k ,                                     (2)
143
144   where f : S → RD is a preprocessing function and A is a k × D matrix with i.i.d. entries drawn
145   from a standard Gaussian distribution N (0, 1). The parameter k determines the granularity of the
146   hash: larger values result in fewer collisions, thereby enhancing the ability to distinguish between
147   different states.

      3.1   ϵt-G REEDY: E XPLORATION WITH SEARCH
155
156   In principle, exploration should be highest at the beginning of training, as discovering rewarded
157   transitions during early steps is essential for escaping local optima (Matheron et al., 2019). Moti-
158   vated by the success of the fast exploration algorithms RRT (LaValle, 1998) and ϵz-greedy (Dabney
159   et al., 2020), we introduce ϵt-greedy, which combines ϵ-greedy with a tree search procedure. Like
160   ϵ-greedy, ϵt-greedy selects a greedy action with probability 1 − ϵ, and an exploratory action with
161   probability ϵ. However, instead of exploring uniformly at random, the exploratory action in ϵt-
      greedy is the first step of an option generated via a search with time budget N .

      To execute the search process, the agent requires access to the environment’s transition function T
163   of the corresponding MDP. This is used to generate new nodes within the search tree. However,
164   since our exploration strategy is built on DDPG, the model-free algorithm, the transition function T
165   is not known. Instead, the agent utilizes its replay buffer to advance the search. We briefly discuss
166   the impact of having access to T on the exploration process in Supplementary Material A.2. We also
167   assume that the agent has a SimHash function ϕ, which discretizes the large continuous environment.
168   For each state s, n(ϕ(s)) serves as an estimate of the number of visits to a neighbourhood of s
169   throughout the entire learning process.
170   The replay buffer contains transitions observed during training. It can be used as a transition
171   model for observed transitions and an approximate one for transitions similar to those already
172   seen. For simplicity, we identify each bucket with its hash code ϕ(s). We use a buffer BM which
173   stores observed transitions based on the hash of their states ϕ(s). If the agent makes a transition
174   (st , at , rt , st+1 ) in the environment, the transition is stored in bucket b = ϕ(st ). All transitions are
175   assigned to their buckets upon being added to the replay buffer. As training may take a long time,
176   we limit the number of transitions in each bucket, and randomly replace one of the old transitions in
177
      a full bucket with the new transition.

267   3.2   GDRB: G OAL - CONDITIONED D UAL R EPLAY B UFFER
268
269   The experience replay buffer is an indispensable part of deep off-policy RL algorithms. It is common
      to use only one buffer to store all transitions and use FIFO as the retention policy, with the most


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      recent data replacing the oldest data (Mnih et al., 2013). As an alternative, in the reservoir sampling
271   (Vitter, 1985) retention policy, each transition in the buffer has an equal chance of being overwritten.
272   This maintains coverage of some older data over training. RS-DRB (Zhang et al., 2019) uses two
273   replay buffers, one for exploitation and the other for exploration. The transitions made by the agent’s
274   policy are stored in the exploitation buffer, and the random exploratory transitions are stored in the
275   exploration buffer. For the retention policy, the exploration buffer uses reservoir sampling, while the
276   exploitation buffer uses FIFO.
277   Inspired by this dual replay buffer framework, we propose a Goal-conditioned Double Replay Buffer
278   (GDRB). The first buffer Dβ stores all transitions during training, and the second buffer De stores the
279   transitions that belong to successful episodes (i.e., goal reached). Dβ uses reservoir sampling, and
280   De uses FIFO. Since Dβ needs to cover transitions from the entire training process, it is larger than
281   De . We balance the number of samples taken from the two buffers with an adaptive sampling ratio.
282   Specifically, in a training process of E episodes, at current episode i, the sampling ratios τe and τβ
283   for De and Dβ are set as follows: τe = Ei , τβ = 1−τe . To select C mini-batches, max(⌊τβ ∗ C⌋ , 1)
284   mini-batches are chosen from Dβ and the rest from De . Later stages of training still sample from
285   Dβ to not forget previously acquired knowledge, as we assume the policy is more likely to reach the
286   goal as the training progresses. In case that De is empty, since there are no successful episodes yet,
287
      we draw all mini-batches from Dβ .

289   3.3   U SING L ONGEST n- STEP RETURN
290
      In standard DDPG, Q-values are updated using one-step TD. In goal-reaching tasks with sparse
291
      rewards, only one rewarded transition per successful episode is added to the replay buffer. The
292   agent needs rewards provided by these transitions to update its policy toward reaching the goal.
293   With few rewarded transitions, the agent should exploit a successful path to the goal many times
294   so the reward is propagated backward quickly. Multi-step updates can accelerate this process by
295   looking ahead several steps, resulting in more rewarded transitions in the replay buffer (Meng et al.,
296   2021; Hessel et al., 2018). For example, Meng et al. (2021) utilize n-step updates in DDPG with n
297   ranging from 1 to 8. In our design, to share the reward from the last step of a successful episode for
298   all transitions in the episode, we use longest n-step return (Mnih et al., 2016), shown in Equation 6.

469   5     R ELATED WORK
470
471   Exploration. Intrinsic motivation methods (Burda et al., 2018; Pathak et al., 2017; Ostrovski et al.,
472   2017; Tang et al., 2017) provide a reward bonus for unexplored areas of the state space. These
473   methods make the reward function non-stationary, which breaks the Markov assumption of MDP.
474   Decoupled RL algorithms (Schäfer et al., 2021; Badia et al., 2019) resolve the non-stationarity of the
475   reward function by training two separate policies for exploration and exploitation. However, such
476   methods require double the computation cost. Colas et al. (2018) use a policy search process to gen-
477
      erate diverse data for training of DDPG. Liu et al. (2018) introduce a competition-based exploration
      method where two agents (A and B) compete with each other. Agent A is penalized for visiting
478
      states visited by B, while B is rewarded for visiting states discovered by A. Plappert et al. (2018)
479
      directly inject noise into the policy’s parameter space instead of the action space. Eysenbach et al.
480   (2019) build a graph using states in the replay buffer, allowing the agent to navigate distant regions
481   of the environment by applying Dijkstra’s algorithm. Lobel et al. (2022) present Deep Optimistic
482   Initialization for Exploration (DOIE), which improves exploration in continuous control tasks by
483   maintaining optimism in state-action value estimates. Lobel et al. (2023) demonstrate that DOIE
484   can estimate visit counts by averaging samples from the Rademacher distribution instead of using
485   density models. Dey et al. (2024) present COIN, a continual optimistic initialization strategy that
      extends DOIE to stochastic and non-stationary environments.

      Experience Replay Buffer and Reward Propagation. Rather than uniformly sampling from the
      buffer, Prioritized Experience Replay (PER) (Schaul et al., 2015) prioritizes transitions in the buffer
512
      based on reward, recency, or TD error at the expense of O(log N ) per sample, where N is the buffer
513
      size. CER (Zhang & Sutton, 2017) includes the last transition from the buffer to each sampled
514   batch with O(1) complexity. Zhang et al. (2022) learn a conservative value regularizer only from
515   the observed transitions in the replay buffer to improve the sample efficiency of DQN. Pan et al.
516   (2022) theoretically show why PER has a better convergence rate than uniform sampling policy
517   when minimizing mean squared error. Furthermore, Pan et al. (2022) identify two limitations of
518   PER: outdated priorities and insufficient coverage of the state space. Reward shaping (Laud, 2004;
519   Hu et al., 2020) creates artificial intermediate rewards to facilitate reward propagation. However,
520   designing appropriate intermediate rewards is hard and often problem-specific. Trott et al. (2019)
521   address this issue by introducing self-balancing reward shaping in the context of on-policy learning.
522
      To extract more information from an unsuccessful episode, Andrychowicz et al. (2017) introduce
      imaginary goals. An imaginary goal for state s is a state that is encountered later in the episode.
523
      Devidze et al. (2024) introduce a novel reward informativeness criterion that adaptively designs
524
      interpretable reward functions based on an agent’s current policy in sparse-reward tasks.

527   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   We have introduced the ETGL-DDPG algorithm with three components that improve the perfor-
530   mance of DDPG for sparse-reward goal-conditioned environments. ϵt-greedy is a temporally-
531
      extended version of ϵ-greedy using options generated by search. We prove that ϵt-greedy achieves
      a polynomial sample complexity under specific MDP structural assumptions. GDRB employs an
532
      extra buffer to separate successful episodes. The longest n-step return bootstraps from the Q-value
533
      of the final state in unsuccessful episodes and becomes a Monte Carlo update in successful episodes.
534   ETGL-DDPG uses these components with DDPG and outperforms state-of-the-art methods, at the
535   expense of about 1.5x wall-clock time w.r.t DDPG. The current limitation of our work is that we
536   approximate visit counts through static hashing. For image-based problems such as real-world navi-
537   gation, the future direction is to leverage dynamic hashing techniques such as normalizing flows (Pa-
538   pamakarios et al., 2021) as these tasks demand more intricate representation learning.
