009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The clinical efficacy of Computed Tomography (CT) is well-established, yet con-
012            cerns regarding its radiation exposure persist. To mitigate this risk, a reduction in
013            X-ray photon count or projection views is typically pursued, albeit at the expense of
014            image quality. In this study, we introduce an innovative diffusion posterior sampling
015            approach for CT image reconstruction at reduced radiation doses. This method
016            initiates with a predictive step, leveraging data enhancement on the posterior ap-
017
               proximation derived from a pre-trained diffusion model and the measurement data.
               Subsequently, a forward sampling phase ensues, which maps the output to a noisy
018
               timestep, followed by a diffusion estimation process. Additionally, we propose
019
               an acceleration strategy that employs superior initialization to significantly curtail
020            the sampling steps required. Our experimental findings indicate that this method
021            not only enhances the quality of reconstructed images by an average of 3.5 db but
022            also accelerates the process to over ten times faster than existing diffusion-based
023            techniques. These outcomes underscore the method’s potential in clinical settings.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Computed tomography (CT) imaging technology has undergone significant advancements in the past
029   decades, leading to substantial improvements in diagnostic performance. The CT reconstruction
030   problem can be written as the following ill-posed inverse problem:
031                                               y = Ax + n                                              (1)
032
033
      where y, x denote the measured projection and the unknown CT image to be reconstructed; A is
      the forward projection operator and n is the measurement noise which is often modelled by i.i.d.
034
      random variables. However, the increasing use of medical CT has raised concerns about potential
035
      radiation risks, including genetic damage and cancer. Consequently, there is a pressing need to reduce
036   radiation doses. The two primary strategies for dose reduction are lowering the X-ray tube current and
037   decreasing the number of scanning views (Slovis, 2002). Nonetheless, these approaches inevitably
038   introduce noise and artifacts in the reconstructed images, which can significantly degrade image
039   quality and complicate accurate clinical diagnosis.
040
      In existing literature, sinogram filtering methods, such as structural adaptive filtering (Balda et al.,
041
      2012), bilateral filtering (Manduca et al., 2009), and penalized weighted least-squares (Wang et al.,
042   2006), have been proposed to enhance LDCT image quality by applying filters to sinogram data.
043   However, due to low signal-to-noise ratios, these methods often fail to produce high-quality images.
044   Iterative reconstruction methods address this by optimizing an objective function that incorporates
045   prior knowledge from both the sinogram and image domains. Regularization techniques, such as
046   total variation (TV) regularization (Sidky & Pan, 2008), wavelet-based sparsity priors (Jia et al.,
047   2011), nonlocal total variation (Jia et al., 2010), and low-rank patch priors (Cai et al., 2014), are
048   commonly used in these approaches. These sparse regularization models can be efficiently solved
049   using first-order methods like ADMM (Boyd et al., 2011) or the Split Bregman method (Goldstein &
050
      Osher, 2009), enhancing CT image quality by leveraging statistical properties and prior knowledge.
051   In recent years, deep learning (DL) methods have attracted significant attention due to their impressive
052   capabilities in reconstructing LDCT images. DL-based image postprocessing techniques utilize deep
053   neural networks (DNNs) as denoisers to eliminate artifacts in images reconstructed by conventional
      filtered back projection (FBP) methods. Typical networks include FBPConvNet (Jin et al., 2017),


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      REDCNN(Chen et al., 2017), EDCNN (Liang et al., 2020) and so on. As the artifacts in the
055   reconstructed images often cannot be modeled as independent random noise, the performance
056   gain brought by postprocessing method is limited. The optimization unrolling scheme (Adler &
057   Öktem, 2018; Ding et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2018) and plug-and-play methods (Ye et al., 2018) are
058   indicated more effective approaches. Unlike traditional post-processing denoising networks, these
059   methodologies derive their foundation from iterative reconstruction processes. By unrolling iterative
060   algorithms or incorporating pre-trained denoising sub-networks, these methods facilitate the design
061   of advanced deep reconstruction networks. However, these methods heavily depend on paired low
062   and normal dose images for supervised learning, which is both challenging and costly in clinical
063
      settings. Acquiring such paired data increases workload, expenses, and raises concerns about patient
      safety and privacy. Additionally, these approaches often produce over-smoothed or hallucinated
064
      images, which can obscure critical details or introduce errors, potentially leading to misdiagnoses by
065
      radiologists.
066
067   Recently, the score-based diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021c) has garnered significant
068   attention due to its innovative approach to generative modeling, which effectively reconstructs data
069
      by progressively denoising purely gaussian noise. The properties of the diffusion model, such as
      its robustness to mode collapse and its capacity for generating high-fidelity outputs, indicate its
070
      potential in the field of image generation. Diffusion models have also shown remarkable progress in
071
      solving inverse problems, including super-resolution (Saharia et al., 2022), image inpainting (Song
072   et al., 2021c), Magnetic Resonance Image (MRI) reconstruction (Song et al., 2021b), and CT
073   reconstruction (Song et al., 2021b; Chung et al., 2022). Many of these works preserve the original
074   training process but modify the inference procedure to enable sampling from a conditional distribution.
075   This kind of approach utilizes the pre-trained score function as a generative prior for the data
076   distribution, thus avoiding the need for paired data. Such flexibility allows for application across
077   various tasks while maintaining superior reconstruction quality. However, achieving high-quality
078   reconstruction generally requires a large number of diffusion posterior sampling steps, typically
079   1000 or 2000 steps as noted in (Chung et al., 2022; Song et al., 2021b). This requirement leads to
080
      significant computational costs due to repeated forward-backward operator evaluations and inference
      steps, which limits the efficiency in solving inverse problems. In imaging inverse problems, it is
081
      crucial to reduce noise and artifacts in the final image while preserving sharpness. The inherent
082
      randomness of diffusion models can introduce unpredictable elements into the reconstructed image if
083   insufficient projection constraints are applied.
084
085   To enhance both the efficiency and quality of CT image reconstruction using diffusion model, we
086
      introduce an Efficient Diffusion Posterior Sampling (EffiDPSRecon) scheme. This method integrates
      diffusion sampling as generative priors within the iterative reconstruction process. Unlike previous
087
      algorithms that rely on a single-step gradient descent, resulting in an inaccurate approximation of
088
      the posterior log-likelihood, our method utilizes the conjugate gradient algorithm, initialized with
089   denoised data, to improve data fidelity and accelerate convergence. Additionally, we introduce a
090   forward resampling step, mapping the denoised data back into the noisy data space before evaluating
091   a diffusion step. To further enhance efficiency, we initialize the process by incorporating prior
092   information from Filtered Backprojection (FBP) images, which reduces the required number of
093   sampling steps. Experiments on dose-reduced CT reconstruction, including both low-dose noisy CT
094   (LDCT) and undersampled (Sparse-view CT) data, demonstrate that our approach reduces the number
095   of diffusion steps from 1000 to just 50, while maintaining high image fidelity, with an average PSNR
096   improvement of 3.5 dB. These results highlight the effectiveness of the proposed EffiDPSRecon
097
      method for clinical applications.
098   The remainder of this paper follows this structure: Section 2 provides a concise overview of prior
099   literature relevant to the subject. Section 3 outlines the specifics of the proposed methodology.
100   Section 4 showcases the experimental evaluation and comparison to other methods. Ultimately, our
101   conclusions are presented in Section 5.

      In this section, we introduce our proposed method, namely Efficient Diffusion Posterior Sampling
189   (EffiDPSRecon), which aim to improve the sampling speed and reconstruction quality of existing
190   diffusion-based posterior sampling methods for inverse problems. The key idea of EffiDPSRecon
191   is to incorporate measurement information at multiple stages of the diffusion sampling process to
192   enhance data consistency and accelerate the sampling process with a FBP sampled initialization.
193   In the following, we first present our method in steps and then draw connections and difference to
194   existing DPS based methods.

399   reconstructed across varying dose levels and view counts, demonstrating that our method consistently
400   outperforms others across all conditions. Corresponding visualizations of the predicted reconstructed
401   images, generated using these distinct approaches for 32-views and Ii = 104 LDCT reconstruction
402   are illustrated in Figure 4. Visualizations for other cases are provided in the Appendix (Section A).
403   The display window is set to be [-160,240] HU for all windows with µair = −1000HU . These figures
404
      also incorporate difference maps which highlight discrepancies between the reconstructed images

405
      presented in Figure 5 corresponding to the green zoomed-in box in Figure 4 respectively.

407   It can be observed that FBP and ADMM-TV methods significantly distort images, severely degrading
408   fine structures and informative features. In contrast, the supervised FBPConvNet method substantially
409   improves the recovery of prominent structures and edges but tends to omit fine details and present
410
      blurry boundaries due to missing data in sparse-view projections and noisy data in LDCT projections.
      Diffusion-based methods like DPS, MCG, and our proposed EffiDPSRecon excel in generation
411
      capabilities; however, DPS and MCG struggle to accurately reconstruct structures and fail to capture
412
      fine details, as shown in Figure 5. Our EffiDPSRecon method overcomes these challenges, enhancing
413   image fidelity with increased sharpness and detail resolution. This demonstrates the robustness of
414   our algorithm in handling variations in LDCT and sparse-view CT imaging. Visual and quantitative
415   metrics clearly show that our method surpasses other competing CT reconstruction methods in
416   accuracy and image fidelity.

519   In this paper, we proposed an efficient diffusion posterior sampling scheme for CT image recon-
