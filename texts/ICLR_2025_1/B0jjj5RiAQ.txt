009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have transformed natural language processing.
012            While their scale challenges fine-tuning downstream tasks, prompt engineering
013            offers a scalable, cost-effective solution to optimize their performance. Black-box
014            prompt learning is crucial for leveraging the generative abilities of LLMs, espe-
015            cially in the Language-Model-as-a-Service scenario, where parameters and gradi-
016            ents are inaccessible. LLMs generate output exclusively in the form of encoded to-
017            kens processed through their backbone network. Existing black-box prompt learn-
018
               ing methods rely on outputs corresponding to a predefined label vocabulary—a
               small subset of the token vocabulary of LLMs—to optimize prompts. However,
019
               in real-world applications, some datasets lack specific label vocabulary, and even
020
               manually assigned labels may perform inconsistently across different LLMs. To
021            address these challenges, in this paper, we propose a novel label-vocabulary-free
022            black-box discrete prompt learning method. Our approach employs an alternating
023            optimization strategy to simultaneously learn discrete prompt tokens and a learn-
024            able matrix that directly maps the outputs of LLMs corresponding to the token
025            vocabulary to categories. We provide theoretical convergence guarantees for our
026            method under standard assumptions, ensuring its reliability. Experiments show
027            that our method effectively learns prompts and outperforms existing baselines on
028            datasets without label vocabulary.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) with their
      remarkable performance across various tasks, including text classification, machine translation, and
034
      dialogue (Touvron et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020). For a given task, the
035
      user provides natural text input, which is tokenized according to a predefined token vocabulary
036   for processing by a pre-trained LLM. The model then computes the most probable tokens from
037   the vocabulary and decodes them back into human-readable text as output. A prompt, typically
038   a sentence appended before or after a query input, can enhance the output quality of LLMs by
039   guiding the model towards task-specific behavior without requiring additional training (Gao et al.,
040   2021). This technique leverages the inherent knowledge embedded within pre-trained models to
041   elicit desired responses and provides a cost-effective alternative to directly training or fine-tuning
042   LLMs, making model adaptation both effective and efficient (Liu et al., 2023a; Chang et al., 2024).
043   Currently, companies developing LLMs typically offer only online application programming inter-
044   faces (APIs) for user interaction to safeguard their core technologies, a setup known as Language-
045   Model-as-a-Service (LMaaS). In this context, users lack direct access to the model’s parameters and
046   gradients, resulting in an inevitable black-box scenario (Sun et al., 2022b). Within such a scenario,
047   prompts become the only variables available for optimization (Diao et al., 2023). Consequently,
048   optimizing prompts relies solely on probability evaluations from the LLM’s API, necessitating the
049   use of derivative-free methods.
050   Building upon these insights, several black-box prompt learning methodologies have emerged,
051   demonstrating strong performance in text classification tasks. Continuous prompt learning ap-
052   proaches, such as BBT (Sun et al., 2022b), optimize continuous prompts that are prepended to
053   the input text through derivative-free optimization within a low-dimensional embedding subspace.
      Furthermore, SSPT (Zhang et al., 2024) enhances this framework by employing subspace learning


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and selection strategies to identify optimal low-dimensional subspaces within the BBT approach.
055   However, continuous prompt learning exhibits limited applicability across diverse tasks. For in-
056   stance, it cannot be directly applied to API prediction tasks that require discrete inputs. In contrast,
057   discrete prompt learning methods, exemplified by BDPL (Diao et al., 2023), conceptualize prompt
058   learning as a discrete token selection problem. In BDPL, prompt tokens are sampled from a cate-
059   gorical distribution and optimized using a policy gradient algorithm. Specifically, BDPL generates
060   prompts based on their associated parameters, concatenates the tokenized prompt vectors with the
061   tokenized sentence vectors, and feeds them into a LLM. The LLM’s API then provides probability
062   estimates for a predefined label vocabulary, which constitutes a small subset of the LLM’s overall
063
      token vocabulary. These probability estimates are subsequently combined with one-hot label vectors
      to compute the objective function, which is then optimized using black-box optimization techniques.
064
065   While existing black-box discrete prompt learning are effective in scenarios with predefined label
066   vocabularies, they face significant challenges when applied to real-world contexts where the label
067   vocabulary is not predefined or may not align well with the LLM’s token vocabulary. For instance,
068   shopping websites generate data with rating preferences based on user-provided star ratings, such as
069
      those in the Amazon Books dataset (McAuley et al., 2015). These ratings are numerical values that
      do not directly correspond to the appropriate tokens within an LLM’s vocabulary. As a result, it is
070
      not possible to directly obtain probability estimates for task categories from the LLM. Furthermore,
071
      when label words are missing, it is also cumbersome and difficult to use manual annotation to render
072   existing black-box prompt learning methods effective across various downstream tasks. Therefore,
073   a key problem remains underexplored: how to optimize discrete prompts in black-box scenarios
074   with missing label vocabulary.
075
      In this paper, we propose a novel label-vocabulary-free black-box discrete prompt learning method
076
      (LEAP) to address the problem. Specifically, we introduce a trainable matrix M that serves as a
077
      learnable mapping mechanism, directly associating the LLM’s output tokens with the desired task
078
      categories. This matrix effectively bridges the gap between the LLM’s token vocabulary and the
079   task-specific numerical value labels, allowing for flexible and adaptive prompt learning. Simultane-
080   ously, we employ an unbiased variance-reduced policy gradient approach to optimize the discrete
081   prompt tokens. By leveraging policy gradient, we can iteratively refine the prompts based on the
082   outputs from the LLM, ensuring that the prompts evolve in a direction that enhances task perfor-
083   mance. A notable feature of our method is its end-to-end alternating optimization framework, which
084   jointly learns the mapping matrix M and the prompt parameters. This alternative optimization strat-
085   egy ensures that both components evolve in harmony, leading to more coherent and effective prompt
086   learning.
087   To the best of our knowledge, no previous studies have discussed how to learn prompts in the con-
088   text of missing label vocabulary within the LMaaS scenario. We highlight the contributions and
089   advantages of our work as follows:
090
          • We introduce LEAP, a label-vocabulary-free black-box discrete prompt learning method that
091
            employs an innovative end-to-end alternating optimization framework. This framework jointly
092
            learns prompts and an output mapping matrix for LLMs, allowing both components to evolve
093         in harmony and enhancing LLMs’ adaptability in scenarios where label vocabulary is missing.
094
          • We provide a rigorous convergence analysis of our   optimization framework, demonstrating that
095
            LEAP achieves a convergence complexity O ϵ14 under standard assumptions. Our theoretical
096
            analysis highlights that the variance occurring during the alternating process is controlled by the
097         prompt’s sampling times and mini-batch size, thereby guaranteeing the efficacy of our approach
098         in label-free prompt learning.
099
          • We conduct an extensive evaluation of our approach across multiple LLMs to ensure its gen-
100         eralizability. The experimental results show that our method outperforms baseline methods,
101         highlighting its effectiveness in scenarios where label vocabulary is missing.

105   2.1    P ROMPT L EARNING
106
107   Prompt learning has recently gained prominence as a powerful paradigm in natural language pro-
      cessing, leveraging pre-trained language models to tackle a wide range of downstream tasks with


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      minimal task-specific training data. Early work in this field centered on prompt engineering, where
109   manually crafted prompts were employed to guide language models toward producing desired be-
110   haviors (Petroni et al., 2019; Schick & Schütze, 2021). These handcrafted prompts, while effective,
111   often necessitated considerable expertise and domain knowledge. To mitigate this limitation, re-
112   searchers developed prompt tuning techniques that automate the optimization of prompts by learn-
113   ing optimal representations. Notable works in this area, such as P-tuning (Liu et al., 2023b), Prefix-
114   tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), P-tuning V2 (Liu et al., 2021), and Prompt-tuning (Lester et al., 2021),
115   focus on learning continuous embeddings of soft prompts with tunable parameters.
116
117   2.2    B LACK -B OX P ROMPT L EARNING
118
119   Despite the success of prompt tuning in white-box settings, where model parameters and gradients
120   are accessible, there has been increasing interest in black-box prompt learning. This approach is
121
      particularly pertinent in scenarios where language models are offered as services via APIs, restrict-
      ing user access to the model’s internal mechanisms. In these black-box environments, the primary
122
      challenge lies in optimizing prompts based solely on the model’s outputs, without the capability to
123
      directly modify or fine-tune the model’s parameters.
124
125   Several significant works have been proposed to tackle this challenge, which can be primarily cat-
126   egorized into two paradigms: continuous prompt learning and discrete prompt learning. BBT (Sun
127
      et al., 2022b) and BBTv2 (Sun et al., 2022a) utilize Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strat-
      egy (CMA-ES) to optimize continuous prompt embeddings within a low-dimensional embedding
128
      subspace. SSPT (Zhang et al., 2024) incorporates subspace learning and selection techniques to
129
      identify the optimal low-dimensional subspace within BBT. However, the learned continuous prompt
130   embeddings are less interpretable compared to discrete prompts and cannot be directly applied to
131   prediction APIs that only accept discrete inputs. This limitation significantly restricts their practical
132   usability in many real-world applications.
133
      In contrast, black-box discrete prompt tuning emphasizes the optimization of human-readable and
134
      interpretable prompts, which are directly applicable in scenarios where only discrete text inputs are
135
      accepted, such as prediction APIs. Discrete prompts offer the dual advantages of interpretability and
136   deployability in real-world applications without necessitating additional processing steps. Building
137   on this foundation, RLPrompt (Deng et al., 2022) employs reinforcement learning to optimize dis-
138   crete prompts in black-box settings. By framing the prompt optimization process as a reinforcement
139   learning problem, RLPrompt iteratively refines prompts based on feedback derived from the model’s
140   outputs. GAP3 (Zhao et al., 2023) is a genetic algorithm that evolves discrete prompts from empty
141   templates by leveraging predictive probabilities from large pre-trained language models, thereby
142   eliminating the need for manual prompts or API injections. Additionally, BDPL (Diao et al., 2023)
143   utilizes the policy gradient method to optimize the categorical distribution of prompt vocabularies.

186   Alternating Optimization. We propose a Label-vocabulary-free Black-box Discrete Prompt Learn-
187   ing (LEAP), an end-to-end alternating optimization framework specifically designed for prompt
188
      learning. Initially, we conceptualize the prompt learning process as a discrete token selection prob-
      lem, where appropriate prompt tokens are sampled based on the classification distribution. This
189
      approach allows for the optimization of prompt tokens independently from the parameters and gra-
190
      dients of the pre-trained model. To enhance stability, we employ a unbiased variance-reduced policy
191   gradient estimator to optimize the categorical distribution of prompt Φ, thereby mitigating the in-
192   stability caused by the high variance inherent in prompt sampling. Subsequently, we optimize the
193   mapping matrix M by incorporating ℓ1 -regularization terms that promote feature sparsification and
194   reduce redundant information. Our alternating optimization framework alleviates the complexity
195   associated with jointly optimizing Φ and M , enabling the focused optimization of each parameter
196   individually and thereby improving the overall performance of the model.

      Proof skeleton: For the LEAP algorithm: We begin by establishing the Lipschitz smoothness
394
      (Lemma 1 and 2) of the objective function with respect to the parameters α and M , based on
395
      the clipped loss function (Assumption 3). This Lipschitz smoothness is a crucial prerequisite for
396   analyzing the nonconvex optimization problem. Subsequently, we examine two sources of stochas-
397   ticity in the alternating optimization process: the stochasticity introduced by mini-batch sampling
398   (Assumption 1) and the randomness inherent in prompt sampling (Lemmas 3 and 4). Building on
399   these foundational assumptions and lemmas, we then prove the convergence of the LEAP algorithm
400   (Theorem 1) and analyze its convergence complexity (Corollary 1). The proof of Lemmas 1-4 are
401   in the Appendix A.4. The proof of Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 are in the Appendix A.5.

407   Datasets. To evaluate the performance of our method, we conduct experiments using eight datasets:
408   BOOK (McAuley et al., 2015), CoLA (Warstadt et al., 2019), ELEC (McAuley et al., 2015),
409   QNLI (Wang et al., 2019), RTE (Dagan et al., 2005), SNLI (Bowman et al., 2015), SST-2 (Socher
410
      et al., 2013), and AG (Zhang et al., 2015). These datasets cover a variety of standard language under-
      standing tasks. Detailed descriptions of these datasets are given in the Appendix A.6. We follow the
411
      experimental settings in (Diao et al., 2023) to simulate realistic few-shot learning scenarios. Specif-
412
      ically, we randomly sample ζ examples from each class in the original training data to construct the
413   training set and use a separate set of ζ examples for the development set. The original development
414   set is designated as the test set. Accuracy is employed as the evaluation metric across all datasets.
415
416
      Baselines. We consider the following black-box prompt learning methods as our baselines:
417       • Manual Prompt (Manual): directly conducts zero-shot evaluations on pre-trained, fixed
418         LLMs without engaging in any additional learning or fine-tuning processes.
419
          • GAP3: leverages additional LLMs to generate prompts from an empty template and employs
420
            a genetic algorithm to select the most effective prompts (Zhao et al., 2023).
421
          • BBT: projects the original space onto a subspace via a random matrix, after which the prompt
422
            is optimized within this reduced-dimensional space (Sun et al., 2022b).
423
424       • SSPT: extends the BBT optimization paradigm by incorporating subspace learning and selec-
425
            tion techniques to identify the optimal ultra-low-dimensional subspace, thereby replacing the
            previously utilized random subspace (Zhang et al., 2024).
426
427       • BDPL: frames the prompt learning problem as a distributed optimization task and optimizes it
428
            using policy gradient methods (Diao et al., 2023).

      6                  C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose LEAP, a novel solution to the critical challenge of black-box prompt
534   learning within the context of LMaaS, particularly in scenarios where label vocabulary is missing.
535   Our method employs an alternating optimization framework to jointly learn discrete prompt tokens
536   and a mapping matrix that converts the full token vocabulary outputs of LLMs into task-specific
537   categories. Notably, LEAP is the first work to effectively learn discrete prompts without relying
538   on a predefined label vocabulary. Theoretical analysis confirms the convergence of our proposed
539   algorithm under standard assumptions, ensuring its reliability. Extensive evaluations across various
      LLMs demonstrate the superior performance of our approach.

       A.6   DATASETS
1513
1514     • ELEC & BOOK: The Amazon-Electronics (ELEC) and Amazon-Books (BOOK) dataset
1515       are collections of user reviews extracted from the electronics category on the Amazon plat-
1516       form, widely used in research on natural language processing and recommendation sys-
1517       tems (McAuley et al., 2015). This dataset includes a large volume of user reviews on electronic
1518       products, encompassing review texts, ratings (typically ranging from 1 to 5 stars), product in-
1519       formation, and users’ purchase histories.
1520     • CoLA: The Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability (CoLA), introduced by (Warstadt et al., 2019),
1521       consists of 8,500 training examples drawn from books and journal articles on linguistic theory.
1522       The task involves determining whether a given sentence is linguistically acceptable.
1523     • QNLI: The Question Natural Language Inference (QNLI) task comprises 108,000 training
1524       examples derived from the Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) (Rajpurkar et al.,
1525       2018). The objective of the task is to determine whether a given sentence contains the answer
1526       to a corresponding question.
1527     • RTE: The Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) task (Dagan et al., 2005) includes 2,500
1528       training examples sourced from various textual entailment challenges. The task involves deter-
1529       mining whether a given premise sentence entails a corresponding hypothesis sentence.
1530     • SNLI: The Stanford Natural Language Inference (SNLI) dataset is a widely used benchmark in
1531       the field of natural language processing, specifically designed for the task of Natural Language
1532       Inference (NLI). Created by (Bowman et al., 2015), the dataset comprises 570,000 manually
1533       annotated sentence pairs and aims to evaluate models’ abilities to understand and reason about
1534       the logical relationships between sentences.
1535     • SST-2: The Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST) (Socher et al., 2013) contains 67,000 training
1536       examples of movie reviews with human-provided annotations. The task aims to determine
1537       whether a given sentence expresses a positive or negative sentiment.
1538     • AG: The AG’s news (AG) dataset is a widely used benchmark for text classification tasks in nat-
1539       ural language processing. It consists of news articles collected from over 2,000 news sources,
1540       divided into four distinct categories: World, Sports, Business, and Science/Technology (Zhang
1541       et al., 2015). The dataset includes 120,000 training examples and 7,600 test examples, with
1542       each example being a short news article headline and description.
