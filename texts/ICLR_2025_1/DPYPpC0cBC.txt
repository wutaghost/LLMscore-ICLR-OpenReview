011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Location-Routing Problem (LRP), which combines the challenges of facility
014            (depot) locating and vehicle route planning, is critically constrained by the re-
015
               liance on predefined depot candidates, limiting the solution space and potentially
               leading to suboptimal outcomes. Previous research on LRP without predefined
016
               depots is scant and predominantly relies on heuristic algorithms that iteratively
017
               attempt depot placements across a planar area. Such approaches lack the ability to
018            proactively generate depot locations that meet specific geographic requirements,
019            revealing a notable gap in current research landscape. To bridge this gap, we pro-
020            pose a data-driven generative DRL framework, designed to proactively generate
021            depots for LRP without predefined depot candidates, solely based on customer
022            requests data which include geographic and demand information. It can operate
023            in two distinct modes: direct generation of exact depot locations, and the creation
024            of a multivariate Gaussian distribution for flexible depots sampling. By extract-
025            ing depots’ geographic pattern from customer requests data, our approach can
026
               dynamically respond to logistical needs, identifying high-quality depot locations
               that further reduce total routing costs compared to traditional methods. Extensive
027
               experiments demonstrate that, for a same group of customer requests, compared
028
               with those depots identified through random attempts, our framework can proac-
029            tively generate depots that lead to superior solution routes with lower routing cost.
030            The implications of our framework potentially extend into real-world applications,
031            particularly in emergency medical rescue and disaster relief logistics, where rapid
032            establishment and adjustment of depot locations are paramount, showcasing its
033            potential in addressing LRP for dynamic and unpredictable environments.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The Location-Routing Problem (LRP) is a critical optimization challenge in the urban logistics
039
      industry, combining two interdependent decisions: selecting depot locations where vehicles com-
      mence and conclude their tasks, and planning vehicle routes for serving customers. This integration
040
      is crucial as the depot locations can directly affect the vehicle route planning, thereby impacting
041
      overall costs Salhi & Rand (1989). The LRP can be formally defined as Nagy & Salhi (2007):
042   Given a set of customers with specific location and quantity of demands, and a set of potential depot
043   candidates each with a fleet of vehicles featuring fixed capacity, aiming to properly select a subset of
044   depots and plan routes for vehicles departing from these chosen depots to meet customers’ demands,
045   while minimizing both depot-related and route-related costs, without violating specific constraints.
046
      In this traditional problem configuration, solving LRP have relied on a predefined set of depot can-
047
      didates Contardo et al. (2014); Nguyen et al. (2012); Pourghader Chobar et al. (2022); Wang et al.
048   (2023) instead of directly generating desired optimal depot locations, thereby limiting the solution
049   space and potentially leading to suboptimal outcomes. This constraint is particularly pronounced
050   in scenarios where the optimal depot locations are not included in the candidates set, or when the
051   problem configuration demands a high degree of flexibility in depot placement, requiring quickly
052   establish and adjust depot locations. The real-world application that underscores the necessity of
053   generating depots without predefined candidates is medical rescue and disaster relief logistics: In
      the aftermath of a natural disaster, such as an earthquake or flood, the existing infrastructure may be


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      severely damaged, rendering previously established depots unusable. In such scenarios, the ability
055   to dynamically generate new depot locations based on current needs and constraints is crucial for
056   efficient and effective relief operations.
057
058
      Regarding this extended LRP scenario without predefined depot candidates, only a limited number
      of studies do the exploration by considering the concept of an infinite candidates set, represented
059
      by a planar area, for depot selection. However, these works primarily focus on designing heuristic
060
      algorithms to iteratively initiate new locations as depots across the planar area, and only manage to
061   consider up to 2 depots (single-depot work Schwardt & Dethloff (2005); Schwardt & Fischer (2009),
062   double-depot work Salhi & Nagy (2009)). Furthermore, these attempts demonstrate low efficiency
063   and adaptability in tackling specific location constraints for depots, such as the required specific dis-
064   tance range among depots. In this situation, if simply expanding the search of the depot candidates
065   across the map and aimlessly attempting new points, then undesired increasing on problem scale
066   will be incurred, thereby leading to excessive time consumption and expensive computation. There-
067   fore, devising a method to proactively generate high-quality depots, satisfying the depot location
068   constraints for LRP scenario without predefined depot candidates, is well-motivated.
069   Motivated by this necessity of proactively generating depots when no candidates are predefined, we
070   propose a generative deep reinforcement learning (DRL) framework, uniquely crafted to address
071   LRP in depot-generating fashion. By leveraging customers’ logistical requests data, which encom-
072   pass geographic locations and specific demands, our framework generates depot locations and plans
073   efficient routes for vehicles dispatched from these generated depots for serving the customer re-
074   quests. Specially, our framework encompasses two models: (1) Depot Generative Model (DGM), a
075
      deep generative model capable of generating depots in two distinct modes: direct generation of exact
      depot locations or production of a multivariate Gaussian distribution for flexible depots sampling.
076
      The exact mode ensures precision when necessary, while the Gaussian mode introduces sampling
077
      variability, enhancing the model’s generalization and robustness to diverse customer distributions.
078   (2) Multi-depot Location-Routing Attention Model (MDLRAM), an end-to-end DRL model focusing
079   on providing an efficient LRP solution for serving customers based on the generated depots, with
080   minimized objective including both route-related and depot-related cost.
081
      In summary, the contributions of our work include: (1) A generative DRL framework for LRP that
082
      proactively generates depots based on customer requests data, eliminating the reliance on predefined
083
      depot candidates, with a particular emphasis on applications requiring rapid adaptability, such as
084   disaster relief logistics; (2) The component model - DGM - provides two distinct operational modes
085   for depot generation: direct generating exact depot locations and producing a multivariate Gaussian
086   distribution for flexible depots sampling, catering to a diverse range of real-world scenarios; (3) The
087   component model - MDLRAM - provides an integrated LRP solution, minimizing the objectives
088   including both route-related and depot-related cost, while also offering flexibility to adjust inter-
089   depot cost distribution for balanced cost management across multiple depots. (4) The detachability
090   of our framework allows both independent or combined usage of its components. DGM’s depot-
091   generating ability can be fine-tuned to adapt to various LRP variants through integration with other
092
      models, while MDLRAM can be freely used in traditional LRP configuration with predefined depot
      candidates, and also can be fine-tuned to accommodate various real-life constraints.

095   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Methods for LRP with Predefined Depot Candidates: In addressing the LRP with Predefined De-
098   pot Candidates, traditional methods have predominantly employed exact and heuristic approaches.
099
      Exact methods, such as Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) models enhanced by branch-and-cut Be-
      lenguer et al. (2011); Akca et al. (2009) or column generation techniques Contardo et al. (2014), offer
100
      precision but often struggle with scalability in larger and complex scenarios due to an exponential in-
101
      crease in binary variables. This limitation has pivoted attention towards heuristic methods, which are
102   categorized into: matheuristic approaches Rath & Gutjahr (2014); Danach et al. (2019); Ghasemi
103   et al. (2022) that blend heuristic rules with exact methods, learning-aided heuristics Prins et al.
104   (2006); Nguyen et al. (2012) that leverage learning-based algorithms to refine heuristic operations,
105   and pure meta-heuristic algorithms. Among pure meta-heuristics, cluster-based heuristics Billionnet
106   et al. (2005); Barreto et al. (2007) and iterative methods Salhi & Nagy (2009); Pourghader Chobar
107   et al. (2022); Albareda-Sambola et al. (2007) have been notable. However, the cluster-based heuris-
      tics, which focus on geographically clustering customers, exhibit limitations in handling additional


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      constraints like customer-specific time windows, while the iterative methods present insufficient
109   conjugation between the two stages of depot-selecting and route-planning. Besides, these methods
110   typically require initiating a new search process for each case, leading to inefficiencies when even
111   minor alterations occur to current problem instance.
112
      The advancements in DRL have shown promise in addressing routing problems, both in “learn-to-
113
      construct/generalize” Kool et al. (2019); Xin et al. (2021a); Lin et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024)
114
      and “learn-to-improve/decompose” Xin et al. (2021b); Ma et al. (2021); Ye et al. (2024). However,
115   its application in LRP, which integrates the challenges of facility locating with routing problem,
116   still remains notably underexplored due to their inherent limitations in problem formulation for
117   scenario involving multiple depots and the inability to organically integrate depot-selecting with
118   route-planning. The works Arishi & Krishnan (2023); Rabbanian et al. (2023); Anuar et al. (2021)
119   focus on resolving routing problems involving multiple depots, but without considering the depot-
120   relate cost, which technically confine them as multi-depot VRP, instead of LRP which considers both
121   route-related cost and depot-related cost. The work Wang et al. (2023) considers depot-related cost
122   but adopts a two-stage process, clustering customers with an assigned depot location first and plan-
123
      ning routes second, thereby separating depot selection from route planning, which fails to capture
      the interdependencies between these two critical aspects, while also lacking verification on standard
124
      LRP setup align with real-world datasets. Most importantly, all these methods are constrained to the
125
      predefined depot candidates, falling short in dealing with LRP without predefined depot choices.
126
127   Exploration of LRP without Predefined Depot Candidates: Only a scant number of studies ex-
128   plore the LRP without predefined depot candidates, predominantly employing heuristic strategies for
129
      attempting new depots across a planar area devoid of predefined depot choices. The work Schwardt
      & Dethloff (2005); Schwardt & Fischer (2009) concentrate on single-depot scenario, where a sin-
130
      gle and uncapacitated depot is to be selected from a planar area. Specifically, Schwardt & Fischer
131
      (2009) extends the cluster-based method in Schwardt & Dethloff (2005), proposing a learning-aided
132   heuristic method to recurrently initiate new depot. Based on the same single-depot scenario, the
133   work Manzour-al Ajdad et al. (2012) proposes a hierarchical heuristic method to iteratively update
134   candidate circle to select new depot and then plan routes based on this depot. Furthermore, Salhi &
135   Nagy (2009) extends the iterative heuristic method to explore multi-depot scenario, but only man-
136   ages to deal with cases with up to two depots.
137
      It is notable that, compared with our method’s endeavors on actively and directly generating the
138   recommended depots, these works employ heuristic algorithms to iteratively attempt new depot and
139   then decide if it is a better one by re-planning routes based on it, limited to single or double-depot
140   scenarios. Moreover, all these works lack ability on considering specific location constraints for
141   depots, highlighting the necessity for a more adaptable and flexible solution.

      Overview: Chain-of-Thoughts Solely based on the customer requests data which include ge-
      ographic and goods demand information within an area, in pursuit of a solution that proactively
147
      generates high-quality depots satisfying specific location requirements, and subsequently plans op-
148
      timized vehicle routes from these generated depots to efficiently serve customers, we propose the
149   generative DRL framework, which is overviewed in Fig. 1.
150
151   The Depot Generative Model (DGM) takes in the customer requests information including the po-
152
      sitions and specific quantity of demands, generating the required amount of depots in two distinct
      modes: the exact depot locations or a multivariate Gaussian distribution for flexible depots sampling.
153
154   Training DGM for generating desired depots hinges on a robust evaluation mechanism to assess the
155   quality of the generated depot set, i.e., based on the same group of customer requests and identical
156   route-planning strategies, determining which set of depot can lead to solution routes with lower
157   routing cost. This necessitates a critic model to score the generated depot locations or the distribution
158
      during training. Additionally, to facilitate an efficient training process, this critic model must be able
      to instantly provide scores for the generated depot set, and also has to operate batch-wisely.
159
160   In this pursuit, we modify the Attention Model Kool et al. (2019) to accommodate the multi-depot
161   scenario, introducing the Multi-depot Location-Routing Attention Model (MDLRAM) as a critic
      model placed after the DGM, constituting the entire framework. By taking in the customer requests

      Because MDLRAM serves as a critic model for DGM, it should be robust enough to provide a

      reliable score for assessing the generated depots from DGM. That means, the score is expected to

189   to be able to provide the LRP routing solution with minimized overall cost based on given requests
190   and depots, and then set fixed to participate in the training of DGM. In this manner, during training
191   the DGM, the different sets of depots generated by DGM for a same group of customer requests
192   will get different scores from MDLRAM, solely reflecting the influence of depot locations, thereby
193   facilitating a robust training process for DGM.

195   2.1   C RITIC M ODEL : MDLRAM
196
197   As a critic model for DGM, the MDLRAM takes in the customer requests and the generated de-
198   pots, aiming to output the integrated LRP routing solution with minimized objectives including both
199   route-related and depot-related cost.
200   MDLRAM-Configuration: In alignment with the conventional setting Belenguer et al.
201   (2011), the configuration is defined on an undirected graph G = (V, E), where the V =
202   {vD1 , . . . , vDm , vS1 , . . . , vSn } denote the vertices set comprising n customers and m depots.
203   Specifically, vDk signifies the coordinates (xDk , yDk ) for depot Dk , where k ∈ {1, . . . , m}, and
204   vSe represents the coordinates (xSe , ySe ) for customer Se , where e ∈ {1, . . . , n}. The Euclidean
205   edge set is defined as E ⊆ V × V , with dij representing the Euclidean distance from vi to vj .
206   Each customer vSe has a specific quantity of demands for goods denoted as qe . Each depot vDk
207   is characterized by two attributes: (i) the maximum supply Mk (soft constraint), indicating the
208   desired maximum total goods dispatched from this depot; (ii) the fixed opening cost Ok , indicating
209   the expense for using this depot facility. Regarding the vehicles, we operate a homogeneous fleet,
210   with each vehicle having the same maximum capacity Q (hard constraint) indicating the maximum
211   vehicle load during service, and a setup cost U for using this vehicle in service. (More details for
212
      LRP configuration are available in Appendix A.1).

      Training Setup: To ensure comparability with prior methods, we establish the training dataset fol-
381
      lowing the setup outlined in prior routing studies Kool et al. (2019). As for the depot-related setting,
382
      we adopt the data format prevalent in real-world LRP benchmark datasets which are conventionally
383   employed in pertinent studies Belenguer et al. (2011); Prins et al. (2006). Every single problem in-
384   stance in the training dataset is defined on a unit square [0, 1] × [0, 1], where the customers’ requests
385   are uniformly scattered, with their corresponding demands uniformly sampled from [0, 10].
386
      The problem instances for MDLRAM’s pre-training are from three problem scales: n = 20, 50, 100
387
      customers, respectively coupled with m = 3, 6, 9 depot candidates. Corresponding to each scale:
388
      (1) The vehicle’s maximum capacity Q is selected as 30, 40, 50 respectively; (2) The vehicle’s setup
389   cost U is set as 0.3; (3) The depot’s desired maximum supply Mk is uniformly selected from
390   [50, 80], [80, 120], [120, 170]; (4) The depot’s opening cost Ok is uniformly selected from [2, 5],
391   [2, 5], [12, 19]; The coefficients in objective function Eq. (1) are defined as α = 1, β = 1, δ = 2; For
392   each scale, we train MDLRAM on one A40 GPU for 100 epochs with 1,280,000 problem instances
393   generated on the fly as training dataset, which can be split into 2,500 batches with batchsize of 512
394   (256 for scale 100 due to device memory limitation).
395   As for DGM’s problem instance, only including customer requests, we also consider three problem
396   scales: n = 20, 50, 100 customers. The expected distance among the generated depots ranges
397   within [0.2, 0.7]. The coefficients in objective function Eq. (3) are specified as λ = 10, ε = 10.
398   Correspondingly, for each scale, we train DGM on one A40 GPU for 100 epochs. Within each
399   epoch, 2,500 main-batches of problem instances are generated on the fly as training dataset and
400   iteratively fed into DGM. In multivariate Gaussian distribution mode, the main-batch size Bmain is
401   set as 32 (16 for scale 100), and the sub-batch size Bsub for sampling in each distribution is selected
402   as 128, 64, 32 for scale 20, 50, 100 respectively. In exact position mode, where no sampling is
403
      performed, we set main-batch size as 512 (256 for scale 100).

533
      In this study, we propose a generative DRL framework for depot generation without predefined
534   candidates. Based on customer requests data, the DGM proactively generates depots, while the
535   MDLRAM efficiently plans routes from these generated depots, demonstrating flexibility and cost
536   reductions, especially in scenarios requiring quick depot establishment and flexible adjustments.
537   This modular framework can be adapted to various LRP variants and further optimized for inter-
538   depot cost balancing (see Appendix B.3 for extended results). For more detailed discussions on
539   the framework’s limitations and future work, such as incorporating additional depot constraints and
      generating depots adaptive to multiple routing tasks, please refer to the Appendix B.4.

653   A.1    LRP CONFIGURATION
654
      Assumptions: Following the established assumptions Belenguer et al. (2011): (1) Each customer’s
655
      demand must be served by a delivery from exactly one depot and load transfers at intermediate
656   locations are not allowed; (2) Each customer must be served exactly once by one vehicle, i.e.,
657   splitting order is not allowed; (3) No limits on the number of vehicles utilized, but the vehicle cost
658   should be minimized as part of the objective.
659
      Constraints: The constraints in LRP includes three aspects. (1) Customer Demand: The vehi-
660
      cle’s remaining capacity must suffice to cover its next target customer’s demand during service; (2)
661
      Vehicle Capacity: The cumulative demands delivered in a single vehicle route cannot surpass the
662   vehicle’s maximum capacity; (3) Depot Supply: The aggregate demands dispatched from a specific
663   depot is expected not to exceed its desired maximum supply.
664
665
      Remark 1: The first two items are hard constraints determining solution feasibility, whereas the last
      item is a soft constraint manifesting as a penalty in the objective function.
666

722   A.3    M ULTI - DEPOT MASK MECHANISM
723
724   In each decoding step, guided by the context embedding htc , the decoder produce the corresponding
725   probabilities for all the feasible points within the selection domain. This selection domain should ex-
726   clude all the points that current vehicle cannot visit in next step, which is subject to vehicle capacity
727
      and current state in MDP. Because the model processes problem instances in batches, simultaneous
      updates to their respective selection domains at each decoding iteration is necessary.
728
729   We identify four key scenarios to categorize the selection domain of each instance at any given
730   step, based on the vehicle’s location (depot or customer) and the completion status of delivery tasks.
731   Specifically, these four potential patterns are summarized as follows:
732
             • (i) When current vehicle is at a depot and all the customers’ delivery tasks are finished: it
733
               can only stay at current depot.
734
735          • (ii) When current vehicle is at a depot but not all the customers’ delivery tasks are finished:
736            it can choose from the vertices set including all the unserved customers and unplanned
737
               depots but excluding current depot.
738          • (iii) When current vehicle is at a customer and all the customers’ delivery tasks are finished:
739            this represents the current customer is the last delivery task, implying that the only selection
740            is the vehicle’s departure depot.
741          • (iv) When current vehicle is at a customer but not all the customers’ delivery tasks are
742            finished: it can choose from the vertices set including all the unserved customers and its
743            departure depot.
744
745   Based on these four patterns, the selection domain is updated before each decoding iteration.
746   As discussed, the model operates in batch-wise manner, necessitating simultaneous updating each
747   instance’s selection domain at each decoding iteration. The challenge is, in each decoding step,
748   the selection domain of each problem instance within one batch can be very different. Thus, an
749   efficient boolean mask matrix specific to the LRP scenario is devised for batch-wise manipulation
750   on selection domain, avoiding repeated operation on individual problem instance.
751   The Algorithm 1 specifies our mask mechanism specifically tailored for LRP scenario. which in-
752   cludes manipulations on the selection domain of customers and depots. Firstly, by masking the
753   customers which have been served or cannot be satisfied with remaining capacity, the selection do-
754   main of customers can be simply derived. Crucially, for the depot selection domain, we notice
755   that among the four patterns above: three patterns (i, iii, and iv) include only the departure depot,
      whereas one pattern (ii) excludes the departure depot. Thus, at each decoding step for a batch of

951   B.4   F URTHER D ISCUSSION
952
953   In this study, we extend the exploration of the LRP by addressing a real-world challenge: the gen-
954   eration of depots when no predefined candidates are presented. For this purpose, a generative DRL
955   framework comprising two models is proposed. Specifically, the DGM, based on customer requests
956
      data, enables proactive depot generation with dual operational modes flexibly- the exact mode en-
      sures precision when necessary, while the Gaussian mode introduces sampling variability, enhancing
957
      the model’s generalization and robustness to diverse customer distributions. Meanwhile, the MDL-
958
      RAM subsequently facilitates rapid planning of LRP routes from the generated depots for serving
959   the customers, minimizing both depot-related and route-related costs. Our framework represents a
960   transition from traditional depot selection to proactive depot generation, showcasing cost reductions
961   and enhanced adaptability in real-world scenarios like disaster relief, which necessitates quick depot
962   establishment and flexible depot adjustment.
963
      The framework’s detachability offers flexible extension for its application. The DGM’s depot-
964
      generating ability can be fine-tuned to adapt different LRP variants by jointing with other down-
965   stream models, making DGM a versatile tool in real-world logistics. Meanwhile, the end-to-end
966   nature of MDLRAM enable its flexible usage on addressing LRP variants with requirements of
967   adjusting inter-depot cost distribution, which has been detailed in Appendix B.3.
968
      Based on the framework design details and the application scenario description, we spot following
969
      limitatioins and arranging a research landscape for future works.
970

      the generated depot set when planning routes for vehicle from the generated depot set, the number
