009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Training large transformer-based models like GPT-4 and Llama3 is prohibitively
012            expensive, often requiring vast resources, such as tens of thousands of GPUs run-
013            ning simultaneously for months. Traditionally, these models are trained in special-
014            ized clusters with high-speed, uniform interconnections and computational capa-
015            bilities, enabling efficient data and pipeline parallelism. However, these clusters
016            are costly, while more affordable GPUs are widely distributed across the globe.
017            Existing approaches, such as Swarm and Dapple, primarily focus on distributed
018
               learning across data centers. In this paper, we introduce a novel framework de-
               signed to handle heterogeneous devices and unstable communication environ-
019
               ments. Our framework employs a hybrid approach, combining parameter server
020
               architectures, pipeline parallelism, and task pool strategies to effectively man-
021            age device disconnections. Through comprehensive time-cost analysis and graph
022            clustering techniques, we derive a near-optimal resource allocation scheme. We
023            compare our method with existing large-scale training approaches and demon-
024            strate its effectiveness by training a large language model using gaming GPUs in
025            real-world internet conditions.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As Large Language Models (LLMs) have gained popularity in recent years, many companies have
031   invested in powerful AI GPUs, such as the A100, to train these models for months until convergence.
032   The resources required to train such networks are immense, limiting this capability to only a few
033
      companies. For the vast majority of commercial GPUs, training an LLM is simply not feasible.
      Similarly, the bottleneck of computation and communication has hindered further development in
034
      these scenarios. Table 1 shows the computation cost of single-token inference and the approximate
035
      training time of well-known language models using mainstream GPUs Hobbhahn & Sevilla (2021);
036   Casson (2023).
037
038   Existing works in distributed learning have thoroughly explored methods to run large transformer
039
      models on multiple GPUs. There are three main strategies to achieve this: model parallelism Dean
      et al. (2012); Shazeer et al. (2017), data parallelism Zinkevich et al. (2010); Goyal et al. (2017),
040
      and tensor parallelism Shoeybi et al. (2020); Narayanan et al. (2021a). Recently, hybrid pipeline
041
      approaches have been investigated Harlap et al. (2018); Fan et al. (2020). These works have signif-
042   icantly reduced the computation requirements for single GPUs and utilized GPU clusters to speed
043   up training linearly. However, these studies primarily focus on large data centers with homogeneous
044   GPU resources and high-bandwidth communication. Building and maintaining such infrastructure
045   is notoriously costly, limiting access to only a few well-funded organizations. As a result, most
046   researchers cannot afford to conduct the experiments required for a thorough evaluation of their
047   ideas.
048   On the other hand, decentralization is a natural and promising direction. Research reports that the
049   PC and Add-In Boards (AIB) GPU market shipped 9.5 million units in Q4 2023 alone wccftech.
050   Furthermore, many of these GPUs are underutilized. If we could make use of these devices in a de-
051   centralized open-volunteering paradigm for foundation model training, this would be a revolutionary
052   alternative to the expensive solutions offered by data centers. This vision has inspired many recent
053   efforts in decentralized learning, encompassing both theoretical and algorithmic advancements Ren
      et al. (2021); Diskin et al. (2021); Atre et al. (2021).

168   3.1.1    H ARDWARE S ETTING
169
170   In an internet scenario, connections between devices are affected by geo-location and congestion,
171   leading to heterogeneous throughput and temporary disconnections. Devices may have varying
172   communication bandwidth and latency. Congestion can cause packet loss and random disconnec-
173   tions, which we assume are resolved within a reasonable time using TCP timeout and reconnection
174
      protocols.
175   We consider two groups of devices: reliable and unreliable. Reliable devices remain committed to
176   the training process and attempt to reconnect after disconnections. Unreliable devices can join or
177   leave the process freely. All devices have both CPU and GPU resources.
178
179
      3.1.2    PARALLELISM S ETTING
180
181
      We work on transformer models, particularly those described in Vaswani et al. (2023). Transformer
182
      models are composed of multiple identical encoder or decoder layers, which can be divided into
183   several stages for pipeline parallelism. Each stage consists of consecutive layers. In transformer
184   models, the model dimension remains constant across all layers, meaning the activations and gra-
185   dients communicated between each layer are of the same size. We also assume that the data fed
186   into the network consists of millions of tokens, which can be split in any ratio as required for data
187   parallelism. Given the two groups of devices and their communication topology, our method aims
188   to allocate the model stages and minibatches on different devices to run distributed learning in both
189   pipeline and data parallelism.

      3.2.1    A SYNCHRONOUS P IPELINE S CHEDULING
219
220   Conventional pipeline parallelism may prioritize communication efficiency, but this alone is insuf-
221   ficient for our specific setup. In our scenario, training devices exhibit varying computational and
222   network capabilities. Consequently, constructing a pipeline with such heterogeneous devices would
223   encounter bottlenecks at the weakest participant, i.e., the node with the lowest training throughput.
224   As a result, more powerful nodes in the pipeline would remain underutilized due to insufficient input
225   or slow subsequent stages. Furthermore, any instance of node failure or premature departure from
226   training would halt the entire training process.
227   To address the challenges inherent in conventional rigid pipeline stages, we propose leveraging a
228   parameter server-based pipeline stage structure, as shown in Figure 1. Each stage in the pipeline
229   incorporates a training network based on the parameter server model. Traditional parameter servers
230   typically consist of one server and several clients, where each client sends training parameters and
231   retrieves averaged parameters. In our framework, we introduce a partially-collocated parameter
232   server architecture, operating across multiple parameter servers. All parameter servers belong to
233
      the reliable group S, while clients are drawn from the unreliable group U . In this configuration,
      parameter servers not only aggregate parameters but also relay activations and gradients among
234
      other parameter servers in adjacent pipeline stages. These parameter servers are categorized into
235
      frontal servers (FS) and backward servers (BS).
236
237   Forward and Backward. There are two processes in this scheme: the training process and the
238   aggregation process. In the forward pass, shown in Figure 1, the frontal servers (FS) receive forward
239
      activations from their predecessors and distribute the activations to each client according to their
      relative computational power. Then, each client performs forward aggregation and sends the activa-
240
      tions to the backward servers (BS). The BS passes the activations to the next stage while maintaining
241
      a list of which clients generated which activations. This list is implemented as a linked list, where
242   each node contains the client ID (or IP address), starting index, and ending index of the activations.
243   During the backward phase, the BS receives the gradients from the next stage and allocates them
244   according to the linked list.
245
      The transmission of activations and gradients between the frontal servers (FS) and the backward
246
      servers (BS) is evenly segmented based on the number of servers on the receiving side. For example,
247
      when two BS are sending activations to three FS, the first BS sends the first 23 of activations to the
248
      first FS and the remaining 13 to the second server. The second BS sends the first 31 to the second FS
249
      and the remaining 23 to the third FS. This segmentation ensures uniformity of indices for backward
250
      propagation while optimizing communication efficiency.
251
252   Task Pool. The motivation behind this is to leverage asynchronous training. If one client completes
253   its task faster, it can request additional activations from the parameter server. Additionally, clients in
254   this setting can freely join or leave. When a client leaves, other clients can take over the unfinished
255
      tasks. This is managed by maintaining a task pool in both the frontal servers (FS) and backward
      servers (BS). Whenever an FS detects that a client is unresponsive, it generates a forward task after
256
      a timeout and signals the other FS to add it to the task pool. Similarly, if the BS does not receive
257
      activations from a client, it posts the task to all FS. When any client completes its task, it requests
258   the FS to take a new task from the task pool. Similarly, in the backward phase, when a client finishes
259   working, it requests the BS for a task and asks the FS for the corresponding activation. Once it
260   receives both the gradient and the activation, it recomputes the activation and completes the leftover
261   task. This scheme ensures maximum throughput and allows training to continue even in the absence
262   of unreliable clients.
263
      Aggregation Process. In the aggregation process, shown in Figure 1b 1, we utilize Delayed Pa-
264   rameter Updates (DPU) Ren et al. (2021) to maximize efficiency. Each client sends its sharded
265   parameters to both the frontal servers (FS) and backward servers (BS), processing them using the
266   CPU, similar to the method in Jiang et al. (2020). Each client segments its parameters into the same
267   number as the parameter servers. Then, the client sends its parameters to each parameter server
268   accordingly, all in parallel. The parameter servers average the parameters on the CPU and send the
269   averaged parameters back to the clients. DPU has demonstrated similar per-iteration convergence in
      theory Arjevani et al. (2020) and in experiments Diskin et al. (2021).

      Dynamic Client Participation. In our scheme, client devices can freely join and leave the training
271   process. When a client leaves the process, it can simply disconnect without any prior warning. The
272   leftover tasks are posted to the task pool and executed by other clients. When a client wants to join,
273   it first tests its connectivity to all parameter servers. It then averages the communication bandwidth
274   and selects the pipeline stage with the highest bandwidth rate. Once selected, the client sends its
275   computing power and IP address to all parameter servers in that pipeline stage. The parameter
276   servers maintain and update a list that records all active clients and their computing power. At the
277   end of each epoch, the parameter servers recompute the task segmentation based on the clients’
278   computing power to balance the computation load.

385   4.1   P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
386
      Experiment Setup In this experiment, we aim to compare our method with previous methods in
387   both homogeneous and heterogeneous scenarios. We utilize a server cluster with 16 V100 GPUs
388   to run the experiment. Servers at different geographic locations are used to simulate heterogeneous
389   communication. We evaluate various configurations, using two transformer models: ”Llama-33B”
390   Touvron et al. (2023) and ”ALBERT-xxlarge” Lan et al. (2019). The batch size is set to 512, and the
391   minibatch size is set to 4 for both models. We measure the time taken per batch to compare overall
392   training time, as all frameworks are equivalent in whole training process.
393   The purpose of this experiment is to compare training throughput under ”ideal” conditions (homoge-
394   neous, reliable devices, and balanced layers). Deviations from these conditions make training with
395   baseline systems infeasible. We compare our approach with PipeDream Harlap et al. (2018), Dapple
396   Fan et al. (2020), and Swarm Ryabinin et al. (2023). Since Swarm does not provide an allocation
397   algorithm, we use the same allocation as our optimized scheme for a fair comparison.
398
      Case 1: Single Datacenter
399   We utilize 16 V100 GPUs with a 2GB/s inter-node connection, a common configuration in server
400   environments. We do not control bandwidth or introduce disconnections in this secure datacenter

526   5       C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we propose a Distributed collaborative learning framework. We combine parameter
529   server and pipeline parallel, creating a distributed pipeline that support large scale transformer mod-
530   els training on unreliable devices. We carefully anaylized communication and computation cost and
531   propose a heuristic graph clustering solution to provide optimal network allocation. We show that
532   our method is effective compared to previous works and our scheduler provide an optimal configu-
533
      ration. As such, our work enables researchers without access to dedicated computing infrastructure
      to train large models.
