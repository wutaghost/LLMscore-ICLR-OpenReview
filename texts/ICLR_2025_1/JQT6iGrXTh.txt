009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Foundation models have recently shown remarkable promise by leveraging ex-
012            tensive pre-training on diverse datasets to acquire generalizable representations,
013            which enable effective transfer to a wide range of downstream tasks. In the graph
014            domain, however, most existing pre-training models are tailored to specific do-
015            mains, primarily due to the inherent differences in semantic meanings of graph
016
               features across various contexts. Additionally, most existing models struggle to
               capture the rich topological complexity of graph structures, leading to inadequate
017
               exploration of the embedding space. To address these challenges, we propose
018
               a novel Graph Foundational Structural Encoder (GFSE) that identifies univer-
019            sal structural patterns, facilitating a unified feature embedding space suitable for
020            diverse domains, including molecular structures, social networks, and citation
021            networks. GFSE is the first cross-domain graph structural encoder pre-trained
022            with multiple self-supervised learning objectives. Built on a Graph Transformer,
023            GFSE incorporates attention mechanisms biased by graph structural information,
024            allowing it to encode intricate multi-level and fine-grained topological features
025            within complex graph structures. The pre-trained GFSE produces generic and
026            theoretically expressive positional and structural encoding for graphs, which can be
027
               seamlessly integrated with various downstream graph feature encoders, including
               graph neural networks for graphs with vectorized features and Large Language
028
               Models for text-attributed graphs. Comprehensive experiments on synthetic and
029
               real-world datasets demonstrate GFSE’s capability to significantly enhance the
030            model’s performance while requiring substantially less task-specific fine-tuning.
031            Notably, GFSE boosts the performance by an average margin of 20.48% across
032            eight real-world datasets, highlighting its potential as a powerful and adaptable
033            foundational encoder for graph-structured data.

038   Foundation models are recently attracting a surge of interest in natural language processing (Achiam
039
      et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), computer vision (Radford et al., 2021; Ramesh
      et al., 2021), audio (Yang et al., 2023; Borsos et al., 2023), etc. However, the application of such
040
      models in the graph domain remains relatively under-explored. Due to the inherent difference in
041
      dataset-specific features, most prior graph pre-training models are specialized for certain areas, such
042   as molecules (Zhang et al., 2020; Sypetkowski et al., 2024), proteins (Nijkamp et al., 2023), and
043   knowledge graphs (Galkin et al., 2023). These specialized models require domain-specific knowledge
044   and suffer from limited transferability to different graph domains. Recent efforts attempt to harness
045   LLMs to unify feature spaces of different graph domains using text (Chen et al., 2024b; Tang et al.,
046   2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Kong et al., 2024). However, the text-based representations used by LLMs
047   inherently lose the rich structural information encoded in the graph structure, leading to unsatisfactory
048   performance on graph learning tasks (Fatemi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024).
049   To advance the applicability of graph pre-trained models across diverse domains, we propose a
050   paradigm shift that emphasizes the inherent structural patterns within graphs as universal attributes
051   that are domain-agnostic and not tied to specific datasets. These patterns facilitate knowledge
052   transfer across downstream tasks and datasets. For instance, social networks commonly exhibit small-
053   world properties and community structures, while biological networks reveal recurring motifs and
      hierarchical modularity. Similarly, citation networks and the World Wide Web share characteristics

      Proposed work. To address the challenges of cross-domain pre-training and effectively capturing
082
      universal structural encoding, we propose GFSE, a Graph Foundational Structural Encoder, as shown
083   in Figure 1. GFSE is pre-trained across diverse graph domains using multiple self-supervised pre-
084   training tasks, including shortest path distance regression, motif counting, local community detection,
085   and graph-level contrastive learning. Each pre-training task targets a critical and necessary aspect of
086   graph structure, enabling GFSE to capture a comprehensive understanding of graph topology. GFSE
087   employs a Graph Transformer enhanced with biased attention mechanisms. Notably, the relative
088   positional encoding, derived from the random walk matrix, is explicitly integrated into the attention
089   bias term. This design allows GFSE to effectively capture intricate structural dependencies among
090   node pairs during pre-training, ensuring both efficiency and theoretically guaranteed expressiveness.
091
      GFSE’s versatility extends to various graph learning scenarios. The pre-trained GFSE can produce
      generic and expressive Positional and Structural Encodings (PSE) for topological tasks. In feature-
092
      enriched contexts, the generated PSE can seamlessly augment vectorized features or integrate with
093
      text encoders (e.g., LLMs) for text-attributed graphs. This applicability enables GFSE to serve as a
094   powerful component in any graph foundational model.
095
096   The contributions of this work: (1) We propose GFSE, the first cross-domain graph structural encoder
097
      pre-trained with four essential self-supervised learning objectives. Extensive experiments show the
      effectiveness of these pre-training tasks, leading to an average performance improvement of 20.48%
098
      across eight real-world datasets on downstream graph models. (2) We provide theoretical justification
099
      and empirical results demonstrating GFSE’s ability to generate expressive PSE. (3) GFSE serves as a
100   plug-and-play solution for any graph foundational model to incorporate structural information. By
101   focusing on universal structural patterns, GFSE paves the way for more generalizable and adaptable
102   graph encoding, potentially reducing the need for domain-specific pre-training in many applications.

106   Graph Pre-training. Graph self-supervised learning approaches are typically pre-training graph
107   models, e.g., GNNs or Graph Transformers, on a massive amount of labeled graphs with inherent
      features by reconstructing the structures or masked attributes (Cui et al., 2020; Hou et al., 2022; Kipf

109   et al., 2024b; Mizera et al., 2024). Some works also utilize contrastive learning to enhance node and
110   graph-level representation learning (Han et al., 2022; Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020; Velickovic et al.,
111   2019; Hu et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2019; 2021; Xu et al.,

117
      these models rely on one singular pre-training task (i.e., contrastive learning), and usually fail to
      capture fine-grained structural features at node level or edge level (Mao et al., 2024).
118
119   LLM-based Graph Foundation Model. With the success of foundation models in the NLP realm,
120   recent efforts also harness LLMs to develop domain-specific graph foundation models by flattening
121   graph structures and associated textural information into prompts (Chen et al., 2024a; Tang et al.,
122   2023; Ye et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Liu
123   et al., 2023a; Kong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c; Fan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
124
      2024). Nevertheless, recent studies show that LLM demonstrates an unsatisfying ability to reason
      and understand complicated structures within graph (Fatemi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Wang
125
      et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on developing a foundation model dedicated to encoding the rich
126
      topological information, without relying on associated text. Our approach complements LLMs on
127   text-attributed graphs, serving as a foundational structural encoder for general graphs.
128
129   Positional and Structural Encoding (PSE). Traditional PSEs include hand-crafted features such
130   as Laplacian PE (Davies et al., 2024; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Beaini et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022),
131
      shortest-path distance (Li et al., 2020; Ying et al., 2021), kernel distance (Mialon et al., 2021), random-
      walk encoding (Ma et al., 2023; Dwivedi et al., 2021; Brüel-Gabrielsson et al., 2022; Rampášek
132
      et al., 2022), node degree centrality (Ying et al., 2021), etc. Some studies have introduced specialized
133
      networks designed to adaptively learn PSE to enhance performance (Kreuzer et al., 2021; Dwivedi
134   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2022). GPSE (Liu et al., 2023b) proposes to pre-train a
135   positional and structural encoder on domain-specific graphs to generate PSE. However, GPSE still
136   suffers from limited transferability and expressiveness across other domains, due to its simplistic
137   pre-training backbone and randomized node features. Consequently, the effectiveness of GPSE varies
138   with specific tasks and graph models. Finding the most effective and versatile PSE remains an open
139   challenge that requires further innovation.

      As shown in Figure 1, we collect graph pre-training datasets from six different domains, including
144   molecules, proteins, social networks, images, product networks, and academic networks. GFSE
145   utilizes a transformer-based architecture with biased attention to incorporate relative inductive bias
146   within graph structures (Sec. 3.1). GFSE is pre-trained with four challenging self-supervision tasks
147   simultaneously, each designed to enhance a crucial aspect of structural awareness and promote
148   encoding quality (Sec. 3.2). GFSE generates expressive positional and structural encoding (PSE)
149   for topological tasks. Moreover, the generated PSE can be seamlessly integrated into graphs with
150   vectorized features or textual features, to enhance the downstream performance (Sec. 3.3).

243   3.3   C OMBINATION WITH D OWNSTREAM F EATURE E NCODER
244
245   Application on Graphs with Vectorized Features. GFSE can be readily employed to generate
246   expressive PSE for various graph applications. Let X0 ∈ RN ×dx denote the initial node features for
247   a given graph with N nodes and PL ∈ RN ×de denote PSE generated by GFSE, where dx and de are
248   dimensions of node features and PSE, respectively. PL can then be concatenated with the initial node
249   features X0 to create a new feature matrix Xnew = [X0 ∥PL ] ∈ RN ×(dx +de ) , which augments the
250   node features with structural information. This structure-enriched feature Xnew can subsequently be
251   fed into downstream graph models, such as graph neural networks or graph transformers, enhancing
252
      their performance on various tasks. For large-scale graphs, where computing PSE for the entire graph
      may be computationally prohibitive, we thereby sample the neighborhood structure around each node
253
      and compute the PSE for these localized subgraphs. This process can be efficiently parallelized,
254
      enabling scalable and efficient generation of PSE for large graphs.
255
256   Application on Text-attributed Graphs. Language models are typically employed to process the
257   text-attributed graphs, where GFSE can be seamlessly applied to incorporate structural information.
258   Given the generated PL ∈ RN ×de , an MLP is employed to project PL into the embedding space
259
      of the language model. This projected PSE is then prepended as a soft token to the associated
      text, effectively incorporating the graph’s structural information into the model input. Subsequently,
260
      these structure-enriched tokens are fed into downstream large language models (LLMs), enhancing
261
      their performance on graph-related tasks. The process, involving training a lightweight MLP and
262   fine-tuning LLM with Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques such as LoRA (Hu et al.,
263   2021), makes it scalable and efficient for large-scale text-attributed graph applications.
264
265   3.4   E XPRESSIVE P OWER OF GFSE
266
267   We show that GFSE can generate highly expressive PSE by incorporating relative edge encoding into
268   the attention computation in the Graph Transformer backbone. Specifically, we employ the Structural
269   Encoding enhanced Global Weisfeiler-Lehman test (SEG-WL) (Zhu et al., 2023), a generalized WL
      test that incorporates relative structural encoding into the isomorphism algorithm, to characterize


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the expressiveness of GFSE. For an input graph G(V, E) with node set V and edge set E, let
271   fP : V → X and fR : V × V → X indicate the node-level and edge-level structural encoding,
272   respectively. Different from traditional WL test, SEG-WL updates the node labels at the t-th iteration
273   by gt (v) = hash ({{(gt−1 (u), fR (v, u)) : u ∈ V }}) and g0 (v) = hash(fP (v)). SEG-WL can be
274   viewed as a high-level abstraction of the learning paradigm of our pre-training architecture with
275   biased attention (Eq. 2), where relative structural encoding between any two nodes is considered for
276   updating node representations (Zhu et al., 2023). Let RW(d)-SEG-WL denote the case that fP and fR
277   are determined by P and R with d dimension, i.e., fP (vi ) = Pi ∈ Rd and fR (vi , vj ) = Rij ∈ Rd
278   for the i-th and j-th nodes. We have the following propositions.
279   Proposition 3.1. RW(d)-SEG-WL (d ≥ 3) is strictly more expressive than 1-WL in testing non-
280   isomorphic graphs.
281   Proposition 3.2. There exist pairs of graphs that RW(d)-SEG-WL can distinguish, but 3-WL can not.
282
283   The theoretical proof and empirical verification are given in Appendix D. RW-SEG-WL is able to
284
      distinguish all low-order graphs with orders equal to or less than 8 and successfully distinguishes
      most strongly regular graphs where 3-WL fails to distinguish. RW-SEG-WL stands as an expressivity
285
      upper bound of our proposed GFSE. The pre-training tasks are meticulously designed to push GFSE
286
      towards achieving the upper bound established by RW-SEG-WL. These pre-training tasks optimize
287   both node-level and edge-level structural encoding, progressively refining the effectiveness of the
288   model in generating expressive encoding.

290   3.5   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
291
292   The complexity of developing GFSE comprises two parts: pre-computation of self-supervision labels
293   and pre-training. For the pre-computation, we adopt Dijkstra Fibonacci-heap solution (Dijkstra, 1959)
294   to compute the shortest path distance between node pairs, which results in the time complexity of
295   O(|E| + |V | log |V |) with node set V and edge set E. A brute-force implementation of the subgraph
296   isomorphism counting of fixed size t is O(|V |t ). We consider the graphlets with at most 5 nodes.
297
      One can also choose special graphlet types, e.g., paths, cycles, and triangles, which can be efficiently
      enumerated (Giscard et al., 2019). Approximating and scalable algorithms can be further used to
298
      accelerate this pre-processing step (Fu et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2020; Pashanasangi & Seshadhri,
299
      2020). For pre-training, the complexity is O(|V |2 ) for full attention computation and O(d|V ||E|)
300   for initial encoding computation of P and R. Notably, the model’s PSE generation process requires
301   less than five minutes for all downstream datasets. See runtime evaluations in Appendix F.3.

312
313   Dataset. We utilize a diverse collection of cross-domain datasets for pretraining, ensuring a broad
314   spectrum of graph structures and scales, including MolPCBA, MolHIV, MNIST, peptides, ogbn-

316   2013; Szklarczyk et al., 2019; Chiang et al., 2019; Takac & Zabovsky, 2012; Dwivedi et al., 2023;
317   2022). These datasets cover several real-world graph domains, such as social networks, academic
318   networks, etc. Table 7 in Appendix C.1 presents the detailed statistics of datasets used for pre-training.
319
      For large-scale graphs, we first partition them into sets of subgraphs by the METIS algorithm (Karypis
      & Kumar, 1997) to handle scalability issues. Training samples from different datasets are mixed and
320
      randomly shuffled to form a large-scale pre-training dataset.
321
322   Pre-training Setting. The pre-training stage is conducted on the standard train/validation/test splits
323   of the pre-training datasets. The dimension of initial encoding d is set as 8. We adopt GIN (Xu et al.,
      2018) as the message-passing layer in the GPS and adopt 8 GPS layers with 8 heads and 128 hidden

      dimensions in each layer. The output dimension is 64 by default. We use Adam as the optimizer with

471   a lightweight MLP to align the PSE generated by GFSE with the language model’s embedding
472   space, which ensures seamless integration of structural information into the language model. We
473   concatenate the textual description of a central node with those of its one-hop neighbors and prepend

      likelihood. We train the MLP and fine-tune the language model with LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) using

      a contrastive loss (Hadsell et al., 2006). More evaluation details can be found in Appendix E.2.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   GFSE represents a significant advancement in cross-domain graph foundational models, leveraging
529   multiple self-supervised learning objectives to capture comprehensive structural information from
530   diverse graph domains. By integrating relative positional encoding within a Graph Transformer,
531   GFSE provides a robust framework for generating expressive positional and structural encodings.
532   Extensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets validate GFSE’s ability to enhance the
533   performance of various graph feature encoders, broadening its applicability across numerous graph-
534
      related tasks. Building upon the promising results of GFSE, one potential direction is to explore
      the impact of pre-training dataset diversity on the model’s ability to capture multi-level topological
535
      features. Investigating techniques to curate more representative and varied pre-training datasets could
536
      further enhance GFSE’s generalization capabilities across different graph domains.

920
      Random Walk Structural Encoding. Random walk structural encoding (RWSE) (Dwivedi et al.,
921
      2021; Rampášek et al., 2022) uses landing probabilities derived from random walks of varying
922
      lengths, starting from each node in the graph to capture both structural and positional relationships
923   among nodes. Formally, let G(V, E) represent an input graph, where V and E denote the set of N
924   nodes and edges, respectively. A ∈ RN ×N indicates the adjacency matrix and D is the degree matrix.
925   Random Walk matrix is defined as M = D−1 A, where Mi,j indicates the transition probability
926   from the i-th node to the j-th node. The RWSE with d steps for the i-th node is defined as
927
928                                    RWSEi = [I, M, M2 , · · · , Md ]i,i ,                              (5)
929
930   Laplacian Positional Encoding. Laplacian positional encoding (LapPE) (Kreuzer et al., 2021;
931   Dwivedi et al., 2023) method emerges as a significant advancement for enriching node representations
932   with spectral information. LapPE utilizes the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian matrix to encode the
933   relative positions of nodes within a graph. These eigenvectors reflect a local coordinate system that
934
      captures meaningful structural information, while also preserving the global topological properties of
935
      the original graph. The Laplacian matrix L = D−A has the full eigendecomposition as L = UΛU⊤ .
      We use the k smallest non-trivial eigenvectors of the Laplacian matrix to create the LapPE. The
936
      LapPE with k eigenvectors for the i-th node is expressed as:
937
938                                  LapPEi = [u1,i , u2,i , · · · , uk,i ] ∈ Rk ,                        (6)
939
940   where ut denotes the t-th smallest non-trivial eigenvectors and kis the number of eigenvectors
941   used. Laplacian PE is particularly useful in situations where nodes are inherently anonymous and
942
      lack unique features. However, the Laplacian encoding faces challenges from the arbitrary sign of
      normalized eigenvectors, introducing 2k possible configurations for k eigenvectors. To manage this
943
      complexity during training, eigenvectors are randomly sampled from these possibilities. Alternatively,
944
      resolving the sign ambiguity by taking the absolute values of eigenvectors simplifies the model but
945   can significantly reduce the expressiveness of the positional features.
946
947   Pre-trained Positional and Structural Encoder. Graph Positional and Structural Encoder
948   (GPSE) (Liu et al., 2023b) is a graph encoder pre-trained on molecule datasets by reconstruct-
949
      ing traditional positional encoding, such as LapPE, RWSE, CycleSE, etc. The model takes as input
      graph adjacency matrix and randomly generates node features by X ∼ N (0, I) to improve the
950
      expressiveness. The pre-training architecture is a deep MPNN with 20 layers and residual connection
951
      and gating mechanism. Moreover, GPSE utilizes a virtual node technique in each graph to enable
952   global message passing. However, GPSE suffers from poor generalizability across other domains,
953   due to its pretraining setting and randomized node features.

974    Motif Counting is formulated as a node-level reconstruction task. We explicitly use subgraph
975    isomorphism counting as a form of self-supervision during pretraining, allowing the model to better
976    leverage structural information and identify each node’s role in the surrounding subgraphs (Bouritsas
977    et al., 2022). We follow previous works on subgraph isomorphism to count the number of certain
978    motifs surrounding each node. A key concept in our preprocessing step is "Automorphism orbits," as
979    introduced by Pržulj (Pržulj, 2007). This concept helps in identifying unique roles of nodes within
980    the counted motifs. The detailed counting method is as follows:
981
             1. Define the subgraph structures to be counted: Enumerate all graphlets with the number
982
                of nodes less than or equal to 4. This limitation is due to the exponential growth in the
983
                number of graphlets with increasing nodes, which becomes computationally infeasible.
984
985          2. Assign indexed orbits: For each subgraph (or motif), assign each vertex a uniquely indexed
986             orbit to facilitate accurate counting.
987          3. Count specified subgraphs: Utilize the subgraph_isomorphism function from the
988             Python package graph-tool to count specified subgraphs throughout the entire graph.
989             This count is used to determine orbits for each node in the entire graph, forming an orbit
990             degree vector.
991
             4. Save and prepare node-level labels: Save these vectors with the data as node-level labels,
992
                which are then prepared for our node-level reconstruction task.

       For an input graph G(V, E) with node set V and edge set E, let fP : V → X and fR : V × V → X
1162
       indicate the node-level and edge-level structural encoding, respectively. SEG-WL updates the
1163
       node labels at the t-th iteration by gt (v) = hash ({{(gt−1 (u), fR (v, u)) : u ∈ V }}) and g0 (v) =
1164   hash(fP (v)).
1165
       Proposition D.1. RW(d)-SEG-WL (d ≥ 3) is strictly more expressive than WL in testing non-
1166
       isomorphic graphs.
1167
       Proposition D.2. There exist pairs of graphs that RW(d)-SEG-WL can distinguish, but 3-WL can not.
1168
1169
       Proof. We first introduce Neighbor-SEG-WL, which is the SEG-WL test when fP is an identity
1170
       encoding and fR (u, v) equals 1 if (u, v) ∈ E and 2 otherwise. Previous works have proved the
1171
       following Proposition (Zhu et al., 2023).
1172
1173   Proposition D.3. Two non-isomorphic graphs can be distinguished by WL if and only if they are
1174   distinguishable by Neighbor-SEG-WL.
1175
1176
       Therefore, Neighbor-SEG-WL is a specific example of SEG-WL test that has equivalent expres-
       siveness to the 1-WL test. We then prove that RW-SEG-WL is strictly more expressive than
1177
       Neighbor-SEG-WL. Let dneg (u, v) indicate the edge-level encoding fR in Neighbor-SEG-WL.
1178
       Note dneg (vi , vj ) = 2 if and only if Aij = 0. Recall that fR (·, ·) in RW-SEG-WL satisfies
1179
       fR (vi , vj ) = Rij ∈ Rd with R = [I, M, · · · , Md ] where M = D−1 A. Therefore, fR in RW-
1180   SEG-WL strictly contains the information of dneg . Therefore, if two non-isomorphic graphs can be
1181   distinguished by WL, they can be distinguished by RW-SEG-WL. Proposition D.1 is proved.
1182
1183

       the Rook’s 4 × 4 graph, a pair of strongly regular graphs SRG(16,6,2,2). It is proved that they cannot
1184
       be distinguished by 3-WL (Arvind et al., 2020). We empirically verified that RW(d)-SEG-WL with
1185
       d > 4 can distinguish these two graphs.
