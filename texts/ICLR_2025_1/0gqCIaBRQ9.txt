                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this paper, we study nonparametric estimation of instrumental variable (IV) re-
010
               gressions. While recent advancements in machine learning have introduced flexi-
011
               ble methods for IV estimation, they often encounter one or more of the following
012            limitations: (1) restricting the IV regression to be uniquely identified; (2) requir-
013            ing minimax computation oracle, which is highly unstable in practice; (3) absence
014            of model selection procedure. In this paper, we analyze a Tikhonov-regularized
015            variant of the seminal DeepIV method, called Regularized DeepIV (RDIV) re-
016            gression, that can converge to the least-norm IV solution, and overcome all three
017            limitations. RDIV consists of two stages: first, we learn the conditional distribu-
018            tion of covariates, and by utilizing the learned distribution, we learn the estimator
019            by minimizing a Tikhonov-regularized loss function. We further show that RDIV
020
               allows model selection procedures that can achieve the oracle rates in the mis-
               specified regime. When extended to an iterative estimator, we prove that RDIV
021
               matches the current state-of-the-art convergence rate. Furthermore, we conducted
022
               numerical experiments to justify the efficiency of RDIV empirically. Our results
023            provide the first rigorous guarantees for the empirically well-established DeepIV
024            method, showcasing the importance of regularization which was absent from the
025            original work.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
      Instrumental variable (IV) estimation is an important problem in various fields, such as causal infer-
030
      ence (Angrist and Imbens, 1995; Newey and Powell, 2003; Deaner, 2018; Cui et al., 2020; Kallus
031   et al., 2021; 2022), missing data problems (Miao et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2014), dynamic discrete
032   choice models Kalouptsidi et al. (2021) and reinforcement learning (Liao et al., 2021; Uehara et al.,
033   2022a;b; Shi et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022).
034
      In this paper, we focus on nonparametric IV (NPIV) regression (Newey and Powell, 2003). NPIV
035
      concerns three random variables X ‚ąą Rd (covariate), Y ‚ąą R (outcome variable), and Z ‚ąą Rd
036
      (instrumental variables). We are interested in finding a solution h0 of the following conditional
037   moment equation (Dikkala et al., 2020b; Chernozhukov et al., 2019):
038
039                                            E[Y ‚ąí h(X)|Z] = 0.
040
      This is equivalently written as T f = r0 where T : L2 (X) ‚ąč f (X) 7‚Üí E[f (X)|Z] ‚ąą L2 (Z) and
041
      r0 (Z) = E[Y |Z] by denoting L2 (X), L2 (Z) to be the L2 space defined on X and Z with respect
042   to the underlying distribution. Both the operator T and E[Y |Z] remain unknown. Hence, we aim to
043   solve T f = r0 by harnessing an identically independent distributed (i.i.d.) dataset {Xi , Yi , Zi }i‚ąą[n] .

      mation such as deep neural networks beyond classical nonparametric models (Hartford et al., 2017;
046
      Singh et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Dikkala et al., 2020b; Bennett and Kallus,
047
      2020; Bennett et al., 2023a;b; Kallus et al., 2022; Singh, 2020). Despite these extensive efforts,
048   existing approaches encounter several challenges. The first challenge is the ill-posedness of the in-
049   verse problem. Many existing works (Liao et al., 2020a; Newey and Powell, 2003; Florens et al.,
050   2011; Kato et al., 2021) require that the NPIV solution h0 is unique, and further impose quantitative
051   bounds on measures of ill-posedness. However, it is known that the uniqueness assumption is easily
052   violated in practical scenarios, such as weak IV (Andrews and Stock, 2005; Andrews et al., 2019) or
053   proximal causal inference (Kallus et al., 2021). The second challenge involves the reliance on mini-
      max optimization oracles in many methods (Bennett et al., 2023a; Dikkala et al., 2020b; Liao et al.,

076
      selection in existing works (Chen and Pouzo, 2012; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Cui et al.,
      2020; Hartford et al., 2017). Such a procedure, including techniques such as cross-validation, has
077
      played a pivotal role in modern machine-learning algorithms (Bartlett et al., 2002a; Gold and Sollich,
078
      2003; Guyon et al., 2010; Cawley and Talbot, 2010; Raschka, 2018; Emmert-Streib and Dehmer,
079   2019; McAllester, 2003). In NPIV problems, model selection becomes essential, particularly in
080   scenarios where the ground-truth solution h0 lies outside the chosen function classes optimized by
081   the algorithm. However, model selection remains an open question for minimax approaches due
082   to having a test function for the inner maximization problem that might change when generaliz-
083   ing from the empirical distribution to population distribution. On the other hand, while some prior
084   works employ a loss minimization approach (e.g. Chen and Pouzo (2012); Zhang et al. (2023)),
085   model selection would be restricted to a specific hypothesis space, such as kernels or sieves, and no
086   theoretical discussion for model selection under general function approximation has been discussed.
087   In this paper, we propose and analyze a variant of the well-established DeepIV method (Hartford
088   et al., 2017), that addresses the aforementioned challenges, which we refer to as the Regularized
089   DeepIV (RDIV). This approach consists of two steps. First, we learn the operator T by maximum
090   likelihood estimation (MLE). Secondly, we obtain an estimator for h0 by solving a loss incorporating
091   the learned T and Tikhonov regularization (Ito and Jin, 2014) to handle scenarios where solutions
092   of the conditional moment constraint are nonunique. While RDIV can be viewed as a regularized
093
      variant of the DeepIV method of Hartford et al. (2017) with a non-parametric MLE first-stage, no
      prior theoretical convergence guarantees exist for the DeepIV method. We show that our estimators
094
      can converge to the least norm IV solution (even if solutions are nonunique) and derive its L2 error
095
      rate guarantee based on critical radius. Subsequently, we introduce model selection procedures for
096   our estimators. Particularly, we provide theoretical guarantees for model selection via out-of-sample
097   validation approaches, and show an oracle result in our context. Finally, we further illustrate that
098   RDIV can be easily generalized to an iterative estimator that more effectively leverages the well-
099   posedness of h0 .
100
      Our contribution is to propose the first formal theoretical results for the well-established NPIV
101
      method DeepIV, with an additional Tikhonov regularization. Although simple, such regularization
102   imparts strong convexity to the loss function, thereby enhancing its generalization ability. Specifi-
103   cally, we show that RDIV (a) operates in the absence of the uniqueness assumption, (b) does not rely
104   on the minimax computational oracle, and (c) allows for model selection. Subsequently, we demon-
105   strate that RDIV can be extended to an iterative estimator. We show that our estimators achieve
106   a state-of-the-art convergence rate in terms of L2 error analogous to Bennett et al. (2023b) for the
107   iterative version, as well as the non-iterative version when h0 is well-posed. In contrast, Bennett
      et al. (2023b) relies on a minimax computational oracle and does not permit us to perform model

529   10       C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we study NPIV regression with general function approximation. We analyze a
532   Tikhonov-regularized variant of the well-established DeepIV estimator, namely the Regularized
533   DeepIV (RDIV). We show that our estimator converges to the least norm solution, and derive its
534   convergence rate. Notably, we prove that such an estimator does not rely on uniqueness or minimax
535
      computation oracle. We further illustrate that RDIV can be incorporated into model selection and
      show that our procedure can achieve the oracle rate with respect to the minimal model misspecifi-
536
      cation error. When extended to an iterative estimator, RDIV achieves a state-of-the-art convergence
537
      rate. Moreover, we justify our method through numerical simulations. Our experiments show that
538   RDIV outperforms existing benchmarks in a wide range of circumstances.

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
811
812   Nonparametric IV problem. Nonparametric IV estimation has been extensively explored in past
813   decades. Such estimation is tough to solve even when both the linear operator T and the response
814   r0 are known, known as ill-posedness. The ill-posedness often refers to the presence of one or more
815
      of the following characteristics: (1) the absence of solutions, (2) the existence of multiple solutions,
      and (3) the discontinuity of the inverse of operator T . Many traditional nonparametric estimators
816
      have been proposed to address these challenges, such as series-based estimators (Florens et al.,
817
      2011; Ai and Chen, 2003; Chen, 2021; Chen and Pouzo, 2012; Darolles et al., 2011) and kernel-
818   based estimators (Hall and Horowitz, 2005; Horowitz, 2007; Singh et al., 2019). However, these
819   methods cannot directly accommodate modern machine-learning techniques like neural networks
820   with theoretical soundness.
821
      Recently, there has been growing interest in the application of general function approximation tech-
822
      niques, such as deep neural networks and random forests, to IV problems in a unified manner.
823
      Among those methods, Bennett and Kallus (2020); Dikkala et al. (2020b); Lewis and Syrgkanis
824   (2018); Liao et al. (2020a); Zhang et al. (2023) reformulate the conditional moment constraint into
825   a minimax optimization and use its solution as the estimator. Notably, Liao et al. (2020a); Bennett
826   et al. (2023b;a) establish L2 convergence by linking minimax optimization with Tikhonov regu-
827   larization under the assumption of the source condition. Moreover, Liao et al. (2020b) assumes
828   uniqueness of solution h0 . Dikkala et al. (2020b); Lewis and Syrgkanis (2018) provide a guarantee
829   for the projected MSE without further assumptions. However, they could not guarantee the con-
830   vergence rate in strong L2 metric when multiple solutions to conditional moment constraint exist.
831   Furthermore, these methods require a computation oracle for minimax optimization, which further
832
      makes model selection challenging. In contrast, our method does not require computational oracles
      and enables model selection with statistical guarantees.
833
834   Several existing works eschew the need for minimax optimization oracles (Chen and Pouzo, 2012;
835   Hartford et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2021). As the most related work, DeepIV (Hartford et al., 2017)
836   introduces a similar loss function to us. However, it lacks an explicit regularization term, which
837   results in the lack of theoretical guarantee and the lack of guarantee for model selection. As another
838
      work, Xu et al. (2021) extends the two-stage kernel algorithm in Singh et al. (2019) to deep neural
      networks, but their algorithm is essentially a bilevel optimization problem, which is hard to solve
839
      in general (Hong et al., 2023; Khanduri et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021). Notably, Chen and Pouzo
840
      (2012) considers a more general conditional moment restriction of E[ŌĀ(X, h0 ) | Z] = 0 and obtains
841   an estimator of h0 by minimizing a penalized sieve minimum distance (PSMD). Their method is
842   similar to ours: first, they assume the existence of an estimate mŐā(h, Z) for m(h, Z) = E[h(X) | Z]
843   for all h ‚ąą L2 (X). Then they minimize En [‚ą•mŐā(h, Z)‚ą•2] + őĽn PŐān (h) over all possible h ‚ąą H,
844
      where H is a sieve space with a growing dimension, and PŐān is a nonrandom penalty function.
845   However, their theory is limited to the case when h0 is identifiable and is well approximated by the
846   sieve estimator, therefore can not be straightforwardly generalized to general function approximation
847   with model misspecification. Recently, Chen et al. (2024) proposed a TOSG method under the
848   lens of optimization, and established its convergence rate for including linear function class and
849   general linear function class with a known link function g. However, their techniques are not directly
850   transferable to general function approximation, and no guarantee or procedure for model selection
851   for g is discussed. Moreover, Chen et al. (2024) assumes a two-sample oracle that outputs two
852   independently sampled X and X ‚Ä≤ conditioned on the same instrument Z, which is often hard to
853
      satisfy in practice.
854
855   Model selection. Model selection has been well studied in the regression and supervised machine
856   learning literature (Bartlett et al., 2002a; Gold and Sollich, 2003; McAllester, 2003). The objective
857   can be described more concretely as follows: given M candidate models, {f1 , . . . , fM }, each having

863   form above implies that using the observed data we can compete (up to constants) with an oracle that
      knows the approximation errors and chooses the best model space. We leave the detailed summary of

      Model Selection. Under the classical supervised learning setting, a common approach is to per-

880

881
      cross-validation which splits the data into M folds, and evaluates the risk on the different held out set
882   for each model (Vaart et al., 2006). As an alternative to selecting a single model, convex aggrega-
883   tion or linear aggregation is employed to find the best convex/linear combination of models (Lecu√©,
884   2013; Lecu√© and Mendelson, 2014). However, it can be shown that the aforementioned approaches
885   are sub-optimal in the sense that they cannot achieve the optimal log(M  n
                                                                                  )
                                                                                    rate for the model selec-
886   tion residual. To tackle this challenge, Lecu√© and Mendelson (2009) proposed a different approach
887   for convex aggregation by first finding a subset of "almost minimizers" - a subset of the candidate
888   functions that is sufficiently close to the minimizer within the candidates on the validation set, and
889   then finding a best aggregate in the convex hull of this subset. This approach achieves the optimal
890   model selection rates as it performs ERM on a subset that is much smaller than the convex hull of all
891   candidate models, thereby reducing the statistical error. Furthermore, other optimal model selection
892
      approaches include the Q-aggregation approach which performs ERM with a modified loss that adds
      an additional penalty based on individual model performance (Lecu√© and Rigollet, 2014).

1655
1656   For RDIV, we use Adam as the optimizer for both density estimation and Tikhonov regression, with
1657   a default learning rate of 10‚ąí4 , a batch size of 50, and a training epoch of 300. All results are run on a
1658   32GB CPU. We will show how to choose these hyperparameters with our model selection procedure
1659   (Algorithm 2) in Section J.2. For all baselines except for AGMM, we adapt the hyperparameters
1660   in their original codebase. For AGMM, we tune the learning rate for the learner and adversary
1661   for every g(¬∑) independently. We follow Singh et al. (2019) to use Gaussian RKHS for function
1662   approximation and their method for tuning the regularization parameter. When n = 500, the learning
1663
       rate of the learner and adversary in AGMM are manually set to 10‚ąí4 for LogSigmoid, Piecewise,
       and Sigmoid, and 10‚ąí3 for Id, Poly, and CubicRoot. When n = 1000, the learning rate of the
1664
       learner and adversary in AGMM are manually set to 10‚ąí4 for Piecewise and Sigmoid, and 10‚ąí3
1665
       for LogSigmoid, Piecewise, and CubicRoot. The training parameter of DFIV is adopted from Xu
1666   et al. (2021). Note that tuning DFIV is highly intractable in practice, as their method is essentially a
1667   bilevel optimization, which is known to be hard to solve (Hong et al., 2023).
