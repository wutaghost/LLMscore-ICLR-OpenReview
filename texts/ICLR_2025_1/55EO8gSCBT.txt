009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional methods for optimizing neural networks often struggle when used
012            to train networks in settings where the data distributions change, and plasticity
013            preservation methods have been shown to improve performance in such settings
014            (e.g. continual learning and reinforcement learning). With the growing interest
015            in nonstationary optimization and plasticity research, there is also a growing need
016            to properly define experimental design and hyperparameter search protocols to
017            enable principled research. Each new proposed work typically adds several new
018
               hyperparameters and makes many more design decisions such as hyperparame-
               ter selection protocols, evaluation protocols, and types of tasks examined. While
019
               innovation in experiment design is important, it is also necessary to (1) question
020
               whether those innovations are leading to the best progress and (2) have standard-
021            ized practices that make it easier to directly compare to prior works. In this paper,
022            we first perform an extensive empirical study of over 27,000 trials looking at the
023            performance of different methods and hyperparameters across different settings
024            and architectures used in the literature to provide an evaluation of these methods
025            and the hyperparameters they use under similar experimental conditions. We then
026            examine several core experiment design choices made by the community, affirm-
027            ing some while providing evidence against others, and provide concrete recom-
028            mendations and analysis that can be used to guide future research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep learning has seen success across a wide range of tasks and datasets. The trend of larger mod-
      els and larger amounts of data, however, suggests the need to be able to update our models in an
034
      online fashion on changing tasks and datasets. Most of our currently successful machine learning
035
      systems were developed with training methods that assumed a static training distribution. When the
036   same training methods are applied in settings with changing, or nonstationary, data distributions,
037   they struggle to achieve the same level of performance (Ash & Adams, 2020; Abbas et al., 2023-
038   08-22/2023-08-25; Nikishin et al., 2022). In fact, in settings where nonstationarity is inherent either
039   by construction (e.g. continual learning) or because the solution methods require it (e.g. reinforce-
040   ment learning), many solutions instead focus on trying to transform the optimization problem into
041   something as stationary as possible (Mnih et al., 2015; Rolnick et al., 2019; Chaudhry et al., 2019b).
042   Recently, there has been a growing recognition of the limitations of traditional optimization methods
043   when applied to nonstationary problems. Specifically, recent works have looked at the phenomena
044   where neural networks get worse at both reducing training error and improving generalization per-
045   formance when exposed to different distributions than what they were trained on initially, problems
046   often collectively referred to as loss of plasticity. There are many different methods proposed to
047   mitigate this loss of plasticity, and mostly can be described as one of architectural (Abbas et al.,
048   2023-08-22/2023-08-25; Lyle et al., 2023-07-23/2023-07-29), regularization (Lyle et al., 2023-07-
049   23/2023-07-29; Lewandowski et al., 2024), or resetting (Lyle et al., 2024b; Dohare et al., 2021)
050
      based.
051   The works introducing these methods also introduce many other experimental design choices.
052   As a consequence of the decentralized nature of research, these choices include everything
053   from the types of nonstationarities used to evaluate the methods to the protocols used to se-
      lect hyperparameters, and often, these choices are hidden or unclear. In settings that require

054
      nonstationary optimization such as continual learning (Cha & Cho, 2024) and reinforcement
055   learning (Henderson et al., 2018; Obando-Ceron et al., 2024), these choices have been shown to
056   have a huge impact on the evaluation of different methods: simply tuning an old method on a new
057   setting can result in new state-of-the-art results (Chaudhry et al., 2019b; van Hasselt et al., 2019;
058   Schwarzer et al., 2023).
059
      With that in mind, our work examines a representative sample of plasticity preserving methods
060
      across several different types of nonstationary settings and architectures that have been used in the
061   literature. Specifically, we look at permuted input, label shuffled, and noisy label versions of the
062   CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) datasets, using Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) and
063   ResNets (He et al., 2016). We choose settings and architectures that the community already works
064   with so that our findings can be directly useful to people working on these problems. We first per-
065   form an extensive hyperparameter sweep for each of these methods and evaluate them with a con-
066   sistent setup to evaluate which methods work well on different architectures and settings. We assess
067   the transferability of these methods and the impact of their hyperparameters on their performance.
068   We then further explore the experimental design decisions that need to be made when creating an
069   experiment for nonstationary optimization such as the number of seeds to use when evaluating a
070   hyperparameter configuration, or which metric to use to evaluate a model. Our goal is to not only
071   provide the research community with empirical results to guide the decision making that is often
072   done based on arbitrary intuition, but also enable research for groups with access to fewer resources
073   by telling them where to focus their resources.
074   To summarize, our contributions include a comprehensive evaluation of widely used current plas-
075   ticity preserving methods and their hyperparameters across several types of nonstationarities and
076   architectures, and an empirical evaluation of various experimental design decisions that go into do-
077   ing research on nonstationary optimization including:
078
079       • Which hyperparameter selection protocol to use?
080       • Whether we should be optimizing for both training and test accuracy when doing nonstationary
081         optimization research.
082       • How can we do a resource efficient hyperparameter search?
083
084   As part of our work, upon acceptance we will also be releasing the code and all of the intermediate
085   results for all the evaluations described in the rest of this paper as a base for future research.

089   2.1    P LASTICITY AND N ONSTATIONARY O PTIMIZATION
090
091   After training on a task, neural networks have been shown to struggle to adapt to new data distribu-
092   tions in both the continual supervised learning (Dohare et al., 2024) and the reinforcement learning
093
      settings (Nikishin et al., 2022; Abbas et al., 2023-08-22/2023-08-25). In fact, Ash & Adams (2020)
      show that even networks pretrained on a subset of the task data underperform relative to randomly
094
      initialized (untrained) networks when they are later trained on the full dataset. This type of non-
095
      stationarity has been shown to affect the network’s ability to reduce both the training error (Dohare
096   et al., 2024) and the generalization, or testing error (Ash & Adams, 2020; Lee et al., 2024b). We
097   will refer to these as trainability and generalizability in the rest of the paper. The term loss of plas-
098   ticity usually refers to the first problem and sometimes the second problem. We will use “loss of
099   plasticity” as the umbrella term for both problems and use “trainability” and “generalizability” when
100   referring to the specific problems. Several different works have started exploring mechanisms and
101   causes for plasticity loss (Lyle et al., 2023-07-23/2023-07-29; Lewandowski et al., 2024; Lyle et al.,
102   2024b; Kumar et al., 2023). There has also been a growing literature showing that addressing plas-
103   ticity issues can lead to significant performance improvements not only in the standard continual
104
      learning settings, but also for reinforcement learning agents (Abbas et al., 2023-08-22/2023-08-25;
      Schwarzer et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024a).
105
106   Plasticity loss mitigation measures usually fall into one of three categories: regularization, archi-
107   tectural, or resetting. Regularization based approaches involve adding a penalty or constraining
      the parameters in some way, such as regularizing towards the initialization (Kumar et al., 2023;

128   2.2   H YPERPARAMETER S EARCH IN N ONSTATIONARY S ETTINGS
129
      Because hyperparameters are intimately tied to how we use and evaluate any machine learning
130
      algorithm, doing proper and equitable hyperparameter optimization is critical to properly compare
131
      against prior work and make progress as a research community. For example, in reinforcement learn-
132   ing (RL), simply tuning an existing baseline created a new state-of-the-art result on the Atari100k
133   benchmark (Kaiser et al., 2019). Given the complexity and resource intensiveness of RL, further
134   work explored how to best and most efficiently tune hyperparameters for RL algorithms, including
135   examining reproducibility issues (Henderson et al., 2018), how to properly use multiple seeds to
136   evaluate an algorithm (Agarwal et al., 2021), and the consistency of selected configurations in a
137   search (Obando-Ceron et al., 2024).
138   Hyperparameter search in continual supervised learning suffers from similar issues of complexity
139   and resource requirements, with the added ambiguity of the criterion that should actually be used for
140   the selection and evaluation of a hyperparameter configuration. Previous works on hyperparameter
141   optimization in continual supervised learning focused on settings trying to mitigate forgetting, i.e.
142   performance degradation on tasks seen earlier in training, while learning new tasks. A common
143   approach is to set aside a portion of the training data from each incoming task as a validation set,
144   select the hyperparameters based on some aggregate metric on the validation sets across all tasks,
145   and then use the test set metrics as the final evaluation (Masana et al., 2023). Chaudhry et al. (2019a);
146
      Cha & Cho (2024) propose tuning hyperparameters on one sequence of tasks and evaluating on a
      different sequence of tasks. Lee et al. (2024c) explore different protocols where hyperparameters
147
      were chosen after just a single task or were dynamically adapted after each task.
148
149   Our work evaluates several of these proposed protocols in the context of nonstationary optimization,
150   where we only try to maximize the model’s performance on the current task and do not attempt to
151   maintain performance on previous tasks. Since several previous works in this area are unclear on
152
      some or all of the details of the experimental setup such as which hyperparameter selection protocol
      was used or how many seeds were used, we also reimplement a representative sample of previous
153
      works and evaluate them on a consistent setup.

156   3     B ENCHMARKING S ETUP
157
158   In this section, we outline the details of the different hyperparameter searches we conducted. We
159   outline the methods we analyzed in Section 3.2. For each method, we randomly sample 40 con-
160   figurations from the full search space for that method, as grid search would become prohibitively
161   expensive for some methods, and other methods like Bayesian search would impose a temporal
      order on the sampling of the configurations that would make the analysis in Section 4 more difficult.


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      We study two different architectures, a 3 layer MLP with 128 hidden units at each layer and a
163   ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016). These architectures not only allow us to examine the methods at
164   different scales with different layer types, but they are also commonly used in the literature, making
165   our analysis more valuable for the community. We do a separate search for each combination of
166   method, architecture, and task stream. For each configuration sampled, we evaluate n = 20 seeds
167   for MLP runs and n = 10 seeds for ResNet-18 runs, with each seed having a different task stream
168   as well as model initialization.
169   The community has targeted both training accuracy (Dohare et al., 2021; Kumar et al., 2023;
170   Lewandowski et al., 2024; Lyle et al., 2024b) and test accuracy (Lee et al., 2024b; Elsayed et al.,
171   2024) as measures of interest when doing plasticity research. We study both, and particularly the
172   relation between them in Sections 4 and 4.3, and focus on test accuracy in the rest of the study. For
173   the Shuffled and Permuted settings, we compute the average accuracy across all tasks, while for the
174   Noisy setting, we only use the testing accuracy on the final task.

176   3.1   N ONSTATIONARITIES
177
178   Each run in our setting involves training on a series of m tasks in sequence, with task k involving
                                            nk
179
      learning on dataset Dk = {(xki , yik )i=1 }. The goal of the learner is to maximize performance on
      task k, without trying to preserve performance on tasks 1 . . . k − 1. Several different nonstationarity
180
      types have been proposed and studied in the plasticity literature. Most of them involve taking some
181
      base dataset and applying some transformation. We will take a similar approach, with CIFAR-10
182   and CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) being used as the base datasets for the MLP and the ResNet-18
183   experiments respectively. In our study, we focus on the following three transformations (also shown
184   in Figure 1a):
185
186   Shuffled Label Our first transformation is the shuffled label transformation, where each task is
187   created by remapping the labels of the original dataset to new labels. Specifically, given the original
188   dataset D = {(xi , yi )ni=1 } and a randomly generated permutation function Pyk : Y → Y that
189   remaps the label space, task k involves learning on Dk = {(xi , Pyk (yi ))ni=1 }. We prefer this output
190   transformation to the other commonly used output transformation where each sample is assigned a
191   random label as this allows us to probe the network’s ability to maintain generalizability and not just
192   trainability. We set the number of tasks m = 100 and m = 30 for MLP and ResNet-18 experiments
193   respectively.
194
195   Permuted Input Our second transformation is similar to the first, except instead of permuting
196
      the output space, we permute the input space. This is a commonly used benchmark in continual
      learning (Goodfellow et al., 2013), where given D and a randomly generated permutation function
197
      Pxk : X → X that permutes the locations of the pixels in input space, task k involves learning on
198
      Dk = {(Pxk (xi ), yi )ni=1 }. Similar to the shuffled label setup, we set the number of tasks m = 100
199   and m = 30 for MLP and ResNet-18 experiments respectively.
200
201   Noisy to Clean Label Our final transformation was proposed in (Lee et al., 2024b). Assuming
202   there are m total tasks in the task sequence, the dataset is split into m equal chunks, and then label
203   noise is applied to each chunk, going from a high level of noise at the beginning of training to a
204   clean chunk at the end of training. Given D, a corruption function T : Y × P → Y, and corruption
                                                                                          n
                                                                                      k·⌊ m ⌋
205   probability pk for task k, task k involves learning on Dk = {(xi , T (yi , pk ))i=(k−1)·⌊ n }. For both
                                                                                                m⌋
206   MLP and ResNet-18 experiments, we split the datasets into 10 chunks and linearly interpolate the
207   corruption probability from .5 to 0 over the course of the task sequence.

257   3.3                    S ELECTION P ROTOCOLS
258
259   In standard machine learning, selecting a hyperparameter configuration is fairly straightforward. To
260   avoid overfitting to the test set, practitioners perform k-fold cross-validation where they split the
261   dataset into k pieces, retraining k times each time leaving out one of the pieces to use for evaluation
262   (Hastie et al., 2009). With standard deep learning, this becomes more difficult as retraining is ex-
263   pensive, and so we simply set a piece of the training data aside as validation to use for selecting the
264   best configuration, then evaluating on the test set. With continual learning, this procedure becomes
265   ambiguous. Since we now have m different train, test, and validation datasets, as well as different
266
      task sequences between n seeds, how do you select and evaluate your configurations?
267   We describe three protocols proposed in the literature for continual learning (Figure 1b). Each
268   protocol consists of a hyperparameter configuration selection procedure used to select amongst the
269   different configurations being evaluated in the search and a final evaluation procedure, which is used
      to report the performance of the method and compare it to other methods.


                                                                                                  5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Protocol 1: A commonly used protocol in continual learning (Masana et al., 2023) is to split the
271   training dataset for each incoming task into a training dataset and validation dataset. The average
272   validation metric (loss, accuracy, or any other metric of interest) across the different seeds and tasks
273   is used to select the best configuration, and the average test metric is reported for evaluation. The
274   selection and evaluation are done on the same task sequences.
275
276   Protocol 2: An alternative is to use multiple streams of tasks in the protocol (Chaudhry et al.,
277   2019a; Cha & Cho, 2024). Specifically, the test metric on one task sequence is used to select the
278   configuration, and the test metric on another task sequence is reported as the final evaluation. Cha &
279   Cho (2024) claim that Protocol 1 can overfit to the specific task sequence used, and this procedure
280   can mitigate that risk. In our study, each seed results in different transformations being applied and
281
      thus represents a different task sequence.
282
      Protocol 3: Finally, (Mesbahi et al., 2024) propose a protocol in the context of continual rein-
283
      forcement learning where the test metric on the first k% of training is used for selection, and the
284
      test metric on the rest of training is used for evaluation, claiming that this is a more realistic and
285   challenging protocol for continual learning.

      4.3   C ORRELATION BETWEEN TRAINING AND TESTING PLASTICITY
379
380   Many previous works exploring plasticity and nonstationary optimization often focus on trainabil-
381   ity, arguing that fixing trainability is a precondition to fixing generalizability. Many works do not
382   even report testing accuracy and often use distribution shifts such as Random Label Assignment,
383   where each individual example is assigned a random label, with the goal being to test the limits of
384   trainability and ignoring generalizability.
385   In this section, we question this line of reasoning. In Figure 4, we see the correlation between train
386   and test accuracy of different configurations. The train and test accuracies were obtained by averag-
387   ing the results of all seeds for that configuration. In Figure 4a, we see a positive correlation when
388   plotting all configurations, but there is a leveling off of the test accuracy after a certain point. When
389   focusing on just the top 20% of configurations in each setting (Fig. 4b) (i.e. the configurations that
390   are likely to be actually chosen after a hyperparameter search), the positive correlation essentially
391   disappears for most settings. There is in fact a (statistically insignificant) negative correlation for
392
      a couple of the MLP settings, and even for the settings with a positive correlation, Noisy CIFAR-
      100 with ResNet is the only setting where we see a statistically significant result (p < .05). Thus,
393
      trainability correlates with generalizability only up to a point, after which continuing to improve
394
      trainability does not end up correlating with the end goal of improving model performance. This
395   suggests that (1) for the types of settings presented in this study (which are representative of what
396   is currently studied in the literature), we should shift our focus from improving trainability to the
397   problem of improving generalizability. (2) Studying trainability could still be a valuable problem,
398   but we should find harder settings to do so.
399
400   4.4   H OW MANY SEEDS DO YOU NEED TO EVALUATE A M ETHOD ?
401
402   Many prior works use anywhere between 1 and 5 seeds for hyperparameter selection and then run
403   the selected configuration with more seeds. Figure 5 looks at the effect of the number of seeds used
404
      when selecting hyperparameter configurations. For a given number of seeds, we perform statistical
      bootstrapping with 1000 trials by resampling the seeds used to do model selection. We use the
405
      sampled measurements to create rankings over the configurations being evaluated for each method,
406
      and compare them to the “oracle” rankings which we compute by taking the average of all available
407   seeds.
408
409   Figure 5a shows that to ensure that the absolute best configuration is chosen for every method, you
410
      likely need to use almost all the available seeds. If we relax our requirements slightly, however,
      in Figures 5b and 5c, we see that for most settings (aside from Permuted CIFAR-10 with MLP
411
      and Noisy CIFAR-100 with ResNet-18), just a couple of seeds are enough to ensure that the top
412
      configuration is actually the oracle best and that the oracle best configuration is evaluated as a top 3
413   configuration in the selection process.
414
415   These results imply that we do not need very many seeds to do effective hyperparameter selection.
416
      Furthermore, the results from Figures 5b and 5c point to a potentially effective use case of sequential
      hyperparameter optimization approaches that can refine configurations or request more resources for
417
      promising configurations.

509
510    Hyperparameter Search Protocol We should not do model selection on the same task sequences
511    that we use to report our evaluation on as that can lead to performance overestimation and method
512    rankings that do not transfer to different task sequences. Furthermore, our current methods, do not
513    perform well in the case where the first few tasks are used to do model selection for the rest of the
514    model’s “lifetime”, as proposed in (Mesbahi et al., 2024). The ability to select hyperparameters that
515    transfer to timescales not seen in the hyperparameter selection stage is essential to create lifelong
516    agents with unbounded lifetimes. The failure of current methods/protocols to do so invites further
517    research.
518
519
520    Training vs Testing Plasticity The plasticity community has focused much of its effort on cre-
521
       ating methods to mitigate the loss of training accuracy, as a prerequisite to eventually improving
       performance on generalization accuracy. For the types of datasets currently studied by the commu-
522
       nity, this approach is unsound, as improvements in the ability to maintain training accuracy do not
523
       lead to improvements in the ability to maintain generalizability. We should be studying trainability
524    on harder settings where improvements in trainability lead to improvements in generalizability.
525
526
527
       Creating Resource Efficient Hyperparameter Searches We explore the effect of three factors
528    that can affect the resources used in a nonstationary optimization hyperparameter search: number
529    of seeds, number of tasks used in the search, and number of configurations sampled. For many
530    settings, you do not need a large number of seeds while doing a hyperparameter search. When using
531    a few seeds, a viable approach could also be to select multiple configurations and train them with
532    more seeds to get a better estimate before selecting a final configuration. We also find that in many
533    settings, you can reduce the number of tasks by as much as 50% and still be able to identify the best
534    configuration for the full task sequence. Finally, even when doing an unintelligent random search,
535    you do not need to sample more than 20-30 configurations on most settings to find performant
536
       configurations.
537    We specifically designed our study around datasets, distribution shifts, and architectures that have
538    been used by several prior works in this field to ensure that our findings are directly useful to the
539    community. We hope that these findings will also enable good research for researchers with access
       to fewer resources, as our work points to several ways to make experiments more efficient.

       D.6                             CReLU ADJUSTED FOR N UMBER OF PARAMETERS
906
907    The CReLU activation function changes the architecture by doubling the size of the activation output.
908    This also increases the number of parameters in the network since for the intermediate layers, the
909    input size is doubled compared to a non-CReLU activation.
910
       To adjust for the number of parameters, we ran a search with a smaller architecture such that the
911
       number of parameters in the network approximately matches an architecture without CReLU. For
912    the MLP architecture, this meant a reduction of the hidden size from 128 to 120, and for the ResNet
913    architecture, we reduced the number of filters in each convolutional layer from 64 to 48. We ran a
914    slightly shortened search with 30 configurations for each setting, and show the results in Figure 17.
915    We can see that the MLP results are approximately the same, with a slight decrease in performance

       E               B OOTSTRAPPING P ROCEDURE
1215
       With statistical bootstrapping, we sample B datasets of size n, compute some statistic using each
1216
       of these datasets, and use the empirical distribution over the statistic to create confidence intervals
1217   or compute standard errors for the sample mean of the statistic (Hastie et al., 2009). In our case,
1218   we are sampling partitions over the seeds, P , and using the partition to estimate some statistics that
1219   are a function of this partition, f (P ). Specifically, statistics such as the rank correlation between
1220   the rankings generated by two protocols or the binary random variable indicating whether the best
1221   config was selected by a protocol are a deterministic function of the partition. In our case, since
1222   when people do hyperparameter search, they usually only sample one partition, we set n = 1 and
1223   B = 1000. Thus, we are sampling 1000 different partitions (with replacement), calculating the
1224   statistic for each of the partitions, and displaying the 95% empirical confidence interval that results.
