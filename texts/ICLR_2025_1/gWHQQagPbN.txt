009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To date, 2:4 sparsity has stood as the only sparse pattern that can be accelerated
012            using sparse tensor cores on GPUs. In practice, 2:4 sparsity often possesses low
013            actual speedups (≤ 1.3) and requires fixed sparse ratios, meaning that other ratios,
014            such as 4:8, 8:16, or those exceeding 50% sparsity, do not incur any speedups on
015            GPUs. Recent studies suggest that V:N:M sparsity is promising in addressing
016            these limitations of 2:4 sparsity. This sparsity divides a weight matrix into mul-
017            tiple V×M blocks, pruning (M-4) columns within each block and applying 2:4
018            sparsity to the remaining columns. V:N:M sparsity inherently encompasses 2:4
019
               sparsity but allows for higher and more flexible pruning ratios, typically resulting
               in greater practical speedups. However, regarding accuracy, the effects of V:N:M
020
               sparsity on broader Transformer models, such as vision Transformers and large
021
               language models (LLMs), are largely unexamined. Moreover, Some specific is-
022            sues related to V:N:M sparsity, such as how to select appropriate V and M values,
023            remain unresolved. In this study, we thoroughly investigate the application of
024            V:N:M sparsity in vision models and LLMs across multiple tasks, from pretaining
025            to downstream tasks. We propose three key approaches to enhance the applica-
026            bility and accuracy of V:N:M-sparse Transformers, including heuristic V and M
027            selection, V:N:M-specific channel permutation and three-staged LoRA training
028            techniques. Experimental results show that, with our methods, the DeiT-small
029            achieves lossless accuracy at 64:2:5 sparsity, while the DeiT-base maintains ac-
030
               curacy even at 64:2:8 sparsity. In addition, the fine-tuned LLama2-7B at 64:2:5
               sparsity performs comparably or better than training-free 2:4 sparse alternatives on
031
               downstream tasks. More importantly, V:N:M-sparse Transformers offer a wider
032
               range of speedup-accuracy trade-offs compared to 2:4 sparsity. Overall, our explo-
033            ration largely facilitates the V:N:M sparsity to act as a truly effective acceleration
034            solution for Transformers in cost-sensitive inference scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Transformer has gained significant popularity as backbones across various domains due to its re-
040
      markable performance in data modeling and scalability. However, Transformers are often charac-
041
      terized by a large number of parameters and high computational demands, resulting in prolonged
042   inference latency. It is essential to compress Transformer models for efficient inference, especially
043   in resource-constrained or latency-sensitive applications.
044
045
      One possible way to accelerate Transformers is 2:4 sparsity, where only two out of every consecutive
      four parameters are retained in weight tensors. 2:4 sparsity is widely supported by Nvidia Ampere
046
      or newer GPUs. However, the current ecosystem for 2:4 sparsity exhibits three weaknesses that are
047
      rarely addressed. 1) Low practical speedups. Unlike the theoretical claims of a twofold speedup,
048   in most cases, neural networks with 2:4 sparsity achieve only a speedup in the range of 1.1 to 1.3x
049   (Cai, 2023; Pool et al., 2021). 2) Only one sparsity pattern, i.e., 2:4, can be accelerated. Other
050   patterns at 50% sparsity, like 4:8 and 8:16, cannot yield any speedups on existing GPUs. 3) Failure
051   to exploit higher sparsity ratio. For some Transformer models with high weight redundancy, or
052   in scenarios where inference overheads are more sensitive while model accuracy can be relatively
053   relaxed, the optimal sparsity ratio can be larger than 50%, and 2:4 sparsity cannot fully leverage the
      potential performance gains from higher sparsity levels.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To address the weaknesses, Castro et al. (2023) proposes V:N:M sparsity. As shown in Figure
055   1, V:N:M sparsity divides the weight matrices of linear layers in Transformers into multiple blocks,
056   each sized V × M. Within each block, (M - 4) columns are pruned, leaving 4 columns that implement
057   2:4 sparsity. V:N:M sparsity enables practical speedups for sparsity above 50% on GPUs. Notably,
058   any GPU that supports 2:4 sparsity can also accelerate V:N:M sparsity. Due to higher compression
059   ratios, V:N:M sparse Transformers deliver greater speedups compared to those using 2:4 sparsity.
060
      The initial work on V:N:M sparsity primarily focuses on designing its acceleration kernel. Impor-
061
      tantly, the impact of V:N:M sparsity on broader Transformer models, such as vision Transformers
062
      and large language models (LLMs) like the Llama series, remains under-explored. Additionally,
063
      fundamental issues regarding V:N:M sparsity, such as how to select appropriate values for V and
064
      M in a Transformer architecture, have never been resolved. Without addressing these issues, V:N:M
065
      sparsity can not comprehensively outperform 2:4 sparsity in compressing Transformers with high re-
066
      dundancy. In this work, we aim to bridge these gaps by systematically investigating the application
067
      of V:N:M sparsity across multiple Transformer models, with a particular emphasis on enhancing
068
      their accuracy. Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
069
070         • We propose a framework to enable the generation of highly accurate V:N:M-sparse Trans-
071           formers under different constraints, which broadens the applicability of V:N:M sparsity.
072         • We propose three techniques to address challenges specific to V:N:M sparsity. First, we
073           present a heuristic method for selecting V and M values that yield optimal accuracy-
074           speedup trade-offs for V:N:M sparse Transformers. Second, we introduce V:N:M-specific
075           channel permutation, which improves the accuracy of V:N:M-sparse Transformers within
076           limited training budgets. Finally, we propose a three-stage LoRA training technique that
077           adapts V:N:M sparsity for LLMs.
078         • Extensive experiments demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed scheme and techniques.
079           Impressively, DeiT-base with 64:2:8 sparsity (75% sparse) achieves nearly lossless accu-
080           racy, with a minimal difference of less than 0.3% compared to the dense counterpart. As
081           for speedups, the 64:2:8-sparse DeiT-base achieves a 1.7x speedup, while the 2:4 sparsity
082           only provides a 1.15x speedup compared to the dense counterpart.
083   Our methods and results demonstrate that in scenarios with high inference costs or stringent latency
084   requirements, V:N:M sparsity is a superior alternative to 2:4 sparsity for Transformers exhibiting
085   significant redundancy. We advocate for the inclusion of V:N:M sparsity as a key consideration in
086   deploying Transformers on GPUs that support 2:4 sparsity.

107   Sparsity in Transformers Weight sparsity in Transformers can be categorized into three types: un-
      structured sparsity, like S2 ViTE (Chen et al., 2021b), SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023), and


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      Wanda (Sun et al., 2023); structured sparsity, represented by ViT-Slim (Chavan et al., 2022), VTP
109   (Zhu et al., 2021), UVC (Yu et al., 2022), and SAViT (Zheng et al., 2022); and semi-structured
110   sparsity. In particular, semi-structured sparsity generally offers a preferable trade-off between ac-
111   curacy and speed. Yu et al. (2023) introduce 2:4 sparsity to vision Transformers, demonstrating
112   that the DeiT series with 2:4 sparsity can maintain a nearly lossless performance. Xu et al. (2024)
113   implement block-wise sparsity in Transformers, achieving notable speed improvements on neural
114   processing units. Nevertheless, this approach results in unavoidable accuracy declines. In contrast,
115   our V:N:M sparse DeiT-base can preserve nearly lossless accuracy even at 75% sparsity. Beyond
116   weight sparsity, other components, such as tokens and attention heads, can also be pruned in Trans-
117
      formers. Some works in this aspect include T2T-ViT-24 (Yuan et al., 2021), PVT (Wang et al.,
      2021), Evo-ViT (Xu et al., 2022), EViT (Liang et al., 2022), DynamicViT (Rao et al., 2021), PS-ViT
118
      (Tang et al., 2022), and AdaViT (Yin et al., 2021). However, in this study, we primarily focus on the
119
      effects of V:N:M sparsity on Transformers, rather than extreme compressing a Transformer.
120
121   Optimization for sparse Transformers The sparse Transformers can be retrained to restore accu-
122   racy. The retraining process can be combined with techniques such as neural architecture search
123
      (Chen et al., 2021a; Chavan et al., 2022). During retraining, sparse masks can be updated period-
      ically (Zhang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023). To address training instability, SR-STE (Zhou et al.,
124
      2021) proposes suppressing the weights that are masked out, allowing the updated masks to become
125
      progressively consistent as training advances. For large-scale Transformers, such as large language
126   models (LLMs), post-training pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
127   2024) is effective when sparsity levels are low or moderate (less than 50%). Additionally, some
128   studies (Kuznedelev et al., 2023; Kale-ab Tessera & Rosman, 2021) indicate that sparse networks
129   are often under-trained provided with the same number of training epochs as their dense counter-
130   parts. Our study also finds that as training duration increases, the accuracy of sparse Transformers
131   improves significantly, while dense Transformers exhibit minimal accuracy gains.
132   Channel permutation Channel permutation (CP) is extensively utilized in model quantization
133   (Yuan et al., 2023) and sparsification (Ji et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Pool &
134   Yu, 2021). In particular for model sparsity, rearranging the order of weight or activation tensors
135   prior to pruning can significantly reduce the subsequent one-shot pruning loss. Furthermore, as a
136   specialized form of teleportation (Zhao et al., 2023; Mishkin et al., 2024), CP enhances the gradient
137   flow of sparse models during training. However, other methods, such as extended training dura-
138   tions (Kuznedelev et al., 2023) and gradual pruning strategies (Bambhaniya et al., 2024; Jaiswal
139   et al., 2022), can also improve gradient flow in sparse models, CP is particularly advantageous in
140
      low-training-budget scenarios, where CP can promote model convergence.

      Pruning of V:N:M sparsity Pruning a weight matrix W to achieve the V:N:M-sparse pattern in-
145
      volves three steps: 1) Calculate importance scores. First, compute the importance score for each
146
      weight in W. 2) Column Pruning. Next, prune the columns within each V×M block. Within each
147   block, the L1 norms of the importance scores for each column are compared, and the weights cor-
148   responding to minimal M−4 columns are pruned. 3) Conduct 2:4 Sparsity. After the column-wise
149   pruning, each block retains exactly four columns. Subsequently, for each row, the weights corre-
150   sponding to the last two importance scores are further pruned to establish the final V:N:M-sparse
151   pattern. For descriptive convenience, we signify this V:N:N-sparse pruning process as SV :N :M .
152

      Acceleration of V:N:M sparsity As illustrated in Figure 2(b), the acceleration of V:N:M-sparse
180   Transformers involves three steps: weight padding, conversion to compressed formats, and the ap-
181   plication of V:N:M accelerated kernels. First, the weights of all linear layers are zero-padded to
182   ensure that the input and output channels of weight matrices in linear layers are divisible by M and
183   V, respectively. Next, the padded weights are converted into sparse storage formats that include
184   only the compact non-zero values and their indices. The inference kernels then directly take these
185   sparsely stored weights as input and leverage sparse tensor cores to accelerate V:N:M-sparse matrix
186   multiplications (MMs) (Castro et al., 2023), as detailed in Appendix A. Due to the effective uti-
187   lization of higher sparsity greater than 50%, V:N:M-sparse MMs possess fewer computations than
188
      2:4-sparse MMs. Thus, for a dense Transformer, the V:N:M-sparse version typically achieves higher
      speedups than its 2:4 counterpart, with maximal end-to-end speedups reaching over 2x.

      method—which incorporates V and M selection, V:N:M-specific channel permutations, and three

349   The datasets used for the tasks include ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009), Cifar-10 and Cifar-100

351   four datasets are used to form downstream tasks. 2) Swin Transformers. This category of vision
352   Transformers, known for its hierarchical architecture and shifted window mechanisms, demonstrates
353
      increased sensitivity to model compression (Liu et al., 2021). In this work, the V:N:M-sparse Swin
      Transformers are assessed across two tasks: image classification on the ImageNet-1K dataset and
354
      object detection on the COCO 2017 dataset (Lin et al., 2014). 3) Llama2-7B on downstream tasks in-
355

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
      This study focuses on enhancing the accuracy and accuracy-speedup trade-offs of V:N:M-sparse
532
      Transformers in multiple scenarios. We address the crucial yet unexplored questions specific to
533
      V:N:M sparsity, including selecting appropriate values for V and M, and CP tailored for V:N:M
534   sparsity. Additionally, we propose a three-staged LoRA training technique, which for the first ex-
535   tends V:N:M sparsity to LLMs. Extensive experiments demonstrate that, with our methodology,
536   V:N:M-sparse Transformers can attain nearly lossless accuracy or perform comparably to those with
537   post-pruning 2:4 sparsity. Given its superior speed performance, we conclude that V:N:M sparsity is
538   more effective than 2:4 for compressing highly redundant Transformers in inference-cost-sensitive
539   scenarios. We hope our work to promote the widespread use of V:N:M sparsity as a truly effective
      solution for compressing Transformers.

      A    V:N:M SPARSITY DETAILS
716
717
718   As illustrated in Figure 6(a), the sparse weight matrix A is transformed into three smaller, more
719   compact matrices: An , Ai1 , and Ai2 . The matrix An contains the non-zero values from the sparse
720   matrix A, preserving their relative positions; that is, non-zero values in the same rows or columns of
721   A remain in the same rows or columns in An . The matrix Ai1 lists the indices of the four columns
722   within each block of An that are designated for 2:4 sparsity. Meanwhile, Ai2 mirrors the shape of
723
      An , with each non-zero value in An having a corresponding index in Ai2 that indicates its position
      among four consecutive elements. Figure 6(b) further illustrates the process of V:N:M-sparse matrix
724
      multiplication (MM) using sparse tensor cores. The primary function of the V:N:M-sparse MM
725
      kernel is to retrieve the retained weights and the corresponding tiles of the input matrix B. This is
726   done to align the data layout with that of a 2:4-sparse MM. By doing so, the sparse tensor core can
727   efficiently compute the output C in chunks.
