009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow matching has recently emerged as a powerful paradigm for generative model-
012            ing and has been extended to probabilistic time series forecasting in latent spaces.
013            However, the impact of the specific choice of probability path model on forecasting
014            performance remains under-explored. In this work, we demonstrate that forecasting
015            spatio-temporal data with flow matching is highly sensitive to the selection of the
016
               probability path model. Motivated by this insight, we propose a novel probability
               path model designed to improve forecasting performance. Our empirical results
017
               across various dynamical system benchmarks show that our model achieves faster
018
               convergence during training and improved predictive performance compared to
019            existing probability path models. Importantly, our approach is efficient during infer-
020            ence, requiring only a few sampling steps. This makes our proposed model practical
021            for real-world applications and opens new avenues for probabilistic forecasting.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Generative modeling has achieved remarkable success in recent years, especially for generating
027
      high-dimensional objects by learning mappings from simple, easily-sampled reference distributions,
      π0 , to complex target distributions, π1 . In particular, diffusion models have pushed the capabilities
028
      of generating realistic samples across various data modalities, including images (Ho et al., 2020;
029
      Song et al., 2020b; Karras et al., 2022), videos (Ho et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Gupta et al.,
030   2023), and spatio-temporal scientific data like climate and weather patterns (Pathak et al., 2024;
031   Kohl et al., 2024). Despite their impressive performance, diffusion models often come with high
032   computational costs during training and inference. Additionally, they typically assume a Gaussian
033   reference distribution, which may not be optimal for all data types and can limit modeling flexibility.
034
      One promising alternative is flow matching, where the mappings are learned via a stochastic process
035
      that transforms π0 into π1 through a random ordinary differential equation (ODE), approximating its
036   marginal vector flow (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2023;
037   Pooladian et al., 2023). While score-based generative models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al.,
038   2020a;b; Ho et al., 2020) are specific instances of flow matching, with Gaussian transition densities,
039   the general framework allows for a broader class of interpolating paths. This flexibility can lead
040   to deterministic sampling schemes that are faster and require fewer steps (Zhang & Chen, 2022).
041   Recent work has demonstrated the remarkable capabilities of flow matching models for generating
042   high-dimensional images (Esser et al., 2024) and discrete data (Gat et al., 2024).
043   Building on this, flow matching in latent space has recently been applied to forecasting spatio-
044   temporal data (Davtyan et al., 2023) — predicting future frames in videos. This approach leverages
045   latent representations to capture the complex dynamics inherent in temporal data. However, spatio-
046   temporal forecasting, especially for video and dynamical systems data, presents unique challenges. A
047   video prediction model capable of generalizing to new, unseen scenarios must implicitly “understand”
048   the scene: detecting and classifying objects, learning how they move and interact, estimating their
049   3D shapes and positions, and modeling the physical laws governing the environment (Battaglia
050
      et al., 2016). Similarly, accurate climate and weather forecasting requires capturing intricate physical
      processes and interactions across multiple scales (Dueben & Bauer, 2018; Schultz et al., 2021).
051
052   We observe that, in the context of spatio-temporal forecasting, the performance of flow matching is
053   highly sensitive to the choice of the probability path model, an important topic which has not been
      widely explored within a unified framework. Different probability paths can significantly impact the

054
      accuracy and convergence of forecasting models, particularly when dealing with complex dynamical
055   systems characterized by partial differential equations (PDEs) and chaotic behaviors. Motivated by
056   this, we propose a novel probability path model specifically designed for probabilistic forecasting of
057   dynamical systems. Our model leverages the continuous dynamics intrinsic to spatio-temporal data
058   by interpolating between consecutive sequential samples. This approach ensures better alignment
059   with the constructed flow, leading to improved predictive performance, more stable training, and
060   greater inference efficiency. Existing probability path models often fail to fully capture the continuous
061   nature of spatio-temporal data, resulting in a misalignment with flow-based methods and suboptimal
062   outcomes. Our proposed model addresses these limitations directly.
063   Building on previous approaches, we provide a theoretical framework and efficient algorithms
064   tailored to probabilistic forecasting using flow matching in latent space. Within this framework, we
065   demonstrate that our probability path model outperforms existing methods across several forecasting
066   tasks involving PDEs and other dynamical systems, achieving faster convergence during training
067   and requiring fewer sampling steps during inference. These advances enhance the practicality of
068   flow matching approaches for real-world applications, particularly in scenarios where computational
069
      resources and time are critical constraints. Our main contributions are the following.
070
071
      • Theoretical Framework and Efficient Algorithms: We present a theoretical framework and
        efficient algorithms for applying flow matching in latent space to the probabilistic forecasting
072
        of dynamical systems (see Algorithms 1-2), extending the approach of Lipman et al. (2022) and
073
        Davtyan et al. (2023). Our approach is specifically tailored for time series data, enabling effective
074     modeling of complex temporal dependencies inherent in dynamical systems.
075
076   • Novel Probability Path Model: We propose a new probability path model (Eq. (9)), specifically
077
        designed for modeling dynamical systems data. We provide intuitions to understand why our model
        leads to smoother training loss curve and faster convergence when compared to other models. We
078
        also provide theoretical insights to show that the variance of the vector field (VF) generating our
079
        proposed path can be lower than that of the optimal transport VF proposed by Lipman et al. (2022)
080     for sufficiently correlated spatio-temporal samples (see Theorem 3 in App. C).
081
082   • Empirical Validation: We provide extensive empirical results to demonstrate that our proposed
083     probability path model can outperform other flow matching models on several forecasting tasks
084
        involving PDEs and other dynamical systems (see Section 6). Our results demonstrate that the
        proposed probability path model outperforms existing flow matching models, achieving faster
085
        convergence during training and improved predictive performance.

088   2    R ELATED W ORK
089
090   Generative models have gained significant attention in learning complex data distributions, particularly
091   through the use of score-based diffusion models and flow-based models. These models have shown
092   promise in generating realistic high-dimensional data such as images (Song et al., 2020b; Karras
093   et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024), videos (Davtyan et al., 2023; Shrivastava & Shrivastava, 2024) and
094   time series (Meijer & Chen, 2024), and dynamical systems (Pathak et al., 2024; Kohl et al., 2024;
095   Ren et al., 2024). The recent work of Rasul et al. (2021); Biloš et al. (2023); Ruhe et al. (2024);
096   Kollovieh et al. (2024); Rühling Cachay et al. (2024) focuses on modeling time series with score-
097
      based diffusion models, which often require using many steps for sampling, whereas our proposed
      method requires as few as 10 steps. Related to our work is (Chen et al., 2024), which proposed a
098
      stochastic differential equation (SDE) framework using stochastic interpolants (Albergo et al., 2023)
099
      for probabilistic forecasting of time series, but their SDE based sampler typically requires many steps
100   during generation. Another relevant work is (Tamir et al., 2024), which proposed a flow matching
101   framework for time series modeling within the data space, but it focuses on small ODE datasets and
102   does not consider forecasting tasks.

      In this section, we present efficient algorithms for training and inferencing the flow matching model.
307   Recently Davtyan et al. (2023) proposed an efficient algorithm for latent flow matching for the task
308   of video prediction, using the probability path generated by the optimal transport VF of Lipman et al.
309   (2022). To enable efficient training, we shall follow Davtyan et al. (2023) and leverage the iterative
310   nature of sampling from the learned flow and use a single random conditioning element from the past
311   at each iteration. However, our method differs from Davtyan et al. (2023) as we shall use different
312
      probability paths and target vector fields.
313   Training. We set Z1 to be the target element and Z0 to be the reference element chosen to be the
314   previous element before the target element, i.e., if Z1 = z τ , then Z0 = z τ −1 , for τ = 2, . . . , m.
315   Note that this differs from Davtyan et al. (2023), where Z̃ = z1 (i.e., they do not use a reference
316   element to define their probability path, whereas we use a pair of elements (Z0 , Z1 )). In this way, our
317   probability path model maps a distribution centered around a previous state to the distribution of the
318   current states, which is more natural from the point of view of probabilistic forecasting whose goal is
319   to obtain an ensemble of forecasts. Algorithm 1 summarizes the training procedure of our method.
320
      Both the autoencoder and the VF neural network can also be jointly trained in an end-to-end manner,
      but our results show that separating the training can lead to improved performance. Moreover, doing
321
      so allows us to better assess the impact of using different probability paths.
322

328   procedure of Davtyan et al. (2023), which simply uses a mean-zero Gaussian sample instead. We then
329   use an ODE solver to integrate the learned vector field along the time interval [0, 1] to obtain YNT . We
330   use D(YNT ) as an estimate of xT , and forecasting is done autoregressively. Algorithm 2 summarizes
331   this procedure when the sampling is done using the forward Euler scheme. Note that we can also use
332   computationally more expensive numerical schemes such as the Runge-Kutta (RK) schemes.

512   6.3        D ISCUSSION
513
514   Our empirical results showed that our proposed model consistently outperformed other models
515   across different forecasting tasks involving different types of dynamical systems. Our model shows
516
      improved training efficiency, with faster convergence reducing the computational resources and time
      required for model training. Moreover, our model is efficient during inference time since it only
517
      requires a few sampling steps, making it practical for real-world applications where computational
518
      efficiency is crucial. Additionally, the model maintained better temporal consistency, as indicated
519   by a slower decay of Pearson correlation coefficients over longer prediction horizons, as well as
520   improved probabilistic accuracy and reliability in forecasting, as reflected by lower CRPS values.
521   These findings validate the effectiveness of our approach in modeling complex dynamical systems.
522
      Our ablation study further validated the advantage of our proposed probability path model. We found
523
      that larger σ values not only contributed to smoother training loss curves but also enhanced the
524
      overall stability and efficiency of the model. Moreover, we saw that σsam = 0 can be fixed without
525   compromising accuracy. The fact that our model achieved improved performance even with the
526   simplest sampler (Euler scheme) and a minimal number of sampling steps (as few as five) validates
527   its practical applicability, especially in scenarios where computational resources and time are limited.

      7       C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we investigated the use of flow matching in latent space for probabilistic forecasting
532
      of spatio-temporal dynamics, providing a theoretical framework and an efficient algorithm. We
533
      demystified the critical role of the probability path design in this setting and proposed an improved
534   probability path model. Our model leverages the inherent continuity and correlation in the spatio-
535   temporal data, leading to more stable training and faster convergence. Our empirical evaluations on
536   several PDE forecasting tasks demonstrated that our model performs favorably when compared to
537   existing models. These findings highlight that while flow matching techniques hold great promise
538   for probabilistic forecasting of spatio-temporal dynamics, it is important to select an appropriate
539   probability path model to achieve optimal test performance. Future directions include extending the
      framework to go beyond Gaussian probability paths to better capture heavy-tailed distributions.

       E.2   D ETAILS ON P RE -T RAINING THE AUTOENCODER
1163
       We provide details on pre-training the autoencoder here. The choice of first pre-training an autoen-
1164   coder is motivated by the computational challenges of working directly with the high-dimensional
1165   spatial resolution of PDE datasets. Training directly in the ambient space requires substantial GPU
1166   memory and computational resources, making it impractical for large-scale or high-resolution datasets.
1167   By leveraging a latent-space representation, we achieve significant dimensionality reduction while
1168   preserving the essential structure of the data, enabling efficient training and inference with standard
1169   hardware configurations. For these datasets, latent-space modeling provides a critical balance between
1170   computational efficiency, scalability, and performance.
1171   We use the same architecture for the encoder and decoder for all the tasks, with the architecture
1172   parameters chosen based on the complexity of the task.
1173
       The encoder. The encoder first applies a 2D convolution (conv_in) to the input frame, which
1174
       reduces the number of channels from in_channels to mid_channels, and processes the spatial
1175
       dimensions. Then, a series of ResidualBlock layers, which progressively process and downsample
1176   the feature map, making it smaller in spatial dimensions but more enriched in terms of features,
1177   are applied. After the residual blocks, the feature map undergoes an attention process via a multi-
1178   head attention layer. This layer helps the encoder focus on important parts of the input, learning
1179   relationships between spatial positions in the image. For the post-attention step, the feature map
1180   is further processed by residual blocks and normalized, preparing it for the final convolution. The
1181   output of the encoder is obtained by applying a final 2D convolution (out_conv), which maps the
1182   processed feature map to the desired number of output channels (out_channels).
1183   The decoder. The decoder takes the encoded feature map and transforms it back into an output with
1184   similar spatial dimensions as the input. Similar to the encoder, the decoder starts with a convolution
1185   that adjusts the number of channels from in_channels to mid_channels. Then, an attention
1186   mechanism (similar to the encoder) is applied to focus on important aspects of the encoded features.
1187   Next, a series of ResidualBlock layers, combined with UpBlock layers, are used to progressively
       increase the spatial dimensions of the feature map (upsampling), undoing the compression applied by


                                                         22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       the encoder. After the upsampling, the output is normalized and passed through a final convolution
1189   (out_conv), mapping the internal feature representation to the desired number of output channels
1190   (out_channels).

       Architecture. The vector field regressor is a transformer-based model designed to process latent
1210

1211

1217   through a series of transformer layers, with intermediate outputs from the input blocks concatenated
1218   with the output layers to refine predictions. Finally, the model projects the processed data back to the

1240
1241   In addition to the standard mean squared error and relative Frobenius norm error (RFNE), we use
       the Pearson correlation coefficient to measure the linear relationship between the forecasted frames


                                                          23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       and the target frames. The range of this coefficient is [−1, 1], with zero implying no correlation.
1243   Correlations of −1 or +1 imply an exact linear relationship. Positive correlations imply that as x
1244   increases, so does y. Negative correlations imply that as x increases, y decreases. In addition, we use
1245   peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) to evaluate the quality of signal representation against corrupting
1246   noise, and structural similarity index measure (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004) to assess perceptual results.
1247   The presented results are computed by averaging over batch size and number of sample generations.
1248
       To better quantify probabilistic forecasting performance, we use the Continuous Ranked Probability
1249   Score (CRPS) (Matheson & Winkler, 1976) to compare the predicted cumulative distribution function
1250   (CDF) to the actual observed value. More precisely, CRPS measures the compatibility of a CDF F
1251   with an scalar-valued observation x as:
1252                                                   Z
1253                                 CRP S(F, x) = (F (z) − 1{x≤z} )2 dz,                           (68)
                                                        R
1254
1255   where 1{x≤z} is the indicator function which is one if x ≤ z and zero otherwise. CRPS is a proper
1256   scoring function, attaining its minimum when the predictive distribution and the data distribution
1257   matches. Therefore, a lower CRPS value indicates that the predicted distribution is closer to the true
1258   outcome. For high-dimensional arrays (such as forecasts for multiple variables or at multiple spatial
1259
       locations), the CRPS can be extended by treating the multidimensional forecasts as multivariate
       distributions. In our experiments, we use 50 ensemble members to compute the empirical CDF, and
1260
       the CRPS values presented are obtained by averaging over batch size and data dimensions.
