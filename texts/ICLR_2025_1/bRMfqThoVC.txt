011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Data scarcity poses a significant challenge in training machine learning models
014            for stock forecasting, often leading to low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and data
015            homogeneity that degrade model performance. To address these issues, we in-
016
               troduce DiffsFormer, a novel approach utilizing artificial intelligence-generated
               samples (AIGS) with a Transformer-based Diffusion Model. Initially trained on a
017
               large-scale source domain with conditional guidance to capture global joint distri-
018
               bution, DiffsFormer augments training by editing existing samples for specific
019            downstream tasks, allowing control over the deviation of generated data from
020            the target domain. We evaluate DiffsFormer on two datasets using eight com-
021            monly used machine learning models, achieving relative improvements of 7.3%
022            and 22.1% in excess return, respectively. Extensive experiments provide insights
023            into DiffsFormer’s functionality and its components, illustrating their roles in mit-
024            igating data scarcity and enhancing model performance.

029   Accurate stock forecasting plays a crucial role in effective asset management and investment strate-
030   gies (Zou et al., 2022). Its objective is to predict future stock behavior (e.g., return ratios or prices)
031   by analyzing relevant historical factors. Previous research (Zhang et al., 2017b; Feng et al., 2019;
032
      Xu et al., 2021) has explored various machine learning techniques; however, achieving desirable per-
      formance with these methods often requires an ample supply of high-quality data. The challenges
033
      posed by high random and homogeneous data make it difficult to meet the requirements for data
034
      quality, resulting in elevated forecasting errors and increased uncertainty. Figure 1 demonstrates the
035   significance of addressing the data scarcity issue. As demonstrated, when this challenge is miti-
036   gated, the model exhibits a progressive and substantial excess return (§2 Eq.(3)). This improvement
037   highlights the potential performance gains achievable through effective data augmentation strategies.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
163
164   The stock forecasting task is challenging primarily because of the scarcity of data. To harness the full
165   potential of machine learning models, a sufficient amount of high-quality data is crucial. However,
166   obtaining such high-quality stock data for a specific target domain is rare and can often be restricted
167   as commercial secrets. In this work, we utilize the power of DM and introduce a novel approach,
168   DiffsFormer. It generates additional data points and facilitates factor augmentation, enabling us to
169   forecast the likely RR of real-world stocks despite data scarcity.

      3.3   C ONDITIONAL D IFFUSION AUGMENTATION
289
290   Most generative tasks do not have the demands for label generation. However, in the stock forecast-
291   ing task, a clean and informative supervised signal is essential for training the forecaster. According
292   our experiments in Appendix E.3, we suppose that direct generated label fails to serve as the accurate
293   supervised signal for the generated feature. As an alternative, we pave the way to control the syn-
294   thesis process through guidance inputs, including labels and industry information (Rombach et al.,
295
      2022). We can expect that the generated factors will align with the sectors and labels of the original
      factors, thereby enabling DiffsFormer to generate labels. Our inspiration is drawn from classifier-
296
      free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), and since our labels are continuous rather than discrete, we
297
      refer to this mechanism as “predictor-free guidance.”
298
299   Technically, according to (Ho & Salimans, 2022), the guiding effect can be achieved by jointly
300   training conditional and unconditional DMs. Specifically, the inference process is in the form of:
301                                   ϵ̂θ (xt , c) = ϵθ (xt , ∅) + ω · (ϵθ (xt , c) − ϵθ (xt , ∅)),                             (10)
302
303
      where c denotes the condition vectors and ∅ denotes a learnable null vector. During training, c is
304   randomly replaced with ∅ with a fixed probability to train an unconditional DM. As the guidance
305   strength ω gets larger, DM receives more rewards when generating xt having a high probability
306   pθ (c|xt ). Note that ω shall be greater than 1 to be effective. The advantages of predictor-free
307   guidance are: 1) it is a simple approach since no auxiliary predictor is needed; 2) it is flexible since
308   it supports other types of conditionings beyond return-ratio labels. In our work, we further explore
309   the use of industry information. We observe that stocks in different industries tend to perform in
310   different patterns. For instance, financial stocks (e.g., banks) usually have low yields but enjoy low
311   volatility, while many information technology stocks have high yields but undertake high volatility.
312
      Furthermore, we can synthesize industry-specific data to improve model performance in specific
      industry sector. One of the unappealing properties of the predictor-free guidance is that it injects the
313
      conditionings during the training of DMs. As a result, when adding or modifying conditionings, we
314
      need to retrain the DMs although it is time-consuming.
315

      3.4   L OSS - GUIDED N OISE A DDITION
317
318   We identify that there are certain easy-fitted points within the dataset, and we hypothesize that
319   alleviating the overfitting issues associated with these extreme data points can reduce volatility.
320   § 4.2 illustrates the training loss over time. Notably, the loss for stock forecasting remains quite low
321   during the stock market crash from June 2015 to June 2016, which we suspect is due to the increased
322   proportion of retail investment, characterized by simpler action patterns. A model that overly fits
323   the data from around 2015 is likely to struggle in the present, as market dynamics have become
      more complex. However, discarding this data is sub-optimal, as it would exacerbate data scarcity.


                                                                   6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      To address this, we propose a novel strategy termed loss-guided noise addition. Specifically, we
325   utilize training loss as a proxy to introduce stronger noise to data points with lower training loss.
326   As demonstrated in Figure 7c, loss-guided diffusion results in flatter training losses compared to
327   uniform noise addition, effectively alleviating overfitting and decreasing volatility.

331   Figure 5 elucidates the overall framework of our stock price forecasting model. The framework is
332   designed with several considerations: 1) DMs acts as a plug-and-play data augmentation module, so

524   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
525
526   Conclusion. We address the critical challenge of data scarcity in stock forecasting by introducing
527   DiffsFormer. Our approach augments stock factors using label and sector information, while incor-
528   porating transfer learning in a Diffusion Model framework. By training on a larger source domain
529   and synthesizing with target domain data, DiffsFormer effectively distills new knowledge, mitigat-
530   ing data limitations and enhancing forecasting accuracy. This work pioneers data augmentation in
531   stock forecasting using diffusion models, opening avenues for future research. We find that con-
532   ditioning on factors like industry sectors enhances performance, suggesting potential for targeted
533
      improvements through factor editing or generating stocks with specific attributes. Our study also
      underscores the challenges of homogeneity in stock forecasting. Limitations are listed in E.4.

887   C.10    M ORE A RCHITECTURE I MPROVEMENTS
888
889   Following (Peebles & Xie, 2022), we adjust the transformer structure to fit predictor-free guidance.
890   Specifically, as shown in Figure 5, we feed the diffusion steps into the transformer module. Further-
891   more, it contains an adaptive layer norm module and a zero-initialized scalar module.
892   Time step encoding. During the training, DiffsFormer needs to know which diffusion step it is
893   trained for. In our work, we encode the current diffusion step into Sinusoidal positional encoding and
894   add it to the feed forward network. This embedding scheme is appropriate to encode the time step
895   information, the positional encoding at position p + k can be linearly represented by the positional
896   encoding at position p.
897   Adaptive layer norm (ALN). Adaptive layer norm (Perez et al., 2018) are widely adopted in gen-
898   erative models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Brock et al., 2019). In the transformer block, we append
899   an additional ALN layer which regresses the scale and shift parameters γ and β from two affine
900   transformations f and h to the sum of conditioning vectors.
901
      Zero initialization. In addition to ALN layer, many DMs (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al.,
902
      2020; Peebles & Xie, 2022) also incorporate zero initialization in the framework, meaning that the
903   model parameters are initialized as zero such that the conditioning is ineffective when training just
904   starts. In this case, MLP is initialized to make α1 and α2 equal to 0, and thus transformer block
905   becomes an identity function (Peebles & Xie, 2022; Goyal et al., 2017) (i.e., input tokens are directly
906   fed to the next layer).
907
      Time improvement. As stated in §3.2, we initialize α and β with total diffusion steps T to
908
      ensure correctness; however, we sample training step t from U nif orm{1, 2, · · · , T ′ } instead of
909   U nif orm{1, 2, · · · , T }: compared to traditional DM, the probability of sampling useful steps that
910   are smaller than T ′ is increased. The loss curves with maximum sampling steps within the set {100,
911   300, 500, 700, 1000} are elucidated in Figure 9. Note that the figure represents the average loss
912   within sampling step 100 instead of diffusion step 1000. We discover that with the decrease of
913   sampling steps, DMs embrace with a more sharp loss curve, which means they can converge faster.

      D      R ELATED W ORKS
917
      In this section, we will introduce related works in stock forecasting.


                                                          17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Stock forecasting is a field that utilizes historical time-series data to predict future stock prices.
919   Machine learning models, particularly time-series models such as LSTM, GRU, and Bi-LSTM,
920   have gained popularity in this domain (Zou et al., 2022; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chung
921   et al., 2014; Graves & Schmidhuber, 2005; Xia et al., 2024).
922
      Researchers have proposed tailored models to better fit the financial scenario. For example, Li et
923
      al. (Li et al., 2018) introduce extra input gates to extract positive and negative correlations between
924
      factors. Ding et al. (Ding & Qin, 2020) propose a novel LSTM model to simultaneously predict
925   the opening, lowest, and highest prices of a stock. Agarwal et al. (Rather et al., 2015) propose a
926   hybrid prediction model (HPM) that combines three time-series models. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al.,
927   2017b) propose a State Frequency Memory (SFM) network that decomposes the hidden states of
928   memory cells into multiple frequency components to model different latent trading patterns. Feng
929   et al. (Feng et al., 2019) incorporate a temporal attentive aggregation layer and adversarial training
930   into an LSTM variant. Chen et al. (Chen et al., 2019) use Bi-LSTM to encode stock data and
931   financial news representations in their SSPM and MSSPM models.
932   CNNs are also believed to capture important features for predicting stock fluctuations. For instance,
933   Deng et al. (Deng et al., 2019) propose the Knowledge-Driven Temporal Convolutional Network
934   (KDTCN), which integrates knowledge graphs with CNNs to fully utilize industrial relations. Lu
935   et al. (Lu et al., 2021) enhance a CNN-based model by extracting historical influential stock fluc-
936   tuations with attention mechanism. Chandar (Chandar, 2022) transforms technical indicators into
937   images and used them as input for a CNN model.
938   To handle non-Euclidean structured data, some researchers have incorporated Graph Neural Net-
939   works (GNNs) into stock forecasting. Velickovic et al. (Velickovic et al., 2018) construct a graph
940   with stocks as nodes and used graph attention network (GAT) to aggregate neighbor embeddings.
941   Xu et al. (Xu et al., 2022) construct a stock market relationship graph and extracted information
942   hierarchically. Li et al. (Li et al., 2020) propose an LSTM Relational Graph Convolutional Network
943   (LSTM-RGCN) model that handles both positive and negative correlations among stocks.
944   The Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017), with self-attention and positional encoding mech-
945   anisms, has shown great potential in stock forecasting. Ding et al. (Ding et al., 2020) improve
946   the Transformer by incorporating multi-scale Gaussian prior, optimizing locality, and implement-
947   ing Orthogonal Regularization. Yoo et al. (Yoo et al., 2021) propose a Data-axis Transformer
948   with Multi-Level Contexts (DTML) to learn the correlations between stocks. Yang et al. (Yang
949   et al., 2020a) introduce the Hierarchical, Transformer-based, multi-task (HTML) model for predict-
950   ing short-term and long-term asset volatility. FTS-Diffusion (Huang et al., 2024) consists of three
951
      modules to model irregular and scale-invariant patterns and generate synthetic financial time series.
