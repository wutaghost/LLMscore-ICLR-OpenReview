009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning has emerged as a powerful tool for atmospheric sciences, showing
012            significant utility across various tasks in weather and climate modeling. In line
013            with recent progress in language and vision foundation models, there are growing
014            efforts to scale and finetune such models for multi-task spatiotemporal reasoning.
015            Despite promising results, existing works often evaluate their model on a small set
016            of non-uniform tasks, which makes it hard to quantify broad generalization across
017            diverse tasks and domains. To address this challenge, we introduce AtmosArena,
018            the first multi-task benchmark dedicated to foundation models in atmospheric
019
               sciences. AtmosArena comprises a suite of tasks that cover a broad spectrum of
               applications in atmospheric physics and atmospheric chemistry. To showcase the
020
               capabilities and key features of our benchmark, we conducted extensive experi-
021
               ments to evaluate two state-of-the-art deep learning models, ClimaX and Stormer
022            on AtmosArena, and compare their performance with other deep learning and
023            traditional baselines. By providing a standardized, open-source benchmark, we aim
024            to facilitate further advancements in the field, much like open-source benchmarks
025            have driven the development of foundation models for language and vision.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Modeling of large-scale atmospheric systems is an omnipresent challenge for science and society.
031   Traditionally, numerical methods are the dominating approach in atmospheric sciences, which
032   operationalize rigorous systems of differential equations to simulate such phenomena (Lynch, 2008;
033   Bauer et al., 2015). Despite their widespread use in practice, numerical methods suffer from many
034   challenges, such as inadequate resolution of important small-scale physical processes and substantial
035   computational demands (Balaji et al., 2017; Lavers et al., 2022; Leung et al., 2003; Rauscher et al.,
036   2010). Deep learning has emerged as a powerful complement due to its ability to learn complex
037   systems from historical data and produce fast predictions within seconds. Deep learning methods
038   have proven great utility and performance across various atmospheric tasks, including but not limited
039
      to precipitation nowcasting (Ravuri et al., 2021b; Sønderby et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2023),
      medium-range weather forecasting (Weyn et al., 2020; Rasp & Thuerey, 2021; Keisler, 2022; Pathak
040
      et al., 2022b; Bi et al., 2022; Lam et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2023b;a; Kochkov
041
      et al., 2023), climate projection (Watson-Parris et al., 2022b), climate downscaling (Baño Medina
042   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Nagasato et al., 2021; Rodrigues et al., 2018; Sachindra et al., 2018;
043   Vandal et al., 2019), air pollution forecasting (Ayturan et al., 2018; Bekkar et al., 2021; Tao et al.,
044   2019; Bui et al., 2018; Heydari et al., 2022), and greenhouse gas emission prediction (Hamrani et al.,
045   2020; Bakay & Ağbulut, 2021; Altikat, 2021).
046
      Recent years have witnessed a paradigm shift from training task-specific models to developing
047
      foundation models for atmospheric sciences (Nguyen et al., 2023a; Bodnar et al., 2024), similar to
048   models such as GPT-x (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023) in natural language processing, or
049   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) in computer vision. These foundation models are trained on large-scale
050   and diverse datasets, enabling them to develop a rich, general understanding of the atmosphere. Once
051   pre-trained, they can adapt efficiently to various downstream tasks, ranging from weather nowcasting
052   to long-term climate projections, via lightweight finetuning. This approach is particularly attractive
053   for atmospheric sciences, where there is an increasing availability of high-quality datasets and tasks
      have non-trivial global and regional structure.

      Standardized open-source benchmarks are crucial for the advancement of foundation models. In lan-
      guage, benchmarks such as HeLM (Liang et al., 2022), LLM Foundry, LM Evaluation Harness (Gao
074
      et al., 2023), and Big Bench (Srivastava et al., 2022) have aided researchers to systematically evaluate
075
      the performance of large language models. Similarly, for perception, comprehensive benchmarks
076   such as VQA (Antol et al., 2015), SciBench (Wang et al., 2023), MMMU (Yue et al., 2023), and
077   MathVista (Lu et al., 2023), have significantly accelerated research in multimodal foundation models.
078   In stark contrast, there is no standardized multi-task benchmark for benchmarking atmospheric foun-
079   dation models and existing works (Nguyen et al., 2023a; Bodnar et al., 2024) limit their evaluation to
080   a relatively small set of non-overlapping tasks, which creates challenges in objective assessment of
081   progress in the field.
082   To address this gap, we introduce AtmosArena, an open-source benchmark for foundation models
083   in atmospheric sciences. To the best of our knowledge, AtmosArena is the first of its kind to offer a
084   comprehensive evaluation framework tailored for this domain. AtmosArena encompasses a suite of
085   tasks that span a wide spectrum of problems from both atmospheric and machine learning perspectives.
086   Each task within AtmosArena is supported by datasets, fine-tuning protocols, evaluation code,
087   standardized evaluation metrics, and a collection of deep learning and traditional baselines. This
088   suite not only facilitates a fair assessment of model performance but also serves as a crucial tool
089   for identifying opportunities for future development in the field. AtmosArena aims to set a new
090
      standard in the evaluation of atmospheric models, providing a solid foundation for the development of
      new methodologies. Table 1 summarizes the tasks, datasets, and metrics supported by AtmosArena.
091
092   To showcase the utility of AtmosArena, we conduct extensive experiments across all tasks included
093   in the benchmark. We test and compare three representative classes of models: (1) deep learning
094   with no pretraining, (2) single-source pretraining, and (3) multi-source pretraining. We also include
095   traditional methods as simple baselines. To ensure fairness, we maintained consistent fine-tuning and
096
      evaluation settings across all models. The experimental results indicate that pretrained models gener-
      ally outperform baselines without pretraining in most tasks. However, no single model consistently
097
      dominates across all tasks. This underscores the comprehensiveness of AtmosArena and highlights
098
      potential opportunities for future model development. In line with our commitment to openness and
099   reproducibility, we will make all our data, code, and model checkpoints publicly available.

105   in recent years in both speed and accuracy. In weather forecasting, notable models like Pangu (Bi
106   et al., 2022), Graphcast (Lam et al., 2023), and Stormer (Nguyen et al., 2023c) have surpassed the
107   accuracy of the gold-standard IFS HRES system. This progress spans from simple models like
      ResNet (Rasp & Thuerey, 2021) to advanced architectures such as Graph Neural Networks (Keisler,

146   3    K EY C OMPONENTS OF A T M O S A R E N A
147
148
      As a first benchmark, we aim to build a comprehensive suite of tasks in atmospheric sciences,
149
      emphasizing diversity from both domain-specific and machine learning perspectives. Domain-wise,
150   tasks are broadly classified into atmospheric physics or atmospheric chemistry. Atmospheric physics
151   focuses on physical variables like temperature, humidity, and wind, essential for modeling weather
152   patterns in the short-term and climate trends in the longer term. Atmospheric chemistry, on the other
153   hand, focuses on the composition and transformation of atmospheric constituents, such as pollutants
154   like carbon monoxide and dioxide, crucial for studying air quality and environmental health.
155
      Due to space constraints, this section presents the six tasks under atmospheric physics: Medium-range
156   Weather Forecasting, S2S Forecasting, Extreme Weather Events Detection, Climate Downscaling,
157   Climate Data Infilling, and Climate Model Emulation. Tasks related to atmospheric chemistry are
158   detailed in Appendix G. From a machine learning perspective, many common predictive tasks in
159   atmospheric sciences can be mapped to well-defined problems in machine learning. Within this
160   perspective, our benchmark can be seen as spanning five distinct categories of tasks defined on a grid:
161   forecasting, segmentation, super-resolution, inpainting, and counterfactual prediction. This diverse
      suite of tasks allows us to obtain a holistic evaluation of atmospheric foundation models.

      3.1   TASKS
163
164   Medium-range weather forecasting is the task of predicting the global weather conditions at a
165   future time step t + T given the weather conditions at or before the current step t, where the lead time
166   T ranges from a few hours to two weeks. A deep learning model takes an input of shape V × H × W
167   and outputs a prediction of shape V ′ × H × W , in which V and V ′ are the numbers of input and
168   output atmospheric variables, respectively, while H × W denotes the spatial resolution of the data.
169   Sub-seasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) forecasting is similar to medium-range forecasting but with a
170   longer lead time range between 2 weeks and 2 months (Vitart & Robertson, 2018; Vitart et al., 2022).
171   This task bridges the gap between weather forecasting and climate modeling and holds significant
172   socioeconomic value in disaster mitigation, but has received much less attention than the other two
173   well-established tasks. Since the weather becomes too chaotic for any model to perform accurate
174   point prediction after two weeks, we instead task the models to forecast the average statistics of key
175   variables over a two-week window.
176   Extreme weather events detection is the task of identifying weather patterns that may lead to
177   extreme weather events, such as tropical cyclones and atmospheric rivers. Deep learning models are
178   trained to perform pixel-level detection and segmentation of these events in climate data. Specifically,
179   the input typically consists of key atmospheric variables, and the output is a segmented map where
180   each pixel is classified as part of an extreme event or as background. This approach allows for precise
181   quantification of the frequency, intensity, and spatial extent of extreme events under various climate
182
      scenarios, providing valuable insights for climate research and policy-making.
183   Climate downscaling is the task of improving the spatial resolution of climate model outputs, which
184   typically operate on large grid cells due to their high computational demands. This refinement is
185   crucial for accurately representing local phenomena and informing regional policy decisions. In this
186   task, deep learning models transform an input grid of dimensions V × H × W into a higher-resolution
187   output V ′ × H ′ × W ′ , where H ′ > H and W ′ > W .
188   Climate data infilling involves estimating missing or incomplete data in historical and current
189   climate datasets. This task aims to provide a more comprehensive and continuous historical record
190   of important atmospheric variables, such as near-surface air temperature, enabling robust climate
191   analysis and modeling. In data infilling, deep learning models are trained to predict missing values
192   by leveraging patterns found in available data. The typical input to these models includes incomplete
193   datasets of dimensions V × H × W , and the output is a complete dataset of the same dimensions,
194
      where the previously missing values are estimated by the model.
195   Climate model emulation involves predicting the annual mean global distributions of crucial climate
196   variables like surface temperature and precipitation indices, given different scenarios of anthropogenic
197   forcing factors such as carbon dioxide (CO2 ) and methane (CH4 ). The input is a tensor of shape
198   T × V × H × W which captures the forcing conditions over T consecutive years, and the output
199   shape is V ′ × H × W . Unlike temporal forecasting, this task assesses a model’s ability to predict
200
      the response of the climate system to varying levels of external factors, providing a foundation for
      long-term climate strategy and policy decisions.
201
202
      3.2   DATASETS
203
204   ERA5 maintained by ECMWF (Hersbach et al., 2020) is a common dataset for training and evaluating
205   data-driven methods in atmospheric sciences (Bi et al., 2022; Lam et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023c).
206   ERA5 is a reanalysis dataset that provides the best guess of different climate variables at any point in
207   time by integrating observational data with an advanced forecasting model known as the Integrated
208   Forecasting System (IFS) (Wedi et al., 2015). ERA5 offers hourly data from 1979 to the present and
209   at a 0.25◦ (721 × 1440) global grid, totaling nearly 400,000 data points at 37 different pressure levels.
210   Given its extensive scale, we regrid the original data to 1.40625◦ (128 × 256) grid and consider
211   data from 1979 to 2020 for training and evaluation. We use ERA5 for four tasks in AtmosArena,
212
      including medium-range weather forecasting, S2S forecasting, climate downscaling, and data infilling.
213   Berkeley Earth provides a variety of high-quality temperature data products that incorporate a large
214   set of temperature observations (Rohde & Hausfather, 2020). In AtmosArena, we use the global
215   monthly average temperature data at 1◦ (180×360) grid as an independent test dataset for the infilling
      task. We regrid the data to the common resolution of 1.40625◦ .

      ClimateBench is a benchmark for testing data-driven methods for climate model emulation (Watson-
217   Parris et al., 2022b). ClimateBench consists of simulation outputs of the Norwegian Earth System
218   Model (NorESM2) (Seland et al., 2020) from CMIP6 (Eyring et al., 2016a) that are run under
219   different forcing scenarios for the period 2015 − 2100. The dataset includes four input forcing factors
220   – carbon dioxide (CO2 ), sulfur dioxide (SO2 ), black carbon (BC), and methane (CH4 ), and the annual
221   mean global distributions of four target variables – surface temperature, diurnal temperature range,
222   precipitation, and the 90th percentile of precipitation.
223   ClimateNet is an expert-labeled dataset of tropical cyclones (TCs) and atmospheric rivers (ARs), two
224   important weather patterns that may lead to extreme weather events (Prabhat et al., 2020). ClimateNet
225   consists of 459 data points of simulation runs of the Community Atmospheric Model (CAM5.1) from
226   1996 − 2013. Each data point has a spatial resolution of 768 × 1152 with a total of 16 atmospheric
227   variables, and each pixel is labeled with one of three classes – TCs, ARs, and Background.

258   share similar transformer architectures and training objectives, comparing them helps examine if and
259   when multi-source pretraining is beneficial to the model. ClimaX has 100M parameters.

261   3.4   F INETUNING PROTOCOLS
262
263   ClimaX and Stormer share a similar architecture, which consists of an embedding layer, a transformer
264   backbone, and a prediction head. The embedding layer transforms an input of shape V × H × W
265   to a sequence of shape (H/p × W/p) × D, where (H/p × W/p) is the sequence length, p is the
266
      patch size, and D is the hidden dimension. The transformer backbone processes this sequence and
      outputs a sequence of the same shape, and finally the prediction head outputs a prediction of shape
267
      V ′ × H ′ × W ′ . We refer to the original papers for a detailed description of these models.
268
269   We consider two finetuning settings, one where we freeze the core transformer backbone, and the other
      where we finetune the entire network. The frozen setting helps examine the direct transferability of the

270
      pretrained backbone to new tasks without further training. In tasks where the input or target variables
271   were unseen during pretraining, we replace the pretrained embedding layer and prediction head with
272   newly initialized networks. For datasets having a different spatial resolution from pretraining data,
273   we interpolate the pretrained positional embedding to match the new sequence length.

      5            C ONCLUSION
509
      We presented AtmosArena, the first benchmark dedicated to foundation models in atmospheric
510
      sciences. AtmosArena offers a diverse suite of tasks, datasets, and evaluation metrics to evaluate a
511
      foundation model holistically. AtmosArena not only provides a standard benchmark for comparing
512   model performance but also serves as a crucial tool for identifying future research works. In
513   addition, we release all our data, code, and model checkpoints, facilitating reproducible research and
514   broadening collaborations. Given the vast development of scientific foundation models, we believe
515   our contribution is timely and useful for both machine learning and atmospheric communities.
516
      Limitations and Future Work With academic resource constraints, we acknowledge that there are
517
      various directions to improve AtmosArena in each of four dimensions – datasets, tasks, models, and
518   evaluations. One such direction involves integrating regional datasets and expanding the collection of
519   supported data sources. On the task side, we plan to include probabilistic tasks that are an important
520   aspect of modeling weather and climate. For models and evaluations, we plan to find platforms for
521   hosting atmospheric foundation models, along with an accompanying leaderboard.

1201   E.1.1    E XTENSIONS FOR CLIMATE MODEL EMULATION
1202
1203
       We modify the architecture of ClimaX and Stormer for this task to account for the time dimension T
       in the input. Each time slice of the input goes through the embedding layer and the transformer blocks
1204
       independently, resulting in an output tensor of shape T × h × w × D where D is the embedding
1205
       dimension. This tensor then goes through a global pooling layer along the spatial dimensions h and w,
1206   outputting a tensor of shape T × D. This sequence of tensors is aggregated by a cross-attention layer
1207   over the time dimension to a single vector of D dimensions. Finally, a linear layer predicts the output
1208   of shape V × H × W . The cross-attention layer along the time dimension is randomly initialized and
1209   trained together with the new embedding and prediction layer, as well as the transformer backbone.
1210
1211   E.1.2    E XTENSIONS FOR EXTREME WEATHER EVENTS DETECTION
1212
1213   Since the spatial resolution of ClimateNet is 768 × 1152, training the original ClimaX and Stormer
1214
       with patch sizes of 4 and 2, respectively, is too computationally expensive. To address this issue, we
       use a stack of 6 convolutional layers to embed the input before the attention blocks which outputs
1215
       a tensor of shape 96 × 144 × D, reducing the spatial resolution by 8. This tensor goes through the
1216
       transformer blocks and a linear prediction head which outputs a tensor of shape 3 × 96 × 144 where 3
1217   is the number of classes. Finally, this output is bilinearly interpolated to the original spatial resolution
1218   of 768 × 1152. The bilinear interpolation module is also used by the baseline CGNet (Wu et al.,
1219   2020).

       G     ATMOSPHERIC CHEMISTRY
1459
1460
1461
       This section presents the atmospheric chemistry tasks that AtmosArena includes.
1462
1463   G.1     ATMOSPHERIC CHEMISTRY DOWNSCALING
1464
1465   Atmospheric chemistry simulations are essential for understanding various global processes such
1466   as air pollution, biogeochemical cycles, and climate change. High-resolution models can capture
1467   fine-scale chemical interactions, providing insights into local pollution levels and their health impacts.
1468   However, these models are computationally intensive. Deep learning offers a solution by transforming
1469   coarse-resolution inputs into finer-resolution outputs (Geiss et al., 2022). Specifically, the input is a
1470
       grid of dimensions V × H × W , and the output is a higher-resolution grid V ′ × H ′ × W ′ , where
       H ′ > H and W ′ > W . This allows for precise monitoring of atmospheric pollutants and their
1471
       effects on human health and the environment, enabling more informed policy decisions and scientific
1472
       research.
1473
1474   Dataset We utilize GEOS-CF, a simulated dataset from the NASA GEOS Composition Forecast
1475   (GEOS-CF) system (Knowland et al., 2022). GEOS-CF combines the NASA GEOS model with the
1476
       GEOS-Chem chemical transport model to simulate the atmospheric composition (Keller et al., 2021).
       The dataset offers outputs on a 0.25◦ grid, which we downsample to 5.625◦ for the low-resolution
1477
       input and 1.40625◦ for the high-resolution output. For our benchmark, we use the meteorological
1478
       replay simulation (“das” files), covering the years 2018 to the present. We focus on downscaling the
1479   five near-surface atmospheric pollutants: NO2, SO2, CO, O3, and PM2.5, averaged over a 1-hour
1480   window (“chm_tavg_1hr” files).
1481
1482
1483   G.2     ATMOSPHERIC COMPOSITION FORECASTING
1484
1485
       This task involves predicting the global atmospheric composition of important air pollutants such as
       carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide at different lead times. This task is crucial for understanding air
1486
       quality, which directly impacts human health by influencing the prevalence of non-communicable
1487
       diseases. This task presents a significant challenge to data-driven models due to the complexity of
1488   atmospheric dynamics and the influence of human activities on emission levels. The task formulation
1489   and input and output shapes are similar to weather forecasting.
1490
1491
       Dataset We use CAMS Analysis maintained by ECMWF for the atmospheric composition fore-
       casting task in AtmosArena. As part of the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS),
1492
       this dataset integrates meteorological variables with concentrations of air pollutants such as carbon
1493
       monoxide and carbon dioxide, providing a comprehensive overview of global atmospheric compo-
1494   sition. The dataset offers 12-hourly data at a 0.4◦ (450 × 900 grids) resolution from 2017 to the
1495   present. Similar to ERA5, we regrid this dataset to the common resolution of 1.40625◦ for easier
1496   training and evaluation.
1497
