009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose LogicVista, an evaluation benchmark that examines multimodal large
012            language models’ (MLLMs) integrated Logical reasoning capacities in Visual
013            contexts. Recent advancements in MLLMs have demonstrated various fascinating
014            abilities such as crafting poetry based on an image to engaging in mathematical
015            reasoning. Despite these feats, there remains a gap in the systematic examina-
016            tion of MLLMs’ proficiency in logical reasoning tasks. These skills are routinely
017            invoked in navigation, puzzle-solving, etc. Thus we present LogicVista, which
018
               evaluates general logical cognition abilities across a spectrum of 5 logical reasoning
               tasks with 3 broad capabilities and 11 specific capabilities through a sample of
019
               448 multiple-choice questions. Each is annotated with not only the correct answer
020
               but also the human-written reasoning behind the selection, allowing for rich open-
021            ended evaluation as well as MCQ evaluation. A total of 11 MLLMs undergo com-
022            prehensive evaluation using LogicVista. We are also introducing a crowdsourced
023            annotation tool to further scale LogicVista with support from the community. Code
024            and Data Available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LogicVista.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) are gradually turning the vision of a
030   generalist AI agent into reality. These models exhibit near-human expert-level performance across a
031   variety of tasks and have recently been augmented with visual understanding capabilities, enabling
032   them to tackle even more complex visual challenges. This branch of work, led by both proprietary
033
      projects such as Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) and open-source
      works such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), Mini-GPT4 (Zhu et al., 2023) enhances existing LLMs by
034
      incorporating visual comprehension. These models, known as Multimodal Large Language Models
035
      (MLLMs), utilize LLMs as the foundation for processing information and generating reasoned
036   outcomes (Yin et al., 2023), bridging the gap between language and vision. Recent MLLMs have
037   demonstrated a range of such intriguing abilities, such as writing poems based on an image (Fu
038   et al., 2023a), engaging in mathematical reasoning (Alayrac et al., 2022), and even aiding in medical
039   diagnosis (Zhang et al., 2023).
040
      Challenges Many works have benchmarked MLLM’s performance on common multimodal tasks
041
      such as recognizing objects (Antol et al., 2015), understanding the text in an image (Singh et al.,
042   2019a), or performing math (Yu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024). However, there are two major concerns
043   with existing benchmarks: lack of evaluation of explicit logical-visual-language reasoning skills and
044   potential data leakage in benchmarking data.
045
      Evaluating explicit logical-visual-language reasoning is essential, as it reflects a key aspect of human
046
      creativity and intelligence. Proficiency in reasoning skills is widely recognized as a reliable indicator
047
      of cognitive ability across various domains (Kahneman, 2012; Träff et al., 2019). While many datasets
048   and benchmarks have been designed to assess the logical reasoning capabilities of AI agents, most
049   are limited to text-based formats, leaving visual reasoning largely underexplored (Liu et al., 2023;
050   Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Lin, 2024; Yang et al., 2024). While some datasets like GLoRE,
051   MathVista, MM-vet, and RAVEN (liu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
052   2019) have explored aspects of visual logical reasoning, they focus primarily on specific tasks such
053   as mathematical reasoning, spatial reasoning, or world knowledge retrieval, with logical reasoning
      only partially embedded and not directly analyzed. General-purpose visual question answering and


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      captioning datasets like TextVQA and VQAv2 (Goyal et al., 2017a; Singh et al., 2019a) contain even
055   fewer examples of visual logical reasoning, concentrating instead on the recognition and identification
056   of visual details. Similarly, specialized benchmarks such as MMMU and OlympiadBench (Yue et al.,
057   2024; He et al., 2024) focus on academic domain questions in subjects like math, science, or history,
058   without directly evaluating the visual logical reasoning capabilities of modern MLLMs.
059
      Moreover, many existing benchmarks rely on publicly available data from the internet, which can
060
      easily be included in the training datasets of various models due to its low friction for scraping
061   (as demonstrated in Appendix K). This increases the likelihood that many benchmarking samples
062   are inadvertently leaked into the training data, leading to unfair comparisons of models that do not
063   effectively isolate their reasoning capabilities. In Appendix A, we provide a more comprehensive
064   overview of the gaps in the current literature on MLLM benchmarks.
065
      Recently, MLLMs have demonstrated impressive problem-solving and understanding capabilities
066
      across various domains. Researchers have aimed to strengthen these models’ logical reasoning
067   abilities through novel pre-training techniques, such as directly embedding logical reasoning, as
068   demonstrated with IDOL (Xu et al., 2023). However, their capacity for explicit visual logical
069   reasoning remains largely untested in a comprehensive, systematic way. Thus, developing a scalable
070   and thorough benchmark to assess MLLMs’ visual logical reasoning abilities is essential. This would
071   drive advancements in logical reasoning systems within visual contexts, especially as VQA agents
072   gain traction in fields like robotics, biology, and software engineering (Muennighoff et al., 2024;
073   Hong et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024), while also providing a framework to evaluate progress in visual
074   understanding and reasoning in MLLMs.

      This Work With these motivations, we propose a comprehensive benchmark for general visual
102   logical reasoning to address these challenges. Our benchmark utilizes rigorously sourced data
103   to ensure high quality and fair evaluation of the explicit visual-logical reasoning skills of current
104   state-of-the-art MLLMs. We argue that an effective universal evaluation benchmark should have
105   the following characteristics: (1) coverage of a broad range of human logical reasoning skills,
106   including deductive, inductive, numeric, spatial, and mechanical reasoning; (2) presentation of
107   information in various formats such as Optical Character Recognition (OCR), graphs, charts, and flow
      diagrams to accommodate diverse data inputs; (3) responses structured for convenient quantitative


                                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      analysis, enabling rigorous assessment and comparison of model performance; and (4) scalability to
109   accommodate community feedback and growth, ensuring sustainability and effective evaluation of
110   the benchmark for future models and formats.
111
112
      To this end, we collect a comprehensive MLLM evaluation benchmark, named LogicVista, which
      fulfills all the criteria:
113
114          • LogicVista covers the examination of 5 representative categories of logical reasoning tasks:
115            inductive (sample = 107), deductive (sample = 93), numerical (sample = 95), spatial
116            (sample = 79), and mechanical (sample = 74).
117          • LogicVista covers 3 broad capabilities and 11 specific capabilities to give a comprehensive
118            view of how well MLLMs reason with various visual formats.
119
             • All images- instructions-solution-reasoning are rigorously manually annotated and validated
120
               using our robust annotation pipeline.
121
             • Benefiting from our instruction design “please select from A, B, C, D, and E." and our
122
               LLM answer evaluator, we can evaluate different reasoning skills and capabilities and easily
123
               perform quantitative statistical analysis based on the natural language output of MLLMs.
124            We also provide more in-depth human-written explanations for why each answer is correct
125            for more through open-ended evaluation.
126
             • To ensure the scalability and sustainability of LogicVista for future evaluations, we introduce
127
               the annotation tool used for community crowdsourcing, as detailed in Appendix L.
128
129   As shown in Figure 3, LogicVista covers a broad range of reasoning skills, evaluating both open- and
130   closed-source SOTA MLLMs. For example, the question “Which of these images is the top view of
131   the given object" in Figure 1(b) requires spatial reasoning from a different perspective, not just object
132   recognition. Since these questions and diagrams are presented without real-world context, they test
133   the MLLM’s core reasoning abilities.
134   We perform comprehensive evaluations on 11 representative open- and closed-source MLLMs, using
135   448 samples across 5 key logical reasoning categories, providing the first in-depth assessment of
136   visual logical reasoning in state-of-the-art models like GPT-4 Omni, Claude-3.5 Sonnet, and Gemini-
137   Pro. LogicVista’s evaluation framework breaks down each model’s performance by reasoning skill
138   and capability, offering more nuanced insights than a single overall score. We employ two evaluation
139   methods: MCQ for quick assessments and open-ended chain-of-thought (CoT) for a deeper analysis
140
      of the reasoning process, identifying where models succeed or fall short. This approach offers a
      clearer understanding of each model’s strengths and weaknesses.
141
142   Our findings indicate that LogicVista is a highly challenging benchmark, with top-performing models
143   averaging 65% in deductive reasoning but scoring below 30% in other reasoning categories. Notably,
144   GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet exhibit state-of-the-art performance on LogicVista, as detailed in
145   Table 2. We observe that most models struggle the most with inductive, numerical, and spatial
146
      reasoning, while performing better in deductive and mechanical visual reasoning tasks. Additionally,
      our analysis shows that MLLMs achieve higher accuracy with MCQ prompts compared to CoT-based
147
      prompts, suggesting that MCQs rely more on educated guesses and require less in-depth reasoning.
148
      In contrast, CoT prompts often lead to incorrect explanations and lower performance, as models
149   struggle with reasoning or generate hallucinated answers. This pattern reflects human behavior, where
150   selecting a single answer is generally easier than providing a detailed explanation.

      2.1   DATA S OURCES
155
156   Most multimodal evaluation benchmarks source images from the open internet, which risks data
157   leakage into MLLM training datasets, potentially giving some models an unfair advantage. To ensure
158   the integrity of LogicVista’s evaluations, we prevent data leakage by collecting and annotating our
159   samples from licensed IQ test sources, with permission from the test creators. These tests are not
160   publicly accessible and require either payment or registration, significantly reducing the likelihood
161   that LogicVista’s samples have been seen by MLLMs during training. Licensing details and sources
      are also included in the dataset annotations. Additionally, we conducted Google reverse image

      Motivation LogicVista comprises images designed to assess the underlying reasoning capabilities
      of MLLMs. Real-life scenes can complicate explicit tests of logical reasoning because they often
184
      provide contextual clues that enable an AI agent to deduce answers without engaging in direct
185
      reasoning about the scene. To address this, LogicVista features multiple-choice questions across
186   3 broad capabilities and 11 specific capabilities, clearly specifying the type of reasoning required
187   without the additional context of real-life scenarios. Such question formats are commonly found
188   in intelligence and reasoning tests. Consequently, we initially reviewed over 50 intelligence test
189   distributors for suitable tests and formats, focusing on a diverse range of reasoning categories and test
190   sizes. This process led us to filter down to approximately 10 closed-source test banks, from which we
191   gathered our datasets, seeking permission from the test creators to use their materials for our project.
192   Annotation Process To ensure high-quality annotations, we established a rigorous data collection and
193   annotation pipeline involving six annotators and two project leads, all of whom are STEM students,
194   as detailed in Figure 2. The annotators were organized into pairs, each responsible for annotating the
195   same batch of images. They classified each image based on its logical reasoning, broad capability,
196   and specific capability, while also providing the correct answer and open-ended reasoning annotations.
197   Using an answer key as a reference, annotators developed in-depth explanations for why each answer
198   choice was correct.
199   To maintain accuracy and consistency in the open-ended reasoning annotations, the teams collaborated
200   to reach a consensus on the correct answers and reasoning for each sample in LogicVista. After
201   each annotation sprint, the teams conducted peer reviews, exchanging and refining their annotations.
202   Suggested edits were merged into a single batch for each group, which was then submitted to project
203   advisors who acted as independent reviewers to ensure the quality of the open-ended reasoning
204   annotations and correct answers. Each batch underwent cross-validation by an independent group
205   of annotators, providing an objective quality check before incorporation into the final LogicVista
206
      dataset.
207   At the end of the project, the group reconvened to verify the robustness of all samples, ensuring that
208   key annotations, such as open-ended reasoning and question classifications, were both accurate and
209   comprehensive. This meticulous process spanned approximately four months. All data were collected
210   and annotated from closed sources requiring payment or registration for access, significantly reducing
211   the likelihood of the dataset being included in prior training or benchmarking datasets, as outlined in
212
      Appendix K.
213   Annotation Categories To enable a thorough analysis of MLLM performance on visual logical
214   reasoning tasks, we provided fine-grained data annotations that allow for examination across various
215   aspects. With this goal in mind, we annotated each sample in LogicVista with the following details:
      the question, the answer, the correct MCQ answer, an open-ended reasoning explanation for why

284   3.2   E VALUATION P ROTOCOLS
285
      We evaluate the models on LogicVista using two setups: MCQ-based prompting assessed with an
286
      LLM-based answer choice extractor, and CoT-based prompting evaluated by an LLM-as-judge. We
287   chose an MCQ-only evaluation strategy for its straightforward and efficient approach to gauging
288   MLLM performance. The binary nature of MCQ answers (correct/incorrect) simplifies grading and
289   allows for easy comparisons across various tasks and datasets. This method is also used by several
290   other datasets, such as MathVista (Lu et al., 2024), establishing its reliability.
291
      However, we recognize that MCQ-only evaluations have limitations, as they obscure the reasoning
292
      processes of MLLMs by reducing the evaluation to a binary output without revealing the rationale
293   behind the answers. To address this, we also incorporate a chain-of-thought evaluation format, where
294   we ask an LLM judge (GPT-4o) to analyze CoT responses from MLLMs. This judge compares
295   these responses to the ground truth and explains which aspects were incorrect, providing a finer
296   understanding of whether MLLMs arrive at the correct answer with sound reasoning or if they
297   produce incorrect answers despite valid reasoning.
298
      To calculate accuracy scores for each model, we use different methods depending on whether we are
299   evaluating with the MCQ or CoT approach. For MCQ, an LLM-based extractor isolates the selected
300   answers from the MLLMs’ outputs (which are often full sentences rather than single letters) and
301   compares them to the correct answers. In the CoT approach, an LLM judge assesses the open-ended
302   responses against the ground truth. In both cases, the overall logical reasoning score is determined by
303   dividing the number of correct responses by the total number of samples in that particular category,
304   whether it pertains to reasoning skills or capabilities.

313   Our results show that most models struggle with inductive, numerical, and spatial reasoning, while
314
      generally performing well in deductive and mechanical reasoning tasks.
315   Training Limitations: We believe this disparity arises from the limited exposure visual encoders
316   like CLIP (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021) have to inductive, numerical, and spatial
317   reasoning scenarios in their training data. These encoders are typically trained on standard computer
318   vision (CV) datasets focused on object recognition, classification, and segmentation using text labels.
319   While this equips models to excel in tasks like identifying and labeling objects or understanding
320
      cause-effect relationships, it leaves them ill-prepared for reasoning on spatial dynamics or inductive
      patterns.
321
322   For instance, LLaVA models, often fine-tuned with data capturing object names and coordinates,
323   show stronger spatial, inductive, and deductive reasoning than other open-source counterparts. This
      underscores the need for vision encoders that capture detailed image information. Despite the

      4.2   V ISUAL C APABILITIES P ERFORMANCE
379
380   We highlight the performance of MLLMs on various broad and specific visual capabilities in Appendix
381   J, Tables 1, 4, and 5.
382
      Broad Capabilities Our results show that, on average, most models perform better on OCR-type
383
      questions than on diagram-format questions within the broad capability category.
384
385   A possible reason why multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs) perform better on OCR-type questions compared
386   to diagram-based questions is the difference in visual reasoning complexity. OCR tasks mainly involve
387
      recognizing and extracting textual information from images, which plays to the strengths of visual
      encoders in object recognition and classification. The text in OCR tasks is typically structured, with
388
      clear boundaries and minimal need for spatial or abstract reasoning. This allows the MLLM to focus
389
      on straightforward text recognition, followed by reasoning using the LLM backbone, which has been
390   shown to excel at various textual reasoning tasks (liu et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024; Touvron et al.,
391   2023). As a result, the multi-modal reasoning task is simplified into a more manageable text-based
392   reasoning process.
393
      In contrast, diagram-based questions typically demand more complex spatial reasoning, pattern
394
      recognition, and an understanding of relationships between visual elements. These tasks go beyond
395
      merely recognizing objects or labels, requiring the ability to interpret how objects interact, and their
396   relative positions, and sometimes even apply inductive or deductive reasoning. Visual encoders,
397   often not optimized for spatial or abstract relationships, tend to struggle with these challenges. The
398   complexity of interpreting geometric shapes, spatial arrangements, and abstract concepts in diagrams
399   is much greater than the more straightforward task of recognizing and interpreting text in OCR
400   scenarios, as it requires more than simple recognition and identification of basic relationships.
401
      Specific Capabilities We found that MLLMs generally perform well on tasks involving complex
402   OCR, common sense OCR, advanced mechanical reasoning, common sense mechanical reasoning,
403   and rotational patterns. However, they tend to struggle with tasks that require understanding 3D
404   patterns and sequential patterns.
405
      This reinforces our earlier hypothesis that MLLMs excel in OCR and mechanical reasoning tasks
406
      because these visual formats primarily focus on recognizing simple relationships and identifying
407
      objects, rather than interpreting complex spatial interactions. Mechanical formats often depict real-life
408   scenes, making it easier to discern relationships compared to abstract patterns, where the spatial
409   relationships in 3D and sequential formats are more challenging to extract.
410
411
      In contrast, tasks involving 3D and sequential pattern recognition require a more nuanced under-
      standing of spatial relationships, movement, and order—capabilities that may be underdeveloped
412
      in these models due to limitations in their training data and architectures. Spatial and sequential
413
      diagram-based tasks, as well as 3D reasoning, demand an advanced ability to comprehend spatial
414   hierarchies and continuous pattern changes—areas where current visual encoders typically struggle.
415   This lack of spatial depth and temporal awareness contributes to the weaknesses observed in MLLMs
416   when addressing more complex reasoning scenarios.

484
485   Vision Component: In our evaluation, we considered only open-source vision models for benchmark-
      ing. The primary vision encoders used were CLIP-ViT (428M) and EVA-ViT-G (1.13B). When paired


                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      with large language models (LLMs) such as Vicuna 7B and 13B, the LLaVA variants incorporating
487   CLIP-ViT demonstrated superior performance in spatial, deductive, and inductive reasoning tasks
488   compared to InstructBLIP, which utilized the EVA-ViT-G encoder. Despite these observations, it
489   is challenging to declare a definitive superior model for logical reasoning due to the absence of a
490   comprehensive ablation study, which would provide a more thorough analysis of the strengths and
491   weaknesses of each model configuration. We also present a case study of the failures of modern
492   visual encoders in Appendix F.
493   Language Modeling Component: A range of LLMs, including Vicuna, Flan-T5, and LLaMA, were
494   tested by LogicVista to evaluate their performance. With EVA-ViT-G as the vision encoder, the BLIP-
495   2-12B model combined with Flan-T5-XXL outperformed InstructBLIP, which used Vicuna-13B,
496   particularly in spatial reasoning tasks. This suggests that the Flan-T5 model may possess stronger
497   spatial language processing capabilities compared to Vicuna-13B. This observation highlights the
498   potential impact of different LLMs on the effectiveness of multimodal systems. The performance
499   difference indicates that the choice of LLM can significantly affect the effectiveness of multimodal
500   systems in specific reasoning tasks. Flan-T5’s demonstrated strength in spatial reasoning underscores
501
      the importance of choosing LLMs that align with specific reasoning capabilities. However, a more
      detailed analysis of how LLM logical reasoning performance relates to multimodal logical reasoning
502
      could provide better insights into how different LLMs impact the overall performance of visual
503
      reasoning systems.
504
505   Training Data: The comparison of training data performance reveals that MiniGPT, with its datasets
506   including CC3m, SBU, LAION-400M, and a custom set of 3500 images, excels particularly in
507
      induction tasks. This suggests that MiniGPT’s training data could be highly effective for tasks
      requiring the model to generalize from specific inductive examples to broader patterns. On the other
508
      hand, InstructBLIP’s training data, which encompasses BLIP2 and 26 transformed datasets, shows
509
      stronger performance in a broader range of evaluation categories. This indicates that the suitability of
510   training data may vary depending on the specific types of reasoning or tasks. Some of these datasets
511   may have more samples covering specific reasoning tasks causing different datasets to provide distinct
512   advantages for different reasoning skills.
513
      Closed/Open-Source Models: The results suggest that closed-source models like GPT, Gemini,
514
      and Claude significantly outperform open-source models in deduction and mechanical reasoning,
515
      often with double the accuracy. This advantage likely stems from proprietary optimizations, training
516   techniques, model size, or undisclosed data. Additionally, the continuous updates and fine-tuning
517   specific to these models may contribute to their superior performance. However, in numerical, spatial,
518   and inductive reasoning tasks, both open- and closed-source models show similar effectiveness, with
519   accuracy rates between 22% and 31% across leading closed-source models (GPT, Claude, Gemini)
520   and open-source models (13B LLaVA, Yi models, InstructBLIP). While closed-source models excel
521   in deduction and mechanical reasoning, both model types struggle similarly with spatial and inductive
522   reasoning, suggesting the challenges lie more in the fundamental limitations of current MLLM
523   technologies for visual logical reasoning than in proprietary enhancements. Greater transparency
524
      and research could clarify these performance differences and inform future advancements in both
      open-source and closed-source models, potentially bridging the gap in reasoning capabilities.

527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduce LogicVista, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate MLLM
530   performance on complex visual logical reasoning tasks, covering inductive, deductive, spatial,
531   numerical, and mechanical visual reasoning skills. We assess 11 state-of-the-art open and closed-
532   source MLLMs, offering insights into the current landscape of these models. Our detailed analysis
533
      reveals that MLLMs often struggle with intricate spatial and logical details in images, as their visual
      encoders are typically trained for broad object recognition. This focus leads to failures in tasks that
534
      require a deep understanding of spatial relationships, particularly in inductive, spatial, and numerical
535
      reasoning. Our fine-grained CoT case study underscores this limitation, showing that MLLMs tend to
536   generalize rather than capture precise spatial details in both abstract and real-life scenes. We also
537   find that MCQ evaluations often overestimate MLLM performance, as they fail to assess reasoning
538   as effectively as CoT methods. Therefore, we propose future benchmarks emphasize open-ended
539   evaluations that assess the reasoning process, not just final answers.

981    test sources suggest that average human performance is around 75%, this figure varies across different
982
       reasoning skills, which is why we chose not to include it in our study for the sake of reliability and

983

       annotation tool, detailed in Appendix L, to further scale LogicVista in the future.
989    To address both concerns and promote further research, we have also open-sourced these reasoning
990    annotations. They are now publicly available for the community, providing a valuable resource for

992    work to make full use of these annotations to develop more comprehensive and contextually rich

       A    R ELATED W ORKS
997
998    LLM-Based Evaluation. LogicVista adopts an open-ended LLM-based evaluation approach, which
999    facilitates the generation and assessment of diverse answer styles and question types beyond the
1000   limitations of binary or multiple-choice responses. This innovative method leverages the capabilities
1001   of large language models (LLMs) for comprehensive model evaluation, a technique that has been
1002
       effectively applied in natural language processing (NLP) tasks and other VQA benchmarks (Chiang
       & yi Lee, 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Fu et al., 2023b; Jin et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). Our findings
1003
       show that this LLM-based evaluation framework is both versatile and robust, providing a unified
1004
       and flexible assessment across different modalities, including open- and closed-ended responses.
1005   By accommodating a broad range of answer styles and question types, this approach deepens and
1006   expands model evaluation, leading to a more comprehensive understanding of model performance.

       Vision-Language Benchmarks Traditional vision-language benchmarks have largely focused on
       evaluating specific perceptual abilities. Datasets like MM-vet, RAVEN, CLEVR-X, and TextVQA
1009
       each address distinct aspects of visual recognition: TextVQA emphasizes recognition-based VQA,
1010
       testing how well models can caption and accurately describe key image details; MM-vet evaluates
1011   world knowledge, basic math, detail capture, and OCR in recognition tasks and everyday scene
1012   reasoning. Meanwhile, RAVEN and CLEVR-X assess spatial relation recognition in 2D and 3D
1013   objects, providing insights into how well MLLMs understand spatial reasoning (Goyal et al., 2017b;
1014   Yu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2019a; Sidorov et al., 2020; Salewski et al., 2022).
1015   Image captioning and description generation have also been extensively studied (Chen et al., 2015;
1016   Agrawal et al., 2019), along with more specialized tasks like scene text understanding (Singh et al.,
1017   2019b; Sidorov et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020) and integrating external knowledge (Marino et al.,
1018   2019). Other benchmarks, such as OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024), focus on Olympiad-level math
1019
       and science challenges to compare MLLMs with human performance. Large-scale multidisciplinary
       benchmarks like MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) assess MLLMs across a range of subjects, including
1020
       science, math, humanities, and history.

       B.1   L OGICAL R EASONING S KILLS
1027
1028   We define our 5 logical reasoning skills based on common human visual reasoning abilities. Our
1029   goal is to assess how effectively MLLMs perform in general reasoning skills that humans rely on for
1030   everyday problem-solving. These skills reflect the types of reasoning that MLLM agents are likely to
1031   encounter in real-world settings where they may be deployed. Our definitions are largely inspired by
1032   traditional human IQ and intelligence tests.
1033
              • Inductive Reasoning the ability to infer the next entry in a pattern given a pattern of
1034
                observations. It is the ability to make generalizations based on some observations and
1035            make an educated guess. It moves from many specific observations to a generalization. An
1036            example could be given observations that when John eats dairy products, he gets a stomach
1037            ache. An inductive conclusion can be drawn that he is most likely lactose intolerant.
1038
              • Deductive Reasoning the ability to conclude a specific case when given a general principle
1039            or pattern. It moves from the general to the specific. An example could be given the
1040            statement “all men are mortal”, one can conclude that “John is mortal” because John is a
1041            man.
1042
              • Numerical Reasoning the ability to read arithmetic problems in the image and solve the
1043            math equations. An example could be given the equation “10 + 10 = ?”, the answer would
1044            be “20”.
1045
              • Spatial Reasoning the ability to understand the spatial relationship between objects and
1046
                patterns and reason with those relationships. An example could be seeing an unfolded box
1047            and understanding what the box could look like when it is folded up.
1048
              • Mechanical Reasoning the ability to recognize a physical system and solve equations based
1049
                on that system or answer questions about that system. An example could be seeing a set
1050
                of 3 gears and understanding which gears will turn clockwise and which ones will turn
1051            counterclockwise.

       B.2   B ROAD AND S PECIFIC C APABILITIES
1054
1055   We categorize our multi-modal capabilities into broad and specific classifications to gain hierarchical
1056   insights into which information formats are better or worse understood by MLLMs.
1057
       Here we present our definitions for broad capabilities:
1058
1059          • Optical Character Recognition (OCR): refers to the ability to reason over text inside
1060            images and scenes.
1061          • Diagrams: refers to the ability to reason about diagrams that represent real-life scenes,
1062            abstract logic, spatial relationships, and more.
1063
              • Mixed (Both OCR and Diagram): refers to an integration of both OCR and diagrams,
1064            where comprehending the text and the visual elements within the image is essential for
1065            accurately answering the question.
1066
1067   Here we present our definitions for specific capabilities:
1068
1069          • Chart: refers to numerical charts and graphs.
1070          • Infographic: refers to infographic-style puzzles that illustrate both real-life and abstract
1071            scenes.
1072          • Table: refers to words and numbers only tables depicting some trend or concept.
1073          • Common Sense OCR: refers to text questions describing common everyday situations
1074            using common English words.
1075
              • Complex OCR: refers to text questions describing technical or highly abstract situations
1076
                using jargon and complex sentences.
1077
1078
              • Rotation Pattern: Patterns and puzzles that necessitate an understanding of 2D and/or 3D
                object rotations.
1079
              • 3D Pattern: Patterns that require 3D spatial relation understandings.

1080
                 • Rule Based Pattern: Patterns that require understanding of a set of externally defined rules.
1081
1082             • Sequence Pattern: Patterns presented in a strictly sequential format, typically involved
1083               with induction.
1084
                 • Common Sense Mechanical: Puzzles concerned with a common sense understanding of
1085               basic physics and mechanics.
1086
1087             • Advanced Mechanical: Puzzles concerned with an advanced and specialized understanding
1088               of physics and mechanics.

1274   Given that Claude 3.5 Sonnet is the top-performing MLLM across multiple of LogicVista’s question
1275
       types and formats, we conducted a case study to examine its shortcomings in capturing the spatial
       and logical relationships essential for complex visual reasoning.
1276
1277   In Figures 9 and 10, Claude 3.5 Sonnet struggles to identify key spatial relationships, such as the shape
1278   and number of stars, while also overlooking the logical context necessary for answering LogicVista
1279   questions in a CoT format. Similarly, in Figure 10, Claude misinterprets the sequential movement
1280   between the circle and triangle. These examples highlight a common issue with modern MLLM
1281
       vision encoders: they tend to focus on object recognition rather than understanding the relationships
       between objects, which is essential for accurate visual logical reasoning.
1282
1283   Building on this insight, we conducted an additional experiment to assess the specific details a modern
1284   SOTA MLLM can capture. Using Claude 3.5 Sonnet, the top-performing model for LogicVista,
1285   we prompted it to provide detailed descriptions of LogicVista samples with a focus on spatial
1286   relationships between objects. As shown in Figure 11, MLLMs excel at simpler recognition tasks,
1287
       such as identifying spring lengths, enabling the model to solve the problem easily. However, when
       tasked with recognizing more complex spatial relationships, current MLLMs struggle. For instance,
1288
       in Figure 12, Claude misses intricate spatial patterns and instead focuses on broad features—reflecting
1289
       a limitation of traditional CV encoders, which are good at general visual recognition but struggle to
1290   accurately capture specific spatial arrangements like the positioning of hexagons, squares, and circles.
1291   Even in less abstract cases, such as the one on the right in Figure 12 depicting a sled, Claude fails to
1292   distinguish key details like the width and size of runners, instead hallucinating differences in sled
1293   sizes. When asked specifically about the runner sizes, Claude either misidentifies them as similar or
1294   fabricates relationships. This demonstrates the need for vision encoders to be able to capture more
1295   intricate spatial details and focus less on recognition, which it already excels at but focus rather more
       on extracting these key spatial and visual-logical relations.

1484   G    MCQ VS C OT E VALUATIONS
1485
1486   We found that when MLLMs are prompted to produce CoT reasoning, they often provide incorrect
1487   reasoning, leading to lower scores, as open-ended evaluations account for the quality of the reasoning
1488   itself.
1489   According to the benchmark outcome, we show that MCQ-based evaluations consistently result in
1490   higher raw scores compared to CoT-based evaluations in almost all categories for reasoning and
1491   capabilities. This mirrors human behavior, where it is often easier to make an educated guess and get
1492   the answer right without fully understanding the question or providing correct reasoning. Since MCQ
1493   evaluations may overlook cases where MLLMs guess the answer correctly without valid reasoning,
1494   we argue that CoT evaluations offer a more reliable measure of MLLM reasoning capabilities, as
1495
       they assess both the answer and the reasoning behind it.
