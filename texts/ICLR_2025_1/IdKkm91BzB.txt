010

               In continuous action online reinforcement learning, actor-critic methods are pre-
012            dominantly used. However, compared to Q-learning-based discrete action algo-
013            rithms that model the optimal Q-value, continuous action algorithms that model
014            the Q-value for the current policy and perform policy improvement solely through
015            policy updates suffer from low sample efficiency. This study investigates whether
016            an algorithm that implicitly estimates the optimal Q-value, typically used in offline
017            RL, is also effective in online RL. It is demonstrated that a loss function aimed at
018            achieving optimality distorts the distribution of Q-values, leading to overestima-
019            tion bias, and that this distortion and bias increase as learning progresses. To
020
               address this issue, we propose a simple algorithm that anneals optimality. Our
               method significantly outperforms widely used methods such as SAC and TD3 in
021
               online DM Control tasks. Additionally, we demonstrate that annealing improves
022
               performance and enhances robustness to the hyperparameter related to the opti-
023            mality.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, online reinforcement learning (RL) has been widely applied in fields such as robotics
030   (Schulman et al., 2015; Haarnoja et al., 2018) and games (Mnih et al., 2013; 2015; Silver et al.,
031   2016), particularly demonstrating remarkable performance in discrete-action tasks. In Q-learning-
032   based algorithms, commonly used for discrete-action tasks, the optimal value is estimated through
033
      optimal Bellman operator, where the target value is calculated as r + γ maxa′ Q(s′ , a′ ). However,
      in continuous-action tasks, it is challenging to compute maxa′ Q(s′ , a′ ) over an infinite number of
034
      actions, so actor-critic-based algorithms estimate the Q-value through Bellman backups based on the
035
      current policy, where the target value is r + γEa′ ∼π Q(s′ , a′ ). In these cases, policy improvement is
036   achieved solely through policy updates, resulting in slower performance improvements and reduced
037   sample efficiency. This is a significant issue for continuous-action tasks, such as robot control, which
038   are highly complex and require sample efficiency due to data collection difficulties. While previous
039   studies have proposed methods for modeling the (soft) optimal value, they have encountered issues
040   such as increased computational complexity and learning instability due to the exponential terms
041   involved (Haarnoja et al., 2017; Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Garg et al., 2023).
042   This study examines the effectiveness of modeling optimal value in online RL. To model the optimal
043   value, we employ the expectile loss (Kostrikov et al., 2022), which is typically used in offline RL
044   and implicitly models the maximum value. Expectile loss is a stable loss function that allows control
045   over the degree of optimality via the hyperparameter τ . As τ approaches 1, the function behaves
046   similarly to a maximum operator.
047
      First, this study examines the increase in Q-value skewness and the accumulation of overestimation
048   bias resulting from estimating the optimal value. Using expectile loss, an asymmetric loss function,
049   for estimation may lead to skewness in the Q-value distribution. We explore how this skewness in-
050   fluences bias. While Thrun & Schwartz (1993) demonstrated the impact of distribution variance and
051   the number of actions on bias after taking the max, our study numerically shows that the skewness
052   of the original Q-value distribution can also affect bias. In other words, as optimality increases,
053   the skewness of the Q-value distribution grows, leading to significant bias, which accumulates and
      potentially destabilizes the learning process toward the end.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Thus, while pursuing optimality carries the risk of performance degradation, a certain degree of
055   bias is beneficial during the early stages of learning as it encourages exploration (Lan et al., 2020).
056   It is reasonable to pursue optimality during the early phases before significant skewness and bias
057   have accumulated. Toward the end of the learning process, the current policy approaches the (sub)-
058   optimal policy, and the Q-value nears the (sub)-optimal Q-value, making stable estimation more
059   desirable than highly optimal estimation.
060
      In this context, we propose annealing the optimality in Bellman backups by gradually shifting from
061   a Q-learning-based target r + γ maxa′ Q(s′ , a′ ) to a SARSA-based target r + γEa′ ∼π Q(s′ , a′ ).
062   By using expectile loss, we smoothly interpolate between these two estimations. By annealing the
063   parameter corresponding to the expectile being estimated, we can tolerate bias and pursue optimality
064   through Q-learning-based updates during the exploration-heavy early learning stages. In the later
065   stages, when the policy is close to (sub)-optimal, we transition to SARSA-based updates to prevent
066   further accumulation of bias.
067   We conducted experiments using continuous-action online RL tasks in DM Control, combining the
068   proposed annealed Implicit Q-learning (AIQL) with SAC. Compared to widely-used algorithms like
069   SAC and TD3, AIQL demonstrated significantly superior performance. Additionally, when com-
070   pared to static optimality estimation, annealing was shown to enhance robustness to the optimality
071   hyperparameter and improve performance.
072
      The contributions of this study are as follows:
073
074          • We investigated and experimentally tested the modeling of optimal Q-value using implicit
075            Q-learning loss in continuous-action online RL.
076          • Through numerical experiments, we confirmed that modeling optimal Q-value using im-
077            plicit Q-learning loss can amplify bias due to skewness in the Q-value distribution.
078
             • We proposed optimality annealing for the value function modeling, based on the assump-
079
               tion that early bias is beneficial for exploration and the Q-value approaches optimality in
080            the later stages of learning. Experimental results demonstrated that annealing improves
081            both robustness to the hyperparameter and performance.

105   Implicit Q-learning (IQL) is an algorithm widely used in offline RL to learn the value function and
106   the policy using only actions from the offline dataset, without relying on actions from the policy
107   being learned. By avoiding the use of out-of-distribution actions in learning, the problem of dis-
      tribution shift in offline RL can be mitigated. IQL can also learn near-optimal value functions in

139   overestimation bias becomes more pronounced. The next section discusses the problem of overesti-

      3.3   A NNEALING OF O PTIMALITY τ
194
195   As discussed, overestimation bias arises from the calculation of maxa′ Q(s′ , a′ ). With expectile
196   loss, as τ approaches 1, the computation increasingly resembles a max operation, while at τ =
197   0.5, it calculates the expectation Ea′ [Q(s′ , a′ )]. In other words, overestimation bias increases as τ
198   nears 1 and decreases as it approaches 0.5. At the same time, when τ is near 1, the computation
199   approximates the optimal value; at τ = 0.5, it represents the value function of the policy.
200   Thus, τ controls the trade-off between optimality and bias. Considering the impact of optimality
201   and bias at different stages of learning, optimality, which leads to high sample efficiency, is crucial
202   early in learning. A certain amount of bias promotes exploration (Lan et al., 2020), making a higher
203   τ beneficial initially. Bias accumulates gradually, so it remains manageable early on. Therefore, a
204   high τ that trades off some bias for optimality is preferable at the start.
205   In the later stages, accumulated bias needs to be minimized. As learning progresses, the value
206   function and policy gradually converge, reducing the necessity for policy improvement. Hence, a
207   smaller τ is favorable to control bias.
208
      This study proposes setting τ close to 1 at the start and annealing it to 0.5 by the end of learning.
209
      At τ = 0.5, the loss becomes an L2 loss, equivalent to estimating Q under the current policy. While
210
      our approach is not restricted to specific annealing strategies, our implementation linearly anneals
211   τ : given an initial τ value τinit and a maximum number of timesteps T , τ at timestep t is:
212
                                                                        min(t, T )
213                                       τ (t) = τinit − (τinit − 0.5)            .
                                                                           T
214
215   We name this method Annealed Implicit Q-learning (AIQL). The next section validates the effec-
      tiveness of this approach through experimental results.

248    4.1                   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
249
250   We used 10 challenging continuous control tasks from DM Control as the experimental environment
251
      for online RL. As baseline methods, we employed SAC and TD3, widely used in continuous action
      tasks for online RL, along with XQL (Garg et al., 2023). XQL uses Gumbel regression, assuming
252
      a Gumbel distribution for the error distribution, and estimates the soft-optimal value in entropy-
253
      maximizing RL, offering a comparison as another method for estimating the optimal value.
254
255   Our proposed method, AIQL, was integrated with SAC. The only modification to SAC was the
256   use of expectile loss instead of L2 loss for estimating the value function. The experiments that
257
      integrate TD3 and AIQL are described in appendix D. While Kostrikov et al. (2022) incorporated
      both a Q-function and a V-function to mitigate performance degradation observed in stochastic en-
258
      vironments, AIQL demonstrated superior performance without employing a V-function, even in
259
      extremely stochastic environments. As a result, only the Q-function is utilized in our approach.
260   Further details are provided in appendix F. The parameter τ was annealed linearly, starting at 0.9 at
261   the beginning of the training and decreasing to 0.5 at 3 million steps. The results for using differ-
262   ent annealing durations are presented in appendix E. Other hyperparameters of AIQL were kept the
263   same as SAC, and at τ = 0.5, AIQL is identical to SAC. The measurements of skewness and bias
264   are based on Chen et al. (2021).

      5   R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      Off-Policy Online RL with Continuous Action Space In continuous action space online RL
435
      tasks, methods such as SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) and TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018) are widely
436   used. SAC is an actor-critic method based on Haarnoja et al. (2017) that incorporates entropy maxi-
437   mization. The value function adds the policy’s entropy to the target value and models the soft value
438   function for the current policy. In policy learning, SAC aims for policy improvement by aligning the
439   policy distribution with the exponential of the Q-function. TD3 is an extension of DDPG (Lillicrap,
440   2015) that incorporates techniques like clipped double Q-learning, action noise, and delayed policy
441   updates. Like SAC, TD3 updates the value function based on SARSA, learning the value function
442   under the current policy. The policy itself is improved using the deterministic policy gradient, as
443   in DDPG. Both methods employ SARSA-based updates, focusing on learning the value function
444
      for the current policy. Our study demonstrates that by replacing SAC’s value function update with
      expectile loss, shifting to a Q-learning-based maximization, we can achieve improved performance.
445
446   Overestimation bias is a recurring issue in RL. Thrun & Schwartz (1993) formalized how noise in
447   function approximators leads to overestimation bias, showing how noise variance and the number of
448   actions contribute to the increase in bias. Techniques like Lan et al. (2020) and Chen et al. (2021)
449   acknowledge that overestimation can sometimes be beneficial for exploration, while using ensem-
450
      bles to control the mean and variance of bias. Incorporating these ensemble techniques into our
      proposed method could potentially enhance sample efficiency, which could be a promising direction
451
      for future work.
452
453
      Value Function Maximization in Continuous Action Space In continuous action tasks, calcu-
454
      lating the maximum Q-value is generally intractable. Various methods have tackled this issue by
455
      using asymmetric loss functions (Kostrikov et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Omura
456   et al., 2024; Sikchi et al., 2024), evaluating multiple actions (Kalashnikov et al., 2018; Kumar et al.,
457   2019; 2020), or discretizing actions (Tavakoli et al., 2018; Seyde et al., 2023; 2024).
458
459
      IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022) approaches Q-value as a random variable with inherent randomness
      related to the actions, modeling the maximum value using expectile loss with an expectile parame-
460
      ter close to 1. By adjusting the hyperparameter τ from 0.5 to 1, the value function estimate shifts
461
      from SARSA-based to Q-learning-based. XQL (Garg et al., 2023) models the soft optimal value
462   in maximum entropy RL using Gumbel loss, derived from maximum likelihood estimation under
463   the assumption of a Gumbel error distribution. MXQL (Omura et al., 2024) stabilizes the Gumbel
464   loss by employing a Maclaurin expansion. By increasing the order of the expansion from 2 to infin-
465   ity, MXQL transitions from SARSA-based learning to (soft) Q-learning-based learning. Although
466   MXQL may benefit from annealing the expansion order to improve performance, tuning hyperpa-
467   rameters can be challenging due to the infinite range of the optimality parameter.
468   Kalashnikov et al. (2018); Kumar et al. (2019; 2020) improve sample efficiency by modeling the
469   optimal Q-value through sampling multiple actions from the policy distribution and selecting the
470   action that maximizes the Q-value. These methods can shift from SARSA-based to Q-learning-
471   based by increasing the number of samples from 1 to infinity, but tuning poses challenges, and
472   computational costs rise significantly as the number of samples increases.
473
      Some methods, such as (Tavakoli et al., 2018; Seyde et al., 2023; 2024), achieve optimal Q-value
474   estimation through action discretization. However, if the number of intervals is small, accurate
475   action selection becomes difficult, while increasing the intervals reduces sample efficiency.
476
477
      In this study, we chose expectile loss for optimal value estimation and annealing due to its relatively
      simple hyperparameter tuning and consistent computational complexity compared to these other
478
      approaches.

482
483   In this study, we evaluated the effectiveness of a method that uses expectile loss to model the op-
484   timal Q-value in online RL for continuous action tasks. We demonstrated that the distortion of the
485   Q-value distribution and overestimation bias can be amplified based on the level of optimality. Based
      on this, we proposed an annealing method for adjusting optimality, which promotes exploration in

      the early stages of learning and maintains stability in the later stages. The proposed Annealed Im-
487   plicit Q-learning (AIQL) outperformed existing algorithms such as SAC and TD3. Additionally, the
488   annealing process stabilized learning and improved robustness to hyperparameter changes. While a
489   simple linear annealing method was found to contribute to performance improvement, further opti-
490   mization of methods to adjust optimality is a promising direction for future research.

746   F    T HE N ECESSITY OF THE V - FUNCTION
747
748   In IQL, both the Q-function and V-function were trained to handle environmental stochasticity.
749   However, as shown in Figure 14, this study confirms that training with only the Q-function achieves
750   better performance even in stochastic environments. Therefore, AIQL utilizes only the Q-function.
751   Figure 14 presents results comparing AIQL using only the Q-function versus AIQL using both the Q-
752   function and V-function in the stochastic hopper-hop environment. Stochasticity in the environment
753   is introduced by adding Gaussian noise with a mean of 0 to the actions input into the DM Control
754   tasks. The left figure shows the results when τ is annealed, while the right figure corresponds to
755   fixed τ . The values used were τinit = 0.9 for annealing and τ = 0.7 for the fixed case, as these
      settings yielded the best performance.

756
      In both cases, whether annealing or fixed, using only the Q-function consistently outperformed using
757   both the Q-function and V-function across all levels of noise standard deviation. This performance
758   difference is attributed to the additional network required when using the V-function, which increase
759   approximation error and degrade performance.
760
      For the annealing case in the left figure, the degradation in average return as noise standard deviation
761
      increases is similar whether or not the V-function is used. This is likely because annealing results in
762
      learning behavior similar to SAC toward the end of training, mitigating the impact of environmental
763   randomness on the IQL loss.
764
765   In the fixed τ case shown in the right figure, performance degradation due to increased noise standard
766
      deviation is more pronounced when the V-function is not used. Nevertheless, using only the Q-
      function still outperforms the setup with both Q-function and V-function.
