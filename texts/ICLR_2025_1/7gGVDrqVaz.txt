009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A key challenge in automated formal reasoning is the intractable search space,
012            which grows exponentially with the depth of the proof. This branching is caused
013            by the large number of candidate proof tactics which can be applied to a given
014            goal. Nonetheless, many of these tactics are semantically similar or lead to an
015            execution error, wasting valuable resources in both cases. We address the prob-
016            lem of effectively pruning this search, using only synthetic data generated from
017            previous proof attempts. We first demonstrate that it is possible to generate se-
018
               mantically aware tactic representations which capture the effect on the proving
               environment, likelihood of success, and execution time. We then propose a novel
019
               filtering mechanism which leverages these representations to select semantically
020
               diverse and high quality tactics, using Determinantal Point Processes. Our ap-
021            proach, 3D-Prover, is designed to be general, and to augment any underlying tactic
022            generator. We demonstrate the effectiveness of 3D-Prover on the miniF2F-valid
023            and miniF2F-test benchmarks by augmenting the ReProver LLM. We show that
024            our approach leads to an increase in the overall proof rate, as well as a significant
025            improvement in the tactic success rate, execution time and diversity.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Interactive Theorem Proving, as the name suggests, has traditionally involved a human guiding a
031   proving system to verify a formal proposition. It has found applications in a wide range of fields,
032   from secure software (Tan et al., 2019) to the verification of mathematical results (Hales et al.,
033
      2017). There has been significant interest in automating this process, with formalization efforts
      requiring a high level of human expertise (Klein et al., 2009). Beyond this, it is considered a ‘grand
034
      challenge’ for AI, requiring a high level of reasoning and planning to be successful (Reddy, 1988).
035
      Even the largest current models struggle with the complexity of the task, with for example GPT-4
036   only able to solve 13.5% (Thakur et al., 2023) of the high school level miniF2F-test (Zheng et al.,
037   2021) benchmark. This has motivated the development of specialized models and search algorithms
038   to address the unique challenges of the domain (Wang et al., 2024; Polu et al., 2022; Jiang et al.,
039   2022b; Han et al., 2022; First et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Whalen, 2016; Wu
040   et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2018; Wang & Deng, 2020; Rabe et al., 2020; Polu & Sutskever, 2020;
041   Mikuła et al., 2023; Loos et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2021;
042   2022a; Gauthier et al., 2017).
043   With most non-trivial proofs requiring long chains of correct reasoning, it is a challenge to generate
044   them in one pass without mistakes. The addition of a search algorithm is common for addressing
045   this, as is done by the current state-of-the-art DeepSeek-Prover-V1.5 (Xin et al., 2024). Under this
046   paradigm, candidate tactics are generated and executed in the proving system, which (if successful)
047   results in new subgoals to prove. This generates a tree of possible proof paths, where a search algo-
048   rithm selects the most promising nodes to expand. The primary challenge faced by these approaches
049   is the exponential growth in the number of proof paths, limiting the complexity of the problems that
050
      can be solved efficiently.
051   Many of the generated tactics are equivalent, modulo variable renaming and other semantics-
052   preserving transformations. See Figure 1 for a sample search tree from the ReProver (Yang et al.,
053   2023) system, where several semantically similar paths are explored, wasting valuable resources.
      Simple lexical similarity scores fail to cover the semantics (meaning) of a tactic, as captured by the

080   These challenges motivate our proposed approach, Diversity Driven Determinantal Point Process
081   Prover (3D-Prover). 3D-Prover adds an extra ‘dimension’ to existing proving systems by including a
082   filtering mechanism on top of the existing tactic generation and search components. 3D-Prover uses
083
      Determinantal Point Processes (Kulesza, 2012) to prune the proof search space by filtering tactic
      candidates to a diverse and high quality subset. The large amount of synthetic data generated from
084
      proof attempts enables us to learn the effect tactics have on the environment, including the likelihood
085
      of an error and the execution time. We leverage this to generate tactic representations which reflect
086   their semantics, which 3D-Prover uses to filter tactics based on a combination of their diversity and
087   quality. 3D-Prover allows for a direct tradeoff between search objectives, with hyperparameters
088   controlling the weighting of error, time and diversity in the filtering process. 3D-Prover is a general
089   approach which can be used to augment any underlying tactic generator. We demonstrate this by
090   augmenting the open source ReProver LLM to obtain a significant improvement in the success rate,
091   execution time and diversity of tactics, and the overall proof success rate.
092   We summarize our contributions as follows:
093
094          • We study the feasibility of learning the environment dynamics of proving systems. We
095            demonstrate tactic representations which capture the likely effect on the environment, using
096            them to predict the likelihood of success and execution time of a tactic, as well as the
097            resulting proof state or error message.
098          • We propose a novel edge filtering approach using Determinantal Point Processes (Kulesza
099            & Taskar, 2011), which leverage these representations to select semantically diverse subsets
100            of quality tactics. Our method is modular and can be used with any underlying tactic model.
101          • We evaluate our approach by augmenting ReProver (Yang et al., 2023) on the
102            miniF2F (Zheng et al., 2021) benchmark, where we demonstrate a significant improvement
103            in the tactic success rate, diversity and overall proof success rate.

      1.2   BACKGROUND : D ETERMINANTAL P OINT P ROCESSES
121
122   Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are a class of probabilistic models for sampling subsets from
123   a ground set Y. They provide an inherent trade-off between the diversity and quality of the sampled
124   subsets, successfully being applied to this end across a variety of domains (Kulesza, 2012; Hsiao &
125   Grauman, 2018; Zhang et al., 2016). This motivates their use in our filtering approach (Section 3.2).
126
      In line with Kulesza (2012), for |Y| = n we define the kernel L ∈ Rn×n of a DPP as the Gram
127
      matrix L = B T B for B ∈ Rn×d , where column bi ∈ Rd of B is a vector representing element
128   i ∈ {1, . . . , n} of Y. These vectors bi are commonly decomposed into a set of unit norm diversity
129   features ϕi ∈ Rd and quality scores qi ∈ R+ , so that bi = qi ϕi , ||ϕi || = 1 for all i ∈ {1, . . . , n}.
130   The similarity matrix S is then defined as Sij = ϕTi ϕj . The probability of sampling a subset
131   A ⊆ Y from a DPP is then    Q proportional to the determinant of the submatrix of L indexed by
132   A, P(A) ∝ det(LA ) = ( i∈A qi2 )det(SA ). Geometrically, this determinant is the volume of the
133   parallelepiped spanned by the submatrix LA , which as we see in Figure 3, is maximised based on
134   a combination of the similarity and length (quality) of the chosen elements. In this way, DPPs
135   elegantly trade off between the quality and diversity of elements. Normally the size of the sampled
136
      subset |A| is variable, however Kulesza & Taskar (2011) introduce k-DPPs which restricts the size
      of the subset to a fixed k ∈ N, and where the probability  of sampling A is normalised over subsets
137                                                     P
      of size k. That is, for a k-DPP, P(A) = det(LA )/ |A′ |=k det(LA′ ).

140   2     T RANSITION AWARE R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
141
142   One proof attempt can generate a rather large amount of data. A single pass of the miniF2F-valid
143   benchmark of 244 proofs results in approximately 500,000 transitions, capturing rich information
144   about the error likelihood, execution time and resulting proof state or error message. This section ex-
145
      plores the feasibility of using this data to learn how tactics affect the environment. We operationalise
      this as a supervised learning task: given a goal and tactic, we predict the error status, execution time
146
      and environment output. We effectively learn these targets from only this synthetic data, and embed
147
      this information into a compact tactic representation. The upshot, as we show in Section 3, is that
148   these representations can be utilised to improve the performance of subsequent proof attempts.
149
150
      2.1   T RANSITION M ODELS
151
152   The result of a proof attempt (formalised in A.1) is the dataset D of transitions {(g, t, s, τ, o)},
153   which captures the results of applying tactics t ∈ T to goals g ∈ S. The status s ∈ {0, 1}, indicates
154   a success (1) or failure (0), τ ∈ R gives the execution time of the tactic and the output o ∈ O
155   is the environment response, which is an error message, new goals to prove, or a proof success.
156   We propose a method of learning tactic representations e ∈ Rd which capture the result (s, τ, o)
157   of applying t to g. By using these as diversity features for DPP, we can filter tactics based on the
158
      diversity of their outcomes, before they are executed.
159   We define our transition model ξ : S × T → [0, 1] × R × O as a mapping from a goal g and tactic
160   t to an estimate of the status s, time τ and output o. To ensure ξ admits effective representations in
161   e, we construct it with three components. The Encoder E : S × T → Rd takes the goal g and tactic
      t as input, and outputs our representation E(g, t) = e. As e will be used as the diversity feature for

301   3      F ILTERING M ODEL
302
303   In the previous section we used synthetic proof data to generate semantically aware tactic represen-
304   tations, allowing us to predict the likelihood of success, execution time and environment response.
305   We now take this a step further by using these representations to augment proof search. We begin
306   by introducing our filtering model 3D-Prover, which prunes tactic candidates based on their quality
307   and the semantic diversity of their representations. We show that 3D-Prover is able to improve the
308
      performance of the ReProver LLM on the miniF2F-valid and miniF2F-test benchmarks, particularly
      when a deeper search configuration is used. We conclude with a multifaceted ablation study show-
309
      ing the effect of our filtering model on the success rate, number of unique responses and execution
310
      time.

344       3.2     E XPERIMENTS
345
346       To test the performance of 3D-Prover, we use ReProver (Yang et al., 2023) as the underlying tactic
347       policy π0 , with the Encoder E and Predictor P components as defined in Section 2. We chose
348       ReProver as it is a small (∼ 300M parameters), popular and performant open source model, allowing
349       us to run our experiments in a reasonable timeframe. We run our experiments in Lean 3 (De Moura
350       et al., 2015) using the BAIT (Lamont et al., 2024) platform with a modified LeanDojo (Yang et al.,
351
          2023) environment, where we set an environment timeout of 600 seconds per proof attempt. We train
          a combined transition model on the miniF2F-valid benchmark, and use the Encoder and Predictor
352
          components to generate tactic embeddings and quality scores as per Algorithm 1. We first examine
353
          the performance of 3D-Prover without any hyperparameter tuning, setting λs = λτ = 0, θ = 1. We
354       then perform ablation studies using miniF2F-valid to examine the influence of the hyperparameters
355       on the tactic success rate, execution time and diversity of the environment response. For miniF2F-
356       test, we allow the model four attempts per proof to increase confidence in the results, while for
357       miniF2F-valid we allow one attempt per configuration to facilitate a wider set of ablations.
358
          We set the search policy for all experiments to be Best First Search (BestFS), where nodes are
359
          expanded in order of their cumulative log probability. For each node selected for expansion, we
360       generate N = 64 candidate tactics from the underlying ReProver model using beam search with
361       default settings, as done in the original ReProver implementation. This forms the ground set for
362       the node, to be sub-sampled by the filtering algorithm. We use beam search decoding because
363       it is deterministic and so ensures that the ground set for a given node remains fixed across runs,
364       allowing us to isolate and compare the effect of the filtering algorithm. The filtering algorithm
365       returns K tactics, which are then executed in the environment and used to update the proof tree, as
366       outlined in A.1. We test three different levels of filtering, with K ∈ {8, 16, 32}. Lower values of K
367       correspond to more filtering, for which the choice of filtering algorithm will have a greater impact.
368
          We compare the filtering approach of 3D-Prover, as outlined in Algorithm 1, with two baselines.
          The Top-K baseline takes the top K tactics from the ground set as judged by their log probabilities,
369
          corresponding to the top K beams. We take K tactics at random from the ground set to form the
370
          Random baseline, as an exploration-focused comparison.

523   4   C ONCLUSION
524
525   Future work One might consider structured DPPs (Kulesza, 2012), which operate at the tree level
526   to select diverse paths, rather than the node level, which selects diverse edges. Continual learning
527   of the transition model is another avenue, where training on new data as it is generated could lead
528   to more accurate assessments of diversity and quality. Our approach could also be combined with
529
      a separate search algorithm such as HTPS (Lample et al., 2022), rather than BestFS. Testing larger
      models would be a natural extension, for both the transition model and the underlying tactic gen-
530
      erator. Our methodology may also be useful to enhance search in domains beyond formal proving,
531
      such as code generation or game playing.
532
533
      Summary We introduce 3D-Prover, a method to augment proof search by filtering candidate tac-
534   tics to generate diverse and high quality subsets. By generating tactic representations which reflect
535   the response of the proving environment, 3D-Prover is able to filter tactics based on their likely out-
536   come. We evaluate 3D-Prover by augmenting the ReProver LLM on the standard miniF2F bench-
537   mark, where we find an improvement in the overall proof success rate (Table 2), particularly for
538   deeper searches. Our ablation studies confirm the utility of our tactic representations, which allow
539   the selection of tactics with improved success rates, diversity, and/or execution time. By effectively
      pruning the search space, 3D-Prover is a step towards enabling deeper automated proofs.

746   A.1   P ROOF S EARCH S ETUP
747
      We first define the space of goals S, tactics T and failures F. For our purposes, these all contain
748
      arbitrary strings, with the goal being a formal proposition, the tactic a command and the failure
749
      an error message. We then define the output space as O := P(S) ∪ F. A proof tree is a DAG
750   G = (V, E) where V ⊂ S is the set of goals and E the edges between them. A proof attempt for a
751   goal g0 first initialises the proof tree with V = {g0 }, E = ∅. The search policy πS : G×V → R+ is
752   a distribution over goals given a proof tree, being used to select a goal g to expand. The tactic policy
753   πT : S ×T → R+ is a distribution over tactics given a goal, where N ∈ N tactics are sampled to give
754   tactics {ti }N
                   i=1 ⊂ T . The goal, tactic pairs (g, ti ) are then passed to the environment E : S ×T → O.
755   For each pair, after τi ∈ R seconds, it returns either a new set of goals gi′ ⊂ S or an error, ei ∈ F.
      We define this response as the output oi ∈ O. We further define the status si ∈ {0, 1} as 0 if oi ∈ F,


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      1 if oi ∈ P(S) and the transition as the tuple (g, ti , si , τi , oi ). The proof tree is then updated with
757   G = G ∪ gi′ for all gi′ , and the associated transitions are added as edges to E. This is repeated until a
758   proof is found, or a budget is exhausted. A proof of g is found when E(g, ti ) = ∅ for any ti , or if all
759   {gi′ } are proven for E(g, ti ) = {gi′ } ⊂ S. The result of a proof attempt is then the set of transitions
760   {(gk , tki , ski , τki , oki )} for all selected goals gk and their expanded tactics ti . For simplicity, we
761   drop the indices to denote the set of transitions as {(g, t, s, τ, o)}.

793   As discussed by Chen et al. (2021), the Pass@k metric favours exploratory approaches as k in-
794   creases, at the cost of lower performance for smaller k. This is because, over many attempts, a
795
      highly exploratory approach is more likely to find at least one proof of a given goal, even though

796
      in Lample et al. (2022) finds that randomly sampling search parameters also improves Pass@k.
797
      With Pass@k being expensive to estimate, we fix our parameters over the four runs to give a more
798   accurate estimate of Pass@1. Given this, a large scale experiment sampling these hyperparameters
799   could lead to improved Pass@k results, as Lample et al. (2022) show for their HTPS approach.

863   Embedding Comparison We now investigate whether the transition model (Figure 2) captures
      tactic semantics rather than syntax in its tactic embeddings. To test this, we examine the cosine


                                                                                 16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      similarity of tactic embeddings which lead to unique subgoals. Figure 4 takes an example node,
865   examining all tactics which lead to a unique subgoal. The upper value displays the cosine similarity
866   given by the transition model, while the lower value displays that given by the Autoencoder in
867   Section 3.2.2. We observe that in most cases, the similarity given by the transition model is much
868   lower than that given by the Autoencoder, which is only considering the syntax of the tactic. For
869   example, the similarity between tactic 3 and 4 is very high for the Autoencoder, given the similar
870   syntax between the two as they use the same lemma. Despite this similar syntax, the transition
871   model embeddings show a high degree of dissimilarity, reflecting the different outcome they have
872   on the environment. We present additional examples in the supplementary code. To generalise
873
      beyond these examples, we ran this comparison over the tactic embeddings which lead to unique
      subgoals for all 244 root nodes in minF2F-valid. Figure 5 shows the distribution of the average
874
      cosine similarity for each node, for both the transition model and the Autoencoder. The average
875
      cosine similarity for the transition model embeddings was 0.44 while the Autoencoder gave 0.57.
876   While this comparison does not account for similarity between the unique subgoals, it is still clear
877   that the transition model embeddings better separate unique tactics than Autoencoder embeddings
878   which are based on syntax alone. The result of this is a higher likelihood of 3D-Prover selecting
879   tactics which give unique subgoals, which as we show in Section 3.2.2, results in the transition
880   model outperforming the Autoencoder for proof discovery.
881
882   Embedding Objective As outlined in Section 2, we train our embeddings to be reflective of the
883   tactic semantics across all three components of Status, Time and Output. Hence 3D-Prover, which
884   selects diverse embeddings, may lead to tactics predicted to have errors, where the errors are diverse
885   in terms of their predicted message. The hyperparameter λs can alleviate this by weighting the
886
      scores based on their likelihood of success. From our experiments (Table 10), there is not necessarily
      a benefit to Pass@1 by filtering out strongly based on the predicted error likelihood. To speculate,
887
      the error prediction, although quite good, is imperfect with many false negatives (Table 1). This can
888
      lead to potentially useful tactics being ignored if the error prediction is overly trusted, even though
889   there is a higher tactic success rate overall as in Table 4. Given these prediction errors, it may be
890   the case that selecting goals which are predicted to lead to (diverse) errors may be preferable, given
891   the possibility they result in successful new subgoals. These subgoals may be be quite different
892   from those previously selected, as they are mispredicted, so are clearly outside the space of tactics
893   where the transition model is confident about the outcome. Further analysis could be worthwhile
894   to investigate this. An embedding architecture trained only on successful tactics could be used,
895   however given the high error rate of tactics, this would ignore a large proportion of the transition
896   data.

898   A.5   C OMPUTATIONAL OVERHEAD
899
900
      On our hardware, we found 3D-Prover adds a constant overhead, taking approximately 2x as long for
      tactic generation. The majority of this is in generating embeddings for the 64 tactics, which we were
901
      unable to batch on our hardware due to memory constraints. The DPP algorithm itself added almost
902
      no overhead once the embeddings were generated. This could be sped up by batching (if memory
903   permits), or through a different architecture. For example, the S EPARATE model in Section 2.2 could
904   be used, where tactics can be batched with much less memory. An augmented architecture which
905   embeds the goal in isolation, which is then given to the tactic encoder as a single vector, could be
906   used. This would provide a speed up while allowing some attention between the tactic and the goal,
907   although not to the degree allowed for by our C OMBINED model. As a proof of concept, we used
908   the C OMBINED model as it provides the most goal-aware embeddings to test our filtering algorithm.
