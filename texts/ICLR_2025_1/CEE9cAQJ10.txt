                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Synthesizing high-quality data for continual training has been proven to be effec-
012            tive in enhancing the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs). However,
013            previous synthetic approaches struggle to easily scale up data and incur high costs
014            in the pursuit of high quality. In this paper, we propose the Graph-based Synthetic
015            Data Pipeline (GSDP), an economical and scalable framework for high-quality
016            reasoning data synthesis. Inspired by knowledge graphs, we extracted knowl-
017            edge points from seed data and constructed a knowledge point relationships graph
018            to explore their interconnections. By exploring the implicit relationships among
019            knowledge, our method achieves ×255 data expansion. Furthermore, GSDP led
020
               by open-source models, achieves synthesis quality comparable to GPT-4-0613
               while maintaining ×100 lower costs. To tackle the most challenging mathemat-
021
               ical reasoning task, we present the GSDP-MATH dataset comprising over 1.91
022
               million pairs of math problems and answers. After fine-tuning on GSDP-MATH,
023            GSDP-7B based on Mistral-7B achieves 37.7% accuracy on MATH and 78.4% on
024            GSM8K, demonstrating the effectiveness of our method. The dataset and models
025            trained in this paper will be available.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Despite the remarkable capabilities large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated in various
031   linguistic tasks, significant gaps remain in their ability to comprehend and solve intricate reasoning
032   tasks (e.g., mathematics, coding, physics, and chemistry). One effective approach to bridging these
033
      gaps is using large-scale, high-quality synthetic data. However, it is still a challenge to develop a
      low-cost and effective synthesis pipeline.
034
035   Take mathematics as an example. The two main approaches for building high-quality mathematics
036   reasoning datasets are data filtering and data synthesis. Data filtering (Yue et al., 2024b; Shao et al.,
037   2024; Ying et al., 2024) involves extracting data from pre-training corpora such as Common Crawl,
038   and rewriting it using advanced commercial models or human annotation. However, the vast scale
039
      and inherent noise of these corpora result in high post-processing costs and inconsistent data quality.
      Data synthesis (Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
040
      2024a; Li et al., 2024a; Toshniwal et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b) leverages frontier large language
041
      models, such as GPT-3.5 (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), to augment
042   or regenerate high-quality mathematical reasoning datasets. One approach entails rewriting or re-
043   generating similar problems based on seed data for data augmentation. Another approach entails
044   generating new problems using knowledge points. The “knowledge points” refers to fine-grained
045   math concepts (e.g., the Pythagorean theorem, polynomial factorization skills) in problem solving,
046   and they can be generated freshly via LLMs or extracted from existing seed data. Although data
047   synthesis is straightforward, it still suffers from three significant drawbacks: (1) Limited scalabil-
048   ity: Existing methods have poor scalability, making it difficult to synthesize larger-scale data from
049   smaller seed data. (2) High cost: Data synthesis relies on the assistance of closed-source models,
050
      which introduces considerable synthesis costs. (3) Similarity to seed data: Due to the over-reliance
      on seed data during synthesis, the new data becomes highly similar to the seed.
051
052   To address these drawbacks, we introduce Graph-based Synthetic Data Pipeline (GSDP), a novel
053   framework to scale the synthesis of high-quality data. As presented in Figure 2, GSDP encompasses
      four critical stages: (1) The knowledge base consists of Knowledge Points (KPs). Its construction

      The highlight of the KPRG is its ability to explore both explicit and implicit relationships among
082
      knowledge points. Unlike previous methods that either relied entirely on seed data or focused solely
083
      on explicit relationships, our approach leverages both types of connections by KPRG. This allows us
084   to address the drawbacks mentioned above: (1) High scalability: By utilizing implicit relationships,
085   we can synthesize a much larger volume of data, as implicit relationships are far more abundant than
086   explicit ones. (2) Low seed similarity: Since implicit relationships do not appear in the seed data,
087   they help synthesize problems that are different from the seed data. (3) Low cost: The entire pipeline
088   uses only open-source models, significantly reducing synthesis costs.
089

090   seed similarity and data diversity in Appendix E, we demonstrate the advantages of our method from
091   a quantitative perspective. Specifically, GSDP achieves an expansion ratio of up to 255, incurs less
092   than 1% of the cost compared to other methods, and GSDP-MATH exhibits lower seed similarity and
093   greater diversity. Moreover, we validate the effectiveness of GSDP-MATH on several base models,
094   including Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), LLaMA3-8B (Meta, 2024), and Qwen1.5-7B (Bai et al.,
095   2023). The GSDP models demonstrably surpass the base models across four mathematical reasoning
096   benchmarks: MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), Gaokao-Bench (Team,
097   2024), and SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021). Notably, GSDP-7B based on Mistral-7B achieves 78.4% on
098
      GSM8K and 37.7% on MATH, surpassing all competitors under the same conditions.

      2.2     K NOWLEDGE BASE CONSTRUCTION
135
      In order to help LLMs better understand complex information like math problems, we decompose
136
      seed data into simpler meta information and then construct a knowledge base to represent the entire
137   dataset in a more flexible form. The meta information contained in a problem includes elements such
138   as “Subject”, “Topic”, and “Knowledge Point”. For instance, the “Subject” might be “Mathematics”,
139   the “Topic” could be “Algebra”, and the “Knowledge Point” could be “methods for solving quadratic
140   equations”. To simplify the extraction process and facilitate comprehension by the model, the meta
141   information we extract is limited to “Knowledge Point”, and we demonstrate through experiments
142   that ”Knowledge Points” sufficiently encapsulate most of the information about a problem.
143   GSDP use the MATH training set as the seed data which consist of 7.5k math problems. As shown in
144   Figure 2, we first extract no more than 10 relevant knowledge points (KPs) from each seed problem
145   with prompt engineering of DeepSeek-Math-RL (Shao et al., 2024) (see Prompt 2.1 for the prompt).
146   After extracting the KPs, we employ an embedding model (Chen et al., 2023a) and a large language
147   model (LLM) for dual filtering of the KPs. Initially, the LLM filters out KPs with vagueness, math-
148   ematical errors, or excessive detail. Subsequently, the embedding model clusters KPs with similar
149   meanings, followed by a second validation using the LLM. Finally, the most appropriate and accu-
150   rate KP from each cluster is chosen to represent the cluster. For more details on dual filtering and
151
      KP examples, please refer to Appendix D.

181   2.3      KPRG CONSTRUCTION
182
183   To structure the disordered KPs in the knowledge base and explore their specific interconnections,
184   we designed a Knowledge Point Relationships Graph (KPRG) to get all related KPs pairs.
185
      In the KPRG, each node is represented as a KP, and each edge represents that the connected KPs
186   have co-occurred in the same problem. Specifically, the KPRG G can be represented as G = (K, E).
187   The nodes K, which refer to knowledge points, are denoted as K = {k1 , k2 , . . . , k|K| }. The edges
188   E are denoted as E = {Eex , Eim }, and there are two types: (1) Explicit (Eex ): Knowledge points
189   that appear together in the same seed problem are connected by a solid edge. The explicit edges Eex
190   can be denoted as Eex = {(ki , kj )|D(ki , kj ) = 1}, where the edge distance D(ki , kj ) represents
191   the number of solid edges in the shortest path between ki and kj . Additionally, the edge weight
192   W(ki , kj ) is recorded to denote the co-occurrence frequency between ki and kj . (2) Implicit (Eim ):
193   Knowledge points with more than one solid edge between them are connected by a dashed edge, as
194   shown in Figure 3. The implicit edges Eim can be denoted as Eim = {(ki , kj )|D(ki , kj ) > 1}.
195
196   2.4      S YNTHESIS BASED ON DIVERSE KNOWLEDGE POINT COMBINATIONS
197
198   Different from previous methods that primarily explored explicit relationships between KPs, we
199   proposed four types of knowledge point relationships: one-hop, two-hop, three-hop, and community,
200
      which fully integrate both explicit and implicit relationships to generate more diverse synthetic data.
      Additionally, the exploration of implicit relationships has helped us obtain more combinations of
201
      knowledge points, which is also a key reason why GSDP can achieve a high expansion ratio. Figure
202
      3 illustrates an example of constructing KPRG from an existing knowledge base and identifying
203   these four KPs combinations.
204
205   One-hop represents explicit relationships, consisting of all pairs of KPs that are directly connected
206
      by a single edge. The weight of the edge represents the frequency of their co-occurrence. One-hop
      combinations have appeared in the seed data, thus they have high relevance.
207
208   Two-hop is a type of implicit relationship, consisting of all pairs of KPs that are indirectly connected
209   by two edges. Two-hop is the nearest implicit relationship, connected indirectly through one node,
210   maintaining a certain degree of relevance.
211   Three-hop further explores implicit relationship, consisting of all pairs of KPs indirectly connected
212   through three edges, with one core knowledge point and one other knowledge point in each pair.
213   Core KPs are those with the highest number of connections in the KPRG, indicating their importance
214   and wide applicability within the knowledge system. When the graph is large, there may be multiple
215   core KPs. As the distance between knowledge points increases, the relevance tends to weaken, so we
      focus on core knowledge points to ensure that Three-hop remains meaningful. For example, assume


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      that a core knowledge point is “calculus”. In the seed data, problems only associate “calculus” with
217   fundamental concepts such as “limits” and “derivatives”. However, in the KPRG, “calculus” is likely
218   not adjacent but close to KPs such as “Fourier transforms” and “complex functions”. By integrating
219   these three-hop KPs to construct new problems (the same applies to two-hop), it can significantly
220   increase the diversity of the problems and simultaneously promote the model’s ability to learn new
221   types of problems, thereby enhancing its mathematical capabilities.
222
      Community represents explicit relationships, consisting of three knowledge points, each pair of
223   which is mutually connected by edges. There is a strong correlation between the three KPs.
224
225   Accordingly, the one-hop combinations are used to synthesize high-quality variant problems directly
226
      related to the seed data. Implicit relationships combinations are used to synthesize new distribution
      data, increasing the diversity of the dataset. Community combinations are utilized to generate com-
227
      plex and challenging problems, enriching the dataset’s difficulty levels.
228
229   After listing all valid knowledge point combinations, we input the prompt and knowledge point com-
230   binations into the math model to synthesize new problems. We do not include few-shot examples in
231   the prompt, as this would cause the model to generate problems too similar to them. The prompt is
232
      shown in Prompt 2.2. Before solution generation, a rating model assigns a difficulty level to each
      problem. For medium and low difficulty issues, DeepSeek-Math-RL generates the solutions, while
233
      LLaMA3.1-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) handles high difficulty problems.
234
235   Moreover, we implement a decontamination process to remove all math problems found in the
236   MATH dataset.

      2.5     E VALUATION OF PROBLEMS AND SOLUTIONS
246
247
      To achieve the same effectiveness as closed-source models, we use multiple open-source models
248   jointly scoring to filter the data. The more models involved, the higher the data quality, but this
249   also increases the amount of data filtered out and the time required. Therefore, we need to find an
250   optimal combination of open-source models to balance high-quality rate and total data volume. In
251   Experiment 3.8, supervised by GPT-4, we find a combination that achieves 94% of GPT-4-0613’s
252   effectiveness while retaining 45% of the data: Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024), InternLM2 (Cai et al.,
253   2024), and LLaMA3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024).
254   For problems evaluation, we use a weighted scoring filtering strategy. Problems are evaluated on
255   two criteria: logical completeness (absence of mathematical errors and accurate relation to provided
256   knowledge points) and presentational completeness (clarity, completeness, and absence of prompts
257   or answers). We use the multiple open-source models for evaluation, assigning each problem a
258   score between 0 and 1 based on a weighted sum of model scores (with a threshold of 0.85). For
259   solutions evaluation, we adopt a single-vote veto strategy. The evaluation model requires solutions
260   to be mathematically error-free and to fully satisfy the problem requirements. Each solution is
261   scored as either 0 or 1, depending on whether all models unanimously agree on its correctness.
262
      Solutions flagged as problematic by any model are filtered out. Finally, we constructed a dataset
      named GSDP-MATH comprising 1.91 million high-quality mathematical questions at low cost.

      4     R ELATED WORK
498
499
      4.1   LLM S AND MATHEMATICAL REASONING
500
501
      Recent research has focused extensively on enhancing the reasoning capabilities of foundational
502   large language models (LLMs). However, general-purpose LLMs such as LLaMA3 (Meta, 2024)
503   , Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), InternLM2 (Team, 2023), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023) , Yi (Young et al.,
504   2024), and DeepSeek (Bi et al., 2024) have demonstrated suboptimal performance in mathematical
505   reasoning tasks. Consequently, researchers have explored various strategies to augment the mathe-
506   matical reasoning capabilities of LLMs. The primary approaches encompass continued pre-training
507   and instruction fine-tuning. Continued pre-training involves training LLMs on extensive pre-training
508   datasets (Lewkowycz et al., 2022; Taylor et al., 2022; Azerbayev et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Ying
509   et al., 2024). In contrast, instruction fine-tuning focuses on applying supervised fine-tuning losses to
510
      small-scale, high-quality instruction-response pairs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024). Both
      approaches necessitate the availability of high-quality mathematical inference data to be effective.
511
512
513   4.2   DATA SYNTHESIS
514
515   In the domain of mathematical reasoning, data synthesis is predominantly utilized for instruction
516
      fine-tuning, where each data sample comprises a question text and its corresponding answer text.
      Leveraging our method’s exceptional scalability, we are capable of synthesizing extensive quantities
517
      of high-quality mathematical inference data from a limited amount of seed data, thus making it
518
      equally suitable for continued pre-training tasks. Research efforts primarily concentrate on two
519   pivotal aspects: improving data quality and generating novel questions. Regarding the generation of
520   novel questions, one approach (Yu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024a; Tang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a;
521   Toshniwal et al., 2024) entails rewriting or generating similar questions based on seed data for
522   data augmentation. Another approach (Huang et al., 2024a;b; Li et al., 2024b) involves generating
523   new questions using knowledge points, either by generating new knowledge points via GPT-4 or
524   extracting them from existing knowledge point databases. However, this approach often suffers
525   from limited scalability, high cost, and high similarity to the seed data, because it primarily focuses
526   on the explicit relationships between KPs and uses GPT-4 or GPT-3.5. Our method overcomes these
527
      limitations by exploring both explicit and implicit relationships between KPs with the help of KPRG,
      and using open-source models instead of closed-source models to synthesize data at low cost.
528
529
530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce GSDP, a low-cost, highly scalable paradigm for synthesizing high-quality
533
      data. Utilizing this method in mathematics, we constructed the GSDP-MATH dataset, comprising
534   1.91 million high-quality question-answer pairs specifically designed to enhance the mathematical
535   reasoning capabilities of large language models. By leveraging this dataset, GSDP-7B have demon-
536   strated outstanding performance across multiple mathematical test sets. Our research indicates that
537   thoroughly exploring the implicit relationships between knowledge points is an effective method
538   for synthesizing larger-scale and more diverse data. Furthermore, combining multiple open-source
539   models can achieve performance close to closed-source models, which is key to the low-cost syn-
      thesis of high-quality data.

898   D.1   D UAL F ILTERING
899
900   Ensuring the quality of KPs is crucial, as using meaningless KPs can result in low-quality syn-
901   thesized problems, while using overly similar KPs can lead to duplicated problems. These issues
902
      increase the computational and time costs for both problem synthesis and problem quality validation.
      We employ a dual filtering strategy using both embedding models and LLMs to remove low-quality
903
      and duplicated KPs. The three main steps are as follows:
904
905         • Eliminating Low-Quality KPs: LLMs are used to filter out KPs that are vague, contain
906           mathematical errors, or are overly detailed. This is because vague KPs can be too broad in
907           meaning, failing to standardize the model’s output effectively. Erroneous KPs may lead the
908           model to synthesize incorrect questions, while overly detailed KPs can overly constrain the
909           model’s output.
910         • Categorization: We first use an embedding model to calculate pairwise similarity scores
911           between KPs. KPs with similarity scores between 0.90 and 1.0 are deemed to have the same
912           meaning, while those with scores between 0.70 and 0.90 undergo an additional check by
913           the LLM to confirm if they are truly synonymous. KPs with scores below 0.70 are treated
914           as distinct. Based on this process, KPs are grouped into classes with similar KPs placed in
915           the same class. These thresholds were determined through an analysis of the KP set.
916         • Summarization: For each KP class, the LLM identifies the most representative KP to act
917           as the class representative. If no existing KP in the class is suitable, the LLM synthesizes a
              new KP to represent the class.


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      When only the embedding model was used for de-duplication, the quality check revealed that only
919   26% of the synthesized problems met the quality standard. After introducing dual filtering with the
920   LLM, this proportion increased to 45%. This demonstrates that the dual filtering process signifi-
921   cantly improves dataset quality while reducing problem synthesis costs.
