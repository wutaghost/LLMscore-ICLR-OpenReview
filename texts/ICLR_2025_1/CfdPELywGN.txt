011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Language models’ ability to extrapolate learned behaviors to novel, more complex
014            environments beyond their training scope is highly unknown. This study introduces
015            a path planning task in a textualized Gridworld to probe language models’ extrapo-
016
               lation capabilities. We show that conventional approaches, including next-token
               prediction and Chain of Thought (CoT) fine-tuning, fail to extrapolate in larger,
017
               unseen environments. Inspired by human cognition and dual-process theory, we
018
               propose cognitive maps for path planning—a novel CoT framework that simulates
019            human-like mental representations. Our experiments show that cognitive maps not
020            only enhance extrapolation to unseen environments but also exhibit human-like
021            characteristics through structured mental simulation and rapid adaptation. Our
022            finding that these cognitive maps require specialized training schemes and cannot
023            be induced through simple prompting opens up important questions about devel-
024            oping general-purpose cognitive maps in language models. Our comparison with
025            exploration-based methods further illuminates the complementary strengths of
026            offline planning and online exploration.

031   1.1   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN HUMAN COGNITION AND LANGUAGE MODELS : COGNITIVE MAP
032
033
      Recent advancements in language models have demonstrated remarkable proficiency across various
      complex tasks, ranging from natural language understanding to code generation (Brown et al., 2020;
034
      Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021). These models, primarily trained on
035
      next-token prediction, excel in general planning tasks by leveraging their extensive learned knowledge
036   and pattern recognition abilities (Ahn et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023). However,
037   they often struggle with robust, long-horizon planning tasks. Experiments in controlled settings,
038   such as Einstein’s puzzle, digit multiplication (Dziri et al., 2024), or Blocksworld (Valmeekam et al.,
039   2022), demonstrate that even advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) fail to generalize
040   effectively to compositional tasks when faced with longer, more complex planning scenarios. In
041   contrast, humans excel in such tasks, solving them effortlessly even in challenging environments.
042   What cognitive mechanisms enable humans to generalize so effectively? According to the dual-
043   process theory, humans employ two types of reasoning: fast, automatic System 1 processes and
044   slower, deliberative System 2 processes (Kahneman, 2011). System 2 reasoning, characterized by
045   constructing and utilizing mental representations, allows humans to adapt learned behaviors to novel,
046   complex environments (Yousefzadeh & Mollick, 2021; Fellini & Morellini, 2011; Stojic et al., 2018).
047   Cognitive science literature refers to this aspect of human cognition as cognitive maps—mental
048   representations of environments that enable flexible planning. These cognitive maps involve a
049   network of brain regions, including the hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and parietal cortex, and play
050
      a critical role in complex decision-making and adaptation to new situations (O’Keefe & Nadel, 1978;
      Daw et al., 2005; Doll et al., 2012).
051
052   While the next-token prediction paradigm equips language models to perform System 1-like pattern
053   recognition, it does not naturally encompass System 2 processes. Recent approaches, such as
      Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning, show promise for mimicking System 2 reasoning via in-context

      learning (Wei et al., 2023), prompting (Kojima et al., 2023), fine-tuning (Kim et al., 2023), or even
055   without explicit prompting (Wang & Zhou, 2024). However, conventional CoT methods fall short of
056   exhibiting cognitive map-like behavior (Momennejad et al., 2023). In essence, while these approaches
057   improve reasoning capabilities, they still fall short of implementing true cognitive maps that enable
058   flexible, map-like planning and navigation of problem spaces.

060   1.2    T ESTING COGNITIVE MAPS FOR LANGUAGE MODELS THROUGH EXTRAPOLABILITY
061
062   Before asking how to create cognitive maps for language models, we first need to establish a method
063   to test whether a language model possesses a cognitive map. We propose that extrapolability—the
064   ability to generalize learned knowledge to novel, complex environments after training on simple
065   demonstrations—can serve as a proxy for this1 .
066   We posit that the fundamental distinction between AI models and human cognition lies in extrapolation
067   rather than interpolation capabilities. While language models excel at interpolation—performing
068   effectively on tasks within the training distribution due to KL divergence loss minimization during
069   training (LeCun et al., 2015)—they struggle with extrapolation, particularly in compositional tasks or
070   novel, complex environments.
071   To investigate cognitive maps in language models, we evaluated their extrapolation capabilities using
072   a textualized Gridworld path-planning task (Brown, 2015). This choice aligns with cognitive science
073   foundations, where spatial tasks have proven valuable for studying mental representations (Epstein
074   et al., 2017; O’Keefe & Nadel, 1978; Kessler et al., 2024; Kadner et al., 2023). We designed controlled
075   environments to examine models’ ability to construct and utilize cognitive maps rather than focusing
076   on scalability. Our experiments reveal that language models, whether trained via imitation learning
077   or conventional Chain of Thought fine-tuning, fail to effectively extrapolate to larger environments
078   (Figure 1).

080   1.3    C OGNITIVE MAPS FOR LANGUAGE MODELS IN T EXTUALIZED G RIDWORLD SETTING
081
082   Then is it possible to inject human-like cognitive maps into language models? Theoretical findings
083
      suggest that complex problems outside the T C 0 (polynomial-sized constant-depth circuits) com-
      plexity class can be solved with an ample amount of CoT tokens (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2024; Feng
084
      et al., 2024). However, the precise form of CoT required remains unclear. Recent advancements,
085
      such as the GPT-o1 model (OpenAI, 2024), achieve remarkable performance across various domains,
086   including mathematics and coding. Yet, its CoT is opaque, and the training data remains inaccessible,
087   making it challenging to determine whether the model genuinely exhibits extrapolation.
088
089
      Building upon prior research discussed in Appendix A, this paper presents a novel training approach
      for language models using datasets augmented with cognitive maps for path planning - as a form of
090
      Chain of Thought (CoT) reasoning. We design the cognitive maps to emulate human mental models
091
      used in spatial reasoning and decision-making by explicitly verbalizing complete decision trees
092   (detailed in Section 3). Our experimental results demonstrate that fine-tuning models to generate such
093   cognitive maps significantly improves their planning capabilities in novel, extrapolated environments.
094   Importantly, unlike traditional CoT approaches that can be implemented through prompting alone,
095   cognitive maps require specific training mechanisms. This finding addresses a critical gap identified in
096   recent studies examining spatial reasoning and cognitive mapping capabilities in language models (Xu
097   et al., 2024; Momennejad et al., 2023; Yamada et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
098   Our findings demonstrate that integrating cognitive maps into language models significantly en-
099   hances their capacity to generalize to complex and novel environments, bridging the gap between
100   System 2 reasoning and extrapolability. Beyond emergent extrapolability, our cognitive maps exhibit

143   2.1   D ESCRIPTION OF THE T EXTUALIZED G RIDWORLD
144
145   We introduce Textualized Gridworld, inspired by Brown (2015), as our primary task. In this path
146
      planning challenge, an agent navigates from a start state to a goal state, avoiding obstacles. Each
      environment is designed with exactly one valid path to ensure clear evaluation of the model’s planning
147
      capabilities.
148
149   The uniqueness of our approach lies in the textualized nature of both input and output. We provide
150   the model with a textual description of the Gridworld environment, including the dimensions of the
151   grid, the locations of the start and goal states, and the positions of obstacles. The model interacts
152
      with this environment by generating textual commands (either “up”, “down”, “left”, or “right”), and
      receives textual descriptions of the resulting state transitions (See Appendix B.1 for examples of
153
      textualized input instructions and state descriptions).
154
155   Our primary objective is to investigate whether a language model trained on grid up to n × n, can
156   successfully plan paths in a × b grids, where a and b can vary in relation to n. We define interpolation
157   when both a and b are smaller than or equal to n, and extrapolation when either a or b (or both) are
158
      greater than n. This challenge tests the model’s ability to generalize its understanding of spatial
      relationships and apply path planning strategies beyond the scope of its training data.
159
160   Specifically, we train the model on 50,000 samples with grid sizes up to 10 × 10 for one epoch
161   and tested on 3,000 samples with grid sizes up to 20 × 20, allowing us to evaluate the model’s
      extrapolation capabilities. Also, we design each environment to have exactly one valid path to

      ensure a clear evaluation of the model’s planning ability. We describe further details such as setup
163   information and data statistics in Appendices B.2 and B.4.
164
165   By framing this task in natural language, we explore the limits of language models’ abstract thinking
166
      and problem-solving capacities, probing their ability to form and manipulate mental representations
      of complex spatial environments described solely through text.

      2.2   J USTIFICATION FOR C HOOSING T EXTUALIZED G RIDWORLD
170
      The selection of Textualized Gridworld as our primary task is motivated by four key factors:
171
172          • Cognitive science foundations: Gridworld serves as an ideal testbed because probing
173            mental representations through spatial tasks is foundational in cognitive science (Epstein
174            et al., 2017; O’Keefe & Nadel, 1978; Kessler et al., 2024; Kadner et al., 2023). Similar
175            to seminal studies in this field, our work leverages controlled environments to provide
176            valuable insights into specific cognitive capabilities, such as the ability to construct and
177
               utilize cognitive maps. Through testing different CoT patterns during navigation tasks, we
               aim to identify representations that enable robust extrapolation capabilities.
178
179          • Minimal knowledge requirements: Gridworld provides an ideal environment to probe the
180            extrapolability of language models while minimizing the influence of world knowledge. Un-
181
               like more complex board games such as Einstein’s puzzle (Brainzilla, 2017) or Blocksworld
               (Valmeekam et al., 2022), Gridworld requires only a basic understanding of four actions (up,
182
               down, left, right) and simple, explicit state transitions. This simplicity allows us to focus
183
               on the model’s ability to reason and plan, rather than its capacity to leverage pre-existing
184            knowledge.
185
             • Scalability and generalization testing: A crucial advantage of Gridworld is its ability to
186
               generate environments of arbitrary size. This feature is essential for testing the model’s
187
               capacity to extrapolate beyond its training experience. Unlike other planning tasks such as
188            coding or mathematical problem-solving, Gridworld allows us to systematically increase
189            the problem space by expanding the grid dimensions. This scalability facilitates a clear and
190            controlled assessment of the model’s ability to generalize its planning strategies to larger,
191            unseen environments.
192          • Complexity class and computational limitations: Gridworld’s placement outside the T C 0
193            complexity class is crucial for our study. Unlike tasks such as multiplication or addition
194            which fall within T C 0 and can be solved by embedding modification (McLeish et al.,
195            2024) or CoT distillation (Deng et al., 2023; 2024), Gridworld requires more sophisticated
196            computational processes. This characteristic makes it impossible to solve Gridworld through
197            mere next-token prediction, as suggested by existing research on the limitations of fixed-
198            precision transformer architectures (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023). By choosing a task outside
199            T C 0 , we create a rigorous test of language models’ capacity for complex reasoning and
200
               planning, challenging them to go beyond simple pattern recognition and construct multi-step
               plans. This allows us to explore the boundaries of what these models can achieve with their
201
               current architectures and training paradigms, potentially revealing insights into their ability
202
               to tackle more complex, sequential decision-making tasks.
203
204   Especially, we show that Gridworld environment complexity increases with grid size. We demonstrate
205   systematic complexity differences between training and test datasets, highlighting the additional chal-
206   lenges posed by more complex Gridworld environments during inference. This analysis establishes
207   that testing beyond the training bounds in Gridworld represents extrapolation not only in spatial scale
208   but also in computational complexity. We provide a formal definition of environment complexity and
209
      detailed analysis in Appendix B.3.

213   We now propose a universal design for cognitive maps for path planning as a CoT for language
214   models. Our approach consists of three stages: sampling, propagation, and backtracking, enabling
215   the model to construct a comprehensive mental representation and generate optimal solutions without
      external interaction.

376   that closely mirror the maze’s structural elements. Additionally, humans display sophisticated
377   search strategies, dynamically balancing between depth and breadth-first approaches based on the
      environment’s complexity.

455   strategies, and iteratively recognize and correct its mistakes. This could make it more adept at
456   reasoning that forms mental representations of spatial relationships, even without explicit prompting
457   for cognitive maps.
458
      However, it is unclear whether GPT-o1’s reasoning capabilities align with the core principles of
459   cognitive maps, particularly their extrapolability to unseen, complex environments. Without direct
460   evidence of its performance in such settings, we can only speculate whether GPT-o1 represents a
461   step toward generalized cognitive maps for language models. These findings hint at the potential for
462   specialized training approaches to enable models to construct and utilize cognitive maps effectively,
463   but further investigation is needed to draw definitive conclusions.
464
465
      6.3   C OMPARISON WITH E XPLORATION -BASED P LANNING
466
467
      How does our cognitive map approach compare to exploration-based planning methods? Recent
468   works have introduced various exploration strategies for language models, including Tree of Thoughts
469   (ToT) (Yao et al., 2023), which combines language model sampling with value estimation for tree
470   construction and traversal. Additional methods enhance exploration through techniques like Monte
471   Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006; Hao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). However,
472   two significant challenges emerge in direct comparisons.
473
      First, exploration-based methods are inherently constrained to reachable planning scenarios, where
474   decisions occur incrementally during inference. This fundamental limitation prevents direct compar-
475   isons with optimal planning approaches like our cognitive maps. Second, our analysis of conventional
476   LLMs’ sampling behavior reveals consistent overconfidence in specific directions, leading to ineffec-
477   tive tree structure generation and exploration in Gridworld environments. This behavioral limitation
478   undermines the effectiveness of methods like ToT (Yao et al., 2023) and RAP (Hao et al., 2023) in
479   tasks requiring systematic search and robust spatial reasoning.
480   To enable meaningful comparison, we developed an exploration-based baseline where language
481   models were specifically trained to perform Depth-First Search (DFS) for reachability analysis. This
482   controlled approach allowed us to evaluate structured exploration under conditions comparable to our
483   cognitive map method, while focusing on reachability analysis in planning tasks. Our experiments
484   show that DFS-based exploration achieves higher reachability rates (94.5%) compared to our cogni-
485   tive map approach (88.5%) in reachable planning scenarios (Table 11). However, cognitive maps
      demonstrate superior efficiency in path optimality. While DFS requires O(n2 ) steps for paths of


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      optimal length n, our method achieves near-optimal performance (O(n)) even in reachable planning
487   settings (Figure 10) (details in Appendix E.3).
488
489   These findings highlight a crucial trade-off: while exploration-based methods achieve higher success
490
      rates through exhaustive tree search in reachable planning scenarios, cognitive maps enable more effi-
      cient planning through structured environmental representations as a CoT. This efficiency difference
491
      illuminates two fundamental planning paradigms:
492
493          • Offline Planning via Cognitive Maps: Cognitive maps construct mental representations
494            during the CoT stage, enabling complete decision process simulation before action.
495          • Online Planning through Exploration: Exploration-based planning utilizes active explo-
496            ration for incremental spatial understanding and adaptive plan refinement.
497
498   Both approaches play vital roles in human cognition, serving complementary functions. Offline
499   planning facilitates high-level strategy formation and global understanding, while online planning
500
      enables real-time adjustments and responsive problem-solving in dynamic environments.
501
502   7   C ONCLUSION
503
504   Our work serves as a proof-of-concept for training language models with enhanced spatial reasoning
505   capabilities through targeted supervision of cognitive map construction. While we do not claim to
506   replicate exact human learning processes, our results demonstrate that enabling structured mental
507   representations through cognitive maps leads to superior extrapolation abilities in path planning tasks.
508   Our findings raise two important considerations for future research directions:
509
510          • Development of general-purpose cognitive maps: Our experiments reveal the unprompt-
511            able nature of cognitive map reasoning, indicating that current pre-training approaches
512
               are insufficient for developing such cognitive capabilities. While our implementation
               demonstrates success in the Gridworld domain, extending these principles to more general
513
               tasks and abstract problem spaces requires further investigation. Human cognitive maps
514
               are significantly more complex than our current implementations, encompassing not only
515            spatial reasoning but also abstract concept spaces and relational knowledge. The unique
516            performance of models like GPT-o1 suggests that alternative training paradigms, such as
517            reinforcement learning combined with large-scale Chain of Thought (CoT) data augmen-
518            tation, might offer a more organic path to acquiring cognitive map-like representations.
519            Additionally, exploring techniques for generating diverse reasoning traces through tree
520            search could provide valuable insights into embedding these capabilities more effectively
521            into foundation models.
522          • Integration of different planning paradigms: Our work highlights the complementary
523            nature of different planning paradigms. While this study primarily examines the offline
524            planning capabilities of cognitive maps, our comparison with exploration-based methods
525            reveals interesting trade-offs between planning efficiency and success rates. Future research
526            could explore hybrid approaches that integrate the strengths of both cognitive maps and
527
               online exploration. Such frameworks could bridge the gap between offline and online
               reasoning while more closely approximating the dual planning modes observed in human
528
               cognition.
529
530   Despite these open challenges, our work makes several significant contributions to the field. We
531   systematically demonstrate that a specific form of CoT prompting, structured as a cognitive map,
532   can enable extrapolation in spatial reasoning tasks. Our implementation exhibits characteristics
533   that parallel human cognitive processes, including structured mental representations and rapid
534   adaptation during training. The unpromptable nature of cognitive map reasoning suggests fundamental
535   limitations in current pre-training approaches and highlights the need for specialized training schemes.
536   Looking ahead, this work opens new avenues for developing more sophisticated cognitive architec-
537   tures in language models. While our current implementation represents a simplified version of human
538   cognitive maps, it provides a foundation for understanding how structured mental representations can
539   enhance planning and reasoning capabilities. Future work in this direction could lead to more robust
      and adaptable AI systems capable of human-like reasoning across diverse problem domains.

      A     R ELATED WORKS
757
758
759   A.1   P LANNING IN COGNITIVE SCIENCE
760
761   Dual-process theories of cognition distinguish between System 1 and System 2 reasoning, where
762   System 1 involves fast, automatic, and intuitive thinking, and System 2 involves slow, deliberate, and
763   analytical thought (Kahneman, 2011). Especially, System 2 cognition for human typically engages in
764   iterative mental simulations, refining steps until reaching the goal while avoiding dead-end states.
765   This process involves the formation and use of “cognitive maps,” which are mental representations of
766   spatial environments allowing for flexible navigation and planning. While traditionally associated with
767   the hippocampus (O’Keefe & Nadel, 1978), cognitive maps involve a network of brain regions. The
768
      prefrontal cortex plays a crucial role in utilizing these maps for planning and decision-making (Daw
      et al., 2005), the hippocampus is central to their formation, and regions like the parietal cortex
769
      contribute to spatial processing. This distributed neural system allows for deliberate, complex
770
      decision-making (Doll et al., 2012), enabling humans to adapt learned behaviors to novel, complex
771   environments.
772
773   One of the key characteristics of human cognition is extrapolation. There are multiple evidences
774
      across cognitive science domains proving that humans are able to solve problems in larger, more
      complex environments that were not encountered during the initial learning phase (Yousefzadeh &
775
      Mollick, 2021; Fellini & Morellini, 2011; Stojic et al., 2018). This capability allows humans to apply
776
      learned knowledge to novel situations, demonstrating a remarkable level of generalization.
777
778
779
      A.2   P LANNING IN LANGUAGE MODEL DOMAIN
780
781
782   Language models are primarily trained using next-token prediction, which facilitates model-free
783
      planning by allowing them to generate text based on learned patterns and associations from vast
      amounts of data. This training approach enables language models to perform zero-shot planning
784
      in various domains, such as general planning and code generation, without requiring task-specific
785
      training Chen et al. (2021). Language models excel at pattern matching and leverage their learned
786   world model to plan and generate coherent text in given tasks Brown et al. (2020).
787
788   Despite their successes, language models often struggle with planning tasks that require extensive
789
      foresight and detailed reasoning. Studies shows that language models perform poorly on tasks
      requiring robust planning and complex decision-making, highlighting the limitations of model-free
790
      approaches (Xie et al., 2024; Valmeekam et al., 2022).
791
792   One way to inject planning capability for language models is by imitating the “thought” of the
793   reasoning as a part of the generation. We call it as Chain of Thought (CoT)(Wei et al., 2023)
794   methodology, and it has demonstrated significant improvements in language models’ reasoning and
795
      planning abilities. They involve guiding the model through a series of chains generated by human or
      ground truth that simulates step-by-step reasoning, enhancing the model’s decision-making processes
796
      via few-shot demonstration or fine-tuning. They imitate ground truth reasoning demonstrations to
797
      improve the performance in tasks requiring complex reasoning (Nye et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022).
798
799   One limitation of conventional CoT is that there is lack of opportunity to explore a wide range of
800   world states. To address this, exploration-based methods have been designed to enable language
801
      models to explore more effectively. One such example is Tree of Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2023),
      which leverages language models’ knowledge to sample child nodes and simultaneously evaluate the
802
      value of the current state to structure and search the tree structure at the same time. Also one can
803
      enhance the searching by using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm (Kocsis & Szepesvári,
804   2006) to explore states and update their value (Hao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). These exploration-
805   based planning methods enhance the model’s ability to reach the goal, with a perfect probability
806   if we have infinite time and inference cost. Methods such as Searchformer (Lehnert et al., 2024)
807   experimentally shows that imitating A* algorithm rather than human demonstration is also helpful
808   when planning. The searching algorithm conducts tree search via heuristic cost function, yet they
809   show that the policy of the model can further imply optimal solutions. It implies that the language
      models can somewhat “extract” optimal plans from heuristic searching algorithms.

      A.3   E XTRAPOLATION INTO MORE COMPLEX ENVIRONMENTS
811
812   Extrapolation to complex environments remains a significant challenge for language models in
813   planning tasks. Despite their expressive power, conventional approaches struggle with generalization
814   to more intricate scenarios:
815
816
             • Imitation-based planning, even with advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024),
817            fails to generalize effectively to compositional tasks such as multiplication, Einstein’s puzzle
818            (Dziri et al., 2024), or Blocksworld puzzle (Valmeekam et al., 2022; Stechly et al., 2024)
819            when faced with extrapolated data.
820
821          • Chain of Thought (CoT) methods, while enhancing reasoning capabilities (Merrill & Sabhar-
822            wal, 2024; Feng et al., 2024), show limited effectiveness in extrapolation. Theoretical work
823
               suggests that extensive CoT demonstrations are needed for System 2 reasoning problems,
               yet this often proves insufficient for true extrapolation (Peng et al., 2024).
824
825          • Tree search methods in language models also enhances the planning ability of language
826            models (Yao et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023) but their extrapolation poten-
827            tial remains understudied. Existing research primarily focuses on general improvements
828            in planning or reasoning without specifically addressing extrapolation to more complex
829            environments (Daw et al., 2005).
830
831   These limitations contrast sharply with the adaptability observed in human cognitive planning,
832   highlighting a critical gap in current language model applications. The challenge lies in developing
833   approaches that can effectively bridge this gap, enabling more robust planning ability.
834
835
836
      A.4   E VALUATING C OGNITIVE M APS IN L ANGUAGE M ODELS
837
      Recent researches focus on evaluating the spatial understanding and cognitive mapping capabilities
838
      of LLMs. This section discusses key findings from studies that explore how well LLMs can represent
839
      and reason about spatial relationships, which are crucial for cognitive mapping.
840
841
842
             • Spatial Understanding: Recent study (Yamada et al., 2024) shows that LLMs can implicitly
               capture aspects of spatial structures despite being trained only on text. These models
843
               were tested on tasks involving navigation through various grid structures such as squares,
844
               hexagons, and trees. The results indicated variability in performance, suggesting that while
845            LLMs exhibit some spatial reasoning capabilities, there is significant room for improvement.
846
847          • Cognitive Mapping and Planning: Recent study (Momennejad et al., 2023) highlights the
848            challenges LLMs face in forming cognitive maps necessary for effective planning. Although
849            these models can handle basic spatial tasks, their ability to generalize and plan in more
850            complex environments remains limited. This limitation is evident when comparing their
851
               performance to human benchmarks, where non-expert humans often outperform LLMs in
               spatial reasoning tasks.
852
853          • Benchmarking and Error Analysis: Comprehensive benchmarking studies (Li et al., 2024;
854            Xu et al., 2024) systematically evaluate LLMs on spatial tasks. These studies use multi-task
855            evaluation datasets to assess the models’ performance across different spatial reasoning
856            challenges. Error analyses reveal that LLMs’ mistakes often stem from both spatial and
857            non-spatial factors, indicating areas where further refinement is needed.
858
859   These findings highlight the importance of developing more advanced training techniques and
860   benchmarks to improve the cognitive mapping abilities of LLMs, enabling them to better emulate
861   human-like planning and reasoning. Building on these studies, we evaluate the challenges language
862   models face in demonstrating spatial reasoning (Gridworld), particularly in extrapolated environments
863   that are unseen during training. However, our approach differs in that we propose designing cognitive
      maps specifically for path planning as a potential solution to address these limitations.

1028
       Planning task with environment feedback in language model can be formalized as a Markov decision
1029   process with instruction space U, state space S, action space A, observation space O, metric space
1030   C, transition function T : S × A → S, and metric function R : U × (S × A)n → C where n is
1031   the trajectory length. Note that for language model domain, U, A, and O are given as sequences of
1032   language tokens.
1033
       Given an instruction u ∈ U, the model θ first generates the action a1 ∼ πθ (·|u) ∈ A according to its
1034
       policy πθ . For each state st ∈ S and its observation ot ∈ O, the agent generates the corresponding
1035   action in the t + 1 step at+1 ∼ πθ (·|u, a1 , o1 , . . . , at , ot ) ∈ A, which concludes to a new state
1036   st+1 = T (st , at ) and its observation ot+1 . The interaction loop repeats until the task has terminated
1037   for some reason(succeeded, failed, number of steps exceeded maximum value, etc.), and the action
1038   trajectory is denoted as:
1039

1045   reward is computed based on the metric function R(u, k).
1046

1047   Namely, we have a thinking space M(of language subset) which generates m ∼ πθ (·|u) ∈ M, then
1048   generate a1 ∼ πθ (·|u, m) ∈ M upon the generated thought.

1051

1052
       to generate an optimal plan without further observations. The model generates the entire action
1053

1054   optimal path k ∗ , which is the shortest successful trajectory arg mink∈{k|r(u,k)=success} |k|. Since we
1055   ensure there is only one possible k ∗ , we only need to check whether the generated trajectory is k ∗ .
1056   Now we can define R(u, k) as follows:
1057
1058          • R(u, k) = SUCCESS if k = k ∗
1059          • R(u, k) = FAIL otherwise

1061   Reachable planning analysis: multi-turn setting Reachable planning investigates whether
1062   the model can produce a reachable (not necessarily optimal) plan in a multi-turn setting. The

1064   πθ (·|u, m, a1 , o1 , . . . , at , ot ) ∈ A. We define success as reaching the goal state, with failure cases
