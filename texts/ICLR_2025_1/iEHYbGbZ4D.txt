009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The recent integration of spiking neurons into graph neural networks has been
012            gaining much attraction due to its superior energy efficiency. Especially because
013            the sparse connection among graph nodes fits the nature of the spiking neural net-
014            works, spiking graph neural networks are considered strong alternatives to vanilla
015            graph neural networks. However, there is still a large performance gap for graph
016            tasks between the spiking neural networks and artificial neural networks. The gaps
017            are especially large when they are adapted to graph classification tasks, where
018            none of the nodes in the test set graphs are connected to the training set graphs.
019
               We diagnose the problem as the existence of neurons under starvation, caused by
               the sparse connections among the nodes and the neurons. To alleviate the prob-
020
               lem, we propose DAS-GNN. Based on a set of observations on spiking neurons on
021
               graph classification tasks, we devise several techniques to utilize more neurons to
022            deliver meaningful information to the connected neurons. Experiments on diverse
023            datasets show significant improvements compared to the baselines, demonstrating
024            the effectiveness of the DAS-GNN.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) are popular neural networks that learn representations from graphs,
030   which comprise multiple nodes and edges between them. Because of their flexibility to model any
031   kind of connection that exists in nature, they have various applications ranging from drug discov-
032   ery (Borgwardt et al., 2005; Wale et al., 2008; Debnath et al., 1991), social influence prediction (Qiu
033   et al., 2018; Arazzi et al., 2023), traffic forecasting (Bai et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2020), and recom-
034   mendation systems (Pal et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023). One known challenge
035   of GNNs is their sparse memory and computational pattern. Because many messages are passed
036   between randomly connected nodes, there is a significant inefficiency in processing them with con-
037   ventional systems (Yan et al., 2020; Yoo et al., 2022; 2023; Geng et al., 2020).
038   To address the inefficiency, spiking neural networks (SNNs) are considered strong alternatives. In-
039   spired by the biological behavior of brains, SNNs process information by communicating binary
040   spikes between the neurons. Because SNNs utilize intermittently occurring spikes, they have supe-
041   rior energy efficiency, especially in the domain of GNNs (Aimone et al., 2021).
042   Although the spiking graph neural network (SGNN) has been recently studied by many re-
043   searchers (Li et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2022; Wang & Jiang, 2022), we observe huge performance
044   drops when adapted to graph classification, compared to that of the conventional GNNs implemented
045   with artificial neural networks (ANNs). Upon closer analysis of the performance degradation, we
046   identify spike frequency deviation of the neurons within the model. In our investigation, many neu-
047   rons experience starvation, which does not emit any spike during the inference. This leads to severe
048   information loss, due to being unable to deliver signals to the subsequent neurons.
049   Such a problem was less exposed in previous studies on spiking GNNs. This is because the test set
050   nodes are available during the training time (transductive learning (Kipf & Welling, 2016)) or they
051   are part of the training graph (inductive learning (Hamilton et al., 2017)). In such settings, the model
052   could be trained on the nodes to mitigate the performance drop. However, in graph classification
053   tasks, the graphs are independent of each other, and the test set comprises multiple unseen graphs,
      aggravating the problem.


                                                         1
054
      Fortunately, our further analysis reveals that such phenomena are related to the degree of the vertices
055   in input graphs. We discover that a strong pattern exists among the neurons in the GNN, where 1)
056   neurons in a node have similar behaviors, 2) each feature dimension presents different behaviors,
057   and 3) neurons in high-degree nodes tend to emit more spikes.
058
      Motivated by the observations, we propose to group the neurons according to the degree of the node
059
      (degree-aware group-adaptive neurons). The neurons in each group adapt the threshold voltage
060
      together to steer the firing rate toward ideal rates. To further improve the neuron groups being
061   sensitive to the base threshold value at the first inference step, we further propose to learn the base
062   threshold voltage values (learnable inference base threshold).
063
064
      We evaluate DAS-GNN over multiple GNN models and datasets. Experiments reveal that DAS-
      GNN achieves superior performance over the SNN baselines, setting a new state-of-the-art method
065
      for SNN-based graph classification. Compared to the ANN counterparts, we demonstrate that DAS-
066
      GNN has advantages in energy efficiency, similar to what has been reported in many SNN-based
067   approaches. Notably, contrary to the usual studies where ANNs serve as performance upper bounds
068   for SNNs, we observe several cases where DAS-GNN significantly outperforms the ANN counter-
069   parts. This suggests that GNN-based graph classification might be a field where SNNs could excel.
070
      Our contributions are summarized as the following:
071
072          • We identify the starvation problem of spiking neurons in GNNs for graph classification
073            tasks that harms the performance. We further observe that the spike frequency patterns
074            have a strong correlation with the vertex degree.
075          • Based on the observations, we propose degree-aware group-adaptive neurons, which dy-
076            namically adjusts the threshold voltage together with the other neurons in the group to
077            address the spike frequency deviations.
078          • We propose techniques to reduce the sensitivity of the neuron groups to the inference base
079            threshold at the first step, by learning the base threshold for each group.
080
             • We evaluate DAS-GNN on several public graph classification datasets, and the experimen-
081            tal results show that DAS-GNN achieves superior performance over existing techniques.

      2.1   S PIKING N EURAL N ETWORKS AND S PIKE T RAINING
086
087   Spiking neural networks (SNNs) are third-generation neural network designs that mimic human bio-
088   logical neural systems (Maass, 1997). They use spike-based communication and adopt event-driven
089   characteristics that promote better energy efficiency than current ANNs. Similar to human neural
090   systems, SNNs consist of spiking neurons that can model spatio-temporal dynamics of the actual
091   biological neurons. The early forms of such neuron models are Hodgkin-Huxley neurons (Hodgkin
092   & Huxley, 1952), which accurately model the biophysical characteristics of the membrane through
093   differential equations. However, its mathematical complexity prohibits its practical use and scalabil-
094   ity. Instead, the Leaky Integrated-and-Fire (LIF) model finds a middle ground between mathematical
095
      simplicity and biological plausibility, and is popularly adopted as the baseline architecture (Hodgkin
      & Huxley, 1952). In the LIF neuron, the weighted sum of input spikes is accumulated over time
096
      within the neuron as membrane potential, and the output spike is generated only when the mem-
097
      brane potential exceeds a threshold value. This process is represented as a differential function:
098

111   will denote this process of forwarding through LIF neurons as SN (·) in this paper.

113   Direct SNN Training. Since SNNs were first implemented through ANN-SNN conversion (Cao
114   et al., 2015), various studies have aimed to address the accuracy degradation that occurs during the
115   conversion from ANNS to SNNs (Han et al., 2020; Rueckauer et al., 2017; Hunsberger & Eliasmith,
116   2015; Sengupta et al., 2019). However, due to the non-differentiable step function in Equation (4),
117   direct SNN training with backpropagation is prohibited.
118   To enable direct SNN training, many approaches have been proposed (Shrestha & Orchard, 2018;
119   Bohte et al., 2002; Esser et al., 2015; 2016; Che et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2018; Deng et al., 2022) to
120   bypass the step function. Recent research has demonstrated that directly training SNNs can yield
121   competitive results by addressing the challenges posed by non-differentiability. To this end, our
122   work focuses on directly training Graph SNNs. While exploring ANN-SNN conversion methods
123   would be an interesting scenario, they target a different dimension from ours.

125   2.2   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
126
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) take graph-represented data as input, which consist of nodes and
127
      their connected edges G = (V, E), with node features X ∈ R|V |×F and optionally edge features
128
      E ∈ R|E|×D . The common GNN architectures follow a message passing paradigm (Gilmer et al.,
129
      2017), which learns node or edge representations by aggregating information from its neighboring
130   nodes and updating the node features iteratively. Thus, a single forward of GNN layer consists of

132

133
      features in the message are aggregated, the combination phase uses ϕ(·) for feature updates. For
134
      graph convolutional network (Kipf & Welling, 2016), the overall process can be simplified as:

138   is updated through iterations of message passing (AX) and combination (XW ). After iterative up-
139   dates of X through the layers, the learned node or edge embeddings are passed through an additional
140   classification layer for node-level or edge-level predictions.
141
142   Graph Classification follows the same node-wise message-passing framework to obtain node
143   embeddings but appends a readout layer to turn them into a single graph embedding:
                                                          (L)
144                                            hG = R(hi |Vi ∈ G),                                          (6)
145   where R denotes the readout function. The readout function reduces the node features to a single
146   embedding regardless of the number of nodes. This is due to the inductive nature of graph clas-
147   sification tasks where the number of nodes is not known in advance. While GNN layers focus on
148   communicating features only from a local neighbor of a vertex, the readout layer considers the entire
149   graph to generate global features. The obtained graph embedding is passed through a classification
150   layer for predictions. Graph classification tasks usually hold more difficulty than node-level classi-
151   fication due to its inductive nature, where inference is done on unseen graphs and thus cannot utilize
152
      any graph-specific statistics from the train set. In this paper, we mainly focus on graph classification.

      3    A NALYSIS ON S PIKE F REQUENCY VARIATION OF GNN S
190
191
      To analyze the cause of the accuracy drop, we plot the behavior of the neurons during inference in
      Figure 1a, on an IMDB-BINARY dataset over five timesteps (T = 5). We create a histogram of
192
      spike counts created from each node, which is associated with 128 neurons. As depicted in the plot,
193
      it is clear that most of the neurons are under starvation. This is caused by the inputs of those neurons
194   being insufficient to reach the threshold, and this leads to severe information loss between the layers.
195   While unveiling the exact dynamics would require more research, we hypothesize that this is caused
196   by the varying degrees of vertices in real-world graphs.
197
      To validate the hypothesis and further investigate the phenomena, we display the spike frequency
198
      heatmap of the neurons sorted by the degree of the nodes in Figure 1b. From the heatmap, we make
199
      three observations:
200
201   1 (Brighter on the top and darker at the bottom) High-degree nodes tend to exhibit higher spike
202     frequencies.
203   2 (The horizontal strips) The spike frequencies are associated with the corresponding nodes.
204
      3 (The vertical strips) The feature neurons within a node behave differently according to their
205     positions.
206
207   We believe such patterns come from the connectivity of the nodes and the distinct role of the neurons
208   assigned to each node. The connectivity affects the number of receiving spikes of neurons associated
209   with each node. It is known that most of the real-world graphs exhibit an extremely skewed distri-
210   bution of degrees (i.e., power-law distribution (Leskovec et al., 2007)). Due to such a characteristic,
211   there are a few nodes with a very high degree, while a majority of nodes have a low degree. Because
212
      a GNN layer communicates signals between the neighbors through messages, a high-degree node
      will likely receive a lot of spikes, while a low-degree node will receive only a few.
213
214   These three observations shed light on how to close the performance gap between spiking GNNs and
215   ANN-based GNNs. In the next section, we describe how the observations are used to build better
      spiking GNNs for graph classification.

      4.1   OVERALL G RAPH C LASSIFICATION A RCHITECTURE
236
237
      Many recent studies have tried to adapt SNN architectures into GNN tasks, however, they simply try
238   to contact with only node classification tasks (Zhu et al., 2022; Wang & Jiang, 2022). In this work,
239   we propose a spiking neural network specifically designed for graph classification tasks and show
240   that it can be trained using spikes. We demonstrate the overall architecture of DAS-GNN in Figure 2.
241   For each timestep, the input graphs are first translated into spike representations through the Poisson
242   encoder, then the message passing is done in spike format. After the combination operation in the
243   SNN layer, the node features are transformed into a spike format by passing the neurons. In the
244   classification layer, we perform an extra message passing on the spike features before passing the
245   readout layer. The readout operation reduces the multiple rows of node embedding by computing
246
      feature-wise averages, thus representing them into a single row to represent the entire graph. A batch
      of graph embeddings is passed through a classification head that outputs logits for each timestep.
247
      To make the final prediction, we simply take the sum of logits from all inference timesteps and use
248
      softmax to obtain the class probabilities.
249

251   4.2   D EGREE -AWARE G ROUP -A DAPTIVE N EURONS
252
253   As discussed in Section 3, GNNs suffer from a huge gap in spike frequencies between neurons.
254   As observed, there exists some patterns (Figure 1) that we can utilize to address the issue. One
255   naive way of addressing the issue is to use learnable (Wang et al., 2022), or adaptive (Bellec et al.,
256   2018) threshold for each neuron. By adjusting the threshold, one can expect the neurons to naturally
257   change, such that neurons under starvation will have lower thresholds to fire more often, and a few
258   neurons with high firing rates will have higher thresholds to shift toward an ideal distribution.
259   Unfortunately, such an idea cannot be directly applied unless all the testset nodes are available at
260   training time (i.e., transductive task). However, such a setting would be considered a data leak
261   for graph classification and would also lose the advantage SNNs have on lightweight inference.
262   Moreover, the number of nodes in a real-world dataset often ranges from at least thousands to several
263   billions. Considering that GNNs often involve only a sub-million number of learnable parameters,
264   storing such a large number of thresholds is considered too much overhead.
265   To address the aforementioned issues, we propose degree-aware group adaptive neurons (DAG),
266   which groups the neurons by their degrees and lets each group adapt its threshold. The neurons are
267   split according to the degree of the associated vertex and further split along the feature dimensions.
268   In Figure 1, we illustrate each group as vertical bars as depicted by the green boxes. Thus, we
269   reformulate Equation (4) as below. In the equations, we focus on a single feature position, and use

290   with similar membrane potential variance, which aligns with the observations from Section 3.
291   The major advantage of this scheme is that it is straightforward to put an unseen node or an unseen
292   graph into a group at inference because only the degree information is required to assign a vertex into
293   a group. To further consider intra-node deviation, we split the group into F (number of features)
294   neurons, which is a fixed parameter determined by the model architecture. For any unseen node,
295   finding out its degree is trivial because visiting its neighbors is one of the fundamental requirements
296   of graph data structures (Khorasani et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2017).

458
      To determine whether DAS-GNN attains similar energy consumption benefits that are typically seen
459   in SNN, we calculated the theoretical energy consumption as shown in Table 5. These calculations
460   are based on Horowitz (2014); Yao et al. (2023), which are widely used for SNN energy consumption
461   analysis. We calculated energy consumption based on each layer’s spike sparsity µ and FLOPs
462   (floating point operations). Assuming MAC and AC operations are implemented on 45nm hardware,
463   we used EM AC = 4.6pJ and EAC = 0.9pJ. The theoretical energy consumption for the SNN was
464   calculated with EAC × µ × F LOP s.
465   In the table, the ‘Additional Cost’ row represents the overhead caused by updating the per-group
466   threshold as in Equation (11). The additional cost calculated by 2 ×EAC × (number of unique
467   degrees) × (hidden dimension) based on Equation (10). To share the per-group threshold among
468   neurons without communication, we assume that neurons within a group are mapped to the same
469   processing element. This way, the shared threshold value is stored in the local memory of the
470   processing element of neuromorphic hardwares (Merolla et al., 2014; Davies et al., 2018; Akopyan
471   et al., 2015), and the firing rate can be updated simply by counting.
472   As shown in Table 5, DAS-GNN achieves superior energy efficiency compared to the ANN coun-
473   terparts of around 80% improvements in all datasets. In addition, DAS-GNN maintains energy
474   consumption comparable to other baseline SNN models despite diversifying the spike frequency
475   distributions. It is worth noting that the additional cost for adaptively updating the thresholds is
476   almost negligible. This is because the update operations happen only once per group, whereas the
477   base computation requires operations per every connection for individual neurons. Because a group
478   typically has a few thousands of neurons and each neuron has multiple connections, the overhead
479
      becomes an order of magnitude smaller compared to that of the main computation. For more energy
      consumption results, please refer to Table 14 in the Appendix J.

513   Spiking Neural Networks SNNs are a type of neural network where information is transmitted
514   via spikes, similar to how biological neurons work. They use different neuron models for capturing
515   spike signals effectively (Hodgkin & Huxley, 1952; Hunsberger & Eliasmith, 2015) or adjusting
516   parameters dynamically to complement the accuracy (Fang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Bellec
517   et al., 2018; Lian et al., 2024). One major research area is converting traditional ANNs into SNNs
518   by mapping ANN activation functions into spike signals (Han et al., 2020; Rueckauer et al., 2017;
519   Hunsberger & Eliasmith, 2015; Sengupta et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2023). Another focus is directly
520
      training SNNs using backpropagation, which involves using various techniques such as surrogate
      functions for backpropagation (Shrestha & Orchard, 2018; Che et al., 2022) and adapting normal-
521
      ization techniques (Sengupta et al., 2019; Duan et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024).
522
523
      SNN for Graphs Previous attempts to apply SNNs to graph datasets have primarily focused on
524
      node-level classification tasks (Zhang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2022; Xu et al.,
525   2021) and have not yet been extended to graph-level tasks. While Wang & Jiang (2022) explored
526   the application of spike training to Graph Attention Networks (GAT), it implemented the message
527   passing phase after the spiking phase, which deviates from previous structures. Additionally, recent
528   efforts have begun to integrate SNNs with other techniques for contrastive learning (Li et al., 2024),
529   particularly in dynamic graphs (Yin et al., 2024), to adopt collaboration between GNNs and SNNs.

      7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we explore the application of SNNs to graph neural networks for graph classification
534   for the first time. After thoroughly analyzing the graph’s uneven spike distribution, we identify that
535   the degree of each node correlates to this phenomenon. To better accommodate such characteristics
536   of graphs, we propose degree-aware group-adaptive neurons, where we place neurons from vertices
537   sharing the same degree into groups. In addition, we propose to learn the inference base threshold
538   and adaptively adjust the threshold simultaneously to reduce its sensitivity and facilitate training
539   using spikes. Combined with the modified architecture for graph classification, the proposed DAS-
      GNN outperforms existing works by a noticeable margin.

850   The baseline settings, SpikeNet, PGNN, GGNN are the neuron-variant models that replace each
851   SNN layer with different types of neurons. SpikeNet successfully adopted SNN into temporal GNN
852   domains by proposing adaptive LIF neuron models. We adopted adaptive LIF into the static graph
853
      datasets since it shows state-of-arts performance in the SGNN area. Another effective neuron model
      is the parametric leaky integrate-and-fire (PLIF), widely adopted to other SNN tasks such as com-
854
      puter vision and etc. They make the neuron’s membrane time constant into a learnable parameter
855
      which makes it robust on the initial values. GLIF is another effective neuron model that enhances the
856   idea of PLIF neuron models by adopting linear leakage, introduced to fully parameterized neuron.
857   These three neuron models were selected to compare with our DAS-GNN which showed compet-
858   itive performance for the neuron-variant models. For all experiments including both baselines and
859   ours, we kept the GNN architecture unchanged (GCN, GAT, GIN), and only replaced the neurons

1124        T ECHNIQUES
1125
1126   To show the effectiveness of DAS-GNN, we conducted experiments using three techniques from (Li
1127   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2021) (SpikeGCL, GC-SNN, GA-SNN), with and without our proposed
1128   method in Table 15. The results show that DAS-GNN is orthogonal to these techniques and can be
1129   additionally applied to further enhance the performance in all the tested cases.
1130   Another dimension of technique we can explore is the temporal encoding of spikes. Although our
1131   work focuses on the spike rate, we believe it could be adapted to temporal encoding schemes such
1132   as Xiao et al. (2024), by integrating degrees to synaptic delays or encoding community information
1133   as a temporal property. These opportunities are orthogonal to this work, and we leave them as our
       future work.
