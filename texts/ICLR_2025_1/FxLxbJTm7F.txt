011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013                Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable in-context learning
014                (ICL) capabilities. In this study, we explore a surprising phenomenon related to ICL:
015                LLMs can perform multiple, computationally distinct ICL tasks simultaneously,
016
                   during a single inference call, a capability we term “task superposition”. We provide
                   empirical evidence of this phenomenon across various LLM families and scales
017
                   and show that this phenomenon emerges even if we train the model to in-context
018
                   learn one task at a time. We offer theoretical explanations that this capability is
019                well within the expressive power of transformers. We also explore how LLMs
020                internally compose task vectors during superposition. Furthermore, we show that
021                larger models can solve more ICL tasks in parallel, and better calibrate their output
022                distribution. Our findings offer insights into the latent capabilities of LLMs, further
023                substantiate the perspective of “LLMs as superposition of simulators”, and raise
024                questions about the mechanisms enabling simultaneous task execution.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various domains,
030   with one of the most intriguing being in-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022).
031   ICL enables LLMs to perform tasks during inference without the need to fine-tune for that particular
032   task, simply by providing a few examples within the input prompt. This ability has sparked significant
033
      interest in the research community, as it suggests that LLMs can adapt to novel tasks on-the-fly, using
      the capabilities that they acquired during pretraining, and the context provided.
034
035   While ICL has been extensively studied from both theoretical and empirical perspectives (Xie et al.,
036   2022; Agarwal et al., 2024), many aspects of its underlying mechanisms remain elusive. In this work,
037   we study a surprising phenomenon related to ICL that, to the best of our knowledge, has not been
038   thoroughly studied before: LLMs can perform multiple distinct ICL tasks simultaneously, in a single
039
      inference call, a capability we refer to as “task superposition”.
040   Our study suggests that pretrained autoregressive LLMs such as Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) or
041   GPT-3.51 (Brown et al., 2020) display superposition of tasks purely in-context2 . When presented
042   with multiple in-context examples from different tasks, in the same prompt, the models can generate
043   outputs that correspond to solutions for all these individual tasks. For instance, given examples of
044   addition and translation, the model can concurrently produce correct answers for both tasks, as well
045
      as the composition of these tasks (e.g., the result of addition translated into another language).
046   Figure 1 illustrates this phenomenon. In Figure 1a (left), given in-context examples of addition in
047   different languages and the query “91 + 83 →”, the model generates probabilities for the correct sum
048   in various languages, demonstrating its ability to perform addition and translation concurrently.
049   This discovery aligns and lends further support to the view of LLMs as superposition of simulators
050   (Janus, 2022; Shanahan et al., 2023; Nardo, 2023) and the Bayesian perspective of ICL proposed by
051   Xie et al. (2022). While not a mathematically rigorous formulation, we can conceptualize the output

123
124   Theory and practice of in-context learning. There is rich literature which formalizes in-context
125   learning under diverse definitions. For example, prior works study in-context learning through a
126   Bayesian framework for task retrieval (Xie et al., 2022; Panwar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023),
127   martingales (Falck et al., 2024), optimizers (Akyürek et al., 2023; Oswald et al., 2023; Dai et al.,
128
      2022) and more (Reddy, 2024; Olsson et al., 2022). Other works confirm the theoretical framing of
      in-context learning by using it to implement a variety of algorithms and methods (Zhou et al., 2023;
129
      Ahn et al., 2023; Giannou et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Laskin et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022), or to
130
      approximate general-purpose computing machines (Giannou et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022).
131
132   To bridge the gap between theory and practice, many works have used these theoretical insights to
133   study in-context learning behaviors, such as in many-shot in-context learning, (Agarwal et al., 2024),
134
      long-context (Li et al., 2024), or eliciting personas (Choi & Li, 2024). Other works study the factors
      that influence how well models can learn through context, such as task diversity (Raventos et al.,
135
      2023; Chan et al., 2022), the balance between pre-training priors and in-context (Wei et al., 2023;
136
      Lin & Lee, 2024), in-context labels (Min et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2022), and the in-context format
137   (Lampinen et al., 2022). In-context learning has also been proposed as a means of fine-tuning to
138   improve non-language tasks (Dinh et al., 2022).
139
140
      The development of new architectures such as state space models (Gu & Dao, 2023) has further
      motivated studying whether in-context learning is prevalent in alternative architectures such as Mamba
141
      (Park et al., 2024; Grazzi et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024) or in looped transformers (Yang et al., 2023).
142
143   Steering models through in-context learning has been a growing area of interest. Recent work has
144   hypothesized that in-context learning can be encapsulated by a high-dimensional description of a
145   task, which can be used to replace, (Hendel et al., 2023) compose (Todd et al., 2024) or augment (Liu
146
      et al., 2024) the latent states of a model, in order to alter its default behavior. Task vectors can be
      combined via arithmetic operations to solve a variety of tasks (Ilharco et al., 2023). Prior work has
147
      also been investigating the power of tokens in defining a task (Bai et al., 2024).
148
149   Other definitions of superposition. Our findings on superposition are inspired by notions of language
150   models as multiverse generators (Reynolds & McDonell, 2021; moire, 2021). One consequence of
151   LLMs as a superposition of tasks is that the outputs may collapse to unintended simulacra, a behavior
152
      known as the “Waluigi effect” (Nardo, 2023).
153   Superposition has been defined in various related contexts of learning models. Feature superposition
154   (Elhage et al., 2022) refers to a neural network’s ability to represent multiple learned concept in
155   a single neuron. Though our discovery of task superposition describes the same abstract idea, we
156   stress that it is distinct from feature superposition because task superposition is most apparent in
157   the final output of a model. Feature superposition is a microscopic-level observation whereas task
158
      superposition is a macroscopic-level observation.
159   Superposition is also described as a way to store multiple models in a single set of parameters
160   (Cheung et al., 2019), processing multiple inputs simultaneously (Shen et al., 2024a; Murahari et al.,
161   2022). In our work, we demonstrate task superposition directly as a result of language pre-training,
      without the necessity of additional adapters or decoding strategies.

319
      tasks in superposition with a single inference call. To this end, we provide a theoretical construction

      depending on the examples given in-context.

323   per attention layer can perform K tasks on vectors of dimension d in superposition, with weighting
      based on m different in-context examples each of length n .

      Averaging and task superposition. As a last step, we average all the task identifiers and place the
357   result in the last column, in which the next prediction will happen. We then use the averaged task
358   identifier to weight the prediction of each task based on it, as in task superposition. If the task has
359   been identified multiple times in the context, it would be assigned a higher weight/probability.

      8    L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
518
      One limitation of our work is the current gap between the demonstrated capability of LLMs to
519
      perform task superposition and its practical application in real-world scenarios. While we have shown
520   that LLMs possess the capacity to execute multiple tasks simultaneously, conventional decoding
521   algorithms are not equipped to fully leverage this capability. This limitation stems from what we
522   term ”generation collapse,” a phenomenon where, after the first token is generated, the model tends
523   to converge on predicting tokens for a single task, effectively negating its ability for multi-task
524   execution.
525
      This collapse presents a substantial challenge in harnessing the full power of task superposition. It
526   highlights a critical area for future research: developing decoding strategies that can maintain the
527   model’s multi-task state throughout the generation process. Recent work by Shen et al. (2024b) offers
528   some hope that this direction may be fruitful, by proposing a “superposed decoding” algorithm. Their
529   method efficiently generates multiple streams of tokens from a single inference pass by utilizing
530   superposed token embeddings. While this approach represents a significant step forward, it also
531   highlights the potential for further innovation in this area.
532
533   9    C ONCLUSION
534
535   We report on the discovery of task superposition, which is the ability of LLMs to simultaneously
536   solve distinct tasks from in-context examples. Task superposition is present in a variety of pretrained
537   models, and becomes more accurate at predicting the distribution of tasks as the model size increases.
538   We also find evidence that while displaying task superposition, models internally mix the task vectors
539   of each individual task. We hope that our findings will contribute to understanding in-context learning
      mechanisms and enhance our knowledge of LLMs overall.

      In this section we provide details on how we calculate probabilities of different outputs given a
813   prompt in our setting.
814
815   Notations. Let V be the token vocabulary, LM be an LLM, T be the tokenizer. We use “...” to
816   represent a string, <...> to represent a single token where the content within the angle brackets
817   is an integer representing token’s index in vocabulary. For example, token <266> corresponds to
818   “at”. We use [<...>, ..., <...>] to represent a sequence of tokens. Given a tokenizer, we
819   use two functions tok(·) and detok(·) to tokenize strings and detokenize tokens. For example
820   tok(“superposition”) = [<9712>,<3571>] and detok([<16>,<10>,<16>,<28>,<17>]) =
821   “1+1=2”.
822   In our in-context learning setting, an input string consists of in-context examples (separated by the
823   delimiter “\n”) and a query. For example, an example prompt can be “1+1=2\n2+2=4\n3+3=”.
824
      We view an LLM as a next-token predictor that outputs a probability distribution over the token
825
      space given input and there is a corresponding P(·|·) such that given a sequence of tokens [v1 , ..., vM ]
826
      where vj ∈ V, P(u | [v1 , ..., vM ]) measures the probability of the next token being u where u ∈ V.

870           examples of a particular task and a query x(m+1) . Each task example (x(j) , y (j) ) follows
871           the form “{op1}@{op2}={op1}”, where x(j) has the form “{op1}@{op2}=” and y (j)
872           is performing task copy(op1) on x(j) , namely op1.

874

876           layer ℓ.

879           simulating the effect of a complete context. We repeat this process 100 times for different
880           query x and get an accuracy accℓ of performing task copy(op1) with vector v(ℓ).
881
           4. The task vector layer ℓ∗ is selected by

      D     C ONSTRUCTION DISPLAYING SUPERPOSITION
919
920
      In this section we construct a Transformer that is performing superposition of multiple tasks at
921   inference. For this purpose, we first construct a Transformer that copies from n-tuple in-context
922   examples the i-th one, as well as any function using the ReLU layers. We then create indicator
923   vectors, for each task, which show whether a specific task is present in-context or not. As a last step,
924   we combine these indicator vectors to create the superposition of different tasks. Notice that using
925   the parallel heads of the transformer architecture we can process each task independently until the
926   last step in which the predictions are combined.

1409   the desired properties can execute k tasks in parallel. Lemma 1 identifies positions within the context

1412   them into a single task identifier. Lastly, Lemma 4 uses this task identifier to create a weighted sum
