009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a new type of indirect, cross-modal injection attacks against lan-
012            guage models that operate on images: hidden “meta-instructions” that influence
013            how the model interprets the image and steer its outputs to express an adversary-
014            chosen style, sentiment, or point of view. We create meta-instructions by generat-
015            ing images that act as soft prompts. In contrast to jailbreaking attacks and adver-
016            sarial examples, outputs produced in response to these images are plausible and
017            based on the visual content of the image, yet also satisfy the adversary’s (meta-
018
               )objective. We evaluate the efficacy of meta-instructions for multiple models and
               adversarial meta-objectives, and demonstrate how they “unlock” capabilities of
019
               the underlying language models that are unavailable via explicit text instructions.
020
               We describe how meta-instruction attacks could cause harm by enabling creation
021            of self-interpreting content that carries spam, misinformation, and spin.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large language models (LLMs) operating on third-party content—webpages, wikis, forums, so-
027   cial media, emails and messages, and user-generated content in general—are vulnerable to indirect
028   prompt injection (Greshake et al., 2023). By hiding prompts in content under their control, adver-
029   saries can try to influence outputs and actions generated by LLMs when processing this content.
030   Many modern LLMs accept inputs in multiple modalities. We refer to LLMs that operate on images
031   as Visual Language Models (VLMs). Multi-modal LLMs are known to be vulnerable to adversarial
032   examples (Dong et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), but prior research on injection
033   attacks in non-text modalities has mainly focused on jailbreaking and extracting sensitive informa-
034   tion. In these scenarios, the user of the VLM is the attacker, aiming to evade the VLM’s defenses
035   against generating unsafe outputs.
036   In this work, we focus on scenarios where VLM users are victims of adversarial content produced
037   by other users, i.e., indirect prompt injection. We demonstrate how adversaries can create images
038   such that VLMs’ outputs (1) correctly respond to users’ queries about these images, yet (2) simulta-
039   neously satisfy an adversary-chosen predicate. This distinguishes our approach from prior methods,
040   which often output strings from predefined distributions (e.g., toxic text) that satisfy the adversarial
041   predicate, but fail to preserve the model’s ability to generate meaningful and contextually appropri-
042   ate responses to user queries about the images.
043   We introduce and evaluate a new class of indirect, cross-modal attacks against visual language mod-
044   els: adversarial meta-instructions that enable creation of malicious, self-interpreting content. We
045   define a meta-instruction to be a stealthy image perturbation that steers outputs produced by a VLM
046   to satisfy some adversarial meta-objective, e.g., express a style, sentiment, or point of view chosen
047   by the adversary. For example, in Figure 1 meta-instructions hidden in image perturbations change
048   how the VLM answers the question about a stock performance chart. In all cases, the answer is based
049   on the image, but, depending on the meta-instruction, the interpretation changes to positive or nega-
050
      tive, or includes adversary-chosen spam or specific URLs. Figure 2 is another example—motivated
      by our prior experience with LLM-generated conference reviews—where we steer the model’s in-
051
      terpretation of an image depicting our methodology (appendix A.2) to positive or negative.
052

088   Differences from jailbreaking attacks and adversarial examples. Jailbreaking attacks (see Sec-
089
      tion 2) use text or image perturbations to cause models to generate toxic or unsafe outputs. The user
      is the attacker who submits adversarial inputs into the model. In meta-instruction attacks, users are
090
      victims of adversarial third-party content that they ask the model to process (see Section 3).
091
092   By design, jailbreaking and adversarial examples produce contextually incoherent outputs that do
093   not actually answer users’ questions about images. While Dong et al. (2023) demonstrate adver-
094   sarial perturbations that produce contextually coherent outputs, they force the model to generate
095
      text strings from a specific distribution, regardless of the user’s prompts. These approaches limit the
      model’s ability to provide meaningful, query-specific responses, and thus cannot be used for indirect
096
      attacks because users would notice that the VLM’s outputs are wrong given the conversation context
097
      and inputs (See Figure 2). By contrast, meta-instructions produce outputs that are plausible given
098   the user’s prompt and the visual content of the image—yet also satisfy the adversary’s objective.
099
100   Our contributions. We design, implement, and evaluate a method for creating a new type of image
101   perturbations that act as cross-modal soft prompts for a language model while preserving the visual
102   semantics of the image. Soft prompts (Lester et al., 2021) are vectors that are concatenated to input
103   embeddings to steer a language model’s response to its inputs. While highly effective, soft prompts
104
      cannot be used for prompt injection because they are embeddings (i.e., input encodings), not actual
      inputs, and the adversary cannot input embeddings into the model directly or indirectly.
105

107   as a soft prompt. Our method optimizes for two objectives: outputs of the VLM should correctly

183   Jailbreaking and adversarial examples. There are multiple examples2 of adversarial images that
184   cause VLMs to generate outputs violating their safety guardrails, e.g., toxic text. Shayegani et al.
185   (2024) generate adversarial images that look like noise. Qi et al. (2024) and Schwinn et al. (2024)
186   generate jailbreak images by maximizing similarity between the VLM’s outputs and fixed harmful
187   text sequences. Training soft prompts on a dataset of fixed sequences induces VLM responses
188   that may satisfy a particular meta-objective (such as toxicity) but do not match the context of the
189   conversation and do not correctly answer the user’s prompts about the image. Such responses are
190
      implausible, not stealthy, and cannot be used for indirect attacks in our threat model (see Section 3).
191   VLMs (Dong et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023) and multi-modal embeddings (Zhang et al., 2024) are
192   vulnerable to adversarial examples. By definition, adversarial examples cause VLMs to produce
193   answers that are incorrect and not based on how images are perceived by humans.
194
      Prompt injection. Indirect prompt injection attacks were introduced in Greshake et al. (2023).
195
      There are examples of hiding prompts in images3 by adding pixels that spell out the prompt in an
196   imperceptible shade or color. In our experiments, this technique did not work against MiniGPT-4,
197   LLaVa, nor InstructBLIP because they fail to recognize even non-stealthy words in images (e.g.,
198   black text on a white background). By contrast, the soft-prompt method introduced in this paper
199   works regardless of the target model’s OCR capabilities.
200
      Bagdasaryan et al. (2023) give several examples, without systematic evaluation, of adversarial im-
201
      ages that cause multi-modal LLMs to generate arbitrary fixed strings chosen by the attacker. If and
202   only if the string output by the LLM is consumed by the same LLM as part of its context for subse-
203   quent autoregressive generation, the LLM follows the instruction contained in the string. This attack
204   is not stealthy because the adversary’s instruction is always visible in the target model’s first text
205   output. By contrast, our method does not rely on forcing the VLM to output a fixed text string, nor
206   assume that the VLM adds its own outputs to the generation context.
207
      Bailey et al. (2023) describe two methods for prompt injection via images. Behavior matching
208   outputs predefined, query-independent text strings (suitable for jailbreaking, not suitable for stealthy
209   indirect attacks). Prompt matching generates images to match the logits computed by the victim
210   model in response to the adversary’s text prompts. This enables some forms of misinformation
211   attacks, e.g., outputting a factually incorrect statement about the content of the image.
212

253   3      T HREAT M ODEL
254
255   The main proposed application of visual language models is to answer questions about images (Li
256   et al., 2023). They can also summarize content from websites, social media, and messaging apps
257
      originating from anywhere, including adversaries pursuing an agenda (Recorded Future, 2024) or,
      as we call it, “meta-objective”. While it is possible to create an image perturbation that forces a
258
      pre-defined text output (Bagdasaryan et al., 2023; Bailey et al., 2023), in general the adversary does
259
      not know the context in which the VLM will be queried about the image, nor the specific prompts
260   that will be used. The fixed output is likely to be incorrect, implausible, or incoherent.
261
262   To steer VLMs into generate contextually coherent outputs that satisfy their meta-objective, an ad-
263
      versary can exploit the following observation. Whereas in classification tasks each input has a single
      correct output, there is a large range of “correct” or at least plausible answers that a generative model
264
      can produce in response to a given prompt. The model can thus be steered to generate a response
265
      that is contextually coherent (i.e., plausible and based on the visual content of the image) but also
266   has some property or “spin” chosen by the adversary (Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2022). Examples
267   include positive or negative sentiment and political bias (Figure 4 shows an example of the latter).
268
269   Meta-instructions. We say that t∗ is a meta-instruction if it causes the model to generate text y z ∈
      Y that satisfies a meta-objective z ∈ Z (we use “meta-objective” and “spin” (Esser et al., 2001)

271   sentiment. This instruction tells the model to produce outputs that (a) respond to the user’s prompts
272   about the image and (b) are positive. It is important that output y z preserve input semantics, i.e.,
273   correctly responds to the user’s question about the image, otherwise the victim will notice the attack.

515   6     D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE R ESEARCH
516
517   We introduced a new type of attack that enables adversaries to add stealthy “meta-instructions” to
518   images that influence how visual language models respond to queries about these images. Meta-
519   instructions keep responses contextually coherent and relevant to the visual content of the image
520   while steering them to satisfy some adversary-chosen meta-objective or “spin” (e.g., positive or
521   negative sentiment or political bias or spam). In instruction-tuned visual language models such as
522   LLaVA, meta-instructions can be more powerful than explicit instructions and unlock capabilities of
523   the base LLM that are not available via explicit prompts in the VLM.
524   We designed, implemented, and evaluated a novel method for creating images with meta-
525   instructions. This method generates adversarial perturbations that act as “soft prompts” for the
526   target model. We demonstrated that image soft prompts generated with our method transfer across
527   VLMs, including models using different architectures. This demonstrates that meta-instructions can
528   be a viable method to create self-interpreting adversarial content even if the creator does not know
529   the specific VLM that will be used to process their content.
530   Smaller, stealthier perturbations reduce the efficacy of meta-instructions. Furthermore, the current
531   version of the attack can be defeated by simple countermeasures (see Appendix C). An interest-
532   ing direction for future research is to investigate local soft-prompt perturbations, akin to adversarial
533   patches (Brown et al., 2017), that can be applied to any image. Another question for future research
534   is measuring, with various prompts about the original and perturbed images, how much semantic
535   information about the image is lost due to applying soft-prompt perturbations. Future user-oriented
536
      research can study whether humans find VLMs responses to meta-instructions plausible and persua-
      sive for various adversarial meta-objectives.
537
538   On the defense side, developers of multi-modal language models should understand how their mod-
539   els can be used as conduits for attacks, and how untrusted content can expose model users to risks
      such as phishing and misinformation.

      C     D EFENSES
865
866
      There is a large body of research on training adversarially robust models (Madry et al., 2018; Shafahi
867   et al., 2019). For better or for worse, little of this research has found its way to real-world LLMs,
868   whether production models or available research prototypes. Implementors of LLMs have not been
869   interested in adversarial robustness, with a few exceptions, such as protecting models from jail-
870   breaking (Robey et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) and prompt injection (Wallace
871   et al., 2024). One of the reasons could be the negative impact of adversarial robustness on model
872   performance, which is especially pronounced for multi-modal models. For example, adversarially
873   robust contrastive learning significantly reduces accuracy even on basic tasks such as CIFAR (Yu
874   et al., 2022).
875   Inference-time defenses aim to filter adversarial inputs and/or outputs. Llama Guard (Inan et al.,
876   2023) is an LLM-based model that detects unsafe content in LLM inputs and outputs. Lakera AI
877   (2024) provides an API service to detect malicious inputs to LLMs. These defenses are independent
878   of the model and don’t affect LLM performance. The types of adversarial inputs and outputs tackled
879   by these defenses are different from those considered in this paper.
880   We focus on practical inference-time defenses that can be implemented as wrappers around existing
881   models, primarily via input pre-processing.
882
883   C.1   F EATURE D ISTILLATION
884
885   Defenses of this type apply transformations that preserve visual features of the image while destroy-
886   ing adversarial features (Liu et al., 2019b). JPEG compression is an example of such a transfor-
887   mation. In our case, adding a JPEG compression layer before encoding input images significantly
888   reduces the efficacy of meta-instructions hidden in image perturbations.
889   Table 7 shows that when JPEG compression is applied to the perturbed images, success of the attack,
890   i.e., percentage of outputs that satisfy the adversary’s meta-objective (sentiment, in this case) drops
891   significantly. This indicates that JPEG compression disrupts adversarial features while maintaining
892   the visual content of the image. Note that attack success rates are non-zero even on clean images
893   because responses to clean images occasionally satisfy the meta-objective without any instructions
894   from the adversary.

909   Defenses of this type can often be evaded by an adaptive adversary who incorporates the defense
910   mechanism into the perturbation generation process. We evaluated the moving patch technique (Bai-
911   ley et al., 2023), which slightly improved the success rate of our attack for “Positive” and “Nega-
912   tive” meta-objectives to 0.55 and 0.2 (see Table 7). We leave evasion of feature distillation and other
913   countermeasures to future work.
