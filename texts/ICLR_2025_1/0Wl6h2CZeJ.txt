032                                               A BSTRACT
033
034            Most state-of-the-art point trackers are trained on synthetic data due to the diffi-
035            culty of annotating real videos for this task. However, this can result in suboptimal
036            performance due to the statistical gap between synthetic and real videos. In or-
037            der to understand these issues better, we introduce RealTracker, comprising a new
038            tracking model and a new semi-supervised training recipe. This allows real videos
039
               without annotations to be used during training by generating pseudo-labels using
               off-the-shelf teachers. The new model eliminates or simplifies components from
040
               previous trackers, resulting in a simpler and often smaller architecture. This train-
041
               ing scheme is much simpler than prior work and achieves better results using 1,000
042            times less data. We further study the scaling behaviour to understand the impact
043            of using more real unsupervised data in point tracking. The model is available in
044            online and offline variants and reliably tracks visible and occluded points.

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   Tracking points is a key step in the analysis of videos, particularly for tasks like 3D recon-
050   struction and video editing that require precise recovery of correspondences. Point trackers have
051   evolved significantly in recent years, with designs based on transformer neural networks inspired by
052   PIPs (Harley et al., 2022). Notable examples include TAP-Vid (Doersch et al., 2022), which intro-
053   duced a new benchmark for point tracking, and TAPIR (Doersch et al., 2023), which introduced an
      improved tracker that extends PIPs’ design with a global matching stage. CoTracker (Karaev et al.,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2024b) proposed a transformer architecture that tracks multiple points jointly, with further gains in
055   tracking quality, particularly for points partially occluded in the video.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce a new point tracking model, RealTracker, that builds on the ideas of
058
      recent trackers but is significantly simpler, more data efficient, and more flexible. Our architecture,
      in particular, removes some components that recent trackers proposed as necessary for good perfor-
059
      mance while still improving on the state-of-the-art. For the first time, we also investigate the data
060
      scaling behaviour of a point tracker and show the advantages of different model architectures and
061   training protocols in terms of final tracking quality and data efficiency.
062
063   The excellent performance of recent trackers is due to the ability of high-capacity neural networks to
064
      learn a robust prior from many training videos and use this prior for tackling complex and ambigu-
      ous tracking cases, such as occlusions and fast motion. Therefore, the availability of high-quality
065
      training data (Muller et al., 2018) is of crucial importance in obtaining solid tracking results.
066
067   While, in principle, there is no shortage of videos that could be used to train point trackers, it is
068   difficult to manually annotate them with point tracks (Doersch et al., 2022). Fortunately, synthetic
069   videos (Greff et al., 2022), which can be annotated automatically, have been found to be a good
070
      substitute for real data for low-level tasks like point tracking (Harley et al., 2022). Still, a diverse
      collection of synthetic videos is expensive at scale, and the sim-to-real gap is not entirely negligible.
071
      Hence, using real videos to train point trackers remains an attractive option.
072
073   Recent works have thus explored utilizing large collections of real but unlabelled videos to train
074   point trackers. BootsTAPIR (Doersch et al., 2024), in particular, has recently achieved state-of-the-
075   art accuracy on the TAP-Vid benchmark by training a model on 15 million unlabelled videos. While
076
      the benefits of using more training data have thus been demonstrated, the data scaling behaviour of
      point trackers is not well understood. In particular, it is unclear if the millions of real training videos
077
      used in BootsTAPIR are necessary to train a good tracker. The same can be said about the benefits
078
      of their relatively complex semi-supervised training recipe.
079
080   Another largely unexplored aspect is the competing designs of different trackers. Transformer ar-
081   chitectures like PIPs (Harley et al., 2022), TAPIR (Doersch et al., 2023), and CoTracker (Karaev
082
      et al., 2024b), as well as more recent contributions like LocoTrack (Cho et al., 2024), propose each
      significant changes, extensions, new components, and different design decisions. While these are
083
      shown to help in the respective papers, it is less clear if they are all essential or whether these designs
084
      can be simplified and made more efficient.
085
086   RealTracker contributes to answering these questions. Our model is based on a simpler architec-
087   ture and training protocols than recent trackers such as BootsTAPIR and LocoTrack. It outperforms
088
      BootsTAPIR by a significant margin on the TAP-Vid and Dynamic Replica (Karaev et al., 2023)
      benchmarks while using three orders of magnitude fewer unlabelled videos and a simpler training
089
      protocol than BootsTAPIR. We also study the data scaling behaviour of this model under increas-
090
      ingly more real training videos. LocoTrack benefits in a similar manner to RealTracker from scaling
091   data but cannot track occluded points well.
092
093   RealTracker borrows elements from prior models, including iterative updates and convolutional fea-
094
      tures from PIPs, cross-track attention for joint tracking, virtual tracks for efficiency, and unrolled
      training for windowed operation from CoTracker, as well as the 4D correlation from LocoTrack. At
095
      the same time, it significantly simplifies some of these components and removes others, such as the
096
      global matching stage of BootsTAPIR and LocoTrack. This helps to identify which components are
097   really important for a good tracker. RealTracker’s architecture is also flexible as it can operate both
098   offline (i.e., single window) and online (i.e., sliding window) if trained in the same way.

101   2    R ELATED WORK
102
103   Tracking-Any-Point. The task of tracking any point was introduced by PIPs (Harley et al., 2022),
104   who revisited the classic Particle Video (Sand & Teller, 2008) method and proposed to use deep
105   learning for point tracking. Inspired by RAFT (Teed & Deng, 2020), an optical flow algorithm, PIPs
106   extracts correlation maps between frames and feeds them into a network to refine the track estimates.
107   TAP-Vid (Doersch et al., 2022) improved the framing of the problem and proposed three different
      benchmarks for point tracking. TAPIR (Doersch et al., 2023) combined TAP-Vid-like global match-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ing with PIPs, resulting in a much-improved performance. (Zheng et al., 2023) introduced another
109   synthetic benchmark, PointOdyssey, and PIPs++, an improved version of PIPs that can track points
110   over extended durations. CoTracker (Karaev et al., 2024b) noted that a strong correlation exists
111   between different tracks, which can be exploited to improve tracking, particularly behind occlusions
112   and out-of-frame. Le Moing et al. (2024) further improved CoTracker by densifying its output.
113   VGGSfM (Wang et al., 2024) proposed a coarse-to-fine tracker design where tracks are validated
114   through 3D reconstruction, but it only targets static scenes. Inspired by DETR (Carion et al., 2020),
115   Li et al. (2024) introduced TAPTR, an end-to-end transformer architecture for point tracking, rep-
116   resenting points as queries in the transformer decoder. LocoTrack (Cho et al., 2024) extended 2D
117
      correlation features to 4D correlation volumes while also simplifying the point-tracking pipeline and
      making it more efficient. Our work proposes a further simplified framework that runs 27% faster
118
      than LocoTrack, maintaining the ability to track occluded points via joint tracking, like CoTracker.
119
120   Annotating data for point tracking is particularly challenging due to the required precision: the
121   annotation should have (at least) pixel-level accuracy. The prevailing paradigm in point tracking
122   is thus to train models using synthetic data, where such annotations can be obtained automatically
123
      and without errors, and show that the resulting models generalize to real data. All the methods
      mentioned above follow this paradigm, and most are trained solely on Kubric (Greff et al., 2022).
124
125   Semi-supervised correspondence. An alternative to synthetic data is unlabelled real data in com-
126   bination with unsupervised or semi-supervised learning. For example, one can use photometric
127   consistency as a proxy for correspondences. Such training is well suited for optical flow and dense
128   tracking but often leads to false matches due to occlusions, repeated textures, or lighting changes.
129   Therefore it usually requires multi-frame estimates (Janai et al., 2018), explicit reasoning about
130   occlusions (Wang et al., 2018), hand-crafted loss terms (Liu et al., 2019b; Meister et al., 2018),
131   or various data augmentation strategies (Liu et al., 2020). Alternatively, one can use an existing
132
      tracker to train another in a process akin to distillation (Liu et al., 2019a). More robust unsuper-
      vised learning signals for long-range tracking can be obtained via simple colorization of gray-scale
133
      videos by copying colors from the reference frame (Vondrick et al., 2018) or utilizing richer visual
134
      patterns (Lai et al., 2020).
135
136   Accounting for cycle consistency (Wang et al., 2019; Jabri et al., 2020) or temporal continu-
137   ity (Földiák, 1991; Wiskott & Sejnowski, 2002) in videos is another way to obtain a reliable
138
      proxy signal to learn correspondences without full supervision, or even learn generic visual fea-
      tures (Goroshin et al., 2015; Wang & Gupta, 2015). Shen et al. (2022) proposed an unsupervised
139
      learning framework based on siamese networks for training trackers with cycle consistency. Re-
140
      cently, (Sun et al., 2024) proposed refining PIPs and RAFT on a pre-generated dataset with pseudo-
141   labels using color constancy and cycle consistency signals. This pipeline improves tracking, but
142   performance quickly saturates. Wu et al. (2021) introduced an unsupervised learning framework
143   that does not require any annotated videos in visual tracking. Tumanyan et al. (2024) combined test-
144   time per-video optimization with DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) features to improve point tracking.
145   Karaev et al. (2024a) introduced a more efficient architecture and a simple pseudo-labelling pipeline
146   to further improve its performance by training on real data.
147   Most relevant to our work, BootsTAPIR (Doersch et al., 2024) improved TAPIR trained on Kubric
148   by fine-tuning it on 15 million real videos using self-training while retaining a small synthetic dataset
149   with ground-truth supervision to avoid catastrophic forgetting. They proposed applying augmenta-
150   tions to student predictions and trained the model with an exponential moving average (EMA) while
151   computing three different loss masks for robustness. In contrast, our approach uses a simpler design
152   which does not require augmentations, masks, or EMA for training. We also do not need ground-
153   truth supervised data during finetuning on pseudo-labels. Instead, our idea is to train a student
154   model by utilizing existing trackers with complementary qualities as teachers. We also show that
155
      this protocol only requires a small fraction of the real videos utilized in BootsTAPIR.

161   In this section, we formally introduce the task of point tracking and then outline the proposed Real-
      Tracker architecture and the pseudo-labelling training pipeline we use to train it.

      Given a video (It )Tt=1 , which is a sequence of T frames It ∈ R3×H×W , and a query point
163   Q = (tq , xq , yq ) ∈ R3 where tq indicates the query frame index and (xq , yq ) represents the initial
164   location of the query point, our goal is to predict the corresponding point track Pt = (xt , yt ) ∈ R2 ,
165   t = 1, . . . , T , with (xtq , ytq ) = (xq , yq ). As is common in modern point tracking models (Doersch
166   et al., 2023; Karaev et al., 2024b), RealTracker also estimates visibility Vt ∈ [0, 1] and confidence
167   Ct ∈ [0, 1]. Visibility shows whether the tracked point is visible (Vt = 1) or occluded (Vt = 0)
168   in the current frame, while confidence measures whether the network is confident that the tracked
169   point is within a certain distance from the ground truth in the current frame (Ct = 1). The model
170   initializes all tracks with query coordinates Pt := (xtq , ytq ), t = 1, . . . , T , confidence and visibility
171
      with zeros Ct := 0, Vt := 0, then updates all of them iteratively.

173   3.1   T RAINING USING UNLABELLED VIDEOS
174
175   Recent trackers are trained primarily on synthetic data (Greff et al., 2022) due to the challenge of
176   annotating real data for this problem at scale. However, BootsTAPIR (Doersch et al., 2024) has
177   shown that it is possible to train better trackers by adding to the mix unlabelled real videos. In order
178   to do so, they propose a sophisticated self-training protocol that uses a large number of unlabelled
179   videos (15M), self-training, data augmentations, and transformation equivariance.
180   Here, we propose a much simpler protocol that allows us to surpass the performance of (Doersch
181   et al., 2024) with 1,000× less data: we use a variety of existing trackers to label a collection of real
182   videos, using them as teachers, and then use the pseudo-labels to train a new student model, which
183   we pre-train utilizing synthetic data.
184
      Importantly, the teacher models are also trained using the same synthetic data only. One may thus
185   wonder why this protocol should result in a student being better than any of the teachers. There are
186   several reasons for this: (1) the student benefits from learning from a much larger (noisy) dataset than
187   the synthetic data alone; (2) learning from real videos mitigates the distribution shifts between syn-
188   thetic and real data; (3) there is an ensembling/voting effect which reduces the pseudo-annotations
189   noise; (4) the student model may inherit the strengths of the different teachers, which may excel in
190   different aspects of the task (e.g., offline trackers track occluded points better and online trackers
191   tend to stick to the query points more closely near the track’s origin).
192
      Dataset. In order to enable such training, we collected a large-scale dataset of Internet-like videos
193   (around 100,000 videos of 30 seconds each) featuring diverse scenes and dynamic objects, primarily
194   humans and animals. We demonstrate that performance improves when training on increasingly
195   larger subsets of this data, starting from as few as 100 videos (see Figure 1).
196
197   Teacher models. To create a diverse set of supervisory signals, we employ multiple teacher mod-
198   els trained only on synthetic data from Kubric (Greff et al., 2022). Our set of teachers consists of
199
      our proposed models RealTracker online and RealTracker offline, CoTracker (Karaev et al., 2024b),
      and TAPIR (Doersch et al., 2023). During training, we randomly and uniformly sample a frozen
200
      teacher model for every batch (meaning that it is likely that, over several epochs, the same video
201
      will receive pseudo-labels from different teachers), which helps to prevent over-fitting and promotes
202   generalization. The teacher models are not updated during training.
203
204   Query point sampling. Trackers require a query point to track in addition to a video. After ran-
205   domly choosing a teacher for the current batch, we sample a set of query points for each video. To
206   select such queries, we use the SIFT detector (Lowe, 1999) sampling, biasing the selection of points
207   to those which are “good to track” (Shi & Tomasi, 1994). Specifically, we randomly select T̂ frames
208   across a video and apply SIFT to generate points to start tracks on these keyframes. Our intuition
209
      behind using a feature extractor is guided by its ability to detect descriptive image features when-
      ever possible while failing to do so when meeting ambiguous cases. We hypothesise that this will
210
      serve as a filter for hard-to-track points and will thus improve the stability of training. Following
211
      this intuition, if SIFT fails to produce a sufficient number of points for any frame, we skip the video
212   completely during training to maintain the quality of our training data.
213
214   Supervision. We supervise tracks predicted by the student model with the same loss used to pre-
215   train the model on synthetic data, with only minor modifications for handling occlusion and tracking
      confidence. These details are given later in Section 3.3.

237   in a sliding window manner, processing the input video sequentially and tracking points forward-

239
      point tracking in both forward and backward directions. The offline version tracks occluded points

240
      of tracked frames is memory-bound, while the online version can track in real-time indefinitely.

305   Online model. Both online and offline versions of RealTracker have the same architecture. The
306   main difference between them is the way of training. The online version processes videos in a
307

314

315
      overfitting to a specific length. With this intuition in mind, for the offline version, we randomly trim
316   a video between T /2 and T frames and linearly interpolate time embeddings during training.

345   Track, RealTracker does not use a global matching module as we found it redundant.
346
      A benefit of these simplifications is that RealTracker is considerably leaner and faster than other
347   similar trackers. Specifically, RealTracker has 2× fewer parameters than CoTracker, while the ab-
348   sence of global matching and the use of an MLP to process correlations makes RealTracker 27%
349   faster than the fastest tracker (LocoTrack) despite cross-track attention.

456
457   In Figure 1, we show how RealTracker, LocoTrack, and CoTracker (Karaev et al., 2024b) improve
458   with our pseudo-labeling pipeline as the training set size increases. Starting with models pre-trained
459   on a synthetic dataset (Greff et al., 2022) (0 at x-axis), we train them on progressively larger real
460   data sets: 0.1k, 1k, 5k, 10k, 30k, and 100k videos. Models are trained to convergence on their
461
      respective subsets. All models improve with just 0.1k real-world videos and continue improving
      with more. Improvements for RealTracker online, offline, and LocoTrack tend to plateau after 30k
462
      videos, likely because the student surpasses the teachers. This may also explain why CoTracker,
463
      initially much weaker than two of its teachers (RealTracker online and offline), keeps improving up
464   to and possibly beyond 100k videos, which is the maximum we can afford to explore. Our training
465   strategy is effective for all these models. We analyse the effect of using a scaled RealTracker as a
466   new teacher in the supplement. For comparison, BootsTAPIR (Doersch et al., 2024) uses 15 million
467   real videos and a complex protocol involving augmentations, loss masks, and more.
468
      Interestingly, we found that training RealTracker with its own predictions as annotations without
469
      other teachers (i.e., self-training) further improves the results on all the TAP-Vid benchmarks by
470   +1.2 points on average (see Table 4). Presumably, fine-tuning on real data, even with its own
471   annotations, helps the model reduce the domain gap between real and synthetic data.

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We introduced RealTracker, a new point tracker that outperforms the state-of-the-art on TAP-Vid
533
      and other benchmarks. RealTracker’s architecture combines several good ideas from recent trackers
534   but eliminates unnecessary components and significantly simplifies others. RealTracker also shows
535   the power of a simple pseudo-labelling training protocol, where real videos are annotated utilizing
536   several off-the-shelf trackers and then used to fine-tune a model that outperforms all teachers. With
537   this protocol, RealTracker can surpass trackers trained on ×1,000 more videos. By tracking points
538   jointly, RealTracker handles occlusions better than any other model, particularly when operated in
539   offline mode. Our model can be used as a building block for tasks requiring motion estimation, such
      as 3D tracking, controlled video generation, or dynamic 3D reconstruction.

      A   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
709
      We pre-train both online and offline model versions on synthetic TAP-Vid-Kubric (Doersch et al.,
710
      2022; Greff et al., 2022) for 50,000 iterations on 32 NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs with a batch size
711   of 1 video. We train RealTracker online on videos of length T = 64 with a window size of 16,
712   and sample 384 query points per video with a bias towards objects. Since the online version tracks
713   only forward in time, we sample points primarily at the beginning of the video. We train the offline
714   version on videos of length T ∈ {30, 31, . . . , 60} with time embeddings of size 60. We interpolate
715   time embeddings to the current sequence length both at training and evaluation. We sample 512
716   query points per video uniformly in time. Both models are trained in bfloat16 with gradient norm
717   clipping using PyTorch Lightning (Falcon & The PyTorch Lightning team, 2019) with PyTorch
718   distributed data parallel (Li et al., 2020). The optimizer is AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017)
719
      with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, learning rate 5 · 10−4 , and weight decay 1 · 10−5 . The optimizer adopts
      a linear warm-up for 1000 steps followed by a cosine learning rate scheduler.
720
721   We scale RealTracker on a dataset of Internet-like videos primarily featuring humans and animals.
722   We visualize the scaling pipeline in Figure 5. To ensure the quality and relevance of our training
723   data, we use caption-based filtering with specific keywords to select videos containing real-world
724   content while excluding those with computer-generated imagery, animation, or natural phenomena
725
      that are challenging to track, such as fire, lights, and water.
726   When training on real data, we use a similar setup while reducing the learning rate to 5e − 5 with
727   the same cosine scheduler without warm-up. We train both online and offline versions for 10,000
728   iterations with 384 tracks per video sampled with SIFT on eight randomly selected frames with
729   frame sampling biased towards the beginning of the video.
730   Following (Karaev et al., 2024b), when evaluating RealTracker online on TAP-Vid, we add 5×5
731   points sampled on a regular grid and 8×8 points sampled on a local grid around the query point to
732   provide context to the tracker. We do the same for the scaled offline version during inference. The
733   Kubric-trained offline version, however, relies on uniform point sampling during training. For this
734   model, during evaluation on TAP-Vid, we instead sample 1000 additional support points uniformly
735   over time.

898   First, we show that RealTracker can successfully utilize the temporal context to track points through
899   occlusions. In order to do so, we occlude all tracked points in each video for half of the video length,
900
      starting right after the query frame, but let the points be visible in the second half. The occluding
      circle is centered in the ground truth track. We vary the radius of the occluding circle and increase it
901
      from 0% to 100% of the video width. As Table 11 shows, the tracking accuracy is still 19.9% even
902
      with a radius of 100%; this is because the model sees the second half of the video and can track
903   points there. If we occlude all rather than half the frames with a radius of 100%, the accuracy drops
904   to 2%.
905
906
      Second, we show that RealTracker can also successfully utilize the spatial context given by other
      tracked points. In order to do so, we fix the radius of the occlusion to 8% of the image width and
907
      vary the duration of the occlusion from 0 to 100% of the video length, with average video length
908
      being 60 frames. In Table 12, short occlusions of 20% of video length (12 frames on average) affect
909   performance, but not significantly: accuracy drops from 76.9% to 61.3%. When occluding points
910   for the whole duration of the video (100%), the model can still approximate the location of these
911   points due to the presence of unoccluded support points (21.1% accuracy vs 2% when all points are
912   occluded).

      E   L IMITATIONS
922
923   A key limitation of our pseudo-labeling pipeline is its reliance on the quality and diversity of teacher
924   models. The observed saturation in performance on TAP-Vid during scaling suggests that the stu-
925   dent model absorbs knowledge from all the teachers and, after a certain point, struggles to improve
926   further. Thus, we need stronger or more diverse teacher models to achieve additional gains for the
927   student model.
