                 Error slice discovery is crucial to diagnose and mitigate model errors. Current clustering or
012              discrete attribute-based slice discovery methods face key limitations: 1) clustering results in
013              incoherent slices, while assigning discrete attributes to slices leads to incomplete coverage
014              of error patterns due to missing or insufficient attributes; 2) these methods lack complex
015              reasoning, preventing them from fully explaining model biases; 3) they fail to integrate
016              domain knowledge, limiting their usage in specialized fields e.g., radiology. We propose
017              LADDER (Language-Driven Discovery and Error Rectification), to address the limitations
018              by: (1) leveraging the flexibility of natural language to address incompleteness, (2) em-
019              ploying LLM’s latent domain knowledge and advanced reasoning to analyze sentences
020
                 and derive testable hypotheses directly, identifying biased attributes, and form coherent
                 error slices without clustering. Existing mitigation methods typically address only the
021
                 worst-performing group, often amplifying errors in other subgroups. In contrast, LADDER
022
                 generates pseudo attributes from the discovered hypotheses to mitigate errors across all
023              biases without explicit attribute annotations or prior knowledge of bias. Rigorous evalua-
024              tions on 6 datasets spanning natural and medical images – comparing 200+ classifiers with
025              diverse architectures, pretraining strategies, and LLMs – show that LADDER consistently
026              outperforms existing baselines in discovering and mitigating biases. The code is available1 .

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Discovering error slices in models is essential for mitigating their limitations. Existing slice discovery
032   methods typically cluster misclassified samples by similar attributes or directly analyze model biases using a
033   set of predefined attributes. Although these methods are intuitive and support straightforward mitigation like
034   rebalancing, they face several key issues: 1) unsupervised clustering approaches often produce incoherent
035
      attribute groupings, while direct attribute-based methods suffer from incompleteness due to missing or
      insufficient attribute annotation; 2) they lack reasoning capability about the deeper complexities of model
036
      errors; and 3) they do not incorporate essential domain knowledge critical in specialized fields, e.g., radiology.
037
      In contrast, our approach, LADDER, is the first to use LLMs for slice discovery beyond simple keyword-based
038   attribute searches and addresses these issues by (1) using the flexibility of natural language rather than merely
039   relying on the presence/absence of attributes; (2) leveraging the reasoning capabilities and domain knowledge
040   of LLMs to identify coherent error slices from language without relying on unsupervised clustering.
041
      Language-aware slice discovery methods e.g., DrML (Zhang et al., 2023), leverage language through the
042
      text encoder to mitigate biases in the CLIP vision encoder by closing the modality gap with cross-modal
043
      transferability. However, this reliance on cross-modal transfer hinders their applicability to non-multimodal
044   models. Also, DrML relies on user-defined prompts, introducing subjectivity and potential human bias into
045

      the error rectification process. Similarly, Facts (Yenamandra et al., 2023) amplifies the spuriousness in the
048
      initial training stage by setting large weight decay, deviating from standard supervised learning practices.
049   Methods like Domino (Eyuboglu et al., 2022) and Facts discover slices by clustering samples with similar
050   attributes within the vision-language representation (VLR) space. However, the slices often exhibit semantic
051   inconsistencies – attributes within slices lack coherence, leading to unreliable interpretations of model errors.
052   PRIME (Rezaei et al., 2023) relies on expensive tagging models (Zhang et al., 2024), limited to detecting the
053   presence or absence of attributes. HiBug (Chen et al., 2024) prompts LLMs for potentially biased attributes
054   (via keywords) based solely on general user prompts without incorporating any textual context from the
055   dataset itself. These tags or attributes can be incomplete. Also, these methods lack reasoning capabilities and
056   domain knowledge needed for complex error patterns, failing to capture relevant biases for specialized tasks.
057   Existing bias mitigation methods e.g., GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020), JTT (Liu et al., 2021),
058   DFR (Kirichenko et al., 2022) rely on expensive and often incomplete attribute annotations in the training
059   or validation sets. While these methods improve the performance of the worst-performing group, they can
060   inadvertently amplify errors in other groups, highlighted by Li et al. (2023b). Although Li et al. (2023b)
061   addresses errors across multiple biases, it assumes prior knowledge about the number and types of biases
062   to design specific data augmentations. This reveals a critical gap: the need for an automated method for
063
      discovering slices from data and mitigating multiple biases w/o prior knowledge or annotations.
064   Contributions. This paper introduces LADDER to address the gaps in slice discovery and bias mitigation.
065   It detects slices on any off-the-shelf supervised classifier, over-
066   coming the specific training requirements of Facts and DrML.

      2.1   R ETRIEVING SENTENCES HIGHLIGHTING MODEL’ S ERROR
109
110
      First, for a particular class, LADDER retrieves the sentences that describe the visual attributes contributing to
111   correct classifications but missing in misclassified ones, leading to model errors. Following Moayeri et al.
112   (2023a), it learns a projection function π : Φ → ΨI (Appendix A.2 for details) to align the representation
113   of the classifier, Φ, with the image representation, ΨI of the VLR space. Then, for a class label Y , we
114   estimate the difference in mean of the projected representations of the correct and misclassified samples
115   as ∆I = EX,Y |f (X)=Y [π(Φ(X)] − EX,Y |f (X)̸=Y [π(Φ(X)]. Assuming the mean representations preserve
116   semantics, this difference captures key attributes contributing to correct classifications but are poorly captured
117   or misrepresented in misclassified ones. Denoting the  text embedding of tval as ΨT (tval ), we retrieve the
118   topK sentences as: topK = R ⟨∆I , ΨT (tval )⟩, tval , where R is a retrieval function retrieving topK
119   sentences from the text corpus having the highest similarity score with the mean difference of the projected
120
      image representations. Next, the LLM analyzes the sentences and constructs hypotheses to find error slices.

      For the attributes linked to a hypothesis, LADDER treats sH as a logit and converts it to a probability. If the
161   probability exceeds a threshold (0.5 in all experiments), LADDER assigns a pseudo-label 1 to the attribute and
162   0 otherwise. Thus, it generates pseudo-labels for all relevant attributes, enabling error mitigation without
163   annotations. To do so, LADDER adopts an ensemble-based strategy, either reweighting or rebalancing.
164   Strategy 1: reweighting. We assume the data-generating process for each hypothesis, where an attribute
165   influences both the label Y and the image X, and Y subsequently influencing X. Inspired by IPTW (Austin,
                                                                1
166   2011) in causal inference, we apply a weight of P (Y |attribute)  to each training sample and finetune the last
167   layer g of the classifier to upweight the minority samples. We calculate the weight using the pseudo attributes.
168   We repeat this process for each hypothesis, resulting in a debiased model per hypothesis. During inference,
169   we compute the similarity score sH (X) for all hypotheses and select the classifier head gH ∗ associated with
170   the hypothesis that has the highest similarity score for the test image X, where H ∗ = arg maxH∈H sH (X).
171
      We refer to this as LADDERreweight . Strategy 2: rebalancing. Following DFR, we create a balanced
      dataset from a held-out validation set, for each pseudo-labeled attribute per hypothesis. We then fine-tune
172
      the classification head g using this balanced dataset, producing a debiased model per hypothesis. During
173
      inference, we again compute the similarity score sH for all hypotheses and select the classifier head gH ∗
174   associated with the hypothesis having maximum similarity: H ∗ = arg maxH∈H sH (X). We term this as
175   LADDER bal . Empirically, in our experiments, LADDER bal outperforms LADDER reweight .

      7   C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATION
463
464
      This paper presents LADDER, a method to discover and mitigate error slices using natural language and LLM
465   reasoning, without relying on costly external attribute annotations. LADDER generates hypotheses to detect
466   model errors and mitigates biases using pseudo-labels tailored to each identified slice. Extensive experiments
467   show LADDER’s efficacy compared to baselines. However, its performance depends on the quality of available
468   captions and the vision-language model. Future work will focus on iterative refinement of the discovery
469   process based on slice complexity and leveraging language inversion to eliminate the need for a text corpus.

      A.5    C LIP S CORE
903
904   Kim et al. (2024) introduces the CLIP score, a metric that leverages the similarity between language and
905   vision embeddings to quantify the influence of specific attributes on misclassified samples. In their method,
906   attributes frequently present in misclassified images receive a high CLIP score, while absent ones score lower.
907   For instance, in the Waterbirds dataset, the CLIP score for "bamboo" is high, as many misclassified waterbirds
908   appear with bamboo in the background.
909   We propose a modification to the CLIP score. As discussed in Sec. 2.1, our goal is to identify visual attributes
910   that are prevalent in correctly classified samples but absent in misclassified ones. This approach provides
911   deeper insights into the attributes contributing to correct classifications, which is particularly valuable for
912   medical images. In scenarios such as pneumothorax detection in the NIH dataset, understanding biases
913   incorrectly classified cases—such as the presence of chest tubes—can help isolate features that lead to reliable
914   diagnoses while addressing spurious correlations. Formally we define the CLIP score corresponding to the
915   attribute attr and a dataset D as,

921   embedding of a word ΨT (attr) and images ΨI (x) for x ∈ D, where

1038   A.8   E XTENDED DETAILS ON SLICE DISCOVERY ALGORITHMS
1039
1040   Domino. Domino Eyuboglu et al. (2022) identifies systematic errors in machine learning models by leveraging
1041   cross-modal embeddings. It operates in three main steps: embedding, slicing, and describing.
1042
1043
             1. Embedding: Domino uses cross-modal models (e.g., CLIP) to embed inputs and text in the same
                latent space. This enables the incorporation of semantic meaning from text into input embeddings,
1044
                which is crucial for identifying coherent slices.
1045
1046
             2. Slicing: It employs an error-aware mixture model to detect underperforming regions within the
                embedding space. This model clusters the data based on embeddings, class labels, and model
1047
                predictions to pinpoint areas where the model performance is subpar. The mixture model ensures
1048
                that identified slices are coherent and relevant to model errors.
1049
1050
             3. Describing: Domino generates natural language descriptions for the discovered slices. It creates
                prototype embeddings for each slice and matches them with text embeddings to describe the common
1051
                characteristics of the slice. This step provides interpretable insights into why the model fails on
1052
                these slices.
1053
1054   Domino’s approach improves slice coherence and generates meaningful slice descriptions.
1055
       Facts. Facts Yenamandra et al. (2023) (First Amplify Correlations and Then Slice) aims to identify bias-
1056
       conflicting slices in datasets through a two-stage process:
1057
1058         1. Amplify Correlations: This stage involves training a model with a high regularization term to
1059            amplify its reliance on spurious correlations present in the dataset. This step helps segregate
1060            biased-aligned from bias-conflicting samples by making the model fit a simpler, biased-aligned
1061            hypothesis.
1062         2. Correlation-aware Slicing: In this stage, FACTS uses clustering techniques on the bias-amplified
1063            feature space to discover bias-conflicting slices. The method identifies subgroups where the spurious
1064            correlations do not hold, highlighting areas where the model underperforms due to these biases.
1065
       Facts leverages a combination of bias amplification and clustering to reveal underperforming data slices,
1066
       providing a foundation for understanding and mitigating systematic biases in machine learning models.

1246   A.11    T OY DATASET CONSTRUCTION
1247
1248   We construct a synthetic dataset based on the CUB-200-2011 (Wah et al., 2011) dataset, classifying bird
1249
       species into two categories: Class 0 (y = 0) and Class 1 (y = 1). Class 1 consists of the following bird
       species: Albatross, Auklet, Cormorant, Frigatebird, Fulmar, Gull, Jaeger, Kittiwake, Pelican, Puffin, Tern,
1250
       Gadwall, Grebe, Mallard, Merganser, Guillemot, and Pacific Loon. All remaining bird species are assigned
1251
       to Class 0. To introduce spurious correlations, we overlay two 3D boxes on each image. In the training set for
1252   Class 0, the majority of samples (95%) were biased, with the yellow box consistently placed to the left of the
1253   red box. For Class 1, the boxes were randomly placed, introducing variability in their positioning. In the
1254   validation and test sets, we split the positioning evenly, with 50% biased and 50% random samples across
1255   both classes, ensuring a balanced evaluation of the model’s reliance on spurious cues.
1256
       The primary goal of this dataset is to introduce a form of reasoning beyond the mere presence or absence
1257
       of spurious correlations. Unlike prior datasets that rely on background cues (e.g., Waterbirds or Metashift)
1258   or attributes like gender (e.g., CelebA), our dataset integrates positional reasoning. Specifically, for Class
1259   0, the yellow box is consistently placed to the left of the red box, creating a spurious correlation. For Class
1260   1, the boxes are randomly positioned, removing this shortcut. The relative positioning of the boxes allows
1261   the captions to encode spatial relationships, which can be consumed by large language models (LLMs) to
1262   reason about these spatial cues. We train an ImageNet pretrained-ResNet model (RN Sup IN1k) on this
1263   dataset. Predictably, the classifier latches onto the spurious correlation of rectangle position, leading to
1264   underperformance on subsets where the shortcut is absent. The model achieves a mean accuracy of 85.6%
1265   and a worst-group accuracy (WGA) of 65.2%.
1266   To analyze the model’s errors, we generate a corpus of rich captions for the validation set using a GPT-4o-
1267   based captioner. These captions describe both the presence of the rectangle and its position relative to the bird.
1268   Using LADDER, we aim to detect the reason for the classifier’s mistakes and mitigate it. LADDER leverages

1339   For each hypothesis, we sample subsets from our dataset where the specific attribute mentioned in the
1340   hypothesis is present. This allows us to compute the mean observed accuracy for images exhibiting attributes
1341   relevant to each hypothesis. To construct a null distribution, we randomly sample an equal number of images,
1342   both with and without the attribute. This balanced sampling approach is crucial as it simulates the null
1343   hypothesis scenario that the attribute does not influence the classification accuracy.
1344
       The experiment is conducted with 1000 iterations for each hypothesis. In each iteration, 100 data points are
1345   randomly selected to calculate the classification accuracy against the ground truth. This repetitive sampling
1346   ensures robustness in our statistical testing by accurately approximating the distribution of accuracies under
1347   both observed and null conditions.
1348
       We apply a t-test (using ttest_ind from the scipy.stats package) for each hypothesis to compare
1349
       the mean accuracies of the observed and null distributions. The t-test, chosen for its suitability in comparing
1350
       means from two independent samples, provides a t-statistic and a p-value. These statistics quantify the
1351   evidence against the null hypothesis, offering a measure of the impact of the hypothesized attribute on model
1352   performance.
