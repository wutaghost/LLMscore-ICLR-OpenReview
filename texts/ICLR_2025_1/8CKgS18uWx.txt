011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Proteins, as essential biomolecules, play a central role in biological processes,
014            including metabolic reactions and DNA replication. Accurate prediction of their
015            properties and functions is crucial in biological applications. Recent development of
016
               protein language models (pLMs) with supervised fine tuning provides a promising
               solution to this problem. However, the fine-tuned model is tailored for particular
017
               downstream prediction task, and achieving general-purpose protein understanding
018
               remains a challenge. In this paper, we introduce Structure-Enhanced Protein
019            Instruction Tuning (SEPIT) framework to bridge this gap. Our approach integrates a
020            noval structure-aware module into pLMs to inform them with structural knowledge,
021            and then connects these enhanced pLMs to large language models (LLMs) to
022            generate understanding of proteins. In this framework, we propose a novel two-
023            stage instruction tuning pipeline that first establishes a basic understanding of
024            proteins through caption-based instructions and then refines this understanding
025            using a mixture of experts (MoEs) to learn more complex properties and functional
026            information with the same amount of activated parameters. Moreover, we construct
027
               the largest and most comprehensive protein instruction dataset to date, which
               allows us to train and evaluate the general-purpose protein understanding model.
028
               Extensive experimental results on open-ended generation and closed-set answer
029
               tasks demonstrate the superior performance of SEPIT over both closed-source
030            general LLMs and open-source LLMs trained with protein knowledge.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Proteins are large biomolecules and macromolecules, composed of one or more long chains of amino
036   acid residues, playing pivotal roles in catalyzing metabolic reactions, DNA replication, and other
037   significant biological processes (Anfinsen & Haber, 1961; Hartley, 1951). Generally, proteins are
038   represented in two types of forms: one-dimensional (1D) sequence detailing the order of amino acids,
039
      and three-dimensional (3D) structure illustrating the spatial configuration of the protein. The 1D
      sequence of protein is generated by transcribing and translating a gene’s DNA sequence and folds
040
      into a specific 3D structure, and the 3D shape determines the protein’s properties and functions (Pei
041
      et al., 2024b). Conventional machine learning methods (Radivojac et al., 2013; Whisstock & Lesk,
042   2003) have achieved notable accuracy in protein property and function prediction via supervised
043   learning. However, these methods are all task-specific as each model is restricted to predicting a
044   particular property. The increasing need for comprehensive protein analysis in various fields, such as
045   pathology and drug discovery (Guo et al., 2023), calls for the development of general-purpose protein
046   understanding models capable of accurately predicting various protein properties and functions.
047
      In the fast-evolving era of large language models (LLMs), large efforts have attempted to leverage
048   their capabilities of semantic understanding and complex reasoning to achieve general-purpose protein
049   property and function prediction. Initially, some methods treat the 1D protein sequence as natural
050   language input to LLMs (Wang et al., 2023b; Luo et al., 2023b; Taylor et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2024;
051   Pei et al., 2024a). However, they focus on the learning of associations between protein sequences and
052   their properties or functions using a small part of real-world protein sequences, which hinders the
053   LLMs from generating reliable results for proteins at an evolutionary scale. To cope with this issue,
      ProtST (Xu et al., 2023) and ProteinCLAP (Liu et al., 2023) utilize pLMs pre-trained on evolutionary-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      scale protein databases as protein sequences encoders and leverage contrastive learning (Radford
055   et al., 2021b) to train on protein-text paired data, thereby incorporating functional information from
056   text with high-quality protein representations from pLMs. Unfortunately, these methods can be solely
057   applied to prediction and retrieval tasks about proteins whereas real-world proteins with complex
058   and diverse properties and functions require full understanding in an open-ended generation fashion
059   instead of these specific tasks. Therefore, Prot2Text (Abdine et al., 2024), ProteinChat (Guo et al.,
060   2023) and ProtT3 (Liu et al., 2024b) propose the protein-to-language generation via integrating
061   protein sequence or structure encoding from pre-trained models.
062   However, existing methods still show limitations in providing reliable general-purpose protein under-
063   standing for their application in scientific research. Firstly, although some studies have considered
064   the determinant role of protein 3D structure on its properties and functions and used it as input, the
065   proteins with directly usable 3D structural information is very rare. This leads to a situation where
066   we need to learn the relationship between 1D sequences and functional information while relying on
067   limited 3D information, making it challenging to provide reliable property and function predictions.
068   Secondly, existing protein-related instruction datasets neglect 3D structures and have limited coverage
069
      of property and function types, which impedes the evaluation of model reliability and generalizability
      in general-purpose protein understanding tasks. Thirdly, the diversity of protein properties and
070
      functions poses a challenge for their accurate prediction. Using a single general-purpose model to
071
      predict a wide range and complex set of properties and functions is more challenging than fine-tuning
072   multiple specialized models for different specific tasks.
073
074   To address these challenges, we propose a generalized instruction tuning framework called Structure-
075
      Enhanced Protein Instruction Tuning (SEPIT) for general-purpose protein understanding. To compre-
      hensively evaluate the reliability and generalizability of our models, we construct the largest protein
076
      instruction dataset to date which covers the most types of protein properties and functions, based
077
      on large-scale protein knowledge bases (Bairoch & Apweiler, 1997; Varadi et al., 2022). Before
078   instruction tuning, in order to obtain a protein sequence/structure fused encoder that supports different
079   types of protein input (1D or 1D&3D), we specially design a structure-aware module into pLMs.
080   Then, we warm it up through protein-text contrastive learning and structure denoising, which provides
081   a foundation to leverage a small amount of structural information for enhancing the understanding of
082   large-scale sequence-only proteins. After that, based on the protein sequence/structure fused encoder,
083   we design a two-stage protein instruction tuning pipeline to enable LLMs for general-purpose protein
084   understanding. In stage 1, we instill basic understanding of proteins into the model through protein
085   caption instructions. In stage 2, we initialize mixture of experts through upcycling (Komatsuzaki
086
      et al., 2023), which allows the model to learn more complex and diverse functions and properties,
      based on the basic understanding from stage 1, without increasing additional activated parameters.
087
      In summary, our contributions include: 1) By designing a structure-aware module and integrating it
088
      into pLMs, we enable the models to handle different types of protein inputs, thereby improving the
089   quality of embedding over the vanilla sequence-only pLMs. 2) We construct the largest and most
090   comprehensive protein instruction dataset to date, which addresses the lack of comprehensive dataset
091   for general-purpose protein understanding. 3) We design a two-stage protein instruction tuning
092   pipeline that enables a single model to learn a wide range and complex set of protein properties and
093   functions. This is achieved by leveraging Mixtures of Experts (MoEs) built upon foundational knowl-
094   edge. 4) Based on the proposed SEPIT framework and protein instruction dataset, we demonstrate
095   the feasibility of enabling LLMs with the capability of general-purpose protein understanding.
096
097   2    R ELATED W ORK
098   In this section, we will present the related work pertinent to our study, focusing primarily on protein
099   language models and multimodal instruction tuning. Additionally, an introduction to related work
100   concerning learning with 3D structural information is provided in Appendix A.
101
      Protein Language Models. Employing context-aware language models (Rosenfeld, 2000), protein
102   sequences can be likened to sentences wherein amino acids serve as the elemental words. Through pre-
103   training on databases containing hundreds of millions of such protein sequences (e.g., UniRef (Suzek
104   et al., 2015), BFD (Steinegger et al., 2019; Steinegger & Söding, 2018)), pLMs enable effective
105   modeling and prediction of protein structures and functions (Hu et al., 2022). In earlier works (Alley
106   et al., 2019; Strodthoff et al., 2020; Heinzinger et al., 2019), LSTM and its variants (Hochreiter &
107   Schmidhuber, 1997; Yu et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2015; Krause et al., 2016) were utilized to model
      the dependencies between residues in single protein sequences. With the rise of the Transformers


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), Transformers-based pLMs emerged. ESM-1b (Rives et al., 2021),
109   leveraging the Transformers architecture along with a masking strategy for pretraining, significantly
110   enhances the prediction accuracy for mutational effects, secondary structure, and long-range contacts.
111   After this, ProtTrans (Elnaggar et al., 2022) released two auto-regressive models (Dai et al., 2019;
112   Yang et al., 2019) and four auto-encoder models (Devlin et al., 2018; Lan et al., 2019; Clark et al.,
113   2020; Raffel et al., 2020) pre-trained on protein sequence databases. Beyond merely focusing on
114   single protein sequences, MSA-Transformer (Rao et al., 2021) integrate multiple sequence alignments
115   (MSA) of homologous proteins, provided a solid foundation for the success of AlphaFold2 (Jumper
116   et al., 2021). Moreover, ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023) further scaled up pLMs, achieving protein structure
117
      prediction performance comparable to previous works without utilizing MSA information, and
      significantly reduced inference overhead (Lin et al., 2022). Additionally, there were other studies
118
      that attempted to incorporate additional knowledge into the pre-training of protein sequences. For
119
      instance, ProteinBERT (Brandes et al., 2022) and OntoProtein (Zhang et al., 2022) integrated gene
120   ontology (GO) information into the representations of protein sequences, enhancing the model’s
121   understanding of protein functions. Although these pLMs can provide high-quality representations of
122   proteins, they cannot generate natural language predictions about protein properties and functions.
123
124   Multimodal Instruction Tuning. With the emergence of MLLMs such as GPT4 (OpenAI et al.,
125   2024) and Genimi (Team et al., 2023), MLLMs had become a focal point of research. Initially, works
126   like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021b), ALBEF (Radford et al., 2021a), VLMo (Radford et al., 2021c),
127
      SimVLM (Wang et al., 2021) emphasized exploring the cross-modal alignment between vision and
      language. Subsequently, based on modal alignment, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and BLIP2 (Li
128
      et al., 2023) established bridges between visual encoders and LLMs using the Perceiver Resampler
129
      and the Q-Former, respectively. Following this, PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023) introduced "multimodal
130   sentences" as input, injecting real-world continuous sensor data into the LLMs in the form of
131   language tokens, thereby endowing the model with a general multi-task capability. Additionally,
132   efforts such as InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2024), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a), MiniGPT4 (Zhu et al.,
133   2023), mPLUGOwl (Ye et al., 2023), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023a)
134   applied the crucial instruction tuning technique from LLMs to MLLMs, enhancing the MLLMs’
135   ability to follow multimodal instructions. At the same time, they introduced innovations from the
136   perspectives such as the construction of instruction datasets, training paradigms, and model design,
137   which in turn refreshed the performance of MLLMs in a variety of visual-language downstream
138
      tasks (Yin et al., 2023). In this paper, we attempt to apply this paradigm to the protein domain,
      investigating the potential of endowing LLMs with general-purpose protein understanding capabilities.

141   3    C ONSTRUCTION OF P ROTEIN I NSTRUCTION DATASET
142
143   To endow LLMs with general-purpose protein understanding capabilities and evaluate their reliability
144   and generalizability, in this paper, we construct a protein instruction dataset contains open-ended
145   generation and closed-set answer tasks. For the open-ended generation subset, we mainly constructed
146   it based on Swiss-Prot (Bairoch & Apweiler, 1997). We include almost all protein properties
147
      and functions contained therein (Function, Similarity, Subcellular location, Induction, Molecular
      Function, Biological Process, Cellular Component, Developmental Stage, Short Sequence Motif,
148
      Tissue Specificity, Activity Regulation, Pathway), and used ChatGPT (OpenAI et al., 2024) to aid in
149
      designing question templates based on the structured annotations. For the closed-set answer subset,
150   we constructed it mainly based on the RCSB PDB (RCSB, 2024). We follow the data organized
151   by previous researchers (Guo et al., 2023) and select parts of their proposed Q&A samples that are
152   highly related to protein properties and functions, filtering out other samples related to metadata (e.g.
153   discovery time and discovery methods). We have also sampled some examples related to Enzyme
154   Commission (EC) and Gene Ontology (GO) predictions (Gligorijević et al., 2021) for inclusion
155   in the closed-set answer subset. More detailed information about the dataset, including statistical
156   information and examples, are shown in Appendix B.
157   Compared to previously proposed protein-text related datasets (Xu et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024;
158   Wang et al., 2023b; Guo et al., 2023), our advantages are as follows: First, our dataset contains the
159   most comprehensive set of instructions, covering almost all critical protein properties and function
160   types found in databases (Bairoch & Apweiler, 1997). Second, our dataset includes the largest volume
161   of instructions, comprising over 10 million instructions (with an additional 5 million supplementary
      instructions from TrEMBL). Third, our dataset incorporates structural information, offering the

189   4.1     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
190
      In this subsection, we introduce three main components of the SEPIT framework: 1) a se-
191
      quence/structure fused protein encoder, 2) a linear projector, and 3) a large language model with
192   mixture of experts modules. The illustration of the model architecture is depicted in Figure 1.
193
194
      Sequence/Structure Fused Protein Encoder. Considering the abundance of sequence-only data
      and the relatively small amount of sequence-structure paired data (whether experimentally-determined
195
      structures or computed structures) within the protein domain (Varadi et al., 2022; Steinegger et al.,
196
      2019; Steinegger & Söding, 2018; Suzek et al., 2015), we propose a Sequence/Structure Fused Protein
197   Encoder that is capable of accommodating inputs in either form. Additionally, through leveraging the
198   limited sequence-structure paired data, we aim to enhance the model’s performance when dealing
199   with sequence-only inputs.
200

202   structural information, we have designed structure-aware modules for pLMs. Mainstream pLMs,
203   such as ESM (Rives et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023), are encoder-only architectures, consisting of
204   multiple Transformer encoder layers, which primarily comprise self-attention modules (Vaswani
205
      et al., 2017) and feed-forward network (FFN). Here, our main focus is on the self-attention modules

286   4.2 T RAINING P IPELINE
287   In this subsection, we will discuss the details of the training pipeline for SEPIT framework, based on
288   the model architecture presented before. As shown in Figure 2, the whole pipeline includes three
289   stages: in Stage 0, we warm up our proposed sequence/structure fused protein encoder based on
290   pre-trained pLM primarily through protein-text contrastive learning and structure denoising. In Stage
291   1, we pre-train on the protein captioning task to further align protein representations with natural
292   language, while concurrently infusing foundational protein knowledge into LLMs. In Stage 2, we
293   initiate the MoEs modules using the sparse upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023) from the FFNs of the
294   LLMs trained in Stage 1 and perform instruction tuning on our proposed protein instruction dataset.

329   consistency in the model’s handling of different forms of protein inputs, we randomly input protein

331   probabilities of 15% and 85%, respectively. The output protein representation sequences are mapped

      Workload of Experts in SEPIT. In SEPIT, we utilize mixture of experts, and in Figure 3, we

      6      C ONCLUSION
534   In this work, we introduce SEPIT, a novel approach for general-purpose protein understanding.
535   SEPIT aims to enable LLMs to interpret both the sequence and structural information of proteins,
536   thus following instructions to generate specific understanding on protein properties and functions. To
537   achieve this, we integrate structure-aware enhancements into pre-trained pLM and connect them to
538   LLMs via a linear projector. The models are then trained in a two-stage instruction tuning pipeline
539   on protein instruction dataset we constructed which is the largest protein instruction dataset to date.
      Experimental results show that SEPIT significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art models.

       Learning with 3D Structural Information. Although pLMs pre-trained on protein sequences
       have been proven to be effective in numerous tasks, the protein structure is inherently a determinant
978
       of protein function (Anfinsen & Haber, 1961; Hartley, 1951). More effectively utilizing 3D structural
979
       information can help to understand proteins more comprehensively. To capture the impact of
980    geometric positioning and interaction relationships among residues on protein’s function, a class of
981    methods encoded 3D geometric information into rotation-invariant scalars, which was then processed
982    through graph neural networks (GNNs) for message passing (Wu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a).
983    For example, IEConv(Hermosilla et al., 2021) utilized a multi-graph to depict primary and secondary
984    structures through covalent and hydrogen bonds and represented the tertiary structure with the
985    spatial 3D coordinates of atoms. By blending intrinsic and extrinsic node distances and employing
986    hierarchical pooling, it effectively perceived all three structural levels of proteins. GearNet went
987    further by incorporating three types of directed edges (sequential edges, radius edges, and k-NN
988
       edges) into the graph, capturing information at various structural levels. On this basis, CDConv (Fan
       et al., 2023) innovatively utilized MLP to parameterize the kernel matrices, as opposed to employing
989
       distinct kernel matrices for varying edge types, which enabled a more flexible and efficient modeling
990
       of complex interactions between residues. Additionally, another class of methods sought to prevent
991    the loss of 3D structural information by incorporating 3D rigid transformations into the network
992    operations. This led to the development of geometric GNNs/Transformers characterized by SE(3)
993    invariance and equivariance (Liao & Smidt, 2022; Liao et al., 2024; Fuchs et al., 2020; Zhou et al.,
994    2023; Luo et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024). Representative examples of this approach, such as
995    GVP (Jing et al., 2021) and EvoFormer (Jumper et al., 2021), were utilized by ESM-IF (Hsu et al.,
996    2022), AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., 2021), respectively. Furthermore, to leverage information from
997    both evolutionary-scale protein sequences and the relatively limited protein structures, some other
998    methods (Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a) attempted to build a bridge between these two
999
       domains.

1246   As depicted in Figure 2, we extensively utilize group learning rates throughout the entire training
1247   pipeline of SEPIT. We tend to assign higher learning rates to randomly initialized parameters, while
1248   opting for lower learning rates for pre-trained parameters in order to mitigate forgetting, setting the
1249   ratio between lower and higher learning rates at 0.1. In Stage 0, we actually employ ESM2-650M (Lin
1250   et al., 2023) as the pLM and PubMedBert (Gu et al., 2021) as the text encoder to better encode
1251
       biomedical text. We set the number of Gaussian Basis Kernel to 128. In Stage 1, we choose the
       representation from the penultimate layer of the protein encoder as input to LLMs to minimize the
1252
       discrepancy between pre-training tasks and the current task. For the LLMs, we opt for TinyLlama-
1253
       1.1B (Zhang et al., 2024). In Stage 2, we continue most of the settings from Stage 1, while setting the
1254   number of experts to 4, with Top-1 expert being activated at a time. At this stage, our protein encoder
1255   is frozen. Regarding the hyper-parameter settings for SEPIT-TinyLlama-MoEs, we set higher learning
1256   rate to 5e−5 and trained for 5 epochs in Stage 0. In Stage 1, we set higher learning rate to 2e−5 and
1257   trained for 1 epoch. In Stage 2, we set higher learning rate to 5e−5 and trained for 1 epoch. For all
1258   stages, we trained on 32 Tesla V100 GPUs, with a batch size per GPU set to 4, employing a warm-up
1259   and linear decay learning rate scheduler, and set the warm-up ratio to 0.06. In all experiments, we
1260   employe AMP, Zero Optimizer (Rasley et al., 2020) based on AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019)
1261   and gradient checkpointing. For the hyper-parameter settings of other models, see Table 9. For all
1262
       MoE models, we implement them based on DeepSpeed-MoE, employing expert parallelism during
       the training process and setting the expert parallel size to 4.
