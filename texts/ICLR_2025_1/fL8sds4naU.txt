009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            It was once believed that mathematical capabilities in language models required
012            either large model scales or extensive math-related data pre-training. However, this
013            paper demonstrates that the small-scale LLaMA-2 7B model already possesses
014            strong mathematical potential. This is evidenced by its impressive scores of 97.6%
015            on GSM8K benchmark and 70% on MATH benchmark, achieved by selecting the
016
               oracle response from 1024 generations. Equipped GPT-4 Turbo as an additional
               verification, LLaMA-2 7B also achieves 91.8% accuracy on GSM8K benchmark.
017
               This indicates that the primary issue within current models is the difficulty in
018
               consistently eliciting the inherent mathematical capabilities. We find that scaling
019            up synthetic SFT data, which proves to be nearly as effective as real data, can
020            significantly enhance the reliability of generating correct answers. Surprisingly,
021            even with approximately one million samples, we observe no clear performance
022            saturation. And our method is more efficient with large data scale than previous
023            works. This approach achieves an accuracy of 82.4% on GSM8K and 40.1% on
024            MATH using LLaMA-2 7B model, surpassing GPT-3.5 Turbo. Our 70B model
025            even exceeds an early version of GPT-4 on MATH and out-of-domain Hungarian
026            National High School Math Exam. These results demonstrate our method signifi-
027
               cantly elicits the general mathematical capabilities of language models. Also, we
               provide insights into scaling behaviors across different reasoning complexities.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Mathematical capabilities have traditionally been viewed as so challenging that they are believed to
033   emerge in common language models only when these models reach a very large scale. For instance,
034   studies by Wei et al. (2022a;b) suggest that only models with size exceeding 50 billion parameters can
035   attain decent accuracy. This statement is proved by the observation that when testing small models on
036   mathematical reasoning benchmarks such as GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021) and MATH Hendrycks et al.
037   (2021), the accuracy is significantly low, with LLaMA-2 7B model achieving only 14.6% and 2.5%
038   respectively Luo et al. (2023). A natural strategy to equip smaller language models with mathematical
039
      abilities involves creating math-specific base models trained on hundreds of billions of math-related
      data Lewkowycz et al. (2022); Azerbayev et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2024) since it directly enrich the
040
      model’s repository of background knowledge. However, the accuracy of such models remains modest;
041
      for example, Llemma-7B Azerbayev et al. (2023) only achieves 36.4% on the GSM8K benchmark
042   and 18.0% on the MATH benchmark.
043
044   In this paper, we present a counterintuitive observation that general pre-trained language models
045
      of small sizes, such as LLaMA-2 with 7B parameters Touvron et al. (2023b), already possess
      strong intrinsic mathematical capabilities. To substantiate this point, we first assess the limits of
046
      mathematical reasoning capabilities in pre-trained models. We employ the standard 1-shot setting
047
      to prompt the LLaMA-2 7B base model, generating 1024 answers for each question with different
048   random seeds. Surprisingly, we find that 97.6% of GSM8K benchmark questions Cobbe et al. (2021)
049   and 70.0% of MATH benchmark questions Hendrycks et al. (2021) can be solved within the 1024
050   generated responses. It is worth noting that these accuracies have even outperformed those of the
051   GPT-4 model (92.0% on GSM8K and 42.5% on MATH) Achiam et al. (2023). The performance
052   of GPT-4 models are continuously being improved. The GPT-4 Turbo (1106) API has increased
053   accuracy to 94.8% on GSM8K and 64.5% on MATH. However, LLaMA-2 7B model using 1024
      generations still outperforms it. We further incorporate GPT-4 Turbo to verify the correctness of

      As a consequence, we turn to synthetic data, employing a high-performing language model, namely
096   GPT-4 Turbo, to produce synthetic math questions. Although synthetic math data has been utilized
097   in previous works Luo et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023), the challenge of generating
098   large quantities of high-quality synthetic data remains unresolved. We find that a straightforward
099   “brand-new” generation strategy, which prompts the GPT-4 Turbo to create a completely new question
100   based on preference ones and then applies a simple verifier (also GPT-4 Turbo based), has been
101   highly effective. The data collected using our approach exhibits significant gains over previous
102   methods under the same conditions. Specifically, as indicated in Table 1, the use of synthetically
103   generated math questions can achieve accuracy nearly on par with that of real questions, highlighting
104
      the potential of synthetic SFT math questions for the scaling purpose.

106   960K on GSM8K and from 7.5K to 480K on MATH, respectively. This data scaling shows excellent
107   scaling behavior, as drawn in Figure 1. Specifically, by simply scaling the SFT data, our model has

116   In addition to the strong results, we have also gleaned insights into the effectiveness of our approach:
117
118   • As the scale of SFT data increases, the model’s accuracy tends to plateau when utilizing 256
119     attempts; however, there is a marked increase using 1 response. This indicates that while the
120     model’s upper capability limit remains fairly constant, the performance gains are primarily due to
121     enhanced stability in generating correct answers.
122   • The accuracy of solving math problems follows a power law with respect to the number of chain-
123     of-thought (CoT) steps with different SFT data quantities. An expanded SFT dataset improves the
124     reliability of each reasoning step. Further increasing the proportion of training samples with longer
125     CoT steps through resampling can significantly improve the accuracy of difficult questions.
126   • Experiments on decontamination and out-of-domain benchmarks demonstrate that this method
127     does not suffer from information leakage.

144   When assessing the correctness of responses, the usual practice is to employ automated evaluation

171   3    S CALING SFT DATA USING S YNTHETIC M ATH Q UESTIONS
172
173   In this section, we first demonstrate that scaling up the limited real SFT data can significantly alleviate
174   the ranking issue. We also observe that the accuracy has not yet plateaued when using the full
175   available GSM8K and MATH training data. We consider further scaling up SFT data using synthetic
176
      math questions. To this aim, we introduce a straight-forward method for synthetic data generation
      utilizing the GPT-4 Turbo API. The synthetic data proves to be as effective as real math questions.
177
      Consequently, we boldly scale the synthetic SFT data to 960K on GSM8K and 480K on MATH,
178
      respectively, resulting in excellent scaling behavior, and reach state-of-the-art accuracy.
179
180
      Scaling using Real Math Questions We begin by examining the scaling behavior of real math
181   questions across the entire GSM8K and MATH training sets. As indicated in Table 1, we observe a
182   consistent accuracy improvement, increasing from 26.7% to 50.2% on GSM8K, and from 4.2% to
183   8.4% on MATH, with no signs of saturation.
184
185   Synthetic SFT Data Generation Since the real data has been exhausted, we contemplate further
186   scaling up SFT data using synthetic math questions. We introduce a straightforward three-step
187   approach with the assistance of the GPT-4 Turbo API. The prompts are shown in Appendix A.
188
189   • Step 1. Generate a new math question. We request the GPT-4 Turbo API to generate a brand-new
190     question using a reference math question as a starting point. To improve the validity of the new
191
        questions, we incorporate three rules into the prompt: Firstly, the new question must obey common
        knowledge; secondly, it should be solvable independently of the original question; and thirdly, it
192
        must not include any answer responses.
193
194   • Step 2. Verify the question. We further enhance the quality of the generated questions by validating
195
        and refining them through attempted solutions. By integrating solving and verification steps into a
        single prompt, we have found that this approach consistently elevates the validity of questions.
196
197   • Step 3. Generate chain-of-thought (CoT) answers. We request GPT-4 Turbo to produce a chain-of-
198     thought (CoT) answer response for each newly generated question. Besides, we have set specific
199     formatting requirements for answers tailored to various target datasets.
200
201   Comparison of Synthetic SFT Data versus Real Data To assess the quality of the synthetically
202
      generated math questions, we evaluate their effectiveness against real questions from the GSM8K
      and MATH training sets, utilizing a LLaMA-2 7B model, as detailed in Table 1. The results indicate
203
      that the synthetic math questions are nearly as effective as the real ones. We also explore various
204
      other synthetic methods as proposed in previous works Xu et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); An et al.
205   (2023). These methods also prove to be effective, though marginally less so than our approach, as
206   illustrated in Figure 6.
207
208   Scaling to about a Million SFT Math Data Considering the effectiveness of the synthetic approach,
209   we substantially increase the scale of the SFT data for both GSM8K and MATH problems, to 960K
210   and 480K, respectively. Figure 1 presents the main results utilizing various sizes of the LLaMA-2
211   series. The straightforward scaling strategy yields state-of-the-art accuracy. It is also worth noting
212   that the accuracy has not yet reached its peak.
213
214   Data Contamination Test We evaluate benchmark leakage for the GSM8K dataset using 10-gram
215   feature cosine similarity. Three questions from the test set have a similarity score above 0.35 with
      both the original and synthetic training sets. The maximum similarity in each of the two scenarios

223   4.1     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
224
      In data synthesis, we utilize the GPT-4 Turbo API, setting the temperature to 1.0 for both question and
225
      answer generation. For supervised fine-tuning, we employ the Adam optimizer with a cosine learning
226
      rate schedule spanning a total of 3 epochs of training. The maximum learning rate is set to 2e-5
227   (except that 2e-6 for the Mistral-7b model) and there is a 4% linear warm-up. The maximum token
228   length is set 2048, and the Vicuna-v1.1 Zheng et al. (2023) system prompt is used. All experiments
229   are conducted on 8×Nvidia H100 GPUs. Our biggest experiment, involving a 70B model and 1440K
230   data, takes 2800 H100 GPU hours. For generation, we use the same system prompt as used in SFT
231   and set the maximum sequence length to 2048. The vLLM Kwon et al. (2023) is used in answer
232   generation.
233   We conduct experiments on GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021), MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021), SVAMP Pa-
234   tel et al. (2021), ASDiv Miao et al. (2021) and Hungarian National High School Math Exam xAI
235   (2023) to evaluate the efficacy of the proposed method. It is worth noting that the score of Hungarian
236   National High School Math Exam is judged by human, while other benchmarks are evaluated using
237   automatic scripts, similar to previous works Luo et al. (2023); Gou et al. (2023).

343   Pass@256 v.s. PassRatio@256 To deepen the understanding behind the performance improvements,
344   we track Pass@N metric and PassRatio@N metric under different data size. The results are shown in
345   Figure 4. With very limited synthetic data (e.g. 7.5K samples), the Xwin-Math-70B model already
346
      has very high Pass@256, indicating the strong ability to generate correct answers through multiple
      attempts. Meanwhile, the Pass@256 metric only change slightly with increasing the amount of used
347
      data. In contrast, PassRatio@256, which reflects the stability to generate correct answer, increases
348
      significantly with the amount of synthetic data, and its growth trend is similar to that of Pass@1. This
349   result confirms our hypothesize that the performance improvements is mainly caused by the better
350   stability in answer generation rather than stronger ability to answer the question.

      Estimated Stepwise Reasoning Accuracy Because of the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) are adopted
      in inference, the process of answer mathematical problems is completed by a multi-step reasoning
353
      process. Therefore, we hypothesize that the increase in final answer accuracy can be interpreted by
354
      the improvement in stepwise reasoning accuracy. Based on this assumption, if one question can be
355   theoretically answered by s reasoning steps in CoT, the final answer accuracy can be approximate by
356   the power function of the stepwise reasoning accuracy:
357
358                                             Accfinal = Accsstep                                       (3)
359
360   With this equation, step accuracy can be estimated from the final answer accuracy. We experiment on
361   GSM8K. For each question in the test set, we use Xwin-Math 7B to generate 256 responses and use
362   the number of steps in the CoT annotations as the theoretical CoT steps. We draw the mean accuracy
363   and the fitted curve based on Equation. 3 in Figure 5 with different amount of synthetic data. The
364   solid line is fitted using all seven points and Table 4 shows the estimated stepwise accuracy when
365   using different amounts of data using all data points, and it can be seen that the stepwise accuracy
366   improve significantly with more data.
367   However, when we fit based on Equation. 3 with the first four points, as shown in dashed lines, we
368   find that the latter three points are significantly below the curve. We believe this phenomenon may
369   be related to the smaller proportion of more complex problems in the training data. Therefore, we
370   resample the 960K synthetic data according to the number of sentences in CoT solution. As can be
371   seen from Figure 5 (right), when the proportion of complex problems is increased, the accuracy for
372   simpler problems remains virtually unchanged, but the accuracy for more complex problems can
373   be significantly improved. Moreover, the utilization of data resampling can increase the model’s
374
      PassRatio@256 from 71.1 to 72.8. This experimental result provides new insights into data selection
      for mathematical reasoning tasks.
375
376   In addition, we further use the GPT-4 Turbo to find the position where the first step in our answer is
377   wrong and normalized that position by the total number of steps in each answer. As the estimated
      stepwise accuracy gets higher, the normalized first error position is postponed.

407   Comparison with Other Data Synthetic Methods We compare our approach with following
408   common used data synthetic methods in WizardMath Luo et al. (2023), MuggleMath Li et al. (2023)
409   and MetaMath Yu et al. (2023):
410   1) Add Constraint. Adding one more constrain while keeping others unchanged. 2) Change Numbers.
411   Changing the numbers while keeping the context intact. 3) Change Background. Changing the back-
412   ground while keeping others the same. 4) The Combination of Changing Numbers and Background.
413   A hybrid approach that combines changing both numbers and background. 5) MetaMath Approach.
414   The synthetic methods proposed in MetaMath Yu et al. (2023), including answer augmentation,
415   rephrasing question, self-verification question and FOBAR question. We follow their implementation
416   but use GPT-4 Turbo to generate response data using their released questions.
417   The experimental results in the Figure 6 show that when the data size is relatively small, e.g., 7.5k and
418   30k samples, the performance gap between the different methods is negligible. However, as the data
419   size increases, our method and the method with added constraints show stronger performance. This
420   suggests that the choice of data synthetic strategy becomes more critical as the data size increases,
421   and that our method can scale the data more efficiently, thus improving the performance.

423   the diversity gain metric used by MetaMath Yu et al. (2023). The results in Table 7 show that when
424   the scale of synthetic data continues to increase, our data always maintains a relatively high diversity.

461
462   Large Language Models Large language models Brown et al. (2020); Achiam et al. (2023);
463
      Touvron et al. (2023a;b) have made significant achievements, with impressive performance on a wide
      range of tasks. Currently, closed-source large language models, represented by GPT Brown et al.
464
      (2020); Achiam et al. (2023), Gemini Team et al. (2023), Grok xAI (2023), and Claude-2 Anthropic
465
      (2023), are the most advanced models in terms of performance. However, open-source models,
466   represented by LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023a), LLaMA-2 Touvron et al. (2023b) and Mixtral Jiang
467   et al. (2024), have also progressed rapidly, and have even shown competitive performance with
468   the closed-source models on some tasks. Our work, which aims to improve the performance of
469   open-source LLMs on mathematical tasks by fine-tuning them on synthetic data.
470
471   Reasoning Framework for Improving Mathematical Capability Chain-of-thoughts Wei et al.
472   (2022b) encourages the LLMs perform multi-step reasoning by specific designed prompts and can
473   improve reasoning performance. Based on this work, many subsequent works suggesting further
474   improvements Fu et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022); Kojima et al. (2022). The above works focus
475
      primarily on how to improve performance through better prompt design or inference strategies without
      fine-tuning the model, whereas our work focuses on how to improve the model itself, and thus these
476
      approaches are complementary to ours.
477
478
      Fine-tuned LLM for Math Another sort of works Lightman et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023);
479
      Azerbayev et al. (2023); Yue et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023); An et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Gou
480   et al. (2023) try to improve performance directly by training the model on mathematical data. A
481   direct way is to use fine-tuning to improve models. One widely used method is to use synthetic data,
482   which is very close to our approach: MetaMath Yu et al. (2023) presents to bootstrap questions to
483   augment data. LeMA An et al. (2023) collects mistake-correction data pairs by using GPT-4 as a
484   corrector. And MuggleMath Li et al. (2023) augments the GSM8K dataset by incorporating GPT-4
485   with a series of pre-defined operations. Compared to these synthetic data based efforts, our data
      synthetic method is much simpler and more scalable.

      SFT Data Scaling Recently, some research efforts have focused on the data scale for supervised
487   fine-tuning. For instance, LIMA Zhou et al. (2023) mentions that fine-tuning with 1,000 high-quality
488   instructions can yield impressive results in various general tasks. Other studies have indicated that
489   performance scales with data size in mathematical and coding tasks Dong et al. (2023). Recent
490   work Bi et al. (2024) even uses 1.5 million data for instruct fine-tuning to obtain top performance.
491   However, the intrinsic reasons behind this scaling effect have not been thoroughly investigated.

      6   C ONCLUSION
494
495   This study reveals that common 7B language models, such as LLaMA-2 7B, already exhibit strong
496   mathematical capabilities, challenging the previous belief. By significantly scaling up SFT data,
497   we have markedly improved the stability of the model’s mathematical problem-solving skills. Our
498   methodology has enabled the Xwin-Math models to reach performance levels comparable to, and in
499   some instances surpassing, those of their larger counterparts. In out-of-domain evaluation, our model
500
      also surpassed the performance of GPT-4, indicating that our methodology enhances the general
      mathematical reasoning capabilities. Our analysis also indicates that the enhancements are primarily
501
      attributable to heightened accuracy in reasoning and a extra resampling of training data can improve
502
      the accuracy of harder questions. Our research contributes valuable insights into the mathematical
503   capabilities of large language models.

506   7   L IMITATIONS AND S OCIAL I MPACT
507
508   While our work demonstrates impressive performance in mathematics reasoning, generating synthetic
509   data incurs significant computational expenses. Although the proposed method may have the potential
510   to generalize to other capabilities, the computational costs increase linearly as the amount of data
511
      scales up. Meanwhile, since our method fundamentally relies on supervised fine-tuning, the associated
      training costs become significant when scaling up. Using OpenAI’s API to synthesize data with
512
      GPT-4 Turbo costs approximately $100,000. Although this expense could decrease as API service
513
      continues to become cheaper, this remains a significant expenditure for researchers. On the other
514   hand, our data scaling method has the potential to introduce noise during the generation of new
515   data, which could constrain the effectiveness of the method depending on the model employed for
516   data generation. Furthermore, the utilization of pre-trained language models in this work may entail
517   certain ethical risks similar to those associated with other deep learning models, such as biases and
518   privacy violations.
