009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the vast applications and rapid development of deep learning (DL), under-
012            standing and improving the generalization ability of deep neural networks (DNNs)
013            remains a fundamental challenge. To tackle this challenge, in this paper, we first
014            establish a novel bias-variance decomposition framework to analyze the general-
015            ization error of DNNs. Based on our new generalization error formula, we then
016            present a new form of DL dubbed generalization error minimized (GEM) DL by
017            jointly minimizing the conventional optimization target and an analytical proxy
018
               for the generalization error. Extensive experimental results show that in com-
               parison with DNNs trained within the standard DL, GEM DNNs have smaller
019
               generalization errors and better generalization ability, thereby improving DNN
020
               prediction accuracy. Notably, GEM DL can increase prediction accuracy by as
021            much as 13.19% on ImageNet in the presence of data distribution shift between
022            training and testing.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In the past decade, deep neural networks (DNNs) have demonstrated impressive success in a wide
028   range of applications. In spite of this, the overfitting problem remains prevalent and significant,
029   greatly affecting the generalization performance of DNNs. Broadly speaking, overfitting is a phe-
030   nomenon that DNNs perform good or even perfect on training data, but much worse on new, unseen
031   data. In other words, DNNs that suffer from overfitting have poor generalization ability.
032   In the literature, the overfitting issue of DNNs has been extensively studied. Theoretical frame-
033   works, such as bias-variance tradeoff (Geman et al., 1992) and generalization bounds (Kawaguchi
034   et al., 2017) shed some light on the generalization behavior of DNNs and provide practitioners with
035   qualitative guidance to train DNNs with better generalization. However, it’s rare or difficult for
036   them to be directly applied in learning algorithms to prevent overfitting. On the other hand, there are
037   also a myriad of empirical works that manage to reduce overfitting and train DNNs with improved
038   generalization performance. These empirical works, in general, lack strong theoretical foundations,
039
      resulting in limited universality and explainability. To overcome these drawbacks, the purpose of
      this paper is to bridge the gap between these two lines of research.
040
041   To begin with, we define the generalization error of a learned DNN as the expectation of the squared
042   difference between training performance and testing performance of the DNN. Using this definition,
043   we then establish a novel bias-variance decomposition framework within which the generalization
044   error of the learned DNN can be decomposed into the sum of three terms: (1) the expectation of
045
      conditional testing variance, (2) the expectation of conditional training variance, and (3) the expec-
      tation of bias between training and testing. In parallel, we also define the conditional generalization
046
      error of a learned DNN given its weight vector, which can be decomposed similarly into the sum
047
      of conditional testing variance, conditional training variance, and bias between training and testing.
048   The decomposition formula has clear implications on how to design an effective learning algo-
049   rithm. It suggests that to achieve a small generalization error, we should search for a model that
050   minimizes these three terms jointly with the conventional training objective such as the empirical
051   negative log-likelihood risk in the case of classification. Among the three terms, it is difficult to
052   represent the conditional training variance analytically. To make such a joint optimization prob-
053   lem tractable, we further propose analytical proxies for the generalization error and the conditional
      generalization error. Upper bound the expectation of conditional training variance by the uncondi-

      tional training variance. By demonstrating empirically that the unconditional training variance is
055   negligible in comparison with the generalization error, it turns out that the analytical proxies are
056   close approximations to the generalization error and the conditional generalization error. Based on
057   the analytical proxy of the conditional generalization error, we then modify the conventional deep
058   learning (DL) by jointly optimizing the conventional training loss and the analytical proxy, yielding

066   DL is demonstrated in few-shot and imbalanced data scenarios as well.
067
068   The major contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
069

      2    R ELATED W ORK
084
085
      Extensive efforts have been made in recent years to understand generalization in DL. Among them
086   are a body of principled mathematical works on deriving generalization bounds (Kawaguchi et al.,
087   2017; Xu & Raginsky, 2017; Jakubovitz et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2019; Neu et al., 2021), which
088   upper bound the population risk by terms related to the training data and the model’s properties.
089   While significant progress has been made, many of these established bounds remain loose or even
090   vacuous, offering limited insight into the remarkable generalization abilities of neural networks
091   observed in practice (Gastpar et al., 2023). Moreover, although generalization bounds are primarily
092   developed to explain generalization behavior and provide generalization guarantees, they often fall
093   short in being applied to improve generalization in DL empirically.
094   Also related to this above line of theoretical works are those works on bias-variance tradeoff (Geman
095   et al., 1992; Domingos, 2000). It is well known that the expected error on an unseen test sample can
096   be decomposed into the bias, which measures the discrepancy between the model class and the data
097   distribution, and the variance, which measures the variability of the model given the randomness
098   involved in training. Conventional wisdom suggests that as a model’s complexity—often measured
099   by the number of its parameters—increases, its variance increases while its bias decreases (Geman
100
      et al., 1992). This belief has long influenced model selection practices, advocating for a model that
      is neither too simple nor too complex in order to strike the optimal balance between bias and vari-
101
      ance, thereby minimizing generalization error. However, this view has been called into question by
102
      numerous recent works (Zhang et al., 2017a; Novak et al., 2018; Neal et al., 2018; Belkin et al.,
103   2019; Yang et al., 2020), which present evidence that modern neural networks often benefit from
104   increased capacity, contradicting the classical bias-variance tradeoff. Controversy aside, like gener-
105   alization bounds, the classical bias-variance decomposition primarily serves as theoretical guidance
106

      for model selection but does not directly contribute to training a given DNN model with reduced

      generalization error.

111   In contrast, in this paper, we analyze, for any given learned DNN, its generalization error and con-
112   ditional generalization error defined as the unconditional and conditional expectation of the squared
113
      difference between training performance and testing performance of that DNN. We establish a new
      form of bias-variance decomposition, which can be applied directly to train a given DNN model

      conventional training loss and the analytical proxy for the conditional generalization error. Its prac-
116   ticality is similar to that of existing empirical methods aimed at reducing overfitting and improving
117   generalization of DNNs, including weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1991), early stopping (Morgan
118   & Bourlard, 1989), dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016), con-
119   fidence penalty (Pereyra et al., 2017), and data augmentation such as Cutout (DeVries & Taylor,
120   2017), Mixup (Zhang et al., 2017b) and Cutmix (Yun et al., 2019). Compared to these empirical
121   methods, our approach is grounded in solid mathematical foundations, as it directly leverages the
122   new bias-variance decomposition formula. More importantly, our method is orthogonal to existing
123   techniques in modern DL designed to improve DNN generalization, as it can further enhance DNN
124
      performance when combined with those methods.

458   frequent class, is used to measure the degree of imbalance. Clearly, a smaller imbalance factor stands
459   for higher degree of imbalance. In our evaluation, we consider four different imbalance factors: 0.01,

464   shift cannot be characterized by any common signal processing over the data, and therefore the
465   imbalance data scenario doesn’t fall into either Case 1 or Case 2. Nevertheless, we still apply GEM
466
      in Case 1 for this task.

471   the gain is becoming less and less with increased imbalance which induces increased distribution
472

      The paper studies generalization error in DL and provides a novel learning algorithm to reduce it. A
534   novel bias-variance decomposition is established to analyze the generalization error between train-
535   ing and testing, based on which we propose a new learning framework dubbed GEM by jointly
536   minimizing the conventional training loss and an analytical proxy for the conditional generalization
537   error. The proposed method is applied to classification and experimentally evaluated on popular im-
538   age classification datasets, where it consistently and significantly mitigates overfitting and improves
539   generalization performance of DNNs across a variety of scenarios. Overall, GEM is a simple yet
      effective approach to improving generalization, backed by solid theoretical support.

      Algorithm 1 Pseudo-code of GEM in Case 1 in a Pytorch-like style.
732
      # z: DNN output logits
733   # y: the ground truth label
734   # λ: the weight for the second moment
735   # β: the weight for the squared mean
736   # α: the amount of label smoothing
737
      ce_ls = F.cross_entropy(z, y, label_smoothing=α)
738   ce = F.cross_entropy(z, y, reduction=’none’)
739   ce_mean = torch.mean(ce)
740   second_moment = torch.mean(ce**2)
741   squared_mean = ce_mean**2
742
      # GEM
743   gem_loss = ce_ls + λ * second_moment + β * squared_mean

      A.3   G UIDANCE FOR H YPERPARAMETERS S ELECTION
748
      Qualitatively speaking, λ in LGEM should not be too large. At the beginning of training, DNNs
749
      are randomly initialized, so the loss L(fθ (x), y) = − log pθ (y|x) is generally quite large, which is
750   much greater than 1. Therefore, further squaring it will result in a even larger value. Consequently,
751   if λ is not small enough, DNNs will suffer from slow convergence or even gradient explosion at
752   the beginning of training. As for β, it’s suggested by (16) and 17 that β should not be less than
753   λ. Empirically, selecting β to be about an order of magnitude greater than λ often gives good
754   results. Actually, as long as λ and β are appropriately small to avoid slow convergence and gradient
755   explosion at the beginning of training, and β is not less than λ, the experimental results are generally
      not sensitive to hyperparameters tuning.

770   A.5    M ORE B ENCHMARK C OMPARISON IN C ASE 1
771
      Dropout and confidence penalty are two popular regularization techniques used to improve DNN
772
      generalization performance. In this section, we compare GEM in Case 1 with these two benchmarks
773
      empirically.
774
775   It’s observed that the overconfidence of a DNN is a sign of overfitting and poor generalization
776   (Szegedy et al., 2016). Thus, Pereyra et al. (2017) proposed to penalize confident predictions to
777
      improve DNN generalization performance. Since a confident prediction generally corresponds to
      pθ (·|X)) with low entropy, they enforce confidence penalty (CP) by introducing a negative entropy
778
      regularizer into the learning objective, which is formulated as
779
                                  LCP (θ) = E[− log pθ (Y |X) − ηH(pθ (·|X))],                          (24)
780
781   where H(·) is the entropy of a probability distribution, and η controls the strength of the confidence
782
      penalty.
783   Under the experimental setting for CIFAR-100 reported in Section 5, we perform a grid search for η
784   over {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5} following the original paper and choose η = 1 as the optimal weight.
785   The comparison between CP and GEM in Case 1 on CIFAR-100 is presented in Table 4. Clearly,

      Spiral dataset is a classical synthetic dataset for binary classification. In order to analyze the differ-

853   spiral dataset and visualize the resulting decision boundaries.
854
855
      We first create a spiral dataset with 2 classes, each consisting of 500 samples. Then, we split the
      dataset into training and testing sets with 4:1 ratio. A five-layer fully connected neural network,
856
      comprising four hidden layers each with a width of 1024, is constructed as the model to be trained.
857
      An Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) with learning rate 0.001 is used to train the model. When the
858   model is trained using ERM, the test accuracy is 96.5%, and the decision boundary is visualized in
859   Fig. 6(a). In the case of GEM, the test accuracy reaches 98.5%, and the learned decision boundary
860   is visualized in Fig. 6(b). From the plots, we can clearly see that the decision boundary learned by
861   ERM has some zigzag and dent (highlighted by red circles), while the decision boundary learned
862   by GEM is much smoother and demonstrates a desirable central symmetry pattern. This clearly
863   indicates that the model trained by GEM successfully captures the true data distribution, while the
      one trained using ERM overfits the noise.
