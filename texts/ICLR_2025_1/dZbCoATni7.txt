011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in robotic learning have enabled robots to perform a wide
014            range of tasks. However, generalizing policies from training environments to de-
015            ployment environments remains a major challenge, and improving these policies
016            by collecting and annotating demonstrations in target environments is both costly
017
               and time-consuming. To address this issue, we propose Embodied Scene Cloning,
               a novel visual-prompt-based framework that generates visual-aligned trajectories
018
               from existing data by leveraging visual cues from the specific deployment envi-
019
               ronment. This approach minimizes the impact of environmental discrepancies
020            on policy performance. Unlike traditional embodied augmentation methods that
021            rely on text prompts, we propose to "clone" source demonstrations into the target
022            environment and edit it with visual prompt to effectively improve the generalization
023            ability on specific embodied scene. Experimental results demonstrate that samples
024            generated by Embodied Scene Cloning significantly enhance the generalization
025            ability of policies in the target deployment environments, representing a meaningful
026            advancement in embodied data augmentation.

      Despite recent advancements in developing multi-task generalist policies for robot learning, their
055   generalization capabilities remain far from practical application. Most existing policies are designed
056   for specific embodied scene, where the target tasks and environments closely resemble—or are
057   identical to—those seen during training. As illustrated in solution 1 in Fig.1, adopting one of the
058   current advanced embodied policies, faces a significant performance drop of about 30%—from 96.4%
059   to 69.4% —when deployed in novel embodied scene with unseen foreground and background.
060   Generalizing to unseen objects or environments typically requires additional data collection and
061   model fine-tuning. This falls short of the expected performance, especially considering that the
062   policies, often derived from large-scale vision-language models (VLMs) (Liu et al., 2024; Padalkar
063
      et al., 2023), are inherently few-shot or even zero-shot learners. One key factor contributing to these
      limited generalizations is the insufficient amount of data in the robotics domain. However, scaling
064
      up robot learning datasets is particularly challenging due to the labor-intensive and time-consuming
065
      nature of real-world data collection. For example, assembling the RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2022) dataset
066   took 17 months and 13 robots to gather just 130,000 demonstrations.
067
068   To address generalization challenges, researchers have turned to synthetic methodology. With the
069
      rapid advancement of diffusion-based image synthesis techniques, it has become possible to generate
      high-fidelity visual content from language instructions. In embodied scenarios, methods such as
070
      ROSIE(Yu et al., 2023) , GenAug(Chen et al., 2023) and GreenAug(Teoh et al., 2024) have been
071
      developed to scale robotic learning with diverse synthetic samples, demonstrating that synthetic data
072   can be highly effective in building generalizable policies.
073
074   However, when the goal is to develop a policy for a specific new embodied scene, we could develop
075
      a more effective and efficient approach: Embodied Scene Cloning. As shown in solution 3 in Fig.
      1, existing generative data augmentation methods primarily aim to create diverse samples based on
076
      text prompts. However, these methods often struggle to fully capture the detailed characteristics of
077
      the deployment environment, such as the detailed textures of specific foreground objects or exact
078   color distribution of specific background. In contrast, embodied scene cloning depicted in solution 4
079   in Fig. 1, focuses on replicating precise visual samples of the deployment environment, allowing
080   for a more accurate representation of real-world conditions during deployment. Consequently, the
081   synthetic samples are highly effective, closely resembling the deployment environment data, making
082   them as useful as actual data collected from the deployment scenarios. For instance, Embodied
083   Scene Cloning outperformed GreenAug (Teoh et al., 2024), a state-of-the-art text-based generative
084   augmentation method, by increasing the embodied policy’s evaluation success rate from 69.4% to
085   84.0%, representing a 6.6% improvement beyond GreenAug’s 77.4%.
086   The key feature of embodied scene cloning is its ability to enable visual instruction. To ensure that the
087   synthetic data closely mirrors real-world conditions, we developed a novel image editing framework
088   that offers precise control over the visual appearance of generated content. This is accomplished
089   by incorporating external visual objects as conditions during the editing process, along with control
090   mechanisms for specifying the position and shape of these objects. Our visual-instruction guided
091   image editing framework operates in two stages: an initial stage that encodes the input image while
092
      preserving key non-edited regions, and a generation stage that focuses on the edited areas. Through a
      dynamic blending mechanism, the framework ensures smooth transitions between edited and non-
093
      edited regions, maintaining the integrity of the original scene while adapting to new visual elements.
094
      This allows for the generation of high-fidelity samples that are not only visually accurate but also
095   consistent with the target deployment environment.
096
097   We explore how embodied scene cloning can aid in developing generalizable policies in both repro-
098
      ducible simulation environments and practical real-world settings. To demonstrate the effectiveness of
      our approach, we compare embodied scene cloning with state-of-the-art generative data augmentation
099
      methods. Empirical results show that our customized samples are significantly more effective when
100
      targeting specific new embodied scene. In summary, our key contributions are:
101
102          • We propose a novel data synthesis method called Embodied Scene Cloning, designed to
103            effectively address the generalization challenge in specific novel embodied scene. This
104            approach is distinguished by its visual instruction capability, allowing precise control over
105            both the generated objects and the background environments.
106          • Experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements in various language-
107            conditioned visuomotor policies when using our method, showcasing its clear advantages
               over existing generative augmentation approaches.

113   Recent advancements in language-conditioned embodied policies have greatly enhanced robots’ abil-
114   ity to execute complex tasks by interpreting natural language commands and visual cues(Driess et al.,
115   2023; Shah et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Radosavovic et al., 2023). Models like CLIPort(Shridhar
116   et al., 2022) and PerAct(Shridhar et al., 2023), leveraging CLIP(Radford et al., 2021), have shown
117   strong performance in robotic manipulation. More recent approaches, such as Hiveformer(Guhur
118   et al., 2023), use transformers to jointly model language, visual history, and multiple views, further
119
      improving task completion. Large Language Models (LLMs), like those in SayCan(Ahn et al., 2022)
      and RT-2(Brohan et al., 2023), translate high-level instructions into executable actions, generalizing
120
      across diverse tasks through multimodal fine-tuning. However, generalization remains challenging,
121
      with models often struggling in novel environments or with unseen tasks and configurations. Pre-
122   trained models like RoboFlamingo(Li et al., 2023) and RT-2 improve generalization by leveraging
123   large datasets but still face limitations in transferring knowledge to new scenarios.

      2.2   G ENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION IN E MBODIED I NTELLIGENCE .
127
128
      Generative models, particularly diffusion models, have advanced robotic learning by enabling syn-
129
      thetic augmentation, reducing reliance on real-world data collection. Early methods like ROSIE(Yu
130   et al., 2023) and GenAug(Chen et al., 2023) used text-to-image models for dataset augmentation.
131   ROSIE employed diffusion-based inpainting to integrate unseen objects and backgrounds, improving
132   generalization. GenAug introduced depth-guided visual diversity, enhancing robustness to new
133   environments. Recent approaches, such as CACTI(Mandi et al.) and GreenAug(Teoh et al., 2024),
134   adopted alternative strategies. CACTI combined generative models with expert data for task-specific
135   augmentations like kitchen object manipulation. GreenAug employs chroma keying to replace
136   green-screen backgrounds with diverse textures and uses generative inpainting in GreenAug-Gen
137   to add realistic scenes like kitchens or living rooms, enhancing visual generalization with a simpler,
138
      effective approach. Our visual-prompt-based framework builds on these advancements, focusing on
      precise adaptation to specific deployment environments, achieving consistent generalization beyond
139
      general scene augmentation methods.

      2.3   V ISUAL P ROMPTING IN V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND G ENERATION .
144
145   Visual prompting has advanced across domains, with LLAVA(Liu et al., 2024) integrating visual
146   instruction tuning into language models. In robotics, RT-2(Brohan et al., 2023) leveraged visual
147
      prompts for task execution, and PaLM-E(Driess et al., 2023) extended these capabilities in embodied
      agents, VIMA(Jiang et al., 2022) further advanced the field by introducing multimodal prompting,
148
      which combines textual and visual tokens to specify tasks for generalist robotic manipulation. While
149
      effective, these approaches do not address the challenges of embodied scene transfer beyond semantic
150   understanding. Generative models have explored controlled generation using visual prompts. Control-
151   Net(Zhang et al., 2023) introduced structural conditioning with visual guidance, IP-Adapter(Ye et al.,
152   2023) improved prompt integration, and MS-Diffusion(Wang et al., 2024) enabled layout-preserving
153   multi-content synthesis. However, these methods focus on general image generation, overlooking the
154   unique needs of embodied scenes.
155
      Our method targets three key requirements for embodied scene cloning: (1) precise control of visual
156   prompt placement via Grounding-Resampler and cross-attention, (2) semantic consistency in non-
157   edited regions using Progressive Masked Fusion to prevent artifacts while preserving task-relevant
158   features, and (3) trajectory feasibility through depth-consistent editing with ControlNet, ensuring
159   spatial relationships for valid manipulation sequences. This design enables reliable policy transfer
160   by maintaining visual adaptation, scene consistency, and physical plausibility. For instance, when
161   transferring a grasping task, our method ensures correct object placement, preserves scene context,
      and maintains valid interaction trajectories—capabilities beyond existing approaches.

      2.4   I MAGE TRANSLATION FOR ROBOTICS
163
164
      Recent advancements in image translation for robotics have utilized both GAN-based and diffusion-
165   based methods to address the sim-to-real gap. GAN-based approaches like RL-CycleGAN(Rao
166   et al., 2020) adapted CycleGAN(Chu et al., 2017) with scene consistency losses, ensuring that
167   simulated images retain key semantic and spatial features critical for downstream tasks during domain
168   adaptation. RetinaGAN(Ho et al., 2021) extended this concept by introducing object-detection
169   consistency during GAN training, enforcing invariance in object features between simulated and
170   translated images to ensure reliable domain alignment. Diffusion-based methods, in contrast, focus on
171   precise control over image generation. ALDM-Grasping(Li et al., 2024) employed a layout-to-image
172   diffusion process, enabling photorealistic transformations while preserving spatial layout and object
173
      relationships critical for robotic manipulation. Similarly, LucidSim(Yu et al., 2024) utilized depth-
      conditioned ControlNet to achieve geometrically consistent scene generation, effectively bridging the
174
      sim-to-real gap for diverse and dynamic environments.
175
176   Unlike these approaches, which primarily target general scene adaptation, our Embodied Scene
177   Cloning method focuses on deployment-specific scene transfer. By leveraging visual prompts from
178   specific deployment environment, it enables precise and efficient adaptation to target environments
179
      while maintaining spatial and semantic consistency. This tailored approach allows for more effective
      scene transfer, addressing challenges in environment-specific deployment.

      3.1   OVERVIEW OF E MBODIED S CENE C LONING
217
218   Unlike traditional augmentation methods that rely on text prompt cues, Embodied Scene Cloning
219   focuses on replicating specific deployment environments by incorporating visual cues from the
220   scene. This approach enables the reuse of existing trajectories, improving the generalization of
221   embodied strategies while achieving more efficient training-free data augmentation. The framework
222   of Embodied Scene Cloning is illustrated in Fig.2. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the process begins by
223   generating the conditional inputs for the subsequent generation stage. Based on the input image
224
      and target instructions, we extract the required depth map, segmentation map of the target object,
      and its coordinate location. These conditional inputs are then processed using ControlNet(Zhang
225
      et al., 2023) and GroundingResampler(Wang et al., 2024), deriving the extraction of refined depth
226
      embedding and grounded visual prompt embedding. In the next phase, the editing process is divided
227   into two key stages: Inversion and Denoising. Both stages employ a subject-conditioned denoising
228   U-Net(Wang et al., 2024) as the backbone, with the refined depth embedding and grounded visual
229   prompt embedding as condition.
230
231   3.2   C ONDITIONAL F EATURE E XTRACTION
232
233   The conditional inputs required for our editing framework include depth maps, edges, and segmen-
234   tation maps. To extract the depth features, we use DepthAnythingV2(Yang et al., 2024), while
235   Grounded-SAM2(Liu et al., 2023) is employed to generate the bounding boxes and segmentation
236
      maps of objects based on locations derived from the text prompts. In the conditional feature pro-
      cessing stage, the visual prompt, text prompt, and the bounding box features of the target object
237
      are processed through the CLIP image feature processor(Radford et al., 2021), CLIP text feature
238
      processor(Radford et al., 2021), and Fourier encoding, respectively. As a result, we obtain the visual
239   prompt embedding fv , the text prompt embedding ft , and the grounding embedding fg . Additionally,
240   the text prompt embedding and the grounding embedding are used to initialize a query embedding
241   fq , which later interacts with the visual-prompt embedding to inject the coresponding semantic and
242   location information. Next, these embeddings are aggregated using the Grounding-Resampler(Wang
243   et al., 2024) to fuse three types of feature and output the grounded visual-prompt embedding. The
244   attention mechanism within the Resampler is described by the following equation:
245

252   3.3   V ISUAL -P ROMPT G UIDED I MAGE E DITING WITH P ROGRESSIVE M ASK F USION
253
254   We propose a visual-prompt-guided image editing model that explicitly divides the editing process
255
      into two parts: the reconstruction of non-editing regions and the generation of the editing regions.
      Specifically, this process is carried out in two stages: the inversion stage and the denoising stage.
256
      In the inversion stage, the original image is mapped into the latent diffusion space, and the states
257
      at each step are stored for the reconstruction of non-editing regions during the denoising stage. In
258   the denoising stage, we start from a randomly initialized noise and inject grounded visual-prompt
259   embedding along with ControlNet condition features. Using the previously extracted binary mask,
260   the newly generated denoised latent diffusion features at each step are progressively fused with the
261   corresponding latent diffusion features stored in the cache queue. This gradual fusion ensures a
262   harmonious blend between the editing and non-editing regions, ultimately generating an object in the
263   designated image area that matches the visual prompt in a “cloning” manner. In the following, we
264   present a detailed explanation of our design choices in both stages, with prior knowledge of diffusion
265   models and DDIM Inversion provided in Appendix A.1.
266   Progressive Masked Fusion. In Embodied Scene Cloning, the goal is to seamlessly integrate visual
267   elements from a new environment into an existing scene using visual-prompt guided image editing
268   techniques. Whether adapting the foreground or background, it’s crucial to preserve non-editing
269   regions to ensure consistency. For instance, if only the background changes while the task remains
      unchanged, we must maintain the integrity of other areas to prevent interference with task performance,

332   sliders. It supports 34 unique tasks and challenges agents to follow sequential language instructions

      et al., 2024) as the policy learning methods for end-to-end action prediction. For each real-world
336
      experimental condition, we conducted 10 trials and reported the average success rate, which is

      5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we presented Embodied Scene Cloning, a novel visual-prompt-based framework
530   designed to address the challenge of generalizing robotic policies to specific deployment embodied
531
      scene. By leveraging visual cues from the target environment, Embodied Scene Cloning generates
      visual-aligned trajectories, enabling effective "cloning" of source demonstrations into the target
532
      environment. This approach minimizes the need for costly and time-consuming data collection
533
      and annotation in new environments. Unlike traditional methods that foucs on general scene data
534   augmentation, our framework focuses on visual-grounded signals, allowing for more accurate and
535   coherent adaptation. Our experimental results demonstrate that Embodied Scene Cloning significantly
536   improves policy generalization in both simulated and real-world settings, providing substantial
537   performance gains across a variety of tasks. This highlights the potential of our method as an effective
538   tool for addressing environmental discrepancies in embodied intelligence tasks. Moving forward,
539   Embodied Scene Cloning offers a promising approach to embodied data augmentation, paving the
      way for more efficient and scalable solutions in robotic learning and deployment.

      A.1   P RELIMINARIES OF D IFFUSION M ODELS AND DDIM I NVERSION
651
652   Diffusion models generate data by progressively transforming random noise into a structured output,
653   such as an image, over a series of timesteps. The process begins with a noise vector zT , and by
654   gradually denoising through multiple steps, the model recovers the original latent representation z0 .
655   During the training phase, the model learns to predict and remove the noise added at each timestep.
656   At time t, the noisy data zt is generated using the following equation:
                                                  √          √
657                                          zt = αt z0 + 1 − αt ϵ
658
659
      where ϵ ∼ N (0, 1) represents Gaussian noise, and αt is a predefined hyperparameter controlling the
      noise schedule. The network ϵθ is trained to predict the noise ϵ using an L2 -based loss objective. The
660
      denoising process occurs iteratively, and at each step, the model predicts zt−1 from zt using:
661                                 √
662                                   αt−1         p               √
                           zt−1 = √          zt +     1 − αt−1 − 1 − αt ϵθ (zt , t)
663                                     αt
664
      By iterating this equation over several timesteps, the model gradually removes the noise and retrieves
665
      the clean data z0 .
666
667   DDIM(Song et al.) provides a deterministic alternative to traditional diffusion models by enabling
668   the inversion of the diffusion process. This allows the model to map an image or data representation
669
      z0 back to a noisier state zT while preserving the structure of the latent space. The inversion from zt
      to zt+1 can be performed using a similar formulation as the denoising process:
670
                                     √
671                                     αt+1       p               √
                             zt+1 = √        zt +      1 − αt+1 − 1 − αt ϵθ (zt , t)
672                                      αt
673
      This enables applications such as reconstructing latent representations for real images and editing,
674
      where precise control over the generative process is necessary. The reversibility of DDIM is grounded
675
      in treating the denoising process as a differential equation, allowing the model to operate efficiently
676   both forwards and backwards.
