009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have opened new avenues for applying multi-
012            agent systems in very large-scale simulations. However, there remain several challenges when
013            conducting multi-agent simulations with existing platforms, such as limited scalability and low
014            efficiency, unsatisfied agent diversity, and effort-intensive management processes. To address these
015            challenges, we develop several new features and components based on a user-friendly multi-agent
016            platform, enhancing its convenience and flexibility for supporting very large-scale multi-agent
017            simulations. Specifically, we propose an actor-based distributed mechanism as the underlying
018
               technological infrastructure towards great scalability and high efficiency, and provide flexible
               environment support for simulating various real-world scenarios, which enables parallel execution
019
               of multiple agents, automatic workflow conversion for distributed deployment, and both inter-agent
020
               and agent-environment interactions. Moreover, we develop an easy-to-use configurable tool and an
021            automatic background generation pipeline, simplifying the process of creating agents with diverse yet
022            detailed background settings. Last but not least, we provide a web-based interface for conveniently
023            monitoring and managing a large number of agents that might deploy across multiple devices. We
024            conduct a comprehensive simulation to demonstrate the effectiveness of these proposed enhancements,
025            and provide detailed observations and insightful discussions to highlight the great potential of applying
026            multi-agent systems in large-scale simulations.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; ANTHROP, 2024; MistralAI, 2024) demonstrate remarkable abilities
032
      in understanding, generating, and interacting with human language. Recent advancements in LLMs have sparked a
      revolution in natural language processing and related fields, paving the way for novel applications that were previously
033
      inconceivable. Building on the capabilities of LLMs, there is growing interest in the development of intelligent
034
      agents, which have the potential to redefine the landscape of simulations (Matsumoto et al., 2024; Sorokovikova et al.,
035   2024; Sreedhar & Chilton, 2024; Yue et al., 2024) towards more interactive, adaptive, and realistic, while requiring
036   substantially fewer human efforts.
037
038
      Recently, several platforms (Hong et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2023; Team, 2023) have been proposed to streamline the
      development of multi-agent systems, providing some fundamental functionalities including unified LLM services,
039
      various tools, and advanced reasoning algorithms. Despite significant progress, we identify several challenges in
040
      conducting simulations with multi-agent platforms, particularly when the number of agents becomes extremely large:
041
042   (i) Scalability and Efficiency Limitations. The scale of involved agents can be critical since simulations at a small scale
043   run the risk of inaccurately representing real-world complexities, making simulations less realistic and reliable (Macal &
044
      North, 2010; Macal, 2016). However, increasing the scale of agents brings challenges to the simulation platform in terms
      of scalability and efficiency. It is non-trivial to efficiently organize agents to execute their tasks and communications
045
      following an appropriate order, with the aim of reducing the running time while ensuring accurate results.
046
047   (ii) Unsatisfied Population Distributions and Agent Diversity. For a large-scale simulation, it is essential that the
048   involved agents exhibit diverse behaviors while generally following a specific population distribution (Gao et al., 2023;
049   Ren et al., 2024). Assigning agents with simple backgrounds may result in a significant number of highly homogeneous
050
      agents, making it difficult to derive meaningful insights. The existing studies rarely consider how to specify population
      distributions of agents from different perspectives, such as age, education, occupation, etc.
051
052   (iii) Difficult Management Processes. As the scale of agents increases, it becomes rather effort-intensive to manage
053   the simulations, including initialization, execution, and termination of a large number of agents spread across multiple
      devices, as well as monitoring their status, behaviors, and interactions (Mou et al., 2024). Such difficulties in managing


                                                         1

054
      make it challenging to promptly identify valuable group-level and individual-level behaviors, which can further hinder
055   the discovery of critical insights for optimizing simulations and advancing research.
056
057   To tackle these challenges, we adopt a user-friendly multi-agent platform, named AgentScope (Gao et al., 2024), as the
058
      foundation framework to provide the basic functionalities, and further develop several new features and components
      upon it to improve its usability, convenience, and flexibility for supporting very large-scale multi-agent simulations.
059
060   Specifically, we propose a distributed mechanism based on the actor model (Agha, 1985), featuring agent-level parallel
061   execution and automatic workflow conversion to provide great scalability and high efficiency for multi-agent-based
062   simulations. The proposed actor-based distributed mechanism enables us to further expand the scale of agents in the
063   simulation with a limited number of devices, and provides linear benefit on running time from the addition of devices.
064
      Users can convert the simulations from a centralized workflow into a distributed one without further modifications
      except for adding a to_dist function. Besides, we support both inter-agent and agent-environment interactions in the
065
      simulations. Agents can communicate with each other, and can query some shared states in the environment, respond to
066
      their changes, and make modifications as needed. We enable user-defined functions within the environment to support
067   flexible extension of various states and their corresponding query and modification behaviors.
068
069   To satisfy the requirements of population distribution and agent diversity, we provide a configuration tool and an
070
      automatic background generation pipeline. Users only need to simply specify the distributions of the population
      from several aspects, a large number of agents with detailed and diverse characteristics can be effortlessly generated
071
      accordingly. These agents can be managed and monitored conveniently through Agent-Manager, a proposed module for
072
      simplifying the organization and observation process of large-scale agent-based simulations. Using a web-based visual
073   interface, Agent-Manager provides a comprehensive overview of all agents across multiple devices, allowing users to
074   efficiently configure, launch, and terminate these agents.
075
076
      With such an agent-based simulation platform, we conduct a comprehensive simulation on the classic “guess 23 of the
      average” game (Nagel, 1995; Camerer et al., 2004) to demonstrate the improvements and advances brought by the
077
      infrastructure introduced above. Firstly, we conduct agent-based simulations involving 1 million agents using only 4
078
      devices, showing the scalability and efficiency of the platform. Then, we incorporate agents using different LLMs
079   of different sizes, equipped with different prompts and diverse background settings, resulting in various and realistic
080   behaviors in the simulations. We provide detailed observations on both collective and individual behaviors, drawing
081   meaningful and valuable insights from a series of simulation experiments, along with further discussions on helpful tips
082   and open questions. These experimental results confirm the feasibility and great potential of conducting large-scale
083   agent-based simulations with LLM-powered agents.

088   LLM-Powered Agent Platforms A significant number of agent platforms have been developed to integrate LLMs
089   into real-world applications and assist humans in problem-solving (Hong et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023b; Significant-
090   Gravitas, 2023; Team, 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2024). These platforms can be categorized into single-agent
091   platforms and multi-agent platforms. The single-agent platforms include AutoGPT (Significant-Gravitas, 2023),
092   LangChain (langchain ai, 2024a), ModelScope-Agent (Li et al., 2023a), and Transformers Agents (Wolf et al., 2020),
093
      which are proposed to resolve practical tasks using LLMs. On the other hand, multi-agent platforms like MetaGPT (Hong
      et al., 2024b), Auto-Gen (Wu et al., 2023), CAMEL (Li et al., 2023b), and LangSmith (langchain ai, 2024b) employ
094
      multi-agent collaboration to tackle more complex challenges, including software programming (Hong et al., 2024b;
095
      Qian et al., 2024), data science (Hong et al., 2024a), social simulation (Park et al., 2023), game-playing (Chen et al.,
096   2024a), etc. Although remarkable progress has been made, applications built on these platforms can currently be limited
097   in the scale of agents and suffer from low efficiency, hindering their potential for large-scale simulations.
098
099
      Agent-Based Simulation Frameworks Recently, agent-based simulation has become an attractive topic in the
      research community (Gürcan, 2024; Sorokovikova et al., 2024; Sreedhar & Chilton, 2024; Ye & Gao, 2024; Team
100
      et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023). Previous studies have explored the integration of LLMs in various fields, including
101
      education (Yue et al., 2024), economic (Matsumoto et al., 2024), societal study (Park et al., 2023; Ye & Gao, 2024;
102   Gao et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024), transportation (Jin et al., 2023), healthcare (Zhang et al., 2023), etc. Researchers
103   have built up several LLM-based or agent-based simulation frameworks. For instance, Vidur (Agrawal et al., 2024) is a
104   simulation framework that focuses on providing high-throughput LLM services, SOTOPIA (Zhou et al., 2024) provides
105   an environment to simulate various social scenarios and evaluates the social intelligence of agents, Cheng et al. (2023)
106   proposes to evaluate the level of caricature, and Ren et al. (2024) designs a framework to simulate the behaviors of web
107   search users. However, these existing frameworks are domain-specific and lack flexibility and extensibility, making it
      challenging for users to conduct large-scale agent-based simulations for a wide variety of applications.

125   3     I NFRASTRUCTURE
126
127   To provide the basic functionalities required for conducting agent-based simulations, we adopt AgentScope, a user-
128   friendly multi-agent platform, as our foundation framework. We further develop several new features and components,
129   making it more convenient and feasible to support very large-scale simulations involving multiple agents. In the
130   following subsections, we elaborate on the details of these proposed enhancements.
131
132   3.1    ACTOR -BASED D ISTRIBUTED M ECHANISM
133
134
      The actor model is a mathematical model of concurrent computation, where each actor acts as a basic computing unit,
      receives messages, and computes independently (Agha, 1985). Based on the actor model, we design a distributed
135
      mechanism to provide agent-level parallel execution for achieving high efficiency and great scalability for agent-based
136
      simulation, and to support automatic workflow conversion for migrating the centralized orchestrated workflow into
137   distributed scenarios effortlessly.
138
139   Agent-Level Parallel Execution In a multi-agent simulation, the interactions between agents follow an atomized
140
      pattern, where interactions occur within small isolated cliques (Matsumoto et al., 2024; Sorokovikova et al., 2024).
      Such a pattern holds significant potential for parallelization, leading to substantial gains in efficiency.
141
142   With the actor model, agents that do not rely on the outputs of others or whose dependencies have all been satisfied,
143   can be executed in parallel for enhancing efficiency. As agents complete their executions and produce results that may
144   be needed by others, some previously waiting agents become active and initiate their executions. An example of the
145   proposed agent-level parallel execution can be found in Appendix B.1.
146   We propose two multi-process modes for various simulation scenarios, supporting both one-to-one and many-to-one
147   relationships between agents and processes. Communication across processes and devices utilizes Remote Procedure
148   Call, and we provide implementations in both Python and C++. The one-to-one multi-process mode, where each agent
149   runs in a separate process, is well-suited for agents performing computation-intensive tasks, ensuring that each agent
150   has sufficient computational resources and that the parallelization would not be hindered by Global Interpreter Lock
151   (GIL). For the many-to-one multi-process mode where multiple agents run within a single process, agents within a
152
      single process share CPU cores, communication ports, and global variables. Such a mode is well-suited for situations
      where agents experience I/O wait times or are awaiting responses from remote APIs, allowing the CPUs to leverage the
153
      time-sharing mechanism to maximize resource utilization.
154
155   In this way, the proposed framework achieves higher resource utilization in parallelism compared to existing ones (Wu
156   et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024b) that rely on asynchronous I/O in Python, which can be constrained by GIL. Furthermore,
157   it also makes a significant advancement over existing actor-based distributed frameworks, such as Ray (Moritz et al.,
158
      2017), which allocate a new worker process for each actor, resulting in wasted computational resources when applying
      for large-scale agent-based simulations.
159
160   Automatic Workflow Conversion To ease the distributed deployment of large-scale simulations, we provide an easy-
161   to-use function called to_dist to convert a centralized workflow into a distributed one effortlessly. The conversion
      involves two automatic stages. In the first stage, each agent in the center (i.e., the main procedure) is distributed to a


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      specified device, with a proxy left in the center as a substitute. This proxy can be utilized for users to orchestrate the
163   workflow, maintaining parity with the centralized mode, and is responsible for automatically forwarding messages to the
164   corresponding distributed agents. In the second stage, placeholders are introduced to ensure that calculations performed
165   by the distributed agents do not block the workflow execution in the center. When a proxy receives a message, it
166   immediately returns a placeholder and forwards the message to its corresponding distributed agent for processing. For a
167   distributed agent, when receiving a message, it first checks if the message contains placeholders to decide whether it
168   needs to request and wait for the results from the agents indicated on the placeholders. This mechanism allows the main
169   procedure to continue executing without waiting for the results from the distributed agents.
170   The example in Fig. 1 shows how a centralized workflow can be automatically converted to a distributed one, without
171   any further modifications except for adding to_dist. Firstly, all agents are transformed into distributed agents, with
172   corresponding proxies left at the center. Then, the center can continue its execution until it needs to print msg_b
173   (i.e., line 15 of the code), thanks to the usage of placeholders that replace messages msg_a/b/c/d as responses.
174   Once receiving msg_a from agent-A, agent-B and agent-C run in parallel to produce msg_b and msg_c, respectively,
175   which would be finally sent back to the center for printing out.

177   3.2   AGENT-E NVIRONMENT I NTERACTIONS
178
179   Agent-environment interactions are also crucial alongside the above inter-agent communication in agent-based
180   simulations, which indicates that agents can access the shared states in the environment, respond to their changes, and
181   make modifications as needed.
182   For large-scale simulations, the environment is expected to meet the following requirements: (a) High concurrency
183   access. Given a large number of agents, the environment should support high concurrency access, especially when
184   agents need to frequently check and interact with the environment’s states. (b) Diverse states. Different simulation
185   scenarios may need different states to be maintained in the environment (Yang et al., 2024b; Xi et al., 2024). For
186   example, in a chat room simulation, the states might include participants and conversation history, while in a maze
187   simulation, the states contain the locations of agents and other interactable items. These diverse states pose challenges
188   regarding the flexibility and extensibility of the environment module. (c) Bi-directional interaction. The interactions
189
      between agents and the environment should be bidirectional (Beer, 1995), indicating that agents can query or modify
      the states in the environment, and the environment can also actively send notifications or requests to agents. For example,
190
      a chatroom can notify specific participants when they are mentioned or called. (d) Multiple environments. Some
191
      simulation scenarios might need to have multiple environments concurrently. For example, in a social simulation where
192   agents are divided into subgroups, agents within a subgroup may share one dedicated environment for collaborative
193   tasks or information synchronization without interference from other groups. Multiple environments allow for more
194   tailored interactions and state management specific to each subgroup’s unique objectives.
195
      To satisfy all the aforementioned requirements, we abstract the environment as a special type of agent with rich
196
      functionalities, which allows the environment to maintain shared variables as states and to communicate with other
197
      agents through Remote Procedure Calls for ensuring high concurrency. Meanwhile, we enable user-defined functions
198   within the environment to support flexible extension of various states and their corresponding query and modification
199   behaviors. For bi-directional interactions, we introduce listeners, which can be attached to user-defined functions in the
200   environment, automatically triggering the sending of messages from the environment to agents when specific conditions
201   are met. Furthermore, we support the nesting of environments and agents to allow multiple environments within a
202   single simulation. An example of a multi-environment designed for group-wise information synchronization is shown
203   in Appendix B.2, and a use case can be found in Sec. 4.5.
204
205   3.3   H ETEROGENEOUS C ONFIGURATIONS
206
207   In a simulation, agents are expected to act as humans with diverse backgrounds, including different ages, genders,
208   careers, nationalities, education, experiences, etc. An intuitive approach is to add these background settings of agents in
209   their system prompts, providing guidance for agents on the roles to play and actions to take. However, for large-scale
210   simulations, providing diverse, heterogeneous, and reasonable background settings for agents can be laborious and
211   time-consuming, especially when precise control of different population distributions is required in certain simulations.
212
      This problem motivates us to provide easy-to-use tools to assist users in effortlessly setting up large-scale agents with
      diverse background settings.
213
214   Configurable Tool Specifically, users can begin by defining the total population of the simulation, and then specify
215   the distributions of the population from various perspectives. We provide some widely-used distribution templates
      for convenient usage, from the aspects of age, gender, occupation, nationality, and education. Besides, the proposed


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      configurable tool allows for easy extension of new aspects, enhancing its flexibility to meet diverse requirements. An
217   example configuration file is shown in Appendix B.3 for a group of people with different educational levels, in which
218   the proportions of its different components can specify a distribution.
219
220
      Automatic Background Generation Pipeline After configurations have been provided via the above tool, more
      detailed and heterogeneous background settings can be automatically generated to instantiate the agents. Specifically,
221
      when users start a simulation, we draw specific values from the distributions based on the configurations, convert
222
      them into a JSON format, and fill them into a meta prompt to produce the completed instructions for background
223   generation tasks. These instructions are utilized by LLMs to generate heterogeneous background settings. To introduce
224   more diversity, the generation process involves adjusting the random seed and the temperature used by LLMs. Several
225   examples of the generated background settings can be found in Sec. 4.4, along with the results and analysis of the
226   simulations involving diverse agents.

228   3.4   M ANAGEMENT FOR L ARGE -S CALE AGENTS
229
230   In a simulation, users need to manage and monitor a large number of agents distributed across different devices, which
231   might become intractable to handle manually as the scale and complexity of the simulation increase. To tackle this, we
232   incorporate advanced forms of agent management and monitoring, named Agent-Manager. Specifically, when users
233   start a simulation, servers are first launched on all the remote devices, which provide resident services to remotely
234   create, monitor, and stop distributed agents. These servers are responsible for managing the lifecycle of distributed
235
      agents and synchronizing their information to a web-based visual interface, which is provided for a comprehensive
      overview of all registered servers and all deployed agents on different devices, as shown in Appendix B.4.
236
237   The Agent-Manager also simplifies the management and monitoring processes for conducting multiple simulations.
238   Since the servers can be reused in different simulations, users don’t need to restart the distributed servers between two
239   simulations. Users can efficiently configure, launch, and terminate servers and agents during the simulations as needed.
240   With such a design, we streamline the management process by focusing on servers rather than individual agents, thereby
241
      improving the efficiency and effectiveness of managing large-scale agent systems.

249   4.1   S ETTINGS
250
251   We set up a large number of agents to participate in the classic game guess the 23 of the average, where each agent
252   reports a real number between 0 and 100 and the agent who reports a number closest to 23 of the average of all the
253   reported numbers wins the game. In this game, intuitively the highest possible average is 100. Therefore, for winning
254   the game, agents tend to report a number no larger than 100 × 23 = 66 23 . Once all agents adopt this strategy, 66 32
255   becomes the new highest possible average and thus they tend to report a number no larger than 66 23 × 32 = 44 49 . This
256   process continues until the average becomes 0 and all agents report 0, indicating that the game has reached its Nash
257   equilibrium. However, considering that agents may not always be rational, those agents who report 0 cannot always win
258   the game since the average does not converge to 0 immediately. Agents should carefully take into account the possible
259   actions of others before reporting their numbers. Meanwhile, agents can adjust their strategies in a multi-round game
260
      according to the average reported numbers in previous rounds. Note that all the experiments in this section follow
      the aforementioned settings.
261

265   different sizes, including Llama3-8B / Llama3-70B (Meta, 2024), Qwen2-7B / Qwen2-72B (Yang et al., 2024a), and
266
      MistralAI-8x7B / MistralAI-8x22B (MistralAI, 2024). We adopt their instruction versions due to their enhanced ability
      to follow instructions. The details of the adopted LLMs and implementations can be found in Appendix C.

297   4.2              S CALABILITY AND E FFICIENCY
298
299
      First of all, we conduct a series of experiments to show the scalability and efficiency of the agent-based simulations
      supported by the proposed actor-based distributed mechanism (see Sec. 3.1). Specifically, we illustrate how the overall
300
      simulation running time changes as the number of participating agents grows when using LLMs of different sizes,
301
      including Llama3-8B and Llama3-70B. In addition to the model sizes, the system prompt provided to agents is also a
302   factor that can influence the running time, since some prompts (e.g., Prompt 2) may encourage agents to generate longer
303   responses and thereby lead to longer response time. From the experimental results shown in Fig. 2, we can obtain the
304   following observations and insights.
305
      (i) We support an agent-based simulation involving 1 million agents, which can be completed in 12 minutes using
306
      4 devices. In Fig. 2a, we fix the device number to 4 and record the simulation running time as the number of agents
307
      grows from 100 to 1M. It can be observed that the simulation involving 1 million agents finishes in 12 minutes when
308   using Llama3-8B with Prompt 1, while it takes 85 minutes if we choose Prompt 2, as the number of averaged response
309   tokens grows by more than 150-fold1 . For the heaviest inference workload, i.e., when agents adopt Llama3-70B and
310   Prompt 2, it takes around 10.6 hours to complete the simulation.
311
      (ii) The proposed actor-based distributed mechanism significantly improves the efficiency of large-scale agent-
312
      based simulations. To better demonstrate the improvements brought by the proposed actor-based distributed mechanism,
313
      we adopt a dummy model request (i.e., agents sleep for 1 second and generate random numbers rather than posting the
314   requests) in the simulation to remove the impact of the LLM inference speed. The experimental results summarized in
315   Fig. 2b show that, completing an agent-based simulation with the proposed actor-based distributed mechanism involving
316   1 million agents only takes 40 seconds, whereas simulations using serial execution or asynchronous mode in Python
317   (adopted by existing works (Wu et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024b)) require around 12 days and 8.6 hours, respectively.
318
      (iii) Increasing the number of devices can proportionally reduce the simulation running time. As shown in Fig. 2c,
319
      we maintain the number of agents at 10,000 and vary the number of devices used in the simulation. For Llama3-70B
320   with Prompt 2, the simulation running time decreases from 22 minutes to 5.6 minutes as the number of devices increases
321   from 1 to 4. Such a phenomenon can be attributed to a reduction in the number of agents served within one device,
322   which demonstrates the horizontal scalability of the proposed framework.

      the fifth round, the average reported number of agents using Qwen2-72B with Prompt 2 is 2.02, which is significantly

      When the Nash Equilibrium Is Not 0 We also set up another variant of the game to validate the agents’ capabilities
510
      in understanding and reasoning. Specifically, we modify the winning criteria so that the Nash equilibrium becomes 10,
511
      instead of 0 as in the classic game. As a result, LLMs might not exhibit reasonable thoughts and behaviors if they have
512   a limited understanding of this game or have poor reasoning ability. The adopted prompt is shown in Appendix H.1.
513
514   As shown in Fig. 9, we observe that some agents using the Qwen2-72B and MistralAI-8×22B are able to reason out
515
      the Nash equilibrium point in the first round. As the game progresses, the numbers reported by these agents gradually
      approach 10, demonstrating their understanding of this game and ability to make reasonable decisions.
516
517   The above findings are further confirmed by observations of individual-level behaviors shown in Appendix H.1. Besides,
518   from these observations, we can identify some typical mistakes made by agents. For example, some agents might
519   make simple calculation errors, such as calculating a wrong average value, and leading to incorrect results despite
520   having a correct reasoning process. Some agents may follow a logical step-by-step process (e.g., calculating the Nash
521
      equilibrium) but still make incorrect decisions (e.g., directly reporting 0). But even so, these mistakes are infrequent
      and do not affect the overall conclusions drawn from group-level observations, as we previously demonstrated.
522
523   These observations indicate that although agents may make simple calculation errors (especially in decimal calculations),
524   they exhibit powerful reasoning abilities and show great potential for usage in large-scale simulations. More experimental
525   results and further discussions regarding the impact of temperature and the role-playing ability of LLMs can be found
526   in Appendix H.2 and H.3, respectively.

      In this paper, we first discuss several key factors of concern for conducting large-scale agent-based simulations,
      including scalability and efficiency, population distribution and agent diversity, and ease of management. Motivated
532
      by these factors, we propose and implement several enhancements based on AgentScope, including an actor-based
533
      distributed mechanism that provides agent-level parallel execution and automatic workflow conversion, the flexible
534   environment support to simulate various real-world scenarios, the heterogeneous configurations that allow users to
535   specify population distributions and to automatically generate agents with diverse background settings, and a web-based
536   interface to simplify the management of large-scale agents. These enhancements make the framework more flexible and
537   convenient for supporting large-scale agent-based simulations. We conduct a series of simulation experiments with the
538   proposed framework and provide detailed observations on the diverse and realistic behaviors of agents, highlighting its
539   great potential to further advance research and applications in agent-based simulations. We will release the source code
      to inspire further research and development in large-scale multi-agent simulations.

      C.1                 D ETAILS OF A DOPTED LLM S
813
814   The details of the adopted LLMs are provided below:
815
816                        • Llama3-8B / Llama3-70B (Meta, 2024): A series of open-source LLMs developed by Meta, which have been
817                          pre-trained and fine-tuned on a massive corpus.
818
                           • Qwen2-7B / Qwen2-72B (Yang et al., 2024a): The second generation of Qwen open-source LLMs, developed
819
                             by Alibaba.
820
821                        • MistralAI-8x7B / MistralAI-8x22B (MistralAI, 2024): The open-source mixture-of-experts (MOE) LLMs
822                          released by MistralAI, where each MOE LLM consists of eight 7B/22B models.
823
824   C.2                 I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
825
826   Due to the limited GPU memory, unless otherwise specified, we deploy eight Qwen2-7B / Llama3-8B models, two
827   Qwen2-72B / Llama3-70B / MistralAI-8x7B models, or one MistralAI-8x22B model on each device. The generation
828   temperature for all LLMs is set to 1.0 to promote the diversity of responses. Besides, to prevent errors in response
829   format, each agent executes two LLM server calls in every game round. The first call is used to generate the thought
830   process and the reported number, while the second call is made to extract the reported number correctly.
