011                                             Abstract
012
013           The ability to plan a course of action that achieves a desired state of affairs
014           has long been considered a core competence of intelligent agents and has been
015           an integral part of AI research since its inception. With the advent of large
016           language models (LLMs), there has been considerable interest in the question
017           of whether or not they possess such planning abilities, but–despite the slew of
018
              new private and open source LLMs since GPT3–progress has remained slow.
              OpenAI claims that their recent o1 (Strawberry) model has been specifically
019
              constructed and trained to escape the normal limitations of autoregressive
020
              LLMs–making it a new kind of model: a Large Reasoning Model (LRM). In
021           this paper, we evaluate the planning capabilities of two LRMs (o1-preview
022           and o1-mini) on both planning and scheduling benchmarks. We see that
023           while o1 does seem to offer significant improvements over autoregressive
024           LLMs, this comes at a steep inference cost, while still failing to provide
025           any guarantees over what it generates. We also show that combining o1
026           models with external verifiers–in a so-called LRM-Modulo system–guarantees
027           the correctness of the combined system’s output while further improving
028           performance.
029

031   1   Introduction
032
033   The recent release of OpenAI’s o1 (Strawberry) (OpenAI, 2024a) brings with it the op-
034   portunity to freshly evaluate the progress of large pre-trained AI models on planning and
035   scheduling benchmarks. Unlike the Large Language Models (LLMs) which came before
036   it–which can roughly be viewed as approximate retrievers–o1 seems to have been trained to be
037   an approximate reasoner, capable of scaling the amount of compute it uses depending on the
038
      query.1 Following OpenAI2 , we draw a distinction between previous Large Language Models
      (LLMs) and o1, a Large Reasoning Model (or LRM), as its new (unknown) architecture,
039
      operation, and capabilities all seem to be fundamentally different from those of vanilla LLMs,
040
      likely during both the pre-training phase and at inference time. Our aim in this paper is
041   twofold: to comprehensively evaluate the performance of o1 on established planning and
042   scheduling benchmarks as well as more difficult extensions; and to demonstrate how to
043   provide correctness guarantees and boost performance by embedding an LRM in a loop
044   with a sound verifier, in a similar vein to the LLM-Modulo framework (Kambhampati et al.,
045   2024).
046

      system in the same vein as AlphaGo, but where the ‘moves’ being generated and evaluated are

      Chains of Thought. Note that this is very different use of RL from that in RLHF, which can be

      To properly evaluate this new kind of model and understand its abilities and limitations will
055   require new tools and evaluation methods, especially if details of the overall model structure
056   are kept secret and internal traces remain inaccessible to outside researchers.3 In this paper,
057   we evaluate performance on established benchmarks and compare to previous state-of-the-art
058   results, extending these benchmarks to more difficult problems when possible and necessary.
059   For planning, we use PlanBench (Valmeekam et al., 2023), which consists of both specific
060   test sets and a suite of tools intended for evaluating language models on arbitrary IPC
061   planning domains. To evaluate scheduling capabilities, we test on TravelPlanner (Xie et al.,
062   2024), on the three domains from Natural Plan (Zheng et al., 2024), and on graph coloring
063
      problems (Stechly et al., 2024b).
064   Using these benchmarks as our basis for analysis, we investigate the performance jump
065   that LRMs from the o1 family promise. We then demonstrate how these benchmarks can
066   be elaborated on in order to remain relevant metrics for LRMs. We argue that, to be
067   complete, new approaches to measuring LRM reasoning capabilities must take into account
068   efficiency, cost, and guarantees. We also note the steep inference cost of LRMs and discuss
069
      the tradeoffs between using LLMs vs LRMs, arguing that in some cases an LLM-Modulo
      (Kambhampati et al., 2024) approach may be significantly cheaper than o1 models for
070
      comparative performance, and with guarantees. Our results also show that that same
071
      LLM-Modulo approach can indeed be adapted to LRMs to further improve their performance
072   and provide guarantees. In essence, LRMs can replace LLMs as significantly better–but still
073   fallible–generators in the LLM-Modulo framework.

      2     Background and Related Work
077
078
      Though they are trained as text completion systems, Large Language Models (LLMs) have
      shown some promise on many other tasks. Initial claims were wildly positive, claiming they
079
      are general purpose reasoning systems (Bubeck et al., 2023), especially when prompted in
080
      just the right way (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022), but later results showed that their
081   seeming reasoning capabilities are brittle and break down even in simple domains (Luo et al.,
082   2023; Dziri et al., 2024; Stechly et al., 2024b; Qian et al., 2022) and may be attributable to
083   dataset contamination (Roberts et al., 2023). In planning, Valmeekam et al. (2023) showed
084   that LLMs fail even on problems as trivial as three block stacking.
085
      Based on what little has been revealed by OpenAI, o1 seems to be a new class of model (a
086
      Large Reasoning Model or LRM), designed to combine the fuzzy language capabilities of
087   LLMs with some implementation of approximate reasoning. With this in mind, we believe it
088   is time to bring up the same questions that were asked about LLMs for these LRMs. We use
089   benchmarks from the LLM literature, extending them where possible and necessary to show
090   how well and how robustly o1 does or doesn’t perform on various planning and scheduling
091   tasks.
092
093   2.1   Domains: Planning
094
095   The LLM literature abounds with claims of the ’emergent’ planning capabilities of
096   LLMs (Huang et al., 2022a). However, closer inspection reveals that many of the em-
097
      pirical results supporting these claims comes from evaluations on simpler, commonsense
      domains, such as ALFworld (Yao et al., 2023), BEHAVIOR (Srivastava et al., 2022), kitchen
098
      environments (Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b), and virtual home (Huang et al., 2022a).
099
      Not only do the the instances tested on tend to have fewer interactions, but many of them
100   conflate reactive acting and deliberative planning (Ghallab et al., 2016).
101
102   In contrast, we focus on classical planning problems, or STRIPS planning problems, which
103
      are a formalism for automated planning in discrete, deterministic spaces. To define a
      planning problem, we specify an initial state, a domain, and a goal. The domain contains all
104
      relevant information about the types of objects that may exist and the allowable actions
105
      from any given state, specified by defining the preconditions and effects of each named

189   2.2       Domains: Scheduling
190
      More recent text-based benchmarks have provided full, static descriptions of their domains,
191   initial states, and goals. However, many of these, despite the word "planning" in their
192   titles, would be better characterized as testing scheduling abilities (Ghallab et al., 2016).
193   Classical planning problems are canonical graph search problems which are PSPACE-complete.
194   Scheduling problems are only NP-Hard (Carlier, 1982), and mainly revolve around resource
195   allocation. These problems are equivalent to constraint satisfaction problems, and thus easier
196   than the planning problems we describe above.
197   We evaluate o1 on three scheduling benchmarks on which LLMs have failed. Zheng et al.
198   (2024)’s Natural Plan benchmark consists of three scheduling domains: trip planning, calendar
199   scheduling, and meeting planning. Xie et al. (2024)’s Travel Planning benchmark consists
200   of a large dataset of travel information (flights, accommodations, restaurants, etc.) with
201   prompts that ask the model to create a three to seven day itinerary based on natural language
202   instructions. Finally, Stechly et al. (2024b) translate graph coloring, a classical constraint
203   satisfaction problem, into natural language prompts and evaluates GPT-4’s accuracy on
204   these problems. We take their test set and extend it to more difficult instances.
205

      no plan of attack exists for a specified system (Boddy et al., 2005). So far, LLMs have
325   struggled to recognize that some problems cannot be solved, instead confidently confabulating
326   nonsensical answers. o1 was launched with the claim that it has started to overcome this
327   issue, and can now accurately identify unsolvable problems (Brown, 2024). To test this
328   systematically, we modified 100 instances from the easier three to five block test set by adding
329   one on(x,y)-type conjunct to each instance’s goal state, making the goal unsatisfiable. We
330   ensured our instances were unambiguous by giving the full PDDL representation of both
331   the domain and the instance, to avoid quibbles such as "A is on B because A is on C and
332   C is on B" where the model redefines the meanings of a potentially ambiguous natural
333
      language statement. The results are in Table 3. On Blocksworld, only 27% of all instances
      were correctly and explicitly identified by o1 as unsolvable. In 19% of all cases, the model
334
      returned a dot or some kind of "[empty plan]" marker, without any explanation or indication
335
      of unsolvability. We consider these incorrect, as "empty plan" is only the correct answer if
336   the goal is already satisfied. In the remaining 54% of cases, the model generated a full (and
337   therefore impossible and incorrect!) plan.
338
339

340

341
      to be a problem for LRMs. Furthermore, this ability to sometimes note impossible plans
342   correctly comes at a cost: now the model sometimes falsely claims that solvable problems are
343   actually unsolvable. On Randomized Mystery Blocksworld, 11.5% of instances are incorrectly

346   o1’s Creative Justifications While our main focus has been on providing a quantitative
347   evaluation of o1’s performance on PlanBench, we have also noticed an o1 idiosyncrasy that is
348   worth commenting on. When the model gives an incorrect answer, it also sometimes provides
349   a creative, but nonsensical, justification for its decision. It is almost as if o1 has gone from
350   hallucinating to gaslighting! In one case, it decided that an unsolvable problem is solvable
351   because a goal condition, while not present in the final state, had been true for at some
352   point during the execution, and thus should continue to count. In another, it declared that
353
      on(a,c) was true because, as it explained in a brief parenthetical, a was on b which was on
      c, and was thus a was somewhere above c, which should count as being "on top" of it. As
354
      we mentioned earlier, we changed our unsolvable instance prompts from natural language to
355
      PDDL in order to make it extremely clear that divergences from our exact definitions were
356   disallowed.

      Extending to Harder Domains: While STRIPS planning is in principle PSPACE-
359   complete, the problems presented above are actually of a lower computational complexity.
360   Plan existence for both Blocksworld and Logistics is polynomial (Hoffmann et al., 2006).
361   Sokoban is a non-ergodic domain in which an agent moves around a constrained grid, pushing
362   boxes by running into them, where the goal is to move every box to one of a set of final
363   locations. It can be represented in PDDL form, and is thus amenable to classical planning
364   techniques, and is known to be PSPACE-complete (Culberson, 1997).
365   Using a generator from the 2008 International Planning Competition(IPC, 1998), we generate
366   55 Sokoban instances with grid sizes ranging from 4 × 4 to 10 × 10, 1 to 4 boxes, and 1
367   to 4 walls. We then create corresponding prompts in PDDL. o1-preview answers 12.7% of
368   these instances correctly, while o1-mini is not far behind with 10.9%. For comparison, when
369   evaluated on these same instances, Llama3.1-405B, despite doing the best of all LLMs on
370   the Blocksword sets, does not answer a single question correctly.

      Natural Plan: This benchmark consists of three domains: calendar scheduling, trip
401
      planning, and meeting planning. On calendar scheduling, o1-mini solves 94% of all instances
402   correctly. Given cost constraints, we did not test o1-preview on this domain, as o1-mini

408   3.3   Accuracy/Cost Tradeoffs and Guarantees
409
410   With LRMs showing better performance on planning and scheduling problems, our evaluations
411   must explicitly take into account the trade-offs that come from choosing general models
412   over established deep and narrow systems. While o1-preview may provide higher accuracy
413
      than LLMs, it still fails to provide any correctness guarantees, and it is unclear that it is at
      all cost-effective. Unlike previous models, whose APIs only charge based on the number of
414
      input tokens and the number of output tokens (usually at a rate that is five times higher
415
      for the latter), o1’s price-per-call includes a surcharge based on the number of "reasoning
416   tokens" it used–tokens generated as part of inference and not revealed to the user–which are
417   charged at the significantly higher output token rate. Currently, end users have no control
418   over the number of these tokens generated, a number which is expanded or limited by the
419   model in its own opaque way. We have already run up a bill of over $4000 for just the o1
420   model experiments reported in this evaluation!6 )
421
      Without exposing the ability to scale inference time to particular specifications, influence
422   the internal ‘thinking’ process in task-specific ways, or ensure that intermediate steps are
423   evaluated by trusted or sound verifiers, the o1 models are a coarse-grained choice in the
424   space of cost, inference time, guarantees, and performance trade-offs. They aren’t, however,
425   the only choices in that space, and reasonable LRM evaluations must take this into account
426   (see similar arguments in (Katz et al., 2024; Kapoor et al., 2024)).
427
      Classical planners like Fast Downward (Helmert, 2006) achieve 100% on our dataset in a
428   fraction of the time, compute, and cost, while providing guarantees that their answers are
429   correct. Running Fast Downward on a personal computer was essentially free in dollar terms

464   4    LRM-Modulo to Improve o1 with Guarantees
465
466
      We propose augmenting o1 with external verifiers to endow the combined system with
467
      soundness guarantees. While o1 is a stride in the direction of general-purpose, expressive
468   planning systems, our results show that it cannot plan robustly when faced with harder
469   instances, nor can it consistently recognize when instances are unsolvable, still providing
470   incoherent plans in a majority of such cases. In other words, o1 is still fallible and without
471   guarantees. Prior to the release of these models, the best way to coax planning capabilities
472   out of LLMs has been to pair them with sound external verifier in generate-test frameworks,
473   in what are known as LLM-Modulo systems (Kambhampati et al., 2024; Trinh et al., 2024).
474   This framework is broadly applicable even beyond LLMs, and–given a sound verifier for
475   some domain–requires only a generator expressive enough to provide guesses for that domain.
476
      Moreover, because of the built-in verification, it guarantees that any answer output is correct.
      For safety-critical systems, this is essential! High accuracies are not sufficient, especially
477
      when the underlying system–as is the case for both LLMs and even more so for LRMs–is an
478
      opaque black box. Therefore, we investigated integrating LRMs into LRM-modulo systems
479   to both boost their overall performance and to provide much-needed guarantees over their
480   outputs.
481
482      7
           The current model is also set to a default temperature of 1.0, which further reduces replicability
483   and interpretability–for any given problem, it is never clear whether the result is merely the result
484   of stochasticity. This compounds a problem with OpenAI models that has existed since at least
485   GPT3. Temperature 0 never gave deterministic outputs, and worse, the logprobs provided by the
      OpenAI API for any given prompt have long been known to fluctuate wildly (Xuan, 2023).

486
      Generate-test systems are limited by how good the generator is. A poor generator, such
487   as one that produces completely random strings, may be capable of eventually producing
488   the correct answer, but be so unlikely to do so at each iteration as to be useless, while an
489   incomplete generator may never output the correct answer at all. LLMs and LRMs can be
490   backprompted–that is, we can take feedback from the sound verifier and send it back to
491   the model or modify the next prompt in some other way to increase the diversity of the
492   responses generated–which may steer their next output towards the correct answer. Based
493   on our results, o1 models are much better generators than anything that came before them,
494   but, a priori, it is unclear if they are any more complete or capable of effectively utilizing
495
      backprompts or advice.
496   We test LRM-modulo setups on our five hardest test sets: 20+ length plan Blocksworld,
497   Sokoban, 20 vertex graph coloring, OSU’s Travel Planning, and 10 city trip planning. Due
498   to cost constraints, we limit the number of iterations to a maximum of ten, but we stop the
499   system early once the performance increase from round to round has become mostly flat.8
500   Even with so few iterations, we see significant jumps in performance across almost all of
501
      our domains. o1-preview’s performance on harder Blocksworld saturates within 7 iterations,
      with the combined system achieving 98.2%. Harder graph coloring shows similar results,
502
      going up to 94%. Perhaps most surprising, our most difficult domain, Sokoban, shows a
503
      significant jump from 12.7% to 43.6%. o1-mini-Modulo performance, while also impressive,
504   only comes close on graph coloring and travel planning.
505
506   Our results seem to imply that these models are not only better generators, but also benefit
507
      more from the sound verification signal and provided feedback. However, we can’t really
      know how they use the critique provided–this question is likely crucial, but also unanswerable
508
      with OpenAI’s current stance against revealing the internal workings of the model or the
509
      intermediate reasoning tokens it generates.
510
511   With the high cost of o1 model queries, it is also crucial to examine in which situations these
512   models are not just the best option, but the cost-effective one. Returning to LLM-modulo,
513
      where we use a smaller, cheaper, and faster LLM in a loop with a verifier, can provide similar
      or better performance in some domains. For example, in calendar scheduling, o1-mini costs
514
      $2.70 to run over the entire test set, and has a final accuracy of 94%. GPT-4o-mini can be
515
      run for 50 iterations in a modulo loop for only $2.48, beating that performance to get 97%,
516   a figure that comes with guarantees not provided by just the base model.

519   5   Conclusion
520
521   In this paper, we investigated the performance of o1-preview and o1-mini–the new so-called
522   LRMs–on a variety of planning and scheduling benchmarks. While LLMs have thus far
523   failed to make much progress on the obfuscated (or "Mystery") versions of PlanBench
524   domains, o1 shows the first bit of real progress. In general, it seems to have made impressive
525   headway on benchmarks that were previously unassailable. However, when we evaluated
526   the model on longer problems and on the question of determining solvability of potentially
527   impossible instances, we found that these accuracy gains are not general nor robust. While
528
      o1 made some gains on scheduling problems, performing much better on graph coloring than
      previous models, these were not evenly distributed, only making some progress on OSU’s
529
      Travel Plan domain and the Natural Plan benchmark suite. We also discussed the critical
530
      accuracy/efficiency tradeoffs that are brought up by the fact that o1 that uses (and charges
531   for) significant inference-time compute, as well as how it compares to other LLM-based
532   approaches (such as LLM-Modulo (Kambhampati et al., 2024)) and dedicated solvers. Future
533   evaluations will have to maintain a focus on these factors if they are to remain meaningful
534   or relevant. Finally, we showed that approaches like LLM-Modulo (Kambhampati et al.,
535   2024) can indeed be adapted to LRMs to further improve their performance and to provide
536   much-needed guarantees. In essence, LRMs can replace LLMs as significantly better–but
537   still fallible–generators in LLM-Modulo frameworks.

      A   Further Discussion of LLM Planning Performance
705
706   LLMs are highly capable at providing translations between equivalent representations (Olmo
707   et al., 2021). This fact, combined with their significantly higher performance on the
708   unobfuscated version of the Blocksworld domain, predicts that–if they are capable of com-
709   posing reasoning operations–the performance gap between Mystery Blocksworld and classic
710   Blocksworld should shrink substantially if the translation from Mystery Blocksworld back
711   into Blocksworld is explicitly provided. However, when we provide this in the prompt (see
712

714   B   Speculations about o1 internal operation
715
      While our evaluation of o1 did not depend on any specific assumption about its operation,
716
      we did have a working model of o1 based on the very skimpy description that was provided
717
      in the blog post that accompanies o1’s release (OpenAI, 2024b). Verifying our model is
718   unfortunately made infeasible by the fact that o1 doesn’t actually provide any trace of its
719   operations (even during the costly inference stage), and OpenAI warns that API access will
720   be revoked if any attempts are made to surface its reasoning tokens.
721
      There are two things–“reinforcement learning" and “Private Chain-of-Thought (CoT)” that
722
      are mentioned in the writeup. So imagine you are trying to transplant a “generalized
723
      AlphaGo”–let’s call it GPTGo–onto the underlying LLM token prediction substrate.
724
725   To do this, you need to know
726
727        1. What are the GPTGo moves? For AlphaGo, we had GO moves). What would be
728
              the right moves when the task is just “complete the prompt the right way"?
729        2. Where is it getting its external success/failure signal from? For AlphaGo, we had
730           simulators/verifiers giving the success/failure signal. The most interesting question
731           in transplanting the self-play idea to a general AI agent is where is it getting this
732           signal?
733
734
      Our guess is that the moves are auto-generated CoTs (thus the moves have a very high
      branching factor). Let’s assume–for simplification–that we have a CoT-generating LLM, that
735
      generates these CoTs conditioned on the prompt. (It is not clear if the CoT’s are domain
736
      independent of the “think step by step" variety (Kojima et al., 2022) or domain/task specific,
737   or a combination.)
738
739   The success signal is likely from massive amounts of synthetic training data with correct
740
      answers. When the completed prompt is seen to contain the correct answer (presumably
      judged by the LLM itself), then the episode is considered a success, and a failure otherwise.
741
742   The task for the reinforcement learner then is: Given the original problem prompt, generate
743   and select a CoT, and use it to continue to extend the prompt (possibly generating subgoal
744   CoTs after every few stages). Get the final success/failure signal for the example (for which
745   you do have answer).
746   The RL stage may involve training on a a huge number of training examples with answers.
747   The training examples with answers can either be coming from benchmarks, or from synthetic
748   data with problems and their solutions–using external solvers. In this phase the RL part
749   attempts to learn the q-values of the CoT moves (much like AlphaGo learns the q-values of
750   the moves of the Go). (The q-values learning may be incorporated into the internal weights
751   of the CoT generator LLM). At this point, we have a CoT move generator that is better
752
      than the random one before the RL stage
753   During the inference stage–which OpenAI says can be indefinitely long (although it is
754   currently capped internally by them, with no external control), like AlphaGo, o1 might be
755   further improving its evaluation of the q-values of the CoT moves in the context of the
      current prompt. While AlphaGo used MCT-based rollouts, we obviously don’t know the
