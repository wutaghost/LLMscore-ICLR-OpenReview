011                                            Abstract
012
013
              Efforts to enable Large Language Models (LLMs) to understand human
014           speech and following spoken instructions have led to the development of
015           several Speech-Conditioned Large Language Models (SLMs). While these
016           models have demonstrated success on various speech-related tasks, such as
017           automatic speech recognition (ASR), speech-to-text translation (ST), the
018           design space of SLMs has not been thoroughly explored. With the goal
019           to build an SLM that is able to follow spoken instructions (to understand
020           non-speech audio is out-of-scope for this work), we revisit key design choices
021           for SLMs in this work. Our experiments allow us to gain insights into
022
              how these choices impact the ability of SLMs to understand human speech
              and follow spoken instruction and how we could optimize important design
023
              choices to achieve better results. Surprisingly, our experiments reveal that
024
              existing SLMs struggle to follow spoken instructions or respond to speech
025           inputs, even for simple queries like “who has been to the moon?”. Our
026           findings indicate that using spoken instruction following data is crucial for
027           improving such a capability. Leveraging this insight, we build a synthetic
028           spoken instruction following dataset and train our SLM with it. Combining
029           the findings from our other experiments, we provide an effective recipe for
030           developing SLMs. Our model, called SiM, not only achieves strong ASR
031           performance but also significantly outperforms existing SLMs in spoken
032           instruction following.
033

035   1   Introduction
036
037   Since the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Jiang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023;
038   Brown, 2020; Radford et al., 2019; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Javaheripi et al., 2023; Zhou
039
      et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Tunstall et al., 2023), researchers have been striving to endow
      them with the ability to understand and follow multi-modal instructions, which may contain
040
      images, videos, speech, and non-speech sounds, in addition to texts (Li et al., 2024a;b;c; Lin
041
      et al., 2024). As speech is one of the most frequently used modes of communication among
042   humans, developing models capable of understanding speech and respond to spoken queries,
043   i.e., follow spoken instructions, is of great importance. Such a capability is indispensable for
044   building models with natural human-computer interaction interfaces, enabling a wide range
045   of applications such as voice assistants, speech-to-text transcription, and language learning
046   tools. Speech language models have the potential to revolutionize how we interact with
047   machines, making it more intuitive and accessible for users of all backgrounds. Furthermore,
048   these models can facilitate communication for individuals with disabilities, bridging the gap
049   between humans and technology.
050   An SLM takes one or more speech clips (and optionally texts) as inputs and generates text re-
051   sponses accordingly (Tang et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; 2024). Witnessing the great success
052   of LLMs and vision-conditioned LLMs (VLMs) (Liu et al., 2024b;a; Xue et al., 2024; Fang
053   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c;b; Chen et al., 2024), which adopt simple Transformer-based
      architectures and next-token prediction loss, most SLMs have followed a similar approach.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      They leverage pre-trained speech encoders, such as Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) or Con-
055   former (Gulati et al., 2020), to extract speech features from the input audio. These features
056   are treated as speech token embeddings and then projected into embedding space of text
057   tokens. The projected features are fed into an LLM alongside text token embeddings. While
058   being straightforward, this approach enables SLMs to be trained using next-token prediction
059   loss, harnessing the capabilities of both pre-trained speech encoders and LLMs. Such an
060   approach leads to the success development of several SLMs, including SALMONN (Tang
061   et al., 2023) and Qwen Audio Chat (Chu et al., 2023).
062   While SLMs share many similarities, they differ from one another in various aspects, such as
063   model architecture and training data. However, drawing meaningful comparisons between
064   existing SLMs is challenging due to the lack of consistent experimental settings across dif-
065   ferent works. As a result, it is diﬀicult to determine which design choices are truly superior
066   and how they impact model performance. This gap highlights the need for comprehensive
067   research that thoroughly examines the design space of SLMs and systematically compares
068   their design choices under comparable settings. Motivated by this need, we conduct rigorous
069
      experiments with consistent experimental setups to investigate the design space of SLMs,
      with the goal of identifying important design choices, understanding their influence on model
070
      performance, and exploring ways to optimize these choices for better overall performance.
071
072   In this work, we begin with the commonly used ”speech feature-as-token” approach. We
073   first explore different axes of alignment training, which aims to align features extracted by
074   pre-trained speech encoders with embeddings of text tokens, a process referred to as stage 1
075
      training or pre-training in some literature. We systematically investigate various aspects, in-
      cluding architecture, data, and the choice of LLM, in a step-by-step manner. Subsequently,
076
      we delve into how instruction tuning can enhance our models’ ability to follow speech in-
077
      structions. Surprisingly, we found that existing methods lack such an ability due to the
078   absence of spoken instructions in their instruction tuning data. While these models are
079   trained using thousands of hours of speech data, the speech clips serve as context, and the
080   instructions are provided in text form. Based on this finding, we build a synthetic spoken
081   instruction following dataset containing 50K samples with spoken instructions and demon-
082   strate that this significantly improves our model’s capability to follow speech instructions.
083   In summary, our key contributions are as follows::
084
            • We conduct rigorous experiments to thoroughly explore the design space of SLMs
085
              and compare different design choices.
086
087
            • Benefiting from our exploration, we provide an effective recipe for training our SLM,
              SiM, which achieves strong ASR performance and significantly outperforms existing
088
              models in spoken instruction following.

104   2.1   Model Architecture
105
106   Following SALMONN (Tang et al., 2023), Qwen Audio Chat (Chu et al., 2023), and recent
107   VLMs, including LLaVA, Prismatic VLMs, etc., we adopt the encoder-adaptor-LLM archi-
      tecture. Specifically, this architecture is composed of three components: a speech encoder,


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      an adaptor and an LLM. The speech encoder encodes speech clips, whose output is then
109   projected into the embedding space of the LLM by the adaptor. Formally, given an audio
110                                                                    ′      ′
      clip x, a speech encoder E encodes it into e = E(x). Here, e ∈ Rc ×t . c and t represent the
111
      dimension of speech features and number of speech feature vectors. An adaptor A projects
112
      speech features into the embedding space of text tokens via a = A(e), where a ∈ Rc×t . c
113   denote the dimension of token embeddings of the LLM. The projected features a are treated
114   as audio token embeddings and are (optionally) concatenated with text token embeddings
115   before being fed into the LLM.
116
117   2.2      Default Training Settings
118
119   We introduce the default training settings with which we start. Most of these settings will
120   be examined through experiments and will be changed if we find a better one.
121   We use Llama3 8B Instruct, which is a strong open source model, as our LLM. While some
122   existing SLMs are built upon Vicuna1 or Llama2, results obtained on a more recent and
123   stronger LLM will be more valuable for future researchers. We use a 2-layer Multi-Layer
124   Perceptron (MLP) as the adaptor. We adopt pre-trained Whisper Large v2 (Radford et al.,
125   2023) as our speech encoder (Chen et al., 2022; Baevski et al., 2020; Schneider et al., 2019;
126   Hsu et al., 2021) and process raw speech clips following Whisper.
127   For alignment training, we start with LibriSpeech dataset and use its train-clean 360 hour
128   subset, denoted as LibriSpeech 360, due to its wide adoption and high quality. We will
129   introduce instruction tuning data we used in later sections as we build our own dataset. We
130   perform full finetuning when optimizing paramters of the LLM.
131
132   2.3      Evaluation Suite
133
134   For alignment training, as we train our models to perform automatic speech recognition
135
      (ASR), we evaluate their ASR performance on LibriSpeech dataset (test-clean subset) and
      adopts word error rate (WER) as the evaluation metric. Following common practice (Rad-
136
      ford et al., 2023), we normalize both reference transcripts and model predictions before
137
      computing WER.
138
139   For instruction tuned models, we evaluate their ASR performance in the aforementioned
140   way. More importantly, we measure how well they follow spoken instructions on Alpaca
141
      Audio dataset (Wang et al., 2024a), OpenHermes Audio dataset (Wang et al., 2024a) and
      LLaMA Questions dataset (Nachmani et al., 2023). These datasets are composed of open-
142
      ended questions and reference answers. Model responses to spoken instruction from Alpaca
143
      Audio dataset and OpenHermes Audio dataset are judged by Llama3 70B Instruct (Dubey
144   et al., 2024). It assigns a score s, s ∈ Z ∩ [0, 5] to each model response. A score of 0 indicates
145   that the repsonse is of low quality, while a score of 5 are assigned to high quality responses.
146   More details can be found in (Wang et al., 2024a). Accuracy is used as the evaluation metrics
147   to measure model performance on LLaMA Questions dataset (Nachmani et al., 2023). We
148   refer our readers to (Nachmani et al., 2023) for more details.

229   time and the potential of harming the LLM’s capability to handle text prompts. Hence,
230   we choose to train the adaptor only and leave an in-depth exploration of joint adaptor and
231
      LLM training for future work.

417   Qwen achieves the lowest WER, SiM’s WER is comparable to existing SLMs. Note that
418   SiM only uses less than one thirds of ASR samples examples compared to Qwen. We also
419   present qualitative ASR examples in Figure 3.

421   4    Conclusion
422
423   In this work, we conduct a thorough exploration of the design space of SLMs through
424   rigorous experiments under comparable settings. Our key insights: (1) Surprisingly, a
425   simple 2-layer MLP adaptor works better than more complex architectures. (2) Using an
426   instruction-tuned LLM is favorable over a base LLM. (3) A combination of the Common-
427   Voice and LibriSpeech datasets is a suitable choice for alignment training. In addition,
428
      we uncover that including spoken instructions in the instruction tuning data is essential
      to enable SLMs to follow spoken instructions effectively, a capability lacking in existing
429
      models.
430
431   Leveraging these insights and our synthetic spoken instruction data, we present SiM, our
      SLM model. SiM significantly outperforms existing SLMs in spoken instruction following
