009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Task Learning (MTL) aims to minimize negative transfer within a shared
012            network. Common strategies involve separating task-generic and task-specific
013            representations and coordinating them to work together effectively within MTL
014            frameworks. However, the absence of a clear rule for determining task-specific
015            network components challenges the design of efficient MTL architectures. Our
016            method tackles negative transfer by employing token-based network expansion
017            and modulation without directly modifying predefined architectures, making it
018
               adaptable to any transformer-based MTL architectures. To evaluate negative trans-
               fer, we treat tokens as parameters, assessing gradient conflicts during backpropa-
019
               gation. Conflicts between tasks are analyzed by examining the token’s range space
020
               and null space. Based on conflict types, we expand the network following rules.
021            If task-specific gradients clash in the tokens’ range space, we modulate existing
022            tokens to align their task gradients. Conversely, if the gradients conflict in the null
023            space of tokens, we add new task-specific tokens, spanning a new feature space.
024            Our approach effectively boosts multi-task performance across various datasets by
025            being integrated into previous state-of-the-art multi-task architectures.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multi-task learning in computer vision is an essential technique for creating efficient and effective
031   deep learning models that can work with a unified architecture for multiple tasks (Caruana, 1997),
032   resulting in better generalization and faster convergence. Additionally, by combining related tasks
033
      into one model, the need for expensive computing and storage resources is reduced, making it a
      more viable option for a variety of applications.
034
035   MTL aims to minimize negative transfer (Crawshaw, 2020) across various tasks, as negative trans-
036   fer occurs when learning one task hinders the performance of others. This can lead to a trade-off
037   among tasks due to their distinct objectives. To address this, prior research on multi-task archi-
038   tectures predominantly concentrates on determining the type of information the architecture should
039
      learn for accurate predictions. Ye & Xu (2022b) classifies this information into three dimensions:
      task-generic representations, task-specific representations, and cross-task interactions. In previous
040
      studies (Eigen & Fergus, 2015; Xu et al., 2018; Vandenhende et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019; Dai
041
      et al., 2016; Ma et al., 2018; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Zhang et al., 2014), a shared encoder
042   is employed to learn generic representations, while task-specific features are refined in the decoder
043   through cross-task interactions. Conversely, cross-talk architecture utilizes separate symmetrical
044   networks for each task, incorporating cross-task interactions (Gao et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2018).
045   Another approach (Maninis et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2021; Sinha et al., 2018; Fernando et al., 2017)
046   involves dividing task-generic and task-specific information using task-specific modules.
047
      Lately, multi-task architectures based on transformers have not only shown impressive performance
048   across various tasks but have also excelled in the few-shot learning setting (Kim et al., 2023). These
049   advancements draw inspiration from the success observed in the NLP domain (Shazeer et al., 2017).
050   The two most prominent types of transformer-based multi-task paradigms are MoE (Riquelme et al.,
051   2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) and Task Prompter
052   (Xu et al., 2023a;b; Ye & Xu, 2022b). MoE (Mixture of Experts) employs distinct specialized expert
053   modules to learn various aspects of tasks. It utilizes a gating mechanism to decide which combi-
      nation of experts should contribute to the final output. Task Prompter guides the model to learn

054
      task-specific information by providing task-specific prompts. Previous research relies on manually
055   designed modules, leading to a lack of generality in distinguishing shared and task-specific repre-
056   sentations. MoE involves predefined number of expert modules into a backbone network. Similarly,
057   Task Prompter requires predefined task prompts and modules that support their interaction.
058
      In this context, a pivotal question arises: can a pre-defined module efficiently handle parameters, or
059
      is a pre-defined task space sufficient to encompass all task-specific information? From the analysis
060
      of previous works in transfer learning (Dwivedi & Roig, 2019), we identify three reasons why a
061   predefined space cannot efficiently capture task-specific information. Firstly, the similarity between
062   tasks changes as we move through the network’s depth, implying that the extent of shared network el-
063   ements should differ based on the network’s depth. Secondly, the required task-specific space within
064   the network for a task is not consistently uniform across depth. For tasks such as semantic segmen-
065   tation, a substantial amount of space in deeper layers is required to leverage semantic information.
066   In contrast, low-level vision tasks like surface normal estimation might necessitate more space in
067   relatively shallower layers. Thirdly, these variations are dependent on the dataset being used. As a
068   result, current multi-task architectures face inefficiencies due to the predefined modules for learning
069
      shared and task-specific information. In this study, we present a network expansion paradigm that
      can be applied to any transformer-based multi-task architecture to mitigate this inefficiency.
070
071   In order to improve the adaptability of a multi-task architecture by dynamically partitioning task-
072   generic and task-specific representations, we focus on the concept of conflicting gradients (Yu et al.,
073   2020). Conflicting gradients are recognized as a cause of negative transfer that emerges when the
074   gradients of two tasks move in opposing directions. In contrast to previous approaches (Guangyuan
075
      et al., 2022) that transform shared parameters into task-specific ones by duplicating them, we lever-
      age tokens of the transformer in our method. This choice not only enhances parameter efficiency but
076
      also extends applicability across various architectures. To expand the network based on tokens and
077
      prevent negative transfer by guaranteeing adequate space for tasks, we start by defining the token
078   space as the output of each layer in the transformer block using singular value decomposition (SVD).
079   Subsequently, we categorize gradient conflicts into two types: conflicts in the range space and null
080   space of tokens. If task-specific gradients conflict within the token range space, we modulate tokens
081   in that layer to align the gradients of different tasks. Conversely, if conflicts arise within the null
082   space of tokens, we introduce new task-specific tokens to the network to learn new task-specific
083   features. Importantly, our methods can be applied concurrently to previous multi-task architectures
084   (Shazeer et al., 2017; Riquelme et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2022;
085   Chen et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a;b; Ye & Xu, 2022b) or network expansion methods (Guangyuan
086
      et al., 2022). In summary, our main contribution is three-fold:
087   • We introducea Dynamic Token Modulation and Expansion (DTME-MTL) approach for
088     transformer-based multi-task architectures, which effectively reduces negative transfer caused by
089     gradient conflicts. As far as we know, this is the first work dynamically expanding the network by
090     manipulating tokens for MTL.
091   • We analyze conflicts between tasks in both token range space and null space, proposing diverse
092     methodologies for token manipulation based on the nature of the conflict. If task-specific gradients
093     conflict within the token range space, we modulate existing tokens in that layer. On the other hand,
094     if conflicts arise within the null space, we introduce new task-specific tokens to the network. This
095     approach, involving distinct response strategies for each conflict type, leads to the creation of an
096     efficient network expansion system applicable to various existing multi-task architectures.
097   • DTME-MTL can be applied to existing state-of-the-art multi-task architectures in an off-the-shelf
098     manner to enhance multi-task performance. We compare it with other off-the-shelf multi-task
099     optimization methods to evaluate how effectively it mitigates negative transfer.

102
103   Multi-Task Learning in Vision Transformers. Originally designed for NLP tasks, transformers
104   have outperformed existing CNN models in various computer vision tasks. Attempts have been
105   made to incorporate Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021c; Wang et al.,
106   2021a; Yang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021b) in MTL. MTFormer (Xu et al., 2022)
107   employs a shared transformer encoder and decoder with a cross-task attention mechanism. MulT
      (Bhattacharjee et al., 2022) utilizes a shared attention mechanism to model task dependencies based

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on the Swin transformer. InvPT (Ye & Xu, 2022a) focuses on global spatial position and multi-
109   task context for dense prediction tasks through multi-scale feature aggregation. Mixture of Experts
110   (MoE), inspired by the NLP domain, divides the model into predefined expert groups, adaptively
111   shared or devoted to specific tasks during the learning phase (Riquelme et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
112   2022; Fan et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). Task prompter
113   (Xu et al., 2023a;b; Ye & Xu, 2022b) uses task-specific tokens to encapsulate task-specific infor-
114   mation and employs cross-task interactions to enhance multi-task performance. Prior studies need
115   either a manually designed module to divide shared and task-specific representations, leading to a
116   lack of generality. On the contrary, our methods can be applied to a diverse range of multi-task
117
      architectures, including those mentioned earlier.
118   Multi-Task Optimization. Optimizing the MTL aims to address negative transfer by adjusting the
119   relative weighting of task losses or directly manipulating gradients. Task-dependent uncertainty
120   (Kendall et al., 2018) is utilized to weigh the loss of multiple tasks. Liu et al. (2019) considers
121   the rate of loss descent for achieving balance, while (Guo et al., 2018) prioritizes tasks based on
122   difficulty. Recently, Liu et al. (2024) proposed updating task weights based on the loss history. In
123
      contrast, approaches like (Désidéri, 2012; Sener & Koltun, 2018; Yu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a;b;
      Navon et al., 2022; Senushkin et al., 2023) directly modify task gradients to achieve the desired bal-
124
      ance. PCGrad (Yu et al., 2020) analyzes negative transfer by identifying conflicting gradients in the
125
      shared parameters of the network. Recon (Guangyuan et al., 2022) transforms shared parameters di-
126   rectly into task-specific ones to handle conflicting gradients. Normalized gradients are employed to
127   prevent spillover between tasks (Chen et al., 2018), whereas Chen et al. (2020) introduce stochastic-
128   ity to the network’s parameters based on the consistency in the sign of gradients. RotoGrad (Javaloy
129   & Valera, 2021) rotates the feature space of the network to narrow the gap between tasks.

      3   P RELIMINARIES
132
133
      In multi-task learning, the network learns a set of tasks {τi }K  i=1 jointly, where K is the number
134   of tasks. Each task τi has its own loss function Li . The network parameter Θ can be classified
135   into Θ = {Θs , Θ1 , ..., ΘK } where Θs is shared parameter across all tasks and Θi is task-specific
136   parameters devoted to task τi . Then, the objective function of multi-task learning is to minimize
                                                                PK
137   the weighted sum of all tasks’ losses: Θ∗ = arg minΘ i=1 wi Li (Θs , Θi ) where wi represents the
138   scale of the task-specific loss Li . Negative transfer between tasks occurs when the gradients of each
139   objective point in different directions, a phenomenon called conflicting gradients (Yu et al., 2020).
140   Definition 1 (Conflicting gradients). Define gi as the gradient of task τi with respect to the shared
141   parameters Θs as gi = ∇Θs Li (Θs , Θi ). Let gi and gj represent the gradients for a pair of tasks τi
142   and τj where i ̸= j. If gi · gj ≤ 0, these two gradients are termed conflicting gradients.
143
144   The relationship between negative transfer and conflicting gradients is debated, with some studies
145
      taking opposing views. Jiang et al. (2024) presents a counterexample challenging the positive link
      between negative transfer and conflicting gradients in the context of auxiliary task learning. How-
146
      ever, we adopt the conventional stance that conflicting gradients are widely seen as a key factor con-
147
      tributing to negative transfer in multi-task learning optimization (Désidéri, 2012; Sener & Koltun,
148   2018; Yu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a;b; Navon et al., 2022; Senushkin et al., 2023; Jeong & Yoon,
149   2024), where tasks are primarily learned jointly rather than serving as auxiliary tasks for others.
150   Existing methods that use pre-defined architectures for MTL have limitations in reducing negative
151   transfer since they cannot preemptively prevent the occurrence of conflicting gradients. Guangyuan
152   et al. (2022) involves transforming a shared layer into task-specific layers when conflicting gradi-
153   ents are detected in that layer. However, this method exhibits inefficiency in terms of the number of
154   parameters, as it duplicates layers by a factor of the number of tasks, K. In our approach, we adopt
155   a more efficient token-based network expansion system instead of merely increasing the number of
156
      layers. Furthermore, we categorize gradient conflict into two types, presenting varied methodologies
      based on the nature of the conflict.

301
302   From the types of gradient conflicts we defined in section 4.2, we present effective methods for token
303   modulation and expansion in multi-task architectures based on transformers. For each transformer
304
      block with a depth of d, we can compute task-specific gradients {gi }K      i=1 for the shared token Ts . By
      utilizing eq. (4), we identify the specific types of conflicts that arise in a transformer block for a given
305
      input data Xi . The extent of conflict is assessed by counting the occurrences of gradient conflicts
306
      across all data {Xi , Yi }ni=1 . To identify the layers with the most severe competition between tasks,
307   we select the most conflicting layers to relieve negative transfer. The number of layers is a tunable
308   hyperparameter controlled through network expansion.
309
310
      Token Modulation. In situations where task-specific gradients conflict within the range space of
311   Tesd , such as gR,i · gR,j ≤ 0, modulators Mi and Mj are added after the shared token Tsd as shown
312
      in fig. 1-(b). The token modulator M is a straightforward affine transformation that modulates the
      shared token Ts along the channel dimension. To elaborate, considering the embedding dimension of
313
      the transformer as dmodel (distinct from the layer depth d) and assuming the number of shared tokens
314
      is N , we can arrange Ts in the form [Ts,1 , . . . , Ts,N ]. This arrangement turns Ts into a dmodel × N
315   matrix. The modulator M then performs the transformation W [Ts,1 , . . . , Ts,N ] + b using weight W
316   and bias b, both of which have dimensions 1 × dmodel .
317
318

      encing the range space spanned by tokens, as shown in fig. 3-(a). During the learning process, the
319

320
      ents. This simple affine transformation is highly parameter-efficient in dealing with negative transfer
321   resulting from conflicts in the range space of gradients.

531   6      C ONCLUSION
532
533   This paper presents Dynamic Token Modulation and Expansion for Multi-Task Learning (DTME-
534   MTL), a novel approach aimed at improving transformer-based multi-task architectures by address-
535   ing gradient conflicts among tasks. We categorize conflicts between tasks based on whether they
536   occur within token range space or null space. Using this categorization, we adaptively apply token
537   modulation and expansion to mitigate these conflicts. The proposed system effectively reduces task
538
      conflicts, leading to enhanced multi-task performance. Our method can be easily integrated into dif-
      ferent transformer-based multi-task architectures with only a small number of additional parameters,
539
      achieving superior performance on various multi-task benchmarks.

908   Multi-Task Architectures. Various multi-task architectures can be categorized based on how the
909
      parameters or features of the sharing network are distributed among tasks. The widely used shared
      trunk structure comprises a common encoder shared by multiple tasks and a dedicated decoder for
910
      each task (Dai et al., 2016; Ma et al., 2018; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Zhang et al., 2014). A
911
      tree-like architecture, with multiple division points for each task group, offers a more generalized
912   structure (Lu et al., 2017; Vandenhende et al., 2019; Bruggemann et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020).
913   The cross-talk architecture employs separate symmetrical networks for each task, utilizing feature
914   exchange between layers at the same depth for information sharing between tasks (Gao et al., 2019;
915   Xu et al., 2018). The prediction distillation model (Eigen & Fergus, 2015; Xu et al., 2018; Vanden-
916   hende et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2019) incorporates cross-task interactions at the end of the shared
917   encoder, while the task switching network (Sun et al., 2021; Sinha et al., 2018; Fernando et al., 2017;
      Maninis et al., 2019) changes network parameters depending on the task.
