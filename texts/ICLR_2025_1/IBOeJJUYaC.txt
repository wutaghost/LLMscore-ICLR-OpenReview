009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Mesh-free Lagrangian methods are widely used for simulating fluids, solids, and
012
               their complex interactions due to their ability to handle large deformations and
013
               topological changes. These physics simulators, however, require substantial com-
014            putational resources for accurate simulations. To address these issues, deep learn-
015            ing emulators promise faster and scalable simulations, yet they often remain ex-
016            pensive and difficult to train, limiting their practical use. Inspired by the Material
017            Point Method (MPM), we present NeuralMPM, a neural emulation framework for
018            particle-based simulations. NeuralMPM interpolates Lagrangian particles onto a
019            fixed-size grid, computes updates on grid nodes using image-to-image neural net-
020            works, and interpolates back to the particles. Similarly to MPM, NeuralMPM
021            benefits from the regular voxelized representation to simplify the computation of
022
               the state dynamics, while avoiding the drawbacks of mesh-based Eulerian meth-
               ods. We demonstrate the advantages of NeuralMPM on 6 datasets, including fluid
023
               dynamics and fluid-solid interactions simulated with MPM and Smoothed Parti-
024
               cles Hydrodynamics (SPH). Compared to GNS and DMCF, NeuralMPM reduces
025            training time from 10 days to 15 hours, memory consumption by 10x-100x, and in-
026            creases inference speed by 5x-10x, while achieving comparable or superior long-
027            term accuracy, making it a promising approach for practical forward and inverse
028            problems. A project page is available at [URL].

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The Navier-Stokes equations describe the time evolution of fluids and their interactions with solid
035   materials. As analytical solutions rarely exist, numerical methods are required to approximate the
036   solutions. On the one hand, Eulerian methods discretize the fluid domain on a fixed grid, simplifying
037   the computation of the dynamics, but requiring high-resolution meshes to solve small-scale details
038   in the flow. Lagrangian methods, on the other hand, represent the fluid as virtual moving particles
039
      defining the system’s state, hence maintaining a high level of detail in regions of high density. While
      effective at handling deformations and topological changes (Monaghan, 2012), Lagrangian methods
040
      struggle with collisions and interactions with rigid objects (Lind et al., 2020; Vacondio et al., 2021).
041
042   Regardless of the discretization strategy, large-scale high-resolution numerical simulations are com-
043   putationally expensive, limiting their practical use in downstream tasks such as forecasting, inverse
044   problems, or computational design. To address these issues, deep learning emulators have shown
045
      promise in accelerating simulations by learning an emulator model that can predict the system’s
      state at a fraction of the cost. Next to their speed, neural emulators also have the strategic advantage
046
      of being differentiable, enabling their use in inverse problems and optimization tasks (Allen et al.,
047
      2022; Forte et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022). Moreover, they have the potential to be learned directly
048   from real data, bypassing the costly and resource-intensive process of building a simulator (He et al.,
049   2019; Jumper et al., 2021; Lam et al., 2023; Lemos et al., 2023; Pfaff et al., 2021). In this direction,
050   particle-based neural emulators (Prantl et al., 2022; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Ummenhofer
051   et al., 2020) have seen success in accurately simulating fluids and generalizing to unseen environ-
052   ments. These emulators, however, suffer from the same issues as traditional Lagrangian methods,
053   with collisions and interactions with rigid objects being particularly challenging. These emulators
      may also require long training and inference times, limiting their practical use.

054
      Taking inspiration from the hybrid Material Point Method (MPM) (Nguyen et al., 2023; Sulsky et al.,
055   1993) that combines the strengths of both Eulerian and Lagrangian methods, we introduce Neu-
056   ralMPM, a neural emulation framework for particle-based simulations. As in MPM, NeuralMPM
057   maintains Lagrangian particles to represent the system’s state but models the system dynamics on
058   voxelized representations. In this way, NeuralMPM benefits from a regular grid structure to simplify
059   the computation of the state dynamics but avoids the drawbacks of mesh-based Eulerian methods.
060   By interpolating the particles onto a fixed-size grid, it also bypasses the need to perform an expen-
061   sive neighbor search at every timestep, substituting it with two interpolation steps based on cheap
062   voxelization (Xu et al., 2021). By defining the system dynamics on a grid, NeuralMPM can also
063
      leverage well-established grid-to-grid neural architectures. The resulting inductive bias allows the
      model to more easily process the global and local structures of the point cloud, instead of having to
064
      discover them, and frees capacity for learning the dynamics of the system represented by the grid.
065
      Compared to previous data-driven approaches (Prantl et al., 2022; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020;
066   Ummenhofer et al., 2020), these improvements reduce the training time from days to hours, while
067   achieving higher or comparable accuracy.

070   2    C OMPUTATIONAL F LUID DYNAMICS
071
072   Computational fluid dynamics simulations can be classified into two broad categories, Eulerian and
073
      Lagrangian, depending on the discretization of the fluid (Rakhsha et al., 2021). In Eulerian simula-
      tions, the domain is discretized with a mesh, with state variables uti (such as mass or momentum)
074
      maintained at each mesh point i. Well-known examples of Eulerian simulations are the finite differ-
075
      ence method, where the domain is divided into a uniform regular grid (also called an Eulerian grid),
076   and the finite element method, where the domain is divided into regions, or elements, that may have
077   different shapes and density, allowing to increase the resolution in only some areas of the domain
078   (Iserles, 2008; Morton & Mayers, 2005). Lagrangian simulations, on the other hand, discretize the
079   fluid as a set of virtual moving particles {pti , uti }N
                                                             i=1 , each described by its position pi and state
                                                                                                     t

080   variables ui that include the particle velocity vi . To simulate the fluid, the particles move according
                  t                                    t

081   to the dynamics of the system, producing a new set of particles {pt+1   i    , ut+1
                                                                                      i    i=1 at each timestep.
                                                                                          }N
082   Simulations in Lagrangian coordinates are particularly useful when the fluid is highly deformable, as
083   the particles can move freely and adapt to the fluid’s shape. Among Lagrangian methods, Smoothed
084   Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is one of the most popular, where the fluid is represented by a set of
085
      particles that interact with each other through a kernel function that smooths the interactions.
086   Hybrid Eulerian-Lagrangian methods combine the strengths of both frameworks. Like Lagrangian
087   methods, they carry the system state information via particles, thereby automatically adjusting the
088   resolution to the local density of the system. By using a regular grid, however, they simplify gradient
089   computation, make entity contact detection easier, and prevent cracks from propagating only along
090   the mesh. Among hybrid methods, the Material Point Method has gained popularity for its ability
091
      to handle large deformations and topological changes. MPM combines a regular Eulerian grid with
      moving Lagrangian particles. It does so in four main steps: (1) the quantities carried by the particles
092
      are interpolated onto a regular grid Gt = p2g({pti , uti }) using a particle-to-grid (p2g) function, (2)
093
      the equations of motion are solved on the grid, where derivatives and other quantities are easier to
094   compute, resulting in a new grid state Gt+1 = f (Gt ), (3) the resulting dynamics are interpolated
095
      back onto the particles as {ut+1
                                    i   } = g2p(Gt+1 , {pti }), using a grid-to-particle (g2p) function, (4) the
096   positions of the particles are updated by computing particle-wise velocities and using an appropriate
097   integrator, such as Euler, i.e., pt+1
                                         i    = pti + ∆tvit+1 . The grid values are then reset for the next
098   step. MPM has been used in soft tissue simulations (Ionescu et al., 2005), in molecular dynamics
099   (Lu et al., 2006), in astrophysics (Li & Liu, 2002), in fluid-membrane interactions (York II et al.,
100   2000), and in simulating cracks (Daphalapurkar et al., 2007) and landslides (Llano Serna et al.,
101   2015). MPM is also widely used in the animation industry, perhaps most notably in Disney’s 2013
102   film Frozen (Stomakhin et al., 2013), where it was used to simulate snow.
103   Notwithstanding the success of numerical simulators, they remain expensive, slow, and, most of the
104   time, non-differentiable. In recent years, differentiable neural emulators have shown great promise
105   in accelerating fluid simulations, most notably in a series of works to emulate SPH simulations
106   in a fully data-driven manner. Graph network-based simulators (GNS) (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al.,
107   2020) use a graph neural network (GNN) and a graph built from the local neighborhood of the
      particles to predict the acceleration of the system. The approach requires building a graph out


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of the point cloud at every timestep to obtain structural information about the cloud, which is an
109   expensive operation. In addition, the GNN needs to extract global information from its nodes, which
110   is only possible with a high number of message-passing steps, resulting in a large computational
111   graph and long training and inference times. This large computational graph, along with repeated
112   construction, makes fully autoregressive training over long rollouts impractical, as the gradients need
113   to backpropagate all the way back to the first step. Cheaper strategies exist, like the push-forward
114   trick (Brandstetter et al., 2022b), but they have been shown to be inferior to fully backpropagating
115   through trajectories (List et al., 2024; Sharabi & Louppe, 2023). As autoregressive training is not
116   available, the stability of the learned dynamics can be compromised, making the model prone to
117
      diverging or oscillating. Noise injection training strategies can be used to increase the stability
      of the rollouts, but the magnitude of the noise becomes a critical parameter. Han et al. (2022)
118
      introduce improvements to GNS to make them subequivariant to certain transformations. They show
119
      increased accuracy on simulations involving solid objects. An alternative approach is the continuous
120   convolution (CConv) (Ummenhofer et al., 2020; Winchenbach & Thuerey, 2024), an extension of
121   convolutional networks to point clouds. In this method, a convolutional kernel is applied to each
122   particle by interpolating the values of the kernel at the positions of its neighbors, which are found via
123   spatial hashing on GPU, a cheaper alternative to tree-based searches that allows for autoregressive
124   training. In (Prantl et al., 2022), Deep Momentum Conserving Fluids (DMCF) build upon CConv to
125   design a momentum-conserving architecture. Nevertheless, to account for long-range interactions,
126   the authors introduce different branches, with different receptive fields, into their network. The
127   number of branches, and their hyperparameters, need to be tuned to capture global dependencies,
128
      leading to long training times even with optimized CUDA kernels. Finally, Zhang et al. (2020),
      propose an approach that uses nearest neighbors to construct the local features of each particle.
129
      Those local features are then averaged onto a regular grid. Like GNS, this method suffers from
130
      the need to repeat the neighbor search at every simulation timestep. Ultimately, the performance of
131   point cloud-based simulators is tightly linked to the method used to process the spatial structure of
132   the cloud. Brute force neighbour search is O(N 2 ), K-d trees are O(N log N ), and voxelization and
133   hashing are O(N ) (Hastings & Mesit, 2005; Xu et al., 2021).
134
      An alternative to data-driven modeling is the use of hybrid models, where parts of a classical solver
135
      are replaced with learned components. For instance, Yin et al. (2021) employ a neural network to
136
      learn unknown physics, which is then integrated into a simulator. Similarly, Li et al. (2024) use
137   a neural network to bypass computational bottlenecks in MPM simulators, while Ma et al. (2023)
138   learn general constitutive laws, allowing for one-shot trajectory learning. These approaches achieve
139   impressive results by leveraging extensive physics knowledge, but this reliance also limits their
140   applicability. Hybrid models inherit both the strengths and weaknesses of classical and ML methods.

      3    N EURAL MPM
144
145
146   We consider a Lagrangian system evolving in time and defined by the positions pti and velocities vit
147   of a set of N particles i = 1, ..., N . We denote with P t and V t the set of positions and velocities
148   of all particles at time t and with S t = (P t , V t ) the full state of the system. In a more complex
149
      setting, the state of the system can include other local properties, such as pressure or elastic stiffness
      of materials, and global properties, such as an external force. In this work, for simplicity, we let
150
      the network learn the relevant simulation parameters implicitly. The evolution of the particles is
151
      described by a function f mapping the current state of the system to its next state S t+1 = f (S t ).
152   Given a starting system S 0 = (P 0 , V 0 ), its full trajectory, or rollout, is denoted by S 1:T . Our goal
153
      is to build an emulator fˆθ (·) capable of predicting a full rollout fˆθ1:T (S 0 ) of T timesteps from the
154   initial state S 0 . Following MPM, NeuralMPM operates in four steps, as illustrated in Figure 1:
155
156
157   Step 1: Voxelization. Using the particle positions P t , the velocities V t are interpolated onto
158   a regular fixed-size grid. This interpolation is performed through voxelization, which divides the
159   domain into regular volumes (voxels). Each grid node is identified as the center of a voxel (e.g.,
160   square in 2D) in the domain, and the velocities of the particles in the voxel are averaged to give
161   the node’s velocity. Similarly, the density is computed as the normalized number of particles in the
      voxel. This results in the grid tensor Gt that contains the grid velocities Vgt and density Dgt .

      Figure 1: NeuralMPM works in 4 steps. (1) The positions P t and velocities V t of the particles are
179
      used to compute the velocity Vgt and density Dgt of each grid node through voxelization. (2) From
180   this grid, the processor neural network predicts the grid velocities at the next m timesteps. The
181   next m positions are computed iteratively by (3) performing bilinear interpolation of the predicted
182   velocities onto the previous positions and (4) updating the positions using the predicted velocities.
183
184
185   Step 2: Processing. Taking advantage of the regular grid representation of the cloud, the grid
186   velocities {V̂ i }t+m
                        i=t+1 of the next m timesteps are predicted using a neural network. We chose a U-
187   Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) as it is a well-established image-to-image model, known to perform
188   well in various tasks, including physical applications. The combination of kernels applied with
189   different receptive fields (from smaller to larger) allows the U-Net to efficiently extract both local
190
      and global information. Nonetheless, any grid-to-grid architecture could be used. We experiment
      with the FNO (Li et al., 2021) architecture in Appendix B and find it to underperform, leading us
191
      to keep the U-Net. A fully convolutional U-Net and an FNO have the additional advantage of being
192
      able to generalize to different domain shapes, a desirable property (Section 4.3).
193
194
      Step 3: Update of particle velocities. The predicted velocities V̂ t+1 at the next timestep are then
195
      interpolated back to the particle level onto the positions P t using bilinear interpolation. The velocity
196
      of each particle is computed as a weighted average of the four surrounding grid velocities, based on
197   its Euclidean distance to each of them.
198
199
      Step 4: Update of particle positions. Finally, the positions of the particles are updated with Euler
200
      integration using the next velocities and known current positions of the particles, that is P̂ t+1 =
201
      P t + ∆tV̂ t+1 . Steps 3 and 4 are performed m times to compute the next m positions from the set
202
      of grid velocities computed at step 2.
203
204   Additional features of the individual particles can be included in the grid tensor Gt by interpolating
205   them in the same way as the velocities. Local, such as boundary conditions, or global, such as gravity
206
      or external forces, features are represented as grid channels. For simulations with multiple types of
      particles, the features of each material are interpolated independently and stacked as channels in Gt .
207
208   NeuralMPM is trained end-to-end on a set of trajectories S 0:T to minimize the mean squared error
209   ||P t+1 − P̂θt+1 (S t )||22 between the ground-truth and predicted next positions of the particles. At
210   inference time, the model is exposed to much longer sequences, which requires carefully stabiliz-
211   ing the rollout procedure to prevent the accumulation of large errors over time. To address this,
212   we first make use of autoregressive training (Prantl et al., 2022; Ummenhofer et al., 2020), where
213   the model is unrolled K times on its own predictions, producing a sequence of Ŝ k = fˆθ (Ŝ k−1 )
214   for k = 1, ..., K and initial input Ŝ 0 = S 0 , before backpropagating the error through the entire
215   rollout. Unlike more costly methods that require alternative stabilization strategies, such as noise
      injection (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020), NeuralMPM’s efficiency makes autoregressive training


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      possible. Nevertheless, to further stabilize the training, we couple autoregressive training with time
217   bundling (Brandstetter et al., 2022b), resulting in a training strategy where the model predicts m
218
      steps Ŝ 1:m at once from a single initial state, inside an outer autoregressive loop of K steps of
219   length m. We show in Section 4 that this training strategy leads to more accurate rollouts.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
223
224
      We conduct a series of experiments to demonstrate the accuracy, speed, and generalization capa-
      bilities of NeuralMPM. Specifically, we examine its robustness to hyperparameter and architectural
225
      choices through an ablation study (4.1). We compare NeuralMPM to GNS and DMCF in terms of
226
      accuracy, training time, convergence, and inference speed (4.2). We also evaluate the generalization
227   capabilities of NeuralMPM (4.3) and illustrate how its differentiability can be leveraged to solve
228   an inverse design problem (4.4). Through these experiments, we demonstrate that NeuralMPM is a
229   flexible, accurate, and fast method for emulating complex particle-based simulations. The baselines
230   established by Winchenbach & Thuerey (2024) and hybrid simulators (Li et al., 2024; Ma et al.,
231   2023) have promising results. However, we do not compare against them as they either use differ-
232   ent benchmarks or are specifically tailored for certain physical domains, requiring material-specific
233   knowledge. In contrast, NeuralMPM, like GNS and DMCF, requires only particle positions without
234   being restricted to any particular domain.
235
236
      Data. We consider 6 datasets with variable sequence lengths, numbers of particles, and materials.
      The first three datasets, WATER R AMPS, S AND R AMPS, and G OOP, contain a single material, wa-
237
      ter, sand, and goop, respectively, with different material properties. The first two datasets contain
238
      random ramp obstacles to challenge the model’s generalization capacity. The fourth dataset, M ULTI -
239   M ATERIAL, mixes the three materials together in the same simulations. These four datasets are taken
240   from Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) and were simulated using the Taichi-MPM simulator (Hu et al.,
241   2018b). They each contain 1000 trajectories for training and 30 (G OOP) or 100 (WATER R AMPS,
242   S AND R AMPS, M ULTI M ATERIAL) for validation and testing. The fifth dataset, DAM B REAK 2D,
243   was generated using SPH and contains 50 trajectories for learning, and 25 for validation and testing.
244   The last dataset, VARIABLE G RAVITY, was also generated using Taichi-MPM. It consists of simu-
245   lations with variable gravity of a water-like material, and contains 1000 trajectories for training and
246   100 for validation and testing.
247
248
      Protocol. NeuralMPM is trained on trajectories with varying initial conditions and number of
      particles. The training batches are sampled randomly in time and across sequences. We use
249
      Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with the following learning rate schedule: a linear warm-up over
250
      100 steps from 10−5 to 10−3 , 900 steps at 10−3 , then a cosine annealing (Loshchilov & Hutter,
251   2017) for 100, 000 iterations. We use a batch size of 128, K = 4 autoregressive steps per iteration,
252   bundle m = 8 timesteps per model call (resulting in 24 predicted states), and a grid size of 64 × 64.
253   For most of our experiments, we use a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) with three downsampling
254   blocks with a factor of 2, 64 hidden channels, a kernel size of 3, and MLPs with three hidden lay-
255   ers of size 64 for pixel-wise encoding and decoding into a latent space. For a fair comparison, we
256   ran training and inference for NeuralMPM, DMCF, and GNS on the exact same hardware. GNS
257   and DMCF were trained until convergence (a maximum of 120 and 240 hours, respectively), while
258   NeuralMPM required 20 hours or less to converge. For WATER R AMPS, S ANDRAMPS, G OOP, and
259
      M ULTI M ATERIAL, we use the same parameters as those reported by authors. We hyperparameter
      search DMCF for DAM B REAK 2D and both GNS and DMCF for VARIABLE G RAVITY and report
260
      the best performance obtained for a budget of 60 GPU-days per dataset. Further details on training
261
      can be found in Appendix A.

      Figure 3 summarizes the ablation results. A larger number K of autoregressive steps yields more
350
      accurate rollouts without the need to add noise. Indeed, injecting noise does not improve accuracy
351   and is even detrimental for K = 4. Individually tuning the noise levels for grids and particles
352   can modestly lower error rates, but is either very sensitive or negligible. The model performs bet-
353   ter when bundling more timesteps, enabling faster rollouts as a single forward pass predicts more
354   steps. We found m = 8 to be optimal with the other default hyperparameters, outperforming larger
355   bundling. This is because more network capacity is needed to extract information for the next 16 or
356   32 timesteps from a single state. Instead, we opted for a shallower and narrower network to balance
357   speed and memory footprint with performance gains. In terms of network architecture, we chose a
358   U-Net. We experiment with an FNO (Li et al., 2021) in Appendix B and find it to underperform,
359
      leading us to keep the U-Net architecture. We find the U-Net’s width and depth to have a minor
      impact on performance, confirming that a larger network is not needed. The grid size, however, is
360
      critical. A low resolution loses fine details, while a high resolution turns meaningful structures, such
361
      as liquid blobs or walls, into isolated voxels.

497   4.4   I NVERSE DESIGN PROBLEM
498
      Finally, we demonstrate the application of NeuralMPM for inverse problems on a toy inverse design
499
      task that consists in optimizing the direction of a ramp to make the particles reach a target location,
500   similar to (Allen et al., 2022). We place a blob of water at different starting locations, and we then
501   place a ramp at some location, with a random initial angle α. The goal is to spin the ramp by tuning
502   α in order to make the water end up at a desired location. The main challenges of this task are the
503   long-range time horizon of the goal and the presence of nonlinear physical dynamics. We proceed
504   by selecting the point where we want the water to end up and compute the average distance between
505   the point and particles at the last simulation frame. We then minimize the distance via gradient
506   descent, leveraging the differentiability of NeuralMPM to solve this inverse design problem. We
507   show am example optimization in Figure 8, and additional examples in Appendix B.

518   5     C ONCLUSION
519
520   Summary. We presented NeuralMPM, a neural emulation framework for particle-based simula-
521   tions inspired by the hybrid Eulerian-Lagrangian Material Point Method. We have shown its ef-
522   fectiveness in simulating a variety of materials and interactions, its ability to generalize to larger
523   systems and its use in inverse problems. Crucially, NeuralMPM trains in 6% of the time it takes to
524   train GNS and DMCF to comparable accuracy, and is 5x-10x faster at inference time. By interpo-
525   lating particles onto a fixed-size grid, global information is distilled into a voxelized representation
526   that is easier to learn and process with powerful image-to-image models. The use of voxelization
527   allows NeuralMPM to bypass expensive graph constructions, and the interpolation leads to easier
528
      generalization to a larger number of particles and constant runtime. The lack of expensive graph
      construction and message passing also allows for more autoregressive steps and parallel rollouts.
529
530
      Limitations. Like other approaches, NeuralMPM is limited by the computation used to process
531
      the structure of the point cloud. In our case, voxelization means we cannot deal with particles that
532   lie outside of the domain and are limited to regular grids. Additionally, the size of the voxels is
533   directly related to the number of particles within a given volume. If the voxels are too large, the
534   model will fail to capture finer details. Conversely, if they are too small, the model may struggle
535   due to insufficient local structure. Similarly, performance can degrade in very sparse domains.
536   Compressible fluids might also present challenges, though this requires further verification.
537
538   Future work. Our work is only a first step towards hybrid Eulerian-Lagrangian neural emulators,
539   leaving many avenues for future research. Extending NeuralMPM to 3D systems is a natural contin-
      uation of this work. Future studies could also explore alternative particle-to-grid and grid-to-particle


                                                        10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      functions, like the non-uniform Fourier transform (Fessler & Sutton, 2003), or more sophisticated
541   interpolation methods from classical MPM literature (Nguyen et al., 2023). A less traditional di-
542   rection is to make NeuralMPM probabilistic and encode richer distributional information about the
543   particles in the grid nodes, instead of maintaining only a mean value. This could potentially im-
544   prove NeuralMPM’s ability to resolve subgrid phenomena. Finally, advances in Lagrangian Particle
545   Tracking (Schröder & Schanz, 2023) will eventually make it possible to create datasets from real-
546   world data, enabling the training of NeuralMPM directly from data without the need for the costly
547   design process of a numerical simulator.

      A    T RAINING DETAILS
757
758
      Hardware. We run all our experiments using the same hardware: 4 CPUs, 24GB of RAM, and an
759   NVIDIA RTX A5000 GPU with 24GB of VRAM. For reproducing the results of DMCF, we kept
760   the A5000 GPU but it required up to 96GB of RAM for training.
761
762   Data Preprocessing. Similar to Prantl et al. (2022), we slightly alter the original MPM datasets
763   to add boundary particles, as the original data from Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) does not have
764   them. We define the velocity at a timestep to be vt = vt − vt−1 . We therefore skip the first step
765   during training for which no velocity is available.
766
767   Implementation. Our implementation, training scripts, experiment configurations, and instruc-
768   tions for reproducing results are publicly available at [URL]. We implement NeuralMPM using
769   PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), and use PyTorch Geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019) for implement-
770
      ing efficient particle-to-grid functions, more specifically from the Scatter and Cluster modules. For
      memory efficiency, we do not store all (up to) 1,000 training trajectories in memory, and rather use a
771
      buffer of about 16 trajectories over which several epochs are performed before loading a new buffer
772
      of random trajectories.
773
774
      Baselines. We use the official implementations and training instructions of GNS (Sanchez-
775   Gonzalez et al., 2020) and DMCF (Prantl et al., 2022) to reproduce their results and conduct new
776   experiments. More specifically, we train GNS as instructed for 20M steps on all four datasets, using
777   their provided configuration. For DMCF, we follow their default configurations and train for 400K
778   iterations for each dataset. In datasets that were not used by the original authors, VARIABLE G RAV-
779   ITY and DAM BREAK 2D, we performed hyperparameter search. GNS and DMCF both were trained
780   for a total budget of 60 GPU-days per dataset, and the best performance was reported.
781
782   Normalization. We normalize the input of the model over each channel individually. We investi-
783   gated computing the statistics across a buffer, resembling (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), and precomput-
784   ing them on the whole training set and found no difference in performance. During inference, we
785
      use the precomputed statistics.
