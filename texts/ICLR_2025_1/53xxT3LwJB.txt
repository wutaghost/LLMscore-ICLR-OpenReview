011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Analyzing long-term behaviors in high-dimensional nonlinear dynamical systems
014           remains a significant challenge. The Koopman operator framework has emerged
015           as a powerful tool to address this issue by providing a globally linear perspec-
016
              tive on nonlinear dynamics. However, existing methods for approximating the
              Koopman operator and its spectral components, particularly in large-scale sys-
017
              tems, often lack robust theoretical guarantees. Residual Dynamic Mode Decom-
018
              position (ResDMD) introduces a spectral residual measure to assess the conver-
019           gence of the estimated Koopman spectrum, which helps filter out spurious spec-
020           tral components. Nevertheless, it depends on pre-computed spectra, thereby in-
021           heriting their inaccuracies. To overcome its limitations, we introduce the Neural
022           Network-ResDMD (NN-ResDMD), a method that directly estimates Koopman
023           spectral components by minimizing the spectral residual. By leveraging neural
024           networks, NN-ResDMD automatically identifies the optimal basis functions of
025           the Koopman invariant subspace, eliminating the need for manual selection and
026           improving the reliability of the analysis. Experiments on physical and biological
027
              systems demonstrate that NN-ResDMD significantly improves both accuracy and
              scalability, making it an effective tool for analyzing complex dynamical systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the study of complex dynamical systems, a critical challenge lies in accurately extracting and
033   analyzing long-term behavior in high-dimensional nonlinear systems. Various data-driven methods
034   (Brunton & Kutz, 2019; Schetzen, 2006; Wiggins, 2003; Slotine & Li, 1991; Lan & Mezić, 2013;
035   Mezić, 2005) have been developed to address this challenge, with the Koopman operator (Koopman,
036   1931; Koopman & Neumann, 1932) framework emerging as a powerful tool due to its ability to
037   globally linearize nonlinear systems. Unlike local linearization methods (Hartman, 1960; Grobman,
038   1959), which approximate dynamics near fixed points, the Koopman operator transforms the entire
039   system into a linear form within an infinite-dimensional space, which allows the use of spectral
040
      analysis techniques to study complex dynamics.
041   Despite its promise, practical computational challenges arise from the infinite-dimensional nature
042   of the Koopman operator. Numerical methods such as Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition
043   (EDMD) (Williams et al., 2015) have been developed to approximate the Koopman operator using a
044   finite set of observables, making it possible to extract dynamic modes from data. However, EDMD
045   lacks theoretical guarantees of convergence and may fail to capture the full Koopman spectrum
046
      accurately, particularly in large-scale, complex systems.
047   To address these limitations, Residual Dynamic Mode Decomposition (ResDMD) (Colbrook &
048   Townsend, 2024) was introduced, offering convergence guarantees through a spectral residual mea-
049   sure that quantifies how well the estimated Koopman spectrum converges to the true spectrum. By
050   assessing convergence, ResDMD eliminates spurious spectral components that do not represent the
051   system’s true dynamics, enhancing the reliability of spectral estimation. However, ResDMD pri-
052
      marily filters precomputed spectra rather than directly approximating Koopman spectra, limiting its
      ability to independently refine spectral estimates.

      In this paper, we propose Neural Network-ResDMD (NN-ResDMD), which overcomes this limi-
055   tation by providing a method to directly compute Koopman eigenpairs by minimizing the spectral
056   residual. Additionally, NN-ResDMD employs neural networks to automatically select basis func-
057   tions, eliminating the need for manual intervention, a common challenge in EDMD-based methods.
058   Through experiments on both toy models and real-world high-dimensional systems, we demonstrate
059   that NN-ResDMD significantly improves accuracy and scalability, making it a practical and effective
060   tool for analyzing complex dynamical systems.

062   2   P RELIMINARY ON KOOPMAN OPERATOR
063
064   Consider a discrete-time dynamical system (Ω, µ) governed by a map F : Ω → Ω, where Ω ⊆ Rd
065   is the state space, and µ is a probability measure. The evolution of the system is described by:
066
067                                       xk+1 = F (xk ),      k ∈ Z+ .
068   The Koopman operator K acts on observables g ∈ L2 (Ω, µ) as:
069
070                                               Kg = g ◦ F.
071   Although F is nonlinear, the Koopman operator K is linear, enabling spectral analysis of the system
072   in the infinite-dimension function space.
073
      A key aspect of modern Koopman operator theory is Koopman Mode Decomposition (KMD)
074
      (Mezić, 2005), which represents system dynamics through its spectral components, i.e. the eigen-
075
      values, Koopman modes, and eigenfunctions. The discrete spectrum is particularly important for
076   insights into long-term behavior, such as periodicity and stability. Our analysis emphasizes these
077   spectral components derived from KMD. Specifically, we seek eigenpairs (λi , ϕi ), where λi are
078   eigenvalues and ϕi are the corresponding Koopman eigenfunctions.
079
      One of the most prominent numerical methods to approximate the Koopman operator and its
080
      spectral components is the Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (EDMD) method, intro-
081

102   3   KOOPMAN O PERATOR L EARNING
103
104   While EDMD approximates the Koopman operator, it suffers from spectral pollution as increasing
105   dictionary sizes introduce spurious eigenvalues, obscuring the system’s true dynamics. Residual
106   Dynamic Mode Decomposition (ResDMD) (Colbrook & Townsend, 2024) addresses this by filtering
107   out spurious eigenvalues using spectral residuals but relies on precomputed eigenpairs, inheriting
      inaccuracies from methods like EDMD.

111   functions for the Koopman invariant subspace, eliminating the need for manual basis selection.

      In this framework, neural networks parameterize the dictionary functions Ψ(x; θ), where θ repre-
163   sents the network parameters. By minimizing the spectral residual J, this approach directly op-
164   timizes the dictionary functions towards better approximation of Koopman spectral components,

166   system. This is fundamentally different from traditional methods like EDMD, which focus on min-
167   imizing prediction errors in the observable space without explicitly considering spectral accuracy.
168   The neural network architecture serves as a flexible function approximator, that allows the frame-
169   work to adaptively learn the optimal dictionary that minimizes spectral residuals, thereby producing
170   more accurate and reliable Koopman spectral decompositions. This spectral-oriented optimization
171
      improves the accuracy of eigenvalues approximations and enhances the quality of the computed
      eigenfunctions, which leads to better characterization of the system’s dynamic behavior.

259       While the practical advantages of NN-ResDMD are demonstrated through experiments, it is impor-
260       tant to note its computational demands. The algorithm’s computational complexity stems primarily
261       from its iterative optimization process. Each iteration involves a gradient descent update with com-
262       plexity scaling linearly with both system dimensionality and neural network parameters. Although
263       individual gradient steps are computationally lightweight for standard network architectures, the
264       algorithm’s efficiency issue lies in its repeated least-squares optimizations. Compared to standard

      4     A PPLICATION IN PHYSICAL AND BIOLOGICAL SYSTEMS
282
283
      In this chapter, we present three examples demonstrating the effectiveness of NN-ResDMD in esti-
      mating the key quantities of Koopman Mode Decomposition (KMD): spectrum, eigenfunctions, and
284
      Koopman modes. In the first low-dimensional example of a classical pendulum system, our method
285
      requires significantly fewer dictionary observables than (Colbrook & Townsend, 2024, Section 4.3.1,
286   Section 6.3) to compute the Koopman spectrum. The second high-dimensional example on turbu-
287   lence highlights our method’s ability to detect acoustic vibrations and distinguish the pressure field
288   through Koopman modes. The third example, a real-world high-dimensional neural system, com-
289   pares NN-ResDMD with three popular methods—Hankel-DMD (Arbabi & Mezic, 2017), EDMD
290   with RBF basis, and kernelized-ResDMD (Colbrook & Townsend, 2024)—and demonstrates its su-
291   periority in identifying and clustering latent dynamic structures. Together, these examples showcase
292   NN-ResDMD’s performance across diverse systems and comprehensively evaluate its capabilities.
293   Specifically, in all three experiments, we compare NN-ResDMD with Hankel-DMD, a theoretically
294   grounded method that analyzes dynamical systems using time-delayed state measurements (see Ap-
295   pendix A.7.1 for a justification). Although its performance rivals NN-ResDMD in the simple pen-
296   dulum system, it fails to capture key dynamics in higher-dimensional systems.
297
298   4.1   P ENDULUM
299
300   The pendulum system is a measure-preserving system due to its Hamiltonian nature, which theoret-
301   ically implies its whole spectrum lies on the unit circle. For its dynamical behaviors, if the initial
302
      position of the pendulum is sufficiently far from the peak and the initial angular speed sufficiently
      small, the pendulum will oscillate; otherwise, the pendulum will pass the peak and rotate. In other
303
      words, this complex system exhibits two types of dynamical behaviors: rotation and oscillation.
304
      Here we simulate two cases with different numbers of initial points. We choose 90 and 240 initial
305   points uniformly in the domain [−π, π]per × [−15, 15]. Each point evolves 1000 steps with a step
306   size of 0.5. Thus, the total data size in each set is approximately 9 × 104 and 2.4 × 105 , respectively.
307
308   As shown in Figure 2, only NK = 300 observables are needed to approximate the full spectrum,
309
      significantly fewer than the nearly 1000 required in (Colbrook & Townsend, 2024, Section 4.3.1) for
      the same data size. Even with a much larger data size (Figure 3), the required observables remain
310
      small (NK = 350), demonstrating the robustness of efficient observables across data sizes.
311
312   As shown in Figure 4, we compare our method to four approaches: EDMD, EDMD with Dictionary
313   Learning (EDMD-DL), Hankel-DMD, and ResDMD, on the dataset with 90 initial points to compute
314   the Koopman matrix and its corresponding spectral information. The first three methods (EDMD
315
      (Williams et al., 2015), EDMD-DL (Li et al., 2017), and Hankel-DMD are limited to computing
      eigenvalues associated with the point spectrum. In these experiments, both EDMD and ResDMD
316
      use the hyperbolic cross approximation with Hermite functions up to order 15 and Fourier functions
317
      up to order 20. Hankel-DMD uses a time delay of 150. While Hankel-DMD yields accurate eigen-
318   values and shows points near the unit circle which matches the ground truth for this Hamiltonian

320   + continuous spectrum). It also still suffers from spectral pollution and requires careful tuning of
321   the time delay parameter. With 300 basis functions, ResDMD is still unable to fully capture the
322   entire spectrum, i.e., the unit circle, due to the insufficient number of basis functions. In the origi-
323   nal ResDMD work, 964 basis functions using a hyperbolic cross approximation of order 100 were

372
373   Recovering spatial patterns is a typical goal of DMD-based methods, especially in fluid dynamics,
374   where Kernel ResDMD has been particularly successful in capturing such patterns and detecting
375   acoustic vibrations in the turbulence system. However, Kernel ResDMD requires careful selection
376   of kernel functions, while NN-ResDMD bypasses this by using neural networks to train observables
377   and compute Koopman modes.

      In Figure 5, the first Koopman mode estimated by NN-ResDMD with the smallest residual value
385   successfully highlights a clear global spatial separation that aligns with the pattern observed in the
386   original pressure field. This advantage allows the first Koopman mode to directly distinguish spatial
387   features present in the true pressure field, making it a powerful tool for interpreting complex fluid
388   dynamics data. Subsequent Koopman modes also reveal strong acoustic waves that are critical in
389   various aeronautical engineering fields. In contrast, Kernel ResDMD with a generic normalized
390   Gaussian kernel function, as shown in the original work, is unable to produce a Koopman mode
391   similar to the first Koopman mode from NN-ResDMD that clearly distinguishes the pressure field.
392   For comparison, we also plot four Koopman modes computed by Hankel-DMD with a time delay of
393
      5, corresponding to the four smallest residual values, which similarly do not reveal the pressure field
      patterns as in NN-ResDMD. These results are presented in Appendix Figure 7 and Appenxic A.7.2.

      4.3   I DENTIFICATION OF NEURAL DYNAMICS IN MICE VISUAL CORTEX
408
409   Since NN-ResDMD directly minimizes the residuals based on eigenfunctions, its estimated evo-
410   lution of eigenfunctions over time should ideally capture latent dynamics. To evaluate how ef-
411   fectively NN-ResDMD reveals latent temporal dynamics in real data, we apply it to a dataset of
412   high-dimensional neural signals and demonstrate its advantages over a series of classical methods:
413   the Hankel-DMD, EDMD (combined with RBF basis) and Kernel ResDMD. These methods are
414   selected as representative approaches for handling high-dimensional data.
415   The dataset is part of the open dataset on mice from the competition ”Sensorium 2023” (Turishcheva
416   et al., 2023; 2024). In the experiments, mice viewed natural videos while their neural signals were
417   recorded via calcium imaging in the primary visual cortex, reflecting the activity of thousands of
418   neurons. Here, we focus on the state partitioning of neural signals. Specifically, in each mouse, six
419   video stimuli were repeatedly shown, creating ideal conditions to define brain states. Neural activity
420   during repeated trials with the same stimuli is assumed to reflect the same underlying dynamic
421   system, enabling Koopman decomposition methods to uncover and separate these brain states.
422   The dataset consists of neural recordings from five mice, each exposed to 6 video stimuli, repeated
423   9-10 times for a total of around 60 trials. Each recording captures the activity of over 7,000 neurons,
424   with each 10-second video sampled at 50 Hz, resulting in 300 data points per trial.
425
      We applied NN-ResDMD and three classical Koopman decomposition methods (Hankel-DMD,
426   EDMD with RBF basis, and Kernel ResDMD) to these datasets, using different implementations
427   and Koopman subspace dimensions. For NN-ResDMD, we trained dictionaries on all snapshots
428   from each mouse to avoid overfitting, reduced the data to 300 dimensions via SVD, and selected
429   501 eigenfunctions. The decomposed eigenfunctions are shown in Figure 6A(top), with markers
430   indicating ground truth state separations. For Hankel-DMD, we built a Hankel matrix with a delay
431   of 50, producing 50 eigenfunctions per trial. In EDMD with RBF basis, we used the SVD-truncated
      300 basis and 1000 RBF functions, resulting in 1301 eigenfunctions. For Kernel ResDMD, we


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      used normalized Gaussians as kernel functions, setting the Koopman subspace dimension to 299
433   eigenfunctions based on Colbrook et al. (2023). See Appendix A.8.4 for method details and Ap-
434   pendix A.9 for dictionary size justification. These eigenfunctions, shown in Figure 6A(bottom),
435   Appendix Figure 9A, and Appendix Figure 10A, are compared to the ground truth trial identities.
436
      The Koopman eigenfunctions represent dynamical features corresponding to the video stimuli. To
437
      evaluate their effectiveness, we assess how well eigenfunctions of the same stimuli cluster together,
438
      distinguishing them from other states. If the eigenfunctions capture key dynamics related to the
439   stimuli, those from trials with the same video should be separable from others. This turns the
440   problem into a clustering task based on the separability of eigenfunctions across different stimuli.
441   Note that averaged trial differences are even visibly clear for the NN-ResDMD case.
442
      We use Multi-dimensional Scaling (MDS) to visualize how these eigenfunction-based features clus-
443
      ter according to ground truth states. MDS reduces data dimensionality based on similarities, making
444
      it ideal for visualizing clustering performance. While UMAP and t-SNE are alternative methods,
445   we show MDS results in 2D space (Figure 6B-E), with similar results for UMAP and t-SNE in the
446   supplementary materials (Appendix Figure 8, Appendix Figure 9C,D and Appendix Figure 10C,D).
447
448
      The 2D MDS visualization reveals clear separation of features for all 5 mice using NN-ResDMD
      (Figure 6B), whereas no other method shows clear clustering (Figure 6C-E, Appendix Figure 9B,
449
      Appendix Figure 10B). To quantify this clustering, we calculate the Davies-Bouldin index (DBI), a
450
      measure of clustering quality that assesses how compact and well-separated the clusters are. A lower
451   DBI indicates more compact clusters that are farther apart from each other, which corresponds to
452   better clustering. The DBI is significantly lower for NN-ResDMD (Figure 6F), suggesting that it
453   captures the latent dynamic structure more effectively than all three other methods. Similar cluster-
454   ing patterns are confirmed with UMAP and t-SNE (Appendix Figure 11).

      5   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
457
458
      Koopman spectral components (eigenpairs) are fundamental to understanding dynamical systems, as
459
      they reveal intrinsic patterns and structures underlying complex temporal behavior through a linear
460   framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamics. In this paper, we introduced NN-ResDMD, a method
461   for estimating eigenpairs by minimizing spectral residuals, eliminating ResDMD’s need to filter
462   precomputed results. Despite higher computational costs, using neural networks to learn eigenpairs
463   provides a significant advantage by capturing patterns automatically and reducing manual interven-
464   tion in basis selection. This flexibility is particularly beneficial for high-dimensional systems where
465   traditional methods often struggle. Our experiments demonstrate that NN-ResDMD outperforms
466   classical methods—including EDMD, Hankel-DMD, ResDMD, and their variants—in uncovering
467   critical spatiotemporal characteristics of nonlinear dynamics.
468   Despite the advantages, NN-ResDMD has several limitations and we discuss the major ones here.
469   First, the neural network structure incurs higher computational costs compared to classical ap-
470   proaches, making it unsuitable for real-time learning tasks (see a brief discussion in Appendix A.5).
471   Second, the deterministic nature of the framework does not account for stochastic aspects of the sys-
472   tem, such as those addressed by methods like VAMP Mardt et al. (2018), limiting its applicability
473   to highly noisy data. Additionally, the performance of NN-ResDMD is sensitive to hyperparame-
474
      ter tuning, including network architecture, dictionary size, and training criteria, which can require
      significant effort to optimize.
475
476   Koopman eigenpairs provide unique perspectives into the interpretation of nonlinear dynamical
477   mechanisms, and feedforward neural networks (FNNs) represent an initial step in learning spec-
478   tral properties directly from data. In recent years, various deep neural network structures have been
479   employed to learn the Koopman representations with different optimization targets other than the
480
      spectral residuals (e.g. Lusch et al. (2018); Takeishi et al. (2017); Mardt et al. (2018); Yeung et al.
      (2019); Otto & Rowley (2019); Azencot et al. (2020); Alford-Lago et al. (2022b); Iwata & Kawahara
481
      (2020)(see Appendix section A.4 for a comparison with the VAMP framework). With our approach,
482
      we demonstrate that even basic architectures can achieve significant improvements in Koopman op-
483   erator estimation by using the spectral residual loss. Therefore, future work could focus on refining
484   neural network architectures to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of Koopman eigenpair estima-
485   tion. One promising direction is the incorporation of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

847   A.4   H IGHLIGHTS OF NN-R ES DMD COMPARED WITH TYPICAL EXISTING NEURAL
            NETWORK - BASED KOOPMAN FRAMEWORK
848
849
      Our NN-ResDMD method takes a fundamentally different approach from existing deep learning
850   methods by building upon the residual-based framework of ResDMD rather than the different
851   Koopman-approximating loss functions following the variational principles of VAMPnets (Tian &
852   Wu, 2021; Wu & Noé, 2020; Mardt et al., 2018) or the deep autoencoder structure in (Lusch et al.,
853   2017). By incorporating spectral residual measures into deep learning and introducing a structured
854   representation that captures dependencies among eigenvalues, we achieve more compact and inter-
855   pretable models for nonlinear systems with continuous spectra. This approach enables us to directly
856   minimize Koopman spectral approximation errors while avoiding the high-dimensional representa-
857   tions or point-spectrum limitations of previous methods.
858   If we take the VAMP framework as an example, here are the connections and differences. The
859   proposed loss function and the VAMP score share the goal of optimizing approximations of the
860   Koopman operator’s spectral properties, establishing a connection in their ultimate purpose. How-
861   ever, although they both depend on the covariance matrices (in our manuscript Equation 3.2), their
862   methodologies differ significantly. Our residual-based method directly minimizes the spectral ap-
863   proximation error of the Koopman operator and accommodates both point and continuous spectra,
      while the VAMP score follows a variational framework, maximizing the sum of singular values to


                                                           16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      approximate the point spectrum, primarily for stochastic systems (though see an exception in Tian &
865   Wu (2021)). Moreover, while VAMP is specifically designed for Markov processes and requires the
866   Koopman operator to be Hilbert-Schmidt, our approach focuses on deterministic systems and en-
867   ables a more comprehensive spectral analysis that incorporates continuous spectra. This distinction
868   in scope and methodology highlights how the two frameworks complement each other in addressing
869   different aspects of spectral estimation.

871   A.5   D ISCUSSION OF COMPUTATION COSTS
872
873   Despite the various advantages of the NN-ResDMD framework, one significant limitation is its
874   higher computational cost compared to the original ResDMD and other classical methods.
875   Theoretical Perspective: The NN-ResDMD algorithm’s computational demands stem primarily
876   from its iterative optimization process. Each iteration involves a gradient descent update with com-
877   plexity scaling linearly with both system dimensionality and neural network parameters. Though
878   individual gradient steps are computationally lightweight for standard network architectures, the
879   algorithm’s efficiency issue lies in its repeated least-squares optimizations. Below we provide a
880   detailed comparison between computation costs of several methods we used in this paper.
881   Comparison over methods: The computational costs of EDMD, ResDMD, EDMD-DL, Hankel-
882   DMD, and NN-ResDMD vary significantly based on their core computational steps and specific
883   configurations. For a dataset with m = 105 data points and NK = 300 dictionary functions (for
884   EDMD-based methods), the theoretical complexity and runtime differ across methods. EDMD in-
                                                                                      2         3
885   volves least squares and eigenvalue decomposition, with a complexity of O(NK       m + NK   ), making
886   it the fastest method, and typically requiring only seconds to minutes for computation. ResDMD
887
      extends EDMD by adding residual evaluation and pseudospectrum computation. The residual eval-
                                               3
      uation introduces an additional O(NK       ), and pseudospectrum computation across nz grid points
888                   3                                            2           3
      incurs O(nz NK    ), resulting in a total complexity of O(NK   m + n z NK  ). This leads to runtimes
889
      ranging from minutes to hours, depending on the resolution of the pseudospectrum grid. EDMD-
890   DL incorporates dictionary learning through stochastic gradient descent (SGD), where each iteration
891                                         2                                          3
      involves matrix construction (O(NK      m)), Koopman matrix computation (O(NK      )), and neural net-
892   work forward/backward propagation (O(d|H|), with d|H| representing the total network parameter
                                                                             2        3
893   size). With k SGD iterations, the total complexity becomes O(k(NK        m + NK    + d|H|)), leading
894   to runtimes also in the range of minutes to hours depending on k. NN-ResDMD, which builds
                                                             2        3
895   on EDMD-DL, shares the same complexity, O(k(NK           m + NK   + d|H|)), but includes the explicit
896   use of Koopman matrix eigenvectors and optional pseudospectrum computation, making its runtime
897   slightly longer than EDMD-DL for high-resolution spectral analysis. Hankel-DMD, using a time
898
      delay embedding dimension T , constructs a Hankel matrix (O(T m)), performs singular value de-
      composition (SVD) (O(T 2 m)), and computes the eigenvalues of a reduced T × T matrix (O(T 3 )).
899
      The total complexity is O(T m + T 2 m + T 3 ), and the runtime is heavily influenced by T , typi-
900
      cally ranging from minutes to hours. While EDMD is computationally the most efficient, ResDMD
901   and Hankel-DMD provide higher precision and robustness in spectral analysis at the expense of in-
902   creased runtime, and EDMD-DL and NN-ResDMD offer flexibility and accuracy through dictionary
903   learning, with additional SGD iterations and optional pseudospectrum computation contributing to
904   their computational burden.
905
      Empirical Perspective: In our experiments, without computing the pseudospectrum, the compu-
906
      tational cost of ResDMD typically ranges from seconds to minutes. NN-ResDMD, on the other
907   hand, can require tens of minutes to several hours, depending on factors such as data dimensionality,
908   the number of snapshots, hidden layer configurations, dictionary sizes, and training convergence
909   criteria.
910
      Trade-off Between Cost and Accuracy: While NN-ResDMD’s additional computational steps
911
      introduce higher costs, they enhance the accuracy and robustness of Koopman eigenpair estimation
912
      by allowing automatic dictionary learning and minimizing spurious spectral components. This trade-
913   off makes NN-ResDMD particularly valuable in applications where precision is critical. However,
914   its computational demands render it less suitable for real-time or online Koopman model learning
915   tasks.

926   A.7.1    J USTIFICATION OF U SING H ANKEL -DMD AS COMPARISON IN ALL EXPERIMENTS
927
      Hankel-DMD operates by constructing a Hankel matrix from time-delayed measurements of the
928   system state, based on Takens’ embedding theorem, which states that time-delayed coordinates can
929   reconstruct the state space of dynamical systems. Hankel-DMD also falls within the framework
930   of Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (EDMD), as it effectively uses time-delayed states
931   as dictionary functions. This connection introduces convergence conditions specific to time-delay
932   embeddings, differing from those associated with standard EDMD implementations. This makes
933   Hankel-DMD a natural choice for comparison in the pendulum system. Specifically, the method
934   enables a more detailed extraction of the system’s modes and dynamics, with theoretical guarantees
935   established in works like (Arbabi & Mezic, 2017), which proved its convergence for ergodic systems.
936   Practically, the approach involves constructing a large matrix of time-shifted copies of measured
937   data, where the number of delays determines how many past states are considered. This theo-
938   retically grounded framework is particularly effective when the system states have good temporal
939   resolution and has shown strong performance in analyzing high-dimensional dynamical systems.
940   Consequently, we also apply Hankel-DMD to the turbulence and neural dynamics experiments to
941   evaluate its effectiveness in these representative high-dimensional settings.
942   As results, although its performance rivals NN-ResDMD in the simple pendulum system by showing
943   eigenvalues points near the unit circle and containing some polluted eigenvalues, which are close to
944   the ground truth unit circle, we would like to emphasize that it capture the point spectrum and miss
945   the full spectral information. When it comes to high-dimensional systems, it fails to capture key dy-
946   namics in higher-dimensional systems, as seen in the later experiment (Section 4.2 and Section 4.3).
947

950   ResDMD results. As shown in Figure 7, despite having small residuals, these modes fail to clearly
951   capture the fundamental pressure field structure that was successfully identified by NN-ResDMD’s
952   first Koopman mode (see Figure 5). This comparison demonstrates the superior ability of NN-
953   ResDMD to extract physically meaningful patterns from complex fluid systems.

1054   A.9   C HOICE JUSTIFICATION OF DICTIONARY SIZES
1055
1056   In this section, we provide justifications for the use of different dictionary sizes (i.e., the number of
1057   Koopman eigenfunctions) in the aforementioned four methods for the neural dynamics experiment.
1058
       First, the high-dimensional data was pre-processed using SVD to reduce its dimensionality to 300.
1059   Then, for the four methods:
1060
1061         1. For NN-ResDMD, we selected 300 trained basis functions and 300 first-order monomial
1062            basis functions as the dictionary for the 300 reduced observables. This choice ensures the
1063            dictionary is rich enough to span the Koopman invariant subspace. Hence, the size of the
1064            trained dictionary was set to be at least equal to the original observable size. Then based
1065            on the rank of estimated Koopman eigenvalues, we select the dominant 501 eigenfunctions
1066            to avoid the eigenfunctions with zero eigenvalues.
1067         2. For Hankel DMD, the number of delays (as dictionary size/number of eigenfunctions) is
1068            first constrained by the temporal sample size (i.e., snapshot size) because it cannot exceed
1069            the maximum snapshot size. Therefore, it is impossible to choose the same dictionary size
1070            as the NN-ResDMD example. Choosing the delay too small will result in an insufficient
1071
                dictionary size to span the Koopman invariant subspace, and too large will reduce the actual
                snapshot size to estimate the covariance matrices in the estimation of the Koopman matrix.
1072
                Therefore, we chose a compromise delay number of 50 that satisfies both needs.
1073
1074         3. For RBF basis, in principle, we can use the same dictionary size. However, our previous
1075
                experience with a similar dataset and the results of using the RBF basis for the EDMD
                method all suggest that the performance will be better with more dictionary functions.
1076
                Therefore, we chose 1000 RBF basis and the original 300 first-order monomial basis as a
1077
                better condition compared to the same dictionary size with NN-ResDMD.
1078

1081           snapshots. Therefore, we cannot make the dictionary size consistent with the NN-ResDMD
1082           example.
