009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Preference alignment has become a crucial component in enhancing the perfor-
012            mance of Large Language Models (LLMs), yet its impact in Multimodal Large
013            Language Models (MLLMs) remains comparatively underexplored. Similar to
014            language models, MLLMs for image understanding tasks encounter challenges like
015            hallucination. In MLLMs, hallucination can occur not only by stating incorrect
016
               facts but also by producing responses that are inconsistent with the image content.
               A primary objective of alignment for MLLMs is to encourage these models to
017
               align responses more closely with image information. Recently, multiple works
018
               have introduced preference datasets for MLLMs and examined different alignment
019            methods, including Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) and Proximal Policy
020            Optimization (PPO). However, due to variations in datasets, base model types,
021            and alignment methods, it remains unclear which specific elements contribute
022            most significantly to the reported improvements in these works. In this paper, we
023            independently analyze each aspect of preference alignment in MLLMs. We start by
024            categorizing the alignment algorithms into two groups, offline (such as DPO), and
025            online (such as online-DPO), and show that combining offline and online methods
026            can improve the performance of the model in certain scenarios. We review a variety
027
               of published multimodal preference datasets and discuss how the details of their
               construction impact model performance. Based on these insights, we introduce a
028
               novel way of creating multimodal preference data called Bias-Driven Hallucination
029
               Sampling (BDHS) that needs neither additional annotation nor external models,
030            and show that it can achieve competitive performance to previously published
031            alignment work for multimodal models across a range of benchmarks.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Recent advancements in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have significantly improved
037   our understanding of vision-language tasks. By integrating visual signals with Large Language
038
      Models (LLMs), these models have demonstrated enhanced capabilities in multimodal understanding,
      reasoning, and interaction (Liu et al., 2023c; 2024; Bai et al., 2023; McKinzie et al., 2024).
039
040   Typically, MLLMs are pre-trained on large image-text datasets to develop foundational multimodal
041   knowledge and skills, then undergo post-training for conversational capabilities, instruction following,
042   helpfulness, and safety. Despite rapid advancements in recent years, significant challenges persist.
043   A notable problem is the tendency of MLLMs to produce responses that are not factually grounded in
044   the visual input, commonly referred to as hallucinations, leading to inaccuracies such as incorrect
045   descriptions of non-existent visual elements (Liu et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2023). This undermines the
046   trustworthiness of MLLMs in many practical applications.
047
      Preference alignment methods have proven effective in reducing hallucinations and generating
048   responses more closely aligned with human preferences for LLMs (Zhao et al., 2023b; Rafailov
049   et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Ahmadian et al., 2024; Tang et al.,
050   2024a). These methods utilize pairwise preference data to fine-tune the model, which can be based
051   on Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al.,
052   2020), Direct Alignment from Preferences (DAP) (Rafailov et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
053   2023b), or Online Direct Alignment from Preferences (Online-DAP) (Yuan et al., 2024; Guo et al.,
      2024).

054
      While alignment in LLMs has been extensively studied, alignment for MLLMs has not yet been
055   investigated to the same extent. Sun et al. (2023) and Zhou et al. (2024) aligned LLaVA 1.5 (Liu
056   et al., 2023b) using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO),
057   respectively, while Li et al. (2023a) and Yu et al. (2023b) employed DPO and its variations to
058   align Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) and Muffin (Yu et al., 2023a) models. Notably, besides different
059   alignment strategies and often different base models, all these works also introduce novel preference
060   datasets for alignment with various sizes, collection, and generation schemes. As a result, while each
061   of these studies offers valuable insights into alignment for MLLMs, it can sometimes be difficult to
062   strongly attribute reported improvements to the individual proposed choices.
063   In this paper, we examine each component of multimodal alignment independently. First, we
064   categorize alignment methods into two types: offline methods, which utilize preference pairs collected
065   prior to training (e.g., DPO), and online methods, which involve sampling from the model during
066   policy optimization (e.g., RLHF and Online-DAP). We conduct a comprehensive study over popular
067   online and offline alignment methods, all aligning the popular LLaVA 1.6 model (Liu et al., 2024)
068   using a fixed data regiment and study their benefits and shortcomings. To our knowledge, this is the
069
      first time that such study is conducted with MLLMs.
070   Further, we study the different methods for building pairwise preferences using public datasets. We
071   break down the main components of preference data into three parts: prompts, chosen responses
072   and rejected responses (Table 1). For each of those components, we investigate how their source,
073   diversity, and quality can affect the resulting alignment. Additionally, we examine how the size of the
074   alignment dataset impacts downstream performance.
075   Based on our comprehensive ablations, we identify a few key desiderata in alignment strategies
076   for MLLMs and introduce a simple, novel preference data sampling scheme we call Bias-Driven
077   Hallucination Sampling (BDHS). Despite not utilizing any human annotation nor the input of any
078   external teacher model such as GPT4-V, we show that BDHS can achieve competitive performance
079   against even much larger preference datasets constructed under different regimes.

081   2     A LIGNMENT
082
083   Preference alignment uses pairwise preference data. Each pair is linked to a text prompt, denoted
084   as xtext , and an associated image, ximg , together forming the input x = (ximg , xtext ). The responses
085   include a preferred one, y + , and a non-preferred or rejected one, y − . See Section 3 for a more
086   thorough discussion of these components. In this section, we focus on the various ways that a
087   preference dataset, D = {(x, y + , y − )}Ni=1 , is used by alignment approaches.
088
089   2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK (RLHF)
090
091

092

093

094   straightforward cross-entropy loss, treating the binary choice – preferred or rejected – as a categorical
095   label. The objective function for training the reward model, rϕ , is as follows:

      It is important to note that most preference datasets are not derived from the model being aligned
132
      and are collected offline. Even when the data is constructed based on the model that is undergoing
133
      alignment, the samples encountered during training do not account for changes in the model over
134   training. This leads to a distribution shift between the model that generated the data and the model
135   being aligned, which can be considered a disadvantage of these methods.

      2.3   O NLINE D IRECT A LIGNMENT FROM P REFERENCE
138
139   Recently, a new family of algorithms has been proposed for aligning LLMs. These methods do not
140   train a separate reward model. Instead, they employ either the model that is being aligned (Yuan
141   et al., 2024) or a different LLM (Guo et al., 2024) to obtain online feedback to create preference pairs.
142   These pairs are then used to optimize the objective function via for example DPO. This approach
143   eliminates the complexity of training a separate reward model while still taking advantage of online
144
      samples from the model, thereby avoiding distribution shifts.
145   We explore the use of LLaVA 1.6-34B (Liu et al., 2024) as annotator to generate online preference
146   pairs, motivated by its strong performance on a multitude of multimodal benchmarks. Additionally,
147   we investigate a hybrid approach that combines online and offline approaches. This method involves
148   sampling from the offline preference data with a probability p, (y + , y − ), and sampling from the
149   model with a probability 1 − p, (ỹ + , ỹ − ). Equation 4 details this approach.

206   3.1                  B IAS -D RIVEN H ALLUCINATION S AMPLING (BDHS)
207
      Hallucinations in MLLMs often express the underlying language models’ inherent biases, for example
208
      towards frequently cooccuring objects or object attributes (Li et al., 2023b; Qian et al., 2024; Zhou
209
      et al., 2024). In other words, the MLLM may choose to draw from its parametric knowledge or textual
210   context xtext when instead it should have more strongly considered information from the image ximg
211   in question. Zhou et al. (2024) suggest to trigger inherent biases directly by presenting noisy images
212   x̃img to the model when generating the non-preferred response ỹ − via teacher-forcing (POVID-style
213   image distortion). While this method has desirable characteristics, such as not requiring external
214   teacher models or human annotation to construct preference pairs, as well as generating samples that
215   are at least partially informed by the policy under alignment, it carries some notable drawbacks. Zhou
      et al. (2024) show that selecting too few diffusion steps can yield insufficient corruption, whereas


                                                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      too many diffusion steps negatively impacts the generated responses, as the model mainly identifies
217   noise respective to pixels. In our experiments, we further found that the proposed teacher-forcing can
218   introduce non-sensical responses. Further details about POVID-style image distortion are provided in
219   Appendix G.1 and an example with non-sensical responses in Appendix G.7.
220
      Inspired by the POVID work, we aim to address its main identified shortcomings: 1. We propose
221
      to rethink the method of corrupting the signal from the input image from a pixel-based approach
222
      to one that limits access in the latent space via attention masking, which we argue more directly
223   achieves the underlying motivation of triggering the inherent bias of the underlying language model.
224   2. We introduce a new reference-guided generation strategy that allows corrupted responses to remain
225   largely true to the chosen response while still introducing meaningful divergence, without introducing
226   non-sensical continuations introduced by token-based teacher forcing. 3. We use an off-the-shelf
227   sentence embedding to verify that the generated rejected response is meaningfully distinct from
228   the original reference to focus the resulting feedback signal on hallucinations over mere stylistic
229   difference. We refer to our novel technique as Bias-Driven Hallucination Sampling (BDHS). BDHS
230   is annotation free and computationally efficient to the point that rejected responses can be generated
231
      online, which we explore in Section 4.4. An overview of the method can be found in Figure 1, with
      further details provided in the following subsections.
232
233   Let x̃ = (xtext , x̃img , m) denote the modified input with (optional noisy) image x̃img and attention mask
234   m. Suppose the MLLM encodes image x̃img to k embedding vectors, each vector with dimension d.
235   Then, m is defined as a boolean mask of dimension k. We suggest to randomly sample the mask
236   m = (m1 , m2 , . . . , mk ) according to a uniform distribution U(0, 1) and threshold ρth ∈ [0, 1] where
237
      each element mi = 1 if ρi ≥ ρth for ρi ∼ U(0, 1) or mi = 0 otherwise. By masking the image
      embeddings using m, the model only pays attention to a subset of the k embedding vectors to generate
238
      the response ỹ − . Where the remaining signal is not sufficient, the MLLM can only draw on its
239
      parametric knowledge to answer, thus inducing hallucination. By allowing access to some part of the
240   image, we encourage more realistic hallucinations.
241
242   Keeping the generated corrupted response ỹ − close to the preferred one, y + , supports the optimizer
243
      in paying more attention to the image as only the non-overlapping portion is affected by the modified
      input x̃. Otherwise, responses ỹ − and y + could diverge early on or ỹ − could even represent a generic
244
      response hinting on missing image information. Instead, in order to maintain consistency in style and
245
      structure we propose a reference-guided sampling strategy, where we “diverge” and “rejoin” from y +
246   at random points in every sentence to form ỹ − . A formal description of the algorithm is provided in
247   Appendix G.3.
248
249
      Similar to our observation in Online-DAP, BDHS responses ỹ − can still be very similar to the ground
      truth y + , especially when the pivot position is late in the sentence. To maximize learning utility of
250
      BDHS preference pairs, this is undesirable.
251
252   While further increasing ρth or biasing towards early pivot positions in the reference-guided generation
253   could minimize such trivial generations, this introduces additional hyperparameters and can lead to
254   less realistic dispreferred responses. Instead, we realize BDHS in an iterative fashion.
255   Once ỹ − is generated, a semantic similarity score w.r.t. y + is computed using an off-the-shelf sentence
256   embedding model1 . A new response ỹ − is sampled if the cosine similarity is above a pre-defined
257   threshold ϵs . After reaching the maximum number of iterations NBDHS , ỹ − is generated according
258   to input x̃ without any reference guidance. Appendix G provides the actual algorithm for BDHS
259   including similarity scoring and several examples.
260   This additional comparison avoids ỹ − responses that are trivial rephrasings. Moreover, measuring the
261   number of examples that need re-generation allows intuitive tuning of the ρth hyper parameter.

297   4.1      K EY C OMPONENTS IN MLLM A LIGNMENT P IPELINE
298
299   We summarize our main findings and compare results with other SOTA models in Table 2. First,
300   we fixed the base model (LLaVA 1.6-7B) and studied the effects of online vs. offline methods
301   using the POVID alignment data (Zhou et al., 2024). While offline DPO shows more significant
302   improvement on benchmarks that consider hallucination, such as POPE and MMHALBench-V,
303
      Online-DPO enhances benchmarks evaluating the quality of answers in an open question answering
      setup, like LLaVABench-in-the-Wild. This is intuitive, as the preference pairs in POVID
304
      are specifically designed to reduce hallucinations whereas the online samples from the model may
305
      provide other signals. Mixed-DPO allows to incorporate the benefits of both approaches and the
306   results show consistent improvement over both online and offline methods.
307
308   When using Online-DPO or Mixed-DPO strategies, we typically depend on advanced models like
309
      LLaVA 1.6-34B to rank the online samples generated by the model. However, access to such models
      is not always guaranteed. We discuss this limitation in more detail in Appendix D.2. Additionally,
310
      the construction of the POVID dataset also involves using a superior model such as GPT-4V to
311
      inject noise into SFT data. Our proposed BDHS method does not require additional annotators or
312   preference data, and relies exclusively on SFT data already available from the instruction tuning of
313   the base model. Despite this simplicity, it consistently outperforms the models that utilize the larger
314   POVID dataset (i.e. both offline and Mixed-DPO) in most benchmarks. Implementing BDHS in
315   an online format further closes this performance gap in MMHALBench-V, establishing BDHS as a
316   compelling and cost-effective alternative to other more resource-intensive approaches. Combining

513   5      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
514
515   In this study, we investigate preference alignment’s role in improving MLLM performance, particu-
516   larly in reducing hallucinations. We evaluate various alignment strategies, including offline, online,
517   and hybrid approaches, across different datasets. Our analysis of existing multimodal preference
518   datasets allows for directly comparing their impact on model performance, controlled for data size
519
      and the base model under alignment. We further isolate individual differences between these datasets
      via additional ablations and show that corruption-based preference data can achieve hallucination
520
      reduction comparable to sampling and ranking-based approaches, and that learning from novel
521
      and diverse inputs, or from responses from superior models surprisingly does not lead to further
522   improvements. Based on these insights, we present a simple preference dataset generation strategy
523   we call BDHS, which uses only existing SFT data, eliminating the need for a superior model, human
524   labelers, or other complex means of constructing preference data. Applied to LLaVA 1.6, this simple
525   methods yield significant improvements across benchmarks, demonstrating its potential.
526
      This study not only enhances our understanding of preference alignment but also establishes a
527
      foundation for further research into MLLM preference alignment. We identify several gaps in the
528   community’s approach to aligning MLLMs. Firstly, while considerable research has been conducted
529   on various alignment methods for LLMs, including both online and offline approaches, these studies
530   are less common in the context of MLLMs. For instance, RLH(AI)F is extensively discussed in
531   LLM literature, highlighting its potential over simpler methods like DPO. Although we provide some
532   insights into RL-based alignment for MLLMs and the evaluation of reward models, a significant gap
533   remains between LLM and MLLM research in this domain. Secondly, we emphasize the need for
534   better hallucination benchmarks to help understand model improvements. Moreover, our thorough
535   analysis of published multimodal preference data reveals insufficient coverage in existing datasets,
536
      which may contribute to the absence of significant improvements in some benchmarks. Lastly, while
      BDHS effectively generates preference data to reduce hallucination, further research is needed to
537
      address other factors causing hallucination in MLLMs, such as insufficient real-world knowledge.
538
539   We hope these insights inspires further research and helps the community tackle ongoing challenges
      in this field.

732
      Benchmarks We adopt multiple benchmarks to assess the capabilities of MLLMs, centered around
733   both measuring the models visual faithfulness, i.e. its tendency to hallucinate, as well as overall
734   helpfulness, i.e. the overall quality of its responses. Results have been obtained using an internal fork
735   of lm-eval-harness (Gao et al., 2023b; McKinzie et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
736
      LLaVABench-in-the-Wild (Liu et al., 2023b), TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), and GQA (Hud-
737
      son & Manning, 2019) help measure the model helpfulness, i.e. the effectiveness at following
738
      instructions and the completeness of the responses. LLaVABench-in-the-Wild expects free-
739   form answers while both TextVQA and GQA require concise responses. We additionally report
740   MMVet (Yu et al., 2023c), which evaluates the knowledge and visual reasoning capabilities of the
741   MLLM. Such capabilities are not a direct target for most MLLM alignment strategies to improve.
742   Nevertheless, MMVet offers a useful indicator for ensuring that such capabilities are not lost due to a
743   possibly too simple or not sufficiently diverse alignment regiment.
744
      POPE (Li et al., 2023b) and MMHALBench (Sun et al., 2023) evaluate the visual faithfulness of a
745   model by identifying hallucinations in model responses. For POPE, we noticed that most of our
746   experiments would reach a seeming plateau between 86% and 88% despite improvements in the
747   other benchmarks. We conducted an initial manual review of 100 reported losses and observed
748   incorrect or disputable ground truth on as many as 20 % of those samples (see Appendix B.2). While
749   re-annotating those examples is beyond the scope of this work, we invite the community to consider
750   it as many recent SOTA models exhibit such plateau2 .
751   Additionally, we noticed unexpected results on MMHALBench, and subsequent analysis showed
752   limitations in its scoring. Specifically, MMHALBench uses text-only GPT-4 to detect hallucinations
753   by comparing model responses to a reference response and a short list of objects known to be in
754   the image. Sometimes this leads to entirely correct model responses to be marked as hallucinations
755
          2
              See Table 4 in McKinzie et al. (2024) where all the models reported are demonstrating a plateau on POPE.

757   straightforward derivative we call MMHALBench-V(ision), which relies on GPT-4o, i.e. provides
758   the input image as additional context to the judge, to more reliably evaluate model capabilities. Data
759   and evaluation prompts are unchanged. We empirically found this to be more reflective of true
760   hallucinations in a human comparison. See Appendix B.4 for our review. Throughout experiments,
761   we mainly focus on MMHALBench-V numbers and report MMHALBench primarily for reference.
762
      While responses that have fewer hallucinations are often also inherently more helpful, we observe that
763   these dimensions are nevertheless distinct and optimizing for reduction in hallucination crucially does
764   not necessarily imply a more helpful model. In fact, in some instances, we even observed an inverse
765   relationship. For example, as discussed in (Zhu et al., 2023), a given model would be more likely
766   to hallucinate when asked to produce longer captions than shorter ones. This implies that models
767   could learn to hallucinate less simply by providing more concise, arguably less useful, responses,
768   and that models that aim to provide more detailed responses may find it more difficult to remain
769   faithful to visual context in all respects3 . For this reason, we report the recall metric from Object
770   HalBench Yu et al. (2023b), styled Recallcoco in our tables. This measures how many objects known

      B.3       CHAIR AND O BJECT H AL B ENCH
814
815   We evaluated two widely used benchmarks in the community for measuring hallucination, focusing
816   on the computation of CHAIR metrics. We investigated approaches described by Rohrbach et al.
817   (2018), which uses COCO annotations to compute CHAIR scores, and the more recent method by Yu
818   et al. (2023b), named Object HalBench, which combines COCO annotations with a GPT model
819   to enhance the detection of hallucinated objects.
820   Our analysis reveals that both benchmarks are significantly noisy (Figure 3). We also found that any
821   improvements in CHAIR scores strongly depend on the ability of these benchmarks to detect specific
822   types of hallucinations and cannot be attributed solely to the improvement of the model.
823
      Furthermore, it is common to report CHAIR metrics without including recall metrics. Considering
824
      the trade-off between CHAIR and recall, omitting recall does not provide a full picture of how much
825
      a model has improved in reducing hallucinations. For instance, a model that generates short and
826   conscise responses might not produce many hallucinations, but this may be at the cost of potentially
827   providing an unhelpful answer.
828
829
      Hence, the recall metric from Rohrbach et al. (2018) proves particularly informative for comparing
      different models and helping with our understanding of other benchmarks. We report this metric in
830
      our evaluations, styled Recallcoco in our tables.

1115   F.1        S TATE OF THE ART P REFERENCE DATASETS
1116
1117   This section presents recently published multimodal preference datasets. We categorize those
1118
       contributions into two categories according to their annotation source:
1119   Human annotations In LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023), authors collect human preferences by
1120   asking crowdworkers to prioritize responses that minimize hallucinations. Using this process, the
1121   authors built a 10k preferences dataset. The prompts are from LLaVA-Instruct-150k (Liu et al.,
1122   2023c), while responses are sampled from LLaVA base model. As is common for many other
1123   preferences datasets, the source of the images is COCO (Lin et al., 2014).
1124   In RLHF-V, Yu et al. (2023b) collect human preferences at the segment level by asking annotators
1125   to correct mistakes in model responses. Used in conjunction with a token-weighted DPO training,
1126   authors reported a reduced hallucinations level. Prompts and images are originally from the UniMM-
1127   Chat SFT dataset introduced by Yu et al. (2023a), and responses sent for annotation are sampled from
1128   Muffin (Yu et al., 2023a).
1129   Synthetic annotations In DRESS (Chen et al., 2023), authors introduce NLF, a 63k pairwise
1130   preference dataset built from LLaVA-Instruct-150k images and prompts. Authors leverage GPT-4 to
1131   provide critique and refinement on the responses of their in-house DRESS model. In VLFeedback (Li
1132   et al., 2023a), authors sample responses from a pool of 12 multimodal MLLMs — including GPT-4V,
1133   the LLaVA 1.5 series models and Qwen-VL-Chat— on a pool of prompts datasets (LLaVA-Instruct-
       150k, SVIT (Zhao et al., 2023a) (Visual Genome images), LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023) (LAION-5B


                                                                 21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       images)). This synthetic approach scales up both the number of examples generated and the diversity
1135   of the responses. GPT-4V is used to select the best responses.
1136
1137   In POVID, Zhou et al. (2024) generate dispreferences from a ground truth dataset directly, removing
1138
       the need for ranking responses. Specifically, 17k examples are selected randomly from the LLaVA-
       Instruct-150k dataset, with the original answers assumed to be a preferred response, while the
1139
       dispreferred response is derived by prompting GPT-4V to introduce mistakes in the preferred response.
1140
1141   Preference signal Transversally to the source of annotations, we consider how the preference signal
1142   is composed. LLaVA-RLHF (Sun et al., 2023) and VLFeedback Li et al. (2023a) use ranking:
1143   responses are sampled, then ranked by humans or GPT-4V. Other works use a construction approach
1144
       where the signal is obtained by correcting responses, such as RLHF-V (Yu et al., 2023b) and DRESS
       (Chen et al., 2023), or by corrupting the responses such as POVID (Zhou et al., 2024).

1348   response which is then taken as ỹ − regardless of the similarity score. Figure 8 shows the number

1391   purpose line 12 introduce a regular expression which matches any yes or no at the beginning of each
1392
       sentence and optionally skips any preceding newline or whitespace characters. This expression can
       be extended to further use-cases if desired. We choose to generate the full response without any SFT
1393
