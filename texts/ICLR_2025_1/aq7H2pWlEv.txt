011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We present VoxelKP, a novel fully sparse network architecture tailored for human
014            keypoint estimation in LiDAR data. The key challenge is that objects are dis-
015
               tributed sparsely in 3D space, while human keypoint detection requires detailed
               local information wherever humans are present. First, we introduce a dual-branch
016
               fully sparse spatial-context block where the spatial branch focuses on learning the
017
               local spatial correlations between keypoints within each human instance, while
018            the context branch aims to retain the global spatial information. Second, we use
019            a spatially aware multi-scale BEV fusion technique to leverage absolute 3D co-
020            ordinates when projecting 3D voxels to a 2D grid encoding a bird’s eye view for
021            better preservation of the global context of each human instance. We evaluate our
022            method on the Waymo dataset and achieve an improvement of 27% on the MPJPE
023            metric compared to the state-of-the-art, HUM3DIL, trained on the same data, and
024            12% against the state-of-the-art, GC-KPL, pretrained on a 25× larger dataset. To
025            the best of our knowledge, VoxelKP is the first single-staged, fully sparse network
026
               that is specifically designed for addressing the challenging task of 3D keypoint
               estimation from LiDAR data, achieving state-of-the-art performance. Our code is

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Human pose estimation is a critical area of research with applications spanning computer vision,
033   robotics, human-computer interaction, and augmented/virtual reality. Previous works (Toshev &
034   Szegedy, 2014; Newell et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2019) are mostly based on 2D images and videos.
035   Compared to regular RGB input, LiDAR sensors provide detailed 3D structural information by
036   measuring the distance to objects using laser light. Apart from its robustness under occlusion and
037   illumination changes, LiDAR also offers privacy protection as it can not retain facial details. In
038   recent years, significant progress has been made in 3D object detection from LiDAR point clouds,
039
      with methods like PointRCNN (Shi et al., 2019a), Part-A2 (Shi et al., 2019b), and PV-RCNN (Shi
      et al., 2020) achieving impressive results, while human pose estimation from LiDAR is still an open
040
      research problem with much room for improvement. Typically, object detection methods focus on
041
      capturing objects scattered sparsely across the 3D space while the keypoints tend to be distributed
042   densely within localized regions around the human body. This fundamental discrepancy in the con-
043   text captured by existing detectors limits their suitability for precise 3D keypoint prediction due to
044   the lack of fine-grained spatial information. To address this gap, we aim to extend the success of 3D
045   object detection to 3D keypoint estimation for Lidar point cloud data by introducing novel compo-
046   nents to preserve fine-grained spatial information. As shown in Figure 1, our method significantly
047   improves the precision of the estimated keypoints.
048   This work identifies the importance of learning from spatial information of varying densities to
049   capture the intricate spatial relationships between keypoints for precise human pose estimation. For
050   this purpose, we introduce the VoxelKP architecture. VoxelKP is a novel, fully sparse neural network
051   tailored specifically for human keypoint estimation within LiDAR point clouds. We first introduce
052   a dual-branch fully sparse spatial-context block that integrates local dense features with the global
053   spatial context from the sparse representations of LiDAR scans. More precisely, the spatial branch
      is used to extract local spatial correlation between keypoints whilst the context branch is used to

089   2.1      D EEP L EARNING ON P OINT C LOUDS
090
091   Many neural network architectures have been adapted for processing point clouds. Earlier methods
092   like VoxNet (Maturana & Scherer, 2015) applied 3D CNNs to voxel grids for object classification.
093
      PointNet (Qi et al., 2017a) was one of the first works to operate directly on point clouds using MLPs
      and max pooling to extract global features of entire scenes represented by point clouds. Follow-
094
      up works like PointNet++ (Qi et al., 2017b) introduced hierarchical and localized feature learning.
095
      Meanwhile, another branch of works such as PointCNN (Li et al., 2018) and KPConv (Thomas et al.,
096   2019) introduced novel convolutional operators for learning features on the unordered point clouds,
097   overcoming the limitations of typical convolutions for this irregular data type.
098
099
      Typical LiDAR-generated point clouds contain more than 100, 000 points, making point-by-point
      computations overwhelming due to the massive data scale. VoxelNet (Zhou & Tuzel, 2018) pro-
100
      posed a voxel feature encoding (VFE) layer as a workaround for the high computational and memory
101
      issues brought by point-by-point computations. Meanwhile, sparse and submanifold sparse convo-
102   lution operations (Graham et al., 2018) exploit sparsity in the voxel grid to reduce computations.
103   SECOND (Yan et al., 2018) introduced an efficient sparse convolutional approach that benefits from
104   the sparse operations. Following SECOND, subsequent works like PointPillars (Lang et al., 2019),
105   3DSSD (Yang et al., 2020), PV-RCNN Shi et al. (2020), CenterPoint (Yin et al., 2021) further ad-
106   vanced sparse convolutional detection on point clouds, introducing ideas like pillar encoding for
107   faster detection, multi-scale detection stacks with anchor boxes, shared voxel encoders, and detect-
      ing small objects by center points. VoxelNeXt (Chen et al., 2023) further demonstrates a fully sparse


                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      voxel-based method without sparse-to-dense conversion or NMS post-processing. However, these
109   approaches are targeted at improving bounding box localization accuracy, which does not require
110   fine-grained spatial features for precise keypoint estimation tasks. Instead, We propose VoxelKP, a
111   novel sparse convolutional architecture tailored for learning discriminative local features from sparse
112   LiDAR data for accurate human pose estimation.

115   2.2   H UMAN P OSE E STIMATION ON P OINT C LOUDS
116
117   Human pose estimation has been extensively studied in images, with methods like DeepPose (Toshev
118   & Szegedy, 2014), Stacked Hourglass (Newell et al., 2016), and HRNet (Sun et al., 2019) achieving
119   high accuracy on benchmarks like COCO-wholebody (Jin et al., 2020). However, compared to
120   RGB images, point clouds provide explicit 3D structural information about the shape and depth of
121
      objects. Shotton et al. (2011) pioneered point cloud human pose estimation from a single depth
      image. Recent works such as Zhou et al. (2020); Ma et al. (2021) proposed a deep learning-based
122
      3D human pose estimation from depth images. Waymo (Sun et al., 2020) has released keypoint
123
      annotations for LiDAR-collected point cloud scenes, while only 3% of the frames are annotated with
124   keypoint human poses. Due to the scarcity of the keypoint annotations within LiDAR point cloud
125   data, many works have taken semi-supervised or weak-supervised approaches to compensate for the
126   limited availability of labeled 3D pose data. For example, Zanfir et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2022)
127   took a multi-modal approach to utilize the enriched image annotations to assist the recognition from
128   point clouds. Weng et al. (2023) proposed an unsupervised approach that generates pseudo ground
129   truth without using annotated keypoint data, along with a fine-tuning approach that pretrains the
130   model with synthetic data and then fine-tunes on the training set. A concurrent work (Ye et al.,
131   2023) adopted a fine-tuning strategy that used a frozen backbone pretrained on a large-scale dataset
132
      as a feature extractor, achieving plausible performance. In general, multi-person pose estimation
      from sorely point clouds remains relatively unexplored due to the lack of ground-truth 3D human
133
      pose annotations. This work proposes a single-staged keypoint estimation method with only LiDAR
134
      point clouds, achieving comparable performance without extra training data.

      3     M ETHOD
139
140   LiDAR point clouds typically contain sparsely distributed objects that occupy only small regions of
141   the full 3D space. While the distribution of humans in space is sparse, in contrast, human keypoints
142   require dense information wherever a human is present. To handle this density variation, we aim
143   to improve the learning of spatial details in the regions where keypoints need to be located and
144   detailed information is required. As illustrated in Figure 2, our VoxelKP framework contains two key
145   components: 1) fully sparse spatial-context blocks, and 2) spatially aware multi-scale BEV fusion.
146
      In this section, we first present the formulation of the task, then introduce the key components
      proposed in our network, and finally elaborate on the details of the network architecture.

159   keypoints corresponds to anatomical joints of interest such as shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees,
160   and ankles. Our objective is to predict the 3D keypoint locations from the input point cloud, i.e. to
161   learn a function F such that Ĝ = F (P ), where Ĝ ∈ RM ×K×3 is the tensor of predicted 3D keypoint
      locations of M humans.

187   3.2     F ULLY S PARSE S PATIAL -C ONTEXT B LOCK
188
189   Our approach is designed with specialized building blocks to process spatial information of varying
190   densities effectively. Each building block starts with a basic sparse 3D block, subsequently branch-
191   ing into two distinct pathways for local and global spatial feature learning. Spatial branches are used
192   for learning local spatial correlations between keypoints, incorporating sparse selective kernel mod-
193   ules and sparse box-attention modules to improve the representational power to encode and localize
194
      the intricate keypoint features. Meanwhile, global spatial feature learning is achieved through con-
      text branches, where a straightforward MLP is employed to maintain detailed per-voxel information,
195
      ensuring the retention of spatial details. Our proposed hybrid feature learning strategy captures the
196
      nuanced local details with an understanding of the global context.

253   3.2.3    H YBRID F EATURE L EARNING
254
255   The convolutional operations focus on understanding spatial hierarchies and local geometric struc-
256   tures to extract local neighborhood information. Concurrently, inspired by the previous point-voxel
257   networks (Liu et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022), we include an MLP branch for each
258
      stage. The integration of an MLP branch alongside a convolutional branch is a strategic approach to
      capture both fine-grained per-voxel details and relatively coarse-grained local neighborhood infor-
259
      mation. Each MLP branch is composed of three sequential blocks, each consisting of a linear layer,
260
      batch normalization, and a ReLU activation function. The number of channels in each linear layer
261   is set to match the channels of the incoming tensor. We then merge the output features from the
262   MLP and convolutional branches through element-wise summation to create hybrid features of the
263   per-voxel and per-neighborhood information. This hybrid feature learning approach is deployed to
264   retain and process fine details across the voxel space, which is critical for the accurate localization
265   of keypoints.

      3.4   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
325
326   We propose a single-stage, fully sparse neural network, designed for human pose estimation within
327   LiDAR point clouds. The architecture is demonstrated in Figure 2. The input is a point cloud RN ×C
328   where N is the number of points and C is the number of features (e.g. x, y, z, intensity). We voxelize
329   the point cloud into a sparse voxel representation. Our method consists of an input stem network
330   and four stages with gradually decreased feature map size, where each stage reduces the spatial
331   shape of the sparse voxel space by a factor of two. The input stem network is a simple stack of
332
      convolution layers, as shown in Appendix A.1, to extract low-level features from the voxelized point
      cloud. Next, four fully sparse spatial-context blocks are used for each subsequent stage for capturing
333
      features for accurate keypoint localization. The sparse box-attention modules are only applied for
334
      our last two blocks to emphasize local-region features. Note that we do not increase the number of
335   channels for the last three stages. We then convert the resulting 3D feature maps from the last three
336   blocks to 2D spatial-encoded BEV representations. Note that we increase the number of channels
337   for the BEV representation to compensate for the information loss of the BEV conversion. These
338   2D features are further refined with 2D convolutions to aggregate spatial context. In the end, we
339   obtain the estimated keypoints Ykp ∈ RK×3 and the corresponding predicted visibilities Ykp ∈ RK ,
340   where K is the number of keypoints.
341
342   3.5   R ELATIONSHIP TO P RIOR W ORKS
343
344   Our VoxelKP fundamentally differs from the traditional LiDAR-based methods that predominantly
345   focus on object detection or semantic segmentation tasks. Majorly, we implement the training
346   pipeline for keypoint estimation on top of OpenPCDet (Team, 2020), and we use sparse convo-
347
      lution operators from spconv (Contributors, 2022). We choose VoxelNeXt Chen et al. (2023), a fully
      sparse network for 3D object detection, as the baseline architecture. To enhance the keypoint lo-
348
      calization accuracy, our approach differs from VoxelNeXt from two perspectives: 1) we employ a
349
      dual-branch solution where an additional context branch is used to preserve the global spatial de-
350   tails, and 2) we enhance spatial awareness by projecting absolute 3D coordinates to 2D BEVs. The
351   effectiveness of the proposed modules is supported by the ablation results in Table 2. Notably, unlike
352   previous point-voxel blocks such as PVCNN (Liu et al., 2019) and PVT (Zhang et al., 2022), our
353   spatial-context blocks are fully based on sparse voxels, without the need for voxelization and devox-
354   elization within each building block. Essentially, the context branch captures per-voxel features to
355   mitigate the loss of global context during successive convolutional blocks to ensure that each voxel
356   retains a comprehensive understanding of its surroundings, thereby enhancing the accuracy and
357   robustness of keypoint detection. In contrast to PVT’s box-attention strategy, which inefficiently
358
      handles dense tensors by repeatably converting between dense tensors and sparse representations,
      we take advantage of the fully sparse architecture for a more efficient implementation.

363   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
364
365   Dataset We use the Waymo v1.4.2 dataset (Sun et al., 2020). During the training, we merged “Pedes-
366   trian” and “Cyclist” classes together as a “Human” class. Note that there are only 8, 125 human
367   examples with keypoint annotations whilst over 1 million bounding box annotations. We, therefore,
368
      removed the points inside those bounding boxes without keypoint annotations. Each human object is
      labeled with 14 3D keypoints (nose, left/right shoulders, left/right elbows, left/right wrists, left/right
369
      hips, left/right knees, and left/right ankles, head).
370
371   Network The architecture of the network is composed of a stem module followed by four stages,
372   with output channels set to 64, 128, 256, 256, and 256, respectively. Given the high resolution (e.g.
373   1504 × 1504 × 61) of the voxelized point cloud input, we employ larger sparse convolution kernels
374
      (kernel size k = 5) for the downsampling block in both the stem module and the initial stage. For
      the subsequent three stages, we revert to a smaller kernel size (k = 3). To compensate for the
375
      information loss in the BEV projection, we increased the channels from 256 to 384.
376

      6     C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we identify the challenge of learning locally dense features within a sparse environ-
523   ment for human keypoint estimation. We proposed a new 3D fully sparse neural network for esti-
524   mating dense human poses from point clouds. We present a comprehensive solution to the intricate
525
      challenges posed by spatial information of varying densities in the context of human keypoint es-
      timation. Our method combines several novel components including sparse selective kernel layers,
526
      box-attention layers, spatially aware multi-scale BEV fusion, and hybrid feature learning to accu-
527
      rately predict human body keypoints. By combining components that enhance local feature capture
528   with those that safeguard global contextual information, our method ensures effective estimation of
529   human keypoints. Experiments on the Waymo dataset demonstrate the advantages of our approach
530   compared to prior art and we demonstrate improved performance compared to other approaches
531   trained on the same data as well as other approaches trained with additional data. Through the iden-
532   tified challenge and the innovative framework, we pave the way for more nuanced and adaptable
533   systems in this area.
534   Despite these advancements, we further identify certain areas for future exploration and improve-
535   ment. As mentioned above, this work used a small volume of training data, but it could benefit
536   from a larger-scale dataset. While we focus on single-frame point clouds, future work could lever-
537   age temporal information across sequences of LiDAR point clouds. Additionally, instead of the
538   straightforward estimation of keypoints, future work may adopt inverse kinematics to include phys-
539   ical constraints on human body movement. Aside from refining estimated keypoint locations, this
      may especially be useful to handle real-world challenges such as occlusion within motion.
