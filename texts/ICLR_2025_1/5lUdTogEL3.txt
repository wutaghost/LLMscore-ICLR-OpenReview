011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Lifelong person re-identification (L-ReID) focuses on learning sequentially col-
014            lected datasets from different domains to match the same person. Advanced L-
015            ReID methods typically balance the domain gap between different datasets via
016
               domain knowledge modeling, such as knowledge rectification or distribution pro-
               totyping. However, existing methods dismiss balancing discriminative knowl-
017
               edge within different datasets, resulting in conflicts when sequentially accumulat-
018
               ing differential discriminative information in different datasets, e.g., sequentially
019            learning cloth-changing/cloth-consistent knowledge simultaneously, which brings
020            critical catastrophic forgetting problems of old discriminative knowledge. In this
021            paper, we focus on a new but practical task called Cloth-Irrelevant Lifelong Per-
022            son Re-identification (CIL-ReID), which requires matching the same person wear-
023            ing different clothing using sequentially collected data. To tackle the above is-
024            sue, we proposed an Adaptive Discriminative Knowledge Consolidation (ADKC)
025            framework to balance the discriminative information of different domains on L-
026            ReID. Specifically, we propose a Selective Knowledge Forgetting (SKF) module
027
               to correct potential overfitting to specific discrimination (e.g., clothing informa-
               tion) based on new knowledge. In addition, we design a Selective Knowledge
028
               Retention(SKR) module to adaptively compensate for the potential lack of dis-
029
               criminative information based on old knowledge and accelerate differential dis-
030            crimination into a unified framework. To validate our method, two CCL-ReID
031            benchmarks are first established, while extensive experiments on the above two
032            benchmark datasets demonstrate that our method leads to existing advanced meth-
033            ods in the CCL-ReID task.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   As a fundamental task in computer vision, person re-identification (ReID) focuses on retrieving
039   the same person across different locations. However, recent studies have revealed its limitations
040   when it comes to involving continual learning of training data, which is also called the catastrophic
041   forgetting problem. To tackle the above issue, lifelong person re-identification (L-ReID) intends
042   to continuously accumulate knowledge from old and new training data, balancing preserving old
043   knowledge while acquiring new knowledge.
044   To this end, some L-ReID methods preserve old knowledge by storing and replaying additional
045   historical data when training on new data. However, despite some progress, these methods often
046   exhibit a significant performance decline in the data privacy scenario that prohibits data replay.
047   Therefore, the other methods are devoted to preserving old knowledge without historical data, known
048   as the non-replay L-ReID method. Specifically, acknowledging the domain discrepancies among
049   continuously collected datasets, e.g., style, colour, and resolution variations, these methods often
050
      balance old and new knowledge by developing robust modelling strategies, including distribution
      modelling, image patch modelling, and relationship modelling, to mitigate the discrepancies across
051
      different data domains.
052
053   However, these methods typically overlook the discrepancies of identical information among dif-
      ferent datasets, resulting in challenges in effectively accumulating various knowledge across do-

054
      mains. Specifically, as shown in Figure 1, existing L-ReID methods will inevitably handle both
055   cloth-consistent and cloth-changing data when continuously collecting new data. Notably, while
056   clothing information may often effective in the cloth-consistent scenario, it does not always perform
057   equally in the cloth-changing one, which relies more on other discriminative information, such the
058   figure, body, and shape.
059
      Consequently, these methods often encountered significant challenges when adapting to new sce-
060
      narios and preserving the performance in old scenarios, requiring balancing the individual discrimi-
061   nations and the domain discrepancies across datasets. However, drastic changes in the sequentially
062   collected data bring serious conflicts when matching the same person, ultimately hindering the av-
063   erage performance of the both cloth-consistent and cloth-changing scenarios.
064
      Inspired by the above observations, in this paper, we focus on a practical but challenging task, called
065
      Cloth-Irrelevant Lifelong person Re-IDentification (CIL-ReID), which requires employing stream-
066
      ing cloth-consistent and cloth-changing data to perform lifelong learning and perform well in both
067   scenarios. To this end, we proposed an Adaptive Discriminative Knowledge Consolidation (ADKC)
068   framework to balance the discriminative information of different domains on L-ReID. Specifically,
069   we propose a Selective Knowledge Forgetting (SKF) module to correct potential overfitting to spe-
070   cific discrimination (e.g., clothing information) based on new knowledge. In addition, we design
071   a Selective Knowledge Retention(SKR) module to adaptively compensate for the potential lack of
072   discriminative information based on old knowledge and accelerate differential discrimination into a
073   unified framework.
