                                                 Parameter generation has struggled to scale up for a long time, significantly lim-

                                                 iting its range of applications. In this study, we introduce Recurrent diffusion for
013                                              large-scale Parameter Generation, called RPG. We first divide the trained parame-
014                                              ters into non-overlapping parts, after which a recurrent model is proposed to learn
015                                              their relationships. The recurrent model’s outputs, as conditions, are then fed into
016                                              a diffusion model to generate the neural network parameters. Using only a sin-
017                                              gle GPU, recurrent diffusion enables us to generate popular vision and language
018                                              models such as ConvNeXt-L and LoRA parameters of LLaMA-7B. Meanwhile,
019                                              across various architectures and tasks, the generated parameters consistently per-
020                                              form comparable results over trained networks. Notably, our approach also shows
021
                                                 the potential to generate models for handling unseen tasks. This suggests that
                                                 recurrent diffusion largely increases the practicality of parameter generation.

031   2024), has struggled to scale up effectively, limiting their practical applications. As illustrated in
032   Fig. 1, the scale gap between vision (or language) models and the generated parameters is at least
033   104 , posing significant challenges for this field.

      How can we model parameter relationships and leverage them for efficient parameter generation?
071
      ViTs often outperform CNNs in vision tasks by modeling patch relationships and capturing global
072   information via self-attention. In language tasks, LLMs use next token prediction to model token re-
073   lationships, capturing long-range dependencies. Inspired by these approaches, we can consider treat-
074   ing parameter parts as tokens in neural network parameter generation, potentially enabling methods
075   to model inter-parameter relationships and capture dependencies.
076
      Based on the above analysis, we propose Recurrent diffusion for large-scale neural network
077
      Parameters Generation (RPG). Our approach first divides the trained network parameters into a
078   set of non-overlapping parameter parts that are used for supervision (simply called ’tokens’ in the
079   following). Subsequently, we use a recurrent model to learn the relationships among the tokens.
080   Finally, the outputs of the recurrent model, as conditions, are fed into a diffusion process to generate
081   the neural network parameters.
082
      Our approach has the following properties: i) With a single GPU, our approach successfully syn-
083
      thesizes large-scale vision models such as ResNet (He et al., 2016), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021),
084   and ConvNeXt (Liu et al., 2022) series, as well as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) parameters of LLaMA-
085   7B (Touvron et al., 2023). ii) Across various architectures and tasks, the generated parameters
086   maintain comparable performance to the original models. iii) Empirical evidence suggests our ap-
087   proach has potential for generating models in unseen tasks. We anticipate that this work will inspire
088   future research in large-scale parameter generation.

      2.1   OVERVIEW
093
094   Our approach comprises two key components: parameter tokenization and recurrent diffusion. We
095   show the inference process of recurrent diffusion in Fig. 3. The permutation state and position
096   embedding are fed into the recurrent model. Then, the outputs of the recurrent model serve as
097   conditions for the diffusion process, which generates the entire neural network parameters.
098

      2.3    R ECURRENT D IFFUSION
141
142   Permutation state. Neural network symmetries (Badrinarayanan et al., 2015; Kunin et al., 2021)
143
      do not affect the model outcomes but increase the difficulty of learning the parameter distribution.
      To address this, we introduce a unique state for each trained model W via one-hot embedding.
144
      This operation provides a guide for the generated parameters to mitigate the influence of parameter
145
      symmetries. For simplicity, we use S to represent the permutation state of W .
146
147   Position embedding. Inspired by ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), we also encode the layer and token
148   information described in the parameter tokenization (Sec. 2.2) using a two-dimensional sinusoidal
149
      position encoding. Specifically, the first dimension encodes the layer index of the token in the
      original model, while the second dimension encodes the position of the token within its layer. For
150
      i-th layer parameter tokens K[i], the position embedding can be formulated as follows,
151
152
                                       position embedding
153                            K[i] −−−−−−−−−−→ e[i] = [e1i , · · · , eji , · · · , eJi ],                  (3)
154
155   where eji denotes the positional embedding of the parameters belong to j-th token of i-th layer.
156
      Recurrent model. After obtaining the parameter tokens, permutation states, and position embed-
157   dings, we use a recurrent model to learn the representation of the parameter tokens. For clarity, we
158   will refer to the output of the recurrent model as the ‘prototype’ in the following. This operation can
159   be written as follows:
160
161
                                Pij , Hij = f (Hij−1 , eji , S), i ∈ [1, I], j ∈ [1, J],                    (4)

162
      where Pij and Hij denote the prototype and hidden state of the parameters belonging to j-th token of
163   i-th layer, respectively. f (·, ·, ·) denotes the state transition function. The structure of the recurrent
164   model is simple. Considering efficiency, we default to using Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) followed
165   by an MLP to project the feature dimension to the required size for the diffusion model. We also
166   conduct ablation studies with other recurrent model architectures, such as LSTM (Hochreiter et al.,
167   1997) and transformer with its decoder in a causal manner (Vaswani, 2017).
168
      Parameter diffusion. Inspired by p-diff (Wang et al., 2024) and MAR (Li et al., 2024a), we use
169   1D convolution to build the diffusion model. In this part, the parameter prototypes, serving as
170   conditions, are fed into the diffusion process along with random noise. We optimize our approach
171   through the following equation:

180
181   In this section, we first introduce our experimental setup for reproducing. Then, we report the re-
182   sults on classification, semantic segmentation, object detection, and commonsense reasoning tasks,
183   respectively. After that, the ablation studies are presented for a better understanding of the benefits
184   of our approach. Finally, we compare our approach with the previous works.

186   3.1       S ETUP
187
188   Datasets and architectures. We mainly evaluate our method across a wide range of tasks, including
189   ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009) for the classification, ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017) for the semantic
190
      segmentation, COCO (Lin et al., 2014) for the object detection, and BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019),
      PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), SIQA (Sap et al., 2019), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), and ARC (Clark
191
      et al., 2018) for the commonsense reasoning tasks. To verify the scalability of our approach, we
192
      conduct experiments on various architectures with parameter counts ranging from several to hundred
193   million. Details of parameter counts can be found in Tab. 1, 2, 3.
194
195   Trained parameters collection. We take parameters collection on the ImageNet-1K as an example.
196
      To save the cost, we finetune the full parameters of the models released in timm 1 and save 50
      checkpoints as the training data. For each checkpoint, we assign a unique permutation state to guide
197
      the generated parameters.
198
199   Training details. We default to using Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) as the architecture of the recurrent
200   model. The length of parameter tokens, permutation states, position embeddings, and prototypes is
201   set to 8192. It is worth noting that the permutation states and position embeddings are fixed during
202
      the training by default. We also study the influence of the token length, varying it from 1024 to
      16384. The parameter diffusion consists of 1D convolutional layers. More details about the model
203
      architectures, hyperparameters, and training process can be found in Appendix A.1.
204
205   Inference details. We input permutation states and position embeddings into the recurrent model to
206   generate the prototypes. Then, the diffusion model utilizes the prototypes as conditions, along with
207   random noises, to synthesize the entire network parameters. We repeat the above process 10 times
208
      and report the best, average, medium, minimum, and standard deviation results.

      5         R ELATED W ORKS
484
485   Parameter generation. The core idea of parameter generation is to learn the distribution of trained
      parameters. Stochastic neural networks (Sompolinsky et al., 1988; Bottou et al., 1991; Wong, 1991;


                                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Schmidt et al., 1992; Murata et al., 1994; Graves, 2011) and Bayesian neural networks (Neal, 2012;
487   Kingma & Welling, 2013; Rezende et al., 2014; Kingma et al., 2015; Blundell et al., 2015; Gal
488   & Ghahramani, 2016) model the priors or probability distributions over the parameters. These ap-
489   proaches mainly employ the learned prior knowledge of parameters to improve robustness and gen-
490   eralization, and to mitigate overfitting and uncertainties in neural networks. However, these methods
491   are limited by their poor generalization to large-scale or more complex real-world scenarios.
492
      HyperNetworks (Ha et al., 2017), i.e., a small network, is proposed to generate various architectures’
493   parameters for a larger network. One year later, Smash (Brock et al., 2018) extends the range of the
494   architectures via a memory read-writes scheme. With the development of diffusion technology,
495   many works (Peebles et al., 2022; Chou et al., 2023; Erkoç et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Soro
496   et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Jin et al., 2024) use the diffusion process to generate the
497   neural network parameters. G.pt (Peebles et al., 2022) collects 23 million checkpoints as the training
498   data and uses conditional diffusion to learn the distribution of the parameters. Besides the heavy
499   cost of collecting so many checkpoints, G.pt can only generate less than 10K parameters. P-diff
500   proposes unconditional diffusion to mimic the parameter updating and extend the size of generated
501
      parameters to 150K. COND P-DIFF (Jin et al., 2024) and Tina (Li et al., 2024b) introduce the task-
      or text-controlled parameter generation method. Unfortunately, the above methods have a common
502
      drawback: can not generate large-scale parameters, such as a whole ResNet, ViT, ConvNeXt, or
503
      LoRA. Therefore, our approach indeed largely increases the practicality of this field.
504
505   Recurrent models. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) were first proposed to process sequen-
506   tial data, for example, the text. To tackle the vanishing gradient problem in early RNNs, long
507   short-term memory (LSTM) (Hochreiter, 1991; 1997). In recent years, transformer-based mod-
508   els (Vaswani, 2017) starts to dominate the sequential data processing, due to their parallelized train-
509   ing and scalability. Although most transformers (Radford, 2018; Touvron et al., 2023) are used in
510
      the auto-regressive manner, they can be seamlessly converted into a recurrent model. However, the
      transformer-based models suffer have also been suffering from the the quadratic complexity prob-
511
      lem. In recent year, various attempts, such as linear attentions (Wang et al., 2020; Choromanski
512
      et al., 2020), RWKV (Peng et al., 2023), Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), and xL-
513   STM (Beck et al., 2024), have been don to tackle this problem. Different models above, which
514   mainly focuses on modeling the language data, in this paper, we employ the recurrent model to
515   build the relationship between parameters in neural networks.
516
517   Diffusion models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol,
518   2021) witness an emergence in recent several years, due to their superiority in image generation.
519
      Many following works focus on improving the generation quality and efficiency of the diffusion
      model. For the first problem, Rombach et al. (2022) propose to conduct diffusion in the latent space,
520
      enabling high-resolution image synthesis. Peebles & Xie (2023) leverage the transformer (Vaswani,
521
      2017) to explore scalability of diffusion models, proving the possibility of generating higher quality
522   images with increasing size models. To solve the second problem, efficient samplers (Song et al.,
523   2020; Lu et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023), efficiency models (Fang et al., 2023; So et al., 2024; Yang
524   et al., 2023), and global acceleration approaches (Ma et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024) are proposed.
525   These method s facilitate generating high quality images with less computational and/or memory
526   cost. Although significant progress on diffusion models has been made in image generation, how
527   to improve quality and efficiency in large-scale parameter generation is still under-explored. In this
528   paper, we propose the recurrent parameter generation model to tackle this problem.
529
530   6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
531
532   Our approach demonstrates promising results in large-scale parameter generation across various
533   vision and language tasks. However, we acknowledge that achieving true ‘AI creating AI’ remains
534   a distant goal. Firstly, while our method shows potential in generating models for unseen tasks,
535   it currently faces limitations in generating parameters for novel model architectures. Secondly,
536   our approach is constrained by modeling parameter relationships within a single task, potentially
537   limiting its practical applicability. More importantly, future work should focus on simultaneously
538   modeling parameter relationships across diverse architectures and tasks. Such an approach could
539
      yield a more powerful and versatile parameter generator, potentially advancing us closer to the ‘AI
      creating AI’ era. We hope our approach will inspire future research in this field.

840   A.1   T RAINING RECIPE
841
842   In this section, we provide detailed training recipes and supplementary information. The number
843   of parameters generated by our approach ranges from approximately 3K to 200M. The significant
844   disparity necessitates different training settings. Generally, as the number of parameters increases,
845   the learning process becomes more challenging, requiring higher training costs, particularly for
846
      generating parameters beyond 50 million. Therefore, our training settings are divided into two
      categories: the default setting and the setting for parameters exceeding 50 million, as is shown in
847
      Tab. 11.
848
849   Data parallelism: When the number of parameters is less than 50 million, we adopt a single GPU
850   to run the training process. For larger number of parameters, we employ distributed data parallelism
851   to facilitate the training.
852   Diffusion batch size: In our approach, the diffusion model is shared across all tokens. Typically, all
853   tokens can be fed as a single batch into the diffusion model for training. However, in practice, we
854   randomly select a subset of tokens from a long sequence for training, rather than feeding all parts at
855   once. This approach significantly reduces memory usage without compromising performance. The
856   “diffusion batch size” in Tab. 11 refers to the number of tokens fed into the diffusion model during
857   a single training iteration.

      In this section, we provide specific details about the proposed recurrent model and diffusion model

899   Details of recurrent model. By default, the recurrent model consists of two Mamba layers (Gu &
900   Dao, 2023). As the increasing of parameters to generate, we need a larger recurrent model to capture
901   the information in these parameters. The size of the recurrent model is mainly determined by the

903   size, we categorize our model into four versions: Tiny, Small, Base, and Large.

905
      one-dimensional convolutional architecture. The parameters of the diffusion model are significantly
906   fewer than those of the recurrent model. We feed the prototypes from the recurrent model as condi-
907   tions into the diffusion model by directly adding them to the feature map.

1164
1165   We demonstrate the importance of the permutation state in mitigating model symmetry is-
1166   sues (Badrinarayanan et al., 2015; Kunin et al., 2021). Model symmetry refers to a characteristic
1167   in neural networks where parameters exhibit permutation symmetry—swapping certain parameters
1168   changes the parameters themselves, but the model output remains unchanged. This feature brings a
1169   significant challenge in modeling and understanding of neural network parameters. During parame-
1170   ter generation training, using checkpoints from different random seeds can introduce this problem.
1171   To demonstrate the effectiveness of the permutation state in RPG, we conduct experiments by train-
1172   ing our RPG with and without it, using checkpoints (ViT-Tiny on CIFAR-10) from various random
1173   seeds. Results in Tab. 15 demonstrates that our permutation state design obviously alleviates the
1174   learning difficulties caused by parameter symmetry. It enables the generated parameters to achieve
1175   performance comparable to the original checkpoints, even with 10 random seeds. However, our
1176   design may not be the optimal solution and the performance declines when the seeds increase to 20.
1177
       This encourages us to further explore this issue in the future.
