011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have been applied in expert
014            systems across various domains, providing new opportunities for AI in Educa-
015            tion (AI4Education). Educational interactions involve a cyclical exchange be-
016
               tween teachers and students. Current research predominantly focuses on using
               LLMs to simulate teachers, leveraging their expertise to enhance student learn-
017
               ing outcomes. However, the simulation of students, which could improve teach-
018
               ers’ instructional skills, has received insufficient attention due to the challenges
019            of modeling and evaluating virtual students. This research poses the question:
020            “Can LLMs be utilized to develop virtual student agents that mimic human-like
021            behavior and individual variability?” Unlike expert systems focusing on knowl-
022            edge delivery, virtual students must replicate learning difficulties, emotional re-
023            sponses, and linguistic uncertainties. These traits present significant challenges
024            in both modeling and evaluation. To address these issues, this study focuses on
025            language learning as a context for modeling virtual student agents. We propose
026            a novel AI4Education framework, termed SOE (Scene - Object - Evaluation), to
027
               systematically construct LVSA (LLM-based Virtual Student Agents). By curat-
               ing a dataset of personalized teacher-student interactions with various personality
028
               traits, question types, and learning stages, and fine-tuning LLMs using LoRA, we
029
               conduct multi-dimensional evaluation experiments that integrate both subjective
030            human evaluations and objective metrics. Specifically, we: (1) develop a theo-
031            retical framework for generating LVSA; (2) integrate human subjective evalua-
032            tion metrics into GPT-4 assessments, demonstrating a strong correlation between
033            human evaluators and GPT-4 in judging LVSA authenticity; and (3) validate that
034            LLMs can generate human-like, personalized virtual student agents in educational
035            contexts, laying a foundation for future applications in pre-service teacher training
036            and multi-agent simulation environments.

299
      The evaluation of LVSA followed a four-step process (Figure 1 (e)), enabling both human and GPT-
300
      4-based assessments of LVSA performance. (1) Inference dataset construction: teacher questions
301   tailored to student personality traits were generated by GPT-4 using prompt engineering, creating
302   a dataset with two fields: system (teaching scenario and traits) and query (teacher questions). (2)
303   Fine-tuned inference: Fine-tuned models then generated responses for five distinct personality
304   traits (HE, LO, HN, HA, LC), producing personalized outputs. (3) Direct inference: To establish
305   a baseline, direct inference was conducted using non-fine-tuned models, highlighting improvements
306   in personalization and realism after fine-tuning. (4) Subjective evaluation dataset reconstruction:
307   A total of 12,312 responses, spanning various learning stages and question types, were generated
308   across four foundation models and five personality types. For human evaluation, 115 samples were
309
      randomly selected, including 40 fine-tuned and 40 direct inference responses, along with 35 real
      student responses as controls, with five fatigue test items to ensure alertness. The remaining 12,232
310
      responses, split evenly between pre- and post-fine-tuned models, were used for large-scale GPT-4
311
      evaluations, providing insights into the effectiveness of LVSA construction (see Appendix C.3).

      5.6   BAD C ASE A NALYSIS
490
491   The experimental results indicate that fine-tuned virtual students demonstrated strong performance
492   across various educational scenarios, effectively simulating personalized, human-like behaviors.
493   However, some noteworthy anomalies were identified, revealing limitations of LLMs in role-playing
494   contexts and suggesting areas for future research.
495   Poor fine-tuning performance of LC LVSA:The suboptimal performance of LC personality mod-
496   eling arises from the sparse representation of its associated behaviors and language traits in existing
497   data, limiting the model’s ability to capture typical LC characteristics. Hallucinations, such as repet-
498   itive language or contextually inconsistent knowledge errors (see Appendix D.7.1), further compro-
499   mise output quality and personality alignment. Additionally, LC traits, potentially conflicting with
500   educational ethics (e.g., lack of responsibility), often trigger the ethical safeguards of large language
501   models, increasing generation constraints. To address these issues, we implemented personalized
502   prompt design, context-specific data generation, and content validation to enhance LC personality
503
      simulation. Future efforts will focus on expanding data diversity, refining techniques such as person-
      alized fine-tuning or constraint strategies, and developing robust mechanisms to monitor and correct
504
      hallucinations, aiming to improve the accuracy and applicability of LC personality modeling.
505
506   Inconsistent fine-tuning effects across question types: Although all four models improved in han-
507   dling both closed and open-ended questions, none achieved statistically significant improvements
508   across both types. This inconsistency likely stems from inherent differences in cognitive demands
509
      (see Appendix D.7.2). Closed-ended questions are more structured, making them easier for models
      to manage, whereas open-ended questions require deeper reasoning and creative thinking, resulting
510
      in greater variability in performance.
511
512   Suboptimal fine-tuning performance of LLaVa: Despite improvements in personalization, ques-
513   tion types, and learning stages, LLaVa’s overall performance remained weaker than that of others
514   (see Appendix D.7.3). This disparity is primarily due to differences between pre-training and fine-
515
      tuning data domains, particularly cross-language issues. Given that LLaVa predominantly relies on
      English pre-training data, its adaptability and generalization to Chinese contexts are constrained.

      6     C ONCLUSION
518
519   This study addresses the challenges of modeling and evaluating early adolescent students by propos-
520   ing the SOE framework, leveraging LLMs to construct virtual students and explore their potential for
521   human-likeness and personalization. Its modular design—Scene, Object, Evaluation—enables flex-
522   ible adaptation to educational systems, supports discipline-specific adjustments, and optimizes for
523
      students’ age and cultural diversity. Through a combination of subjective and objective evaluations,
      the SOE framework validates the performance of virtual students across diverse personality traits,
524
      question types, and teaching stages. Experimental results demonstrate a high degree of similarity
525
      between the generated dialogues and real student behaviors, highlighting the significant potential of
526   LLMs in simulating human-like roles to support pre-service teacher training.
527
528   Moreover, the paper outlines critical future directions, including real-world applications, model opti-
529
      mization, and the reduction of hallucinations. Regarding real-world applications, findings currently
      stem from controlled environments complemented by human feedback. Future research should ex-
530
      tend LVSA implementations to actual educational settings by incorporating multimodal data, thereby
531
      enriching the complexity of teaching scenarios and conducting longitudinal studies to thoroughly
532   assess their educational impact and feasibility. Concerning model optimization, the study applies
533   Swift and LoRA techniques for efficiency enhancement, yet further investigations into model distil-
534   lation and response sampling are essential for reducing computational demands and elevating model
535   adaptability. Lastly, while initial steps to mitigate hallucinations include refined prompt design and
536   post-processing checks, continued improvements are necessary. These should involve integrating
537   authentic classroom data, refining modeling approaches, maintaining ethical standards, and devel-
538   oping comprehensive strategies for identifying and rectifying content discrepancies. Ongoing re-
539   finement and expansion of the SOE framework aim to robustly support pre-service teacher training
      and drive innovation in educational technology.

980    A.1   V IRTUAL S TUDENT D EVELOPMENT AND R ELATED T ECHNOLOGIES
981
982
       Teachers, as a central resource for educational development, play a critical role in ensuring the
983
       quality of education. Pre-service teachers, who represent the future of the teaching workforce, de-
984
       pend heavily on practical training for their professional growth (Loewenberg Ball & Forzani (2009);
985    Zeichner (2012); Ward et al. (2018)). Virtual students provide a promising tool for simulating class-
986    room interactions, offering a cost-effective, highly interactive, and flexible platform for pre-service
987    teacher training and feedback (Dotger et al. (2015; 2010); Shaughnessy & Boerst (2018)). As tech-
988    nology in this domain has evolved, virtual students can be classified into three main categories:
989    role-playing virtual students, programmatically pre-set virtual students, and virtual students powered
990    by large language models (LLMs). Each of these approaches has distinct characteristics, technical
991    underpinnings, and limitations.
992    Role-playing virtual students are among the earliest forms of simulated student behavior (Kersh
993    (1963); Colwell (2013); Frederick et al. (2010); Shapira-Lishchinsky (2015); Dalgarno et al. (2016)).
994    In this approach, teachers or other adults imitate the cognitive and emotional responses of students,
995    offering real-time interaction (Dieker et al. (2015); Hume (2012); Teasdale et al. (2016)). While
996    this method allows for some degree of realism in mimicking student behaviors, it relies heavily on
997    the participants’ ability to accurately simulate a student’s responses. Consequently, this approach
998    is resource-intensive, lacks scalability, and cannot provide the level of automation or flexibility
999
       required for modern teacher training systems (Sparks (2011)).
1000   Programmatically pre-set virtual students use pre-defined algorithms and scripted rules to simu-
1001   late student interactions, providing a degree of automation for classroom simulations (Christensen
1002   et al. (2011); Delamarre et al. (2021); Shernoff et al. (2018); Kelleci & Aksoy (2021)). These vir-
1003   tual students are typically powered by finite state machines or pre-scripted scenarios (Kervin et al.
1004   (2006); Lugrin et al. (2019)). For instance, a virtual student might respond to a teacher’s question
1005
       based on a pre-defined set of rules or dialogues. This approach is advantageous in its repeatability
       and operational control, which makes it useful for specific teaching tasks that require consistency
1006
       (Carrington et al. (2011); Hixon & So (2009)). However, the rigidity of the pre-defined scripts often
1007
       leads to uniform and mechanical responses, limiting their ability to adapt to the diverse and dynamic
1008   interactions that characterize real-world classrooms (Badiee & Kaufman (2015); Rayner & Fluck
1009   (2014)).
1010
1011
       Recently, LLM-based virtual students have gained attention with the development of models like
       GPT-4 (Achiam et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024c); Zhang et al. (2024); Lee et al.; Markel et al. (2023)).
1012
       These LLM-driven agents leverage advanced natural language processing capabilities to generate
1013
       dynamic and contextually relevant student responses. Although LLMs introduce greater flexibil-
1014   ity and linguistic variability into virtual student interactions, current research in this area remains
1015   nascent. Most existing studies focus on using prompt engineering to generate virtual student agents,
1016   yet the exchanging responses of LLMs inevitably faces significant challenges including role flip-
1017   ping, instruction repeating and fake replies (Qian et al. (2024)). In this way, these agents often
1018   struggle to replicate the full range of behaviors, cognitive abilities, and emotional nuances that real
1019   students exhibit. While LLMs have demonstrated strong capabilities in natural language generation
1020   and knowledge understanding (Jiang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023); Park et al. (2023)), they have
1021   yet to fully mimic the language expression levels and cognitive challenges faced by real students.
1022   This study builds on this line of research by exploring whether LLMs can be leveraged to create
1023   human-like and personalized virtual student agents that better simulate student cognition and emo-
1024   tional responses. Our goal is to push beyond basic prompt-based LLM applications by construct-
1025   ing virtual students that can provide more authentic, dynamic, and adaptive learning interactions,
       thereby improving their applicability in pre-service teacher training.

       A.2   LLM S ’ MAIN CAPABILITIES TO SUPPORT AI4E DUCATION
1027
1028
1029   In recent years, LLMs have demonstrated exceptional performance across a range of core language
1030
       processing tasks (Brown (2020); Achiam et al. (2023)), including language understanding, genera-
       tion, reasoning, knowledge transfer, and multimodal processing. Trained on vast amounts of textual
1031
       data, these models have acquired the ability to comprehend complex linguistic structures and are
1032
       widely applicable to various natural language processing tasks.
1033
1034   The language understanding capabilities of large language models are primarily reflected in their
1035   ability to infer meaning from context, capture complex syntactic structures, and accurately grasp the
1036
       intent of a given text. In widely-used natural language understanding benchmarks such as GlUE
       (Wang (2018)), SuperGLUE (Wang et al. (2019)) and SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al. (2018)), models like
1037
       GPT-4 (Achiam et al. (2023)), LLaMA (Touvron et al. (2023)), and MiniCPM (Hu et al. (2024b))
1038
       have demonstrated performance that rivals, and in some cases surpasses, human levels (T.Simonite
1039   (2018); Li et al. (2023a)). For instance, GPT-4’s high scores on SuperGLUE indicate its ability to
1040   process extended text and maintain a deep understanding of contextual relationships (Achiam et al.
1041   (2023); Guo et al. (2023)). This is particularly important for building virtual students, as they must
1042   respond to teachers’ questions with appropriate cognitive reactions, simulating the authentic thought
1043   processes of real students.
1044
       In benchmark tests like CommonGen (Lin et al. (2019)) and Wikitext-103 (Merity et al. (2017)),
1045   models such as GPT-4 and T5 (Raffel et al. (2020)) have demonstrated strong language generation
1046   capabilities, particularly in generating natural language paragraphs, dialogues, and long-form text
1047   (Li et al. (2024b)). Wikitext-103, in particular, focuses on long-text generation tasks and evaluates
1048   the model’s ability to maintain contextual consistency throughout the generation process. Current
1049   language models not only generate coherent responses in dialogue systems but also preserve logical
1050   consistency across multiple conversational turns (Zeng et al. (2023)). This provides a solid technical
1051   foundation for this study, where GPT-4 is used to generate teacher-student dialogue datasets under
1052   different personality traits.
1053   In language reasoning benchmarks such as the ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge) (Clark et al.
1054   (2018)) and MNLI (Nangia et al. (2017)), models are required to perform complex logical infer-
1055   ence (Hu et al. (2020)) and commonsense knowledge (Clark et al. (2018)) based on the given text.
1056   These tasks demand that the model not only understand the semantics of the questions but also use
1057   contextual information to provide appropriate answers. Current top-tier LLMs have demonstrated
1058   strong reasoning abilities in such tests (Zhong et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2024)). For example,
1059
       GPT-4 and PaLM (Chowdhery et al. (2023)) have shown significant improvements in the ARC test
       compared to earlier models, enabling them to handle more complex reasoning tasks. This reasoning
1060
       capability provides a solid technical foundation for virtual students to exhibit diverse reactions in
1061
       the classroom, particularly in responding to open-ended questions, where the virtual student can
1062   demonstrate multi-step reasoning abilities.
1063
1064   Language transfer is a significant strength of large language models, enabling them to apply knowl-
1065
       edge learned from one task to other domains or language tasks. In multilingual benchmarks such
       as Cross-Lingual Natural Language Inference (XNLI) (Conneau et al. (2018)) and TyDiQA (Clark
1066
       et al. (2020)), models have demonstrated strong cross-linguistic capabilities, effectively handling
1067
       comprehension and reasoning tasks across various languages. Models like LLaMA, T5, Qwen (Bai
1068   et al. (2023)), PaLM and GPT-4 have shown high generalization abilities in these tests, success-
1069   fully addressing the challenges posed by different languages and tasks (Chowdhery et al. (2023);
1070   Ahuja et al. (2023)). This ability is particularly crucial for developing multilingual virtual students,
1071   as teachers may interact with students in diverse linguistic and cultural contexts. By leveraging
1072   the knowledge transfer capabilities of large language models, virtual students can adapt to various
1073   language environments, providing flexible instructional feedback.
1074   In recent years, multimodal models like Flamingo (Alayrac et al. (2022)) and InternVL (Chen et al.
1075   (2024b)) have become research hotspots, combining visual and textual processing capabilities and
1076   extending the application range of language models. In multimodal benchmarks such as Visual
1077   Question Answering (VQA) (Antol et al. (2015); Kafle & Kanan (2017)), models like InternVL
1078   have demonstrated their ability to generate accurate text responses based on visual inputs, showcas-
1079   ing their strong text generation and comprehension skills. These multimodal models offer greater
       potential for the future development of virtual students. For instance, by integrating both visual

1232   B.2.1     C ONCEPTUAL T HEORY: C HARACTERISTICS OF E ARLY A DOLESCENT S TUDENTS

1234   Early adolescents (typically aged 10 to 15) represent a critical developmental stage where students
1235   undergo substantial physiological, cognitive, social-emotional, and moral-spiritual changes (Hall
1236   (1905); Caskey & Anfara (2014)). These underlying developmental traits manifest as explicit be-
1237   haviors in the classroom, providing essential theoretical insights for constructing virtual student
1238
       agents.
1239   At the physiological level, rapid development of the prefrontal cortex, which governs complex cog-
1240   nitive functions such as decision-making, reasoning, and impulse control, contrasts with the faster
1241   development of the limbic system, responsible for emotional regulation (Blakemore & Choudhury
       (2006); Giedd (2004)). This imbalance often leads to intense emotional responses coupled with lim-


                                                                 23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       ited cognitive regulation, which influences classroom behavior and language expression. Adoles-
1243   cents may display strong emotional reactions, cognitive confusion, and variability in participation,
1244   establishing a physiological basis for their learning behaviors.
1245
1246
       Cognitively, adolescents transition from concrete operational thinking to abstract logical reasoning
       (Elkind (1981)). According to Piaget’s theory (Piaget (1952)), they gradually develop the ability
1247
       to handle abstract concepts and logical analysis, though they remain partially bound by concrete
1248
       experiences. They exhibit early signs of critical thinking but are easily influenced by emotions and
1249   external factors, leading to repetitive and uncertain language expression (Nippold (1993); Nippold
1250   et al. (1999)), especially when faced with abstract issues.
1251
1252
       Social-emotionally, as self-awareness increases, adolescents become more concerned with their
       roles within social groups, experiencing frequent emotional fluctuations. This heightened sensitiv-
1253
       ity affects their classroom behavior—teacher feedback and peer reactions significantly impact their
1254
       engagement. In constructing virtual students, it is crucial to simulate such emotional dynamics to
1255   accurately reflect students’ responses to classroom stimuli, such as nervousness, anxiety, or excite-
1256   ment.
1257
1258
       Morally and spiritually, adolescents begin developing their own values and face complex moral
       issues (Scales (2010)). According to Kohlberg (Kohlberg (1987)), they transition from external rule
1259
       adherence to internalizing societal norms. However, their moral judgments are often emotionally
1260
       driven and oversimplified. In class, adolescents interpret moral situations from texts through per-
1261   sonal experiences (Scales (2010)), reflecting emerging critical thinking while simplifying complex
1262   moral issues.
1263
1264
       These implicit traits manifest in the classroom as individualized behaviors and language use (Pen-
       nebaker & King (1999)). Adolescents’ classroom participation varies greatly—some engage actively
1265
       while others remain hesitant. Their language often involves repetition and uncertainty, especially
1266
       with abstract questions, using filler words like “um” or “uh”. This reflects their cognitive insecuri-
1267   ties and limited grasp of complex issues. They also frequently relate classroom content to personal
1268   experiences, which should be considered when designing instructional tasks. Additionally, emerg-
1269   ing critical thinking shows students beginning to analyze multiple perspectives, though these skills
1270   are not yet fully mature.

1286   recalling facts and accurately understanding specific knowledge. Careful design of question types

       In this study, we focus on early adolescents (ages 10-15, i.e., middle school students) as the target
       group for virtual student modeling, drawing insights from cognitive and developmental psychology.
1421
       Students at this stage demonstrate unique cognitive development and language expression character-
1422
       istics, presenting distinct challenges. Prior research indicates that early adolescents are transitioning
1423   from concrete to abstract thinking, with cognitive development not yet fully matured (Piaget (1952)).
1424   Compared to other age groups, these students have relatively weaker logical reasoning skills and of-
1425   ten rely more on intuitive perception and concrete experiences for problem-solving. This cognitive
1426   limitation frequently results in unclear language expression and inconsistent thought processes when
1427   dealing with complex tasks (Kail et al. (2016)). Therefore, modeling students at this developmental
1428   stage offers a more accurate representation of diverse classroom behaviors while posing significant
1429   challenges for large language models in handling tasks requiring complex cognitive abilities.
1430   Early adolescents often exhibit limited language expression, characterized by high levels of uncer-
1431   tainty and repetition in verbal responses during classroom interactions, especially with open-ended
1432   questions. This hesitation and lack of confidence stem not only from lower cognitive development
1433   but also from limited language proficiency (Cole & SCRIBNER (1978)). Thus, selecting this age
1434   group as the target for virtual student modeling holds substantial representational value, particularly
1435   for assessing whether large language models can effectively simulate students’ language and cog-
1436   nitive responses, creating human-like, personalized virtual students. Compared to students at other
1437
       developmental stages, early adolescents tend to offer simpler logical responses and are more easily
       influenced by external stimuli, making modeling this group both more challenging and valuable for
1438
       research.
1439
1440   Building on this foundation, we use Information Processing Theory to further analyze the specific
1441   cognitive processes involved when students answer questions in the classroom. According to this
1442   theory, the thinking process can be divided into three stages: focusing on the question, understanding
1443
       the question, and providing a response (Atkinson (1968)). First, students must direct their attention
       to the teacher’s question, requiring adequate attention and selective information-processing abilities.
1444
       Next, they need to understand the question’s meaning and relate it to their existing knowledge,
1445
       demonstrating cognitive processing skills. Finally, students generate a response, relying not only
1446   on their knowledge but also on their ability to articulate their thoughts clearly. In virtual student
1447   modeling, it is crucial to accurately represent these three stages to ensure appropriate responses
1448   within realistic classroom scenarios, effectively simulating students’ thought processes and cognitive
1449   reactions.
1450
       We selected language-based subjects as the modeling context because language teaching empha-
1451
       sizes language expression, emotional experience, and value cultivation (Bruner et al. (1966)). This
1452   focus aligns closely with the core capabilities of large language models (LLMs), which are adept
1453   at processing and generating natural language. Language subjects require not only understand-
1454   ing and applying fundamental knowledge such as grammar and vocabulary but also involve deep
1455   interpretation of complex texts and emotional expression. This combination of emotion and cog-
1456   nition provides an ideal platform for evaluating and optimizing virtual student models, particularly
1457   in assessing whether they can replicate real students’ linguistic styles, emotional expressions, and
       responses to value-based questions.

       In summary, this study selects early adolescents as the target group for virtual student modeling due
1459   to their developing cognitive and linguistic abilities, as well as the representative complexity of their
1460   classroom interactions. Additionally, language-based subjects serve as an optimal testing ground
1461   for evaluating large language models, offering comprehensive insights into language expression,
1462   emotion, and attitudes. This approach provides a solid theoretical foundation and practical value for
1463   the future development of virtual student agents.

1516   C.1.1    DATA SOURCE
1517
1518   The dataset utilized in this study is sourced from the National Smart Education Platform, an
1519   initiative developed by the Ministry of Education to provide authoritative and standardized educa-
1520   tional materials for teachers and students nationwide. This platform integrates the official junior
1521   high school Chinese language curriculum, comprising textbook passages, practice exercises, com-
1522   prehension assessments, and recitation tasks. These resources encapsulate the essential knowledge
1523
       and skills required for junior high school Chinese education, adhering to national standards.
1524   The dataset is focused on two pivotal aspects: text comprehension and text memorization. Both
1525   are critical not only for evaluating student performance in junior high school Chinese courses but
1526   also for assessing overall language proficiency. To ensure comprehensive and diverse content, we
1527   selected a wide range of lessons and corresponding exercises.
1528   For text comprehension, the dataset includes various passages, chapters, and comprehension ques-
1529   tions designed to assess students’ ability to understand the structure of texts, analyze sentence and
1530   paragraph organization, infer details, explain vocabulary in context, and interpret emotional tones.
1531   These tasks allow us to evaluate whether LLMs can effectively process and understand Chinese texts
1532   at the junior high level, replicating the skills expected of students in language comprehension.
1533   Text memorization, particularly through recitation, plays a unique role in Chinese language educa-
1534   tion, where students are required to internalize the content, language structures, and literary qualities
1535   of texts. Our dataset includes a series of standardized recitation tasks aimed at evaluating how ac-
1536   curately and completely LLMs can simulate students’ text recitations. This aspect tests the model’s
1537   ability to recall and reproduce text, serving as a benchmark for assessing long-term memory and
1538   linguistic retention.
1539   The dataset is standardized and regularly updated by expert teams to ensure consistency with the
1540   national curriculum. This ensures that the data maintains a high degree of authority, objectivity,
1541   and applicability across educational settings. Data was collected from various question types to
1542   thoroughly assess the performance of foundational models in junior high school Chinese language
1543   scenarios, providing a solid foundation for future model development and refinement.
1544
1545   C.1.2    DATA CONSTRUCTION PROCESS
1546
1547   The data construction process is divided into three main steps: data preparation, prompt design,
1548   and expert revision. The goal of this process is to ensure the accuracy and scientific rigor of the
1549   data, providing high-quality inputs for the foundational model’s scenario evaluation.
1550   Data Preparation: The junior high school Chinese exercises were organized unit by unit, ensuring
1551   that each lesson covered the core knowledge points and skill requirements related to the text. The
1552   exercises for each unit were compiled and converted into PDF format for subsequent input into the
1553   GPT-4 model. This approach not only maintained the integrity of the data structure but also provided
1554   a clear framework for the model’s processing in later stages.
1555   Prompt Design: An instruction-based learning method was employed, in which structured prompts
1556   with “task description-task requirements-specific output” were provided to guide the model in ex-
1557   ecuting designated tasks. By providing clear task instructions, the model could focus on specific
1558   objectives and generate outputs aligned with expectations. This design ensured that the model’s
1559   outputs were accurate, particularly for complex tasks such as text comprehension and recitation.
1560   Expert Revision: To ensure the accuracy of the generated data, a team of professional junior high
1561   school Chinese teachers was invited to review and refine the content produced by GPT-4. The
1562   expert team evaluated the model’s outputs for both text comprehension and recitation, checking
1563   for accuracy, consistency, and alignment with educational standards. Any errors identified were
1564   carefully corrected by the teachers, ensuring that the dataset met the practical requirements of junior
1565   high school Chinese education. This expert revision process further optimized and enhanced the
       quality of the generated content.

1808
       The fine-tuning dataset used in this work aligns with the Basic Chinese Understanding Ability Eval-
1809   uation Dataset in previous section, drawing from real classroom video recordings, textbook content,
1810   and lesson plans prepared by teachers. The diversity and credibility of these data sources ensure that
1811   the large language model can generate responses grounded in authentic classroom interactions and
1812   instructional content, accurately reflecting real-world teaching scenarios.
1813
       Real Classroom Videos: These videos capture genuine interactions between teachers and students,
1814
       including questioning, responses, and feedback. By transcribing and analyzing these videos, we
1815   extract teacher-student dialogue data from various classroom scenarios, enabling the large language
1816   model to learn real classroom communication patterns. This ensures that virtual students’ linguistic
1817   behavior closely mirrors the interactional characteristics of actual classrooms.
1818
       Textbook Content: Textbooks are the core material for classroom instruction, encompassing subject
1819
       knowledge and educational objectives that students are expected to master. Utilizing textbook data
1820
       ensures that virtual students demonstrate a solid understanding of relevant subject matter and align
1821   with instructional goals when generating responses. This provides a knowledge-based foundation
1822   for logical and scientifically accurate responses.
1823
1824
       Lesson Plans: Lesson plans outline the objectives and content of each teaching session and detail
       the design of each instructional phase. This framework guides GPT-4 in generating different types
1825
       of questions, allowing virtual students to adapt to diverse instructional contexts accurately. The in-
1826
       structional design embedded in lesson plans ensures that the generated questions align with specific
1827   teaching objectives, leading virtual students to produce appropriate responses at different stages.
1828   Fine-tuning based on high-quality teacher lesson plans enhances the relevance and effectiveness of
1829   virtual student interactions in classroom settings.

1831   C.2.2    DATA CONSTRUCTION PROCESS
1832
1833   The construction process of the fine-tuning dataset involves several critical steps to ensure the gener-
1834   ated virtual student dialogues are highly realistic and personalized. These steps include data prepa-
1835   ration, prompt design based on real teacher-student dialogues, small-scale expert revision, large-
       scale generation of Big Five personality-based dialogues, and the creation of instruction fine-tuning

       datasets. By integrating in-context learning prompt techniques, the model’s ability to generate dia-

1839   Data Preparation: Representative Chinese language texts for middle school were selected from
1840
       the National Smart Education Resource Platform, encompassing eight genres from the first-year
       curriculum. Corresponding real classroom videos were transcribed to capture key teacher-student
1841
       interactions, such as questioning, responses, and summaries. The extracted dialogue data served as

       valuable linguistic material for fine-tuning, ensuring that virtual students could generate language

       Five personality traits, we employed few-shot in-context learning prompts based on real classroom

1847

       to produce realistic dialogues that reflect different personality traits, enhancing the personalized

3136   To ensure that the large language model accurately captures the linguistic behavior traits of students
3137   with different personalities, the experiment was conducted using a high-performance computing
3138   cluster, equipped with 8 A6000 GPUs. The use of this high-performance cluster not only minimized
3139   training time but also maximized the model’s stability and performance in handling large datasets.
3140   During the model fine-tuning process, the SWIFT framework was employed, which is specifically
3141   designed for efficient fine-tuning of large-scale models. SWIFT integrates Low-Rank Adaptation
3142   (LoRA) (Hu et al. (2021)), a method that introduces low-rank matrices to minimize parameter up-
3143   dates, significantly reducing the computational cost of fine-tuning. This approach enables the per-
3144   sonalization of large models without requiring extensive computational resources. The integration
3145   of LoRA effectively lowers the computational burden while maintaining high precision, making it
3146   particularly suitable for tailoring the model to generate personalized virtual student behaviors based
3147
       on different personality traits.
3148   For each type of virtual student, hyper-parameters were fine-tuned according to their distinct lan-
3149   guage characteristics and cognitive development, ensuring that the generated dialogues accurately
3150   reflect the diversity of student behaviors.

3513   Using the InternVL model as an example, we analyzed LVSA performance across different teaching
3514   stages and question types, revealing clear distinctions in behavior aligned with personality traits.
3515   Pre-lesson Introduction Phase: Questions in this phase are simpler, focusing on setting up the
3516   learning context. High neuroticism (HN) students exhibite nervousness and repetition in response to
3517   closed questions. For instance, when asked “Do you know who wrote Hymn to the Yellow River?”,
3518   they repeated phrases like ”Uh, it’s. . . it’s Guang Weiran.” High agreeableness (HA) students re-
3519   spond positively with emotional language, such as, ”I think the Yellow River is magnificent, like a
3520   giant dragon winding through mountains.” High extraversion (HE) students show strong emotional
3521   engagement in responses to open questions, such as, ”The Yellow River is the symbol of the Chinese
3522
       people’s resilience and strength.” In contrast, low conscientiousness (LC) students display loose and
       illogical language, e.g., ”Uh, she seems, seems like Lu Xun was her teacher, maybe.” Low open-
3523
       ness (LO) students give short, evasive answers, e.g., ”Uh. . . ”. New Lesson Instruction Phase:
3524
       With increasing question complexity, students need to analyze content from the text. HN struggle
3525   with details and give fragmented answers, e.g., ”Uh, Lu Xun, uh, when he laughed, uh, his eyes
3526   were smiling.” HA provide logical and in-depth answers, such as, ”I think it describes the powerful
3527   flow of the Yellow River, as strong as its surging waves.” HE give both structured and expressive
3528   responses, e.g., ”These descriptions made me feel the deep love he has for his hometown.” By com-
3529   parison, LC’s answers are scattered and lacked clear opinions, while LO give brief and monotonous
3530   responses, e.g., ”The water of the Yellow River is muddy, with lots of twists and turns.”
3531   Knowledge Consolidation Phase: This phase requires students to recall and summarize key points.
3532   HN display nervousness, with filler words like, ”Uh, there’s black soil, uh, there’s sorghum. . . ” HA
3533   summarize clearly, e.g., ”The Yellow River symbolizes the greatness and resilience of the Chinese
3534   people, inspiring awe.” HE demonstrate strong organizational ability, e.g., ”This sentence shows the
3535   volunteers’ perseverance and determination to overcome challenges.” LC exhibite repetition and a
3536   lack of logic, while LO demonstrate low confidence and poor knowledge retention, e.g., ”Uh, maybe
3537   it’ s because he really loves his hometown?”
3538   Class Exercise Phase: Questions in this phase emphasize the application of knowledge. HN over-
3539   focus on accuracy in closed questions and struggled with coherence in open questions. HA and HE
3540   provid accurate, diverse answers with emotional and personalized insights, while LC give vague
3541   responses, and LO continue their pattern of brief and incomplete answers.
3542
       Lesson Summary Phase: Differences in students’ summarization abilities become most apparent.
3543   HN give repetitive and vague summaries, e.g., ”Uh, we should, uh, love our hometown and land,
3544   uh, and protect it.”HA offer rich, emotionally resonant summaries, e.g., ”Through this lesson, I’ve
3545   gained a deeper understanding of the resilience and warmth of the Chinese people.” HE provid com-
3546   prehensive and personalized conclusions, e.g., ”Duanmu Hongliang’s oath reflects his deep love for
3547   his hometown and belief in liberation—it reminds us that our hometown is always our harbor.”LC
3548   give overly general summaries, e.g., ”We learned Hymn to the Yellow River,”while LO avoid en-
3549   gagement, e.g., ”Uh, the Yellow River is big and important.”
3550   Overall, LVSA demonstrated significant differences in behavior aligned with personality traits, par-
3551   ticularly in open-ended questions, where cognitive depth and emotional expression were more ev-
3552   ident. For instance, HA and HE students excelled in reflective and emotional answers, while HN
3553   students showed tension and poor coherence. LO students tended toward brief, simplistic responses,
3554   avoiding complex thinking, while LC students produced scattered, occasionally erroneous answers.
3555   These findings suggest that LVSAs can simulate diverse personality-driven behaviors across various
3556   teaching scenarios, enhancing their ability to adapt to different educational contexts.

       D.3   H UMAN T URING E VALUATION Q UESTIONNAIRE
3934
       This experiment was inspired by the classic Turing test and aimed to evaluate whether LVSA could
3935
       emulate the language expression and cognitive levels of real students to the extent that human eval-
3936   uators could not distinguish between them. The core idea of the Turing test is to assess whether a
3937   machine’s language output can be perceived as human by comparing the language generation be-
3938   haviors of humans and machines. Adopting a similar framework, this study examined the alignment
3939   of virtual students’ language with real students’ expressions and focused on whether human evalu-
3940   ators could recognize the distinct personality traits of virtual students. Each participant acted as a
3941   ”judge,” using their teaching experience to determine if the generated dialogues resembled natural
       human student responses.


                                                        73
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


3942
       The experiment involved three main steps: questionnaire completion, think-aloud protocol, and
3943   semi-structured interviews. The questionnaire contained 120 teacher-student dialogues, including
3944   responses from both virtual and real students. Participants, relying on their junior high teaching
3945   experience, were asked to judge whether each response ”resembled a real student’s answer” or not.
3946   The think-aloud protocol was employed during the questionnaire, where participants verbalized their
3947   thought processes in real-time, mirroring the Turing test’s core concept and providing insights into
3948   how linguistic nuances influenced their judgments. These verbalizations were recorded for subse-
3949   quent analysis to determine the criteria used in distinguishing virtual from real student language
3950   performances. After completing the questionnaire, participants engaged in semi-structured inter-
3951
       views to further elaborate on the factors affecting their evaluations. The interview responses were
       coded for consistency and used to develop a scientific evaluation framework to optimize virtual
3952
       students and support future large-scale GPT-4 evaluations.

3955   prior to the formal experiment. Detailed explanations of the experimental procedure and key points
3956   were provided to ensure participants fully understood each stage and adhered to the guidelines,
3957

4901
       thought patterns characteristic of low conscientiousness students are likely to be extremely rare or
4902   nearly absent. This significant gap between the training data and the requirements of the fine-tuning
4903   task makes it difficult for the model to learn and accurately represent these behaviors from limited
4904   data. Additionally, the sparsity of this data increases the likelihood of “hallucination phenomena,”
4905   where the model struggles to maintain semantic coherence, resulting in content that is often dis-
4906   connected from reality. Furthermore, this personality type may align with traits linked to antisocial
4907   behavior or marginalized groups, and large language models typically employ measures to avoid
4908   reinforcing such negative characteristics during training. This further complicates the modeling of
4909   this personality type.

4911   improvements. First, explicit personality-specific prompts were designed to ensure that the model
4912   accurately understands and generates behaviors and language expressions consistent with the LC
4913   personality traits. Second, data generation and guidance were tailored to specific teaching scenarios
       to enhance the task relevance and educational adaptability of the model’s outputs. Lastly, validation

4914
       and optimization of the generated content were conducted to improve the consistency and quality of
4915   the model’s responses.

4918

4919

4920
