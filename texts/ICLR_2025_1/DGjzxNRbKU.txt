009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In Bayesian persuasion, an informed sender strategically discloses information to
012            a receiver so as to persuade them to undertake desirable actions. Recently, Markov
013            persuasion processes (MPPs) have been introduced to capture sequential scenarios
014            where a sender faces a stream of myopic receivers in a Markovian environment.
015            The MPPs studied so far in the literature suffer from issues that prevent them
016            from being fully operational in practice, e.g., they assume that the sender knows
017            receivers’ rewards. We fix such issues by addressing MPPs where the sender has no
018
               knowledge about the environment. We design a learning algorithm for the sender,
               working with partial feedback. We prove that its regret with respect to an optimal
019
               information-disclosure policy grows sublinearly in the number of episodes, as it
020
               is the case for the loss in persuasiveness cumulated while learning. Moreover, we
021            provide a lower bound for our setting matching the guarantees of our algorithm.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Bayesian persuasion (Kamenica & Gentzkow, 2011) studies how an informed sender should strategi-
027   cally disclose information to influence the behavior of a self-interested receiver. Bayesian persuasion
028   has received a growing attention over the last years, since it captures several fundamental problems
029   arising in real-world applications, such as, e.g., online advertising (Bro Miltersen & Sheffet, 2012;
030   Emek et al., 2014; Badanidiyuru et al., 2018; Bacchiocchi et al., 2022), voting (Cheng et al., 2015;
031   Alonso & Câmara, 2016; Castiglioni et al., 2020a; Castiglioni & Gatti, 2021), traffic routing (Vasser-
032   man et al., 2015; Bhaskar et al., 2016; Castiglioni et al., 2021a), recommendation systems (Mansour
033
      et al., 2016), e-commerce (Castiglioni et al., 2022), security (Rabinovich et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016),
      marketing (Babichenko & Barman, 2017; Candogan, 2019), clinical trials (Kolotilin, 2015), and
034
      financial regulation (Goldstein & Leitner, 2018).
035
036   The vast majority of works on Bayesian persuasion focuses on one-shot interactions, where informa-
037   tion disclosure is performed in a single step. Despite the fact that real-world problems are usually
038   sequential, there are only few exceptions that consider multi-step information disclosure (Wu et al.,
039
      2022; Gan et al., 2022; 2023; Bernasconi et al., 2022; 2023b; Iyer et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024).
      Specifically, Wu et al. (2022) initiated the study of Markov persuasion processes (MPPs), which
040
      model scenarios where a sender sequentially faces a stream of myopic receivers in an unknown
041
      Markovian environment. In each state of the environment, the sender privately observes some
042   information—encoded in an outcome stochastically determined according to a prior distribution—and
043   faces a new receiver, who is then called to take an action. The outcome and receiver’s action jointly
044   determine agents’ rewards and the next state. In an MPP, sender’s goal is to disclose information at
045   each state so as to persuade the receivers to take actions that maximize long-term sender’s rewards.
046
      The MPP formalism finds application in several real-world settings, such as e-commerce and rec-
047
      ommendation systems (Wu et al., 2022). For example, an MPP can model the problem faced by an
048   online streaming platform recommending movies to its users. The platform has an informational
049   advantage over users (e.g., it has access to views statistics), and it exploits available information to
050   induce users to watch suggested movies, so as to maximize views. However, the MPPs studied by Wu
051   et al. (2022) rely on some limiting assumptions that prevent them from being fully operational in
052   practice. For instance, they make the assumption that the sender has perfect knowledge of receiver’s
053   rewards. In the online streaming platform example described above, such an assumption requires that
      the platform knows everything about users’ (private) preferences over movies.

      1.1   O RIGINAL CONTRIBUTIONS
055
056
      We relax the assumptions of Wu et al. (2022), by addressing MPPs where the sender does not
057
      know anything about the environment. We consider settings in which they have no knowledge
058
      about transitions, prior distributions over outcomes, sender’s stochastic rewards, and receivers’ ones.
059   Ideally, the goal is to design learning algorithms that are persuasive and attain regret sublinear in
060   the number of episodes T . The regret is the difference between sender’s rewards cumulated over the
061   episodes and what they would have been obtained by always using an optimal information-disclosure
062   policy. Persuasiveness is about ensuring receivers are correctly incentivized to take desired actions.
063   Learning in MPPs without knowledge of receivers’ rewards begets considerable additional challenges
064   compared to the case of Wu et al. (2022). Indeed, the latter design a sublinear-regret algorithm that
065   is persuasive at every episode with high probability, while we show that this is not attainable in our
066   setting. Intuitively, this is due to the fact that, since the sender does not know receivers’ rewards,
067
      some episodes must be used to learn how to be “approximately” persuasive. As a consequence, in this
      work, we look for algorithms that attain sublinear regret while ensuring that the cumulative violation
068
      of persuasiveness grows sublinearly in T . This is the most natural requirement in all cases in which
069
      persuasiveness cannot be achieved at every episode, and it has already been addressed in settings
070   related to MPPs (see, e.g., (Bernasconi et al., 2022; Cacciamani et al., 2023; Gan et al., 2023)).
071
072   As a warm-up, we start studying a full feedback case where, after each episode, the sender observes
073
      the reward associated with every possible action in all the state-outcome pairs encountered during the
      episode. We propose an algorithm, called Optimistic Persuasive Policy Search (OPPS), which uses
074
      information-disclosure policies computed by being optimistic with respect to both sender’s expected
                                                                                                        √
075
      rewards and persuasiveness requirements. We show that, under full feedback, OPPS attains O(     e T)
076
      regret and violation. Then, we switch to the partial feedback case, where the sender only observes the
077
      rewards for the state-outcome-action triplets actually visited during the episode. We extend the OPPS
078   algorithm to this setting, by adding a preliminary exploration phase having the goal of gathering as
079   much feedback as possible about persuasiveness. After that, the algorithm switches to an optimistic
080   approach over information-disclosure policies that are “approximately” persuasive. We prove that
081   OPPS with partial feedback attains O(Te α ) regret and O(T e 1−α/2 ) violation, where α ∈ [1/2, 1] is a
082   parameter controlling the amount of exploration. Finally, we provide a lower bound showing that the
083   trade-off between regret and violation achieved by means of OPPS is tight.

086   1.2   R ELATED WORKS
087
088
      We refer the reader to Appendix A for additional details on related works.
089
090   The work most related to ours is (Wu et al., 2022), studying MPPs where the sender knows everything
091   about receivers’ rewards, with the only elements unknown to them being their rewards, transition
092   probabilities, and prior distributions. Moreover, Wu et al. (2022) also assume that the receivers know
093
      everything about the environment, so as to select a best-response action, and that all rewards are
      deterministic. In contrast, we consider MPPs in which sender and receivers have no knowledge of
094
      the environment, including their rewards, which we assume to be stochastic. Other related works
095
      are (Gan et al., 2022), studying Bayesian persuasion problems where a sender sequentially interacts
096   with a myopic receiver in a multi-state environment, and (Bernasconi et al., 2023b), addressing MPPs
097   with a farsighted receiver. These two works considerably depart from ours, as they both assume that
098   the sender knows everything about the environment, including transitions, priors, and rewards. Thus,
099   they are not concerned with learning problems. Finally, (Bernasconi et al., 2022) studies settings
100   where a sender faces a farsighted receiver in a sequential environment with a tree structure, addressing
101   the case in which the only elements unknown to the sender are the prior distributions over outcomes,
102   while rewards are deterministic and known. The tree structure considerably eases learning, as it
103   intuitively allows to factor the uncertainty about transitions in the rewards at the leaves of the tree.
104   Our work is also related to learning in one-shot Bayesian persuasion played repeatedly (Castiglioni
105   et al., 2020b; 2021b; Zu et al., 2021; Bernasconi et al., 2023a), and works on online learning in
106   Markov decision processes (MDPs) (Auer et al., 2008; Even-Dar et al., 2009; Neu et al., 2010;
107   Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019; Jin et al., 2020), in particular those on constrained MDPs (Wei et al.,
      2018; Zheng & Ratliff, 2020; Efroni et al., 2020; Qiu et al., 2020; Germano et al., 2023).

      2.1     BAYESIAN PERSUASION
111
112   The classical Bayesian persuasion framework introduced by Kamenica & Gentzkow (2011) models a
113   one-shot interaction between a sender and a receiver. The latter has to take an action a from a finite
114   set A, while the former privately observes an outcome ω sampled from a finite set Ω according to a
115   prior distribution µ ∈ ∆(Ω), which is known to both the sender and the receiver.1 The rewards of
116   both agents depend on the receiver’s action and the realized outcome, as defined by the functions
117   rS , rR : Ω × A → [0, 1], where rR (ω, a) and rS (ω, a) denote the rewards of the sender and the
118
      receiver, respectively, when the outcome is ω ∈ Ω and action a ∈ A is played. The sender can
      strategically disclose information about the outcome to the receiver, by publicly committing to a
119
      signaling scheme ϕ, which is a randomized mapping from outcomes to signals being sent to the
120
      receiver. Formally, ϕ : Ω → ∆(S), where S denotes a suitable finite set of signals. For ease of
121   notation, we let ϕ(·|ω) ∈ ∆(S) be the probability distribution over signals employed by the sender
122   when the realized outcome is ω ∈ Ω, with ϕ(s|ω) being the probability of sending signal s ∈ S.
123
124
      The sender-receiver interaction goes as follows: (i) the sender publicly commits to a signaling
      scheme ϕ; (ii) the sender observes the realized outcome ω ∼ µ and draws a signal s ∼ ϕ(·|ω);
125
      and (iii) the receiver observes the signal s and plays an action. Specifically, after observing
126
      s under a signaling scheme ϕ, the receiver infers a posterior distribution over outcomes and
127   plays a best-response   action bϕ (s) ∈ A according to such distribution. Formally, bϕ (s) ∈

130   Xu, 2016)), we assume that the receiver breaks ties in favor of the sender, by selecting a best response
131   maximizing sender’s expected reward when multiple best responses are available.

136
137   An MPP (Wu et al., 2022) generalizes one-shot Bayesian persuasion to settings where the sender
138   faces a stream of receivers in an MDP, with each receiver myopically taking an action maximizing

139   immediate reward. An (episodic) MPP is a tuple M := X, A, Ω, µ, P, {rS,t }Tt=1 , {rR,t }Tt=1 , where:
140
               • T is the number of episodes.2
141
               • X, A, and Ω are finite sets of states, actions, and outcomes, respectively.
142            • µ : X → ∆(Ω) is a prior function defining a probability distribution over outcomes at each
143              state. We let µ(ω|x) be the probability of sampling outcome ω ∈ Ω in state x ∈ X.
144            • P : X × Ω × A → ∆(X) is a transition function. We let P (x′ |x, ω, a) be the probability of
145              going from x ∈ X to x′ ∈ X by taking action a ∈ A, when the outcome in state x is ω ∈ Ω.
146            • {rS,t }Tt=1 is a sequence specifying a sender’s reward function rS,t : X × Ω × A → [0, 1] at
147              each episode t. Given x ∈ X, ω ∈ Ω, and a ∈ A, each rS,t (x, ω, a) for t ∈ [T ] is sampled
148              independently from a distribution νS (x, ω, a) ∈ ∆([0, 1]) with mean rS (x, ω, a).
149            • {rR,t }Tt=1 is a sequence defining a receivers’ reward function rR,t : X × Ω × A → [0, 1] at
150
                 each episode t. Given x ∈ X, ω ∈ Ω, and a ∈ A, each rR,t (x, ω, a) for t ∈ [T ] is sampled
151
                 independently from a distribution νR (x, ω, a) ∈ ∆([0, 1]) with mean rR (x, ω, a).3
152   We focus w.l.o.g. on loop-free episodic MPPs, as customary in online learning in MDPs (see,
153   e.g., (Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019)). In a loop-free MPP, states are partitioned into L + 1 layers
154   X0 , . . . , XL such that X0 := {x0 } and XL := {xL }, with x0 being the initial state starting the

164   state x ∈ X and outcome ω ∈ Ω. For ease of notation, we denote by ϕ(·|x, ω) ∈ ∆(S) such

249   Our goal is to design learning algorithms for the sender in an episodic MPP. We would like algorithms
250   that prescribe sequences of signaling policies ϕt that maximize sender’s cumulative reward over

381
382   The crucial aspect that makes the case of partial feedback more challenging than the one of full
383   feedback is that, after committing to a signaling policy ϕt , the sender does not observe sufficient
384   feedback about the persuasiveness of ϕt . This makes achieving sublinear violation in the partial
385
      feedback case much harder than in the full feedback case. In order to overcome such a challenge, some

386
      constraints.

388   This is necessary to build a suitable approximation of such constraints to be exploited in the remaining
389   episodes, in which an optimistic approach similar to that employed with full feedback must be adopted
390   to control the regret.

392   exploration performed. In the rest of this section, we design an algorithm that is able to optimally
393   control such a trade-off.

463   5.3   L OWER BOUND
464
      We conclude the section and the paper by showing that the regret and violation bounds attained by the
465   OPPS algorithm (see Theorems 3 and 4) are tight for any choice of α ∈ [1/2, 1]. We do so by devising
466   a lower bound matching these bounds (Theorem 5). Its main idea is to consider two instances of
467   episodic MPP involving a receiver with two actions a1 , a2 such that only a1 provides positive reward
468   to the sender. In one instance, receiver’s rewards by playing a1 are higher than those obtained by
469   taking a2 , while in the second instance the opposite holds. As a result, recommending action a1
470   results in low regret in the first instance and high violation in the second one, while recommending
471   action a2 results in low violation in the second instance and high regret in the first one. This leads to
472   the trade-off formally stated by the following theorem.
473   Theorem 5. Given α ∈ [1/2, 1], there is no learning algorithm achieving both RT = o(T α ) and
474   VT = o(T 1−α/2 ) with probability greater or equal to a fixed constant ψ > 0.
475
476   Theorem 5 shows that the bounds in Theorems 3 and 4 are tight for any α ∈ [1/2, 1]. Moreover, it
477
      also proves that it is impossible to achieve sublinear regret while being persuasive at every episode
      with high probability, when the sender has no information about the receivers. Notice that, in our
478
      MPP setting with partial feedback, we deal with a trade-off between regret and violation that is
479
      similar to the one faced by Bernasconi et al. (2022) in related settings. Differently from them, we
480   are able to achieve an optimal trade-off for any α ∈ [1/2, 1]. Indeed, Bernasconi et al. (2022) only
481   obtain optimality for α ∈ [1/2, 2/3], leaving as an open problem matching the lower bound for the
482   other values of the parameter α. Crucially, we are able to achieve trade-off optimality by using a
483   clever exploration method. Indeed, when considering a triplet (x, ω, a) in the first phase, the OPPS
484   algorithm does not simply commit to a signaling policy that maximizes the probability of visiting such
485   a triplet, but it rather does so while also optimistically accounting for persuasiveness constraints. This
      allows to reduce the violation cumulated during the first phase, thus achieving trade-off optimality.

      A    R ELATED WORKS
664
      Sequential Bayesian persuasion The work that is most related to ours is (Wu et al., 2022), which
665
      introduces MPPs. Specifically, Wu et al. (2022) study settings where the sender knows everything
666
      about receivers’ rewards, with the only elements unknown to them being their rewards, transition
667   probabilities, and prior distributions over outcomes. Moreover, they also assume that the receivers
668   know everything they need about the environment, so as to select a best-response action, and that
669   all rewards are deterministic. In contrast, we consider MPP settings in which sender and receivers
670   have no knowledge of the environment, including their rewards, which    √ we assume to be stochastic.
671   Moreover, Wu et al. (2022) obtain a regret bound of the order of O(   e T /D), where D is a parameter
672   related to receivers’ rewards. Notice that such a dependence is particularly unpleasant, as D may be
673   exponentially large in instances in which there are some receivers’ actions that are best responses only
674   for a “small” space of information-disclosure policies. Other works related to ours are (Gan et al.,
675   2022), which studies a Bayesian persuasion problem where a sender sequentially interacts with a
676   myopic receiver in a multi-state environment, and (Bernasconi et al., 2023b), which addresses MPPs
677
      with a farsighted receiver. These two works considerably depart from ours, as they both assume
      that the sender knows everything about the environment, including transitions, priors, and rewards.
678
      Thus, they are not concerned with learning problems, but with the problem of computing optimal
679
      information-disclosure policies. Finally, (Bernasconi et al., 2022) studies settings where a sender
680   faces a farsighted receiver in a sequential environment with a tree structure, addressing the case in
681   which the only elements unknown to the sender are the prior distributions over outcomes, while
682   rewards are deterministic and known. The tree structure considerably eases the learning task, as it
683   allows to express sender’s expected rewards linearly in variables defining information-disclosure
684   policies. Intuitively, this allows to factor the uncertainty about transitions in the rewards at the leaves
685   of the tree.
686
687   Online Bayesian persuasion It is also worth citing some works that study learning problems in
688   which a one-shot Bayesian persuasion setting is played repeatedly (Castiglioni et al., 2020b; 2021b;
689   Zu et al., 2021; Bernasconi et al., 2023a). These works considerably depart from ours, since they do
690
      not consider any kind of sequential structure in the sender-receiver interaction at each episode.
691
692   Online learning in constrained MDPs Our paper is also related to the problem of designing
693
      no-regret algorithms in online constrained Markov decision processes. The literature on online
      learning in Markov decision processes is extensive (see, e.g., Auer et al. (2008); Even-Dar et al.
694
      (2009); Neu et al. (2010) for fundamental works on the topic). In such settings, two types of feedback
695
      are usually investigated. The full-information feedback setting (Rosenberg & Mansour, 2019), in
696   which the entire reward function is observed after the learner’s choice and the partial feedback
697   setting (Jin et al., 2020), where the learner only observes the reward gained during the episode.
698   Over the last decade, there has been significant attention to the field of online Markov decision
699   processes in presence of constraints. The majority of previous works on this topic have focused on
700   settings where constraints are stochastically sampled from a fixed distribution (see, e.g., Zheng &
701   Ratliff (2020)). Wei et al. (2018) deal with adversarial reward and stochastic constraints, assuming
      known transition probabilities and full information feedback. Efroni et al. (2020) propose two


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      approaches to address the exploration-exploitation dilemma in episodic constrained MDPs. These
703   approaches guarantee sublinear regret and constraint violation when transition probabilities, rewards,
704   and constraints are unknown and stochastic, and the feedback is partial. Qiu et al. (2020) provide a
705   primal-dual approach based on optimism in the face of uncertainty. This work shows the effectiveness
706   of such an approach when dealing with episodic constrained MDPs with adversarial rewards and
707   stochastic constraints, achieving both sublinear regret and constraint violation with full-information
708   feedback. Finally, Germano et al. (2023) propose a best-of-both-worlds algorithm in constrained
709   Markov decision processes with full information feedback. While the previous works are related
710   to ours, the aforementioned techniques cannot be easily generalized to our setting as they are not
711
      designed to properly handle the presence of outcomes and IC constraints.
