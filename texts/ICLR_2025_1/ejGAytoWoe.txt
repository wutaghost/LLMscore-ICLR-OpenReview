009                                                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011
012                   Recent advancements in predictive models have demonstrated exceptional capa-
013                   bilities in predicting the future state of objects and scenes. However, the lack of
014                   categorization based on inherent characteristics continues to hinder the progress
015                   of predictive model development. Additionally, existing benchmarks are unable
016
                      to effectively evaluate higher-capability, highly embodied predictive models from
                      an embodied perspective. In this work, we classify the functionalities of predic-
017
                      tive models into a hierarchy and take the first step in evaluating World Simula-
018
                      tors by proposing a dual evaluation framework called WorldSimBench. World-
019                   SimBench includes Explicit Perceptual Evaluation and Implicit Manipulative
020                   Evaluation, encompassing human preference assessments from the visual per-
021                   spective and action-level evaluations in embodied tasks, covering three represen-
022                   tative embodied scenarios: Open-Ended Embodied Environment, Autonomous
023                   Driving, and Robot Manipulation. In the Explicit Perceptual Evaluation, we intro-
024                   duce the HF-Embodied Dataset, a video assessment dataset based on fine-grained
025                   human feedback, which we use to train a Human Preference Evaluator that aligns
026                   with human perception and explicitly assesses the visual fidelity of World Simu-
027
                      lators. In the Implicit Manipulative Evaluation, we assess the video-action con-
                      sistency of World Simulators by evaluating whether the generated situation-aware
028
                      video can be accurately translated into the correct control signals in dynamic envi-
029
                      ronments. Our comprehensive evaluation offers key insights that can drive further
030                   innovation in video generation models, positioning World Simulators as a pivotal
031                   advancement toward embodied artificial intelligence.

067   1      I NTRODUCTION
068
069
070
      Before taking action, humans make predictions based on their objectives and observations of the
      current environment. These predictions manifest in various forms, e.g., textual planning, visual
071
      imagination of future scene changes, or even subconscious planning at the action level. With the
072
      development of generative models, agents driven by these models are exhibiting predictive capabili-
073   ties that enable them to complete embodied tasks by making human-like predictions, e.g., high-level
074   planning (Driess et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), image-based guidance (Lai et al., 2023; Black et al.,
075   2023), or future video prediction to drive actions (Du et al., 2023; 2024)). We refer to these mod-
076   els as Predictive Models. Recently, these models have been widely applied across various domains
077   spanning from developing agents to solve inference tasks to leveraging predictions for driving robots
078   to perform specific actions.
079   Nevertheless, the rich application scenarios and diverse model designs make predictive models a
080   broad family. However, without categorizing them based on their inherent characteristics, the ad-
081   vancement of predictive model development remains limited. This leads to our first question: Can
082   we establish a reasonable hierarchical system for Predictive Models based on their output modality?
083   With a well-defined categorization, we can better target the evaluation of Predictive Models from
084   different perspectives in diverse embodied environments, ensuring that their strengths and weak-
085   nesses are adequately assessed. In the literature, existing evaluations have typically focused on task
086   planning capabilities by assessing text outputs or evaluating visual outputs from an aesthetic per-
087
      spective. However, such approaches significantly limit the evaluation of highly embodied Predictive
      Models, as embodied scenarios are more concerned with physical properties (e.g., perspective con-
088
      sistency, object breakability), which these methods fail to effectively assess. This brings us to our
089
      second question: Can we conduct a more detailed evaluation of highly embodied Predictive Models
090   from an embodied perspective?
091
092   To answer the first question, we categorize the functionalities of Predictive Models into a hierarchy
093
      from S0 to S3 , defined by the model’s capabilities and output modality, accompanied by corre-
      sponding evaluation benchmarks as illustrated in Fig. 1. Models are classified based on the output
094
      modality in their output modalities. From lower to higher stages, the models are capable of generat-
095
      ing: text, images, videos, and actionable videos (i.e., the videos that can be translated into actions).
096   It is worth noting that Predictive Models at S3 capable of generating actionable videos integrate
097   robust 3D scene understanding and physical rule priors to provide precise guidance for generating
098   executable actions. These models are closely aligned with the recently proposed concept of World
099   Simulators (Yang et al., 2023).
100
      To answer the second question, we review the related benchmarks, as listed in Tab. 1. Evaluations on
101
      models in S0 that generate text primarily focus on assessing task planning capabilities, while S1 and
102   S2 assessments on visual output measure aesthetic quality through feature similarity analyses with
103   ground truth data. With clearly defined evaluation dimensions and extensive annotated datasets,
104   both types of assessments can be effectively conducted. However, evaluating World Simulators
105   introduces complexities due to the intricate physical definitions involved. Additionally, conventional
106   evaluation methods are inadequate for assessing the actionablilty of the generated videos, as there is
107   no definite ground truth for actionable videos towards completing a specific embodied task. These
      factors pose significant challenges to the evaluation of World Simulators.

108
      We argue that an evaluation aligned with human perception could provide a more intuitive and
109   accurate reflection of the characteristics of the synthesized videos, including their adherence to
110   physical rules. Besides, the actionability can be assessed through a closed-loop manner in simu-
111   lations deployed with a unified video-to-action policy network. Considering these aspects, we take
112   the very first step in evaluating World Simulators by proposing a dual evaluation framework called
113   WorldSimBench. As shown in Fig. 1, WorldSimBench assesses World Simulators through two
114   complementary approaches: Explicit Perceptual Evaluation, which focuses on the Visual Qual-
115   ity, Condition consistency, and Embodiment of the generated content, and Implicit Manipulative
116   Evaluation, which measures the World Simulator’s performance through the conversion of video
117
      into control signals. We present three representative embodied scenarios: Open-Ended Embodied
      Environment (OE), Autonomous Driving (AD), and Robot Manipulation (RM), to thoroughly eval-
118
      uate the capability of World Simulators in generating and representing scenario-specific attributes.
119
120   In the Explicit Perceptual Evaluation, we first define evaluation criteria which is used to construct a
121   comprehensive set of prompts specific to each scenario. The prompt lists are then used by various
122   video generation models to produce a large number of video clips. Following extensive human feed-
123
      back and annotation, these video clips are compiled into the HF-Embodied dataset which consists of
      a total of 35,701 tuples with multi-dimensional scores and fine-grained human feedback. Addition-
124
      ally, we train Human Preference Evaluator, using the HF-Embodied dataset to assess World Simu-
125
      lators at the perceptual level, offering a robust evaluation of both their visual fidelity and contextual
126   accuracy. For the Implicit Manipulative Evaluation, we deploy three simulation environments for
127   the three embodied scenarios respectively. These environments are used to collect data and train
128   inverse dynamic or goal-based video-to-action models capable of mapping future videos to actions.
129   In each of these embodied scenarios, the World Simulator is tasked with generating situation-aware
130   videos in real-time, based on current observations and provided text instructions. These generated
131   videos are then converted into actions using the pre-trained video-to-action models. The effective-
132   ness of the World Simulator is implicitly evaluated by measuring the performance of the tasks, using
133   relevant metrics to reflect the quality and accuracy of the generated video.
134   In summary, the main contributions are as follows: (1)We categorize the functionalities of Predic-
135   tive Models into a hierarchy, defined by the model’s capabilities and output modality, to advance
136   research and development in the field and take the very first step in evaluating World Simulators.
137   (2)We propose a dual evaluation framework called WorldSimBench, through Explicit Perceptual
138   Evaluation and Implicit Manipulative Evaluation, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of the
139   World Simulator’s capabilities from an embodied perspective, focusing on both the visual and ac-
140
      tion levels. (3)We conducted extensive testing across multiple models and performed a thorough
      analysis of the experimental results. Our findings highlight the strengths and limitations of current
141
      World Simulators and provide actionable insights for improving future video generation models.
142
      (4)We developed HF-Embodied Dataset, which includes fine-grained human feedback across three
143   scenarios and 20 dimensions, with a total of 35,701entries. This dataset, containing both human
144   ratings and the reasons behind them, not only enables the evaluation of World Simulators but also
145   provides broader applications (e.g.,alignment) for future video generation models.

148   2   R ELATED W ORK
149
150   Predictive Models. Predictive models are capable of generating process representations that map
151   the current state to future states by incorporating current state representations and control over future
152   trends. Predictive Text Model, built on LLMs (Radford et al., 2019; Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang
153
      et al., 2023) and MLLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Yin et al., 2023),
      generate future predictions in the text modality by accepting current state representations and text
154
      instructions. These models have demonstrated impressive performance in high-level planning tasks
155
      for embodied agents (Driess et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang
156   et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2024). Similarly, image generation models (Brooks et al., 2023; Fu et al.,
157   2023) as Predictive Image Model (Lai et al., 2023; Black et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) can produce
158   future goal images, showcasing strong capabilities during the decision-making phase of embodied
159   agents. Predictive Video Model (Du et al., 2024; 2023), based on video generation models (Janner
160   et al., 2022), have made some progress in embodied control. However, due to limitations in data or
161   models, the generated videos often lack essential physical representations and logical consistency,
      restricting their applicability to fixed scenarios and single tasks.

      With the advancement of diffusion transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023) and the extensive utiliza-
163   tion of large-scale internet video datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Ebert et al., 2021; Goyal et al., 2017;
164   Grauman et al., 2022), certain Predictive Actionable Video Model (Yang et al., 2023) models, also
165   known as World Simulators, have achieved more precise representations of physical laws and 3D

      Evaluation of Predictive Models. With the advancement of predictive models, research has also
168
      expanded to evaluate the capabilities of models at different stages. Liu et al. (2023b); Chen et al.
169   (2023); Shi et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a) conducted text-level and task completion evaluations

171   an aesthetic perspective for Predictive Image Model at the S1 stage. Huang et al. (2024); Liu et al.
172   (2024b) also assessed the aesthetic quality of videos generated by Predictive Video Model at the S2
173   stage. We take the first step in evaluating World Simulators through an embodied perspective.

      3   P REDICTIVE M ODEL C ATEGORY D EFINITION
177
178   In this section, we concretely categorize predictive models based on the model’s capabilities and
179
      output modality. The detailed categorization stage of Fig. 1 is illustrated below,
180   • Stage S0 : At this stage, predictive models can generate corresponding predictions based on in-
181   structions and observations but are limited to textual modality. Benchmarks at this stage conduct
182   text-level and task-completion evaluations through output text planning.
183   • Stage S1 : At this stage, predictive models can generate visual predictions based on instructions
184   and observations, but without incorporating temporal information. Benchmarks at this stage conduct
185   aesthetic evaluation for generated images.
186
      • Stage S2 : At this stage, predictive models can generate corresponding video predictions based on
187
      both instructions and observations. Yet, due to limited model capabilities, the evaluation at this level
188
      focuses solely on the aesthetic quality of the generated outputs.
189
190   • Stage S3 : At this stage, predictive models can generate corresponding video predictions based on
191   instructions and observations, with the predicted video content adhering to physical rules and align-
192
      ing with the executed actions. These models are known as World Simulators (Ha & Schmidhuber,
      2018; Yang et al., 2023), and WorldSimBench is a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate these
193
      World Simulators.
194
195   The rapidly evolving field of World Simulators offers exciting opportunities for advancing Artifi-
196   cial General Intelligence, with significant potential to enhance human productivity and creativity,
197   especially in embodied intelligence. Therefore, conducting a comprehensive embodied evaluation
198
      of World Simulators is crucial.

200   4   W ORLD S IM B ENCH C ONSTRUCTION
201
202
      WorldSimBench evaluates the embodied capabilities of World Simulators across two distinct lev-
203
      els. The Explicit Perceptual Evaluation assesses the simulators based on human-perceived qual-
204   ity across different embodied scenarios, while the Implicit Manipulative Evaluation implicitly
205   evaluates the simulators’ capabilities by converting the generated videos into control signals and
206   observing their performance in various closed-loop embodied tasks.
207
      The evaluation of World Simulators encompasses three critical embodied scenarios: Open-Ended
208
      Embodied Environment (OE), Autonomous Driving (AD), and Robot Manipulation (RM). Minecraft
209
      serves as a popular testbed for OE, providing a challenging platform for agents to handle complex,
210   unstructured tasks. In the context of AD, especially in outdoor settings, ensuring the stability and
211   robustness of the agent’s actions is crucial, making it an essential domain for assessing a World
212   Simulator’s capability in dynamic and uncertain environments. RM, a core task in embodied in-
213   telligence, demands precise and adaptive control, testing the world simulator’s ability to generate
214   actionable predictions that align with physical interactions. Together, these scenarios provide a
215   comprehensive benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of World Simulators across a range of
      real-world tasks.

239   4.1      E XPLICIT P ERCEPTUAL E VALUATION
240
241   In Explicit Perceptual Evaluation, we propose Hierarchical Evaluation Dimensions, based on which
242   we build a video assessment dataset annotated through fine-grained human feedback, named HF-
243   Embodied Dataset. The dataset is constructed based on three key resources, each corresponding to
244
      a specific embodied scenario: a curated dataset of Minecraft videos from the internet for OE (Baker
      et al., 2022), real-world driving data for AD (Caesar et al., 2020), and real-world robot manipulation
245
      videos annotated with text instructions for RM (Chen et al., 2024). Using HF-Embodied Dataset,
246
      we train a Human Preference Evaluator to perform perceptual evaluations of World Simulators.
247
248
      4.1.1        H IERARCHICAL E VALUATION D IMENSION
249
250   We develop a hierarchical evaluation dimension checklist for the three embodied scenarios, as il-
251   lustrated in Tab. 2, which can be categorized into three main aspects: Visual Quality, Condition
252   Consistency, and Embodiment. (1) Visual Quality primarily assesses the overall quality of video
253   generation, including Aesthetics, Background and Foreground Consistency. (2) Condition Consis-
254   tency focuses on the alignment with the input instruction. For tasks in OE that involve distinct
255   scenarios, we additionally define Scenario Alignment to assess the alignment to the specific scenar-
256   ios outlined in the instruction. (3) Embodiment has different definitions depending on the scenario.
257   As all tasks require movement along a certain trajectory, we uniformly define Trajectory to evaluate
258
      the rationality of object movement in the video (e.g., whether a robotic arm avoids obstacles during
      motion). In AD and RM, we define Perspectivity to assess whether the video exhibits a clear sense
259
      of depth. In OE and RM, we define Embodied Interaction to evaluate the plausibility of interactions
260
      with objects. We also define Velocity in OE to determine whether speed varies appropriately across
261   different environments (e.g., slower movement in water). In AD, we define Key Element to evaluate
262   the rendering quality and consistency of crucial embodied elements, e.g., pedestrians. We also in-
263   troduce Safety in AD to assess whether the embodied actions comply with traffic rules. More details
264   in Sup. A.

286   4.1.3    HF-E MBODIED DATASET G ENERATION
287
      Data Preparation. We select multiple video generation models and train them using a large corpus
288
      of videos and corresponding captions from the key resources. Due to the capabilities of the open-
289   source video generation model, we conduct targeted training for each of the three distinct embodied
290   scenarios individually, thereby developing several data generation models for different embodied
291   scenarios. These models are then used to produce a substantial amount of instruction-following em-
292   bodied videos, based on the corresponding captions, and the initial image condition where applicable
293   (first frame conditioned text-to-video to generate situation-aware videos).
294
      Human Annotation. We use human annotation to label the generated videos. Based on the Hierar-
295   chical Evaluation Dimension, we establish specific annotation guidelines and numerous in-conttext
296   examples for the annotators. For each dimension, annotators are instructed to score the video solely
297   based on its performance within that particular dimension and provide corresponding reasoning.
298   For instance in RM, as illustrated in Fig. 2, under the dimension of Trajectory, annotators are re-
299   quired to evaluate the video exclusively on the generation quality of the motion trajectory. They
300   are instructed not to consider other elements (e.g., the rendering quality of the robot arm) or other
301   dimensions (e.g., consistency with instructions). Additionally, annotators are asked to provide fine-
302   grained feedback on any deficiencies, e.g., “inconsistent trajectory”. As a result, we construct the
303
      HF-Embodied Dataset, which consists of a total of 35,701 tuples, each comprising a video, text
      instruction, multi-dimensional scores, and the potential reasons. More details in Sup. B.1.

      4.1.4    H UMAN P REFERENCE E VALUATOR
307   The objective is to develop a video scoring model that assesses videos across multiple dimensions
308   aligning with human perception. The model takes a generated video and a prompt as input and
309   outputs a score ranging from 1 to n (n is defined specifically for each embodied scenario). The
310   prompt includes both the video generation instructions and an explanation of the evaluation criteria.
311   Leveraging the strong video understanding capabilities of multimodal large language models, we
312   fine-tune Flash-VStream (Zhang et al., 2024a), a VideoLLM, aligning it with human perception on
313   HF-Embodied Dataset. Only LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) parameters are trained. This enables the model
314
      to effectively grasp the evaluation metrics for embodied tasks and produce accurate scores, while
      maintaining its video perception and reasoning ability. We prove the effectiveness and generaliz-
315
      ability of our Human Preference Evaluator in Sec. 5.2.

      4.2     I MPLICIT M ANIPULATIVE E VALUATION
344
345   The Implicit Manipulative Evaluation assesses the capabilities of World Simulators across various
346   embodied scenarios by treating the simulator as a low-level decision maker for situational contexts.
347   Using pre-trained video-to-action models, we implicitly evaluate the performance of the World Sim-
348   ulators by observing their effectiveness in closed-loop embodied task tests.
349
350
      4.2.1     S IMULATION CONSTRUCTION
351
352   The Implicit Manipulative Evaluation is conducted using the following three simulation platforms,
353   for specific settings, please refer to the Supplementary Material.
354
      OE We employ MineRL as the Minecraft simulator, with the observation space limited to RGB
355   images and the action space confined to keyboard and mouse controls. We adopt the Steve-1 bench-
356   marks (Lifshitz et al., 2024), with task descriptions e.g., ”chop a tree.”
357
358
      AD We conduct standard closed-loop evaluations using the CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) sim-
      ulator on the LangAuto Benchmark (Shao et al., 2024). Task descriptions include instructions like
359
      ”do not deviate from this route.”
360
361   RM We employ CALVIN (Mees et al., 2022) as the robot manipulation simulator, using only RGB
362   images for the observation space and limiting the action space to the 7-DOF (degrees of freedom)
363   of the robot arm. Task descriptions include commands e.g., ”pull the handle to open the drawer.”
364
365   4.2.2     E MBODIED TASK EVALUATION
366
367   Evaluation Pipeline. As illustrated in Fig. 3, we first leverage existing or custom-trained video-to-
368   action models as intermediaries between the World Simulator and the agent performing closed-loop
369
      tasks, for the selected benchmarks across three simulation environments. This approach enables
      the transformation of the predicted future videos from the World Simulator into executable control
370
      signals in real-time, thereby indirectly evaluating the World Simulator’s capability through the suc-
371
      cessful completion of embodied tasks. The evaluation process is tailored to the specific nature of
372   the models under consideration, establishing distinct protocols for closed-loop task evaluation. We
373   fine-tune the models on simulation datasets tailored to each task. These datasets, derived from the
374   three aforementioned benchmarks, include task instructions and corresponding videos, ensuring the
375   models are well-adapted to the specific embodied scenarios. Finally, the evaluated World Simulator
376   is integrated with the video-to-action model to jointly form an embodied agent that performs the
377   given tasks. The agent’s performance across various tasks serves as a direct measure of the World
      Simulator’s effectiveness.

      5.3   D ESIGN F EATURES AND D ISCUSSIONS
457
458   In this section, we discuss the Design features and corresponding observations we draw from our
459   comprehensive evaluation experiments. More details can be found in the Supplementary Material.
460
      Human Prefrence with Feedback. Given the complexity and diversity in the representation of
461
      physical rules in videos, even a specific dimension may manifest in various ways (for example, both
462
      illogical and discontinuous object motion fall under trajectory-related issues). This makes it chal-
463   lenging to evaluate using score-based models or a single fixed set of evaluation criteria. WorldSim-
464   Bench addresses this challenge effectively by employing a human preference scoring mechanism
465   and a fine-grained feedback system. Fig. 4 illustrates the evaluation results of Explicit Perceptual
466   Evaluation, more detail analyze could be found in Sup. C. In OE, most models struggle with Em-
467   bodied Interaction, particularly in generating plausible object deformations, e.g., block shattering,
468   due to the complexity of physical rules. In AD, the variation between models is minimal, with high-
469   performing models excelling across all dimensions. The simpler instructions, like moving forward
470   or turning, lead to high Instruction Alignment, but many generated videos suffer from poor 3D depth
471
      (Perspectivity) and fail to depict realistic embodied elements like pedestrians and vehicles, affecting
      the overall Aesthetic. In RM, models perform uniformly well in static scene depiction, excelling
472
      in Perspectivity and Foreground/Background Consistency. However, they struggle with Instruction
473
      Alignment, often generating aimless actions. Despite this, the lack of unreasonable trajectories
474   results in relatively high Trajectory scores, though robotic manipulation remains a significant chal-
475   lenge for current models.
476
477
      Close-loop Interactive Evaluation. Given the dynamic nature and real-time requirements of inter-
      active environments, evaluating World Simulators through static benchmarks often fails to capture
478
      the full spectrum of their capabilities. Close-loop Interactive Evaluation addresses this by enabling
479
      continuous feedback and adaptation, ensuring that the model’s predictions and actions evolve in re-
480   sponse to the changing environment, thus providing a more accurate and realistic assessment of its
481   performance. Fig. 5 presents the Implicit Manipulative Evaluationevaluation results, showing signif-
482   icant variation in the performance of video generation models across different tasks. In the OE, video
483   generation models conditioned on the first frame have a significantly lower success rate compared
484   to those without image conditioning. This suggests that models with image conditioning struggle to
485   generate physical laws and 3D scene representations accurately. Tasks like travel, requiring high-
      quality trajectories and 3D representation, show the greatest variation in model performance, while

527
      improvements are needed before they can function as true World Simulators.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we classify the functionalities of predictive models into a hierarchy and take the first
532   step in evaluating World Simulators by proposing a dual evaluation framework called WorldSim-
533   Bench. We conducted a comprehensive evaluation and analysis of multiple video generation models
534   as World Simulators through both Explicit Perceptual Evaluation and Implicit Manipulative Evalua-
535   tion processes. We summarize key findings from the evaluation and hope these insights will inspire
536
      and guide future research on World Simulators.
537   Limitations. Although we evaluate physical rules and 3D content from the perspective of embodied
538   intelligence, the World Simulator can be applied to more scenarios than just robots, and different
539   scenarios have more physical representations, so how to effectively evaluate the World Simulator in
      other scenarios requires more exploration.

1245   In Implicit Manipulative Evaluation-Autonomous Driving, we adapt the action space of LM-
1246   Drive (Shao et al., 2024) to develop a pipeline for the Video Generation Model, enabling it to
1247
       function as a low-level embodied controller. Additionally, we employ LangAuto (Language-guided
       Autonomous Driving) CARLA benchmark, to evaluate the low-level embodied control capabilities
1248
       of the Video Generation Model as World Simulators. These tasks are designed to be comprehen-
1249
       sive, spanning all 8 publicly available towns in CARLA, covering a diverse range of scenarios e.g.,
1250   highways, intersections, and roundabouts. Additionally, they account for 16 different environmen-
1251   tal conditions, combining 7 distinct weather settings (Clear, Cloudy, Wet, MidRain, WetCloudy,
1252   HardRain, SoftRain) with 3 daylight conditions (Night, Noon, Sunset). Each aspect rigorously tests
1253   the coherence of the generated content, the consistency with given instructions, and the model’s
1254   ability to interact effectively with the environment.
1255
       Testing. We evaluated performance in Autonomous Driving using the LangAuto-Tiny benchmark
1256   setting where the route length is shorter than 150 meters. We posit that shorter driving distances pro-
1257   vide a more effective test of the low-level control capabilities of World Simulators. Longer routes
1258   typically involve more instructions, which are prone to misalignment with the real-time simulation
1259   environment. Therefore, we opt to evaluate performance on shorter routes to minimize these dis-
1260   crepancies. During testing, we randomly select one description from various task instructions and
1261   input it into the World Simulator to generate the corresponding video. The video is then continu-
1262   ously translated into actions by a pre-trained goal-based video-to-action model, which executes un-
1263   til the test time expires. We use the corresponding LangAuto-Tiny instructions and the first-person
1264
       view rendered by the real-time CARLA simulation environment as input to the video generation
       model. The generated video is then continuously transformed into downstream control signals using
1265
       a pre-trained video-to-action model until the agent reaches a predefined success zone or the task is
1266
       terminated due to factors e.g., timeouts or collisions.
1267
1268   Training. Due to the low video quality produced by the open-source video generation model based
1269   on the provided instructions, we applied additional fine-tuning using data from the AD simulation
1270
       environment. For Video Generation Model training, we use LMDrive Training Dataset (Shao et al.,
       2024). We preprocessed the training data according to each model’s default input format and per-
1271
       formed fine-tuning following the official implementation without changing the training settings. We
1272
       fine-tuned each model using a short video generation setting with approximately 20 frames. For the
1273   video-to-action model, we use pre-trained LMdrive model. Additional fine-tuning was conducted
1274   based on the test requirements. We provided the model with arbitrary text instructions and replaced
1275   the visual input with the future frame while keeping all other training settings consistent with LM-
1276   Drive.
1277
       Metrics. We consider eight key metrics introduced by the CARLA Leaderboard (Dosovitskiy et al.,
1278
       2017): Route Completion (RC), Infraction Score (IS), Driving Score (DS), Vehicle Collisions (VC),
1279   Pedestrian Collisions (PC), Layout Collisions (LC), Red Light Violations (RV), and Offroad In-
1280   fractions (OI). Route Completion refers to the percentage of the total route length that the agent has
1281   completed. This metric only accounts for the distance traveled along the predetermined route, where
1282   each segment corresponds to a navigation instruction. If the agent strays too far from the route, it
1283   is considered to have violated the instruction, resulting in the episode being marked as a failure and
1284   terminated. The Infraction Score tracks any infractions caused by the agent, with penalties applied
1285   for collisions or traffic violations through a corresponding discount factor. The Driving Score is the
1286   product of the route completion ratio and the infraction score, reflecting both driving progress and
1287
       safety, and is widely regarded as the primary ranking metric. The precise definitions of the residual
       metrics can be found in the CARLA documentation (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017).

1457   The generated videos have the potential to significantly enhance the performance of Vision-
       Language-Action (VLA) models by addressing two key challenges in training such models: the


                                                             27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       availability of diverse, high-quality training data and the need for effective reward functions in real-
1459   world scenarios.
1460
1461   Data Augmentation and Hindsight Relabeling for Imitation Learning. Generated videos can
1462
       serve as a valuable source of synthetic data for training VLA models. By leveraging the diversity
       and scalability of generative models, we can create a wide array of training scenarios, covering
1463
       edge cases and rare events that are difficult to capture in real-world datasets. Additionally, these
1464
       videos enable hindsight relabeling, a process where we retrospectively adjust the labels of generated
1465   data to align with desired outcomes. This approach is particularly effective for imitation learning,
1466   allowing VLA models to learn optimal behavior by mimicking successful trajectories represented
1467   in the generated videos. By expanding the data distribution and improving its quality, generative
1468   videos can lead to more robust and generalizable VLA models.
1469
       Reward Generation for Online Reinforcement Learning. Beyond data augmentation, generated
1470
       videos can act as a Reward Generator in reinforcement learning (RL) contexts. Unlike traditional
1471   RL setups that rely on pre-defined reward functions within a simulator, generative videos enable the
1472   creation of dense and context-aware reward signals tailored to real-world tasks. For example, they
1473   can simulate desirable outcomes or intermediate goals, providing detailed feedback to the agent.
1474   This capability is particularly crucial for transferring RL models to real-world environments, where
1475   designing explicit reward functions is often impractical. By aligning the generated rewards with
1476   real-world objectives, we can bridge the gap between simulation and reality, allowing VLA models
1477   to achieve higher performance in real-world tasks.
