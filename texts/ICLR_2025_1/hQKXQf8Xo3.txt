009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In psychotherapy, therapeutic outcome assessment, or treatment outcome evalu-
012            ation, is essential for enhancing mental health care by systematically evaluating
013            therapeutic processes and outcomes. Existing large language model approaches
014            often focus on therapist-centered, single-session evaluations, neglecting the client’s
015            subjective experience and longitudinal progress across multiple sessions. To ad-
016
               dress these limitations, we propose IPAEval, a client-Informed Psychological
               Assessment-based Evaluation framework that automates treatment outcome evalu-
017
               ations from the client’s perspective using clinical interviews. IPAEval integrates
018
               cross-session client-contextual assessment and session-focused client-dynamics
019            assessment to provide a comprehensive understanding of therapeutic progress.
020            Experiments on our newly developed TheraPhase dataset demonstrate that IPAEval
021            effectively tracks symptom severity and treatment outcomes over multiple ses-
022            sions, outperforming previous single-session models and validating the benefits of
023            items-aware reasoning mechanisms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      In psychotherapy, therapeutic outcome assessment, a.k.a treatment outcome evaluation under clinical
028
      settings, refers to the systematic evaluation of therapeutic processes and outcomes (Groth-Marnat,
029
      2009), focusing on factors such as therapist effectiveness (Johns et al., 2019) and treatment effi-
030   cacy (Jensen-Doss et al., 2018) to improve mental health care delivery. It plays a significant role in
031   enhancing the quality and effectiveness of mental health care by providing actionable insights that
032   guide therapists in refining their treatment approaches (Wampold & Imel, 2015), ultimately leading to
033   better client outcomes and improved therapeutic relationships in real-world clinical practice (Maruish
034   & Leahy, 2000).
035
      Over the last couple of years, the emergence of large language models has demonstrated their
036   effectiveness in automatic evaluations, showing a high degree of alignment with human judgment
037   when provided with proper instruction and contextual guidance (Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b;
038   Kim et al., 2024). This aligns with the “LLMs-as-a-judge” paradigm, where LLMs are employed to
039   simulate human evaluators by providing assessments based on natural language input (Zheng et al.,
040   2023; Wang et al., 2024b). This paradigm has been extended to therapeutic outcome assessment by
041   harnessing LLMs’ ability to model complex therapeutic procedures and interactions, offering a novel
042   pathway for automating the assessment of therapeutic efficacy (Chiu et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Li
043   et al., 2024a).
044   In the assessment, compared to psychometric tests (Furr, 2020) that are often constrained by the
045   limitations of self-reported data, susceptibility to social desirability biases (Braun et al., 2001; Paulhus,
046   2017), clinical interviews not only provide richer, more nuanced insights into the client’s emotional
047   and behavioral states but also offer data that is more readily obtainable through natural, conversational
048   interactions. Therefore, many recent works leverage clinical interviews, potentially enriched by
049   the client’s profile (Lee et al., 2024), to evaluate therapists from multiple perspectives, including
050
      behavioral labels (Chiu et al., 2024), skills adherence (Lee et al., 2024), and therapeutic rapport (Li
      et al., 2024a; Yosef et al., 2024), offering a holistic view of their effectiveness in psychotherapy.
051
052   While the above therapist-centered assessments focus on evaluating the therapist’s techniques and
053   adherence to therapeutic models, they often overlook the subjective experience and evolving needs of
      the client, limiting the depth of the evaluation (Wang et al., 2024a; Yosef et al., 2024). In contrast,

071

072   broader context of the client’s journey across multiple sessions. This narrow scope limits the ability
073   to assess longitudinal progress or capture the dynamic shifts in a client’s mental state and therapeutic
074   needs over time, which are crucial for a comprehensive treatment outcome evaluation (Hayes &
075   Andrews, 2020).
076
      Motivated by the above therapist-centered and single-session limitations (please see Table 1 for com-
077
      parisons), we design a new evaluation framework, dubbed client-Informed Psychological Assessment-
078   based Evaluation (IPAEval), for treatment outcomes in the format of clinical interviews.
079
080   Specifically, to achieve treatment outcome evaluation, we formulate an information extraction task
081
      that leverages clinical interviews to automatically populate psychometric tests for psychological
      assessments, bridging the gap between subjective client dialogues and standardized metrics. As such,
082
      treatment outcomes are evaluated through these assessments of clients conducted both before and after
083
      therapy, allowing for a more comprehensive understanding of therapeutic progress. Upon this new
084   framework, we first propose a cross-session client-contextual assessment module that integrates client
085   history and contextual information across multiple sessions to enhance the accuracy of psychological
086   assessments. Then, we present a session-focused client-dynamics assessment module that evaluates
087   the effectiveness of individual therapy sessions by tracking real-time client responses and treatment
088   outcomes within each session. In the meantime, to boost reasoning capability in the extraction, we
089   also present an items-aware reasoning prompt technique for psychometric test-oriented rationale
090   generation.
091   To evaluate the proposed framework, we first develop a new dataset, called TheraPhase, based on
092   CPsyCoun (Zhang et al., 2024), which includes transcripts from initial and final therapy sessions.
093   This dataset offers valuable insights into therapy progress and serves as a key resource for evaluating
094   psychological assessments and treatment outcomes across multiple sessions. Then, we tested nine
095   LLMs, including closed-source models. These models were evaluated for their performance in
096   psychological assessments and treatment outcome prediction, particularly in multi-session evaluations.
097   IPAEval consistently tracked symptom severity and treatment outcomes across multiple sessions, a
098
      capability lacking in previous single-session models. Our ablation study confirmed that the items-
      aware reasoning mechanism significantly boosts model performance in both symptom detection and
099
      outcome prediction.

      more on results, specifically determining whether the therapy has brought about meaningful changes

185   [s1 , s2 , . . . ], we aim to evaluate the efficacy of a certain session sk in clinical interviews as treatment

      3.2         C ROSS - SESSION C LIENT- CONTEXTUAL A SSESSMENT
205
206   Existing research using client information with LLMs for mental health assessment, particularly
207   for depression and PTSD, shows promising results (Galatzer-Levy et al., 2023; Arcan et al., 2024;
208   Rosenman et al., 2024). However, these studies typically focus on specific symptoms and lack broad
209   coverage of psychological conditions and transparency in interpreting scale results, which may erode
210   trust among clinicians and clients, limiting clinical applications (Martin & Rouas, 2024).
211   To address these gaps, we introduce a two-stage prompt scheme that populates information from
212   clinical interviews to fill psychometric tests by making the best of LLMs’ capability in natural
213   language understanding (Zhao et al., 2023a; Hua et al., 2024). It’s applicable to various psychometric
214   tests, specifically designed to provide interpretable psychological assessments. Without sacrificing
215   generality, in this work we utilize the Symptom Checklist-90 (SCL-90) (Derogatis & Unger, 2010), a
      widely used and comprehensive psychometric test for screening psychological symptoms.

217   using LLMs, leveraging client information. Here, the items of the SCL-90 represent psychological
218   symptoms. It correlates specific client information with the corresponding symptoms and items from
219   the SCL-90, determining their presence and providing an interpretation. Given an out-of-the-box
220   LLM (Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) able to follow instructions, we first
221   curate a prompt to steer the LLM to extract information from interviews to structured psychometric
222   tests with thought augmentations. Inspired by a recent work Schulhoff et al. (2024), our prompt

224   symptoms, the SCL-90 as additional information T, output formatting o(pi) , and specific directives

      d(pi) . Based on these, we can define psychometric interpretation prompt p(pi) as follows:

      3.4   T HERA P HASE DATASET
296
297   Popular datasets such as High-Low Quality (Pérez-Rosas et al., 2019), and AnnoMI (Wu et al., 2023),
298   which contain client information primarily in the form of a single session, originate from public video
299   sharing sources. These datasets only include client information relevant to the current stage and do
300   not provide data for subsequent stages. To assess the changes in clients across different stages, we
301   have constructed the TheraPhase Dataset based on the CPsyCoun (Zhang et al., 2024), which exhibits
302   significant changes during a single session. Our dataset includes 400 pairs of client information from
303
      both the initial and completion stages of treatment.
304
305
      Construction Process. To construct the TheraPhase Dataset, we utilize a 5-shot prompting approach
      with GPT-4 to extract the initial stage information from a client’s comprehensive information. This
306
      method isolates the beginning portion of the client’s data, forming a paired dataset where each pair
307
      consists of the initial client information and the corresponding full client information. This setup
308   allows for an analytical comparison between the initial conditions and the outcomes after therapeutic
309   interventions. The statistics of the resulting dataset are listed in Table 3.

520   5                                        C ONCLUSION
521
522   We introduced IPAEval, which can address the limitations of existing therapeutic outcome evaluation
523   approaches by shifting the focus from therapist-centered, single-session assessments to a comprehen-
524   sive, client-informed framework. By leveraging clinical interviews and integrating both cross-session
525   client-contextual and session-focused client-dynamics assessments, IPAEval provides a more holistic
526   evaluation of treatment outcomes. Experiments on the TheraPhase dataset validate its effectiveness in
527   tracking symptom severity and therapeutic progress over multiple sessions, demonstrating significant
528   improvements over previous single-session models. This advancement highlights the importance of
529
      client-centered, multi-session evaluations for enhancing mental health care and guiding treatment
      adjustments.

531
532   L IMITATIONS
533
534   The limitations of this paper are as follows: (1) Due to the shortage of professional psychological
535   annotators, only two individuals were involved in a limited amount of data labeling. This resulted in
536   fewer human-aligned experimental data. Future research should focus on developing more multi-
537   session datasets that include psychological assessment scores. (2) As the amount of client information
538   increases, smaller models with fewer parameters struggle to follow instructions effectively. This
539   limits the scalability and performance of these models in more complex scenarios. Future research
      should explore strategies to enhance model adaptability in handling larger client information inputs.
