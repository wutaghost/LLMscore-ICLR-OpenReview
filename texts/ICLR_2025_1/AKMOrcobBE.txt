009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Image restoration (IR), which aims to recover high-quality images from degraded
012            inputs, is a crucial task in modern image processing. Recent advancements in deep
013            learning, particularly with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transform-
014            ers, have significantly improved image restoration performance. However, existing
015            methods lack a unified training benchmark that specifies the training iterations and
016            configurations. Additionally, we construct an image complexity evaluation metric
017            using the gray-level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM) and find that there exists a bias
018            between the image complexity distributions of commonly used IR training and
019
               testing datasets, leading to suboptimal restoration results. Therefore, we construct
               a new large-scale IR dataset called ReSyn, that utilizes a novel image filtering
020
               method based on image complexity to achieve a balanced image complexity distri-
021
               bution, and contains both real and AIGC synthetic images. From the perspective of
022            measuring the model’s convergence ability and restoration capability, we construct
023            a unified training standard that specifies the training iterations and configura-
024            tions for image restoration models. Furthermore, we explore how to enhance
025            the performance of transformer-based image restoration models based on linear
026            attention mechanism. We propose RWKV-IR, a novel image restoration model
027            that incorporates the linear complexity RWKV into the transformer-based image
028            restoration structure, and enables both global and local receptive fields. Instead
029            of directly integrating the Vision-RWKV into the transformer architecture, we
030
               replace the original Q-Shift in RWKV with a novel Depth-wise Convolution shift,
               which effectively models the local dependencies, and is further combined with
031
               Bi-directional attention to achieve both global and local aware linear attention.
032
               Moreover, we propose a Cross-Bi-WKV module that combines two Bi-WKV mod-
033            ules with different scanning orders to achieve a balanced attention for horizontal
034            and vertical directions. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and
035            competitive performance of our RWKV-IR model.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Image restoration (IR), which aims to recover high-quality images from low-quality degraded inputs,
041
      is a crucial task in modern image processing. This field encompasses various sub-tasks, including
042   super-resolution, image denoising, and compression artifacts reduction. Recently, the advancements
043   of deep learning techniques, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) Dai et al. (2019);
044   Dong et al. (2014); Lim et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2017a; 2018b) and Transformers Chen et al.
045   (2021; 2023a;d); Li et al. (2023a); Liang et al. (2021), have significantly enhanced image restoration
046   performance, driving continuous progress in this field. Reviewing the previous IR methods, more
047   complex and deeper models Zhou et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023a) often achieve better performance.
048   To meet the data requirements for IR model training, a large number of images need to be collected
049   to construct a paired training dataset. Due to limited photography and compression techniques, the
050   images in the previous training datasets often have the problem of blurring or noise, meanwhile the
051   images in some test datasets have more image details, this causes the domain gap (different image
052   complexities) between the commonly used training and test datasets Li et al. (2023b). And most
053   datasets Timofte et al. (2017); Li et al. (2023b) focused on collecting a large number of images of
      high resolution, with few datasets considering how to measure and address this domain gap.

071   In this paper, we construct an image complexity metric based on the Gray-Level Co-occurrence
072   Matrix (GLCM) analysis method, and reveal a significant bias in image complexity distribution
073   within classic IR training datasets and test datasets. We further utilize this metric as a criterion for
074   filtering the image restoration dataset and construct a new image restoration dataset, which is called
075   ReSyn dataset. In many previous datasets Li et al. (2023b); Timofte et al. (2017), resolution and
076
      Bits Per Pixel (BPP) have been used as important criteria for image filtering, but they are insufficient
      for constructing image complexity balanced datasets. To this end,we further utilize the GLCM-based
077
      image complexity metric we proposed to filter and retain some images of medium resolution but
078
      with high image complexity. Moreover, with the rapid development of AI-generated content (AIGC),
079   there is a surging demand for synthetic image restoration. We consider the generated images as an
080   essential part of the dataset and filter them in the same manner, which also enriches the sources of our
081   dataset. The final ReSyn dataset comprises 12,000 images, with 30% being high-quality synthetic
082   images sourced from the web. Our ReSyn dataset also presents a wide range of image resolutions,
083   ranging from 0.25K to 4K, and originates from various sources. Experiments have also demonstrated
084   the effectiveness of this dataset construction approach.
085   We also review the training processes of previous image restoration models and find that there
086   is a lack of a unified IR training benchmark, i.e., the training iterations and configurations are
087   not unified. Considering the model’s convergence and restoration capability, we construct a set of
088   training standards. To measure the model’s convergence capability, we use a shorter number of
089   training iterations; to measure the restoration capability, we use a longer number of training iterations.
090   This combination of short and long training iterations allows users to have a more comprehensive
091   understanding of the model’s capabilities, facilitating the selection of the model. We conduct a
092   comprehensive evaluation of state-of-the-art IR models using the unified training standard, on our
093
      ReSyn dataset and other commonly used IR datasets. Both the ReSyn dataset and the constructed
      unified training standard form our proposed benchmark.
094
095   When comparing various image restoration models, we notice that the linear attention mechanism
096   (e.g., Mamba-based IR models) has a great potential for enhancing the effective receptive fields of
097   models. Therefore, we aim to incoperate a linear complexity attention mechanism, RWKV Peng
098   et al. (2023), with the image restoration models. We propose RWKV-IR, a novel image restoration
099
      model with both global and receptive fields, which can effectively restore low-quality images with
      linear computational complexity. Our RWKV-IR consists of three stages: shallow feature extraction,
100
      deep-feature enhancing, and HQ image reconstruction modules; and we incorporate RWKV into
101
      the deep-feature enhancing. We first introduce the Vision RWKV module to extract the deep image
102   features, where a Spatial Mix Layer is employed to enable our model with global receptive fields with
103   only linear complexity. Then, to enhance the modeling of relationships in local receptive fields and
104   eliminate the negative effects caused by the original Q-shift operation, we exploit the characteristics
105   of the local receptive fields in convolutional operations, and propose a Depth-wise Convolution
106   Shift (DC-shift) module, as a replacement of the Q-shift in the original RWKV. Moreover, the
107   Bi-WKV method of original Vision-RWKV has an unbalanced position embedding, paying more
      attention to the horizontal direction and less attention to the vertical direction, which is not suitable

108
      for the IR tasks. We propose a Cross-Bi-WKV module, which combines two Bi-WKV modules with
109   different scanning orders to achieve a balanced attention to surrounding features. By cross-scanning
110   and synchronizing the calculations of the two Bi-WKV modules in both horizontal and vertical
111   directions, we achieve a balanced attention to all four directions. Extensive experiments demonstrate
112   the effectiveness of our RWKV-IR.

      The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
114
115          • We propose a comprehensive benchmark for image restoration tasks, which includes a novel
116            large-scale benchmark dataset, and a unified training standard that specifies the number
117            of training iterations and the configuration of batch size. We conduct a comprehensive
118            evaluation of state-of-the-art IR models using the unified training standard on the new
119            dataset and other commonly used IR datasets.
120          • We construct a large-scale dataset called ReSyn, that integrates both real and synthetic
121            images. This dataset encompasses a variety of data sources and utilizes novel image filter
122            methods based on our newly proposed GLCM-based image complexity metric.
123          • We design RWKV-IR, a novel image restoration model with both global and local receptive
124            fields, which can effectively restore low-quality images with linear computational complexity.
125            We replace the the original token-shift method (Q-shift) with Depth-wise Convolution shift
126            for local dependencies modeling, and proposes Cross-Bi-WKV to replace Bi-WKV for a
127            more balanced attention for horizontal and vertical directions, which enables RWKV to be
128            effectively transferred to IR models.

      2.1   I MAGE R ESTORATION
133
134   Image restoration has witnessed significant progress with the advent of computer vision Liang et al.
135   (2021); Zhang et al. (2022a); Chen et al. (2023b); Guo et al. (2024), exemplified by pioneering CNN-
136   based methods like SRCNN Dong et al. (2014), DnCNN Zhang et al. (2017a), and ARCNN Dong
137   et al. (2015), targeting super-resolution, denoising, and artifact reduction Kim et al. (2016); Zhang
138   et al. (2021c); Cavigelli et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2018d); Lai et al. (2017); Wei
139
      et al. (2021); Fu et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2018c); Dai et al. (2019). Despite their success, CNN-
      based approaches often struggle to model global dependencies effectively. Meanwhile, Transformers,
140
      proven competitors to CNNs in various computer vision tasks Carion et al. (2020); Dosovitskiy
141
      et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021a; 2023b), show promise in restoration tasks.
142   However, they encounter challenges due to the quadratic computational complexity of the attention
143   mechanism Vaswani et al. (2017). Strategies like IPT Chen et al. (2021) and SwinIR Liang et al.
144   (2021) address this by employing patch-based processing and shifted window attention. But the
145   trade-off persists between efficient computation and global modeling Zhang et al. (2023a); Chen et al.
146   (2023a); Li et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2023d); Zamir et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023c). Recently,
147   MambaIR Guo et al. (2024) has been proposed to incorporate Mamba Gu & Dao (2023); Liu et al.
148   (2024) into the image restoration task, which globally processes the image features with only linear
149   computational complexity. Following this trend, this paper explores the possibility of integrating
150
      another linear attention mechanism, RWKV (which has shown better performance than mamba in
      other vision tasks Fei et al. (2024); He et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2024), into image restoration models.
151
152
153
      Single Image Restoration Datasets. Learning-based image restoration methods rely on the ex-
      ternal training dataset to learn the mapping between degraded and GT images. But most training
154
      datasets Timofte et al. (2017); Li et al. (2023b) focus on collecting higher resolutions and larger
155
      quantities of real images, few considering the bias between the training and testing datasets, and there
156   is no dataset taking the synthetic images into account. In this paper, we construct an image complexity
157   metric based on GLCM analysis and find that the commonly used training datasets Timofte et al.
158   (2017); Lim et al. (2017) for SR task and Arbelaez et al. (2010); Ma et al. (2016) for image denoising
159   task, exhibit a certain distribution difference in resolution compared to the testing datasets Bevilacqua
160   et al. (2012); Zeyde et al. (2012); Martin et al. (2001); Huang et al. (2015); Matsui et al. (2017).
161   Therefore, we utilize this metric as a filter criterion and construct a new image ReStoration dataset
      which including the Real and Synthetic images (ReSyn dataset).

      2.2   R ECEPTANCE W EIGHTED K EY VALUE MODEL (RWKV)
170
171   The attention mechanism has shown promising performance in both CV and NLP fields. Various
172   operators with linear complexity Peng et al. (2023); Gu & Dao (2023); Qin et al. (2023) have been
173   explored to optimize the global attention mechanism in recent years. A modified form of linear
174   attention, the Attention Free Transformer (AFT) Zhai et al. (2021), paved the way for the RWKV
175   architecture by using some attention heads equal to the size of the feature dimension and incorporating
176   a set of learned pairwise positional biases. RWKV-v4 Peng et al. (2023) employed exponential decay
177   to model global information efficiently. Vision-RWKV Duan et al. (2024) transfers the RWKV-v4
178   to the vision domain through a Q-shift mechanism and bidirectional attention. RWKV-5/6 Peng
179
      et al. (2024) further refined the architecture of RWKV-4. RWKV-5 adds matrix-valued attention
      states, LayerNorm over the attention heads, SiLU attention gating, and improved initialization. It also
180
      removes the Sigmoid activation of receptance. RWKV-6 further applies data dependence to the decay
181
      schedule and token shift. We modify the RWKV-v4 module of Vision-RWKV, use a depth-wised conv
182   to replace the Q-shift, and also a Cross-Bi-Direction attention to replace the Bi-Direction attention.

185   3     R E S YN DATASET
186
187   Due to limited photography techniques and compression techniques, many images in previous
188
      datasets Deng et al. (2009); Timofte et al. (2017) suffer from noise, blurring, and other problems.
      However, most datasets focus solely on obtaining high-resolution images, using resolution and Bits
189
      Per Pixel (BPP) for image filtering, but lacking a sufficient consideration for image complexities.
190
      This causes a distribution bias (different image complexities) between the classic image restoration
191   training datasets and the test datasets. We propose an image complexity metric based on the Gray
192   Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM) analysis method to directly analyze this complexity distribution
193   bias. As shown in Fig. 2, we analyze the GLCM complexity distribution of the classic SR training
194   datasets (DIV2K Timofte et al. (2017) and DF2K Lim et al. (2017)), and test datasets (Urban100,
195   Manga109 and BS100). It can be seen that the classic SR training datasets and test datasets often
196   have different image complexity distributions. And the test dataset that often achieves a better PSNR
197   performance, e.g., Manga109, has a complexity distribution closer to that of the training dataset.
198   We further analyze the relationship between the GLCM complexity indicator and the restoration
199
      performance metric PSNR. As shown in Fig. 3, GLCM complexity can better predict the restoration
      performance compared to the BPP indicator. Moreover, datasets with lower GLCM complexity
200
      images show better restoration performance.
201
202   To enhance the performance of existing methods and incorporate images generated by the AIGC
203   method, we construct the ReSyn dataset, a new large-scale dataset for both the Real and Synthetic
204   image ReStoration. Our dataset introduces the GLCM complexity indicator as a criterion for filtering
205
      images to help improve the quality of shuffled images and achieve a balanced complexity distribution.
      Examples of the images from our ReSyn dataset are shown in Fig.1b. The data collection pipeline
206
      and the dataset analysis are introduced in details below. Tab. 1 presents the differences between our
207
      ReSyn dataset and commonly used IR training datasets. More details are in the Appendix Sec. A.

      3.1   DATA COLLECTION
210
211   Data Source. Our dataset consists of both real images and AI-generated images. Many previous
212   methods Chen et al. (2023a); Zhou et al. (2023) improve performance by pretraining on large-scale
213   datasets like ImageNet Deng et al. (2009). We follow this trend and collect images from these datasets
214   to form the real image part of our dataset. The real images are collected from the commonly used
215   large-scale datasets for high-level tasks, including ImageNet Deng et al. (2009), COCO2017 Lin
      et al. (2014), and SAM Kirillov et al. (2023). The images are filtered and low-quality images are


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      discarded, only 9K images are retained. To introduce the AI-generated images into this dataset, we
217   automatically crawl images from Midjourney, and after filtering we retain 3K synthetic images for
218   our dataset. The origins of these images are detailed in the distribution chart shown in Fig. 1a, while
219   the diversity of their resolutions is illustrated in Fig. 1b. Different from previous datasets that filter
220   solely based on resolutions, we do not completely discard images with resolutions below 2K, since
221   many images in the test dataset are below 2K resolution and have a high image complexity. Our
222   dataset encompasses a broad range of image resolutions, with images ranging from 0.5K to 4K, and
223   most images have a resolution larger than 1K.
224   Data Selection Criteria. The images used for image restoration model training need to have a high
225   pixel-level quality. To this end, we divide the image filtering process into three steps. 1) Firstly,
226   the images of resolution smaller than 800 × 800 are discarded, since for super-resolution tasks, the
227   images need to be down-sampled. This can help remove most low-quality images. 2) Secondly,
228   to remove the blurry or noisy degraded images, we follow the blur and noise suppression process
229   proposed in LSDIR Li et al. (2023b). The remaining images are under blur detection by the variance
230   of the image Laplacian, and flat region detection through the Sobel filter. 3) Thirdly, all the images
231
      are shuffled through the GLCM complexity metric (detailed below) to ensure a balanced distribution.
      We ensure that the number of images with complexity values below zero is equal to that above zero.
232
      Therefore, we can form a dataset of balanced image complexity distribution. It should be mentioned
233
      that images from different sources are filtered separately.
234
235   Image Complexity Analysis. As shown in previous works Timofte et al. (2017), the PSNR metric
236   of super-resolution images is strongly correlated with the Bits Per Pixel (BPP), an indicator for the
237
      quantity of image information. Although BPP reflects the quality of an image by measuring its color
      depth, it lacks consideration for the relationships between pixels and cannot adequately measure
238
      the texture variation and complexity of an image. Therefore, this paper further measure the image
239
      complexity based on the Gray Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM) and investigate its correlation
240   with PSNR metric. Since human eyes are more sensitive to texture, we utilize the GLCM, which is
241   closely related to the complexity of image texture, to construct an image complexity analysis metric.
242   We calculate relevant statistical quantities from the GLCM, and use Entropy, Energy, and Dissimilarity
243   to build a formula for image complexity analysis as follows: Icomplexity = EN T − EN E + DISS,
244   where EN T , EN E, and DISS represent entropy, energy, and dissimilarity respectively, all of
245   which are statistical quantities calculated from GLCM. As shown in Fig. 3, we analyze the correlation
246   between the PSNR-Y metric and GLCM-based image complexity, as well as BPP. The PSNR metrics
247   are measured on super-resolution results generated by two pre-trained models and a direct bicubic
248
      upsample for the Urban100 test images, and sorted according to GLCM complexity and BPP. Our
      GLCM complexity measure has a stronger Pearson correlation (ρ = −0.86) compared to BPP
249
      (ρ = 0.65), indicating that the proposed image complexity is a stronger predictor for the PSNR metric
250
      of the restored images. Furthermore, the distribution of GLCM complexity is symmetric with respect
251   to the origin, making it an excellent metric for image filtering.
252
253   Post Processing. For the classic image restoration tasks, e.g., super-resolution, the training requires
254
      paired down-sampled LR images and ground truths. We employ the classic bicubic down-sampling
      method1 to obtain the LR images. We use the commonly used scale factors of ×2, ×3, and ×4.
255
256   Partitions. After the image filtering and the post-processing, there are 12K images left, of which 9K
257   are real images and 3K are synthetic images. We then randomly partition our ReSyn dataset into a
258   training set of 10K images, a validation set of 1K images, and a test set of 1K images.

422
423
      Few previous methods have focused on the impact of training criteria on the performance comparison
      of restoration models. Many approaches Liang et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2023); Guo et al. (2024)
424
      have instead opted to extend training time continuously to improve model performance. This can lead
425
      to unfair comparisons in subsequent evaluations. Therefore, we construct a comprehensive training
426   benchmark from two perspectives: measuring the model’s convergence ability and its restoration
427   capability. In this section, we present the experimental benchmark and the training details of our
428   proposed linear-complexity attention-based image restoration model. We then conduct a benchmark
429   study comparing our method with other models. Due to space limitations, only the results of classical
430   SR tasks are shown in the main paper. Please refer to the Appendix Sec. C for the experiments
431   of other IR tasks (light-weight SR, image denoising, JPEG artifacts reduction). Source codes are
      provided in the supplementary material.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
433
434   Experimental Benchmark. We propose a unified training benchmark for different kinds of image
435   restoration tasks. 1) Super Resolution: The commonly conducted SR tasks are lightweight SR and
436   classical SR. For all compared methods, we set the same batch size and the same number of iterations
437   for training. To show models’ convergence and restoration abilities, we compared different models in
438   two number levels of training iterations. The batch size for lightweight SR model training is 64, and
439   the training iterations are 50K and 500K. The batch size for classical SR model training is 32, and
440   the training iterations are 100K and 500K. 2) Image Denoising: For Gaussian color denoising and
441   gray-scale denoising tasks, the training batch size and the training iterations are set to 16 and 100K
442
      (500k for long training), respectively. 3) JPEG Compression Artifact Reduction: the batch size and
      training iterations are set to 16 and 100K (500k for high number level), respectively. For more details
443
      about the model settings, please refer to the Appendix Sec. B.
444
445   Training Details. We conduct super-resolution (SR) training experiments on three datasets:
446   DIV2K Timofte et al. (2017), DF2K Lim et al. (2017), and our ReSyn. For lightweight SR, models are
447   trained separately on the DIV2K and ReSyn datasets. For classic SR, models are trained separately on
448
      the DF2K and ReSyn datasets. In the training of image denoising models, we compare performance
      on the DFWB RGB dataset (a combined dataset of DIV2K Wang et al. (2023), Flickr2K Lim et al.
449
      (2017), BSD500 Arbelaez et al. (2010), and WED Ma et al. (2016)) and our ReSyn dataset. During
450
      training, we crop the compressed images into 64×64 patches for image SR. We do not use pre-trained
451   weights from the ×2 model to initialize those of ×3 and ×4 but train the ×3 and ×4 models from
452   scratch. For the denoising task, we crop the original images into 128 × 128 patches. We employ
453   the Adam optimizer for training our RWKV-IR with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. The initial learning
454   rate is set at 2 × 10−4 and is decreased during training using the multi-step scheduler. Our models
455   are trained with 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs. Except for the batch size and training iterations for the
456   compared methods, all other settings remain consistent with their official training codes.

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we review the task of image restoration. We utilize the Gray Level Co-occurrence
532
      Matrix to construct an image complexity metric, which demonstrates the bias between complexity
533
      distributions of classic training datasets and test datasets. Based on this metric, we construct ReSyn, a
534   new image restoration dataset that includes both real and generated images with balanced complexity.
535   Additionally, we develop a novel benchmark for comparing image restoration models, focusing on
536   two aspects: the convergence speed and the restoration capability. Moreover, from the perspective
537   of linear attention mechanisms, we propose a novel RWKV-IR model, which integrates the RWKV
538   into image restoration models, constructing a linear attention-based image restoration model. This
539   inspires further exploration and enhancement of the effective receptive field of the model. Extensive
      experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed benchmark and RWKV-IR model.

813   A    M ORE DETAILS OF O UR R E S YN DATASET
814
815   In this section, we further provide details on the construction of our ReSyn dataset. The process of
816   final filtering based on our GLCM image complexity metric is shown in Fig. 7.
817   Our ReSyn dataset contains images from four sources, including ImageNet Deng et al. (2009),
818   COCO2017 Lin et al. (2014), SAM Kirillov et al. (2023), and MidJourney. For ImageNet, we
819   first remove images with a resolution of less than 800×800. Since the details in the images from
820   ImageNet are not rich and relatively blurry, then the images of blurring and noise degradation are
821   filtered. Finally, through image complexity filtering, we choose 1,200 images from the ImageNet.
822   Half of the images have a complexity greater than zero.
823   For COCO2017 Lin et al. (2014), almost all of the images are medium-resolution images, we follow
824   the method of ImageNet filtering and include 1,200 images from COCO2017.
825
      For SAM, most images have a resolution of over 2K. For privacy purposes, many of the images in the
826
      dataset containing faces and sensitive information are mosaiced. Therefore, we manually remove
827   the images that include the mosaics, leaving only the clear images. After this step, we use the same
828   filtering method as ImageNet and include 6,000 images from SAM.
829
830
      For MidJourney, we first crawl more than 30,000 high-quality images from the web. And then after
      filtering, 3,600 images are left to form the dataset.
