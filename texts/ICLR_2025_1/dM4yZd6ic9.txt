                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Deciphering language from brain activity is a crucial task in brain-computer in-
               terface (BCI) research. Non-invasive cerebral signaling techniques including
011
               electroencephalography (EEG) and magnetoencephalography (MEG) are becom-
012
               ing increasingly popular due to their safety and practicality, avoiding invasive
013            electrode implantation. However, current works under-investigated three points: 1)
014            a predominant focus on EEG with limited exploration of MEG, which provides
015            superior signal quality; 2) poor performance on unseen text, indicating the need
016            for models that can better generalize to diverse linguistic contexts; 3) insufficient
017            integration of information from other modalities, which could potentially constrain
018            our capacity to comprehensively understand the intricate dynamics of brain activity.
019            This study presents a novel approach for translating MEG signals into text using
020            a speech-decoding framework with multiple alignments. Our method is the first
021            to introduce an end-to-end multi-alignment framework for totally unseen text
022            generation directly from MEG signals. We achieve an impressive BLEU-1 score on
023            the GWilliams dataset, significantly outperforming the baseline from 5.49 to 10.44
024
               on the BLEU-1 metric. This improvement demonstrates the advancement of our
               model towards real-world applications and underscores its potential in advancing
025
               BCI research.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Decoding brain to language has emerged as a rapidly developing area of neurotechnology, offering
032   semantic communication and control for general Brain-Computer-Interface (BCI) tasks. This region
033
      has garnered growing focus as it may profoundly impact individuals with verbal and movement
      disabilities resulting from conditions such as severe spinal cord trauma or end-stage amyotrophic
034
      lateral sclerosis (ALS). Moreover, the scope of brain-to-text technology extends to pioneer novel
035
      human-machine interfaces, allowing seamless control of prosthetic limbs, software, and virtual
036   environments, shifting the paradigm of interaction for both able-bodied individuals and those with
037   disabilities, and re-defining what is achievable in both everyday life and professional spheres.
038
039
      Under this scope, various previous works have explored this area in multiple ways. Pioneer researchers
      first verify this idea by using invasive signals such as Electrocorticography (ECoG) Anumanchipalli
040
      et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020); Willett et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021). Recently, these invasive
041
      methods Willett et al. (2023); Metzger et al. (2023) concentrate on decoding speech, phonemes or
042   letter from ECoG signals and have achieved remarkably high accuracy using limited word sets for
043   real-time brain-to-text translation. However, these invasive-signal-based approaches pose significant
044   medical risks and challenges for long-term use.
045
      Non-invasive techniques, therefore, present a safer and more sustainable alternative, albeit with their
046
      own set of challenges. Wang et al. Wang & Ji (2022) showcased a method for translating EEG signals
047
      into text with an extensive lexicon, utilizing language models that had been pretrained on EEG data
048   features at word-level. Duan et al. Duan et al. (2023) progressed this methodology by interpreting
049   raw EEG signals directly, devoid of reliance on temporal indicators, but their models still relied
050   heavily on teacher-forcing for evaluation, limiting their ability to generate meaningful sentences
051   autonomously in real-life scenarios. At the same time, although Magnetoencephalography (MEG)
052   provides better signal quality, previous works Dash et al. (2020); Csaky et al. (2023); Ghazaryan
053   et al. (2023) on MEG have primarily focused on decoding limited classes or short phrases from MEG
      signals, showing limited success in generating whole sentences and complete semantic segments.

054
      Furthermore, as pointed out by Jo et al. Hyejeong et al. (2024), all previous works in EEG-to-Text
055   translation following Wang’s method Wang & Ji (2022) meets the “decoder dominated" problem. It
056   means that given a strong decoder and noisy EEG input, these models are more likely to memorize
057   the text distribution corresponding to certain statistical features rather than mapping EEG to semantic
058   texts. Thus, these models have similar performances even when we replace EEG input with random
059   noise. Besides, due to the nature of limited data and the non-understandability of the neural signal, it
060   is difficult to train and evaluate the model. Yang et al. Yang et al. (2024) proposed NeuSpeech model
061   on MEG to text task, however, their model is evaluated on the text that is seen in the training set,
062   which does not meet the need for open-vocabulary translation. Defossez et al. Défossez et al. (2023)
063
      highlighted the potential to decode speech perception from MEG signals, where they matched MEG
      signals with corresponding speech segments. However, their approach was limited to classification
064
      tasks and could not generate sentences directly from MEG signals. This underscores a significant gap
065
      in the current state of MEG-based brain-to-text decoding.
066
067   In this paper, we propose an end-to-end framework for open-vocabulary MEG-to-Text translation
068   capable of generalized on unseen text for real-life utilization. However, as mentioned by Yang et
069
      al. Yang et al. (2024), relying solely on traditional text loss, as done in previous works, is inadequate
      in unseen text scenarios. Our intuition is that incorporating additional information from different
070
      modalities can enhance the model’s performance. Therefore, we conducted experiments using various
071
      combinations of modalities and loss functions to determine the optimal configuration for the brain-to-
072   text model in this limited-data situation. More specifically, we utilize Brain Module Défossez et al.
073   (2023) and a pre-trained whisper model Radford et al. (2023) to align brain representation in three
074   aspects as shown in Figure 1, the Mel spectrogram, hidden state, and text. 1) We first align the Brain
075   module with audio in the Mel spectrogram feature space to learn low-level features, such as acoustic
076   features. 2) Secondly, we align the hidden state output in latent space from both whisper encoders
077   of which input is predicted and ground truth Mel spectrogram respectively, enhancing the model’s
078   ability to extract high-level semantic features. 3) Lastly, we align the text representation from both
079   streams within the framework.
080   Comprehensive experiments are conducted by utilizing non-invasive public MEG data from
081   GWilliams Gwilliams et al. (2023) dataset, which captured MEG signals during a speech listen-
082   ing task. We have identified several noteworthy findings regarding brain-to-text alignment. 1)
083   High-level semantic representations play a predominant role in MEG-to-text decoding, outperforming
084   low-level acoustic features or direct text alignment strategies. 2) While low-level features contribute
085   to improved performance when combined with high-level semantic information, they are insufficient
086
      in isolation to achieve satisfactory decoding results. 3) Explicit text alignment mechanisms prove
      detrimental to the task, significantly compromising the model’s generalization capabilities. 4) Fine-
087
      tuning large-scale pre-trained models on this specialized, small-scale MEG dataset results in severe
088
      over-fitting, highlighting the challenges of transfer learning in this domain.
089
090   Remarkably, MAD is capable of generalizing to unseen text. Performance is evaluated using
091   translation text relevancy metrics Papineni et al. (2002); Lin (2004). On raw MEG waves, MAD
092
      achieves 10.44 BLEU-1 on GWilliams without teacher-forcing evaluation on entirely unseen text
      which largely exceeds the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. This paper also provides
093
      insights through numerous ablation studies to help people understand the impact of each component
094
      on aligning the MEG signal with texts. The contributions of this research could be summarized as
095   follows:
096
097
             • MAD presents an end-to-end neural network design for the direct conversion of MEG signals
098            into text in open vocabulary, eliminating the dependence on word time segmentation provided
099            by eye-tracker, teacher-forcing, or pretraining, representing the initial implementation of
100            translating raw MEG waves into text for unseen content.
101
102          • We are the first to investigate various alignments and demonstrate the benefits of aligning
103            with speech modality rather than text modality in the MEG-to-Text transcription task,
104            offering significant insights for network improvement.
105
106          • Our extensive experimentation and thorough analysis of the proposed model showcase its
107            effectiveness and highlight its superiority over existing methods in terms of translation
               accuracy, efficiency, and reliability.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

      The discipline of converting brain signals into textual output has undergone considerable development
112   in the contemporary era. In 2019, Anumanchipalli et al. Anumanchipalli et al. (2019) introduced a
113   pioneering model capable of translating ECoG patterns into the articulatory movements necessary
114   for speech production, subsequently generating acoustic properties such as MFCCs, leading to the
115   production of intelligible speech. This landmark study ignited further exploration within the field. In
116   the subsequent year, Wang et al. Wang et al. (2020) leveraged the capabilities of generative adversarial
117   networks (GANs) to decipher ECoG data and synthesize speech. The year following, Willett et al.
118   Willett et al. (2021) engineered a system that utilized a recurrent neural network (RNN) alongside
119   a probabilistic language model to decode letters from neural activity during the act of handwriting.
120
      Most recently, Metzger et al. Metzger et al. (2022) constructed a sequence of processes that converted
      ECoG signals into textual information using an RNN, enhancing the results with the GPT-2 language
121
      model.
122
123   Within the domain of open-vocabulary interpretation, Metzger et al. Metzger et al. (2023) unveiled
124   an RNN architecture capable of real-time decoding of speech, text, sentiment, and facial expressions
125   from ECoG data. Simultaneously, Willett et al. Willett et al. (2023) managed to interpret text directly
126
      from neural activity. Liu et al. Liu et al. (2023) introduced a tripartite model designed to decode
      logo-syllabic languages, such as Chinese, by transforming ECoG signals into Chinese pinyin inclusive
127
      of tones and syllables, followed by speech synthesis. In a related development, Feng et al. Feng
128
      et al. (2023) achieved text interpretation from SEEG recordings. It is essential to highlight that these
129   functional systems are predominantly reliant on invasive neural recordings.
130
131   In the domain of non-invasive neural recording, Meta unveiled a brain-to-speech system that leverages
132
      contrastive learning with MEG and EEG data Défossez et al. (2023). While this system is proficient
      in categorizing a constrained set of sentences, it is not conducive to open-vocabulary textual interpre-
133
      tation. Ghazaryan et al. Ghazaryan et al. (2023) explored the decoding of a restricted vocabulary from
134
      MEG responses. Wang et al. Wang & Ji (2022) crafted a mechanism for translating EEG features
135   at the word level into text, employing a pretrained BART model Lewis et al. (2020). Subsequent
136   investigations, including Dewave Duan et al. (2023), adopted the methodology established by Wang
137   et al. Wang & Ji (2022), proposing a schema that incorporates wave2vec Baevski et al. (2020) and
138   discrete codex for robust representations, which are subsequently funneled into a BART Lewis et al.
139   (2020) model for text synthesis. These approaches, however, are dependent on teacher-forcing and
140   disregard the necessity of comparing results with noise-injected inputs, potentially resulting in an
141   inflated assessment of system efficacy. Recent scholarship Hyejeong et al. (2024) has revealed the
142   limitations of these methods.
143   Yang et al. Yang et al. (2024) proposed an end-to-end paradigm for converting MEG signals to
144   text, demonstrating high performance when training and evaluation sets were fully overlapped.
145   Nevertheless, the model failed to demonstrate comparable performance when applied to unseen text.
146   Our approach diverges from these methods by employing transfer learning with assistance of extra
147   modality (Mel spectrogram) to align the model through multiple stages with low-level and high-level
148   features of the ground truth. This enables our model to learn more effectively and generalize better to
149
      unseen text.

156   3.1   TASK D EFINITION
157
158   Given a sequence of raw segment-level MEG signals ε, the goal is to decode the associated open-
159   vocabulary text tokens T . This task also incorporates additional information in the form of speech Ξ.
160   The MEG-Speech-Text pairs ⟨ε, Ξ, T ⟩ are collected during speech perception. Our approach focuses
161   on decoding T using only the raw MEG signal ε, with the support of Ξ. MAD represents the first
      attempt at tackling this MEG to unseen text translation challenge.

184   3.2   A RCHITECTURE
185
      Figure 1 presents an overview of our proposed model architecture. Our approach leverages transfer
186
      learning techniques to enhance performance on unseen text. We utilize the encoder and decoder mod-
187   els from the Whisper model Radford et al. (2023), a transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture
188   known for robust speech recognition in challenging environments. We used AdaLoRA Zhang et al.
189   (2023) module to train the whisper encoder in our architecture for saving memory.
190
      The key innovation in our design lies in its multi-modal architecture, which is intentionally streamlined
191
      to facilitate experiments with different modalities. This design allows us to investigate which
192
      modalities contribute most effectively to the task, providing valuable insights into the MEG-to-Text
193   decoding process.
194
195   Our architecture combines two primary modalities: speech and MEG signals. Both inputs are first
196
      converted to Mel spectrograms (M1 for MEG, M2 for speech) and then transformed into encoder
      features (E1 and E2 respectively). The speech input’s features serve as the ground truth for the
197
      MEG-derived features. For the MEG input pathway, we further employ a decoder to predict text (T1 ),
198
      using the actual transcription (T2 ) as ground truth.
199
200   The Brain Module, adapted from Défossez et al. (2023), processes the MEG signals to Mel spec-
201   trograms. While we don’t modify its internal architecture, its integration into our multi-modal
202
      framework is crucial for our experimental design. The brain module processes MEG signals through
      a deep neural network. It begins with a spatial attention layer, followed by a 1x1 convolution without
203
      activation. A subject-specific ’Subject Layer’ is then selected using the subject index s, applying a
204
      1x1 convolution unique to each participant. A residual dilated convolution block is applied, repeated
205   five times as described in the paper Défossez et al. (2023). Each block consists of three convolutional
206   layers. The first two dilated convolution layers in each block incorporate residual skip connections,
207   while the third convolution layer uses a GLU (Gated Linear Unit) activation, which reduces the
208   number of channels by half. And two 1x1 convolutions were applied. The final output is a predicted
209   Mel spectrogram (M1 ), which shares the same time points as the input MEG signals (ε). This modular
210   approach allows us to systematically evaluate the contribution of each modality and their interactions,
211   providing a flexible framework for exploring various alignment strategies in MEG-to-Text decoding.
212
213   3.3   L OSS
214
215   We employ three distinct loss functions tailored to align different modalities, each chosen for its
      established effectiveness in the respective context. The Mel spectrogram alignment loss, Lm , utilizes

216
       CLIP loss Radford et al. (2021), which is particularly suited for this task due to its ability to learn
217    a joint embedding space that enhances the representation of semantically related Mel spectrogram
218    pairs. This is crucial as it allows the model to capture the nuances of MEG-speech relationships,
219    thereby improving performance in multi-modal understanding. For the encoder model to effectively
220    learn high-level features from the extracted representations, we adopt Maximum Mean Discrepancy
221    (MMD) loss Borgwardt et al. (2006) as Le . This choice stems from MMD’s ability to measure and
222    minimize the divergence between probability distributions, ensuring that the encoder’s output aligns
223    closely with the target distributions and facilitating better generalization across different domains.
224    Finally, we implement cross-entropy loss, denoted as Lt , for the comparison between predicted and
225
       ground truth text. This loss is fundamental in classification tasks, as it quantifies the discrepancy
       between the predicted probabilities and actual labels, driving the model to refine its predictions
226
       iteratively. Collectively, these loss functions not only optimize the learning process for each modality
227
       but also foster an integrated approach to multi-modal learning that enhances overall model robustness
228    and accuracy. The overall loss L is below:
229
230
                                         L = λm · Lm + λe · Le + λt · Lt ,                                    (1)
231    where Lm is LCLIP , LE is LMMD , Lt is LCE in default.
232
       The CLIP loss Radford et al. (2021) function originally operates on feature representations derived
233
       from both image and text modalities. It calculates similarity scores between these representations,
234    with the objective of minimizing the distance between matching pairs while maximizing the distance
235    between non-matching pairs. This framework enables the CLIP model to learn a joint embedding
236    space, positioning semantically similar image-text pairs in close proximity. This capability facilitates
237    tasks such as zero-shot image classification and text-based image retrieval. It has also been proved to
238    be useful in predicting speech features as studies by Défossez et al. (2023). In our application, Lm
239    uses CLIP loss, which is applied on the Mel spectrogram in default. Mel spectrogram M1 and M2 is
240    represented in three dimensions, we first flatten the batch size and time length dimensions to create a
241    single dimension. The loss is then computed accordingly as follows:

512   Although our MAD model outperforms previous SOTA models, we have to point out that this model’s

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we presented MAD, a novel end-to-end training framework for MEG-to-Text translation.
520   Our model leverages multiple alignment utilizing auxiliary modalities, which aligns brain activity
521   data more effectively with corresponding textual outputs. Experimental results suggest that the
522   newly proposed MAD framework achieves 10.44 BLEU-1 on GWilliams without teacher-forcing
523   evaluation on entirely unseen text, significantly surpassing the current state-of-the-art performance.
524   Through comprehensive ablation studies, we share valuable insights into the efficacy of our approach,
525   designing better loss function combinations to inspire future research. Our findings highlight the
526   importance of high-level semantic alignment, the complementary role of low-level features, and the
527   potential pitfalls of explicit text reconstruction and over-reliance on large pretrained models. These
528   insights underscore the potential of the MAD framework in neural decoding, offering a robust strategy
529   for MEG-to-Text translation by effectively capturing complex patterns in MEG signals and translating
530   them into coherent text, which can be used by later-on research.
531   In conclusion, our proposed MAD framework significantly advances the state-of-the-art(SOTA) in
532   MEG-to-text decoding, offering new avenues for enhancing communication tools for individuals
533   with severe speech and motor impairments. This work sets the stage for further exploration into
534   multi-modal alignments and their impact on neural decoding systems. Future research can focus on
535   refining alignment mechanisms, exploring more sophisticated feature integration techniques, and
536   extending the application of our model to more diverse linguistic tasks and larger-scale MEG datasets.

      B.2   P ROCEDURE
780
781          • Recording Sessions:
782              – Duration: Each session lasted approximately 1 hour.
783              – Equipment: Recorded with a 208 axial-gradiometer MEG scanner (Kanazawa Institute
784                of Technology).
785              – Sampling Rate: 1,000 Hz.
786              – Filtering: Online band-pass filtered between 0.01 and 200 Hz.
787              – Task: Participants listened to four distinct stories through binaural tube earphones
788                (Aero Technologies) at a mean level of 70 dB sound pressure level.
789
             • Pre-Experiment Exposure:
790
791
                 – Participants were exposed to 20 seconds of each distinct speaker voice.
792              – Purpose: To clarify session structure and familiarize participants with the voices.
793          • Story Presentation Order:
794              – Assigned pseudo-randomly using a "Latin-square design."
795              – Same order used for both recording sessions for each participant.
796
             • Attention Check:
797
798
                 – Participants answered a two-alternative forced-choice question every 3 minutes.
799              – Example Question: "What precious material had Chuck found? Diamonds or Gold."
800              – Average Accuracy: 98%, confirming engagement and comprehension.
801          • MRI Scans:
802              – T1-weighted anatomical scans were performed after MEG recording if not already
803                available.
804              – Six participants did not return for their T1 scan.
805
             • Head Shape Digitization:
806
807
                 – Head shape digitized with a hand-held FastSCAN laser scanner (Polhemus).
808
                 – Co-registered with five head-position coils.
809              – Coil positions collected before and after each recording, stored in the ’marker’ file.
                 – Experimenter continuously monitored head position to minimize movement.

             • Audio Tracks:

820               – Synthesized using Mac OS Mojave’s (c) text-to-speech.
821               – Voices and speech rates varied every 5-20 sentences to decorrelate language from
822                 acoustic representations.
823               – Voices used: ‘Ava’, ‘Samantha’, and ‘Allison’.
824               – Speech rate: Between 145 and 205 words per minute.
825               – Silence between sentences: Varied between 0 and 1,000 ms.

                  – Each story divided into 5-minute sound files.
828

                  – Very small fraction (<1%) of non-words introduced in natural sentences.

      C     D ISCUSSION ABOUT THE MAIN TABLE
838
      We used BLEU-1 Papineni et al. (2002),ROUGE-1-F Lin (2004),BertScore Zhang et al.
839
      (2019),CER Martins & Garland Jr (1991),Self-BLEU Zhu et al. (2018) as metrics in the main table to
840
      show the capability of previous models and our models. However, as observed, NeuSpeech Yang
841   et al. (2024) model has the best score for ROUGE-1,Bert,CER, which is incredible, therefore we
842   measured the Self-BLEU of this model, which is almost 100%, and found out NeuSpeech predicts
843   almost the same sentence “He looked at me and said to me" all the time for different sentences in
844   Supp. 1. Generation of this bad quality has best score on these three metrics, which means these three
845   metrics are not effective in measuring the generation quality. Therefore, we think BLEU-1 the most
846   reliable metric in this task for now. Besides, we randomly selected sentences, which is RS in the table,
847   from the test set as another baseline, we found out that the BLEU-1 score is higher than NeuSpeech,
848   which means the NeuSpeech model is not effective, which is very reasonable. After all, it seems that
849
      using BLEU score is the only reasonable metric of evaluating the quality of generated text.
850   As observed in the table, it is very clear that our MAD model is significantly higher than RS and
851   NeuSpeech and Wav2vec2CTC on BLEU-1, which means our MAD model is effective on unseen
852   text.
