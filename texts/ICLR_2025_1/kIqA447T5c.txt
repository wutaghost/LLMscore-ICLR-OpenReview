011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013             Deep Generative Models (DGMs), including Score-based Generative Models,
014             have made significant progress in approximating complex continuous distribu-
015             tions. However, their application to Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), partic-
016             ularly in distributional Reinforcement Learning (RL), is underexplored. The field
017
                remains dominated by classical histogram-based methods, which suffer from dis-
                cretization errors, leading to instability and slower convergence. This work high-
018
                lights that this gap stems from the nonlinear operators used in modern DGM’s
019
                modelings, which map neural network functions to the target distribution. These
020             nonlinearities conflict with the linearity required by the Bellman equation, which
021             relates the return distribution of a state to a linear combination of future states’
022             return distributions. To address this, we introduce Bellman Diffusion, a new DGM
023             that preserves the necessary linearity by modeling both the gradient and scalar
024             fields. We propose a novel divergence-based training technique to optimize neural
025             network proxies and introduce a new stochastic differential equation for sampling.
026             With these innovations, Bellman Diffusion is guaranteed to converge to the target
027             distribution. Our experiments show that Bellman Diffusion not only achieves ac-
028
                curate field estimations and serves as an effective image generator, but also con-
                verges 1.5ˆ faster than traditional histogram-based baselines in distributional RL
029
                tasks. This work paves the way for the effective integration of DGMs into MDP
030
                applications, enabling more advanced decision-making frameworks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), particularly distributional Reinforcement Learning
036
      (RL) (Bellemare et al., 2017), learn the distribution of returns rather than just the expected value
037
      (i.e., the Q-function). This allows the model to capture the intrinsic randomness (stochastic dynam-
038   ics and rewards) of returns, demonstrating its efficacy and broad applicability (Lowet et al., 2020;
039   Lyle et al., 2019; Dabney et al., 2018). Despite Deep Generative Models (DGMs), such as Energy-
040   Based Models (EBMs) (Teh et al., 2003), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
041   et al., 2020), and emerging Score-based Generative Models (SGMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
042   Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020), are well-developed for learning complex continuous distribu-
043   tions, their application to Markov Decision Processes and distributional RL remains underexplored1 .
044   Instead, classical histogram-based methods (e.g., C51 (Bellemare et al., 2017)) remain widely used
045   in MDPs: These methods leverage Bellman’s equation (Bellman, 1954; Mnih et al., 2013) to effi-
046
      ciently update the model with partial trajectories and approximate return distributions using discrete
      bins, rather than directly modeling the continuous return distribution. The discretization inherent
047
      in these methods can accumulate errors, causing instability and slower convergence. Specifically,
048
      for continuous return distributions, histogram-based methods inevitably introduce discretization er-
049   rors at each state, leading to error propagation along the state-action trajectory. This accumulation
050   of errors, combined with potentially long trajectories, makes histogram-based methods, unstable to

533   7     C ONCLUSION
534
535   This work addresses the limitations of modern DGMs in MDPs and distributional RL, emphasizing
536   the need for linearity in modelings. We propose Bellman Diffusion, a novel DGM that maintains
537   linearity by modeling gradient and scalar fields. Through new divergence measures and a SDE-based
538   sampling method (Bellman Diffusion Dynamics), we ensure convergence to the target distribution.
539   Experimental results show that Bellman Diffusion provides accurate estimations and outperforms
      traditional RL methods, offering a promising approach for integrating DGMs into RL frameworks.

760   Deep generative models (DGMs) have gained significant attention in recent years due to their ability

762   ers several prominent categories of DGMs, including Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), Generative
763   Adversarial Networks (GANs), energy-based methods, flow-based methods, and diffusion models.
764
765   Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) are a class of generative
766
      models that leverage variational inference to approximate the posterior distribution of latent vari-

771   Kullback-Leibler divergence. VAEs have shown remarkable success in generating images and other
772   complex data types Kingma (2013).

774   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) consist
775   of two neural networks, a generator G and a discriminator D, that compete against each other.

      A.2    R ELATED W ORK ON MDP S
811
812   Limited by the linearity of the distributional Bellman equation, Previous works (Bellemare et al.,
813   2017; Hessel et al., 2018; Dabney et al., 2018) in planning and distributional RL have relied on
814   conventional generative models to represent state-level return distributions. For instance, the widely
815   used C51 (Bellemare et al., 2017) is a histogram model, resulting in discrete approximation errors. In
816   contrast, Bellman Diffusion is a new type of diffusion model that serves as an expressive distribution
817   approximator without discretization errors.
818   Recent work (Messaoud et al., 2024) (S2 AC) also explores to leverage DGMs for RL tasks. How-
819   ever, they do not align with the problem setting and objectives of our paper. S2 AC is designed for
820   Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) RL, where the primary goal is learning a stochastic policy. In contrast,
821   our work focuses on distributional RL, which aims to model the return distribution for each state.
822   These are fundamentally different RL paradigms, addressing distinct challenges and requiring tai-
823   lored methodologies. Similar to the Langevin dynamics used in vanilla diffusion models, the SVGD
824
      sampler relies on the score function. This dependency introduces challenges when applied to distri-
      butional RL, where we aim to model the return distribution without direct reliance on the score of
825
      the updated particle.
826
827   One concurrent work (Schramm & Boularias, 2024) (BDM) may share some conceptual connec-
828   tions as Bellman Diffusion, they address different problem settings and have distinct goals. BDM
829   is grounded in standard value-based RL and aims to estimate the successor state measure (SSM).
830
      In contrast, our work focuses on distributional RL, where the goal is to model the entire return dis-
      tribution for each state, rather than just its first moment (i.e., the value). This difference reflects
831
      a fundamental distinction in the types of information each method seeks to capture. Additionally,
832
      while BDM estimates the SSM, it does not explicitly discuss how a policy can be derived from it. In
833   comparison, distributional RL, as utilized in our method, provides a direct framework for deriving
834   a feasible policy from the return distribution. This makes our approach more readily applicable to
835   practical RL tasks. At last, BDM focuses primarily on introducing the concept of SSM estimation
836   using diffusion models, but it does not include experimental validation to support its methodology.
837   In contrast, we provide a comprehensive framework with experimental results that demonstrate the
838   effectiveness of Bellman Diffusion in both RL tasks and generative modeling.

1287   D.2   D ISFAVORED F ULL T RAJECTORY S AMPLING
1288
       As mentioned in Sec. 2, a DGM that is qualified to be applied with the efficient Bellman update
1289
       needs to satisfy some linearity condition, otherwise one can only sample full state-action trajectories
1290   to train the DGM, which is too costly for many RL environments. To understand this point, suppose
1291   that there is an 1-dimensional maze with N blocks, with a robot moving from the leftmost block
1292   to the rightmost block. If one directly trains the return model with the returns computed from full
1293   trajectories, then the robot has to try to move to the final block after each action, resulting in a time
1294   complexity at least as OpN ¨ N q “ OpN 2 q for every episode. In contrast, if the return model can be
1295   trained with partial trajectories (e.g., 1 step) through the Bellman equation, then the time complexity
       would be significantly reduced (e.g., OpN 2 q). There are many RL environments where the number
