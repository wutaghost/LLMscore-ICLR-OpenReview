009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper explores the theoretical foundations of Adam, a widely used adap-
012            tive optimizer. Building on recent developments in non-convex optimization and
013            online learning, particularly the discounted-to-nonconvex conversion framework,
014            we present two aspects of results: First, we introduce clip-free FTRL, a novel
015            variant of the classical Follow-the-Regularized-Leader (FTRL) algorithm. Unlike
016            scale-free FTRL and the recently proposed ╬▓-FTRL, our clip-free variant elimi-
017            nates the need for clipping operations, aligning more closely with AdamРђЎs prac-
018
               tical implementation. This modification provides deeper theoretical insights into
               AdamРђЎs empirical success and aligns the theoretical framework with practical im-
019
               plementations. By incorporating a refined analysis, our second result establishes
020
               a theoretical guarantee for the Last Iterate Convergence (LIC) under the proposed
021            discounts-to-nonconvex conversion algorithm in LIC, which differs from the pre-
022            vious guarantee that has convergence evenly distributed in all iterations. Addition-
023            ally, we extend this result to provide the last iterate convergence guarantee for the
024            popular ╬▓-FTRL algorithm under the same framework. However, the derived last
025            iterate convergence of ╬▓-FTRL reveals a persistent fixed error, potentially suggest-
026            ing either limitations in popular online learning methods or the need for additional
027            assumptions about the objective function.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Adaptive optimizers, particularly Adam (and AdamW)(Kingma, 2014; Loshchilov, 2017), are fun-
033
      damental to the success of large-scale first-order optimization tasks, such as training large language
      models (Devlin, 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Bommasani et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023; Team
034
      et al., 2023). However, the theoretical underpinnings of AdamРђЎs performance remain elusive. While
035
      many efforts have been made to establish AdamРђЎs convergence equivalence to stochastic gradient
036   descent (SGD), Adam usually demonstrates superior performance over SGD in practical scenarios.
037   Unfortunately, most existing theoretical analyses are often not enlightening and fail to adequately
038   account for AdamРђЎs key components, such as momentum update and bias correction for the first and
039   second moments. These components are often seen as obstacles in theoretical proofs or are entirely
040   disregarded (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Understanding the design principles behind Adam
041   and explaining its performance advantages is an area in need of further exploration.
042   Recent advancements in the discounted-to-nonconvex conversion framework offer a promising av-
043   enue for understanding AdamРђЎs effectiveness. Cutkosky et al. (2023) introduced the online-to-
044   nonconvex conversion framework, which deeply bridges non-smooth non-convex optimization with
045   online learning. Building upon this foundation, subsequent works (Zhang & Cutkosky, 2024; Ahn
046   & Cutkosky, 2024; Ahn et al., 2024) introduced the discounted-to-nonconvex conversion frame-
047   work, offering new insights into the relationship between adaptive optimizers and online learners.
048   This framework holds promise in revealing AdamРђЎs underlying mechanisms and effectiveness from
049   a novel perspective.
050   The discounted-to-nonconvex framework consists of two primary components: the discounted-to-
051   nonconvex conversion algorithm and the corresponding online learning algorithm. The theoretical
052   foundation of the conversion algorithm ties the optimality condition of non-convex optimization,
053   such as the gradient norm, to the discounted regret of the associated online learner. Notably, it com-
      mits the fact that each online learner is one-to-one corresponding to a specific optimizer, implying


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that the design of an effective non-convex optimizer is tantamount to creating an online learner that
055   minimizes discounted regret.
056
057   To further motivate this framework, the pioneering work (Ahn & Cutkosky, 2024) also proposes
058
      the performance criterion, gradient adaptivity, to demonstrate AdamРђЎs performance superiority over
      SGD. For instance, the online learning method ╬▓-FTRL (Orabona & Pa╠Ђl, 2018; Zhang et al., 2024),
059
      which is closely aligned with Adam, has been shown to better adapt to problem-dependent proper-
060
      ties, i.e., offering stronger theoretical guarantees in scenarios where these properties are unknown.
061
062   In this paper, we extend the exploration of the discounted-to-nonconvex conversion framework,
063   mitigating the discrepancy between the framework and the practical practices of Adam and obtaining
064
      notable theoretical improvements. Our key contributions are summarized as follows.
065
             Рђб We develop an online learning method, clip-free FTRL, which improves from previous
066
               methods by eliminating the unrealistic clipping operation present in the previous ap-
067            proaches. To achieve clip-free, we adopt components from AdamРђЎs update and incorporate
068            them in the recently proposed ╬▓-FTRL, leading to close alignment with Adam. This re-
069            sults in achieving comparable discounted regret to ╬▓-FTRL without the need for clipping,
070            offering a more comprehensive and practical understanding of AdamРђЎs performance.
071          Рђб Recognizing the limitations of previous discounted-to-nonconvex conversion algo-
072            rithm (Ahn & Cutkosky, 2024), which relied on Exponential Moving Average (EMA) pa-
073            rameters (Polyak & Juditsky, 1992; Ruppert, 1988) and spread convergence evenly across
074            all iterations, we propose a new conversion algorithm. This algorithm establishes a theo-
075            retical guarantee that bridges discounted regret and last iterate guarantees in non-convex
076            optimization. Additionally, we extend this framework to provide a last iterate convergence
077            guarantee for the popular ╬▓-FTRL algorithm, and the results reveal the necessity of further
078            investigation along this avenue.

080   1.1   R ELATED WORK
081
082
      Significant efforts have been dedicated to understanding AdamРђЎs superior performance from two
      perspectives: convergence rate and adaptivity. Various studies have analyzed AdamРђЎs convergence
083
      behavior, demonstrating that it achieves a convergence rate comparable to SGD for convex or smooth
084
      nonconvex functions under different stochastic gradient conditions and hyper-parameter configura-
085   tions (Reddi et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2018; Alacaoglu et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
086   2022; Wang et al., 2024). However, these analyses often fail to capture the contributions of AdamРђЎs
087   core components. Moreover, it is well established that under these function assumptions, SGD al-
088   ready achieves the minimax optimal convergence rate. Beyond convergence speed, studying the
089   adaptivity of Adam over complex deep-learning environments is also a popular trend to support the
090   success of Adam. Wang et al. (2023) showed that AdaGrad, a precursor to Adam, can adapt to
091   functions satisfying the generalized smoothness condition (Zhang et al., 2019), while plain SGD
092   may converge arbitrarily slowly. Subsequent work (Li et al., 2024) extended this analysis to Adam,
093
      demonstrating its convergence under the generalized smoothness condition. Additionally, Craw-
      shaw et al. (2022) highlighted the theoretical benefits of momentum updates, a component shared
094
      by Adam, for SignSGD algorithm under the generalized smoothness condition.
095
096   Another important line of inquiry is the Last Iterate Convergence (LIC), which has garnered sub-
097   stantial attention in the literature and has been widely utilized. Most existing works focus on char-
098   acterizing the convergence behavior of SGD and SGD with Momentum (SGDM) under the convex
099
      and strongly convex assumptions (Ghadimi & Lan, 2012; Sebbouh et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2019;
      Tao et al., 2021). More recent work (Jin et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022) have extended these analyses to
100
      non-convex functions.
