009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image (T2I) models are remarkable at generating realistic images based on
012            textual descriptions. However, textual prompts are inherently underspecified: they
013            do not specify all possible attributes of the required image. This raises two key
014            questions: Do T2I models generate diverse outputs from underspecified prompts?
015            How can we automatically measure diversity? We propose GRADE: Granular
016            Attribute Diversity Evaluation, an automatic method for quantifying sample diver-
017
               sity. GRADE leverages the world knowledge embedded in large language models
               and visual question-answering systems to identify relevant concept-specific axes
018
               of diversity (e.g., “shape” and “color” for the concept “cookie”). It then estimates
019
               frequency distributions of concepts and their attributes and quantifies diversity us-
020            ing (normalized) entropy. GRADE achieves over 90% human agreement while ex-
021            hibiting weak correlation to commonly used diversity metrics. We use GRADE to
022            measure the overall diversity of 12 T2I models using 405 concept-attribute pairs,
023            revealing that all models display limited variation. Further, we find that these mod-
024            els often exhibit default behaviors, a phenomenon where the model consistently
025            generates concepts with the same attributes (e.g., 98% of the cookies are round).
026            Finally, we demonstrate that a key reason for low diversity is due to underspeci-
027            fied captions in training data. Our work proposes a modern, semantically-driven
028
               approach to measure sample diversity and highlights the stunning homogeneity in
               outputs by T2I models.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Text-to-image (T2I) models have the remarkable ability to generate realistic images based on textual
035
      descriptions. However, prompts are inherently underspecified (Hutchinson et al., 2022; Rassin et al.,
      2022), meaning they do not fully describe all attributes that appear in the resulting image. Often, we
036
      expect T2I models to produce diverse outputs that represent the full spectrum of possible attributes.
037
      For example, when generating images of “a cookie in a bakery”, we expect to see cookies with
038   different shapes, colors, and textures, among other variations. But are current T2I models capable of
039   generating diverse outputs? Evaluating diversity is inherently challenging because the set of possible
040   attributes is virtually infinite. Existing metrics, such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel
041   et al., 2017) and Precision-and-Recall (Sajjadi et al., 2018; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) are supposed
042   to measure diversity, but they are limited in their ability to capture granular forms of diversity,
043   instead, they capture feature-level similarities. These metrics also rely on a set of reference images
044   that typically reflects the training data distribution, which might not be diverse. Furthermore, such
045   set is often hard to obtain, and does not specify attributes of interest. Our desiderata from a diversity
046   metric is to be reference-free, independent of the training data distribution, and human-interpretable.
047
      We propose Granular Attribute Diversity Evaluation (GRADE), a method for measuring sample di-
048   versity in T2I models at a granular, concept-dependent manner, focusing on attributes of concepts,
049   such as the shape of a cookie or the state of an umbrella. Our approach (illustrated in Fig. 2) involves
050   using a large language model (LLM) to generate prompts that elicit diverse outputs from T2I mod-
051   els. These prompts are accompanied by questions that tailor common, specific attributes–relevant
052   axes of diversity–for each concept (e.g., “What is the shape of the cookie?” and “Is the umbrella
053   open or close?”). We use a visual question-answering (VQA) model to extract attribute values from
      images using the questions. We then use an LLM to approximate the support of the concept and

103   2      R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Most diversity measurements are distribution-based: a set of images generated by the evaluated
106   model is compared to a reference set that captures the desired diversity, typically in feature-space,
107   using a feature extractor such as Inception v3 (Szegedy et al., 2014; Salimans et al., 2016) or CLIP
      (Radford et al., 2021).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Perhaps most popular, Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) outputs a score representing both fidelity
109   and diversity and is the standard for evaluating image generating models. However, it has multiple
110   documented issues, like numerical sensitivity, data contamination, and biases (Parmar et al., 2022;
111   Bińkowski et al., 2018; Chong & Forsyth, 2020; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2022; Jayasumana et al.,
112   2024). Precision-and-Recall (Sajjadi et al., 2018) separated fidelity and diversity to two metrics.
113   Additional metrics were proposed Kynkäänniemi et al. (2019); Naeem et al. (2020); Kim et al.
114   (2023); Alaa et al. (2022), which decouple between different properties and offer more interpretable
115   methods. Crucially, all these methods rely on a set of diverse reference images, by comparing the
116   distribution of generated images to the reference set with the desired level of diversity. This can be
117
      the model’s training data, or an established dataset, like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). However,
      acquiring reference images that faithfully reflect diversity is not straightforward and often requires
118
      using a feature extractor that was trained on similar data, to capture the similarities between the
119
      distributions. These requirements make it difficult to reproduce the results of previous work and
120   maintain the integrity of the metrics as they are sensitive to data contamination, which could make
121   them favor models that produce patterns similar to those seen in their training set, regardless of
122   diversity (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2022).
123
      In addition to the significant requirement of obtaining a feature extractor and training data that match
124
      the target domain, previous metrics do not use fine-grained feature extractors, which can evaluate
125
      diversity over the semantics of images. Instead, they use ones that are trained over well-established
126   datasets. As a result, they lack the ability to distinguish between two similar concepts that are
127   different on a specific axis, like a color. For example, if we compare two nearly-identical images of
128   a bottle, with only the color of the bottle as the difference, they would consider them very similar.
129   However, our metric would capture such difference, as we show in Appendix C.
130
      Similar to GRADE, Vendi Score (VS) (Friedman & Dieng, 2022; Pasarkar & Dieng, 2023) is a ref-
131
      erence-free metric, defined as the entropy of the eigenvalues of a user-provided similarity metric.
132
      However, VS is sensitive to the choice of similarity function and like previous approaches, it is not
133
      fine-grained and interpretable in natural text.

204   3.2   M ETHOD
205
206   GRADE measures model diversity in four steps, as shown in Fig. 2. First, an LLM generates
207   prompts, attributes (as questions), and attribute values. Then, it estimates the distribution over con-
208   cepts and attributes by generating images with the evaluated T2I model and answering questions via
209
      a VQA model. Finally, we quantify the diversity of the distribution using entropy. We now describe
      each of these steps, and then evaluate each one individually (Section 4).
210
211   (a) Generating images of the concept c. To thoroughly assess the diversity of attribute values
212   that T2I models associate with a concept c, GRADE generates two types of prompts. Common
213   prompts depict the concept in familiar and frequently encountered settings (e.g., “a cookie dur-
214   ing Christmas festivities”). They are likely to produce images reflecting common attribute asso-
215   ciations learned from the training data (e.g., a tree-shaped cookie). Uncommon prompts place the
      concept in rare contexts (e.g., “a cookie in a volcano crater”). Such prompts are designed to push


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      the model beyond typical scenarios, potentially revealing attribute associations that are not solely
217   driven by common context cues. By analyzing the attribute values generated from both common and
218   uncommon prompts, we can identify which attributes are consistently associated with the concept
219   across different contexts. This consistency indicates that certain attributes are inherently linked to
220   the concept in the model’s internal representation, rather than being influenced mainly by the content
221   of the prompts. For instance, if the model frequently generates round cookies in both common and
222   uncommon contexts, it suggests that “roundness” is a fundamental attribute of “cookie” within the
223   model’s learned knowledge.
224   (b) Generating attributes and their values. After generating prompts and images, we look for
225   attributes relevant for a concept c. GRADE uses an LLM to first outline the concept’s attributes and
226   then generate questions that probe for their values. For example, upon receiving the input “cookie”,
227   the LLM noted that cookies can be made in different shapes, and proposed the question “what is
228   the shape of the cookie?”. Next, we generate attribute values (a support), which we use in the next
229   step to map VQA outputs to attribute values. We pair prompts with questions, and for each pair, we
230   instruct an LLM to propose attribute values based on the prompt (e.g., what could be the answer to
231
      “what is the shape of the cookie?” for an image generated by the prompt “a cookie in a bakery”?).
      We then use an LLM to unify the attribute values to a set without semantic repetitions (e.g., “round”
232                                                               shape
233
      and “circle” will be unified to a single value in the set Ṽcookie . Table 1 shows a sample of concepts,
      questions, and attribute values.
234
235   (c) Counting occurrences of attribute values in images. We pair all images and questions associ-
236   ated with the same concept and feed it to a VQA model, which outputs answers in natural language.
237   Then, we provide an LLM the answers and the attribute values Ṽca , and instruct it to match the an-
238   swer to the closest matching attribute value (e.g., “The cake is round” will be mapped to the “round”
239   value). To address edge cases, where the T2I model fails to depict the concept in the image or that
240
      the answer is not covered by the support, we add “none of the above” as an additional attribute value
      and discard all answers that are matched to it. This step results with frequency distributions which
241
      we normalize to cumulatively sum to 1.
242
243   (d) Quantifying distributions. GRADE outputs probability distributions. Every concept and
244   attribute pair results in a multi-prompt distribution, and every prompt and attribute pair results in
245   a single prompt distribution. We quantify the diversity of these distributions using entropy as our
246
      score. While not a direct focus in this work, a diversity score spanning multiple attributes of a
      concept can be achieved simply by averaging the entropy values from the multi-prompt (or single
247
      prompt) distributions of a concept.
248
249   Implementation details. In step (a) we generate three prompts from each type (six in total) and 100
250   images per prompt. In step (b) we generate an average of four questions (which represent attributes)
251   per concept. We use GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024) (gpt-4o-2024-08-06) in all steps, with max
252
      tokens set to 1, 000, and temperature set to 0. While we present the question answering and attribute
      value mapping as separate steps, we perform them in one step, using the Structured Outputs feature
253
      (OpenAI, 2024). All prompts are detailed in Appendix H.
254
255   The cost of estimating a multi-prompt distribution is approximately $0.75, and a single prompt
256   distribution is $0.12, achieved through batch inference. In our experience, wait time is several
257   minutes. The Images were generated using a single A100-80GB.

462
              Our analysis consists of two steps: first, we test if the models replicate the multi-prompt distributions
463
              based on underspecified caption-image pairs in the training data, by comparing them to multi-
464
              prompt distributions based on generated images with the same captions; second, we test if this
465           replication extends to images generated with underspecified prompts not found in the training set.
466
467           Replicating training data diversity. We aim to assess whether models reproduce the underspec-
468           ified diversity of their training data. For each model, we sample 50 triplets of concepts (e.g.,
469           “cookie”), attributes (e.g., “shape”), and attribute values, and measure their diversity distribution
470           using GRADE. Then, using the same concepts and attributes we sample captions from the corre-
471           sponding LAION dataset that explicitly mention the concept but not the attribute. To ensure the
472           captions meet our criteria, we apply two filtering conditions: (1) the concept must be mentioned as
473           an object and not as a modifier (e.g., “cookie” but not “cookie cutter”), and (2) the caption must not
474           mention or imply the attribute of interest (e.g., “a classic chocolate chip cookie” implies the cookie
475
              is round). After filtering, we remain with 150 captions per concept and compute their distributions
              (full details are presented in Appendix F). We generate 20 images per caption, resulting in 3,000
476
              images per concept and then estimate their distribution. Finally, we compute the TVD between
477
              these distributions and evaluate how closely the models’ outputs mirror the diversity present in their
478           corresponding, underspecified training data.

484           prompts, like the filtered captions, mention the concept without specifying the attribute (e.g., “a
485           cookie in a bakery”). By calculating the TVD between these distributions, we assess whether models
              have generalize the learned concept-attribute associations to new, underspecified prompts.

497   6.1     R ESULTS
498
499   The diversity in the generated images closely corresponds to the diversity in the training data (see
500   Table 5). LAION exhibits moderate diversity, with dataset entropy values of 0.64 for LAION-2B
501   and 0.65 for LAION-5B. The models’ entropy values when using LAION captions as prompts are
502   comparable, ranging from 0.62 to 0.68. High PCC, ranging from 0.73 to 0.88, indicate a strong
503   correlation between the multi-prompt distributions of the generated images and those in the training
504
      data. The low TVD values (0.10 to 0.13) further suggest that the distributions are similar.
505   When using the generated prompts instead of LAION captions, we observe a slight decrease in PCC
506   (ranging from 0.61 to 0.72) and a modest increase in TVD (ranging from 0.17 to 0.18), but the
507   overall trend remains consistent. This indicates that the models continue to reflect the training data’s
508   multi-prompt distributions even when presented with new, underspecified prompts.
509   These findings support our hypothesis that the low diversity observed in the generated images is
510   rooted in the underspecified training captions. Models learn to associate concepts with their most
511   common attribute values since those are unspecified in captions but are present in the corresponding
512   images, leading to a lack of diversity in the outputs that models learn to mimic.

      7     L IMITATIONS
515
516
      There are three main limitations to GRADE. First, because GRADE considers specific attributes
517
      of specific concepts, the diversity scores do not reflect the diversity of concepts and attributes that
518
      were not measured. Second, GRADE relies on an underlying LLM and VQA, which have unknown
519
      biases that influence the suggestions by the LLM and affect the quality of information extracted by
520
      the VQA. Finally, while our definition of diversity is clearly defined, human perception of diversity
521
      is an open question, and we do not know if it aligns with GRADE.

524   8     C ONCLUSION
525
526   We introduce GRADE, an automated fine-grained evaluation method for measuring sample diversity
527   in T2I models based on concepts and their attributes. By estimating the distribution of attributes in
528   generated images for a given concept and computing entropy, GRADE provides a diversity score
529   that can be used to interpret model behavior. Unlike traditional diversity metrics, GRADE does not
530   rely on reference images. Our experiments demonstrate that humans GRADE is accurate, while at
531
      the same time showing weak correlation with traditional metrics like FID and Recall. We conduct
      a comprehensive analysis of 12 state-of-the-art T2I models and uncovered a prevalent limitation:
532
      these models default to generating images with the same attributes for a concept on anywhere from
533
      78% to 90% of the concepts we tested, with an increasing trend as models scale in size and improve
534   in prompt adherence, highlighting a limited ability to capture the rich diversity inherent in visual
535   concepts. We further hypothesize that diversity in generation is linked to diversity in the training
536   data, and that under-specification encourages default behavior. Future work could explore methods
537   to enrich training data, incorporate diversity-promoting mechanisms during model training, and
538   extend GRADE to evaluate relationships between different attributes of a concept or the relationship
539   between multiple concepts in a scene. We hope our work will inspire more nuanced evaluations and
      drive advancements in generating diverse visual content from textual descriptions.

       D.3    D ISCUSSION OF RESULTS
1152
1153   Our findings reinforce the observations made in the main text regarding the interplay between model
1154   scale, diversity, and prompt adherence:
1155
1156
       Inverse-scaling law. There is a negative correlation between diversity and model size, suggesting
       that increasing model parameters leads to decreased diversity. This phenomenon is most apparent
1157
       within each model family and aligns with the concept of an inverse-scaling law.
1158
1159   Fidelity-diversity trade-off. The negative correlation between diversity and prompt adherence indi-
1160   cates a trade-off between a model’s ability to generate images that match the prompt and the diversity
1161   of its outputs. This is consistent with previous findings on fidelity-diversity trade-offs (Dhariwal &
1162
       Nichol, 2021; Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019), where improving a model’s prompt-specific generation
       may reduce the overall diversity of its outputs.

1300   Filtering Captions from LAION. We aimed to measure the diversity of training images whose
1301   captions satisfy two conditions: (1) they mention the concept as an object and not as a modifier
1302   (e.g., “cookie” but not “cookie cutter”), and (2) the caption must not mention or imply the attribute
1303   of interest (e.g., “a classic chocolate chip cookie” implies the cookie is round). We queried LAION
1304
       using WIMBD (Elazar et al., 2024) and sampled 500 captions for each concept.
1305   To efficiently filter the captions, we utilized GPT-4o in a few-shot setup. For each caption, we
1306   provided the caption text, the concept (e.g., “cookie”), and the question regarding the attribute of
1307   interest (e.g., “what is the shape of the cookie?”). We instructed GPT-4o to analyze each caption
1308   and determine whether it satisfies both filtering conditions. The model was prompted to reply with
1309   “yes” if both conditions are met and “no” otherwise.
1310   We then downloaded the images associated with the captions that GPT-4o classified as satisfying
1311   both conditions. To ensure the reliability of our filtering method, we conducted a human evaluation,
1312   achieving an F1 score of 90.3%. Detailed methodology and results of the human evaluation are
1313   provided in Appendix G.
