009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-training is prevalent in deep learning for vision and text data, acquiring knowl-
012            edge from other datasets to improve the downstream tasks. However, when it comes
013            to tabular data, the inherent heterogeneity in the attribute and label spaces among
014            datasets makes it hard to learn shareable knowledge and encode it in a model. We
015            propose Tabular data Pre-Training via Meta-representation (TAB PTM), aiming to
016            pre-train a general tabular model over a set of heterogeneous datasets. The key is
017            to embed data instances from any dataset into a common feature space, in which
018
               an instance is represented by its distance to a fixed number of nearest neighbors
               and their labels. Such a meta-representation standardizes heterogeneous tasks into
019
               homogeneous local prediction problems, enabling training a model to infer the label
020
               (or the score to each possible label) of an input instance based on its neighborhood
021            information. As such, the pre-trained TAB PTM can be directly applied to new
022            datasets without further fine-tuning, regardless of their diverse attributes and la-
023            bels. Extensive experiments on 72 tabular datasets validate TabPTM’s effectiveness
024            (with and without fine-tuning) in both tabular classification and regression tasks.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Pre-training has been the driving force for recent advances in artificial intelligence (Devlin et al.,
030   2019; Liu et al., 2019; Kirillov et al., 2023; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). Foundation models in vision
031   and language all benefit from pre-training, using abundant images and documents collected from
032   multiple sources (Zhou et al., 2023a; Oquab et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021). Once trained, these
033
      models demonstrate remarkable generalizability to new tasks, even without fine-tuning. Such a
      success, however, has yet to be witnessed in tabular data, even though the data format is ubiquitous
034
      in many real-world applications such as financial prediction (Cao & Tay, 2001), recommendation
035
      system (Richardson et al., 2007), and healthcare (Ogunleye & Wang, 2020).
036
037   One fundamental challenge is the inherent heterogeneity in tabular data from different sources and
038   tasks. A tabular dataset is typically represented by a matrix, with rows corresponding to instances and
039
      columns to attributes (features) (Borisov et al., 2022). Beyond this homogeneity, tabular datasets can
      be quite different in their dimensionality (i.e., numbers of columns) and the semantic meaning of each
040
      dimension, even if they are for the same application. For example, different healthcare datasets can
041
      encode different granularities and aspects of patient information; even at the same feature entry (e.g.,
042   the d-th column), the meaning is not necessarily the same (e.g., “age” or “height”). This is drastically
043   different from vision and text data (of the same language), in which different data sources typically
044   share the same “vocabulary” (e.g., pixel, patch, or sub-word definition) and similar relationships
045   between vocabulary “elements” (e.g., nearby pixels usually have similar colors). The lack of shared
046   vocabulary and relationships in tabular data hampers the joint training of a model on multiple datasets,
047   let alone the direct application of the pre-trained model to new downstream tasks.
048
               We thus hypothesize, to pre-train a useful model for tabular data, one must first
049
               answer the question: What is the shareable vocabulary across datasets, and what
050            transferable knowledge could be learned from these datasets?
051
052   Some recent work addresses this by taking advantage of the semantic meanings of columns (i.e.,
053   attributes). By transforming a data instance into the textual form (e.g., “Age is 20, height is 170, ...”),
      one could tackle tabular data using language models (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Wang & Sun, 2022;

078   Liu et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2023). When the semantic meanings are inaccessible or ill-defined —
079   which is quite common in the real world, such as healthcare or measurement data — some approaches
080   attempt to learn dataset-specific token representation for each attribute to transform different datasets
081
      into a shareable feature space (Iwata & Kumagai, 2020; Kumagai et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b;
      Wydmanski et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). However, for new downstream tasks, these approaches
082
      must first re-train task-specific token representations before applying the pre-trained model.
083
084   Seeing the limitations of this line of approach, in this paper, we explore a novel direction to pre-train a
085   general model for tabular data. We take inspiration from traditional work in dimensionality reduction
086   and manifold learning and recent work in comparing different neural network representations to
087
      answer the question about shareable vocabulary across heterogeneous tabular data. In multidimen-
      sional scaling, regardless of what the original tabular datasets encode, as long as they lead to the same
088
      pairwise distance matrix between instances, they can be embedded into the same, low-dimensional
089
      tabular matrix (Davison & Sireci, 2000; Carroll & Arabie, 1998; Torgerson, 1952). Likewise, in
090   manifold learning, a high-dimensional dataset can be re-represented by a lower-dimensional one if
091   they encode the same neighborhood relationship (Weinberger et al., 2004; Yan et al., 2006; Van der
092   Maaten & Hinton, 2008; Hinton & Roweis, 2002). Such cross-instance relationships are also used in
093   recent work to analyze learned deep representations: two pre-trained neural networks encode similar
094   knowledge if their features lead to similar affinity matrices among data instances, even if they are
095   built upon different architectures (Kornblith et al., 2019; Huh et al., 2024). In short, the relationships
096   among instances within a dataset could effectively serve as the shareable vocabulary across different
097   tabular datasets, regardless of their dimensionality and semantic meanings.
098   What transferable knowledge can we learn on top of the shareable vocabulary, i.e., the relationship
099   among instances? We draw inspiration from the legendary nearest-neighbor based machine learning
100   algorithms (Boiman et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2019; Sánchez et al., 1997; Chaudhuri, 1996; Zhang
101   et al., 2006; Chao et al., 2013). The key insight in these approaches is that an instance’s relationship
102   to a particular label could be inferred from its relationship to the nearest neighbors (of that label).
103   Putting things together, we propose Tabular data Pre-Training via Meta-representation (TAB PTM), a
104   general tabular model and pre-training approach over heterogeneous tabular datasets. TAB PTM starts
105   with representing a tabular data instance (i.e., a row) by the local context of its nearest neighbors in
106   (the training set of) the dataset. In this paper, we meta-represent each instance by a fixed-dimensional
107   vector encoding the distances to a fixed number of nearest neighbors (ordered by distances) and their
      labels, while other methods may apply. Such a “meta-representation” standardizes heterogeneous

      datasets, rendering instances into a unifying form of the same dimensionality and semantic meaning,
109   and transforms heterogeneous prediction tasks into homogeneous local prediction tasks. TAB PTM
110   then trains a joint neural network on the meta-representations of a large number of datasets to
111   learn to map the meta-representation — the neighborhood information — of each instance to the
112   ground-truth label. Such an ability to predict the label of a given instance in the local context can
113   then be transferred to new downstream tasks. Specifically, the pre-trained model by TAB PTM can be
114   directly applied to a downstream tabular dataset (represented by the meta-representation) without
115   further fine-tuning, albeit that fine-tuning could further boost the performance. (See Figure 1 for a
116   comparison of TAB PTM with other existing strategies that train and deploy tabular models.)
117   We extensively validate TAB PTM on 72 tabular datasets with various scenarios. The results demon-
118   strate TAB PTM’s effectiveness in acquiring shareable knowledge over datasets to achieve promising
119   generalizability on new classification and regression tasks. Our contributions are two-folded:
120   • We utilize meta-representations to reduce attribute heterogeneity and enable the pre-training of a
121     general model over tabular datasets.
122   • Based on the meta-representations, the pre-trained TAB PTM can directly generalize to new tabular
123     datasets without further training, and achieve state-of-the-art accuracy in many datasets after
124     fine-tuning, as evidenced by extensive experiments on both classification and regression tasks.
125
126   Remark. Our TAB PTM is conceptually related to TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023), which aims
127   to learn a general in-context model for tabular classification datasets. Given a downstream dataset,
128   TabPFN inputs the (whole) training set and each test instance into the pre-trained model, using
129   the learned interactions between the test instances and the training ones to make predictions. Our
130   TAB PTM is particularly advantageous in three aspects. First, unlike TabPFN, which generates
131
      in-context tasks in every training step and feeds all the corresponding instances into the model at once,
      TAB PTM pre-meta-represents each instance, enabling smaller batch sizes in training (hence more
132
      training-efficient). Second, a similar property applies to testing: TAB PTM can pre-meta-represent
133
      every test instance and input only the meta-representations of the test instances into the model, hence
134   more testing-efficient. Third, putting these two points together, Hollmann et al. (2023) claimed that
135   their approach applies only to small datasets (with fewer than a thousand instances), while TAB PTM
136   can be trained on and applied to much larger tabular datasets.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
140
141   This section outlines key methods related to our paper, with a more detailed discussion available in
142
      the appendix. Tabular data is one of the most common data forms in many fields (Richardson et al.,
      2007; Vanschoren et al., 2014; Hamidieh, 2018). Recent efforts have extended deep neural network
143
      successes to the tabular domain (Cheng et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017; Gorishniy et al., 2021; Huang
144
      et al., 2020; Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Grinsztajn et al., 2022), and explore the feasibility of
145   general discriminative models that can adapt to a broad array of downstream datasets. The potential
146   for in-distribution generalization of pre-trained tabular models has been demonstrated in contexts
147   such as multi-task learning (Argyriou et al., 2006; Zhang & Yang, 2022; Rubachev et al., 2022; Luetto
148   et al., 2023) and self-supervised learning (Ucar et al., 2021; Bahri et al., 2022), where data from
149   various sources are in a consistent format. Some recent approaches utilize the deep neural network
150   to pre-train a more generalizable tabular model, taking the difference in attributes and labels into
151   account. The main difficulty lies in the ambiguity of shareable vocabulary and transferable knowledge
152   across datasets. One representative kind of approach addresses this by harnessing the semantic
153
      meanings of column names (i.e., attribute names) to transform instances into text, thereby leveraging
      large language models to enhance classification capabilities across diverse datasets (Liu et al., 2022a;
154
      Hegselmann et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023). Concurrently, other researchers
155
      have focused on learning shared components, such as attribute-agnostic transformations, to provide
156   effective initial weights for models adapting to new tasks (Iwata & Kumagai, 2020; Liu et al., 2022b;
157   Zhang et al., 2023b; Shen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). Hollmann et al. (2023) addressed dataset
158   heterogeneity by padding attributes across datasets into the same size and utilizing the contextual
159   learning capabilities of transformers for classification tasks. Our proposed TAB PTM standardizes
160   heterogeneous datasets into a uniform format using meta-representation. This enables the effective
161   pre-training of a model that can be directly applied to or minimally fine-tuned for downstream datasets
      without necessitating additional parameters.

      Learning with a single tabular dataset. We denote a tabular classification dataset as D =
165   {(xi , yi )}N
                  i=1 which has N examples (rows in the table) and C classes. The label yi ∈ {1, . . . , C}
166   and its one-hot form is yi ∈ {0, 1}C . The same discussion could be extended to the regression
167   scenario where the label yi ∈ R. Each instance xi ∈ X is depicted by d attributes (columns).1 The
168   goal is to estimate the posterior probability given an instance, i.e., Pr(yi | xi , D), often implemented
169   by a model f mapping from X to the label set Y. The generalization ability of f is measured by the
170   prediction accuracy on an unseen instance sampled from the same distribution as D.
171
      Pre-training across multiple tabular datasets. Tabular datasets could be collected from various
172   sources. Assume there are T datasets D = {D1 , . . . , DT }, in which the attribute and label spaces of
173   the t-th dataset are denoted by Xt and Yt . Due to the inherent heterogeneity across datasets, we define
174   the number of instances, attributes (i.e., the dimension of an instance), and classes in dataset Dt as
175   Nt , dt , and Ct , respectively. Unlike multi-task or multi-view learning, which assumes one of Xt and
176   Yt is homogeneous (i.e., identical) across all datasets, we consider heterogeneous datasets where
177   the meanings of attributes and classes vary from one dataset to another. Regarding pre-training a
178   model f over T heterogeneous datasets, we expect f to deal with different attribute and label sets and
179   generalize its discerning ability to an unseen downstream task Du . There are two primary challenges
180
      to pre-train f . The first is to determine the shareable “vocabulary” across datasets so that f can
      learn from D and be applied to any heterogeneous dataset. The second is to identify and encode the
181
      “transferable knowledge” from D into f , enhancing the generalizability of f such that it can improve
182
      an unseen downstream task.
183
184   There are two types of generalization. The first is the direct generalization, which means the learned
185   f is able to predict an unseen instance xu∗ in task Du directly without additional training:
186                                               ŷ∗u = f (xu∗ | Du ) ,                                         (1)
187   where the label of xu∗ could be inferred via the shareable knowledge learned on the pre-training
188   datasets. In addition, the learned f can act as the initialization to be updated by several steps of
189   gradient descent, minimizing the empirical risk over the target dataset Du . This fine-tuning strategy
190   learns the task-specific property by fitting to Du . Due to the heterogeneity of datasets, additional
191   parameters such as the feature tokenizer are introduced to adapt the model on downstream tasks (Zhu
192   et al., 2023), which requires special tuning methods. Our goal is to develop a versatile general model
193   f that can be either directly applied to a target task or fine-tuned without introducing new parameters.

402   The transformation TΘ , pre-trained across T datasets, links the meta-representation to the final
403   score. The loss ℓ(·, ·) measures the discrepancy between a prediction and the label, and we set
404   it as cross-entropy for classification and mean square error for regression. Given a downstream
405   dataset Du , we first obtain the meta-representation for each instance. Then the learned TΘ could
406   be applied directly without further training, or acts as a good initialization for fine-tuning without
407   additional parameters. Appendix C provides more details of TAB PTM, including the pre-training
408   and deployment workflows of TAB PTM in Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2, respectively.
409   In summary, we treat the relationships among instances as a form of shareable vocabulary. Meta-
410   representation leverages these relationships between an instance and its nearest neighbors, transform-
411   ing the tabular prediction task into inferring an instance’s label based on its relationship to neighbors
412   of that label. TAB PTM not only standardizes heterogeneous datasets but also facilitates the learning
413   of transferable knowledge.

      of a given dataset, which is denoted by XGBoostMR . Then, we also train the top-layer MLP in

526
      proposed meta-representation helps both classification and regression. By comparing TAB PTM and
527   TAB PTMS , we find TAB PTM achieves better performance, which indicates the pre-trained top-layer
528   MLP indeed encodes shareable knowledge that helps the downstream tasks. The superiority of
529   TAB PTM over XGBoostMR further validates its strong generalization ability. The additional ablation
530   studies discussed in subsection E.5 further validate that the pre-trained model successfully encodes
531   shareable knowledge, significantly enhancing downstream tabular prediction capabilities.

533   6           C ONCLUSION
534
535   Considering the large amount of heterogeneous tabular datasets in many machine learning fields,
536   we explore a way to pre-train a general model and extend its classification or regression ability to
537   downstream datasets. We address the primary challenge of disparate attribute and label spaces across
538   datasets via meta-representation. Our pre-trained TabPTM can be directly applied to unseen datasets
539   or be fine-tuned without modifying the architecture. The model achieves competitive performance in
      various scenarios, which validates its effectiveness.

875   A PPENDIX A         A DDITIONAL R ELATED M ETHODS
876
877   Learning with tabular data. Tabular data is one of the most common data forms in many
878   fields (Richardson et al., 2007; Vanschoren et al., 2014; Hamidieh, 2018), and a lot of classical
879   machine learning methods have been developed for tabular data, such as XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin,
880
      2016), LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017), and CatBoost (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018). Recently, researchers
      have tried to extend the success of deep neural networks such as multi-layer perceptron (Gorishniy
881
      et al., 2021), Transformer (Huang et al., 2020), and diffusion models (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) from
882
      visual and textual domains to the tabular fields (Borisov et al., 2022). Attribute embeddings (Song
883   et al., 2019) and deep architectures have been designed (Guo et al., 2017; Katzir et al., 2021; Chen
884   et al., 2023b) for tabular data, and some simple baselines can achieve competitive results as the classi-
885   cal methods after carefully tuned (Kadra et al., 2021; Jeffares et al., 2023). Deep tabular models have
886   the flexibility for various scenarios and can be incorporated well with the classical methods (Cheng
887   et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017; Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Grinsztajn et al., 2022).
888
      Reuse a heterogeneous tabular model. Instead of training a tabular model from scratch on a new
889   task, reusing a pre-trained model from a related task becomes a useful choice, especially when
890   efficiency is emphasized (Tommasi et al., 2014; Kuzborskij & Orabona, 2017; Aghbalou & Staerman,
891   2023). In addition to the distribution shift between the pre-trained and the target tasks, the changes in
892   their attribute spaces as well as the label spaces make the transfer of a tabular model challenging (Hou
893   & Zhou, 2018; Ye et al., 2021). Tabular model reuse across heterogeneous datasets usually relies on
894   some assumptions, e.g., the existence of a set of overlapped attributes between two datasets (Hou
895   et al., 2022; Levin et al., 2023; Onishi et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b) and the column meanings (the
896   textual names of all attributes) (Wang & Sun, 2022).
897   Learning with multiple tabular datasets. One more step to take advantage of the ability of deep
898   neural networks on tabular fields is to pre-train a discriminative model on a large number of tabular
899   datasets and extend its ability to downstream tasks. The in-distribution generalization ability of a
900   pre-trained tabular model has been validated in multi-task learning (Argyriou et al., 2006; Zhang
901   & Yang, 2022; Rubachev et al., 2022; Luetto et al., 2023) and self-supervised learning (Ucar et al.,
902   2021; Bahri et al., 2022), where all datasets are collected in a homogeneous form. In multi-view
903
      learning, a model is required to capture the consistent nature among heterogeneous views, but those
      multiple views of an instance are paired and share the same label space (Xu et al., 2013). Some recent
904
      approaches utilize the deep neural network to pre-train a more generalizable tabular model, taking
905
      the difference in attributes and labels into account. One representative kind of approach assumes the
906   existence of attribute names along with a dataset so that each instance could be transformed into a
907   text, then a large language model could be applied to generalize the classification ability (Liu et al.,
908   2022a; Hegselmann et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023). Another thread of method
909   learns shared components such as attribute-agnostic transformation across datasets, which provides a
910   good model initialization for partial parameters given a downstream task (Iwata & Kumagai, 2020;
911   Liu et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023b; Shen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). We propose TabPTM
912   to transform all datasets into a uniform form with meta-representation to enable the pre-training.
913   Then, the pre-trained model could be applied directly to a downstream dataset, or fine-tuned without
914
      introducing additional parameters.
915   Meta-Representation. The notion of meta-representation has been previously leveraged in multi-
916   label learning to express the relationship between an instance and a specific label, which facilitates
917   decoupling the label correlations (Yang & Gopal, 2012; Zhang & Wu, 2015). The meta-representation
      is also used together with the raw tabular features to assist text classification (Canuto et al., 2014;


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      2018). In this paper, we provide a more general form of meta-representation, which contains both
919   the distance values as well as the label information, working in both classification and regression
920   tasks. Moreover, we employ meta-representation as a pivotal tool to construct a pre-trained model
921   that can effectively operate across multiple heterogeneous tabular datasets. We also emphasize the
922   metric-based variant and the strategies to deal with few-shot scenarios when a deep neural network is
923   pre-trained over the meta-representations.

1212   Possible explanations of the better regression results. The a bit improvement of regression results
1213   to the classification ones may result from the form of meta-representation and the sharing of prediction
1214   experience in TAB PTM.
1215   Regression tasks may benefit more from the local context. The core idea of meta-representation is to
1216   make predictions in a local context (based on the neighborhood of an instance). Then, the model’s
1217   ability depends on the “locality” of the true hypothesis, i.e., whether the decision function of an
1218   instance depends on its neighborhood or not. Since we may provide estimates of the regression
1219   function or conditional expectation by specifying the nature of the local neighborhood Ormoneit
1220   & Hastie (1999); Hastie et al. (2009); Kpotufe & Garg (2013), it is more probable to predict the
1221
       continuous label of a center instance based on the weighted labels of its neighbors. Therefore, making
       predictions via meta-representation may help regression tasks.
1222
1223   More shareable knowledge in regression. As depicted in Figure 3, the strategy for inferring an
1224   instance’s label from the distribution of distances to its neighbors is applicable across heterogeneous
1225   tabular tasks. For classification tasks, if an instance resides within a high-density region of a class
1226   (akin to being near the class center), the majority of values in the meta-representation would typically
1227
       be small, indicating close proximity to neighboring instances of that class. Conversely, if only a
       few values in the meta-representation are small, while most are large, it indicates that the instance is
1228
       likely located at the boundary among classes. Such a kind of discerning strategy is more shareable in
1229
       regression tasks, which works in a way that determines the label via the weighted combination of its
1230   neighbors. Therefore, using a pre-trained model (the top-layer MLP in TAB PTM) can be generalized
1231   to downstream tasks.
1232
1233
       Rich label information in regression. In addition to the distance values between a given instance
       and its nearest neighbors, we append the labels of the neighbors into the meta-representation. The
1234
       labels in regression tasks are continuous, and even if two instances are close to each other, they
1235
       may have different label values, which introduces rich information. In contrast, since the labels in
1236   classification tasks are discrete, two close instances may have the same label and only differ in their
1237   distance values in meta-representation. In summary, the labels in regression tasks may introduce
1238   richer supervision. We validate this assumption in the previous experiments. We use the prediction of
1239   a well-trained XGBoost as the label of an instance in meta-representation, which transforms the labels
1240   in classification tasks into continuous ones. The experiments validate that the TAB PTM equipped
1241   with such continuous labels outperforms the original one, which explains the different results in
       regression and classification to some extent.
