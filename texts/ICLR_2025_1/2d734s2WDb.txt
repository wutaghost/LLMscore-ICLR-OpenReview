009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present VIBeID, a dataset and benchmark designed for advancing non-invasive
012            human gait recognition using structural vibration. Structural vibrations, produced
013            by the rhythmic impact of the toe and heel on the ground, are distinct and can
014            be used as a privacy-preserving and non-cooperative soft-biometric modality. We
015            curated the largest dataset VIBeID consists of footfall generated structural vibra-
016            tions of 100 subjects. Existing datasets in this field typically include around ten
017            subjects and lack comprehensive exploration of domain adaptation. To thoroughly
018
               explore the domain adaptation aspect of this biometric approach, we recorded
               vibration data on three distinct floor types (wooden, carpet, and cement) and at
019
               three distances from the geophone sensor (1.5 m, 2.5 m, and 4.0 m), involving 40
020
               and 30 subjects, respectively. Additionally, we benchmarked our dataset against
021            video recordings from 15 individuals in an outdoor setting. Beyond providing 88
022            hours of raw vibration data, VIBeID establishes a comprehensive benchmark for
023            a) person identification: where the aim is to recognize individuals through their
024            unique structural vibrations, b) domain adaptation: assessing model performance
025            across different walking surfaces and sensor positions, and c) multi-modal com-
026            parison: comparing vibration-based and vision-based identification methods. Our
027            experiments, using both machine learning and deep learning approaches, establish
028            a baseline for future research in this field, and introduce a large-scale dataset for
029
               the broader machine learning community. 1

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Structural vibration-based person identification is an emerging topic in the field of soft biometrics (Pan
034   et al., 2017; Anchal et al., 2020). As humans walk, our bodies exert an impact force on the ground,
035   generating vibrations that propagate through the structure. These structural vibrations are unique to
036   each individual as they depend on various factors such as height, weight, gait strides, stride length
037   along with structural properties, and background noises (Succi et al., 2000). Our technique uses these
038   vibrations to identify individuals. This paper introduces VIBeID, a novel dataset containing structural
039   vibration signals recorded by geophone sensors from human walking. VIBeID is designed to address
040   three key questions: a) Is it possible to accurately identify individuals from a large population based
041   on their unique structural vibration patterns?, b) Can humans be identified at varying distances from
042
      the sensor and on different floor types using the same model?, and c) How does the performance
      compare to established modalities like camera?
043
044   Early biometric research was primarily focused on physiological traits, also known as “hard bio-
045   metrics”, such as fingerprints, iris scans, and facial recognition (Jain et al., 2007; Dantcheva et al.,
046   2015). While “hard biometrics” offer uniqueness, they can be inflexible in deployment scenarios,
047   where non-intrusive or remote identification methods performs better (Zewail et al., 2004). “Soft
048
      biometrics” like gait, voice, and structural vibration, prioritizes convenience over uniqueness, have
      gained traction to address this limitation. (Jain et al., 2004), has defined the ideal characteristics for
049
      "soft biometrics" as, a) inexpensive computation, b) usability from a distance, and c) functionality
050
      with uncooperative subjects. Our proposed system aligns perfectly with these criterias. Structural
051   vibrations offer many advantages over existing methods, such as being low-cost, non-invasive, less

094   2.1   R ELATED W ORK ON P ERSON I DENTIFICATION USING S TRUCTURAL V IBRATION
095
096   As shown in Table 1, early research focused on establishing proof-of-concept using small datasets,
097   typically involving around ten participants (Pan et al., 2017; Anchal et al., 2020; Dong & Noh,
098   2023; Mirshekari et al., 2018; Chakraborty & Kar, 2023; Xu et al., 2024). These studies aimed
099
      to demonstrate that unique characteristics of an individual’s walking pattern can be recorded and
      classified through the vibrations transmitted to the supporting structure. However, as highlighted
100
      by (Mirshekari et al., 2018), data scarcity is a serious bottleneck, where extensive experiments with
101
      humans have not been conducted. Another aspect of these early investigations is the emphasis on
102   mathematical modeling of the vibration signals, which prioritized developing models that capture the
103   physical dynamics of the system rather than focusing on robust statistical analysis of the acquired
104   data (Succi et al., 2000; Mirshekari et al., 2018). Additionally, previous datasets were highly
105   supervised and tailored to singular use cases (training and testing models on data from the same
106   underlying structure), limiting their applicability to real-world scenarios. We adopted a comprehensive
107   approach to address this limitation by creating a large dataset for person identification and task-specific
      datasets. This strategy enhances the real-life applicability of our research.

128   2.2   R ELATED W ORK ON P ERSON I DENTIFICATION OTHER THAN S TRUCTURAL V IBRATION
129
130   Human movement (gait), the unique way individuals walk, is a valuable biometric for identification
131   using cameras, particularly over long distances. Significant research has been conducted to capture
132   and analyze human gait patterns. Previous studies have concentrated on various features extracted
133   from walking videos, such as Gait Energy Image (Han & Bhanu, 2005), Gait Entropy Image (Bashir
134
      et al., 2009), and Gait Flow Image (Lam et al., 2011). As shown in Table 1, early research was
      conducted using limited datasets of twenty people (Yu et al., 2006). Recent work, however, has
135
      shifted towards large-scale datasets, incorporating both indoor and outdoor walking scenarios from
136
      various perspectives (Song et al., 2022). Our dataset aims to pave the way for further exploration in
137   structural vibration analysis, similar to the advancements seen in video-based research. Despite the
138   advantages of traditional gait-based video surveillance systems, it face limitations due to low-light
139   conditions, restricted line-of-sight, adverse weather conditions, and extensive data processing (Song
140   et al., 2022). A notable drawback of vision-based systems is the privacy concerns and user discomfort
141   associated with continuous monitoring. Structural vibration analysis offers a promising alternative
142   that addresses these issues. LiDAR offers a strong alternative to RGB cameras (Shen et al., 2023), but
143   its high cost and need for a clear line of sight pose challenges, especially indoors. Event stream-based
144   gait recognition (Wang et al., 2022) can produce sparse, noisy data in low-contrast or variable lighting
145
      conditions.
146   As shown in Table 1, Pressure plates and Inertial Measurement Units (IMUs) are prominent soft
147   biometric modalities based on human movement (Ngo et al., 2014; Burdack et al., 2020; Derlatka
148   & Parfieniuk, 2023). Pressure plates capture the unique Ground Reaction Forces (GRFs) exerted
149   by the foot during walking by being placed directly beneath it. Pressure plates exhibit limitations
150   as a soft biometric modality due to their requirement for direct user interaction. Deployment often
151
      requires explicit user consent, hindering their applicability in scenarios demanding unobtrusive
      identification (Leporace et al., 2015). IMUs, often embedded in wearable devices, offer gait-based
152
      identification through their ability to track body movements (Ngo et al., 2014). IMUs attachment
153
      to the body can be perceived as intrusive (Subramanian & Sarkar, 2018). In contrast, structural
154   vibration-based monitoring is non-intrusive and does not require direct body contact.

205   (initial contact) generate higher frequency vibrations than lift-off (acceleration) due to the impulsive

209

211   over different space-time, and the wave profile will exhibit intrinsic and extrinsic randomness as a
212   function of weight, height, structural properties, and background noise, among other factors (Pan et al.,
213   2017). Naturally, a substantial subject database is crucial for such robust statistical characterization.
214   The impact force (kgms2 or Newton (N)) due to footstep impact on the ground is transmitted through
215   the structure and recorded as a vibration signal by a geophone sensor(in Volt(V)). Thus, the recorded
      signal has induced properties of both the structure and the individual walking pattern.

237   3.2   R ECORDING S ETUP
238
      A single geophone sensor with a sensitivity of 2.88 V/m/sec and a gain of 10 was used for both indoor
239
      and outdoor environments. Vibration signals were captured using a Logic sound card hat equipped
240
      with a 16-bit analog-to-digital converter (ADC) operating at a sampling rate of 8 kHz. The sound
241   card interfaced with a Raspberry Pi 3B+ having 1 GB of RAM and 16 GB of storage for indoor data
242   (VIBeID A1, A2, and A3). For outdoor data (VIBeID A4), a single seismic sensor was interfaced
243   with a Sony CXD5602 Spresense embedded microcontroller. Vibration signals were initially recorded
244   at 16 kHz due to hardware limitations and subsequently downsampled to 8 kHz for consistency with
245   the indoor datasets. Details of each recording environment are provided in detail in the supplementary
246   section.

318   across different locations due to floor heterogeneity. Thus, identifying each footstep event from
319   background noises is an essential goal for data preprocessing. We preprocessed the data using the
320   toolkit provided by (Anchal et al., 2020), which uses unsupervised clustering to extract footstep
321   events based on both statistical features (skewness and kurtosis) and spectral features based on energy
322   bins(40-80Hz, 80-120Hz, and 120-160Hz). We take a rolling window of 375ms with a 50% overlap
323   and calculate 134 features. After preprocessing, we structured the dataset based on footstep events,
      grouping them so that each row represents one sample consisting of 2-10 footstep events (Anchal et al.,

      4.2   D OMAIN A DAPTATION ON DIFFERENT DISTANCE AND FLOOR TYPES
459
460   Building on Section 2.1, we address the limitations of existing methods that assume consistent data
461   distributions between training and testing. We explore person identification using deep learning on
462   12 cross-domain datasets (6 from VIBeID A2, and 6 from VIBeID A3). We present results for both
463   the source domain and the fine-tuned models.
464
465   Baselines: We use only the ResNet18 (M) for domain adaptation use-case because of its fast
466   computation compared to other baseline models. We use a 70-20-10 split for the train-test-validation
467
      process, where 70% of data are used for source model training, 20% of target data is used for
      fine-tuning and 10% of target data is used for model evaluation. Our approach are as follows:
468
469
             • Source-Only: This baseline approach trains a model solely on data from the source domain
470            (70% Source data) and is tested on the target domain (10% target domain). This represents
471            the lower bound of performance achievable in our domain adaptation task.
472
             • Fine-tuning with limited Target Data: Following standard transfer learning principles, we
473
               fine-tuned a pre-trained source model (trained on 70% Source data) with limited labeled
474
               data from the target domain (20% target domain) and evaluated the performance (10% target
475            domain). We experimented with two fine-tuning approaches: a) unfreezing and fine-tuning
476            the last three layers, and b) unfreezing the entire model. The hyper-parameters remained
477            consistent with those used for training the source model.
478
479
      Results: Analysis of Table 6 shows that both multi-distance (Table 6a) and multi-floor (Table 6b) data
480   perform well during fine-tuning with limited labeled data, indicating the approach’s effectiveness in
481   adapting to domain variations. For scenarios with limited computational resources, fine-tuning with
482   only the last three layers unfrozen is a viable alternative, offering faster training time while maintaining
483   comparable accuracy. We observe variations in model performance across structures (multi-floor)
484   and sensor distances (multi-distance). This likely arises from (a) material non-homogeneity, and
485   (b) background noise. Despite these challenges, fine-tuning with limited data has achieved accurate
      person identification using standard classification methods.

524   5     L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
525
526
      This work establishes a foundation for exploring structural vibrations in soft biometrics for person
527
      identification. We are committed to actively maintaining and expanding both the VIBeID dataset and
528   its benchmark. we recognize opportunities for growth, such as increasing the number of participants
529   and exploring a broader range of environments, we view these as exciting challenges. Our goal is to
530   enhance the dataset’s robustness by including more individuals and expanding into diverse domains,
531   incorporating varied environments, materials, and conditions to unlock new possibilities and drive
532   innovation in this field.
533
      VIBeID offers a significant leap forward in data volume, containing over 88.66 hours of vibration
534   recordings and also provides a baseline using established methods. We anticipate open-sourcing
535   the dataset will accelerate future research to explore more efficient approaches. VIBeID can serve
536   as a springboard for significant advancements in the field of deep learning and structural vibration
537   based person identification. Beyond person identification, VIBeID’s value extends to structural
538   vibration analysis. The metadata, including subject age, gender, height, weight, and structural
539   details, is invaluable for researchers exploring applications in bio-mechanics, where understanding
      the relationship between body characteristics and structural vibration is crucial.

       6.4     W ILD S ET EVALUATION INVOLVING CONCURRENT HUMAN AND NON - HUMAN ACTIVITY
992
993    To evaluate scenarios involving concurrent human and non-human activities, we conducted additional
994    experiments that yielded an additional 30 minutes of data. We named it “Wild set”. For such
995    mixed-activity data, we modified the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to three clusters, denoted as
996    Ci where i ∈ {1, 2, 3}. Each cluster Ci is parameterized by its mixing coefficient πi , mean vector µi ,
997    and covariance matrix Σi . The decision process relies on the determinant of the covariance matrices,
998    |Σi |, which provides a measure of the spread or variance of the cluster in the feature space. The
999
       following procedure is adopted:
1000
               1. For each cluster Ci (i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , k}), compute the determinant of its covariance matrix,
1001
                  denoted as |Σi |, where:
1002
                                                            |Σi | = det(Σi ),
1003
1004              and Σi ∈ Rd×d is the covariance matrix of cluster Ci in d-dimensional feature space.
1005

1006              covariance determinants:
1007
1008                                  |Σmax1 | = max(|Σi |),        |Σmax2 | = max(|Σi | \ |Σmax1 |),
1009
                  where |Σmax1 | and |Σmax2 | are associated with complex noise or human activities due to their
1010

1013              {max1, max2}. Convert the footstep events into spectrogram representations.

       6.7   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
1351
1352   Structural Vibration-based person identification is an emerging behavioral bio-metric technology.
1353   However, we observed it has many limitations, this are as follows:
1354
1355          • Footwear Dependency: Our current research focuses on soft-soled flat footwears. Vari-
1356            ations in footwear design can alter the induced vibrations. To address this limitation, in
1357
                the future we will build a comprehensive different footwear dataset. This would enable the
                model to learn features that are invariant to footwear type, enhancing identification accuracy
1358
                across diverse footwear choices.
1359
1360          • Surface Heterogeneity: Our experiments involved data collection on different floor types
1361
                and at varying distances from the sensor, which has never been addressed in such details
                before. We observed a corresponding change in identification accuracy. This highlights
1362
                the need for further research on domain adaptation techniques within the deep learning
1363
                community. By leveraging these techniques, the model’s ability to generalize across diverse
1364            floor surfaces could be significantly improved.
1365
              • Simultaneous person identification: While our current work focuses on single-person
1366
                identification, we see substantial potential in extending it to recognize multiple individuals.
1367
                This would require the development of advanced signal processing and deep learning
1368            algorithms inspired by techniques employed in speech recognition and anomaly detection.
1369
1370   Our dataset can serve as a valuable starting point for research in large-scale structural-vibration
1371   based person identification. It holds potential for future development, enabling researchers to address
1372   current limitations in this field.
