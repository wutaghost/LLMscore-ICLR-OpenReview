009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Theory of Mind (ToM) presents a significant generalization challenge in computa-
012            tional modeling. This paper explores how neural networks with varying architec-
013            tures and training regimes learn and represent ToM-related features. We introduce
014            a novel method for quantifying feature representation within neural networks and
015            apply it to a set of theoretically-grounded features designed to differentiate be-
016
               tween hypothesized ToM strategies. We examine the relationship between feature
               representation and task accuracy across different model architectures and train-
017
               ing datasets. This work provides insights into the mechanisms underlying ToM
018
               capabilities in neural networks and offers a framework for future research in com-
019            putational ToM.
020
021
      The study of Theory of Mind (ToM)—the ability to attribute mental states to others—began with
022   Premack and Woodruff’s work on chimpanzees in 1978 Premack & Woodruff (1978). Since then,
023   it has grown into a fundamental concept in cognitive science. Over the past five decades, develop-
024   mental and comparative psychologists have proposed various theories to explain how humans and
025   non-human animals acquire and use ToM skills. These theories have sparked heated debates and led
026   to numerous experimental paradigms, from false-belief tasks to nuanced measures of mental state
027   attribution. However, traditional behavioral studies are limited by methodological constraints and
028   the inherent difficulty of inferring cognitive processes from observable behavior alone.
029   Recent advancements in computational modeling provide new tools for simulating and analyzing
030   ToM processes in controlled, replicable environments. These simulated models offer unique in-
031   sights into the cognitive mechanisms underlying ToM, complementing traditional behavioral stud-
032   ies. However, researchers continue to face challenges in creating models that generalize learned
033   skills to novel scenarios, mirroring difficulties observed in non-human primate studies.
034   Psychologists have proposed a spectrum of theories to explain ToM task performance in human
035   and non-human subjects, ranging from simple behavior reading (no mentalizing) to more powerful
036   belief-desire mentalizing. There are ongoing debates about whether different strategies are capable
037   of solving certain ToM tasks as well as robustly generalizing to new tasks. Computational models
038   like neural networks can be used to experimentally study how an agent might learn from experience
039   to do ToM tasks. Without analyzing the features represented within these models, we cannot reliably
040   differentiate between these strategies in neural networks, impeding our ability to implement and
041   study mechanisms and curricula that foster genuine ToM capabilities and robust generalization.
042   In this paper, we:
043
044          • Develop and validate a novel technique for quantifying the implicit representation of fea-
045            tures within neural networks’ activations, providing a robust method for analyzing internal
046
               model representations.
047          • Describe a comprehensive set of theoretically-grounded features to empirically evaluate
048            our feature extraction method and differentiate between three hypothesized ToM strategies
049
               in neural networks
050          • Quantify the feature representation capabilities of diverse neural network architectures and
051            training regimes, offering insights into their respective ToM capabilities and potential un-
052
               derlying mechanisms
053          • Investigate the relationship between feature representation and model performance, assess-
               ing the necessity and sufficiency of specific features for accurate task completion

057   1.1   C OMPUTATIONAL T HEORY OF M IND
058
059   This work is heavily inspired by the ToMnet experiments of Rabinowitz et al. Rabinowitz et al.
060   (2018). In their study, they implement machine learning models with explicit ToM-like representa-
061
      tions about agents’ attributes and mental states, and are able to leverage the computational setting to
      probe those models for representations of those features.
062
063   Recently, Horschler et al. Horschler et al. (2023) used computational modeling to investigate ToM
064   capabilities in non-human primates, focusing on visual perspective-taking tasks similar to the one
065   investigated by this paper. They developed seven models of varying complexity to represent dif-
066   ferent theories of primates’ social cognition, and parameterize the subjects’ reliance on their ToM
067
      inferences to determine how well the theories explain primate behavior.
068   Computational ToM skills have also been particularly well-studied recently in the context of large
069   language models (LLMs). The ToMi dataset by Le et al. Le et al. (2019) consists of short, structured
070   narratives based on the Sally-Anne false belief test. ToMi focuses primarily on first-order ToM rea-
071   soning about physical world states. More recently, Xu et al. developed OpenToM Xu et al. (2024) to
072   benchmark ToM capabilities in large language models using longer, more natural narratives, cover-
073
      ing both physical and psychological aspects of ToM. Despite recent advancements, LLMs continue
      to significantly underperform humans on ToM tasks. The significant gap between LLMs and humans
074
      on such tasks highlights the difficulty in acquiring robust ToM skills in machine learning models.

077   1.2   H YPOTHESES ABOUT T O M IN N ONHUMAN A NIMALS
078
079   Developmental and comparative psychologists have produced a variety of theories about potential
080   ToM mechanisms. In this section we briefly overview some theories about ToM in developing hu-
081   mans and non-human animals. This list is incomplete, and much work remains to be done regarding
082   the extent to which any of these theories can be (and/or have already been) used to generate specific
083   cognitive hypotheses, which might be implemented computationally and tested empirically. We also
084   emphasize the importance of noting the context in which these theories were generated, and which
085   scientific problems they address.
086   Simulation theory involves using one’s own mental states as a model for replicating or simulating
087   those of others. It could be facilitated by mirror neurons, which are thought to enable the internal
088   mirroring of others’ actions and emotional states. Additionally, it emphasizes the role of pretend
089   play during childhood development (Gallese & Goldman, 1998), (Harris, 1992). Theory theory in-
090   volves updating (often Bayesian) beliefs based on empirical evidence about others. Children refine
091   their understanding by testing internal models empirically during social interactions (?). Mentaliz-
092   ing/systemizing theory differentiates between systematizing, reasoning about non-social rules, and
093
      mentalizing, reasoning about social rules (Baron-Cohen, 2000). This theory has been used to explain
      differences in the ToM skills of individuals on the Autism spectrum, who might tend to systemize
094
      more strongly relative to mentalizing.
095
096   Of particular relevance to the work in this paper are theories about ToM in animal cognition, as
097   reviewed and categorized by Heyes Heyes (2015). Heyes outlines several key conceptual frame-
098   works for understanding potential mindreading abilities in animals. Under behavior reading, an-
099
      imals predict others’ behavior based solely on observable cues, without attributing mental states
      Heyes (1998). Perception-goal psychology is a framework proposed by Call and Tomasello Call
100
      & Tomasello (2008) as an alternative to full-blown ToM in animals. It suggests that great apes
101
      may understand what others perceive and what concrete goals they may have, without attributing
102   abstract mental states like beliefs. Minimal ToM is a more recent framework by Butterfill and Ap-
103   perly Butterfill & Apperly (2013), who propose a limited form of mindreading that allows for some
104   attribution of mental states, such as goals and perceptions, but without full metarepresentation of
105   propositional attitudes. Whiten’s intervening variable approach Whiten (2013) views animal min-
106   dreading as analogous to how comparative psychologists use intervening variables to explain animal
107   behavior. Finally, full-blown ToM involves the ability to attribute complex mental states, including
      false beliefs Premack & Woodruff (1978).

      2     T EST E NVIRONMENT
109
110
      In this paper, we use the Standoff test environment, a gridworld setting that replicates the competitive
111
      feeding paradigm computationally Michelson et al. (2024). The competitive feeding paradigm is a
112   test setup designed to distinguish whether a non-verbal subject will change its behavior to account
113   for what it believes someone else knows, based on evidence relating to what the other person sees
114   (Hare et al., 2000). Specifically, the environment implements a version of competitive feeding in
115   the style of Penn and Povinelli Penn & Povinelli (2007). In Standoff tasks, two treats of different
116   sizes are visibly hidden in any of five boxes, which are then shuffled around. The player’s challenge
117   is to select the box containing the best possible treat; this is made difficult by the presence of an
118   opponent. The opponent follows simple behavioral rules: if it believes the larger treat is somewhere,
119   it will claim that box, preventing the player from getting whatever treat is inside. Otherwise, the
120
      opponent will attempt to reach the smaller treat, or will select a preferred box. Those rules are
      obfuscated by the fact that the opponent’s vision might be obscured at any point during the setup.
121
      The opponent might be unaware that either treat exists, or it might harbor a counterfactual belief
122
      about either of the treats’ locations. The player’s best option is always to either stay clear of the
123   opponent or to take advantage of the opponent’s unawareness.
124
125   We use this setting as a source of data for supervised learning; each datapoint is collected from a
126
      single trial of five timesteps, or a (5, 5, 7, 7)-sized video. The target output to be learned is the
      correct box, meaning the player’s best choice of the five boxes, given the opponent’s selection.
127
      Twenty percent of all datapoints are reserved for the evaluations referenced throughout this paper.
128
      Previously, Michelson et al. found that models trained on different subsets of Standoff were able to
129   learn the tasks present in their training data to high accuracies, but struggled to generalize to novel
130   settings Michelson et al. (2024).
131
132
      In this paper, we use three training settings, patterned off of Penn and Povinelli’s description of
      systematic competitive feeding: Stage-1 has all tasks without an opponent present. Stage-2 has
133
      all tasks in Stage-1, plus those with a fully-informed opponent. Stage-3 has all tasks allowed in
134
      the environment. Since ToM is a generalization challenge, we split many results in this paper by
135   novelty. Note that all tasks are familiar to models trained on Stage-3.

138   3     E XPERIMENT 1: F EATURE E XTRACTION
139
140   In this experiment, we demonstrate the process by which we quantify features’ presence within
141   neural networks’ inner activations.
142
143   3.1   M ODELS
144
145   We train neural networks with three different architectures as our task-models. The selected architec-
146   tures are a multilayer perceptron (MLP), a convolutional neural network (CNN), and a convolutional
147   LSTM (CLSTM). The MLP has two hidden layers of 32 units and ReLU activations. The CNN
148
      model has two convolutional layers, followed by a single 32-unit hidden layer. Finally, the CLSTM
      model has a convolutional layer that is applied to data at each timestep, whose results are fed to a
149
      three-layer LSTM with 32 hidden units in each layer. A 32-unit hidden layer is used to produce
150
      output predictions from the last timestep of the LSTM’s outputs. For each dataset and architecture,
151   we train three task-models with different random seeds for batches and weight initialization. Inputs
152   to the MLP are flattened, and inputs to the CNN stack all five timesteps into the channel dimension.
153
154
      3.2   M ETRIC : I NTRINSIC F EATURE R EPRESENTATION S CORES
155
156   The models that we train to complete our task (task-models) are connectionist, so we cannot expect
157   to find explicit, conveniently structured representations of discrete features. While methods like
158   correlation analysis or mutual information measurements provide some insight into feature repre-
159   sentations, they do not capture the complex, non-linear relationships that exist between perceptron
160   activations and high-level features, making the use of more sophisticated feature extraction tech-
161   niques necessary for accurate quantification. To quantify the degree to which features are implic-
      itly represented within neural networks’ activations, we train additional machine learning models.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      These additional extraction-models take as inputs the internal activations of the task-models’, col-
163   lected when the task models are being evaluated. These internal activations might be sourced from
164   all hidden layers, or any specific hidden layer. Using those activations, each extraction-model is
165   trained to predict one of our defined features. Its accuracy in making that prediction is considered
166   the Intrinsic Feature Representation (IFR) score. We use two architectures for extraction-models:
167   linear regression, and a multilayer perceptron with one hidden layer of 32 units. The extraction-
168   model architectures are intended to be simpler than the original model, to ensure that they do not
169   make inferences the original model could not. We assume that, because these models might extract
170   connectionist features in the same manner that a task-model’s internal layers might, their ability
171
      to predict a given feature corresponds with the task-model’s ability to use that feature. Hence, the
      extraction-models’ accuracy scores quantify task-models’ feature representations in terms of their
172
      usefulness to those models.
173
174   We train our extraction-models for 10,000 batches of 64 datapoints each. They are trained using the
175   Adam optimizer and an exponentially decaying learning rate schedule, starting at 0.01 and decreas-
176   ing by 1% each epoch. Since the extraction models must produce vectors of varying types which
177
      may not be one-hot, their loss function is the mean squared error of their outputs. Accuracy is re-
      ported by rounding outputs’ values to the nearest integer (all features tested in this paper are binary).
178
      Each task-model provides data for three differently-seeded extraction-models.
179
180   The features of our set might also be accurately inferred from random chance—especially when
181   a feature is dominated by one value—or from the raw perceptual inputs of the environment. To
182   account for these factors, we imitate the IFR scoring procedure using the task-models’ inputs, pro-
183
      ducing raw input IFR scores to compare activation IFR scores against. Activation IFR scores might
      be lower than raw input IFR scores for several reasons: for example, the connectionist nature of
184
      the activations might be more difficult to parse than the discrete inputs, or the neural network might
185
      discard information about the given feature before the target layer. When activation IFR scores are
186   higher than the raw input IFR scores, we take that as evidence that the task-model performs useful
187   computations towards better-representing the given feature.
188
189
      3.3   F EATURES
190
191   In this experiment, we investigate the IFR scores of two features: opponents, and b-loc. Opponents
192   is a binary scalar that indicates the presence or absence of an opponent, since the environment has at
193   most one opponent in each trial. We expect that all models should represent opponents well, even on
194   novel tasks, because the feature is easily observable in the perceptual input. B-loc represents a more
195   complex inference that our models might make: the opponent’s ground-truth belief state regarding
196   the location of both treats. This feature is a 10-length vector representing the two treats’ potential
197
      presence in each of five locations. We expect that b-loc might be well-represented on familiar tasks
      for all models. We do not expect, however, that any models should represent b-loc particularly well
198
      on novel tasks. Doing so would be evidence for strong ToM skills, and b-loc is particularly useful
199
      for predicting the opponent’s selection across all tasks in the environment.
200
201

      3.4   R ESULTS
203

204   sible given a sufficiently powerful extractor model. b-loc, on the other hand, becomes more difficult
205   to predict with each sequential dataset in the familiar case. The feature extractor generalizes better
206   to novel cases after being trained to predict b-loc on S2, which covers more unique b-loc possibili-
207   ties. Although we tested both linear and nonlinear extractors, we find that the nonlinear extractors’
208   performance is universally identical or better than that of its linear counterpart. While linear models

212
      extractors’ performance across different inputs.

      In this experiment, we examine three hypotheses about the nature of our models’ ToM capabilities.
254   In order to evaluate these three hypotheses empirically, we first enumerate a list of features whose
255   presence we may quantify in our models. Next, we calculate the degree to which each of these
256   features is represented in our models, as in Experiment 1, and discuss what that evidence implies
257   regarding the models’ learned strategies for solving the task.
258
259   4.1   R EPRESENTATION STRATEGIES
260
261   Similar to Horschler et al. Horschler et al. (2023), we consider a small number of mindreading strate-
262   gies, ordered by their representation power. We refer to these three strategies as non-mindreading,
263   low-mindreading, and high-mindreading. These are inspired by psychologists’ theories about the
264   evolution and development of ToM in humans and primates, in particular Butterfill & Apperly
265   (2013), though we refrain from using the same terminology in order to make specific claims about
266
      what computational features each strategy allows for.
267   No-mindreading strategies are the hypothesis that a model does not make inferences about the mental
268   states of others. When using these strategies, a model would only ever solve a task by learning
269   surface-level statistics about the environment and the perceived behavior of others. These strategies
      are closely associated with behavior reading, outlined by Heyes (Heyes, 2015). A no-mindreading


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      model is incapable of accounting for an opponent’s knowledge and beliefs, except for where those
271   states happen to correlate with correct solutions in its past experience. Such correlations could be
272   purely coincidental, so they are not meaningful in novel circumstances.
273
274
      Low-mindreading strategies represent a more advanced level of social cognition, in which limited
      inferences might be made about other agents. In this framework, the subject is capable of under-
275
      standing that an agent could have perceptions and goals that are different to its own. Similarly to
276
      both minimal ToM and perception-goal psychology, these states must be grounded in reality: the
277   other’s perceptions are a part of the visible world-state, and the other’s goal is a concrete world state
278   that the other will attempt to reach. While this rudimentary mentalization is not capable of making
279   inferences about counterfactual beliefs, it does allow for an understanding that the opponent is or is
280   not aware of some factual information.
281
      High-mindreading refers to the ability to attribute abstract mental states to others. This representa-
282
      tion allows for the inference of specific counterfactual beliefs, and is capable of replacing concrete
283   goals with abstract desires, e.g. instead of reasoning that an opponent is going to go to that treat
284   over there, we might reason that the opponent wants the largest available treat, wherever it may be.
285   High-mindreading models might be likened to agents with full-blown ToM.

407   5     E XPERIMENT 3: F EATURE REPRESENTATIONS AND TASK ACCURACY
408
409   We have shown how features may be measured in model activations, but we have yet to examine the
410   relationship between feature representation and task accuracy. In addition to our predictions about
411   which features should be present in models of different representation strategies, we also predict
412   that those features make meaningful contributions to the model’s outputs. Such contributions might
413
      exist even for novel tasks. We may examine them by investigating how model accuracy depends on
      any given feature: if, on a given datapoint, the feature is accurately represented, does that imply that
414
      the task-model’s prediction will be accurate? Inversely, if the feature is inaccurately represented,
415
      will the task-model be inaccurate? We may represent these two quantities as probabilities, P (A|F )
416   and P (¬A|¬F ), the probability that the model will be Accurate given that a Feature is present, and
417   the corresponding case when the Feature is absent.
418
419
      In this experiment, we are investigating when those two accuracy values coincide, as opposed to a
      causal relationships between them. As such, this technique does not directly measure sufficiency
420
      and necessity; findings should be regarded as evidence for sufficiency and necessity instead.
421
422

      5.1   R ESULTS
424
      Among all the features tested, none have overall negative effects on accuracy, supporting their rel-
425
      evance to the task. Interestingly, while many features exhibit evidence of either necessity or suffi-
426   ciency, few provide both, highlighting the complex nature of features in this task. In the CLSTM
427   model, most tested features appear to be more necessary than they are sufficient, particularly in the
428   models trained on S2. This suggests that either combinations of these features, or additional fea-
429   tures not in our list, are required for robust generalization to novel tasks. Several such features are
430   necessary for almost every datapoint in the evaluation set. In the CNN model, there exist several
431   features that are more sufficient than they are necessary. This implies that there are multiple viable
      strategies for robust generalization that the CNN model is able to employ. The single feature which

432
      provides the most sufficiency across all models is (b-loc). This result is reasonable, as b-loc encodes
433   the opponent’s goal, and often might contain information about the other treat’s location as well. Its
434   prominence suggests that understanding the opponent’s beliefs about treat locations is a key factor
435   in successful task completion, regardless of model architecture.
436
437
438   6     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
439
440   6.1   D ISCUSSION
441
442   In general, we find that the best-generalizing features present in our models are those that do not
443   require mindreading. Models that represent low- and high-mindreading features do perform better
444   on the task overall, however, and all features in our set aid prediction accuracy when represented
445
      well. As for the strategies used by our selected models architectures, the CNN and MLP models
      do not even succeed at no-mindreading strategies on familiar tasks. The CLSTM models are adept
446
      at no-mindreading strategies, but they are unable to robustly generalize low- and high-mindreading
447
      features to novel tasks. While the training regime has a strong effect on the features represented
448   for familiar tasks, its effect on novel task features is weak relative to that of model architecture,
449   suggesting that model engineering could be more important for learning robust ToM skills than
450   learning curricula.
451
452
      6.2   F EATURE E XTRACTION
453
454
      Our feature extraction technique is subject to causes of both over- and under-performing, producing
455   scores that differ from models’ true ability to represent various features. Over-performance could
456   be caused by the introduction of nonlinearity to the models. Because we assume features might
457   be difficult to uncover within a connectionist task-model’s inner state, we require some amount of
458   nonlinearity in our extractor-models. But that same nonlinearity could be leveraged to synthesize
459   information that the task-model ignores. Additionally, the task itself likely contains spurious corre-
460   lations that could be abused by the extractor-model. Under-performance could be caused by both
461   over- and under-fitting (over-fitting only in the case of novel-task performance). We used identical
462   training for all task-models, despite their inputs and outputs having different shapes and distribu-
463
      tion, so certain features are likely underfit. Additionally, the task-models often feature layers that
      perform significantly more complex operations than the extractor-models, so they might synthesize
464
      the information more easily. Overall, feature extraction using extractor-models is a measure of only
465
      one piece of evidence that any feature is actually represented by the task-model.
466
467
468   6.3   ACCURACY D EPENDENCY
469
470
      Our technique for determining accuracy dependency itself depends on feature extraction, so it is
      subject to the same limitations. Its direct comparison of accuracy on individual datapoints allows
471
      for a more fine-grained view of features’ contributions, but these effects could likewise include
472
      spurious correlations.
473
474
475   6.4   F UTURE WORK
476
477
      With minor environmental extensions, further mechanisms could be tested using the same method-
      ology that we have presented, by identifying and quantifying differentiating features, e.g. those
478
      included in theorized mechanisms that reason about uncertainty, preferences, and multiple agents.
479
      The work presented in this paper is intended to serve as a precursor to the engineering and investi-
480   gation of models which embody mechanistic hypotheses about ToM skills. Only by understanding
481   the specific features that those models represent do we believe we can empirically validate models’
482   robustness to varied tasks. In addition to handcrafted mechanisms for mentalization, we intend to
483   evaluate contemporary machine learning techniques including transformer architectures. The grid-
484   world used in this paper is implemented primarily as a reinforcement learning environment, so we
485   also plan to examine the effect of embodied cognition, where the player is an agent with their own
      goals and actions in which environmental knowledge may be grounded.
