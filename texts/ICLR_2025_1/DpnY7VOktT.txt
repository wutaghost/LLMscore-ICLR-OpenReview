                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Deep neural networks are misguided by simple-to-craft, imperceptible adversar-
013            ial perturbations to inputs. Now, it is possible to craft such perturbations solely
014
               using model outputs and query-based black-box attack algorithms. These at-
               tacks compute adversarial examples by iteratively querying a model and inspect-
015
               ing responses. The attacks’ success in near information vacuums poses a sig-
016
               nificant challenge for developing mitigations. We explore a new idea for a de-
017            fense driven by a fundamental insight—to compute an adversarial example, the
018            attacks depend on the relationship between successive responses to queries to op-
019            timize a perturbation. Therefore, to obfuscate this relationship, we investigate
020            randomly sampling a model from a set to generate a response to a query. Ef-
021            fectively, this model randomization violates the attacker’s expectation of the pa-
022            rameters of a model to remain static between queries to extract information to
023            guide the search toward an adversarial example. It is not immediately clear, if
024            model randomization can lead to sufficient obfuscation to confuse query-based
025
               black-box attacks or how best to build such a method. Our theoretical analysis
               proves model randomization always increases resilience to query-based black-
026
               box attacks. We demonstrate with extensive empirical studies using 7 state-of-
027
               the-art attacks under all three perturbation objectives (l∞ , l2 , l0 ) and adaptive
028            attacks, our proposed implementation injects sufficient uncertainty through ob-
029            fuscation to yield a highly effective defense. Code to be released on GitHub at
030            https://github.com/disco-defense/.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Many studies comprehensively demonstrate the vulnerability of deep learning models to adversarial
037   attacks (Szegedy et al., 2014; Papernot et al., 2017; Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Madry et al., 2018;
038   Athalye et al., 2018). These attacks craft and apply imperceptible perturbations to inputs to mislead
039   or hijack the decision of deep learning models.
040
      In white-box settings, malicious actors can mount strong attacks like Projected Gradient Descent
041
      (PGD) with access to model internals and gradient information. However, in practical deployments
042   of machine learning, as with growing numbers of machine learning as a service (MLaaS) offerings,
043   access to model information is highly restricted to external parties. Under these practical settings,
044   an attacker is limited to interacting with a model through a query-response mechanism and only
045   gains access to model outputs. Consequently, in many real-world scenarios, query-based attacks in
046   black-box access settings pose the greatest threat. In fact, Ilyas et al. (2018); Guo et al. (2019); Vo
047   et al. (2024) demonstrated practical query-based attacks against models in a real-world system.
048   Query-based attack algorithms extract response differences to small modifications to the input to
049   estimate gradients or to search for a direction towards an adversarial example. But, the iterative
050   process of making small modifications and estimating gradients or search directions necessitates
051   a myriad of time-consuming interactions with a model (query-responses). This exposes a critical
052   weakness defenders can exploit. The large number of model queries with similar inputs over large
053   time periods is anomalous and raise suspicions. Therefore, a defense objective is to prevent the
      extraction of useful information from model responses to compute adversarial examples.

      Our Study. We seek to achieve the objective by injecting uncertainty directly into model responses
055   without using random noise.
056
057
058        The fundamental insight behind our idea is that computing an adversarial example neces-
059
           sitates successive queries and inspecting responses to make incremental progress towards
           an adversarial example—the non-source class in Figure 1—but, this progress hinges on the
060
           relationship between successive query responses to optimize a perturbation, a process that
061
           expects the model parameters to remain static between queries.
062
063
064   So, we propose randomizing models to obfuscate the relationship between the successive queries
065   and responses to confuse the iterative optimization process. To achieve obfuscation through ran-
066   domization, we investigate sampling models (or functions) from a set of diverse models to respond
067
      to each query as illustrated in Figure 1 (last tile). To minimize potential impacts of a defense strategy
      on performance we investigate learning a set of well-performing models.

085   Our theoretical analysis shows the diversity of responses from randomly sampled models can in-
086   troduce sufficient uncertainty to degrade gradient estimates or misdirect random search attempts.
087   Consequently, building adversarial examples with score-based or decision-based attack algorithms
088   are made significantly more difficult. Unlike previous methods to confuse attackers, we avoid adding
089   random noise to inputs or features, our thinking mitigates compromising performance for robustness.
090   Our key contributions can be summarized as follows:
091
092       • We investigate the effectiveness of injecting uncertainty into responses by randomized sampling
093
            of diverse and well-trained models to respond to queries with a theoretical analysis.
094       • We implement our idea with techniques for promoting model diversity and because we also want
095         random model parameter combinations to be well performing, we introduce a new learning ob-
096         jective to diversity promotion. The defense framework we investigate is flexible to incorporate
097
            other model diversity promotion methods or even existing random noise adding techniques.
098       • Extensive evaluations with both score-based and decision-based attacks as well as all three
099         perturbation objectives (l∞ , l2 , l0 ) validate our theoretical analysis and demonstrate our method
100         can enhance the robustness against query-based attacks.

102   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED S TUDIES
103
104   Query-based Black-Box Adversarial Attacks. In contrast to white-box attacks, black-box attack-
105   ers do not have access to a victim model. One approach is transfer-based black-box attack crafting
106   adversarial examples from a surrogate model to create adversarial examples transferable to a victim
107   model (Papernot et al., 2017; Chen & Liu, 2023). But, transfer-based attacks’ success varies signif-
      icantly due to factors like model hyperparameters, training conditions and constraints in generating


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      adversarial samples (Chen et al., 2017) and similarity between the surrogates and target models
109   (Suya et al., 2024). In this paper, we focus on defending against query-based adversarial attacks.
110
111   Query-based black-box adversarial attacks submit an input to obtain a response from a model. When
112
      the response is a confidence score, the attacks operate in a score-based threat model; when it is a
      hard label, a decision-based threat model. Two primary approaches to query-based attacks are:
113
114       • Gradient estimation methods (Liu et al., 2018a; Ilyas et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
115         2020a). In general, these methods estimate the model’s gradient with respect to an image x by
116         exploring images surrounding x with queries to assess the model’s gradient.
117       • Gradient-free (search-based) methods (Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Croce et al., 2022; Vo et al.,
118         2022). These methods introduce small random modifications to an image x and observe query
119
            response to assess the perturbation’s goodness rather than relying on gradient information.
120   In general, adversarial attacks aim to yield imperceptible perturbations. Specific query-based attack
121   algorithm are formulated to minimize three common perturbation objectives l2 -norm; l∞ -norm;
122   or l0 -norm. In our work, we use both score-based and decision-based attacks. And, in contrast to
123   past studies, we evaluate attack algorithms covering all three perturbation objectives (l∞ , l2 , l0 ).
124   Defenses against Black-Box Attacks. Methods to understand and exploit the anomalous nature of
125   queries attempt to detect attacks (Chen et al., 2020b; Pang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022). Adversarial
126   training, as a more general method, can be used to defense against adversarial attacks (Tramèr
127   et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020; 2022). Similarly, training with noise (Cohen et al., 2019; Salman
128   et al., 2019) can make models robust against adversarial inputs. But, these training approaches can
129   diminish model performance (Zhang et al., 2019; Shafahi et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020).
130   By contrast, this paper considers methods to distort information available in responses to deceive at-
131   tackers. Intuitively, these methods seek ways to alter the response provided to attackers to misdirect
132   their search towards an adversarial example. To distort information, past work studied: i) adding
133   noise to inputs Cao & Gong (2017); Qin et al. (2021); or ii) injecting noise to model’s parame-
134   ters, activation or adding noise layers (Liu et al., 2018b; He et al., 2019) or iii) adding noise to
135   features (Nguyen et al., 2024). We primarily evaluate the following recent defenses in this paper:
136       • Randomize Noise Defense (RND) Recently, Qin et al. (2021) proposed adding random noise
137         to the input once and theoretically analyzed effectiveness against query-based attacks. Interest-
138         ingly, Byun et al. (2022) also proposed a randomize noise defense dubbed SND. However, since
139         the two methods are similar, our comparison with RND will extends to both methods.
140       • Randomized Features Nguyen et al. (2024) introduced and explored the idea of adding random
141         noise in feature space.
142
      In contrast, we explore randomization in the function space (effectively the parameter space) to
143
      enhance robustness and perhaps do better than noise injections to inputs or features as a defense
144
      whilst mitigating the performance impacts of adding noise.
145
146   Adversarial Robustness with Ensemble Diversity. We consider randomization of functions or
147   models from an ensemble of diverse, well-performing models. Prior studies (Kariyappa & Qureshi,
148
      2019; Pang et al., 2019; Doan et al., 2022) explored ensemble diversity to improve adversarial ro-
      bustness in white-box settings at the cost of sacrificing clean accuracy (Tsipras et al., 2019; Qin
149
      et al., 2021). In contrast, we study the potential of ensemble diversity for fortifying models against
150
      query-based black-box attacks without adversarial training, to mitigate potential performance loss.
151
152   A number of diversity promotion methods exist in the literature—we elaborate further in Ap-
153   pendix H.1. In this paper, we evaluate with: i) Deep Ensembles (Ensemble) (Lakshminarayanan
154
      et al., 2017; Fort et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2020); ii) DivDis (Lee et al., 2023); iii) DivReg (Teney
      et al., 2022) and iv) together with a learning objective we propose for Stein Variational Gradient
155
      Descent (SVGD) method to learn not only a diverse but also a set of well-performing models.

186   3.2   C ONFUSING ATTACKERS WITH M ODEL R ANDOMIZATION
187
      Recall that query-based black-box attack algorithms do not have prior knowledge of a target model
188
      and are not aware of the defense mechanism employed by the model owner, so they need multiple
189
      queries and observations of the model response to estimate a gradient or a search direction. Our
190   fundamental insight is to obfuscate the relationship between query-response pairs. With this in
191   mind, we investigate two ideas we conceptualize in the following hypotheses:
192
        • Hypothesis 1. Randomly selecting a model from a set for responding to a query can obfuscate
193
          the relationship between successive pairs of queries and responses. By employing a different
194       function or a learned model to process a query input and generate a response, we can expect to
195       hide the relationship between query-response pairs. Because the attack problem is totally reliant
196       on this relationship, this should lead to sufficient uncertainty to confuse the task of estimating
197       gradient directions or determining search directions towards an adversarial example.
198     • Hypothesis 2. Enhancing model parameter diversity enhances obfuscation. Randomly sam-
199       pling functions or models from a set with very diverse parameters should increase diversity in
200       outputs to further degrade the quality of information extracted from pairs of query-responses.
201

308   3.4.1    PARAMETER D IVERSITY A PPROACH
309
      To answer Question 1, we consider the diversity in parameters achieved by adopting an ensemble
310
      training framework incorporating a Bayesian formulation of deep learning with Stein variational
311
      gradient descent (SVGD) method (Liu & Wang, 2016; Wang & Liu, 2019). This framework allows
312   us to learn a posterior distribution of parameters (weights) and the model parameters sampled from
313   that posterior distribution can result in diverse representations, leading to model diversity.
314
315
      In this approach, a neural network f (x, θ) with parameters θ are considered random variables. Then
      Bayesian deep learning begins with a prior p(θ) and a likelihood function p(D|θ) that assesses how
316
      well the network with weights θ fits the data D. The Bayesian inference integrates the likelihood
317
      and the prior using Bayes’ theorem to derive a posterior distribution, p (θ|D), over the space of
318
      weights, given by p (θ|D) = p(D|θ)p(θ)
                                       p(D)    .
319
320   The exact solution for the posterior is often impractical, due to the complexity of deep neural net-
321   works and the high-dimensional integral of the denominator, even for networks of moderate size.
322   The true Bayesian posterior distribution is typically a complicated multimodal distribution, making
323   it challenging to accurately sample from. To address these issues, Liu & Wang (2016) proposed
      Stein Variational Gradient Descent (SVGD) as a general-purpose variational inference algorithm.

532   5      C ONCLUSION
533
      This study investigates the effectiveness of a model randomization defense against query-based
534   black-box attacks in both score-based and decision-based settings. We theoretically analyze the
535   defense and prove the link between diversity of model outputs to model robustness; hence, model
536   randomization always increases resilience to query-based black-box attacks. We realize the ap-
537   proach by learning a set of diverse yet well performing models for random selection to provide
538   robustness whilst minimizing the clean accuracy drop of defended models. We demonstrate the ap-
539   proach leads to an effective defense with 7 state-of-the-art query-based black-box attacks under all
      three perturbation objectives (l∞ , l2 , l0 ).

923
924   Adaptive attack setting. Now, we assume the attacker has knowledge of the defense mechanism
925   and is aware that a subset of K models is randomly selected to generate the response to a model
926
      query. If an adversary has prior knowledge of our defense mechanism, they can employ Expectation

927
928   It is worth noting that, in the original EOT method, the adversarial perturbation gradient is calcu-
929   lated based on a series of transformed inputs (adversarial examples). The reason is to address the
930   issue incurred by the ineffectiveness of an adversarial example yielded by an adversary when the
931   adversarial example is randomly transformed (Athalye et al., 2017) i.e. with different view angles.
932
      Therefore, to maintain the effectiveness of an adversarial example over different transformations,
      they model these transformations in their optimization procedure by transforming the inputs. Simi-
933
      larly, to maintain the effectiveness of an adversarial example over different models whose selection
934
      is represented by different π, an adversary seeks a perturbation gradient direction such that it is
935   effective over different models. Therefore, in our study, the so-called EOT is performed over π. In-
936   terestingly, the same reasoning is in Athalye et al. (2018) and Nguyen et al. (2024) when applying
937   EOT to attack defenses involving stochasticity, like with ours.
938

939
      target model to estimate the gradient, but for each query, it feeds a target model with the same
940

942   if a non-adaptive attack uses 10K queries and m = 10, the total number of queries needed by an
943   adaptive attacker is 100K. Likewise, for gradient-free attacks, each input is fed into a target model

1025   In this section, we provide an additional analysis of the trade-off between the selection of N (the
       subset set size) from K models and the number of queries to achieve a low error estimation.


                                                                19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
             • Intuitively, a larger subset size N reduces the number of combinations of model subsets.
1027           This results in a reduction in the number of random models presented to the attacker.
1028
             • In addition, a larger subset size N also reduces the variance in estimates of gradient at-
1029
               tempted by an attacker. Because, the prediction from a larger subset of models is more
1030
               confident and the variance, for example, in output scores between these large subsets is
1031           less.
1032
             • Consequently, averaging across larger subsets of models leads to more informative re-
1033
               sponses (better gradient estimates, for example) and fewer queries to obtain low error esti-
1034           mations.
1035
             • In contrast, smaller K values increase the uncertainty, which leads to increased variance
1036
               in gradient estimation, or in other words, the difference in upper and lower bound for the
1037
               gradient’s value will be larger. Then following Proposition 1, this increases the cost of the
1038           attack, which forces the attacker to expend more queries to obtain a low error estimation of
1039           a gradient.
