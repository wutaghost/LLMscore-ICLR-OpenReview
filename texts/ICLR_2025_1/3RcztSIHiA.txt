011                                                  Abstract
012
013             PDEs optimal control (PDEOC) problems aim to optimize the performance
014             of physical systems constrained by partial differential equations (PDEs) to
015             achieve desired characteristics. Such problems frequently appear in scien-
016
                tific discoveries and are of huge engineering importance. Physics-informed
                neural networks (PINNs) are recently proposed to solve PDEOC problems,
017
                but it may fail to balance the different competing loss terms in such prob-
018
                lems. Our work proposes PDE-GAN, a novel approach that puts PINNs
019             in the framework of generative adversarial networks (GANs) “learn the
020             loss function” to address the trade-off between the different competing
021             loss terms effectively. We conducted detailed and comprehensive exper-
022             iments to compare PDEs-GANs with vanilla PINNs in solving four typ-
023             ical and representative PDEOC problems, namely, (1) boundary control
024             on Laplace Equation, (2) time-dependent distributed control on Inviscous
025             Burgers’ Equation, (3) initial value control on Burgers’ Equation with Vis-
026             cosity, and (4) time-space-dependent distributed control on Burgers’ Equa-
027
                tion with Viscosity. Strong numerical evidence supports the PDE-GAN
                that it achieves the the best control performance and shortest computation
028
                time without the need of line search which is necessary for vanilla PINNs.

031   1     Introduction
032
033   In physics, partial differential equations (PDEs) hold significant scientific and engineering
034   importance. Controlling the behavior of systems constrained by PDEs is crucial for many
035   engineering and scientific disciplines (Chakrabarty & Hanson, 2005). PDEs optimal control
036   (PDEOC) problems aim to optimize the performance of physical systems governed by PDEs
037   to achieve desired characteristics (Lions, 1971). The standard mathematical expression of
038   the PDEOC problem is as follows.
039   Consider a physical system defined over a domain Ω ⊂ Rd , governed by the following PDEs
040   and cost objectives.
041

088   Their works address the trade-off between the different competing loss terms in PDEOC
089   problems through line search on the cost objective weights (ω). The vanilla PINNs make
090
      use of a two-step line search method to identify the optimal weight w. In details, the two-
      step method trains a separate pair of solution and control networks for each weight, and
091
      then predict their corresponding final states. Such final states will be compared with the
092
      analytical solution, and the w attaining the least error is then chosen. In other words, such
093   method searches for the optimal weight w exhaustively. One of the obvious drawbacks is
094   thus its heavy cost of computation time. Also, such line search method serves only as a
095   heuristic, lacking strong theoretical support, which makes further analysis in robustness and
096   stability challenging. Therefore, it is imperative to develop an effective strategy to handle
097   PDEs constraints for solving PDEOC problems.
098
      To address this theoretical gap, we put PINNs in the framework of Generative Adversarial
099   Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020) to solve PDEOC problems in a fully unsuper-
100   vised manner. Inspired by Zeng et al. (2022), we adaptively modify the entire loss function
101   throughout the training process, rather than just changing the weights of the loss terms,
102   to improve the accuracy of the solution. Our PDE-GAN uses the discriminator network
103   to optimize the generator’s loss function, eliminating the need for predefined weights and
104   offering greater flexibility compared to line search methods.
105   Our contributions in this work are summarized as follows:
106
107         • We propose a novel approach for solving PDEs optimal control problems, namely,
              PDEs-GANs, which is capable of ”learning the loss function” in the learning process.

122   2   Related work
123
124   Various methods have been developed for solving PDEOC problems, which can be mainly
125   divided into traditional numerical method and deep-learning based approaches.
126   The adjoint method (Herzog & Kunisch, 2010), as one of the traditional approaches for
127   solving PDEOC problems, has been successfully applied to optics and photonics (Bayati
128   et al., 2020; Molesky et al., 2018; Pestourie et al., 2018), fluid dynamics (Borrvall & Peters-
129   son, 2003; Duan et al., 2016), and solid mechanics (Bendsoe & Sigmund, 2013; Sigmund &
130   Maute, 2013). It is based on Lagrange’s famous 1853 paper (Lagrange, 1853), which laid
131   the foundation for Lagrange multipliers and adjoint-based sensitivity analysis. This method
132   involves iteratively computing the gradient of the cost objective with respect to optimal
133   control solutions until stopping conditions are met. It works by solving a second adjoint
134
      PDEs equation in addition to the original control equation.
135   Although the adjoint method is a powerful tool for solving PDEOC problems, it has sig-
136   nificant drawbacks. First, deriving the adjoint PDEs equations for simple optimal control
137   problems with complex PDEs is a challenging task. Moreover, the adjoint method relies on
138   finite element or finite difference methods, and its computational cost increases quadrati-
139   cally to cubically with the mesh size. Therefore, solving PDEOC problems with large search
140
      spaces and mesh sizes becomes extremely expensive and may even become intractable, which
      is known as the curse of dimensionality.
141
142   To resolve those problems, various deep-learning based methods have been developed for
143   solving PDEOC problems. Some of these are supervised, such as Lu et al. (2019), where the
144   authors use DeepONet to replace finite element methods. They use DeepONet to directly
145   learn the mapping from optimal control solutions to PDEs solutions and further replace
146
      network constraints with PDEs networks. However, these methods require pre-training a
      large operator network, which is both complex and ineﬀicient. Moreover, if the optimal
147
      solution lies outside the training distribution, performance may degrade (Lanthaler et al.,
148
      2022).
149
150   To improve training accuracy, Demo et al. (2023) utilized physical information in various
151   ways. They used physical information as enhanced input (additional features) and as a
152
      guide for constructing neural network architectures. This approach accelerated the training
      process and improved the accuracy of parameter predictions. However, it remains to be
153
      verified which type of physical information is most suitable for use as enhanced input.
154
155   There is also an unsupervised neural network approach. For example, as we mentioned
156   before, Mowlavi & Nabi (2023) proposed using a single PINN to solve PDEOC problems.
157   This method introduces a trade-off between the cost objective and different competing loss
158
      terms, which is crucial for performance (Nandwani et al., 2019).
159   To resolve the trade-off between the different loss terms, Hao et al. (2022) formulated the
160   PDEOC problem as a bi-level loop problem. They used implicit function theorem (IFT)
161   differentiation to compute the hypergradient of the control parameters θ in the outer loop.
      In the inner loop, they fine-tuned the PINN using only the PDEs loss.

      Although the bi-level method splits different competing loss terms, it creates an extra prob-
163   lem about the computation of hypergradient, the accuracy of which largely depends on
164   the specific numerical methods applied. Therefore, applying the bi-level methods do not
165   solve the trade-off problem directly but actually transform it to another pair of problems in
166   solving hypergradient and PINN solution at the same time.

      3     PDE-GAN
170
      In this section, we introduce our method, PDE-GAN, which integrates PINNs into the GAN
171
      framework. Through the generative-adversarial interplay between the generator network and
172
      the discriminator network, the loss function is continuously optimized to learn the weights
173   between the cost objective and the different competing loss terms in PDEOC problems.
174
175
      3.1   Generative Adversarial Networks
176
177   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020) are generative mod-
178   els that use two neural networks to induce a generative distribution p(x) of the data by
179   formulating the inference problem as a two-player, zero-sum game.
180
      The generative model first samples a latent random variable z ∼ N (0, 1), which is used
181
      as input into the generator G (e.g., a neural network). A discriminator D is trained to
182   classify whether its input was sampled from the generator (i.e., “generated data”) or from
183   a reference data set (i.e., “real data”).
184
185
      Informally, the process of training GANs proceeds by optimizing a minimax objective over
      the generator and discriminator such that the generator attempts to trick the discriminator
186
      to classify “generated data” samples as “real data”. Formally, one optimizes
187

188
             min max V (D, G) = min max Ex∼pdata (x) [ln D(x)] + Ez∼pz (z) [1 − ln D(G(z))] ,
189          G    D               G    D
190
      where x ∼ pdata (x) denotes samples from the empirical data distribution, and pz ∼ N (0, 1)
191   samples in latent space. In practice, the optimization alternates between gradient ascent
192   and descent steps for D and G respectively.

      3.2   Hard-Constrained Physics-Informed Neural Networks
195
196   In the Introduction, we presented the construction method of the loss function for solving
197   PDEOC problems based on soft-constrained PINNs (Equations (3) and (4)). During the
198   optimization process, the four loss terms—PDE residual condition, boundary condition,
199   initial condition, and cost objective—compete for gradients, making the training results
200   highly dependent on the choice of weights ω.
201   To mitigate this issue, another PINNs-based method employs function transformations or
202   neural network numerical embeddings to explicitly enforce the initial and boundary con-
203   ditions on the surrogate system state neural network model uθu (x, t). This reformulation
204   reduces the four loss terms to just the PDE residual term and the cost objective term,
205   significantly improving the performance of solving PDEOC problems.
206   Clearly, adjusting the weight relationship between two loss terms is more effective than
207   adjusting four terms. To ensure the exact satisfaction of initial and boundary conditions,
208   various methods can be employed. For instance, the neural network output uθu (x, t) can
209   be modified to meet the initial condition u(x, t)|t=t0 := u0 (Lagaris et al., 1998). Ones can
210   apply the re-parameterization :
211
                                       ûθu (x, t) = u0 + tuθu (x, t),                          (5)
212
213   which exactly satisfies the initial condition. Flamant et al. (2020) proposed an augmented
214   re-parameterization

240   solve PDEOC problems. Our method, PDE-GAN, innovatively combines the framework of

244   adjusts the weight ω to linearly balance the relationship between the two loss terms, the

      4     Experiments
433
434   4.1   Experimental Setup and Evaluation Protocol
435
436   Benchmark Problems: We select several classic PDEOC problems, including both linear
437   and nonlinear problems, as well as optimal control problems for boundary, spatio-temporal
438   domain, and time-domain distributed equations. It is worth noting that, to verify the
439   effectiveness of our method, we attempted the control function and cost objective in differ-
440   ent scenarios: on the same boundary (Laplace problem), on opposite boundaries (Viscous
441
      Burgers initial value control problem), in the spatio-temporal domain (Viscous Burgers dis-
      tributed control problem), and in the time-domain (Inviscid Burgers equation). On the four
442
      optimal problems, we test and compare the performance of (1) Soft-PINNs, (2) Hard-PINNs
443
      and (3) PDE-GAN respectively. More details of problems are listed in Appendix A.
444   (1) Laplace’ Equation. The optimal boundary control problem of the Laplace equation is
445   widely applied in various engineering and scientific fields, particularly in heat conduction,
446   fluid mechanics, acoustics, and material design.
447   (2) Inviscid Burgers’ Equation. The time-dependent distributed control problem for the
448   inviscid Burgers’ equation refers to adjusting control inputs over a given time interval to
449   ensure that the system’s state reaches a desired target in both time and space. Such prob-
450   lems are commonly used in the optimal control of dynamic systems and are relevant to fields
451   such as fluid dynamics, traﬀic flow, and meteorological models.
452
      (3) Viscous Burgers’ Equation (Initial value control). The initial value control problem for
      the viscous Burgers’ equation also has wide applications in fluid mechanics, traﬀic flow, me-
453
      teorological simulation, and other fields. By optimizing and adjusting the system’s initial
454
      state, it is possible to effectively control the subsequent evolution of the system to achieve
455   desired physical or engineering goals.
456   (4) Viscous Burgers’ Equation (Distributed control). The space-time-dependent distributed
457   control problem for the viscous Burgers’ Equation primarily involves adjusting the system
458   in both time and space by optimizing control inputs to achieve effective control of fluid
459   dynamic behavior.
460
461   Hyperparameters and Evaluation Protocols: For above problems, we construct the gener-
462   ator networks (Gu ,Gc ) and discriminator networks (Du ,Dc ) using four multi-layer percep-
463   trons (MLPs). We train these networks with the Adam optimizer (Diederik, 2014), where
464
      the learning rate decreases proportionally to the steps number by a factor of β. Since our top
      priority is on finding the optimal control for the problems, we apply high-precision numerical
465
      methods (Forward Euler Method, Finite Element Method and Spectral Method) to evaluate
466
      the trained optimal control cθ directly. The uθ will not be evaluated as it is only a side
467   product of our training process. The cost objective (J ) obtained from numerical methods
468   serves as our evaluation metric. In Soft-PINNs and Hard-PINNs, we simulated all results
469   with weights ranging from 1e-03 to 1e11 (large cross-domain). In Appendix B, a comparative
470   analysis of the three methods in different numerical experiments is presented. Additional
471   details and method-specific hyperparameters (weights, neural network structures, learning
472   rate, decay steps, decay rate, Adam optimizer parameters, activation function, and training
473   termination criterias) are reported in Appendix C. The experiments are run on a single
474   NVIDIA GeForce 4060 Ti GPU.

533   5     Conclusion
534
535   This paper introduces PDE-GAN, a novel deep learning method for solving PDEs optimal
536   control problems. By embedding the PINN structure into the GAN framework, we use two
537   additional discriminator networks to adaptively adjust the loss function, allowing for the
538   adjustment of weights between different competing loss terms. Compared to Soft-PINNs
539   and Hard-PINNs, PDE-GAN can find the optimal control without the need for cumbersome
      line search, offering a more flexible structure, higher eﬀiciency, and greater accuracy.
