                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural PDEs have emerged as inexpensive surrogate models for numerical PDE
012            solvers. While they offer efficient approximations, they often lack robust uncer-
013            tainty quantification (UQ), limiting their practical utility. Existing UQ methods for
014            these models typically have high computational demands and lack guarantees. We
015            introduce a novel framework for calibrated physics-informed uncertainty quantifi-
016            cation to address these limitations. Our approach leverages physics residual errors
017            as a nonconformity score within a conformal prediction (CP) framework. This
018            enables data-free, model-agnostic, and statistically guaranteed uncertainty esti-
019
               mates. Our framework utilises convolutional layers as finite difference stencils for
               gradient estimation, our framework provides inexpensive coverage bounds for the
020
               violation of conservation laws within model predictions. In our experiments, we
021
               utilise CP to obtain marginal coverage for each cell and joint coverage over the
022            entire prediction domain of various PDEs.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Numerical Partial Differential Equation (PDE) solvers are crucial for scientific simulations across
028   various fields (Danabasoglu et al., 2020; Giudicelli et al., 2024), but they often incur significant
029   computational costs and carbon footprints (Horwitz, 2024). Machine learning offers an efficient
030   alternative for approximating numerical simulations (Bertone et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021),
031
      finding applications in weather modelling (Lam et al., 2023; Kurth et al., 2023), fluid dynamics
      (Jiang et al., 2020; Pfaff et al., 2021), and nuclear fusion (Poels et al., 2023; Carey et al., 2024;
032
      Gopakumar & Samaddar, 2020). Unlike traditional numerical solvers, which may suffer from nu-
033
      merical instabilities, neural PDE solvers always produce an output. However, their accuracy and
034   usefulness are not assured. They may violate system physics and confidently assert incorrect solu-
035   tions due to various errors (Gopakumar et al., 2023a). Existing uncertainty quantification methods
036   for neural PDE solvers often lack coverage guarantees (Zou et al., 2024), require expensive simula-
037   tion data (Gopakumar et al., 2024a), or necessitate model modifications (Abdar et al., 2021).
038
      To address these challenges, we propose a framework that leverages PDE residuals over neural PDEs
039
      to provide uncertainty estimates with guarantees. Our approach, outlined in fig. 1, takes obtaining
040   predictions from the neural PDE solver, evaluates the associated Physics Residual Errors (PRE),
041   and performs calibration using Conformal Prediction (CP) with both marginal (univariate) and joint
042   (multivariate) formulations. This method provides statistically valid, guaranteed coverage within
043   the residual space (Vovk et al., 2005), offering error bounds based on the violation of physical
044   conservation laws. The framework is model-agnostic, requires no additional data, and is physics-
045   informed. Through this work, we explore the (over)confidence issue of neural PDE solvers(Zou
046   et al., 2024). This novel approach to uncertainty quantification can potentially be extended to ODEs
047   and other systems where model performance can be expressed as a residual. Our contributions are:
048
            • Physics Residual Error as a nonconformity score for CP: We introduce a novel non-
049           conformity metric for CP over the residual space. Our approach is data-free and yields
050           input-independent prediction sets while relaxing exchangeability restrictions.
051
            • Marginal and Joint CP: Our approach provides guaranteed coverage bounds both
052
              marginally (per dimension) and jointly (entire prediction domain), enabling the identifi-
053           cation of regions of volatile predictions and rejection sampling across predictions.

071   2     R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Recently, CP, as a method of performing UQ, has been gaining popularity for usage with spatio-
074   temporal data Sun (2022). Several works have explored the inductive CP framework for spatial and
075   sequential data (Stankeviciute et al., 2021; Xu & Xie, 2021; Xu et al., 2023) including in the operator
076   space (Ma et al., 2024). In Gopakumar et al. (2024a), the marginal-CP framework is extended to
077   pre-trained as well as fine-tuned surrogate models for physical system modelling across an infinite-
078
      dimensional setting
079   The usage of PDE residuals under the guise of Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML) (Kar-
080   niadakis et al., 2021) was made popular as an optimisation strategy for Physics-Informed Neural
081   Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) and has found application in optimising neural operators
082   (Li et al., 2024) and soft/hard enforcement of the physical constraints to deep learning models (Du
083   et al., 2024; Chalapathi et al., 2024). However, they have rarely been used as a tool for providing
084
      UQ to the surrogate models; and where they have found application, UQ remained uncalibrated (Zhu
      et al., 2019). The majority of literature in UQ for neural PDE solvers has been looking at Bayesian
085
      methods, such as dropout, Bayesian neural networks and Monte Carlo methods (Geneva & Zabaras,
086
      2020; Zou et al., 2024; Psaros et al., 2023), which lack guarantees or are computationally expensive.

091   3.1   N EURAL PDE S OLVERS
092
093   Consider the generic formulation of a PDE modelling the spatio-temporal evolution of n field vari-
094   ables u across a range of initial conditions:
095
096                       D = Dt (u) + DX (u) = 0     u ∈ Rn , X ∈ Ω, t ∈ [0, T ],                       (1)
097                                    u(X, t) = g    X ∈ ∂Ω,                                            (2)
098                                   u(X, 0) = a(λ, X).                                                 (3)
099
100   Here, X defines the spatial domain bounded by Ω, t the temporal domain, DX and Dt , the composite
101   operators of the associated spatial and temporal derivatives. The PDE is further defined by the
102   boundary condition g and initial condition a, which can be parameterised by λ. The set of solutions
103   of field variables are expressed as u ∈ U.
104   Neural PDE solvers aim to learn the behaviour governed by eq. (1) using a parameterised neural
105   network (N N θ ). Starting from the initial conditions, the network is trained to solve the spatio-
106   temporal evolution of the fields given by Ω ∪ [0, T ]. Neural operators (N Oθ ) are a special class of
107   neural networks that learn the operator mapping from the function space of the PDE initial conditions
      a ∈ A to the function space of solutions u ∈ U. A neural operator for solving an initial value

110                 U = N Oθ (A),         u(X, t) = N Oθ u(X, 0)             X ∈ Ω, t ∈ [0, T ].             (4)

112   A Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) is an autoregressive neural operator that learns the spatio-
113   temporal evolution of PDE solution by leveraging the Fourier transform as the kernel integrator
114   (Li et al., 2021). The field evolution is learned using tuneable weight matrices of the network,
115   parameterised directly in the Fourier space of the PDE solutions.
116
      Since CP and our extension of it provide a post-hoc measure of quantifying the uncertainty of a
117   neural PDE, it remains agnostic to model choice and training conditions. Considering the model
118   independence of our approach, we restrict our experiments to modelling PDEs with an FNO. The
119   FNO is chosen due to its cost-accuracy trade-off and efficiency as demonstrated by de Hoop et al.
120   (2022) and Gopakumar et al. (2023b). CP over a range of neural-PDE solvers is has been applied by
121   Gopakumar et al. (2024a), where the authors demonstrate that the coverage guarantees are upheld
122   irrespective of the model choice, not needing us to experiment with various model architectures.

154

155

160   trained on approximate numerical data, are further prone to non-convergent predictions. In PDE
161   numerical analysis, the norm of the PDE residual is often used as a criterion for stability, conver-
      gence, and accuracy (Iserles, 2009). The PRE typically represents the violation of conservation laws

      The norm of the residual operator itself (|D(N Oθ (u))|) provides a measure of UQ for the neural
178   PDE. However, it is limited by the accuracy of the gradient estimation method and can become com-
179   putationally expensive when exploring a vast solution space (Tolsma & Barton, 1998). By using the
180   residual norm as a nonconformity score, we are further calibrating the approximate physics residual
181   error that is obtained by an inexpensive and coarse differential operator. CP using PRE provides
182   statistically valid and guaranteed error bars across the PDE’s residual space, incorporating physi-
183   cal information into the calibration procedure, and providing a calibrated measure of the physical
184   misalignment of the surrogate model.
185   PRE as a nonconformity score enables data-free conformal prediction. The estimated scores rely
186   only on the neural PDE predictions over a range of initial conditions, not on the target as in AER and
187   STD. The only criterion is that the calibration and prediction domains arise from exchangeable initial
188   conditions of the PDE. As shown in eq. (9), PRE allows for obtaining “prediction sets” indepen-
189   dent of the prediction inputs (see appendix B for formalism). Traditional CP methods by design
190   ensure that the predictions always fall within the estimated error bars (see eqs. (7) and (8)). They
191   only provide guarantees that the true solution will obey coverage determined by α. our CP-PRE
192
      formulation provides no guarantee that the true solution lies within the coverage bounds. Instead, it
      ensures that prediction outputs will lie within these bounds, allowing for the identification of predic-
193
      tions falling outside the coverage bounds Cα . Through our method, prediction sets can be validated
194
      without needing the true solution.

      4.1   M ARGINAL -CP
197
198   The CP formulation was initially conceptualised for calibrating univariate functions with single-
199   point outputs (Vovk et al., 2005). It has recently been extended to spatio-temporal data, with multi-
200   dimensional outputs with an immutable tensor structure (Gopakumar et al., 2024a). Within such
201   spatio-temporal settings, CP has been implemented to provide marginal coverage, i.e. the calibration
202   procedure provides independent error bars for each cell within the spatio-temporal domain. For an
203   output tensor (Y ) of shape (Y ∈ RNx ×Ny ×Nt ), where Nx , Ny , Nt represent the spatio-temporal
204   discretisation of the domain, marginal-CP uses the non-conformity scores outlined in eqs. (7) to (9)
205
      across each cell of Y to obtain error bars which will be compliant eq. (5) for each cell. Marginal-CP
      using PRE helps indicate regions within a single prediction that lie outside the calibrated physical
206
      bounds and require specific attention, treating those predictions with caution.
207
208
      4.2   J OINT-CP
209
210   The joint-CP formulation constructs a calibration procedure that provides coverage bands for mul-
211   tivariate functions. These coverage bands expand across the entire simulation domain Ω × [0, T ]
212   (discretised as RNx ×Ny ×Nt ) rather than an individual cell within it. For a coverage band (Cα ),
213   the joint-CP formulation ensures that 1 − α predictions/solutions would lie within the bounds. For
214   performing joint-CP, the non-conformity scores are modified to reflect the supremum of the score
215   functions S in eqs. (7) to (9). They are modulated by the standard-deviation σ of the calibration
      scores (Diquigiovanni et al., 2021) to allow for obtaining prediction bands with varying widths

      based on local behaviour (Diquigiovanni et al., 2022). The modifications of the score functions and
217   prediction sets to perform CP are given by

      where S and P are the formulations of the nonconformity scores and prediction sets used for
223   marginal-CP as shown in eqs. (7) to (9). Joint-CP becomes particularly useful in identifying pre-
224   dictions that fail to fall within coverage, allowing us to perform rejection sampling, where we can
225   accept or reject a prediction based on a predetermined probability. Similar to that demonstrated by
226   Casella et al. (2004), our framework can perform rejection sampling using a CP-based criterion. The
227   acceptance probability is based on confidence level α, allowing the joint-CP formulation using PRE
228   to filter through predictions of the neural PDE solver. Upon being rejected, the initial conditions that
229   led to those predictions could be provided to the expensive physics-based numerical PDE solver for
230   further evaluation.

232   4.3   D IFFERENTIAL O PERATOR : F INITE -D IFFERENCE S TENCILS AS C ONVOLUTIONAL
233
            K ERNELS
234
      Calibrating neural PDEs using PRE nonconformity scores requires frequent evaluations of the com-
235   posite differential operator (D) in eq. (1). For PDEs, this involves estimating numerous spatio-
236   temporal gradients across the discretised domain, ranging from millions in simple cases to billions
237   for complex physics. To address this computational challenge, we developed a scalable gradient
238   estimation method for evaluating physics residual error.
239
      We employ convolution operations with Finite Difference (FD) stencils as convolutional kernels
240
      for gradient estimation (Actor et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024a;b). For instance, the 2D Laplacian
241   operator ∇2 , using a central difference scheme with discretisation h, can be approximated by
242
                                                    1 0 1 0
                                                      "            #
243                                             2
                                             ∇ ≈ 2 1 −4 1                                           (12)
244                                                h 0 1 0
245
246   and used as a kernel. This approach is justified by the mathematical equivalence of FD approxima-
247
      tions and discrete convolutions in the discretised domain. Both represent matrix-vector multiplica-
      tions of a block Toeplitz matrix with a field vector (Strang, 1986; Fiorentino & Serra, 1991). The
248
      efficiency of this method stems from the optimised implementation of convolution operations in ma-
249
      chine learning libraries like PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and TensorFlow (Abadi et al., 2015). The
250   Basic linear Algebra subroutines (BLAS) within these libraries leverage vectorisation and efficient
251   memory access, resulting in significant performance improvements. Our experiments show a 1000x
252   speed-up using torch.nn.functional.conv3d compared to a numpy implementation of the equivalent
253   FD approximation on a standard CPU.
254
      The FD approximation offers several advantages over Automatic Differentiation (AD) for our ap-
255
      plication. It is compatible with CP as a post-hoc measure, requires no architectural modifications,
256   and is model-agnostic. Furthermore, FD implemented via convolutions is more memory-efficient
257   than AD, which requires storing the entire computational graph. Our focus on the (mis)alignment
258   of neural PDEs with eq. (1) allows us to disregard boundary conditions in our error bar estimations.

503   6   D ISCUSSION
504
505   If “All models are wrong, but some are useful” (Box, 1976), through this work, we explore a novel
506
      framework for providing data-free, model and domain agnostic measure of usefulness of neural PDE
      models. We deploy a principled method of evaluating the accuracy of the solution, i.e. its (calibrated)
507
      obedience to the known physics of the system under study. As opposed to other methods of UQ
508
      for neural PDEs, our method is physics-informed and offers coverage guarantees. This calibration
509   procedure is not limited to PDE modelling but may apply to ODEs or any other scenario where
510   the model outputs can be framed as a residual. We conclude with a discussion of the strengths,
511   limitations and potential improvements.
512
513   Strengths The PRE estimates the violation of conservation laws in neural PDE predictions, en-
514   abling calibration of physics deviation with statistical coverage guarantees. This post-hoc uncer-
515   tainty quantification is model- and physics-agnostic, scaling linearly with model complexity and
516   quasi-linearly with PDE complexity due to the additive nature of differential operators. Our frame-
517   work reformulates CP to be data-free, expressing model inaccuracy solely through PRE, and does
518   not require a labelled dataset. This approach reduces calibration costs and loosens exchangeabil-
519
      ity restrictions. The PRE formulation (section 4, appendix B) yields input-independent prediction
      sets, allowing for the identification of weak predictions within single simulations (marginal-CP) and
520
      across multiple predictions (joint-CP). The latter enables rejection sampling, potentially serving as
521
      an active-learning pipeline for neural PDE solvers (Musekamp et al., 2024).
522
523
      Limitations Our method’s coverage bounds exist in the PDE residual space rather than the Eu-
524   clidean space of physical variables. Transforming to physical space involves challenging set propa-
525   gation through integral operations, which may require linear approximations (Teng et al., 2023) or
526   expensive MCMC sampling (Andrieu et al., 2003) for complex PDEs. The data-free approach lacks
527   a grounding target for calibration, though we argue that a large sample of model outputs provides
528   a statistically significant overview of uncertainty. The sampling cost from the neural-PDE solver
529   for calibration must be considered. The modulation function in Equation (11) improves local bound
530   representation but compromises CP guarantee assumptions in the joint setting (fig. 2). PRE estima-
531   tion using finite-difference stencils also introduces the errors associated with Taylor expansion. The
532
      current formulation is limited to regular grids with fixed spacing, though extensions to unstructured
      grids via graph convolutions are possible (Eliasof & Treister, 2020).
533
534
535   7   C ONCLUSION
536
537   The PRE-CP formulation provides a novel framework for providing guaranteed and physics-
538   informed uncertainty estimates for each cell within a prediction as well as across predictions. Our
539   work enhances the reliability of neural PDE solvers, potentially broadening their applicability in
      science and engineering domains where robust uncertainty quantification is crucial.

       K    U TILISING PRE-CP AS A MEASURE OF MODEL QUALITY
2161
2162
       While evaluating the performance of a neural-PDE, it is important to their fit not just to the data but
2163   to the underlying physics. PRE-CP will provide guaranteed coverage irregardless of the quality of
2164   the model. It will have considerably wider error bounds when the neural-PDE (whether PINN or a
2165   Neural Operator) fails to comply with the physics. However, we believe that this is an advantage
2166   of our method. In PRE-CP formulation, the bounds are estimated across the PDE residual, where
2167   the ground truth for a well-fit solution should always be near zero. If we get wide error bars further
2168   away from the 0 for potentially high coverage estimates, it is a strong indication that statistically the
2169   model violates the physics of interest.
2170   Consider the example with the Advection equation. We have two models, a well-fit (good model)
2171   and a poorly fit one (bad model). As shown in fig. 23, though we obtain guaranteed coverage in
2172   the case of both the bad and good models, the width of the error bars indicates the quality of the
2173   model. Taken for 90 % coverage, the width of the coverage bounds obtained over the bad model is
2174   substantially larger than that obtained by the good model.
