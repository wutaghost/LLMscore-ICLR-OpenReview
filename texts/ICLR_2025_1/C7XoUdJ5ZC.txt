009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL) enables collaborative model training across decentralized
012            clients while preserving data privacy. However, its performance declines in chal-
013            lenging heterogeneous data settings. To mitigate this, existing FL frameworks not
014            only share locally trained parameters but also exchange additional information –
015            such as control variates, client features, and classifier characteristics – to address
016            the effects of class imbalance and missing classes. However, this leads to in-
017            creased communication costs and heightened risks of privacy breaches. To strike
018
               a balance between communication efficiency, privacy protection, and adaptabil-
               ity to heterogeneous data distributions, we propose FLAIR, a novel FL approach
019
               with augmented and improved feature representations. FLAIR utilizes Class Vari-
020
               ational Autoencoders (CVAE) for feature augmentation, mitigating class imbal-
021            ance and missing class issues. It also incorporates Reptile meta-training to fa-
022            cilitate knowledge transfer between model updates, adapting to dynamic feature
023            shifts. To generalize model update, FLAIR shares only local CVAE parameters
024            instead of local model parameters, which reduces both communication costs and
025            privacy risks. Our experiments on benchmark datasets – such as MNIST, CIFAR-
026            10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet – demonstrates a significant enhancement in
027            model convergence and accuracy compared to state-of-the-art solutions, while re-
028            ducing communication overhead and privacy risks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Federated Learning (FL) has gained prominence as an effective approach for collaboratively training
      machine learning models across decentralized datasets, ensuring data privacy by eliminating the
034
      need to share raw data between clients McMahan et al. (2017). Despite its potential, FL performance
035
      tends to degrade significantly when data distributions across clients are highly heterogeneous or non-
036   identically distributed (non-IID) Zhao et al. (2018), posing a critical challenge for many real-world
037   applications.
038
039
      Addressing this issue has sparked substantial research, as recent advancement embraces various
      strategies, such as variance reduction Acar et al. (2021); Karimireddy et al. (2020), adaptive aggre-
040
      gation Hsu et al. (2019); Reddi et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2023), feature distillation Yang et al. (2023),
041
      representation learning Zhang et al. (2020); Tan et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024) etc., to mitigate the
042   impact of non-IID data on model convergence and performance in FL settings. As these strategies
043   often involve sharing additional information among clients and the server, they lead to increased
044   communication costs and heightened risks of privacy breaches, such as membership inference, fea-
045   tures inference, gradient leakage, and model inversion attacks, etc., Nasr et al. (2019); Melis et al.
046   (2019); Wang et al. (2019b). These factors can limit the practical applicability of state-of-the-art
047   approaches, especially in scenarios where communication efficiency and privacy are critical, such
048   as in mobile edge computing Wang et al. (2019a), internet of things (IoT) networks Nguyen et al.
049   (2021), and healthcare applications Xu & Wang (2021). A comparative summary of the communi-
050
      cation cost incurred by state-of-the-art approaches is shown in Table 1. Therefore, there is a clear
      need for a holistic approach that improves performance in heterogeneous settings while addressing
051
      communication overhead and enhancing privacy protection in the FL system.
052
053   In response to this, we present FLAIR (Federated Learning with Augmented and Improved fea-
      ture Representations), a novel FL framework designed to systematically guide local training based

071

084   representation learning techniques. This holistic strategy not only addresses the symptoms of non-
085   IID data (e.g., model divergence) but also improves communication overhead and privacy of clients.

087
088          1. We propose FLAIR, a novel approach that addresses the challenges of learning from ex-
089             treme non-IID data distributions in federated settings, which reduces communication over-
090             head and privacy risks.

094          3. We adopt Reptile meta-training approach in FL to mitigate dynamic features drift for sta-

096

097             through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets.

      2.3   CVAE FOR F EATURE G ENERATION
175
176   To alleviate the impact of non-IID data in federated learning, we propose training a CVAE
177   model on each client’s local dataset to model the class-conditional feature distributions p(z|fx , y)
178   and p(fy |z, y). The CVAE consists of an encoder network qϕ (z|fx , y) and a decoder network
179   pψ (fy |z, y), parameterized by ϕ and ψ, respectively. Given an input feature vector fx ∈ Rd and its
180   corresponding label y ∈ 1, . . . , C, the encoder maps (fx , y) to a latent code z ∈ Rl , where l is the
181   dimensionality of the latent space. The latent code z is assumed to follow a multivariate Gaussian
182   distribution N (µ, diag(σ 2 )), where the mean µ ∈ Rl and variance σ 2 ∈ Rl are outputs of the
183   encoder network. The decoder network takes as input a latent code z sampled from N (µ, diag(σ 2 ))
184   and the label y, and reconstructs the input feature vector fˆx . The goal is to maximize the likelihood
185   of the input features given the latent code and labels, i.e. pψ (fx |z, y). For each client k, the CVAE
186   model is trained by maximizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO) of the log-likelihood:

      2.4   R EPTILE - BASED CVAE M ODEL T RAINING
241
242   To address the challenge of dynamic feature shifts in federated learning, we utilize Reptile meta-
243   learning based CVAE model training. This technique is employed after each client’s local update to
244   adapt the CVAE model to the local data distribution while preserving the global knowledge.
245
      The Reptile algorithm is a first-order meta-learning approach that aims to find a good initialization
246
      of model parameters that can quickly adapt to new tasks with a few gradient steps. In the context
247   of federated learning, we treat each client’s local data as a separate task and use Reptile to learn a
248   meta-initialization of the CVAE parameters that can rapidly adapt to the local data distributions. For
249   a client k and its local dataset Dk , the Reptile-based CVAE training proceeds as follows:
250
      In each communication round t, the server sends the global CVAE parameters Φt , Ψt to the selected
251
      clients. Each client k initializes its local CVAE parameters ϕk,t , ψk,t with Φt , Ψt and performs E
252
      epochs of training on its local dataset Dk . During CVAE model training, the client’s locally trained
253   model F(θk,t+1 ) is used to extract local features fx from the input data x, and the CVAE model
254
      takes the features fx and labels y as input to reconstruct the features fˆx , generate latent variables
255   z, and predict the labels ŷ. The CVAE parameters ϕk,t , ψk,t are updated by minmizing LCVAE loss.
256   The overall training process is depicted in Algorithm 1.
257
258
      The Reptile-based CVAE training allows the model to adapt to the local data distributions of each
      client while maintaining the global knowledge learned across all clients. This approach helps miti-
259
      gate the impact of dynamic feature shifts and enables more effective federated learning in non-IID
260
      settings.

      4.1       I MPLEMENTATION
332
      We implement FLAIR and the baseline methods using Python 3.9, leveraging the PyTorch library1 .
333   The codebase consists of 7,718 lines of code (LoC). For local training, we utilized two distinct neural
334   network architectures: LeNet-5 and ResNet-18. The details of their architectures are as follows:
335
336             • LeNet-5: A 7-layer convolutional neural network (CNN) featuring 5x5 convolutional lay-
337               ers, tanh activations, and average pooling.
338             • ResNet-18: A CNN beginning with a 7x7 convolutional layer, followed by 4 residual
339               blocks, batch normalization, ReLU activation, and global average pooling.
340
341   In addition to local classification models, we have a CVAE model meant to learn enhanced features
342   representation. The CVAE model architecture consists of two components encoder and decoder
343
      described as follows:
344
                • Encoder: Two fully connected layers with batch normalization and ReLU activations. In-
345
                  put is the concatenation of input features and one-hot encoded class labels.
346
347
                • Decoder: Two fully connected layers, batch normalization, ReLU activations, and an out-
                  put layer for reconstruction.
348
349
      We employ the PyTorch SGD optimizer for updating model parameters during training.

      4.2       E XPERIMENT S ETUP
352
353   In this study, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of FLAIR, by comparing its performance
354   against state-of-the-art approaches. To this aim, we consider the following 6 baseline methods:
355   FedAvg, SCAFFOLD, FedFA, FedProto, Elastic, and FLUTE, which represent a diverse range of
356   strategies for federated learning. Our experiments are performed on three widely-used datasets:
357   MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet, each presenting unique challenges and char-
358   acteristics. To investigate the effectiveness of FLAIR and the baseline methods under different model
359   architectures, we employ a variety of neural networks for local training. Specifically, when training
360
      on the MNIST dataset, we utilize LeNet-5 model, which is well-suited for the task of handwrit-
      ten digit recognition. For more complex CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet datasets, we
361
      employ ResNet-18 model, which already depicted a superior performance on image classification
362
      tasks. To ensure fairness and reproducibility of our comparisons, we maintain fixed random seeds
363   and consistent settings across all experiments. This allows us to isolate the impact of federated
364   learning algorithms on model performance, minimizing the influence of random variations. For all
365   the experiments we use local model learning rate ηl = 0.01, batch size 16, number of local epochs
366   5, and for FLAIR’s CVAE model training we use Adam optimizer with learning rate ηCVAE = 0.001.
367   We conduct 150 communication rounds FL training for MNIST, 250 rounds for CIFAR-10, and 200
368   rounds for CIFAR-100 and TinyImageNet dataset.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
477
      Federated learning was first introduced in FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) algorithm. This method
478
      allows clients to collaboratively train models without directly sharing their data, thus preserving
479
      privacy. However, despite the inherent privacy benefits, it presents several key challenges, partic-
480   ularly high communication costs and instability in model training due to data heterogeneity across
481   clients. FedAvgM Hsu et al. (2019) integrates momentum into the global model updates, speeding
482   up convergence in non-IID settings. While this reduces the number of communication rounds re-
483   quired, it introduces the need for careful hyperparameter tuning to maintain stability in diverse data
484   environments. FedProx Li et al. (2020) introduced a proximal term in the local objective function to
485   reduce client drift, which indirectly reduces communication by requiring fewer updates to converge.
      However, FedProx still struggles in extreme non-IID distribution settings. SCAFFOLD Karimireddy


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      et al. (2020) attempts to directly address client drift using control variates to stabilize local updates,
487   achieving better convergence with fewer rounds of communication, albeit with an increase in per-
488   round communication overhead due to additional information exchanged between clients and server.
489   Eastic Chen et al. (2023) improves convergence through aggregation by interpolating client models
490   according to its parameter sensitivity. However this requires sharing of additional client’s parameters
491   sensitivity with the server and struggle in extreme heterogeneous settings. FedFA Zhou et al. (2024)
492   is mainly used to address the issue of feature skewness by utilizing feature anchors but struggles
493   in others heterogeneous settings. FedProto Tan et al. (2022) addresses data heterogeneity in feder-
494   ated learning by aligning global feature distributions across clients. While this approach effectively
495
      improves model performance, it shares class-specific features, potentially exposing sensitive class
      information and making the system vulnerable to privacy attacks, such as feature inversion Wang
496
      et al. (2019b). FedBN Li et al. (2021), on the other hand, applies client-specific batch normalization
497
      layers to handle feature skew, but fails to generalize in settings with severe data imbalance, leading
498   to increased communication rounds as the global model struggles to converge. Recent works such
499   as FedFed Yang et al. (2023) and FLUTE Liu et al. (2024) explore more advanced techniques to
500   address data heterogeneity. FedFed enhances model accuracy by performing feature augmentation
501   across clients but introduces significant communication and computational overhead due to the need
502   for sharing additional feature information. Similarly, FLUTE employs feature learning and classi-
503   fier calibration to address heterogeneity, but it suffers from the need for extensive hyperparameter
504   tuning and full client participation, which leads to increased communication demands. In summary,
505   while most of the existing approaches primarily focus in addressing data heterogeneity, they often
506
      introduce increased communication costs and heightened risks of privacy breaches.

508   6    C ONCLUSION
509
510
      This paper presents a novel FL approach, aiming to achieve a balance between performance, commu-
511   nication cost, and privacy. This is achieved by utilizing CVAE based feature augmentation approach
512   which helps in developing generalized and robust local models, making them effective in addressing
513   extreme heterogeneity. In particular, the augmented features helps the local models to promote con-
514   sistency in intra-class latent representations while simultaneously amplifying inter-class distinctions.
515   Further to adapt dynamic feature shifts in CVAE model we utilize Reptile meta-training approach.
516   Unlike existing approaches, the sharing of only CVAE model parameters, rather than local model
517   parameters, reduces privacy risks and communication overhead. Experimental results demonstrates
518   a significant performance improvement by 2.61% on an average in terms of accuracy with respect
519
      to the second best performer in the literature. This highlights FLAIR’s ability to enhance the per-
      formance of underrepresented classes in clients, leading to a more balanced and equitable learning
520
      outcome. Furthermore, FLAIR exhibits faster convergence rates with reduced communication cost
521
      compared to existing methods, maintaining its performance advantages even in extreme heteroge-
522   neous settings.
