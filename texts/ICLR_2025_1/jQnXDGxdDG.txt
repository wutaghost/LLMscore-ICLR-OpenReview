009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Anomaly detection involves identifying data patterns that deviate from the an-
012            ticipated norm. Traditional methods struggle in high-dimensional spaces due to
013            the curse of dimensionality. In recent years, self-supervised learning, particu-
014            larly through contrastive objectives, has driven advances in anomaly detection
015            by generating compact and discriminative feature spaces. However, vanilla con-
016            trastive learning faces challenges like class collision, especially when the In-
017            Distribution (ID) consists primarily of normal, homogeneous data, where the
018
               lack of semantic diversity leads to increased overlap between positive and neg-
               ative pairs. Existing methods attempt to address these issues by introducing hard
019
               negatives through synthetic outliers, Outlier Exposure (OE), or supervised ob-
020
               jectives, though these approaches can introduce additional challenges. In this
021            work, we propose the Focused In-distribution Representation Modeling (FIRM)
022            loss, a novel multi-positive contrastive objective for anomaly detection. FIRM ad-
023            dresses class-collision by explicitly encouraging ID representations to be compact
024            while promoting separation among synthetic outliers. We show that FIRM sur-
025            passes other contrastive methods in standard benchmarks, significantly enhancing
026            anomaly detection compared to both traditional and supervised contrastive learn-
027            ing objectives. Our ablation studies confirm that FIRM consistently improves
028            the quality of representations and shows robustness across a range of scoring
029
               methods. It performs particularly well in ensemble settings and benefits substan-
               tially from using OE. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
030
               science/r/firm-8472/.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Anomaly detection is essential for identifying rare or unusual patterns in data, ensuring robust-
037   ness, maintaining data quality, and preventing failures across critical applications like cybersecu-
038   rity, healthcare, and autonomous systems. This task is closely related to fields such as Out-of-
039
      distribution (OOD) detection, novelty detection, and one-class classification, all of which define a
      specific ID to identify data points that do not conform to expected patterns under the open-world
040
      assumption (Yang et al., 2024). While both anomaly and OOD detection deal with deviations from
041
      the ID, OOD detection typically involves distinguishing between predefined classes and relies on
042   a labeled dataset, where encouraging interclass variance in the representation space is crucial for
043   classification. In contrast, anomaly detection focuses on identifying deviations from a singular nor-
044   mal class, where minimizing intraclass variance and tightly clustering ID representations is essen-
045   tial (Ruff et al., 2018; Ming et al., 2023), particularly when the ID is naturally homogeneous.
046
      Recent methodologies in anomaly detection have increasingly leveraged distance-based metrics,
047
      utilizing deep representation learning extracted from models (Ruff et al., 2018; Tack et al., 2020; Sun
048   et al., 2022; Reiss & Hoshen, 2023). These methods operate under the assumption that OOD samples
049   are situated relatively far from the compact clusters formed by ID data. By quantifying angular
050   separation or spatial distance between test samples and ID representations, distance-based methods
051   distinguish between ID and OOD instances based on their relative positioning in the representation
052   space. Their success, however, is closely tied to the quality of the learned embeddings—compact,
053   well-separated ID representations are essential for ensuring clear boundaries between normal and
      anomalous data (Ming et al., 2023).

054
      Recent progress in self-supervised learning has highlighted the efficacy of contrastive learning in
055   learning representations across various domains, including applications such as computer vision
056   and audio processing (Sohn, 2016; Wu et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020a;b; He et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
057   2020c; 2021; Tian et al., 2024). These advancements have also extended to anomaly and OOD
058   detection. Recent studies on contrastive learning have achieved state-of-the-art results in anomaly
059   detection and related tasks such as one-class classification and outlier detection (Sohn et al., 2021;
060   Tack et al., 2020; Sehwag et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022; Reiss & Hoshen, 2023). These methods
061   rely on synthetic outliers, also referred to as virtual outliers (Du et al., 2022), which can be gener-
062   ated through transformations such as rotations (Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2019),
063
      adversarial training (Lee et al., 2018), or sampled in the form of OE (Hendrycks et al., 2018). Even
      though not necessarily from the same distribution as the actual anomalies, the synthetic outliers
064
      serve as hard negatives (Robinson et al., 2020) to the ID during contrastive training. These strate-
065
      gies show effectiveness in reducing the uniformity of representations (Sohn et al., 2021), a problem
066   that occurs when learning ID representations with vanilla contrastive objectives (Sohn, 2016; Oord
067   et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020a) that promote a uniform distribution on the unit hypersphere (Wang
068   & Isola, 2020a), especially in scenarios with large batch sizes.
069
      Traditional contrastive learning training objectives maximize the similarity between semantically
070
      related instances while minimizing the similarity from less related, randomly chosen negative pairs.
071
      Applying these objectives within anomaly detection settings that rely solely on ID samples inadver-
072   tently leads to class collision (Arora et al., 2019). Although incorporating synthetic outliers partially
073   addresses this issue, the foundational structure of vanilla contrastive objectives (Sohn, 2016; Oord
074   et al., 2018), such NT-Xent (Chen et al., 2020a), which relies on a single-positive pairing strategy,
075   continues to promote unnecessary intraclass variance among ID representations. Alternatively, Su-
076   pervised Contrastive (SupCon) (Khosla et al., 2020) could be employed to reduce intraclass variance
077   by encouraging tighter clustering of representations for both ID and synthetic outliers. However, this
078   approach assumes homogeneity within both groups and does not account for the inherent semantic
079   variability among synthetic outliers, as is commonly observed with OE. To overcome these limi-
080
      tations, we propose the FIRM loss function, a multi-positive contrastive training objective tailored
      for anomaly detection. The FIRM objective is designed to (1) reduce intraclass variance among
081
      ID samples, encouraging stronger alignment of ID representations in the feature space, (2) promote
082
      representation diversity among synthetic outliers, preventing mode collapse and ensuring clear dis-
083   tinction from ID samples. Our main contributions include:
084
085          • We introduce FIRM, a novel contrastive objective for anomaly detection, demonstrating
086            superior performance compared to recent similar methods (Sohn et al., 2021; Tack et al.,
087            2020) that rely on traditional contrastive learning approaches.
088          • We extend the applicability of FIRM to unlabeled multiclass OOD detection, demonstrat-
089            ing capabilities while handling non-homogeneous and multimodal ID, even on large-scale
090            datasets like ImageNet.
091          • Through extensive ablation studies, we provide insights into the behavior of FIRM and
092            its advantages over other contrastive objectives for anomaly detection. Our experiments
093            highlight significant improvements in representation quality, particularly with OE.

      Anomaly detection aims to effectively distinguish normal (ID) samples from OOD anomalies. Given
098
      a dataset Din = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xN } of N ID samples drawn from Pin over the input space X , the ob-
099
      jective is to learn an encoder fθ : X → Rd that ideally maps all ID samples to a single embedding
100
101
      v ∈ Rd , such that fθ (x) = v for all x, while anomalies x′ that diverge from Pin are projected to dis-
102
      tinct points such that fθ (x′ ) ∈ / {v}. While this idealized behavior may not be practically attainable,
103
      it serves as a guiding principle for designing objectives, particularly for those cases where dis-
104   tance-based metrics such as cosine similarity are employed wherein ID samples are concentrated in
105   high-density areas, and OOD samples are mapped to dispersed regions, maximizing angular separa-
106   tion. Conventional contrastive learning objectives, such as NT-Xent, encourage excessive separation
107   among ID samples, countering the goal of compact ID clustering. Similarly, binary approaches like
      SupCon, which treat all anomalies as a single class, fail to capture their inherent diversity, yielding

      Learning through contrastive objectives. Self-supervised learning frameworks, like contrastive
116
      representation learning, optimize a loss function to bring representations of semantically similar
117
      samples closer together while pushing dissimilar ones farther apart. For a given unlabeled sample
118   xi ∼ X , a stochastic data augmentation function α is applied to create two correlated instances, de-
119   noted as a positive pair (x̃i , x̃i+ ). In a minibatch of n samples, the augmentation process leads to a
120   multiview minibatch B = {1, . . . , 2n}. Within this multiview batch, each view x̃i and its counterpart
121   x̃i+ serve as the anchor and the positive respectively, while all other samples are treated as negatives.
122   Each view within B is encoded via a neural network encoder fθ , parametrized by θ, into representa-
123   tion vectors fθ (x̃i ) ∈ Rd . The encoder’s output is further transformed by a projection network gψ ,
124   parametrized by ψ, into a lower-dimensional space, resulting in vectors zi = gψ (fθ (x̃i )) ∈ Rdhead
125   where dhead < d (Gidaris et al., 2018). The core of the learning process is driven by a contrastive
126   objective function (Sohn, 2016; Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020a), which encourages the maxi-
127   mization of the similarity between the representations of the anchor and the positive while minimiz-
128
      ing similarity to negatives. Following this notation, the contrastive loss (Chen et al., 2020a) takes
      the following form:

139   Learning ID representations with synthetic outliers. Contrastive learning relies heavily on neg-
140   ative samples for effective training, as evidenced by the denominator in Equation (1). Learning
141   robust ID representations is challenging when the training data consists solely of normal samples.
142
      This limitation results in minibatches B consisting solely of ID samples, where negatives for any
      given positive pair, derived by augmenting an ID sample, are also other ID samples, which exacer-
143
      bates class collision (Arora et al., 2019). To address this issue and promote clustering of ID samples’
144
      representations, the training data can be expanded through the inclusion of synthetic outliers, either
145   generated from Pin to closely mimic ID characteristics while lying on the low-density boundary of
146   the ID space, or sourced from external datasets in the form of OE. These synthetic outliers, whether
147   generated or sourced, act as hard negatives that enhance the model’s discriminative capability by
148   challenging its ability to differentiate between closely similar ID and synthetic OOD samples.

188   2.1                    M ULTI -P OSITIVE O BJECTIVE FOR I N -D ISTRIBUTION R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
189
      Integrating synthetic outliers into the contrastive learning framework provides essential negative
190
      samples that enhance the learning of ID representations for anomaly detection. However, employ-
191   ing standard objectives such as the NT-Xent loss within this setting, which inherently rely on a
192   single-positive pairing strategy (see the numerator in Equation (1)), inadvertently still encourages
193   intraclass variance among ID samples. This approach can hinder the model’s ability to learn effec-
194   tive ID representations for anomaly detection, particularly when the ID is naturally homogeneous
195   or unimodal, as it works against the goal of guiding the model toward optimal regions of the pa-
196   rameter space, i.e., regions where ID samples are consistently mapped to well-defined areas in the
197   representation space, with minimal variance, and clear separation from OOD samples.
198   To address this challenge, we propose FIRM training objective that relies on a multi-positive con-
199   trastive learning strategy that extends the traditional contrastive loss formulation by incorporating
200   multiple positive pairings exclusively for ID samples. Specifically, for each ID anchor, we identify
201   multiple ID positives and modify the objective to align the anchor with all of these positives. In
202   contrast, synthetic outliers are not assigned multiple positives, as they may be semantically diverse,
203   and this diversity can be leveraged to learn a discriminative representation space.
204
205   Training objective. Let the ID dataset be denoted by Din , and the synthetic outliers by Dsout . We
206   assign labels such that each ID sample xi ∈ Din is assigned yi = 1, and each synthetic outlier
207
      xj ∈ Dsout is assigned yj = ysout , where ysout ̸= 1 indicates a label for synthetic outliers. Consider
      a multiview minibatch B = {1, . . . , 2n}, which comprises indices representing pairs of augmented
208
      instances derived from samples in Din ∪ Dsout . For each anchor i ∈ B, let P(i) ⊆ B denote the
209
      set of corresponding positive samples for i. In the traditional NT-Xent loss, P(i) is a singleton,
210   i.e., P(i) = {i+ }. To incorporate multiple positives for ID samples, we redefine the set of positive
211   samples as P(i) = {i+ } ∪ {j ∈ B \ {i} | yj = 1}. For ID anchors i, P(i) now includes not
212   only its paired augmented view but also all other ID samples within minibatch. For anchors j
213   corresponding to synthetic outliers, P(j) consists solely of the paired index j + . Since synthetic
214   outliers may exhibit significant semantic variation relative to the ID and among themselves, we
215   retain a single-positive strategy for outliers. This approach maintains the optimization focus on
      minimizing intraclass variance for ID representations while preserving the model’s ability to capture

481   Self-supervised learning. Self-supervised learning (Gidaris et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2019;
482   Kolesnikov et al., 2019), particularly contrastive learning (Oord et al., 2018) via instance discrim-
483   ination (Wu et al., 2018), has demonstrated exceptional success in visual representation learning
484   within unsupervised settings (Chen et al., 2020a; He et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020c; 2021). Con-
485   trastive objectives like InfoNCE (Oord et al., 2018) and NT-Xent (Chen et al., 2020a) employ a
      self-labeling approach, aligning positive samples in the latent space while separating negatives. Su-


                                                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      pervised methods such as SupCon (Khosla et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2024) extend this by leveraging
487   label information, enabling multiple positive samples from the same class to be clustered more
488   tightly while enhancing class separation.
489
490
      Anomaly detection. Traditional methods for anomaly detection span a range of approaches,
491   from reconstruction-based techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Jolliffe, 2002)
492   to distance-based methods such as k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) and density estimation techniques
493   like KDE (Parzen, 1962) and mixture models (Bishop & Nasrabadi, 2006). One-class classifica-
494   tion algorithms, including OC-SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001) and Support Vector Data Descrip-
495   tion (SVDD) (Tax & Duin, 2004), are also commonly employed. However, these approaches face
496   significant challenges in high-dimensional spaces, where the curse of dimensionality (Ghosal et al.,
497   2024) makes it difficult to model the data effectively. They rely heavily on compact representations
498   that capture the intrinsic structure of the ID data manifold (Sohn et al., 2021).
499
500   Representation learning for anomaly detection. The limitations of classical approaches have
501   driven significant interest in developing deep learning methods for anomaly detection and related
502   tasks (Zhai et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2017; Ruff et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2018; 2019; Bergman
503
      & Hoshen, 2020; Liznerski et al., 2022; Reiss & Hoshen, 2023). Most of these approaches operate in
      an unsupervised setting, utilizing a large corpus of unlabeled data containing normal samples (Ruff
504
      et al., 2018; Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018). However, it has been shown that incorporating additional
505
      data in the form of synthetic outliers can significantly enhance detection performance (Lee et al.,
506   2018; Tack et al., 2020; Sohn et al., 2021; Du et al., 2022). Along similar lines, leveraging random
507   samples from large datasets, commonly referred to as OE (Hendrycks et al., 2018), in combination
508   with self-supervised learning (Hendrycks et al., 2019) or transfer learning (Reiss et al., 2021) has
509   also been demonstrated to improve anomaly detection capabilities. Recent works have explored
510   the application of contrastive learning for anomaly detection (Sohn et al., 2021; Reiss & Hoshen,
511   2023), particularly in OOD detection (Li et al., 2021c; Tack et al., 2020; Wang & Isola, 2020b;
512   Sehwag et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021b; Wang & Liu, 2021; Sun et al., 2022; Ming et al., 2023) and
513   open-set domains (Bucci et al., 2022), with most approaches employing either NT-Xent or SupCon
514
      contrastive objectives. Some works have proposed modified formulations for these tasks. Reiss
      & Hoshen (2023) adapts the contrastive loss for anomaly detection, improving performance but
515
      still relying on pre-trained models and offering no gains when training from scratch. Ming et al.
516
      (2023) focused on compactness and dispersion in labeled OOD detection, highlighting intraclass
517   compactness and interclass separation. Similarly, Wang et al. (2023) proposes UniCon-HA, a con-
518   trastive learning framework combining hierarchical augmentations and re-weighting mechanisms to
519
      enhance the concentration of inliers and dispersion of outliers. Unlike UniCon-HA, which relies
520
      on complex data augmentation strategies to improve anomaly detection performance with a multi-
521
      positive contrastive loss, our work focuses on defining the characteristics of an optimal encoder for
522
      anomaly detection and aligning a contrastive learning objective with these characteristics. We aim
523
      to establish a clear connection between the design of the training objective and the optimal encoding
524
      behavior for effective anomaly detection, without relying on intricate data augmentations.

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529   This work introduces the Focused In-distribution Representation Modeling (FIRM) loss func-
530   tion, designed to enhance in-distribution representation learning for robust anomaly and out-of-
531   distribution detection. Through comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets, FIRM has
532   proven effective in leveraging synthetic outliers to improve the discriminative capabilities of mod-
533   els, particularly under outlier exposure settings. Our results demonstrate the intricate interplay be-
534   tween various factors, including the training objective, which plays a critical role in achieving robust
535
      representations and improved model performance.
