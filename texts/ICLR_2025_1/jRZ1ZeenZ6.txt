009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Being prompted to engage in reasoning has emerged as a core technique for using large
012
               language models (LLMs), deploying additional inference-time compute to improve task
013            performance. However, as LLMs increase in both size and adoption, inference costs
014            are correspondingly becoming increasingly burdensome. How, then, might we optimize
015            reasoning’s cost-performance tradeoff? This work introduces a novel approach based
016            on computational models of metareasoning used in cognitive science, training LLMs to
017            selectively use intermediate reasoning steps only when necessary. We first develop a reward
018            function that incorporates the Value of Computation by penalizing unnecessary reasoning,
019            then use this reward function with Expert Iteration to train the LLM. Compared to few-shot
020            chain-of-thought prompting and STaR, our method significantly reduces inference costs
021
               (20-37% fewer tokens generated across three models) while maintaining task performance
               across diverse datasets.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLMs) rely on substantial computational power to handle complex problems
028   (OpenAI et al., 2024; Chowdhery et al., 2022; de Vries, 2023). While initial studies mostly focused on the
029   cost of training (Verdecchia et al., 2023), LLMs’ widespread deployment has made inference-time costs an
030   increasingly important factor. Model compression techniques such as quantization, pruning, and knowledge
031   distillation can lower post-training costs (Wan et al., 2024). However, there is a fundamental tension between
032   inference cost and task performance: while many of these methods reduce costs at the expense of performance,
033
      others, such as chain-of-thought prompting (CoT; Wei et al., 2023; Kojima et al., 2023), do the opposite,
      raising inference costs to enhance task performance (Snell et al., 2024). It is worth noting that none of the
034
      previous approaches are adaptive: model compression modifications and existing CoT methods tend to raise
035
      or lower the inference cost on all queries, regardless of task complexity.
036
037   In stark contrast to this static tradeoff, humans are able to adaptively allocate computational resources based
038   on task difficulty (Krämer, 2014; Russell, 1997; Lieder & Griffiths, 2017). In this work, we draw inspiration
039
      from rational metareasoning – literally, reasoning about reasoning – a concept originally from the artificial
      intelligence literature (Russell & Wefald, 1991) that has been used to explain how humans adaptively manage
040
      computational resources (Lieder & Griffiths, 2017; Lieder et al., 2018; Griffiths et al., 2019a). Building
041
      on this, we develop a novel reward function based on the Value of Computation (VOC; Russell & Wefald,
042   1991), which formalizes the trade-off between inference cost and task performance. We adopt an iterative
043   reinforcement learning process inspired by the Expert Iteration algorithm (Anthony et al., 2017). In each
044   iteration, we generate multiple reasoning chains for each question. These reasoning chains are ranked using
045   the reward function, and the dataset is filtered to retain only the best reasoning chain for each question. The
046   model is then fine-tuned using this filtered dataset. However, unlike previous applications of Expert Iteration

                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      to LLMs (Zelikman et al., 2022), which filter generated examples based on correctness, our method optimizes
048
      for both the correctness and the cost of the reasoning process.
049
050   We evaluated the effectiveness of our solution across a diverse set of tasks, from science knowledge (ARC;
051   Clark et al., 2018) to commonsense reasoning (CommonsenseQA; Talmor et al., 2019), mathematical problem
052
      solving (GSM8K; Cobbe et al., 2021), and logical deductive reasoning (ProofWriter; Tafjord et al., 2021).
      Additionally, we assess the out-of-domain generalization on MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), a multitask
053
      benchmark. Our approach achieves a substantial reduction in generated tokens (35.6-37.4% compared to
054
      few-shot prompting and 20.1-27.3% compared to STaR; Zelikman et al. 2022) while retaining comparable
055   performance. Thus, we make the following contributions:
056
057
            1. We introduce the idea of using rational metareasoning to optimize the tradeoff between inference
058            cost and performance of LLMs.
059
060         2. We formalize a novel reward function inspired by the Value of Computation (VOC) and integrate it
061            into LLM training.
062
063         3. We empirically demonstrate that rational metareasoning achieves the same task performance at lower
064            inference costs (20-37% fewer tokens on average) across various datasets and reasoning tasks.

067   2    R ATIONAL M ETAREASONING
068
069
      Unlike artificial intelligence, humans have limited time and cognitive resources (Griffiths et al., 2019b;
070
      Griffiths, 2020). We face diverse challenges requiring different approaches: avoiding a sudden obstacle when
071
      driving needs quick, intuitive thinking, whereas selecting a retirement investment strategy requires slow,
072   deliberate reasoning (Krämer, 2014). Rational metareasoning (Russell & Wefald, 1991) suggests agents
073   should adapt their reasoning based on the problem at hand. Intuitively, while reasoning solves a problem,
074   metareasoning solves the problem of how to solve a problem: deciding which computations to perform
075   while problem-solving. The essence of rational metareasoning is calculating the value of computation (VOC;
076   Russell & Wefald, 1991) for each potential computation. The VOC balances the benefit of computation c
077   (characterized by the expected increase in the agent’s eventual utility) against its cost (usually time or energy).
078   To formalize this, agents are assumed to have some internal belief state b ∈ B, which determines their
079   expectation about the value of each action a ∈ A: E[U (a)|b]. A rational agent would simply choose the
080   highest-value action: a∗ = arg maxa∈A [U (a)|b]. In contrast, a meta-rational agent can perform computation
081   to change their belief state before choosing an action. Each computation c ∈ C updates the agent belief to
082   b′ with probability P (b′ |c), which in turn affects their beliefs about the value of actions. However, each
083   computation has an associated cost (cost(c)). The VOC then quantifies the value of performing computation
084   c given a starting belief state b,
085
086
087                       V OC(c, b) = EP (b′ |c) [max
                                                    ′
                                                       E[U (a′ )|b′ ] − max E[U (a)|b]] − cost(c).                   (1)
                                                    a                    a
088
089
090   Thus, a meta-rational agent should pursue the computation c∗ with the highest VOC: c∗ =
091   arg maxc∈C V OC(c, b). If no computation has positive VOC, the agent should stop thinking and act in
092   the world. Rational meta-reasoning can explain how humans allocate cognitive resources in various tasks
093   (Lieder & Griffiths, 2017; Lieder et al., 2018; Callaway et al., 2018; 2021; 2022; Russek et al., 2022).

      To achieve an optimal balance between performance and efficiency, our approach introduces a new VOC-

098   2022), fine-tuning a LLM to produce reasoning chains adaptively depending on task difficulty.

100   3.1   R EWARD MODELING
101
102   Chain-of-thought prompting is a technique in which a language model is encouraged to generate an interme-
103   diate output – a “chain of thought” – prior to producing the answer to a question (Wei et al., 2023; Kojima
104   et al., 2023) We define the reward of a chain of thought as the difference between its utility and its cost,
105                                           Rπ (x, y, z) = Uπ (z|x, y) − C(z)                                        (2)
106
107   where x denotes the input for the task, z represents the chain of thought, and y is the target solution. The
108
      utility of the chain of thought is represented by Uπ (z|x, y), and the cost of the intermediate computations is
      denoted by C(z). Equation 2 mirrors the VOC Equation 1: here, individual reasoning tokens correspond to
109
      intermediate computations c, making the reasoning chain z a sequence of computations c, while the actions
110
      a ∈ A map to the potential outputs y ∈ Y of the language model. In the context of LLMs, utility quantifies
111   the increase in the likelihood of generating the target sequence y when the chain of thought z is added to the
112   input x, under the policy π:
113
114
                                        Uπ (z|x, y) = log πθ (y|z, x) − log πθ (y|x).                                  (3)
115   Specifically, πθ (y|z, x) indicates the probability of generating the target sequence y given both the chain of
116   thought z and the input x, while πθ (y|x) denotes the probability of generating y with only the input x. With
117   respect to Equation 1, the language model’s initial belief about the value of actions or outputs is described by
118   πθ (y | x) (from Equation 3), whereas its final belief after generating a sequence of computations or tokens
119   (a chain of thought z) is described by πθ (y | z, x). The cost is directly proportional to the logarithm of the
120
      number of tokens in the chain of thought l(z):
121                                                  C(z) = γ · log l(z).                                              (4)
122
      The hyperparameter γ scales the cost and utility to the same magnitude. We found that both linear and
123
      logarithmic cost functions perform adequately in our training algorithm, but the latter was better at balancing
124   utility and cost, especially with higher sequence-length variance. A key benefit of this reward function is that
125   it is parameterized by the same weights θ as the generative policy πθ , eliminating the need for an external
126   reward model. This allows for direct estimation of the utility of a reasoning chain using the policy itself.
127
128   3.2   C OT G ENERATION
129
130   We begin with a pretrained language model πθ and an initial dataset of problems x along with their corre-
131   sponding correct final answers y: D = {(xi , yi )}D    i=1 . Following prior work in online RL (Tang et al., 2024),
132   we utilize the model itself to generate the reasoning chains, with few-shot prompting as a guide. Specifically,
133
      we concatenate a small set of examples, denoted as P, each containing intermediate reasoning chains z, to
      each example in D. For each task τi = (xi , yi ) in the original dataset D, we generate K reasoning chains:
134
      τ̂i = {(xi , zk,i , yi )}K
                               k=1 . If none of the K generated reasoning chains for a task τi leads to the correct answer,
135
      we discard all samples. To minimize the likelihood of this outcome, we adopt the rationalization approach
136   introduced by STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022): if the model fails to generate the correct answer, we generate a
137   new reasoning chain by supplying the model with the correct answer in the prompt. Intuitively, rationalization
138   increases the sample size, providing more options when selecting the arg max of the VoC reward. We found
139   that it provided marginal reduction in length with no effect on performance. Finally, we assess each reasoning
140   chain using the Rational Metareasoning reward function (Equation 2).

      3.3   M ETAREASONING T RAINING
142
143   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our reward using a variation of the Expert Iteration algorithm (EI,
144   Anthony et al., 2017). EI is known for its sample efficiency and strong performance on reasoning tasks
145   (Havrilla et al., 2024; Zelikman et al., 2022). As an example of an online reinforcement learning (RL)
146   algorithm, EI involves both exploration and policy improvement phases, with the policy πθ being updated
147   using data from the exploration phase. STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) applies EI to LLMs by iteratively
148   generating reasoning chains via few-shot prompting and compiling them into a fine-tuning dataset to refine the
149
      model. We build on this framework for our rational metareasoning training process, described in Algorithm 1.
150

156
       4:     Dn ← {T ⊆ D | |T | = T } ∪ Dn−1                                             ▷ Sample batch from dataset
       5:     for k in 1...K do                                                 ▷ Perform reasoning chain generation
157

158
       7:         (zi,k , yi,k ) ← πn−1 (add_hint(xi )) ∀i ∈ [1, Di ] ∧ yi,k ̸= yi           ▷ Compute rationalization

166   Initially, in the exploration phase, we approximate the optimal policy π̂ ∗ (whose computations maximize
167   the VoC reward) by using rejection sampling on our student policy πθ . After generating K intermediate
168   reasoning chains z1 , . . . , zK for a given question x (Section 3.2), we evaluate them using our reward function
169   Rπ (Section 3.1). We then construct D1∗ = {(xi , zi , yi )}N      i=1 by setting the rejection sampling threshold
170   dynamically to the highest reward for each task i, therefore increasing robustness to hyperparameter choices :
171
      zi = arg maxk {Rπ (x, zk , y)}K    k=1 . These rollouts are distilled into a policy π1 using standard cross-entropy
      loss. This process can be iteratively repeated to refine the policy πn on the dataset Dn∗ . Finally, similar to
172
      STaR (Zelikman et al., 2024), instead of utilizing the entire training dataset at each iteration, as is typical
173
      in standard EI algorithms (Anthony et al., 2017; Gulcehre et al., 2023), we begin with a batch of T steps
174   during the first iteration and progressively increase the number of fine-tuning training steps by T with each
175   subsequent iteration. This approach allows the model to encounter new examples gradually, resulting in
176   slower training initially, which ultimately enhances model performance.

186   by combining the training sets from these datasets into one dataset D and then evaluated the model on all
187   corresponding test sets T . Below is a detailed description of the datasets used:

208   4.2   BASELINES
209
210   We illustrate the advantages of our model by comparing its performance to two types of prompting strategies:
211
      Direct prompting, where the model is required to provide an immediate answer, and Chain of Thought
      prompting (CoT), where the model is encouraged to reason through the problem step-by-step before arriving
212
      at a solution. Since we are using pretrained models which are not specifically trained for instruction following,
213
      we provide five few-shot examples for each task from the unused portion of the training dataset. These
214   examples are carefully chosen to ensure that the length of the reasoning chain matches the perceived difficulty
215   of the question. In addition to these prompting methods, we adopt a finetuning baseline, comparing our
216   method to STaR (Self-Taught Reasoner; Zelikman et al. 2022), which also uses the expert iteration algorithm.
217   While CoT prompting may not yield optimal trajectories, more advanced methods (Yao et al., 2023a; Zheng
218   et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2023) often increase sequence lengths. Focusing on efficiency, we find CoT’s
219   simplicity ideal for testing our method. Reducing reasoning tokens without performance loss here suggests
220   similar gains in more complex approaches.
221
222   4.3   T RAINING D ETAILS
223
224   For our experiments, we use Microsoft Phi-2 (Javaheripi et al., 2023), Meta Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024),
225   and Mistral-7B-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023) as the pretrained base models. We have chosen γ = 0.1 to align
226   the distributions of costs and rewards more closely, although we have found the method to be robust to
227
      small variations in the choice of this hyperparameter. We sample K = 4 reasoning chains for each question,
      using a temperature t of 0.5 and a topp value of 0.9. These parameters are chosen to balance the exploration
228
      and exploitation trade-off, allowing us to generate diverse yet relevant reasoning chains. For each iteration
229
      n, we sample a dataset Dn of size 512 from the union of four training datasets D described in 4.1. In the
230   self-supervised fine-tuning step, we use a batch size of 16 and a learning rate of 1e-6. We believe that
231   further improvements are possible through a more comprehensive hyperparameter search; however, due
232   to computational constraints, we leave this for future work. Finally, we evaluate all models using greedy
233   decoding to ensure consistent and deterministic output generation. We use pattern matching techniques to
234   extract the answers; an exact match between the generated answer and the ground truth is considered correct.

399   6     R ELATED W ORK
400
401   6.1   R EDUCING INFERENCE COSTS
402
      The rising cost of deploying large language models (LLMs) has driven efforts to reduce inference costs.
403
      Techniques such as Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023) and Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) improve
404
      efficiency through parallelization, while Mixture of Experts (Jacobs et al., 1991; Zhou et al., 2022) activates
405   only a subset of LLM parameters during decoding. Though effective, these methods require significant
406   architectural changes and don’t adapt computation based on task difficulty. Other approaches have developed
407   neural architectures that enable adaptive computation (Graves, 2017; Banino et al., 2021; Dehghani et al.,
408   2019; Mohtashami et al., 2023; Schuster et al., 2022) but involve new architectures or training methods. In
409   contrast, our approach uses existing architectures and pretrained models, modifying only the fine-tuning
410   process. More similar to our approach, model routing (Ong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) optimizes resource
411   utilization based on query complexity by routing easier queries to smaller models and harder queries to larger
412   ones. However, this necessitates multiple models and a router, while our approach trains a single model to
413
      adaptively adjust its own outputs to match task complexity.
414
415   6.2   R EASONING IN LLM S
416
      Techniques such as Chain of Thought (CoT) and related methodologies (Wei et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023a;
417
      Yasunaga et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024) have proven effective at enhancing LLM
418   performance across a wide range of tasks. CoT boosts LLMs’ performance on complex reasoning by guiding
419   them through a series of intermediate reasoning steps, increasing inference costs to improve task performance.
420   This method can be implemented through in-context learning (Wei et al., 2023), prompting (Kojima et al.,
421   2023), or training (Li et al., 2023). The benefits of CoT can be attributed to both a greater computation
422   depth (Goyal et al., 2024; Pfau et al., 2024) and the semantic values of the thought tokens, which function as

                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


423
      intermediate variables in the computation of the answer (Prystawski et al., 2023). However, recent studies
424
      have raised concerns regarding the meaningfulness of such reasoning chains in reaching the target solution,
425   and whether models effectively utilize them to solve tasks (Turpin et al., 2023; Paul et al., 2024; Sprague et al.,
426   2024). We further demonstrate that standard prompting and training methods fail to teach the model to use
427   CoT purposefully, resulting in inefficient inference. Reasoning can also be used to bootstrap language models.
428   Self-improving techniques (Huang et al., 2022; Zelikman et al., 2022) consist of generating reasoning chain-
429   augmented answers for unlabeled questions and fine-tuning the LLM using those self-generated solutions
430   as target outputs. Similar techniques, such as ReST (Gulcehre et al., 2023), can also be used to better
431   align LLMs with human preferences and needs. Our approach builds on these techniques to optimize the
432   inference cost of reasoning chains in addition to their task performance. Another related work, Quiet-STaR
433
      (Zelikman et al., 2024), has improved LLM reasoning performance by modifying pretraining to generate
      intermediate s between tokens. While effective for downstream tasks, it increases computational costs by
434
      generating reasoning chains at every step, even when unnecessary. More recently, chat models that “think
435
      before answering” have been developed (OpenAI, 2024), using inference-time computation to enhance their
436   outputs. Although these models outperform others, they expend more computational resources on all tasks,
437   even when it may not be necessary. Our method could be incorporated into their training process to help the
438   model determine when this additional computation is genuinely beneficial.
439

441
      7    C ONCLUSION
442
      In this work, we introduced a novel method inspired by previous research in AI and cognitive science aimed
443
      at reducing inference costs in LLMs. We operationalize the concept of rational metareasoning – which
444
      formalizes humans’ adaptive use of cognitive resources – with a novel reward function based on the Value of
445   Computation (VOC). This reward function trains the LLM to optimize the use of intermediate reasoning steps
446   in task execution, enabling it to balance task performance with computational efficiency. Empirically, we
447   find that this approach significantly reduces the number of generated tokens and input context length while
448   maintaining comparable performance across diverse datasets.
449
      However, we note some limitations to our approach. First, the focus of our work here is enhancing the
450
      efficiency of large language models in reasoning tasks, rather than the overall performance of such systems.
451   Thus, we aimed to reduce unnecessary intermediate reasoning steps, rather than improve the quality of
452   reasoning per se. It remains to be seen whether our approach can be extended to enhance task performance in
453   addition to computational efficiency. Additionally, we tested our approach on well-established datasets in
454   science, commonsense reasoning, and math, but it remains untested in more specialized domains. Notably,
455   one of these untested settings is the agentic one, where LLMs act as agents to perform complex tasks in digital
456   environments (Yao et al., 2023b; Schick et al., 2023). Our method could be adapted by including the number
457   of LLM API calls to such tools in the cost section of our reward function, ideally encouraging the model to
458   minimize unnecessary tool calls. Broader testing could help assess its generalizability and effectiveness across
459
      diverse contexts. Additionally, the VoC reward function is highly adaptable and can be used with various
      algorithms. For instance, it can provide numerical rewards for PPO or guide DPO by training on samples
460
      with the highest and lowest rewards. In this work, we focused on EI, the most sample-efficient algorithm
461
      for reasoning tasks Havrilla et al. (2024), to demonstrate its generalization across models and benchmarks.
462   Exploring its application to other algorithms is an exciting direction for future research.
463
464   Most excitingly, our work demonstrates how cognitively-inspired reward functions can endow LLMs with
465
      desirable inference-time properties, opening a broad avenue of future work. Given its flexibility, this method
      could be integrated into instruction tuning to potentially enhance performance, even in scenarios where
466
      verifying the correctness of answers is challenging. Since the utility measure within the reward function can
467
      be tailored to prioritize any desired, measurable property, this approach offers the potential to guide models
468   toward achieving these enhanced qualities while still benefiting from the reduced computational costs.

826   D    C OMPARISON TO INSTRUCTION TUNED MODELS
827
828   It is reasonable to question whether instruction-tuned models can dynamically adjust the length of their
829
      reasoning chains based on task complexity. To explore this, we tested Meta-Llama-3-8B-Instruct using the
      following prompt:
830
831   Answer the following question, thinking step by step to get to the
832   answer. You can think however long you need, but answer as soon as
833   you’re ready. Keep you response concise and use the minimum number of
834   steps to get to the answer. Once you’re finished thinking, write your
835
      answer after the ’Answer: ’ prompt.
836   Unfortunately, this method did not effectively adapt response lengths to align with task complexity in our
837   experiments, as shown in Tables 7 and 8 and Fig. 4. While the observed performance drop might partially
838   result from deviations from the expected input format, it is evident that the length of the reasoning chains was
839   generally much higher and not meaningfully adaptive to the complexity of the task.
