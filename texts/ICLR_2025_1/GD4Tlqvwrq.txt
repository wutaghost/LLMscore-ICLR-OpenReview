009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Latent diffusion models (LDMs) have emerged as powerful tools for generating
012            diverse and realistic samples across domains. However, their efficacy in captur-
013            ing intricate details and small-scale objects remains a challenge. Our investigation
014            reveals that VAE compression induces errors in the latent space and limits the gen-
015            eration quality. Furthermore, LDMs trained on fixed-resolution images struggle to
016
               produce high-resolution outputs without distortions, making simple resolution in-
               creases ineffective. In this paper, we propose a novel noise re-sampling strategy
017
               that enables multi-scale generation of LDMs, allowing LDMs to “zoom in” and
018
               improve generation quality of local regions. By increasing the sampling rates from
019            the noise perspective in the latent space, we effectively bypass the constraints im-
020            posed by VAE compression, thus preserving crucial high-frequency information.
021            Our approach, a simple yet effective plugin for current LDMs, enhances the qual-
022            ity of image generation in local regions while maintaining overall structural con-
023            sistency and providing fine-grained control over the scale of generation in latent
024            diffusion models. Through extensive experimentation and evaluation, we demon-
025            strate the efficacy of our method in enhancing the generation quality across various
026            LDM architectures. Our approach surpasses existing methods, including stable
027
               diffusion (SD) models, SD-based super-resolution methods and high-resolution
               adaptation methods, in generating high-fidelity samples of complex objects.

032   In recent years, the advance of latent diffusion models (LDMs) (Ho et al., 2020b; Rombach et al.,
033   2022; Diffusion, 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2022) has marked a sig-
034   nificant leap in the field of generative modeling, offering a new paradigm for creating diverse and
035   lifelike samples across various domains. These models utilize a latent space, compressed by Varia-
036   tional Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013), to efficiently generate detailed and coherent
037   images. Despite their considerable capabilities, LDMs frequently encounter challenges in accurately
038   rendering complex objects such as hands, faces, and textual elements (Lu et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
039
      2025). This deficiency primarily stems from the compression errors associated with the latent space
      where VAEs tend to disproportionately affect high-frequency details—details that are crucial for the
040
      realism and perceptual quality of the generated samples. While intuitively, increasing the resolution
041
      of LDMs with higher sampling rates might seem like a solution, LDMs trained on fixed-resolution
042   images (Diffusion, 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Chai et al., 2022) struggle to generate high-resolution
043   outputs without introducing distortions (Zheng et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025), making
044   simple resolution increases ineffective.
045
      Efforts have been made to enhance LDMs for generating high-resolution content to improve visual
046
      details (Podell et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023;
047
      Ho et al., 2022). These methods, which include both fine-tuning (Podell et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
048   2023; Ho et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2025) and tuning-free (He et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023; Si et al.,
049   2023) approaches, adapt LDMs trained at a fixed resolution to operate at higher or lower resolutions.

051   (He et al., 2024) and additional steps (Guo et al., 2025). Such calibration is necessary, without which
052   potentially leads to inconsistencies in the quality of the generated images. Fine-tuning or adaptor-
053   based (Guo et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023) methods requires extensive high quality
      high resolution data and incurs significant computational expenses due to exponentially increased

139   2      R ELATED W ORK
140
141   Latent diffusion models. Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022; Diffusion, 2022;
142   Podell et al., 2023) represent a significant advancement in the field of generative models, particularly
143   in the domain of image generation. Introduced as an efficient alternative to traditional diffusion
144   models, LDMs operate in a compressed latent space rather than the pixel space, drastically reducing
145   computational demands and enabling faster model training and sampling. This approach leverages
146   an Variantional Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Oord et al., 2017) to map high-
147
      dimensional data into a lower-dimensional latent space. The diffusion process is then applied within
      this latent space, preserving the ability to generate high-quality images while enhancing the model’s
148
      efficiency. LDMs have demonstrated remarkable performance in tasks such as image synthesis,
149
      super-resolution, and conditional image generation, establishing them as a pivotal development in
150   generative deep learning.
151
152   Any resolution training. While existing latent diffusion methods (Diffusion, 2022; Podell et al.,
153
      2023) excel in image synthesis, they struggle to generate high-resolution images due to the com-
      plexities of high-dimensional data and the scarcity of high-quality datasets. The primary approaches
154
      (Podell et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022; Teng et al., 2023) include cascaded models that initially produce
155
      low-resolution images and then incrementally upscale them to higher resolutions. Alternatively,
156   end-to-end models (Podell et al., 2023; Hoogeboom et al., 2023) directly create high-resolution im-
157   ages but require extensive training and substantial datasets. Other strategies involve fine-tuning (Hu
158   et al., 2021), where only part of the model’s parameters are adjusted to adapt to higher resolutions,
159   necessitating numerous tuning steps. Additionally, some recent methods (He et al., 2024; Si et al.,
160   2023) propose training-free approaches, employing techniques like dilated convolution to adjust
161   convolutional networks for different resolutions. However, these methods can introduce semantic
      inconsistencies and visual artifacts.

162
      Super-Resolution Super-resolution techniques employ generative models to create high-resolution
163   images by exploiting the self-similarity attributes of images, refining the output from these models
164   (Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024). These techniques generally tackle a localized,
165   conditional challenge, relying heavily on the structure provided by the initial low-resolution input.
166   Consequently, when applied to synthetic data from Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), which may
167   contain distorted structural information, super-resolution methods might struggle to correct these
168   errors effectively. Our method takes a different approach by starting in the noise space and applying
169   a re-sampling method to increase sampling rates in complex local regions. This technique helps
170   recover the high-frequency details and correct distorted structural information that were originally
171
      lost, offering a robust solution to the limitations of traditional super-resolution methods.

      Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Ho et al., 2020b; Diffusion, 2022; Rombach et al., 2022) denoise
      images by gradually refining noise samples drawn from a Gaussian distribution. However, one
202
      crucial aspect, the sampling rate of the noise samples, has been largely overlooked, which directly
203
      decides how many pixels are allocated to an object and affects the quality of the generated object in
204   image. In this work, we conceptualize each image as a discrete sampling from a bounded, continuous
205   scene, structured within a normalized coordinate domain of [0, 1]2 . Under this formulation, current
206   LDMs (Ho et al., 2020b; Diffusion, 2022; Podell et al., 2023) typically produces discrete images at
207   a limited sampling rate 1/∆, where ∆ denotes the spatial distance between pixels. The introduction
208   of VAEs, which compress image data into compact latent codes for efficiency and scalability, further
209   reduces the sampling rate, leading to distorted and low-quality results, as shown in Figure 3.
210   To address these problems, we propose a novel Noise Re-sampling approach, which adjusts sam-
211   pling rate at local regions with complex details or small-scale objects and bypass the negative impact
212   imposed by VAE compression. The overall pipeline begins with the generation of a global image
213   using the standard LDM denoising process as shown in Figure 4 (b). Local regions requiring en-
214   hancement are identified, and their noise patches are re-sampled from the same noise space at an
215   increased sampling rate to restore fine-grained details. These high-resolution noise patches are then
      denoised, decoded into image space and seamlessly merged back into the global image. To ensure

308   4.3    N OISE R E - SAMPLING
309
      In LDMs, the quality of generated objects is heavily influenced by their size relative to the im-
310
      age (Zheng et al., 2023). Larger objects benefit from more allocated pixels, enabling more de-
311
      tailed reconstruction, whereas smaller objects suffer from reduced resolution. Such phenomenon
312   coins with the concept of sampling rate. The sampling rate, typically defined as the number of
313   pixels per unit area, quantifies how well an object or scene is represented in an image. Accord-
314   ing to the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, high-frequency details are inherently limited by the
315   sampling rate. Thus, when the sampling rate is low—meaning fewer pixels are allocated to an
316   object—capturing its finer details becomes increasingly difficult. Compounding this issue, current
317   LDMs operate extensively within the compressed latent space of VAEs to enhance computational
318   efficiency, which further reduces the effective sampling rate.
319   Given the strong correlation between the reconstruction error in VAEs and the frequency of compo-
320   nents, our objective is to minimize this error through a Re-sampling approach by increasing sampling
321   rate at complex local regions. Specifically, since LDMs denoise noise samples to generate images
322   without altering their resolution, the sampling rate of the resulting image is directly determined by
323   the sampling rate of the noise sample. Hence, our re-sampling approach begins within the noise
      space itself.

327   Therefore, current LDMs often starts with a bounded, discrete noise sample Zt∆ with sampling
328   rate 1/∆. We reformulate the process of initial noise sampling in the noise space as a bounded
329   discretization process of Zt (x, y):

373   contribute to the visual fidelity of the generated images.

397   upsampling in the latent space does not function equivalently to upsampling operations in the image
398   space. In the latent space, upsampling can distort the semantic content encoded within the latent
399   vectors, leading to artifacts in the resulting images as shown in Figure 5 (b). To mitigate this issue,
400   we propose a VAE-based upsampling method where the latent vectors are decoded back to the image
401   space for upsampling.
402   As shown in Figure 4 (c), before upscaling, we employ the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) de-
403   coder to project latent vectors back into image space. The VAE decoder reconstructs compressed
404   latent patch to image space, including its high-frequency components. This reconstruction is not a
405   straightforward interpolation rather, it generates a new, higher-resolution image with reconstructed
406   high frequency components based on its learned distribution of image features. With latent codes
407   decoded into the image space, we upsample the decoded image by s to match the increased sampling
408   rate at local region in proposed Noise Re-sampling, demonstrated in Figure 5 (c).
409   Once the VAE has provided this initial, imperfect high-resolution output, we employ the proposed
410   Noise Re-sampling process. This subsequent phase is tailored to specifically target and refine the
411   inaccuracies in the high-frequency details originally presented in the low-sampling-rate latent code
412   as well as potential artifacts introduced by the VAE, as shown in Figure 5 (e). Through noise re-
413   sampling, this process iteratively adjusts and sharpens these details, enhancing the overall fidelity of
414   the image while preserving structural consistency.

522   6    L IMITATIONS
523
524   Our proposed noise re-sampling technique effectively enhances sampling rates and achieves high-
525   fidelity image generation across various scales. However, it encounters limitations in complex in-
526   teraction scenarios, such as hands engaging with objects. This limitation is rooted in the inherent
527   constraints of latent diffusion models (LDMs), which lack the capability to fully understand complex
528   scenes. Consequently, even with enhanced sampling rates, our method while effective at improving
529   perceptual quality of local patches, can not address the semantic complexities. This highlights the
530
      need for approaches that can grasp the dynamics of complex object interactions.
531
532   7    C ONCLUSION
533
534   This study has explored the enhancement of latent diffusion models (LDMs) through a novel noise
535   re-sampling strategy, significantly improving the generation quality across different scales. By fo-
536   cusing on the frequency domain and adjusting sampling rates in latent space, our method effectively
537   circumvents the constraints imposed by VAE compression, thereby preserving and enhancing high-
538   frequency details essential for realistic image synthesis. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that
539   our approach outperforms existing methods, including stable diffusion models and super-resolution
      techniques, particularly in generating detailed and high-fidelity images of complex objects.
