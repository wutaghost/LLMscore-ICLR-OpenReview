010                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012                This paper proposes a simple yet effective jailbreak attack named FlipAttack
013                against black-box LLMs. First, from the autoregressive nature, we reveal that
014                LLMs tend to understand the text from left to right and find that they struggle to
015
                   comprehend the text when noise is added to the left side. Motivated by these in-
                   sights, we propose to disguise the harmful prompt by constructing left-side noise
016
                   merely based on the prompt itself, then generalize this idea to 4 flipping modes.
017
                   Second, we verify the strong ability of LLMs to perform the text-flipping task,
018                and then develop 4 variants to guide LLMs to denoise, understand, and execute
019                harmful behaviors accurately. These designs keep FlipAttack universal, stealthy,
020                and simple, allowing it to jailbreak black-box LLMs within only 1 query. Ex-
021                periments on 8 LLMs demonstrate the superiority of FlipAttack. Remarkably, it
022                achieves ∼98% attack success rate on GPT-4o, and ∼98% bypass rate against 5
023                guardrail models on average. The codes are available at Anonymous GitHub1 .

049
050       Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024;
051       Team, 2024; Hui et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024a) have demonstrated remarkable potential across
052       various domains, including numerous security-critical areas like finance (Zhao et al., 2024) and
053

      medicine (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023). As these AI-powered tools become increasingly integrated
055   into our digital infrastructure, it is important to ensure their safety and reliability. However, recent
056   studies on jailbreak attacks (Ding et al., 2023; Lv et al., 2024) have revealed that LLMs can be vul-
057   nerable to manipulation, potentially compromising their intended safeguards and producing harmful
058   contents, underscoring the critical importance of understanding and mitigating such risks.
059
      Recent studies have made significant progress in developing attacks to expose LLM vulnerabilities,
060
      however, our analyses highlight three key limitations in recent state-of-the-art jailbreak attack meth-
061   ods. 1) White-box methods, like GCG (Zou et al., 2023) and AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b), while
062   powerful, require access to model weights and involve computationally intensive search-based opti-
063   mization, limiting their applicability to closed-source LLMs and compromising time efficiency. 2)
064   Iterative black-box methods, like PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) and ReNeLLM (Ding et al., 2023), re-
065   quire iterative interactions with the LLM interface, leading to high token usage and extended attack
066   time. 3) Other black-box methods, such as SelfCipher (Yuan et al., 2023) and CodeChameleon (Lv
067   et al., 2024), rely on complex assistant tasks such as ciphering and coding, which raise the difficulty
068   level for LLMs to understand and execute, resulting in suboptimal attack performance. These lim-
069
      itations highlight the need for more efficient, broadly applicable jailbreak techniques to understand
      LLM vulnerabilities better while maintaining practicality and effectiveness.
070
071   To this end, we propose FlipAttack, a simple yet effective jailbreak attack method targeting black-
072   box LLMs, as shown in Figure 2. First, to make our proposed method universally applicable to
073   state-of-the-art LLMs, we study their common nature, i.e., autoregressive, and reveal that LLMs
074   tend to understand the sentence from left to right. From this insight, we conduct analysis exper-
075
      iments to demonstrate that the understanding ability of LLMs is significantly weakened by intro-
      ducing noises to the left side of the sentence. Based on these findings, we propose to disguise the
076
      harmful prompt, by adding left-side noises iteratively to the prompt and then generalize this idea
077
      to develop four flipping modes: Flipping Word Order, Flipping Characters in Sentence, Flipping
078   Characters in Word, and the Fool Model Mode, therefore keeping stealthy. Second, we conduct
079   verification experiments to demonstrate that the strong LLMs, e.g., Claude 3.5 Sonnet, can effi-
080   ciently perform text flipping, while the weak LLMs can also complete this task with assistance.
081   Therefore, based on chain-of-thought, role-playing prompting, and few-show in-context learning,
082   we design a flipping guidance module to teach LLMs how to flip back/denoise, understand, and
083   execute harmful behaviors. Importantly, FlipAttack introduces no external noise, relying solely on
084   the prompt itself for noise construction, keeping the method simple. Benefiting from universality,
085   stealthiness, and simplicity, FlipAttack easily jailbreaks recent state-of-the-art LLMs within only 1
086
      single query. Extensive experiments on black-box commercial LLMs demonstrate the superiority of
      FlipAttack. Notably, it achieves a 25.16% improvement in the average attack success rate compared
087
      to the runner-up method. Specifically, it reaches a success rate of 98.85% on GPT-4 Turbo and
088
      89.42% on GPT-4. The detailed attack performance of FlipAttack and the runner-up ReNeLLM on
089   8 LLMs for 7 categories of harmful behaviors are shown in Figure 1.
090
091   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
092
093
      • We reveal LLMs’ understanding mechanism and find that introducing left-side noise can signifi-
        cantly weaken their understanding ability on sentences, keeping the attack universally applicable.
094
095   • We propose to disguise the harmful request by adding left-side noise iteratively based on request
096     itself and generalizing it to four flipping modes, keeping the attack stealthy to bypass guards.
097   • We design a flipping guidance module to teach LLMs to recover, understand, and execute the
098     disguised prompt, enabling FlipAttack to jailbreak black-box LLMs within one query easily.
099   • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the superiority and efficiency of FlipAttack.

124       3     M ETHODOLOGY
125
126       This section presents FlipAttack. We first give a clear definition of jailbreak attacks on LLMs. Then,
127       we analyze the mechanism behind the understanding capabilities of recent mainstream LLMs. In
128       addition, based on the insights, we propose FlipAttack, which mainly contains the attack disguise
129       module and flipping guidance module. Subsequently, we explore the potential reasons why FlipAt-
130
          tack works. Finally, we design two simple defense strategies against FlipAttack.
131       Problem Definition. Given a harmful request X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn } with n tokens, e.g., “How
132       to make a bomb?”, and a victim LLM Fvictim , e.g., GPT-4, we get a response S from Fvictim by
133       inputting X , i.e., S = Fvictim (X ). Typically, S includes refusal phrases, e.g., “I’m sorry, but I
134       can’t...” and Fvictim rejects X . However, a jailbreak attack method J aims to transfer X to an attack
135       prompt X ′ and manipulate LLMs to bypass the guardrail and produce harmful contents to satisfy X ,
136
          S ′ = Fvictim (X ′ ), for example, S ′ =‘ ‘Sure, here are some instructions on how to make a bomb...”.
137       Evaluation Metrics. To evaluation a jailbreak attack J , the dictionary-based evaluation (Zou et al.,
138       2023) only considers whether LLMs reject the harmful request. It keeps a dictionary of rejection
139       phrases and checks whether the response S ′ contains the rejection phrase in the dictionary. If so,
140       J fails and vice versa. Differently, GPT-based evaluation (Wei et al., 2024) considers the rejection
141       status, the completion of the harmful request, and the illegal/unsafe output. It uses a strong LLM,
142
          for example, GPT-4, to score S ′ through the prompt in Figure 31. This paper focuses primarily on
          GPT-based evaluation, which is more accurate, as shown in (Wei et al., 2024) and Section A.4.
143
144       Mechanism behind Understanding Ability. To better jailbreak the victim LLMs, we first analyze
145       the mechanism behind LLMs’ strong and safe understanding ability, e.g., how LLMs understand and
146       recognize a harmful input. It may stems from various techniques, like high-quality data (Gunasekar
147       et al., 2023), scaling law (Hoffmann et al., 2022), RLHF (Achiam et al., 2023), red-teaming (Gan-
148
          guli et al., 2022), long CoT2 , etc. Although different LLMs may leverage diverse techniques, one
          common nature is that all recent state-of-the-art LLMs are autoregressive and utilize the next-token
149
          prediction task during training. Therefore, 1) LLMs tend to understand the sentence from left to right
150
          even if they can access the entire text. 2) Introducing noise at the left side of the sentence affects
151       the LLMs’ understanding more significantly than introducing noise at the right side. Experimental
152       evidence can be found in Section 4.3. These insights inspire the design of FlipAttack.

180   3.1.1     ATTACK D ISGUISE M ODULE
181
182   This section designs an attack disguise module to disguise the harmful prompt X =
183   {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }, allowing it to circumvent guard models and evade detection by safety-aligned
184   LLMs. Based on the insights presented in the previous section, we aim to undermine LLMs’ un-
185   derstanding of the disguise prompt by adding noises on the left of the harmful prompt. Rather than
186
      introducing new noise, which increases the difficulty of denoising, we construct the noises merely
      based on information from the original prompt by simply flipping. Concretely, when LLMs attempt
187
      to understand the first character x1 in the harmful prompt X , we isolate x1 and treat the remaining
188
      characters {x2 , . . . , xn } as the noise. Then we disrupt LLMs’ understanding of x1 by moving the
189   noise {x2 , . . . , xn } to the left of x1 , i.e., {x2 , . . . , xn , x1 }. Next, we retain the noised character and
190   repeat this process on the remaining un-noised characters until all characters have undergone the
191   noising process. For example, adding noise on X = “This is a bomb” can be formulated as follows,
192   where Bold and italic denote the target character and noised characters in each step. “This is a
193   bomb” → “his is a bombT ” → “s is a bombihT ” → . . . → “bomb a si sihT ” → . . . → “bmob
194   a si sihT ”. Eventually, each character is noised by the information from the original prompt. In
195   this case, before noising, LLMs or guard models can easily understand and recognize the harmful
196   word “bomb” and refuse to respond. However, after noising, LLMs may become confused about the
197
      corresponding word “bmob”, allowing the disguised prompt to bypass the guardrails more easily. To
      support our claim, we conduct experiments in Section 4.3, demonstrating that even state-of-the-art
198
      guard models exhibit higher perplexity when processing these disguised prompts than other seem-
199
      ingly stealthy methods like ciphers and art words. Besides, in Figure 7, we conduct case studies to
200   show that the perplexity is increasing while adding noise.
201
202   We attribute these results to two primary reasons. 1) LLMs are accustomed to reading and under-
203
      standing sentences from left to right due to the nature of the next-token prediction task. 2) It is
      likely that the training data contains very few flipped instructions, as such data would generally be
204
      meaningless and could negatively impact the overall performance of the LLMs on standard language
205
      tasks. Building on this foundational idea, we design four flipping modes as follows.
206
207
         (I) Flip Word Order: this mode flips the order of words while keeping the characters within each
208
         word unchanged. For example, “How to build a bomb?”→“bomb a build to How”.
209
210
         (II) Flip Characters in Word: this mode flips the characters within each word but keeps the order
         of the words intact. For example, “How to build a bomb”→“woH ot dliub a bmob”.
211
212      (III) Flip Characters in Sentence: this mode flips each character in the prompt, resulting in a
213      complete reversal of the sentence, e.g., “How to build a bomb”→“bmob a dliub ot woH”.
214      (IV) Fool Model Mode: this mode flips each character in the sentence, but it misleads the LLMs
215      by prompting it to flip word order instead of characters to recover the original prompt. For
         example, we input LLMs “bmob a dliub ot woH” but ask LLMs to flip word order in the sentence.

      3.1.2    F LIPPING G UIDANCE M ODULE
222
      This module aims to guide LLMs in decoding the disguised prompt through a flipping task, enabling
223   them to understand and subsequently execute the harmful intents. First, we analyze the difficulty of
224   the flipping task for LLMs via experiments in Section 4.3. We found that 1) reversing the flipped
225   sentence is easy for some strong LLMs, e.g., Claude 3.5 Sonnet. 2) Some relatively weaker LLMs,
226   for example, GPT-3.5 Turbo, struggle with denoising and sometimes misunderstand the original
227   harmful intent. The cases are shown in Figure 12, 14. To this end, we develop four variants to help
228   LLMs understand and execute harmful intents, based on chain-of-thought reasoning, role-playing
229   prompting, and few-shot in-context learning, as follows.
230
231     (A) Vanilla: this variant simply asks LLMs first to read the stealthy prompt and then recover it
232     based on the rules of different modes. During this process, we require LLMs to never explicitly
233     mention harmful behavior. We also impose certain restrictions on the LLMs, e.g., not altering the
234
        original task, not responding with contrary intentions, etc.
235     (B) Vanilla+CoT: this variant is based on Vanilla and further asks LLMs to finish the denoising
236     task by providing solutions step by step in detail, which help LLMs understand better.
237     (C) Vanilla+CoT+LangGPT: this variant is based on Vanilla+CoT and adopts a role-playing struc-
238     ture to help LLMs understand the role, profile, rules, and targets clearly to complete the task.
239     (D) Vanilla+CoT+LangGPT+Few-shot: this variant is based on Vanilla+CoT+LangGPT and pro-
240     vides some few-shot demonstrations to enhance the performance of finishing the flipping task.
241     Rather than introducing new information (which increases the burden of understanding), we
242     merely construct the demonstration based on the original harmful prompt.
243
244   For the demonstration construction method in (D), we first split the harmful prompt X = X[:lX /2] +
245   X[lX /2:] into two halves, and then construct the flipping process based on the split sentences, using
246   them as demonstrations. For example, “how to make a bomb”= “how to make” + “a bomb”, the
247   demonstrations are 1. “ekam ot woh” → “how to make” 2. “noitcurtsni ym wollof” → “follow
248
      my instruction” 3. “bmob a” → “a bomb”. In this manner, we further decrease the difficulty of
      the flipping task and avoid the original harmful behavior appearing in its entirety. We acknowledge
249
      that this process may introduce the risk of detection since harmful words such as “bomb” may still
250
      be present. Thus, developing a better splitting method is a promising future direction. By these
251   settings, we guide LLMs to better denoise, understand, and execute harmful behaviors.
252
253   In summary, FlipAttack first bypasses the guardrails by noising the harmful prompt and then guides
254
      LLMs to uncover and understand the disguised prompt, jailbreaking LLMs with only one query.
255
256   3.2     D EFENSE S TRATEGY
257
258   To defend against FlipAttack, we present two simple defense strategies: System Prompt Defense
259   (SPD) and Perplexity-based Guardrail Filter (PGF). Concretely, for SPD, we guide the LLMs to
260   become safe and helpful by adding a system-level prompt. Besides, for PGF, we adopt the existing
261   guard models to filter the attacks based on the perplexity. However, our observations indicate that
262   these defenses are ineffective against FlipAttack. Designs and details are in Section A.5.
263
264   3.3     W HY DOES F LIPATTACK W ORK ?
265
266   This section aims to discuss the reasons why FlipAttack succeeded. 1) It utilizes a common and un-
267   avoidable feature of LLMs, i.e., auto-regressive property, to formulate the attacks, keeping universal.
268   2) It conceals the harmful prompt by merely using the prompt itself and avoiding introducing exter-
269   nal noises, keeping it stealthy. 3) It guides LLMs to understand and execute the harmful behavior
      via an easy flipping task, keeping it simple. We provide experimental support in Section 4.3.

530
531       In this paper, we first analyze the mechanism behind the understanding ability of LLMs and find that
532       they tend to understand the sentence from left to right. Then, we tried to introduce the noises at the
533       beginning and end of a sentence. We found that introducing noises at the beginning of a sentence can
534
          affect the understanding ability more significantly. From these insights, we generalize the method of
          introducing noises at the left of the sentence to FlipAttack via constructing noises merely based on
535
          the part of the flipped original prompt. From this foundational idea, we design four flipping modes
536
          and four variants. We keep FlipAttack universal, stealthy, and simple. Extensive experiments and
537       analyses on 8 state-of-the-art LLMs demonstrate the superiority of our method. Although achieving
538       promising attack performance, we summarize three limitations of FlipAttack in Section A.8.

893
894   A.1.1    S AFETY A LIGNMENT OF LLM
895
896   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Team,
897   2024) demonstrate impressive capabilities in various scenarios, such as coding, legal, medical, etc.
898
      To make AI helpful and safe, researchers (Ganguli et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2019; Solaiman &
      Dennison, 2021; Korbak et al., 2023) make efforts for the alignment techniques of LLMs. First,
899
      the alignment of LLMs begins with collecting high-quality data (Ethayarajh et al., 2022), which can
900
      reflect human values. Concretely, (Bach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022c) utilize the existing NLP
901   benchmarks to construct the instructions. And (Wang et al., 2022b) adopt stronger LLMs to generate
902   new instructions via in-context learning. Besides, (Xu et al., 2020; Welbl et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
903   2022a) filter the unsafe contents in the pre-training data. Then, in the training process, SFT (Wu
904   et al., 2021) and RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023) are two mainstream techniques.
905   Although the aligned LLMs are successfully deployed, the recent jailbreak attacks (Ding et al., 2023;
906   Lv et al., 2024) reveal their vulnerability and still easily output harmful content.
907
908   A.1.2    JAILBREAK ATTACK ON LLM
909
      Jailbreak attacks on LLMs, which aim to enable LLMs to do anything, even performing harmful
910
      behaviors, are an essential and challenging direction for AI safety. The jailbreak attack methods
911
      can be roughly categorized into two classless, including white-box and black-box methods. The
912   pioneer white-box method GCG (Zou et al., 2023) is proposed to jailbreak LLMs by optimizing a
913   suffix via a greedy and gradient-based search method and adding it to the end of the original harm-
914   ful prompts. Interestingly, they find the transferability of the generated attacks to public interfaces,
915   such as ChatGPT. Following GCG, MAC (Zhang & Wei, 2024) introduce the momentum term into
916   the gradient heuristic to improve the efficiency. In addition, AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024b) proposes
917   the hierarchical genetic algorithm to automatically generate stealthy harmful prompts. And (Zhu
      et al., 2023) enhance the readability of the generated prompts to bypass the perplexity filters more


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      easily by designing the dual goals of jailbreak and readability. Moreover, COLD-Attack (Qin et al.,
919   2022b) enables the jailbreak method with controllability via the controllable text generation tech-
920   nique COLD decoding (Qin et al., 2022a). And EnDec (Zhang et al., 2024) misguide LLMs to
921   generate harmful content by the enforced decoding. Besides, (Huang et al., 2023) propose the gen-
922   eration exploitation attack via simple disrupt model generation strategies, such as hyper-parameter
923   and sampling methods. I-FSJ (Zheng et al., 2024b) exploit the possibility of effectively jailbreaking
924   LLMs via few-shot demonstrations and injecting system-level tokens. (Geisler et al., 2024) revisit
925   the PGD attack (Madry, 2017) on the continuously relaxed input prompt. AdvPrompter (Paulus
926   et al., 2024) proposes the training loop alternates between generating high-quality target adversar-
927
      ial suffixes and finetuning the model with them. (Rando & Tramèr, 2023) consider a new threat
      where the attack adds the poisoned data to the RLHF process and embeds a jailbreak backdoor to
928
      LLMs. Although achieving promising performance, the white-box methods (Hong et al., 2024; Li
929
      et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024a; Abad Rocamora et al., 2024; Volkov, 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Jia
930   et al., 2024; Liao & Sun, 2024) need to access the usually unavailable resources in the real attacking
931   scenario, e.g., model weights or gradients. Besides, their transferability to closed-source chatbots is
932   still limited.
933
      To solve this problem, the black-box jailbreak attack methods (Shen et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024;
934
      Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2023a; Russinovich et al., 2024) are increasingly
935
      presented. They merely access the interface of the Chat-bot, i.e., requests and responses, and no need
936   to access the model weights or gradients, thus making it possible to effectively attack the commercial
937   Chat-bots, e.g., GPT (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude (Team, 2024), Gemini (Anil et al., 2023; Reid
938   et al., 2024), etc. One classical method named PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) can produce a jailbreak with
939   fewer than twenty queries by using the attacker LLM to iteratively attack the target LLM to refine the
940   jailbreak prompts. In addition, TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2023) improves the iterative refine process via
941   the tree-of-thought reasoning. Besides, (Yu et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024) are proposed from the idea
942   of the fuzzing techniques in the software testing. PromptAttack (Xu et al., 2023b) guides the victim
943   LLM to output the adversarial sample to fool itself by converting the adversarial textual attacks
944
      into the attack prompts. IRIS (Ramesh et al., 2024) leverages the reflective capability of LLMs
      to enhance the iterative refinement of harmful prompts. DRA (Liu et al., 2024a) jailbreak LLMs
945
      by the proposed disguise-and-reconstruction framework. Motivated by the Milgram experiment,
946
      (Li et al., 2023) proposes DeepInception to hypnotize the LLM as a jailbreaker via utilizing the
947   personification ability of LLM to construct a virtual and nested scene. (Anil et al., 2024) explore
948   the jailbreak ability of LLMs via the many-shot learning of harmful demonstrations. In addition,
949   some methods misguide LLMs via the codes (Lv et al., 2024), ciphers (Yuan et al., 2023; Wei
950   et al., 2024), art words (Jiang et al., 2024b), and multilingual (Deng et al., 2023; Yong et al., 2023)
951   scenarios. ReNeLLM (Ding et al., 2023) ensemble the prompt re-writing and scenario constructing
952   techniques to effectively jailbreak LLMs. (Lin et al., 2024) find that breaking LLMs’ defense is
953   possible by appending a space to the end of the prompt. SoP (Yang et al., 2024a) uses the social
954   facilitation concept to bypass the LLMs’ guardrails. (Halawi et al., 2024) introduce covert malicious
955
      finetuning to compromise model safety via finetuning while evading detection. (Jawad & BRUNEL,
      2024) optimize the trigger to malicious instruction via the black-box deep Q-learning. (Wang et al.,
956
      2024e) utilize the harmful external knowledge base to poison the RAG process of LLMs. (Lapid
957
      et al., 2023) disrupt LLMs’ alignment via the genetic algorithm. Besides, (Gu et al., 2024) extends
958   the jailbreak attack to the LLM-based agents. And recent papers (Luo et al., 2024; Shayegani et al.,
959   2023; Chen et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024) propose multi-modal attacks to jailbreak large multi-modal
960   models (LMMs).
961
      Although verified effectiveness, the existing jailbreak attack methods have the following drawbacks.
962
      1) They need to access the model parameters or gradients. 2) They utilize iterative refinement and
963
      cost a large number of queries. 3) They adopt complex and hard assistant tasks such as cipher,
964   code, puzzle, and multilingual, and the assistant tasks easily fail and lead to jailbreaking failure.
965   To this end, this paper mainly focuses on jailbreaking recent state-of-the-art commercial LLMs and
966   proposes a simple yet effective black-box jailbreak method to jailbreak LLMs with merely 1 query.
967
968   A.1.3    JAILBREAK D EFENSE ON LLM
969
970   Jailbreak defense (Xu et al., 2024b) on LLMs aims to defend the jailbreak attacks and keep LLMs
971   helpful and safe. We roughly categorize the jailbreak defense methods into two classes, including
      strategy-based jailbreak defense and learning-based jailbreak defense. For the strategy-based meth-

974        nizes the gradients of safety-critical parameters in LLMs to detect harmful jailbreak prompts. (Phute

           rewindable auto-regressive inference to guide LLMs to evaluate their generation and improve their
