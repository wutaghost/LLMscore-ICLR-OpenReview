007                                               A BSTRACT
008
009            While great success has been achieved in building generalizable language mod-
010            els, three fundamental issues hinder GNN-based graph foundation models: the
011            scarcity of labeled data, different levels of downstream tasks, and the conceptual
012            gaps between domains. In depth, though the labels of real graphs are associated
013            with semantic information, most graph learning frameworks ignore it by turning
014            semantic labels into numerical labels. In this work, to address these issues, we
015            present a new paradigm that leverages the text modality to align downstream tasks
016
               and data with any pre-trained GNN given only a few semantically labeled samples.
               Our paradigm embeds the graphs directly in the same space as the LLM by learning
017
               both graph prompts and text prompts simultaneously. To accomplish this, we
018
               improve state-of-the-art graph prompt method based on our theoretical findings.
019            Then, we propose the first multi-modal prompt learning approach for exploiting
020            the knowledge in pre-trained models. Notably, in our paradigm, the pre-trained
021            GNN and the LLM are kept frozen, so the number of learnable parameters is much
022            smaller than fine-tuning any pre-trained model. Through extensive experiments on
023            real-world datasets, we demonstrate the superior performance of our paradigm in
024            few-shot, multi-task-level, and cross-domain settings. Moreover, we build the first
025            zero-shot classification prototype that can generalize GNNs to unseen classes. The
026            code is provided in the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Foundation Models [4] learn generalizable representations from large-scale data and can be adapted
030   to a wide range of downstream tasks. Although foundation models have shown remarkable capability
031   and been thriving in NLP [14, 7, 112, 80], computer vision [2, 15, 65, 66, 44, 84], and time-series
032   analysis [83, 110, 49], graph-related foundation models still remain in a very nascent stage. This is
033
      due to the significant difference of non-euclidean graph data from other data types. First, compared
      with language or vision data, graph data is very scarce [50, 10, 59] for foundation models. Second,
034
      the task space of graph data could be on node-level [86], edge-level [71], and graph-level [67]. Third,
035
      in general, language tokens and visual objects retain the same conceptual meaning across different
036   distributions, but the same graph structure may have distinct interpretations in different domains,
037   depending on how graphs were constructed from real scenarios. Thus, even if we have a pre-trained
038   model, adapting it to various downstream tasks is not trivial.
039
      Recently, some works [98, 8, 3, 82] reformulate the graphs into natural language descriptions and
040
      the graph tasks into natural language prompts, then query LLMs to generate the answer. However,
041
      since the LLMs are not directly trained from structured graph data [52], it is uncertain how LLMs
042   could correctly solve those tasks without hallucinating [1, 31, 95, 105]. Nevertheless, graph neural
043   networks (GNNs) are well-studied architectures for learning graph data [90, 17, 106], with theoret-
044   ically provable expressiveness [94, 69, 61], better interpretability [13, 34, 74] and experimentally
045   outstanding performance [89, 40]. Therefore, GNNs are expected to leverage their inherent advances
046   for structure learning and inference on graphs in the era of big data and foundation models.
047
      However, though tremendous efforts have been devoted to pre-train GNNs through self-supervision
048   [92, 29, 54], a key problem in building a GNN-backboned graph foundation model is that GNNs do
049   not capture semantics, given that current GNNs are optimized according to numerical labels. In other
050   words, GNNs do not really understand what a label represents in the real world, even though the
051   graphs are constructed from real scenarios. To solve the issue of predetermined numerical categories,
052   CLIP [65] leverages natural language supervision by jointly training an image encoder and a text
053   encoder in the same embedding space to predict the correct image-text pairs at scale. The excellent
      generalization ability of pre-trained V-L models [65, 33, 47] comes from the alignment between


                                                        1
054
      the vision and language representations. Notably, some works have explored prompt learning for
055   better alignment and obtained improvement in vision prediction [114, 41]. The idea of alignment
056   with text modality has also been applied in video [93, 6], 3D images [103, 25, 26], speech [70] and
057   audio [23, 85] areas. As for graphs, so far such CLIP pipelines have only be applied in the molecular
058   domain [58, 56, 72, 51], where the paired graph-text data are relatively sufficient for pre-training
059   to align representations. But for other domains, such text-labeled graph data are rarely available,
060   which means we have to rely more on self-supervised GNN pre-training to build graph foundation
061   models. With this assumption, it is necessary to study how to make the pre-trained GNN aware of the
062   semantics of downstream graph representations, which motivates the following question:
063
      How to adapt pre-trained GNNs to the semantic embedding space given limited downstream data?
064
065   This paper aims to answer this question based on the following observations: (1) Semantic text
066   embedding spaces do not necessarily result from joint pre-training. In fact, the embedding spaces of
067   encoder LLMs are inherently semantic and high-quality, as LLMs are trained on massive text data
068   and demonstrate strong reasoning performance. (2) When the downstream data are limited, prompt
069   learning [48, 28, 102, 45] provides a better option than fine-tuning as much fewer parameters not only
070
      makes the optimization more efficient but also requires less resource than computing the gradient
      of a large model. Inspired by these two observations, we propose a prompting-based paradigm
071
      with an LLM that, while keeping the parameters of both GNN and LLM frozen, aligns the GNN
072
      representations in the LLM’s semantic embedding space.
073
074   Notably, when attempting to adapt the representation from one modality to another, solely prompting
075   a single modality could be sub-optimal, as it limits the adjustment to downstream tasks in the other
076
      modality [41]. To this end, we propose Multi-modal Prompt Learning for Graph Neural Networks
      (Morpher). Given a pre-trained GNN and few-shot semantically labeled graph data, we introduce a
077
      pre-trained LLM. Then, to leverage its high-quality semantic embedding space, Morpher connects and
078
      aligns the graph embeddings to it through prompting on both modalities with a cross-modal projector.
079   Nonetheless, designing such a paradigm is more challenging than vision-language models. First, we
080   lack jointly pre-trained encoders for the two modalities; instead, we only have two encoders whose
081   embedding dimension is possibly different, pre-trained independently in each modality. Second,
082   determining how to prompt the graph modality is non-trivial and remains a trending research topic.
083   Third, the downstream data for GNN usually have much fewer labeled classes than V-L models, so in
084   the few-shot setting, the available downstream data is extremely limited. Our contributions towards
085   tackling these challenges are summarized as follows:
086   • Theoretically, we analyze that, in many cases, state-of-the-art graph prompt [76] is unable to learn
087     good representations of the downstream data. We show that the optimization of the graph prompt
088     is restricted by design. From the theoretical findings, we further improve state-of-the-art graph
089     prompt according to the attention mechanism to prevent failure in optimization.
090   • To connect and adapt the pre-trained GNN with LLM, we propose Morpher, a graph-text multi-
091     modal prompt learning paradigm. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first approach to align
092     the representations of GNN and LLM without fine-tuning any of their parameters.
093   • Experimentally, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our improved graph prompt and Morpher on
094     real-world datasets under few-shot, multi-task, and cross-domain settings.

102   Graph Neural Networks. We use G = (A, X) to denote a graph with node set V and edge set E,
103   where A ∈ R|V|×|V| is the adjacency matrix and X ∈ R|V|×d is the node feature matrix. A(u, v) = 1
104   if there is an edge connecting u and v; otherwise A(u, v) = 0. A Graph Neural Network fϕg (·) with
105   hidden dimension dg encodes G into the embedding space: fϕg (G) ∈ R|V|×dg , which could preserve
106   both feature and structure information of G. The extracted embeddings fϕg (G) can be used for various
107   downstream tasks such as classification. Nowadays, a popular paradigm to train GNNs is to first
      pre-train GNNs via self-supervised learning [29] and then fine-tune on the downstream tasks.

126   3       R EVISITING AND I MPROVING P ROMPT AS G RAPHS
127
      Unlike prompting text data (which can be easily achieved by appending learnable text tokens to
128
      the original text sequence) and prompting image data (which pads a learnable image area above
129
      the original image), prompting graph data presents a significant challenge due to the non-euclidean
130   nature of graphs. The recent pioneering work [76] designs the graph prompt still as a graph, then
131   inserts it into the original graph by computing the inner-connections within the prompt graph and
132   the cross-connections between the prompt graph and the original graph. An advantage of prompting
133   at the graph level is that the downstream tasks of GNN can be reformulated into graph-level tasks.
134   For the node classification task, we can induce the γ-ego-graph of each node by extracting the
135   subgraph within a pre-defined distance γ. Then, we treat the node label as the induced ego-graph
136   label. Similarly, for the edge classification task, we can extract a subgraph for each edge by extending
137   the node pair to their γ distance neighborhood, and use the edge label as the induced graph label. By
138
      inducing subgraphs, we can reformulate node-level and edge-level downstream tasks to graph-level.
139

315   We evaluate our Morpher and the improved graph prompt through extensive experiments. In particular,
316   we show that, compared to state-of-the-art baseline methods, they both more effectively adapt pre-
317   trained GNNs to the specific downstream classification task, and introducing the text modality brings
318   Morpher additional advantages over others. We use RoBERTa [53] as the LLM encoder for Morpher
319   in the main experiments. We also validate the performance of Morpher with ELECTRA [12] and
320
      DistilBERT [68] in section 5.6 and Appendix C.3.
321   Datasets. We use real-world graph datasets from PyTorch Geometric [21], including one molecular
322   dataset MUTAG [60]; two bioinformatic datasets ENZYMES and PROTEINS [5]; one computer
323   vision dataset MSRC_21C [63]; three citation network datasets Cora, CiteSeer and PubMed [97]. We

      Pre-trained algorithms and GNN backbones. To pretrain GNNs for evaluation, we adopt GraphCL
325   [99] and SimGRACE [91] to pre-train three widely used GNN backbones: GCN [43], GAT [100]
326   and GraphTransformer (GT) [42]. Additionally, in Appendix C.4, we verify the effectiveness of our
327   methods on GNNs pre-trained using GraphMAE [27] and MVGRL [24], two other representative
328   GNN self-supervised learning algorithms. For each dataset, to pre-train GNNs, we leverage self-
329   supervised learning methods on all the graphs without any label information.

      Baselines and metrics. We compare our methods with the following baselines: (1) training a GNN
331   from scratch supervised by few-shot data (“supervised”); (2) fine-tuning a task head together with
332   pre-trained GNN (“fine-tune”). We allow GNNs to be tunable for “supervised” and “fine-tune”;
333   (3) state-of-the-art graph prompting algorithms: All-in-one (“AIO”) [76], which is the only graph
334   prompting algorithm that supports multiple tasks in node-level, edge-level and graph-level to the best
335   of our knowledge; GPF-plus [19] which prompt on graph features and Gprompt [55] which is based
336   on subgraph similarity. We use accuracy and weighted F1 as classification performance metrics.
337   5.1 F EW- SHOT L EARNING
338
      We investigate the ability of our improved graph prompt (“ImprovedAIO”) and Multimodal prompt
339
      (“Morpher”) to adapt frozen pre-trained GNNs using few-shot data. We focus on graph-level
340   classification here and will further investigate the few-shot learning ability at other task levels in
341   Section 5.2. Our few-shot learning setting is more challenging than existing works [76, 75] as we only
342   allow no more than 10 labeled training and validation samples for each class. The results are shown
343   in Table 1, where we report the average performance of 5 runs and calculate the absolute average
344   improvement of our methods. From the results, given the same pre-trained GNN, our ImprovedAIO
345   outperforms all the existing baseline methods except for ENZYMES F1 and MSRC_21C accuracy.
346   Yet the performance of our ImprovedAIO on ENZYMES F1 and MSRC_21C accuracy is clearly
347   better than those of the original AIO. Our Morpher can achieve an absolute accuracy improvement
348
      of 0.60% to 7.14% over the baselines across all datasets. Supervised by very limited labeled data,
      training a GNN from scratch is sub-optimal. Passing a GNN pre-trained on the dataset and fine-
349
      tuning it with a task head achieves sub-optimal but better results as the pre-trained GNN learns
350
      generalizable representations over the dataset through self-supervised learning. To mitigate the gap
351   between the pre-training task and downstream tasks, AIO [76] proposes to learn graph prompts for
352   downstream data. However, as we discussed in Section 3, when the node features are sparse vectors,
353   the optimization would fail. Using the official implementation of AIO, we observe that the loss value
354   tends to fluctuate, and the performance of AIO is usually even worse than supervised training. By
355   restricting the cross-connections, our ImprovedAIO becomes more stable and constantly outperforms
356   the fine-tuning baseline. Compared to the aforementioned methods, Morpher demonstrated superior
357   performance due to its capability to adapt both graph and language representation spaces dynamically.

      5.4    Z ERO - SHOT C LASSIFICATION P ROTOTYPE
414
415   An advantage of adapting pre-trained GNNs to the semantic embedding space is that GNNs might
416   be empowered to “reasoning”. Here, we conduct a novel experiment that generalizes GNN to an
417
      unseen class. Since no real-world data is available for this setting, we synthetically create three
      datasets, ZERO-Cora, ZERO-CiteSeer, and ZERO-PubMed, all from real-world connections. We
418
      aim to simulate a citation network with two research areas and an interdisciplinary research area
419
      in between. For each citation network, we randomly sample 120 nodes and induce their 2-hop
420   ego-graphs, then replace the node features in 10 ego-graphs with [1, 0] and another 10 ego-graphs
421   with [0, 1] to construct 20 training graph samples. For the remaining ego-graphs, we uniformly
422   randomly replace the node features with [1, 0] and [0, 1] to construct 100 testing graph samples. We
423   assign text labels of the first research area (e.g., “biology”) to the [1, 0] training graphs, the second
424   research area (e.g., “informatics”) to the [0, 1] training graphs, and the interdisciplinary area (e.g.,
425   “bioinformatics”) to the testing graphs. Intuitively, the nodes with feature [1, 0] are papers in the first
426   area, and other nodes with feature [0, 1] are in the second area, which makes the datasets rational.
427   For each dataset, using GraphCL+GCN, we pre-train GNNs on all graphs. Then, we train Morpher
428   on the training graphs, only knowing the text labels of the two training classes. Since we do not
429   have validation data in zero-shot learning, we report the results of each epoch in Figure 3 (left). We
430   observe that, while Morpher quickly adapts the GNN to downstream training data, the CLIP-like
431   framework can predict the graphs in the novel class with good accuracy (red curve). Moreover, the
      training samples can be classified correctly from training and novel classes. Before the training

      6        R ELATED W ORK
507
508
      GNN Pre-training. Recently, a surge of graph pre-training strategies have emerged to address the
      issue of label scarcity in graph representation learning [29, 57, 75, 46, 39, 113]. The main idea of pre-
509
      trained graph models is to capture general graph information across different tasks and transfer this
510
      knowledge to the target task using techniques such as contrastive predictive coding [42, 20, 64, 91],
511   context prediction [62, 30], prompt tuning [75, 19], and mutual information maximization [62, 73, 35].
512   For instance, [29] proposes to learn transferable structural information from three levels of graph
513   topology, including node-level, subgraph-level, and graph-level. Different from these approaches, this
514   paper aims to build up foundational GNNs by leveraging multi-modal prompt learning techniques.
515   Graph Prompt Learning. Prompting is now mainstream for adapting NLP tasks, and recent studies
516   exploring prompt learning for GNNs mark a thriving research area [77, 87]. It is a promising way to
517   adapt GNNs to downstream tasks through token-level [19, 78, 9, 75, 116] or graph-level [76, 32, 22]
518   prompting. Among all the existing methods, All-in-one (AIO) [76] is the only algorithm to learn
519   tunable graph prompts for node-level, edge-level or graph-level downstream tasks given few-shot
520   labeled data (Table 8). Based on our improved AIO, we present a pioneer study to explore learning
521   prompts in multiple modalities simultaneously while keeping the pre-trained models frozen.
522   LLM on Graphs. Inspired by the advances of large language models in NLP [111], researchers have
523   begun to explore their potential for graph-related tasks [36]. Current approaches can be divided into
524   two main categories. The first category employs LLMs as pre-trained feature extractors to enhance
525   GNNs [16, 11, 115]. For example, GLEM [109] proposes to input the language representation as
526   initial features for the GNN and train them iteratively. The second category focuses on integrating
527
      graph structures directly into LLM architectures [96, 104, 38]. A notable example is Patton [37],
      which pre-trains a joint architecture on text-attributed graphs. Despite these advancements, none of
528
      them have explored the collaboration between LLMs and GNNs under graph prompt learning.
529
530   7        C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we introduce Morpher, the first multimodal prompt learning paradigm that can semanti-
532
      cally adapt pre-trained GNNs to downstream tasks with the help of LLM, while keeping both the
533   pre-trained models frozen. To build Morpher, we first analyze the limitations of the state-of-the-art
534   graph prompting technique and propose an improved version. Through extensive experiments, we
535   demonstrate that our improved AIO can achieve outperformance, and our Morpher has further im-
536   provements in few-shot, multi-level task, or domain transfer settings. Additionally, using Morpher,
537   we build the first GNN zero-shot classifier prototype that can be generalized to novel testing classes.

1366   D    L IMITATIONS
1367
1368   Graph prompt learning assumes the “pre-train + prompt” framework to build graph foundation
1369   models, yet there could be other paths to achieve graph-related foundation models. Also, graph
1370   prompt learning only works on the graph neural network architecture, and might not work for other
1371   architectures that are proposed in the future. Another limitation of this work is the requirement of
1372
       language encoder. While RoBERTa is one of the most advanced encoder-only language models
       and can be considered an LLM with over 0.1B parameters, more recent LLMs such as Llama or
1373
       Mistral cannot be used in Morpher because they are decoder-only LLMs and do not explicitly have
1374
       an encoder. Yet it is possible to retrieve the hidden representation before the decoder layer. We leave
1375   this direction as future work.
