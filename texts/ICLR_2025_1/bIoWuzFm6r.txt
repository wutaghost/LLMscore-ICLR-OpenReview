                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Machine unlearning aims to remove knowledge derived from the specific training
010
               data that are requested to be forgotten in a well-trained model while preserving
011            the knowledge learned from the remaining training data. Currently, machine
012            unlearning methods typically handle all forgetting data in a single batch, removing
013            the corresponding knowledge all at once upon request. However, in practical
014            scenarios, requests for data removal often arise in a streaming manner rather than in
015            a single batch, leading to reduced efficiency and effectiveness in existing methods.
016            Such challenges of streaming forgetting have not been the focus of much research.
017            In this paper, to address the challenges of performance maintenance, efficiency, and
018            data access brought about by streaming unlearning requests, we introduce an online
019
               unlearning paradigm, formalizing the unlearning as a distribution shift problem.
               We then estimate the altered distribution and propose a novel online unlearning
020
               algorithm to achieve efficient streaming forgetting without requiring
                                                                                 ‚ąö access to the
021
               original training data. Theoretical analyses confirm an O(VT T + ‚ąÜT ) error
022
               bound on the streaming unlearning regret, where VT represents the cumulative total
023            variation in the optimal solution over T learning rounds and ‚ąÜT represents the
024            cumulative total divergence between remaining and forgetting data distributions.
025            This theoretical guarantee is achieved under mild conditions without the strong
026            restriction of convex loss function. Experiments across various models and datasets
027            validate the performance of our proposed method.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Machine unlearning aims at safeguarding the privacy rights of individuals concerning sensitive and
033
      private data (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017; Bourtoule et al., 2021; de la Torre, 2018). The objective
      of machine unlearning is to remove information associated with a selected group of data, referred
034
      to as forgetting data, from a well-trained model while retaining the knowledge encapsulated in the
035
      remaining data. (Bourtoule et al., 2021). Presently, research in this field has made some progress
036   in designing effective unlearning algorithms. Current unlearning methodologies typically consider
037   the forgetting data as a single batch and approach unlearning as a singular adjustment process. This
038   process removes all information from the forgetting data at once, then uses the remaining data to
039   repair and update the model, preserving its functionality (Kurmanji et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
040
      In practical scenarios, data removal requests from sensitive information owners are usually made
041
      incrementally, rather than in predetermined batches. For example, social media users might request
042   the deletion of recommendations learned from their personal browsing history at any time, resulting in
043   a continuous stream of individual requests. This streaming nature means that requests are submitted
044   immediately as users identify their needs and often arise in a streaming manner rather than being
045   grouped and processed together. To address the streaming forgetting problem, where removal requests
046   occur incrementally or in streams, existing batch unlearning approaches often handle each request
047   from scratch, one by one. This method faces several issues, especially when requests are frequent.
048   The first issue is the accumulated performance drop. Although the performance degradation usually
049   happens in machine unlearning, previous methods have tried to reduce the degradation in the batch
050   unlearning (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Chundawat et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Graves et al., 2021;
051   Thudi et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2024b). However, this degradation can accumulate across multiple
052   rounds, leading to a significant decline in overall model performance over time. The second issue
053   is efficiency, which stems from the repeated performance repairs on the remaining data (Bourtoule
      et al., 2021; Chundawat et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024b). Due to the high


                                                        1

054
      overlap of remaining data across different rounds, there are significant time and computational costs
055   associated with reprocessing the same data, making the unlearning process inefficient. In addition,
056   frequent access to the remaining data can be problematic due to data regularization policies Voigt
057   & Von dem Bussche (2017); de la Torre (2018). In many cases, parts of the training data may no
058   longer be accessible or may be subject to strict access controls (Sekhari et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023;
059   Chundawat et al., 2023b), hindering the necessary updates and repairs to the model. These limitations
060   emphasize the need for a novel streaming forgetting method that can handle long sequences of data
061   removal requests without performance degradation (Shen et al., 2024a; Gupta et al., 2021). It should
062   optimize time and memory consumption (Nguyen et al., 2022; Tarun et al., 2023) with minimal
063
      reliance on the training data. Although some prior works have explored stream forgetting in the
      context of meta-learning tasks (Chen et al., 2022) and ensemble models (Liu et al., 2022), these
064
      approaches predominantly focus on model forgetting rather than data forgetting. Only two studies
065
      have directly tackled the problem of stream data forgetting (Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2021), both
066   of which impose specific constraints on model structures to realize unlearning (Zhao et al., 2024;
067   Li et al., 2021). As a result, the problem of streaming unlearning for data instances from general
068   well-trained models remains an open research challenge.
069
      In this paper, we propose a novel streaming unlearning method that addresses the accumulated drops
070
      in both effectiveness and efficiency while reducing the need for frequent access to training data. To
071
      estimate the unlearning risk without training data, we formalize unlearning as a distribution shift
072   problem. The shifted distribution caused by removing forgetting data serves as prior knowledge to
073   make the unlearning process more efficient and accurate. To incrementally update the model towards
074   unlearning, we propose a risk estimator to achieve the optimal model in each streaming round and
075   propose the corresponding streaming unlearning approach ‚Äď SAFE (Stream-Aware Forgetting). Our
076   approach departs from traditional batch unlearning by incorporating dynamic regret risk and reducing
077   reliance on original training data. Furthermore, our theoretical analysis guarantees
                                                                                         ‚ąö the effectiveness
078   of SAFE by providing the upper bound on the unlearning regret risk of O(VT T + ‚ąÜT ), where
079   VT represents the cumulative total variation in the optimal solution over T learning rounds, and
              PT
080   ‚ąÜT = t=1 div(Dt , Ft ) represents the cumulative divergence between the remaining and forgetting
081   data distributions. This result holds without assuming the convexity of the loss function. To evaluate
082   the practical performance of SAFE, we conduct empirical experiments on both basic machine learning
083   models and deep neural networks across various datasets.
084   The contributions of this paper can be summarised as follows:
085
086          ‚ÄĘ We introduce the online unlearning paradigm and the SAFE algorithm to address the stream-
087
               ing unlearning problem. SAFE maintains high predictive performance on the remaining
               data while ensuring high unlearning efficiency. Notably, it does not require repeated access
088
               to the original training data during unlearning.
089                                                    ‚ąö
090
             ‚ÄĘ We are the first to provide an O(VT T + ‚ąÜT ) upper bound on the unlearning regret
               risk of the proposed algorithms through theoretical analysis, showing that the unlearning
091
               performances are proportion to the distribution divergence of remaining and forgetting data.
092
             ‚ÄĘ Through empirical evaluations across multiple datasets and models, we demonstrate that
093
               SAFE achieves higher or comparable performance more efficiently than other state-of-the-art
094
               batch unlearning methods, especially on neural network-based models.

315   models with weights {wt }Tt=1 , fulfilling the unlearning requests {Ft }Tt=1 .
316   In each unlearning round, the primary algorithmic time consumption occurs during distribution shift
317   inference and the gradient update for RŐāt (w0 ). Since the computational load for these stages remains
318   constant regardless of the number of unlearning rounds, the time consumption per round is constant,
319   resulting in a linear increase in total time consumption with the number of rounds. Regarding memory
320   consumption, the storage requirements for distribution shift inference variables and model gradients
321   remain unchanged in each unlearning round, leading to a constant total memory consumption across
322   all unlearning requests.

515   4.4     A BLATION S TUDY
516
517
      Table 2 presents the results of the ablation study for the proposed algorithm, where we sequentially
      remove the distribution shift loss (DSR), the forgetting data gradient (FR), and the initial training
518
      data gradient (TR). First, when the distribution shift loss is removed, the forgetting data accuracies on
519
      all four datasets are similar to the original forgetting data accuracies, implying that the information of
520   the streaming forgetting data has not been effectively removed from the model. Second, when only
521   the forgetting data gradient is removed, the forgetting data accuracies approach those of the retrained
522   models. However, the results of the complete SAFE still outperform those without the forgetting
523   data gradient. Lastly, when the initial training data gradient is removed, there is a significant drop in
524   accuracies across all datasets.
525
      These experimental results indicate that the distribution shift loss and the forgetting data gradient
526   contribute significantly to the unlearning process. The distribution shift loss is the dominant factor,
527   while the forgetting data gradient also provides a substantial contribution. Additionally, the initial
528   training data gradient is crucial in maintaining overall performance.

      5      C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we address the practical requirements of long sequential unlearning by introducing an
533
      online unlearning paradigm. This paradigm is designed to realize sequential unlearning requests with
534   high forgetting accuracy and efficiency. We first conceptualize unlearning as the distribution shift
535   problem and estimate the Multivariate Gaussian distribution of low-dimensional vectors of the training
536   data of each class. Then, we propose a novel SAFE (Stream-Aware Forgetting) algorithm alongside
537   a first-order optimization that can reach a low regret bound. We conducted extensive experiments
538   and the results show that SAFE consistently achieves top or near-top performances across various
539   evaluations, including more than double the time efficiency compared with the second-most efficient
      algorithm, demonstrating its clear advantages over other baseline methodologies.

      A.1     R ELATED W ORK
705
706   A.1.1    M ACHINE U NLEARNING
707
708   Machine unlearning requires the removal of information of forgetting data from the original model
709
      while preserving the knowledge contained in the remaining data (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
      2024). Current research on machine unlearning can be categorized into two primary branches based
710
      on unlearning requests: batch unlearning and streaming unlearning. Batch data unlearning focuses
711
      on removing a specific data group within the same batch (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Thudi et al., 2022;
712   Chundawat et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Kurmanji et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024a;b). This approach
713   typically requires access to the original training data and fine-tuning it to maintain high prediction
714   performance. For instance, (Bourtoule et al., 2021) proposes retraining the model using small data
715   shards from the remaining dataset and ensembling the final results for increased efficiency. Similarly,
716   (Thudi et al., 2022) performs incremental training with the forgotten data in the first batch, recording
717   gradients during the initial batch and adding these recorded gradients to the weights after incremental
718   training. In contrast, streaming unlearning addresses continuous data removal requests (Gupta et al.,
719   2021; Li et al., 2021; Neel et al., 2021; Chien et al., 2024). For example, (Gupta et al., 2021) extends
720
      (Bourtoule et al., 2021) to be more adaptive to incremental and decremental learning requests in
      a streaming context. (Neel et al., 2021) proposes a perturbed gradient-descent algorithm on data
721
      partitions to update models for stream unlearning requests. (Chien et al., 2024) fine-tunes the model
722
      with noisy gradients for unlearning, which can be extended to streaming unlearning with limited
723   error increase. However, these approaches still face limitations. Some are restricted to convex
724   loss functions (Neel et al., 2021), while others still rely on full training data access and retraining
725   throughout the unlearning process (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Chien et al., 2024). These divergent
726   methodologies underscore the challenges of efficiently applying machine unlearning across various
727   data types and model structures (Gupta et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Neel et al., 2021; Chien et al.,
728   2024).
729
730   A.1.2    O NLINE L EARNING
731
      Online learning focuses on the learning task of a continuous data stream, which regards the mini-
732
      mization of regret risk as the objective (Hoi et al., 2021). With the increasing size of the incoming
733   data, the training data distribution will face a significant shift. Therefore, adapting to the shifted
734   distribution effectively and efficiently becomes one of the main tasks of online learning. Among
735   these works, the extensions of the regret risk become a requisite research. The basic regret compares
736   the cumulative risks among models in each unlearning step with the global optimal model after
737   learning all data in the stream (Zinkevich, 2003b); the adaptive regret reduces the time length of
738   the whole stream into smaller time windows and compares the cumulative risks with the optimal
739   models in different time windows (Hazan et al., 2006); the dynamic regret directly compares the
740   updated models with the optimal ones for each learning request (Zinkevich, 2003a; Besbes et al.,
741
      2015; Zhang et al., 2018). In addition, optimizing the regret risks determines the effectiveness and
      efficiency of the online learning algorithm. Specifically, the optimization methods include Online
742
      Gradient Descent (OGD) (Zinkevich, 2003b) for the first-order optimization and Online Newton Step
743
      (ONS) (Hazan et al., 2007) for the second-order optimization. In addition, Online Mirror Descent
744   (OMD) (Duchi et al., 2010) is also a common approach, which generalizes OGD to perform updates
745   in the dual space, which can be transformed through a regularize. Noticed that under different types
746   of regret, different optimization methods can reach different rigorous error bounds. Last but not
747   least, in practical problems like label shift problems, several online learning algorithms have been
748   proposed (Wu et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022; Baby et al., 2023), which connect the label shift with
749   online learning via continuously updated classification margins. All the algorithm algorithms focus
750   on the learning tasks with data stream while there is still a research gap in exploring online algorithms
751   for the streaming unlearning request.
752   Online unlearning differs from online learning in the following aspects: Firstly, in machine unlearning,
753   the initial model already has comprehensive knowledge about all the training data, including the data
754   to be forgotten. Online unlearning aims to remove the information about the forgotten data, whereas
755   online learning focuses on learning from newly incoming data. This makes the unlearning process
      inherently more challenging than the learning process. Secondly, the availability of training data in


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      online learning and online unlearning differs. In online unlearning, the size of the remaining data
757   progressively decreases, and the models must adapt to new optimal states based on this remaining
758   data. However, online unlearning does not provide access to the remaining data and must be addressed
759   based on the provided forgetting data. Thirdly, from a practical standpoint, our online unlearning
760   method, SAFE, does not assume any convexity or pseudo-convexity for the training loss. We only
761   assume bounded weights and gradients on the training data. These assumptions are more practical
762   as the initial model in unlearning problems has been well-trained, and the gradients on the current
763   training data have stabilized.

       In the experiments, we choose four datasets: DIGITS (MNIST) (LeCun, 1998), FASHION (Fashion

1057
       use the original DIGITS, FASHION, and CIFAR10 datasets from torchvision module. For CIFAR10
1058
       Features, we choose a well-trained ResNet-18 model to extract the 512-dimensional features.

1061   for the two MNIST datasets. The output channels for the two convolutional layers are 16 and 32,
1062

1063

       B.2    BASELINES
1069
1070
1071
       We compare the performance of the SAFE algorithm with Retrain which are the standard results
       from the retraining models, and four other state-of-the-art unlearning works with high efficiency and
1072
       potential to handle sequential unlearning requests: L-CODEC (Mehta et al., 2022), Descent-U (Neel
1073
       et al., 2021), Unrolling (Thudi et al., 2022), and CaMU (Shen et al., 2024a). L-CODEC (Mehta
1074   et al., 2022) first apply the pruning strategy to select the model parameters that are associated with the
1075   selected forgetting data and then apply gradient ascent algorithm as shown in (Sekhari et al., 2021)
1076   on the selected parameters for unlearning; Descent-U (Neel et al., 2021) calculate the gradients of
1077   remaining data and use the perturbed gradient descent for unlearning; Unroll (Thudi et al., 2022)
1078   records gradients when learning the first epoch and adds recorded gradients on weights after the
1079   incremental training; CaMU construct the counterfactual samples for each forgetting sample and
       implement unlearning on both representation and prediction levels.

1115   SAFE was implemented in Python 3.9.16 and Cuda 11.6.1. The main Python packages‚Äô versions are
1116   the following: Numpy 1.23.5; Pandas 2.0.1; Pytorch 1.13.1; Torchvision 0.14.1.
1117   All the experiments on these baselines are conducted under 10 random seeds based on the original
1118   models trained in the four datasets. We train two CNN models on two MNIST datasets for 20 epochs
1119   with a learning rate of 1e-3 and a weight decay of 1e-4. In contrast, we train the logistic regression
1120   model on the CIFAR10 Feature dataset for 30 epochs using an Adam optimizer with a learning rate
1121   0.05. We train another ResNet-18 model on the CIFAR10 dataset for 20 epochs, where the learning
1122   rate is set as 0.1 and other hyperparameters are the same as the code2 . For the two MNIST datasets,
1123
       the batch size is set as 32, and for the other two datasets, the batch size is 128. For the retrained
       models, we adopt the same hyperparameters as the training process of the original model.

1129   the gradients of each parameter together because the different parts in RŐāt are linearly added.

1389    B.7    A BLATION S TUDY
1390
1391    Table 9 presents the results of the ablation study for the proposed algorithm, where we sequentially
1392    remove the distribution shift loss (DSR), the forgetting data gradient (FR), and the initial training
1393    data gradient (TR).
1394    First, when the distribution shift loss is removed, the forgetting data accuracies on all four datasets
1395    are similar to the original forgetting data accuracies, implying that the information of the streaming
1396    forgetting data has not been effectively removed from the model. Second, when only the forgetting
1397    data gradient is removed, the forgetting data accuracies approach those of the retrained models.
1398    However, the results of the complete SAFE algorithm still outperform those without the forgetting
1399    data gradient. Lastly, when the initial training data gradient is removed, there is a significant drop in
1400
        forgetting data accuracies, remaining data accuracies, and test accuracies across all datasets.
1401    These experimental results indicate that the distribution shift loss and the forgetting data gradient
1402    contribute significantly to the unlearning process. The distribution shift loss is the dominant factor,
1403    while the forgetting data gradient also provides a substantial contribution. Additionally, the initial
        training data gradient is crucial in maintaining overall performance.


                                                                  26
        Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
        In addition, from the comparison of JS-Divergences, we observe that although experiments without
1405    distribution shift loss and forgetting data gradient do not achieve perfect unlearning, they still result
1406    in lower JS-Divergences compared to the retrained model. This phenomenon indicates that minor
1407    adjustments to the original model can lead to lower JS-Divergences on all the remaining data, as well
1408    as forgetting and test data. This implies that the unlearning algorithm can produce prediction results
1409    similar to those of the retrained model. However, there remain differences in the model parameters
1410    and soft prediction results compared to the retrained models.

1474

1475   experiments on the MNIST, CIFAR10, and TinyImagenet datasets to assess the feasibility of stream-
1476   for-class unlearning. For the TinyImagenet dataset, we choose the ResNet-18 model as well. In these

1478   UA (training accuracy on the forgotten class), TA(R) (test accuracy on the remaining classes), and

1481   the task to full-class unlearning.

1506
1507
       As the first work to introduce online scenarios and dynamic regret into the unlearning problem, this
1508
       paper has some limitations.
1509                                                                                                      ‚ąö
1510   First, in the theoretical part, we prove an upper bound of dynamic regret proportional to (1 + VT ) T .
1511   However, this upper bound is suboptimal because we cannot determine the real value of VT in
       experiments, making it difficult to obtain a tighter upper bound. Second, in the experimental part, we


                                                                28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       use the maximal weight of the original model as the upper bound of the model weight W . However,
1513   the weight of the updated model may exceed W , which could affect the accuracy of our results. Third,
1514   in practical applications, the size of the forgetting data at each time point of the streaming unlearning
1515   requests may not be fixed. For experimental convenience, we fixed the size of the forgetting data in
1516   this work, but this does not fully reflect real-world scenarios.
1517
       These limitations inspire several directions for future work. First, we aim to find better optimization
1518
       approaches and more effective learning rates to narrow the upper bound of dynamic regret, ensuring
1519   both theoretical correctness and practical feasibility. Second, we plan to explore more extensions of
1520   this work in practical unlearning applications, particularly focusing on irregular streaming unlearning
1521   requests and the sample-to-class unlearning task.
