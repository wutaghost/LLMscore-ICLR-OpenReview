                                                       A BSTRACT
009
010
011            This paper investigates a fundamental yet overlooked design principle of artificial neural
012            networks (ANN): We do not need to build ANNs layer-by-layer sequentially to guarantee
013            the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) property. Inspired by biological intelligence, where
014
               neurons form a complex, graph-structured network, we introduce the transformative Cyclic
               Neural Networks (Cyclic NN). It emulates biological neural systems’ flexible and dynamic
015
               graph nature, allowing neuron connections in any graph-like structure, including cycles.
016
               This offers greater flexibility compared to the DAG structure of current ANNs. We further
017            develop the Graph Over Multi-layer Perceptron, the first detailed model based on this new
018            design paradigm. We experimentally validate the advantages of Cyclic NN on widely tested
019            datasets in most generalized cases, demonstrating its superiority over current layer-by-layer
020            DAG neural networks. With the support of Cyclic NN, the Forward-Forward training
021            algorithm also firstly outperforms the current Back-Propagation algorithm. This research
022            illustrates a transformative ANN design paradigm, a significant departure from current
023            ANN designs, potentially leading to more biologically similar ANNs.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Artificial intelligence (AI) has reshaped our daily lives and is expected to have a much greater impact
029   in the foreseeable future. Lying behind the most profound AI applications (Silver et al., 2017; OpenAI,
030   2023; Ramesh et al., 2021; Jumper et al., 2021), artificial neural networks (ANN) such as multi-layer
031   perception (MLP) (Rumelhart et al., 1986), convolution neural network (CNN) (LeCun et al., 1995) and
032   Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) are designed specifically for different domains to fit the training data.
033   Regardless of the structure, they are stacked layer-by-layer to form deep ANNs for greater learning capacity.
034   It has been a de facto practice until now that data is first fed into the input layer and then propagated through
035   all the stacked layers to obtain the final representations at the output layer. This paper seeks to answer a
036   fundamental question in ANNs: “Do we really need to stack neural networks layer-by-layer sequentially?"
037   To answer this question, let’s first examine the evidence from biological intelligence (BI). Neuroscientists
038   have studied the biological neurons for decades. The connectome of C. elegans is the most thoroughly
039   studied biological neural system, and biologists depicted the most detailed connection between 302 biological
040   neurons (White et al., 1986; Cook et al., 2019) as shown in Figure 1(a). Rather than being stacked layer-
041   by-layer, all the neurons form a complicated connection graph, where each can connect to several other
042   neurons within the system. We cannot even determine which neuron serves as the input/output within
043
      the neural system to process information. The same findings have also been observed in the latter more
      complicated neural systems, such as the biology neural connectome of drosophila larva (Winding et al., 2023),
044
      zebrafish (Brooks et al., 2022), mouse (Sporns & Bullmore, 2014) and the human brain (Shapson-Coe et al.,
045
      2024). Observed biological intelligence exhibits graph-structured, flexible, and dynamic neural systems, which
046   are apparently different from the current layer-by-layer ANNs we build nowadays, as depicted in Figure 1(b).

070
071   Cyclic NN distinguishes itself from the current layer-by-layer ANNs in several aspects. 1) More flexible
072   neuron connections. Cyclic NN greatly increases the design space of ANNs beyond the DAG structure. In
073   Cyclic NN, the information flow is not as unidirectional as in DAG. Former neurons can also adjust based on
074
      the information encoded by the latter neurons, which largely enhances information communication within the
      network. The flexible connection design also makes Cyclic NN more like the biological neural system. 2)
075
      Localized training. Instead of current dominating global loss-guided BP-based training, Cyclic NN is based
076
      on localized training, i.e., each neuron is optimized with its own local loss function. There is no gradient
077   propagating between neurons. Localized training has its unique advantages. It frees the need to build DAG
078   dependency between neurons, which is the bedrock of supporting cycles within the network. Also, each
079   neuron is optimized independently without waiting for gradients from the latter layers. 3) Computational
080   neuron. Different from current ANNs that a neuron is considered as a d dimension to 1 dimension vector
081   mapping; the neuron within Cyclic NN is considered the computational neuron with greater computation
082   capacity because it is the optimization unit to fit the local task, which requires more parameters. This paper
083   uses a linear layer to parameterize each computational neuron to fit the local classification task. It is also
084   evident by the study of biological neuron (Beniaguev et al., 2021), which empirically proves the learning
085
      capacity of a biological neuron is much larger than a d dimension to 1 dimension vector mapping function as
      the neuron defined within current ANNs. We take this observation and propose the computational neuron in
086
      Cyclic NN with more capable computation to fit the local optimization task. In summary, our contributions
087
      can be summarized as follows:
088
089   • Conceptually, we compare BI and AI to investigate a fundamental yet overlooked design principle: We do
090     not need to satisfy the DAG constraint when designing ANNs.
091   • Methodologically, we propose the transformative Cyclic NN, a novel ANN design paradigm that supports
092     a much more flexible connection between neurons, which discards current directed acyclic computation
093     graph constraints.

100   2     C YCLIC N EURAL N ETWORK
101
      One Cyclic NN model is one graph G = (V, E), where V = (N1 , N2 , ..., N|V| ) is the computational
102
      neuron set and E = (S1 , S2 , ..., S|E| ) is the synapse set denoting the connections among neurons. Similar
103
      to the BI system, Ni (∀i ∈ {1, 2, · · · , |V|}) is the neuron that tackles the detailed computation, and Sj
104
      (∀j ∈ {1, 2, · · · , |E|}) is the synapse that propagates information between computational neurons. In the
105
      Cyclic NN, computational neurons can be connected flexibly in any way, like the BI system.

127   2.3   L OCAL O PTIMIZATION
128
      Local optimization is the bedrock to support Cyclic NN, which is also a distinguishing point compared
129   with current ANNs. For current ANNs, inputs are propagated through computational neurons to obtain the
130   final representation. Designing a global loss function Lglobal based on the final representation and ground-
131   truth labels is the de facto practice to train ANNs. Lglobal is optimized with BP algorithm to propagate the
132   error signal layer-by-layer, which also prohibits the formation of computation cycle. Conversely, Cyclic
133   NN depends on local optimization, i.e., each computational neuron is optimized locally without gradients
134   propagated from other computational neurons. For the computational neuron N , Cyclic NN has a local loss
135   function LLocal to optimize its parameters. The local optimization principle is similar to the BI system, where
136   each neuron can learn from its local context.

291   3.5   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
292   Assume we have a Cyclic NN represented as G = (V, E), where each v ∈ V is a linear layer and each
293   e ∈ E represents an edge between two linear layers. To make the analysis easier, let’s firstly assume the
294   time complexity for each computation neuron is O(1). Then given timestep T , the input goes within the
295   graph structure and cycles along all edges within E for T times, which takes O(T ∗ |E|). Within each cycle,
296   a O(|V|) is needed to calculate both the forward/backward pass within each computation block. The total
297
      time complexity during each step training is O(T ∗ |E| ∗ |V|). Inference time complexity is the same as we
      only omit the backward pass. By comparison, current layer-by-layer structure is a special case for Cyclic
298
      NN when the graph is organized as chain graph and enabling back-propagation between layers to remove T .
299
      Under this case, the time complexity of current layer-by-layer form would be O(|V|).
300
301   To be noted that, experiment in Section 4.3 finds the timestep T is usually very small (≤ 5 in all tested
302   datasets). So the complexity of proposed Cyclic NN can be O(|E| ∗ |V|) in practice. Besides, Cyclic NN is
303
      also optimized with local losses on each computation block, which frees the backward lock problem Dean
      et al. (2012); Löwe et al. (2019) inherited within the Backward-Propagation algorithm. Cyclic NN enables
304
      asynchronous parallel update of all computation neurons V at the same time. So the time complexity of
305
      Cyclic NN can be further reduced to O(|E|) in practice.
306
307   Let’s take the example of GOMLP and further consider the time complexity of each computation neuron.
308   The maximum complexity for each computation neuron is O(|V − 1|d ∗ d) = O(|V|d2 ) when it receives
309
      information from all the other computation neurons. So the total time complexity of GOMLP is O(|E||V|d2 ).

      3.6   A NALYSIS ON A DVANTAGE OF C YCLIC S TRUCTURE
311
312
313   With piecewise linear function (such as ReLu Nair & Hinton (2010) used in this work) as the activation
314   function, the neural network splits the whole input space as different linear regions, and the network
315
      expressiveness can be quantitatively measured by the maximal number of those regions Montufar et al. (2014);
      Raghu et al. (2017). Previous research has proved a rectifier neural network with n0 input units and L hidden
316
      layers of width n ≥ n0 can compute functions that have Ω(( nn0 )(L−1)n0 nn0 ) linear regions Montufar et al.
317
      (2014). It shows neural network depth has an exponential advantage impact on its expressive power.
318
319   To analyze the impact of the proposed cyclic structure on the network’s expressiveness, we compare two
320   scenarios: one without cyclic connections and another with cyclic connections, as illustrated in Figure 3(a)
321   and (b), respectively. In the absence of a cyclic structure, as shown in Figure 3(a), the network depth remains
322   fixed, determined solely by the number of layers. However, when a cyclic structure is introduced, as depicted
323
      in Figure 3(b), the model depth effectively increases with the propagation steps T . Specifically, at T = 1,
      the output of each layer corresponds to a depth of 1, as it directly incorporates information from the input.
324
      At T = 2, each layer aggregates two types of information: depth-0 information directly from the input and
325
      depth-1 information propagated from neighboring computational neurons, resulting in a maximum depth
326   of 2. As T increases, the depth of the information available to each layer grows proportionally, enhancing
327   the network’s expressiveness. The cyclic structure increases the model’s effective depth through iterative
328   propagation, allowing the network to achieve greater expressiveness without additional parameters.

397
398   This section tests the impact of introduced hyper-parameters (T and θ). Results are shown in Figure 4. T
399   controls the number of propagation between computational neurons. Larger T indicates more times the
400   information is propagated. We can observe an error rate trend that first decreases and then increases on all
401
      three datasets. It indicates that the computational neurons need a suitable propagation number. When T is
      small, computational neurons can not draw sufficient lessons from each other. When T is large, computational
402
      neurons are over-propagated, which leads to the over-smoothing problem. Different from graph neural
403
      network (Chen et al., 2020) where the graph is data and the over-smoothing occurs among node representation,
404   in GOMLP, the graph is the model, and the over-smoothing occurs among computational neurons.
405
406   θ controls the goodness threshold of each computational neuron. We can observe a sharp error rate decrease
407
      when θ increases from 0 to 1, and then it gets stable with larger θ. It indicates the existence of the goodness
      threshold matters more than the threshold value. When θ = 0, there is little room to optimize the computational
408
      neuron, which can lead to the training collapse as the computational neuron can not differentiate the negative
409
      samples. When θ is larger, there is more room to optimize the goodness score toward the negative samples, as
410   all the goodness scores under the threshold can represent a negative sample.

      5.1   A RTIFICIAL N EURAL N ETWORKS
428
429   Artificial neural networks (ANNs) have evolved through various paradigms, each suited to specific tasks and
430   data structures. Feedforward neural networks (MLPs Rumelhart et al. (1986), CNN LeCun et al. (1995), and
431   Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017), etc) form the foundational class of ANNs. These models are characterized
432   by their layer-by-layer processing, making them effective for structured data tasks He et al. (2016); Vaswani
433
      et al. (2017). Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) Elman (1990) and their variants such as Long Short-
      Term Memory Schmidhuber et al. (1997) and Gated Recurrent Units Cho (2014) introduced recurrent loops,
434
      enabling temporal modeling for sequential data. Graph Neural Networks (Graph Convolutional Networks Kipf
435
      & Welling (2016), Graph Attention Networks Velickovic et al. (2017), and Graph Isomorphism Networks Xu
436   et al. (2018), etc) extend neural computation to graph-structured data. While GNNs support message passing
437   between nodes, they are typically constrained by acyclic computational graphs. Recently, there are also
438   new ANN designs inspired by biology nerve systems. Liquid neural networks Hasani et al. (2021) adapt
439   dynamically to changing inputs, exhibiting flexible, real-time computation inspired by biological intelligence.
440   Spiking Neural Networks Tavanaei et al. (2019) mimics the communication pattern of biology neurons with
441   discrete spike events instead of continuous activations.
442   Cyclic NN firstly focuses on the network topology similarity with biology neural network by introducing
443   cyclic structures within ANNs. It represents a transformative departure from these existing paradigms
444   by removing the Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) constraint. Inspired by the flexible and dynamic nature
445   of biological neural systems, Cyclic NN introduces cyclic connections between neurons, enabling richer
446   information flow. This design achieves enhanced expressiveness, biological plausibility, and flexibility.
447
448   5.2   L OCALIZED L EARNING A LGORITHM
449   Although the end-to-end BP algorithm is the dominant training algorithm for deep neural networks, studies
450   have revealed notable limitations in such end-to-end training with global objectives (Bengio et al., 2015;
451   Crick, 1989). Numerous works have proposed alternative training methods to make ANNs more biologically
452   plausible. Inspired by Hebbian theory (Hebb, 2005), Hebbian Learning (Gerstner & Kistler, 2002) updates
453   weights locally between two active, connected neurons, ensuring long-term stability so previously learned
454   information is not lost. Addressing the weight transportation problem, Feedback alignment methods (Lillicrap
455   et al., 2016; Nøkland, 2016) replace downstream synaptic weights with random weights, eliminating the
456   need for feedback weights in neurons. Unlike the two phases of the BP algorithm, Equilibrium Propagation
457
      (Scellier & Bengio, 2017) performs both inference and weight updates using only one type of computation.
      The approach in (Nøkland & Eidnes, 2019) reduces memory consumption and increases training parallelism
458
      by adopting subnetworks and layer-wise training. (Hinton, 2022) introduces a simple yet efficient local
459
      objective function that measures the goodness of positive and negative data to optimize ANNs locally.
460
461   The localized learning algorithm is the bedrock that supports the cyclic structure within the neural networks.
462   In this paper firstly beats global BP training with pure localized learning algorithm based on cyclic structure.
463

465   This research introduces Cyclic NN, a novel ANN architecture inspired by the complex, graph-like neural
466   networks in biological intelligence. This transformative design diverges from traditional directed acyclic
467   ANN structures. Our findings, demonstrated through the GOMLP model and validated on various datasets,
468   showed enhanced performance over conventional DAG networks. This significant development paves the way
469   for more flexible and biologically realistic AI systems, representing a major shift in ANN design.
