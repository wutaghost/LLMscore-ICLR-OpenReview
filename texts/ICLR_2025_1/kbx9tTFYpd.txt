009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
                Computational efficiency in deep neural networks is critical for object detection, especially
012
                as newer models prioritize speed over efficient computation (Parameters and FLOP). This
013             trend is evident in the latest YOLO architectures, which focus more on speed at the ex-
014             pense of lightweight design. This evolution has somewhat left lightweight architecture
015             design behind for object detection applications. Unlike speed-oriented object detectors in
016             the literature, SSDLite and low-parameters/FLOP-oriented classifier combinations are the
017             only proposed solutions, leaving a gap between YOLO-like architectures and lightweight
018             object detectors. In this paper, we pose the question: Can an architecture optimized for
019             parameters and FLOPs achieve precision comparable to mainstream YOLO models? To
020             explore this, we introduce LeYOLO, an efficient object detection model, and propose sev-
021
                eral optimizations to enhance the computational efficiency of YOLO-based models. This
                approach bridges the gap between SSDLite-based object detectors and YOLO models,
022
                achieving high precision in a model as lightweight as MobileNets. Our novel model fam-
023
                ily achieves a FLOP-to-accuracy ratio previously unattained, offering scalability that spans
024             from ultra-low neural network configurations ( < 1 GFLOP) to efficient yet demanding ob-
025             ject detection setups ( > 4 GFLOPs) with 25.2, 31.3, 35.2, 38.2, 39.3 and 41 mAP for
026             0.66, 1.47, 2.53, 4.51, 5.8 and 8.4 FLOP(G).

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032
      Initially introduced by Redmon et al. (2016), YOLO models are known for their object detection speed.
      These models have seen significant improvements in neural network architecture in recent years, taking
033
      advantage of modern computing power like GPUs. Essentially, YOLO models feature a backbone like
034
      that of classifiers, a neck that aggregates multiple levels of semantic information, and a head that refines
035   detections across these levels. Detections are made on a grid, with spatial information greatly reduced,
036   meaning detection boxes are aligned pixel by pixel.
037
038
      Despite their inherent speed, there has been a noticeable shift in the development of YOLO models in recent
      years Jocher et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023); Jocher et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024).
039
      With rapid advancements in GPU capabilities and new architectural innovations, the focus has shifted from
040
      lightweight models to those prioritizing speed. Consequently, YOLO models have become significantly
041   faster despite increased parameters and FLOP 1 2 .

117   2   R ELATED WORK
118
119   Our work focuses on finding an optimal architecture for object detection. We have combined two approaches:
120   object detectors optimized for speed and low-cost classifiers that use well-established techniques to reduce
121   the number of parameters.
122
      Lightweight classifiers. LeYOLO draws inspiration from elements known for their high efficiency in op-
123
      timizing the number of parameters. Notably, we leverage the performance of inverted bottlenecks, initially
124
      designed in MobileNetv2 Sandler et al. (2018) and later refined by the EfficientNet Tan & Le (2019; 2021)
125   and GhostNet Han et al. (2020); Tang et al. (2022) families. Inverted bottlenecks, pointwise Lin et al. (2013),
126   and depthwise convolutions are critical in architecture optimization. They play a crucial role in algorithmic
127   contributions like MNASNet Tan et al. (2019), and many recent studies focused on hybridization, which
128   aims to reduce the number of parameters in architectures utilizing self-attention, such as MobileViT Mehta
129   & Rastegari (2022a) and others Wadekar & Chaurasia (2022); Mehta & Rastegari (2022b); Vasu et al. (2023).
130   In our paper, we demonstrate that it is still possible to achieve a higher level of optimization, particularly in
131   object detection, by further refining the use of inverted bottlenecks.
132   Lightweight object detectors. Originally designed to reduce the cost of object detectors by leveraging
133
      the widely respected VGG Simonyan & Zisserman (2014) feature extractor, SSD Liu et al. (2016) is an
      object detector closely related to the early YOLO models Redmon & Farhadi (2018). With the rise of low-
134
      cost classifiers, it became necessary to contribute to this growing field, leading to the creation of SSDLite,
135
      an optimized version of SSD primarily based on the principles of MobileNets with grouped convolutions.
136   Since then, there hasn’t been a method that surpasses this approach. However, SSDLiteX Kang (2023) has
137   emerged as an attempt to improve SSDLite’s performance on MobileNets. On the YOLO side, other re-
138   searchers have also explored optimizing the mainline by incorporating the mentioned elements. Tinyssimo
139   YOLO Moosmann et al. (2023) seeks to reduce overall costs by building on the earliest YOLO architectures
140   Redmon et al. (2016). While the optimization is promising, it doesn’t quite compete with even the lowest


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      scaling levels of YOLO or classifiers combined with SSDLite. Similarly, other studies Fang et al. (2020);
142
      Yang et al. (2022); Hajizadeh et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2020) have used lightweight classifier elements like
143   depthwise convolutions and older techniques such as fire modules Iandola et al. (2016) to reduce the number
144   of parameters. More recently and notably, YOLOX Ge et al. (2021) and YOLOv9 have offered a solid al-
145   ternative to their base scaling by presenting a very lightweight model in terms of parameter count. YOLOX
146   achieves this by using depthwise convolutions instead of standard convolutions, with a kernel size greater
147   than three and a reduced image size. As for YOLOv9, we are exceptionally inspired by the authors’ sig-
148   nificant contribution to the parameters and information optimization field. YOLOv9 keeps an optimization
149   to a mainline YOLO scaling level, discarding mobile-oriented and low-crafted-parameters-oriented neural
150   networks. There is more discussion on the state-of-the-art deep neural network in Appendix A.3.1.
151   Discussion on contribution. We introduce LeYOLO, a model inspired by architectures renowned for pa-
152   rameter optimization. LeYOLO proves that optimizing low-cost object detectors is possible, offering a
153   strong alternative to the lowest scalings of YOLO models. It also provides a significant performance boost
154   compared to the state-of-the-art object detectors in downstream tasks, largely reliant on the well-known
155   SSDLite. Our study offers a highly modular approach compared to existing YOLO solutions and state-of-
156   the-art lightweight classifiers, with YOLOv9 as an example of a comparable study at a larger scale. Our
157
      focus is on ”mobile” or lightweight neural networks, so our proposal is less optimized for block acceleration
      and parallelization techniques, as seen in YOLOv7. Also, the extensive use of depthwise convolution, as
158
      presented in Chapter 3.1.1, might reduce the throughput of our solution, as first introduced in the ShuffleNet
159
      Ma et al. (2018) study.
160
161   LeYOLO brings several optimization and contributions: (i) Better classifier downstream performance.
162   For a given parameter scaling budget, LeYOLO outperforms state-of-the-art low-cost classifiers combined
163
      with SSDLite by reducing the number of parameters and increasing the precision of MSCOCO. Adding
      LeYOLO neck and head to sota low-parameters and cost-oriented backbone enables better throughput, ac-
164
      curacy, and much lower parameters for several scaling. (ii) Tiny scaled YOLO alternative. LeYOLO,
165
      with its optimized backbone, neck, and detection head, surpasses nano and tiny-scaled mainline YOLO neu-
166   ral networks on object detection. The architectural choice of the LeYOLO backbone proves its superiority
167   compared to other low-cost backbones combined with the LeYOLO neck and head, being better at scaling
168   and accuracy-to-parameters and FLOP ratio. (iii) Improved throughput. LeYOLO gets better throughput
169   than sota object detector with low-oriented parameters thanks to its optimized architecture. Both complete
170   LeYOLO and downstream tasks on low-cost classifiers get better throughput. However, LeYOLO reaches
171   its peak strength on mobile, embedded, or low-powerful devices, getting closer to what we seek in terms of
172   parameter efficiency: enabling the power of reliable object detectors directly to inference on small devices,
173   having few parameters to share within clusters of peripheral devices, bringing YOLO closer to edge AI step
174
      by step.

      4.1   D ICUSSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
424
425   LeYOLO FPANet + DNiN Head: Considering the cost-effectiveness of our FPANet and model head, there
426   is a significant opportunity for experimentation across different backbones of state-of-the-art classification
427   models. LeYOLO emerges as a promising alternative to SSD and SSDLite. The promising results achieved
428   on MSCoco with our solution suggest potential applicability to other classification-oriented models We
429   focused our optimization efforts specifically on MSCOCO and YOLO-oriented networks. However, we
430   encourage experimentation with our solution on other datasets as well.
431   Computational efficiency: We have implemented a new scaling for YOLO models, proving that it is pos-
432   sible to achieve very high levels of accuracy while using very few computational resources (FLOP). Nev-
433   ertheless, we are not the fastest in state of the art, as there are speed imperfections due to the (deliberate)
434   lack of parallelizable architecture like our predecessors Sandler et al. (2018); Howard et al. (2019); Tan et al.
435   (2020). However, only YOLOv7 and v6 are faster than our solution on a powerful enough GPU to ensure
436   enough memory space on every YOLO benchmarked. As for the Jetson TX2, it seems LeYOLO is better
437
      blabla-finir. We could further analyze scaling for different edge powers to propose parallelizable column and
      block scaling.

440   4.2   F UTURE WORKS
441
      We encourage further experimentation with our proposal, going deeper into experimental outcomes while
442   exploring various dataset variants tailored to specific industry needs, such as intelligent agriculture and
443   medicine.
444   We aim to provide a broader range of comparisons for LeYOLO in scenarios involving mobile devices
445   with very limited computational resources, thereby demonstrating the ability of LeYOLO’s low-memory-
446   cost Neck to compute different levels of centralized semantic information on P4. Finally, as discussed in
447   the limitations, LeYOLO could occupy a niche between parameter optimization and high execution speed,
448   further advancing current object detection solutions for embedded systems.

      To start, we want to provide more explanation on the main component of this paper: the computation for-
753
      mula. We consistently use the FLOP (Floating Point Operations) metric to compare our work with other
754   state-of-the-art neural networks throughout the paper. By basing computations on the number of multipli-
755   cations and additions required for the neural network, we establish a solid foundation for efficient model
756   comparisons. The FLOP metric remains reliable regardless of the hardware used, making it a good indicator
757   of computational efficiency. Although we could use other metrics such as speed, these are highly depen-
758   dent on factors like neural network architecture parallelization, the hardware used, the accelerator software
759   (TensorRT, CoreML, TFLite), and memory usage and transfer speeds.
760   The second main element we consistently use throughout the paper is mAP (mean Average Precision).
761   Researchers widely use this metric to compare object detection-based neural networks. mAP measures
762   the precision of the model by evaluating the overlap between the proposed bounding boxes and the actual
763   annotated bounding boxes. While some papers compare models using mAP at a fixed threshold of 50%
764   overlap (mAP50), we primarily use mAP50-95. This metric averages the precision over different overlap
765   thresholds, ranging from 50% to 95%, covering a broader range of evaluation criteria.
766   In object detection, where the spatial size of the feature map is essential, we define Pi as the feature map size
767   of our deep neural network. The sizes range from P 0 (640x640 pixels) to P 5 (20x20 pixels) for LeYOLO-
768   Small to Medium, with i representing the number of strides used. Similarly, Pi−1 denotes the size of the
769   preceding feature map for the explicitly described feature map i.
770
      Similarly, when describing hidden layers in the neural network, we refer to the entire block rather than a
771
      single convolution. For example, the paper describes a single inverted bottleneck as one hidden layer hj that
772   consists of two pointwise convolutions and one depthwise convolution. Throughout the paper, this lets us
773   directly refer to the preceding inverted bottleneck as hj−1 .

776   A.3   E XTENDED DISCUSSION ON RELATED WORK
777
778   Sandler et al. (2018); Howard et al. (2019): Inspired by MobileNetv2’s achievements in reducing the num-
779
      ber of parameters and FLOPs in neural networks through inverted bottlenecks, MobileNetv3 demonstrated
      once again that it is possible to further leverage this architecture, which dates back to 2018, to significantly
780
      improve accuracy. This idea led to the development of LeYOLO, an optimization of the inverted bottleneck
781
      focused on finding the optimal number and arrangement of layers based on the spatial size of the inputs.
782   We advanced the concept of a more efficient object detection model by first implementing MobileNetv3-
783   Small within the YOLOv8 API Jocher et al. (2023), yielding much more promising results than those
784   achieved with SSDLite. However, we found the MobileNetv3-Small backbone too slow to reach the ex-
785   tremely fast execution speeds desired in YOLO-related research.
786
      Tishby & Zaslavsky (2015); Han et al. (2021): Inspired by the study by Tishy et al., we compared the layer
787
      count of MobileNetv3 with a more reduced and consequently faster configuration. This led us logically to
788   analyze the work of Dooyan et al. Through a deeper examination of layer count to understand its impact, they
789   concluded that reducing the number of expansion layers rather than extending them, regardless of scaling
790   is more beneficial. We extended this study by demonstrating that it can improve accuracy with expansions
791   smaller than 3 throughout the neural network.
792   In LeYOLO, each part of the model never exceeds an input and output layer count difference of more than 6.
793   The backbone of LeYOLO starts with 16 layers at P1 and ends with 96 layers. The Neck of LeYOLO has a
794   layer count between 32 and 96, with a maximum expansion of 192, ensuring that the layer difference never
795   exceeds 6. Finally, LeYOLOs Head is proportional to the number of input layers and MSCOCO classes.
796   Nakkiran et al. (2021): Nakkiran et al. demonstrated a new way of observing the scaling of deep neural
797   networks, primarily addressing why many parameters on very deep neural networks work well for solving
798   computer vision problems. Their study shows that there is indeed a model scaling that can enhance accuracy


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


799
      with a very large number of parameters. However, they also observed a phenomenon of double descent
800
      when the training curve is plotted not against the number of epochs on the x-axis but against scaling. A
801   regime with few parameters can outperform much larger scalings in this scenario. Therefore, this case study
802   suggests that focusing on a very small number of parameters and performing scaling that fits this range rather
803   than trying to surpass this barrier is worthwhile.
804   As a result, LeYOLO proposes a simple yet controlled scaling approach with a small amplifier for the number
805   of layers and layer repetitions. The final scaling proposed is not based on the number of parameters in the
806   neural network but on its input to maintain the integrity of a modest scaling approach.
807   Howard et al. (2017): MobileNetv4, which came out in the same period we initially worked on LeYOLO,
808   proposes a similar alternative to the inverted bottleneck as we did. They propose to add an optional convo-
809   lution in the inverted bottleneck: a new optional depthwise convolution right at the beginning. We are very
810   interested in the study of MobileNetv4 as they focus on neural architecture search with speed rewards on
811   mobile devices, and we raised the question, does focusing on mobile hardware speeds reduce the number of
812   parameters and FLOP of the neural network?
813   MobileNetv4 is very fast but uses many more parameters than previously done in the state-of-the-art
814   lightweight neural network but paved a very interesting way into shaping a more speed-efficient inverted
815
      bottleneck.
816   We consider LeYOLO and MobileNetv4 complementary as we prove that inverted bottleneck might still be
817   more parameters-efficient, reaching better accuracy with a much smaller number of parameters and compu-
818   tation cost.

      For the sake of readability, we have omitted a discussion of the semantic shcostg strategy in the neck. In
882   the bottom-up pathway, we employ lightweight blocks for upsampling the feature map, while the top-down
883   pathway utilizes standard convolutions. Despite their pronounced cost, strived standard convolutions prove
884   efficient in this context due to their low spatial size and the restrained number of channels used.
885
      We experimented with inverted bottlenecks instead of convolutions but found them more costly and less
886
      accurate. Additionally, we refrain from using any convolutions before the first 20x20 upsampling, diverging
887
      from the approach taken in most YOLO architectures. We question the necessity of another convolution
888   after the Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fusion (SPPF) Jocher et al. (2022; 2023), as it may already be sufficiently
889   efcostent in the backbone. Lastly, the 80x80 aspect is resource-intensive and requires careful consideration.
890   Following similar reasoning, we avoided computation for the 80x80 top-down pathway, as the cost seemed
891   disproportionate to the marginal accuracy improvement, as demonstrated in ablation studies on FPANet’s
892   80x80 pointwise component. Figure 6 represents the complete architecture of LeYOLO.
