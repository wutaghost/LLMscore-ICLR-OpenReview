009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Momentum-based optimization methods, such as Heavy-Ball (HB) and NesterovвҖҷs
012            accelerated gradient (NAG), are essential in training modern deep neural net-
013            works. This work sheds light on the learning dynamics of momentum-based
014            methods and how they behave differently than standard gradient descent (GD)
015
               in theory and practice. A promising approach to answer this question is investi-
               gating the continuous differential equations to approximate the discrete updates,
016
               an area requiring much attention for momentum methods. In this work, we take
017
               HB as a case study to investigate two important aspects of momentum methods.
018            First, to enable a formal analysis of the Heavy-Ball momentum method, we pro-
019            pose a new continuous approximation, HB Flow (HBF), with a formulation that
020            allows the control of discretization error to arbitrary order. As an application of
021            HBF, we leverage it to investigate the implicit bias of HB by conducting a series of
022            analyses on the diagonal linear networks to inspect the influence of momentum on
023            the modelвҖҷs generalization property. We validate theoretical findings in numerical
024            experiments, which confirm the significance of HBF as an effective proxy of mo-
025            mentum methods to bridge between discrete and continuous learning dynamics.
026
027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Gradient descent (GD) and its variants momentum methods, such as PolyakвҖҷs Heavy-Ball momen-
030   tum (HB) [31], NesterovвҖҷs method of accelerated gradients (NAG) [27], and Adam [17], are at the
031   core of the success of training deep neural networks. However, analyzing such discrete learning
032   dynamics is challenging. Thus there is an extensive body of work in developing continuous ap-
033   proximations of GD and SGD (stochastic version of GD) [1; 2; 4; 5; 9; 14; 16; 22; 23; 24; 25].
034   These works advanced our understanding of the black-box generalization ability of the highly over-
035   parameterized deep neural networks trained by GD and SGD. Despite the progress in analyzing GD,
036
      it remains unclear when and why momentum methods are effective. As momentum methods are
      now essential in many practical scenarios, such as the training of Transformer-based models [37],
037
      there is a critical need to understand the working mechanism of momentum methods.
038
039   Existing approaches for analyzing momentum methods typically use continuous differential equa-
040   tions to approximate discrete iterative updates. Representative works in the area include [19; 20; 33;
041   35; 40], which developed several second-order ODEs to analyze the convergence properties of HB
042
      and NAG. [18] demonstrated that the continuous approximation of HB and NAG, which solves the
      optimization problem minОІ L(ОІ), can be expressed as a rescaled gradient flow (RGF):
043
044                                                       вҲҮL(ОІ)

045                                                       1вҲ’Вө
046   where L is the objective function and Вө is the momentum factor. Despite its convenience for studying
047   discrete momentum methods, this approximation has two fundamental drawbacks. First, it overlooks
048
      a substantial portion of discretization error when approaching the continuous limit. Second, it is
      insufficient to differentiate between HB and GD due to its overly simplified nature. For example,
049
050
      the solutions of Eq. (1) and the gradient flow ОІМҮ = вҲ’вҲҮL are similar, suggesting that GD and HB will
      converge to similar points. However, this is inconsistent with actual behavior (Fig. 1(a)), where GD
051
      and HB behave differently and converge to different points.
052
053   To address these issues, the recent work [11] adopted backward error analysis [4; 13] to construct
      a continuous equation for HB as a perturbed version of Eq. (1) that admits smaller discretization

      error. While promising, these approaches still do not account for the gaps between actual discrete
055   algorithms and their continuous approximations. Furthermore, these results are model-agnostic and
056   do not consider other aspects of training, such as model architectures for specific learning tasks.
057
058
      To this end, we develop HB Flow (HBF), a novel continuous approximation of the HB momentum
      method. HBF is designed to be arbitrarily close to HB (see Definition 2.1 for a formal definition).
059
      Specifically, we add a perturbation term to cancel the discretization error to the improved version of
060
      Eq. (1). This term helps to fill the gap between our continuous approximation and the discrete HB.
061   We emphasize that one can control the precision of HBF as a proxy of the discrete learning dynamics
062   of HB to arbitrary orders. Our approximation provides a more reliable foundation for continuously
063   analyzing the less well-studied momentum methods.
064
      In addition, we highlight that one immediate benefit of HBF is that we can apply it to analyze various
065
      intriguing properties of HB while the direct study of discrete learning dynamics is cumbersome.
066
      Among these properties, an important one is its implicit biasвҖ”the preference for particular solutions
067   among all possible ones. For GD/GF, there are already abundant results for understanding their
068   implicit bias for diagonal linear networks [3; 10; 29; 30; 41; 42], a non-convex setting that shares
069   similar properties with more complex neural networks. As an interesting application of the proposed
070   HBF, we analyze the implicit bias of HB for this popular model through the HBF to arbitrary orders
071   of discretization error.
072
073   Contributions. Our primary contribution is a continuous approximation of HB, namely HBF, that
074   can be arbitrarily close to the discrete learning dynamics of HB (Theorem 3.1). HBF provides a reli-
075   able foundation for analyzing HB in a continuous manner. We also present the comparison between
076   HBF and continuous approximation of GD to different orders of approximation, as summarized in
077   Table 1. In addition, as an application of HBF, we explicitly investigate the implicit bias of HBF for
078
      the diagonal linear network (Theorem 4.1 for arbitrary order of approximation and Corollary 4.2 for
      order 2) and reveal its difference compared to that of GF. Our findings are helpful for the understand-
079
      ing of the crucial while less studied implicit bias of momentum methods in a non-convex setting.
080
      This indicates the importance of the proposed HBF as a proxy of HB for analyzing its properties.

      R ELATED W ORKS
084
      The backward error analysis was applied to GD in [4] where the implicit gradient regularization
085   effect was also proposed. With a similar idea, [25; 32] further developed continuous approximations
086   of GD with discretization error that can be arbitrarily smaller. [33; 35; 39; 40] constructed con-
087   tinuous ODEs for momentum methods and primarily focused on the convergence aspects. [18; 11]
088   studied the discretization error of continuous time limit of HB to the first 2 orders. [6] focused on the
089   continuous limit of Adam. As a comparison, we firstly propose the HBF that can be O (О· Оұ )-close
090   to the discrete learning dynamics of HB.
091   The implicit bias of GD for various deep neural networks has been widely studied in, e.g., [16;
092   21; 34; 42] for linear networks and [7; 24; 26] for homogeneous networks. For diagonal linear
093   networks, [3; 10; 29; 30; 41] examined the implicit bias of GD and revealed the interesting transition
094   from kernel to rich regime by altering the scale of initialization. As a comparison, the study of
095   implicit bias of momentum methods is not as fruitful as that of GD. [38; 12] investigated momentum
096   methods for single-layer linear network and showed that they share a similar implicit bias with GD.
097   [15] showed that momentum leads to better generalization in a special setting. The recent work
098   [28] studied HB for diagonal linear networks using continuous approximation that is O (О·)-close
099
      to the discrete HB. To achieve a better understanding of the momentum methods for deep neural
      networks, in this paper, we characterize the implicit bias of HB for diagonal linear networks through
100
      the proposed HBF to arbitrary order of discretization error, which is absent in previous works, and
101
      compare it with that of GF to explicitly reveal their difference.

      discrete learning dynamics of HB. Our initial attempt is based on [18], where the authors showed
128

129

130   ous approximation, one may attempt to directly model HB by perturbing the RGF with an additional
131   term that accounts for the discretization error using the backward error analysis [4]. However, this is
132   problematic due to the fact that each iteration of momentum methods exploits the history of previous
133   iterations, which renders the local error analysis unreliable since it ignores previous updates.
134

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we have established a new continuous approximation of HB, namely HBF, with O (О· Оұ )
489   discretization error to the discrete HB for arbitrary Оұ вүҘ 1. Our results provide a reliable foundation
490   for analyzing the less well-studied momentum methods through the continuous time limit. As an
491   important and interesting application, we have studied the implicit bias of HBF for the popular proxy
492   model diagonal linear networks and revealed the difference between the implicit bias of HB and that
493   of GD which cannot be captured by RGF.
494
      There are also many other momentum and adaptive algorithms, such as Adam [17] and AdaGrad [8],
495   and more complex neural networks. A clear characterization of properties for these momentum
496   methods and neural networks with methods developed in the current work is an interesting direction.
