                                                                                    A BSTRACT
032
033
                                      Foundation models have significantly advanced machine learning applications
034
                                      across various modalities, including images. Recently numerous attempts have
035                                   been made on developing foundation models specifically tailored for remote sensing
036                                   applications, predominantly through masked image modeling techniques. This
037                                   work explores the essential characteristics and performance expectations for a
038                                   foundation model in aerial imagery. We introduce a benchmark designed to evaluate
039                                   the model’s performance as well as robustness to changes in scale and spectral
040                                   bands of the input. Our benchmarks encompass tasks unique to aerial imagery,
041                                   such as change detection and scene classification, and utilize publicly available
042                                   datasets RESISC45, BigEarthNet, LEVIR-CD and OSCD. We evaluate recently
043
                                      proposed foundation models on the benchmark. Furthermore, we explore the
                                      impact of various design choices in pretraining and fine-tuning on the performance
044
                                      of the models on our benchmark. Specifically, we pretrain several variations of a
045
                                      self-distillation based self-supervised model on aerial imagery datasets, including
046                                   one without scale-augmentations and another one with a pretrained mask decoder
047                                   module.
048
049
050        1              I NTRODUCTION
051
052        The rapid advancements in remote sensing technologies have led to an increased reliance on foun-
053        dation models for interpreting vast amounts of imagery data captured by satellites (e.g., Sentinel-1,
           Sentinel-2) (Akiva et al., 2022; Mall et al., 2023; Mañas et al., 2021; Wanyan et al., 2023; Cong et al.,


                                                                                            1

      fi
054
      2022; Reed et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Muhtar et al., 2023; Mendieta et al., 2023;
055   Tang et al., 2023; Fuller et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c; Bastani
056   et al., 2023). Usually, this data is raw and unlabeled, whereas creating labels is time-consuming and
057   expensive. For many critical downstream tasks, including change detection, image classification, and
058   semantic segmentation (utilized for tasks such as land cover mapping, flood or disaster monitoring,
059   urban growth analysis, vegetation health monitoring, and terrain analysis), having a large amount of
060   labeled data is crucial to train effective models. In line with recent advancements in self-supervised
061   and semi-supervised learning for vision tasks, the current trend is to train a self-supervised model
062   (either contrastive or based on masked image modeling) which later serves as a backbone for subse-
063
      quent downstream tasks. Subsequently, a small amount of labeled data can be used to fine-tune this
      self-supervised learning-based backbone, resulting in a competitive model for specific downstream
064
      tasks.
065
066   In this work, we focus on evaluating the performance of established foundation models, specifically
067   designed for remote sensing imagery, in the context of scene classification (Cheng et al., 2017; Yang
068   & Newsam, 2010; Sumbul et al., 2019) and change detection (Chen & Shi, 2020; Lebedev et al., 2018;
069
      Caye Daudt et al., 2018), by focusing on their generalization capabilities across image resolutions
      and bands. To analyze the impact of the design choices made in those foundation models, we develop
070
      another model using a self-distillation approach.
071
072   Our contributions are as follows. (a) We develop a benchmark for remote sensing (RS) foundation
073   models that evaluate them with respect to generalization capabilities of the derived models across
074   scale and input bands. (b) We pretrain several versions of iBOT (Zhou et al., 2022), a self-distillation
075
      based ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), on MillionAID (Long et al., 2021), an aerial imagery dataset, to
      analyze the impact of design choices on our benchmark. One of the versions includes a pretrained
076
      UperNet-like (Xiao et al., 2018) head for segmentation and change detection downstream tasks. (c)
077
      We show that the publicly available foundation models we have tested (Bao et al., 2023; Bastani
078   et al., 2023; Mendieta et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022) have a lot of room for improvement in obtaining
079   generalization capabilities and transferring those capabilities to downstream models.

081
082   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
083
084   Some recent developments in the field include various approaches using either supervised or self-
085   supervised learning algorithms. Surprisingly, for some transformer-based models, performance on
086   ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) in certain instances outperforms those pre-trained on remote sensing
087
      imagery (Vanyan et al., 2023a). The effect of pre-training on ImageNet vs a large remote sensing
      scene recognition dataset is studied in Remote Sensing Pretraining (RSP) (Wang et al., 2023a).
088
      To serve as a pre-training dataset, some existing techniques involve gathering data from available
089
      open-source large remote sensing datasets and employing it to train the self-supervised algorithm.
090   The two main methods to train self-supervised foundation models are contrastive learning-based
091   methods and generative-based methods (masked image modeling).
092
093
      The contrastive learning-based approaches include: SECO (Mañas et al., 2021) employs a gener-
      alization of contrastive learning, defining various types of augmentations (seasonal, artificial, and
094
      mixed). They also created a dataset from Sentinel-2, by first picking locations closer to urban areas
095
      and later generating several images for these locations for various seasons. CACo (Mall et al., 2023)
096   introduces a new contrastive loss called Change Aware Contrastive Loss which considers long-term
097   temporal information in satellite imagery to encourage invariance to seasonal variations while main-
098   taining sensitivity to permanent changes. MATTER (Akiva et al., 2022) presents a material and
099   texture-based approach for self-supervised pretraining to generate good representations for remote
100   sensing downstream tasks. Dino-MC (Wanyan et al., 2023) utilizes the DINO pre-training framework
101   for self-supervised learning by using multiple crops of the same image with different sizes. Finally,
102   (Tolan et al., 2024) pretrained DINOv2 on MAXAR imagery with a 0.59m Ground Sample Distance
103   (GSD) and collected a new high-resolution dataset to further enhance performance.
104   Another stream of works, SatMAE (Cong et al., 2022) Scale-MAE (Reed et al., 2023), RingMO (Sun
105   et al., 2023), SpectralGPT (Hong et al., 2024) extend on Masked Autoencoders (MAE) (He et al.,
106   2022), which is a successful foundation model, based on masked image modeling, where the pretext
107   task is to recover the image, based on its masked version. Scale-MAE (Reed et al., 2023) makes two
      significant contributions to the MAE (He et al., 2022) framework. First, it introduces the GSD-based


                                                         2
108
      positional encoding. Second, it introduces the Laplacian-pyramid decoder to the MAE framework,
109   encouraging the network to learn multiscale representations. SatMAE (Cong et al., 2022) utilizes
110   temporal and spectral metadata in a positional encoding to encode spatiotemporal relationships in
111   data. RingMo (Sun et al., 2023) modified the masking strategy of MAE (He et al., 2022) to adapt to
112   dense and small objects in complex RS images and trained a self-supervised representation learning
113   model on a dataset of two million unlabeled RS images. SpectralGPT (Hong et al., 2024) is an
114   MAE-based RS foundation model that utilizes a 3D masking for processing spectral data, an encoder
115   to learn spectrally visual representations, and a decoder for multi-target reconstruction. SpectralGPT
116   is pretrained on 1 million multispectral images from the Sentinel-2 satellite (containing 12 spectral
117
      bands) from fMoW (Christie et al., 2018) and BigEarthNet (Sumbul et al., 2019).
118   A more recent direction of works aimed to combine reconstruction-based and contrastive learning-
119   based approaches: CMID (Muhtar et al., 2023) learns representations with both global semantic
120   separability and local spatial perceptibility by combining contrastive learning with masked image
121   modeling in a self-distillation way. GFM (Mendieta et al., 2023) also utilizes a masked image
122   modeling framework. They first gather a pretraining dataset of 1.3 million Sentinel-2 images using
123
      the sampling technique from SECO (Mañas et al., 2021). They utilize the dataset to pre-train a Swin-B
      Transformer model with the MIM objective from SimMIM (Xie et al., 2022). GFM (Mendieta et al.,
124
      2023) observed that some of the state-of-the-art methods for aerial imagery often do not perform
125
      better than ImageNet-22k pretrained ViTs. Cross-Scale-MAE (Tang et al., 2023) enhances MAE
126   framework by incorporating several additions, including scale augmentations and enforcing cross-
127   scale information consistency to improve its performance across different scales. CROMA (Fuller
128   et al., 2023) encodes masked-out multispectral optical and SAR samples, aligned in space and time
129   to perform cross-modal contrastive learning.
130
      Some other works aim to utilize various channels simultaneously, considering that many satellites
131
      can capture multiple bands simultaneously. For example, Sentinel-2 is capable of capturing images
132   with 13 different bands. ChannelViT (Bao et al., 2023) constructs patch tokens independently for
133   each input channel and then uses learnable channel embeddings added to the patch tokens, similar to
134   positional embeddings. ChannelViT generalizes well even when there is limited access to all channels
135   during training. SkySense (Guo et al., 2023) is pretrained on a curated multi-modal RS dataset of
136   21.5 million multimodal RS image triplets involving RGB high-resolution images and multitemporal,
137   multispectral and SAR sequences. It incorporates a factorized multi-modal spatiotemporal encoder
138   taking temporal sequences of optical and SAR data as input. CROMA (Fuller et al., 2023) and
139   DeCur (Wang et al., 2023c) explored multi-modal pre-training.
140   Another direction of works focused on multi-task pertaining: Satlas (Bastani et al., 2023) introduced a
141   large dataset for RS supervised pertaining as well as proposed a multi-task model, facilitating training
142   on the multitask annotated Satlas dataset. A more recent, Multi-Task Pretraining (MTP) (Wang et al.,
143   2024) proposes to use a shared encoder and a task specific decoder for pertaining stages to address the
144   issue of transferring the pretrained model into specific downstream tasks. MTP uses the the SAMRS
145   dataset (Wang et al., 2023b), for pre-trained. The SAMRS dataset leverages the segment anything
146
      model (SAM) and the well known RS datasets to develop an efficient pipeline for generating a large
      scale RS segmentation dataset.
147
148   Recently, for the change detection task, an end-to-end super-resolution-based network for high-
149   resolution image change detection SRCDNet (Liu et al., 2022), was developed to address the change
150   detection problem, for various resolution images. We extend this idea to more classification and
151   change detection datasets.

154   2    E XPECTATIONS FROM F OUNDATION M ODELS
155
156
157   Most of the evaluation strategies for remote sensing foundation models are based on fine-tuning on
158
      downstream classification, segmenetation or other datasets, and measuring the performance on the
      corresponding test sets. While this is an important aspect, it does not capture the potential benefits of
159
      large-scale pretraining.
160
161   We argue that the foundation models should bring features and capabilities to the fine-tuned models
      that would not be possible by leveraging solely the labeled data. These capabilities include general-


                                                         3
162
      ization across various kinds of shifts. In this paper we focus on two axis of generalization: image
163   resolution (i.e. ground sampling distance) and the set of bands of the capturing device.
164
165   A notable example from natural language processing is described in (Garcia et al., 2023). A large
166
      language model is trained on a mixture of non-parallel English and Chinese texts, and then it is
      prompted to translate with only five pairs of translated sentences. With such prompts the language
167
      model almost matches the performance of Google Translate production model. Hence, its translation
168
      capabilities generalize to vocabularies and topics way beyond the five examples can cover.
169
170   There are many axes of variation in aerial imagery: resolution, weather conditions, time of the year,
171   time of the day, geographical location etc. We expect strong foundation models for remote sensing to
172
      enable models derived from them to generalize across all possible variations. New benchmarks are
      required to measure this kind of generalization.
173
174   Note that in natural language processing, large language models pretrained on web-scale data,
175   allow few-shot learning, where the downstream task is described by a few samples written in the
176   prompt, or even zero-shot learning, where the task is described in human languages without explicit
177   examples. This aspect is not measured for pure image-based methods as they do not have an interface
178
      for describing the task at the input. Vision-language models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or
      Chameleon (Team, 2024) allow evaluations of similar capabilities, but their analysis is beyond the
179
      scope of this work.
180
181   This implies that pure image-based models require fine-tuning of some form to be adapted for a
182   downstream task. We are interested in measuring the generalization of the adapted models. Hence,
183   the way the models are adapted can be critical in retaining generalization capabilities. Developers
184
      of the foundation models should ideally provide recipes for adaptation on downstream tasks that
      preserve the generalization properties.
185
186
187
      Compute constraints. Foundation models should target specific compute requirements. Many
      downstream applications require the models to run on low power devices or need to support large
188
      volumes of data in deployment, and hence require limited number of FLOPs per image. It is important
189
      to note that this requirement refers to the fine-tuning stage and the deployment of the final model,
190   and not to the pretraining process. For example, DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) has a ViT-B version
191   which is distilled from a larger ViT-g model. While the large model was trained using hundreds of
192   GPUs, the distilled version can be easily fine-tuned on a single consumer-grade GPU.
193
      In summary, a typical foundation model for remote sensing can use unlimited compute and data for
194
      pretraining, but should be able to run on limited compute during inference, should support a simple
195
      recipe for fine-tuning for any downstream task, and, most importantly, should inherit remote-sensing
196   specific generalization capabilities. We attempt to formalize these expectations by designing a
197   benchmark in the next section.

200   3     B ENCHMARK D ESCRIPTION
201
202   In this section, we propose a benchmark suite for evaluating foundation models for remote sensing in
203
      the spirit of the expectations defined in Section 2.
204
205   3.1   C HOOSING A XES OF G ENERALIZATION
206
      In this subsection we analyze possible axes of generalization for foundation models.
207
208   Generalization to lower spatial resolutions. This is an important direction with many practical
209   applications. Low resolution satellites like Landsat and Sentinel constantly provide publicly available
210   imagery, while higher resolution imagery is usually harder to find. In many scenarios image labeling
211   is being done on higher quality imagery, but in test time the images might come from satellites with
212
      lower resolution. We expect the models to perform on such distribution shifts as good as on the
      images with the original resolution.
213
214   Generalization to higher spatial resolutions. This scenario might also happen in practical applica-
215   tions, but retaining the original performance on higher resolution is trivial by downsizing the images
      to the original resolution. One can expect that the additional details visible in higher resolution


                                                        4
216
      might allow to exceed the performance on the original images, but this is beyond the scope of a
217   generalization benchmark.
218
219   Generalization to other bands. This is very practical and is covered by our proposed benchmark.
220
      In remote sensing, different satellites capture imagery across various spectral bands. Often, models
      are trained on imagery from a limited number of bands (e.g., RGB or a specific multispectral range),
221
      but in real-world applications, they may encounter images with additional or fewer bands. The
222
      ability to generalize across different spectral compositions is crucial as additional bands may provide
223   complementary information as well as for some applications some bands may be missing.
224
225   Generalization to other seasons. In important applications, such as change detection or segmentation,
226
      a good foundation model should generalize well across seasons. For example, when identifying areas
      with new construction, whether the landscape is snowy, sunlit or foggy should not affect the model’s
227
      performance. This is important, but is challenging to collect data because the seasons are not coherent
228
      in different geographical regions. This is left for future work.
229
230   Generalization to other times of the day. Similar to season generalization, a good foundation model
231   should generalize well across different times of the day. Variations such as changes in shadows,
232
      lighting conditions, and overall brightness should not impact the model’s performance. However, this
      is not a practical problem, as even the same satellite will visit the same location at different times of
233
      the day, so every practical dataset will have internal variability across this dimension.
234
235   Generalization to other geographical locations. A strong foundation model should be able to
236   generalize accross different geographical regions, as terrain types, nature and human-made struc-
237   ture/architecture change significantly based on location. Evaluating this kind of generalization is
238
      notoriously hard because of significant label shift that exists between geographic locations which
      strongly affects the performance of the models. FMoW-WILDS (Koh et al., 2021) dataset contains
239
      geographical split, but due to severe shifts in label distributions it is hard to isolate and properly
240
      measure the generalization abilities with respect to pure domain shift.
241
242   Hence, in this work we focus on two shifts: generalization to unseen resolutions and bands. We will
243   measure generalization on two types of tasks: scene classification and change detection.
244
245   3.2     G ENERALIZATION TO S MALLER I MAGE R ESOLUTION
246
      3.2.1    S CENE C LASSIFICATION
247
248   We take two commonly used benchmark datasets in the literature: RESISC45 (Cheng et al., 2017)
249   and UC Merced (Yang & Newsam, 2010) see Appendix A.
250
      We measure the performance not only on the original resolutions of the images, but also on the
251
      images with 1/2, 1/4 and 1/8 resolutions. The images are resized by 1/x factor and the scaled back by
252   x which produces an image with the same number of pixels but with lower quality. This mimics how
253   the image could have been captured if the satellite had a lower resolution. As an evaluation metric,
254   we draw a curve where x-axis is the scaling parameter (1/8, 1/4, 1/2, 1) and y-axis is the accuracy
255   score for each version. We report the area under this curve as our final metric, and call it AUC-Acc.
256
      In this benchmark we restrict the models to use 50 GFLOPs on a single image. This threshold is
257
      independent from the neural architecture, and ViT-B/16 on an image of size 256x256px is within the
258   limits.
259
260   3.2.2    C HANGE D ETECTION
261
262   For change detection we use another two commonly used datasets: CDD (Lebedev et al., 2018) and
263   LEVIR-CD (Chen & Shi, 2020) see Appendix A.
264   We create partially scaled versions of the test sets of these datasets. We maintain the scale of the first
265   image unchanged, while for the second image, we distort it by reducing its quality by a factor of
266   2, 4, and 8. Note that a similar setup has been first proposed in (Liu et al., 2022). We evaluate on
267   the original resolution, as well as on the scaled versions. We compute micro-averaged F1 score for
268   each of the versions. Finally we draw a curve where x-axis is the scaling parameter and y-axis is the
269   micro-averaged F1 score for each version. We report the area under this curve as our final metric, and
      call it AUC-F1.


                                                          5
270
      For this benchmark, we restrict the models to use 100 GFLOPs on a pair of images.

      3.3     G ENERALIZATION TO U NSEEN BANDS
274
      Most of the publicly available multi-band remote sensing datasets originate from European Sentinel
275   satellites. Sentinel-1 uses C-band synthetic aperture radar (SAR) and captures VH and VV bands
276   that store complex numbers. Different papers use various preprocessing schemes for those values. In
277   order to be able to merge images from various sources, we use the absolute values of the complex
278   numbers, and do not perform any additional preprocessing. Hence, for SAR data we have two bands,
279   denoted by VV and VH. Sentinel-2 has 12 bands of varying resolution. Following (Bao et al., 2023)
280   and many other papers, we drop 60m resolution bands (e.g. “coastal”), and use the bands with 10m
281   and 20m resolution (resizing all of them to 10m). Details are in Table 4 in Appendix.
282
283   3.3.1    S CENE C LASSIFICATION
284
      To create a benchmark for evaluating the generalization on unseen bands for a classification task,
285
      using the BigEarthNet (Sumbul et al., 2019) dataset, we utilize BigEarthNet-medium, which contains
286   approximately 10% of the images from the original BigEarthNet dataset. This dataset is created
287   for multi-label classification. First, we remove the images tagged as clouds and snow using the
288   lists available at http://bigearth.net. We use the BigEarthNet-medium dataset as follows: for each
289   experiment, we train on the RGB channels and evaluate on four tri-channel triplets and one bi-channel
290   pair: RGB, RGE1, RE1E2, N’S1S2, and VV VH (bi-channel). We compute the micro average
291   precision (mAP) for each experiment and report the average over these five values. The goal is to
292   determine if a model trained on RGB channels can generalize to other channel combinations.
293
294   3.3.2    C HANGE D ETECTION
295
      To create a benchmark for evaluating the generalization on unseen bands for a change detection task,
296
      we use the Onera Satellite Change Detection (OSCD) dataset see Appendix A.

299   4     FACTORS C ONTRIBUTING TO THE P ERFORMANCE
300
301   4.1     I BOT PRETRAINING
302
303   To perform analysis of various factors on the generalization capabilities of the fine-tuned models, we
304   pre-trained several iBOT models using satellite imagery. As demonstrated in (Vanyan et al., 2023a),
305
      self-distillation-based models, such as iBOT, outperform MIM-based models in obtaining robust
      image representations, even in satellite imagery. Drawing on the findings of (Vanyan et al., 2023a),
306
      we selected iBOT for pre-training with the MillionAID dataset (Long et al., 2021). To accommodate
307
      the varying image sizes, we divided the original images into smaller square tiles, with each side
308   limited to a maximum of 550 pixels, resulting in a total of 2106700 images. Note that even the
309   original iBOT pretrained on ImageNet is quite strong, so we also included it in our comparative
310   analyses.
311
      We trained iBOT for 200 epochs with peak learning rate 5 × 10−4 that linearly decreases to 2 × 10−6
312
      over 5 warmup epochs. All RandomResizeCrops were converted to RandomCrops in the transforms.
313
      The training was conducted using PyTorch Distributed Data Parallel to utilize multiple GPUs and
314   used 100 batch size per GPU. The experiments were performed on NVIDIA DGX A100 at the local
315   university and an instance with 8 NVIDIA H100s kindly provided by Nebius.ai. The loss curve
316   followed the typical pattern of similar networks (Fig. 4 in Appendix). The resulting model is labeled
317   as iBOT-MillionAID.
318
319   4.2     AUGMENTATION
320
321   Here we analyze the impact of scale augmentation on the robustness to scale changes. The original
322   iBOT algorithm has an augmentation module that randomly resizes the pictures and then crops to a
323   fixed size. For this experiment we have pretrained two versions, one without resizing, i.e. the scale
      augmentation, and another one with resizing. The hypothesis is that the pretrained model will be


                                                        6
324
      more robust to scale changes, and this robustness will be transfered to the fine-tuned models, which
325   will cause higher AUC scores on our benchmarks.
326
327   Furthermore, we perform experiments with scale augmentation during fine-tuning. In this setting, we
328
      randomly shrink the image (or the second image in case of change detection) by 2, 4 and 8 times,
      and then resize it to get to the original resolution. This is the same transformation as we did when
329
      transforming the test sets for our benchmark. While this kind of augmentations during fine-tuning are
330
      beyond the scope of our benchmarks, the results of these experiments can act as an upper bound for
331   the scale robustness of the models.
332
333   Table 7 in Appendix shows the full results. When augmentations are not applied during fine-tuning,
334
      augmentations during pretraining at 1:1 and 1:2 resolutions consistently give better results across all
      datasets. However, this trend does not hold for smaller resolutions.
335
336   On the other hand, augmentations during fine-tuning have a significantly higher impact on the
337   generalization. In case of classification, we leverage 2×, 4×, and 8× versions of the original dataset.
338   Although we obtain 4× more data, this does not add new information, and we keep the total number
339   of optimization steps constant by decreasing the number of epochs by 4×. In case of change detection,
340
      we randomly choose one of the augmented versions of the second image at each epoch, and train for
      the same number of epochs as in the experiment without augmentations.
341
342   These experiments indicate that scale augmentation during pretraining still does not produce general-
343   ization capabilities at a level comparable to what one can obtain by augmenting during fine-tuning.

346   4.3   P RETRAINED M ASK D ECODER
347
348   Many downstream tasks in the remote sensing domain require an additional module on top of the
349   backbone to produce a binary mask. These include segmentation tasks that work on a single input
350   image and change detection tasks that require two input images. Here we develop an extension of
351   iBOT-MillionAID to have an additional mask decoder module that is already pretrained on large
352   amounts of data. As MillionAID does not contain any segmentation or change masks, we leverage
353   the teacher-student structure of iBOT and artificially generate masks the following way. The original
354
      iBOT implementation passes two global crops of the input image to the teacher and the student,
      and additional ten local crops to the student. We draw the mask of the second global crop in the
355
      coordinate space of the first global crop and store it as a target mask. The patch representations of
356
      the first global crop from the teacher and the second global crop by the student are concatenated and
357   passed to an UperNet decoder (Xiao et al., 2018) which produces a binary mask. This module adds
358   an additional pixel-wise cross-entropy loss term. Note that UperNet’s inputs come from four ViT
359   layers (3rd, 5th, 8th and 12th), not only the last one.
360
      We explored the joint training of UperNet and the regular iBOT. We investigated two methods to
361
      integrate mask loss into the iBOT training: either by obtaining the patch representations of both
362
      global crops using only the student model or by using the teacher model to obtain one of them.
363   The training loss of the first approach was unstable, with some of the layer activations increasing
364   significantly during the training. The teacher-student approach didn’t encounter these issues, resulting
365   in successful joint training. The final architecture is shown in Fig. 1.

      4.4   C ATASTROPHIC F ORGETTING D URING F INE -T UNING
409
      While the pretrained models can have inherent generalization capabilities, it is possible that the
410   models “forget” those during fine-tuning. One way to measure this phenomenon is to repeat the
411   fine-tuning experiments with frozen backbones. In this setting the only part of the model that has
412   never seen inputs of diverse scales is the final linear layer (in case of classification) or the decoder.
413
      Table 2 shows that the effect strongly depends on the downstream dataset. Particularly for RESISC45,
414
      the approach with frozen backbone is significantly more robust to lower resolutions than the one
415
      with full fine-tuning. On LEVIR-CD the same trend can be observed on 1:4 and 1:8 resolutions,
416   but the performance of the model with frozen backbone is slightly worse on 1:1 and 1:2 resolutions
417   compared to full fine-tuning. On UC Merced we see the oposite behaviour, when freezing the
418   backbone enhances performance on higher resolutions, but on lower resolutions full fine-tuning
419   outperforms the frozen model.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
508   To adapt the models for classification, we add a linear layer on top of the [CLS] token representation,
509   if available, or on top of the global average pooled vector of all patch representations.
510   To test the models for change detection, we take the backbone, which is either a Swin Transformer,
511   or a ViT, and integrate the UperNet head (Xiao et al., 2018). The two source images go through
512   identical backbones, and the resulting representations are substracted from each other and passed to
513   the head. In the case of ViTs, we use an additional neck module between the backbone and UperNet.
514   The backbone is initialized with the pre-trained weights and further fine-tuned using the change
515   detection datasets. In case of our iBOT trained on MillionAID, the neck and the head modules are
516
      also initialized, and we take the concatenation of features instead of the difference. In the experiments
      involving the ChannelViT backbone we sum up representations along the channel axis before passing
517
      it to UperNet decoder. For more details see Appendix B.

      5.2   R ESULTS AND C ONCLUSIONS
521
      The results are shown in Figures 2 and 3, and Tables 3 and 5 in Appendix. The general conclusion is
522   that all tested models struggle with generalizability both across scales and bands.
523
524   There are cases where the same model with a frozen backbone performs slightly better than its fine-
525
      tuned counterpart (e.g. SatlasPretrain and DINOv2). For generalization across bands, the fine-tuned
      models are always better for the RGB, but may fall behind frozen models on unseen bands. The
526
      performance gap between frozen models and full fine-tuning is relatively large for ChannelViT-S,
527
      Prithvi and especially Clay v1. This performance drop is also noticeable in classification tasks during
528   scale evaluation with the SatlasPretrain model, which could be due to its supervised pretraining.
529   However, we can observe that, when fine-tuning on larger datasets, the weakness of supervised
530   pretraining becomes less significant, as seen with SatlasPretrain on BigEarthNet.
531
      ChannelViT performs quite poor on BigEarthNet, which can be explained by the relatively small size
532
      of the model. We hypothesize that models pretrained in a self-supervised manner require less data
533
      for the downstream tasks compared to those pretrained in a supervised manner. Since Prithvi is an
534   extension of the MAE model, this may explain why its performance drops during linear probing. As
535   mentioned in some studies (He et al., 2022; Vanyan et al., 2023b), models trained with masked image
536   modeling exhibit their advantages when fully fine-tuning them; their representations are not designed
537   for linear probing. Finally, we note that the models that were pretrained on multiple bands are not
538   able to leverage the knowledge on the extra bands they learned during pretraining. When the unseen
539   bands are given in the place of RGB bands (¹), the results are not worse compared to the case when
      the unseen bands are given in their original input locations. This opens a wide avenue for future work.

      A     DATASETS
757
758
      RESISC45 (Cheng et al., 2017) and UC Merced (Yang & Newsam, 2010) datasets contain 256x256px
759   images. Image resolution is 30cm/px for UC Merced and varies 20-600cm/px for RESISC45. Both
760   datasets use RGB bands only. We take the splits defined in (Neumann et al., 2019).
761   The LEVIR-CD dataset (Chen & Shi, 2020) comprises a substantial collection of bitemporal Google
762   Earth images. It includes 637 image pairs, each sized 1024 × 1024px, with 400 images designated
763   for training. The images in the training set have a resolution of 50cm/px. Originating from 20 distinct
764   regions within cities in Texas, USA, these images showcase the construction-induced changes. The
765   fully annotated LEVIR-CD dataset encompasses a total of 31, 333 individual changed buildings.
766   The changes in the LEVIR-CD dataset primarily come from the construction of new buildings. The
767
      average size of each changed area is approximately 987 pixels.
      The CDD (Lebedev et al., 2018) dataset contains season-varying remote sensing images of the same
768
      region, obtained from Google Earth (DigitalGlobe). The dataset comprises 16, 000 image sets (two
769
      images of the same location and the annotated change), each with an image size of 256 × 256 pixels
770   and 0.03-1m/px ground sample distance.
771   Onera Satellite Change Detection (OSCD) dataset contains pairs of aerial images of the same location
772   captured at different times, with changes manually annotated at the pixel level (Caye Daudt et al.,
773   2018). The dataset contains images from a total of 24 cities, divided into smaller chunks (192 ×
774   192) of images. Similar to the classification benchmark, we train on the RGB channels and evaluate
775   on four tri-channel triplets and one bi-channel pair: RGB, RGE1, RE1E2, N’S1S2, and VV VH
776   (bi-channel). We note that for the evaluation, we always keep the first picture as RGB and the second
777   figure with the corresponding band channels. We compute the micro F1 score for each experiment
778
      and report the average over these five values.

786   B.1   C LASSIFICATION
787
788   We perform two kinds of fine-tuning: full fine-tuning and linear probing. For both setups, we train
789   for 100 epochs. For all experiments in the full fine-tuning setup or linear probing, we evaluate
790   using the last checkpoint. However, for full fine-tuning on the BigEarthNet dataset, we select
791   the best checkpoint based on performance on the validation set. In all experiments within the
792
      full fine-tuning setup, we use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 10−4 employing
      W armupCosineAnnealing scheduling and an estimated minimum value of 10−5 . In experiments
793
      within the linear probing setup, we use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 10−3 employing
794
      M ultiStep scheduling and an estimated minimum value of 10−5 .
795
796   In the linear probing setup for the Prithvi model, we conducted a grid search to optimize
797   the hyperparameters. The optimization process involved testing three different optimizers:
798
      {Adam, AdamW, SGD}. For the learning rate, we evaluated three values: {10−3 , 10−4 , 10−6 }
      setting one of the following schedulers: {M ultiStep, W armupCosineAnnealing}. We selected
799
      the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 10−3 and the W armupCosineAnnealing scheduler
800
      for our final configuration based on the performance on the validation set. For linear probing with the
801   ChannelVit model, we use the initial hyperparameters for linear probing provided by the authors and
802   perform the same grid search. Ultimately, we choose the Adam optimizer with an initial learning
803   rate of 10−3 and M ultiStep scheduling.

      the CDD dataset contains samples from different GSD (0.03m-1m). For classification, we compare

841
      We observe that for iBOT (both trained on ImageNET and MillionAID) linear probing has a clear
842
      advantage over full-finetuning for lower resolutions.
