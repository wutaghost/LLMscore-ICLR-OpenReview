009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011            CLIP is one of the most important multimodal foundational models today, aligning visual and
012            textual signals into a shared feature space using a simple contrastive learning loss on large-scale
013            image-text pairs. What powers CLIP’s capabilities? The rich supervision signals provided by
014            natural language — the carrier of human knowledge — shape a powerful cross-modal represen-
015            tation space. As a result, CLIP supports a variety of tasks, including zero-shot classification,
016            detection, segmentation, and cross-modal retrieval, significantly influencing the entire multi-
017
               modal domain. However, with the rapid advancements in large language models (LLMs) like
               GPT-4 and LLaMA, the boundaries of language comprehension and generation are continually
018
               being pushed. This raises an intriguing question: can the capabilities of LLMs be harnessed
019
               to further improve multimodal representation learning? The potential benefits of incorporating
020            LLMs into CLIP are clear. LLMs’ strong textual understanding can fundamentally improve
021            CLIP’s ability to handle image captions, drastically enhancing its ability to process long and
022            complex texts — a well-known limitation of vanilla CLIP. Moreover, LLMs are trained on a
023            vast corpus of text, possessing open-world knowledge. This allows them to expand on cap-
024            tion information during training, increasing the efficiency of the learning process. However,
025            realizing this potential is challenging. Despite LLMs’ powerful internal comprehension, their
026            autoregressive nature hides this capability within the model, leading to output features with
027            poor discriminability. Our experiments show that directly integrating LLMs into CLIP results
028
               in catastrophic performance drops. In this paper, we propose LLM2CLIP, a novel approach that
               embraces the power of LLMs to unlock CLIP’s potential. By fine-tuning the LLM in the caption
029
               space with contrastive learning, we extract its textual capabilities into the output embeddings,
030
               significantly improving the output layer’s textual discriminability. We then design an efficient
031            training process where the fine-tuned LLM acts as a powerful teacher for CLIP’s visual encoder.
032            Thanks to the LLM’s presence, we can now incorporate longer and more complex captions
033            without being restricted by vanilla CLIP text encoder’s context window and ability limitations.
034            Our experiments demonstrate that this approach brings substantial improvements in cross-modal
035            tasks. Our method directly boosted the performance of the previously SOTA EVA02 model by
036            16.5% on both long-text and short-text retrieval tasks, transforming a CLIP model trained solely
037            on English data into a state-of-the-art cross-lingual model. Moreover, when integrated into mul-
038            timodal training with models like Llava 1.5, it consistently outperformed CLIP across nearly all
039
               benchmarks, demonstrating comprehensive performance improvements.

091   In this work, we aim to leverage large language models (LLMs) to enable CLIP to learn more powerful, fine-
092   grained, and rich visual representations. Currently, CLIP is often criticized for its bag-of-words-like perception
093   and the limitations of its text encoder, which suffers from a constrained model size, limited context length, and is
094   trained predominantly on image captioning data, lacking exposure to diverse world corpora. A natural approach
095   would be to replace CLIP’s text encoder with an LLM that embeds rich human knowledge. However, this presents
096
      significant challenges. In the cross-modal contrastive learning framework employed by CLIP, the text encoder
      functions as a set of knowledge anchors in the shared latent space, guiding the alignment of the vision encoder with
097
      human knowledge of the physical world. The structure, richness, and discriminability of these knowledge anchors
098
      are critical to the visual model’s effectiveness. In contrast, LLMs are primarily designed to predict the next word
099   rather than generate explicit representations of the knowledge they contain. Their textual comprehension abilities
100   and open-world knowledge are latent within the model, rather than present in the output embeddings, making them
101   difficult to utilize in the same explicit manner as CLIP’s text encoder. As a result, using LLMs as a text encoder
102   may not produce linearly separable features, which are crucial for effective feature alignment.
103

141
      integration of large language models (LLMs) as shown in Figure 1. This method takes a straightforward yet
142
      audacious step by replacing the original CLIP text encoder and augmenting the CLIP visual encoder with the
143   vast knowledge embedded in LLMs. We have identified key obstacles associated with this innovative idea and
144   proposed a cost-effective fine-tuning strategy to overcome them. Our experiments demonstrate that leveraging

151

152
      of our approach. These promising outcomes affirm that we have successfully transformed CLIP into a more

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
160
161   Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP). CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is one of the most important
162   multimodal models today. By training on web-scale data, image captions serve as rich, fine-grained supervision
163   signals for learning representations that align closely with image, freeing CLIP from the limitations of manually
164   defined labels. The richness of the captions endows CLIP with many remarkable capabilities; besides zero-shot
165   image-text retrieval, CLIP features can even achieve region-level correspondence, supporting zero-shot detection
166
      and segmentation tasks. Furthermore, CLIP is the most widely used encoder in current image and video under-
      standing tasks. In Tong et al. (2024)’s analysis, it stands out among various visual encoders in MLLMs training
167
      as the most significant model, demonstrating its strong capabilities. For generative tasks, CLIP is equally founda-
168
      tional. Its text and image encoders are used to provide text control (Rombach et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022)
169   and image control signals (Blattmann et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) for conditioning generative models. Addi-
170   tionally, the CLIP score (Hessel et al., 2021) has become a crucial metric for assessing the relevance of generated
171   images to text. While the original CLIP only utilized image and text modalities, there have been massive exten-
172   sions of CLIP to incorporate various other modalities (Dai et al., 2024; He et al., 2023; Moon et al., 2022; Zhang
173   et al., 2022). CLIP definitely has a significant impact on the field of vision and multimodal, and its influence is
      still growing.

      CLIP meets Stronger Language Models. Several works have explored the integration of LLMs into CLIP.
175   JinaCLIP (Koukounas et al., 2024) employed Jina-embeddings-v2 (Günther et al., 2023) as the text encoder, which
176   is a BERT variant with 137M parameters, supporting longer texts. Though achieving similar visual performance
177   to EVA-CLIP (Sun et al., 2023), its text encoder is far behind our used Llama3-8B, limiting the potential benefits
178   from LLMs. T5-V (Jiang et al., 2024) took a different approach by aggregating LLM features at the MLLM layer
179   of LLAVA-next-8B (Li et al., 2024a) while freezing the ViT gradient to focus on adjusting the MLLM output.
180   However, freezing visual encoder does not address the problem that ViT inherently lack complex visual feature
181   extraction capabilities, leading to much worse performance than LLM2CLIP. MATE (Jang et al., 2024) designed a
182   learnable adaptor to bridge the gap between CLIP’s text encoder and LLMs. They trained the CLIP visual encoder
183
      using LoRA on a small dataset focused on long-text image retrieval tasks. However, they did not recognize the
      critical issue we propose in this paper: the poor separability of LLM feature space, which is insufficient for direct
184
      support of CLIP training. Our work aims to thoroughly explore the impact of LLMs on CLIP as a foundational
185
      model, going beyond the influence on long-text retrieval.
186
187
      CLIP meets Longer Captions. It has been widely recognized that the quality of CLIP’s text embedding is
188   coarse and limited to only 77 tokens. Many works have attempted to extend the length of CLIP captions and
189   retrain CLIP accordingly. For instance, DCI (Urbanek et al., 2024) hired annotators to expand captions based on
190   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023)’s target hints. LaCLIP (Fan et al., 2024) adopted ChatGPT, Bard, and human rewriting.
191   DreamLIP (Zheng et al., 2024) leveraged ShareCaptioner (Chen et al., 2023) and InstructBLIP to augment 30M
192   captions. Recap-DataComp-1B (Li et al., 2024b) used Llama3-trained LLAVA1.5 to extend 1B captions. In
193   order to handle captions longer than 77 tokens, these methods compromised by summarizing long captions into
194   shorter ones (Urbanek et al., 2024), splitting captions into multiple segments (Fan et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024),
195   or finetuning model’s positional encoding to support longer token inputs (Zhang et al., 2024). LLM2CLIP, in
196
      comparison, leverages Llama3 as the text encoder, which enables comprehensive understanding of long and dense
      captions. It not only resolves the limition of token length but also allows for better understanding using LLMs’
197
      open-world knowledge. As a result, our model achieves a breakthrough in performance.

      3.1   NATIVE LLM S ARE I NEFFECTIVE T EXT E NCODERS FOR CLIP
209
210   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as Llama-3, are trained on massive world corpora through auto-regression,
211   equipping them with open-world knowledge and enabling them to perform various tasks. We initially hoped to
212   leverage the capabilities of LLMs to directly retrain a CLIP model.
213
      Although LLMs exhibit strong text comprehension, they are difficult to use directly as text embedding models.
214
      This is because their knowledge is encapsulated within the model, and their output features are heavily skewed
215   towards individual word predictions. As generative models, they are not trained to ensure good linear separabil-
216   ity of output features, making them less effective when used to interpret captions for CLIP. As highlighted by
217   Chen et al. (2022), cross-modal contrastive learning in CLIP requires that each modality possesses strong internal
218   discriminability.
219
      To evaluate the effectiveness of various language models in terms of text discriminability, and to test whether
220
      the native output features of LLMs, as hypothesized, struggle to distinguish image captions, we introduce a new
221   metric: the MS COCO Caption Retrieve Accracy (CRA). MS COCO is a widely used multimodal dataset, con-
222   taining over 330K images, each with five captions. These captions are written by different human annotators and
223   provide diverse descriptions for each image. In our evaluation on the MS COCO validation set, we use only the
224   first two captions of each image and treat the captions of the same image as positive pairs, while all other captions
225   serve as negative samples. We then perform caption-to-caption retrieval and assess Top-1 accuracy using different
226   language models, defining the result as their CRA score. Higher CRA scores indicate better discriminability of
227   the language models on image captions.
228   As shown in Table 1, using a pure LLM results in a CRA score of only 18.4%, indicating that the majority of
229   captions cannot be well-separated in the output space. In fact, captions with similar distances may be entirely
230   unrelated, as illustrated in Figure 2. However, the text encoder from the original state-of-the-art CLIP model
231   achieves a CRA score of 66%, proving the inadequacy of native LLM output features in caption discriminability.
      Consequently, it is challenging to apply LLMs directly in CLIP model training. Subsequent experiments in Ta-

      3.2   A C RUCIAL L ESSON : LLM L EARNING FOR I MAGE C APTION D ISCRIMINATION
236
237
      In this section, we aim to fine-tune the LLM’s token outputs to better capture features that can distinguish between
238   image captions.
239
240   The process of improving the discriminability of LLM output features on caption text is quite straightforward: we
241
      need the distances between captions of the same image to be closer, and those of different images to be further
      apart. Therefore, we apply a caption contrastive (CC) fine-tuning to the LLM’s output features, treating different
242
      captions of the same image as positive samples and the rest of the captions as negative samples.
243
244   To obtain enough varied descriptions for the same image, we use the ShareCaptioner (Zheng et al., 2024; Chen
245   et al., 2023) modified CC-3M (Sharma et al., 2018) dataset, which provides both original captions and augmented
246   dense captions for each image. These can be treated as positive pairs. We followed the training methodology of
247
      LLM2Vec (BehnamGhader et al., 2024), first expanding the LLM’s attention mechanism to bidirectional attention,
      and employing Masked Next Token Prediction (MNTP) for initialization to achieve better results. Specifically:
248
249   First, we transform the LLM’s causal attention mechanism into bidirectional attention, as we no longer need it to
250   retain generative capabilities but rather function as an encoder. Since autoregressive training is no longer required,
251   switching to bidirectional attention improves its ability to capture contextual information. Second, we employ
252   MNTP to train the newly added bidirectional attention mechanism, providing a strong initialization. For a given
253
      sequence of N tokens, we mask a subset and predict their values, similar to BERT (Devlin et al., 2018). However,
      unlike BERT, we adapt this process to fit the nature of LLMs by predicting tokens just before the masked token.
254
      We train on both image captions and pure text with equal weighting. In addition to CC-3M, we also use the
255
      Wikitext-103 (Merity et al., 2016) dataset to preserve the LLM’s text capabilities and prevent divergence from its
256   original strengths. Finally, we perform the actual caption contrastive fine-tuning, using a supervised SimCSE loss
257   to bring captions of the same image closer together and push apart captions of different images. We use two prompt
258   templates: “Given a caption, retrieve a detailed relevant caption” and “Given a detailed caption, retrieve a short
259   relevant caption,” which are prepended to the query (either original or dense caption) to retrieve the corresponding
260   dense or original caption. Similarly, we use a 1.5M-paired general text dataset curated from Springer et al. (2024)
261   to maintain strong performance in pure language tasks. All training is efficiently conducted using LoRA and is
262   completed in just one epoch, ensuring low computational cost.
263   A remarkable result followed: after fine-tuning the LLM as a caption encoder for only one epoch on CC-3M, the
264   CRA score for Llama-3 8B jumped from 18.4% to 73.0%, surpassing the previous state-of-the-art CLIP and EVA
265   models’ text encoders trained on the massive Laion-2B (Schuhmann et al., 2022) and COYO-700M (Byeon et al.,
266   2022) image-text datasets. As shown in Table 6, subsequent experiments demonstrated that after CC fine-tuning,
267   the LLM finally unleashed its powerful capabilities, significantly boosting the performance of the previously
268   state-of-the-art CLIP model, in stark contrast to the results without CC fine-tuning. This breakthrough uncovers
269   the potential of LLMs in CLIP training and removes a major obstacle in leveraging LLMs to advance vision
270
      foundation models.

272   3.3   LLM2CLIP: M AKES SOTA CLIP MORE SOTA EVER
273
274   With the modifications to the LLM discussed earlier, we have now obtained a super text encoder that is well-suited
275
      for CLIP training. The next step is to use this LLM in conjunction with the pretrained state-of-the-art CLIP visual
      encoder to reconstruct a more powerful cross-modal feature space.
276
277   As shown in Figure 1, in the LLM2CLIP training phase, we freeze the gradients of the LLM to preserve its inherent
278   capabilities, for two primary reasons. First, this significantly reduces the computational cost and memory footprint
279   of fine-tuning. CLIP training requires a very large batch size to maintain the effectiveness of negative samples.
280   Allocating memory to the LLM could compromise CLIP’s performance. Second, by freezing the LLM, we ensure
281
      that the open-world knowledge it has acquired from large-scale corpora remains intact during the multimodal
      alignment process.
282
283   To compensate for the frozen LLM, and inspired by methods like FuseMix (Vouitsis et al., 2023) and APE (Rosen-
284   feld et al., 2022), we introduce several new linear layers after the LLM as adapters. These layers serve as learnable
285   parameters to improve the alignment between the LLM and CLIP visual encoder. Following the original design of
286   CLIP, we also employ a projector layer to align the dimensions of the two encoders, facilitating the use of CLIP
287
      loss for training.
288   With this powerful LLM-based super text encoder, we achieve a substantial qualitative leap in CLIP’s language
289   comprehension capabilities. The LLM’s open-world knowledge allows the CLIP visual encoder to learn more
      structured and globally informed visual representations that are aligned with human knowledge. Moreover, this

      3.4      OVERVIEW AND E FFICIENCY D ISCUSSION
296
297   We propose LLM2CLIP as a method that efficiently incorporates large language models (LLMs) into CLIP train-
298   ing, leveraging the capabilities of LLMs to make cross-modal representation learning significantly more powerful.
299   In our experiments, We evaluated large language models, including Llama with 1B and 8B parameters, as well as
300   Mistral-Nemo with 12B parameters.It might seem that incorporating such large LLMs would greatly increase the
301   computational burden of training CLIP, especially since CLIP itself is computationally expensive, requiring a large
302   batch size. However, our proposed LLM2CLIP is remarkably lightweight. The training overhead is nearly iden-
303
      tical to fine-tuning the original CLIP model, with minimal additional cost, yet the LLM provides much stronger
      supervision.
304
305   Here, we highlight some of the design details that significantly improve training efficiency: 1). During the caption
306   contrastive fine-tuning stage, we employ LoRA training for the LLM. Even for a 12B LLM, training with a batch
307   size of 512 only requires around 70GB of GPU memory, making it feasible to run on a single 80GB 8 A100 GPU
308   node. 2). In the LLM2CLIP stage, we freeze the LLM’s gradients and only train the learnable adapter, CLIP’s
309
      original Vision encoder, and two projectors. The additional trainable parameters are roughly equivalent to those in
      the original CLIP, minimizing the overhead. To further reduce the inference cost of using LLMs, we pre-extract all
310
      the text features from the training data and store them in memory. This ensures that, even though LLMs provide
311
      powerful textual supervision, the memory and computational costs during training remain nearly identical to
312   those of standard CLIP training.
313
314   For instance, when we trained LLM2CLIP using a Mistral-Nemo1 12B model integrated with the commonly used
315
      EVA ViT-L/14-224, with a batch size of 4096 on 8 H100 GPUs, the memory usage per GPU was only 30GB, and
      the entire training process took just 9 hours. Despite this efficient training cost, LLM2CLIP brought transformative
316
      improvements in downstream tasks such as long and short text retrieval, cross-language retrieval, and LLAVA
317
      training.

      Training Dataset. We utilized the ShareCaptioner-modified CC-3M dataset (Zheng et al., 2024; Chen et al.,

      2023), which provides both original captions and augmented dense captions for each image, for contrastive learn-

332   all datasets. We adopted the average of all LLM output tokens as the text global embedding for a caption. All

337   Training Dataset. To compare different configurations, we designed three experimental settings based on

340
      our default setting. All LLM2CLIP models without a specific dataset size label are trained with this 15M data.

344   LLAVA1.5. We primarily used a mix of original captions and dense captions, with the default mixing ratio being

346   pretraining distribution to analyze the model’s benefits separately.

      Training Setting. All experiments with different datasets were conducted for 4 epochs. We froze the LLM
349   gradients by default. During training, we pre-extracted the text features from the captions using the LLM and

      4.3   A BLATION
523
524   Impact of Different Data Ratios. With the integration of an LLM, LLM2CLIP enhances our ability to under-
525   stand dense captions. In Table 5, we present an ablation study that examines the impact of different ratios of
526   dense captions versus original captions on CLIP’s training performance. The “Ratio” column represents the pro-
527   portion of dense captions used during training. Overall, dense captions contribute to a noticeable improvement
528   in performance, but more is not always better. For example, when using 0% dense captions, the performance on
529   datasets like COCO and Flickr30K remains decent, but long-text benchmarks such as ShareGPT4V, Urban-1K,
530
      and DOCCI show poor results. Conversely, with 100% dense captions, performance on short-text retrieval bench-
      marks is the worst. It’s important to note that the dense captions we used were generated by the ShareCaptioner
531
      model, and there may be some distribution differences and noise compared to real data captions, which could
532
      somehow explain why using 100% dense data is suboptimal. Our findings indicate that the best performance is
533   achieved when dense captions make up 50% to 75% of the training data, striking a balance between both short-text
534   and long-text retrieval tasks.
535
536   Impact of Different Data Size. As shown in Table 2, larger training datasets consistently yield positive results
537   for LLM2CLIP, with clear improvements in both long-text and short-text retrieval tasks. Our 60M dataset version
538   has already pushed the limits of what CLIP could previously achieve. Even the 3M version, though lightweight,
539   still delivers significant performance gains, demonstrating the efficiency of the LLM2CLIP approach. It’s worth
540   noting that training on the 3M dataset takes only about 3 hours on an 8 H100 GPUs machine, yet results in a
541   transformative leap in performance for a well-pretrained CLIP ViT model.

      5     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
544
545
      This paper focuses solely on the impact of LLMs on CLIP. In reality, there are many other areas where text can
546   play a transformative role, such as diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021; Blattmann et al., 2023; Tech, 2023),
547   Segment Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023) and GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023b), both of which could potentially
548   see significant performance improvements by applying our method.
549
      Additionally, while LLMs possess open-world knowledge, aligning them with CLIP may require specific data to
550
      fully unlock their potential. LLM2CLIP is a method that requires only a small amount of data for fine-tuning in
551
      conjunction with a pretrained CLIP visual encoder, and we have already demonstrated significant performance
552   gains. However, we did not intentionally select fine-tuning data based on specific characteristics. By focusing
553   on aspects such as data distribution, length, or categories, we could further tailor the LLM to address CLIP’s
554   limitations, allowing the LLM to act as a more comprehensive teacher for various tasks.
555
      Moreover, in this work, we froze the gradients of the LLM during fine-tuning to maintain a large batch size for
556
      CLIP training. However, it would be worth exploring approaches that allow for updating the LLM’s gradients,
557
      as there may be better engineering practices that can balance the two objectives. Re-training LLM2CLIP from
558   scratch on datasets like Laion2B (Schuhmann et al., 2022) and Recaption-1B (Li et al., 2024b) is also a promising
559   direction that we did not pursue due to time constraints.

562   6     C ONCLUSION
563
564   This paper presents a method that enables LLMs to assist in CLIP training. Given the powerful text understanding
565
      capabilities of LLMs, we aim to harness their potential to enhance cross-modal representation learning, allowing
      language — the carrier of human knowledge — to play a pivotal role in spreading the intelligence of LLMs across
566
      multiple modalities and applications. The core contributions of this work are threefold:
567
568   First, we identified the primary reason why LLMs struggle to directly participate in multimodal representation
569   learning — the lack of discriminability in their output features. Second, we introduced the caption contrastive fine-
570   tuning method, which significantly improves feature discriminability, removing the biggest obstacle to utilizing
571
      LLMs in CLIP training. Third, we designed the LLM2CLIP training framework, which has proven to be an
      efficient and effective approach for significantly enhancing the performance of pretrained CLIP models.
572
573   We experimented with LLM2CLIP on both CLIP and EVA models and validated it across different Llama3 models,
574   including 1B, 8B, and 13B, all of which yielded transformative improvements in CLIP performance. LLM2CLIP
575   offers several advantages, such as seamless compatibility with long and complex text inputs, and the ability to
576   incorporate the open-world knowledge of LLMs into CLIP training — as demonstrated by its ability to match
577
      Chinese text even when trained solely on English data.
578   We firmly believe that the potential of LLMs extends far beyond what has been shown, and we hope this work
579   serves as a starting point for using LLMs to enhance various vision foundation models, ultimately spreading their
      general capabilities and open-world knowledge across a wide range of modalities and applications.
