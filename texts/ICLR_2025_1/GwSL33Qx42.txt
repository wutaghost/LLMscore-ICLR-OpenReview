035                                                                                           A BSTRACT
036
037
                        Recent advancements in text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have enabled the
038                     creation of high-quality images from text prompts, but they still struggle to gener-
039                     ate images with precise control over specific visual concepts. Existing approaches
040                     can replicate a given concept by learning from reference images, yet they lack
041                     the flexibility for fine-grained customization of the individual component within
042                     the concept. In this paper, we introduce component-controllable personaliza-
043                     tion, a novel task that pushes the boundaries of T2I models by allowing users to
044                     reconfigure and personalize specific components of concepts. This task is par-
045                     ticularly challenging due to two primary obstacles: semantic pollution, where
046
                        unwanted visual elements corrupt the personalized concept, and semantic imbal-
                        ance, which causes disproportionate learning of visual semantics. To overcome
047
                        these challenges, we design MagicTailor, an innovative framework that leverages
048
                        Dynamic Masked Degradation (DM-Deg) to dynamically perturb undesired visual
049                     semantics and Dual-Stream Balancing (DS-Bal) to establish a balanced learning
050                     paradigm for visual semantics. Extensive comparisons, ablations, and analyses
051                     demonstrate that MagicTailor not only excels in this challenging task but also
052                     holds significant promise for practical applications, paving the way for more nu-
053                     anced and creative image generation. Our code will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al.,
058   2022; Chen et al., 2023) demonstrate remarkable capabilities in generating high-quality visual con-
059   tent from textual descriptions. These models are able to create images that closely match the pro-
060   vided prompts. But when certain visual concepts are difficult to articulate through natural language,
061   they may face difficulties in accurately incorporating such elements into the generated images. To
062   address this limitation, some approaches (Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023) enable T2I models
063   to learn specific concepts from a few reference images, thus allowing for more faithful integration
064
      of these concepts into the generated images. This process, illustrated in Figure 1(a), is referred as
      personalization. However, existing personalization methods are limited to replicating predefined
065
      concepts and lack the capability to offer flexible, fine-grained control over these elements. This
066
      constraint significantly limits their practical applicability in real-world scenarios. A concept often
067   comprises multiple components, such as a house consisting of walls, windows, and doors. There-
068   fore, a more sophisticated challenge in personalization is determining how to effectively control and
069   manipulate these individual components during the personalization process.
070
      This paper introduces a new task, component-controllable personalization, which aims to recon-
071
      figure the elements of a personalized concept using additional visual references (see Figure 1(b)).
072
      In this task, a T2I model is fine-tuned with reference images and corresponding category labels,
073   allowing it to learn and generate the desired concept along with its specified component. Achieving
074   this capability would not only enable users to refine and customize concepts with precise control but
075   also foster innovation and creativity, paving the way for novel ideas, inventions, and artworks across
076   various creative domains.
077
      A straightforward approach to this task is to treat each component as a separate concept and use
078
      existing personalization methods to combine multiple concepts with suitable text prompts. How-
079   ever, this naive strategy falls short in component-controllable personalization due to the inherent
080   complexity of handling visual semantics during learning. One of the key challenges in this task
081   is semantic pollution (see Figure 2(a)), where undesired visual semantics inadvertently appear in
082   generated images, thereby polluting the personalized concept. This occurs because the T2I model
083   tends to blend visual semantics from different regions during the learning process. Simply masking
084   out unwanted visual elements in reference images is not a viable solution, as it disrupts the over-
085   all visual context and leads to unintended compositions. Another significant challenge is semantic
086   imbalance (see Figure 2(b)), which causes the T2I model to focus disproportionately on certain as-
087
      pects, resulting in unfaithful personalization. This issue arises from the semantic disparity between
      the concept and its components, highlighting the need for an effective learning paradigm to better
088
      manage concept-level (e.g., person) and component-level (e.g., hair) visual semantics.
089
090   To address these challenges, we present MagicTailor, a novel framework that enables component-
091   controllable personalization for T2I models (see Figure 1(c)). As shown in Figure 3, we first employ
092   a text-guided image segmenter to generate segmentation masks for both the concept and its com-
093
      ponents. Then, we introduce a technique called Dynamic Masked Degradation (DM-Deg), which
      transforms the original reference images into randomly degraded versions, dynamically perturbing
094
      undesired visual semantics. This approach helps suppress the model’s sensitivity to irrelevant visual
095
      details while preserving the overall visual context, thereby effectively mitigating semantic pollu-
096   tion. Next, we initiate a warm-up phase for the T2I model by jointly training it on these degraded
097   images, using a masked diffusion loss to focus on the desired visual semantics and an attention
098   loss to strengthen the correlation between these semantics and pseudo-words. To tackle the is-
099   sue of semantic imbalance, we employ Dual-Stream Balancing (DS-Bal), a dual-stream learning
100   paradigm designed for balancing the learning of visual semantics, to launch the second phase. In
101   this paradigm, the online denoising U-Net performs sample-wise min-max optimization, while the
102   momentum denoising U-Net applies selective preserving regularization. This balanced approach
103   ensures more faithful and accurate personalization of the target concept and component.
104   We validate the effectiveness of MagicTailor through comprehensive qualitative and quantitative
105   experiments, demonstrating that it can achieve state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in component-
106   controllable personalization. Detailed ablation studies further confirm the impact of the key tech-
107   niques integrated into MagicTailor. Additionally, we showcase its potential to enable a variety of
      further applications. In summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows:

135   2             R ELATED W ORKS
136
137
      Text-to-Image Generation. Text-to-image (T2I) generation has made remarkable advancements in
138
      recent years, enabling the synthesis of vivid and diverse imagery based on textual descriptions. Early
139   methods employed Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Reed et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2018;
140   Qiao et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019), and auto-regressive transformers (Ding et al., 2021; Ramesh
141   et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022) began to show the potential in conditioned image gen-
142   eration. More recently, the advent of diffusion models has ushered in a new era in T2I generation (Li
143   et al., 2024; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024). Leveraging
144   these models, a range of related applications has rapidly emerged, including image editing (Li et al.,
145   2024; Mou et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), image completion and translation (Xie et al., 2023b;a;
146   Lin et al., 2024), and controllable generation (Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Zheng et al.,
147
      2023). Despite advancements in T2I diffusion models, generating images that accurately capture
      specific, user-defined concepts remains challenging. This study explores component-controllable
148
      personalization, enabling precise adjustment of concept’s components through visual references.
149
150   Personalization. Personalization seeks to adapt T2I models to generate specific concepts using ref-
151   erence images. Initial approaches such as textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022) and DreamBooth (Ruiz
152   et al., 2023) addressed this task by either optimizing a text embedding or fine-tuning the entire T2I
153
      model. Additionally, low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) has been widely adopted by the
      research community for personalization (Ryu, 2022), offering an efficient and lightweight solution.
154
      The scope of personalization has further expanded to accommodate multiple concepts (Kumari et al.,
155
      2023; Avrahami et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024; Han et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024). Be-
156   sides, a growing body of works has explored tuning-free approaches to personalization (Xiao et al.,
157   2023; Li et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). However, these meth-
158   ods often rely on training an encoder with extensive domain-specific image datasets. There is also
159   a category of works studying training-free schemes (Jeong et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), but
160   they generally suffer from inferior performance and tortuous inference processes. In light of that,
161   Our MagicTailor goes with a widely-adopted paradigm of test-time optimization to achieve stable
      performance and precise control over the concept during personalization.

      3.2   DYNAMIC M ASKED D EGRADATION
235
236   In this task, one of the major challenges is semantic pollution, where undesired visual semantics
237   could be perceived by the T2I model and thus “pollute” the personalized concept. As shown in
238   Figure 2(a.i), the target concept (i.e., person) could be severely disturbed by the owner of the target
239   component (i.e., eye), resulting in a hybrid person. Unfortunately, directly masking out the regions
240   other than the target concept and component would damage the overall visual context, thus leading to
241   overfitting and weird compositions in Figure 2(a.ii). In light of that, the undesired visual semantics of
242   reference images should be processed properly. Hence, we propose Dynamic Masked Degradation
243
      (DM-Deg) to dynamically perturb undesired visual semantics (see Figure 3), aiming at suppressing
      the T2I model’s perception for them while maintaining overall visual contexts (see Figure 2(a.iii)).
244
245   Degradation Imposition. In each training step, DM-Deg imposes degradation in the regions outside
246   segmentation masks for each reference image. There are various types of degradation that can be
247   adopted to perturb the visual semantics of an image, such as noise, blur, and geometric distortions,
248   but not all of them are easy to use and compatible with mask operations. In DM-Deg, we choose
249
      to employ Gaussian noise due to its simplicity. For a reference image Ink , we randomly sample a
      Gaussian noise matrix Gnk ∼ N (0, 1) with the same shape as Ink . Then, with the corresponding
250
      segmentation mask Mnk , the imposition of degradation is conducted as
251
252                                     Iˆnk = αd Gnk ⊙ (1 − Mnk ) + Ink ,                                  (4)
253
      where ⊙ indicates element-wise multiplication and αd ∈ [0, 1] is a dynamic weight used to regulate
254
      the degradation intensity for Ink . In this way, we can obtain a randomly degraded image Iˆnk where
255
      the original visual contexts are generally retained. Encountering with Iˆnk , it is more difficult for the
256
      T2I model to fit undesired visual semantics in out-of-mask regions, since these semantics would be
257
      randomly perturbed with Gaussian noise at each training step.
258
259   Dynamic Intensity. Unfortunately, the T2I model may gradually memorize the introduced noise
260   in some cases, especially in the later training, thus leading to noise appearing in generated images.
261
      Thus, we design a descending scheme to dynamically regulate the intensity of the imposed noise
      during training. This scheme adopts an exponential curve that maintains a relatively large intensity
262
      in the early and decreases dramatically in the later. Let d denote the current training step and D
263
      denote the total training step. The curve of dynamic intensity is defined as

      3.3   D UAL -S TREAM BALANCING
271
272   Another primary challenge in this task is semantic imbalance, which arises from the inherent visual
273   semantic disparity between the target concept and component. Generally, a concept has richer visual
274   semantics than a component (e.g. person vs. hair). However, the semantic richness of a component
275   might be greater than a concept in some cases (e.g., simple tower vs. intricate roof). This imbalance
276   complicates the joint learning process which could overemphasis on the concept and component,
277   leading to uncoherent generation of the target concept and even the disappearance of the target
278
      component (see Figure 2(b.i)). To address this challenge, we design Dual-Stream Balancing (DS-
      Bal), which uses online and momentum denoising U-Nets (see Figure 3) to balance visual semantic
279
      learning between the concept and component, improving personalization fidelity (see Figure 2(b.ii)).
280
281   Sample-wise Min-Max Optimization. From a loss perspective, the visual semantics of the concept
282   and component are learned by optimizing the masked diffusion loss Ldiff for all the samples. Unfor-
283   tunately, this indiscriminate optimization does not allocate adequate learning efforts to the sample
284
      that is more challenging to learn, gradually leading to an imbalanced learning process. To over-
      come this issue, DS-Bal utilizes the online denoising U-Net to learn only the visual semantics of
285
      the hardest-to-learn sample at each training step. Inheriting the weights of the original denoising U-
286
      Net warmed up by joint learning, here the online denoising U-Net ϵθ only optimizes the maximum
287   masked diffusion loss among N samples, which is defined as

      Dataset, Implementation, and Evaluation. For a systemic investigation, we collect a dataset from
318   various domains, including characters, animation, buildings, objects, and animals. We employ Sta-
319   ble Diffusion 2.1 (Rombach et al., 2022) as the pretrained T2I diffusion model. Reference images

322   (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) as the optimizer. To generate evaluation images, we carefully design 20
323   text prompts covering extensive situations. For each method, we generate 14,720 images to conduct
      a comprehensive evaluation. To ensure fairness, all the seeds are fixed during training and inference.

506   4.5            F URTHER A PPLICATIONS
507
508   Decoupled Generation. After learning from a concept-component pair, MagicTailor can also enable
509   decoupled generation. As shown in Figure 6(a), MagicTailor can generate the target concept and
510   component separately in various and even cross-domain contexts. This should be credited to its
511   remarkable ability to accurately capture different-level visual semantics. Such an ability extends the
512
      flexibility of the possible combination between the concept and component.
513   Controlling Multiple Components. In this paper, we focus on personalizing one concept and one
514   component, because such a setting is enough to cover extensive scenarios in the real world, and can
515   be further extended to reconfigure multiple components with an iterative procedure. However, as
516   shown in Figure 6(b), our MagicTailor also exhibits the potential to handle one concept and multiple
517   components simultaneously. These results reflect a prospective direction of exploring better control
518
      over diverse components for a single concept.
519   Enhancing Other Generative Tools. In Figure 7, we provide some interesting method combina-
520   tions to show that our MagicTailor can enhance other generative tools. The combined tools include
521   ContorlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), CSGO (Xing et al., 2024), and InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024). As
522   we can see, MagicTailor can be seamlessly integrated into these tools, furnishing them with an ad-
523   ditional ability to control the concept’s component in their pipelines. For instance, working with
524
      MagicTailor, InstantMesh can conveniently achieve fine-grained design of 3D mesh, demonstrating
      the practicability of MagicTailor in collaborative applications.

      5            C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we introduce the novel task of component-controllable personalization, which al-
531
      lows for precise customization of individual components within a personalized concept. We tackle
      two major challenges that make this task particularly difficult: semantic pollution, where unwanted
532
      visual elements disrupt the integrity of the concept, and semantic imbalance, which skews the learn-
533
      ing process of visual semantics. To address these challenges, we present MagicTailor, an innovative
534   framework featuring Dynamic Masked Degradation (DM-Deg) to mitigate unwanted visual seman-
535   tics and Dual-Stream Balancing (DS-Bal) to ensure balanced learning of visual components. Our
536   comprehensive experiments demonstrate that MagicTailor not only sets a new benchmark in this
537   challenging task but also opens up exciting possibilities for a wide range of creative applications.
538   Looking ahead, we envision extending our approach to other areas of image and video generation,
539   exploring how multi-level visual semantics can be recognized, controlled, and manipulated to unlock
      even more sophisticated and imaginative generative capabilities.

      A     M ORE D ETAILS OF E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
757
758
      A.1   DATASET
759
760   As there is no existing dataset specifically for component-controllable personalization, we curate a
761   dataset from the internet to conduct experiments. Unlike previous works (Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari
762   et al., 2023) that focus on very few categories of concepts, the dataset contains various domains of
763   concepts and components, such as characters, animation, buildings, objects, and animals. Overall,
764   the dataset consists of 23 concept-component pairs totally with 138 reference images, where each
765   concept/component contains 3 reference images and a corresponding category label. The scale of
766   this dataset is aligned with the scale of those datasets used in compared methods (Gal et al., 2022;
767   Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2023; Safaee et al., 2024).
768
769   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION
770
771   We utilize Stable Diffusion 2.1 (Rombach et al., 2022) as the pretrained T2I diffusion model. As
772   commonly done, the resolution of reference images is set to 512 × 512. Besides, the rank and
773
      alpha of the LoRA module are set to 32. For the warm-up/DS-Bal stage, we set the learning rate
      to 1e-4/1e-5 and the training steps to 200/300. Moreover, the learning rate is further scaled by the
774
      batch size, which is set to completely contain a concept-component pair. MagicTailor is trained
775
      with an AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer and a DDPM Ho et al. (2020) sampler on
776   an NVIDIA A100 GPU. For one concept-component pair, it runs for about 5 minutes. All experi-
777   ments are accomplished with Python 3.10.11 and PyTorch 1.13.1, based on CUDA 11.6. Following
778   (Avrahami et al., 2023), the tensor precision is set to float16 to accelerate training. For a fair com-
779   parison, all random seeds are fixed at 0 in each experiment, and all compared methods use the same
780   implementation above except for method-specific configurations.
781
782   A.3   T EXT P ROMPTS F OR E VALUATION
783
784   To generate images for evaluation, we carefully design 20 text prompts covering extensive situations,
785   which are listed in Table 5. These text prompts can be divided into four aspects, including recontex-
786   tualization, restylization, interaction, and property modification, where each aspect is composed of
787   5 text prompts. In recontextualization, we change the contexts to different locations and periods. In
788   restylization, we transfer concepts into various artistic styles. In interaction, we explore the spatial
789   interaction with other concepts. In property modification, we modify the properties of concepts in
790
      rendering, views, and materials. Such a group of diverse text prompts allows us to systemically
      evaluate the generalization capability of a method.
791
792
793   A.4   S CHEME OF G ENERATING E VALUATION I MAGES
794
795
      We generate 32 images per text prompt for each pair, using a DDIM (Song et al., 2020) sampler
      with 50 steps and a classifier-free guidance scale of 7.5. To ensure fairness, we fix the random seed
796
      within the range of [0, 31] across all methods. This process results in a total of 14,720 images for
797
      each method to be evaluated, ensuring a robust and thorough comparison.
798
799
800   A.5   AUTOMATIC M ETRICS
801
      We utilize four automatic metrics in the aspects of text alignment (CLIP-T (Gal et al., 2022)) and
802
      identity fidelity (CLIP-I (Radford et al., 2021), DINO (Oquab et al., 2023), DreamSim (Fu et al.,
803
      2023)). To precisely measure identity fidelity, we improve the traditional measurement approach for
804   personalization. This is because a reference image of the target concept/component could contain an
805   undesired component/concept that is not expected to appear in evaluation images. Specifically, we
806   use Grounded-SAM (Ren et al., 2024) to segment out the concept and component in each reference
807   and evaluation image. Then, we further eliminate the target component from the segmented concept,
808   e.g., eliminate the hair from the person in a “<person> + <hair>” pair. Such a process is similar to
809   the one adopted in (Avrahami et al., 2023). As a result, using the segmented version of evaluation
      images and reference images, we can accurately calculate the metrics of identity fidelity.

836   A.6      U SER S TUDY
837
838   In addition to using automatic metrics, we further evaluate the methods with a user study. Specifi-
839   cally, we design a questionnaire to display 20 groups of evaluation images generated by our method
840   and other methods. Besides, each group also contains the corresponding text prompt and the ref-
841   erence images of the concept and component, where we adopt the same text prompts that are used
842
      to calculate CLIP-T. The results of our method and all the compared methods are presented on the
      same page. Clear rules are established for users to evaluate in three aspects, including text alignment,
843
      identity fidelity, and generation quality. Users are requested to select the best result in each group
844
      by answering the corresponding questions of these three aspects. We hide all the method names and
845   randomize the order of methods to ensure fairness. Finally, 3,180 valid answers are collected for a
846   sufficient quantitative evaluation of human preferences.

1001   D           M ORE Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS
1002
1003   In Figure 12, we provide more evaluation images for a substantial qualitative comparison. It can be
1004   clearly observed that semantic pollution remains an intractable problem for these compared methods.
1005   While employing the masked diffusion loss, they still fall short in suppressing the appearance of
1006   undesired visual semantics. This is due to the leak of an effective mechanism to alleviate the T2I
1007
       model’s perception for these semantics. To address this, our MagicTailor utilizes Dynamic Masked
       Degradation (DM-Deg) to dynamically perturb undesired visual semantics during the learning phase,
1008
       and thus achieve better performance. On the other hand, the compared methods are also severely
1009
       influenced by semantic imbalance, resulting in overemphasis or even overfitting on the concept or
1010   component. This is because the inherent imbalance of visual semantics complicates the learning
1011   process. In response to this issue, our MagicTailor applies Dual-Stream Balancing (DS-Bal) to
1012   balance the learning of visual semantics, effectively showcasing its prowess in this tough task.
