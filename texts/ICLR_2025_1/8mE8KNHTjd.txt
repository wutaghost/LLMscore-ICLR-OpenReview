                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Image Quality Assessment (IQA) and Image Aesthetic Assessment (IAA) aim to
012
                   simulate human subjective perception of image visual quality and aesthetic ap-
013                peal. Despite distinct learning objectives, they have underlying interconnected-
014                ness due to consistent human assessment perception. Existing unified methods
015                typically combine datasets of two tasks for regression training directly, which
016                fail to learn mutually beneficial representations shared by both tasks explicitly.
017                To confront this challenge, we propose Unified vision-language pre-training of
018                Quality and Aesthetics (UniQA), to extract useful and common representations
019                from two tasks, thereby benefiting them simultaneously. Unfortunately, the lack
020                of text in the IQA datasets and the textual noise in the IAA datasets pose se-
021
                   vere challenges for multimodal pre-training. To address this, we (1) utilize mul-
                   timodal large language models (MLLMs) to generate high-quality text descrip-
022
                   tions; (2) use the generated text for IAA as metadata to purify noisy IAA data.
023
                   To effectively adapt the pre-trained UniQA to downstream tasks, we further pro-
024                pose a lightweight adapter that utilizes versatile cues to fully exploit the extensive
025                knowledge of the pre-trained model. Extensive experiments show that our ap-
026                proach achieves state-of-the-art performance on both IQA and IAA tasks, while
027                also demonstrating exceptional few-label image assessment capabilities.

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Image Quality Assessment (IQA)1 and Image Aesthetic Assessment (IAA) aim to measure the per-
033   ceived quality and beauty of an image. They find broad applications in many scenarios, such as
034   guiding individuals in image photography and editing, and serving as tools for image dehazing
035   model (Zhao et al., 2021). Consequently, huge efforts (Su et al., 2020; Ke et al., 2021; He et al.,
036   2022) have been devoted to establishing effective IQA and IAA models.
037   IQA and IAA concentrate on distinct aspects of image assessment, with IQA primarily focusing
038   on the distortion level of the image, while IAA is oriented towards evaluating the aesthetic appeal
039   of the image. Despite their differences, IQA and IAA have underlying commonality: simulating
040   human subjective perceptions of images. Specifically, in human subjective evaluation of images,
041   quality and aesthetics exhibit a mutual influence, such that high-quality images are more likely to
042   possess a higher aesthetic appeal compared to their low-quality counterparts. Thus, the learning
043   process for both tasks not only acquires features unique to themselves but also involves the learning
044
      of task-agnostic common representations. This commonality sparks an idea:
045            Can we develop a foundational model with robust visual assessment perceptions consistent
046            with human to benefit both IQA and IAA tasks?
047
      Although previous works (e.g., MUSIQ (Ke et al., 2021)) can be applied to IQA and IAA tasks indis-
048
      criminately, they cannot exploit beneficial representations from another task. Wu et al. (2023b) and
049   Zhang et al. (2023a) also find the similarities of two tasks and attempt to tackle them with unified
050   architecture and training. However, they typically unify datasets of two tasks for regression train-
051   ing directly, which cannot explicitly learn the task-shared representations, restricting the extraction
052   of mutual benefits. In this paper, we propose the Unified pre-training of Quality and Aesthetics

076   To achieve unified pre-training, a straightforward solution involves consolidating all IQA and IAA
077
      datasets and then training the model to regress towards the mean opinion scores (MOS) annotated
      by humans. However, existing datasets show variations in perceptual scales due to differences in
078
      subjective testing methodologies (Zhang et al., 2021a). As a result, this training strategy makes the
079
      model develop a score bias toward larger scale datasets. Moreover, it may not effectively capture the
080   unique characteristics of IQA and IAA, as the MOS labels cannot be explicitly interpreted. To this
081   end, we propose to use text descriptions as a bridge to integrate the two tasks, leveraging the rich
082   and fine-grained semantics inherent in text to provide more auxiliary information.
083
      However, existing IQA datasets typically have images only and lack text descriptions. While current
084
      IAA datasets (Ghosal et al., 2019) include text data provided by humans, they often contain con-
085
      siderable textual noise irrelevant for aesthetic assessment. Therefore, a top priority is determining
086   how to acquire high-quality image-text data for both IQA and IAA tasks. Recently, multimodal
087   large language models (MLLMs) (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
088   2023) have demonstrated outstanding capabilities in image understanding, which can generate rea-
089   sonable responses based on images and user instructions. Inspired by this, we propose utilizing
090   MLLMs with tailored prompts to generate quality- and aesthetics-related descriptions for the IQA
091   and IAA datasets, respectively (Step 1 of Figure 1). As observed in Figure 2, this approach provides
092   a comprehensive and precise depiction of image quality and aesthetics. Furthermore, we utilize
093   these generated high-quality aesthetics-related descriptions as metadata to refine the raw aesthetic
094
      caption dataset (Step 2 of Figure 1). Finally, we unify the generated and refined image-text datasets
      to conduct vision-language contrast pre-training (Step 3 of Figure 1). This results in the pre-trained
095
      UniQA with a powerful multimodal image assessment perception.
096
097   After pre-training on image-text pairs, we propose a lightweight adapter, namely the Multi-Cue In-
098   tegration Adapter, to fine-tune the specific dataset of two tasks (Step 4 of Figure 1). This adapter
099   uses versatile cues related to image assessment to prompt the pre-trained UniQA, adeptly extracting
100
      useful knowledge and comprehensively assessing the image. With much fewer tunable parameters
      compared to previous IQA and IAA models, our model outperforms them on both tasks. More sur-
101
      prisingly, benefiting from the powerful representations learned by pre-training, our method achieves
102
      impressive results on few-label IQA, e.g., achieving the SRCC values of 0.828 (vs. 0.760 on CLIVE
103   of GRepQ (Srinath et al., 2024)) and 0.844 (vs. 0.812 on KonIQ of GRepQ).
104

118   2   R ELATED W ORK
119
120   Image Quality Assessment. The rapid development of deep learning has sparked significant in-
121   terest in their application for IQA. Many researchers utilize CNN to solve the IQA problem with
122   various effective techniques, including multi-level feature aggregation (Li et al., 2018), adaptive
123   quality prediction (Su et al., 2020), and patch-to-picture learning (Ying et al., 2020), order learn-
124   ing (Shin et al., 2024) and unsupervised learning (Saha et al., 2023). Recently, transformer-based
125   IQA methods (Ke et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024)
126
      show promising results in the IQA field, which can compensate for the non-local representation
      ability of CNN. Despite these impressive breakthroughs, these methods often transfer models pre-
127
      trained on classification datasets, such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), to IQA tasks, which may be
128
      suboptimal (Li et al., 2023d). Q-Align (Wu et al., 2023b) attempts to jointly perform IQA and IAA
129   tasks, but it uses a language model with a huge number of parameters and does not explicitly extract
130   features of the two tasks through pre-training. Our method can learn more effective representations
131   through joint pre-training on quality-aesthetics image-text data, providing benefits for IQA tasks.
132
133   Image Aesthetic Assessment. With the advent of deep learning, IAA methods have evolved from
134   hand-crafted feature extraction (Datta et al., 2006; Ke et al., 2006; Nishiyama et al., 2011; Sun
135   et al., 2009) to end-to-end feature learning, marking significant advancements in the IAA domain.
136   Various techniques have been developed to boost IAA task, including local and global feature inte-
137   gration (Lu et al., 2015; Hou et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a; He et al., 2023b),
138   graph network (She et al., 2021; Duan et al., 2022) and theme-aware learning (Li et al., 2023c; He
139   et al., 2022). Recently, there has been an emergence of multimodal IAA methods (Zhang et al.,
140
      2020; Zhou et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2021b; Nie et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024b) that incorporate
      text as auxiliary supervision. However, these methods necessitate the use of text during inference,
141
      limiting their flexible application since text is often not easily available. Our method overcomes this
142
      limitation by conducting vision-language pre-training firstly to learn effective representation. The
143   pre-trained model can be flexibly applied to the IAA field using only images.
144
145   Vision-Language Models. Vision-Language Models (VLMs) (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al.,
146   2021; Yao et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023) introduce the contrastive learning strat-
147   egy to acquire image-text correspondences from large-scale image-text pairs. VLMs have exhibited
148   promising results across multiple tasks, including IQA (Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b) and
149   IAA (Hentschel et al., 2022; Sheng et al., 2023). Recently, the Multimodal Large Language Models
150   (MLLMs) have garnered increasing research interest, exhibiting remarkable prowess in compre-
151   hending image content and reasoning through complex instructions (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al.,
152   2023; Li et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023). Most existing MLLMs achieve this by
153
      integrating image features with LLM tokens, subsequently fine-tuning the LLM via multimodal in-
      struction tuning. During inference, MLLMs can reason with given images and user instructions,
154
      generating text responses by leveraging world knowledge learned during pre-training.

237   3.3    DATA P URIFICATION S TRATEGY
238
      In addition to the generated aesthetic captions YIAA , there are also IAA datasets with captions
239
      commented by humans (Ghosal et al., 2019), which directly reflect human aesthetic feelings. In-
240
      corporating comments from various people can offer a more comprehensive description of image
241   aesthetics. However, while enhancing text diversity, it may introduce noise to the data, as individu-
242   als may provide comments unrelated to image aesthetics. To address this issue, we propose a novel
243   data purification strategy to refine raw captions in the original dataset. This process is illustrated in
244   Figure 3(a).
245
      Specifically, we introduce Aesthetics-relevance and Informativeness Rank (AIR) to measure the
246
      quality of text corresponding to an image. The AIR consists of Aesthetics-relevance Rank (AR) and
247   Informativeness Rank (IR). To obtain AR, we first pre-train a CLIP model with generated aesthetic
248   data YIAA to get an aesthetics-aware CLIP model, denoted as CLIPaes . Then, we employ it to
249   measure the aesthetics relevance score (sA ) for an image-text pair. Given an image with n captions,
250   AR can be defined as:
251                           AR = Rank(s1A · · · snA ), siA = CLIPaes (xI , xiT ),                     (3)
252
      where sA represents the aesthetics relevance score between the i-th caption xiT and its corresponding
              i
253   image xI . Note that AR consists of long integers that represent the rank of a caption after sorting
254   by sA . To obtain IR, we simply utilize the sentence length as informativeness score (sI ) to measure
255   the informativeness of text. Accordingly, for an image with n textual captions, IR can be expressed
256   as:
257                              IR = Rank(s1I , · · · , snI ), siI = Length(xiT ),                     (4)

277   4.1   M ULTI -C UE I NTEGRATION A DAPTER
278
279   During pre-training, the model aligns image and assessment-related captions, empowering it with
280   strong comprehension of image quality and aesthetics. With this foundation model, we can slightly
281
      adjust the visual features, efficiently adapting it to score-based image assessment tasks. To this
      end, we introduce a lightweight adapter, namely the Multi-Cue Integration Adapter, to adapt visual
282
      features and inject rich cues for fine-tuning downstream tasks. The adapter consists of two key
283
      processes: visual feature adaptation and multi-cue integration prediction.
284
285   Visual Feature Adaptation. We add a learnable residual module following the pre-trained image
286   encoder to adjust the visual features so as to adapt to specific assessment datasets. We optimize this
287
      module while keeping the image and text backbones frozen, enabling parameter-efficient tuning.
      The structure of the adapter is illustrated in Figure 3(b). Let I denote the image features extracted
288
      from the frozen image encoder, the visual feature adaptation process can be expressed as:
289
290                                     I ′ = Normalize(Adapter(I) + I)                                  (6)
291   where the Adapter(·) consists of two fully connected layers with a ReLU activation in between,
292   and I ′ represents the adapted visual features.
293   Multi-cue Integration Prediction. A straightforward approach to incorporating the CLIP model
294   into perception assessment is to utilize the “good image” as an anchor and take the cosine sim-
295   ilarity between the text anchor and a given image as the assessment score. However, this method
296   shows two shortcomings: (1) using the absolute value of similarity as the perception score may
297   not be optimal because it only reflects the semantic similarity between images and texts (Wu et al.,
298   2023a; Wang et al., 2023); (2) a single prompt may not fully leverage the extensive knowledge of the
299   pre-trained model. Thus, we propose to utilize versatile cues to comprehensively explore the power
300   of the pre-trained UniQA and convert absolute similarity scores into relative values for weighting.
301   Specifically, we utilize the prompt template “{level} image” and five text levels (bad, poor, fair,
302   good, perfect), i.e., “Multi-cue”, to construct prompts. Next, we calculate the cosine similarity
303   between the normalized text features {Ti }5i=1 of five prompts and adapted visual features I ′ , and
304   then use the Softmax(·) to obtain the related value of five image-text correspondence. These related
305   values will weight the predefined score levels to get the final assessment score. This process can be
306   formulated as follows:

      5.1        DATASETS
358
359
360   We employ the IQA dataset FLIVE (Ying et al., 2020) and the IAA dataset AVA (Murray et al.,
361   2012) for quality- and aesthetics-related captioning, respectively, and AVA-Captions (Ghosal et al.,
362
      2019) to provide authentic aesthetic comments. We evaluate the performance on typical IQA and
      IAA datasets, including seven IQA datasets and two IAA datasets.
363
364   IQA Dataset. For the IQA task, four synthetic datasets, including LIVE (Sheikh et al., 2006),
365   CSIQ (Larson & Chandler, 2010), TID2013 (Ponomarenko et al., 2013), KADID (Lin et al., 2019),
366   and three authentic datasets of CLIVE (Ghadiyaram & Bovik, 2015), KonIQ (Hosu et al., 2020),
367   SPAQ (Fang et al., 2020), are used for performance evaluation. FLIVE (Ying et al., 2020) is an
368
      authentic IQA dataset that contains 39,810 images. We employ an AIGC-generated IQA dataset,
      AGIQA-3K (Li et al., 2023b), to evaluate the generalization capability of our UniQA. Details of the
369
      datasets can be found in appendix.
370
371   IAA Dataset. For the IAA task, we conduct experiments on AVA (Murray et al., 2012) and
372   AADB (Kong et al., 2016) datasets. AVA comprises 250k images, with the test set of 19,928 images.
373   AADB dataset consists of 10,000 images in total, with 8,500 images for training, 500 images for
374
      validation, and 1,000 images for testing.
375   AVA-Captions Dataset. AVA-Captions (Ghosal et al., 2019) offer multiple human-annotated com-
376   ments for each AVA image. To avoid potential data leakage, we strictly follow the official data
377   split of AVA, results in a pre-training image-text dataset comprising 234,090 images paired with 3.0
      million captions.

      We use CLIP-B/16 (Radford et al., 2021) as our VLM for pre-training and LLaVA-1.5-7B (Liu et al.,

      2023b;a) as our MLLM for captioning. We pre-train the model using Adam optimizer (Kingma &
406   Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 5e-6 and weight decay of 0.2. The model is trained for 5 epochs
407   with a batch size of 960. We set K = 4 to refine the AVA-Captions dataset. We use MSE loss to
408   optimize the adapter on downstream tasks and different training settings according to the task and
409   size of datasets. More training details are provided in the appendix. For each IQA dataset, 80% of
410   the images are used for training and the remaining 20% for testing. We repeat this process 10 times
411   to mitigate the performance bias and the medians of SRCC and PLCC are reported. For the IAA
412   datasets, we follow the standard data splits.

490   5.6   A BLATION S TUDIES
491
492   Impact of different pre-training data. Table 6 shows the effect of different pre-training data. We
493   observe that unified pre-training achieves the optimal performance on both tasks. In addition, we
494   derive some meaningful observations. (1) Using either the generated YIQA or YIAA improves the
495   performance of both IQA and IAA tasks, proving the mutual benefit of these two tasks and the effec-
496   tiveness of MLLMs captioning. (2) Unifying YIQA and YIAA datasets does not lead to significant
497
      improvements. We believe this is because the MLLMs-generated text tends to have similar sentence
      structures (Liu et al., 2023c) and perception representation, limiting the diversity provided for multi-
498                                                              +
      modal learning. (3) Pre-training with refined authentic YIAA    shows significant improvement on two
499
      tasks, reflecting that human-annotated comments can provide a more comprehensive and effective
500   representation for the model.
501
502   Figure 5 illustrates the Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) visualization of different pre-training.
503
      We can notice that after quality-related and aesthetic pre-training, the model pays more attention
      to blurred subjects and noisy backgrounds. This effect becomes more pronounced with unified pre-
504
      training, underscoring the advantages of such a unified approach. In addition, the unified pre-training
505
      can focus on the areas of quality-related and aesthetic pre-training at the same time. This shows that
506   unified training can learn common representations of the two tasks.
507
508   Effectiveness of data purification strategy. The second part of Table 6 illustrates the ablation study
509
      of the data purification strategy. It can be observed that employing either AR or IR strategy to purify
      data can improve the model’s performance of both IQA and IAA tasks. These results validate the
510
      benefit of obtaining aesthetically relevant and semantically rich textual descriptions for the model.
511
      Finally, when combining these two strategies, it achieves the best performance.
512
513   Effectiveness of the Multi-Cue Integration Adapter. The third part of Table 6 shows the ablation
514   study of the proposed adapter. “Single Prompt” denotes using the similarity between the text “good
515
      image” and images as the assessment score directly, while “Antonym Prompt” represents using the
      relative weights of texts “good image” and “bad image” to weight the predefined score. It is evident
516
      that the “Single Prompt” is considerably inferior to the “Antonym Prompt”, showing the limitations
517
      of using semantic similarity as score directly. Our method integrates more cues into the “Antonym
518   Prompt” to comprehensively assess images, thereby achieving optimal performance.
519
520   Ablation on different MLLMs. The bottom part of Table 6 presents the ablation study of various
521
      MLLMs. We generate YIQA via different MLLMs for pre-training. It is evident that using different
      MLLMs exhibits similar performance, while ensembling different MLLMs can boost performance.
522
      This indicates that MLLMs are capable of generating accurate captions with our text-guided prompt,
523
      and enhancing caption diversity can further improve performance. Considering resource limitations,
524   we use LLaVa-7B and will integrate more MLLMs in the future.
525
526
527   6     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   This paper introduces UniQA, which leverages unified vision-language pre-training to address qual-
530
      ity and aesthetic assessment problems concurrently. We construct a high-quality image-text dataset
      about quality and aesthetics with the assistance of MLLMs. Through large-scale pre-training on
531
      this dataset, UniQA learns shared and effective representations of IQA and IAA tasks, benefiting
532
      both tasks. Additionally, we propose a Multi-Cue Integration Adapter to effectively adapt the pre-
533   trained UniQA to downstream assessment tasks. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance
534   on both IQA and IAA tasks, and demonstrates powerful zero-shot and few-label image assessment
535   capabilities.
536
      Limitations and future work. MLLMs often generate captions with similar sentence structures
537
      and semantic expressions, restricting their ability to provide diverse and enriched representations
538
      for multimodal learning. Future work will explore other techniques to address this issue, including
539   integrating various MLLMs for captioning and employing in-context learning methods.

      A.1   D ISCUSSION ABOUT THE AIR
867
868   We propose the Aesthetics-relevance and Informativeness Rank (AIR) to select the high-quality texts
869   corresponding to an image. The AIR can be expressed as follows:
870
                               AIR = Rank((AR1 + IR1 ), · · · , (ARn + IRn )).                             (9)
871
872   where AR and IR denote the Aesthetics-relevance Rank and Informativeness Rank, respectively; n
873   is the number of comments corresponding to an image. For simplicity, we directly take the summa-
874   tion of IR and AR to reflect the semantic relevance and richness of the text. In fact, we can introduce
875   two factors (α and β) to purify the data more flexibly. Now, the modified AIRm can be formulated
876   as:
877
                          AIRm = Rank((αAR1 + βIR1 ), · · · , (αARn + βIRn )).                           (10)
878   For instance, we can use large α for the highly noisy data. With this strategy, we can more flexibly
879   purify data based on data quality.
