009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance
012            across various NLP tasks, and have extended their capability to long-context sce-
013            narios. However, the increasing context length leads to longer inference time in
014            both the prefilling and decoding stages. Existing token pruning methods primarily
015            evict tokens to compress the KV cache, and only accelerate the decoding stage.
016            Recent studies have extended token pruning to both stages, but they either yield
017
               subtle speedup during the prefilling stage or defer a portion of computations to the
               decoding phase. Critically, these approaches prioritize the attention module, over-
018
               looking the significant computations in the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) module.
019
               In this work, we focus on the prefilling stage and propose a novel token pruning
020
               method named FTP for long-context LLM inference. Our approach is based on
021
               the observation that the FFN module accounts for over 60% of the inference time.
022            FTP reduces this by pruning non-critical tokens before the inference of FFN. The
023            importance of each token, along with the quantity to be pruned, are dynamically
024            determined by the attention scores in each layer. Unlike previous token pruning
025            methods, FTP preserves a substantial amount of information of the pruned tokens
026            through the residual connection, thereby achieving a notable speedup with only
027            a negligible decrease in performance. Specifically, the Qwen2-7B-Instruct model
028            with FTP achieves a speedup of 1.24× in the prefilling stage with only a 1.30%
029            performance drop compared to the baseline model. The speedup is further boosted
030
               to 1.39× on a Qwen1.5-32B-Chat model. Extensive experiments on long-context
               datasets across various tasks demonstrate the potential and effectiveness of FTP.

      Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable performance in a wide range of natural
036   language processing applications, including summarization, question-and-answer, dialogue systems,
037   and contextual learning (Thoppilan et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
038   2023a). Recently, long-context models have emerged as an important trend in the development of
039   LLMs, and several of them have already been launched to process complex and long prompts. For
040   example, GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a) are both capable of 128k
041   context length, and Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2024) can even process up to 200k tokens.
042   Given the advantages of long-context models, they nevertheless pose enormous challenges at in-
043   ference time. As depicted in Figure 1, the inference of transformer-based LLMs with a KV cache
044   typically consists of two stages: 1) the prefilling stage, which applies the self-attention, normaliza-
045   tion, and the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) modules to all input token parallelly, records the key and
046   value in the KV cache, and finally produces the first generated token. 2) the decoding stage, which
047   takes the last generated token as input and iteratively predicts the next token with the input and the
048   KV cache. We refer to the time spent in the prefilling stage as time to first token (TTFT).
049   While there have been various methods trying to reduce the inference time of long-context LLMs
050   by pruning the input prompt (Jiang et al., 2023b;a; Pan et al., 2024), compressing the KV cache (Li
051   et al., 2024; Zandieh et al., 2024), improving the efficiency of decoding (Leviathan et al., 2022; Sun
052   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), and optimizing the attention kernel with sparse computation (Jiang
053   et al., 2024), few works (Yang et al., 2024b; Fu et al., 2024) place attention to the reduction of TTFT.
      However, the computationally intensive nature of the prefilling stage implies that the TTFT for long-

092   To tackle this problem, we focus on the TTFT optimization of long-context LLM inference and
093
      propose a novel token pruning method named FFN Token Pruning (FTP) to alleviate computational
      demands in the prefilling stage. We first investigate the time proportion of the main modules (i.e.,
094
      the self-attention, normalization, and FFN module) in each layer by conducting experiments on the
095

096   in Figure 3, the FFN module consistently takes a large proportion of inference time on both the
097   Llama3 (AI@Meta, 2024) model and the Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024a) model, suggesting a large
098   potential for TTFT acceleration by reducing the tokens involving the FFN computation.
099
      Unlike previous token pruning methods (Xiao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Yang
100
      et al., 2024b) where tokens are directly pruned from the whole layer, FTP dynamically selects a
101
      certain proportion of tokens with an attention-based strategy and prunes them before the inference
102   of FFN. For the pruned tokens, their FFN outputs are logically set to zeros vectors and they remain
103   identical before and after the FFN due to the residual connection. Since the attention scores typically
104   concentrate on a small proportion of tokens, a substantial amount of the FFN calculation can be cir-
105   cumvented, thereby reducing the TTFT. Furthermore, since the attention mechanism of each layer
106   is fully preserved, the underlying context information associated with the tokens is substantially re-
107   tained, leading to negligible accuracy loss. Extensive experiments on LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b)
      have demonstrated that our method has a balanced performance both in speedup and accuracy.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      LLM inference optimization for long-context inputs has emerged as a significant area of NLP re-
111
      search. Several prior works (Dao et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024) directly optimize
112   the computing and memory footprint underlying the common operations in LLMs. In addition to
113   these approaches, a substantial body of work concentrate on the logical inference of the model and
114   optimize the inference process through two kinds of strategies: token pruning and layer skipping.
115
116   2.1   T OKEN P RUNING
117
118   Token pruning for sequence models has become a popular resesarch field before the era of LLM.
119   Ren et al. (2023) proposes a model architecture named SeqBoat to skip non-activated sub-modules.
120   Kim et al. (2022) and Guan et al. (2022) learn certain thresholds or parameterized modules to facil-
121   itate token pruning. For LLMs, many existing works (Liu et al., 2024; Adnan et al., 2024; Nawrot
122   et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024) accerlate inference by dropping irrelevant tokens,
123
      based on the observation that the attention scores are sparse in most layers. StreamingLLM (Xiao
      et al., 2024) only preserves the attention sink (i.e., initial tokens) as well as the recent tokens to en-
124
      able efficient infinite prompt requests. H2O (Zhang et al., 2023b) introduces the concept of “heavy
125
      hitter” and evicts tokens according to the cumulative attention score. SnapKV (Li et al., 2024) votes
126   important previous tokens with the attention scores obtained by the “observation window”, and
127   prunes the unimportant ones to reduce the KV cache used in decoding. While these studies effec-
128   tively compress the computation and memory footprint, they mainly accelerate the decoding phase
129   without reducing the TTFT. On the other hand, PyramidInfer (Yang et al., 2024b) prunes tokens in
130   both the prefilling and decoding phase, only reserving the “pivotal context” tokens in the KV cache.
131   LazyLLM (Fu et al., 2024) also drops tokens from the prefilling stage and proposes an aux cache to
132   avoid redundant computing, enabling the model to pick any subset of the input tokens at each layer,
133   even if some tokens are already pruned. However, these methods either yield subtle speedup during
134
      prefilling or defer some computation to the decoding stage. More importantly, they prioritize the
      attention module and overlook the significant computation demands in the FFN module in practice,
135
      where flash attention (Dao et al., 2022) is a prevalent choice for efficient attention computation. Our
136
      work accelerates prefilling by pruning non-critical tokens prior to the FFN inference.
137
138
      2.2   L AYER S KIPPING
139
140   Layer skipping accelerates the LLM inference by skipping the computation of some decoder layers.
141   LayerSkip (Elhoushi et al., 2024) proposes a layer dropout strategy and an early exit loss during
142   training, enabling layer skipping during inference, without a significant accuracy drop. MoD (Ra-
143   poso et al., 2024) learns a router to determine for a token whether to skip both the self-attention and
144   MLP blocks in a layer. Tyukin et al. (2024) investigates three types of layer skipping: 1) skipping
145   MLP layers, 2) skipping attention layers, and 3) skipping transformer blocks on the 7B and 13B
146   Llama2 Touvron et al. (2023) models, and observes that dropping the attention modules leads to
147   much lower decrease in performance than dropping the FFN modules. Although our work focuses
148
      on reducing the computation in the FFN modules, the modules are not entirely bypassed, which
      results in slight degradation in performance.

158
      the stop condition is met.

160   the forward of the stack of decoder layers, each of which mainly consists of three modules: nor-
161   malization, self-attention and feed-forward network (FFN). As shown in Figure 3, the FFN module
      takes up over 60% of walltime of each decoder layer, highlighting a clear opportunity for accel-

204   3.2   T OKEN PRUNING FOR FFN
205
206   Based on the analysis above, in this work, we propose to reduce the number of tokens before feeding
207   them into FFN to accelerate the prefilling stage. Let us denote the “important” and “unimportant”
208   sets of tokens as TI ∈ RLI ×C and TU ∈ RLU ×C , where LI and LU are the number of “important”
209   and “unimportant” tokens. As shown in Figure 4, only the “important” tokens are involved in the
210   FFN calculation as formulated in Equation 1, and the FFN results for the “unimportant” tokens are
211   logically set to zero vectors (i.e., F F N (TU ) = 0). Thanks to the residual connection surrounding
212
      the FFN module, setting the FFN results to be zeros vectors is equivalent to bypassing the updates
      in the FFN. Consequently, the FLOPs required for the FFN are reduced to 6LI CI from 6LCI.
213
214   To this end, two critical questions arise: 1) how to divide the input tokens into “important” and
215   “unimportant” ones, and 2) how many tokens could be pruned, without causing obvious harm to the
      model performance? We analyse and discuss them in the following Section 3.2.1 and Section 3.2.2.

      3.2.2                                        T OKEN P RUNING P ROPORTION
289
290
      Since the number of pruned tokens is determined by the reserved ratio η in each layer, we quan-
      titatively investigate the relationship between η and the number of reserved tokens by conducting
291
      an experiment on the Qasper (Dasigi et al., 2021) and HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) dataset with a
292
      Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) model. Concretely, we first derive the attention score matrices at each
293   layer, average the scores along the head and query dimension, and obtain the averaged attention
294   score for each token at each layer. Next, for each layer, we sort the tokens descendingly by their
295   averaged attention score. Then we uniformly divide the input length into 100 steps, and record the
296   cumulative percentage of attention score at each step. Finally, we average the cumulative percentage
297   over all layers and all samples in the dataset, and the result is depicted in Figure 6. As shown in the
298   result, a large proportion of attention score (i.e., 95%) is occupied by only 60% of tokens, which is
299   consistent with the first observation in Section 3.2.1, and suggests an opportunity for token pruning.
300   On the other hand, we empirically discover that shallow layers are more sensitive to our FTP (see
301   Section 4.6), which is consistent with the conclusions of previous works (Fu et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
302   2024b). However, we propose to preserve the whole layer (i.e., η = 1.0) for the first F layers, and
303   apply token pruning to the following layers. Thanks to the high pruning efficiency of FTP, the model
304   maintains a considerable overall pruning rate.

      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
379
380   Basic Configuration. We implement FTP on the Llama3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024), Qwen2-
381   7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024a) and two larger models (see Section 4.5). The max context length for
382   Qwen2 and Llama3 are 32k and 8k respectively, and the data samples that exceed the max context
383   length of the models are truncated during the data pre-processing pipeline of LongBench. We set
384   P = 100 and N = 50 for both models. For the Llama3-8B model, we set F = 10 to preserve the
385   first 10 layers and η = 0.90 for the following layers. When it comes to the Qwen2-7B model, F and
386   η are set as 10 and 0.95 respectively. Note that FTP is free of further training and shares the same
387
      weights with the original model. All experiments are conducted on NVIDIA A100 GPUs without
      model quantization.
388
389   Attention Implementation. Our method is implemented with the transformers library (Wolf et al.,
390   2020) and flash attention, which is a prevalent choice in practical inference of LLMs. Given that
391   flash attention inherently does not return attention weights, we recalculate the necessary attention
392   weights to assess token importance, introducing only a negligible cost (refer to Section 4.6.1).
393
394   4.2   R ESULTS ON L ONG B ENCH

396   We conduct experiments on LongBench (Bai et al., 2023b), including six long-context tasks, to

398   incorporate a state-of-the-art prompt compression method (Pan et al., 2024) for comparison, which
399
      formulates prompt compression as a token classification problem and utilizes a transformer encoder
      with a linear classification head to prune the prompt before feeding them into the LLM.
400
401   The performance for each task is calculated by averaging the results of datasets associated with
402   that specific task. As shown in Table 1, FTP generally presents considerable speedup across all six

      a higher reserved ratio η. Furthermore, LLMLingua2 (Pan et al., 2024) on both models can hardly

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we explore a novel approach of prefilling acceleration by pruning tokens for FFN, and
533
      propose the FFN Token Pruning (FTP) technique for long-context inference of LLMs, without addi-
534   tional training or finetuning. We start by analyzing the TTFT proportion in long-context inference of
535   LLMs, and profiling the time proportion of the primary components in a typical LLM layer. Observ-
536   ing the substantial time allocation to FFN during prefilling, we reduces FFN computation time by
537   “pruning” non-critical tokens with an attention-based strategy prior to FFN inference. In a addition,
538   FTP preserves a large amount of information of the pruned toknes through the residual connection.
539   Experiments on long-context datasets across various tasks indicate that FTP is capable of delivering
      significant acceleration while maintaining performance.

787
788   In this section, we evaluate whether FTP results in the loss of intermediate information by conduct-
789
      ing the Needle-in-a-Haystack (Kamradt, 2023) experiment. The Needle-in-a-Haystack test inserts
      a specific statement (referred to as the “needle”) in the middle of a long-context document (the
790
      “haystack”), and requires the LLM to retrieve this statement. For evaluation, a GPT4 (Achiam et al.,
791
      2023) model is utilized to score the relevance between the LLM’s output and the “needle”.
792
793   We iterate the context length from 1000 to the max context length of the model, and position the
794   “needle” from the beginning (0.0%) to the end (100%) in steps of 10% for each context length.
795
      The result for the Llama3-8B-Instruct and Qwen2-7B-Instruct models are presented in Figure 8.
      As demonstrated in the results, the Qwen2 baseline model exhibits substantial retrieval capabilities.
796
      Our method appears to have minimal impact on this feature, successfully retrieve the “needle” across
797
      nearly all context lengths and depths of the “needle”. For the Llama3 model, FTP even enhances
798   performance as the context length approaches its limit. The results in Figure 8 suggest that FTP does
799   not lead to a loss, and may even reduce the loss of intermediate information.
