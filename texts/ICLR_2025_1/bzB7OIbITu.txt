009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-image (T2I) models, though exhibiting remarkable creativity in image
012            generation, may be exploited to produce unsafe images. Existing safety measures,
013            e.g., content moderation or model alignment, fail in the presence of white-box ad-
014            versaries who know and can adjust model parameters, e.g., by fine-tuning. This
015            paper presents a novel defensive framework, named PATRONUS, which equips T2I
016
               models with holistic protection to defend against white-box adversaries. Specifi-
               cally, we design an internal moderator that decodes unsafe input features into zero
017
               vectors while ensuring the decoding performance of benign input features. Fur-
018
               thermore, we strengthen the model alignment with a carefully designed non-fine-
019            tunable learning (NFTL) mechanism, ensuring the T2I model will not be com-
020            promised by malicious fine-tuning. We have conducted extensive experiments to
021            validate the intactness of the performance on safe content generation and the ef-
022            fectiveness to reject unsafe content generation. Experiment results have confirmed
023            the resistance of PATRONUS against various fine-tuning attacks by white-box ad-
024            versaries.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Text-to-image (T2I) models (Rombach et al., 2022; LMU, b; mid; Inc.) dazzle us with their stunning
029
      performance and amazing creativity. However, ethical issues with T2I models regarding unsafe
030   content generation, like sexual-explicit, violent, and political images (Williams, 2023; Milmo, 2023;
031   McQueen, 2023; Hunter, 2023a;b), are also alarming. An unprotected T2I model can easily be
032   prompted to generate a large number of unsafe images. The Internet Watch Foundation discovered
033   that countless images of child sexual abuse produced by T2I models had been distributed on the
034   dark web (Milmo, 2023), causing potential sexual exploitation and sexual abuse (McQueen, 2023;
035   Hunter, 2023a;b). Therefore, shielding T2I models from being exploited for unsafe image generation
036   has significant research implications.
037   Existing defenses can be classified into two categories, i.e., content moderation (Li; LMU, a) and
038   model alignment (Schramowski et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2023). Content moderation aims to
039   detect and block unsafe input prompts (Li) or output images (LMU, a). This is achieved mainly by
040   input filters, output filters, or both. However, the filters are usually external to the T2I model and
041   can be easily removed by white-box adversaries at the code level (Reddit, 2022). Model alignment
042   aims to fine-tune the T2I model to eliminate its learned unsafe concept (Schramowski et al., 2023;
043   Gandikota et al., 2023). Though being internally resistant to unsafe content generation, safely-
044
      aligned models are easily corrupted by fine-tuning with a small number of unsafe images.
045   In this paper, we propose PATRONUS, a defensive framework that strengthens the diffusion and
046   decoder modules of a pre-trained T2I model. The design goal of PATRONUS is three-fold. (1) Re-
047   jection of unsafe content generation. The protected model should refuse to output unsafe content.
048   (2) Resistance to malicious fine-tuning. The protected model should refuse to output unsafe con-
049   tent even if the model is fine-tuned with unsafe samples. (3) Intact performance of benign content.
050
      The protected model should preserve the performance regarding benign content. The workflow of
      PATRONUS is illustrated in Figure 1.

053   not depend on input prompts and is more generalizable to unseen malicious prompts. Therefore, we

095

096

097
098   We summarize our theoretical and technical contributions as follows:
099
100           • We make the pioneer attempt to investigate and validate the feasibility of a defense against
101             white-box adversaries for T2I models. We innovatively apply the concept of non-fine-
102             tunable learning to the T2I scenario.
103
              • We design an inseparable content moderation mechanism that is prompt-independent. Ad-
104             ditionally, our approach can resist malicious fine-tuning within a given budget, imposing
105             significant costs on the adversary.
106
107           • We conduct extensive experiments to verify the effectiveness and robustness of PATRONUS
                against a diversity of adversarial prompts and malicious fine-tuning strategies.

      4.1   OVERVIEW
193
194   Key Idea: To tackle the limitations of existing defenses, which can be structurally removed or
195   disrupted by malicious fine-tuning, we develop PATRONUS. The intuition of PATRONUS contains
196   three aspects: 1) To achieve structurally inseparable output moderation, we embed the output filter
197   within the decoder. Specifically, we govern the decoder module, i.e., making it perform conditional
198   decoding based on the safety of the generated features. 2) To ensure the defensive performance
199   survives the adversary’s fine-tuning, we enhance the defended components, including the conditional
200
      decoder and the aligned diffusion, with non-fine-tunability. 3) We carefully control the defense
      process to exclude the involvement of attack prompts, achieving prompt-independent defense, which
201
      guarantees robustness against various unsafe prompts.
202
203   Overall Pipeline: Starting from a pre-trained T2I pipeline, we implement PATRONUS by fine-tuning
204   the decoder and the diffusion modules. First, we fine-tune a conditional decoder, which refuses to
205   decode unsafe features, to achieve an inseparable moderator. Then, we create a non-fine-tunable
206
      safety mechanism to enable the conditional decoder and the aligned U-Net to resist malicious fine-
      tuning. Additionally, we pay attention to benign performance preservation that encourages the model
207
      to review the knowledge of benign inputs. Figure 2 describes the pipeline of PATRONUS. We
208
      summarize the overall process of PATRONUS in Algorithm 1.

359   expects the fine-tuned model to still decode the unsafe images to smoothed zero vectors.
360   To solve this min-min problem, we utilize the pipeline from (Deng et al., 2024). In practice, we
361   repeat and alternate between inner and outer optimization: At the beginning of each iteration, let
362   M0 denote the decoder’s parameters. First, we use Xtune and strategy ϕ to fine-tune M0 and get
363   the resulting state M1 as
364                                        M1 = ϕ (M0 , Xtune ) .                                  (24)
365   Then, we use Xeval to evaluate M1 ’s performance and calculate the fine-tuning-resistance loss Lr
366   that measures the discrepancy between the current performance and the desired ones, e.g., outputting
367   zeros when taking the unsafe features as inputs.
368   Finally, we update M0 with this loss by doing
369
370                                    θ0 ← θ0 − η · ∇θ0 Lftr (M1 , Xeval ),                            (25)
371
      where θ0 is the parameters of M0 and η is the learning rate of the outer optimization.
372
373   To save the memory requirements, we turn to first-order approximation (Finn et al., 2017) and update
      M0 as follows
                                   θ0 ← θ0 − η · ∇θ1 Lftr (M1 , Xeval ),

376   That is, we use the gradients with respect to M1 to approximate the gradients with respect to M0 .
377   Note that the true update to the model is implemented in Equation 26, while the updation between
      M0 and M1 is merely for calculating Lr and does not modify the model’s existent parameters.

379   data.
380
381   Mixed Sampling Strategy: To improve PATRONUS’s robustness against different fine-tuning pro-
382
      cesses, we propose a mixed sampling strategy for the inner loop. To be specific, we construct a bag
      of fine-tuning strategies containing various optimizers, learning rates, batch sizes, fine-tune sizes,
383
      and iteration numbers. Each time, we sample a fine-tuning strategy for the inner loop.
384
385   The focus of the bag of fine-tuning strategies is on the selection of the optimizer. To ensure effi-
386   ciency and effectiveness, we include two optimizers in the inner optimization, i.e., SGD (Robbins
387   & Monro, 1951) and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015). These two optimizers have complementary dy-
388
      namic characteristics, i.e., SGD is better at escaping local optima, while Adam is better at escaping
      saddle points (Xie et al., 2022). By resisting these two optimizers in the outer optimization, we are
389
      able to move our defense model to a state that is difficult to escape and performs poorly on unsafe
390
      data.
391
392   For other super-parameters like learning rates and batch sizes, we include all commonly used ranges.
393   For fine-tuning size and iteration number, we sample from an excessive range for what is normally
394
      required for fine-tuning. We refer to the Appendix G for detailed instantiations of non-fine-tunable
      decoders and diffusion modules.

884   the gradient (separated from the gradients used by the optimizer) for each single task ∇Li and finds

      the weighting coefficients α1 ..αk that minimize the sum

      In each iteration of non-fine-tunability enhancement, we obtain Lftr and Lbpp , then we calculate γ
      and λ to strike a balance between Lftr and Lbpp , ensuring that the two tasks i.e., the non-fine-tunable
