009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphs typically exhibit distinctive structure and domain-specific knowledge, mo-
012            tivating the development of a Graph Foundation Model (GFM) capable of gen-
013            eralizing across various graphs and tasks. While recent efforts have focused on
014            combining the strengths of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Graph Neural Net-
015            works (GNNs), they often struggle to maximize mutual benefit due to the decoupled
016            architectures. Moreover, existing methods assign out-of-vocabulary (OOV) tokens
017
               to nodes, which are incompatible with the natural language vocabulary for task-
               oriented prompt generation, hindering knowledge transfer in GFM. In this paper,
018
               we introduce PromptGFM, a versatile GFM grounded in graph vocabulary learning,
019
               comprising two key components: (1) Graph Understanding Module, which explic-
020            itly replicates the finest GNN workflow in the language space using LLMs, enabling
021            seamless GNN-LLM integration and elegant graph-text alignment; (2) Graph In-
022            ference Module, where we establish a novel language-based graph vocabulary to
023            ensure expressiveness, transferability, and scalability. This vocabulary enables the
024            generation of readable instructions for LLM inference, resolving modality incom-
025            patibility and facilitating positive transfer. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
026            superiority of PromptGFM in node classification and link prediction, along with its
027            strong transferability across different datasets and tasks. The code is available at
028
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PromptGFM.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
      Graphs, representing intricate and complex relationships between nodes, are ubiquitous across various
032   real-world domains, including citation networks (Eto, 2019; Hu et al., 2020; Buneman et al., 2021),
033   social networks (Kempe et al., 2003; Myers et al., 2014), and molecular graphs (Wieder et al.,
034   2020; Jin et al., 2024). These graphs typically exhibit distinctive non-Euclidean data structures
035   while embody essential domain-specific knowledge. In this context, most graph learning approaches
036   necessitate individual training and deployment tailored to specific datasets or tasks. To overcome
037   this limitation, we aim to build a Graph Foundation Model (GFM) capable of generalizing across
038   different graphs and tasks (Mao et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024).
039   With the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs), significant efforts have been dedicated to
040   harnessing their powerful understanding and inference capabilities alongside traditional Graph Neural
041   Networks (GNNs) to tackle broad challenges in graph machine learning. As Figure 1 shows, existing

      studies focus on modeling text-attributed graphs, which can be summarized as follows: (1) GNN
055   for LLM. GNNs produces structure-aware embeddings that enhance original textual embeddings,
056   boosting LLM inference (Tang et al., 2024; Chai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). (2) LLM for
057   GNN. LLMs contribute additional node features or labels derived from textual data, supervising the
058   training of GNN predictions (Chen et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhu et al., 2024). Nonetheless,
059   concurrent loosely decoupled architectures struggle to maximize the advantages of both GNNs and
060   LLMs simultaneously, resulting in suboptimal graph-text alignment.
061   Recently, a noteworthy trend has emerged toward implementing LLM as GNN, where LLMs function
062   as GNNs to capture graph semantics and structure. A few studies design structure verbalizers to
063   convert graph structures into code-like or heuristic prompts, enabling LLMs to comprehend and
064   encode the graph through text (Ye et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). However, we
065   argue there are currently no genuine instances of this category. As Figure 2(a) shows, the essence of
066   a true GNN lies in its message-passing paradigm, where each layer involves key components such as
067   neighbor sampling, aggregation, update, and optimization (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al.,
068   2017). By stacking multiple GNN layers, structure-less embeddings are gradually transformed into
069
      structure-rich embeddings, capturing higher-order signals. Motivated by the absence of essential
      properties of GNNs, a critical challenge arises: Can we leverage LLMs to faithfully replicate GNNs
070
      to capture both graph semantics and structures simultaneously?
071
072   Even worse, existing models are all confined to a shared embedding space. They intuitively assign
073   each node in the graph as an out-of-vocabulary (OOV) token, relying on ID-based embeddings for
074   downstream graph-specific tasks (Tang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024). Unfortunately, these graph
075
      embeddings are inherently incompatible with language-based token embeddings due to mismatch
      vocabularies, leading to semantic discrepancies when constructing natural language instructions for
076
      LLM inference. More importantly, this incompatibility makes it challenging for this graph-specific
077
      knowledge to transfer or scale to other graphs and tasks. To achieve positive transfer, an urgent
078   challenge emerges: Can we replace the OOV tokens with compatible node representations to build
079   versatile graph foundation model?

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
136
137
      GNN-LLM Integration. LLMs have unlocked unprecedented potential for graph machine learning,
138
      inspiring the integration between GNNs and LLMs. (1) GNN for LLM. A common approach employs
139   graph encoders to capture graph structure, aiding LLMs in comprehensive graph understanding. Many
140   models, like GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2024) and GraphLLM (Chai et al., 2023), and GraphPrompter
141   (Liu et al., 2024b), use GNNs or graph transformers as structure tokenizers, enabling synergistic
142   fine-tuning with LLMs. Despite their prevalence, challenges persist in effectively coordinating the
143   architecture and co-training of GNNs and LLMs. (2) LLM for GNN. Another approach harnesses
144   LLMs to provide additional labels and features for GNN. In practice, LLM-GNN (Chen et al.,
145   2024c), GraphEdit(Guo et al., 2024), and OpenGraph (Xia et al., 2024) leverage LLMs to generate
146   node-level and edge-level labels, addressing data sparsity issues. OFA (Liu et al., 2024a), ENGINE
147
      (Zhu et al., 2024), and TAPE (He et al., 2024) further direct LLMs to produce additional features
      and explanations to overcome semantic deficiencies. Nevertheless, a notable drawback lies in the
148
      heavy reliance on the instructions design and the quality of LLM-generated content, which inevitably
149
      introduces noise and negatively impacts performance. (3) LLM as GNN. In this paradigm, LLMs
150   themselves are designed to function as GNNs. Existing efforts, such as LLaGA (Chen et al., 2024a),
151   InstructGLM (Ye et al., 2024), and InstructGraph (Wang et al., 2024), employ structure verbalizers
152   to convert graph structures into code-like or heuristic prompts for LLM inference. However, they
153   struggle to capture high-order connections without a true GNN mechanism, thus not fully functioning
154   LLMs as GNNs. Overall, the current decoupled integration of LLMs and GNNs fails to fully exploit
155   the strengths of both architectures. This limitation motivates us to propose a new paradigm where
156   LLMs function as GNNs at every level, maximizing their synergistic potential.
157   Graph-Text Alignment in Embedding Space. Existing graph-text alignment preserves features from
158   different modalities, while facilitates their coordination in the embedding space. With graph encoders
159   as prefixes, some studies actively align graph-aware embeddings with LLMs and finetune them
160   alongside language-based embeddings, such as G-Prompt (Huang et al., 2023) and GraphAdapter
161   (Huang et al., 2024). An alternative way is to derive distinctive features via a two-tower architecture
      and apply alignment techniques for mutual benefits, such as contrastive learning (Li et al., 2023a;


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Brannon et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024), iterative training (Zhao et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024), and
163   knowledge distillation (Mavromatis et al., 2023). Nevertheless, these strategies bridge modality
164   gaps by mapping data to a shared embedding space, but they face challenges in terms of flexibility,
165   transferability, and scalability when dealing with natural language tokens from task-specific templates.
166
      Graphs Foundation Models. A GFM aims to enable transferability across different datasets and
167
      tasks (Liu et al., 2023). Observations have shown the key challenge lies in finding a graph vocabulary,
168
      identifying transferable units to encode invariance on graphs (Mao et al., 2024). Specifically,
169   GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2024) assumes a unique ID for each node, creating a dataset-specific
170   vocabulary. MoleBERT (Xia et al., 2023) defines a molecular graph vocabulary by converting
171   atomic properties into chemically meaningful codes. Despite their success, ID-based vocabularies are
172   typically domain-specific, lacking in-context learning capabilities and exhibiting poor transferability
173   across different domains.. Recently, significant efforts (Fatemi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Zhao
174   et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024a) have been dedicated to effectively understanding and inferring graphs
175   in a natural language format, but none of them attempts to extend a language-based graph vocabulary
176   to fully leverage the inherent transferability of natural language. In this paper, we aim to build an
177
      expressive graph vocabulary using natural language tokens, which means to represent each node
      with a finite sequence of language tokens. Our work thoroughly resolves the semantic discrepancy
178
      between graph and text, advancing the development of a versatile GFM.

234   4.1   G RAPH U NDERSTANDING M ODULE
235
236   The graph understanding module aims to generate expressive representations for each node within
237   the graph, supporting the subsequent graph inference module. The key to this task lies in effectively
238   capturing and aligning the semantic and structural information, where LLMs and GNNs offer distinct
239   advantages (Li et al., 2023b; Ren et al., 2024). However, the decoupled nature between GNNs and
240
      LLMs leads to potential semantic discrepancy and information loss. In this module, we propose
      a prompt-based GNN by functioning LLM as GNN, where core GNN operations are faithfully
241
      replicated within natural language space using LLMs.
242
243   GNN Replication with LLMs. Our priority is to design appropriate prompts that reflect the GNN
244   workflow and guide LLMs to execute them. This requires considering three aspects: (1) Graph
245   Representation: How can we effectively convey the node features and local structure to LLMs? (2)
246
      Graph Structure: How can we achieve message passing to capture the global structure? (3) Graph
      Semantics: How can we refine the core semantics to produce concise yet meaningful representations?
247

      The primary role of the graph inference module in our PromptGFM is to acquire transferable open-
305   world knowledge through instruction fine-tuning with LLMs, making it adaptable to different graphs
306   and tasks. Current methods treat nodes as OOV tokens and merge them with task-oriented templates
307   to form instructions. However, distinct vocabularies across modalities create incompatibility between
308   graph and language-based embeddings, leading to semantic discrepancies and limiting the transfer of
309   graph-specific knowledge. In response, we propose a novel language-based graph vocabulary that
310   resolves this incompatibility and enables readable and coherent instructions for LLM inference.

336   in the vocabulary have captured rich semantic and structural information from an open-world setting.
337   (2) Transferability. Like natural language vocabulary, our graph vocabulary also shares natural
338   language tokens as fundamental units, enabling direct transfer across different graphs and tasks. (3)
339   Scalability. Any node, whether previously seen or not, can be comparable to existing nodes via its
340
      language-based ID, effectively resolving the semantic discrepancy of OOV tokens.

359   Constrained Decoding with Prefix Tree Search. Generating candidate neighbors in link prediction
360   may cause LLM hallucination issues. In response, we introduce a constrained decoding method using
361
      a prefix tree search strategy to regulate LLM outputs (Cao et al., 2021; Tan et al., 2024). Specifically,

362
      node equals to a natural language token. Each unique path from the root to a leaf corresponds to the
363
      language-based ID of a node. During autoregressive generation, each new token is constrained by the
364   previous tokens to follow a valid path in the prefix tree. This ensures that the prediction is associated
365   with an actual graph nodes, effectively preventing hallucinations. This success is largely due to the
366   discrete language-based IDs, further demonstrating the flexibility of the proposed graph vocabulary.
367
      Generalization of Graph Foundation Model. We employ prompt-based GNN to directly propagate
368
      textual representations within a graph, capturing semantic and structural information. By decoupling
369
      these nodes from their original graphs, we establish a universal graph vocabulary. To develop a
370   versatile GFM, we can extract nodes and generate instructions from different graphs. Thus, we can
371   co-train across graphs and tasks by fine-tuning a unified LLM. This approach enables the acquisition
372   of open-world global knowledge and inclusive accommodation of any unseen graphs or tasks.

506   5.3    A BLATION AND E XPLORATION S TUDIES
507
508   Ablation Studies. To investigate the contributions of each module, we design the following variants:
509   (1) w/o understanding. This variant removes the prompt-based GNN and uses only the summarized
510
      textual representations of nodes for LLM fine-tuning. (2) w/o inference. This variant retains the GNN
      replication but performs LLM-based inference without additional fine-tuning. (3) w/o both. This
511
      variant relies solely on the initial textual summaries and uses pre-trained LLMs for inference without
512
      any GNN integration or fine-tuning. Figure 6 illustrates the node classification accuracy of these
513   variants in Cora, Citeseer, and PubMed, with our full model consistently outperforming in all settings.
514   First, the decrease in w/o understanding variant suggests that the absence of GNN replication results
515   in the loss of crucial semantic and structural information. Second, the w/o inference variant exhibits a
516   significant decline, highlighting the critical role of fine-tuning in integrating specific knowledge into
517   the pre-trained model. Lastly, the w/o both variant yields the worst results, underscoring the synergy
518   between the understanding and inference modules for overall performance.
519   Hyperparameter Sensitivity. We explore the impact of the number of layers in our prompt-based
520   GNN. As shown in Figure 5, we can observe that PromptGFM progressively improves as the layer
521   increases due to its ability to capture broader context and higher-order relationships over the graph.
522   However, after a certain point, further stacking layers results in diminishing returns or even perfor-
523   mance degradation due to over-smoothing, where node representations become indistinguishable
524   within their local structures. This trend is consistent with traditional GNNs. Optimal performance is
525   achieved with 3-layer GNN for Cora, while Citeseer reaches its best results with two layers. This
526   analysis suggests that textual representations can be propagated over the graph similarly to numerical
527
      embeddings, effectively capturing semantic and structural information simultaneously.
528   Case Study. To provide further insights, we provide a case study showing the layer-by-layer
529   refinement of textual representations in Appendix F.1. Additionally, since the nodes represent
530   research papers, we compare their language-based IDs with key words from the papers, showing the
531   superiority of PromptGFM in capturing essential meanings. Details can be found in Appendix F.2 .

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534   We present PromptGFM, a graph foundation model grounded in graph vocabulary learning. By
535   replicating the GNN workflow within the language space, we decouple the refined textual repre-
536   sentations of nodes and establish an expressive and universal graph vocabulary. This vocabulary
537   endows compatibility and scalability with natural language, enabling seamless transferability across
538   graphs and tasks. Experiments demonstrate superior overall performance and strong cross-graph and
539   cross-task generalization. Our research reveals the potential to function LLM as GNN and opens new
      avenues to build GFMs within the language space.

      B     BASELINES
919
920
      We provide detailed information on the baseline models, categorized into: (1) Graph-agnostic
921
      methods, (2) GNN-based methods, (3) Transformer-based methods, and (4) GNN-LLM integration
922   methods.
923
924
      B.1   G RAPH - AGNOSTIC METHODS .
925
926   • MF. This baseline is a matrix factorization approach with Bayesian personalized ranking as the
927   objective function. It learn dense embeddings to reconstruct the adjacent matrix. For both node
928   classification and link prediction, We adapt it to predict the labels of nodes or edges, respectively.
929
      • MLP. This method adopts a multi-layer perceptron to learn low-dimensional embeddings for each
930
      node. In our work, we randomly initialize node embeddings without textual attributes for both
931   graph-agnostic methos.
932
933
      B.2   GNN- BASED METHODS .
934
935   • GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017). This model introduces a neural network architecture that generalizes
936   convolution operations to graph-structured data, enabling effective semi-supervised learning by
937   aggregating feature information from a node’s local neighborhood.
938
      • GAT (Velickovic et al., 2017). This method incorporates attention mechanisms into graph neural
939
      networks, allowing nodes to assign different importance weights to their neighbors during feature
940   aggregation, which enhances performance by focusing on the most relevant connections.
941
942   • SAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017). GraphSAGE is an inductive representation learning framework on
943
      large graphs; it generates node embeddings by sampling and aggregating features from a node’s local
      neighborhood, facilitating generalization to unseen nodes or graphs.
944
945   • ReVGNN (Li et al., 2021). This method includes a recurrent graph neural network tailored for
946   dynamic graphs, capturing temporal dependencies by updating node representations as events occur
947   over time, which is crucial for modeling evolving graph structures.
948   • AGNN (Thekumparampil et al., 2018). AGNN leverages attention mechanisms to compute
949   attention coefficients based on node features, enabling the network to dynamically weigh the influence
950   of neighboring nodes without introducing additional parameters.
951
      • DNA (Fey, 2019). This work presents a flexible neighborhood aggregation method that dynam-
952
      ically selects and combines information from variable-sized node neighborhoods, enhancing the
953   expressive power and adaptability of graph neural networks.
954
955
      B.3   T RANSFORMER - BASED METHODS .
956
957   • SGFormer (Wu et al., 2023). This work introduces a transformer-based architecture designed for
958   graph data, integrating spectral graph theory into the transformer framework. It aims to capture both
959   local and global graph structures efficiently by incorporating spectral filters, enhancing the model’s
960   ability to learn complex graph representations.
961
      • NodeFormer (Wu et al., 2022). This framework presents a scalable graph transformer model
962   that utilizes a randomized attention mechanism to approximate full attention on graphs. By reducing
963   computational complexity, it enables efficient learning on large-scale graphs while preserving the
964   expressiveness of transformer architectures.
965
966
      B.4   GNN-LLM INTEGRATION METHODS .
967
968   • LLaGA (Chen et al., 2024a). This model effectively integrates LLM capabilities to handle the
969   complexities of graph-structured data. It transforms graph nodes into structure-aware sequences and
970   maps them into token embedding space using a specialized projector. LLaGA excels in generalization
971   and interpretability, performing strongly across various datasets and tasks. It also supports zero-shot
      learning, making it highly adaptable for unseen datasets.

       • OFA (Liu et al., 2024a). This paper proposes a framework that handles various graph classification
973    tasks across different domains using a single model. It introduces the nodes-of-interest (NOI)
974    subgraph mechanism to standardize different tasks with a single task representation. Additionally,
975    a novel graph prompting paradigm to leverage in-context learning and apply the same architecture
976    across diverse graph classification tasks, achieving generalization across multiple domains.

       • GraphPrompter (Liu et al., 2024b). This work introduces a novel framework designed to align
