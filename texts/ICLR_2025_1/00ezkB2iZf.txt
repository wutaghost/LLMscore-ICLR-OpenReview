011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLM) have achieved impressive performance on medical question-
014            answering benchmarks. However, high benchmark accuracy does not imply robust per-
               formance in real-world clinical settings. Medical question-answering benchmarks rely on
015
               assumptions consistent with quantifying LLM performance but that may not hold in the open
016            world of the clinic. Yet LLMs learn broad knowledge that could help the LLM perform in
017            practical conditions regardless of unrealistic assumptions in celebrated benchmarks. We
018            seek to quantify how robust LLM medical question-answering benchmark performance is to
019            violations of unrealistic benchmark assumptions. Specifically, we present an adversarial
               method that we call MedFuzz (for medical fuzzing). MedFuzz attempts to modify benchmark
020
               questions in ways aimed at confounding the LLM. We demonstrate the approach by targeting
021            unrealistic assumptions about patient characteristics presented in the MedQA benchmark.
022            Successful “attacks" modify a benchmark item in ways that would be unlikely to fool a
023            medical expert but nonetheless “trick" the LLM into changing from a correct to an incorrect
024            answer. Further, we present a non-parametric test for calculating the statistic significance
               of a successful attack. We show how to use calculate “MedFuzzed" performance on a
025
               medical QA benchmark, as well to find individual cases of statistically significant successful
026            attacks. The methods show promise at providing insights into the ability of an LLM to
027            operate robustly in more realistic settings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Cutting-edge large language models (LLMs) have attained human competitive performance on
033   medical question and answering benchmarks Singhal et al. (2022; 2023); Nori et al. (2023a;b);
034   Thirunavukarasu et al. (2023). Implicit in this success is the possibility that LLMs might be employed
035   to provide valuable decision support on real-world clinical cases. However, as discussed in Nori
036   et al. (2023a), strong performance on benchmarks does not mean the models will necessarily perform
037   well and provide value to clinicians in practice. One approach to exploring how LLMs might
038   perform in more complex real-world situations is via studies of robustness—the ability for the
039
      model to maintain its benchmark performance in practical conditions that differ from those present
      in the benchmark. Creating a medical question-answering benchmark requires making unrealistic
040
      simplifying assumptions Raji et al. (2021) that reduce complex real-life clinical situations into
041
      canonical multiple choice questions. Thus, these benchmarks are not suited for quantifying how well
042   these models perform in those real-life settings when those assumptions fail. Nonetheless, anecdotal
043   evidence shows that advanced LLMs can handle complex real-life cases Holohan (2023), possibly
044   by drawing from a variety of health and medically relevant information in training data, including
045   medical and scientific pedagogy, journal articles, and health-related conversations in online content.
046   The challenge is finding approaches for quantifying that “beyond-the-benchmark" performance.
047
      We introduce MedFuzz, an automatic red-teaming approach to testing the robustness of medical
048   question-answering benchmarks in more complex challenges. MedFuzz borrows from fuzzing in
049   software testing and cybersecurity, a method that adversarially feeds unexpected data to a target
050   system to “break" it, thereby surfacing its failure modes. In “MedFuzz", an attacker LLM attempts to
051   modify items in the benchmark in ways that “break" a target LLM’s ability to answer those items
052   correctly but that would not confound a human medical expert. The attacker LLM’s modifications
053   are constrained to specifically violating assumptions underlying the benchmark that we expect not to
      hold up in the clinic.

064   2     BACKGROUND
065
066   In this section, we review several key concepts in training and deploying LLMs for answering
067   challenging test questions in medicine, highlight their implications to their use in richer, open-world
068   scenarios, and discuss how “MedFuzz" builds on prior work in these areas.
069
070   2.1   LLM PERFORMANCE ON M EDICAL Q UESTION -A NSWERING
071
072   Driven by the promise of impact in healthcare, medical question-answering remains a key task for
073
      evaluating LLMs. Several medical-question answering benchmarks have emerged for statistical
      evaluation of LLM performance Hendrycks et al. (2020); Pal et al. (2022a); Jin et al. (2019). Some
074
      medical question-answering benchmarks are derived from medical entrance and licensing exams,
075
      such as MedMCQA Pal et al. (2022b) and MedQA Pal et al. (2022a). MedQA, for example, is based
076   the US Medical Licensing Exam (USMLE) Jin et al. (2021). Such benchmarks are interesting to
077   consider from the point of generalizing to the clinic, as medical licensing exam items are designed to
078   evaluate a would-be clinician’s ability to reason through clinical decision-making problems Billings
079   et al. (2021). MedQA items typically start with vignette that describes a patient presentation in a
080   clinical scenario, then prompt the test-taker to select from multiple choice answers involving correct
081   interpretation of evidence, diagnosis, and appropriate treatment Jin et al. (2021) This manuscript
082   uses MedQA as an example, though MedFuzz can be applied to other medical question-answer
083   benchmarks with clinical implications.
084   Recent generations of proprietary LLMs have achieved great increases in accuracy on MedQA
085   relative to previous generations. For instance, Med-PaLM 2 (a medically fine-tuned version of PaLM
086   2) achieved 85.4% accuracy Pal et al. (2022a) on MedQA, in contrast to Flan-PaLM (a medically
087   fine-tuned version of the earlier PaLM 580B), which achieved 67.6% accuracy. Singhal et al. (2022).
088   GPT-4 without fine-tuning and various prompt engineering techniques achieved 90.2% on MedQA
089   Nori et al. (2023b) (the highest reported performance at the time of writing), which stands in contrast
090
      to GPT-3.5’s accuracy of 60.2% Liévin et al. (2022). Recently, a variety of fine-tuned open source
      models have also achieved accuracy superior to GPT-3.5 and Flan-PaLM Ura et al. (2024). Superior
091
      results often rely on prompt engineering techniques such as in-context learning (ICL) Brown et al.
092
      (2020), chain-of-thought prompting (CoT) Wei et al. (2022), and ensembling Wang et al. (2023);
093   Pal et al. (2022a); Nori et al. (2023b). In MedFuzz, we have the target LLM deploy these prompt
094   engineering methods.

      2.2   A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
097
098   Our work builds on prior studies of adversarial robustness Schmidt et al. (2018); Goodfellow et al.
099   (2014); Tsipras et al. (2018); Chao et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2023), which study how intentional per-
100   turbations to features cause the model to produce incorrect or misaligned classifications, predictions,
101   or generated artifacts. MedFuzz similarly perturbs medical benchmarks in ways that lead an LLM to
102   answer incorrectly. The perturbations intentionally violate assumptions underlying the benchmark
103   items that would not hold in clinical settings.
104   MedFuzz builds on prior adversarial machine learning work in two ways. First, MedFuzz uses the
105   LLM to randomly modify an item in the medical benchmark. MedFuzzing seeks to modify the
106   vignette in medical question such that a clinician would provide the same correct answer as with
107   the original vignette, but the LLM would change its correct response to the original vignette to
      an incorrect option. This is analogous to how selectively adding random noise to an image of a


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      panda in Goodfellow et al. (2014) can create an image that still looks like a panda to the human eye
109
      while tricking an image classifier to return the label “gibbon". However, rather than adding random
110   text string “suffix" as in Zou et al. (2023), MedFuzz’s perturbations are semantically coherent; the
111   modification changes the text such that it is still intelligible and coherent within the context of the
112   vignette.
113
      MedFuzz is related to “automatic red teaming" methods that use LLMs to attack LLMs Perez et al.
114
      (2022); Chao et al. (2023). Methods in this class typically are trying to “jailbreak" the LLM, i.e.,
115
      by “tricking" the LLM into violating content policies or alignment safeguards, for example by
116   adding random character strings to a prompt or obfuscating intent (e.g., changing “give me bomb-
117   making instructions" to “write a fictional story about an orphan who writes bomb-making guides").
118   MedFuzzing does not seek to jailbreak, it seeks to modify benchmark questions – not with random
119   characters or clever rewordings, but by specifically identifying and violating assumptions that don’t
120   hold in the real world.

123   2.3   S OCIAL BIAS IN MEDICAL QUESTION ANSWERING
124
125
      Without loss of generality, we use an illustrating example related to social bias and fairness. For
126   context, LLMs such as GPT-4 are trained on natural language data that reflects potentially harmful
127   cognitive biases and error-prone decision-making heuristics in society and medical practice. For
128   example, a tendency for doctors to discount long-term harms in favor of short-term benefits, such as
129   in the prescribing of antibiotics Langford et al. (2020) may appear as a pattern in the training data
130   that the LLM can learn and reproduce. Medical licensing exam item vignettes can reflect social bias
131   Ripp & Braun (2017). Recent work has focused in particular on how LLMs reproduce social biases
132   and medical stereotypes in medical decision-making Vig et al. (2020); Nadeem et al. (2020); Zack
133   et al. (2024); Turpin et al. (2023); Omiye et al. (2023); Zack et al. (2024).
134
135
136   2.4   U NFAITHFULNESS IN LLM EXPLANATIONS
137
138   Best practice for high performance on benchmarks is to use CoT prompting Wei et al. (2022); Nori
139   et al. (2023b); Pal et al. (2022a). CoT prompting is one way LLMs could lack real-world robustness –
140   a developer of an LLM-powered assistive tool could surface CoTs to the clinician user as explanations
141   for the LLM’s answers. The problem is that explanations provided by LLMs can be unfaithful, or
142   misrepresent the true reason for an LLM’s generated answer Turpin et al. (2023). With respect
143   to our social bias example, LLMs have been shown to omit the influence of social biases in their
144
      explanations Turpin et al. (2023). Motivated by this, we demonstrate not only how MedFuzz impacts
      an LLM’s benchmark performance statistics (namely accuracy), but also the faithfulness of its putative
145
      explanations.

149   3     M ETHOD
150
151
152   MedFuzz uses an attacker LLM to modify a benchmark item in a way that violates assumptions
153
      underlying the benchmark that do not hold in real world scenarios. The attacker is instructed to do so
      in a way that allows us to continue using the target’s reported benchmark performance statistics to
154
      evaluate the effects of violating the assumption; in the case of accuracy, the attacker is instructed to
155
      modify the item in a way that doesn’t change the correct answer. The target LLM is then prompted to
156   provide a correct answer to the modified item. The attacker and the target can be different LLMs
157   or two instances of the same LLM – i.e., an LLM can attack itself (using separate sessions for the
158   attacker LLM and target LLM). The attacker knows the correct answer and iteratively attempts to
159   introduce modifications that confound the target, using the target’s history of CoT and other outputs
160   to find effective modifications. The target has no awareness of the attacker nor any previous iterations,
161   it is only ever presented with a benchmark item (either the original item from the benchmark or an
      item modified by the attacker).

      3.1.1    S TEP 1: S ELECT WHICH BENCHMARK ASSUMPTIONS TO VIOLATE
187
188   Step 1 of the MedFuzz workflow is to target key assumptions entailed by the benchmark that don’t
189   hold in clinical settings. MedFuzz will attempt to rephrase the benchmark items in ways that violate
190   these assumptions, then evaluate the target LLM’s ability to answer the modified items despite the
191   violation.
192   Consider our social bias case study. MedQA is derived from the USMLE. The National Board of
193   Medical Examiners (NMBE), who coauthors the USMLE, provides guidelines on acceptable and
194   unacceptable use of patient characteristics (PCs) (details about a patient’s age, sex, gender identity,
195   disability, socioeconomic status, native language, country of origin, behavior, habits, occupation, etc.)
196   in USMLE items Billings et al. (2021). Specifically, the USMLE encourages use of PCs if they are
197   medically relevant, if they are useful as “distractors" (information that draw attention to incorrect
198   answer options), or if the are irrelevant but improve representation of patient populations across exam
199
      items. However, they explicitly prohibit combining PCs with other information that potentially appeal
      to prevalent social biases in answering the exam item, i.e., answering based on medically unfounded
200
      misconceptions or stereotypes about a patient population. For example, there is a documented trend
201
      of misdiagnosis of ischemic heart disease in women as menstruation-related pain van den Houdt et al.
202   (2024); Maas (2018); Crea et al. (2015) – these guidelines would thus discourage using “female" and
203   “is currently menstruating" as PCs in an exam item where ischemic heart disease were an answer
204   option.
205
      This social bias constraint on PCs certainly does not hold in real life – in real life, clinicians have to
206
      distinguish menstrual pain from ischemic heart disease. More generally, while a USMLE test-taker is
207
      “blind" to unmentioned PCs, a clinician could not help but observe them and potentially input them as
208   data in an LLM application used for clinical decision-making, perhaps believing the AI should not be
209   deprived of any data. Thus, performance on MedQA does not reveal the LLMs to avoid reproducing
210   social bias associated with certain PCs. This makes this PC constraint an ideal target for MedFuzz
211   attacks.
212
213   3.1.2    S TEP 2: P ROMPT ATTACKER LLM TO VIOLATE THESE ASSUMPTIONS
214
215   Next, we prompt the attacker LLM to rewrite the questions in the benchmark such that they violate
      the assumptions identified in Step 1.

      In our case study in 3.1, we prompted the attacker LLM to attack the PC assumptions by adding PC
217   information that shifts test taker’s attention to distractors only by appealing to medically unfounded
218   misconceptions or stereotypes held by the test-taker about those PCs. As a result, the attacker added
219   PCs in bold with the goal of shifting attention to distractors in C and D. It does so by potentially

221   in a way that favor more benign conditions in C and D relative to B) and provide poor nourishment
222   and alternative medicine to their child (which might exacerbate anemia-related symptoms in cases of
223   C and D). Further, while the PCs about family and regional history increase the chances of genetic
224   conditions favoring C and D, attempting diagnosis without results of tests that screen for these factors
225
      is also prone to bias. Judgments based on biases about who is likely to overuse health service,
      use alternative medicine, and have unverified genetic conditions could lead to misdiagnosis and
226
      inappropriate treatments and consequent harm, such as in this case given the condition in the correct
227
      option B is life-threatening.

229   The prompt must instruct the attacker further to constrain the modifications such that the performance
230   evaluation statistic can be applied after the attack and be compared to the reported statistic prior to
231
      the attack. In case of accuracy, we instruct the attacker to make modifications that do not change
      the correct answer to the question. For example, in the case study in 3.1, the attacker was instructed
232
      not to make modifications that would change the correct answer of B. As opposed to using an
233
      LLM to generate an entirely new benchmark, anchoring on the original benchmarks questions and
234   correct answers helps causally attribute post-attack benchmark performance to violations of the target
235   assumption.

262   3.1.4   S TEP 4: S TATISTICALLY VALIDATE INTERESTING CASES OF SUCCESSFUL ATTACKS
263
264   While Step 3 focuses on overall benchmark performance statistics, it often interesting and informative
265   to look at individual instances of successful attacks. Individual cases of successful attacks can provide
266

      successful attacks may have only been successful by random chance, rather than as a result of targeted

      violation of the assumptions.

      3.2   T HE M ED F UZZ ALGORITHM
271
272   The MedFuzz algorithm is a multi-turn process where the attacker LLM relies on feedback from
273   the target LLM to tailor the modifications to trick the target LLM into answering incorrectly. The
274   attacker LLM analyzes the target LLM’s CoTs produced in prior turns. In addition, the target LLM
275   provides the attacker with confidence scores on its answer options, allowing the attacker to compare
276   how modifications from previous turns have affected the target LLMs confidence, i.e., providing the
277   attack LLM with a pseudo-gradient that can help orient future attacks. When the attacker LLM fails
278
      to get the target LLM to change its answer in the previous turn, it produces a post-mortem analysis
      of why it failed, then produces a plan for what it will try next, prior to implementing that plan. The
279
      iterative attacks stop after the attacker succeeds in getting the target to change its answer, or it reaches
280
      a user-specified number of tries.

      3.3   LLM- POWERED NON - PARAMETRIC STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE TEST
301
302   Suppose a MedFuzz attack is successful. How do we make sure the success wasn’t a result of random
303   chance? For example, the target LLM could switch to the incorrect answer with the fuzzed prompt due
304   to having had low confidence in the correct answer to begin with. Alternatively, given we know that
305   adding random characters to a prompt can “jailbreak" an LLM, it is possibly the MedFuzz attacker’s
306   modifications in a successful attacker are merely behaving as random string that just happens to be
307   intelligible.
308   MedFuzz is focused on conditions that are likely to appear in the clinic but don’t align with the
309   simplifying assumptions of the benchmark. The above cases reveal a lack of robustness in the LLM,
310   they do not reflect conditions that are not likely to be reproduced in the clinic. So we developed a
311   novel statistical significance test that distinguishes between successes due to violations of targeted
312   assumptions and those due to clinically irrelevant randomness.
313   A key novel element of this statistical procedure is using the attacker LLM to generate a null
314   hypothesis distribution of control fuzzes. A control fuzz is a version of the successfully attacked
315   benchmark item with modifications that semantically mirror the successful modifications, but that
316   do not violate the targeted assumptions. Specifically, we prompt the LLM to generate a systematic
317   lexical substitution of the text of the successful modifications; i.e., it replaces the text of the original
318   “MedFuzz” with new control fuzz text that satisfies the following constraints.
319   Firstly, the control fuzz contains the same type of semantic information as the MedFuzz. This control
320   fuzz constraint address the concern that success is due to the subject matter of the modification
321   rather than the violation of the targeted assumptions. The second control fuzz constraint preserves
322   the syntactic structure, including the word length, of the text of the successful MedFuzz. As an
323   analogy, a good control to compare against a random string that successfully “jailbroke" an LLM
      would be another random string of equal length, applied to the same part of the prompt. This control

421   MedFuzzing accuracy over MedQA benchmark. In each experiment, we run the following
422   procedure 5 times. First, for each benchmark item, we pose the original exam item to the target LLM.
423
      Then, if the target LLM answers correctly, we run a MedFuzz attacks with K=5 iterations. Running
      this procedure five times yields five replicate attack trajectories for each question. Note that the
424
      modified questions generated across the five replicates are typically different. For a given replicate,
425
      the possible outcomes are (1) failed to answer original question correctly, (2) attack fails after K
426   attempts, (3) attack succeeds in K or less attempts, (4) an LLM error occurred. LLM errors occur
427   when the LLM gives an incoherent or unexpected answer or triggering the LLM’s content policy
428   constraints. For each question, we construct an ensemble five results corresponding to the outcome
429   of each replicate. We drop any cases of LLM errors, then average the remaining post-attack binary
430   outcome of 1 for correct/0 for incorrect answer. For our performance statistic, we calculate overall
431   post-attack benchmark accuracy by taking the weighted average of these averages, weighting by the
      number of items in the ensemble.

496   6     D ISCUSSION
497
498   We presented MedFuzz, an automatic red-teaming method that helps quantifies how robust LLMs
499   with high medical benchmark performance are to specific real world conditions that aren’t present
500   in the benchmark. We presented an algorithm where an attacker LLM that analyzes answers to
501
      benchmark questions by a target LLM and tries to modify the question in that violate the unrealistic
      assumptions and confound the target, while preserving the ability to evaluate and interpret the original
502
      benchmark performance statistic. We present a novel non-parameteric statistical test for quantifying
503
      the significance of successful attacks. Our results show how a drop in “post-attack" performance
504   elucidates lack of robustness to the targeted benchmark assumptions. We further showed how the
505   MedFuzz can demonstrate lack of robustness by revealing unfaithfulness of CoT.
506
507
      6.1   L IMITATIONS
508
509   MedFuzz doesn’t address the fundamental problem of contamination of training data by the bench-
510   marks themselves. Furthermore, not all benchmark assumptions that inhibit robustness can be tested
511   using MedFuzz.
512
513   6.2   F UTURE WORK
514
515   In future work, we hope to rigorously evaluate the convergence of post-attack accuracy. We also hope
516   to apply automatic red-teaming methods similar to MedFuzz in non-medical domains.

518   7     E THICS S TATEMENT
519
520
      MedFuzz should never be used to prove that a LLM is safe, fair, or reliable for a particular clinical
521   use case. It also is not meant to substitute for techniques that evaluate LLM performance directly in
522   the clinical context, such as direct comparisons between the LLM and the clinician on clinical tasks,
523   and quantitative and qualitative studies of clinicians using LLMs.
