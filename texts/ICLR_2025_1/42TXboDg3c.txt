011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Concept bottleneck models (CBM) have emerged as a promising solution to address
014            the lack of interpretability in deep learning models. However, recent researches
015
               on CBM prioritize task accuracy at the expense of interpretability, weakening
               their ability to accurately infer key concepts. This work addresses this trade-off
016
               by introducing the energy ensemble CBM (EE-CBM). The EE-CBM leverages
017
               an energy-based concept encoder to effectively extract concepts, overcoming the
018            information bottleneck common in conventional CBMs. Additionally, a novel
019            energy ensemble gate within the EE-CBM architecture efficiently combines energy
020            and concept probability to further address this bottleneck. Moreover, the EE-CBM
021            employs the maximum mean discrepancy loss to enhance concept discrimination
022            within the concept space and facilitate accurate concept inference. An experimental
023            evaluation on benchmark datasets (CUB-200-2011, TravelingBirds, AwA2, CheX-
024            pert, and CelebA) demonstrates that EE-CBM achieve state-of-the-art performance
025            in both concept accuracy and interpretability. This work positions the EE-CBM as
026
               a significant advancement in CBM researches, enabling them to effectively balance
               performance and interpretability for improved model transparency. Our code is
027
               available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/EE-CBM-F48D.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Model interpretation is increasingly important because of the opaque nature of deep learning models,
033   particularly in critical image-based domains such as healthcare and autonomous driving. Concept
034   bottleneck models (CBM) (Koh et al., 2020; Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2022; Yuksekgonul et al., 2023;
035   Chauhan et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Sarkar et al., 2022; Havasi et al., 2022) have emerged as a
036   solution to this challenge; their aim is to make the decision-making process of models transparent
037   by simplifying it into understandable concepts. CBM researches infer the key concepts used in the
038   prediction, and then predict the final label using only the inferred concepts, as shown in Fig. 1 (a).
039
      This approach significantly enhances the transparency of the model using concepts that humans can
      directly understand.
040
041   Early researches on CBM aimed to ensure model transparency, but this often resulted in accuracy
042   that was lower than that of black-box models. To address this issue, recent CBM researches have
043   seen a shift towards using large backbone networks and deep layers to achieve superior performance,
044   contrary to their original purpose. This trend sacrifices the transparency of the model, focusing
045
      solely on improving the accuracy of final label predictions. Therefore, it is imperative to develop
      algorithms that can bridge the performance gap with black-box models while maintaining model
046
      transparency, in line with the original objectives of CBM researches. Model interpretability refers to
047
      the ability of a model to provide human-understandable explanations for its predictions, ensuring
048   transparency in decision-making processes. In contrast, concept accuracy quantifies the correctness
049   of the intermediate concept representations inferred by the model. While these two aspects are
050   distinct, they are closely related. High concept accuracy enhances interpretability by ensuring that
051   the concepts used in explanations align with the ground truth. To address the trade-off between
052   accuracy and interpretability, concept embedding models (CEM) (Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2022)
053   has been proposed. CEM is a modified CBM network (Koh et al., 2020) that incorporate both
      positive and negative semantics, as shown in Fig. 1 (b). Coop-CBM (Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2023) enhanced CBM performance by employing an auxiliary loss to develop rich and expressive
055   concept representations for downstream tasks. Energy-based CBM (ECBM) (Xu et al., 2024) utilized
056   a collection of neural networks to establish the collective energy associated with candidate tuples
057   comprising input, concept, and class. Through this unified framework, tasks such as prediction,
058   concept refinement, and the assessment of conditional dependencies are expressed as conditional
059   probabilities derived from the integration of diverse energy functions. Recent prominent CBM studies
060   (Xu et al., 2024; Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou, 2023; Sarkar et al., 2022), have adopted an approach to
061   enhance model accuracy that incorporates x → c → y and x → y structures to learn the relationship
062   between final labels and concepts (Fig. 1 (c)). While these methods can improve label accuracy, often
063
      struggle to accurately infer concepts, a core goal of CBM research. This hinders model transparency.
      For example, while a CBM model may accurately diagnose pneumonia, it may fail to detect lung
064
      lesions, thereby undermining clinical trust.
065
066   In this study, we introduce a novel approach, the Energy Ensemble CBM (EE-CBM), which aims to
067   enhance the balance between inference accuracy and interpretability in concept learning, as depicted
068   in Fig. 1 (d). The proposed EE-CBM comprises two branch modules, concept extraction and concept
069
      probability. The concept extraction branch predicts concept values C through fully connected (FC)
      layers, similar to other CBM models. The concept probability branch generates probability P using
070
      an energy-based mechanism (LeCun et al., 2006), enhancing concept accuracy. This branch plays
071
      a pivotal role in determining the likelihood of each concept within the input data. It serves as
072   a probability estimator, assigning probability to each concept and reflecting their relevance and
073   contribution to the overall representation.
074
075
      To ensure robust concept inference even in challenging scenarios such as noisy or wild images,
      an EE-CBM integrates samples generated through Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (Nijkamp
076
      et al., 2020a; 2019; 2020b; Han et al., 2017) methods. The concept value C and probability P of
077
      each branch are combined in the energy ensemble gate (EEG) to generate the final concept. The
078   EEG alleviates potential information bottleneck issues in CBM researches. Furthermore, to promote
079   accurate concept learning, an EE-CBM applies the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) (Anderson
080   et al., 1994; Gretton et al., 2012) as a loss to each concept embedding. This loss function fosters
081   orthogonality between concept features, which brings similar concepts closer in latent space and
082   creating distinct spaces for different concepts, resulting in clearer concept inference outcomes. In
083   downstream tasks across various datasets, the EE-CBM effectively addresses balance the between
084   conceptual understanding and label prediction.
085   The main contributions of the proposed EE-CBM are as follows:
086
087          • We introduce EE-CBM, a new architecture that aims to balance task accuracy and inter-
088            pretability in concept learning and consists of two branches: the concept extraction branch
089
               and concept probability branch.
090          • The EEG combines the concept values and concept probabilities. This combination helps
091            address the potential information bottleneck issues present in conventional CBMs.
092          • To ensure robust concept inference, especially for challenging data such as noisy or wild im-
093            ages, the EE-CBM integrates samples generated through MCMC methods. This potentially
094            improves the ability of the model to handle complex data.
095          • The MMD loss function promotes the distinctiveness of concepts in latent space, bringing
096            similar concepts closer together and separating distinct concepts.
097          • The proposed EE-CBM has demonstrated excellent performance and model interpretability
098            through experiments conducted on various benchmark datasets such as CUB-200-2011
099            (Welinder et al., 2010), AwA2 (Xian et al., 2018), CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019), and CelebA
100            (Liu et al., 2015).

129   Concept labeling to reflect the detailed characteristics of objects may require expert knowledge, and

133

134
      parameters. Moreover, a semantic understanding of these models is difficult because heatmaps or
135
      other human-interpretable methods must be used to explain the concepts. Hence, initial CBM for
136   image classification faces a trade-off between classification accuracy and model interpretability. To
137   address this, several approaches have been proposed; however, existing methods (Sarkar et al., 2022;
138   Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou, 2023; Xu et al., 2024) tend to rely on large backbone networks to maintain
139   accuracy, thereby neglecting the primary goal of CBM, which is model interpretability. In this
140   study, we adopt energy-based models (EBM) to resolve this trade-off while maintaining the primary
141   objectives of CBM—accuracy and model interpretability—regardless of the size of the backbone
142   network.

205   3    EE-CBM
206
207
      The proposed EE-CBM consists of the components depicted in Fig. 2. The key element is the
208   concept encoder, which generates the final concept C and comprises two branches. The first branch,
209   called the concept extraction branch, predicts concept value C′ through FC layers as in conventional
210   CBM models. The second branch, called the concept probability branch, employs an energy-based
211   mechanism to determine the presence of each concept, producing probability P and enhancing
212   concept accuracy. The resultant C′ and P are combined into Ĉ. Subsequently, Ĉ together with z,
213   which is generated by the backbone network, is used to perform EEG to create the final concept C,
214   which avoids the concept information bottleneck. Finally, the inferred final label y is deduced via a
215   single FC layer. Additionally, we incorporate the MMD loss to ensure that the latent space of each
      concept remains similar and that each concept possesses orthogonal features.

311   To evaluate the performance of our proposed model, we conducted experiments using four datasets:
312   CUB-200-2011 (Welinder et al., 2010), TravelingBirds (Koh et al., 2020), AwA2 (Xian et al., 2018),
313   CheXpert (Irvin et al., 2019), and CelebA (Liu et al., 2015). The CUB-200-2011 dataset consists
314   of 11,788 images belonging to 200 categories of birds. It is divided into a training set of 5,994
315
      images and a test set of 5,794 images. Each image is annotated with one category label and 312
      attributes (concepts). We followed the approaches of CBM and CEM, utilizing 112 attributes as
316
      concepts and using the same data partitioning. The TravelingBirds, a segmented bird image dataset
317
      derived from CUB, offers a diverse range of background conditions. This dataset is categorized into
318   CUB Random, CUB Fixed, and CUB Black, and it is particularly useful for evaluating a model’s
319   ability to encode object-centric concepts while minimizing the impact of background variations. The
320   AwA2 dataset comprises 37,322 images of 50 animal categories, with 85 attributes. The CheXpert
321   dataset includes 224,316 chest radiographs from 65,240 patients, with two category labels and 13
322   attributes. The CheXpert dataset provides concept uncertainty labels, which were incorporated during
323   training to address ambiguous concepts effectively. The CUB-200-2011 and AwA2 datasets are
      widely used benchmarks for models using attributes, as they contain a relatively large number of

522   5    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
523
524
525
      This work introduced the EE-CBM, which effectively addresses the trade-off between conceptual
      understanding and label prediction in downstream tasks. The core concept of the EE-CBM lies in its
526
      leveraging of energy ensembles and concept probability to tackle the concept information bottleneck
527
      regradless of the backbone size. This approach enables the model to achieve a deeper grasp of
528   concepts. Furthermore, because it incorporates the MMD loss, the EE-CBM facilitates the formation
529   of a latent space in which similar concepts are positioned close together, whereas distinct concepts
530   are separated by a larger distance. The experimental results establish the EE-CBM as a promising
531   CBM because it achieves high concept accuracy and interpretability results across all datasets.
532
      It is encouraging that EE-CBM demonstrates consistent performance not only in model interpretability
533
      but also in task accuracy compared to existing complex black-box models. However, constructing
534   datasets that include concepts entails significant costs, and because of the limited concept resources
535   within datasets, there could be instances where the model fails to learn the concepts actually necessary
536   for training. To address this, future research must focus not only on improving task accuracy but also
537   on exploring concept-free models. Furthermore, although the proposed model benefits from using
538   MCMC for energy learning, which allows it to extract concepts well from images in the wild, it suffers
539   from the drawback of multiple iterations. Therefore, energy-efficient learning algorithms should be
      developed in future to overcome the limitations of the EE-CBM and enhance its performance.

      CUB (Welinder et al., 2010) dataset contains images of 200 bird species. The dataset consists of a
      total of 11,788 images, with 5,994 training images, and 5,794 testing images. Each image is labeled
883
      with 112 attributes, representing various characteristics of each bird species.
884
      CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) dataset is a facial image dataset consisting of a total of 202,599 images
885   from 10,177 celebrities. This dataset includes images taken in various situations, so each image has
886   different facial expressions, lighting, clothing, and so on. Each facial image in the dataset is labeled
887   with 40 attributes, representing various characteristics such as gender, eyeglasses, and hats. However,
888   some attributes have uncertain labeling. Therefore, as with CEM, the eight attributes with the highest

890   the total number of classes is about 240, and only six attributes were optimized using ground-truth
891   labels.

      contains 37,322 images across 50 animal classes, each annotated with 85 attribute labels.

894
      designed to facilitate research in medical image analysis and automated diagnosis. It contains
895
      224,316 chest radiographs from 65,240 patients, labeled for 14 common chest conditions such as
896   atelectasis, cardiomegaly, and pleural effusion. The dataset includes uncertainty labels to account
897   for ambiguous cases, with conditions annotated as positive, negative, or uncertain. CheXpert also
898   provides a standardized validation set with expert-annotated labels for model evaluation. The dataset
899   is split into training, validation, and test sets, enabling robust assessment of model performance.
900   TravelingBirds (Koh et al., 2020) dataset is a synthetic dataset derived from the CUB dataset, created
901   to assess the robustness of models under real-world conditions. By replacing the original backgrounds
902   of CUB images with a variety of diverse scenes, the TravelingBirds dataset introduces a level of
903   uncertainty that mimics the challenges faced by models deployed in real-world applications. This
904
      dataset is particularly useful for evaluating the generalization ability of models and their ability to
      handle variations in background complexity.

966   H    A PPENDIX : E XPLANATION OF EE-CBM’ S ROBUSTNESS TO BACKGROUND
967        S HIFTS
968
969   To provide further clarity on EE-CBM’s improved generalization to background shifts, we elaborate
970   on the mechanisms that enable this robustness. EE-CBM’s ability to focus on concept-specific
971   features rather than spurious correlations with background elements is a key factor in its performance.
      This is achieved through two critical components:


                                                            18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Energy-Based Pathway: The energy-based pathway estimates concept probabilities by capturing
973    the intrinsic properties of target concepts, effectively reducing reliance on background information.
974    This probabilistic approach ensures that the model focuses on meaningful features related to the task.
975
976
       MMD Loss: The Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) loss enforces structured separation in the
       concept space. It clusters similar concepts together while pushing distinct concepts apart, creating
977
       a latent space organization that maintains focus on primary object features, even under varying
978
       background conditions.
979
980    These components work together to enhance EE-CBM’s generalization ability, as evidenced by the
981    experimental results discussed in Section 4.1. This explanation provides a theoretical basis for the
982
       robustness of EE-CBM to background variations.

       I   A PPENDIX : D ETAILED E XPLANATION OF I NTERVENTION E XPERIMENTS
987
988
       Intervention experiments in concept bottleneck models involve modifying the model’s predicted
989    concept values to study their impact on both the final predictions and the individual concepts
990    themselves. This approach allows us to evaluate how effectively the model handles corrections to its
991    concept predictions, which can be especially relevant in applications requiring high interpretability,
992    such as medical diagnostics or environmental monitoring.
993
       In these experiments, interventions are applied during the test phase, where specific predicted
994
       concepts are replaced with their corresponding ground-truth values. This process simulates a scenario
995    in which users or domain experts identify and correct potentially inaccurate concept predictions. By
996    introducing these modifications, we assess how adjustments to one or more concept values influence
997    both downstream classification accuracy and related concepts.
998
       By focusing on test-time interventions, these experiments demonstrate the model’s robustness and
999
       responsiveness to corrections. This highlights the practical utility of concept-based interpretability in
1000
       refining predictions without altering the training process, emphasizing the value of this approach in
1001   real-world applications.
1002
1003   In the experiments, random concepts were selected with probabilities ranging between 0.0 and 1.0,
1004
       and their values were replaced with the corresponding ground-truth values. This methodology follows
       the same approach as used in CEM (Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2022) and ECBM (Xu et al., 2024),
1005
       ensuring consistency across the compared methods.
