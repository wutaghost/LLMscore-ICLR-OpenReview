011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            To answer complex queries on knowledge graphs, logical reasoning over incomplete
014            knowledge needs learning-based methods because they are capable of generalizing
015            over unobserved knowledge. Therefore, an appropriate dataset is fundamental to
016
               both obtaining and evaluating such methods under this paradigm. In this paper,
               we propose a comprehensive framework for data generation, model training, and
017
               method evaluation that covers the combinatorial space of Existential First-order
018
               Queries with multiple variables (EFOk ). The combinatorial query space in our
019            framework significantly extends those defined by set operations in the existing
020            literature. Additionally, we construct a dataset, EFOk -CQA, with 741 query types
021            for empirical evaluation, and our benchmark results provide new insights into how
022            query hardness affects the results. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the existing
023            dataset construction process is systematically biased and hinders the appropriate
024            development of query-answering methods, highlighting the importance of our work.
025            Our code and data are provided in https://anonymous.4open.science/
026            r/EFOK-CQA/README.md.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The Knowledge Graph (KG) is a powerful database that encodes relational knowledge into a graph rep-
032   resentation (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014; Suchanek et al., 2007), supporting downstream tasks (Zhou
033   et al., 2007; Ehrlinger & Wöß, 2016) with essential factual knowledge. However, KGs suffer from
034   incompleteness during their construction (Vrandečić & Krötzsch, 2014; Carlson et al., 2010; Libkin
035   & Sirangelo, 2009). The task of Complex Query Answering (CQA) proposed recently has attracted
036
      much research interest (Hamilton et al., 2018; Ren & Leskovec, 2020). This task ambitiously aims
      to answer database-level complex queries described by logical complex connectives (conjunction
037
      ^, disjunction _, and negation ␣) and quantifiers1 (existential D) (Wang et al., 2022; Ren et al.,
038
      2023; Leskovec, 2023). Currently, learning-based methods dominate the CQA task because they can
039   empirically generalize to unseen knowledge as well as prevent the resource-demanding symbolic
040   search.
041
042
      The thriving of learning-based methods also puts an urgent request on high-quality benchmarks,
      including datasets with comprehensive coverage of queries and sound answers, and fair evaluation
043
      protocol for learning-based approaches. In the previous study, datasets are developed by progressively
044
      expanding the syntactical expressiveness, where conjunction (Hamilton et al., 2018), union (Ren
045   et al., 2020), negation (Ren & Leskovec, 2020), and other operators (Liu et al., 2021) are taken
046   into account sequentially. In particular, BetaE dataset (Ren & Leskovec, 2020) contains all logical
047   connectives and becomes the standard training set for model development. A larger evaluation
048   benchmark EFO-1-QA (Wang et al., 2021) was proposed to systematically evaluate the combinatorial
049   generalizability of CQA models on such queries.
050
      However, the queries in previous datasets (Ren & Leskovec, 2020; Wang et al., 2021) are recently
051   justified as “Tree-Form” queries (Yin et al., 2024) as they rely on the tree combinations of set

055   2000) for database query processing, queries in existing CQA benchmarks (Ren & Leskovec, 2020;
056   Wang et al., 2021) have two common shortcomings: (1) lack of combinatorial answers: only
057   one variable is queried, and (2) lack of structural hardness: all existing queries subject to the

059   existing CQA data under such limited scope can support the future development of methodologies

061   The goal of this paper is to establish a new framework that addresses the aforementioned shortcomings

063   formally motivated by the well-established investigation of constraint satisfaction problems (Rossi

065   folds.
066

067           rigorous definitions, underscoring both combinatorial hardness and structural hardness
068           and extending the existing coverage (Wang et al., 2021) which covers only a subset of EFO1
069           query. The captured query family is denoted as EFOk where k stands for multiple variables.
070   Curated datasets We derive EFOk -CQA dataset, a enormous extension of the previous EFO-1-QA
071           benchmark (Wang et al., 2021) and contains 741 types of query. We design several systematic
072           rules to guarantee that our dataset includes high-quality nontrivial queries, particularly those
073           that contain multiple query variables and are not structure-based tractable.
074   Convenient implementation We implement the entire pipeline for query generation, answer sam-
075
              pling, model training and inference, and evaluation for the undiscussed scenarios of combi-
              natorial answers. Our pipeline is backward compatible, which supports both set operation-
076
              based methods and more recent ones.
077
      Results and findings We evaluate six representative CQA methods on our benchmark. Our results
078           refresh the previous empirical findings and further reveal the structural bias of previous data.

081   2   R ELATED WORKS
082
083
      Answering complex queries on knowledge graphs differs from database query answering by being a
084   data-driven task (Wang et al., 2022), where the incompleteness of the knowledge graph is addressed by
085   methods that learn from data. Meanwhile, learning-based methods enable faster neural approximate
086   solutions of symbolic query answering problems (Ren et al., 2023).
087
      The prevailing way is query embedding, where the computational results are embedded and computed
088
      in the low-dimensional embedding space. Specifically, the query embedding over the set operator
089
      trees is the earliest proposed (Hamilton et al., 2018). The supported set operators include projec-
090   tion(Hamilton et al., 2018), intersection (Ren et al., 2020), union and negation (Ren & Leskovec,
091   2020), and later on be improved by various designs (Zhang et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022). Such
092   methods assume queries can be converted into the recursive execution of set operations, which im-
093   poses additional assumptions on the solvable class of queries (Wang et al., 2021). These assumptions
094   introduce additional limitations of such query embeddings
095
      Recent advancements in query embedding methods adapt query graph representation and graph neural
096   networks, supporting atomics (Liu et al., 2022) and negated atomics (Wang et al., 2023). Query
097   embedding on graphs bypasses the assumptions for queries (Wang et al., 2021). Meanwhile, other
098   search-based inference methods (Arakelyan et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2024) are rooted in fuzzy calculus
099   and not subject to the query assumptions (Wang et al., 2021).
100
      Though many efforts have been made, the datasets of complex query answering are usually subject
101
      to the assumptions by set operator query embeddings (Wang et al., 2021). Many other datasets are
102   proposed to enable queries with additional features, see Ren et al. (2023) for a comprehensive survey
103   of datasets. However, only one small dataset proposed by (Yin et al., 2024) introduced queries and
104   answers beyond such assumptions (Wang et al., 2021). It is questionable that this small dataset is
105   fair enough to justify the advantages claimed in advancement methods (Wang et al., 2023; Yin et al.,
106   2024) that aim at complex query answering. The dataset (Yin et al., 2024) is still far away from the
107   systematical evaluation as proposed in Wang et al. (2021) and EFOk -CQA proposed in this paper
      fills this gap.

      3.3   T HE REPRESENTATION OF QUERY
188
189   To give an explicit representation of existential formula, operator tree (Hamilton et al., 2018) was
190   proposed to represent a formula, where each node represents the answer set for a sub-query, and
191   the logic operators in it naturally represent set operations. This method allows for the recursive
192   computation from constant entity to the final answer set in a bottom-up manner (Ren & Leskovec,
193   2020). We also provide full details of the operator tree and tree-form query in Appendix B. However,
194   this representation method is inherently directed, acyclic, and simple, therefore more recent research
195
      breaks these constraints by being bidirectional (Liu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) or being cyclic
      or multi graph (Yin et al., 2024). To meet these new requirements, they propose to represent the
196
      formula by the query graph (Yin et al., 2024), which inherits the convention of constraint network in
197
      representing CSP instance. We utilize this design and further extend it to represent EFOk formula
198   that contains multiple free variables. We provide the illustration and comparison of the operator tree
199   and the query graph in Figure 1, where we show the strong expressiveness of the query graph. We
200   also provide the formal definition of query graph as follows:
201
      Definition 10 (Query graph). Let γ be a conjunctive formula in equation 2, its query graph is defined
202   by Gpγq “ tph, r, t, tT/Fuqu, where an atomic formula rph, tq in γ corresponds to ph, r, t, Tq and
203   ␣rph, tq corresponds to ph, r, t, Fq.
204
205   Therefore, any conjunctive formulas can be represented by a query graph, in the rest of the paper, we
206   use query graphs and conjunctive formulas interchangeably.

      5.3       A NSWER FOR EXISTENTIAL FORMULA
325
326   As illustrated in Section 3.2, the answer to an existential formula can be solved by a CSP solver,
327   however, we also show in Definition 9 that solve it as CSP leads to huge computation costs. Thus,
328   we develop our own algorithm following the standard solving technique of CSP, which ensures
329   consistency conditions in the first step, and do the backtracking to get the final answers in the
330   second step. Finally, we select part of our sampled queries and double-check it with the CSP
331   solver https://github.com/python-constraint/python-constraint.

334
335
      As the query graph is an extension to the operator tree regarding the express ability to existential
      formulas, we are able to reproduce CQA models that are initially implemented by the operator tree
336
      in our new framework. Specifically, since the operator tree is directed and acyclic, we compute its
337
      topology ordering that allows for step-by-step computation in the query graph. This algorithm is
338   illustrated in detail in the Appendix E. Therefore, our pipeline is backward compatible.
339
340   Conversely, for the newly proposed models that are based on query graphs, the original operator
341
      tree framework is not able to implement them, while our framework is powerful enough. We have
      therefore clearly shown that the query graph representation is more powerful than the previous
342
      operator tree and is able to support arbitrary existential formulas as explained in Section 3.3.

      5.5       E VALUATION PROTOCOL
346   As we have mentioned in Section 3.1, there is an observed knowledge graph KG o and a full knowledge
347   graph KG. Thus, there is a set of observed answers Ao and a set of full answers A correspondingly.
348   Since the goal of CQA is to tackle the challenge of incompleteness, it has been a common practice
349   to evaluate CQA models by the “hard” answers Ah “ A ´ Ao (Ren et al., 2020; 2023). However,
350   to the best of our knowledge, there has not been a systematic evaluation protocol for EFOk queries,
351   thus we leverage this idea and propose three types of different metrics to fill the research gap in the
352   area of evaluation of queries with multiple free variables, and thus have combinatorial answers.
353   Marginal. For any free variable yi , its full answer is Ayi “ tai P E|pa1 , ¨ ¨ ¨ , ai , ¨ ¨ ¨ , ak q P Au,
354   the observed answer of it Ayoi is defined similarly. This is termed “solution projection” in CSP
355   theory (Greco & Scarcello, 2013) to evaluate whether the locally retrieved answer can be extended to
356   an answer for the whole problem. Then, we rank the hard answer Ayhi “ Ayi ´ Ayoi 4 , against those
357   non-answers E ´ Ayi ´ Ayoi and use the ranking to compute standard metrics like MRR, HIT@K for

      the k-length tuple and thus fails to find the correspondence among free variables.

363   the combinatorial answer by each k-length tuple pa1 , ¨ ¨ ¨ , ak q in the hard answer set Ah . Specifically,
364   we rank each ai in the corresponding node yi the same as the marginal metric. Then, we propose the
365   HIT@nk metric, it is 1 if all ai is ranked in the top n in the corresponding node yi , and 0 otherwise.
366   Joint. Finally, we note these metrics above are not the standard way of evaluation, which is based on
367   a joint ranking for all the E k combinations of the entire search space. We propose to estimate the

717
718   We explain the operator tree method, as well as the tree-form queries in this section, which is firstly
719   introduced in Yin et al. (2024). The tree-form queries are defined to be the syntax closure of the
720   operator tree method and are the prevailing query types in the existing datasets (Ren & Leskovec,
721   2020; Wang et al., 2021), see the definition below:
722   Definition 17 (Tree-Form Query). The set of the Tree-Form queries is the smallest set Φ such that:
723
724
              (i) If ϕpyq “ rpa, yq, where a P E, then ϕpyq P Φ;
             (ii) If ϕpyq P Φ, ␣ϕpyq P Φ;
725
            (iii) If ϕpyq, ψpyq P Φ, then pϕ ^ ψqpyq P Φ and pϕ _ ψqpyq P Φ;
726
            (iv) If ϕpyq P Φ and y 1 is any variable, then ψpy 1 q “ Dy.rpy, y 1 q ^ ϕpyq P Φ.
727
728   We note that the family of tree-form queries deviates from the targeted EFO1 query family (Yin
729   et al., 2024). The rationale of the definition is that the previous model relied on the representation
730   of “operator tree” which addresses logical queries to simulate logical reasoning as the execution
731   of set operators (Ren & Leskovec, 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022), where each node
732   represents a set of entities corresponding to the answer set of a sub-query (Yin et al., 2024). Then,
733   logical connectives are transformed into operator nodes for set projections (Definition 17 i,iv), com-
734   plement(Definition 17 ii), intersection, and union(Definition 17 iii) (Wang et al., 2021). Particularly,
735   the set projections are derived from the Skolemization of predicates (Luus et al., 2021). Therefore,
736
      the operator tree method that has been adopted in lines of research (Ren & Leskovec, 2020; Zhang
      et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022) is just a model that neuralizes these set operations: projection, comple-
737
      ment, intersection, and union. These different models basically only differ from each other by their
738
      parameterization while having the same expressiveness as characterized by the tree form query.
739
740   Specifically, the left side of the Figure 1 shows an example of the operator tree, where “Held” and
741   “Located” are treated as two projections, “N” represents set complement, and “I” represents set
742
      intersection. Therefore, the embedding of the root representing the answer set can be computed based
      on these set operations in a bottom-up manner (Ren & Leskovec, 2020).
743
744   Finally, it has been noticed that tree-form query is subject to structural traceability and only has
745   polynomial time combined complexity for inference while the general EFOk , or even EFO1 queries,
746   is NP-complete, with detailed proof in Yin et al. (2024). Therefore, this result highlights the
747   importance of investigating the EFOk queries as it greatly extends the previous tree-form queries.

1101
       In this section, we provide implementation details of CQA models that have been evaluated in our
1102
       paper. For query embedding methods that rely on the operator tree, including BetaE (Ren & Leskovec,
1103
       2020), LogicE (Luus et al., 2021), and ConE (Zhang et al., 2021), we compute the ordering of nodes
1104   in the query graph in Algorithm 2, then we compute the embedding for each node in the query
1105   graph Algorithm 1, the final embedding of every free node are gotten to be the predicted answer.
1106   Especially, the node ordering we got in Algorithm 2 coincides with the natural topology ordering
1107   induced by the directed acyclic operator tree, so we can compute the embedding in the same order as
1108   the original implementation. Then, in Algorithm 1, we implement each set operation in the operator
1109   tree, including intersection, negation, and set projection. By the merit of the Disjunctive Normal
1110   Form (DNF), the union is tackled in the final step. Thus, our implementation can coincide with the
1111   original implementation in the original dataset (Ren & Leskovec, 2020).
1112   For CQD (Arakelyan et al., 2020) and LMPNN (Wang et al., 2023), their original implementation
1113   does not require the operator tree, so we just use their original implementation. Specifically, in a query
1114   graph with multiple free variables, for CQD we predict the answer for each free variable individually
1115   as taking others free variables as existential variables, for LMPNN, we just got all embedding of
1116   nodes that represent free variables.
1117   For FIT (Yin et al., 2024), though it is proposed to solve EFO1 queries, it is computationally costly:
1118   it has a complexity of OpE 2 q in the acyclic graphs and is even not polynomial in the cyclic graphs,
1119   the reason is that FIT degrades to enumeration to deal with cyclic graph. In our implementation, we
1120   further restrict FIT to at most enumerate 10 possible candidates for each node in the query graph, this
1121   practice has allowed FIT to be implemented in the dataset FB15k-237 (Toutanova & Chen, 2015).
1122   However, it cost 20 hours to evaluate FIT on our EFOk -CQA dataset while other models only need no
1123   more than two hours. Moreover, for larger knowledge graph, including NELL (Carlson et al., 2010)
1124
       and FB15k (Bordes et al., 2013), we have also encountered an out-of-memory error in a Tesla V100
       GPU with 32G memory when implementing FIT, thus, we omit its result in these two knowledge
1125
       graphs.

1130   In this section, we discuss possible further development in the task of complex query answering and
1131   how our work, especially our framework proposed in Section 5 can help with future development. We
1132   list some new features that may be of interest and show the maximum versatility our framework can
1133   reach. Our analysis and characterization of future queries inherit the outlook in Wang et al. (2022)
       and also is based on the current development.

1134
       Inductive Reasoning Inductive reasoning is a new trend in the field of complex query answering.
1135   Some entities (Galkin et al., 2022) or even relations (Huang et al., 2022) are not seen in the training
1136   period, namely they can not be found by the observed knowledge graph Go therefore, the inductive
1137   generalization is essential for the model to infer answers. We note that our framework is powerful
1138   enough to sample inductive queries with the observed knowledge graph Go given. Therefore, the
1139   functionality of sampling inductive query is easily contained and implemented in our framework,
1140   see https://anonymous.4open.science/r/EFOK-CQA/README.md. We note there
1141   we have already provided our EFOk -CQA dataset in this setting as discussed in Appendix C.4.
1142   N-ary relation N-ary relation is a relation that has n ą 2 corresponding entities, therefore, the factual
1143   information in the knowledge graph is not a triple but a pn ` 1q-tuple. Moreover, the query graph is
1144   also a hypergraph, making the corresponding CSP problem even harder. This is a newly introduced
1145   topic (Luo et al., 2022; Alivanistos et al., 2022) in complex query answering, which our framework
1146   has limitations in representing.
1147   Knowledge graph with attribute Currently, there has been some research that has taken the
1148   additional attribute of the knowledge graph into account. Typical attributes include entity types (Hu
1149   et al., 2022), numerical literals (Bai et al., 2023a; Demir et al., 2023),triple timestamps (Jia et al.,
1150   2021; Saxena et al., 2021), and triple probabilities (Carlson et al., 2010). We note that attributes
1151   expand the entity set E from all entities to entities with attribute values, it is also possible that the
1152   relation set R is also extended to contain corresponding relations, like “greater”, “less” when dealing
1153   with numerical literals. Then, our framework can represent queries on such extended knowledge
1154   graphs like in Bai et al. (2023b), where no function like “plus”, or “minus” is considered and the
1155
       predicates are also binary.
1156   Overall, our framework can be applied to some avant-garde problem settings given certain properties,
1157   thus those functionalities proposed in Section 5 can be useful. We hope our discussion helps with the
1158   future development of complex query answering.
