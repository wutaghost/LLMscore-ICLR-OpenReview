011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            AI-based methods for solving high-dimensional partial differential equations
014            (PDEs) have garnered significant attention as a promising approach to overcoming
015            the curse of dimensionality faced by traditional techniques. This work establishes
016
               complexity estimates for the Barron norm of solutions of d-dimensional second-
               order linear PDEs, explicitly capturing the dependence on dimension. By lever-
017
               aging well-developed theory for elliptic and parabolic equations, we represent the
018
               solutions of second-order linear equations using Green’s functions. From these
019            representations, we derive complexity bounds for the Barron norm of the solu-
020            tions. Our results extend the prior work of Chen et al. (2021) in two key aspects.
021            First, we consider more general elliptic and parabolic equations; specifically, we
022            address both time-independent and time-dependent equations. Second, we provide
023            sufficient conditions on the coefficients of the PDEs under which the solutions be-
024            long to Barron space rather than approximating the solutions via Barron functions
025            in the H 1 norm. As a result, our approach yields theoretically improved results,
026            providing a more intuitive understanding when approximating the solutions of
027
               PDEs via two-layer neural networks.

      1.1   INTRODUCTION

033
      Partial differential equations (PDEs) are foundational equations used in modeling complex phe-
034   nomena across numerous fields, including physics, social sciences, and engineering. Traditionally,
035   numerical methods such as the finite element method (FEM) and the finite difference method (FDM)
036   have been the primary tools for solving PDEs numerically. However, these methods suffer from the
037   curse of dimensionality, which is an exponential increment of computational cost as the dimension
038   of the equation increases. Motivated by these interesting results, we investigate whether a neural
039   network can effectively approximate the solutions of given PDEs without suffering from the curse
040   of dimensionality.
041   The theoretical analysis of PDEs has traditionally relied on classical function spaces, such as
042   Sobolev spaces (W k,p (Rd )) and Hölder spaces (C k,α (Rd )). However, approximating functions
043   in these spaces with neural networks requires O(ε−d/k ) parameters to achieve an accuracy of ε
044   (Gühring et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Yarotsky, 2017; Yarotsky & Zhevnerchuk, 2020). This indi-
045   cates that approximating classical function classes by neural networks can be challenging and suffers
046   from the curse of dimensionality. This limitation has motivated the search for alternative function
047   spaces better suited for neural network approximations.
048   The Barron space, introduced in the seminal work of Barron (1993), is a potential alternative that
049   consists of a collection of all two-layer functions. Approximating functions in Barron space us-
050   ing neural networks can achieve dimension-free convergence rates. These spaces have recently at-
051   tracted significant attention for their applicability in the approximation theory of two-layer neural
052   networks, also known as shallow networks. Shallow networks play an essential role in developing
053   diverse machine-learning theories. Notable research areas are memory capacities, the representer
      theorem, and the cost of representation, which are discussed in (Ardeshir et al., 2023; Madden &


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Thrampoulidis, 2024; Parhi & Nowak, 2021). In the context of Physics-Informed Neural Networks
055   (PINNs), several advancements have been made in utilizing two-layer network structures to solve
056   PDEs, as shown in (Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Building upon this line of
057   research, estimates of the Barron norm for solutions of PDEs with explicit dependency on the di-
058   mension provide significant theoretical value.
059
      Chen et al. (2021) establishes that if the coefficients and the source term of an elliptic PDE lie in
060
      Barron space, then the solution of the PDE is ε-close to a Barron function in the Sobolev norm. This
061   result has been extended to nonlinear elliptic equations under linear growth conditions in (Marwah
062   et al., 2023). Additionally, Weinan & Wojtowytsch (2022) demonstrate that several model equations
063   belong to Barron space under appropriate assumptions through representation formulas. They also
064   provide counterexamples where the solutions of PDEs do not belong to the Barron space.
065
      Despite these intriguing results, research on the regularity of time-dependent equations, such as
066
      parabolic equations, within the framework of Barron space remains scarce. To the best of our
067   knowledge, Barron norm estimates for the solution of parabolic equations have only been studied in
068   Weinan & Wojtowytsch (2022) for the heat equation. The main reason for the scarcity of results is
069   that the theoretical analysis of parabolic equations presents several key challenges, including time
070   dependencies, handling function spaces involving time, and the anisotropic nature of parabolic equa-
071   tions. We have leveraged and adapted recent advancements in both elliptic and parabolic regularity
072   theory to address these difficulties.
073   Regularity theory for elliptic and parabolic equations aims to understand the smoothness of solu-
074   tions under various assumptions regarding the coefficients, forcing terms, and boundary conditions.
075   For a comprehensive overview of the regularity theory of PDEs, we refer the reader to (Gilbarg
076   et al., 1977; Ladyženskaja, 1968). Among the many techniques in the regularity theory of PDEs, the
077   estimates of Green’s functions for elliptic and parabolic equations are the primary technique used in
078   this work.
079   The Green’s functions of linear differential operators, denoted by N , are solutions of N G = δ,
080   where δ is the Dirac delta function. Section 2.2 provides a detailed definition of these operators
081   for parabolic and elliptic equations. Taking advantage of linearity of the operator, the solution of
082   N u = f can be represented as u = f ∗ G. The construction of Green’s functions for elliptic
083   and parabolic equations has been extensively studied. See for instance (Grüter & Widman, 1982;
084   Littman et al., 1963; Kim & Sakellaris, 2019; Hofmann & Lewis, 2001; Davey et al., 2018) for
085   elliptic equations, and (Nash, 1958; Moser, 1964; Cho et al., 2008; Semenov, 2006; Qian & Xi,
086   2019; Kim & Xu, 2021) for parabolic equations under various assumptions such as coefficients,
087
      domain regularity and so on. In this study, we primarily build upon the methodologies proposed by
      (Kim & Xu, 2021).
088
089   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
090
091         1. Barron norm complexity estimates for time-dependent parabolic equations: We estab-
092            lish, for the first time, estimates on the complexity of the Barron norm for the solutions of
093            general second-order linear parabolic equations the explicit dependency on the dimension.
094
095
096
            2. Improvement of theoretical results for elliptic equations: Even for time-independent
               elliptic equations, our results represent a significant advancement over existing work (Chen
097
               et al., 2021). We establish that, under appropriate assumptions, the solutions of these PDEs
098
               belong directly to Barron space rather than merely approximating them in the Sobolev sense
099            within Barron space. Please refer to Remark 3. This refinement constitutes a theoretical
100            improvement, offering deeper insights into the structure of the solutions.
101
102
103
            3. Theoretical foundation: Our research provides a theoretical foundation demonstrating that
               AI-based PDE solvers or Green’s function learning methods can represent solutions without
104
               suffering from the curse of dimensionality.

508   4   C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORKS
509
510   In this paper, we have demonstrated that solutions of second-order linear PDEs can be effectively
511
      represented in the Barron space using Green’s functions. This result theoretically supports existing
      two-layer PINN methods (Gao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), particularly for high-
512
      dimensional problems. Below, we discuss interesting directions for future research.
513
514          • Barron norm estimates for the nonlinear equations is an interesting and challenging prob-
515            lem. Marwah et al. (2023), examine nonlinear elliptic equations with linear growth condi-
516            tions. Extending these results to parabolic equations would be an intriguing direction for
517            future research.
518          • Many important equations in applied fields, such as finance or kinetic theory, are given in
519            non-divergence form and often exhibit degeneracy. Investigating the curse of dimension-
520            ality in such equations could provide valuable insights. The regularity theory and Green’s
521            function estimates for these equations are discussed in (Di Francesco & Polidoro, 2006).
522          • In this work, we present a theoretical result using Green’s function. Recent studies, such as
523            (Boullé et al., 2022; Teng et al., 2022), demonstrate a growing interest in learning Green’s
524            functions by machine learning. Moreover, our results suggest that neural operators can be
525            trained without encountering the curse of dimensionality since training Green’s function
526            can be applied to the faimily of PDEs instead of one instance of the equation. Exploring
527
               these areas further would be highly interesting.
528          • For connections between the Barron space defined here and the spectral Barron space,
529            see Appendix G. Remarks on function spaces beyond Barron space and their relation to
530
               PDEs are in Appendix H. Numerical experiments validating our theorems are provided in
               Appendix O. We refer Appendix P for the estimate of Γ( d+1          d

934   In practical applications, two-layer networks are rarely employed, making the theoretical analysis of

936   Nagaraj, 2020), the authors demonstrate that if u ∈ F1/K (Rd ), there exists a K-neuron ReLU(x)
937   network uθ capable of approximating u. However, as explained in Section G, our results cannot
938   be directly extended to u ∈ F1/K (Rd ) due to the absence of inverse approximation results for the
939
      spectral Barron spaces.
940   The study of function spaces for multi-layer networks remains highly underexplored. Beyond the
941   limited existing works, such as (Wojtowytsch et al., 2020; Chen, 2023; 2024), there has been little
942   research into defining meaningful functional properties of deep neural networks. Expanding these
943   investigations is crucial for advancing our understanding of multi-layer networks, particularly to
944   leverage such function spaces for approximating solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs).
945   While shallow networks have been analyzed extensively, deep networks are still at a nascent stage
946   in terms of both their theoretical function spaces and practical applications to PDE approximation.
947   Further exploration of these areas is a topic for future research.
