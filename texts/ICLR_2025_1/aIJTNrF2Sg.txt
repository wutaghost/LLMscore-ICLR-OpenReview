009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series data, commonly used in fields like climate studies, finance, and
012            healthcare, usually faces challenges such as missing data and privacy concerns.
013            Recently, diffusion models have emerged as effective tools for generating high-
014            quality data, but applying them to time series is still difficult, especially for cap-
015            turing long-range dependencies and complex information. In this paper, we intro-
016            duce a new diffusion model that uses frequency domain information to improve
017            time series data generation. In particular, we apply Fourier analysis to adaptively
018
               separate low-frequency global trends from high-frequency details, which helps the
               model better understand important patterns during the denoising process. Finally,
019
               our approach uses a specialized frequency encoder to integrate this information,
020
               enhancing the model’s ability to capture both global and local features. Through
021            exhaustive experiments on various public datasets, our model shows an impressive
022            performance in generating time series data for diverse tasks like forecasting and
023            imputation, outperforming existing methods in accuracy and flexibility.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Time series data, which records observations over time, is widely used in various real-world fields
029   such as climate studies (Mudelsee, 2010), finance (Koa et al., 2023), and healthcare (Jeong et al.,
030   2024). However, the process of collecting such data often faces significant challenges, including
031   the need to simulate scenarios that did not occur during data collection (Koa et al., 2023) and con-
032   cerns over personal privacy (Yoon et al., 2020). To address these issues, there has been a surge
033
      in research focused on synthesizing time series data in recent years. Generative models such as
      generative adversarial networks (GANs), variational autoencoders (VAEs), and their variants have
034
      achieved notable success in this area (Ang et al., 2023). With this success, there have been numer-
035
      ous extensions to generative models, including decomposing time series data elements to improve
036   interpretability (Desai et al., 2021), and developing models for imputation (Tashiro et al., 2021) and
037   forecasting (Rasul et al., 2021).
038
039
      Recently, in the field of computer vision, the dominant approach to generative modeling has shifted
      towards score-based diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020). These models offer the benefit of generating
040
      high-quality data while addressing the limitations of traditional generative models like GANs and
041
      VAEs. Building on the success of diffusion-based modeling in computer vision, there is a growing
042   interest in applying those techniques to time series data analysis (Ang et al., 2023). Initially, research
043   focused on task-specific generation like imputation (Tashiro et al., 2021) and prediction (Yan et al.,
044   2021). It has since expanded to explore more general approaches to time series data generation (Lim
045   et al., 2023; Yuan & Qiao, 2024).
046
      Despite the success of diffusion-based time series generation, the existing methods encounter several
047
      critical challenges. First, autoregressive models are limited in long-range performance due to the
048   accumulation of errors, and their inference speed is slow because data is generated iteratively (Lim
049   et al., 2023). In this regard, recent research on the diffusion model in time series modeling is
050   more directed to non-autoregressive methods to address the issue of error accumulation. However,
051   those approaches make it nearly impossible to capture all time-related information, particularly in
052   long-range sequences, making it a highly challenging problem. To handle this, time series data is
053   typically divided into smaller windows for learning (Yuan & Qiao, 2024), but this method has trouble
      effectively modeling gradually changing trends as depicted in Figure 1. Additionally, unconditional

081
082   In this paper, we propose a novel diffusion model that incorporates time-domain information while
083   leveraging rich frequency-domain information to address the aforementioned limitations. By uti-
084   lizing the distinct characteristics of low and high frequencies, we simplify the complex process of
085   decomposing temporal components. This enables us to condition the model on frequency informa-
086
      tion during the denoising process, resulting in the generation of higher-quality samples. Unlike the
      previous approach (Lee et al., 2023), where frequency information was arbitrarily divided, we dis-
087
      sect the frequency components based on the amount of information present in the spectral density.
088
      This dissected frequency information is then cross-attended within the denoising structure through
089   a frequency encoder, allowing the model to more effectively capture and utilize both global and lo-
090   cal features during generation. To demonstrate the validity and robustness of our proposed method,
091   we conducted exhaustive experiments, achieving promising results across various public datasets.
092   Moreover, our method excels in task-specific generation, such as prediction and imputation, where
093   each frequency component plays a critical role, showcasing the model’s versatility across diverse
094   time series generation challenges.
095   In summary, the major contributions of our work are as follows:
096
097          • We propose a novel time series generation diffusion model that operates in the time domain
098            while utilizing frequency as prior knowledge. This approach enables the model to effec-
099            tively capture and represent the characteristics of the data by combining time-domain and
100            frequency-domain information during the diffusion learning process.
101
             • We introduce a module that adaptively dissects frequency information based on the power
102
               spectrum, leading the model to effectively represent temporal aspects in the data. This
103
               facilitates high-quality synthesis, which more accurately reflects the inherent trends and
104            patterns of the dataset.
105
106          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method for generating time series data
107            across various public datasets, showing that it achieves superior performance not only in
               long-range data generation but also in imputation and forecasting tasks.

      2.1   G ENERATIVE M ODELS IN T IME S ERIES
113
114   Deep generative models designed for time series data analysis, especially GAN-based models, have
115   evolved to integrate temporal dynamics and spatial features into their architectures effectively. In
116   C-RNN-GAN (Mogren, 2016), the authors introduced a method that integrates GAN with a re-
117   current model, marking the first approach to applying GANs to sequential data. Subsequently,
118   TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019) introduced a generative model that combines an embedding func-
119   tion and a supervised loss with the original GAN architecture to better control conditional temporal
120   dynamics. GT-GAN (Jeon et al., 2022) addressed the irregularity of time series data using a neural
121
      ODE-based approach, and PSA-GAN (Jeha et al., 2022) was developed to progressively enhance
      GAN performance through self-attention mechanisms. Meanwhile, VAE-based generative models
122
      have also developed rapidly, offering key advantages such as fast sampling speed and high diver-
123
      sity in the generated data. TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021) aimed to enhance the interpretability of
124   time series data by generating it through temporal components. In TimeVQVAE (Lee et al., 2023),
125   time series data is synthesized using vector quantization techniques by separating the data into low-
126   frequency and high-frequency components within the frequency domain. Furthermore, to generate
127   medical time series data that accounts for causality, CR-VAE (Li et al., 2023) introduced a model
128   that integrates causal mechanisms into the recurrent VAE architecture.
129
      Consequently, various time series data synthesis models based on GANs and VAEs have been de-
130   veloped. However, these models face inherent limitations—GANs struggle with adversarial loss
131   training and mode collapse, while VAEs often produce blurry samples and face challenges in opti-
132   mizing the KL divergence. As a result, achieving both high-quality samples and sufficient diversity
133   remains a significant challenge.
134

137   2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS IN T IME S ERIES
138
139
140   Recently, diffusion-based models have become a central focus of research, surpassing other gener-
141   ative models by providing a methodology that successfully achieves both high quality and diversity
142
      in generated data. Significant progress is also being made in applying these models to time series
      data. CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) introduced a diffusion model that imputes missing values by condi-
143
      tioning on observed data, utilizing a self-supervised learning approach. Additionally, for prediction
144
      tasks, TimeGrad Rasul et al. (2021), based on DDPM, and ScoreGrad (Yan et al., 2021), based on
145   SDE, were proposed. These models integrate the temporal feature modeling of RNNs with diffusion
146   processes, enhancing the predictive performance of time series models. While these methods ini-
147   tially struggled with error accumulation in long-range predictions, recent advances have introduced
148   non-autoregressive approaches, such as TimeDiff (Shen & Kwok, 2023), which incorporates future
149   information, and LDT (Feng et al., 2024), which models in latent space. In addition to task-specific
150   models like imputation and prediction, recent research has increasingly focused on diffusion-based
151   models for more general tasks that aim to learn data distribution. In TSGM (Lim et al., 2023), a gen-
152   erative model is developed by combining an RNN-based autoencoder with a conditional diffusion
153
      model. In DiffTime (Coletta et al., 2024), the authors addressed the challenge of generating data
      under constraints like trends and fixed values and proposed a diffusion model specifically designed
154
      to operate within these constraints. As another approach, the Diffusion-TS (Yuan & Qiao, 2024) was
155
      developed to generate data by disentangling temporal components such as trends and seasonalities.
156   In Time Weaver (Narasimhan et al., 2024), the authors improved performance by combining hetero-
157   geneous paired metadata. On the other hand, recent research (Crabbé et al., 2024) has shown that
158   the frequency domain can be a more effective inductive bias for diffusion modeling compared to the
159   time domain, based on both theoretical and experimental analyses. Still, the frequency domain alone
160   lacks the ability to capture temporal information. To address this, we integrate frequency domain
161   information with time domain modeling in a multi-view approach, leading to improved performance
      on real-world datasets.

189   3.2   F OURIER A NLNYSIS
190
191
      Fourier analysis is a mathematical method that transforms signals from the time domain to the
      frequency domain. It shows remarkable performance in applications such as signal processing,
192
      data compression, and machine learning by breaking down signals into their frequency components,
193
      which simplifies their analysis and manipulation (Körner, 2022).
194
195   Signal energy. Signal energy is a crucial characteristic in signal analysis, representing the total
196   energy contained within a signal. Let x be the signal and x0 be the transformed signal into the
197
      frequency domain. It is determined by the amplitude and duration of the signal and can be calculated
      using the squared Frobenius norm ||x||2 . According to Parseval’s theorem, the signal energy remains
198
      the same whether calculated in the time domain or the frequency domain, ||x||2 = ||x0 ||2 .
199
200   When training a diffusion model, relying solely on the energy density, which is evenly distributed
201   over time, may not be sufficient (Crabbé et al., 2024). That is, there is a limit to time domain
202   information. To address this, we condition the model by separating the frequency components into
203
      low and high frequencies based on spectral energy density. This separation allows the model to
      focus on different aspects of the signal, enhancing its ability to capture both global and local features
204
      during the learning process.

      4.1   S IGNAL E NERGY- BASED A DAPTIVE F REQUENCY S ELECTION
243   The distinction between low and high frequencies in time series analysis lacks a uniform standard,
244   and prior methods often rely on arbitrary decisions or domain-specific knowledge (Lee et al., 2023).
245   In our method, we address this limitation by calculating the spectral density based on signal energy,
246   using it as an objective basis to separate low and high frequencies. This approach allows for a more
247   data-driven separation within the time series.
248
      In this process, the spectral density at x0 is calculated to determine the information context for each
249   frequency. We then compute the ratio of each frequency’s information content to the total spectral
250   information. Based on this spectral information, the frequencies containing information up to the
251   threshold , which is a hyperparameter, are divided into low frequency components x0l , and the
252   remaining are designated as high frequency components x0h :

524   6      C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this study, we present a novel diffusion model specifically designed for time series generation,
527   leveraging frequency information as a priori knowledge. Our approach involves dissecting the fre-
528   quency components into low and high frequencies based on spectral density, which allows the model
529   to effectively capture global trends and local semantic details inherent in the data. The proposed
530   method operates by integrating time-domain information during the denoising process, enabling it
531
      to generate data that reflects diverse perspectives from both the frequency and time domains. To
      validate our approach, we conducted extensive experiments on various public datasets, demonstrat-
532
      ing the effectiveness and robustness of our method. We also performed an ablation study to assess
533
      the contributions of each frequency component, revealing how low and high frequencies impact the
534   model’s performance. Our results indicate that the proposed model excels not only in generating
535   high-quality time series data but also in task-specific applications, such as imputation and forecast-
536   ing, surpassing existing methods in versatility and accuracy. Our future direction is to expand toward
537   the development of a foundation model. Through previous experiments, we have validated the ben-
538   efits of incorporating frequency information. Building on this, our goal is to create a model that
539   controls data generation using frequency-based conditions, learning from a variety of datasets, and
      performing tasks tailored to each dataset’s specific characteristics.
