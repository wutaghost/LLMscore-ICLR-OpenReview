011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As the third generation of neural networks, Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) have
014            gained widespread attention due to their low energy consumption and biological
015            interpretability. Recently, SNNs have made considerable advancements in com-
016
               puter vision. However, efficiently conducting feature extraction and fusion under
               the spiking characteristics of SNNs for object detection remains a pressing chal-
017
               lenge. To address this problem, we propose the SpikSSD, a novel Spiking Single
018
               Shot Multibox Detector. Specifically, we design a full-spiking backbone network,
019            MDS-ResNet, which effectively adjusts the membrane synaptic input distribution
020            at each layer, achieving better spiking feature extraction. Additionally, for spik-
021            ing feature fusion, we introduce the Spiking Bi-direction Fusion Module (SBFM),
022            which for the first time realizes bi-direction fusion of spiking features, enhancing
023            the multi-scale detection capability of the model. Experimental results show that
024            SpikSSD achieves 40.8% mAP on the GEN1 dataset, 76.3% and 52.4% mAP@0.5
025            on VOC 2007 and COCO 2017 datasets respectively with the lowest firing rate,
026            outperforming existing SNN-based approaches at ultralow energy consumption.
027
               This work sets a new benchmark for future research in SNN-based object detec-
               tion. Our code is publicly available in supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), unlike traditional Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) that rely on
      continuous values, communicate between neurons through discrete spiking signals and use biolog-
034
      ically interpretable spiking neurons for computation (Maass (1997)). Although the discreteness of
035
      spiking signals may lead to information loss, the temporal property of spikes can compensate for this
036   limitation (Skatchkovsky et al. (2021)). Additionally, due to the spiking nature of SNNs, they can
037   avoid the heavy computation load of multiplication operations, relying instead on simpler addition
038   operations (Hu et al. (2024)). These characteristics give significant advantages to SNNs in terms of
039   energy efficiency and processing speed, positioning them as a promising alternative to ANNs.
040
      Object detection is a crucial task in computer vision, with wide-ranging applications from au-
041
      tonomous driving (Balasubramaniam & Pasricha (2022)) to intelligent surveillance (Zhang & Kim
042   (2019)) and medical imaging (Litjens et al. (2017)). Moreover, object detection is not only a classifi-
043   cation task but also involves regression, requiring the simultaneous identification of object categories
044   and the precise prediction of their locations and sizes.
045
      Recently, SNNs have made significant progress in classification tasks (Zhou et al. (2024); Yao et al.
046
      (2023)). However, their performance in more complex tasks that involve both classification and
047
      regression, such as object detection, still falls short of expectations. In this situation, many studies
048   on SNN-based object detection have focused on improvements to the backbone (Su et al. (2023))
049   and introducing the spiking feature fusion network (Fan et al. (2024)). While these approaches
050   have achieved some success, they fail to consider the network as a whole and instead focus only
051   on optimizing specific parts. To enhance the performance of SNNs in detection tasks, it is essential
052   to focus on both efficient extraction and fusion of spiking features, as a strong backbone requires
053   effective fusion to utilize its features fully, and vice versa. Therefore, this paper proposes SpikSSD
      (Spiking Single Shot Multibox Detector) to address these challenges.

071   For feature extraction, we observe that the most significant difference between SNNs and ANNs
072   lies in their feature representation: in SNNs, features are coded through neuron firing patterns. In
073   EMS-ResNet (Su et al. (2023)), the output of the residual path continuously accumulates to the
074
      shortcut path. Assuming these outputs are approximately independent, the variance of their sum
      equals the sum of their variances, leading to a gradual increase in membrane synaptic input variance
075
      for subsequent neurons. This results in a higher probability of extreme inputs. Specifically, when
076
      the synaptic input is small, the accumulated membrane potential fails to reach the firing threshold.
077   When it is excessively large, neurons exhibit abnormally high firing rates. In both cases, the firing
078   pattern becomes either overly sparse or excessive, failing to reflect the input features accurately.
079   This unstable firing pattern makes it difficult for the network to distinguish and extract meaningful
080   features, which in turn affects the networkŌĆÖs feature extraction capability.
081
      To address this issue, we propose the Membrane-based Deformed Shortcut (MDS), a novel mech-
082
      anism optimizing identity mapping to stabilize membrane synaptic input distribution across lay-
083   ers. Based on MDS, we propose a novel Spiking Residual Network, MDS-ResNet, which exhibits
084   stronger feature extraction capabilities. The firing patterns of MDS-ResNet and EMS-ResNet are
085   shown in Fig. 1, where it can be seen that our MDS effectively stabilizes the firing pattern.
086
      In SNNs, features exist in both spatial and temporal domains. Spiking feature fusion enhances
087
      feature representation in both dimensions, improving multi-scale detection capabilities. This dual-
088
      domain nature makes feature fusion more crucial in SNNs compared to ANNs. Currently, the only
089   fusion method designed for SNNs is the Spiking Fusion Module (SFM) (Fan et al. (2024)). This
090   fusion method adopts a one-way, down-up approach, where large-scale features are upsampled to
091   merge with small-scale features. Without up-down fusion, it only enhances small-scale feature rep-
092   resentation, overlooking the potential improvement of large-scale features. Furthermore, SFM in-
093   corporates SEW-Block internally, which compromises the spiking nature of the network. To address
094   these issues, we propose the Spiking Bi-direction Fusion Module (SBFM). Specifically, this module
095   utilizes Spiking Up/Down Block and membrane addition-based fusion method to process feature
096   maps, performing fusion first bottom-up and then top-down. We employ the proposed MDS Fusion
097
      Block (MDSF-Block) to extract fused features during this process. These SNN-friendly components
      ensure SBFM adheres to spiking characteristics while enhancing the model detection capabilities.
098
099   The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
100
101   ŌĆó We propose MDS-ResNet, which introduces the MDS to stabilize membrane synaptic input dis-
102
        tribution at each layer, thereby enhancing feature extraction capabilities. Additionally, we theo-
        retically demonstrate that MDS-ResNet can avoid gradient vanishing or explosion.
103
104   ŌĆó We propose the SBFM, which achieves bi-directional feature fusion for the first time in SNNs,
105     thereby enhancing the modelŌĆÖs capability to detect multi-scale objects.
106   ŌĆó Based on MDS-ResNet, SBFM, and the SSD Detection Head, we propose SpikSSD, which
107     achieves state-of-the-art mAP on event-based GEN1, frame-based VOC 2007 and COCO 2017
        datasets compared to other SNN-based models, while requiring the lowest firing rate.

      2.1   S PIKING N EURAL N ETWORKS
111
112   SNNs are designed to mimic the behavior of biological neurons more accurately than ANNs. Re-
113   searchers introduce various spiking neural models to achieve this, including the Hodgkin-Huxley
114   (Hodgkin & Huxley (1952)), Izhikevich (Izhikevich (2003)), Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) (Wu
115   et al. (2019)), and Parametric Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (PLIF) (Fang et al. (2021b)) models.
116
      SNNs utilize two training approaches: ANNs-to-SNNs conversion and direct training. The ANNs-
117   to-SNNs conversion approach approximates ReLU activation using average firing rate, allowing
118   trained ANNs to be transformed into SNNs (Cao et al. (2015)). Although this approach has led to
119   powerful SNNs (Kim et al. (2020b)), it requires thousands of time steps and prevents SNNs from
120   exploiting their low-latency advantage. Moreover, this method is suited only for static datasets
121   and the performance of converted SNNs depends on the original ANNs. Conversely, the direct
122   training method leverages surrogate gradients to optimize SNNs (Neftci et al. (2019)), facilitating
123   their training on a variety of datasets and achieving strong performance within a limited number of
124   time steps. This technique enables the widespread application of SNNs in vision tasks (Cordone
125
      et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2022)). Hence, we apply the direct training strategy to our model in this
      study.
126
127
128   2.2   S PIKING R ESIDUAL N ETWORKS
129
      In the Spiking Residual Networks (SRNs) proposed based on the ANN-to-SNN conversion training
130
      method, the primary focus is ensuring that the SNNs maintain lossless accuracy during the conver-
131   sion from ANNs (Hu et al. (2021)). However, networks designed based on this method also face
132   inherent drawbacks associated with this training approach.
133
134
      In directly trained SRNs, Zheng et al. (2021) successfully constructs a 50-layer deep network using
      threshold-dependent Batch Normalization (tdBN), but overlooks spiking characteristics by simply
135
      replacing ReLU with LIF neurons. SEW-ResNet (Fang et al. (2021a)) and MS-ResNet (Hu et al.
136
      (2024)) address gradient issues and train networks exceeding 100 layers. However, the spike addi-
137   tion for residual learning in SEW-ResNet and the neglect of non-spiking convolutions for shortcuts
138   by MS-ResNet prevent full-spiking networks. EMS-ResNet Su et al. (2023) addresses the short-
139   comings of MS-ResNet but fails to improve performance due to inadequate consideration of SNN
140   characteristics. This paper further investigates spiking characteristics to enhance feature extraction
141   while maintaining a full-spiking SRN.
142
143   2.3   O BJECT D ETECTION WITH S PIKING N EURON N ETWORKS
144
145   Unlike ANNs, SNNs have event-driven characteristics suitable for both frame and event cameras.
146   Event cameras have garnered attention due to their advantages in temporal resolution, dynamic
147   range, power efficiency, and pixel bandwidth (Gallego et al. (2020)).
148   Early attempts to apply SNNs to object detection using ANN-to-SNN conversion (Kim et al.
149   (2020b;a)) face challenges of high latency, poor performance, and incompatibility with event cam-
150   eras. VC-DenseNet (Cordone et al. (2022)) and EMS-YOLO (Su et al. (2023)) pioneer direct train-
151   ing for SNN-based object detection, achieving promising results on both frame and event-based
152   datasets. However, they both overlook feature fusion. SFOD (Fan et al. (2024)) introduces an ef-
153   ficient Spiking Fusion Module, but emphasizes less on backbone feature extraction. Hence, our
154
      research enhances SNN-based object detection by improving both feature extraction and fusion.

321
322   Research in feature fusion for SNNs is limited to one-way down-up fusion (Fan et al. (2024)), where
323   large-scale features are only upsampled to enhance small-scale features. This approach restricts up-
      down information transmission and leads to inadequate feature fusion across scales. Furthermore,

339   Spiking Bi-direction Fusion Module (SBFM), which enhances feature fusion through bi-directional
340   information flow while maintaining the network spiking properties.

      The structure of the SBFM is shown in Fig. 2. In this module, feature maps ranging from 1/8 to 1/128
342
      are fused. During the fusion process, we first fuse feature maps of lower resolution with those of
343
      higher resolution through upsampling (down-up). Subsequently, we employ downsampling further
344   to integrate these fused feature maps (up-down). Notably, we perform only a single fusion operation
345   for the 1/8 and 1/128 scale feature maps to avoid introducing feature maps at 1/4 and 1/256 scales,
346   thereby enhancing computation efficiency. Next, we will discuss the methods for spiking feature
347   fusion, upsampling, and downsampling.
348
      The current spiking feature fusion method uses concatenation to preserve spiking characteristics
349
      (Fan et al. (2024)), but this increases model complexity and makes the effective alignment of fea-
350   tures difficult. To address these issues, we propose a membrane addition-based fusion method that
351   maintains spiking characteristics by adding membrane synaptic inputs. This method treats feature
352   maps from different resolutions as supplements with smaller variances, adding them to the target-
353   resolution feature map to mimic residual learning. The MDSF-Block (Fig. 4) enhances fusion
354   through post-addition feature extraction. This block replaces MDS in MDS-Block3 with spiking
355   depthwise separable convolution, enabling fusion across different dimensions while maintaining
356   spike-based computation.
357   To achieve upsampling/downsampling, we propose the Spiking Up/Down Block, whose structure
358   is shown in Fig. 4. In this block, we use Nearest Neighbor Interpolation or Maxpool as the key
359   element, which has been proven to maintain the inherent spiking characteristics of SNNs during
360   the upsampling/downsampling process (Ranc╠¦on et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2021b)). Furthermore, to
361   ensure that the feature maps after sampling are consistent in channel numbers with the feature maps
362   to be fused, and to assign them a smaller variance, we incorporate a LIF-Conv1x1-tdBN structure
363   following the Nearest Neighbor Interpolation or Maxpool operation.

      4.2.4    I NFLUENCE OF T IME W INDOW
452
453   For event data, the time window of samples varies, providing different bases for prediction. The
454   larger the time window, the stronger the model becomes at detecting slowly moving objects. Here,
455   we conduct thorough experiments by adjusting the size of the time window. As shown in rows 8 to
456   12 of Tab. 1, the modelŌĆÖs mAP improves as the window size increases, while energy consumption re-
457   mains constant. However, we argue that an unlimited expansion of the time window would impose
458   an excessive burden on hardware memory, thus we do not experiment with larger time windows.
459
      Furthermore, as indicated in row 12, we discover that training the model with a 100ms window and
      inferring with a 200ms window yields excellent results. We attribute the improved performance to
460
      training with a 100ms window, which provides more sparse samples, thus enhancing model perfor-
461
      mance when inferring with a 200ms window. This approach reduces hardware burden and training
462   time. Therefore, our final model adopts this 100ms/200ms training/inference approach.

468   increases up to 1.3, but declines beyond this point. This decline is likely due to events becoming
469
      more spatially sparse at larger scales, creating challenges for the model inference.

      5            C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel and efficient SNN-based object detector, SpikSSD. Specifically,
532
      to enhance the model feature extraction capability, we introduce MDS-ResNet, which stabilizes
533
      membrane synaptic input through MDS, achieving optimal performance with the most efficient fir-
534   ing pattern. For feature fusion, we introduce the first SNN-based bi-directional fusion module,
535   the SBFM. Experimental results show that our SpikSSD achieves state-of-the-art performance, sur-
536   passing other SNN-based models on the GEN1, VOC 2007, and COCO 2017 datasets. Notably,
537   on the GEN1 dataset, this is the first full-spiking SNN model to achieve an mAP exceeding 40.0,
538   demonstrating superior feature extraction and fusion capabilities. In the future, we will focus on
539   optimizing detection heads for SNN-based object detectors, an unexplored area in both our research
      and SNN-specific designs.
