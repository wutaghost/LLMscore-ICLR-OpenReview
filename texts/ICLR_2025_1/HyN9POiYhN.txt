009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly being adapted to new tasks and
012                deployed in real-world decision systems. Several previous works have investigated
013                the bias transfer hypothesis (BTH) and find that fairness of pre-trained masked
014                language models has limited effect on the fairness of these models when adapted
015                using fine-tuning. In this work, we expand the study of BTH to causal models
016                under prompt adaptations, as prompting is an accessible, and compute-efficient way
017
                   to deploy models in real-world systems. In contrast to previous work, we establish
                   that intrinsic biases in pre-trained Mistral, Falcon and Llama models are strongly
018
                   correlated (ρ ≥ 0.94) with biases when the same models are zero- and few-shot
019
                   prompted, using a pronoun co-reference resolution task. Further, we find that
020                biases remain strongly correlated even when LLMs are specifically pre-prompted
021                to exhibit fair or biased behavior (ρ ≥ 0.92), and also when varying few shot
022                composition parameters such as sample size, stereotypical content, occupational
023                distribution and representational balance (ρ ≥ 0.90). Our findings highlight the
024                importance of ensuring fairness in pre-trained LLMs, especially when they are later
025                used to perform downstream tasks via prompt adaptation.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The adaptability of Large Language Models (LLMs) enables them to excel in various tasks, leading
031
      to their growing use in real-world decision-making systems (Brown et al., 2020; Bommasani et al.,
      2021; Bender et al., 2021). The increasing reliance on adaptation methods, such as prompting
032
      and fine-tuning, to accomplish new tasks makes it a growing ethical priority to comprehensively
033
      evaluate the bias effects of adaptation methods. Several previous works study the correlation between
034   the bias of a pre-trained model and its adapted task-specific counterpart (Steed et al., 2022; Cao
035   et al., 2022; Delobelle et al., 2022; Goldfarb-Tarrant et al., 2020; Kaneko et al., 2022; Schröder
036   et al., 2023), with Steed et al. (2022) coining the term bias transfer hypothesis (BTH); BTH is the
037   theory that social biases (such as stereotypes) internalized by LLMs during pre-training transfer into
038   harmful task-specific behavior after model adaptations are applied. These works largely find that
039   BTH does not hold. In other words, they find that intrinsic biases, which are biases measured using
040   metrics that analyze embeddings in pre-trained models, do not correlate with downstream biases
041   in task-specific fine-tuned models; however, they do not study the bias transfer in prompt-adapted
042
      models, nor evaluate beyond masked language models (MLMs). The notion that bias does not transfer
      (Steed et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; Delobelle et al., 2022; Goldfarb-Tarrant et al., 2020) poses
043
      significant concerns for fairness in task-specific models beyond MLMs. This conclusion suggests
044
      that the fairness of pre-trained models is inconsequential. However, this finding is rooted in studies
045   that focused on fine-tuning pre-trained language models, where intrinsic biases were found to have
046   minimal impact on downstream biases. We argue that this context-specific conclusion may not
047   generalize to other settings, such as causal models under prompting. In fact, our work contradicts this
048   notion, highlighting the crucial importance of considering intrinsic biases in pre-trained models to
049   ensure fairness. Therefore, ignoring these biases can have dire implications for LLM fairness.
050
      Causal models are different from MLMs in their training task, architecture and size (Lin et al., 2022).
051   Causal models are implemented using a uni-directional transformer architecture, whereas MLMs are
052   largely bi-directional. Causal models are trained to predict the next token given a sequence of context

078   Task-specificity of models is no longer achieved only through full-parameter fine-tuning. Since the
079   release of GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), prompting has emerged as a promising adaptation alternative
080   to compute-expensive LLM fine-tuning to perform certain downstream tasks (such as multiple-choice
081
      question answering or translation) (Brown et al., 2020; Kojima et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). Some key
      factors limiting machine learning practitioners’ adoption of fine-tuning based adaptations include (1)
082
      lack of compute budget (specifically number of GPUs, storage and memory), (2) lack of task-specific
083
      data, (3) limited access to pre-trained model gradients and (4) lack of familiarity with ML techniques
084   required to implement fine-tuning strategies. The increased prominence of prompting makes it critical
085   to understand the impact of these lightweight adaptation strategies on model bias. Prompting and
086   fine-tuning differ fundamentally, as prompting modifies inputs rather than model parameters. This
087   creates a new paradigm for interacting with models, where the dynamics of bias transfer are not yet
088   well understood. Our work addresses this knowledge gap by investigating bias transfer in causal
089   models under zero- and few-shot prompting strategies accessible to non-expert users.
090   In this work, we make two key contributions through our study of bias transfer in causal language
091   models under prompt adaptations. First, we evaluate the correlation of intrinsic biases with task-
092   specific (downstream) biases resulting from zero- and few-shot prompting on the task of resolving
093   a gendered pronoun with one of two occupations. On this task, we find that intrinsic biases in
094   performant, open-source causal LLMs are highly correlated with task-specific biases. Second, we
095   probe the extent to which biases transfer when (1) models are conditioned with pre-prompts to
096   be fair or biased using zero- and few-shot adaptations, and (2) few-shot sample composition is
097   systematically varied. We find a strong correlation between intrinsic and adapted biases despite pre-
098
      prompting the model to be fair or biased. Additionally, the few-shot composition choices, including
      number of few-shot samples (ranging between 20 and 100), their stereotypical makeup (pro- or
099
      anti-stereotypical pronoun with respect to the referent occupation) and occupational distribution (in-
100
      or out-of-distribution; balanced or bias-weighted resampling), do not have a significant effect on bias
101   correlation. These findings highlight the importance of pre-training fair causal language models to
102   ensure fair downstream performance when prompt-adapted.

161   In this work, we investigate inherent fairness in adaptations (i.e., correlation of biases pre- and post-
      adaptation) using the instruction fine-tuned versions of performant open source LLMs, including

      Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) (7B params), Falcon (40B params) (Almazrouei et al., 2023) and Llama
163   (8B and 70B params) (Touvron et al., 2023). We evaluate model behavior on a co-reference resolution
164   task using the WinoBias dataset (Zhao et al., 2018), which is a widely used fairness benchmark. The
165   WinoBias corpus are used to evaluate model fairness on the task of resolving pronouns to one of two
166   gender stereotyped occupations (see Fig. 1 for a WinoBias-style sample sentence). The WinoBias
167   dataset consists of 3,160 balanced sentences, with 50% containing male pronouns and 50% containing
168   female pronouns. Additionally, the dataset is divided into two types: 50% ambiguous sentences
169   (Type 1), where the pronoun can syntactically resolve to either occupation, and 50% unambiguous
170   sentences (Type 2), where the pronoun resolves to one occupation only. As illustrated in Fig. 1, we
171
      design evaluation prompts for the task of multiple choice question answering.
172   We treat statistical disparities in model behavior for different demographic categories as biases. We
173   define the intrinsic task as the task the model was originally trained on; this is next token prediction
174   in the models we evaluate. Accordingly, we evaluate the fairness impact of adaptation schemes by
175   comparing biases in intrinsic text generation with those of adapted models for task-specific multiple
176   choice prompts. Fig. 1 illustrates the intrinsic, zero- and few-shot prompt formatting using an example
177
      sentence. We assess the statistical significance of bias transfer by running each prompt-adaptation
      experiment across five random inference seeds impacting the ordering of the multiple-choice options;
178
      random seeds do not affect intrinsic evaluation as they do not possess answer options to randomize.
179
      In the few-shot setup, we offer two non-ambiguous sentences with the referent (occupation that a
180   pronoun unambiguously refers to) as the correct answer, one ambiguous sentence with “Unknown”
181   as the correct answer, and a query sentence from WinoBias to probe model biases (see example in
182   App. A).

187   3.2   M ETRICS
188
189
      Previous bias transfer works have employed different metrics to study intrinsic and extrinsic biases,
190
      leading to inconsistent evaluations and conflicting findings in the literature (Delobelle et al., 2022;
191
      Cao et al., 2022). This discrepancy largely stems from the use of different datasets to investigate
192   intrinsic and extrinsic biases separately. To ensure reliable bias transfer analysis, we designed new
193   unified metrics to evaluate causal models for both intrinsic biases and prompt-induced downstream
194   biases.
195
      We measure performance on the co-reference resolution task using referent prediction accuracy
196
      (RPA), which is the mean model accuracy in predicting the referent in non-ambiguous (Type 2)
197
      sentences across experimental runs. For the intrinsic evaluations, the model prediction is correct if
198   the sum of the log probabilities of referent tokens is higher than sum of the log probabilities of the
199   incorrect answer. For prompting, the model prediction is correct if the referent is present in the next
200   15 tokens generated by the model.
201
      We measure fairness using occupation selection bias (O-SB) and aggregate selection bias (A-SB),
202
      where 0% represents the ideal (no bias) case for both. O-SB is the difference in rates that an
203
      occupation is generated by a model when a male pronoun is present in a sentence vs. a female
204   pronoun (negative values implying a female-leaning bias, and positive a male-leaning bias). The
205   absolute value of these occupation-level selection biases are averaged over all occupations to compute
206   the aggregate selection bias (A-SB). The absolute value here is important to ensure that opposing
207   gendered biases do not cancel one another, so we measure the magnitude of bias.
208
      Lastly, similar to Steed et al. (2022), bias transfer between two adaptations is computed as the
209
      Pearson correlation coefficient (ρ). Here we measure the correlation between O-SB values in
210   intrinsic and prompt-based evaluations. Our bias metrics (O/A-SB) and bias transfer metric (Pearson
211   correlation) provide distinct yet valuable perspectives on model biases; while O/A-SB metrics
212   measure absolute biases, Pearson correlation assesses the alignment between intrinsic and downstream
213   biases, specifically whether biases retain their direction (pro- or anti-stereotypical) with and without
214   prompting across occupations and random seeds. When biases are aligned, it shows that the pre-
215   trained model’s biases are transferrable to downstream tasks, underscoring the need to carefully
      consider bias when selecting or training a foundation model.

408

409   by the US Bureau of Labor Statistics, similar to WinoBias) and one semantically bleached identity
410   bearing no gendered interpretations (such as “teenager” or “someone”). We reformat Winogender
411   samples to contain one stereotypically male occupation and one stereotypically female occupation, to
412   conform to the WinoBias format.
413
      Using the pre-prompt “Choose the right option for the question using the context below”, we
414
      probe Llama 3 8B with 20, 40, 60, 80 and 100 Winogender in-context examples. Each n-shot
415   context will have answers that are (1) anti-stereotypical options in non-ambiguous sentences, (2)
416   pro-stereotypical options in non-ambiguous sentences, or (3) neutral sentences with an approximately
417   equal combination of pro-stereotypical non-ambiguous sentences, anti-stereotypical non-ambiguous
418   sentences, and ambiguous sentences with “Unknown” as the correct answer. Each in-context sentence
419   will contain two occupations where both are (1) in-distribution, i.e., taken from WinoBias, or (2)
420   out-of-distribution, i.e., occupations taken from Winogender after removing duplicate and synonyms
421   to those in WinoBias (such as “physician” and “doctor”). Finally, each n-shot context will comprise
422   of occupations that are distributionally represented (1) equally, or (2) unequally. In the unequal setting,
423
      occupations are weighted such that their distribution is proportional to Llama 3 8B’s occupational
      biases in Fig. 2(a) (higher occupational representation for occupations with worse O-SB).

511   5    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
512
513
514
      Our bias evaluations are limited to the WinoBias dataset, which captures only binary gender categories;
      while Dawkins (2021) and Vanmassenhove et al. (2021) introduce gender neutral variants of the
515
      WinoBias dataset, we are unclear on when a “they / them” pronoun in a sentence is a gender neutral
516
      singular reference vs plural reference. We identify the construction of unambiguously gender neutral
517   fairness datasets as an important opportunity to better understand and improve LLM fairness. Given
518   that the WinoBias dataset captures occupations from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics, we evaluate
519   biases only for US centric occupations. Furthermore, we exclude intersectional biases from this
520   study due to their computational and analytical complexity, and suggest that analyzing intersectional
521   bias transfer is a valuable direction for future research. Next, we evaluate LLM biases using only
522   quantitative methods in this work; while we see fairness gains with the use of positive prompts in
523   Table 2, we do not qualitatively assess if improvements in A-SB come at the cost of other desirable
524   model behaviors (low toxicity or other harms), and leave this as future work.
525   Further, our findings point to important future research directions. These include developing causal
526   explanations for the link between intrinsic and extrinsic biases, understanding how prompts impact
527   models, and creating fairer pre-trained models by mitigating intrinsic biases during pre-training.
528
529
530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we study the bias transfer hypothesis for causal models under prompt adaptations. We
533
      establish that pre-trained and prompt-adapted co-reference resolution biases are strongly correlated
534   which shows that biases do transfer in prompt-adapted causal LLMs. We also find that biases in
535   models are strongly correlated even if pre-prompted to exhibit specific behaviors using fairness- and
536   bias-inducing prompts, and if few-shot composition is varied in its stereotypical makeup, number
537   of in-context samples, or occupational distribution. These findings reinforce the need be mindful
538   of the base fairness of a pre-trained model when it will be used to perform downstream tasks using
539   prompting. Following this work, we will scale up our evaluation to other adaptation strategies (such
      as low-rank and full-parameter fine-tuning).
