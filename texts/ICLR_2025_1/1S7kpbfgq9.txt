009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a manifold analysis technique for neural network representations.
012            Normalized Space Alignment (NSA) compares pairwise distances between two
013            point clouds derived from the same source and having the same size, while po-
014            tentially possessing differing dimensionalities. NSA can act as both an analytical
015            tool and a differentiable loss function, providing a robust means of comparing
016            and aligning representations across different layers and models. It satisfies the
017
               criteria necessary for both a similarity metric and a neural network loss function.
               We showcase NSA’s versatility by illustrating its utility as a representation space
018
               analysis metric, a structure-preserving loss function, and a robustness analysis tool.
019
               NSA is not only computationally efficient but it can also approximate the global
020            structural discrepancy during mini-batching, facilitating its use in a wide variety of
021            neural network training paradigms.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep learning and representation learning have rapidly emerged as cornerstones of modern artificial
027   intelligence, revolutionizing how machines interpret complex data. At the heart of this evolution is the
028   ability of deep learning models to learn efficient representations from vast amounts of unstructured
029   data, transforming it into a format where patterns become discernible and actionable. As these models
030   delve deeper into learning intricate structures and relationships within data, the necessity for advanced
031
      metrics that can accurately assess and preserve the integrity of these learned representations becomes
      increasingly evident. The ability to preserve and align the representation structure of models would
032
      enhance the performance, interpretability and robustness (Finn et al., 2017) of these models.
033
034   Despite the significant advancements achieved through representation learning in understanding
035   complex datasets, the challenge of precisely maintaining the structural integrity of representation
036   spaces remains. Current methodologies excel at optimizing the relative positions of embeddings
037
      (Chen et al., 2020; Schroff et al., 2015), yet they often overlook the challenge of preserving exact
      structural relationships to improve the performance, interpretability, and robustness of neural networks.
038
      A similarity index that is computationally efficient and differentiable would not only empower
039
      practitioners to navigate high-dimensional spaces with ease but also facilitate the seamless integration
040   of similarity-based techniques into gradient-based optimization pipelines.
041
042   We present a distance preserving structural similarity index 1 called “Normalized Space Alignment
043
      (NSA).” NSA measures both the global and the local discrepancy in structure between two spaces
      while ensuring computational efficiency. The compared representations can lie in different dimen-
044
      sional spaces provided there is a one to one mapping between the points. Globally, NSA measures
045
      the average discrepancy in pairwise distances; locally, it measures the average discrepancy in Local
046   Intrinsic Dimensionality (Houle, 2017) of each point.
047
048   While manifold learning (Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Islam et al., 2023; Fefferman et al., 2016; Genovese
049
      et al., 2012) has become integral to deep learning, when applied to deep learning, they encounter
      three primary challenges (Psenka et al., 2023): (1) the lack of an explicit projection map between
050
      the original data and its corresponding low-dimensional representation, making adaptation to unseen
051
      data difficult; (2) the inability to scale to large datasets due to computational constraints; and (3) the
052

      extensive parameter tuning required, along with the need for a predefined understanding of the data.
055   NSA aims to address these challenges while offering versatility in deep learning applications.
056
057   In line with previous works that introduce and theoretically establish similarity indices (Kornblith
058

060
      NSA’s impact in greater depth within specialized fields. Our contributions are summarized below:
061
062          • NSA is proposed as a differentiable and efficiently computable index. NSA’s quadratic
063            computational complexity (in the number of points) is much better than some of the existing
064            measures (e.g., cubic in the number of simplices for RTD (Barannikov et al., 2022)). NSA
065            is shown to be a viable loss function and its computation over mini-batches of the data is
066            proven to be representative of the global NSA value.
067          • NSA’s viability as a similarity index is evaluated in Section 4.1 and as a loss function in

079   2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Early proposed measures of Structural Similarity Indices were based on variants of “Canonical
082
      Correlation Analysis (CCA)” (Morcos et al., 2018; Raghu et al., 2017). “Centered Kernel Alignment
      (CKA)” (Kornblith et al., 2019) utilizes a Representational Similarity Matrix (RSM) with mean-
083
      centered representations and a linear kernel to measure structural similarity. But it was shown to
084
      lack sensitivity to certain representational changes (Ding et al., 2021) and not satisfy the triangle
085   inequality (Williams et al., 2021). Barannikov et al. (2022) proposed “Representation Topology
086   Divergence (RTD),” to measure the dissimilarity in multi-scale topology between two point clouds of
087   equal size with a one-to-one point correspondence. RTD has better correlation with model prediction
088   disagreements than CKA but its high computational complexity and limited practicality for larger
089   sample sizes constrain its wider application. Recently, Chen et al. (2023) proposed a filter subspace
090   based similarity measure which can compare neural network similarity by utilising the weights from
091   the convolutional filter of CNNs. Although not strictly a feature representation based similarity
092   measure, it is capable of evaluating neural network similarity more efficiently than CKA or CCA.
093   Besides RTD and CKA, multiple other similarity indices have been proposed. Alignment based
094   measures (Ding et al., 2021; Williams et al., 2022; Li et al., 2016; Hamilton et al., 2018; Wang
095   et al., 2018) aim to compare pairs of representation spaces directly. Representational Similarity
096   Matrix based measures (Shahbazi et al., 2021; Kriegeskorte et al., 2008; Kornblith et al., 2019;
097   Székely et al., 2007; Tang et al., 2020; May et al., 2019; Boix-Adsera et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022)
098   generate an RSM that describes the similarity between representations of each instance in order
099
      to avoid measuring representations directly. This helps overcome the dimensionality limitation,
      allowing RSM based measures to compute similarity regardless of what dimension the two spaces
100
      lie in. Neighborhood based measures (Schumacher et al., 2020; Hamilton et al., 2016) compare
101
      the nearest neighbors of instances in the representation space by looking at the consistency in the
102   k-nearest neighbors of each instance in the representation space. Topology based measures (Khrulkov
103   & Oseledets, 2018; Tsitsulin et al., 2020b; Barannikov et al., 2022) are motivated by the manifold
104   hypothesis. These measures aim to approximate the manifold of the representation space in terms of
105   discrete topological structures such as graphs of simplicial complexes (Goodfellow et al., 2016b).
106   Previous works are either incapable of comparing spaces across dimensions, do not satisfy one or
107   more of the invariance conditions required to be an effective similarity index, have high computational
      complexity, cannot satisfy the conditions necessary to be a pseudometric or are not differentiable.

      3     N ORMALIZED S PACE A LIGNMENT
109
110   NSA comprises of two distinct but complementary components: Local NSA (LNSA) and Global
111   NSA (GNSA). LNSA focuses on preserving the local neighborhoods of points, leveraging Local
112   Intrinsic Dimensionality to ensure nuanced neighborhood consistency. In contrast, GNSA extends
113   this analysis to a broader scale, aiming to maintain relative discrepancies between points across
114   the entire representation space. Together, these components provide a multifaceted understanding
115   of representation spaces. As a similarity index (Kornblith et al., 2019), NSA does not distinguish
116   between two point clouds that belong to the same E(n) symmetry group.
117
118   3.1     L OCAL N ORMALIZED S PACE A LIGNMENT (LNSA)
119
120
      LNSA focuses on the alignment of local neighborhoods of individual points within a dataset. This
      approach is grounded in the extensive research on Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) (Bailey et al.,
121
      2022; Houle, 2017; Tsitsulin et al., 2020a; Camastra & Staiano, 2016). Prior to defining LNSA, we
122
      provide a foundational overview of LID, setting the stage for a comprehensive understanding of how
123   LNSA leverages these principles to achieve precise alignment in local representation spaces.
124

      3.2     G LOBAL N ORMALIZED S PACE A LIGNMENT (GNSA)
196
197   GlobalNSA is a similarity index based on Representational Similarity Matrices (RSMs) (Klabunde
198   et al., 2023). Given a representation R : N × D, all RSM based measures generate a matrix of
199   instance wise similarities D ∈ RN ×N where Di,j := d(Ri , Rj ). Given two representations R and
200   R′ , two corresponding RSMs are generated. GlobalNSA is calculated by subtracting the pairwise
201   similarities between the two spaces and averaging these discrepancies to produce a single value
202   that reflects the global difference between the two representation spaces. We present the motivation
203   behind GlobalNSA and how RSMs capture global discrepancy in Appendix C.3.

222   3.3     P UTTING IT ALL TOGETHER
223
224
      We utilize GNSA for our empirical analysis as it performs well when comparing entire representation
      spaces. When using NSA as a structure-preserving loss function, we calculate the final NSA Loss
225
      through a weighted sum of local and global NSA, allowing us to adjust the emphasis between local
226
      and global perspectives. One could prioritize local neighborhood preservation, such as in classifiers
227   with tightly clustered data, or emphasize global structure, as needed in link prediction models for
228   densely interconnected graphs. Hence, NSA is defined as:
229
230
                                           NSA = l ∗ LNSA + g ∗ GNSA.                                         (10)
231   Only GNSA is used during empirical analysis while both GNSA and LNSA are used during dimen-
232   sionality reduction. We present ablation studies on how the two components affect performance,
233   quantifying the difference and visualizing it in Appendix Q.
234
235   3.3.1    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
236
      LNSA’s computational complexity is O(N 2 D + kN D) 3 and GNSA’s complexity is O(N 2 D) where
237
      N is the number of data points, k is the number of nearest neighbours considered in LNSA and D is
238
      max(D(R), D(R′ )) with R and R′ being the two representation spaces and D(·) the dimensionality
239   of the space. Hence, overall NSA has complexity of O(N 2 D + kN D). Empirically, we find that
240   computing NSA on a GPU scales quadratically up to a very high batch size (Appendix H). CKA has
241   similar complexity to NSA while RTD has a complexity of O(N 2 D + R3 ), where R is the number
242   of simplices (in practice R >> N ). Running times of NSA-AE are given in Appendix M. NSA-AE
243   is several times faster than RTD-AE and it can run on much larger batch sizes.

      4     A PPLICATIONS
246
247   NSA serves a dual purpose, acting not only as a similarity index to compare structural discrepancies
248   in representation spaces, but also as a structure preserving loss function. This enables a wide range of
249   applications where NSA could potentially be useful. We first validate NSA’s viability as a similarity
250   index using the tests proposed by (Ding et al., 2021). We then present experiments in two broad
251   domains; dimensionality reduction and adversarial attack analysis. Additional experiments on cross-
252   layer analysis (Appendix V and Appendix J), convergence prediction (Appendix W) and manifold
253   approximation (Appendix R) are presented in the appendix.

261   These initial tests are essential for validating the effectiveness of a similarity index.

332   4.2     NSA AS A L OSS F UNCTION
333
334
      The viability of NSA as a loss function is established by demonstrating its non-negativity, nullity and
      continuity, and by developing a differentiation scheme. Proofs for all four properties are presented in
335
      Appendix C and F. We also demonstrate that the expectation of NSA over a minibatch is equal to the
336
      NSA of the whole dataset, cementing its feasibility as a loss function in Appendix G.

338   4.2.1    C ONVERGENCE OF G LOBAL NSA IN MINI BATCHING
339
340   Neural networks are often trained on mini batches, which means that at any step a global structure
341   preserving loss will only have access to a subset of the data. It is vital that a structure preserving loss
342   function like GNSA converge to its global measure when used as a loss function. Similarity indices
343   usually have a high computation cost, therefore most similarity indices work with a sample of the
344
      data and show that the value computed on the sample is a good approximation of the global value.
      Additionally, for a loss function, the expectation is that the mean of the losses from the mini-batches
345
      converges to the true loss over the entire dataset. This principle underlies stochastic gradient descent
346
      (SGD) and its variants, which are foundational to training neural networks. SGD assumes that the
347   expected value of the gradients calculated on the mini-batches converges to the true gradient over the
348   entire dataset, and a similar concept applies to the expectation of the loss. In Lemma G.1 we prove
349   theoretically that in expectation, GNSA over a minibatch is equal to GNSA of the whole dataset.
350
      Figure 2 shows that subset GNSA averaged over increasing trials approximates the GNSA of the
351
      entire dataset while RTD fails to do so. Here, we use the output embeddings of two GCN models
352
      with the exact same architecture but different initializations trained on node classification onthe
353   Cora dataset. Using subset sizes of 200 and 500, we randomly sample from the original dataset and
354   compute the metrics, averaging their results and comparing it to the value of the entire dataset.
355
356
      While LNSA does not exhibit formal convergence under mini-batching, it intuitively demonstrates
      favorable behavior in such settings. LNSA’s design, which relies on nearest-neighbor computations
357
      to assess structural preservation within local neighborhoods, ensures that these neighborhoods are
358
      typically preserved in expectation. This is because the sampling process in mini-batching generally
359   retains a sufficient number of neighboring points in each batch. By appropriately adjusting the number
360   of nearest neighbors k considered in each mini-batch, LNSA is able to approximate the structural
361   consistency of local representations, ensuring that the loss function remains representative of the
362   global structure on a local scale. We present empirical convergence results on LNSA in Appendix Q
363   where we discuss the effects of varying k and observed best practices.

382   autoencoders for dimensionality reduction. A normal autoencoder minimizes the MSE loss between

386
      the encoder attempts to reduce the original data to a latent dimension and the decoder attempts to

423
424   We demonstrate that NSA-AE effectively preserves the structural integrity between the original data
425   and the latent space. However, it is imperative to note that such preservation does not inherently
426   ensure the utility of the resultant latent embeddings. Metrics like NSA, which focus on exact structure
427   preservation, excel in scenarios where the preservation of semantic relationships between data points
428
      holds paramount importance.
429   Link prediction is an ideal task to demonstrate NSA’s structure preserving ability. Successful link
430   prediction mandates a global consistency within the embedding space, necessitating that nodes with
431   likely connections are proximate while dissimilar nodes are distant. Any form of clustering or
      localized alterations to the representation structure can disrupt node interrelationships. In our study,

523    5                      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
524
525   We have demonstrated that the proposed measure of NSA is simple, efficient, and useful across
526   many aspects of analysis and synthesis of representation spaces: robustness across initializations,
527
      convergence across epochs, autoencoders, adversarial attacks, and transfer learning. Its computational
      efficiency and the ability to converge to its global value in mini batches allow it to be useful in many
528
      applications and generalizations where scalability is desired.
529
530   While NSA demonstrates substantial benefits in various applications, certain limitations must be
531   acknowledged to provide a comprehensive understanding of its scope and potential areas for future
532   improvements. The choice of Euclidean distance as the primary metric for NSA may not be optimal in
533
      high-dimensional spaces due to the curse of dimensionality (Bellman, 1961). Despite this, Euclidean
      distance remains prevalent in various domains, including contrastive (Chen et al., 2020) or triplet
534
      losses (Schroff et al., 2015), style transfer (Johnson et al., 2016) and similarity indices. NSA’s
535
      versatility across different applications is a significant strength, yet it is not a universal solution
536   suitable for all scenarios without modification. Finally, NSA is primarily a structural similarity
537   metric, which means that it focuses on preserving the geometrical configuration of data points rather
538   than their functional equivalency. This focus can lead to scenarios where NSA indicates significant
539   differences between datasets that are functionally similar but structurally distinct.

       C.3    M OTIVATION FOR GNSA
996
997    The global part of NSA is inspired by Representational Similarity Matrix (RSM)-Based Measures
998    (Klabunde et al., 2023). RSMs describe the similarity of the representation of each instance to all
999    other instances within a given representation. The elements of the RSM Si,j can be defined using a
1000   valid distance or similarity function, formally: Si,j := s(Ri , Rj ).
1001   The motivation behind RSM-based measures is to capture the relational structure in the data. By
1002   comparing the pairwise similarities of instances within two different representations, RSM-based
1003   measures provide insights into how the internal structure of representations changes. RSMs are
1004   invariant to orthogonal transformations, which means they retain their structure under rotations and
1005   reflections. When using euclidean distance as a similarity function, the RSM is invariant under
1006   euclidean group E(n), assuming translations.
1007   One might think that the most intuitive approach to compare RSMs is to use a matrix norm. This
1008   method works well when the data is similarly scaled, as it is zero when the data is identical and larger
1009   when the data is dissimilar. However, it is not invariant to scaling, which can cause issues if the data
1010   is not similarly scaled. To ensure the data is on a similar scale, we first normalize the data using the
1011   normalization function:
                                                               R
1012                                          Rnorm =
1013                                                    2 ∗ max(||xi ||)
1014   Where xi is the point farthest from the origin in the space. One of the primary reasons for our
1015   metric’s effectiveness is this normalization scheme. Although other normalizations are possible, NSA
1016   performs best when we normalize the space X by twice the distance between the origin of the space
1017
       and the farthest point in the space. This ensures that all the points are within a unit sphere in N
       dimensions where N is the dimensionality of the space. The origin of the space is the point in RN
1018
       around which all points belonging to X are centered.
1019
1020   Most popular similarity measures, such as CKA(Kornblith et al., 2019) and RTD(Barannikov et al.,
1021   2022) also generate a similarity matrix. GNSA is simple in its implementation, but our choice of
1022   distance function and unique normalization scheme make GNSA a pseudometric, a similarity index
1023
       and a valid, differentiable loss function.

1027
1028   Formally, NSA is normalized by the point that is furthest away from the origin for the representation
1029   space. In practice, NSA yields more robust results and exhibits reduced susceptibility to outliers
1030   when distances are normalized relative to a quantile of the distances from the origin (e.g., 0.98
1031   quantile). This quantile-based approach ensures that the distances are adjusted proportionally, taking
1032   into account the spread of values among the points with respect to their distances from the origin. It
1033   also ensures that any outliers do not affect the rescaling of the point cloud to the unit space.

1725   R    A PPROXIMATING THE MANIFOLD OF A REPRESENTATION SPACE
1726
1727   Incorporating a structure-preserving metric with MSE, NSA-AE maintains the Euclidean distances
       between data points as it compresses the data into a lower-dimensional space. High-dimensional data


                                                         32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
       typically resides in a lower-dimensional manifold (Goodfellow et al., 2016a), where the Euclidean
1729   distance approximates the geodesic distance. Utilizing this approximation allows NSA-AE to unfold
1730   complex datasets to their manifold dimensions, providing a clearer interpretation of underlying data
1731   relationships that are often obscured in higher dimensional ambient spaces.
1732
       Our experiments with the Swiss Roll dataset illustrate the effectiveness of this approach. Figure
1733
       14(b) and 14(c) from our results display the outcomes of dimensionality reduction when prioritizing
1734
       Euclidean distances versus geodesic distances. To compute geodesic distances for NSA, we employ
1735   the Floyd-Warshall algorithm to approximate the all pairs shortest path distances on the original
1736   space. Preserving the geodesic distances lets us construct a more accurate representation of the Swiss
1737   Roll’s original manifold structure. Conversely, in cases where the manifold dimension of the input
1738   data remains unknown, the latent dimension at which NSA minimization becomes optimal inherently
1739   corresponds to the manifold dimension of the data. This observation underscores the practical
1740   significance of understanding the manifold dimension when leveraging NSA for dimensionality
1741   reduction within an autoencoder framework.
1742   We also present results on dimensionality reduction for the Spheres Dataset from Moor et al. (2020).
1743   We show that you can use NSA-AE to reduce the 100 dimension dataset to 3 or even 2 dimensions
1744   and still preserve the structure of the representation space in Figure 15. While manifold learning
1745   techniques such as ISOMAP (Tenenbaum et al., 2000) and MDS (Kruskal, 1964) are also capable
1746   of unfolding complex datasets and preserving geodesic distances, they require access to the entire
1747   dataset for computation. This necessitates significant computational resources, particularly for large
1748   datasets. In contrast, NSA is able to achieve the same result while operating on mini-batches, making
1749
       it substantially more computationally efficient and scalable.
1750   Finally, we present reconstruction results on the Mammoth dataset. The original dataset is in 3
1751   Dimensions and NSA-AE successfully preserves the structure of the dataset in 2 Dimensions. We
1752   present this result in Figure 16.

1858   U    L AYERWISE S PECIFICITY T ESTS
1859
1860   In the second experiment, we compare the layerwise dissimilarity of differing initializations to layers
1861   of the same model trained with the same seed. In this setup, a robust metric should not only assign the
1862   lowest dissimilarity to corresponding layers across different seeds but also ensure that the dissimilarity
1863
       between corresponding layers is lower than that between non-corresponding layers within the same
       model (e.g., layer 7 vs. other layers of the same network). This analysis is crucial for quantifying
1864
       a metric’s depth insensitivity, which we define as the average number of non corresponding layers
1865
       within the same network that exhibit a lower dissimilarity than corresponding layers across different
1866   seeds. A high depth insensitivity indicates poor specificity, as it implies that the metric fails to
1867   differentiate layers in different networks from non-corresponding layers within the same network.
1868
1869
       We observe that NSA exhibits the lowest depth insensitivity, with an average of 1.11 layers being
       incorrectly prioritized over corresponding layers in different networks. In contrast, CKA and RTD
1870
       show average depth insensitivity values of 2.33 and 1.88, respectively. These results are in line with
1871
       the experiments proposed by Ding et al. (2021) to quantify the performance of similarity indices.
1872
1873   Both the specificity experiments use the same experimental setup, with six models trained indepen-
1874   dently on CIFAR-10 using different initialization seeds. Similarity computations for all metrics are
1875
       averaged over 10 trials, with subset sizes of 4000 for NSA and 400 for RTD, following their respective
       recommendations. We also average results across three sets of pairwise comparisons between the
1876
       models to ensure reliability and present the standard deviations across the sets. Standard deviations
1877
       across trials are orders of magnitude smaller than the mean and do not affect the reported trends and
1878   are therefore not reported. Figure 18 showcases the results of our layerwise experiments.

       V     E MPIRICAL A NALYSIS ON A DDITIONAL DATASETS
1945
1946
       Empirical analysis of intermediate representations with similarity measures can help uncover the
1947   intricate workings of various neural network architectures. When two networks that are struc-
1948   turally identical but trained from different initial conditions are compared, we expect that, for each
1949   layer’s intermediate representation, the most similar representation in the other model should be
1950   the corresponding layer that matches in structure. We can also perform cross-layer analysis and
1951   cross-downstream task analysis of the intermediate representations of a neural network to help
1952   uncover learning patterns of a network. We use 4 different GNN architectures; GCN (Kipf & Welling,
1953   2017), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), ClusterGCN (Chiang et al., 2019) and Graph Attention
1954   Network (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2018). We evaluate with Sanity Tests, Cross Architecture Tests,
1955
       Downstream Task tests and Convergence Tests. We utilize the Amazon Computers Dataset (Shchur
       et al., 2019), Cora (McCallum et al., 2000), Citeseer (Giles et al., 1998), Pubmed (Sen et al., 2008)
1956
       and Flickr (Zeng et al., 2020) Datasets for our experiments.

       As a neural network starts converging, the representation structure of the data stops changing
2327   significantly and the loss stops dropping drastically. Ideally, a similarity index should be capable of
2328   capturing this change. We examine epoch-wise convergence by comparing representations between
2329   the current and final epochs. We observe that NSA starts dropping to low values as the test accuracy
2330   stops increasing significantly. This shows that NSA can be used to help approximate convergence and
2331   even potentially be used as an early stopping measure during neural network training, stopping the
2332   weight updates once the structure of your intermediate representations stabilize. We show results for
2333   all 4 architectures on node classification and link prediction. Link Prediction models were trained for
2334   200 epochs and show similar patterns to the ones observed with node classification. All convergence
2335
       tests are performed with NSA only. We show the convergence tests on all 4 architectures on Node
       Classification in Figure 31 and on Link Prediction in Figure 30 .
