011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Meta-learning has been demonstrated to be useful to improve the sampling effi-
014            ciency of Bayesian optimization (BO) and surrogate-assisted evolutionary algo-
015            rithms (SAEAs) when solving expensive optimization problems (EOPs). How-
016
               ever, existing studies focuses on only single-objective optimization, leaving
               other expensive optimization scenarios unconsidered. We propose a generalized
017
               few-shot evolutionary optimization (FSEO) framework and focus on its perfor-
018
               mance on two common expensive optimization scenarios: multi-objective EOPs
019            (EMOPs) and constrained EOPs (ECOPs). We develop a novel meta-learning
020            modeling approach to train surrogates for our FSEO framework, an accuracy-
021            based update strategy is designed to adapt surrogates during the optimization pro-
022            cess. The surrogates in FSEO framework combines neural network with Gaussian
023            Processes (GPs), their network parameters and some parameters of GPs repre-
024            sent useful experience and are meta-learned across related optimization tasks, the
025            remaining GPs parameters are task-specific parameters that represent unique fea-
026            tures of the target task. We demonstrate that our FSEO framework is able to im-
027
               prove sampling efficiency on both EMOP and ECOP. Empirical conclusions are
               made to guide the application of our FSEO framework.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Expensive optimization problems (EOPs) aim to find as good as possible solutions within a budget
      of limited solution evaluations. Conventional Bayesian optimization (BO) and surrogate-assisted
034
      evolutionary algorithms (SAEAs) have been widely used to solve EOPs, but they train surrogate
035
      models from the scratch. To further improve the sampling efficiency and optimization performance,
036   many efforts have been made to pre-train surrogates with the prior experience gain from related
037   optimization tasks, resulting in experience-based optimization algorithms Bai et al. (2023); Liu et al.
038   (2017); Tang et al. (2021); Tan et al. (2021).
039
      This work considers solving EOPs on the context of few-shot problems Chen et al. (2019); Wang
040
      et al. (2020), where plenty of expensive related tasks are available and each of them can provide a
041
      small dataset for experience learning. Therefore, many experience-based optimization approaches
042   such as multi-tasking optimization Wei et al. (2021); Bali et al. (2019); Xue et al. (2020), transfer op-
043   timization Tan et al. (2021); Jiang et al. (2020b;a) are not considered as they cannot learn experience
044   from small related tasks (A discussion is available in Appendix A.1). In comparison, meta-learning
045   Hospedales et al. (2021) has been proved to be powerful in solving few-shot problems, leading to
046   a new subcategory of experience-based optimization, namely few-shot optimization (FSO) Wistuba
047   & Grabocka (2021).
048   Existing studies on FSO are mainly few-shot Bayesian optimization (FSBO) where meta-learning
049   approaches are combined with BO to solve EOPs with only one objective. These studies either
050   employ meta-learning models from the literature directly or focus on the meta-learning of acquisi-
051   tion functions (AFs) that are customized for BO. In this paper, we develop a novel meta-learning
052   architecture to enhance modeling performance and propose a generalized few-shot evolutionary op-
053   timization (FSEO) framework to address EOPs from the perspective of SAEAs. We demonstrate
      the generality and applicability of FSEO by considering two popular expensive optimization sce-

054
      narios which have been limited studied but have a higher requirement on modeling performance
055   than expensive single-objective optimization: multi-objective EOPs (EMOPs) and constrained EOPs
056   (ECOPs). Major contributions are summarized as follows.
057
058         • A novel meta-learning method, namely Meta Deep Kernel Learning (MDKL), is devel-
059           oped to gain prior experience from related expensive tasks. Our model architecture and
060           parameter designs make it possible to generate a regression-based surrogate on the prior
061           experience and then continually adapt the surrogate to approximate the target task.
062         • We propose a FSEO framework to solve EOPs from the perspective of SAEAs. FSEO
063           framework is applicable to regression-based SAEAs since FSEO embed our meta-learning
064           models in these SAEAs as their surrogates. In addition, an update strategy is designed to
065           adapt surrogates constantly during the optimization. Note that our FSEO framework is a
066
              general framework but we focus on its performance on EMOPs and ECOPs in this paper.
067         • Experiments are conducted on EMOPs and ECOPs to show our FSEO framework is effec-
068           tive. Our comprehensive ablation studies discover the influence of some factors on FSEO
069           performance and provide empirical guidance to the application of FSEO framework.

071   2   R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Experience-based optimization can be divided into several subcategories according to the techniques
074   of learning prior experience from related tasks. A detailed classification and discussion on these
075   subcategories is available in Appendix A.1. This subsection focuses on related work on FSO.
076   FSO studies in the literature can be classified based on their model architectures. Most studies meta-
077   learn parameters for Gaussian Processes (GPs) Williams & Rasmussen (2006), namely FSBO or
078   Meta Bayesian Optimization (MBO) Shala et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2021); Pan et al. (2023); Tigh-
079   ineanu et al. (2023). In addition, Maraval et al. (2023) meta-learns with transformer neural processes
080   and Wistuba & Grabocka (2021); Chen et al. (2023) meta-learn parameters for the architecture of
081   deep kernel learning (DKL) Wilson et al. (2016). The MDKL model in our FSEO belongs to the
082   last category as its model architecture is relevant to DKL.
083   Our work is different from existing studies in three points: Firstly, the novel architecture of meta-
084   learning model for optimization purpose. Many studies Wistuba & Grabocka (2021) use existing
085   meta-learning models Patacchiola et al. (2020) as their surrogates. During the optimization pro-
086   cess, these surrogates make predictions with newly observed data, which is a kind of data adaptation
087   rather than model parameter adaptation. The parameters in these models are trained and fixed before
088   the optimization process begins, no further parameter adaptations are made during the optimization
089   since these meta-learning models are originally designed for regression or classification tasks rather
090
      than optimization tasks. In comparison, we develop a meta-learning model, MDKL, for optimiza-
      tion purpose. MDKL has novel model architecture with explicit task-specific parameters, which
091
      allows continual model parameter adaptations and thus improves modeling performance during the
092
      optimization. Secondly, the generality and broad applicability of FSEO. Existing works are mainly
093   customized for specific algorithms or optimization problems. For example, the meta-learning set-
094   tings for AFs Watanabe et al. (2023) are not applicable to the SAEAs without AFs. However, our
095   FSEO work on the meta-learning of surrogates and it is applicable to various SAEAs, so our work
096   widens the scope of existing FSO research. A detailed discussion between BO and SAEA is pre-
097   sented in Appendix A.2. In addition, most existing FSO studies investigated only global optimiza-
098   tion, leaving other optimization scenarios such as EMOP and ECOP still awaiting for investigation.
099   In contrast, as our MDKL is designed for optimization and is capable of continually adaptation, we
100   pay attention on EMOPs and ECOPs which require more effective models than global optimization.
101
      Lastly, in-depth ablation studies are lacking in the literature, making it unclear which factors affect
      the performance of FSO. Our extensive ablation studies fill this gap and we conclude some empirical
102
      rules to improve the performance of FSO.

      3.1   M ETA -L EARNING IN F EW-S HOT P ROBLEMS
109
110   In the context of few-shot problems, we have plenty of related tasks, each task T contributes a couple
111   of small datasets D = {(S, Q)}, namely support dataset S and query dataset Q, respectively. After
112   learning from datasets of random related tasks, a support set S∗ from new unseen task T∗ is given
113   and one is asked to estimate the labels or values of a query set Q∗ . The problem is called 1-shot or
114   5-shot when only 1 data point or 5 data points are provided in S∗ . A comprehensive definition of
115   few-shot problems is available in Chen et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020).
116   Meta-learning methods have been widely used to solve few-shot problems Wang et al. (2020). They
117   learn domain-specific features that are shared among related tasks as experience, such experience is
118   used to understand and interpret the data collected from new tasks encountered in the future.
119
120   3.2   D EEP K ERNEL L EARNING (DKL)
121
122   DKL aims at constructing kernels that encapsulate the expressive power of deep architectures for
123   GPs. To create expressive and scalable closed form covariance kernels, DKL combines the non-
124   parametric flexibility of kernel methods and the structural properties of deep neural networks. In
125
      practice, a deep kernel k(xi , xj |γ) transforms the inputs x of a base kernel k(xi , xj |θ) through a
      non-linear mapping given by a deep architecture φ(x|w, b):
126
127                                k(xi , xj |γ) = k(φ(xi |w, b), φ(xj |w, b)|θ),                         (1)
128
129   where θ and (w, b) are parameter vectors of the base kernel and the deep architecture, respectively.
130   γ = {θ, w, b} is the set of all parameters in this deep kernel. Note that in DKL, all parameters
131
      γ of a deep kernel k(xi , xj |γ) are learned jointly by using the log marginal likelihood function of
      GPs as a loss function. Such a jointly learning strategy has been shown to make a DKL algorithm
132
      outperform a combination of a deep neural network and a GP model, where a trained GP model is
133
      applied to the output layer of a trained deep neural network Wilson et al. (2016).
134
135
      3.3   M ETA -L EARNING ON DKL
136
137   An important distinction between DKL algorithms and the applications of meta-learning to DKL
138   is that DKL algorithms learn their deep kernels from single tasks instead of collections of related
139   tasks. Such a difference alleviates two drawbacks of single task DKL Tossou et al. (2019): First, the
140   scalability of deep kernels is no longer an issue as datasets in meta-learning are small. Second, the
141   risk of overfitting is decreased since diverse data points are sampled across tasks.

522   6   C ONCLUSION AND FURTHER WORK
523
524
525
      Conclusion. In this paper, we present a FSEO framework to address EMOPs and ECOPs from the
      perspective of SAEAs. A novel meta-learning approach MDKL is proposed to learn prior experi-
526
      ence from related expensive tasks. Our MDKL model is designed for optimization and has explicit
527
      task-specific parameters, which allows continually update of task-specific parameters during the op-
528   timization process. Our empirical experiments show that the FSEO framework is able to improve
529   the sampling efficiency and thus save expensive evaluations for existing regression-based SAEAs.
530   Ablation studies reveal the influence between optimization performance and solutions similarity as
531   well as the size of datasets for meta-learning.
532
      Limitation and future work. The limitations of this work can be summarized as the following two
533
      points: First, we do not have a mathematical definition of related tasks. As a result, the boundary
534   between related and unrelated tasks is not clear, making it difficult to conduct theoretical analysis on
535   task similarity. Second, the proposed framework is currently for regression-based SAEAs only. A
536   detailed discussion on this point is available in Appendix B. Future work could focus on quantifying
537   task similarity by proposing a metric to measure the similarity between tasks. With an appropriate
538   task similarity measure, systematic studies on few-shot optimization and experience-based optimiza-
539   tion could be conducted. In addition, few-shot optimization framework for other SAEA categories
      can also be a future work.

      A     D ISCUSSION ON R ELEVANT C ONCEPTS
757
758
      A.1   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN E XPERIENCE -BASED O PTIMIZATION
759
760   In the past decade, experience-based optimization has attracted much attention as it uses the ex-
761   perience gained from other optimization problems to improve the optimization efficiency of target
762   problems, which mimics human capabilities of cognitive and knowledge generalization Gupta et al.
763   (2017). The optimization problems that provide experience or knowledge are regarded as source
764   tasks, while the target optimization problems are regarded as target tasks. To obtain useful expe-
765   rience, the tasks that are related to target tasks are chosen as source tasks since they usually share
766
      domain-specific features with target tasks. Diverse experience-based optimization methods have
      been proposed to use the experience gained from related tasks to tackle target tasks. They can be
767
      divided into two categories based on the direction of experience transformation.
768
769   In the first category, experience is transformed mutually. Every considered optimization problem
770   is a target task and also is one of the source tasks of other optimization problems. In other words,
771   the roles of source task and target task are compatible. One representative tributary is EMTO that
772
      aims to solve multiple optimization tasks concurrently Ding et al. (2017); Wei et al. (2021); Liaw
      & Ting (2019); Bali et al. (2019); Xue et al. (2020). In EMTO, experience is learned, updated,
773
      and spontaneously shared among target tasks through multi-task learning techniques. A variant of
774
      EMTO is multiforms optimization Gupta et al. (2017); Zhang et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2022). In
775   multiforms optimization, multi-task learning methods are employed to learn experience from distinct
776   formulations of a single target task.
777
778
      In the second category, experience is transformed unidirectionally. The roles of source task and
      target task are not compatible, an optimization problem cannot serve as a source task and a target
779
      task simultaneously. One popular tributary is transfer optimization which employs transfer learning
780
      techniques to transform experience from source tasks to target tasks Tan et al. (2021); Jiang et al.
781   (2020b;a); Volpp et al. (2020). In transfer learning, experience can be transformed from a single
782   source task, multiple source tasks, or even source tasks from a different domain Zhuang et al. (2020).
783   However, these transfer learning techniques pay more attention to experience transformation instead
784   of experience learning. Despite diverse and complex situations of experience transformation have
785   been studied Ruan et al. (2019; 2020), the difficult of learning experience from small (expensive)
786   source tasks has not been well studied. Actually, a common scenario in transfer learning is that the
787   source task(s) is/are large enough such that useful experience can be obtained easily through solving
788   source task(s) Zhuang et al. (2020). In contrast to transfer optimization, recently, some experience-
789
      based optimization algorithms attempted to use meta-learning methods to learn experience from
      small source tasks, which are known as few-shot optimization (FSO)Wistuba & Grabocka (2021).
790
      Since meta-learning only works for related tasks in the same domain, the situations of experience
791
      transformation are less complex than that of transfer learning. As a result, meta-learning pays more
792   attention to experience learning instead of experience transformation. Domain-specific features are
793   extracted as experience and no related task needs to be solved.
794
795
      Our work belongs to the FSO in the second category discussed above since our experience is trans-
      formed unidirectionally. More importantly, our experience is learned across many related expensive
796
      tasks, rather than gained through solving more or less source tasks. Therefore, our work is different
797
      from transfer optimization.

      A.2   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION AND S URROGATE -A SSISTED
800
            E VOLUTIONARY A LGORITHM
801
802   Bayesian Optimization (BO) and Surrogate-Assisted Evolutionary Algorithm (SAEA) are both
803   model-based optimization methods for solving expensive optimization problems. The difference
804   between BO and SAEA can be summarized as follows:
805
806         1. Surrogate models type. BO uses probabilistic models, such as GPs, as surrogates. In
807
               comparison, SAEAs are flexible and can use any type of approximation model, not limited
               to probabilistic models.
808
809         2. Selection criterion. BO designs an acquisition function (AF) as the selection criterion
               for candidate solutions, explicitly considering the uncertainty in the probabilistic models.


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
               However, SAEAs do not necessarily account for model uncertainty. Instead, they focus on
811            diversity and convergence as selection criteria, which can be implemented through separate
812            functions.
813
            3. Search algorithm. BO has no limitation on the search algorithm and can use either gradient-
814
               based or gradient-free optimization (such as EAs) to search candidate solutions. In contrast,
815
               SAEAs use only EAs as their underlying optimization algorithms.
816
817   As a result, there is some overlap between BO and SAEAs. A typical example is ParEGO, which
818   employs GPs as its surrogates and designs an expected improvement (EI) function as its AF to con-
819   sider uncertainty. Additionally, an EA is used as the underlying search algorithm.
820   Our FSEO framework focuses on meta-learning surrogates instead of AFs, making it compatible
821   with various SAEAs that do not rely on model uncertainty or AFs as selection criteria. In compar-
822   ison, existing studies mainly work on the meta-learning of AFs, which limits their generality and
823
      applicability to SAEAs.
824
825   B    D ISCUSSION ON F RAMEWORK C OMPATIBILITY AND L IMITATION
826
827   Our FSEO framework is applicable to regression-based SAEAs as our MDKL surrogates can be
828   embedded in these SAEAs directly. Classification-based SAEAs are not compatible with our FSEO
829   framework. The classification surrogates in these SAEAs are employed to learn the relation between
830   pairs of solutions, or the relation between solutions and a set of reference solutions. The class labels
831   used for surrogate training can be fluctuating during the optimization and thus hard to be learned
832
      over related tasks. Similarly, in ordinal-regression-based SAEAs, the ordinal relation values to be
      learned are not as stable as the fitness of expensive functions. So ordinal-regression-based SAEAs
833
      are also not compatible with our FSEO framework. In this paper, we focus on FSO for regression-
834
      based SAEAs, while other SAEA categories are left to be discussed in future work.

1071   DirHVEI Zhao & Zhang (2023). Among these algorithms, ParEGO, K-RVEA, KTA2, KMOEA-
1072   TIC use regression-based surrogates, CSEA uses a classification-based surrogate, OREA employs

1227
       In real-world applications, it is optimistic to assume that some related tasks are very similar to the
       target task. A more common situation is that all related tasks have limited similarity to the target
1228
       task. To investigate the relationship between task similarity and FSEO optimization performance, we
1229
       also test the performance in an ‘out-of-range’ situation, where the original DTLZ is excluded from
1230   the range of DTLZ variants during the MDKL meta-learning procedure. As a result, only the DTLZ
1231   variants that are quite different from the original DTLZ problem can be used to learn experience.
1232   The ‘out-of-range’ situation eliminates the probability that MDKL surrogates benefit greatly from
1233   the DTLZ variants that are very similar to the original DTLZ problem. Detailed definitions of the
1234   related tasks used in the ‘out-of-range’ situation are given in Appendix C. Apart from the related
1235   tasks used, the remaining experimental setups are the same as the setups described in Section 5.1.
1236   For the sake of convenience, we denote the situation we tested in Section 5.1 as ‘in-range’ below.
1237   The statistical test results reported in Table 9 show that the ‘out-of-range’ situation achieves com-
1238   petitive IGD+ values to the ‘in-range’ situation on all 7 test instances. This suggests that related
1239   tasks that are very similar to the target task have a limited impact on the optimization performance
1240   of our FSEO framework. Useful experience can be learned from related tasks that are not very sim-
1241   ilar to the target task. Crucially, when comparing the performance of the ‘out-of-range’ situation

1281

1282

1285   useful experience can be learned from related tasks, which further improves the performance of
1286
       experience-based optimization. Therefore, when applying our FSEO framework to real-world opti-
       mization problems, it is preferable to collect more data from related tasks for experience learning.

       MDKL model outperforms the compared meta-learning modeling and non-meta-learning modeling

1515   Second, we demonstrate the main contribution of this work via different EMOP benchmarks: In most
1516
       situations, the proposed FSEO framework can assist regression-based SAEAs to reach competitive
       or even better optimization results, while the cost of surrogate initialization is only 1d samples. Due
1517
       to the effectiveness of saving evaluations, our FSEO framework is preferable to other SAEAs when
1518
       solving problems within a very limited evaluation budget. Moreover, some empirical guidelines are
1519   concluded to help the application of our FSEO framework. For the influence of task similarity, we
1520   find that related tasks that are very similar to the target task are not necessary to the application
1521   of our approach. The influence of these similar tasks on the optimization performance is limited.
1522   Our FSEO framework can achieve competitive results without datasets from very similar related
1523   tasks. Besides, for the related tasks used for meta-learning, we have demonstrated that more useful
1524   experience can be learned if more data points are sampled from related tasks.
1525   Third, the effectiveness of our FSEO framework is validated on a real-world engine calibration
1526   problem. Competitive or better results are achieved on the objective and constraint functions, while
1527   1d samples are used to initialize surrogates. Therefore, our FSEO framework can also be applied to
1528   popular optimization scenarios such as constrained optimization, showing the generality and broad
1529   applicability of FSEO.
