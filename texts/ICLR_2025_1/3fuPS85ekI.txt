009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit varying comprehension
012            levels in language and perception that complicate interacting with a diverse pop-
013            ulation of agents, similar to how miscommunication happens in humans, e.g.,
014            because intentions are not always known. In this work, we investigate whether
015            MLLMs can adapt to the perceptual weaknesses of the communication partners
016            in an online manner, i.e. change the way they describe their environment in a
017            way that is understandable to their partner while communicating with them, via
018
               reinforcement learning. We experiment with two tasks: referring expression iden-
               tification (REI) and referring expression segmentation (RES), where a speaker
019
               agent has to describe an object, and a listener has to identify it. To be successful,
020
               the speaker agent must discern the comprehension level of the listener and adapt
021            accordingly, especially when the listener suffers from perceptual weaknesses such
022            as color blindness or blurred vision. Unlike traditional offline alignment meth-
023            ods for LLMs, we fine-tune a Multimodal LLM (MLLM) online to adapt to other
024            agents’ conceptual understanding. Our experiments with four MLLMs on four
025            datasets show that online adaptation is feasible in both REI and RES settings.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) and by extension Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
031   have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a variety of tasks (Bubeck et al., 2023; Piergio-
032   vanni et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Team, 2024; Anil et al., 2023) When catering MLLMs with
033
      different architectures, e.g. vision backbones, language backbones, trained with different datasets
      etc, in our daily lives, we may notice the variability in their comprehension levels related to task-
034
      specific concepts, i.e. what resonates with some MLLMs might not be clear to others. Disparities
035
      may exist both in their natural language understanding, e.g., some might understand expert termi-
036   nology while another might require descriptive explanations, and in the perceptual understanding of
037   visual information, e.g., some might have disabilities such as blurred vision or color blindness.
038
039
      In this work, we focus on enabling MLLMs to adapt to perceptual misunderstandings of their com-
      munication partners, e.g., not perceiving colors correctly and therefore not responding to color at-
040
      tributes presented to them. Specifically, we fine-tune the MLLM online, i.e. on-the-fly, while it is
041
      interacting with another MLLM, based on its observed behavior. We model sequential interactions
042   between pairs of agents during a vision-language referring expression tasks which is used as an en-
043   vironment for both adaptation and evaluation. Given one or two images, the speaker agent needs
044   to describe the discriminating features of a target object, while the listener agent has to identify the
045   correct object based on this description. To enhance overall task performance, the speaker has to
046   learn which feature of the image allows the listener agent to discriminate the target object and adapt
047   its communication based on the visual concepts understood by the listeners. We consider a referring
048   expression identification (REI) task, where the listener has to identify one target image from a set of
049   two images, and a referring expression segmentation (RES) task, where the listener has to segment
050
      the target object within a single image correctly. We present both settings in Fig. 1.
051   We employ several open-source MLLMs, namely LLaVA-7B, LLaVA-13B (Liu et al., 2023b),
052   Qwen (Bai et al., 2023), and PaliGemma (Beyer et al., 2024) as the speaker and listener agents
053   where the difference in MLLM capabilities and pre-training datasets simulate significant diversity.
      In addition, we introduce perceptual weaknesses to some listeners by providing them with blurred

054
      or grayscaled images to further increase listener variety. As the benchmark, we take inspiration
055   from Corona et al. (2019), but create a more realistic setting by modeling the interactions as free-
056   form text, adding image transformations to simulate challenging adaptation scenarios, and scaling it
057   to MLLMs. We evaluate the REI task on CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017), CUB (Wah et al., 2011),
058   and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), while we use the RefCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) dataset
059   to implement the RES task. We adapt the MLLMs on the fly using PPO (Schulman et al., 2017),
060   KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), and NLPO (Ramamurthy et al., 2023) developed originally as prefer-
061   ence learning methods for LLMs when fine-tuning the LoRA adapters (Hu et al., 2022). Contrary to
062   the typical use case of these algorithms for preference optimization (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ahmadian
063
      et al., 2024) where a carefully curated offline dataset of human preferences is collected, we test their
      efficacy during online interactions which is a more realistic and noisier setting.
064
065   Our contributions are as follows: 1) We introduce a flexible framework for evaluating four MLLMs
066   and adapting them on the fly using four RL algorithms on natural-language-based communication
067   tasks on four datasets to test their efficacy in online adaptation to a diverse set of communication
068   partners. 2) We provide insights into the decision-making process of MLLMs finding that concepts
069
      related to color and shape are most important for performing well on these tasks. 3) Through exten-
      sive experimental results on two different communication tasks, four MLLMs, and four datasets, we
070
      show that adaptation is possible both the REI and RES task.

073   2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075   A number of methods aim for parameter efficient adaption of large (language) models, which adapt
076   a subset or an additional set of the parameters. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) and its variants (Zhang et al.,
077   2023; Lialin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024; Sheng et al., 2023; yang Liu et al., 2024)
078   add a trainable residual low rank adaption for each matrix in the network, potentially quantizing
079   it (Dettmers et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). In contrast, sparse methods (Ben Za-
080   ken et al., 2022; Ansell et al., 2021) only adapt small subsets of the parameters. Adapter based
081
      methods (Pfeiffer et al., 2020) train adapter layers and yet another approach is to train a completely
      separate ladder side networks (Sung et al., 2022; Mercea et al., 2024). As we aim to adapt large
082
      multimodal models online, we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for adaptation.
083
084   For adapting an MLLM to obtain a desired functionality, such as the ability to adapt to a listener
085   online, different RL methods (Snell et al., 2023; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ramamurthy et al., 2023) can
086   be used. Proximal policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) is an on-policy actor critic
087
      algorithm, which is extended by NLPO (Ramamurthy et al., 2023). It restricts the action space to
      a nucleus of most likely tokens. In contrast KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) directly optimizes the
088
      LLM from binary preferences. On the other hand DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) requires positive and
089
      negative pairs for the same context. All of the methods apart from DPO use a single reward per
090   generation making them suitable for our task, thus, we compare their performance. Similar to our
091   work (Guo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) perform (online) adaption based on model feedback in the
092   context of generic model alignment, while we focus on personalization to individual conversational
093   partners and their misunderstandings.
094
      Personalizing generative language models has been studied for a long time, often viewed in the
095
      context of building an efficient conversational partner in dialogue systems (Serban et al., 2015; Song
096   et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019). In contrast, (Ma et al., 2023) reviews several theory of mind
097   (TOM) based approaches to personalization, such as (Takmaz et al., 2023) which proposes a plug-
098   and-play TOM based on an explicit simulator, that updates a copy of the model weights on the
099   fly. Similarly, (Raileanu et al., 2018) internally models the behavior of the listener. In contrast, we
100   only update a small amount of parameters using LoRA and do not need to simulate the listeners
101   behavior. (Wang et al., 2024a) adapts the speaker and listener differently, but studies the text-only
102   task, whereas we consider a multi-modal image reference game. We follow an online approach,
103   while (Ma et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2022) personalizes chatbots by learning from large-scale user
104
      dialogue history.
105   Image identification tasks have been studied in visual dialogue settings in (de Vries et al., 2016; Ni
106   et al., 2021; Alaniz et al., 2021; Das et al., 2016). Our work extends this, by incorporating impair-
107   ments in the communication. (Corona et al., 2019) has studied conceptual image understanding
      through a reference game, but we extend their attribute constrained setting to free text generation.

      We present a framework for referring expression communication tasks (Figure 1) where a speaker
129   agent describes images to a listener agent using visual concepts. The “speaker” is a single learner
130   that participates in sequences of K episodes describing an image to a group of “listeners”.
131
132
      Referring Expression Identification (REI) Task. In the REI task, each episode involves the
133
      speaker π (s) and listener π (l) being presented with a pair of images [xtk , xck ]. The speaker is assigned
      one image as the target xtk and the other as a confounding image xck . The speaker then generates a
134                  (s)
135   description mk as a message to the listener for it to make its guess regarding the target’s identity
        (l)
136   mk , i.e. left or right image. The speaker will observe whether its description led to a correct or
137   incorrect guess via a reward rk ∈ {+1, −1} communicated for every episode.
138
      Referring Expression Segmentation (RES) Task. In the RES task, the speaker π (s) and listener
139
      π (l) are presented with a single image xk in each episode. The speaker additionally receives the
140                                                                                        (s)
      bounding box of a target object otk for which the speaker generates a description mk with the in-
141
      tention to identify the object in the context of the image. Given the speaker’s message, the listener
142                                        (l)
      generates a segmentation mask mk as a guess regarding the target object in the image. The in-
143
      tersection over union (IoU) metric between the predicted and the ground-truth segmentation masks
144
      serves as a reward of the episode for the speaker.
145
146   Based on this feedback from the reward alone, the speaker’s goal is to change its policy π (s)∗ ,
147   i.e., adapt its image description, to maximize the success rate of the listener agent to solve the
148   referring expression task. To further increase the difficulty of each task, any listener may suffer from
149
      a perceptual weaknesses, i.e., color blindness or blurry vision, which is unknown to the speaker.
150   Since the listener operates as a black box from the perspective of the speaker, pinpointing the source
151   of errors when they exhibit unexpected behavior can be challenging. When the listener makes an
152   incorrect guess, identifying the source of the error becomes difficult, e.g., it could be a lack of
153   comprehension in language, or the visual concepts used to describe the image.
154   When the listener fails to guess the correct object, the speaker should explore different descriptions
155   to find a policy tailored for the listener. In this work, we examine, whether LLM adaptation methods
156   can successfully find policies that maximize task performance for a diverse set of listener agents
157   solely from the reward signal in this multimodal, i.e. vision and language-based, framework.

      3.2    E FFICIENT A DAPTATION OF THE S PEAKER AGENT
232
233   Online adaptation of an MLLM does not only require a suitable optimization algorithm, but it should
234   also be feasible in terms of update speed and flexibility as a common use-case may involve a speaker
235   agent interacting with several listeners in parallel. As full-fine-tuning MLLMs is computationally
236   expensive, we adapt these methods by using a parameter-efficient fine tuning method. Given the ver-
237   satility of LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) for both the visual domain and the text domain, and its simplicity,
238   we employ it in our architecture. We add LoRA adapters on each linear layer in the LLM-module
239
      of the network. As a result, the total number of tuneable parameters are orders of magnitude smaller
      than the total number of parameters in the MLLM. One can initialize one set of LoRA adapters for
240
      each listeners and effortlessly swap out LoRA parameters when interacting with multiple listeners.
241
242   We employ LLaVA-7B as the speaker model for all experiments because it fits into the memory of a
243   single GPU while training with LoRA adapters. Since the listener runs in inference mode, we also
244   evaluate on LLaVA-13B, Qwen , and PaliGemma to increase listener diversity.

      Datasets. We propose a framework for referring expression tasks on four datasets: CLEVR(Johnson
      et al., 2017), CUB (Wah et al., 2011), ImageNet(Deng et al., 2009) for REI, and RefCOCO
256
      (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) for RES. CLEVR contains images with objects of varying attributes (size,
257
      color, material), requiring fine-grained reasoning to distinguish between different CLEVR scenes.
258   CUB and ImageNet feature natural images with more conversationally relevant concepts. For REI
259   on these datasets, we sample two images, randomly select one as the target, and ask the speaker to
260   describe it in contrast to the other image. We shuffle their order when presenting the images to the
261   listener to avoid trivial solutions, such as “the left image is the target image”. Further, we ensure
262   the images come from different classes for CUB and ImageNet. For RES, we employ RefCOCO
263   which extends COCO (Lin et al., 2014) with human-annotated referring expressions and bounding
264   box/segmentation mask annotations. This task requires contrasting a specific detail within an im-
265   age’s context, posing a different challenge from REI. To visually prompt the speaker on the target
266
      object, following Shtedritski et al. (2023) we use a red circle as big as the ground truth bounding
      box.

305   4.2    E VALUATING L ISTENERS WITH G ROUND -T RUTH D ESCRIPTIONS ON CLEVR
306
307   CLEVR’s detailed scene descriptions allow us to construct a ground-truth (GT) speaker agent for
308   the REI task that produces image descriptions with perfect perception and reasoning abilities. This
309   enables us to evaluate listeners given an ideal speaker. The produced descriptions mention all at-
310   tributes that appear at least once in the target image, but do not exist in the confounding image. We
311   also ablate the GT speaker by omitting one attribute type, measuring the importance of each attribute
312   for REI. Examples of these image descriptions are shown in Fig. 4.
313   We evaluate our listener agents alongside GPT-4V, to obtain a reference for a state-of-the-art MLLM,
314   and present the results in Fig. 3. We observe that when all attributes are present, GPT-4V performs
315   best (0.99), followed by LLaVA-7B and LLaVA-13B (0.83 and 0.73), with QWEN being the weak-
316   est model (0.63) . Removing size and material attributes has little impact on performance, except
317   for a slight increase in LLaVA-13B and QWEN’s scores, indicating that size information is more
318   confusing than helpful for these models, possibly because of perspective. In contrast, omitting shape
319   information significantly affects GPT-4V’s performance (from 0.99 to 0.84), while the other listeners
320
      are less affected, showing that GPT-4V is more sensitive to shape than other models.
321   Most notably, removing color information results in significant performance drops across all listen-
322   ers, highlighting its importance for solving the REI task on CLEVR. These findings demonstrate
323   that different MLLMs prioritize different attributes and have varying capabilities, as shown in Fig.
      3. Even GPT-4V struggles to solve the task without color or shape information.

524   6   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we introduce a framework for two referring expression tasks (REI/RES) involving com-
527   municating MLLM agents. On these tasks, we study how MLLM agents can adapt to one another
528   on-the-fly. Our online adaptation setting is significantly more challenging than aligning MLLMs on
529   carefully collected offline datasets, while opening up new applications that require individual per-
530   sonalization. Every communication partner understands language and concepts required to solve the
531
      tasks at different levels and we introduce perceptual weaknesses to further control for agent variety.
      The referring expression tasks pose a challenge to currently available MLLMs, especially for images
532
      with fine-grained differences, and when precise segmentation is required. All the adaptation algo-
533
      rithms we have tested could improve task performance on REI with KTO working the best overall
534   and being the only one achieving improvements on the RES task. These results show that, 1) it is
535   possible to improve over the initial pre-trained policy by learning about the listener capabilities, and
536   2) we can perform this learning in an online setting. However, we also observe that current methods
537   do not monotonically improve during the training process, and cannot find an “optimal” policy, since
538   we have demonstrated that better ones exist with our GT agent experiments. With our task setting,
539   we want to encourage further research on how to make online adaptation of MLLM effective and
      practically viable to extend to real-world scenarios for MLLM personalization.

      A    B ROADER I MPACT
760
      In this work we study the capabilities of a speaker to adapt to a listener. We considered MLLMs
761
      adapting to other MLLMs, but one could apply these methods also for adapting MLLMs to hu-
762
      mans. If such techniques were used to adapt MLLMs to humans, people with malicious intend
763   could purposefully teach the MLLMs to produce harmful or otherwise undesirable content. Online
764   adaptation could effectively overwrite previously learned safety measures of the alignment phase. A
765   possible solution could involve intertwining or following online adaptation with alignment training.
766   Additional research is require to measure both opportunities and risks in this scenario.
767
      In the setting where we adapt an MLLM agent to another MLLM agent, malicious actors could try
768
      to exploit systems employing MLLMs by programmatically learning to maximize a desired action
769   of the target MLLM. These “hacks” or “jailbreaks” are a security concern for everyone deploying
770   MLLM, especially if they are deployed adapting to the users. As a result, research on defense
771   mechanisms is just as important as developing more advanced ways to enable personalization.
772
      On the other hand, we believe that allowing MLLMs to adapt to the specific needs of a users can
773
      enable new use cases and improve inclusion across diverse population groups. More effective com-
774
      munication towards users with disabilities could lower the barrier of entry and learning curve to
775   bring MLLM technology and their advancement to a broad audience.

778   B    MLLM P ROMPTING D ETAILS
779
780   The referring expression identification (REI) task starts with the speaker generating a description for
781
      the target image. The prompt given to the speaker is:
782
       Write a description for the left/right image, such that it can be
783     differentiated from the right/left image, but do not talk about
784   the right/left image. Do not name which image you are describing.
785
786   Subsequently, with the help of the speaker’s response, the listener generates a sentence containing
787   its guess. For LLaVA listener agents, we use the query template:
788
789    Does this sentence:              ’{m(s) }’ describe the left image or the right
790
                    image?              Do not explain your reasoning.
791   where {m(s) } is replaced with the description written by the speaker. On the other hand, Qwen gets
792   the prompt:
793
794       Which image does the sentence ’{m(s) }’ describe?                         A. Picture 1 B.
795                                 Picture 2.
796
797   After receiving the listener’s answer, the reward is computed by looking for keywords, i.e. “left, A,
798
      1” and “right, B, 2”, and comparing it with the ground truth label.
799   For the referring expression segmentation (RES) task, the prompt given to the speaker is:
800
801             Write a short description for the highlighted object.
802
      The PaliGemma listener is then prompted with:
803
804
                                             segment ’{m(s) }’
805
806   where “segment” is a PaliGemma specific keyword to induce its segmentation capabilites. The
807   model proceeds to output tokens that can be translated to a segmentation mask. We calculate the
808   intersection over union (IoU) between the predicted segmentation mask and the ground truth seg-
809   mentation mask as a reward for the speaker. Since KTO requires a binary reward, we binarize the
      IoU values with a threshold of 0.5.

897   D            C OMPUTATIONAL R ESOURCES
898
899
      For every experiment, we use 2x A100 40GB GPUs, where one GPU is used for the listener and
900   the other for the speaker. Since the speaker is trained, it requires more computational resources
901   than the listener. It is possible to fit a 13B parameter model into the memory of a single GPU in
902   inference mode for the listener. However, training MLLM only allows models up to 7B parameters
903   on a single GPU, even when using a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method such as LoRA. The
904   training time depends on the lengths of sentences LLaVA generates as the speaker. Longer token
905   sequences take more time to produce as well as to backpropagate through the model. While the
906   length of generations usually diminishes as the speaker adapts to the listener, we also observe the
907   the generated descriptions vary in lengths for the different dataset. Overall, a single experiment of
908
      playing 1800 REI episodes and performing 600 update steps (batch size 3) takes around 5-6 hours
      training time.
