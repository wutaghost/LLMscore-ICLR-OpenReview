                                                             A BSTRACT
010
011
012               Classification with comparative paired inputs, such as pre- and post-disaster satellite
013
                  images, distinguishes classes of samples by encompassing dual feature sets that
                  individually characterize a sample. Representation learning from comparative
014
                  nature of the inputs calls for not only recognizing invariant patterns shared across
015
                  all inputs but also effectively differentiating the contrastive attributes present
016               between each pair of inputs. Supervised Contrastive Learning (SCL) aims to learn
017               representation that maximally separates different classes and condenses within
018               individual classes, thereby attaining an adversarial equilibrium. However, this
019               equilibrium typically relies on the assumption of balanced data and large batch
020               sizes for sufficient negative sampling. These issues are exacerbated when applied
021               to paired satellite images due to increased computational load, high-resolution
022               data, and severe class imbalance. To address these challenges, we introduce
023               Latent Orthonormal Contrastive Learning (LOCAL), an approach that optimizes
024
                  class representations in an orthonormal fashion. By learning each class to a
                  unique, orthogonal plane in the embedding space, LOCAL is efficient with smaller
025
                  batch sizes, provably effective regardless of class size imbalance, and yields more
026
                  discriminative information between pairs of inputs via a feature correlation module.
027               Experimental results on paired image data demonstrate superior performance
028               of LOCAL over SCL, offering a powerful alternative approach for paired input
029               analysis.

034
035   Comparative paired input datasets consists of two paired inputs for each sample that are compared
036
      against each other. These pairs are distinguished by dual feature sets that individually characterize
      each sample. This form of data representation is particularly significant in fields that require the
037
      comparison of two related but distinct sets of data. One notable example is pre- and post-disaster
038
      damage assessment, where paired satellite images captured before and after a natural disaster (such
039   as a hurricane, flooding, or earthquake) are compared, as shown in Fig. 1. Such comparative analysis
040   enhances the effectiveness of damage classification, as the side-by-side analysis helps detect subtle
041   changes and assess the extent of the damage more accurately Kamari & Ham (2022); Ma (2021);
042   Cheng et al. (2021); Berezina & Liu (2022). This principle is similarly applied in fields like natural
043   language inference (NLI), where models aim to understand the relationship between sentence pairs
044   (e.g., premise and hypothesis)MacCartney & Manning (2008); Shen et al. (2022), and in medical
045   imaging, where paired scans (such as pre- and post-treatment MRIs) are compared to track changes
046   over time Kooi & Karssemeijer (2017); Bai et al. (2024); Perek et al. (2019), etc.

      In recent years, self-supervised contrastive learning has emerged as a powerful technique across
055   various domains, especially in computer vision Chen et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Caron et al.
056   (2020); Grill et al. (2020), yielding superior performance in representation learning. The general idea
057   of contrastive learning is to train network models to pull together an anchor sample and a “positive”
058   sample in the embedding space, while simultaneously pushing the anchor away from multiple
059   “negative” samples. SimCLR Chen et al. (2020) is such an example that learns visual representations
060   by constructing positive and negative samples without actual labels, leveraging data augmentations
061   Cubuk et al. (2019; 2020). Additionally, Khosla et al. Khosla et al. (2020) extended it to the fully-
062   supervised setting, proposing a supervised contrastive loss (SCL) that uses label information to align
063
      samples from the same class while separating those from different classes. Further theoretical analysis
      by Graf et al. Graf et al. (2021) examined SCL and the cross-entropy loss, showing that while both
064
      losses aim for a similar geometric solution in the embedding space, SCL converges much closer
065
      to the optimal target, leading to a better generalization performance. Graf et al.’s analysis showed
066   that the optimal embeddings, when minimizing the loss, result in a single embedding for all points
067   within a class, with per-class embeddings forming a regular simplex inscribed in the hypersphere,
068   representing a highly efficient and well-separated geometric arrangement of the embeddings.
069
      However, despite its advantages, SCL faces two critical and interconnected challenges: imbalanced
070
      classes and requirement of large batch sizes.
071
             • First, the theoretical guarantee of SCL, as discussed by Graf et al. Graf et al. (2021),
072
               is contingent upon a critical assumption of balanced data, which is often unrealistic in
073            real-world applications. When SCL is applied to imbalanced datasets, the resulting poor
074            uniformity can significantly degrade model performance Cui et al. (2021); Kang et al. (2020);
075            Li et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021). Classes of higher frequency have
076            a greater lower bound on misclassification loss Cui et al. (2021), which skews the model
077            toward these dominant classes and leads to biased representations. Then, SCL fails to form a
078            regular simplex Zhu et al. (2022) in the embedding space. Instead, it forms an asymmetrical
079            structure where high-frequency classes are more widely scattered, while low-frequency
080            classes are drawn closer together, making it difficult for the model to learn robust and
081
               discriminative features for minority classes.
082
             • Second, the effectiveness of contrastive learning relies on a rich set of negative samples to
               adequately separate representations in the embedding space. Ensuring sufficient negative
083
               diversity typically requires large batch sizes. Both theoretical insights and empirical evi-
084
               dence have shown that increasing the number of negative samples can significantly improve
085            contrastive learning performance Bachman et al. (2019); Tian et al. (2020); Chuang et al.
086            (2020); Wang & Isola (2020). However, this improvement comes at the cost of increased
087            memory consumption, which poses significant challenges for resource-constrained comput-
088            ing environments. As a result, SCL often suffers from performance degradation when batch
089            sizes are small.
090
091   The above challenges inherent to SCL are further exacerbated when applied to comparative paired
092   input datasets, particularly in the context of damage assessment using satellite images, due to
093
      several key factors. First, paired inputs require the model to process both images for each sample
      simultaneously. This effectively doubles the computational workload, thus thus necessitating the use
094
      of smaller batch sizes due to memory constraints. Second, the high-resolution nature intensifies the
095
      issue. These images often contain thousands of pixels, significantly larger than the low-resolution
096   images typically found in benchmarks like CIFAR-10 or CIFAR-100, further limiting batch sizes and
097   reducing the diversity of negative samples in SCL. While resizing images can ease computational
098   constraints issue, this sacrifices important details crucial for accurate analysis, ultimately degrading
099   model performance. Third, the imbalance inherent in these datasets, where categories like “no
100   damage” dominate, but minority classes like “severe structual damage” extremely underrepresented,
101   adds considerable complexity. The smaller batch sizes required for processing paired, high-resolution
102   images reduce the model’s exposure to minority class examples, making it even harder to learn
103   efficient representations for these underrepresented categories.
104   To overcome the aforementioned challenges as a whole, we introduce LOCAL (Latent Orthonormal
105   Contrastive Learning) for paired images, optimizing the representations of distinct classes in an
106   orthonormal fashion. In LOCAL, each class occupies a unique plane, and these planes are orthogonal
107   to one another, enhancing class separation. We theoretically justify that this approach bypasses the
      need to construct a regular simplex, as required in SCL, and alleviates the assumption of balanced

      data without relying on large batch sizes. Additionally, we incorporate a feature correlation module to
109   capture hierarchical features from intermediate layers, further improving joint representation learning
110   between paired inputs. The key contributions of this work are as follows:
111
112          • We propose a novel approach to construct novel orthonormal embeddings for different
113            classes, rather than mapping distinct classes to vertices of a regular simplex inscribed in a
114            hypersphere as in SCL. This enhances the discriminative power between classes and requires
115            only minor adjustments to standard SCL code.
116          • By eliminating the dependency on adversarial equilibrium, our method allows for the use
117            of smaller mini-batches, which is crucial when working with paired input settings and
118            high-resolution images, effectively addressing computational constraints.
119          • Our theoretical analysis proves a lower bound for the proposed new contrastive loss function
120            and shows that minimizing this new loss reaches the lower-bound regardless of the level of
121            class balance.
122          • We incorporate a feature correlation module that utilizes latent hierarchical features derived
123            from intermediate layers to enhance joint representation learning between paired inputs.
124            This is integrated into a broader framework that combines representation learning with
125            classification.

      Smaller batch sizes introduce instability into the optimization process for SCL, by providing fewer
      negative samples which may cause embedding shifting. To illustrate this, consider the following
178
      simplified example. Without loss of generality, assume a mini-batch size of |B| = 2, meaning
179
      that at most two classes are optimized in each batch. Suppose we have the following batches:
180   B1 = {xc1 , xc2 }, B2 = {xc1 , xc3 }, and B3 = {xc2 , xc3 }, where x denotes a sample (can be single
181   or paired settings), and ci indicates that the sample xci belongs to the i-th class. As shown in Fig. 3,
182   during optimization for B1 , ζ1 and ζ2 form a 1-simplex at optimality. However, when B2 comes in,
183   ζ3 is pushed away from ζ1 , creating a situation where ζ3 and ζ2 risk collapsing. When B3 arrives, ζ3
184   and ζ2 repel, but ζ1 and ζ2 (or ζ3 ) collapse. This cyclical collapse causes embedding drift, preventing
185   convergence and breaking equilibrium. The root cause is that there are unique simplex vertices to
186   which per-class images can map. In contrast, we argue that representations of different classes can
187   map into orthogonal subspaces in our proposed method, which reduces the risk of embedding drift in
188
      SCL (as illustrated Fig. 5 in later Section 3.3).
189   Imbalanced Data. It has been proved that SCL reaches its ideal geometry configuration for represen-
190   tation learning when it achieves its minimum on a balanced data batch Graf et al. (2021). This is
191   illustrated in Fig. 4(a), where the angles between the embeddings of distinct classes are equal and
192   maximized, e.g., θ1 = θ2 = θ3 = 2π/3 for K = 3. However, optimizing the SCL may fail to form a
193   regular simplex for imbalanced long-tailed data Zhu et al. (2022). In such scenarios, high frequency
194
      classes dominate the learning process Cui et al. (2021); Kang et al. (2020), leading to unequal angles
      between embeddings, as depicted in Fig. 4(b), where θ1 ≈ θ2 ≫ θ3 . This imbalance can bias the
195
      model towards majority classes, potentially resulting in suboptimal performance for the minority
196
      classes. Existing strategies include balancing the number of positive samples across all classes within
197   each batch Kang et al. (2020), introducing a set of class-wise learnable centers to rebalance from an
198   optimization perspective Cui et al. (2021), incorporating a classifier branch to eliminate the bias of
199   the classifier towards head classes Wang et al. (2021), assigning pre-computed uniformly distributed
200   targets to each class prior to training Li et al. (2022), or optimizing all classes to a balanced feature
201   spaceZhu et al. (2022).
202
      In real-world scenarios such as paired inputs for high-resolution satellite imagery, these imbalances
203   can be even more pronounced. The need for limited batch sizes makes it even harder to properly
204   handle minority classes, increasing the importance of developing more robust contrastive learning
205   frameworks. While the existing methods aim to maintain the regular simplex in the SCL framework
206   under imbalanced data conditions, this simplex requirement may constrain the model’s flexibility.
207   We argue that alternative geometric approaches may offer more effective solutions for handling
208   imbalanced data in such complex scenarios.

      corresponding embedding vectors epre = P roj(rpre ) ∈ RDE and epost = P roj(rpost ) ∈ RDE .
356
      This network is a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with a hidden layer and an output layer of size DE .
357   It has been shown that such a projection module improves the quality of the embeddings of the layers
358   preceding it Khosla et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2020). We apply an ℓ2 normalization to epre and epost
359   to ensure that the inner product can be used as the cosine similarity measure. A similar feature
360   correlation module is incorporated to learn the variation between the pre- and post- outputs of the
361   projection network. z = W([epre , epost ]) is used to compute the OCL loss LOCL (z).
362
      In our framework, the proposed OCL takes effect on both the latent hierarchical feature embedding l
363   and the joint embbedding of the paired inputs z, leading to the latent OCL loss:
364
365                                                 LOCL (z, l) = LOCL (z) + βLOCL (l)                     (5)
366
      where β ≥ 0 is a hyperparameter for tuning and LOCL (l) can be regarded as a regularizer which
367
      regularizes the orthonormality class representations of lower-level features (with high contrast) that
368
      are extracted by the deep neural network. Imposing this regularizer helps learn the final inter-class
369   orthonormal embeddings.
370
371   Classification Network, Clas(·), takes in the concatenated representation, concat(rpre , rpost ), from
372
      the Encoder Network. A non-linear MLP with a hidden layer and an output layer of the class size is
      employed to predict the class-wise logit values c ∈ RDC of the input image pair, which are used to
373
      compute the weighted cross-entropy (WCE) loss LW CE . Combining with the WCE loss for classifier
374
      learning where the weight is the reciprocal of the appearance frequency of each class, we arrive at our
375   final loss function of our proposed LOCAL: Latent Orthonormal Contrastive Learning Framework:
376
377                                               T otal loss = αLOCL (z, l) + (1 − α)LW CE                (6)
      where 0 ≤ α ≤ 1 is a weighting coefficient inversely proportional to the number of epochs.

      5    C ONCLUSION
525
526
527   In this paper, we have addressed the challenge of SCL, to avoid the model drift (class embeddings
528   fail to form a simplex) commonly encountered when batch size is small and class distribution is
529   highly skewed. We introduced Latent Orthonormal Contrastive Learning (LOCAL) as a solution
530   for classification tasks involving paired data. Instead of learning the representations of distinct
531
      classes as vertices of a regular simplex inscribed in a hypersphere, the proposed approach learns
      orthonormal embeddings for different classes where per-class examples are mapped to unit vectors and
532
      perpenticular to the embeddings of all examples in other classes. By a simple change to the original
533
      SCL loss function (adding an absolute value to the inner products of negatives in the denominator of
534   Eq.(2)), we are able to completely revamp the embeddings of different classes to be in orthogonal
535   subspaces. The resultant embeddings, as tested on high resolution remote sensing imagery and natural
536   language inference, show more discriminative power for classification. Our theoretical analysis
537   shows that the proposed loss function has a lower bound and can actually attain its minimum without
538   contingency on data balance unlike the standard contrastive learning. Furthermore, by incorporating
539   the latent hierarchical correlated features via a backbone network, it allows us to further operate on
      small batches of paired inputs, thereby reducing memory burden.
