009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Given an undirected graph G with the normalised adjacency matrix NG , the well-
012             known eigen-gap heuristic for clustering asserts that G has k clusters if there is a
013             large gap between the kth and (k + 1)th largest eigenvalues of NG . Although this
014             heuristic is well-supported in spectral graph theory and widely applied in practice,
015             determining k often relies on computing the eigenvalues of NG with high time
016             complexity. This paper addresses this key problem in graph clustering, and shows
017             that the number of clusters k implied by the eigen-gap heuristic can be computed
018
                in nearly-linear time.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
      Graph clustering is a fundamental problem in unsupervised learning with wide-ranging applications
023
      across computer science and other scientific disciplines. Among the various techniques for solving
024
      graph clustering problems, spectral clustering is probably the easiest to implement, and has been
025   widely applied in practice. Given any graph G = (VG , EG ) and parameter k ∈ N as input, spectral
026   clustering first computes the top k eigenvectors of the normalised adjacency matrix of G and uses
027   these to embed the vertices of G to points in Rk . Afterwards, it applies k-means on the embedded
028   points to partition VG into k clusters (Ng et al., 2001).
029
      Since spectral clustering requires the value of k as input, which is often unknown for real-world ap-
030
      plications, to determine k we usually rely on the eigen-gap heuristic: the correct number of clusters
031   corresponds to the smallest k for which there’s a clear gap between the kth and (k + 1)th largest
032   eigenvalue of the normalised adjacency matrix of G (von Luxburg, 2007). While this heuristic can
033   be well explained in theory (Davis & Kahan, 1970; Lee et al., 2014), applying it in practice can be
034   computationally expensive and requires computing all eigenvalues, particularly for large values of
035   k. Given that spectral clustering itself runs in nearly-linear time (Peng et al., 2017), determining k is
036   the main computational bottleneck in the overall spectral clustering framework. This paper studies
037   this fundamental problem in spectral clustering, and presents the following result:
038   Theorem 1 (Informal Statement of Theorem 6). Let G = (V, E) be an undirected graph with n
039   vertices and m edges as input, and assume the G consists of k well-defined clusters. Then, there is
040   a randomised algorithm that runs in O(m)
                                           e    time1 and with probability at least 1 − o(1) returns the
041   value of k.
042
043   To the best of our knowledge, Theorem 1 represents the first nearly-linear time algorithm that com-
044
      putes the number of clusters in a graph, bridging the gap between the previous high time complexity
      for determining k and the nearly-linear time algorithms for spectral clustering. In addition to its
045
      theoretical guarantees, our algorithm demonstrates strong empirical performance, with our experi-
046
      mental results validating its near-linear runtime in practice.

135   3     A LGORITHM
136
137   In this section we present and analyse the algorithm behind Theorem 1. Recall that graph G has
138   exactly k clusters if (i) G has k disjoint subsets A1 , . . . , Ak of low conductance and hence a small
139   value of ρG (k), and (ii) any (k + 1)-way partition of G would include some A ⊂ V of high
140   conductance, which would be implied by a lower bound on 1 − λk+1 (NG ) due to Lemma 2. To
141   characterise the structure of clusters, we define
142                                                    1 − λk+1 (NG )
143
                                            ΥG (k) ≜                  ,
                                                           ρG (k)
144
      and a large value of ΥG (k) shows that G has exactly k clusters. We prove that, when the input graph
145
      G satisfies ΥG (k) ≥ C · k for some C ∈ R+ , the value of k can be computed in nearly-linear time.
146
      Our result is as follows:
147
148
      Theorem 6 (Formal Statement of Theorem 1). Let G = (V, E) be an undirected graph with n
      vertices and m edges, and assume that G satisfies that ΥG (k) ≥ C · k for a universal constant
149
150
      C ∈ R+ . Then, there is an algorithm that, given G as input, runs in O(m)
                                                                           e     time and with probability
      at least 1 − o(1) returns the value of k.
151
152   Remark 1. Notice that ΥG (k) is a well-studied quantity in spectral clustering (Kolev & Mehlhorn,
153   2016; Peng et al., 2017; Dey et al., 2019; Mizutani, 2021), and it’s known that the performance of
154
      spectral clustering can be rigorously analysed for any graph G with unbalanced clusters, as long
      as ΥG (k) = Ω(k) (Macgregor & Sun, 2022). We prove that, under the same condition, the value of
155
      k can be determined in nearly-linear time.
156
157   The design of our algorithm is based on three components: the first one is an efficient algorithm
158   that constructs a sparse subgraph H of G such that both G and H have the same structure of clus-
159   ters; this gives the normalised adjacency matrix M of H. The second component is the procedure
160   C OUNT E IGENVALUES(M, a, b), which counts the number of eigenvalues of M in [a, b]. As the last
161   one, our main algorithm invokes C OUNT E IGENVALUES(M, a, b) for different intervals and finds the
      right value of k.

      3.1   S PARSIFICATION OF G
163
164   Since an input graph G could be a potentially dense graph and we only need to learn from the cluster
165   structure of G, we first apply a sparsification algorithm of G and obtain a sparse graph H, such that
166   (i) H has O(n)
                 e    non-edges, and (ii) G and H have the same structure of clusters. We achieve this by
167   constructing a cluster-preserving sparsifier of G.
168   Definition 7 (Cluster-preserving sparsifier, (Sun & Zanetti, 2019)). Let G = (V, E, wG ) be any
169   graph, and {Ai }ki=1 the k-way partition of G corresponding to ρG (k). We call a re-weighted sub-
