009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Behavior regularization is a widely adopted technique in offline reinforcement
012            learning (RL) to control distributional shift and mitigate extrapolation errors from
013            out-of-distribution (OOD) actions by keeping the learned policy close to the be-
014            havior policy used to collect the dataset. However, directly applying behavior
015            regularization to offline safe RL presents several issues. The optimal policy in safe
016
               RL should not only favor actions that prevent the agent from entering unsafe re-
               gions but also identify the shortest escape path when the agent finds itself in unsafe
017
               states. Enforcing safety and behavior regularization constraints simultaneously
018
               is inherently difficult and can often lead to infeasible solutions, especially when
019            multiple constraints are involved. Furthermore, adding behavior regularization may
020            cause the learned policy to imitate the behavior policy, even in states where the
021            behavior policy performs poorly (not safe). This issue becomes particularly severe
022            in offline safe RL, where the quality of the dataset collected by the behavior policy
023            heavily impacts the learned policy’s effectiveness. To address these challenges, we
024            propose BARS (Behavior-Aware Region-Based Safe offline RL), a novel algorithm
025            that distinguishes between safe and unsafe states and applies region-specific, selec-
026            tive behavior regularization to optimize the policy in safety-critical applications.
027
               Extensive experiments show that BARS significantly outperforms several state-
               of-the-art baselines in terms of both rewards and safety, particularly in scenarios
028
               where the behavior policy is far from optimal. Notably, when dataset quality is low,
029
               BARS continues to perform well and ensure safety, while all other baselines fail to
030            guarantee a safe policy in most of the environments. Our work has great potential
031            to address a previously overlooked issue in offline safe RL.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Safe reinforcement learning (RL) has been successfully applied in areas such as autonomous driving
037   (Isele et al., 2018), recommend systems (Chow et al., 2017), and robotics (Achiam et al., 2017;
038   Dawson et al., 2023), helping to develop policies that adhere to critical safety constraints, including
039
      collision avoidance, budget management, and system reliability. However, a significant challenge
      lies in gathering the data needed to train these policies in an online setting. In many real-world
040
      applications, interacting with the environment is not only costly and time-consuming but also
041
      inherently risky. Training an RL agent online requires learning through trial and error, which can
042   lead to unsafe behavior during the learning process—an unacceptable risk in safety-critical tasks.
043
044   To overcome the limitations of data collection, offline RL offers a safer alternative by enabling policies
045
      to be learned from pre-collected data, without the need for direct interaction with the environment
      during training. Instead of relying on real-time exploration, offline safe RL leverages datasets gathered
046
      from prior experiences, eliminating the risk of unsafe actions during the learning process. However,
047
      in offline RL, a major problem arises when the learned policy encounters out-of-distribution (OOD)
048   actions (Fujimoto et al., 2019) since the policy is trained on a static, pre-collected dataset. Thus, the
049   policy may attempt actions that were not well-presented during training, leading to extrapolation
050   errors in the value function, which can not be eliminated without requiring coverage of the offline
051   dataset. Existing works tackle this problem by restricting the learned policy to only select actions
052   that have sufficient support under the behavior policy by adding a behavior regularization term in
053   both offline RL (Peng et al., 2019; Siegel et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Jaques et al., 2019; Wu
      et al., 2019; Dadashi et al., 2021) and offline safe RL (Lee et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Lin et al.,

      for managing distributional shift in standard offline reinforcement learning, its direct application to
076
      offline safe RL introduces significant challenges. Safe RL is typically modeled as a Constrained
077
      Markov Decision Process (CMDP), where the goal is to maximize rewards while satisfying safety
078   constraints. In some CMDPs, certain states are so high-risk that no truly safe actions or trajectories
079   are available in those states.. In such cases, the optimal solution is to select the action with the
080   minimum cost. For example, imagine an autonomous vehicle experiencing a brake malfunction.
081   Every available action poses a risk—stopping abruptly could cause an accident, turning might result
082   in a skid, and continuing forward could lead to a more dangerous situation. Here, the vehicle must
083   choose the least harmful action, illustrating the need to navigate risk effectively when no safe options
084   are available. Therefore, the optimal policy in safe RL must not only avoid unsafe regions but also
085   find the quickest escape route when forced to enter an unsafe state. Enforcing safety constraints
086
      alongside behavior regularization often leads to conflicting objectives, making it difficult to find
      feasible solutions.
087
088   Another key drawback of applying behavior regularization directly in offline safe RL is that it may
089   cause the learned policy to overly imitate the behavior policy, even in states where the behavior policy
090   performs poorly or unsafely. Since offline RL relies on pre-collected datasets, the quality of the
091   behavior policy directly impacts the learned policy’s effectiveness. If the dataset contains sub-optimal
092
      or unsafe behavior, behavior regularization may reinforce these undesirable actions, resulting in poor
      performance. This issue is particularly severe in safety-critical environments.
093
094   To address these challenges, we propose BARS (Behavior-Aware Region-Based Safe offline RL),
095   a novel algorithm that distinguishes between safe and unsafe states while applying region-specific,
096   selective behavior regularization to optimize the policy. As visualized in Figure 1, we consider a
097   simple toy example, where the agent aims to find the shortest path from a fixed starting point to a
098
      destination while avoiding hazardous zones. The performance of the behavior policy is regulated by
      controlling the ratio between an expert and a random policy used to collect the offline dataset. The
099
      figure demonstrates that BARS consistently performs well in different scenarios (high/medium/low-
100
      quality offline dataset), while the state-of-the-art algorithm FISOR (Zheng et al., 2024) fails to satisfy
101   the safety constraints as the dataset quality degrades. More discussions on the related work can be
102   found in Section A in the Appendix. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
103
104   • BARS is the first to address the issue of behavior regularization constraints leading to infeasible so-
105     lutions in safe RL using a region-based approach. Infeasible solutions are especially likely to occur
106     when multiple constraints are involved. Unlike traditional methods, BARS adapts regularization
107     based on state safety (Eq.(4)), maximizing rewards in safe regions and minimizing risks in unsafe
        regions by selecting the least harmful actions.

123   We formulate safe-RL as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP), denoted by the tuple
124   M = (S, A, γ, P, r, c, ρ). Here, S and A represent the state and action spaces, ρ is the initial
125   state distribution, γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor, r : S × A → R is the reward function, and
126   c : S × A → R+ is the cost function. WLOG, we use c = 0 to indicate that the state constraint
127
      is satisfied (the state is safe). At each time step, an agent observes a state s ∈ S, takes an action
      a ∈ A according to a policy π, receives a reward r(s, a) and a cost c(s, a), and then transitions to
128
      the next state s′ based on the transition kernel P(·|s, a). Given a policy π, we use Vrπ (s) and Vcπ (s)

131   that the optimal policy of a discounted MDP (or CMDP) (Sutton and Barto, 2018; Altman, 1999;
132   Bertsekas, 2019) depends on the initial state. To better illustrate the idea and address existing issues
133   in offline safe RL, we consider a fixed initial state ρ = s in this paper. The objective for safe-RL is to

153

      reward while maintaining safety in an expected sense. In the unconstrained case, this approach is
154

155

156   in a few critical states where Vcπ (s) > l. These harmful states, though rare, can still cause substantial
157   damage. For example, in autonomous driving, the system may perform well in common scenarios
158   such as highway driving or empty streets. However, in rare but critical situations like navigating
159   a busy intersection or avoiding a pedestrian, the risks are much higher. Simply ensuring safety on
160   average may overlook these high-risk states, where poor decisions can lead to serious accidents. 2),
161   the two constraints Vcπ (s) ≤ l and D(π||πβ ) ≤ ϵ in the optimization problem (2) may not always

      6    R ELATED W ORK
510
511
      In offline safe RL, primal-dual-based approaches are the most commonly used (Tessler et al., 2018;
512   Chow et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2020; Efroni et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022a; Fujimoto et al., 2019;
513   Kumar et al., 2019). CPQ (Xu et al., 2022) employs a VAE to detect and penalize out-of-distribution
514   (OOD) actions based on their associated costs. COptiDICE (Lee et al., 2022), an extension of
515   OptiDICE (Lee et al., 2021), integrates safety constraints and derives a safe policy from the stationary
516   distribution of the optimal policy. Other methods, such as applying decision transformers (Chen et al.,
517   2021) or diffuser models (Janner et al., 2022), have been used to study safe offline RL, though they
518   tend to be computationally inefficient. FISOR (Zheng et al., 2024) decouples the process of satisfying
519   safety constraints from reward maximization, leveraging a diffusion model for policy generation.
520
      VOCE (Guan et al., 2024) addresses extrapolation errors caused by OOD actions by estimating both
      cost and reward Q-values in a pessimistic manner. Additionally, some works (Yang et al., 2023;
521
      Wabersich et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024) draw from control theory to enforce state-wise constraints.
522
      More discussions on the related work can be found in Section A in the Appendix.

525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   We propose BARS, a novel algorithm that tackles the existing issues of applying behavior regulariza-
528
      tion in offline safe RL. We demonstrate that directly applying it in offline safe RL often leads to issues
      such as policy infeasibility and the risk of imitating poor or unsafe behavior. BARS addresses these
529
      challenges by distinguishing between safe and unsafe states and applying region-specific, selective
530
      behavior regularization to optimize the policy accordingly. Extensive experiments demonstrate
531   that BARS significantly outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines in both rewards and safety,
532   particularly when the behavior policy is far from optimal. Even in scenarios where the dataset quality
533   is low, BARS continues to perform robustly, while other baselines fail to maintain safe policies across
534   environments. This highlights BARS’s potential to resolve a previously overlooked issue in offline
535   safe RL and provide a framework for safer and more effective policy learning.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
757
758
      Safe RL has attracted great attention recently, both in online setting (Efroni et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
759
      2021; Bura et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022b;a; 2024a; Müller et al., 2023; Germano et al., 2023; Ding
760   et al., 2024; Müller et al., 2024) and offline setting (Wu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Lee et al.,
761   2022; Liu et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Hong and Tewari,
762   2024).
763
      In theoretical offline RL, certain assumptions about the ratio between the state-action occupancy
764
      distribution of a policy π and the dataset distribution µ are necessary to prove convergence. This
765
      ratio is represented by wπ = dπ /µ. A commonly used assumption is that the ℓ∞ concentrability
766   Cℓπ∞ , defined as the infinite norm of wπ , is bounded for all policies (Liu et al., 2019; Chen and
767   Jiang, 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Liao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020). However, this assumption
768   is difficult to satisfy, particularly in offline safe RL, as it requires the dataset to adequately cover
769   all unsafe state-action pairs. To address this, recent works (Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Zhan et al.,
770   2022; Chen and Jiang, 2022; Xie et al., 2021; Uehara and Sun, 2021) introduce pessimism to reduce
771   the requirement to only the best-covered policy. Zhu et al. (2023) further relaxes this assumption
772   by introducing ℓ2 concentrability, which is always bounded by ℓ∞ . While existing offline safe RL
773   studies (Hong et al., 2024; Le et al., 2019) still require coverage for all policies, a recent work Wei
774
      et al. (2024b) relaxes this assumption to single-policy coverage.
775   Deep offline safe RL algorithms (Lee et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021;
776   Zheng et al., 2024) have shown strong empirical performance but usually lack theoretical guarantees.
777   As we mentioned in the introduction, balancing generalization with preventing undesirable behaviors
778   from out-of-distribution actions is a key challenge in offline RL. A straightforward approach is
779   to directly constrain the learned policy to align with the behavior policy used to collect the data
780
      set (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto and Gu, 2021; Wu et al., 2019). Other
      methods address this by making conservative estimates of future rewards through a lower bound
781
      on the true value function (Kumar et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021) or by penalizing out-of-distribution
782
      (OOD) actions to control distributional shift (Kostrikov et al., 2021; Lyu et al., 2022). Model-based
783   approaches often handle uncertainty by using ensembles, penalizing actions with high variability
784   across models and promoting those that show consistency (Janner et al., 2019; Kidambi et al., 2020).
785   Recently, some studies have explored the use of expressive diffusion models for policy learning to
786   capture more complex distributions, achieving impressive results (Janner et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023;
787   Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024).

      B    M ORE DISCUSSIONS ON THE COMPARISON BETWEEN BARS AND FISOR
790
791
      Our formulation is inspired by FISOR (Zheng et al., 2024), which proposes a feasibility-guided diffu-
792
      sion model for offline safe RL. However, our formulation differs from FISOR in several significant
793   ways:
794
795   • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to address the issue that using behavior
796     regularization constraints in safe RL can lead to infeasible solutions, particularly when multiple
797     constraints are involved. Moreover, adding behavior regularization may result in the learned policy
798     imitating the behavior policy, even in states where the behavior policy performs poorly or is unsafe.
799     This issue is not limited to FISOR; almost all existing algorithms fail to account for this problem
800     and cannot reliably guarantee a safe policy when the behavior policy is suboptimal, as demonstrated
801     in Table 2.
802   • FISOR addresses only the “hard constraint” setting, where the cost value function represents the
803     maximum constraint violation (largest state-wise cost) in the trajectory. This greatly limits the
804     generalization of their approach to other scenarios where a cost limit is considered. Since ristrictly
805     control the hard constraint on some dangerous states can also be done by adding a large penalty to
806     the reward function for such states to avoid a unsafe policy. In contrast, BARS can handle a wide
807
        range of constraints, offering flexibility by adjusting the constraint limit in the algorithm.
808   • FISOR (Zheng et al., 2024) defines the feasible region using the value function for the hard
809     constraint based on Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) reachability (Bansal et al., 2017) from control theory.
        However, the formulation in FISOR applies only to deterministic systems, which is rarely the case


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
        in most RL environments, and cannot be generalized to problems with more flexible cost limits.
811     The Bellman equation used in FISOR to train the value function no longer holds in stochastic
812     systems.
813
      • FISOR optimizes the policy by maximizing the value function to achieve the highest reward without
814
        considering constraints, which overlooks the fact that such a policy may not be safe. In contrast, we
815     propose a “Region-Based Risk-Reward Optimization” in Eq.(7), which accounts for potential errors
816     in value function estimation (likely to be true in practice) and directly optimizes the policy based
817     on the current policy. This approach enables BARS to avoid potential unsafe actions effectively.

1076   A limitation of BARS is its applicability to non-region-based safe offline RL approaches. Our
1077   method removes the behavior regularization for states in the unsafe region. However, for settings
1078   that allow cancellation of the cost (e.g., where both positive and negative costs are permitted), it
1079   becomes challenging to define well-separated safe and unsafe regions. Thus, addressing the behavior
       regularization issue in such cases requires further consideration.
