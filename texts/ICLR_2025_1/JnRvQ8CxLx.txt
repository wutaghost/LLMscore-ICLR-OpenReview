                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               As large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) continue to gain prominence, ensuring
010
               their safety deployment in real-world applications has become a critical concern.
011            Recently, significant research efforts have focused on evaluating the robustness of
012            VLMs against jailbreak attacks. Due to challenges in obtaining multi-modal data,
013            current studies often assess VLM robustness by generating adversarial or query-
014            relevant images based on harmful text datasets. However, the jailbreak images
015            generated this way exhibit certain limitations. Adversarial images require white-
016            box access to the target VLM and are relatively easy to defend against, while
017            query-relevant images must be linked to the target harmful content, limiting their
018            diversity and effectiveness. In this paper, we propose a novel jailbreak method
019
               named IDEATOR, which autonomously generates malicious image-text pairs for
               black-box jailbreak attacks. IDEATOR is a VLM-based approach inspired by our
020
               conjecture that a VLM itself might be a powerful red team model for generating
021
               jailbreak prompts. Specifically, IDEATOR employs a VLM to generate jailbreak
022            texts while leveraging a state-of-the-art diffusion model to create corresponding
023            jailbreak images. Extensive experiments demonstrate the high effectiveness and
024            transferability of IDEATOR. It successfully jailbreaks MiniGPT-4 with a 94%
025            success rate and transfers seamlessly to LLVA and InstructBLIP, achieving high
026            success rates of 82% and 88%, respectively. IDEATOR uncovers previously un-
027            recognized vulnerabilities in VLMs, calling for advanced safety mechanisms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the popularity of OpenAI’s GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Google’s Gemini (Team et al.,
      2023), increasing attention has been paid to large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and their trust-
034
      worthy deployment in real-world applications. Although VLMs are built on well-aligned Large
035
      Language Models (LLMs), the integration of both textual and visual modalities introduces new vul-
036   nerabilities. Recent studies have revealed that VLMs are extremely vulnerable to jailbreak attacks,
037   where a malicious jailbreak prompt can trick the model to output harmful content that it would nor-
038   mally be restricted from generating, making these risks a critical concern for VLMs’ safe release.
039
      However, evaluating the robustness of VLMs against jailbreak attacks presents notable challenges.
040
      This is partly because multi-modal evaluation data remains relatively scarce when compared to the
041
      abundant textual corpora available in the LLM domain. As such, current VLM jailbreak methods (Qi
042   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) rely on existing LLM jailbreak datasets to craft adversarial images
043   or texts to evaluate VLM robustness. While these methods are effective, they require white-box
044   access to the target model which is less practical in real-world scenarios. Moreover, the generated
045   adversarial images and texts are either severely distorted or semantically meaningless, making them
046   easily detectable by the underlying safety mechanism of VLMs (Nie et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
047   2023). This has motivated the development of manually designed pipelines for generating jailbreak
048   images, such as combining typographic attacks with query-relevant images (Liu et al., 2023). One
049   major limitation of manual methods is their heavy reliance on human-engineered processes, which
050
      restricts the flexibility and scalability of generating diverse jailbreak data for robustness evaluation.
051   To address the above limitations, in this paper, we propose the idea of “using VLMs against VLMs”,
052   that is, leveraging a VLM to generate effective, transferable, and diverse multimodal jailbreak data.
053   Our idea is inspired by the red-teaming techniques (Chao et al., 2023) for LLMs, where a powerful
      red team model can automatically generate diverse jailbreak texts to test the robustness of LLMs.

074   In this work, we extend red-teaming to the field of VLMs, and utilize an advanced VLM as a jail-
075   break agent. Considering VLMs can only output texts, the attacker VLM is then integrated with
076
      state-of-the-art image-generation models to generate subtle images, making the jailbreak prompts
      multimodal. By integrating images, the VLM can bypass safeguards more effectively, such as by
077
      concealing malicious content within images or using visuals to strengthen role-playing scenarios.
078
      Figure 1 shows an example of the jailbreak texts and images generated by our proposed attack
079   IDEATOR. Particularly, the attacker VLM in IDEATOR acts as an “ideator” who simulates an ad-
080   versary interacting with the target VLM. The attacker VLM iteratively refines its attack strategy
081   based on all previous responses of the target model, while the target model processes only the cur-
082   rent inputs and cannot access the historical conversations. IDEATOR also employs multiple con-
083   current attack streams to allow the attacker to explore different jailbreak strategies simultaneously.
084   This dual focus on depth and breadth enables a comprehensive examination of how the attacker can
085   jailbreak the target model toward generating harmful outputs while bypassing safety mechanisms.
086   The main contributions of our work are as follows:
087
088             • We study the robustness of VLMs to jailbreak attacks and proposed a novel framework
089               named IDEATOR that leverages VLMs and diffusion models to generate multimodal jail-
090               break prompts. To the best of our knowledge, IDEATOR is the first red team model for
091               VLMs, establishing a new paradigm in this direction.
092             • IDEATOR simulates an adversarial user who iteratively evolves its jailbreak strategies by
093               interacting with the target VLM under a black-box setting, with the assistance of a diffusion
094               model. By considering both breadth and depth in its attack strategy, IDEATOR provides a
095               comprehensive examination of the VLM’s multimodal vulnerabilities.
096             • Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate that IDEATOR can success-
097               fully jailbreak MiniGPT-4 with a success rate of 94%. Moreover, the multimodal jailbreak
098               prompts generated by IDEATOR transfer seamlessly to other VLMs, achieving high suc-
099               cess rates of 82% and 88% on LLVA and InstructBLIP, respectively.

103   2.1      L ARGE V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
104
105   Unlike traditional Large Language Models (LLMs), which are limited to processing textual data,
106   large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) extend their capabilities to visual and textual modalities.
107   Typically, a VLM is built upon a pre-trained LLM. To integrate visual modalities, the VLM uses an
      image encoder to extract visual features, which are then mapped to the token space of the LLM via


                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      an alignment module. Taking several open-source VLMs as examples, MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023)
109   aligned a frozen visual encoder (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) with a frozen LLM (Chiang et al., 2023)
110   using a single projection layer to enable advanced multimodal capabilities. After initial training on
111   short image captions, they fine-tuned the model with a detailed image description dataset to improve
112   language generation quality and usability. InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023) involved vision-language
113   instruction tuning based on the pretrained BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) models. They transformed 26
114   publicly available datasets covering various tasks into an instruction-tuning format. Additionally,
115   they introduced an instruction-aware Query Transformer, designed to extract features relevant to the
116   given instruction. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) was an end-to-end multimodal model that connected a
117
      vision encoder (Radford et al., 2021) with a LLM (Touvron et al., 2023), enabling general-purpose
      visual and language understanding. It leveraged language-only GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to gen-
118
      erate multimodal instruction-following data for both text and images. Through instruction tuning
119
      on GPT-4-generated multimodal data, LLaVA exhibits strong multimodal conversational abilities.
120   While VLMs demonstrate significant potential, the integration of an additional visual modality intro-
121   duces new vulnerabilities (Shayegani et al., 2023). In our study, we assess the robustness of VLMs
122   against the proposed IDEATOR attack and underscore the urgent need for new alignment strategies.
123
124   2.2   ATTACKS AGAINST M ULTIMODAL M ODELS
125
126   To attack multimodal models, Greshake et al. (2023) investigated the effectiveness of manually
127   injecting deceptive text into input images. Gong et al. (2023) proposed FigStep, a method that
128   converts the harmful text into images using typography to bypass the safety mechanisms. Liu et al.
129
      (2023) demonstrated that VLMs can be easily compromised by query-relevant images, behaving
      as though the text query itself were malicious. Consequently, Liu et al. (2023) introduced MM-
130
      SafetyBench, a comprehensive evaluation framework specifically designed to assess the safety and
131
      robustness of VLMs against such image-based manipulations. Bagdasaryan et al. (2023), Bailey
132   et al. (2023), and Carlini et al. (2024) fixed attacker-chosen text as the target output and optimized
133   adversarial images to increase its likelihood. Visual Adversarial Jailbreak Method (VAJM) (Qi et al.,
134   2024) used a single adversarial image to universally jailbreak an aligned VLM. This adversarial
135   attack goes beyond the narrow scope of the initial ”few-shot” derogatory corpus used to optimize
136   the adversarial example, leading to the generation of more harmful content. Wang et al. (2024)
137   proposed a comprehensive attack strategy targeting both text and image modalities in VLMs, aiming
138   to exploit a broader range of vulnerabilities. Their dual optimization approach first generates an
139   adversarial image prefix embedding toxic semantics, followed by an adversarial text suffix, which is
140
      jointly optimized with the image prefix to increase the likelihood of affirmative responses. Niu et al.
      (2024) adopted the same optimization objective as (Wang et al., 2024)’s second stage to generate
141
      adversarial images on VLMs, and then transformed them into adversarial text suffixes for LLMs.
142
      While the aforementioned methods have demonstrated impressive results, they typically rely on
143   manually crafted attack pipelines or white-box adversarial attacks. However, manually designing
144   these pipelines is labor-intensive and limits attack diversity, whereas white-box adversarial attacks
145   require full access to model parameters, making them less practical or easily detectable (Nie et al.,
146   2022; Zhang et al., 2023). In contrast, our method utilizes VLMs to generate diverse image-text
147   pairs for black-box attacks, achieving high success rates and strong transferability.

151   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
152
153   Attack Goals We consider multi-turn conversations between an attacker VLM and a victim VLM.
154   The attacker VLM has access to the history of previous conversations, while the victim VLM can
155   only view the current-turn conversation. The goal of attacker VLM is to bypass the victim’s safety
156   mechanisms and trigger harmful behaviors, e.g., generating unethical content or dangerous instruc-
157   tions restricted by RHLF alignment or system prompts.
158   Adversary Capabilities We assume the attacker has only black-box access to the victim VLM,
159   representing real-world situations where interactions occur through external interfaces. The attacker
160   does not need to know the internal structure or parameters of the victim VLM. Through multiple
161   rounds of interactions, the attacker can infer the behavior patterns and potential weaknesses of the
      victim VLM to find successful jailbreaks.

217
218   In IDEATOR, we adopt the Vicuna-13B (Chiang et al., 2023) version of MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al.,
219   2023) as the attacker VLM. The 13B variant provides greater model capacity and enhanced perfor-
220   mance compared to its smaller 7B counterpart, making it more adept at simulating complex adver-
221   sarial behaviors. Additionally, its open-source nature facilitates modifications to the system prompt
222
      and conversation template, enabling precise control over the model’s behavior to more accurately

223
      pared to LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), increasing the likelihood of producing jailbreak prompts
224
      aligned with attack goals. In contrast, LLaMA tends to be more cautious and resistant to generating
225   adversarial content (Chao et al., 2023). We also test customized versions of GPT-4o (Achiam et al.,

228   anisms. For the text-to-image generation model, we employ the latest Stable Diffusion 3 Medium
229   (Rombach et al., 2022) to ensure that the generated images are of high quality and semantically

      3.2.3   P ROMPT D ESIGN
259
260   System Prompt To allow the attacker VLM to simulate adversarial behavior without specific train-
261   ing on red team datasets, we carefully design a system prompt and conversation template. As shown
262   in Figure 3, our system prompt comprises three key components. First, we configure the attacker
263   model as a red teaming assistant, tasked with crafting adversarial jailbreak prompts that could po-
264   tentially mislead the victim VLM to generate unethical outputs. Second, we force the assistant’s
265   output to follow a structured JSON format comprising three elements: analysis, image prompt, and
266
      text prompt. Lastly, we leverage the concept of in-context learning (Brown, 2020) to guide the red
      team model to generate more effective adversarial JSON outputs by showing relevant examples.
267
268   Chain-of-Thought Reasoning The analysis component in the JSON output enables iterative op-
269   timization of jailbreak prompts based on prior victim responses. This component evaluates the
      effectiveness of previous attacks and facilitates Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wang et al.,


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      2022; Wei et al., 2022) during multi-turn interactions. By guiding the attacker model to generate
271   intermediate reasoning steps, CoT reasoning enhances the attacker’s ability to explore adversarial
272   strategies more efficiently.
273
274
      Enhancing Interaction Quality To ensure that the assistant’s output strictly adheres to the pre-
      defined JSON format, we force the attacker model to start its responses with the JSON key
275
      {"analysis":", thereby maximizing compliance with JSON output standards. Additionally,
276
      we apply post-processing to the victim model’s responses to reiterate the attack goal and incorpo-
277   rate images generated from the previous round. These enhancements significantly improve both
278   coherence and effectiveness within interactions.

321   jailbreak targets to test our attack method. We do not use the full dataset for testing as a portion of
322   the data has to be reserved for the adversarial optimization of white-box attack methods (Zou et al.,
323   2023; Qi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Note that our IDEATOR is a training-free method, and thus
      does not need any harmful goals to train or optimize. Additionally, we extend our assessment by

      Empirical Understanding In this subsection, we empirically demonstrate why IDEATOR
449   achieves impressive ASR even in black-box settings. We define the set of jailbreak attacks gen-
450   erated by IDEATOR under finite exploration breadth and depth as ANbreadth ,Ndepth , and the set of all
451   possible jailbreak attacks generated with infinite exploration breadth and depth as AIDEATOR . This set
452   represents the theoretical limit of attacks IDEATOR could generate without exploration constraints.
453   Mathematically, we formalize this as:
454
455                                AIDEATOR =            lim             ANbreadth ,Ndepth .
                                                 Nbreadth →∞,Ndepth →∞
456
457   Ideally, as Nbreadth and Ndepth increase, ANbreadth ,Ndepth progressively approaches AIDEATOR . This allows
458   IDEATOR to uncover a wider variety of adversarial strategies that could encompass existing attacks.
459   Next, we will empirically show that our attack can indeed cover certain existing types of attacks.

521   5     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we investigated the vulnerabilities of large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to jail-
524   break attacks and introduced a novel black-box jailbreak method called IDEATOR. Unlike existing
525   approaches, IDEATOR transforms a VLM into a jailbreak agent through a carefully designed system
526   prompt and conversation template. The attacking VLM is further integrated with a state-of-the-art
527   diffusion model to autonomously generate multimodal jailbreak prompts. By employing a breath-
528   depth exploration strategy, IDEATOR iteratively refines its attack methods, effectively probing a
529   wide range of vulnerabilities in the target VLMs. Importantly, IDEATOR addresses the limitations
530   of current jailbreak techniques, which often depend on white-box access or manual engineering,
531
      by autonomously generating diverse image-text pairs. Experimental results demonstrate the effec-
      tiveness and transferability of our IDEATOR attack. Notably, IDEATOR successfully jailbreaks
532
      MiniGPT-4 with a 94% success rate and achieves high transfer success rates of 82% and 88% on
533
      LLVA and InstructBLIP, respectively. These findings underscore the potential of using VLMs to
534   identify and exploit multimodal vulnerabilities, establishing IDEATOR as a powerful tool for red
535   teaming and robustness evaluation in VLMs. As a future direction, we plan to release a comprehen-
536   sive benchmark dataset to support further research in this area, targeting a broader range of VLM
537   architectures, utilizing diverse system prompts, and exploring an extensive array of attack objec-
538   tives. Additionally, we will develop a specialized red-team model that will be fine-tuned on existing
539   jailbreak techniques and further enhanced through reinforcement learning for more sophisticated
      and effective attacks.
