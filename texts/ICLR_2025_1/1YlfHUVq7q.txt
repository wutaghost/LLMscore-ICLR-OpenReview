011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce the Error Broadcast and Decorrelation (EBD) algorithm, a novel
014            learning framework that addresses the credit assignment problem in neural
015            networks by directly broadcasting output error to individual layers. The EBD
016
               algorithm leverages the orthogonality property of the optimal minimum mean
               square error (MMSE) estimator, which states that estimation errors are orthogonal
017
               to any nonlinear function of the input, specifically the activations of each layer. By
018
               defining layerwise loss functions that penalize correlations between these activa-
019            tions and output errors, the EBD method offers a principled and efficient approach
020            to error broadcasting. This direct error transmission eliminates the need for weight
021            transport inherent in backpropagation. Additionally, the optimization framework
022            of the EBD algorithm naturally leads to the emergence of the experimentally
023            observed three-factor learning rule. We further demonstrate how EBD can be
024            integrated with other biologically plausible learning frameworks, transforming
025            time-contrastive approaches into single-phase, non-contrastive forms, thereby en-
026            hancing biological plausibility and performance. Numerical experiments demon-
027
               strate that EBD achieves performance comparable to or better than known error-
               broadcast methods on benchmark datasets. The scalability of algorithmic exten-
028
               sions of EBD to very large or complex datasets remains to be explored. However,
029
               our findings suggest that EBD offers a promising, principled direction for both
030            artificial and natural learning paradigms, providing a biologically plausible and
031            flexible alternative for neural network training with inherent simplicity and adapt-
032            ability that could benefit future developments in neural network technologies.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Neural networks have been central to both biological and artificial intelligence research, providing
038   key models for understanding cognitive functions. One major challenge in these networks is deter-
039   mining how to adjust individual synaptic weights to optimize a global objective, a problem referred
040   to as the credit assignment problem. In Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), the most common so-
041   lution to this problem is the backpropagation (BP) algorithm (Rumelhart et al., 1986). This method
042   involves propagating errors—calculated at the network’s output—back through the network using a
043   distinct layered pathway, employing the same synaptic values used during forward processing.
044   In contrast to ANNs, the global mechanisms for credit assignment within biological neural net-
045   works remain less understood. Although there are dynamical models for local synaptic changes
046   (Magee & Grienberger, 2020), a comprehensive and biologically feasible theory of credit assign-
047   ment that integrates these dynamics remains unresolved. The backpropagation algorithm, despite
048   its effectiveness in training ANNs, is not directly implementable in biological systems. This is due
049   to biologically implausible requirements, such as weight symmetry between forward and backward
050
      pathways (Crick, 1989), meaning that the same weights must be used in both signal transmission
      and error feedback—a condition not observed in biological neurons, as illustrated by Figure 1a.
051
052   To address the credit assignment problem in biological networks, researchers have proposed a set
053   of methods collectively known as error broadcasting (Williams, 1992; Werfel et al., 2003; Nok-
      land, 2016; Baldi et al., 2018; Whittington & Bogacz, 2019; Clark et al., 2021). These approaches

055   or symmetric weights, thereby eliminating the weight symmetry issue inherent in backpropagation.
056   This elimination of weight symmetry not only makes error broadcasting potentially useful for mod-
057   eling biological neural networks but also offers practical advantages for hardware implementations.
058   As recently demonstrated by Wang et al. (2024), the straightforward mechanism of error broadcast-
059   ing enables efficient hardware implementations of neural networks, raising hopes for future neu-
060   romorphic systems. Despite promising developments in both theory and applications (Bordelon &
061   Pehlevan, 2022; Launay et al., 2019), error broadcasting schemes still require further theoretical
062   support to confirm and enhance their effectiveness in training networks.
063   In this context, we introduce a novel learning paradigm termed the Error Broadcast and Decor-
064   relation (EBD) algorithm. The fundamental principle of EBD is to adjust the network weights to
065   minimize the correlation between the broadcast errors and the activations of each layer. This method
066   is grounded on two key observations: first, that the output error of an optimal minimum mean square
067   error (MMSE) estimator is orthogonal to any nonlinear function of the input; and second, that each
068   network layer represents a specific nonlinear function of the input. By viewing the network as a
069
      nonlinear MMSE estimator and leveraging the orthogonality property of optimal estimators, we de-
      fine layer-specific training losses that adjust individual layer parameters to make their activations
070
      orthogonal to the broadcast errors. The EBD algorithm directly connects the output errors to the

114   1.1   R ELATED WORK AND CONTRIBUTIONS
115
116   Several frameworks have been proposed as alternatives to the backpropagation algorithm for mod-
117   eling credit assignment in biological networks (Whittington & Bogacz, 2019). These include pre-
118
      dictive coding (Rao & Ballard, 1999; Whittington & Bogacz, 2017; Golkar et al., 2022), similarity
      matching (Qin et al., 2021; Bahroun et al., 2023), time-contrastive approaches (Ackley et al., 1985;
119
      O’Reilly, 1996; Scellier & Bengio, 2017), forward-only methods (Hinton, 2022; Farinha et al., 2023;
120
      Dellaferrera & Kreiman, 2022), target propagation (Le Cun, 1986; Bengio, 2014; Lee et al., 2015),
121   random feedback alignment (Lillicrap et al., 2016), and learned feedback weights (Kolen & Pollack,
122   1994; Ji-An & Benna, 2024).
123
124
      Another significant alternative is error-broadcast methods, where output errors are directly trans-
      mitted to network layers without relying on precise backward pathways or symmetric weights. Two
125
      important examples of this approach are weight and node perturbation algorithms Williams (1992);
126
      Dembo & Kailath (1990); Cauwenberghs (1992); Fiete & Seung (2006), in which global error
127   signals are broadcast to all network units. These signals reflect the change in overall error caused by
128   individual perturbations in the network’s weights or units. A more recent and prominent example
129   of error-broadcast is Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nokland, 2016). In DFA, the output
130   errors are projected onto the hidden layers through fixed random weights, effectively replacing the
131   symmetric backward weights required in traditional backpropagation. This approach first emerged
132   as a modification to the feedback alignment approach (which replaced the symmetric weights of
133   the backpropagation algorithm with random ones). DFA has been extended and analyzed in several
134   studies (Bartunov et al., 2018; Han & Yoo, 2019; Launay et al., 2019; 2020; Bordelon & Pehlevan,
135
      2022), demonstrating its potential in training neural networks with less biologically implausible
      mechanisms. Clark et al. (2021) introduced another broadcast approach for a network with vector
136
      units and nonnegative weights for which three factor learning based update rule is applied.
137
138   Our proposed framework for error broadcasting differentiates itself through
139
140
             • a principled method based on the orthogonality property of nonlinear MMSE estimators,
141          • error projection weights determined by the cross-correlation between the output errors
142            and the layer activations as opposed to random weights of DFA,
143          • dynamic Hebbian updating of projection weights as opposed to fixed weights of DFA,
144          • updates involving arbitrary nonlinear functions of layer activities, encompassing a
145            family of three-factor learning rules,
146          • the option to project layer activities forward to the output layer.
147
148   In summary, our approach provides a theoretical grounding for the error broadcasting mechanism
149   and suggests ways to its effectiveness in training networks.

168   2.2   E RROR BROADCAST AND DECORRELATION LOSS FUNCTIONS
169
170   At the core of our approach lies the well-known orthogonality property of minimum mean square
171   error (MMSE) estimators (Papoulis & Pillai, 2002) (see Appendix A for a brief summary):
172   Let ŷ∗ be the optimal nonlinear MMSE estimator of the desired vector y given the input x, and let
173   ϵ∗ = y − ŷ∗ denote the corresponding estimation error. Then, for any properly measurable function
174   g of the input x, we have
175                                               E(g(x)ϵT∗ ) = 0.                                         (3)
176
       In other words, the estimation error of the optimal nonlinear MMSE estimator is orthogonal to
177
      any arbitrary nonlinear transformation of the input. In linear MMSE estimation, the orthogonality
178
      principle states that the estimation error is orthogonal to the observations and their linear functions.
179   Mathematically, this is expressed equivalent to restricting g(·) to be a linear function. Using this
180   orthogonality condition in reverse to derive linear estimators is a standard practice in the field (see,
181   for example,Kailath et al. (2000)). Techniques such asKalman Filtering are based on this principle,
182   which is firmly grounded in the Hilbert space projection theorem.
183
      For nonlinear MMSE estimation, the orthogonality condition in (3) is even stronger: the estimation
184
      error is orthogonal to any nonlinear function of the input. Exploiting this stronger condition to
185   construct nonlinear MMSE estimators is an open problem, primarily because it raises questions
186   about which nonlinear functions to choose and how many are needed.
187
188
      In the proposed framework, we model the neural network as a parameterized nonlinear MMSE
      estimator and seek as many equations from the orthogonality principle as possible to determine these
189
      parameters. This is exactly the same principle as how the orthogonality condition is used in reverse
190
      to find parameters for linear estimators. To address the challenge of selecting nonlinear functions
191   that yield informative equations for determining network parameters, we choose the activations of
192   the hidden layers in the neural network as these functions. This choice is natural because these
193   activations are directly related to the network’s parameters through differentiation. Therefore, if the
194   network converges to the optimal nonlinear MMSE estimator, the hidden layer activations should be
195   orthogonal to the output errors. We formalize this observation with the following equations:

300   2.4     F URTHER EBD ALGORITHM EXTENSIONS
301
302   We propose further extensions to the EBD framework to address potential activation collapse,
303
      which can arise when minimizing correlations is the sole objective. To prevent unit-level collapse,
      we introduce power regularization, while entropy regularization is employed to prevent dimensional
304
      collapse. Both regularizations can be implemented in ANNs as well as biologically plausible
305
      networks. Although CorInfoMax-EBD inherently includes entropy regularization, it can also
306   benefit from the addition of power regularization for enhanced stability. Additionally, we introduce
307   forward layer activation projections to improve the algorithm’s versatility. We also extend the EBD
308   formulations to more complex architectures, including Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and
309   Locally Connected (LC) networks. Detailed implementations and evaluations of these extensions
310   are provided in Appendix C.

356   3     EBD FOR BIOLOGICALLY MORE REALISTIC NETWORKS
357
358   In the previous section, we introduced the EBD algorithm within the context of MLP networks.
359   While MLPs can resemble biologically plausible networks depending on the credit assignment
360   mechanism, in this section, we extend the application of the EBD approach to neural networks
361   that exhibit more biologically realistic dynamics and architectures. This extension is motivated by
362   two key properties of the EBD framework: first, the error is broadcast directly to the layers, naturally
363   eliminating the weight symmetry issue observed in the BP algorithm; second, the EBD update rules
364   mimic the form of extended Hebbian updates with modulatory components. In the following subsec-
365
      tions, we explore how EBD relates to the biologically plausible three-factor learning rule and demon-
      strate its integration with the biologically more realistic CorInfoMax networks (Bozkurt et al., 2023).
366
367
368
      3.1     T HREE FACTOR LEARNING RULE AND EBD
369   The three-factor learning rule proposed for biological neural networks extends the traditional two-
370   factor Hebbian rule by incorporating a modulatory signal into synaptic updates based on presynaptic

      5   C ONCLUSIONS , EXTENSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
512
      Conclusions and Extensions. In this article, we introduced the Error Broadcast and Decorrela-
513
      tion (EBD) framework as a biologically plausible alternative to traditional backpropagation. EBD
514
      addresses the credit assignment problem by minimizing correlations between layer activations and
515   output errors, offering fresh insights into biologically realistic learning. This approach provides a
516   theoretical foundation for existing error broadcast mechanisms in biological neural networks and
517   facilitates flexible implementations in neuromorphic and artificial neural systems. EBD’s error-
518   broadcasting mechanism aligns with biological processes where global error signals modulate local
519   synaptic updates, potentially bridging the gap between artificial learning algorithms and natural neu-
520   ral computations. Moreover, EBD’s simplicity and parallelism make it suitable for efficient hardware
521   implementations, such as neuromorphic computing systems that emulate the brain’s architecture.
522   We believe that the MMSE orthogonality property underpinning the proposed EBD framework has
523   great potential for developing new algorithms, deepening theoretical understanding, and analyzing
524   neural networks in both artificial and biological contexts. We are currently unaware of similar the-
525   oretical properties for alternative loss functions. Notably, our numerical experiments in Appendix
526   F.2 reveal that similar decorrelation behavior occurs for networks trained with backpropagation and
527   categorical cross entropy loss, suggesting that decorrelation may be a general feature of the learning
528   process and an intriguing avenue for further investigation.
529
530   Limitations. The current implementation of EBD involves several hyperparameters, including
531
      multiple learning rates for decorrelation and regularization functions, as well as forgetting factors for
      correlation matrices. Although these parameters offer flexibility, they add complexity to the tuning
532
      process. Additionally, the use of dynamically updated error projection matrices and the potential in-
533
      tegration of entropy regularization may increase memory and computational demands. Future work
534   could explore more efficient methods for managing these components, potentially automating or
535   simplifying the tuning process to enhance usability. Furthermore, while the scalability of EBD is
536   left out of the focus of the article, we acknowledge its importance. Launay et al. (2020) demonstrated
537   that DFA scales to high-dimensional tasks like transformer-based language modeling. Since DFA is
538   equivalent to EBD with frozen projection weights and without entropy regularization, we anticipate
539   that EBD could scale similarly. However, this remains unvalidated empirically. Examining EBD’s
      scalability and streamlining its components to improve usability are important tasks for future work.

      A    P RELIMINARIES ON NONLINEAR MINIMUM MEAN SQUARE ERROR
706        ESTIMATION
707
708   Let y ∈ Rp and x ∈ Rn represent two non-degenerate random vectors with a joint probability
709   density function fyx (y, x) and conditional density fy|x (y|x). The goal of nonlinear minimum
710
      mean square error (MMSE) estimation is to find an estimator function b : Rn → Rp that minimizes
      the mean squared error (MSE), which is defined as:
711
712                                  M SE(b) = E(∥y − b(x)∥22 ).
713
      Lemma A.1. The best linear MMSE estimate of y given x is:
714
715                                    bM M SE (x) = Ey|x (y|x).
716
717   The proof of Lemma A.1 relies on the following fundamental result (see, for example, the textbook
718   by Papoulis & Pillai (2002)), which is central to the development of the entire EBD framework in
719   the current article:
720   Lemma A.2. The estimation error for bM M SE (x) = Ey|x [y|x], denoted as eM M SE = y −
721   bM M SE (x), is orthogonal to any vector-valued function g : Rn → Rk of x. Formally, we have:
722
723                                     E(eM M SE g(x)T ) = 0.
724
725   Proof. (Lemma A.2) The proof follows simple steps:
726
                    E(eM M SE g(x)T )    = Ex Ey|x (y − Ey|x (y|x))g(x)T |x

727
                                         = Ex ( Ey|x (y|x) − Ey|x (y|x))g(x)T = 0.

728
729
730
731   Using Lemma A.2, we can now prove Lemma Lemma A.1:
732
733   Proof. (Lemma A.1) Let b : Rn → Rp be any arbitrary function. The corresponding MSE can be
734   written as:
735
736                                     M SE(b) = E(∥y − b(x)∥22 ).
737   By adding and subtracting Ey|x [y|x], we can decompose the error as:
738
739                    M SE(b) = E(∥y − Ey|x (y|x) + Ey|x (y|x) − b(x)∥22 )
740                              = E(∥y − Ey|x ∥22 ) + E(∥Ey|x (y|x) − b(x)∥22 )
741
742
                                   + 2E((y − Ey|x )T (Ey|x (y|x) − b(x)))
743                              = E(∥y − Ey|x ∥22 ) + E(∥Ey|x(y|x) (y|x) − b(x)∥22 )
744                                + 2E(T r((y − Ey|x (y|x))(Ey|x (y|x) − b(x))T ))
745
746
                                 = E(∥y − Ey|x ∥22 ) + E(∥Ey|x(y|x) (y|x) − b(x)∥22 )
747                                + 2T r(E(eM M SE (Ey|x (y|x) − b(x))T )).
748
      The third term, representing the cross product, vanishes by Lemma A.2, leaving us with:
749
750               M SE(b) = E(∥y − bM M SE (x)∥22 ) + E(∥bM M SE (x) − b(x)∥22 ).
751
      Since the second term is always non-negative, the MSE is minimized when b(x) = bM M SE (x).

      B.3     O N EBD WITH FORWARD PROJECTIONS
865
866
867
868   In the EBD algorithm introduced in Section 2.3 , output errors are broadcast to individual layers
869   to modify their weights, thereby reducing the correlation between hidden layer activations and out-
870   put errors. To enhance this mechanism, we introduce forward broadcasting, where hidden layer
871   activations are projected onto the output layer. This projection facilitates the optimization of the
872   decorrelation loss by adjusting the parameters of the final layer more effectively.
873   The purpose of forward broadcasting is to enhance the network’s ability to minimize the decor-
874   relation loss by directly influencing the final layer’s weights using the activations from the hidden
875   layers. By projecting the hidden layer activations forward onto the output layer, we establish a direct
876   pathway for these activations to impact the adjustments of the final layer’s weights. This mechanism
877   allows the final layer to update its parameters in a way that reduces the correlation between the out-
878   put errors and the hidden layer activations. Consequently, the errors at the output layer are steered
879   toward being orthogonal to the hidden layer activations.
880   This mechanism could potentially be effective because the final layer is responsible for mapping the
881   network’s internal representations to the output space. By incorporating information from earlier
882   layers, we enable the final layer to align its parameters more closely with the features that are most
883   relevant for reducing the overall error.
884   While the proposed forward broadcasting mechanism is primarily motivated by performance op-
885   timization, it can conceptually be related to the long-range (Leong et al., 2016) and bottom-up
886   (Ibrahim et al., 2021) synaptic connections in the brain, which allow certain neurons to influence
887   distant targets. These long-range bottom-up connections are actively being researched, and incorpo-
888   rating similar mechanisms into computational models could enhance their alignment with biological
889   neural processes. By integrating mechanisms that mirror these neural pathways, forward broadcast-
890   ing may be useful for modeling how information is transmitted across different neural circuits.

       E.9   O N THE SCALING OF THE ORTHOGONALITY CONDITIONS
2215
2216   The orthogonality principle in our method is defined as the uncorrelatedness of a given hidden layer
2217   neuron’s activation with all components of the output error. Specifically, for the ith neuron of layer-k
2218   and the j th component of the output error, ϵj , the orthogonality condition is expressed as,
2219                                         (k)
2220                         Rh(k) ϵj = E(hi ϵj ) = 0,        j = 1, . . . m, i = 1, . . . n              (38)
                                i
2221
       where m is the number of output components, and n is the size of activations for layer k.
2222
2223   Based on Equation 38 above, for each hidden layer, there are m x n orhogonality constraints. There-
2224   fore, even if the hidden layer dimensions and/or the network depth increase, the total number of
2225   constraints also increases, making our system less underdetermined. We use constraints for differ-
2226
       ent neurons separately to adjust their corresponding weight/bias parameters. In other words, the
                                                                                                (k)
2227   constraints in (Eq. A) are used to update the ith row of the j th column of W(k) , i.e. Wi,j and the
2228   bias compoent bi .
2229   Note that, more generality of the orthogonality condition for nonlinear estimators offers potential
2230   to increase the number of constraints per hidden layer neuron: We can increase the number of
2231   the orthogonality conditions per neuron even further by considering the fact that uncorrelatedness
2232   requirement is for any function g of hidden layer neuron activations, i.e.,
2233                                           (k)
2234                      Rg(h(k) )ϵj = E(g(hi )ϵj ) = 0,        j = 1, . . . m i = 1, . . . n
                               i
2235
       Therefore, the number of uncorrelatedness (orthgonality) constraints per hidden layer/output neuron
2236
       can be increased by introducing multiple g functions. However, in our numerical experiments we
2237   haven’t pursued this path.
2238
2239   Although the orthogonality conditions scale with the increasing parameter size, the total number
2240
       of parameters in the network is in general larger than the number of decorrelation conditions. This
       results in fewer constraints than parameters, leading to an overparameterized system, where a unique
2241
       optimal estimator cannot be determined solely based on these conditions. But our results show that
2242
       the learning rule converges effectively within practical timeframes. Particularly in the case of using
2243   Locally Connected Networks (LC), which are highly overparameterized, the improved performance
2244   and generalization observed strongly validate the practicality of our approach to successfully train
2245   in the overparametrized regime.
2246
       Importantly, this issue of overparameterization also exists in standard backpropagation, where the
2247
       number of parameters often exceeds the number of training samples, leading to an overparameter-
2248
       ized and underdetermined system. In both cases, this overparameterization does not hinder learning;
2249   rather, it is a fundamental characteristic of deep learning. Research has demonstrated that the im-
2250   plicit bias in gradient descent introduces a regularization effect, steering the optimization process
2251   toward solutions that generalize well to unseen data (Soudry et al., 2018).
2252
       Similarly, in our method, while the number of orthogonality constraints is smaller than the total
2253
       number of network parameters, the system is guided by the statistical properties of the error and
2254
       activations. While we cannot claim to fully characterize the implicit regularization effect in our
2255   method, we suggest that these statistical constraints play a role similar to the implicit regularization
2256   observed in regular backpropagation. This helps ensure that the learned parameters are not arbitrary
2257   but are shaped by the decorrelation principles inherent to our framework, contributing to the model’s
2258   generalization capabilities. We believe that investigating the inherent implicit bias in Error Broadcast
2259   and Decorrelation (EBD) opens the door to further understanding how this framework naturally
2260   regularizes the learning process.
2261   To further adress limited-data problems, our method incorporates several regularization techniques:
2262   entropy regularization (encourages the network to utilize the full feature space by spreading acti-
2263   vations), sparsity regularization (enforces sparse activations to reduce redundancy), weight decay
2264   (prevents overfitting by penalizing large weights). These regularizers supplement the orthogonality-
2265   based learning rule, particularly in the limited-data regime, improving generalization and stability.
