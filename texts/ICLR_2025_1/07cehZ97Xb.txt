011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Existing large pre-trained models typically map text input to text output in an
014            end-to-end manner, such as ChatGPT, or map a segment of text input to a hierar-
015
               chy of action decisions, such as OpenVLA. However, humans can simultaneously
               generate text and actions when receiving specific input signals. For example, a
016
               driver can make precise driving decisions while conversing with a friend in the
017
               passenger seat. Motivated by this observation, we consider the following ques-
018            tion in this work: is it possible to construct a pre-trained model that can provide
019            both language interaction and precise decision-making capabilities in dynamic
020            open scenarios. We provide a definitive answer to this question by developing a
021            new model architecture termed Visual Language Action model for Chatting and
022            Decision Making (VLA4CD), and further demonstrating its performance in chal-
023            lenging automonous driving tasks. We build VLA4CD on the basis of transformer-
024            based LLM architecture. Specifically, we leverage LoRA to fine-tune a pre-trained
025            LLM with data of multiple modalities covering language, visual, and action. Un-
026
               like the existing LoRA operations used for LLM fine-tuning, we have designed
               new computational modules and training cost functions for VLA4CD. These de-
027
               signs enable VLA4CD to provide continuous-valued action decisions while out-
028
               putting text responses. In contrast, existing LLMs can only output text responses,
029            and current VLA models can only output action decisions. Moreover, these VLA
030            models handle action data by discretizing and then tokenizing the discretized ac-
031            tions, a method unsuitable for complex decision-making tasks involving high-
032            dimensional continuous-valued action vectors, such as autonomous driving. The
033            extensive experimental results on the closed-loop autonomous driving platform
034            CARLA validate that: (1) the model construction method we proposed is effec-
035            tive; (2) compared to the state-of-the-art VLA model, VLA4CD can provide more
036            accurate real-time decision-making while retaining the text interaction capability
037
               inherent to LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Since the emergence of ChatGPT, large-scale pre-trained models, represented by large language
042   models (LLMs), have garnered increasing attention. LLMs are trained on vast amounts of text and
043   code data on the internet, encoding a significant amount of general knowledge about the real world.
044   This equips them with better generalization capabilities compared to traditional AI models, such as
045   in-context learning abilities and certain reasoning capabilities (through techniques such as chain-of-
046   thought (Wei et al., 2022)). A development trend in the field of large-scale pre-trained models is that
047   their application domains are expanding from tasks like dialogue and text generation to decision-
048   making tasks in the open physical world.
049   How to build large-scale pre-trained models for decision-making tasks in the open physical world?
050   Currently, there are three major approaches. An approach is to serialize the decision-making pro-
051   cess and then train a sequence model, such as the decision transformer (Chen et al., 2021), in the
052   same way as processing text. This method relies on the construction of large-scale high-quality
053   decision-making datasets. The second approach involves adopting a hierarchical modular system
      design, where the pre-trained LLM provides high-level planning, such as breaking down the target


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      task into a series of subtasks and then completing each subtask by calling tools or small models
055   aimed at the subtasks (Chen et al., 2024; Carta et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024). This
056   approach requires manual pre-design of system modularization and the establishment of interfaces
057   between modules. Additionally, after the model is deployed, in addition to the latency caused by
058   LLM inference, it also introduces the working latency of other modules, making it unsuitable for
059   decision-making scenarios with high-time requirements. The last approach is to train a multimodal
060   visual language action model (VLA) based on LLM (Padalkar et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). Un-
061   like the hierarchical modular method, the VLA model can provide end-to-end decision generation,
062   eliminating the need for manual module design and interface design between modules.
063   To the best of our knowledge, existing LLM or VLA models, given an input signal (a piece of text
064   prompt, an image, or a video), produce outputs that are single-modal (a piece of text or an action
065   decision). However, we know that for us humans, we can simultaneously generate text and actions
066   when receiving specific input signals. For example, a driver can make precise driving decisions
067   while conversing with a friend in the passenger seat. Inspired by the above observations, we attempt
068   to answer the following question in this paper:
069   Is it possible to develop a pre-trained model that can provide both action decision-making and
070   text interaction capabilities in an end-to-end manner?
071
      We provide a definitive answer to it by developing a new model architecture termed Visual Lan-
072
      guage Action model for Chatting and Decision Making (VLA4CD), and further demonstrating its
073   performance in challenging automonous driving tasks. Like existing VLA models, VLA4CD is a
074   multimodal pre-trained large model developed based on the transformer architecture. However, it
075   has significant differences from current VLAs (such as RT-X (Brohan et al., 2022; 2023)):
076
077          • The operational mechanism of VLA involves executing serialized decisions after receiv-
078
               ing text instructions, without generating text data during the decision-making process. In
               contrast, VLA4CD allows for the synchronous generation of text data during real-time
079
               decision-making.
080
081
             • Current VLA models typically handle action data by discretizing it and then tokenizing
               the discrete values. This approach is not suitable for complex decision-making scenarios
082
               such as autonomous driving, where actions are high-dimensional continuous value vectors.
083
               Our VLA4CD processes actions directly as continuous values, eliminating the need for
084            discretization and making it more suitable for such scenarios.
085
086   In summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows.
087
             • We propose a new problem setting: how to synthesize the capabilities of LLM and VLA us-
088
               ing a single model to achieve end-to-end simultaneous action decision-making and chatting
089
               with people.
090
             • We present a solution to the aforementioned problem. Specifically, we propose a method
091
               for constructing VLA4CD based on pre-trained LLM and have validated the effectiveness
092
               and superiority of this method through extensive closed-loop autonomous driving experi-
093            ments on CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017). The experimental results show that the result-
094            ing VLA4CD model not only outputs more accurate real-time action decisions compared
095            to the SOTA models but also perfectly retains real-time text-based dialogue functionality.
096            Our method combines several experimentally validated ideas: (1) a computational module
097            and cost function term for generating continuous action values; (2) an image reconstruction
098            loss term added in the training cost function to ensure the exploitation of rich information
099            from the visual modality data during text generation and decision-making processes; (3) a
100            label smoothing strategy to maintain dialogue capabilities and enhance decision-making.
101          • We will open source our model, code, and dataset after the reviewing process.

107   Since the publication of (Brown et al., 2020), generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) has be-
      come the most popular training paradigm for building LLMs. LLMs represented by GPT-3.5 and

109   to previous language models (OpenAI, 2023). The release of the open-source LLaMA series models
110   (Touvron et al., 2023a;b) has accelerated the development of LLMs. In (Wei et al., 2022), a gen-
111   eral technique to enhance LLM reasoning capabilities, known as chain-of-thought, was proposed.
112   The work in (Yao et al., 2022) proposed ReAct, which uses LLMs to generate reasoning traces
113   and task-specific actions in an interleaved manner, thereby achieving greater synergy between the
114   two. Additionally, recent works have used LLMs as components in building hierarchical modular

118
      scenario, featured by its capability to simultaneously output action decisions and textual chatting.

      2.2   VLA MODEL FOR DECISON - MAKING
122
      The VLA model is a type of model designed to handle multimodal input of vision, language, and
123   action to accomplish embodied decision-making tasks. Unlike traditional LLMs that are mostly
124   used for constructing conversational AI represented by ChatGPT, VLA has the ability to generate
125   a control signal for a physical entity, e.g., a Robot, that interacts with the environment. VLA has
126   been widely used for instruction-following tasks, wherein it endows the agent with an ability to
127   understand language instructions, visually perceive the environment, and generate appropriate ac-
128   tions (Huang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Zhen et al., 2024; Dorka et al.). Compared to deep
129   reinforcement learning (RL) methods, VLA has shown a remarkable performance gain in versatil-
130   ity, flexibility, and generality in complex environments (Padalkar et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023;
131
      et al, 2024; Team et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023c; Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; 2023a; Liu et al.,
      2024; Tan & Bansal, 2019). However, such VLA models represented by RT-X (Padalkar et al.,
132
      2023) and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024), typically discretize continuous action spaces into fixed
133
      intervals. This action discretization raises significant limitations for them to deal with fine-grained
134   continuous actions that are required for capturing nuanced operations necessary for some complex
135   tasks. This issue is particularly pronounced in scenarios that require high precision and real-time
136   responsiveness, such as autonomous driving.
137
      In our VLA4CD, we propose a technique to avoid action discretization in VLA. In addition, we
138
      consider a new problem setting that differs from the instruction-following one. In our setting, the
139
      agent can chat with a human and make fine-grained decisions simultaneously. We found that if we
140   directly use current VLA models into this setting, they perform unsatisfactorily as they tend to rely
141   more on the text data to generate decisions while neglecting the critical role of visual information.

363

364
      4o (OpenAI, 2023) to compare the quality of answers given by VLA4CD with baseline models.

      4.7    A BLATION STUDIES ON THE LOSS FUNCTION DESIGN
464
465
      As shown in Equation (6), our loss function is composed of three losses, namely action loss Laction ,
      language loss Llanguage , and image loss Limage . We conducted ablation studies to investigate the
466
      effect of each loss on the performance of VLA4CD. The experiment result is shown in Table 4,
467
      where the action-bins loss Laction-bins denotes the action loss used by OpenVLA and RT2. They
468   deal with continuous valued actions by value discretization. We included VLA4CD (no-language)
469   and VLA4CD (no-image), two simplified versions of VLA4CD trained by using Laction +Limage and
470   Laction +Llanguage , respectively.
471
      On the effect of Laction As shown in Table 4, if we compare the performance metrics of Limage +
472
      Llanguage + Laction-bins with that of Limage + Llanguage + Laction , we can see a clear advantage of using
473
      our action loss Laction over using Laction-bins . This explains why VLA4CD outperforms VLA models
474   that use the type of action loss similar to Laction-bins , as shown in Tables 1, 2, and 3. Specifically,
475   from our experiments, we found that doing action discretization and tokenization as in current VLA
476   models lead to low training loss but bad inference performance. This is because adjacent action
477   intervals are represented by consecutive token IDs (e.g., 31830 and 31831), which are close in token
478   space. Consequently, the model tends to output the same token (31830 or 31831) in inference, while
479   the actual action values corresponding to them can have significant differences. In contrast, our
480   approach proposed to deal with continuously valued actions can avoid this phenomenon to happen.

484   quality of decision-making. As shown in Figures 2 and 4, VLA4CD (no-language) has significantly
485   different dialogue capabilities compared to VLA4CD, while VLA4CD (no-image) performs simi-

      4.8    H OW THE QUALITY OF TEXTUAL MODALITY DATA IN TRAINING DATA AFFECTS THE
508          DECISION - MAKING PERFORMANCE OF THE MODEL ?
509
510
      Imagine a driver is operating a car, with a friend sitting inside the vehicle and conversing with the
511   driver. If this friend provides valuable reminders, such as alerting the driver to a car approaching
512   from the blind spot, the friend’s words would be beneficial to the driver’s decision-making. On the
513   contrary, if the friend’s words are irrelevant noise to the current situation, it might interfere with
514   the driver’s ability to make accurate decisions. Therefore, we designed a set of experiments to test
515   whether our model exhibits similar performance to that of human drivers in decision making. The
516   result is presented in Table 5. As is shown, when we add more and more noisy information unrelated
517   to driving scenarios into the text modality data in the training dataset, the quality of the decisions
518   output by our model rapidly decreases. This indicates that the performance of our model is very
519
      similar to that of human drivers.

522   5     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      In this paper, we investigated how to develop a multimodal pre-trained model that simultaneously
      achieves the dialogue function of LLM and the decision-making function of VLA. We use the au-
526
      tonomous driving scenario as an example to explain our problem setup and model development
527
      process. Unlike the instruction-following setup used behind VLA models, our problem setup can
528   be described as making decisions while conversing. In the former, text data appears in the form of
529   instructions before the decision-making process; in the latter, text data and decision data are inter-
530   woven (imagine a large pre-trained model making driving decisions while chatting with people in
531   the car). For the aforementioned problem setup, we provide a method for constructing a multimodal
532   Visual Language Action model for simultaneously Chatting and Decision making (VLA4CD). Ex-
533   perimental results show that, thanks to our proposed way to deal with continuous valued actions,
534   our design of the training cost function, and the use of label smoothing technique, our VLA4CD
535   model significantly outperforms the SOTA VLA model, RL, and decision transformer methods in
536
      decision-making performance, while also possessing smooth dialogue capabilities.
537   VLA4CD can be seen as a functional extension of the VLA model, while its performance depends
538   on the quality of the training data set. Interesting future research directions include: further testing
539   and validation using large-scale real-world driving datasets; and applying our approach to scenarios
      beyond autonomous driving, such as home robots.

      A.5    M EASURE PERFORMANCE METRICS
850
851   We use multiple key metrics to evaluate the performance of autonomous driving models in various
852   driving scenarios. Collision Rate (CR): the frequency at which the vehicle collides with obstacles
853   or other vehicles. This metric is critical for assessing the safety of the driving model. Outlane Rate
854   (OR): the rate at which the vehicle deviates from its designated lane. This metric evaluates the ability
855   of modes to maintain proper lane discipline. Episode Completion Rate (ER): the percentage of driv-
856   ing tasks or episodes that the vehicle successfully completes. Higher completion rates indicate better
857
      task performance. Average Safe Driving Distance (ASD): the average distance driven without inci-
      dents, such as collisions or off-road events. This metric highlights the capability to drive safely over
858
      extended periods. Average Return (AR): A metric that measures the cumulative reward collected by
859
      the vehicle during its driving tasks, often reflecting both task performance and adherence to safety
860   guidelines. Driving Score (DS): A comprehensive metric that reflects the overall performance of the
861   vehicle in terms of safety, efficiency, and compliance with traffic rules.

898   A.7   T HE BENEFITS OF CROSS - ENTROPY LOSS AND LABEL SMOOTHING LOSS FOR VLA4CD
899
900   We found that merely replacing specific numerical values in the translation template (Chen et al.,
901   2024) results in minimal representational differences caused by the sequential nature of data, making
902
      it easy for conventional cross-entropy loss to lead to overfitting in text generation tasks. As shown
      in Table 10, we tested on both town03 and town04, which led to a decline in the decision-making
903
      performance of model. Compared to cross-entropy loss, cross-entropy loss with smoothed labels
904
      performed better. Therefore, we chose cross-entropy loss with smoothed labels as the loss for text
905   generation in VLA4CD in our experiments.
