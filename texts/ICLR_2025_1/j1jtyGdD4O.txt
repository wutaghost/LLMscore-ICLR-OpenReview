                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011
                   Accurate weather forecasting is critical for science and society. Yet, existing
012                methods have not demonstrated high accuracy, low uncertainty, and high com-
013                putational efficiency simultaneously. On one hand, to quantify the uncertainty in
014                weather predictions, the strategy of ensemble forecast (i.e., generating a set of
015                diverse predictions) is often employed. However, traditional ensemble numeri-
016                cal weather prediction (NWP) is computationally intensive. On the other hand,
017                even though most existing machine learning-based weather prediction (MLWP)
018                approaches are efficient and accurate, they are deterministic and cannot capture
019                the uncertainty of weather forecasting. To tackle these challenges, we propose
020
                   CoDiCast, a conditional diffusion model to generate accurate global weather
                   prediction, while achieving uncertainty quantification and modest computational
021
                   cost. The key idea behind the prediction task is to generate realistic weather sce-
022
                   narios at a future time point, conditioned on observations from the recent past.
023                Due to the probabilistic nature of diffusion models, they can be properly applied
024                to capture the uncertainty of weather predictions. Therefore, we accomplish un-
025                certainty quantifications by repeatedly sampling from stochastic Gaussian noise
026                for each initial weather state and running the denoising process multiple times.
027                Experimental results demonstrate that CoDiCast outperforms several existing
028                MLWP methods in accuracy, and is faster than NWP models in the inference
029                speed. CoDiCast can generate 3-day global weather forecasts, at 6-hour steps
030                and 5.625◦ latitude-longitude resolutions, for over 5 variables, in about 12 minutes
031
                   on a commodity A100 GPU machine with 80GB memory. The anonymous code
                   is provided at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CoDiCast/.

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Weather prediction describes how the weather states evolve by mapping the current weather states
037   to future weather states (Palmer, 2012). Accurate weather forecasting is crucial for a wide range of
038   societal activities, from daily planning to disaster preparedness (Merz et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2024).
039   For example, governments, organizations, and individuals rely heavily on weather forecasts to make
040   informed decisions that can significantly impact safety, economic efficiency, and overall well-being.
041   However, weather predictions are intrinsically uncertain largely due to the complex and chaotic
042   nature of atmospheric processes (Slingo & Palmer, 2011; Palmer et al., 2005). Therefore, assessing
043
      the range of probable weather scenarios is significant, as it facilitates informed decision-making.
044   Traditional numerical weather prediction (NWP) methods achieve weather forecasting by approxi-
045   mately solving the differential equations representing the integrated system between the atmosphere,
046   land, and ocean (Price et al., May 2024; Nguyen et al., 2023). However, running such an NWP model
047   can produce only one possibility of the forecast, which ignores the weather uncertainty. To solve
048   this problem, Ensemble forecast1 of multiple models is often employed to model the probability
049
      distribution of different future weather scenarios (Palmer, 2019; Leinonen et al., 2023). While such
      NWP-based ensemble forecasts effectively model the weather uncertainty, they have two primary
050
      limitations: physics-based models inherently make restrictive assumptions of atmospheric dynamics
051
      (Palmer et al., 2005) and running multiple these NWP-models require extreme computational costs
052   (Rodwell & Palmer, 2007).
053
          1
              Generating a set of forecasts, each of which represents a single possible scenario.

054
      In recent years, machine learning (ML)-based weather predictions (MLWP) have been proposed to
055   challenge NWP-based prediction methods (Ben Bouallègue et al., 2024; Bülte et al., 2024). They
056   have achieved enormous success with comparable accuracy and a much (usually three orders of
057   magnitude) lower computational overhead. Representative work includes Pangu (Bi et al., 2023),
058   GraphCast (Lam et al., 2023), ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023), ForeCastNet (Pathak et al., 2022),
059   Fuxi (Chen et al., 2023b), Fengwu (Chen et al., 2023a), and W-MAE (Man et al., 2023). They are
060   typically trained to learn weather patterns from a huge amount of historical data and predict the
061   mean of the probable trajectories by minimizing the mean squared error (MSE) of model forecasts
062   (Hewage et al., 2021). Despite the notable achievements of these MLWP methods, most of them are
063
      deterministic (Kochkov et al., 2024), falling short in capturing the uncertainty in weather forecasts
      (Jaseena & Kovoor, 2022). This limitation motivates us to explore an approach for uncertainty
064
      quantification while being capable of forecasting weather scenarios accurately.
065
066   Denoising probabilistic diffusion models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020) stand out as a probabilistic type
067   of generative models, which can generate high-quality image samples. By explicitly and iteratively
068   modeling the noise additive and its removal, DDPMs can capture intricate details and textures of im-
069
      ages. Furthermore, controllable diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) enable
      the generation process to be guided by specific attributes or conditions, e.g., class labels, textual de-
070
      scriptions, or other auxiliary information. By doing so, the models can generate images that adhere
071
      to the specified conditions. This inspires us to consider the weather “prediction” tasks as “genera-
072   tion” tasks - generating plausible weather scenarios with conditional diffusion models. Promising
073   potentials could be the following: (1) Weather numerical data is usually a 2-D grid over latitude
074   and longitude, sharing a similar modality with the image. Diffusion models can capture the intricate
075   weather distribution with iterative denoising. (2) Weather states from the recent past (i.e., initial con-
076   ditions) can be injected into diffusion models to guide the generation of future weather evolution. (3)
077   More notably, probabilistic diffusion models can generate a set of diverse weather scenarios rather
078   than a single deterministic one. This capability makes them well-suited for modeling the uncertain
079   nature of weather evolution. Our contributions are presented as follows:
080
             • We identify the shortcomings of current weather prediction methods. NWP-based methods
081            are limited to restrictive assumptions and computationally intensive. Moreover, a single
082            deterministic NWP- and MLWP-based method cannot achieve uncertainty quantification.
083
             • To address these problems, we propose CoDiCast, a conditional diffusion model for
084            global weather prediction conditioning on observations from the recent past while prob-
085            abilistically modeling the uncertainty. In addition, we use the cross-attention mechanism
086            to effectively integrate conditions into the denoising process to guide the generation tasks.
087
             • We conduct extensive experiments on a decade of ERA5 reanalysis data from the European
088            Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF), and evaluate our method against
089            several state-of-the-art models in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and uncertainty. It turns out
090            that CoDiCast achieves an essential trade-off among these valuable properties.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      Numerical Weather Prediction. Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) methods obtain weather
095
      forecasts by modeling the system of the atmosphere, land, and ocean with complex differential
096   equations (Bauer et al., 2015). High-Resolution Forecasts System (HRES) (ECMWF, 2023) is
097   a representative NWP method that forecasts possible weather evolution out to 10 days ahead.
098   However, HRES is a deterministic NWP method that only provides a single forecast. The ensemble
099   forecast suite (ENS) (Buizza, 2008) was developed as an ensemble of 51 forecasts by the European
100   Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). ENS provides a range of possible future
101   weather states in the medium range, allowing for investigation of the detail and uncertainty in the
102   forecast. Even if ENS and other NWP-based ensemble forecasts effectively model the weather
103   evolution, they exhibit sensitivity to structural discrepancies across models (Balaji et al., 2022),
104
      regional variability (Verma et al., 2024), and high computational demands (Lam et al., 2023).
105
106   ML-Based Weather Prediction. Numerous machine learning (ML)-based weather prediction
107   (MLWP) approaches have emerged as a compelling alternative to NWP methods on weather
      forecasting. They are trained on enormous historical data and produce the mean of the probable


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      trajectories by minimizing the mean squared error (MSE) between model forecasts and ground-truth
109   (Hewage et al., 2021). Pangu (Bi et al., 2023) employed three-dimensional transformer networks
110   and Earth-specific priors to deal with complex patterns in weather data. GraphCast (Lam et al.,
111   2023) achieved medium-range weather prediction by utilizing an “encode-process-decode” config-
112   uration with each part implemented by graph neural networks (GNNs). GNNs perform effectively
113   in capturing the complex relationship between a set of surface and atmospheric variables. A similar
114   GNN-based work is (Keisler, 2022). Fuxi (Chen et al., 2023b) and Fengwu (Chen et al., 2023a)
115   also employ the “encode-decode” strategy but with the transformer-based backbone. FourCastNet
116   (Pathak et al., 2022) applied Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and Adaptive
117
      Fourier Neural Operators (AFNO) (Guibas et al., 2021), while ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023) also
      uses a ViT backbone but the trained model can be fine-tuned to various downstream tasks. However,
118
      these models fall short in modeling the uncertainty of weather evolution (Jaseena & Kovoor,
119
      2022; Bülte et al., 2024). Additionally, ClimODE (Verma et al., 2024) incorporated the physical
120   knowledge and developed a continuous-time neural advection PDE weather model.
121
122
123
      Diffusion Models. Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have shown their
      strong capability in computer vision tasks, including image generation (Li et al., 2022), image edit-
124
      ing (Nichol et al., 2021), semantic segmentation (Brempong et al., 2022) and point cloud comple-
125
      tion (Luo & Hu, 2021). Conditional diffusion models (Ho & Salimans, 2022) were later proposed
126   to make the generation step conditioned on the current context or situation. However, not many
127   efforts have adopted diffusion models in global medium-range weather forecasting. More recent
128   research has focused on precipitation nowcasting (Asperti et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
129   2024), and are localized in their predictions. GenCast (Price et al., May 2024) is a recently proposed
130   close-sourced conditional diffusion-based ensemble forecasting for medium-range weather predic-
131   tion. However, the conditioning is shown to be insufficient in our paper (see the last case in ablation
132   study). Since GenCast is not open-sourced, we do not have access to details for a fair comparison.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
140
141   Deterministic Global Weather Predictions. Given the input consisting of the weather state(s),
142   X t ∈ RH×W ×C at time t, the problem is to predict a point-valued weather state, X t+∆t ∈
143   RH×W ×C at a future time point t + ∆t. H × W refers to the spatial resolution of data which
144   depends on how densely we grid the globe over latitudes and longitudes, C refers to the number of
145   channels (i.e., weather variables), and the superscripts t and t + ∆t refer to the current and future
146   time points. The long-range multiple-step forecasts could be achieved by autoregressive modeling
147   or direct predictions.
148   Probabilistic Global Weather Predictions. Unlike the
149   deterministic models that output point-valued predic-
150   tions, probabilistic methods model the probability of fu-
151   ture weather state(s) as a distribution P (X t+∆t | X t ),
152   conditioned on the state(s) from the recent past. Prob-
153   abilistic predictions are appropriate for quantifying the
      forecast uncertainty and making informed decisions.          Figure 1: Deterministic vs Probabilistic.

      4.7   E NSEMBLE F ORECAST
325
326   To enhance the reliability of weather forecasts, ensemble forecast strategy is often employed to
327   capture the variability among forecasts by separately running multiple deterministic models, e.g.,
328   ensemble forecast suite (ENS) (Buizza, 2008). In our approach, since CoDiCast is a probabilistic
329   model that can generate a distribution of future weather scenarios rather than a single prediction,
330   following (Price et al., May 2024), we run the trained CoDiCast multiple times to get the ensemble
331   instead. More specifically, by integrating both initial conditions and noise sampled from a Gaussian
332
      distribution, CoDiCast implements the ensemble forecast through multiple stochastic samplings
      during inference, capturing a range of possible forecasts for the uncertainty quantification.

364   between 2017 and 2018 as the testing set. We assess the global weather forecasting capabilities of
365   our method CoDiCast by predicting the weather at a future time t + ∆t (∆t = 6 to 36 hours)
366   based on the past two time units. To quantify the uncertainty in weather prediction, we generate an
367   “ensemble” forecast by running CoDiCast three times during the inference phase. We also analyze
368   and compare the inference efficiency between the NWP-based methods and CoDiCast.

370   Training. We first pretrain an encoder with the Autoencoder architecture. For the diffusion
371
      model, we used U-Net as the denoiser network with 1000 diffusion/denoising steps. The architec-
      ture is similar to that of DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) work. We employ four U-Net units for both the
372
      downsampling and upsampling processes. Each U-Net unit comprises two ResNet blocks (He et al.,
373
      2016) and a convolutional up/downsampling block. Before training, we apply Max-Min normal-
374   ization (Ali et al., 2014) to scale the input data within the range [0, 1], mitigating potential biases
375   stemming from varying scales (Shi et al., 2023). Adam was used as the optimizer, where the learning
376   rate = 2e−4 , decay steps = 10000, decay rate = 0.95. The batch size and number of epochs were
377   set to 64 and 800 respectively. More training details and model configurations are in Appendix C.

      Evaluation Metrics. Following (Verma et al., 2024), we use latitude-weighted Root Mean Square
379   Error (RMSE) and Anomaly Correlation Coefficient (ACC) as deterministic metrics. RMSE mea-
380   sures the average difference between values predicted by a model and the actual values. ACC is the
381   correlation between prediction anomalies relative to climatology and ground truth anomalies rela-
382   tive to climatology. It is a critical metric in climate science to evaluate the model’s performance in
383   capturing unusual weather or climate events. Moreover, following (Rasp et al., 2024) we utilize the
384   continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007) as a probabilistic metric to
385   measure the discrepancy between the predicted distribution and a single ground-truth value. A lower
386   CRPS value indicates higher forecast accuracy. Appendix D contains the formulas of these metrics.

      A       DATASET
760
761
      We introduce a detailed description of the ERA5 dataset. As the predominant data source for learn-
      ing and benchmarking weather prediction systems, the ERA5 reanalysis archive from the European
762
      Center for Medium-Range Weather Forecasting (ECMWF) provides reanalyzed data from 1979 on-
763
      wards. This data is available on a 0.25◦ × 0.25◦ global latitude-longitude grid of the Earth’s sphere,
764   at hourly intervals, with different atmospheric variables at 37 different altitude levels and some
765   variables on the Earth’s surface. The grid overall contains 721 × 1440 grid points for latitude and
766   longitude, respectively. Due to the limited computational resources, we used the preprocessed ver-
767   sion of ERA5 from WeatherBench Rasp et al. (2020) in our work. This dataset2 contains re-gridded
768   ERA5 reanalysis data in three lower resolutions: 5.625◦ , 2.8125◦ , and 1.40625◦ . To guarantee fair
769   comparison with the benchmarks Verma et al. (2024), we follow the ClimODE work and choose
770   the 5.625◦ resolution dataset for variables: geopotential at 500 hPa pressure level (Z500), atmo-
771   spheric temperature at 850 hPa pressure level (T850), ground temperature (T2m), 10 meter U wind
772
      component (U10) and 10 meter V wind component (V10). Single represents surface-level variables,
      and Atmospheric represents time-varying atmospheric properties at chosen altitudes. A sample at
773
      a certain time point can be represented by X t ∈ RH×W ×C where H × W refers to the spatial
774
      resolution of data which depends on how densely we grid the globe over latitude and longitude, C
775   refers to the number of channels (i.e, weather variables). In our work, H, W , and C are 32, 64,
776   and 5, accordingly. Notably, both Z500 and T850 are two popular verification variables for global
777   weather prediction models, while T2m, U10, and V10 directly pertain to human activities.

      We present the detailed architectures of the autoencoder, cross-attention block, and U-Net model
792
      used in our work. Meanwhile, we also illustrate how we organize the input data and how they flow
793   through different machine-learning model blocks. We recommend readers check out Figures 2, 3,
794   and 4 while looking into the following architectures.
795
796
      B.1      AUTOENCODER
797
798   We train an autoencoder model consisting of two main parts: an encoder and a decoder. The en-
799   coder compresses the input to feature representation (embedding) in the latent space. The decoder
800   reconstructs the input from the latent space. After training, the pre-trained encoder can be extracted
801   to generate embedding for input data. In our work, the convolutional autoencoder architecture is de-
802   signed for processing spatiotemporal weather data at a time point, t, represented as X t ∈ RH×W ×C .
803
      The encoder consists of a series of convolutional layers with 2 × 2 filters, each followed by a ReLU
      activation function. The layers have 32, 128, 256, and 512 filters, respectively, allowing for a pro-
804
      gressive increase in feature depth, thereby capturing essential patterns in the data. The decoder
805
      starts with 512 filters and reduces the feature depth through layers with 256 and 128 filters, each
806   followed by ReLU activations. This design ensures the reconstruction of the input data while pre-
807   serving the learned features, enabling the model to extract meaningful embeddings that encapsulate
808   the spatiotemporal characteristics of the input.

      B.3   U-N ET
846
847   Our U-Net architecture is similar to that3 of DDPM Ho et al. (2020) but with necessary changes to
848   adapt to the problems in this work. Each U-Net unit comprises two ResNet blocks He et al. (2016)
849   and a convolutional up/downsampling block. Self-attention was included between the convolution
850   blocks once we reached a specific resolution (4 × 8, 2 × 4), represented in blue arrows. We employ
851   four U-Net units for both the downsampling and upsampling processes. We use MaxPooling in the
852   downsampling units where the channel dimension is 64 × j (j = {1, 2, 3, 4} refers to the layer
853
      index). The upsampling units follow the reverse order. We set the upsampling factor as 2 and the
      “nearest” interpolation. We used the swish activation function throughout the network. We also
854
      had GroupNormalization layer for more stable training where the number of groups for Group
855
      Normalization is 8. Group Normalization divides the channels into groups and computes within each
856   group the mean and variance for normalization.

859
      embedding of past weather states, X t−1:t , and the noisy sample from the last diffusion step, Xnt+1 .
