009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The pace of evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) necessitates new ap-
012            proaches for rigorous and comprehensive evaluation. Traditional human annotation
013            is increasingly impracticable due to the complexities and costs involved in generat-
014            ing high-quality, challenging problems, particularly for tasks such as long-context
015            reasoning. Moreover, the rapid saturation of existing human-curated benchmarks
016            by LLMs further necessitates the need to develop scalable and automatically re-
017            newable evaluation methodologies. In this work, we introduce CHASE, a unified
018
               framework to synthetically generate challenging problems using LLMs without
               human involvement. For a given task, our approach builds a hard problem in a
019
               bottom-up manner from simpler components. Moreover since we want to generate
020
               synthetic data for evaluation, our framework decomposes the generation process
021            into independently verifiable sub-tasks, thereby ensuring a high level of quality and
022            correctness. We implement CHASE to create evaluation benchmarks across three
023            diverse domains: document-based question answering, repository-level code com-
024            pletion, and math reasoning. The performance of state-of-the-art LLMs on these
025            synthetic benchmarks lies in the range of 40-60% accuracy, thereby demonstrating
026            the effectiveness of our framework at generating hard problems. Our experiments
027            further reveal that the Gemini models significantly outperform other LLMs at
028            long-context reasoning, and that the performance of all LLMs drastically drops by
029
               as much as 70% when we scale up the context size to 50k tokens.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      In the past few years, we have witnessed the emergence of powerful Large Language Models
034
      (LLMs) (OpenAI Team et al., 2024; Llama Team et al., 2024; Gemini Team et al., 2024) that
035
      exhibit remarkable performance over a wide range of tasks. However, the methods and resources
036   for evaluating these models have not kept pace with their rapid evolution and increased capabilities.
037   Contemporary LLMs have saturated many existing reasoning benchmarks (Chen et al., 2021; Cobbe
038   et al., 2021). Developing challenging problems for reasoning tasks, such as math reasoning or code
039   generation, can be both expensive and time-consuming, especially for human annotators with average
040   expertise. Moreover, there are some settings, such as tasks requiring long-context reasoning over
041   hundreds of thousands of tokens, where the generation of high quality data by humans is impracticable.
042   Additionally, we are facing a significant depletion of interesting data that is publicly available (such
043   as SAT exams, Olympiad problems, etc.) for creating benchmarks. Hence, we believe that the
044
      conventional approach of developing evaluation benchmarks through human annotation and existing
      Web content is limited, and it is worthwhile to explore using LLMs for generating evaluation data.
045
046   Synthetic data generation has emerged as a powerful paradigm in recent years driven by the wide-
047   spread availability of cheaper and faster LLMs that can effectively follow instructions. The focus
048   of most prior works, however, has been on improving models by generating synthetic data for
049   pre-training (Gunasekar et al., 2023), instruction fine-tuning (Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024),
050
      alignment (Bai et al., 2022), or task-specific fine-tuning (Yue et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024). In
      contrast, using synthetic data for evaluation and benchmarking has been relatively underexplored.
051
052   There are considerable advantages in using synthetic data for evaluation: it is comparatively inexpen-
053   sive, highly scalable, and can be renewed periodically to mitigate contamination concerns. However,
      there are two main challenges: first, how can we create hard and realistic problems? and second, how

080   can we automatically verify the correctness of the generated data? Typical synthetic data generation
081
      pipelines are either focused on self-improvement or on distillation. In the former, a model generates
      large-scale synthetic data that provides useful guidance for training. However, a large portion of such
082
      model-generated data is incorrect (Wang et al., 2023), which makes it difficult to adapt such pipelines
083
      for evaluation. Distillation-based approaches use stronger models to generate complex problems for
084   training weaker models (Xu et al., 2024). However, we would like to design an approach that allows
085   us to craft problems that are challenging to solve even for the LLM that generated them.
086
087
      In this work, we present the CHASE framework: CHallenging AI with Synthetic Evaluations.
      Our methodology is based on two main ideas (see Figure 1) geared towards addressing the above-
088
      mentioned challenges. First, we create problems in a bottom-up manner where we iteratively hide
089
      parts of the solution within the problem’s context. This makes problems challenging because finding
090   the solution will require multiple steps of drawing inferences or reasoning over a longer context.
091   Second, we decompose the generation process into simpler, individually verifiable sub-tasks. This
092   facilitates fine-grained verification for correctness at each stage of the generation process.
093
      We implemented our framework to create challenging benchmarks across three diverse domains. (1)
094
      CHASE-QA is a document-based question answering benchmark. The average length of documents
095
      for each problem is 6k tokens. This benchmark simulates the real-world application of answering
096   user queries based on information that is spread across multiple documents, most of which may be
097   irrelevant. (2) CHASE-C ODE is a repository-level code completion benchmark with an average
098   context length of 17k tokens. It consists of problems from two different domains: data pre-processing
099   functions, and algorithms. This dataset simulates the real-world application of generating code in
100   existing repositories based on user specifications. (3) CHASE-M ATH is a grade-school level math
101   word problems benchmark consisting of problems involving arithmetic reasoning. We built this
102   benchmark to show the utility of our framework in building challenging problems for seemingly easy
103   tasks on which models have already saturated existing benchmarks (Cobbe et al., 2021).
104   Experiments with 15 contemporary LLMs show that the datasets generated using CHASE are
105   challenging for all models. The best performing LLMs only achieve accuracies in the range of
106   ∼ 40 − 60% across the three different domains. We further highlight the utility of CHASE by
107   comparing it with direct prompting-based data generation baselines such as Evol-Instruct (Xu et al.,
      2024), which yield data with significant errors, apart from being relatively easier for models to solve.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
129
130
      Synthetic data generation. Prior works have explored generating synthetic data for various stages
131
      of an LLM’s development pipeline: pre-training (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Ben Allal et al., 2024),
132   instruction-finetuning (Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), alignment (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al.,
133   2024) and task-specific fine-tuning (Wei et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024a). The main focus of our
134   work, however, is to generate high-quality challenging problems for evaluation and benchmarking.
135   There is very limited existing literature in this area. Sprague et al. (2024) created a narrative-based
136   question answering (QA) benchmark using a neuro-symbolic pipeline that first samples facts, and
137   then uses an LLM to build a reasoning tree over them, along with generating a narrative. Bohnet et al.
138   (2024) created another synthetic QA benchmark by extracting entities and their reference chains from
139   existing stories and then prompting an LLM to generate questions over them. Gu et al. (2024) create
140
      a code understanding benchmark by prompting CodeLlama (Rozière et al., 2024) to generate python
      functions and their inputs and designing tasks based on predicting either the input or the output for
141
      a given function. In contrast to these works, we focus on presenting a general framework which
142
      can be used to design scalable pipelines to create challenging benchmarks across multiple domains.
143   Moreover, we focus on realistic tasks such as information-seeking QA and repository-level code
144   generation, both with extremely long contexts that we generate completely from scratch.
145
146   Task-specific synthetic data. Recent works have explored generating synthetic datasets for content-
147   grounded QA tasks. Dai et al. (2022) use an LLM to develop information-seeking dialogue datasets
148   based on text extracted from Wikipedia and the Web. Yehudai et al. (2024) prompt an LLM with
149   wikipedia text to generate a question-answer pair. In contrast, we take a more application-oriented
150   view and design a benchmark for document-based information-seeking questions that model realistic
151   situations. Moreover, our pipeline generates the entire context (i.e., the documents) from LLMs,
152   which allows a higher degree of control. There has also been significant interest in generating
153
      synthetic data for code. Yu et al. (2024b) employ an LLM-based framework to create examples for
      tasks based on existing raw code data. Wei et al. (2024) generate code instruction data by prompting
154
      an LLM with seed code snippets from existing repositories. Luo et al. (2024) generate complex
155
      and diverse code instruction-tuning data by applying the Evol-Instruct method (Xu et al., 2024) on
156   existing code benchmarks. In this work, we focus on repository-level code completion, where we
157   generate the repository contexts completely from scratch. Moreover, we also synthetically generate
158   the corresponding test code to evaluate each example. Generating synthetic data to improve math
159   reasoning has recently been a very active area of research. Previous work has explored generating
160   new math problems by prompting LLMs with examples from existing datasets (Liu et al., 2023;
161   Yu et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024). Similar to our work, some prior works have focused on creating
      challenging math problems. Shah et al. (2024) employ a human-in-the-loop approach to generate


                                                                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      novel and difficult math problems by prompting LLMs with multiple core skills that must be used in
163   the problem. Liu et al. (2024a) employ iterative question composition where they iteratively prompt
164   an LLM with a seed question to generate more complex variations of it. In this work, we design a
165   completely automated pipeline to craft grade-school level math word problems that are challenging
166   to solve even for the LLM that generated them.

      3     T HE CHASE F RAMEWORK AND B ENCHMARKS
169
170
      Our framework for generating synthetic data is based on two key ideas as illustrated in Figure 1.
171
172   1. Bottom-up problem creation.
173   We abandon the forward-thinking approach of first creating a difficult problem and then obtaining the
174   corresponding solution, as followed by most works that create synthetic data for training (Xu et al.,
175   2024; Liu et al., 2024a). If we first synthesize a complex problem and then obtain its corresponding
176   solution from the generating LLM itself, then that problem is inherently solvable by that LLM.
177   However, we wish to craft problems that are challenging even for the model which generates them.
178   Hence, we instead take a different approach where we either generate or start with a simpler problem-
179   solution pair, and then bottom-up build a challenging context. We make the problem’s context
180   challenging by systematically hiding components of the solution or reasoning such that they need to
181
      be either extracted from a long context or inferred based on given information.
182   2. Decomposition into simpler, verifiable sub-tasks.
183
      We design pipelines that break down the generation process into simpler sub-tasks. Each individual
184
      LLM in the pipeline (i.e., each inference call) performs a simpler, specific function in the generation
185   process. This provides us with multiple benefits. First, it grants us more control over each step of
186   the generation process. We can treat each step as a task by itself and optimize the corresponding
187   inference parameters individually. This also allows us to better manage the complexity and diversity
188   of the generated data depending on our requirements. Second, and perhaps more importantly, it
189   facilitates fine-grained verification. We deploy LLMs that are not part of the generation process to
190   check the correctness and quality of the generated data at each possible step. We believe that LLMs
191   can be relied upon for verification because our framework makes each verification task smaller and
192   simpler compared to the main task of generating or solving the problem we are crafting.
193   We show the effectiveness and ease of adaptation of our framework by implementing it to create
194   challenging problems across three diverse domains:
195
      CHASE-QA is an open-domain document-grounded question answering task consisting of 671
196
      problems. Each example in CHASE-QA consists of a set of documents and a question-answer
197   pair, as illustrated in Figure 2 left. Models need to reason over a long context (more than 6k tokens)
198   because the information relevant for answering the question is spread across multiple documents.
199
200
      CHASE-C ODE is a repository-level code completion benchmark consisting of 220 challenging
      problems. Given a repository of Python functions, the task is to implement a new function based
201
      on a set of objectives provided in natural language, as illustrated in Figure 2 centre. We create data
202
      for two broad domains: (1) data pre-processing operations such as dataframe manipulation, string
203   processing, file I/O, etc., and (2) algorithms such as graph operations, array manipulations, etc.
204
205   CHASE-M ATH is a challenging benchmark of 150 grade-school level math word problems involving
206
      only basic arithmetic operations. An example of the task is provided in Figure 2 right.

208   4     C ONSTRUCTION P IPELINES
209
210   In this section, we discuss our implementation of the CHASE framework for all three domains in
211   more detail. Our pipelines use two different LLMs: the generator G, and the verifier V.
212
213   4.1   C ONSTRUCTING CHASE-QA
214
215   We generate CHASE-QA completely from scratch without relying on existing contexts or any seed
      examples from previous datasets. Following the CHASE framework, we create each example in a


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      bottom-up manner by first generating the question-answer pair, and then generating the corresponding
217   documents. Our pipeline for creating CHASE-QA is illustrated in Figure 1 bottom left. We describe
218   it in detail below. The exact prompts are provided in Appendix F.1.
219
220   Generating diverse scenarios. We begin by first generating a set of diverse realistic scenarios in
221   which a user persona seeks to find some information from a collection of documents. An example
222   would be a ‘grad student in NYC’ searching the ‘laws on renting and subletting’. We prompt G to
223   generate diverse scenarios in the form of a tuple (persona, collection name) by bootstrapping
224   it with 5 annotated example scenarios, and later prompting it with its own generated scenarios.
225
226   Generating question-answer pairs. Each scenario (persona, collection name) defines
227   the working environment for creating a particular example. We design programmatic prompts with a
228   given scenario as the variable to prompt G to generate a realistic information-seeking question that
229   the persona might want to know about from collection name set of documents. For example,
230
      a ‘grad student’ might pose the question, ‘what is the procedure for refusing increase in rent?’, whose
      answer can be found spread across multiple documents about the government’s laws on renting.
231
      Apart from the question, G must also generate the corresponding answer. We prompt G to generate
232
      questions and answers where the answers are a composition of multiple points or ideas. Additionally,
233   G must generate the outline of the documents (only title and abstract) which will contain the answer.
234   The idea is that it must separate out the answer points and assign them to these different documents.
235
236   Generating irrelevant information. To make the task more challenging, for each question-answer
237   pair, we prompt G to generate other question-answer pairs where the answer is of a similar type
238   or domain as the ground-truth answer. An example of a similar question for our running example
239   with the grad student is ‘what is the process to increase the rent for an appartment I am subletting?’.
240   The intuition is that the corresponding answers to such similar questions will be of a very similar
241   flavour to the ground-truth answer, but ultimately irrelevant for answering the question. This will
242   make the generated data challenging since it will confuse the model when all of this similar type of
243
      information is spread across a long context. It is, however, important to verify that none of this newly
      generated irrelevant information is actually relevant for the original question (otherwise it will make
244
      our ground-truth answer incomplete). We individually prompt V with the original question and each
245
      of the supposed irrelevant information points to check if any part of them is relevant for answering
246   the question (see Figure 1 bottom left for an example of an irrelevant point discarded by V because it
247   was relevant for the original question).
248
249   Generating documents. For each example, we have generated a question-answer (QA) pair, along
250   with some similar but irrelevant QA pairs. For each of these QA pairs, we separately prompt G
251   to generate long documents where the documents must discuss the corresponding answer points
252   assigned to it, along with many other irrelevant points. All documents discuss distinct information.
253   Together, all these documents form up the context for that example. We verify two main things to
254   ensure the correctness of the task: (1) none of the documents should contain any information relevant
255   for answering the question, apart from the ground-truth answer points, and (2) all of the ground-truth
256
      answer points must be discussed somewhere in the documents. We do this by rigorously prompting
      V with individual documents and the ground-truth answer points.
257
258
259   4.2   C ONSTRUCTING CHASE-C ODE
260
      We generate CHASE-C ODE completely from scratch without relying on existing contexts or any
261   seed examples from previous datasets. Our pipeline for creating CHASE-C ODE is shown in Figure 4
262   in the Appendix. We describe it in detail below. The exact prompts are provided in Appendix F.2.
263
264   Generating Python functions. We begin by first generating a set of diverse and realistic Python
265   functions. We prompt G to generate Python functions for a particular domain by bootstrapping it
266   with 3 annotated functions in that domain, and later prompting it with its own generated functions.
267   These generated functions will act as the helper functions in the repository context which may or
268   may not be called in the answer code function. Given each generated helper function, we prompt
269   V to generate a Python code which initializes sample inputs for the function and then calls it using
      them. We then execute this code to verify whether the generated helper function executes correctly.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Generating problem statement and answer code. To create a single example, we randomly
271   sample n of the previously generated helper functions, and prompt G to create a complex function
272   that calls at least k of these provided helper functions (hereafter called relevant helper functions)
273   apart from implementing some other pieces of logic. This complex function is our answer code.
274   Additionally G must elaborate in natural language what objectives the complex function achieves,
275   which makes up our problem statement. Similar to the case of helper functions, we prompt V to
276   generate test code to check if the generated answer code executes correctly. Moreover, we need to
277   verify whether the generated problem statement sufficiently specifies the answer code. We prompt
278   V with the problem statement and corresponding relevant helper functions and check whether the
279
      output is semantically equivalent to the answer code (using the test code obtained in the next step).
280
      Generating test code. To enable automatic execution-based testing, we prompt G with the gener-
281
      ated answer function to implement the test code for it in Python. The test code must independently
282
      implement the logic of the answer code without access to the corresponding helper functions. It
283   must then initialize the parameters of the answer function with some sample values, and compare its
284   output with its own implementation. We execute the generated test code to check if the corresponding
285   answer code passes. We discard all examples for which (1) the test code does not execute properly, or
286   (2) the test code executes but the answer code fails the test.
287
288   Building code repository. For each example, we build a unique repository of Python files. The
289   repository consists of the relevant helper functions spread across different files, along with m
290   randomly sampled irrelevant Python functions from our previously generated set. The core difficulty
291   of this task arises from understanding the entire long context of code functions, and identifying which
292
      ones are relevant for the provided problem statement.
293
294   4.3   C ONSTRUCTING CHASE-M ATH
295
      We sample math word problems (MWP) from existing datasets as seed examples to build our
296   benchmark. Following the CHASE framework, we bottom-up build a more complex problem by
297   iteratively increasing the reasoning depth of the problem. An illustration of the pipeline used for
298   creating CHASE-M ATH can be seen in Figure 1 bottom right. We describe the pipeline in more
299   detail below. The exact prompts are provided in Appendix F.3.
300
301   Breaking down seed MWP. A seed MWP s is characterised by the tuple s = (p, a) where p is the
302   problem, and a is the answer. We prompt G to break down p into two parts: the context c, which
303   provides all the information, and the question q, which asks about some unknown quantity.
304
305   Create continuation of MWP. We prompt G with an initial seed MWP s0 = (p0 , a0 ) to build a
306
      new problem which is a continuation of the previous problem. More precisely, G should output a new
      problem s1 = (p1 , a1 ), where the context of p1 , i.e., c1 assumes a0 as given information (without
307
      explicitly stating it). For example, in Figure 1 bottom right, the model assumes Jack has 4 pens as
308
      given information, and creates a new continuation context, Jill has thrice as many pens as Jack has
309   now. The model also generates a new question q1 , how many pens does Jill have? whose answer
310   a1 = 12 is obtained by performing an arithmetic operation (here, multiplication by 3) over a0 = 4.
311
312   Combining seed MWP with its continuation. By combining the original seed problem with its
313   continuation, we get a new MWP s = (p, a) with a higher reasoning depth, where the context c of
314   the combined problem p is a concatenation of the contexts of the seed problem and the continuation
315   c = c0 · c1 . The question for the combined problem will be the one generated by the model, i.e., q1 ,
316   and the corresponding answer a = a1 . We refer to Figure 1 bottom right for illustration.
317
318   Iteratively increase reasoning depth. We increase the reasoning depth of a given seed MWP
319   by creating new continuations in an iterative manner. Each new continuation si formed after the
320
      ith iteration becomes the seed problem for the (i + 1)th iteration. The final complex problem after
      j successful iterations, i.e., with a reasoning depth of j + 1, is given by context c = c0 · c1 . . . cj ,
321
      question qj , and answer a = aj .
322
323   Since each individual new problem created by G has a low reasoning depth of the same difficulty
      as the problems in the seed datasets, we verify their correctness using a non-identical ensemble of


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      verifier models {V1 , V2 , . . . , Vn }, each of which has very high performance on the seed dataset.
325   We prompt each Vk with the generated problem context ci and question qi and check whether the
326   prediction is the same as the generated answer ai . If this fails for any verifier, we discard si and begin
327   generating again with si−1 as the seed MWP (see Figure 1 bottom right).

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
332
333   Generating CHASE-QA. We use GPT-4o (OpenAI Team et al., 2024) as the generator G, and
334   GPT-4o-mini as the verifier V. We first sampled 500 different scenarios. For each scenario, we
335   generate 2 different question-answer (QA) pairs. For each of the resulting 1000 unique QA pairs, we
336   obtain irrelevant information by generating 4 similar QA pairs. We then generate the corresponding
337   documents containing the ground-truth answer as well as irrelevant information for each of the 1000
338   examples. To increase the complexity of the resulting benchmark, we carry out a form of rejection
339   sampling. We evaluate GPT-4o-mini twice on the task, and randomly discard half of the problems on
340
      which it was correct both times. This yielded the final benchmark of 671 examples.
341
342
      Generating CHASE-C ODE. We use GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI Team et al., 2024) as the generator G,
      and Gemini-1.5-Flash as the verifier V. We made this choice because generating even a small amount
343
      of challenging code problems required a large number of iterations, since a lot of the model-generated
344
      code at various stages would fail to execute or be semantically incorrect. For each domain, we first
345   sampled 500 different helper functions that execute without errors. Then we prompt the model with
346   n = 10 random helper functions to generate a problem statement and corresponding answer code that
347   calls at least k = 4 helper functions. We do this to create 1000 different examples for each domain.
348   Next, we generate up to 10 test codes for each example and keep only those examples for which a
349   generated test code successfully passed for the corresponding answer code. We also carry out the
350   verification of correctness of problem statement as describe before. This way, we end up with 290
351   examples for the algorithms domain and 300 examples for the data pre-processing domain. We again
352   use GPT-4o-mini for rejection samping and randomly discard around half of the problems on which it
353
      was correct. This way, we end up with a total of 500 examples in the benchmark, with 250 examples
      for each domain. For each example, we randomly sample m = 100 irrelevant helper functions and
354
      distribute them into 10 Python files to constitute the repository context.
355
356
      Generating CHASE-M ATH. We again use GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI Team et al., 2024) as the
357   generator G, and an ensemble of Gemini-1.5-Flash and Llama-3.1-70B as the verifier V. In practice,
358   we observed that many of the model generated problems would fail at various stages of verification,
359   so it is faster and cheaper to query the smaller models. We start with 2.3k seed problems taken from
360   the test sets of GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021) and SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021). We set the maximum and
361   minimum reasoning depth at 8 and 4 respectively. For each problem, we iterate 15 times to generate
362   a problem continuation. Note that many of these iterations fail to produce a correct continuation of
363   the problem, in which case we discard that generation and retry from that point in the subsequent
364   iteration. We carry out this process 3 times. In this manner, we generated around 1500 problems.
365
      We then carry out rejection sampling and roughly discarded 75% of the problems that GPT-4o-mini
      could solve. In the end, we end up with a total of 500 challenging MWPs.

495   6    C ONCLUSION
496
497   In this work, we presented CHASE, a framework to synthetically generate challenging problems for
498   evaluation. Our framework offers multiple benefits. First, it is scalable and can efficiently generate
499   hundreds of challenging problems. Second, it is renewable, which means it can be used to keep
500   regenerating new datasets for a given task at periodic intervals thereby mitigating contamination
501   concerns which plague most evaluation benchmarks. Third, it can effectively help evaluate tasks (such
502   as long-context reasoning) which are inherently difficult or impracticable for humans to evaluate.
503   Fourth, it provides a very high level of quality and correctness because of extensive verification.
504
      And finally, it can be used to generate difficult examples for a given task. We implemented CHASE
      on three different domains: document-based question answering, repository-level code completion,
505
      and math reasoning. With the help of extensive experiments, we showed that CHASE successfully
506
      generates examples that are difficult even for the state-of-the-art LLMs to solve. Our results raise
507   several interesting questions to explore in future work: (a) How can we modify this framework to
508   more easily adapt to different tasks? (b) What is the utility of this framework in generating data for
509   training LLMs? (c) How can we verify LLM generations more efficiently? We hope our work will
510   catalyze many such studies on using synthetic data for evaluation.

      7    L IMITATIONS
513
514
      Size of benchmarks. The datasets we generated are comparatively smaller in size. Our framework
515
      necessitates querying the generator and especially the verifier many times for crafting each example.
516   While this increases the quality and correctness of the data, it significantly increases the cost of
517   generation. Moreover, a large portion of the intermediate generations in our pipeline are discarded
518   because of extensive verification, which significantly reduces the yield. Our focus in this work is to
519   present the CHASE framework and we believe our experiments, albeit on smaller-sized datasets,
520   convincingly show its utility in generating challenging problems for evaluation. Small benchmarks, if
521   generated with a high bar for quality and correctness, can still be very impactful as evidenced by past
522   works like HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021).
523
524   Instance-level issues. Some of the examples we generated using CHASE, while being semantically
525
      correct, use unnatural or difficult-to-parse language. This is a general trait of text generated from
      contemporary LLMs, and our framework is unfortunately susceptible to it. Moreover, while we carry
526
      out extensive verification of correctness at the instance-level using LLMs, it is still possible that some
527
      of the generated examples are incorrect. Verifying long-context reasoning data using non-expert
528   humans is impracticable. To get a sense of the quality, we manually examined 30 random examples
529   each from the generation process of CHASE-QA and CHASE-C ODE. We found only 2 errors in
530   CHASE-QA and none in CHASE-C ODE. We also examined 100 examples from CHASE-M ATH’s
531   pipeline and found 7 that were incorrect. Examples of such errors in generation are discussed in
532   Appendix E. We believe that errors at this scale are acceptable considering the other advantages of
533   the framework and can be factored into the evaluation in future studies.
534
535   Adaptability. While we have shown how we implemented CHASE on three different domains, it
536   is not trivial to adapt the framework to other tasks. Although the high level ideas behind CHASE
537   are easy enough to follow, it takes multiple trials and errors to design a working pipeline for any
538
      given task. However, we are optimistic that advances in LLMs’ abilities to more precisely follow
      instructions will make such pipelines easier to construct in the future.

1116   as academic papers and financial reports, and LooGLE (Li et al., 2024b), which consists of long-

1118   performing models on these datasets achieve scores of around 53% and 54% respectively. The best
1119   performing model on CHASE-QA achieves a score of around 63%, which reduces to around 55%
1120   when we scale the context size to comparable levels of 30k tokens.

1124   challenging benchmark. Recently, some repository-level code benchmarks have also been proposed.
1125   SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2024) is a benchmark of around 2300 software engineering problems
1126   compiled from GitHub issues in popular repositories. EvoCodeBench (Li et al., 2024a) consists
1127   of 275 repository-level code generation problems based on popular GitHub repositories. The best
1128   performing models on these benchmarks achieve around 42% and 20% scores respectively.

1132   mostly become saturated, with many state-of-the-art models achieving more than 90% accuracies. In
1133   comparison, CHASE-M ATH is still very difficult for all models to solve. Moreover, the differences

1161   C.2   A LTERNATIVE M ETRICS OF E VALUATION FOR CHASE-QA
1162
1163   The metric of accuracy for CHASE-QA punishes models for not being concise and generating too
1164   many answer points that are not a part of the ground-truth answer. In this section, we present our
1165   experimental results with other softer evaluation metrics. We adapt two metrics that have been used
1166   by previous works for open-domain question answering (Adlakha et al., 2022): (1) K-Precision,
1167   which for a particular example, evaluates whether all of the answer points in the model’s prediction
1168
       are discussed in the documents, and (2) Recall, which evaluates whether all the ground truth answer
       points are a part of the model’s prediction. K-Precision is used to measure the faithfulness of the
1169
       model’s prediction to the provided documents. Recall is used to measure the correctness of the
1170
       model’s prediction compared to the ground-truth. We define both the metrics as binary per example.
1171   Similar to how we calculated accuracy, we use GPT-4o as a judge with the prompts provided in
1172   Figure 21 and Figure 22 respectively. The results are provided in Table 8.
1173
1174
       Note that the errors in CHASE-QA pertain to the cases where the ground-truth answer may not
       completely encompass all the relevant information about the question that is mentioned in the
1175
       documents. We believe that comparisons of models on the basis of recall is relatively less affected by
1176
       the presence of such errors. This is because if a model has comparatively lesser recall, that means
1177   that it generated more responses where it did not include the ground-truth information (irrespective
1178   of whether it generated any extra relevant information for the question that is not in the ground truth).

       D.1   S YNTHETIC DATA G ENERATION
1233
       Early works explored the potential of pretrained generative language models such as BERT (Devlin
1234
       et al., 2019), GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019), and GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) to generate datasets for
1235
       fine-tuning (Schick & Schütze, 2021; Ye et al., 2022) or for data augmentation (Kumar et al., 2020;
1236   Yoo et al., 2021). With increasingly powerful LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI Team et al.,
1237   2024) being released in recent years combined with the benefits obtained from instruction fine-tuning
1238   (Wei et al., 2022a; Chung et al., 2024), the focus has shifted more towards generating synthetic
1239   instructions data. Honovich et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2023) prompt GPT-3 with seed examples
1240   to automatically generate a large set of diverse tasks. Xu et al. (2024) introduced the Evol-Instruct
1241   pipeline to generate more complex examples from a given seed example. Mukherjee et al. (2023)
       leverage explanation traces from GPT-4 to create a large synthetic instruction-tuning dataset for


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       fine-tuning smaller LLMs. Mitra et al. (2024) design an agentic framework that uses raw text and
1243   code data as seeds to generate large scale synthetic data of prompts and responses.
1244
1245   Apart from synthetic instructions datasets, there has also been interest in using synthetic data for
1246
       pre-training. Gunasekar et al. (2023) and Ben Allal et al. (2024) generate high-quality textbooks for
       pre-training small LLMs to unlock better reasoning performance. Recent works (Bai et al., 2022;
1247
       Lee et al., 2024) have also explored using synthetic preference data for aligning language models.
1248
       Synthetic data has started to become a major component in the post-training development phase
1249   of contemporary LLMs (Llama Team et al., 2024) to improve their reasoning capabilites. For a
1250   comprehensive discussion of major ideas and issues in synthetic data generation, we refer the reader
1251   to Liu et al. (2024b)’s survey.
1252
1253
       D.2   S YNTHETIC DATA FOR C ODE G ENERATION , AND M ATH R EASONING
1254
1255   There has been significant recent interest in generating synthetic data for code. Wen et al. (2024)
1256   utilize I/O specification apart from synthetic intents to generate data science code instructions. Patel
1257   et al. (2024) generate code library specifications using GPT-4 to evaluate whether LLMs can learn
1258   new code libraries in-context.
1259
       Generating synthetic data to improve math reasoning has also been a very active area of research.
1260   Tang et al. (2024) explored extracting topics and knowledge from math problems to prompt an LLM
1261   to generate new data. Toshniwal et al. (2024) create an instruction-tuning dataset by synthesizing
1262   code-interpreter style solutions for existing math problems using open-source LLMs. Yang et al.
1263   (2024b) create a reverse socratic approach to synthesize math problems from optimization scenarios.
1264   Luo et al. (2023) generate diverse math instructions data using Evol-Instruct and then train LLMs
1265   using reinforcement learning. Yue et al. (2024) build an instruction-tuning dataset by using GPT-4 to
1266   generate hybrid CoT and PoT rationales for examples from diverse math datasets. Yu et al. (2024a)
1267   bootstrap problems from existing math datasets by using an LLM to rephrase the question text.

1269   D.3   H UMAN - CURATED E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
1270
1271   Question Answering. There have been numerous context-grounded question answering bench-
1272
       marks proposed in the past. These include SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016; 2018), HotpotQA (Yang
       et al., 2018), DROP (Dua et al., 2019), HAS-QA, Qasper (Dasigi et al., 2021), (Pang et al., 2019)
1273
       TopioCQA (Adlakha et al., 2022), NovelQA Wang et al. (2024a), and RepliQA (Monteiro et al.,
1274
       2024). Different from these works, we focus on extremely long contexts, with documents containing
1275   a total of more than 10,000 tokens. Another important aspect of our work is that all the documents
1276   that make up the context for an example are generated by an LLM from scratch, which reduces
1277   contamination risk. Moreover, our questions are designed to simulate everyday queries that users
1278   might ask an LLM-based agent. Correspondingly, the annotated ground-truth answers are at times
1279   very verbose, which make them impossible to objectively evaluate using automated metrics.
1280
1281   Long Context Reasoning. Kamradt (2023) introduced the Needle-In-a-Haystack (NIH) task in
1282   which a model is asked to retrieve a fact placed in a long context. However, this only assessed
1283   surface-level long-context retrieval abilities without much reasoning. In the past few months, many
1284   more long-context understanding benchmarks have been created. SCROLLS Shaham et al. (2022)
1285   and ZeroScrolls (Shaham et al., 2023) are long-context benchmarks created by aggregating and
1286   repurposing existing datasets. Compared to the QA datasets in ZeroScrolls which consist of contexts
1287
       based on science and literature, CHASE-QA focuses on real-world scenarios where a user is
       looking for information across multiple documents. Many benchmarks such as LongBench (Bai
1288
       et al., 2024), RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024), L-Eval (An et al., 2024), LooGLE (Li et al., 2024b)
1289
       and InfiniteBench (Zhang et al., 2024b) consist of long-context QA tasks. However, the relevant
1290   information for answering the question is comparatively much more centralized in the context,
1291   surrounded by distracting information. In contrast, the relevant information in CHASE-QA is spread
1292   across multiple documents. Moreover, the irrelevant information in the other documents is still very
1293   closely related to the topics of the question, thereby making the task very difficult. Perhaps, the
1294   benchmark most similar to our work is Loong (Wang et al., 2024b). Like CHASE-QA, Loong also
1295   has evidences for answering questions scattered across a long context of documents. Unlike our
       benchmark, however, every document in Loong is known to be relevant for answering the question.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       In contrast CHASE-QA models the more realistic scenario of searching a bunch of documents that
1297   are on very closely related topics, only some of which contain the answer. Moreover, unlike all these
1298   prior works, we create long-context reasoning benchmarks completely from scratch in which the long
1299   text contexts are also generated using LLMs. Further note that in contrast to these prior works that
1300   use manual annotation, our approach is highly scalable, capable of creating thousands of examples
1301   across diverse domains automatically in a much more cost-efficient manner.
