009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Implicit Q-learning (IQL) serves as a strong baseline for offline RL, which never
012            needs to evaluate actions outside of the dataset through quantile regression. How-
013            ever, it is unclear how to recover the implicit policy from the learned implicit
014            Q-function and whether IQL can utilize weighted regression for policy extraction.
015            IDQL reinterprets IQL as an actor-critic method and gets weights of implicit pol-
016            icy, however, this weight only holds for the optimal value function under certain
017            critic loss functions. In this work, we introduce a different way to solve the im-
018
               plicit policy-finding problem (IPF) by formulating this problem as an optimization
               problem. Based on this optimization problem, we further propose two practical
019
               algorithms AlignIQL and AlignIQL-hard, which inherit the advantages of decou-
020
               pling actor from critic in IQL and provide insights into why IQL can use weighted
021            regression for policy extraction. Compared with IQL and IDQL, we find that our
022            method keeps the simplicity of IQL and solves the implicit policy-finding problem.
023            Experimental results on D4RL datasets show that our method achieves competitive
024            or superior results compared with other SOTA offline RL methods. Especially
025            in complex sparse reward tasks like AntMaze, our method outperforms IQL and
026            IDQL by a significant margin.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL), or Batch RL aims to seek an optimal policy without environ-
032   mental interactions (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Levine et al., 2020). This is compelling for having the
033
      potential to transform large-scale datasets into powerful decision-making tools and avoid costly and
      risky online environmental interactions, which offers significant application prospects in fields such
034
      as healthcare (Nie et al., 2021; Tseng et al., 2017) and autopilot (Yurtsever et al., 2020; Rhinehart
035
      et al., 2018). Notwithstanding its promise, applying off-policy RL algorithms (Lillicrap et al., 2015;
036   Fujimoto et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018a;b) directly into the offline context presents challenges
037   due to out-of-distribution actions that arise when evaluating the learned policy.(Fujimoto et al., 2019;
038   Levine et al., 2020).
039
      Although a variety of methods based on constraint and conservative Q-learning have been proposed to
040
      address this problem, IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2021b) stands out among them since IQL avoids visiting
041
      out-of-distribution (OOD) actions and decouples the critic from the actor, which contributes to stability
042   and hyperparameter robust. For implicit policy extraction, IQL extracts policy through advantage-
043   weighted regression (AWR) (Nair et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2019; Peters et al., 2010). However, the
044   general form of extracted policy is π(a|s) ∝ µ(a|s)w(s, a), where µ(a|s) is the behavior policy.
045   The AWR’s weight used by IQL is obtained from the constrained policy search, which does not
046   guarantee that it is the policy the learned IQL’s value function is actually evaluating (Hansen-Estruch
047   et al., 2023).
048   To solve this problem, IDQL (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) reinterprets IQL as an actor-critic method
049   and derives the implicit optimal policy weights. Nevertheless, this optimal weight hinges on the
050   assumption that the optimal value function can be learned under certain critic loss functions. It
051   remains unclear whether using AWR to extract policies for IQL is feasible, and how to extract policies
052   from arbitrary critic loss function, not just the expectile loss. Recently, this issue has become more
053   important since 1) many recent offline RL (Chen et al., 2023) and safe RL methods (Zheng et al.,
      2023) use IQL to learn the Q-function; 2) IQL’s performance is significantly affected by the choice


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      of a policy extraction algorithm (Tarasov et al., 2024). Addressing these issues can lead to a better
055   understanding of IQL-style methods’ bottlenecks, thereby promoting the development of offline RL.
056
057   In this paper, we address the above issues by formulating the implicit policy-finding problem as an
058
      optimization problem, where the objective function is a generalized form of behavior regularizers
      and the constraint is policy alignment. Policy alignment ensures the extracted policy is the policy
059
      implied in the Q-function. By solving this optimization problem, we can get a closed-form solution,
060
      which can be expressed by imposing weight on the behavior policy. The weight consists of a value
061   function, an action-value function, and multipliers, indicating that using AWR to extract IQL policies
062   is feasible only when the certain multiplier is less than 0, and this conclusion can be generalized to
063   any value function loss. Furthermore, our work also explains how the implicit policy in IQL-style
064   methods addresses OOD actions from the perspective of behavior regularizers.
065
      Based on the optimization problem, we further propose two algorithms, AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL.
066
      Both inherit the characteristics of IQL, i.e. the decoupling of actor and critic training. AlignIQL-hard
067   can theoretically achieve a globally optimal solution, but it is more vulnerable to hyperparameter
068   choices than AlignIQL. AlignIQL relaxes the policy alignment constraint and performs better in
069   complex tasks like sparse rewards tasks, but it does not guarantee convergence to the global optimum.
070
      Recently, Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2019) have
071
      been widely used in Offline RL, since behavior policy is often complex and potentially multimodal,
072
      the unimodal Gaussian policy used in IQL is unlikely to accurately approximate the complex behavior
073   policy (Wang et al., 2022; Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; He et al., 2024), which in
074   turn affects the implicit policy extraction. Our method can also be easily combined with diffusion
075   models. We just need to resample the actions generated by the diffusion-parameterized behavior
076   model according to the weights w(s, a) of our method. We verify the effectiveness of our method
077   on D4RL datasets and Atari datasets and demonstrate its state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on
078   sparse reward datasets like AntMaze tasks in which the learning of the critic network is hard and
079   unstable. We also show that, compared to IDQL, AlignIQL is more robust to hyperparameters and
080   achieves more stable training.
081   To summarize, our main contributions are as follows:
082
083          • We propose the policy-finding problem, where the policy alignment term is added as a
084            constraint. By solving this problem, we provide insights into why and when IQL can use
085
               weighted regression for policy extraction, and in turn, make it better to understand the
               bottlenecks of the IQL-style algorithms.
086
087
             • We demonstrate that there is no price to achieving policy alignment in IQL-style methods,
088            all we need is to modify the importance weights of the extracted policy. These results can
089            be generalized to any generalized value loss function, which greatly extends the theoretical
090            results of IDQL.
091
092          • We introduce two IQL-style algorithms: AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL. AlignIQL-hard can
093            theoretically achieve a globally optimal solution. AlignIQL obtains SOTA performance on
094            D4RL AntMaze tasks and show robustness on sparse reward tasks.

096   2   R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Offline RL. Offline RL algorithms need to avoid OOD actions. Previous methods to mitigate this
099   issue under the model-free offline RL setting generally fall into three categories: 1) value function-
100   based approaches, which implement pessimistic value estimation by assigning low values to out-of-
101   distribution actions (Kumar et al., 2020; Fujimoto et al., 2019), or implicit TD backups (Kostrikov
102   et al., 2021b; Ma et al., 2021) to avoid the use of out-of-distribution actions 2) sequential modeling
103   approaches, which casts offline RL as a sequence generation task with return guidance (Chen et al.,
104
      2021; Janner et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Ajay et al., 2022), and 3) constrained policy search (CPS)
      approaches, which regularizes the discrepancy between the learned policy and behavior policy (Peters
105
      et al., 2010; Peng et al., 2019; Nair et al., 2020).
106
107   Implicit Q-learning. Recently, implicit Q-learning (Kostrikov et al., 2021b) has attracted interest
      due to its stable training and simplicity. Many offline RL methods (Chen et al., 2023; Zheng


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023; Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) use IQL-style expectile regression to learn Q-function and
109   realize the advantage of decoupling the training of actor and critic. While IQL achieves superior
110   performance, several issues remain unsolved. SQL (Xu et al., 2023) reinterprets IQL in the Implicit
111   Value Regularization (IVR) framework and provides insights about why in practice a large τ may
112   give a worse result in IQL. However, there is another important open question about IQL, that is,
113   what policy the learned value function is evaluating. IDQL (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) solves this
114   by reinterpreting the IQL as an actor-critic method and getting the corresponding implicit policy for
115   the (generalized) IQL loss function. However, the corresponding implicit policy in IDQL only holds
116   for optimal value function under certain critic loss functions.
117   The closest work to ours is IDQL (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023), which derives the implicit policy for
118   optimal value function under different critic loss functions. Our method is related, but features with
119   AlignIQL can be applied to arbitrary sub-optimal value functions and arbitrary critic loss functions.
120   More importantly, our method explains when and why IQL can use AWR for policy extraction while
121   providing theoretical insights for IQL and other RL paradigms that use Q-values to guide sampling.

      Equation 15. This implies that if we train Q(s, a) and V (s) using IQL and η satisfies Proposition 5.7,
337
      we can extract the implicit policy from the value function using Equation 15. Actually, for IQL, the
338
      expectile loss Equation 4 approximates the maximum of Qθ̂ (s, a) when τ ≈ 1. We can approximately
339   think V (s) = arg maxa∼D Q(s, a), and thus, according to Equation 15, â = arg maxa Q(s, a)
340   has a weight of 1, while other actions are weighted by exp −η(Q(s, a) − V (s))2 . For a fixed
341   η, the weights for other actions are smaller than arg maxa∼D Q(s, a). Therefore, Equation 15
342   approximately recovers the implicit policy π ∗ (a|s) = arg maxa∼D Q∗ (s, a) from IQL learned
343   value functions.
344
345   Two ways to use AlignIQL or AlignIQL-hard: There are two ways to utilize our methods in offline
346   RL (corresponding to Algorithm 3 and Algorithm 1).
347
             • Energy-based implementation: We first use the learned diffusion-based behavior model
348
               µϕ (a|s) to generate N action samples. These actions are then evaluated using weights
349            from Equation 15 or Equation 9 (Algorithm 3). In this setting, the hyperparameter N has a
350            greater influence on performance than η, as a higher N is more likely to find the “lucky”
351            action that satisfies â = arg maxa Q(s, a).
352
             • Policy-based implementation: We use Equation 16 or Equation 12 to train the policy,
353            which needs the exact probability density of the current policy (Algorithm 1).
354
355   In summary, the first method can be used when employing diffusion-based policies, as the probability
356   density of diffusion models is unknown. The second method is applicable when using Gaussian-based
357   policies. Note that in both AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL, we do not impose a limit on the loss
358   function of the Q − V , which means that our conclusion can be generalized to the arbitrary critic loss
359   function and the arbitrary sub-optimal value function. To summarize, both the AlignIQL-hard and
360
      AlignIQL are "IQL-style" algorithms, which means the training of actor and critic are decoupled and
      the critic is learned by expectile regression. The difference between AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL
361
      lies in the calculation of weights and the necessity of training multiplier networks. We summarize
362
      the procedure of AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL in Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3. (Suppose that

517   7   D ISCUSSION
518
519
      Discussion. In our work, we define the implicit policy-finding problem in IQL and propose two
520
      practical algorithms AlignIQL-hard and AlignIQL to solve it. The optimal policy (Theorem 5.1) in
521   AlignIQL-hard shows that it is feasible to extract policy with AWR in certain cases, which builds the
522   bridge between the Implicit Q-learning and Weighted Regression. Our theoretical findings also extend
523   the policy alignment of IDQL to arbitrary critic loss and value functions. Besides the theoretical
524   findings, we also verify the effectiveness of our algorithm on D4RL datasets. Experimental results
525   show that compared to other IQL-style algorithms, our algorithm achieves SOTA performance and
526   is more stable, especially in sparse reward tasks. One future work is to explore better methods
527   for training multiplier networks and explore the impact of different regularization functions of
528   problem IPF. Another future work is to extend our approach to fields of safe RL and offline-to-online
529
      (O2O) learning. In safe RL, prior works (Zheng et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024) have used IQL to learn
      the Q-function. Investigating how to ensure policy alignment while satisfying safety constraints is an
530
      interesting research direction.

851   B    E XTRA R ELATED W ORK
852
853   Diffusion Model in Offline RL. Due to our method using the diffusion model for modeling behavior
854   policy, we review works that incorporate the Diffusion model in offline RL. There exist several works
855
      that introduce the diffusion model to RL. Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022) uses the diffusion model
      to directly generate trajectory guided with gradient guidance or reward. DiffusionQL (Wang et al.,
856
      2022) uses the diffusion model as an actor and optimizes it through the TD3+BC-style objective with
857
      a coefficient η to balance the two terms. AdaptDiffuser Liang et al. (2023) uses a diffusion model
858   to generate extra trajectories and a discriminator to select desired data to add to the training set to
859   enhance the adaptability of the diffusion model. DD (Ajay et al., 2022) uses a conditional diffusion
860   model to generate trajectory and compose skills. Unlike Diffuser, DD diffuses only states and trains
861   inverse dynamics to predict actions. QGPO Lu et al. (2023) uses the energy function to guide the
862   sampling process and proves that the proposed CEP training method can get an unbiased estimation
863   of the gradient of the energy function under unlimited model capacity and data samples. SfBC (Chen
      et al., 2022) first trains a diffusion-based policy and then selects actions based on the Q value, similar


                                                           16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      to AWR.IDQL (Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023) reinterpret IQL as an Actor-Critic method and extract
865   the policy through sampling from a diffusion-parameterized behavior policy with weights computed
866   from the IQL-style critic. EDP (Kang et al., 2024) focuses on boosting sampling speed through
867   approximated actions. SRPO (Chen et al., 2023) uses a Gaussian policy in which the gradient is
868   regularized by a pretrained diffusion model to recover the IQL-style policy. Our method is distinct
869   from these methods because we aim to align the implied policy with the value function.
