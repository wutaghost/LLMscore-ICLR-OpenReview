009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving continuous reinforcement learning (RL) tasks typically requires models
012            with continuous action spaces, as discrete models face challenges such as the curse
013            of dimensionality. Inspired by discrete controlling signals in control systems, such
014            as pulse-width modulation, we investigated RL models with discrete action spaces
015            with performance comparable to continuous models on continuous tasks. In this
016
               paper, we propose an RL model with a discrete action space, designed a discrete
               actor that outputs action distributions and twin discrete critics for value distribution
017
               estimation. We also developed both the training method and exploration strategy
018
               for this model. The model successfully solved BipedalWalkerHardcore-v3, a
019            continuous robot control task in a complex environment, achieved a higher score
020            than the state-of-the-art baselines and comparable results across various other
021            control tasks.

026   Reinforcement Learning (RL) typically applies discrete action space for discrete tasks and continuous
027
      action space for continuous tasks. For discrete tasks, such as Atari games, Q-learning and its variances
      exhaustively evaluate only a small number of actions. For continuous tasks, such as motion control
028
      tasks, evaluating all possible action is not possible, hence RL models with discrete action space can
029

030   problem, models for continuous tasks directly output actions with continuous values (Lillicrap et al.,
031   2016; Schulman et al., 2017; Fujimoto et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Kuznetsov et al., 2020).

062   2     R ELATED W ORK
063
064   The following Deep RL algorithms have been applied in continuous tasks. DDPG (Lillicrap et al.,
065   2015) effectively applies Q-learning principles to continuous action spaces for Deep RL. TD3 (Fuji-
066   moto et al., 2018) an extension of DDPG, utilizes twin critic networks to address value overestimation.
067   SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018) employs stochastic continuous actor and entropy regularization. TQC
068
      (Kuznetsov et al., 2020), building upon QR-DQN (Dabney et al., 2018b), integrates an actor and
      employs a truncated mixture mechanism to mitigate value overestimation. DDPG, TD3, SAC, TQC,
069
      and PPO have demonstrated commendable performance in MuJoCo tasks (Towers et al., 2023).
070
      Stable Baselines 3 (Raffin et al., 2021) and CleanRL (Huang et al., 2022) offer reliable implementa-
071   tions of these fundamental Deep RL algorithms, and we used some of them for comparison in our
072   experiments.
073
074
      Distributional RL models focus on modeling the distribution of cumulative rewards rather than only
      an expected scalar. C51 (Bellemare et al., 2017) employs a discrete value distribution for building
075
      its critic network. QR-DQN (Dabney et al., 2018b) and IQN (Dabney et al., 2018a) utilize quantile
076
      regression to detail the distribution of stochastic reward returns. These methodologies, despite their
077   varied mathematical constructs for their critic networks, predominantly affirm theoretical convergence
078   under Wasserstein Metric (Vaserstein, 1969) and have notable performance on Atari tasks.
079
080
      Some previous works resemble a few aspects of our work but differently. D4PG (Barth-Maron et al.,
      2018) proposed a C51 with an actor module. Our work, however, diverges by investigating discretized
081
      action spaces, moving away from the conventional assumption that action variables conform to a
082
      normal distribution. SAC-Discrete (Christodoulou, 2019) broadens the scope of SAC to discrete
083   action spaces, thus enhancing the model’s capacity to use action entropy for exploration.
084
085   There were explorations connecting continuous action Neunert et al. (2020) explores a feasible ap-
086
      proach for the unified control of discrete and continuous action variables based on the MPO algorithm.
      Metz et al. (2019) decomposes multi-dimensional action variables into a sequence of decision-making
087
      processes for discrete variables, though this method risks oversimplifying complex tasks and can
088
      lead to increased computational demands. Tang and Agrawal (2020) advocates for the discretization
089   of continuous action spaces, which can enhance the performance of on-policy algorithms such as
090   PPO, albeit at the potential expense of capturing continuous dynamics accurately. Luo et al. (2023)
091   emphasizes the benefits of discretizing action spaces in offline reinforcement learning and examines
092   potential solutions, but highlighted data and computational load challenges, impacting real-world
093   performance. Farebrother et al. (2024) argues that the cross-entropy loss function, compared to the
094   mean squared error loss function, is more effective for training Critic networks in reinforcement
095   learning, especially for models with large parameter counts like Transformers. However, it presents
096   difficulties in transitioning to environments that require continuous representations.

359   We designed a toy task called "Trap or Cheese" to illustrate that continuous models averaging good

362
      move straight ahead, it falls into the trap and dies, resulting in a reward of -1.0. When the mouse
      chooses to turn left or right, it can bypass the trap and reach the cheese. However, there is a 50%
363
      chance that the cheese has expired and cannot be eaten, resulting in a reward of 0.0. If the cheese is
364
      not expired, the reward is 1.0. Obviously, a normal mouse would not choose to walk into the trap.

468   6    C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
469
470   In this paper, we proposed an off-policy Deep RL model with a stochastic discrete actor and critics.
471   By approximating continuous values and actions with discrete distributions, this model can capture
472   multimodal distribution in action and value spaces and solves BipedalWalkerHardcore-v3 with the
473   state-of-the-art performance (see the (OpenAI) Leaderboard). The model also has performance near
474   to baselines such as TQC, TD3, and SAC in various tasks.
475   Through this model and experiments, our curiosity about whether a discrete RL model can learn
476   continuous tasks is confirmed. Given discrete control signals can be applied to real-world control
477   systems, a RL model with discrete action space can also be applied to continuous control tasks.
478   Although action resolution become lower, with the new ability to explore and learn multimodal
479   action distribution, as well as distributional-RL-style critics, the model can solve a task with strong
480   punishments and HRHR actions.
481   We noticed that, in BipedalWalkerHardcore-v3 task, the reward returns of TD3 and SAC are much
482   lower than TQC and our model. A possible reason is that TD3 and SAC failed to predict return by
483   high-risk-high-return (HRHR) actions, such as climbing over a box. A successful HRHR action will
484   result in a high return, but a failed one will result in a high punishment, which is much worse than
485   doing nothing. Hence the returns follow a multimodal distribution. TD3 and SAC focus on modeling
      the scalar expectation but not the distribution of returns. Hence, it is can similar situation to the


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      “Trap or Cheese” problem. Models using scalar return expectation tend to believe if a high reward is
487   conditioned on two different actions, it is also conditioned on the middle of actions. Our model uses
488   discrete return expectations and does not have this problem.
489
490
      Our model also benefits from the concept of discrete actions. In BipedalWalkerHardcore-v3, it
      successfully learned a realistic and human-like gait without modification of the reward functions,
491
      whereas baselines tend to master a gait similar to quadrupeds. One reason is that our model explores
492
      the action space with a discrete distribution, which can approximate a multimodal distribution. For
493   example, when the walker tries to step over a large stump, it is better to either jump or maintain
494   balance conservatively. A moderate walk likely results in a meaningless fall. Typical models with
495   continuous actors, such as TQC, adopt Gaussian distribution or the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, are
496   limited in action exploratory and its refinement, and tend to master a limping gait.
497
      The stochastic discrete actor also allows for expansive and efficacious exploration of the action space
498
      and facilitates the acquisition of a natural, harmonious gait resembling human bipedal alternating
499   stepping. Furthermore, the model displays adaptive gait transitions when faced with a diverse
500   array of challenging obstacles, mitigating the risk of toppling; for instance, opting for energy-
501   efficient alternating stepping on even terrain and ascending ladders, transitioning to a safer gait when
502   descending ladders, modifying its step length for minor stumps, and initiating climbing maneuvers
503   for large stumps. Allowing multiple gaits in the same situation helps in exploration and learning in
504   complex environments, so a robot can switch between different motion primitives, or gaits, instead of
505   forgetting one to relearn another one.
506   As a preliminary trial to apply discrete actions to RL models for continuous tasks, our model may
507   not achieve the best performance. A possible improvement of this work is interpolation between
508   discrete actions. That is, using a kernel to map multiple discrete actions to a continuous action, could
509   improve the resolution of the actions. As shown in experiments and discussed, our model is suitable
510   for robot tasks with complex interactions with the environment. In real-world tasks, environments are
511   usually complex and robots need to interact with them. Hence, this model can potentially facilitate
512   the application of robots in the real world. It is worth investigating in the future.
