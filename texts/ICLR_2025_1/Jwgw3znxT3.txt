009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-attention and transformers have been widely used in deep learning. Recent
012            efforts have been devoted to incorporating transformer blocks into different types
013            of neural architectures, including those with convolutions, leading to various vi-
014            sion transformers for computer vision tasks. In this paper, we propose a novel and
015            compact transformer block, Transformer with Information Bottleneck inspired To-
016            ken Merging, or IBTM. IBTM performs token merging in a learnable scheme. Our
017            IBTM is compatible with many popular and compact transformer networks, such
018            as MobileViT and EfficientViT, and it reduces the FLOPs and the inference time
019
               of the vision transformers while maintaining or even improving the prediction
               accuracy. In the experiments, we replace all the transformer blocks in popular vi-
020
               sion transformers, including MobileViT, EfficientViT, ViT, and Swin, with IBTM
021
               blocks, leading to IBTM networks with different backbones. The IBTM is moti-
022            vated by the reduction of the Information Bottleneck (IB), and a novel and sepa-
023            rable variational upper bound for the IB loss is derived. The architecture of mask
024            module in our IBTM blocks which generate the token merging mask is designed
025            to reduce the derived upper bound for the IB loss. Extensive results on image
026            classification and object detection evidence that IBTM renders compact and ef-
027            ficient vision transformers with comparable or much better prediction accuracy
028            than the original vision transformers. The code of IBTM is available at https:
029            //anonymous.4open.science/r/IBTM_Transformers-053B/.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Building upon the success of Transformer in natural language processing (Vaswani et al., 2017),
035   vision transformers have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide range of tasks (Yuan
036   et al., 2021; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021b; Liu et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2021; Cai et al.,
037   2023). However, the achievements of vision transformers are accompanied with heavy computa-
038   tional costs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021b; Touvron et al., 2021), making their deployment impractical
039
      under resource-limited scenarios. The aforementioned limitations have spurred recent research en-
      deavors aimed at developing efficient vision transformers. In this paper, we study the problem of
040
      accelerating vision transformers by token merging.
041
042   Token merging is an effective method for reducing the FLOPs and improving the inference speed
043   of vision transformers (Han et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2024; Bon-
044   naerens & Dambre, 2023; Bolya et al., 2023). However, most existing token merging methods (Rao
045
      et al., 2021; Bolya et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024; Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023) largely sacrifice
      the prediction accuracy of the original transformer networks for reduced computation costs (Bolya
046
      et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024). These methods (Kim et al., 2024; Bolya et al., 2023) generally fo-
047
      cus on identifying and merging similar tokens by averaging their features. However, such merging
048   strategies, which are based solely on feature similarity, can potentially diminish the informative fea-
049   tures in the tokens that are critical to the prediction tasks. Therefore, it remains an interesting and
050   important question whether we can perform token merging while preserving a compelling perfor-
051   mance of the vision transformers after token merging. To this end, we propose a novel transformer
052   block, Transformer with Information Bottleneck inspired Token Merging, or IBTM, which learns
053   how to merge tokens while exhibiting a compelling generalization capability of the transformer with
      merged tokens.

      Motivation. Due to the fact that the FLOPs of a vision transformer largely depend on the number of
055   tokens in all the transformer blocks, the FLOPs of a vision transformer can be significantly reduced
056   by reducing the number of tokens in all the transformer blocks. Our goal is to merge the output
057   tokens of all the transformer blocks into fewer tokens without largely sacrificing the prediction
058   accuracy of the original vision transformer. However, directly merging the output tokens, even
059   by carefully designed methods (Kim et al., 2024; Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023; Bolya et al., 2023),
060   would adversely affect the performance of the model. In this paper, we propose to maintain a
061   compelling prediction accuracy of a vision transformer with token merging by an informative token
062   merging process. In our IBTM block, the original attention output tokens of a transformer block are
063
      merged into fewer target tokens, and every target token is an informative weighted average of the
      original output tokens. All the target tokens, or merged tokens are the final attention output tokens
064
      for the IBTM block, which are fed to an MLP to produce the output of the IBTM block as illustrated
065
      by Figure 1.
066
067   Such a token merging process in IBTM is primarily inspired by the well-known presence of consid-
068   erable redundancy in the original output tokens of transformer blocks (Rao et al., 2021; Bolya et al.,
069
      2023). As different tokens have varying importance in modeling the vision features at a particular
      transformer block, it is natural to compute an informative aggregation of the original attention out-
070
      put tokens as the final (target) attention output tokens so that more informative and more important
071
      tokens contribute more to the merged tokens with a larger weight in the weighted average in the
072   aggregation process. A more detailed introduction on the Information Bottleneck (IB) is deferred to
073   Section A in the appendix.
074
075
      Contributions. The contributions of this paper are presented as follows.
076   First, we present a novel and compact transformer block termed Transformer with Information Bot-
077   tleneck inspired Token Merging, or IBTM. Our IBTM block generates an informative token merg-
078   ing mask which reduces the IB loss. The IBTM blocks can be used to replace all the transformer
079   blocks in many popular vision transformers, rendering compact vision transformers with competi-
080   tive performance. The effectiveness of IBTM is evidenced by replacing all the transformer blocks
081
      in popular vision transformers, including MobileViT (Mehta & Rastegari, 2022), EfficientViT (Cai
      et al., 2023), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021b), and Swin (Liu et al., 2021), with IBTM blocks, for
082
      image classification, object detection and instance segmentation tasks.
083
084   Second, we propose an informative token merging process for vision transformers, which can re-
085   duce the IB loss. As a first step, we derive a novel and separable variational upper bound for the
086   IB loss associated with token merging, which is I(X̃(G), X) − I(X̃(G), Y ) where I(·, ·) denotes
087   mutual information and G is the token merging mask in IBTM. X̃(G), X, and Y denote the random
088   variables representing the input features, the learned features, and the labels. We then view a trans-
089   former with multiple IBTM blocks as an iterative process for the reduction of the IB loss by gradient
090
      descent, and every IBTM block simulates one-step gradient descent on the variational upper bound
      for the IB loss. Inspired by this understanding, the token merging mask at the current layer is gen-
091
      erated from the token merging mask at the previous layer and the input tokens at the current layer
092
      by a learnable mask module, following the formula of gradient descent as in (3) in Section 3.2. As
093   a result, such informative token merging process generated in a network with IBTM blocks enjoys
094   reduced IB loss, which is evidenced in our ablation study in Section 4.2. Due to the separability of
095   the variational upper bound for the IB loss, a neural network with IBTM blocks can be trained in an
096   end-to-end manner with standard SGD.
097
      It is worthwhile to mention that our IBTM models can be either fine-tuned from pre-trained
098
      backbones or trained from scratch. As evidenced in Table 1, our IBTM models always outper-
099   form the currrent state-of-the-art token merging methods, including the fine-tuning-based method
100   LTMP (Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023), when fine-tuned for the same number of epochs. We remark
101   that as shown in Table 2 in Section 4.2 and Table 7 in Section E.1 of the appendix, the baseline
102   token merging method, ToMe, and LTMP, can already reduce the IB loss. By replacing all the trans-
103   former blocks with our IBTM blocks, the networks with IBTM exhibit even smaller IB loss and
104   enjoy higher classification accuracy and less FLOPs, either trained from scratch or fine-tuned from
105   pre-trained models. Furthermore, as shown in Table 3 in Section B.1 of the appendix, our IBTM
106   models also outperform all the competing token merging methods when trained from scratch. Im-
107
      portantly, extensive experiment results on various computer vision tasks demonstrate the compelling
      performance of IBTM networks compared to the competing baselines.

      2.1   E FFICIENT VISION T RANSFORMERS
120
121   Vision transformer models have recently achieved superior performance on a variety of computer
122   vision applications (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021c; Liu et al., 2021; Carion et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021;
123   Liang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022a). However, vision transformers often encounter high compu-
124   tational demands due to the quadratic complexity of the point-wise attention and numerous Multi-
125   Layer Perceptron (MLP) layers. To mitigate the challenges of high computational costs, various
126   strategies have been developed (Zhu et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021), primarily aimed at refining the
127
      network architectures and incorporating sparse mechanisms for efficient computation. These include
      the integration of convolutions into transformer networks (Mehta & Rastegari, 2022; Cai et al., 2023;
128
      Liu et al., 2023), the use of knowledge distillation for training more efficient transformers (Graham
129
      et al., 2021; Radosavovic et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2022), and compressing existing vision trans-
130   formers with methods such as pruning (Chen et al., 2021a; Yu et al., 2022a; Kong et al., 2022a).
131   Techniques for compressing vision transformers generally fall into three categories: (1) Channel
132   Pruning, which targets the elimination of superfluous heads and channels within ViT blocks (Chen
133   et al., 2021a; Chavan et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022a); (2) Block Pruning, which involves removing
134   redundant transformer blocks (Yu et al., 2022b;a); (3) Token Pruning and Token Merging, which
135   prune less important tokens and merge similar tokens in the input of transformer blocks (Rao et al.,
136   2021; Kong et al., 2022a; Bolya et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b; Wu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024;
137   Wei et al., 2023).
138   In this paper, we focus on learning to merge tokens guided by the information bottleneck theory
139   of deep learning and primarily review existing works on Token Pruning and Merging (Wang et al.,
140   2022b; Rao et al., 2021; Bolya et al., 2023; Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023; Kim et al., 2024). Dy-
141   namicViT (Rao et al., 2021) observes that the prediction in vision transformers is only based on a
142   subset of the most informative tokens and proposes a hierarchical token sparsification framework
143   to prune redundant tokens. ToMe (Bolya et al., 2023) proposes a graph-based matching algorithm
144
      that combines similar tokens in each vision transformer block of a pre-trained vision transformer.
      LTMP (Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023) learns threshold masking modules that dynamically determine
145
      which tokens to merge and prune in a unified framework similar to DynamicViT. ToFu (Kim et al.,
146
      2024) also combines token pruning and token merging. Instead of average merging similar tokens,
147   ToFu proposes a conventional average merging module to improve the quality of merged tokens.
148
149
      2.2   R ELATED W ORKS ABOUT I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
150
151   Saxe et al. (2019) provides the first in-depth analysis of conventional information bottleneck (IB)
152   theories and deep learning to establish the connection between the nonlinearity of neural networks
153   and the compression phase of training. Building on the theory of IB, (Lai et al., 2021) proposes a
154   probabilistic attention module reducing mutual information between the input and the masked repre-
155   sentation while increasing mutual information between the masked representation and the task label.
156   Further exploring the mechanics of IB in deep learning, (Zhou et al., 2022) finds that self-attention
157   mechanisms can be interpreted as iterative steps in optimizing the IB objective, which explains the
158   advantages of self-attention in learning robust representation. Distinct from most existing methods
159
      that implicitly incorporate the IB principle, our work adopts a direct and innovative approach. We
      aim to optimize a novel and separable variational upper bound of the IB loss with a learnable to-
160
      ken merging method. The proposed IBTM lead to compelling performance on many popular vision
161
      transformer architecture with lower computation cost, benefiting from the learnable token merging
      mechanism guided by the IB principle.

      3       F ORMULATION
181   In this section, we first illustrate how to perform token merging using a token merging mask. We
182   then describe how to generate the token merging mask from a learnable mask module in a IBTM
183   block, as well as the training algorithm of a neural network with IBTM blocks. We derive a novel
184   and separable variational upper bound for the IB loss, and the token merging masks are generated to
185   reduce such variational upper bound for the IB loss.
186
187   3.1     I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK INSPIRED T OKEN M ERGING
188
189   Given the input feature tokens X ∈ RN ×D where N is the number of tokens and D is the token
190   dimension, the IBTM block first applies the self-attention module on the input feature tokens by
191
      Z = ATTN(X) ∈ RN ×D , where ATTN(·) is the regular QKV self-attention operation (Dosovitskiy
      et al., 2021a). As illustrated in Figure 1, every IBTM block has a learnable mask module that
192
      generates the token merging mask G(ℓ) where ℓ is the index of the current layer or block. The
193
      IBTM block merges the N tokens of Z into P tokens with P < N by multiplying Z with the token
194
      merging mask G(ℓ) ∈ RN ×P . We set P = ⌈r × N ⌉, where r ∈ (0, 1) is termed the compression
195
      ratio for IBTM, and a smaller r renders less merged tokens after token merging. The token merging
196
      mask G(ℓ) of the ℓ-th transformer block is generated by the token merging mask G(ℓ−1) of the
197   previous layer and the feature tokens Z, which is motivated by reducing the IB loss and detailed in
198   Section 3.2. The token merging mask G(1) for the first transformer block is generated by applying an
199   existing learnable token merging method, LTMP (Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023), which generates
200   a binarized token merging mask M ∈ [0, 1]N ×P using Gumbel-Softmax with N × P learnable
201   parameters. After obtaining the merging mask G(ℓ) , the features tokens of Z are merged into P
202
                                        ⊤
      tokens by X̃(G(ℓ) ) = Z ⊤ G(ℓ)      ∈ RP ×D , which is then passed to the following MLP layers in
203   the transformer block.
204
205   In addition to merging tokens in regular transformer blocks such as ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021a)
206
      and Swin (Liu et al., 2021), the IBTM block can also be applied to efficient transformer blocks
      widely applied in efficient vision transformer architectures such as MobileViT (Mehta & Rastegari,
207
      2022) and EfficientViT (Cai et al., 2023). Regular transformer blocks obtain the output by sequen-
208
      tially applying the attention operation and MLP on the input feature tokens. However, efficient
209   transformer blocks usually contain residual connections following the design of residual connec-
210   tions in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). That is, these blocks maintain the same shapes
211   for the input X and the self-attention output Z and concatenate them to produce the output features
212   of the current transformer block. As a result, we cannot only merge the tokens of Z. Instead, our
213   IBTM block merges the tokens of both X and Z so that the number of merged tokens for X and
214   Z have is the same. To this end, we apply the same token merging mask G(ℓ) to merge both X
215   and Z. As a result, the compressed X and Z are of the same shape after the token merging pro-
      cess and they can still be concatenated, which is illustrated in Figure 1b. In addition, transformer

505   5    C ONCLUSION
506
      In this paper, we propose a novel transformer block, Transformer with Information Bottleneck in-
507
      spired Token Merging, or IBTM. IBTM blocks perform token merging so as to render a transformer
508   network with less FLOPs and faster inference speed. An IBTM block generates an informative to-
509   ken merging mask for token merging in a learnable manner, which is inspired by the reduction of
510   the Information Bottleneck (IB) loss. A network with IBTM blocks can be trained from scratch or
511   fine-tuned from a pre-trained backbone with standard SGD, and it enjoys a reduction of IB loss and
512   reduced FLOPs while maintaining a compelling prediction accuracy. We demonstrate the effective-
513   ness of IBTM by replacing all the transformer blocks in several popular vision transformers with
514   IBTM blocks. Extensive experiments on various computer vision tasks demonstrate the effective-
515   ness of IBTM.

      A     T HE I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK (IB) P ERSPECTIVE OF THE T OKEN
757
758
            M ERGING
759
760   Such an idea of informative token merging can also be viewed from the perspective of Information
761   Bottleneck (IB). Let Z be the original attention output tokens, which are merged into the merged
762   tokens denoted by X̃, and let Y be the ground truth training labels for a classification task. X̃ has
763   less tokens than Z. The principle of IB is to maximize the mutual information between X̃ and Y
764   while minimizing the mutual information between X̃ and X. That is, IB encourages the network
765   to learn the merged tokens more correlated with the class labels while reducing their correlation
766   with the input. Extensive empirical and theoretical works have evidenced that models respecting
767   the IB principle enjoy compelling generalization. With the informative token merging process in
768   IBTM, the merged tokens X̃ are the informative aggregation of the original attention output tokens
769   Z, so X̃ are less correlated with the training images and in this manner the IB principle is better
770
      adhered. This is reflected in Table 2 in Section 4.2 and Table 7 in Section E.1 of the appendix,
      where models for ablation study with existing token merging methods, ToMe (Bolya et al., 2023)
771
      and LTMP (Bonnaerens & Dambre, 2023), enjoys less IB loss than the corresponding vanilla trans-
772
      formers. This observation indicates that the IB principle is better respected by the token merging
773   process in ToMe and LTMP. In order to further decrease the IB loss, we propose an Information
774   Bottleneck (IB) inspired token merging process, where a IBTM block generates an informative to-
775   ken merging task which reduces the IB loss for vision transformers. For example, our model termed
776   “IBTM-MobileViT-S” in Table 2 in Section 4.2 and Table 7 in Section E.1 of the appendix is the
777   vision transformer with the IB loss reduced by replacing all the transformer blocks in MobileViT-S
778   with IBTM blocks so that more informative merged tokens are generated by the proposed infor-
779   mative token merging process. While ToMe and LTMP hurts the prediction accuracy compared to
780   the vanilla model, our IBTM enjoys even higher top-1 accuracy than the vanilla MobileViT-S either
781
      trained from scratch or fine-tuned from pre-trained checkpoints, and we have the same observations
      for MobileViT-XS and EfficientViT.

1076   D.1   C OMPUTATION C OST A NALYSIS OF IBTM-E FFICIENT V I T
1077
1078   In this section, we analyze the additional inference computation cost, the FLOPs, of the IBTM
1079   transformer block for token merging in both regular transformers and efficient transformers as il-
       lustrated in Figure 1. Let D be the dimension of input tokens and N be the number of tokens.


                                                      20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       The FLOPs of the token merging in an IBTM transformer block in regular vision transformers is
1081   6CDP + 3C + N D2 + N DP , where 6CDP + 3C + N D2 is the FLOPs for calculating the merg-
1082   ing mask and N DP is the cost for applying the merging mask on the input tokens. In the IBTM
1083   transformer block of efficient vision transformers, the additional FLOPs of the token merging is
1084   6CDP + 3C + N D2 + 2N DP , since the merging mask will be applied to both the input tokens
1085   and the merged tokens.
1086
1087   D.2    T OKEN M ERGING ON H IERARCHICAL V I T S
1088
1089   For the experiments with hierarchical ViTs such as Swin Transformers (Liu et al., 2021), the token
1090   merging is performed between the multi-head self-attention modules with either regular or shifted
1091   windows and the MLP layers. As a result, the input to the MLP layers has a reduced number of
1092
       tokens, thereby decreasing the computational costs in the MLP layers. Following the MLP layers,
       the merged tokens are padded with the same features to restore the original number of tokens. This
1093
       ensures compatibility with the hierarchical structure of the Swin Transformer, maintaining alignment
1094
       with subsequent operations such as patch merging and window-based self-attention. This design
1095   allows the token merging process to integrate seamlessly with the Swin Transformer architecture
1096   while preserving its structural and computational efficiency.
