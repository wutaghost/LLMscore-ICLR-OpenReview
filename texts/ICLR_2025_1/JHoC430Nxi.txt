009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in 6D pose estimation primarily rely on CNNs, but they strug-
012            gle to grasp long-range dependencies and the global context, which are essential
013            for precise pose determination. Although deeper or expanded networks are com-
014            monly used to tackle this, they lead to significant computational burdens without
015            fully addressing these constraints. To overcome these challenges, we present CT-
016            Net, a hybrid network that fuses the strengths of CNN and Transformer, aiming
017            for accurate 6D pose estimation from a solitary RGB-D image. CTNet employs
018
               Transformer to capture elusive long-range dependencies and the global context,
               while lightweight CNNs adeptly extract detailed local features. This complemen-
019
               tary approach offers a comprehensive feature representation, eliminating the ne-
020
               cessity for excessively deep networks. To further bolster the CNNs’ efficiency,
021            we introduce the Hierarchical Feature Extractor (HFE), which enhances the C2f
022            and ELAN modules for optimal feature extraction. Additionally, we integrate
023            a CNN-based PointNet module, designed to extract vital spatial data from the
024            point cloud. The Transformer element captures global contextual insights, which
025            are then seamlessly integrated with the local and spatial features extracted by the
026            CNNs to ensure precise 6D pose estimation. Experiments demonstrate that CT-
027            Net achieves high accuracy with nearly half the FLOPs of current methods on the
028            LineMOD and YCB-Video datasets. Furthermore, the HFE is highly adaptable,
029
               showing excellent transferability across other 6D pose estimation architectures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      6D pose estimation aims to determine the position and orientation of objects in 3D space (Xiang
034
      et al., 2014), with significant applications in complex robotic manipulation tasks (Tremblay et al.,
035
      2018; Collet et al., 2011), immersive augmented reality experiences (Marchand et al., 2015), and
036   advanced autonomous driving systems (Chen et al., 2017; Geiger et al., 2012). In order to fulfill
037   the real-time demands of these applications, pose estimation often needs to be processed on mobile
038   computing platforms (Yang et al., 2024). However, the high computational complexity of current
039   models poses challenges for efficient performance within such resource-constrained environments.
040
      To tackle 6D pose estimation, researchers explore diverse approaches, leveraging CNNs and PCNs
041
      (Zhang & Liu, 2023; Yuan et al., 2018). Methods relying on texture information from RGB images
042   (Peng et al., 2019; Tekin et al., 2018b; Kehl et al., 2017; Kendall et al., 2015) encounter difficulties
043   such as decreased accuracy with weakly textured images and sensitivity to lighting variations. Alter-
044   natively, methods using geometric information from point clouds (Qi et al., 2017a;b) struggle with
045   high redundancy, unstructured data, and sensitivity to occlusion and spatial deformation. To over-
046   come these obstacles, methods fusing RGB images with point cloud data (Mo et al., 2022; He et al.,
047   2021; 2020; Wang et al., 2019) exhibit robustness in handling complex occlusions and textureless
048   objects, surpassing earlier techniques in accuracy and adaptability.
049   Despite these advancements, existing methods still face two significant challenges. Firstly, tradi-
050   tional CNNs struggle to capture long-range dependencies and global context, which are crucial for
051   accurate 6D pose estimation, as they require a deep understanding of the complex relationships be-
052   tween object parts. Secondly, dense fusion networks (Hua et al., 2021; He et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
053   2019) encounter computational redundancy during multimodal data processing, stemming from their
      inherent intricacy in integrating diverse modal information.

080   To address these challenges, we propose two targeted approaches: 1) We explore the combination

082   pendencies and overall context, whereas CNN specialize in extracting detailed local features. By
083
      balancing their strengths in information capture, the network achieves comprehensive feature rep-
      resentation and precise accuracy without the need for deep layers. 2) We design 2D convolutional
084
      networks to concurrently process RGB images and point cloud data, enabling efficient integration
085
      of local features while avoiding the complexities of dense fusion networks.
086
087   Based on these approaches, we introduce CTNet, a hybrid network designed to estimate 6D poses
088   from RGB-D data. Its remarkable efficacy is apparent in Figure 1. We transform the depth image
089
      into an XYZ map that is aligned with the corresponding RGB image. As shown in Figure 2, the ar-
      chitecture of CTNet is as follows: First, we enhance the C2f (Jocher et al., 2023) and ELAN (Wang
090
      et al., 2023) modules to develop the Hierarchical Feature Extractor (HFE). Second, these local fea-
091
      tures are integrated with the XYZ map and subsequently input into a CNN-based PointNet (Qi et al.,
092   2017a) module, which encodes the spatial information present in the point clouds. Then, a Trans-
093   former is utilized to establish global dependencies, compensating for the long-range associations
094   often missed by CNNs. Finally, we aggregate different morphological features for pose estimation.
095   We evaluate CTNet on LineMOD (Hinterstoisser et al., 2011) and YCB-Video (Xiang et al., 2017)
096   datasets, which demonstrates its superiority by balancing between accuracy and inference speed.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Pose Estimation Based on RGB-D Data. Previous methods (Hua et al., 2021; He et al., 2021;
111   2020; Wang et al., 2019) utilize dense fusion networks to integrate RGB and point cloud features,
112   thereby exploiting both texture and geometry information in RGB-D data. However, dense fusion
113   networks encounter computational redundancy during multimodal data processing, stemming from
114   their inherent intricacy in integrating diverse modal information. Another method (Mo et al., 2022)
115   uses 2D convolutional kernels to extract RGB and point cloud features simultaneously, similar to
116   our approach. However, it is limited in capturing long-range dependencies due to its exclusive
117   reliance on CNNs. In contrast, our method surpasses these limitations with a holistic strategy. We
118   devise a CNN module for extracting local features and integrate a transformer module that captures
119
      global dependencies. By seamlessly merging local and global features, we enhance the utilization
      of RGB-D data, ultimately boosting the performance of pose estimation.
120
121
122
      Hybrid CNN-Transformer Architectures for 6D Pose Estimation. Vision transformers, origi-
      nally developed for NLP (Vaswani et al., 2017), now find widespread use in diverse computer vision
123
      tasks, such as image classification (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), object detection (Lee et al., 2022; Liu
124
      et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021), and semantic segmentation (Xie et al., 2021), showing considerable
125   potential. In our approach, we deviate from methods that rely solely on visual transformers (San-
126   dler et al., 2018). Instead, we opt to leverage transformer for capturing long-range dependencies
127   in sequences and integrating them into the feature information for 6D pose estimation. This differs
128   from traditional backbone networks, such as transformer-only architectures (e.g., PVT (Wang et al.,
129   2021) and swin transformer (Liu et al., 2021)) and those entirely based on CNNs (e.g., ResNet (He
130   et al., 2016)). Our hybrid network efficiently balances local and global feature extraction with a
131   lightweight network, leading to a more efficient network design.

136
137   This paper aims to recognize rigid objects and determine their rotations R ∈ SO(3) and translations
138   t ∈ R3 within the camera coordinate system, utilizing a RGB-D image. To achieve this, we develop
139   a network called CTNet, which is illustrated in Figure 2. Refer to Appendix A for input preparation
140   and preprocessing details.

145

146

147   extraction (comprising layers 1 and 2) and advanced feature extraction (comprising layers 3 and 4).
148

150   enhanced C2f module to enable efficient feature extraction, as shown in Figure 2. PConv selectively
151   processes channels, significantly lowering memory usage and computational overhead. The channel
152   ratio r (cp : c) determines this load. Given a feature map with dimensions h×w and a kernel size of k,
153   the computational and memory access loads are minimized, as illustrated in the following equations:

      Advanced Feature Extraction. We optimize the original ELAN architecture into a lightweight
217   variant, referred to as L-ELAN. ELAN first reduces the input data channels Fin to F2 via the CBS
218   submodule M2 (1×1 convolution). It then gathers local features using four CBS submodules (M3
219   to M6 , each with 3×3 convolutions) and utilizes multiple shortcut connections to output multi-
220   scale information F2 , F3 , and F4 . The ELAN module achieves strong performance by reducing
221   parameters through channel shrinking and extracting rich features via residual connections.
222
      In deeper layers, where channels double and features become more abstract, we design the L-ELAN
223   to further lighten the structure. We remove the submodule M4 to reduce overhead and replace the
224   M2 kernel from 1×1 to 3×3, increasing feature diversity by making F1 and F2 scales different.
225   F3 and F4 maintain their levels with two and four 3×3 convolutions. Finally, F1 , F2 , F3 , and F4
226   are concatenated and fitted by M7 to produce Fout . These adjustments preserve multi-scale feature
227   integration while reducing parameters without performance loss. Details of the ELAN and L-ELAN
228   structures are provided in Appendix B.

230   3.3   S PATIAL FEATURE EXTRACTION FOR POINT CLOUDS
231
232   Following the extraction of local features, we utilize a CNN-based PointNet module, as referenced
233
      in (Qi et al., 2017a), to encode the spatial information inherent in the point cloud. This encoding
      process involves the use of 1 × 1 convolutions, which effectively capture both the local features and
234
      the point coordinates. To derive the spatial features of the point cloud, we subject the convolved data
235
      to max pooling and then pad it to match the size of the local features, using average pooling as our
236   padding method.
237
238   In comparison, the method outlined in (Li et al., 2018b) relies on 2D convolutional networks to
239
      extract features from XYZ maps of point clouds. However, this approach proves less effective
      than heterogeneous structure methods, as documented in (Wang et al., 2019; He et al., 2020). The
240
      primary reason for this underperformance lies in the loss of spatial information that occurs during
241
      the 2D convolution operations applied to XYZ maps, as highlighted in (Mo et al., 2022).
242
243
      3.4   G LOBAL F EATURE E XTRACTION
244
245   To address the CNN network’s global perception limitations, we incorporate a Pyramid Vision
246   Transformer (PVT) (Wang et al., 2021) to capture long-range dependencies, thereby enhancing
247   global feature extraction.
248
      PVT employs a pyramid-like architecture, systematically reducing spatial resolution across four
249
      stages to learn multi-level features. Each stage consists of a Spatial Reduction Attention (SRA)
250   layer and a Feed-Forward Network (FFN). The SRA utilizes a multi-head self-attention mechanism
251   to effectively model long-distance dependencies while minimizing computational complexity by
252   decreasing pixel count.
253
      To process an input feature map F ∈ RHi ×Wi ×C at the ith stage with dimensions of height Hi ,
254
      width Wi , and C channels), we first perform Layer Normalization (LN) (Ba et al., 2016). The
255
      features are then rearranged into a flattened format, generating vector tokens X ∈ RN ×C , where
256   N = Hi × Wi , signifies the total pixel count of the feature map. The tokens X are mapped to their
257   corresponding query Q, key K, and value V ∈ RN ×C vectors using trained linear mappings WQ ,
258   WK , and WV ∈ RC×C . To optimize memory usage, the spatial extents of K and V are trimmed
259   down before applying self-attention, calculated as:
260
                                                                    QK T

261                             Attention(Q, K, V ) = Softmax √              V,                      (6)
262                                                                  Chead
263
      where Chead denotes the channel depth per attention head in SRA. As stated in Equation 6, every
264   token in the entire input space F can interact with any other token, including itself. The global
265   feature extraction benefits from the dual self-attention mechanism in PVT: 1) The self-attention in
266   each transformer layer expands the network’s receptive field to cover the entire image, enabling
267   interaction between distant pixels and enhancing long-range dependency capture; 2) By embedding
268   both depth and RGB data into each token, the self-attention mechanism evaluates pixel similarities
269   and depth information together, allowing depth data to propagate and correct pixel errors for more
      accurate feature extraction.

288   4.2   DATASETS
289
290   LineMOD (Hinterstoisser et al., 2011) comprises 13 video sequences, each showcasing a unique
291   low-textured object, serving as a benchmark for evaluating 6D object pose estimation methods
292   (Wang et al., 2019; Vidal et al., 2018; Sundermeyer et al., 2018; Tekin et al., 2018a; Li et al., 2018c;
293
      Buch et al., 2017; Drost et al., 2010). Following segmentation practices outlined in the literature
      (Wang et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2017), we allocate 15% of the RGB-D images
294
      for each object to the training set and reserve the remainder for testing, without incorporating any
295
      additional synthetic data.
296   YCB-Video (Xiang et al., 2017) features 21 objects captured in 92 RGB-D videos, highlighting
297   a diverse range of object shapes and textures under various occlusion conditions. Consistent with
298   previous studies (He et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2017), we utilize 80 videos for
299   training and select 2,949 keyframes from the remaining 12 videos as our test set, augmenting our
300   training data with 80,000 synthetic images provided by (Xiang et al., 2017).
301
302   4.3   M ETRICS
303
304   In accordance with established practices (He et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2017), we
305   employ the ADD and ADD-S metrics for accuracy evaluation, designating ADD for non-symmetric
306   objects and ADD-S for symmetric ones. For the LineMOD dataset, accuracy is assessed based on
307   an ADD(S) value of less than 10% of the model’s diameter, and we calculate the corresponding
308
      percentage accuracy. For the YCB-Video dataset, we utilize the area under the curve (AUC) of the
      ADD-S and ADD(S) metrics, varying the distance threshold from 0 cm to 10 cm to generate the
309
      accuracy-threshold curve, from which we then compute the area between this curve and the XY
310
      axes (Mo et al., 2022). Additional details regarding the ADD and ADD-S metrics are provided in
311   Appendix E.

479
480   This paper introduces CTNet, a hybrid architecture combining CNN and Transformer components
481   for 6D pose estimation. The CNN-based Hierarchical Feature Extractor (HFE) optimizes local fea-
482   ture extraction, and the Pyramid Vision Transformer (PVT) captures a broader global context. Along
483   with CNN-based PointNet spatial encoding, this design achieves superior pose estimation perfor-
484   mance. Experiments on the LineMOD and YCB-Video datasets show that CTNet balances accuracy
485   and efficiency, surpassing current models. Furthermore, HFE enhances other architectures when
      integrated, proving its robustness and adaptability.
