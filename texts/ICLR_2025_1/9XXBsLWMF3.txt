009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Modern recommender systems are typically based on deep learning (DL) models,
012              where a dense encoder learns representations of users and items. As a result, these
013              systems often suffer from the black-box nature and computational complexity of
014              the underlying models, making it difficult to systematically interpret their out-
015
                 puts and enhance their recommendation capabilities. To address this problem, we
                 propose Probabilistic Residual User Clustering (PRUC), a causal Bayesian rec-
016
                 ommendation model based on user clustering. Specifically, we address this prob-
017
                 lem by (1) dividing users into clusters in an unsupervised manner and identifying
018              causal confounders that influence latent variables, (2) developing sub-models for
019              each confounder given the observable variables, and (3) generating recommenda-
020              tions by aggregating the rating residuals under each confounder using do-calculus.
021              Experiments demonstrate that our plug-and-play PRUC is compatible with various
022              base DL recommender systems, significantly improving their performance while
023              automatically discovering meaningful user clusters.
024
025   1     Introduction
026
027   Over the past decade, personalized recommendations have significantly improved user experiences
028   in domains such as e-commerce and social media. The recommender systems driving these ad-
029   vancements often rely on sophisticated deep learning (DL) models (Chung et al., 2014; Vaswani
030   et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2019) capable of handling vast amounts of data, enabling highly accurate pre-
031   dictions and personalized interactions. Despite their effectiveness, these models often function as
032   black boxes, lacking transparency and interpretability. This limitation poses significant challenges,
033
      particularly when diagnosing and enhancing the performance of recommender systems in scenarios
      involving domain shifts, such as changes in users’ countries. Cold-start scenarios, a critical problem
034
      in recommendation systems, exacerbate these issues due to the presence of heterogeneous features
035
      and the influence of diverse and spurious patterns. As a result, existing models exhibit notably low
036   performance in such settings.
037
038   Existing work (Yuan et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Bi et al., 2020; Li et al., 2019; Hansen et al.,
039
      2020; Liang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020) often addresses domain shift by estab-
      lishing connections across different domains through shared users or items. However, in real-world
040
      applications, such overlap is often unavailable. For instance, when recommending distinct items to
041
      users from different countries, there is typically no overlap in either users or items. This scenario
042   demands more sophisticated modeling to account for shared confounders. For example, consider
043   position/exposure bias in recommender systems: if the system ranks the item (e.g., an ad) higher,
044   users are biased to rate it higher or have a higher probability to click it. Another example is popular-
045   ity bias; users have a higher probability to click popular or trending items. A system needs to correct
046   for such biases; otherwise the system’s accuracy will drop significantly when the once popular items
047   become less popular. Additionally, existing methods often fail to consider latent user clusters when
048   cluster IDs are not available in the datasets, therefore failing to model (dis)similarities among users.
049   To address these problems, we propose a novel causal hierarchical Bayesian deep learning model,
050   dubbed Probabilistic Residual User Clustering (PRUC), which divides users into latent clusters and
051   makes recommendations based on causal confounders. Our Bayesian causal framework models the
052   residual between the ground-truth rating (or CTR) and the base model’s predicted rating, thereby
053   achieving more precise recommendations. Notably, PRUC is plug-and-play, meaning that it is com-
      patible with any base DL recommendation model and can enhance the original model’s performance.

      Our contributions are as follows:
070          1. We identify the existence of user clusters in various datasets, as well as latent confounders
071             that have a causal effect on user and item hidden representations in DL models.
072          2. We propose a causal Bayesian framework to discover the latent structures of users, items,

      2.1     Problem Setting and Notations
086
087
      Consider a recommendation dataset containing I users and J items. A DL encoder fv (·) : Rd → Rh
088   encodes each item j’s raw features xvj ∈ Rd into fv (xvj ); assume there exists another decoder deep
089
      learning model fx (·) : Rh → Rd , which decodes latent representation vj back to the raw item
090   features xvj . For a given user i and an item j, there is a ground-truth rating Rij ∈ R, a base
091                    bij ∈ R provided by a base recommender, and a residual rating R   eij = Rij − R
      predicted rating R                                                                               bij .
092
      There is a latent cluster ID k (k ∈ {1, ..., K}) that indicates which user group user i belongs to.
093
      We assume that there exists a user latent vector ui ∈ Rh for each user i and an item latent vector
094   vj ∈ Rh for each item j; they are both impacted by a causal confounder s ∈ Rg , where g ≪ h.
095
096   Our goal is to predict the final rating R using the residual R, i.e., R = Rb + R,e where Rb represents
097   the rating from the original (base) DL recommender. When the original recommender is provided,
098   Rb is fixed; therefore we only need to learn Re in order to predict the final rating R. For generality,
099   we assume M domains, with mi and mj denoting the domain ID of user i and item j, respectively.

      user i. See Appendix A.1 for more details.
345
346   F1-score. The F1 Score@N for user i is the harmonic mean of Precision@N and Recall@N, pro-
347   viding a balance between the two metrics:
348                                               Precisioni @N × Recalli @N
349
                                  F1i @N = 2 ×                               ,                           (29)
                                                  Precisioni @N + Recalli @N
350
      where Precisioni @N and Recalli @N are as previously defined for user i at rank N .
351
352   NDCG. Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@N) evaluates the quality of the ranked
353   list by considering the positions of the relevant items, giving higher scores to items appearing earlier
354   in the list. See Appendix A.1 for more details.
355   Note that all metrics are computed by averaging over all users i.

521   Causal Inference for Recommendation. Causal inference (Pearl, 2009) has been widely applied
522
      to model cause-and-effect relationships between variables in the machine learning community. Re-

524

526   without a causal inference perspective (Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). How-
527   ever, these approaches do not account for user groups in SCM modeling. In contrast, our method
528   divides users into clusters based on a confounder variable and recommends by aggregating users’
529   ratings through do-calculus, offering a more interpretable and sophisticated approach.

      5     Conclusion
532
533
      In this paper, we address the problem of cross-domain recommendation by introducing a novel
534   causal Bayesian framework, named Probabilistic Residual User Clustering (PRUC). PRUC gener-
535   ates recommendations by: (1) inferring the user cluster ID, (2) inferring the residual rating based
536   on our causal debiasing framework, and (3) predicting the final rating as a correction to the base
537   model’s prediction. PRUC can enhance the performance of any base recommenders in a plug-
538   and-play manner, and automatically discover meaningful user clusters. As a general probabilistic
539   framework compatible with various recommendation systems, PRUC can be extended to additional
      modalities beyond textual data in future research.
