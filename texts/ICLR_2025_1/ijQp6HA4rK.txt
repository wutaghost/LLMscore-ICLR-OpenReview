030                                                     A BSTRACT
031                 This research delves into the problem of interactive editing of human motion
032                 generation. Previous motion diffusion models lack explicit modeling of the word-
033                 level text-motion correspondence and good explainability, hence restricting their
034                 fine-grained editing ability. To address this issue, we propose an attention-based
035
                    motion diffusion model, namely MotionCLR, with CLeaR modeling of attention
                    mechanisms. Technically, MotionCLR models the in-modality and cross-modality
036
                    interactions with self-attention and cross-attention, respectively. More specifically,
037
                    the self-attention mechanism aims to measure the sequential similarity between
038                 frames and impacts the order of motion features. By contrast, the cross-attention
039                 mechanism works to find the fine-grained word-sequence correspondence and
040                 activate the corresponding timesteps in the motion sequence. Based on these key
041                 properties, we develop a versatile set of simple yet effective motion editing methods
042                 via manipulating attention maps, such as motion (de-)emphasizing, in-place motion
043                 replacement, and example-based motion generation, etc. For further verification of
044                 the explainability of the attention mechanism, we additionally explore the potential
045                 of action-counting and grounded motion generation ability via attention maps. Our
046
                    experimental results show that our method enjoys good generation and editing
                    ability with good explainability. Codes will be public.

048   1 I NTRODUCTION
049   Recently, text-driven human motion generation (Ahn et al., 2018; Petrovich et al., 2022; Tevet
050   et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024a; Hong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; 2024) has
051   attracted significant attention in the animation community for its great potential to benefit versatile
052   downstream applications, such as games and embodied intelligence. As the generated motion quality
053   in one inference might be unsatisfactory, interactive motion editing is valued as a crucial task in the
      community. To provide more interactive editing capabilities, previous works have tried to introduce

054
      some human-defined constraints into the editing framework, such as introducing a pre-defined
055   trajectory for a controllable generation (Xie et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2024; Shafir et al., 2024) or
056   key-frames for motion in-betweening (Chen et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2022; Harvey et al., 2020).
057
      Despite such progress, the constraints introduced in these works are mainly in-modality (motion)
058
      constraints, which require laborious efforts in the real animation creation pipeline. Such interaction
059
      fashions strongly restrict the involving humans in the loop of creation. In this work, we aim to explore
060   a natural editing fashion of introducing out-of-modality signals, such as editing texts. For example,
061   when generating a motion with the prompt “a man jumps.”, we can control the height or times of
062   the “jump” action via adjusting the importance weight of the word “jump”. Alternatively, we can
063   also in-place replace the word “jump” into other actions specified by users. Moreover, in this work,
064   we would like to equip the motion generation model with such abilities without re-training.
065   However, the key limitation of existing motion generation models is that the modeling of previous
066   generative methods lacks explicit word-level text-motion correspondence. This fine-grained cross-
067   modality modeling not only plays a crucial role in text-motion alignment, but also makes it easier for
068   fine-grained editing. To show the problem, we revisit previous transformer-based motion generation
069   models (Tevet et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2024b; 2023b; Chen et al., 2023b). The transformer-
070   encoder-like methods (Tevet et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2024) treat the textual input as one special
071   embedding before the motion sequence. However, text embeddings and motion embeddings imply
072   substantially different semantics, indicating unclear correspondence between texts and motions.
073
      Besides, this fashion over-compresses a sentence into one embedding, which compromises the
      fine-grained correspondence between each word and each motion frame. Although there are some
074
      methods (Zhang et al., 2024b; 2023b) to perform texts and motion interactions via linear cross-
075
      attention, they fuse the diffusion timestep embeddings with textual features together in the forward
076   process. This operation undermines the structural text representations and weakens the input textual
077   conditions. Through these observations, we argue that the fine-grained text-motion correspondence in
078   these two motion diffusion fashions is not well considered. Therefore, it is urgent to build a model
079   with good explainability and clear modeling of fine-grained text-motion correspondence.
080
      To resolve these issues, in this work, we propose a motion diffusion model, namely MotionCLR, with
081   a CLeaR modeling of the motion generation process and fine-grained text-motion correspondence.
082   The main component of MotionCLR is a CLR block, which is composed of a convolution layer, a
083   self-attention layer, a cross-attention layer, and an FFN layer. In this basic block, the cross-attention
084   layer is used to encode the text conditions for each word. More specifically, the cross-attention
085   operation between each word and each motion frame models the text-motion correspondence explicitly.
086   Meanwhile, the timestep injection of the diffusion process and the text encoding are modeled
087   separately. Besides, the self-attention layer in this block is designed for modeling the interaction
088   between different motion frames and the FFN layer is a common design for channel mixing.
089   Motivated by previous progress in the explainality of the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017;
090   Ma et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2015; Hertz et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2021b;a), this work
091   delves into the mathematical properties of the basic CLR block, especially the cross-attention and
092   self-attention mechanisms. In the CLR block, the cross-attention value of each word along the time
093   axis works as an activator to determine the execution time of each action. Besides, the self-attention
094
      mechanism in the CLR block mainly focuses on mining similar motion patterns between frames. Our
      empirical studies verify these properties. Based on these key observations, we show how we can
095
      achieve versatile motion editing downstream tasks (e.g. motion (de-)emphasizing, in-place motion
096
      replacement, and motion erasing) by manipulating cross-attention and self-attention calculations. We
097   verify the effectiveness of these editing methods via both qualitative and quantitative experimental
098   results. Additionally, we explore the potential of action counting with the self-attention map and
099   show how our method can be applied to cope with the hallucination of generative models.
100
      Before delving into the technical details of this work, we summarize our key contributions as follows.
101
      • We propose an attention-based motion diffusion model, namely MotionCLR, with clear modeling
102
        of the text-aligned motion generation process. MotionCLR achieves comparable generation
103
        performance with state-of-the-art methods.
104   • For the first time in the human animation community, we clarify the roles that self- and cross-
105     attention mechanisms play in one attention-based motion diffusion model.
106   • Thanks to these observations, we propose a series of interactive motion editing downstream
107     tasks (see Fig. 1) via manipulating attention layer calculations. We additionally explore the
        potential of our method to perform grounded motion generation when facing failure cases.

123   network architecture. Although these previous attempts have achieved significant progress in the past
124   years, the technical design of the explainability of the attention mechanism is still not well considered.
125   Motion editing aims to edit a motion satisfying human demand. Previous works (Dai et al., 2024;
126   Dabral et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023) attempt to edit a motion in a controlling fashion, like motion
127   inbetweening and joint controlling. There are some other methods (Raab et al., 2023; Aberman
128   et al., 2020b; Jang et al., 2022) trying to control the style of a motion. However, these works are
129   either designed for a specific task or cannot edit fine-grained motion semantics, such as the height or
130   times of a “jump” motion. Raab et al. (2024a) perform motion following via replacing the queries
131   in the self-attention. Goel et al. (2024) propose to edit a motion with an instruction. However, the
132
      fine-grained text-motion correspondence in the cross-attention still lacks an in-depth understanding.
      There are also some methods designed for motion generation (Li et al., 2002) or editing (Lee & Shin,
133
      1999; Holden et al., 2016; Athanasiou et al., 2024), which are limited to adapt to diverse downstream
134
      tasks. Compared to motion editing, the field of diffusion-based image editing has been largely
135   explored. Previous studies have achieved exceptional realism and diversity in image editing (Hertz
136   et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Parmar et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Zhang
137   et al., 2023c; Mou et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024) by manipulating attention maps. Especially, although
138   Hertz et al. (2023) propose to introduce cross-attention into image editing, these techniques and
139   self-attention-based motion editing are still under-explored. However, relevant interactive editing
140   techniques and observations are still unexplored in the human animation community.
141   Our key insights and contribution over previous attention-based motion diffusion models (Tevet
142   et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2024b; 2023b; Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2024) lie in
143   the clear explainability of the self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms in diffusion-based motion
144   generation models. The cross-attention module in our method models the text-motion correspondence
145   at the word level explicitly. Besides, the self-attention mechanism models the motion coherence
146   between frames. Therefore, we can easily clarify what roles self-attention and cross-attention
147   mechanisms play in this framework, respectively. To the best of our knowledge, it is the first time
148   in the human animation community to clarify these mechanisms in one system and explore how to
149
      perform training-free motion editing involving humans in the loop.

185     text-motion interaction of traditional fashions (Tevet et al., 2022b).
186   • Cross-attention module plays a crucial role in learning text-motion correspondence in the CLR
187     block. It takes word-level textual embeddings of a sentence for cross-modality interaction, aiming
188     to obtain fine-grained text-motion correspondence at the word level. Specifically, the attention map
189     explicitly models the relationship between each frame and each word, enabling more fine-grained
190     cross-modality controlling (Detailed comparison with previous methods in Appendix C.3).
191   • FFN module works as an additional feature transformation and extraction (Dai et al., 2022; Geva
192     et al., 2021), which is a necessary component in transformer-based architectures.
193   In summary, in the basic CLR block, we model interactions between frames and cross-modal cor-
194   respondence, separately and explicitly. More detailed comparisons with previous work are in Ap-
195   pendix C.3. We analyze both self-attention and cross-attention of MotionCLR in following sections.

      4                    V ERSATILE A PPLICATIONS VIA ATTENTION M ANIPULATIONS
279
      Analysis in Sec. 3.2 has revealed the roles that attention mechanisms play in MotionCLR. In this
280
      section, we will show versatile downstream tasks of MotionCLR via manipulating attention maps.
281
282   Motion emphasizing and de-emphasizing. In the text-driven motion generation framework, the
283   process is driven by the input text. As discussed in Sec. 3.2, the verb of the action will be significantly
284   activated in the cross-attention map when the action is executed. As shown in Fig. 4a, if we
285
      increase/decrease the attention value of a verb in the cross-attention map, the corresponding action
      will be emphasized/de-emphasized. Besides, this method can also be extended to the grounded
286
      motion generation, which will be introduced in Sec. 6.
287
288   Motion erasing. Motion erasing is a special case of motion de-emphasizing. We treat it as a special
289   case of motion de-emphasizing. When the decreased (de-emphasized) cross-attention value of an
290   action is small enough, the corresponding action will be erased.
291   In-place motion replacement. In real scenarios, we would like to edit some local motion contents
292   of the generated result. Assuming we generate a reference motion at first, we would like to replace
293   one action in the reference motion with another in place. Therefore, the batch size of inference
294   examples is two during the inference stage, where the first is the reference motion and the other is the
295   edited motion. As discussed in Sec. 3.2, the cross-attention map determines when an action happens.
296   Motivated by this, we replace the cross-attention map of the edited motion as the one of the reference
297
      motion. As shown in Fig. 4b, we use the replaced attention map to multiply the value matrix (text
      features) to obtain the output.
298
299   Motion sequence shifting. It is obvious that the generated motion is a combination of different
300   actions along the time axis. Sometimes, users would like to shift a part of the motion along the
301   time axis to satisfy the customized requirements. As shown in Fig. 4c, we can shift the motion
302   sequentiality by shifting the self-attention map. As discussed in Sec. 3.2, self-attention is only
303
      related to the motion feature without related to the semantic condition, which is our motivation on
      manipulating the self-attention map. Thanks to the denoising process, the final output sequence
304
      should be a natural and continuous sequence.
305
306   Example-based motion generation. As defined by Li et al. (2023b), example-based motion gener-
307   ation aims to generate novel motions referring to an example motion. In MotionCLR system, this
308   task is a special case of the motion sequence shifting. That is to say, we can shuffle the queries of the
309
      self-attention map to obtain the diverse motions referring to the example.
310   Motion style transfer. As discussed in the technical details of the self-attention mechanism, the
311   values mainly contribute to the contents of motion and the attention map determines the selected
312   indices of motion frames. When synthesizing two motion sequences (M1 and M2 respectively), we
313   only need to replace Qs in M2 with that in M1 to achieve the style of M2 into M1 ’s. Specifically,
314   queries (Qs) in M2 determine which motion feature in M2 is the most similar to that in M1 at each
315
      timestep. Accordingly, these most similar motion features are selected to compose the edited motion.
      Besides, the edited motion is with the motion content of M2 while imitating the motion style of M1 .
316
317   We leave more technical details and pseudo codes in Appendix F.

501   6    FAILURE C ASES A NALYSIS AND C ORRECTION
502
      There are few generative methods that can escape the curse of hallucination. In this section, we
503
      will discuss some failure cases of our method and analyze how we can refine these results. The
504
      hallucination of counting is a notoriously tricky problem for generative models, attracting significant
505   attention in the community and lacking a unified technical solution. Considering that this problem
506   cannot be thoroughly resolved, we try to partially reveal this issue by additionally providing temporal
507   grounds. For example, if the counting number of an action is not aligned with the textual prompt, we
508   can correct this by specifying the temporal grounds of actions. Technically, the temporal mask can be
509   treated as a sequence of weights to perform the motion emphasizing and de-emphasizing. Therefore,
510   grounded motion generation can be easily achieved by adjusting the weights of words.
511   Specifically, we show some failure cases of our method. As shown in Fig. 13, the generated result
512   of “a person jumps four times” fails to show four times of jumping actions, but seven
513   times. To meet the requirement of counting numbers in the textual prompts, we additionally input a
514   temporal mask, including four jumping timesteps, to provide temporal grounds. From the root height
515   visualization and the motion visualization, the times of the jumping action have been successfully
516   corrected from seven to four. Therefore, our method is promising for grounded motion generation to
517   reveal the hallucination of deep models.
518   Moreover, other editing fashions are also potential ways to correct hallucinations of generated results.
519   For example, the motion sequence shifting and in-place motion replacement functions can be used
520   for correcting sequential errors and semantic misalignments, respectively.
521
522   7    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523   Conclusion. In this work, we propose a diffusion-based motion generation model, MotionCLR.
524   With this model, we carefully clarify the self-attention and the cross-attention mechanisms in the
525   MotionCLR. Based on both theoretical and empirical analysis of the attention mechanisms in
526   MotionCLR, we developed versatile motion editing methods. Additionally, we not only verify the
527   action counting ability of attention maps, but also show the potential of motion corrections. We build
528
      a user interface in Appendix B to demonstrate how can our method support interactive editing.
529   Limitation and future work. As shown in Sec. 6, our model can also not escape the hallucination
530   curse of generative models. Therefore, we leave the grounded motion generation as future work.
531   As discussed in Lu et al. (2024), the CLIP model used in MotionCLR is still a bit unsatisfactory.
532   Therefore, we will provide token-level text-motion alignment encoders to provide textual conditions.
533   Similar to other generative models, our method also meets some issues on some extreme and OOD
534
      examples, which will be resolved by our future scalable generation solution.
535   Broader impact statement. The development of MotionCLR, a diffusion-based motion generation
536   model, has the potential to impact various fields of motion synthesis and editing significantly.
537   However, the complexity of the MotionCLR and its performance limitations under certain conditions
538   may lead to errors or inaccuracies in practical applications. This could result in negative consequences
539   in critical fields such as humanoid simulation and autonomous driving. Therefore, it is necessary to
      further optimize the model and carefully assess its reliability before widespread deployment.

       E.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
1731
1732   The MotionCLR model is trained on the HumanML3D dataset with one NVIDIA A-100 GPU based
1733   on PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). The latent dimension of the motion embedding is 512. We take
1734   the CLIP-ViT-B model to encoder text as word-level embeddings. The training process utilizes a
1735   batch size of 64, with a learning rate initialized at 2e − 4 and decaying at a rate of 0.9 every 5, 000
1736   steps. Additionally, a weight decay of 1e − 2 is employed to regularize the model parameters. For the
1737
       diffusion process, the model is trained over 1, 000 diffusion steps. We incorporate a probability of 0.1
       for condition masking to facilitate classifier-free guidance learning. During training, dropout is set at
1738
       0.1 to prevent overfitting, and all networks in the architecture follow an 8-layer Transformer design.
1739
1740   In the inference stage, all steps of the denoising sampling are set as 10 consistently. For the motion
1741   erasing application, we set the erasing weight as 0.1 as default. MotionCLR supports both DDIM-
1742   sampling (Song et al., 2021) and DPM-soler-sampling (Lu et al., 2022) methods, with 1, 000 as full
1743
       diffusion steps. For the in-placement motion replacement and the motion style transfer application,
       as the motion semantics mainly depend on the initial denoising steps, we set the manipulating steps
1744
       until 5 as default. For motion (de-)emphasizing, we support both multiplications (larger than 1 for
1745
       emphasizing, lower than 1 for de-emphasizing) and addition (larger than 0 for emphasizing, lower
1746   than 0 for de-emphasizing) to adjust the cross-attention weights. For the example-based motion
1747   generation, the minimum manipulating time of a motion zone is 1s (a.k.a. chunk size=20 for the 20
1748   FPS setting). At each step, all attention maps at all layers will be manipulated at each denoising
1749   timestep. Users can adjust the parameters freely to achieve interactive motion generation and editing
1750   (more details of user interface in Appendix B).
