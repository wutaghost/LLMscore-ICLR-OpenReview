011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Feature transformation tasks aim to generate high-value features by combining ex-
014            isting ones through mathematical operations, which can improve the performance
015
               of downstream machine learning models. Current methods typically use itera-
               tive sequence generation, where exploration is guided by performance feedback
016
               from downstream tasks. However, these approaches fail to effectively utilize his-
017
               torical decision-making experiences and overlook potential relationships between
018            generated features, thus limiting the flexibility of the exploration process. Addi-
019            tionally, the decision-making process lacks the ability to dynamically backtrack
020            on efficient decisions, which hinders adaptability and reduces overall robustness
021            and stability. To address these issues, we propose a novel framework that uses a
022            graph to track the feature transformation process, where each node represents a
023            transformation state. In this framework, three cascading agents sequentially select
024            nodes and mathematical operations to generate new nodes. This strategy benefits
025            from the graph structure’s ability to store and reuse valuable transformations, and
026
               it incorporates backtracking via graph pruning techniques, allowing the frame-
               work to correct inefficient paths. To demonstrate the effectiveness and flexibility
027
               of our approach, we conducted extensive experiments and detailed case studies,
028
               demonstrating superior performance across a variety of datasets.

031   1   INTRODUCTION
032
033   Classic machine learning is highly dependent not only on the structure of the model but also on
034   the quality of the training data (Sambasivan et al., 2021; Strickland, 2022; Borisov et al., 2022;
035   Zha et al., 2023) (as depicted in Figure 1(a)). Traditionally, optimizing the dataset is referred to as
036   feature engineering (Dong & Liu, 2018; Nargesian et al., 2017), which requires extensive manual
037   intervention by domain experts (Conrad et al., 2022) and is time-consuming and labor-intensive.
038   Other models, such as GBTs (Si et al., 2017) and deep neural networks (Bengio et al., 2013), can
039
      capture non-linear feature interactions spontaneously. However, they generally require significant
      amounts of data and computational power to achieve good generalization Grinsztajn et al. (2022),
040
      especially with limited tabular data Shwartz-Ziv & Armon (2022). Consequently, automated feature
041
      transformation has been proposed to adopt a data-centric perspective (Zha et al., 2023; Cui et al.,
042   2024) to ensure both efficiency and automation.
043
044   Background of Automated Feature Transformation: The mainstream of existing automated fea-
045
      ture transformation adopts an iterative perspective: 1) expansion-reduction approaches (Kanter &
      Veeramachaneni, 2015; Khurana et al., 2016b; Horn et al., 2019) randomly combine and gener-
046
      ate features through mathematical transformations, then employ feature selection techniques to
047
      isolate high-quality features. Those approaches are highly stochastic, lack stability, and are not
048   optimization-oriented. 2) iterative-feedback approaches (Tran et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
049   2024) aim to refine the feature space with the transformation towards reinforcement learning (Wang
050   et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023a; 2024) and evolutionary algorithms (Khurana et al., 2018). Although
051   those methods can optimize their strategies during the exploration, they discard the valuable experi-
052   ences from historical sub-transformations and cannot backtrack on individual features. 3) AutoML
053   approaches (Zhu et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023) partially adjust aforementioned issues by learn-
      ing the pattern of the collected historical transformation records (Wang et al., 2024) thus reach a

080   Our Perspective and Contribution: In this work, we pivot to a transformation-centric approach
081   in addressing the challenges outlined earlier (illustrated in the right section of Figure 1(b)). This
082
      shift brings forth three principal benefits that significantly enhance the capabilities of our reinforce-
      ment learning-based automated feature transformation framework: (1) Enhanced Transformation
083
      Agility: Our model is designed to capture and dynamically apply transformations across various
084
      stages of the feature transformation process rather than being restricted to transformations derived
085   from the current feature set. This enables a more flexible and robust handling of features. (2)
086   Historical Insights Utilization: We leverage deep learning techniques to extract and model latent
087   correlations and mathematical characteristics from past transformation efforts. This historical in-
088   sight informs our decision-making process, allowing the algorithm to execute transformation actions
089   based on the lessons learned strategically. (3) Robust Backtracking Mechanism: Our approach
090   incorporates a sophisticated backtracking system that utilizes historical transformation records for
091   traceability. This feature ensures that the transformation process can revert or alter its course to
092   avoid inefficient or suboptimal trajectories, thus optimizing the overall feature engineering pathway.
093   Summary of Proposed Method: A Framework That Maintains Transformation Roadmap.
094   To capitalize on the benefits of a transformation-centric approach, we introduce the Flexible
095   Transformation-Centric Tabular Data Optimization Framework (TCTO), an innovative automated
096   feature transformation methodology employing a cascading multi-agent reinforcement learning
097   (MARL) algorithm (Busoniu et al., 2008; Panait & Luke, 2005). Our framework is structured
098   around an evolving feature-state transformation graph, which is maintained throughout the MARL
099
      process. This graph serves as a comprehensive roadmap, where each node and its path back to root
      node represents a unique sequence of transformations applied to the initial features of the dataset.
100
      Our optimization procedure comprises four steps: (1) clustering each node on the roadmap with
101
      mathematical and spectral characteristics, (2) state representation for each cluster, (3) cluster-level
102   transformation decision generation based on multi-agent reinforcement learning; (4) evaluation and
103   reward estimation for the generated outcomes. Iteratively, TCTO executes these steps while lever-
104   aging the traceability of the roadmap for a precise node-wise and step-wise pruning. This allows for
105   targeted feature reduction and strategic rollbacks, optimizing the transformation pathway. Through
106   rigorous experimental validation, we demonstrate that TCTO not only enhances the flexibility of the
107   optimization process, but also delivers more resilient and effective results compared to traditional
      iterative optimization frameworks.

      Roadmap-based State Representation for Each Agent: Achieving an accurate state representa-
186
      tion is crucial for enabling reinforcement agents to make informed decisions. In our framework,
187
      the transformation roadmap is a repository of intermediate transformation records, complete with
188   their mathematical attributes. At each step, the agents select clusters of nodes, which can be
189   seen as a subgraph on the roadmap. We then integrate a Relational Graph Convolutional Network
190   (RGCN) (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018) to extract latent correlations within these historical records and
191   capture the representation of each cluster. This approach allows our model to take advantage of the
192   global insights gained from the RGCN, facilitating strategic transformation actions that are guided
193   by the detailed state of the selected nodes.

195   has proven effective in addressing complex decision-making challenges across various domains.
196   We employ three cascading agents that collaboratively construct unary and binary mathematical
197   transformations. These agents operate sequentially to select the optimal head cluster, mathematical
198   operation, and operand cluster, respectively. The chosen features undergo the specified mathematical
199   operations, resulting in the generation of new features and the creation of new nodes within the
200   roadmap. Additional details regarding the decision-making process will be provided in Section 3.3.
201   Reward Estimation for Optimizing Agents: Our model is optimized to generate high-quality
202   features with minimal steps, enhancing efficiency. In this context, TCTO evaluates the generated
203   features via the performance of downstream tasks to refine the reinforcement learning algorithm. In
204   addition, we factored the complexity of the generated features into the reward function. This dual
205   focus on performance and complexity ensures that the model aims for effectiveness while avoiding
206   overly complex solutions that could hinder practical applicability and interpretability.
207   Effective Roadmap Backtracking: We have implemented two pruning strategies to manage the
208   expanding complexity as the number of nodes in our roadmap grows. These approaches are designed
209   to reduce the potential explosion in roadmap complexity, ensure the system remains efficient and
210   manageable, and enhance our system’s stability.

310   Cascading Reinforcement Learning Agents: Figure 7 shows an example of the cascading agents’
311   decision-making process. We utilize a series of cascading agents, each performing a specific task
312   in sequential order. These agents collaborate in a step-by-step decision-making process, where the
313   output of one agent serves as the input for the next. The first agent (head cluster agent) is respon-
314
      sible for selecting the head cluster, the second (operation agent) for choosing the most appropriate
      mathematical operation, and the third (operand cluster agent) for identifying the operand cluster. By
315
      using this cascading structure, each decision is informed by the context set by the previous agents,
316
      leading to a more efficient decision-making process. The details of each agent are as follows:
317
318   1) Head Cluster Agent: As described earlier, each node on the roadmap has been clustered into C.
319   The first agent aims to select the head cluster to be transformed according to the current state of each
320
      cluster. Specifically, the i-th cluster state is given as Rep(ci ), and the overall state can be represented
      as Rep(V ). With the head policy network πh (·), the score of select ci as the action can be estimated
321
      by: shi = πh (Rep(ci ) ⊕ Rep(V )). We use ch to denote the selected cluster with the highest score.
322
323   2) Operation Agent: The operation agent aims to select the mathematical operation to be performed
      according to the overall roadmap and selected head cluster. The policy network in the operation

328

351   Optimization of the Pipeline: In the cascading reinforcement learning setup described, the opti-
352   mization policy is critical to refine the decision making capabilities of the agents involved: the Head
353   Cluster Agent, Operation Agent, and Operand Cluster Agent. The overarching goal of this policy
354   is to iteratively improve the actions taken by these agents to maximize the cumulative rewards de-

358   1) Policy Optimization: The learning process for each agent is driven by a reward mechanism that
359   quantifies the effectiveness and efficiency of the transformations applied to the roadmap. Specifi-
360   cally, the optimization policy is framed within a value-based reinforcement learning approach, lever-
361
      aging a dual network setup architecture: a prediction network and a target network. The prediction
      network generates action-value (Q-value) predictions that guide the agents’ decision-making pro-
362
      cesses at each step. It evaluates the potential reward for each possible action given the current state,
363
      facilitating the selection of actions that are anticipated to yield the highest rewards. The target net-
364   work serves as a stable benchmark for the prediction network and helps to calculate the expected
365   future rewards. Decoupling the Q-value estimation from the target values is crucial to reducing
366   overestimations and ensuring stable learning.
367

415
416   This experiment aims to answer the question: Can our framework generate high-quality features to
417   improve the downstream machine learning model? Table 1 presents the overall comparison between
418   our model and other models in terms of F1-score for classification tasks and 1-RAE for regression
419   tasks. We observed that our model outperforms other baseline methods in most datasets. The pri-
420   mary reason is that it dynamically captures and applies transformations across various stages of
421   the feature transformation process rather than being restricted to the latest nodes, thereby enhanc-
422   ing flexibility and robustness. Compared to expansion-reduction, our technique, along with other
423
      iterative-feedback methods, demonstrates a significant advantage in performance. The fundamental
      mechanism is that the reinforcement agent is capable of learning and refining its approach to the
424
      process, thereby achieving superior performance compared to random exploration. Another obser-
425
      vation is that our model performs better than other iterative-feedback approaches, such as NFS, TTG,
426   and GRFG. An explanation could be that our model identifies and incorporates hidden correlations
427   and mathematical properties, enabling it to develop an improved strategy for feature transformation,
428   drawing on extensive historical knowledge from previous efforts. Compared with the AutoML-
429   based approach, DIFER, our technique demonstrates a significant improvement. This is primarily
430   because DIFER relies on randomly generated transformations, which are unstable and prone to sub-
431   optimal results. Overall, this experiment demonstrates that TCTO is effective and robust across
      diverse datasets, underscoring its broad applicability for automated feature transformation tasks.

504   5     R ELATED W ORK
505
506   Feature engineering refers to the process of handling and transforming raw features to better suit
507   the needs of machine learning algorithms (Hancock & Khoshgoftaar, 2020; Chen et al., 2021).
508   Automated feature engineering implies that machines autonomously perform this task without the
509   need for human prior knowledge(Lam et al., 2017). There are three mainstream approaches: The
510   expansion-reduction based method (Kanter & Veeramachaneni, 2015; Horn et al., 2019; Khurana
511   et al., 2016b; Lam et al., 2017; Khurana et al., 2016a), characterized by its greedy or random expan-
512   sion of the feature space(Katz et al., 2016; Dor & Reich, 2012), presents challenges in generating
513
      intricate features, consequently leading to a restricted feature space. The iterative-feedback ap-
      proach (Khurana et al., 2018; Tran et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023a; Zhu et al.,
514
      2022a; Xiao et al., 2024) methods integrate feature generation and selection stages into one stage
515
      learning process, and aims to learn transformation strategy through evolutionary or reinforcement
516   learning algorithms (Ren et al., 2023). However, these methods usually model the feature gener-
517   ation task as a sequence generation problem, ignoring historical and interactive information dur-
518   ing the transformation progress, result in lack of stability and flexibility. The AutoML-based ap-
519   proaches (Wang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022b; Xiao et al., 2023b; Ying et al., 2023) have recently
520   achieved significant advancement. However, they are limited by the quality of the collected trans-
521   formation and also the lack of stability and traceability during the generation phase. To overcome
522   these problems, TCTO introduces a novel framework that integrates structural insights based on
523   roadmaps and a backtracking mechanism with deep reinforcement learning techniques to improve
524
      feature engineering.
525
526   6     C ONCLUSION R EMARKS
527
528   We introduce TCTO, an automated framework for feature transformation. Our approach focuses on
529   managing feature modifications through a transformation roadmap, which keeps track of and orga-
530   nizes the process to ensure optimal feature generation. There are three main benefits to our approach:
531   (1) Preserving Transformation Records: The roadmap structure automatically logs all feature trans-
532   formations, making it accurate to cluster similar features and enhancing the model’s capabilities.
533
      (2) Insightful Decision-Making: By utilizing unique structural and mathematical characteristics, our
      cascading agents can make better decisions based on detailed state representations. (3) Increased
534
      Robustness through Backtracking: The roadmap’s built-in backtracking feature allows the frame-
535
      work to correct or change its path if it encounters inefficient or suboptimal transformations, thereby
536   improving the model’s robustness and adaptability. Extensive experiments show that TCTO is ef-
537   fective and flexible in optimizing data for a wide range of applications. Further discussion including
538   future work and application scenario is listed in Appendix A.4.

      To comprehensively explore the feature space, we conducted exploration training for 50 episodes,
885
      each consisting of 100 steps, during the reinforcement learning agent optimization phase. Following
886
      optimizing, we assessed the exploration ability of the cascading agents by conducting 10 applica-
887   tion episodes, each comprising 100 steps. Following existing research Wang et al. (2024); Xiao
888   et al. (2024), we set the number of clusters k to the square root of the current number of nodes,
889   while the number of nodes triggered for pruning K is set to four times the original number of fea-
890   tures. During step-wise pruning, we utilize the k most importance features. We utilized a two-layer
891   RGCN as the encoder for the transformation roadmap, and an embedding layer for the operation
892   encoder. The hidden state sizes for the roadmap encoder and operation encoder were set to 32 and
893   64, respectively. Each agent was equipped with a two-layer feed-forward network for the predictor,
894   with a hidden size of 100. The target network was updated every 10 exploration steps by copying
895
      parameters from the prediction network. To train the cascading agents, we set the memory buffer to
      16 and the batch size to 8, with a learning rate of 0.01. For the first 30% epochs, we employed a
896
      node-wise pruning strategy to eliminate low-quality features. Subsequently, we utilized a step-wise
897
      backtracking strategy for the remaining epochs to restore the optimal roadmap.

       Regarding the Limitation of State Representation Method: The state representation method
