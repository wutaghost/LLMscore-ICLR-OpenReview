009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning single-cell samples across different datasets and modalities is an im-
012            portant task with the rise of high-throughput single-cell technologies. Currently,
013            collecting multi-modality datasets with paired samples is difficult, expensive, and
014            impossible in some cases, motivating methods to align unpaired samples from
015            distinct uni-modality datasets. While dataset alignment problems have been ad-
016            dressed in various domains, single-cell data introduce additional complexity in-
017            cluding high levels of noise, dropout, and non-isometry between data spaces.
018
               In response to these unique challenges, we propose Wavelet Optimal Transport
               (WOT), a multi-resolution optimal transport method that aligns samples by mini-
019
               mizing the spectral graph wavelet discrepancies across datasets. Filters are in-
020
               corporated into the optimization process to eliminate non-essential scales and
021            wavelets, enhancing the quality of correspondences. We demonstrate the capac-
022            ity of WOT in highly noisy and non-isometric conditions, outperforming previ-
023            ous state-of-the-art methods by significant margins, especially on real single-cell
024            datasets.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Single-cell technologies have revolutionized biological research by offering a detailed understanding
030   of individual cell behaviors within heterogeneous populations. However, most single-cell technol-
031
      ogy is only capable of capturing a single description of the cell state (Trapnell, 2015); experiments
      such as single-cell proteomics or Western blot can even be destructive, meaning they either alter or
032
      destroy the cell being analyzed, preventing further analysis in the same cell (Tang, 2022). Thus, an
033
      increasingly essential but difficult problem within this field is aligning the data produced by each of
034   these technologies in which no paired samples are available.
035
036   The problem of data or manifold alignment is not unique to biology and has been extensively studied
037
      elsewhere. Works in natural language processing have aligned the data spaces of different languages
      (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Schuster et al., 2019; Vulić et al., 2019), and the field of computer
038
      vision has translated images between different domains (Zhu et al., 2017; Grover et al., 2020; Su
039
      et al., 2022). In addition to the strict absence of paired data, the alignment of single-cell data poses
040   additional unique issues that are often not present in other fields. For single-cell data, identifying
041   correspondences between datasets requires navigating the inherent modality-specific variability and
042   noise they present. Although such variability can be attributed to biological variabilities like cell
043   cycle stages, spatial heterogeneity, and cellular differentiation, it can also be caused by technical
044   variabilities like dropout, batch effect, and library preparation (Arzalluz-Luque et al., 2017). Thus,
045   proposed methods in this field must be able to identify correspondences based on the important
046   biological variability while filtering out the unimportant, technical variability—all in a completely
047   unpaired setting.
048   To address the challenges of unpaired single-cell alignment, we propose Wavelet Optimal Trans-
049   port (WOT), a framework that finds a transport plan that agrees with multiple views of a dataset
050   while filtering out uninformative or noisy components. Specifically, our framework considers the
051   relationships of samples in a dataset as the coefficients of spectral graph wavelets, allowing us to
052   decompose the dataset’s signals into both scale and individual sample resolution. WOT aligns points
053   between datasets such that it minimizes the discrepancy between the wavelets of each dataset across
      all views. We incorporate a filter in the optimization of the transport plan that removes uninfor-

083
084         1. We formulate the relationships of points in each dataset in terms of spectral graph wavelet
085            coefficients, and develop a multi-scale optimal transport framework that finds a transport
086            plan between points in each dataset with respect to these wavelets.
087         2. We provide two implementations of our framework, E-WOT and L-WOT, that allow us to
088            filter out unimportant scales and wavelets, elucidating the important structures to match
089            between datasets.
090         3. We demonstrate that WOT is more robust to noise, dropout, and non-isometry—advantages
091            that enable us to consistently outperform previous state-of-the-art methods in aligning
092

096   2.1   S INGLE -C ELL DATASET A LIGNMENT
097
098   Several methods have been proposed to align single-cell datasets. SCOT (Demetci et al., 2022b) and
099
      its updated version, SCOTv2 (Demetci et al., 2022a), use the Gromov-Wasserstein distance and its
      unbalanced formulations to align distributions across different domains. Another approach, Pamona
100
      (Cao et al., 2022), leverages a combination of manifold learning and partial Gromov-Wasserstein
101
      distance. UnionCom (Cao et al., 2020) integrates datasets by preserving both individual and shared
102   structures through a shared low-dimensional embedding. MMD-MA (Liu et al., 2019) is based on
103   maximum mean discrepancy, offering a non-parametric way to integrate single-cell data. Lastly,
104   cross autoencoders (Yang et al., 2021) utilize autoencoders to learn common embeddings that can
105   bridge the gap between different modalities. While these works have shown success in finding high-
106   quality correspondences given prior knowledge of cell types or a subset of paired samples, they
107   (1) still often underperform when aligning datasets in a completely unpaired setting and (2) do not

      explicitly reduce dataset-intrinsic noise or signal (which is later shown in Section 4.3 to be important
109   for accurate alignment across single-cell datasets).

      2.2   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT AND G ROMOV-WASSERSTEIN D ISTANCE
112
113   Optimal transport (OT) (Villani et al., 2009) is an appealing solution to the data alignment problem.
114   The goal of OT is to find the best way to transform one distribution into another. However, when
115   dealing with different spaces (e.g. ATAC-seq space vs nuclei imaging space), a direct comparison
116   becomes challenging. Using the Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) distance (Mémoli, 2011) provides a
117   framework that bypasses this issue and has become a popular choice for many data alignment tasks
118   (Gong et al., 2022; Thual et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). Instead of comparing points directly by their
119
      positions across datasets, OT based on the Gromov-Wasserstein distance compares how the distance
      matrices of points are mapped across different domains.

123   Wasserstein distance identifies a transport plan (or coupling) T∗ among the set of joint distributions
124   between A and B with marginals p and q. Namely, T∗ ∈ Π(p, q) = {T ∈ R≥0              n×m

133   As the field has expanded, variants of Gromov-Wasserstein OT have emerged. The entropy-

141   2.3   S PECTRAL G RAPH WAVELETS
142
143   Wavelets can be viewed as augmentations of the Fourier bases, providing resolution in both time
144   (or space) and frequency. Hammond et al. (2011) extended wavelets to the domain of graphs with
145   Spectral Graph Wavelets (SGWs), allowing localized signal representation on both the vertex and
146   frequency domain. Utilizing the spectral characteristics of the graph Laplacian to analyze and pro-
147   cess signals on graphs, SGWs have been applied widely in machine learning. In the context of
148
      networks, Donnat et al. (2018) demonstrated the application of SGWs for node embeddings, lever-
      aging their ability to encapsulate structural network information. Another work (Mémoli, 2009)
149
      proposed a heat kernel-based Gromov-Wasserstein distance, drawing parallels to geodesic and dif-
150
      fusion distances. We build on SGWs to develop a flexible optimal transport framework that not only
151   generalizes to other wavelets (outside of the heat kernel) but also provides systematic approaches to
152   filtering out uninformative wavelets and frequency bands.

228   kernels (Chan et al., 2004) are a subset of band-pass kernels that conserve the energy of the signal
229   during the wavelet transformation (and its inverse), allowing for more accurate signal representation.
230   In this work, our evaluation is limited to the following set of wavelet kernels: the low-pass heat

490   5   C ONCLUSION
491
492
      We presented Wavelet Optimal Transport, a framework that leverages spectral graph wavelets to
      better align unpaired datasets. Through initial experiments, we demonstrated that WOT is able
493
      to maintain high-quality matches across datasets even in the presence of high noise, dropout, and
494
      non-isometry. Finally, we showed the effectiveness of WOT on two real single-cell datasets, outper-
495   forming the previously most accurate methods.
496
497   A fruitful direction for future research is to incorporate the WOT objective in machine learning
498
      pipelines as a loss function such that we can match data distributions at specific bands of scales or
      space. WOT could also be applied in linking cross-section time series data where cross-sections may
499
      be collected with different modalities; this problem is common in trajectory analysis or modeling
500
      perturbation response in cells. Another direction that warrants more investigation is the design of
501   more effective filters that could be used in our framework.

1111
1112   The runtime of our method is dominated by two steps: (1) the construction of the spectral graph
1113   wavelets and (2) optimizing E-WOT or L-WOT. We provide further analysis of WOT’s complexity
1114   and how our method scales with respect to different input databases and parameterizations of WOT.
1115
       As is common with optimal transport frameworks, the efficiency of WOT can diminish as the volume
1116   of the dataset increases. Gromov-Wasserstein OT with entropic regularization (Peyré et al., 2016)
1117   scales O(n3 ) with n as the number of samples. Since our objective for vanilla-WOT and E-WOT
1118   is optimized similarly to GW-OT, we inherit the cubic complexity with an additional factor of |S|
1119   because we recalculate Proposition 1 in (Peyré et al., 2016) s ∈ S times, resulting in O(|S|n3 ) time
1120   complexity. We further need to consider the O(n2 + |S|n) computational complexity of calculating
1121   ψ for S scales using Cheyshev’s polynomial approximation (Hammond et al., 2011). L-WOT would
1122   inherent greater complexity due to the nested loop with an inner subroutine that requires running
1123   E-WOT and automatic differentiation.

       FOSCTTM (Experiment 1). Fraction of Samples Closer Than the True Match, introduced by Liu
1189   et al. (2019), is a measure of alignment error between two datasets. It quantifies the proportion of
1190   samples in one dataset that are closer to a given sample in the other dataset than its true match,
1191   averaged across all samples in both datasets.
1192
       To compute the FOSCTTM score for dataset A and B, we follow these steps:
1193
1194         1. For each sample point in dataset A, calculate the Euclidean distances between that point
1195            and all the data points in dataset B that have been projected into the dataspace of A (i.e.
1196            using barycentric projection).
1197
             2. Using these calculated distances, determine the fraction of projected samples in dataset B
1198            that are closer to the fixed sample point than its true match (i.e., the corresponding point in
1199            the second dataset that should be aligned with the fixed sample point).
1200
             3. Repeat steps 1 and 2 for all sample points in dataset A and take the average; the final value
1201
                is the FOSCTTM score between A and B when B is projected into dataset A (note that this
1202            score is not equivalent to when A is projected into dataset B)
1203
             4. Perform steps 1-3 for each sample point in dataset B, calculating the distances to all points
1204
                in dataset A projected into dataset B and determining the fraction of samples closer than
1205
                the true match.
1206
1207
             5. Finally, compute the average of the fractions obtained in steps 3 and 4 across all samples
                in both datasets to obtain the final FOSCTTM score.
1208
1209   The FOSCTTM score ranges from 0 to 1, with a perfect alignment resulting in a score of 0. In
1210   other words, when all samples are closest to their true matches, the average FOSCTTM will be zero.
1211   As the alignment quality decreases, the FOSCTTM score increases, indicating a higher fraction of
1212   samples that are closer to other points than their true matches.
1213
       Relative Geodesic Error (Experiment 2). This metric is calculated as
1214
1215                                                                      1
1216                                       ϵ(ai ) = dY (ai , bi )/area(B) 2
1217
       where ai is the projected sample from animal A onto animal B. This projected sample is then
1218
       compared with the ground truth sample bi from animal B. dY represents the geodesic distance on
1219
       the animal which is calculated using a KNN approximation. The area over the animal is calculated
1220   using Delaunay triangulation as the surfaces.
1221
1222   Label Transfer Accuracy (Experiment 3). Introduced in Cao et al. (2020), Label Transfer Ac-
1223
       curacy (LTA) is used when ground truth pairings are not available, and it evaluates how well cell
       types cluster together after alignment. It works by splitting the aligned data in half, training a kNN
1224
       classifier on one half, and testing its accuracy on the other half. The classifier tries to predict cell
1225
       types based on their proximity in the aligned space. Higher scores mean the alignment has grouped
1226   similar cell types closer together, allowing for more accurate predictions. This indicates a better
1227   quality alignment, where cells of the same type are consistently found near each other.

       G    L IMITATIONS
1231
1232
       While the Wavelet Optimal Transport (WOT) framework has demonstrated notable strengths in the
       domain of unpaired single-cell alignment and other noisy and non-isometric matching experiments,
1233
       there are some limitations worth addressing:
1234
1235   Scalability. It is unclear whether WOT and its implementations can be applied directly to very
1236   large-scale datasets without some computation reduction strategies like mini-batch OT (Nguyen
1237   et al., 2022) as shown in Appendix E. Specifically, as the field of single-cell biology continues to
1238
       expand and produce larger datasets, it will be crucial for future implementations of WOT to consider
       strategies for scalable alignment without compromising accuracy.
1239
1240   Hyperparameters. The wavelet kernel, scale aggregation operation, and entropic regularization are
1241   deeply coupled. Having some prior knowledge or validation set to select the optimal values for these
       hyperparameters would be ideal. However, in unpaired settings like ours, the key hyperparameter


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       that requires tuning is entropic regularization ϵ (this issue was similarly seen in Gromov-Wasserstein
1243   OT). Either by using a heuristic like the one proposed in Section 4 or another approach like Demetci
1244   et al. (2022a), readers must ensure that the selected ϵ does not result in a uniform transport plan (i.e.
1245   failed to converge to an informative plan).
1246
       Furthermore, we see a high variance in the experimental results between the two implementations,
1247
       E-WOT and L-WOT. While these different instantiations of WOT offer unique filtering methods, the
1248
       discrepancy in results suggests that there might be inherent complexities or nuances in the datasets
1249   that one method captures better than the other. This variability highlights the need for a deeper ex-
1250   ploration into which filtering method (entropy-based or learned) and which types of wavelet kernels
1251   are more suited for specific types of datasets
1252
       Performance in Low Noise Settings. Our experiment results indicate that while WOT exhibits su-
1253
       perior performance in scenarios with high noise, dropout, and non-isometry, it does not consistently
1254
       outperform Gromov-Wasserstein OT in low-noise settings. This suggests that the benefits of using
1255   WOT will be more clear in situations with substantial technical variability, rather than in cleaner
1256   datasets. Readers should be aware of this trade-off when choosing the alignment technique best
1257   suited to their dataset’s characteristics.
