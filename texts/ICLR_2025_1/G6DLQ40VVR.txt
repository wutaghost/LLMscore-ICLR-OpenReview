009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Object navigation in unknown environments is crucial for deploying embodied
012            agents in real-world applications. While we have witnessed huge progress due to
013            large-scale scene datasets, faster simulators, and stronger models, previous studies
014            mainly focus on limited scene types and target objects. In this paper, we study a
015            new task of navigating to diverse target objects in a large number of scene types.
016            To benchmark the problem, we present a large-scale scene dataset, D IV S CENE,
017            which contains 4,614 scenes across 81 different types. With the dataset, we build
018
               an end-to-end embodied agent, NAT VLM, by fine-tuning a Large Vision Lan-
               guage Model (LVLM) through imitation learning. The LVLM is trained to take
019
               previous observations from the environment and generate the next actions. We
020
               also introduce CoT explanation traces of the action prediction for better perfor-
021            mance when tuning LVLMs. Our extensive experiments find that we can build
022            a performant LVLM-based agent through imitation learning on the shortest paths
023            constructed by a BFS planner without any human supervision. Our agent achieves
024            a success rate that surpasses GPT-4o by over 20%. Meanwhile, we carry out vari-
025            ous analyses showing the generalization ability of our agent.

      most comprehensive range of scene types to the best of our knowledge. Specifically, we collect 81
055   scene types based on MIT Scenes Dataset (Quattoni & Torralba, 2009), which are further catego-
056   rized into five big groups. Then, we use LLMs to automatically compose diverse house descriptions
057   by adding attributes to scene types, such as “a bakery with tile patterned walls.” We input these
058   descriptions into the language-guided framework, Holodeck (Yang et al., 2024), to build houses
059   automatically with the strong ability of GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023). Finally, we compile 4,614 houses

063   With D IV S CENE, we proceed to build an embodied agent that can navigate to diverse objects in
064   diverse types of houses. As aforementioned, existing works, such as RL-based (Maksymets et al.,
065   2021) and IL-based (Ramrakhya et al., 2022) methods, only focus on learning in-distribution knowl-
066   edge for limited target objects and scene types. Meanwhile, recent studies (Yu et al., 2023; Zhou
067   et al., 2023; Dorbala et al., 2023) utilize LLMs as the planning backbone to navigate to diverse
068   objects in a zero-shot manner, leveraging LLM’s commonsense knowledge for high-level guidance.
069
      However, they still face a substantial domain gap between navigation tasks and the LLM training
      corpus (Lin et al., 2024). Moreover, those methods (Chen et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024) require
070
      a captioning model to generate textual descriptions of each scene, serving as the perception of the
071
      backbone LLMs. The captioning model might miss crucial information about objects and lead to
072   suboptimal results, like spatial or color details.
073
074   To tackle the challenge, we propose an end-to-end object navigation agent called NAT VLM
075
      (Navigational Chain-of-Thought VLM), based on the large vision language model Idefics
      2 (Laurençon et al., 2024). As shown in Figure 1, the model is tuned to generate the next action
076
      given the current observation, such as the the egocentric view and the agent’s status. Here, we train
077
      the LVLM with imitation learning (Brohan et al., 2022) using shortest paths from a heuristic plan-
078   ner. To improve the accuracy, we also manually collect complex CoT explanation traces of each
079   prediction (Mitra et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2023) to help the LVLM understand the underlying rationale
080   behind object navigation. By tuning the LVLM, we eliminate the domain gap between navigation
081   tasks and the pre-training corpus. Meanwhile, our navigation is performed in an end-to-end manner
082   with perception, circumventing the captioning model.
083
      Existing IL-based works trained their navigational agents on large corpora of human demonstra-
084   tions (Brohan et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023), which is incredibly expensive. In contrast, our study
085   finds that imitating the shortest paths constructed by a heuristic planner can be an effective approach
086   to training agents based on LVLMs. Specifically, the AI2THOR platform discretizes the environ-
087   ment into a grid map with a fixed step size. Then, we search for the shortest path from the agent
088   to the target objects. In total, we collect about 23K shortest-path episodes in the houses from D I -
089   V S CENE , forming the D IV T RAJ dataset. The dataset contains 5,707 different kinds of target objects,
090   significantly more than other navigation datasets.
091   With shortest-path episodes, we perform extensive experiments and analyses to evaluate our
092   NAT VLM agent. First, we introduce several baselines using various LLMs and VLMs (Liu et al.,
093   2024b; Touvron et al., 2023) to establish baseline performance levels. The evaluation results show
094   that our proposed agent can navigate to diverse objects more effectively than baselines by a large
095   margin. We carry out thorough ablation studies to show the efficacy of CoT explanation traces in
096   action prediction. Moreover, few-shot experiments demonstrate the robustness of our agent. Last
097   but not least, we validate the generalization ability of our agent on two out-of-distribution datasets:
098
      ProcTHOR (Deitke et al., 2022) and iTHOR (Weihs et al., 2021).

101
102
103   Recent breakthroughs in large vision language models (LVLMs) have shown success in several
104
      domains (Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Team, 2023; Jiang et al., 2024). Our work first builds
      a navigational agent based on LVLMs with the following related areas of study:
105
106   Visual Navigation: Visual navigation is a critical task for building intelligent agents imitating hu-
107   man behavior. Conventional methods depend on an environment map but struggle with the low
      generalization ability in unseen houses (Yamauchi, 1997). Recent works (Anderson et al., 2018)

108
      have studied more forms of the visual navigation task, like PointNav (Wijmans et al., 2019), Im-
109   ageNav (Zhu et al., 2017), ObjectNav (Chaplot et al., 2020b), RoomNav (Wu et al., 2018), and
110   visual-language navigation (Huang et al., 2023). In this work, we study the ObjectNav task and
111   build the first end-to-end navigational agent based on LVLMs.
112
      Policy Learning of Object Navigation: Zhu et al. (2017) first proposed a reinforcement learning
113
      (RL) method that used ResNet (He et al., 2016) to encode images. Following them, the RL-based
114
      approaches have gained attention for tackling object navigation (Anderson et al., 2018; Batra et al.,
115   2020; Savva et al., 2017; Wahid et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018; Druon et al., 2020; Ehsani et al.,
116   2021). However, existing works (Ehsani et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2018) show that RL requires ex-
117   tensive reward shaping and is often too slow and ineffective. Imitation learning (Pomerleau, 1988;
118   Zhang et al., 2018) presents a compelling alternative to RL as it reframes the task as a supervised
119   learning problem. RT-1 and RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2022; 2023) scale up the multi-task data, focusing
120   on object manipulation. Wei et al. (2023) also propose a prompt-guided imitation learning method.
121   However, these methods face the tremendous cost of human demonstration. While SPOC (Ehsani
122   et al., 2023) attempts to resolve the issue, they trained a transformer-based agent from scratch, re-
123
      quiring a substantial amount of training data. Meanwhile, their study is still limited to four room
      types in ProcTHOR (Deitke et al., 2022). Compared with those works, we fine-tune pre-trained
124
      LVLM to navigate diverse scenes and construct shortest-path trajectories without expensive cost.
125
126   LLMs and VLMs for Visual Navigation: With the recent advancement, LLMs and VLMs (Achiam
127   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024) have emerged as the foundation for solving em-
128   bodied tasks (Pan et al., 2023; Majumdar et al., 2024). Researchers also explored their usage for
129
      object navigation. A few methods have employed contrastive VLMs (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al.,
      2022), as the visual encoders (Khandelwal et al., 2022; Majumdar et al., 2022) or object-grounding
130
      tools (Gadre et al., 2023; Dorbala et al., 2023). Yu et al. (2023) utilize LLMs as the planning back-
131
      bone for object navigation in a zero-shot manner. LLMs are used as high-level planners with a cap-
132   tioning model to provide perception. Following them, many improvements have been proposed (Cai
133   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023). However, a substantial domain
134   gap exists between navigation tasks and the LLM pre-training corpus (Lin et al., 2024). Meanwhile,
135   using LLMs for navigation might lose important visual details. In contrast, we tune our end-to-end
136   agent from an LVLM through imitation learning without those drawbacks.
137
      Embodied Environment: Embodied environments are the foundation of embodied research. Ex-
138   isting environments are typically crafted through the manual labor of 3D artists (Deitke et al., 2020;
139   Gan et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023a; Khanna et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2018), hard to
140   scale up. Many works construct scenes from 3D scans (Savva et al., 2019; Ramakrishnan et al., 2021;
141   Szot et al., 2021), but the scenes are not interactive. Meanwhile, ProcTHOR (Deitke et al., 2022)
142   proposes a procedural method but only focuses on four scene types. Recently, Yang et al. (2024) in-
143   troduced a language-guided system using GPT-4 to automatically generate customized scenes from
144   textual house descriptions, called Holodeck. In this work, we collect diverse house descriptions with
145   GPT-4, which are further used in Holodeck to collect houses.

150   find the target object belonging to a given category. The object category set is denoted by
151   C = {c0 , c1 , . . . , cm }, where m is the number of categories. The scenes can be described by
152   S = {s0 , s1 , . . . , sn }, and n is the total number of scenes. In each episode, the embodied agent
153   is initialized at a random position pi with rotation ri in a scene si . Then, the embodied agent is

156   each time step t, the embodied agent observes the environment and predicts the next action at . Fol-
157   lowing previous work (Yu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024), the observation comprises an RGB image
158
      of the egocentric view and the agent status (i.e., its position and rotation).

160   space, signified as A, covers four actions for navigation: M OVE A HEAD, ROTATE R IGHT, ROTA -

267
268   Figure 1 illustrates the datils of the proposed NAT VLM model. Here, we build an end-to-end
269   agent that imitates shortest path experts in AI2THOR. Our agent is tuned from the LVLM, Idefics
      2 (Laurençon et al., 2024), in an end-to-end manner. It takes an environment observation at each

      time step t and generates the next action at , following the same format as instruction tuning. Here,
271   we discuss the collection of instructions and corresponding responses.
272
273
      5.1   I NSTRUCTIONS C OMPILATION
274
275   We manually build instructions for each time step in an episode, which contains four parts. First, the
276   instruction provides a brief introduction to the object navigation task, such as possible actions and
277   step length. Then, we add the episode-specific information, including the target object, its positions,
278   the agent’s position and rotation, and visual observation. Third, we provide the agent’s positions
279   and actions for the recent M = 8 steps, along with the visual observations from the recent K = 4
280   steps, to help the agent make better decisions. Finally, the instruction asks the model to predict the
281   next action by considering the agent’s position and visual observation in accordance with the CoT
282
      explanation in the responses. We show the specific instruction template in Appendix B.1.
283
284   5.2   R ESPONSE C OLLECTION WITH C OT E XPLANATION T RACES
285
      We tune the LVLM to generate the next action when instructions are given. In the model, we
286
      encode actions as natural language and use the model’s generative capabilities to decode the next
287   action. Nonetheless, we found that merely requiring LVLMs to output the next action leads to
288   unsatisfactory results. The model only grasps the surface-level styles of the prompt but misses the
289   underlying rationales of object navigation.
290
      To enhance the agent’s understanding of navigation rationale, we manually build responses with
291
      CoT explanation traces during the navigation process. The structure of the responses covers three
292
      steps. In the first step, we have the agent compare its current position with the target and determine
293   whether it needs to move forward or take another action. After this, the agent is asked to check the
294   obstacles in the visual observation to see whether it needs to rotate. In the last step, the agent gives
295   the final decision based on the analyses in the first two steps. In contrast to writing explanations
296   with LLMs (Mitra et al., 2023), we manually write the prompt template of explanation traces for the
297   M OVE A HEAD and D ONE actions, leaving the position information to be filled in with coordinates
298   at each step. For the rotation actions (i.e., ROTATE R IGHT and ROTATE L EFT), we identify very
299   common scenarios during the navigation and design the explanation trace template for each of them.
300   Then, we introduce a few postprocessing steps, like action balancing and conflict filtering. We show
301
      concrete prompts and postprocessing details in Appendices B.2 and B.3, respectively.

411
412
      To better understand the role of CoT explanation traces in our agent NAT VLM, we conduct two
413
      ablation studies to analyze its contribution. First, we verify the efficacy of CoT explanation traces.
414   Then, we analyze different ways to compare the positions of the agent and target object. The results
415   of experiments are shown in Table 2.
416
417
      First, we remove the whole explanation traces in the response of the instruction tuning data (⋄ w/o
      ET). Thus, we fine-tune the agent to only generate the next action. From the result, we can find that
418
      the performance of our agent drastically drops. This verifies that explanation traces can help the
419
      LVLM to better understand the underlying rationales of object navigation. Subsequently, we further
420   enhance the agent’s input by providing the gold label indicating the positional difference between
421   the agent and the target object (⋄ w/o ET & w Gold). As shown in Table 2, we can observe that the
422   gold labels cannot help much as the performance only fluctuates somewhat.
423
      Then, we study the effects of the position comparison, a crucial component and the initial step in our
424
      explanation traces. The default prompt is to directly generate the position difference: “the difference
425
      to the target object is [position diff]” as shown in Table 10, where [position diff] is a placeholder.
426   First, we provide the global label of positional differences in the input instructions (⋄ w Gold Label).
427   The results in Table 2 show that providing global labels can improve the performance of our agent.
428   This observation suggests that our agent may occasionally compute the positional difference with
429   inaccuracies, as providing the gold label can lead to a performance increase. Next, we test the
430   difference-equation prompt (⋄ w Diff-EQ), where we fine-tune our agent to generate an equation for
431   computing the positional difference. However, writing the equation of computation only leads to

453   6.5   D ESIGN I NVESTIGATION
454
455   In this experiment, we thoroughly investigate a few design decisions regarding the image number
456   and step number of recent positions and actions. Besides the default prompt structure, we also
457   conduct experiments with the difference-equation prompt, which is introduced as “⋄ w Diff-EQ” in
458   the ablation study.
459   The default design provides NAT VLM with four images. In addition, we test the agent’s perfor-
460   mance using different numbers of input images, including 2, 6, and 8 images. The results are plotted
461   in Figure 4. First, we observe a decline in the agent’s performance when provided with only two im-
462   ages, showing that using fewer images leads to worse performance. For instance, our agent finishes
463   the navigation successfully only 50% of the time, underperforming the 4-image baseline. Moreover,
464   increasing the number of images to 6 or 8 does not result in further performance variations. Thus,
465
      we choose to provide our agent with four images for the tradeoff between accuracy and efficiency.
466   We also investigate the effect of recent positions and actions on navigation performance. By default,
467   we provide the agent with information about the recent 8 steps. Then, we test the performance when
468   we provided the positions and actions of 4, 12, and 16 steps. As illustrated in Table 3, a substantial
469   performance improvement is evident when increasing the number of steps from 4 to 8. However,
470   further increases in step count yield limited returns. Thus, we provide our agent with 8 steps.

527   7     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we study a new task of building navigational agents capable of generalizing to a wide
530   range of unseen objects in diverse scenes. For benchmarking, we collect a large-scale scene dataset,
531   D IV S CENE, featuring 81 different scene types. Then, we build the end-to-end agent, NAT VLM,
532
      which is fine-tuned from an LVLM. The agent is trained through imitation learning on the shortest
      paths generated by a BFS-based planner. With the planner, we collect over 22K episodes to form
533
      the D IV T RAJ dataset. In the training data, we also build CoT explanation traces to help the agent
534
      better grasp the underlying rationale of navigation. Extensive experiments show that our agent can
535   navigate to diverse objects in diverse scenes significantly better than baselines. We also conduct
536   various analyses to show the generalization ability of our agent. For future work, a promising
537   direction is to expand the memory capacity of LVLMs to enable navigation over longer horizons.

      A.4    BFS-BASED P LANNER
935
936   We use a BFS-based planner to find the shortest path from the initial position to a target point on
937   the grid map. Notice that the target object is not necessarily anchored to a point on the grid map for
938   realism. Thus, we find the grid point nearest to the target object as the destination of the navigation.
939
      The algorithm is shown in Algorithm 1, which is based on a priority queue. We design the BFS-
940
      based planner to pick the path with the fewest rotations to make it easier for LLMs to imitate. In
941   detail, we add more costs when rotation changes since the agent needs one more rotation action
942   before moving ahead, as shown at the 12th line in Algorithm 1.

       B.3   DATA P OSTPROCESSING
1116
       Directly using all actions in every trajectory to conduct imitation learning brings about an extremely
1117   imbalanced dataset. As shown in Table 11, 77.94% actions are M OVE A HEAD. Then we down-
1118   sampled M OVE A HEAD actions in the instruction dataset. We only retain 25% of the M OVE A HEAD
1119   actions resulting in a more balanced dataset.
1120
       Then, we also remove conflicting data. We find that steps from different trajectories within the same
1121
       house occasionally exhibit conflicting information. They have the exact same input information but
1122
       different action predictions. This happens when two overlapped trajectories diverge at some point
1123   due to different target objects. Those conflicting data can confuse the fine-tuned LVLM and lead to
1124   worse performance. Thus, we remove those conflicting data from our dataset.
