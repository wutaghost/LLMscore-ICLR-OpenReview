009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Imparting positional information has been a crucial component in Transformers
012            due to attention’s invariance to permutation. Methods that bias attention weights,
013            like Relative Positional Bias (RPB), have been preferred choice in more recent
014            transformer-based architectures for vision. In parallel, fused attention has become
015            the standard implementation for attention, largely thanks to open source solutions
016            such as Flash Attention and FMHA. However, it is not trivial to fuse explicit biasing
017            or masking of attention weights into a fused attention kernel without affecting its
018
               performance. In this scenario, position embeddings present themselves as a viable
               replacement for attention weight biases. Position embeddings are applied to the
019
               tokens directly, decoupled from the attention mechanism, thereby sidestepping the
020
               problems that arise with attention weight biases in fused kernels. In this work,
021            inspired by the booming LLM landscape, we analyze the applicability of Rotary
022            Position Embeddings (RoPE) as a replacement for RPBs in vision models. Unlike
023            RPB which explicitly biases attention weights, RoPE biases the dot product inputs
024            (query and key) directly and ahead of the attention operation. We empirically show
025            the prowess of RoPE over RPBs in terms of accuracy and speed. We study multiple
026            implementations of RoPE and show that it is sufficient to use only a fraction
027            of hidden dimensions for RoPE to achieve competitive performance. We also
028            develop a fast implementation for Axial RoPE. Together with the most performant
029
               fused attention implementations, and our fast RoPE implementation, we observe
               inference speedups compared to RPB with improved or similar accuracy. We
030
               foresee RoPE as a replacement for RPBs, paving the way for the widespread
031
               adoption of fused attention in transformer-based vision models.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Self attention and transformers Vaswani et al. (2017) have proven to be powerful tools for learning
037   from large amounts of unstructured data. The inception of Vision Transformers Dosovitskiy et al.
038   (2021), or ViTs, further propelled the use of transformers for image and video modalities. ViT follows
039   the isotropic architecture design of the Transformer, with a single downsampling step and identically
040   shaped encoder layers. On the other hand, hierarchical vision transformers started to incorporate the
041   CNN-like design Liu et al. (2021); Hassani et al. (2023); Hassani & Shi (2022); Ryali et al. (2023);
042   Fan et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022), downsampling the token space gradually and increasing the number
043   of attention heads. They also typically restrict their earlier attention layers to local or sparse patterns
044
      in order to avoid scaling issues resulting from performing global self attention.
045   The widespread usage of attention in language and vision inspired the creation of fused attention
046   implementations like Flash Attention Dao et al. (2022); Dao (2023) and FMHA Lefaudeux et al.
047   (2022). These implementations are functionally equivalent to a BMM-style implementation in a
048   deep learning framework like PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019), but provide significant improvements in
049   performance and activation memory footprint by keeping attention weights in fast local memory and
050
      fusing the second half of the operation, instead of storing attention weights as an additional activation
      to the relatively slower global memory, thereby reducing the number of expensive global memory
051
      accesses.
052

      invariant to the permutation of these tokens, additional positional biases are added to inject spatial

135
136   After the inception of the original Vision Transformer (ViT)Dosovitskiy et al. (2021), a considerable

      2.2   A DDING POSITIONAL BIASES TO VISION TRANSFORMERS
146
147   Transformers are primarily comprised of linear layers and attention, both of which are invariant to
148   token permutation, which naturally led to researchers introducing positional information into their
149   models. After the inception of the original transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017), many
150   new methods were introduced to add position information to transformers Ke et al. (2020); Huang
151   et al. (2020). ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) used absolute sinusoidal position embeddings used
152   in the original Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017). Relative Positional Biases (RPBs) Shaw et al.
153
      (2018) quickly became the de facto method used in a plethora of hierarchical vision transformers.
      More recently, Rotary Position Embeddings Su et al. (2021) became the norm in billion-parameter
154
      models like LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023a) and its derivatives Touvron et al. (2023b); Chiang et al.
155
      (2023). RoPE enjoys several benefits, like usability in long contexts, better training stability, and
156   decaying influence with increasing relative distance, to name a few. This makes RoPE a more scalable
157   alternative to RPBs. However, RoPE has not yet been as widely adopted in vision models, and we
158   aim to shed light in this direction through this work. We find that 2D RoPE Heo et al. (2024); Jeevan
159   & Sethi (2022) and AS2DRoPE Chu et al. (2024) are suboptimal extensions of original RoPE in the
160   context of vision transformers. We illustrate the difference between APE, RPB and RoPE in Figure 2.
161   We observe a clear trend; newer and larger models often prefer position embeddings over attention
      weight biases.

      2.3   F USED ATTENTION IN VISION TRANSFORMERS
194
195   For most of its history, dot-product attention, one of the primary operations in the Transformer,
196   has been implemented as back-to-back batched matrix multiplications (BMMs), now commonly
197   referred to as BMM-style attention. The first BMM computes the dot products between query and
198   key tokens, the softmax of which produces attention weights. Attention weights are then “applied”
199   to the values by taking their weighted average using the corresponding scores in the weight matrix
200   through the second BMM. At scale, this implementation can quickly become bounded by memory
201
      bandwidth and capacity. In the case of self attention, in addition to a quadratic time complexity,
      the memory footprint is also quadratic. This inspired “fused” attention implementations, the first
202
      practical example of which is Flash Attention Dao et al. (2022), which was later followed by Flash
203
      Attention V2 Dao (2023), FMHA Lefaudeux et al. (2022), and many more implementations. These
204   methods successfully fuse the two BMMs and the softmax into one kernel by using partial softmax
205   aggregation (since softmax involves a reduction), allowing them to scale to large sequence lengths.
206   Through doing so, they improve performance by significantly reducing accesses to global memory,
207   and instead keeping attention weights on much higher throughput local memory (shared memory).
208   In addition, the global memory footprint is also reduced significantly. As a consequence, fused
209   implementations are naturally less flexible in terms of allowing manipulation of attention weights.
210   This presents a challenge to positional biases, which, even when implemented, can noticeably impact
211   the performance of fused attention kernels.
212   In light of this, Rotary Position Embeddings are much better suited for fused attention because
213   they do not operate on attention weights directly. Instead, RoPE is applied to to the query and
214   key tensors prior to attention. This decoupling of positional biases and attention computation can
215   significantly improve model performance. This work exploits this fact further by developing a fast
      RoPE implementation suited specifically for vision models.

223   3.1   ATTENTION WEIGHT BIASES
224
225   Attention weight biases have become a common choice to add spatial biases in vision transformers.
226   Attention weight biases assign a bias value for every query-key pair in the attention map. Techniques
227
      like Relative Positional Biases (RPB) use the relative position of query and key tokens to add a
      specific bias term to them. Attention weight bias is added directly to the raw attention weights
228
      calculated by taking the dot product between queries and keys. Formally, in a feature map of the size
229
      (H, W ) we will have queries Q ∈ RHW ×d and keys K ∈ RHW ×d where d is the channel dimension.
230   A bias B ∈ RHW ×HW will be added to the attention weights as follows:
231

234   In the case of RPB, the bias B is parameterized as a smaller tensor but “viewed" as a tensor with
235   the same shape as the attention weights. Generally, attention weight biases in vision transformers
236   are learnable and thus need to be interpolated if the spatial resolution of the input image changes.
237   Moreover, they cause a bottleneck in the backward pass of any fused attention kernel. We delve into
238   practical implications of attention weight biases in Section 3.4.

342
343   Image modality is vastly different from language requires a separate treatment compared to language.
344   Modern generative models Karras et al. (2022); Nawrot et al. (2021); Crowson et al. (2024) use a

349    causal, and thus this approach makes sense in the language domain. However, an image does not

354   angles through the function fθ (j, d) = 10000−2j/d (j is the dimension index). We note that using an

358                                fθ (j, d) = exp (j ∗ (log(10π) − log(π))/d)                             (5)

361   RoPE to a fraction of hidden dimensions retains, or in some cases exceeds the performance of
362   applying RoPE to the full feature vector. We hypothesize that this is because applying RoPE to all
363   dimensions greatly mutilates the actual semantic information in the tokens. Additionally, applying
364   RoPE to a fraction of hidden dimensions is sufficient to impart the necessary positional information.
365   We divide the total dimensions by a positive integer divisor and apply RoPE to the resultant number

      3.4   H ARDWARE EFFICIENCY AND SCALABILITY OF RO PE VS RPB
379
380   As mentioned, Rotary Position Embeddings are applied to the query and key tensors, as opposed to
381   attention weights in the case of RPBs. Moreover, RoPE is a more complex operation: for each of
382   the two tensors, two elements are read from the feature vector, to which rotation is applied through
383   reading one more element from the θ tensor, before the elements are stored back into the original
384   tensor. This operation consists of multiple elementwise operations, all of which can grow quickly
385   into a memory-bandwidth-bound bottleneck in eager mode. On the other hand, RPB is typically only
386
      a single elementwise operation, leading one to think that in theory RPB is more efficient.
387   However, there are two key issues with RPB in terms of performance: 1. though it is a single
388   elementwise operation, we do not always have access to attention weights, a clear example of which
389   is fused implementations. For example, Flash Attention V2 Dao et al. (2022) does not support
390   attention biases at all, while FMHA Lefaudeux et al. (2022) only supports when they are fully
391   materialized in global memory, and padded to meet memory alignment requirements, which can
392
      undo some of the performance improvements, as the positional biases will consume the same amount
      of memory that attention weights would. 2. Loading fully materialized attention biases or masks
393
      in fused attention kernels burdens their scalability. Fused attention kernels are typically compute
394
      bound, whereas unfused implementations of attention are heavily bound by memory bandwidth.
395   Adding O(n2 ) loads into fused attention kernels can make them memory bandwidth bound again
396   at scale. FNA Hassani et al. (2024) on the other hand only supports RPB in the forward pass. 3.
397   RPB’s backward pass is typically not an elementwise operation. Local / sparse attention will turn
398   the RPB gradient into a reduction operation, which is considerably more difficult to performance
399   optimize compared to elementwise operations. While fusing an elementwise operation into complex
400   fused attention kernels such as Flash Attention V2 Dao (2023) is relatively trivial, fusing reduction
401   operations into any kernel, especially fused attention kernels, is typically non-trivial. RoPE however
402   is an elementwise operation both in the forward and in the backward pass. In fact, the only difference
403
      between the forward and backward pass is the sign of one element, which means:
404
             • RoPE can be applied efficiently in both the forward pass and backward pass,
405
406          • RoPE’s forward and backward pass implementations are almost identical,
407          • RoPE is completely agnostic to the attention operator, making it compatible with all imple-
408            mentations out of the box.
409
410
      Having said that, a vanilla PyTorch implementation made RoPE slower than RPB, even when using
      powerful fused attention implementations. This is primarily because multiple elementwise oper-
411
      ations used in RoPE are not automatically fused into a single one, and while using tools such as
412
      torch.compile() does exactly that, they simply do not improve performance enough to justify
413   switching from RPB. This motivated us to develop fast-rope, a fused CUDA implementation
414   performing Axial RoPE on feature tensors. The implementation follows HDiT’s specifications Crow-
415   son et al. (2024): it can read operands of mixed precision levels, but computation is done strictly in
416   higher precision. This allows us to perform the operation in place on the original tensor, without any
417   extra type cast operations or kernel calls. We plan to open-source our library and release a Python
418   package which will make using our library as easy as performing one function call.

511   4.3       A NALYZING DESIGN DECISIONS IN RO PE
512
513   We will now analyze the differences between the two implementations mentioned above – 2D RoPE
514   and Axial RoPE. We will consider the choices of krope and whether all heads use the same rotation
515
      angles. Through this analysis, we aim to empirically study the effect of these hyperparameters on
      RoPE and its multi-resolution performance on a wide range of testing resolutions. We perform all our
516
      ablations on ViT-Small trained with 224 px resolution.
517
518   Table 4 presents the performance when all heads share the same rotation angles. We make a striking
519   observation – 2D RoPE with a high value of krope often performs best. In the case where training
520   and testing resolution are the same (224 px), we observe Axial RoPE has its best performance at
521
      krope = 8 and is roughly the same for other values of krope . We observe similar effects for resolutions
      relatively closer to training resolution, specifically 192 and 256 px. Moving to Table 5, we report
522
      the numbers in the case where all heads have different rotation angles. In most cases, we observe
523
      Axial RoPE to outperform 2D RoPE. We speculate that this is due to the nature of the angle generator
524   function, and we delve into its specifics in Appendix C. Both of these ablations suggest that only
525   a fraction of hidden dimensions are enough to impart positional information using RoPE. In both
526   shared and non-shared angles, we observe that Axial RoPE with a high value of krope is superior to
527   2D RoPE for most inference resolutions, including the training resolution itself.

      5        C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we propose using Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) instead of Relative Positional
532
      Biases (RPB), in order to achieve better performance and better accuracy. We presented empirical
533
      evidence and analysis to support this proposition. Further, to accelerate RoPE compared to RPB,
534   we developed fast-rope, a fast, CUDA-based implementation of RoPE, and showed its speedup
535   through careful model-level benchmarking. We conducted empirical analysis on two RoPE methods:
536   Axial RoPE and 2D RoPE. We introduced a new hyperparameter, krope , to control the fraction of
537   hidden dimensions used in RoPE for both implementations, and observed that applying RoPE to
538   only half, 1/4th, or even 1/8th of the hidden dimensions is enough to reasonably introduce positional
539   information and achieve competitive accuracy. As a result of these findings, we foresee widespread
      adoption of RoPE in isotropic and hierarchical vision transformers in the near future.

783   From these observations, we make two hypotheses: first, higher rotation angles imply a stronger

785   is reduced to 8 or 16, but where the angles of rotation are still large in magnitude. Second, in the
786   cases where testing resolution is higher than training resolution, we observe that Axial RoPE is
787   roughly equal or surpasses 2D RoPE. We attribute this to the position co-ordinates assigned in Axial
788   RoPE. Specifically, we speculate that interpolating indices to be between (−1, 1), coupled with the
789   repeating, high-magnitude angles of rotation results in a better encoding of positions in the tokens for
790
      higher test-time resolutions.
