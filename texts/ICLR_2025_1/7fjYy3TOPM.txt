009                                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                 Detecting when an object detector predicts wrongly, for example, misrecognizing
012                 an out-of-distribution (ODD) unseen object as a seen one, is crucial to ensure the
013                 model’s trustworthiness. Modern object detectors are known to be overly confident,
014                 making it hard to rely solely on their responses to detect error cases. We therefore
015                 investigate the use of an auxiliary model for the rescue. Specifically, we leverage an
016
                    off-the-shelf text-to-image generative model (e.g., Stable Diffusion), whose training
                    objective is different from discriminative models. We surmise such a discrepancy
017
                    would allow us to use their inconsistency as an error indicator. Concretely, given
018
                    a detected object box and the predicted class label, we perform class-conditioned
019                 inpainting on the box-removed image. When the predicted object label is incorrect,
020                 the inpainted image is doomed to deviate from the original one, making the recon-
021                 struction error an effective recognition error indicator, especially on misclassified
022                 OOD samples. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our approach consistently
023                 outperforms prior zero-shot and non-zero-shot OOD detection approaches.

      Object detection systems have made significant advancements in recent years, and are widely applied
042   in our lives both in online and offline settings, including environmental science (Beery et al., 2019),
043   manufacturing (Ahmad & Rahimi, 2022), or even healthcare (Ragab et al., 2024). It is thus crucial
044   to ensure the reliability and robustness of these systems. However, one typical error is that the
045   detector sometimes misidentifies something as an object from its predefined classes. While detector
046   confidence is commonly used for filtering out erroneous detections, overconfidence has been observed
047   across many architectures and domains (Pathiraja et al., 2023). This highlights the challenge of
048   relying solely on the internal responses of an object detector’s to identify these errors.
049   Various existing works address this challenge during training. Munir et al. (2022); Pathiraja et al.
050   (2023) introduce auxiliary losses to regularize confidence calibration. Du et al. (2022b;a) approach
051   the problem as an out-of-distribution (OOD) detection task and learn better detector features that are
052   more compatible with classic OOD detection methods. Although these methods are effective, many
053   practitioners use pre-trained, publicly available models for their applications and are unable to retrain
      the detectors to incorporate these techniques.

054
      In this paper, we explore the use of additional pre-trained models as auxiliary tools to address these
055   challenges. In particular, recent text-conditioned generative models (Rombach et al., 2021; Saharia
056   et al., 2022) demonstrate strong language understanding and image synthesis capabilities and show
057   potential for augmenting various discriminative tasks (Li et al., 2023a). We build upon such insights
058   and leverage these models as an additional source of information to help identify erroneous detections.
059
      To this end, we propose ZeRo-shot OOD CONtextual INpainting (RONIN), as illustrated in Figure 1.
060
      RONIN operates upon detected bounding boxes and predicted class labels from a detector. For each
061   class in the detector’s vocabulary, RONIN masks out all detected instances in an image and performs
062   inpainting conditioned on the class name. It then compares the similarities between the original
063   detected objects and their inpainted counterparts, as well as both objects’ similarities to the class
064   labels. Typically, erroneous detections which by definition are incompatible with class labels, will
065   look dissimilar from the inpainted objects. Correct detections, however, will share similar semantics
066   with their inpainted ones. This makes OOD objects easy to distinguish. We evaluate RONIN on
067   real-world benchmark datasets and show that it consistently outperforms existing methods. We also
068   provide in-depth analysis and visualizations to further validate the effectiveness of RONIN.
069
070   Remark. In this paper, we mainly focus on the error cases of object detection when wrongly
071   recognizing unseen OOD objects as seen ID ones, i.e., object-level OOD detection. However, with the
072   large-scale off-the-shelf generative model, RONIN is general enough to also perform the correction
073   when encountering a wrong prediction on a seen ID object, such as “cat instead of dog”. While
074
      in our experiment we mainly explore the OOD detection task because it is considered the harder
      case, we conducted two preliminary studies on: (i) ID misclassification (Section 6.1), where the
075
      inpainting process helps RONIN to correct the false prediction encountered on ID samples; and
076
      (ii) near-OOD detection (Section 6.2), where pre-anticipate closest ID labels as specific inpainting
077   condition support RONIN to tackle near-OOD problems. We hope that this study opens the potential
078   for future studies on both OOD detection and misclassification from different perspectives.

083   Out-of-distribution Detection. The goal of the out-of-distribution (OOD) detection task is to
084   determine whether a given sample belongs to a certain distribution. It has been widely studied at the
085   image level, where a whole image is treated as a sample. Common approaches include leveraging
086   classifier-specific information such as confidence scores (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Lee et al.,
087   2017; Liang et al., 2018; DeVries & Taylor, 2018; Hsu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Wei et al.,
088
      2022) or learned features (Lee et al., 2018; Denouden et al., 2018; Tack et al., 2020; Sehwag et al.,
      2021; Xiao et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022), or directly modeling an image distribution using generative
089
      models (Ren et al., 2019; Serrà et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020; Schlegl et al., 2017; Zong et al., 2018;
090
      Graham et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). Recent works (Ming et al., 2022; Esmaeilpour
091   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) have also explored using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to identify OOD
092   examples in a zero-shot manner, bypassing the need to learn from in-domain data explicitly.
093
094
      OOD detection can also be extended to the object-level, where objects within an image are treated
      as individual samples. Most existing research in this setting focuses on training-time interventions.
095
      For instance, Du et al. (2022b;a) improve detector features to make them more separable between
096
      in-distribution (ID) and OOD data; Wilson et al. (2023) uses adversarial examples to train an MLP
097   for classifying ID and OOD instances. In contrast, our approach RONIN explores the object-level
098   setting through post-hoc interventions instead.
099
100
      Text-to-Image Generative Models. Recent advances in generative modeling have made large-scale
101
      text-to-image models widely available (Rombach et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
102   2022). These models exhibit a deep understanding of language and are highly effective at generating
103   or editing high-quality images from diverse prompts, offering a promising approach to data synthesis
104   across various tasks. Furthermore, recent works have shown that these models can also enhance
105   discriminative tasks. For instance, Li et al. (2023a); Jaini et al. (2024) demonstrate that Stable
106   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) can function as an effective zero-shot classifier, achieving accuracy
107   comparable to or surpassing CLIP and various trained discriminative classifiers. There has also been
      recent progress in applying text-conditioned diffusion models for image-level OOD detection (Du

108
      et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024). Unlike these works, RONIN focuses on exploring
109   the use of such models for object-level tasks.

112   3    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
113
114   We address the task of object-level out-of-distribution (OOD) detection. Specifically, given an object
115   detector trained to detect a pre-defined set of categories (e.g., different kinds of vehicles), we aim to
116   identify the error cases when it wrongly recognizes a novel object (e.g., a wild animal) as one of the
117
      pre-defined categories and detects it. We refer to the pre-defined set of categories as the in-distribution
      (ID) classes, following Du et al. (2022b); the novel categories as the OOD classes.
118
119   Formally, given an image x and an object detector f (.; θ) trained for the ID classes Y in , f (x; θ)
120   outputs a list of bounding boxes b = {b1 , b2 , . . . , bn } and their associated ID class labels ŷ = {ŷ1 ,
121   ŷ2 ,. . . , ŷn }, where ŷi ∈ Y in . Object-level OOD detection is then formulated as a binary classification
122   problem, classifying whether the object within bi truly belongs to ID classes. Typically, one would
123
      develop a scoring function g such that given a bounding box and its predicted label (b, ŷ), g gives a
      higher score g(b, ŷ) if b outlines an ID object and a lower score if it outlines an OOD object.
124
125   Existing works (Du et al., 2022b;a) proposed a specific training process for the object detector so
126   that its responses (e.g., feature vector of each bounding box) would better distinguish between ID
127   and OOD classes. However, doing so requires re-training the object detector, assuming access to the
128   original training data and making it infeasible to off-the-shelf object detectors.
129
130   Aim. To address this limitation, we aim to design an OOD detection mechanism that is post-hoc,
131   without the need to modify (e.g., fine-tune) the pre-trained object detector, and zero-shot (Ming
132   et al., 2022), without the need to access the original training data or any ID-class data. This sharply
133
      contrasts several prior post-hoc methods that need ID-class data (Ren et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020).
134
135   Approach. The emergence of vision-language foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al.,
136
      2022; Liu et al., 2024a) trained on abundant image and free-form text pairs has gradually removed
      the boundary between the closed-set and open-set settings Li et al. (2023a). For example, while not
137
      perfect, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) can match an image with unbounded concepts. Such a zero-shot
138
      capability has been leveraged in prior work to detect OOD samples given a set of ID concepts (Ming
139   et al., 2022; Esmaeilpour et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). In this work, we leverage another kind of
140   foundation model, text-to-image generative models (Rombach et al., 2021), capable of generating
141   images given free-form texts. While not designed for object detection and optimized for the ID data,
142   we surmise their built-in, generic capability would facilitate object-level OOD detection.

      4.2   O BJECT- WISE VS . C LASS - WISE I NPAINTING
197
198   For the masking and inpainting process, two natural approaches can be considered: the “object-wise”
199   strategy and the “class-wise” strategy. The object-wise strategy involves inpainting each object
200   independently. This approach allows for detailed synthesis, potentially leading to more accurate
201   recognition of out-of-distribution (OOD) objects because each object’s unique features are preserved
202   and refined individually. Consequently, this can enhance the overall performance of the OOD object
203   detection task, especially in scenarios where detailed object reconstruction is crucial.
204   On the other hand, the class-wise strategy involves inpainting all objects that share the same predicted
205   class label by taking the union of all bounding boxes bi with the same ŷ and performing inpainting
206   for the entire class at once. This approach is significantly more efficient, particularly in images
207   containing numerous small and distinct objects of the same class, such as multiple cars in an urban
208   street scene. The efficiency gains are due to the batch-inpainting nature of this method, where the
209   computational cost of inpainting scales with |Y in |— the number of distinct classes — rather than the
210   total number of individual objects.
211   In our ablation study in Section 5.3, we analyze the performance of both strategies under various
212   settings. While the object-wise strategy provides a higher level of detail and accuracy in recognizing
213   OOD objects due to its individualized approach, the class-wise strategy offers substantial efficiency
214   improvements, making it more practical for scenes with many objects belonging to the same class.
215   This trade-off between accuracy and efficiency is critical in determining the appropriate inpainting
      strategy based on the specific requirements and constraints of the application at hand.

274   Datasets. We use Pascal-VOC (Everingham et al., 2010) and Berkerly DeepDrive (BDD-100k) (Yu
275   et al., 2020) as ID data, and MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014) and OpenImages (Kuznetsova et al., 2020)
276   as OOD data. We follow the splits from Du et al. (2022a;b), where overlapping categories in the ID
277   and OOD datasets are removed. We select a subset of 200 images from the test set of BDD-100k and
278   400 each from the test sets of Pascal-VOC, MS-COCO, and OpenImages. Data processing details
279   can be found in Appendix A.
280
281   Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate the OOD detection performance using the area under the Receiver
282   Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) and the false positive rate of OOD objects at 95% true
283
      positive rate of in-distribution objects (FPR@95). We additionally report the mean Average Precision
      (mAP) with IoU=0.5:0.95 on the in-distribution data to validate the quality of the object detectors.
284
285
286
      Baselines. We consider two categories of zero-shot baselines: (1) Discriminative approaches:
      MCM (Ming et al., 2022), CLIP-based ODIN (Liang et al., 2018), CLIP-based Energy Score (Liu
287
      et al., 2020), and CLIPN (Wang et al., 2023). (2) Alternative generative approaches: first generating
288
      synthetic data and then applying standard OOD detection methods such as Mahalanobis (Xiao et al.,
289   2021) and KNN (Sun et al., 2022). Additionally, for a comprehensive comparison, we also include
290   two representative training-based object-level methods VOS (Du et al., 2022b) and SIREN (Du et al.,
291   2022a), resulting in a total of seven baselines. Implementation details can be found in Appendix B.
292
293   Implementation Details. For RONIN, we perform class-wise inpainting with Stable Diffusion
294   2 Inpainting (Rombach et al., 2021) with 20 steps. Each object is masked with a center mask
295   covering 0.9 of the height and width of its original bounding box. Object-label similarities are
296   determined by the distance between OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021) features, and object-object
297   similarities by the SimCLRv2 (Chen et al., 2020) features. The triplet OOD score is calculated with
298   α = 2 and β = 1. For both RONIN and the baselines, the detected objects are obtained from the
299   Deformable-DETR (Zhu et al., 2020) detectors from Du et al. (2022a) to ensure comparability.

415   5.4   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
416
417
      Success Cases Analysis. Figure 4 showcases the performances of RONIN in the PascalVOC
      as ID settings. In summary, RONIN effectively differentiates between ID and OOD by closely
418
      replicating the original objects in ID inpainting, resulting in high similarity scores, while creating
419
      distinct differences in OOD inpainting, which yields low scores. RONIN also performed coherently
420   even with cluttered or occluded objects, such as a group of cyclists or elephants, or even drawings,
421   showing the effectiveness of our approach. We provide more visualizations in Appendix F.
422
423   Failure Cases Analysis. Under certain challenging scenarios, RONIN may perform suboptimally.
424   For instance, when the predicted box is too small, the inpainting model may fail to synthesize the
425   object, leading to low similarities for ID cases. This can be mitigated by enhanced training of the
426   inpainting model (Wei et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022). Additionally, when OOD objects highly
427   resemble ID categories both visually and semantically (e.g. “zebra vs. horse”), the inpainting model
428   sometimes produces higher similarity reconstructions for OOD objects. This can potentially be
429   alleviated with more precise prompting in the following sessions.

460    6.1    RONIN FOR M ISCLASSIFIED I N - DISTRIBUTION D ETECTION
461
462    In the first study, we investigate how RONIN also corrects errors in the in-distribution (ID) data
463    when the object detection model makes a misclassification. The intuition behind this is that if a
464    misclassification occurs, RONIN can perform similarly well as it does in OOD detection. This
465    is because misclassified objects will also have low triplet scores, akin to OOD objects. Since
466
       misclassifications are rare in our experiments, we simulate these scenarios by replacing the correct
       label, i.e., the label with the highest score from the detection model, with the second-highest score
467
       label. For instance, in PascalVOC (ID data), typical simulated misclassifications include cases like
468
      “cat vs. dog” or “motorcycle vs. bicycle”, which exhibit both visual and semantic similarities.

490   6.2   RONIN FOR N EAR -OOD D ETECTION
491
492
      The robustness of off-the-shelf generative models can support RONIN to effectively handle near-
      OOD detection, a challenging task for standard OOD detection algorithms, by leveraging a strong
493
      discriminative model to control the generative process based on specific instructions. For instance,
494
      consider an input photo containing an OOD object, such as a “zebra,” misclassified as the ID label
495   “horse” (Figure 6, left column). Due to the similarity between these classes, directly instructing
496   RONIN to inpaint a horse might result in a modified image that closely resembles the original
497   zebra, rendering near-OOD detection ineffective (Figure 6, middle column). To address this, we
498   pre-anticipate near-OOD classes for the given ID label as exclusions. For example, if the ID class
499   is “horse,” we can roughly identify near-OOD classes like “zebra” or “donkey” and condition the
500   diffusion model to generate a “horse” that distinctly differs from these near-OOD objects (Figure 6,
501   right column). This approach ensures inpainted results better align with the predicted ID label,
502   enhancing the robustness of our framework to near-OOD scenarios.

      7     D ISCUSSION
527
528   Overally, RONIN demonstrates strong performance in identifying erroneous detections across diverse
529   scenarios. Even with open-vocabulary object detectors, which have the ability to recognize any kind
530   of object given a specific prompt, close-set definitions of the target objects are still required, allowing
531
      RONIN to identify out-of-vocabulary objects or potentially correct the erroneous predictions flexibly.
      Admittedly, since current leading text-conditioned inpainting models are diffusion-based, this creates
532
      challenges in using RONIN in applications where real-time detection is critical. While low denoising-
533
      step schedules can accelerate the time performance, they often compromise both the inpainting quality
534   and OOD detection performances of RONIN. Nevertheless, for those domains, RONIN remains
535   valuable for retrospectively analyzing the failure modes of deployed models. The advancements in
536   diffusion model acceleration (Song et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) and the development of inpainting
537   models based on other approaches (e.g. GAN) also show great promise for enabling RONIN in
538   real-time scenarios. Finally, there is a wide range of applications that prioritize accuracy over speed
539   – including fields like ecosystem conservation and manufacturing and tasks like automated dataset
      labeling – where RONIN excels.

      8   C ONCLUSION
541
542
      In this paper, we address the task of object-level out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, aiming to
543   identify cases where an object detector incorrectly detects an OOD object belonging to its known cate-
544   gories. We propose RONIN, a novel method that utilizes pre-trained text-to-image generative models
545   for this task without the need to learn from dedicated in-distribution data. Our experiments show
546   that RONIN consistently attains strong and competitive performance across diverse settings, with
547   promising potential for extension to adjacent tasks such as misclassified in-distribution identification.

796   Following Du et al. (2022b;a), we used PascalVOC (Everingham et al., 2010) with 20 categories1 and
797   BDD100k (Yu et al., 2020) with 10 categories2 . We utilized two subsets of MS-COCO (Lin et al.,
798   2014) and OpenImages (Kuznetsova et al., 2020) as the OOD sets, where all images containing ID
799   categories are removed to ensure the disjoint-category requirement. Additionally, for some British
800   labels such as “Aeroplane” or “Couch”, we replaced them with the US counterparts such as “Airplane”
801   or “Sofa” to optimize the usage of CLIP visual-textual representations.
802   Since our method is zero-shot and does not require training, all the experiments are conducted in
803   subsets taken from the testing set of VOC, BDD, COCO, and OpenImages. To ensure intensive,
804   fairness, and effective evaluations, as well as the balance between ID and OOD objects, we sample
805   a subset of 200 images for BDD-100k testing set and 400 images for PascalVOC, MS-COCO, and
806   OpenImages, as BDD-100k often contain a significantly larger amount of objects per image.

848   D    I NPAINTING PERFORMANCES WITH DIFFERENT MASKING RATIOS
849
850   Figure 7 illustrates the performances of RONIN with different ratios between the foreground mask
851   and the semantic background within the predicted box, with the foreground mask covered at 25%,
852   56%, 81%, and 100% of the bounding box respectively, leaving the remained semantic background
853   ranging from 75% to 0%. As presented in Section 4.1 the foreground m determines the region
854   where the diffusion model DDPM performs inpainting, and the background s provides contextual
855
      information to guide the process consistently. On the one hand, a very small region of m prevents
      DDPM from synthesizing objects globally, whereas a redundant s provides so much information to
856
      result in very similar inpainted objects compared to the original ones, both visually and semantically.
857
      This led to high similarity scores on both ID and OOD objects, ineffectively separating them. On
858   the other hand, very high m allow DDPM to freely synthesize any type of object conditioned by
859   the label, without semantic information from s to guide the outcome appearance. As a result, the
860   synthesized objects are highly dissimilar to their original counterparts, both in ID and OOD, leading
861   to poor similarity scores. With a reasonable masked region of 81%, equivalent to the side ratio of
862   r = 0.9, RONIN both attained the similar shape and appearance of the original objects for high
863   scores in ID, but synthesized objects differently based on predicted labels for low similarity scores on
      OOD, thus effectively recognizing OOD objects.
