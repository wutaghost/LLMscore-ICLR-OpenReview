009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong reasoning capabilities
012            and possess extensive common knowledge. This enables them to adapt to a variety
013            of complex tasks in a zero-shot manner, including functioning as controllers to
014            manipulate automated systems and produce executable action sequences. However,
015            a significant challenge in the existing framework is the misalignment between
016            the general pre-trained LLM and the action space of specific control tasks. This
017            misalignment necessitates extensive efforts in designing task-specific prompts,
018
               which are less generalizable and do not ensure consistent output when prompting a
               pre-trained LLM to generate the desired action sequences. To address this issue,
019
               we propose a novel solution, ActionVerse, which encodes action candidates into
020
               a series of modality tokens, coupled with an efficient alignment technique to
021            synchronize the action tokens with the LLM’s language space. By leveraging this
022            approach, the proposed ActionVerse successfully transforms a chat-based multi-
023            modal LLM into a general action executor capable of handling tasks requiring
024            step-by-step execution of various actions. Experiments on several sequential action
025            tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2018; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Radford et al., 2019;
031   Brown et al., 2020) trained on vast amounts of textual data have demonstrated strong reasoning
032   capabilities for completing complex tasks based on human instructions. These emerging capabilities
033
      can be effectively generalized to a variety of text-based tasks in a zero-shot or few-shot manner,
      significantly promoting the application of LLMs in various domains, such as chatbots (OpenAI,
034
      2023), code copilots (Li et al., 2022), and more.
035
036   Recently, benefiting from Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques (Hu et al., 2022;
037   Dettmers et al., 2023), it has become computationally affordable to adapt text-based LLMs to a
038   multi-modal scenario by tuning the foundational LLM with a few aligning projection layers as well as
039
      rank decomposition matrices. This unlocks the potential for text-based LLMs to perceive and generate
      non-textual content with minimal tuning (Liu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
040
      2023). The ModaVerse (Wang et al., 2024) is one of the representative examples. Equipped with the
041
      capabilities of reading, seeing, and hearing, these Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
042   can be applied to broader scenarios like controlling smart devices that pure text-based models cannot
043   handle, such as navigating a robot based on camera inputs (Zhou et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b),
044   automatically controlling smartphone apps (Zhang et al., 2023a; You et al., 2024), and manipulating
045   robots (Zhang et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023b; 2024). These methods leverage the extensive common
046   knowledge embedded within MLLMs to analyze multi-modal inputs, such as a robot’s camera feed,
047   GPS location signals, and mobile phone screenshots, to comprehend the environment. Based on
048   this understanding, they generate a set of well-reasoned operational steps that enable the devices
049   to execute actions and achieve the desired objectives. For instance, NavGPT (Zhou et al., 2024)
050
      proposes a zero-shot pipeline that employs ChatGPT for explicit reasoning and generating navigation
      routes for robots, while AppAgent (Zhang et al., 2023a) introduces a framework that relies on GPT-4v
051
      to operate smartphone applications.
052
053   However, the fact that the foundational MLLMs were trained for general purposes results in a
      redundant tokenizer, creating an inevitable gap between the text space and the desired action space of


                                                         1

054
      the controlling tasks. Therefore, current frameworks typically design complicated system prompts
055   with detailed instructions and sample outputs to guide the MLLMs in generating the expected format
056   of outputs. Despite these efforts, several issues remain with significant room for improvement: (1) The
057   nature of the tokenization process in MLLMs means that the expected output format may not always
058   be achieved, especially when complex non-text action indices are involved. For example, producing
059   an action command that asks the robot to move to a viewpoint indexed by ‘b0a6cf6e9b904324...’
060   can be problematic sometimes. (2) The extremely long system prompts are difficult to design and
061   maintain, often requiring expert manual effort, and they significantly increase the number of input
062   tokens during the inference stage, thereby considerably raising computation costs. (3) Such designs
063
      make it difficult to utilize the history of action sequences to refine decision-making and introduce a
      self-correction mechanism.
064
065   Recently, some works (Chen et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2023; You et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) have
066   explored incorporating object-level visual tokens with MLLMs to enhance visual grounding and
067   referring capabilities. Specifically, these methods typically follow a spatial-wise sampling process
068   to extract candidate object areas in the input images. The image features of each candidate region
069
      are encoded as visual tokens, which are then inserted into the input tokenized sequence for MLLMs,
      enabling MLLMs to refer to certain areas by providing the coordinates on the images. Inspired by
070
      this, to address the aforementioned issues in current MLLMs in generating action-oriented outputs,
071
      we designed an action encoder to explicitly tokenize the action candidates into a series of action
072   tokens, which are then extended into the input sequence of the MLLMs. To align this action
073   feature space with the foundational LLM’s language space, we conduct instruction tuning with a few
074   projection layers and PEFT techniques, thus enabling efficient adaption to turn chat-based MLLMs
075   into action executors. Within this design, the proposed framework is no longer limited to using
076   textual descriptions to represent the action indices but uses a series of unified action tokens such as
077   <ACTION1>, <ACTION2> to represent the action candidate with rich features. This approach is
078   easily adaptable to more generalized scenarios requiring MLLMs to produce action operation orders,
079   avoiding the misalignment between the text space of MLLMs and the action space of controlling tasks.
080
      It also eliminates the need for overly complicated system prompts and benefits from the natural ability
      of chat-based LLMs to use context information to refine the decision-making process. Therefore, to
081
      train the proposed method, we introduce a pipeline to build a conversation-like instruction-following
082
      training set, which also incorporates a data augmentation method that introduces disturbances into the
083   routes to facilitate an implicit self-correction capability. In summary, this paper presents an efficient
084   paradigm to adapt chat-based MLLMs to generate executable action sequences for controlling smart
085   agents. Specifically, the contribution of this work is threefold:
086
087   • We propose ActionVerse, a general framework designed to adapt MLLMs to produce executable
088     action sequences for automatic systems. ActionVerse consists of two core components: an action
089     encoder that tokenizes the action candidates into a series of action tokens, and a projection module
090     that translates these tokens into the language space interpretable by the foundational LLM. With
091
        proper instructions, ActionVerse can select and produce the appropriate action sequence for specific
        controlling tasks.
092
093   • Controlling tasks typically expect a sequence containing multi-step actions to guide the smart
094     agents to fulfill specific goals, matching the nature of multi-round conversations in chat-based
095     LLMs. Therefore, we proposed a conversation-like instruction-following tuning procedure for
096
        training the ActionVerse, which incorporates an implicit self-correction mechanism to rectify
        possible wrong movements executed in previous steps.
097
098   • To evaluate the effectiveness of ActionVerse, we conduct experiments on two controlling tasks:
099     robot navigation (Anderson et al., 2018) and website navigation (Chen et al., 2024b). Experimental
100
        results demonstrate that within minimal fine-tuning, ActionVerse achieves performance comparable
        to state-of-the-art methods.

105   Action-oriented LLMs. The strong reasoning abilities of LLMs enable them to perform complex
106   tasks based on human instructions, making them ideal for embodied tasks (Zeng et al., 2023) that
107   require reasoning and common knowledge (Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2023;
      Mu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023b). In these tasks, LLMs act as agents,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      understanding tasks and environments to plan actions towards a goal, enabling applications like
109   robotic manipulation and smartphone control. For example, RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023) extends LLMs
110   to robotic control but requires extensive trajectory data, while LaMo (Shi et al., 2023) handles simpler
111   scenarios like Atari and MuJoCo using time-series data. AppAgent (Zhang et al., 2023a) leverages
112   ChatGPT-4v for smartphone app control, and NavGPT (Zhou et al., 2024) uses ChatGPT to plan
113   robot navigation routes. LLaRP (Szot et al., 2023) applies LLMs to online reinforcement learning,
114   and RoboFlamingo (Li et al., 2023) frames video observations in a POMDP setting.
115   Most methods, however, depend on commercial LLM APIs (OpenAI, 2023), like ChatGPT or GPT-
116   4v/o, to produce action-oriented text. While avoiding LLM training, this introduces challenges, such
117   as complex system prompts. For instance, NavGPT (Zhou et al., 2024) and AppAgent (Zhang et al.,
118   2023a) use prompts up to three thousand words, which are hard to maintain as APIs evolve and
119   incur high costs due to expensive API calls. Additionally, requiring internet access limits real-time
120   applications in robotics. To mitigate these issues, some works (Lu et al., 2023) train or fine-tune
121   LLMs for action outputs, like Unified-IO 2 (Lu et al., 2023), which manipulates robotic arms using
122   over a million trajectories. However, these methods demand substantial resources, highlighting the
123
      need for more efficient solutions that balance LLM-based agents and training from scratch.
124   Multi-modal LLMs. MLLMs (Zhang et al., 2024a) extend text-based LLMs to perceive and generate
125   non-text content. Mainstream MLLMs can be categorized into two types: native multi-modal
126   LLMs (Team, 2024; Lu et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024) and adapted multi-
127   modal LLMs (Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024). Native MLLMs
128   are inherently designed to handle diverse data modalities, such as original image pixels and audio
129
      signals. This design preserves high-fidelity details of multi-modal inputs, allowing for more natural
      and efficient multi-modal understanding and generation. However, it requires extensive training
130
      resources, including massive paired multi-modal data and significant computational power. For
131
      instance, Unified-IO 2 (Lu et al., 2023) pre-trains an auto-regressive MLLM that can interpret and
132   generate multiple modalities involving vision, language, audio, and action using an extremely large
133   training set containing over 1 trillion text tokens, 1 billion image-text pairs, and millions of videos,
134   3D assets, and agent trajectories. This pre-trained foundation model is then further fine-tuned using
135   an instruction-following set that combines more than 120 datasets covering 220 tasks across vision,
136   language, audio, and action. Similarly, Chameleon (Team, 2024) also pre-trains on an extensive
137   training set containing nearly 3 trillion text-only tokens, 1.5 trillion text-image tokens, and 400
138   billion tokens of interleaved text-image data. On the other hand, instead of training an MLLM
139   from scratch with a mixture of multi-modal data, adapted MLLMs aim at adapting a text-based
140
      foundational LLM into a multi-modal scenario. For example, Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) follows
      a late-fusion design, where the multi-modal inputs are encoded by a series of separate encoders before
141
      being combined together. Similarly, MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) employs a Q-former to encode
142
      images and then translates the visual features to the LLM space through a linear projection layer.
143   NextGPT (Wu et al., 2023) and ModaVerse (Wang et al., 2024) further extend this paradigm to the
144   output space, enabling any-to-any modality transformation. Such designs enable text-based LLMs to
145   interpret and generate non-text content with minimal fine-tuning on limited datasets, offering a more
146   efficient solution for MLLMs. More recently, fine-grained comprehension and open-world referring
147   and grounding have become important for evaluating the reasoning capabilities of visual-focused
148   MLLMs. These methods (Peng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024; You et al., 2023; Bai
149   et al., 2023) are designed to comprehend and answer questions related to specific regions of interest in
150   images using user input bounding boxes or hybrid region representations, and refer to these areas by
151
      outputting the coordinates of specific regions. For example, Ferret (You et al., 2023) accommodates a
      broad range of free-form shapes for referring, including points, bounding boxes, sketches, scribbles,
152
      polygons, and more, facilitating more fine-grained question-and-answer interactions with MLLMs.
153
      However, despite extensive efforts in MLLM research, most work has focused on regular modalities
154   such as image, video, and audio, while a more efficient way to adapt these MLLMs to the action
155   space remains underexplored.
156
157
      Efficient LLMs. LLMs, typically comprising tens to hundreds of billions of parameters, require
      vast amounts of data and computational resources when trained from scratch. PEFT techniques (Hu
158
      et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023) have emerged as a more cost-effective alternative for fine-tuning
159
      these models. Instead of fully fine-tuning the models, PEFT techniques generally freeze most of
160   the parameters and tune only a small fraction, typically less than 1% of the entire model, thus
161   significantly reducing training costs. To address the shortage of training data, one practical approach


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is to use commercial LLMs like ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023) to generate high-quality instruction-
163   following data (Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), facilitating fine-tuning for downstream tasks.
164   Recently, combining these training efficiency and data efficiency techniques has become a widely-
165   used strategy for developing new LLM-based methods, allowing for more affordable adaptation
166   of LLMs to a broader range of tasks and scenarios. Additionally, during the deployment phase,
167   various post-training compression techniques (Frantar et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
168   2024) have been explored to enhance inference speed and optimize performance on resource-limited
169   devices. Given the sparse nature of LLMs, these techniques typically involve compressing the models
170   through pruning or quantization using a calibration dataset. This allows LLMs to be deployed on a
171
      significantly smaller scale while maintaining performance comparable to full-precision models, thus
      greatly reducing computational overhead.

203   Action planning tasks. Unlike generic MLLMs that generate general textual responses in natural
204   language, the action planning task requires the planner π(·) to produce a sequence S consisting of
205   operations {s1 , s2 , s3 , . . . , st }, where st ∈ A and A contains actions executable by intelligent agents,
206   e.g., MoveForward() or TurnLeft(). To produce such a sequence, the planner typically has
207
      access to observations O = {o1 , o2 , o3 , . . . , ot } from the environment. At each time step t, the
      observation ot may consist of various signals, such as images from different angles, GPS signals,
208
      depth information, etc., represented as < o1t , o2t , o3t , . . . , ont >. Based on the observations O and
209
      specified instruction I, the planner is able to make a single-step inference to decide which action
210   should be taken at the next time step, denoted as π(st+1 | ot , I). Furthermore, by incorporating the
211   history of action trajectories s1:t , it is also possible for such planners to refine their predictions given
212   the operation histories, denoted as π(st+1 | ot , s1:t , I). For MLLM-as-agent-based methods, the
213   generic MLLM serves as the planner π(st+1 | ot , s1:t , I; Θ), where ot is typically the camera feed
214   or screenshots that show the current environment, s1:t is the chat history, and I is the instruction
215   accompanied by the system prompts. The weights Θ are trained on a general corpus without specific
      fine-tuning on downstream data.

      3.1     ACTION V ERSE
217
218   ActionVerse is a generic framework designed for action planning tasks, which maps the action
219   space of specific control tasks to the foundation MLLMs using an action encoder and a series of
220   projection layers. As shown in Figure 1, ActionVerse consists of three main components: first,
221   the text instruction is tokenized into a series of text tokens; second, a visual encoder encodes the
222   visual observations and then maps them into visual tokens via projection layers; and finally, an
223   action encoder encodes the action candidates at the current time steps and maps them into action
224
      tokens through projection layers. The aim of this work is to adapt MLLMs to the action space
      efficiently. We built ActionVerse upon existing chat-based MLLM frameworks (Liu et al., 2024),
225
      following the adaptation training of the visual encoder as well as the visual projection layers. The
226
      main difference lies in the newly introduced action encoders that align the action space with the
227   foundational LLM, which will be introduced in Section 3.1.1. Additionally, to explicitly direct the
228   model to perform a Chain-of-Thought-like (CoT-like) reasoning process, ActionVerse is capable
229   of outputting observations based on the given images as well as the reasoning process to fulfill the
230   given instructions. By incorporating historical outputs, it has learned an implicit capability to correct
231   incorrect previous movements. We introduce the details of building the multi-round conversation-like
232   training set in Section 3.1.2.

      by specific devices, which may vary from task to task. Typically, however, the action types for a
237
      particular device are discrete and limited in number, while each of these actions may further take
238
      discrete or continuous arguments as inputs. For example, for an embodied robot, the action set may
239   include operations like Move(), Pick(), and Turn(), whereas a virtual assistant on a smartphone
240   may have actions like Text(), Click(), and Drag(). Additionally, each specific action, such as
241   Turn(), may further take arguments for fine-grained operations, either discrete directions such as
242   left and right, or a continuous numeric value representing the degree of rotation.

      Existing action encoding. In existing MLLM-based control methods (Zhou et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
264   2024a), a typical solution for encoding these actions and arguments relies solely on text descriptions.
265   The actions and arguments are usually defined with examples in the system prompts, then tokenized
266   and treated like plain English words. However, such frameworks may lead to several issues. (1)
267   It necessitates extensive work to design and maintain the system prompts; (2) Due to the context
268   window length capacity, an overly long system prompt will reduce the number of tokens available
269   for other inputs; (3) Without adding special tokens for actions in the vocabulary, it is necessary
      to maintain the system prompts of definitions of the actions in multi-round interactions, which

      wastes considerable resources on processing repeated lengthy prompts; (4) Due to tokenization, text
271   descriptions of actions or arguments tend to be separated into pieces. For example, in some previous
272   works (Zhou et al., 2024) that directly ask ChatGPT to encode or generate long action IDs such as
273   ‘68aafa779b9c41eca16156cfddcedd2b’, the result may be a token sequence like [‘6’, ‘8’, ‘a’, ‘af’,
274   ....]’, which can break the original semantics and result in potential distortion during the next token
275   generation process in MLLMs, and increases the computational burden of processing more tokens.
276   Another approach that partially alleviates the above issues involves introducing visual prompts within
277   the inputs. This approach leverages the emerging grounding capability of MLLMs when explicit
278   marks are given (Yang et al., 2023a). It involves adding specific visual labels to indicate regions
279
      that may contain executable elements (Zhang et al., 2023a), thus avoiding the need for complex
      text descriptions of actions. This simple yet effective strategy bridges the gap between actions and
280
      visual observations. However, it presents challenges for the visual recognition abilities of MLLMs,
281
      such as OCR, to accurately identify the provided marks, especially when they are densely packed.
282   Furthermore, although this approach seems promising for actions that do not require complicated
283   arguments, like Click() buttons on smartphones, it remains difficult to incorporate arguments by
284   embedding visual marks into the images, thus limiting its applicability in more generalized scenarios.
285
      ActionVerse. To address the above challenges in action encoding for MLLMs, it is necessary to
286
      solve two problems: first, to design a more efficient way to represent the action space, and second, to
287
      embed the argument for each action. While the most straightforward approach is to design new action
288   tokenizers and train an action-based foundational MLLM (Lu et al., 2023), this consumes too many
289   training resources. Inspired by recent success (Wang et al., 2024) in efficiently adapting text-based
290   LLMs into multi-modal scenarios by tuning only a few projection layers to align non-textual features
291   with the text space of LLMs, we have designed a similar pipeline. Specifically, as shown in Figure 2,
292   instead of using text descriptions or tagging the executable regions on images with numeric marks,
293   we encode the features of operable areas and incorporate task-specific special action tokens to form a
294   candidate pool of the current executable actions. By doing this, it simultaneously encodes the action
295   and corresponding arguments, which are further mapped into the original MLLM space by some
296
      linear projection layers. Therefore, it becomes capable of embedding action candidates into the input
      sequence, serving as a guidebook for producing expected action sequences without specifying lengthy
297
      system prompts.
298
299   Taking a typical action-oriented task Vision Language Navigation (VLN) (Anderson et al., 2018)
300   as an example, we formally introduce the action encoding process. In VLN, an embodied robot
301   is asked to fulfill an instruction I described in natural language in a simulated environment. The
302
      robot itself can perceive the environment through equipped sensors, such as an RGB camera and
      a depth camera. Based on these observations, the robot is able to make two actions, Move() and
303
      Stop(), which constitute the action space A = {Move(), Stop()} of this task. Specifically, the
304
      Move() action further takes a ViewpointID as input to teleport to a specific location in the simulator,
305   which is a simplified version of movement compared to the real world. The other action, Stop(),
306   should be taken when the agent decides it has navigated to the target position to finish the task, which
307   does not need any input arguments. We first build the task-specific action set D, which is a set of
308   special tokens that need to be added to the vocabulary of the foundational MLLM tokenizer. Each
309   special token matches a specific action in A. There are two types of special tokens in D: the first
310   represents the actions that take arguments as input, e.g., Move(), which is represented by paired
311   tokens like <Move> and </Move>, while others represent actions that do not need inputs, such as
312   Stop(), which is represented by a single token like <Stop>. To encapsulate the Move() action’s
313
      argument, which includes direction information in the VLN task, we use DINOv2 (Oquab et al.,
      2024) to extract visual features of navigable regions (see Figure 2). Each navigable viewpoint in the
314
      panorama image of the current location is cropped into patches and sent into vision transformers to
315
      extract vision features. These features are then translated into argument embeddings through linear
316   projection layers, inserted between the <Move> and </Move> tokens. Since the Stop() action
317   does not require any arguments in this task, there is no argument embedding for this special token.
318
319
      Although we use the VLN task as an example here to explain the action encoding framework in
      ActionVerse due to its simplicity, it is important to note that the proposed framework can be seamlessly
320
      extended to more generalized scenarios. For example, the visual observations can be extended to
321
      broader modalities such as depth and thermal graphs; the viewpoint regions can be transferred to
322   buttons on smartphones, robotic arms, etc.

343   3.1.2    C ONVERSATION - LIKE ACTION I NSTRUCTION - FOLLOWING T UNING
344
345   In control scenarios, the planner is typically expected to produce a sequence of actions in continuous
346   time steps until the goal is achieved. At each time step, the planner receives new observations
347   as inputs and is expected to propose the movement for the next time step. This is analogous to a
348   conversation between the observer and the planners, mirroring the nature of chat-based MLLMs.
349   Therefore, to efficiently train ActionVerse, particularly the projection layers and adaptors of the
350
      foundational MLLMs, and to align with the newly introduced action space, we propose a pipeline to
      build a conversation-like instruction dataset for action-based MLLMs, following recent practices in
351
      the efficient training of MLLMs (Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023).
352
353   Specifically, as shown in Figure 3, we break down the operation flow into a multi-round conversation.
354   At the beginning of each conversation, the instruction is set as the system prompt, which states the
355   target objective to be achieved by the agent. Then, a multi-round conversation follows between the
356
      observer and planner. At each time step, the observer inputs the current observations as well as the
      action candidates, while the planner responds to the observer with a reasoning process and proposes
357
      the action to be taken. The conversation will continue until the planner terminates the interaction upon
358
      achieving the goal. Following this pipeline, we build task-specific conversation-like action instruction
359   training sets, which are used to fine-tune the MLLMs and projection layers. To generate the detailed
360   reasoning processes of the planners and textual descriptions of observations in the conversations, we
361   employ the ChatGPT APIs, as detailed in the Appendix A.
362
      Moreover, to enhance the robustness of action sequence generation, we introduce disturbances in the
363
      routes within the conversation datasets as a data augmentation strategy. For example, for a ground-
364
      truth action sequence A->B->C, we might add an incorrect step from B to D, then self-correct back to
365   B and proceed to the target C: A->B->D->B->C. This method introduces an implicit self-reflection
366   capability that rectifies erroneous movements.

371   4.1     TASK S ETTING
372
373   To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed ActionVerse, we conducted experiments on two action-
374
      based tasks: Room-to-Room Vision Language Navigation (R2R-VLN) (Anderson et al., 2018) and
      Web Vision Language Navigation (Web-VLN) (Chen et al., 2024b). Implementation details including
375
      the model structure, training hyperparameters, and training procedures, are provided in Appendix B.
376
377   R2R-VLN is a classic action-based task where an embodied robot in a simulator is instructed to
      navigate from the initial point to a specific location. At each position, the robot can observe the


                                                              7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      environment through both an RGB camera and a depth camera. The action space of this task is
379   greatly simplified: the robot is allowed to teleport to any reachable viewpoint within a certain distance
380   without needing to consider obstacles or specific routes. When the robot observes that it has reached
381   the target location, it is expected to execute a stop action.
382
      Web-VLN is a web-based version of the VLN task, simulating web browsing activities. Specifically,
383
      given a language instruction, the agent’s goal is to navigate across different websites to find specific
384
      elements. For example, it may need to search for a certain commodity on a shopping website.
385   Web-VLN shares a similar setting and evaluation metrics with R2R-VLN, though the action space
386   varies from moving to specific viewpoints among rooms to clicking specific buttons on websites.
387
388
      Metrics. R2R-VLN and Web-VLN utilize similar metrics, including Trajectory Length (TL), which
      measures the average distance the agent travels; Navigation Error (NE), indicating the average
389
      distance from the agent’s final position to the target; Success Rate (SR), representing the percentage
390
      of navigation attempts where the agent successfully reaches the target location within a 3-meter
391   margin of error; Oracle Success Rate (OSR), which tracks the agent’s success rate when stopping at
392   the closest point to the goal along its path; and Success rate weighted by Path Length (SPL), which
393   evaluates the balance between navigation accuracy and efficiency by adjusting the success rate based
394   on the ratio of the optimal path length to the agent’s actual path length.

507   4.4   E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
508
509   To demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed ActionVerse, we analyze the computational costs for
510   both training and inference, highlighting the advantages in terms of implementation, deployment, and
511   resource usage.
512
      Training Efficiency. Our method leverages Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) as the base LLM and
513   fine-tunes it using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), a low-rank adaptation technique. This approach drastically
514   reduces the computational requirements compared to training action models from scratch, which
515   often demand large-scale resources. With our framework, the training can be completed on a setup of
516   4 Nvidia A100 GPUs in approximately one day, making it significantly more efficient than fine-tuning
517   or training large, general-purpose multi-modal models. Furthermore, techniques like mixed precision
518   training contribute to faster and more resource-effective training.
519   Inference Efficiency. For inference, we compare our method with NavGPT, an LLM-as-agent
520   approach that uses ChatGPT APIs in a zero-shot manner. NavGPT requires elaborate system prompts
521   of approximately 15,000 tokens per instruction, incurring a cost of around $0.75 per 10-round
522   navigation request. Additionally, it depends on continuous network access to external API servers,
523   which can be limiting in real-time applications. In contrast, ActionVerse eliminates the need for
524   complex, manually designed prompts by aligning action tokens directly with the model’s multi-modal
525   space. This allows the system to be deployed locally on a single Nvidia A5000 GPU after quantization,
526   significantly lowering the cost and making the model more accessible for real-time and on-device
527
      applications.

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we introduced ActionVerse, a framework that efficiently adapts chat-based MLLMs into
532
      action planners. Specifically, we designed a novel action encoding process alongside an alignment
533
      technique capable of simultaneously encoding both action types and action arguments into the
534   foundational LLMs. By constructing a conversation-like instruction training set, we treated the action
535   planning task as a dialogue between observers and planners, thus enabling an implicit self-reflection
536   mechanism based on historical movements. We experimented with the proposed framework on two
537   action-oriented tasks, demonstrating promising results with minimal instruction tuning. We anticipate
538   that this work can serve as a baseline unified framework for a broader range of action-based tasks.
539   Future work involves extending the proposed framework to more action-oriented tasks, such as
      smartphone app control, robotic system manipulation, and beyond.

      • Action Encoder: For action tokens, we added task-specific special tokens into the LLM vocabulary,
865     such as <move> and <stop>. These tokens are encoded by the text encoder similar to other
866     words. For the image region features inserted in special action tokens like <move></move>, we
867     crop the regions from hierarchical feature pyramids of the last three layers from the image encoder.

      • Projection Layer: Following LLaVA, we use a multi-layer perceptron to project the vision
869     features into the foundation language model, initializing this projection layer using the LLaVA-1.5
870     pretrained model.
871
      • LLM We employ Vicuna-7b as our foundation language model.
872
873   The proposed ActionVerse framework consists of four main stages:
874
875   • Encoding: Multi-modal information is encoded into tokens. This includes a DINOv2 vision
876     encoder for image tokens, a pretrained LLM tokenizer for text tokens, and action tokens cropped
877     from DINOv2’s feature map based on action candidates.
878   • LLM reasoning: Non-textual tokens are aligned with the LLM space using MLP-based projection
879     layers. All tokens, along with any historical information, are concatenated as input into the LLM.
880
      • Output detokenization: The output tokens from the LLM are detokenized into natural language,
881     containing observation, reasoning, and action, following training formats.
882
      • Execution: The action token is extracted and sent to the navigation task simulator to move the
883
        agent or terminate.
