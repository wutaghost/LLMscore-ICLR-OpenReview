009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating novel molecules is challenging, with most representations of molecules
012            leading to generative models producing many invalid molecules. Spanning Tree-
013            based Graph Generation (STGG) (Ahn et al., 2021) is a promising approach to
014            ensure the generation of valid molecules, outperforming state-of-the-art generative
015            models (Weininger, 1988; Song & Ermon, 2019) for unconditional generation. In
016            the real world, we want to be able to generate molecules conditional on one or
017            multiple desired properties rather than unconditionally. Thus, in this work, we
018            extend STGG to multi-property conditional generation. Our approach, STGG+ ,
019            incorporates a modern Transformer architecture, random masking of properties
020
               during training (enabling conditioning on any subset of properties and classifier-
               free guidance), an auxiliary property-prediction loss (allowing the model to self-
021
               criticize molecules and select the best ones), and other improvements. We show
022
               that STGG+ achieves state-of-the-art performance on in-distribution and out-of-
023
               distribution conditional generation, as well as reward maximization.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Generating novel molecules is challenging, and the choice of molecular representation significantly
029
      impacts the performance of generative models. Traditional methods have mainly focused on SMILES
030   (Weininger, 1988) 1D strings (Segler et al., 2018; Kwon et al., 2023), and 2D graphs (Jo et al., 2022;
031   Vignac et al., 2022; Jo et al., 2023). A significant issue with these representations is that a single error
032   by the generative model can result in invalid molecules, especially as molecule size increases.
033
      Recently, Krenn et al. (2020) proposed Self-referencing Embedded Strings (SELFIES) (Krenn
034
      et al., 2020), a robust 1D string representation similar to SMILES that guarantees the generation
035
      of valid molecules through a carefully designed context-free grammar. However, recent work by
036   Gao et al. (2022) and Ghugare et al. (2023) found that while SELFIES prevent invalid molecules,
037   it makes exploration more difficult and reduces the performance of generative models (in terms of
038   obtaining high-reward samples, i.e., molecules with desired properties). A significant challenge for
039   the generative models based on SELFIES is the need to pre-define the number of tokens contained in
040   a branch (a deviation from the main path in a 1D string) and count backward the number of tokens
041   required to reach the beginning of the ring (starting from the end). This requires extensive planning
042   and counting, making the problem much more challenging for the model to solve.
043   An alternative approach, Spanning Tree-based Graph Generation (STGG) (Ahn et al., 2021), has
044   recently emerged. Unlike SELFIES, STGG is designed explicitly for generative models and works by
045   masking invalid tokens during sampling, preventing the generation of invalid structures (e.g., atoms
046   without bonds between them, branch end before branch start) and ensuring proper valency. STGG
047   uses a simple set of if/else conditions to mask out invalid tokens, which not only prevents invalid
048   molecules but also leads to higher-quality and more diverse generated molecules (Ahn et al., 2021).
049   Jang et al. (2023) shows that STGG generally performs equally or better than other state-of-the-art
050
      generative models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) for unconditional
      molecule generation. However, its application in multi-property conditional settings has not been
051
      explored. We address this setting along with a few additional challenges, as discussed below.
052
053   Any-property-conditioning In real-world applications, we want to generate molecules conditional
      on one or multiple desired properties rather than unconditionally. Furthermore, we want to condition


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      on any subset of desirable properties without retraining the model each time that we condition on a
055   different subset of properties.
056
057   Self-criticism Another critical issue is the synthesis time for molecules, which can take weeks, or
058
      months. Thus, we cannot expect chemists to synthesize all generated molecules. Ideally, we need
      a way to filter the molecules that we provide to chemists. Some properties can be verified through
059
      simulations, but this can be extremely slow, and not all properties can be simulated. Another option
060
      is to rely on external property predictor models, but training, validating, and managing multiple
061   property predictors can be troublesome. What if the generative model could predict the properties
062   of its own generated molecules? This is the idea we propose here: we give the model the ability to
063   predict properties and thus self-criticize its own generated molecules, allowing it to automatically
064   filter out those with undesirable properties.
065
      Out-of-distribution properties We sometimes seek to generate novel molecules with out-of-
066
      distribution (OOD) properties in order to expand the range of our molecular knowledge. These
067   OOD properties generally involve extreme range of values. Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho
068   & Salimans, 2022) is a technique to improve conditioning fidelity; we found CFG useful for in-
069   distribution properties, but problematic for some out-of-distribution conditioning values, especially
070   for extreme values, resulting in poor generative efficiency (% of valid, unique, and novel molecules)
071   and conditioning fidelity. Since guidance can be beneficial to some conditioning values, but not others,
072   we propose a solution: random guidance with best-of-k self-filtering (described further below).
073   In this work, we tackle any-property-conditional molecule generation with self-criticism using an
074   improved STGG. In doing so, we make the following contributions:
075
076         1. Any-property-conditioning: We use an MLP on standardized continuous features and
077            embeddings on categorical features while randomly masking some properties during training,
078            allowing conditional generation on any number of properties (0, 1, 2, or all) and the use of
079            Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) for improved performance (Section 3.2).
080         2. Improved Transformer architecture: We improve on the original Transformer architecture
081            used in STGG by using: Flash-Attention, no bias terms, RMSProp, rotary embeddings, the
082            SwiGLU activation, and better hyperparameters (Section 3.1).
083         3. Improved Spanning-Tree: We extend STGG to 1) allow compound structures with a new
084            token and masking conditions, 2) prevent incomplete samples through special masking when
085            there are too many opened branches, 3) prevent ring overflow, 4) randomize the order of the
086            graph during training for better generalization, and 5) automatically calculate valency and
087            adapt the token vocabulary based on the dataset (Section 3.3).
088         4. Auxiliary property prediction objective: The objective improves conditioning fidelity and
089            enables out-of-the-box self-filtering of molecules with incorrect properties. (Section 3.5)
090
            5. Random guidance for extreme value conditioning: Classifier-free guidance uses guidance
091
               w > 1 to improve performance (Section 3.4), but this can fail when conditioning on extreme
092
               values (which are needed to generate molecules with out-of-distribution properties). We
093            propose using random guidance with best-of-k filtering as a solution (Section 3.6).
094
095
            6. Comprehensive performance evaluation: We demonstrate excellent performance in terms
               of 1) distribution learning and diversity on unconditional generation (Section 4.1), 2)
096
               distribution learning and conditioning fidelity on in-distribution (Section 4.2) and out-of-
097
               distribution (Sections 4.3 and 4.5) conditional generation, and 3) diverse and high-reward
098            samples on reward maximization (Section 4.4).

157   2.2   1D STRING REPRESENTATIONS
158
159   The most popular choice of string-based representation is SMILES (Weininger, 1988), an extremely
160   versatile method capable of representing any molecule. However, when used in generative models,
161   generated SMILES strings often correspond to invalid molecules. A single incorrectly placed token
      often leads to an invalid molecule. Graph-based diffusion methods also face a similar issue. To


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      address this problem, recent methods like Spanning Tree-based Graph Generation (STGG) (Ahn
163   et al., 2021) and SELFIES (Krenn et al., 2022) have been developed to prevent the generation of
164   invalid molecules. For a detailed comparison of SMILES, SELFIES, and STGG, see Appendix A.2.
165
166
      STGG has demonstrated performance on par with or better than state-of-the-art unconditional
      generative models (Ahn et al., 2021; Jang et al., 2023). Conversely, SELFIES has been shown
167
      to perform worse than SMILES on property-conditional molecule generation (Gao et al., 2022;
168
      Ghugare et al., 2023). Therefore, in this work, we focus on STGG for property-conditional molecule
169   generation.
170
171
      2.3   STGG
172
173   STGG (Ahn et al., 2021) uses a SMILES-like vocabulary with begin “(” and end “)” branch tokens,
174   ring start “[bor]” and i-th ring end “[eor-i]” tokens. Contrary to SELFIES, STGG was made
175   from the ground up for unconditional molecule generation. STGG leverages a Transformer (Vaswani
176   et al., 2017) architecture to sample the next tokens conditional on the tokens of the current unfinished
177   molecule. To predict the ring end tokens, STGG uses a similarity-based output layer distinct from
178   the linear output layer used to predict other tokens. STGG also uses an input embedding to track
179   the number of open rings. Invalid next tokens are prevented through masking of next tokens that
180
      would lead to impossible valencies (e.g., atoms, ring-start, and branch-start when insufficient valency
      remains) and structurally invalid tokens (e.g., atom after atom, bond after bond, or ring-i end when
181
      fewer than i ring start tokens are present).
182
183   In the next section, we will show how to improve the STGG architecture, vocabulary, and masking,
184   adapt STGG for any-property conditional generation, and improve fidelity on conditioned properties
185   through several techniques (classifier-free guidance, self-criticism, random classifier-free guidance
186
      for extreme conditioning).

188   3     M ETHOD
189
190   We tackle the problem of any-property conditional generation with self-criticism using STGG.
191
192   3.1   A RCHITECTURE
193
194   We enhance the architecture used in STGG, a regular Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) directly
195   from PyTorch main libraries. To improve it, we leverage recent improvements in Large Language
196   Models following GPT-3 (Radford et al., 2019), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and Llama (Touvron et al.,
197   2023). The improvements include: 1) RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) replacing LayerNorm
198
      (Ba et al., 2016); 2) residual-path weight initialization (Radford et al., 2019); 3) bias-free architecture
      (Chowdhery et al., 2023); 4) rotary embeddings (Su et al., 2024) instead of relative positional
199
      embedding; 5) lower-memory and faster attention with Flash-Attention-2 (Dao et al., 2022; Dao,
200
      2023); 5) SwiGLU activation function (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Shazeer, 2020); 6) changes in
201   hyperparameters following GPT-3 (Radford et al., 2019) (i.e., AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017;
202   Kingma & Ba, 2014) β2 = 0.95, cosine annealing schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016), more
203   attention heads, no dropout). These modifications aim to enhance the model’s efficiency, scalability,
204   and overall performance. We also considered more efficient architectures, see Appendix A.5.
205
206   3.2   A NY- PROPERTY CONDITIONING
207
208   We preprocess continuous properties by applying a simple z-score standardization.
209
      To condition the model on any subset of target properties without retraining the model every time
210   we change the properties, we need to be able to turn off the conditioning of some properties. For
211   continuous variables, we handle missing values through a binary indicator variable: if a property is
212   missing, we set the property value to 0 and the missing indicator to 1. It is important to include these
213   missing indicators because we cannot assume the plausible values for the missing features (e.g., if
214   A is 1.0, B is missing, maybe the only possible range for B is around 3 and 4, so if we leave B at 0
215   without missing indicator, it will not make sense). For categorical variables, we add an extra category
      for missing values. During training, we mask a random subset of t properties, where t is chosen


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      uniformly between 0 and the number of properties. See Appendix A.3.2 for details. This allows us to
217   condition the model on any subset of desired properties at test time while ignoring the rest.
218
219   In the neural network, we process the standardized continuous features (continuous properties
220
      concatenated with their binary missing indicators) in a 2-layer multilayer perceptron (MLP) with
      Swish activation (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Ramachandran et al., 2017). Each categorical feature
221
      is then processed individually using a linear embedding. These processed outputs are added directly
222
      to the embedding of all tokens. We also experimented with injecting these embeddings through
223   adaptive normalization (Huang & Belongie, 2017), a method commonly used for conditioning on
224   noise-level in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), but this approach massively increased the number
225   of parameters without improving performance.
226

      3.3   I MPROVEMENTS TO S PANNING -T REE
228
229   Starting from STGG as base, we implement several improvements. Firstly, we extend the vocabulary
230   to allow for the generation of molecular compounds that are composed of multiple unconnected
231   graphs (e.g., salt is represented as [Na+].[Cl-], where [Na+] and [Cl-] are single-atom molecules
232   connected through a ionic bond), enabling the model to solve a broader range of problems. STGG
233   uses a fixed vocabulary and a fixed set of maximum valencies that determines how many valence
234   bonds each atom can form. Instead of requiring a predefined vocabulary, we automate the process of
235
      building a vocabulary based on the atoms found in the dataset and their maximum valency, again
      derived from the dataset. This data-centric approach allows us to represent complex structures,
236
      including non-molecular compounds containing metals.
237
238   We observe that STGG can occasionally generate incomplete samples by creating too many branches
239   without closing them within the allowed maximum length, particularly when conditioning on extreme
240   out-of-distribution properties. To address this, we modify the token masking process to ensure the
241
      model closes its branches when the number of open branches approaches the number of tokens left to
      reach the maximum length. This additional masking step prevents the rare but problematic situation
242
      of incomplete samples. Additionally, for massive molecules, it is possible for the model to rarely
243
      produce more rings than the maximum number of rings (100); we now mask the creation of rings
244   when the maximum number is reached. With these additional masks, we generally maintain 100%
245   validity, even when generating molecules with out-of-distribution properties).
246
247
      Contrary to STGG, we do not canonicalize molecules and instead use a random ordering of the
      molecules (a different random ordering is sampled for each molecule during training). Doing so
248
      improves generalization (see Appendix A.8).
249
250
251   3.4   C LASSIFIER - FREE GUIDANCE
252
      To enforce better conditioning of the properties, we use classifier-free guidance, originally designed
253
      for diffusion models (Ho & Salimans, 2022), and found beneficial for autoregressive language
254   models as well (Sanchez et al., 2023). This technique involves directing the model more toward
255   the conditional model’s direction while pushing it away from the unconditional model’s direction
256   by an equal amount. Figure 2 illustrates this concept. The amount of guidance typically requires
257   hyperparameter-tuning. However, for simplicity and generality, in all analyses, we arbitrarily set the
258   guidance parameter w to 1.5, where w = 1 means no guidance.
259
260   3.5   S ELF - CRITICISM
261
262   To make the model more powerful, we provide the model with the ability to self-criticise its own
263   generated molecules. The purpose is improve the quality of generated samples by using a jointly-
264   trained property predictor to rank and filter the generated samples. It works as follows: 1) the model
265   generates k molecules for a given set of properties, 2) it evaluates the k molecules molecules properties
266
      based on its own property-predictor (see the paragraph below), and 3) it returns the molecule whose
      properties best match the conditioned properties. This best-out-of-k strategy significantly improves
267
      the quality of its generated molecules.
268
269   For the model to be able to predict properties of the molecules, we add a property-prediction loss to
      the training objective. During training, the model is tasked with predicting both the next token in


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the sequence and the properties of the current unfinished molecule. During sampling, we generate
271   molecules conditioned on the desired properties with classifier-free guidance. Then, we mask out the
272   properties (making them fully missing) and reprocess the molecule until we reach the end-of-sequence
273   (EOS) token. At this point, we extract the predicted property of this molecule.
274
      The architecture with the described loss functions is illustrated in Figure 1. The methodology for
275
      generating molecules using classifier-free guidance and self-criticism is depicted in Figure 2.

      3.6   R ANDOM GUIDANCE FOR EXTREME CONDITIONING
279   Regular guidance can be problematic for some extreme (out-of-distribution) conditioning values,
280   resulting in poor generative efficiency (% of valid, unique, and novel molecules) and conditioning
281   fidelity. However, guidance can still be beneficial to some extreme conditioning values.
282
      To improve generative performance on extreme conditioning values, we propose to randomly sample
283
      a guidance w ∼ U(−0.5, 2) for each sample, ensuring high diversity through a mix of low and
284
      high guidance. Then, using self-criticism, our method selects the best-out-of-k molecule from the
285   molecules generated at different guidance levels, indirectly allowing the model to determine by
286   itself which guidance is best for each sample. This is effectively a way to balance exploration
287   and exploitation (higher guidance means less diversity and better property-alignment, while lower
288   guidance means more diversity and less property-alignment).

362   We follow the same protocol as Liu et al. (2024). We train our model on HIV, BACE, and BBBP

      (Wu et al., 2018). We use the same train, valid, and test splits as Liu et al. (2024). Each dataset has

365
      continuous properties: synthetic accessibility (SAS) (Ertl & Schuffenhauer, 2009) and complexity
366   scores (SCS) (Coley et al., 2018). We evaluate the models using metrics on distribution and fidelity

368   control metrics are the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of SAS (evaluated by RDKit) and accuracy of
369   the categorical property (evaluated by a Random Forest (Breiman, 2001) predictor using the Morgan

373   (Degen et al., 2008), Fréchet ChemNet Distance (FCD) (Preuer et al., 2018). We consider any atom
374
      coverage above the test set coverage to indicate good coverage. The Property accuracy metric depends
      on a RandomForest classifier, thus we consider any accuracy equal or above the test set to indicate
375
      good condition control.

464   4.4      R EWARD MAXIMIZATION
465
      Jain et al. (2023) train reinforcement learning (RL) and GFlowNet (Bengio et al., 2023) agents to
466
      solve a task based on the QM9 (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014) dataset. They seek to produce QM9-like
467   molecules which maximize a reward composed of four properties: HOMO-LUMO gap (Griffith
468   & Orgel, 1957), SAS (Ertl & Schuffenhauer, 2009), QED (Bickerton et al., 2012), and molecular
469   weight. This reward is maximized when the HOMO-LUMO gap is as large as possible, and SAS,
470   QED, and weight are 2.5, 1.0, and 105, respectively. We compare to Envelope QL (Yang et al., 2019),
471   MOReinforce (Lin et al., 2022), MOA2C (Mnih et al., 2016), GFlowNet (MOGFN-PC) (Bengio
472   et al., 2023). The HOMO-LUMO gap is evaluated with MXMNet (Zhang et al., 2020).
473   Instead of giving the reward to our model, we train a STGG+ model conditioned on the four properties.
474   Since the HOMO-LUMO gap needs to be maximized there is no appropriate conditioning value. We
475   arbitrarily set it to 0.5, which corresponds to approximately five standard deviations (a limitation
476   of our conditioning method, as we cannot maximize a property, only set a fixed value). The other
477   properties are set to their optimal values: 2.5, 1.0, and 105.
478
      Results Our experiments are shown in Table 4. Our approach yields slightly better molecules in
479   terms of reward and diversity compared to online methods, using around 11.5% of the molecules.
480   This makes our approach significantly more efficient. However, it is important to note that solving
481   this task with online methods is a steep hill and can be considered more difficult.

      5      C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we demonstrated that with specific techniques, optimization, and architectural improve-
528   ments, spanning tree-based graph generation (STGG) can be leveraged to generate high-quality and
529   diverse molecules conditioned on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution properties. Our method
530   achieves equal or superior performance on validty, novelty, uniqueness, closeness in distribution,
531   and conditioning fidelity compared to competing approaches while being extremely efficient and
532   fast. Using fewer molecules than required by online methods (RL/GFlowNet), we also obtain high
533   multi-property-reward molecules in a one-shot manner from a pre-trained model.
534   While our method generates molecules with relatively good accuracy concerning the desired prop-
535   erties, it is still not perfect and can produce incorrect molecules, especially in out-of-distribution
536   scenarios, which is a challenging task. Additionally, the property predictor of our approach may not
537   be as optimal as property predictors engineered explicitly for this task, meaning our method may not
538   always select the best molecules out of k choices, particularly in out-of-distribution scenarios; we
539   found this to be the case for large out-of-distribution logP conditioning values. Currently, the method
      does not account for stereoisomers, although some properties can be dependent on stereoisomers.

      A.1   1D VS 2D REPRESENTATIONS
813
814   There are many ways to represent molecules in the context of molecular generation. The most popular
815   methods are autoregressive models on 1D strings and diffusion (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al.,
816   2020; Song et al., 2020) models on 2D graphs. We highlight the main distinction between the two
817   representations below in the context.
818   Let D be the size of the training dataset, n be the number of atoms in a given molecule, d is the
819   embedding size, and b is the number of bond types.
820
      Diffusion models on 2D graphs:
821
822          • G = (X, A) where the vertices X contains the list of atoms (size: [n, d]) and A is the
823            adjacency matrix of the edges (size: [n, n, b]) for each bond type.
824
             • A is an extremely sparse matrix with many zero elements
825
             • Input space is O(nd + bn2 ); unless using low-rank projections, the number of parameters
826
               must scale proportionally to this amount
827
828
             • Typically use diffusion models (or related methods) given the large number of steps it would
               take to generate X and A autoregressively
829
830          • Equivariant Graph Neural Networks (E-GNNs) are generally used to ensure a unique
831
               representation for a given molecule
832          • Although it has a single representation per molecule, multiple random noises per graph are
833            needed due to diffusion; thus, sample complexity is O(Dnnoise )
834
      Autoregressive models on 1D strings:
835
836          • X (size: [L, d]) is a string containing the molecule where L is proportional to n
837
             • The string starts from a random atom and traverses the 2D molecular graph
838
             • Input space is O(nd); this makes it efficient to process
839
840
             • Typically use autoregressive models (e.g., Transformers) as it scales well
841          • We can either 1) fix the ordering in some way to make representation unique, or 2) use ran-
842            dom orderings as data augmentation with a non-unique representation for a given molecule;
843
               thus, sample complexity is O(Dnaugments )
844
      As can be seen, both methods have similar sample complexity, but 1D strings are much more
845   compressed representations, leading to less space and parameters and, thus, increased potential for
846   scalability. Furthermore, recent results show that 1D strings are as competitive as 2D molecular graph
847   methods for unconditional molecule generation Jang et al. (2023); Fang et al. (2023) and property
848   prediction Yüksel et al. (2023). In the end, both representations contain as much information. Thus,
849   the choice is a matter of preference. 1D strings are easier to scale and can make good use of the
850   power of Transformers; hence, we focus on this type of representation.
851
852   A.2   S PANNING T REE COMPARED TO OTHER 1D STRING - BASED REPRESENTATIONS
853
854   The most popular choice of string-based representation is SMILES (Weininger, 1988). SMILES
855
      is extremely versatile, allowing the representation of any molecule. However, for the purpose of
      generative models, trying to generate SMILES strings directly can quickly lead to many invalid
856
      molecules. Graph-based diffusion methods encounter the same issue. Recently, methods have been
857
      created to prevent the creation of invalid molecules: Spanning Tree (Ahn et al., 2021) and SELFIES
858   (Krenn et al., 2022). Below, we describe in detail the differences between all three methods.
859
860   SMILES:
861
             • Massive vocabulary allows the representation of every aspect of molecules
862
863
             • There are many ways of representing a single molecule
             • Begin-branch token "(" to deviate from the main path and close branch token ")"


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
             • Pointer token i to indicate both the beginning and end of rings
865
866   SELFIES:
867
868          • Restricted SMILES vocabulary
869          • Prevent invalid molecules through a carefully designed context-free grammar:
870              – Atoms and bonds are combined into single tokens (with other aspects such as charge
871                 and number of hydrogen atoms) so that we cannot have an atom without a bond and a
872                 bond without an atom
873              – Hard-designed rules for maximum valencies of specific elements (slightly more per-
874                 missible than octet rule, but cannot handle every case)
875              – Keep track of valencies; ignore future tokens in the current branch if there is not enough
876                 valency left and reduce bond order if needed
877              – There is an open-branch token Branch-i and close-ring token ring-i where i specifies
878                 the number of future tokens in the branch and how many backward steps (in tokens)
879                 are needed to reach the ring closure; this ensures that all branches and tokens are not
880                 left opened
881
      Spanning-Tree:
882
883          • Restricted SMILES vocabulary
884
             • Begin and end branch tokens, with ring start and i-th ring end tokens
885
             • Similarity-based output layer to determine the probabilities of ring ends and input embedding
886
               injection for how many rings are opened
887
888
             • Prevent invalid molecules through masking of tokens before softmax:
889               – Masking of invalid tokens due to impossible valencies (atoms, ring-start, and branch-
890                 start when not enough valency is left) based on the valencies of the training data
891               – Masking of invalid next tokens (atom after atom, bond after bond, ring-i end when
892                 there are less than i ring start tokens)
893               – Force branch ending through masking when getting too close to maximum sequence
894                 length to prevent unfinished molecules (new)
895
      As can be seen, SMILES has such a large vocabulary that each molecule can be represented in
896
      completely different ways, and its main problem for generative models is that many token choices
897   lead to invalid molecules (e.g., two bonds, incorrect valencies, unfinished branches, or rings, etc.).
898
899   SELFIES prevents invalid molecules through its smart, context-free grammar. Although powerful,
900
      this grammar risks making the job of the generative model more difficult as it requires the model to
      plan in advance how many tokens will be in each branch and also to count backward to determine
901
      how many backward steps it must take to reach the ring-end.
902
903   On the other hand, Spanning-tree use clever masking of incorrect tokens to prevent invalid molecules
904   and doing so does not require the model to do significant planning-in-advance and counting when
905   selecting the next token (including the i-th ring end tokens which require no counting due to the
906
      similarity-based prediction).

962   A.4     H YPERPARAMETERS
963
964   The original STGG (Ahn et al., 2021) used the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017;
965   Kingma & Ba, 2014) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999, no weight decay, a fixed learning rate of 2e-4
966
      and batch-size 128. The Transformer architecture had 3 layers, dropout 0.1, 8 attention heads, and
      embedding size 1024. They processed only one property with a single linear layer.
967
968    STGG+ uses the AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, and weight decay 0.1. The
969   Transformer architecture has 3 layers, no dropout, 16 attention heads, SwiGLU (Hendrycks &
970   Gimpel, 2016; Shazeer, 2020) with expansion scale of 2, no bias term (Chowdhery et al., 2023), Flash
971   Attention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023), RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019), Rotary embeddings
      (Su et al., 2024), residual-path weight initialization (Radford et al., 2019).

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       For QM9 (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014), we train for 50 epochs with batch size 512, learning rate 1e-3,
973    max length 150. For Zinc250K (Sterling & Irwin, 2015), we train for 50 epochs with batch size
974    512, learning rate 1e-3, max length 250. For HIV, BACE, and BBBP (Wu et al., 2018), we train for
975    10K epochs (same as done by Liu et al. (2024)), since these are small datasets, with batch size 128,
976    learning rate 2.5e-4, max length 300.
977
       For Chromophore DB (Joung et al., 2020), we train for 1000 epochs with batch size 128, learning

       rate 2.5e-4, max length 600. For the pre-training on Zinc250K and fine-tuning on Chromophore-DB:
979    we pre-train with batch size 512, learning rate 1e-3, and max length 600 for 50 epochs and fine-tune
980    with batch size 128, learning rate 2.5e-4, and max length 600 for 100 epochs.

982
       We generally use 1 or 2 A-100 GPUs to train the models. Training takes a few hours. Note that we
       use a higher max length than the data max length (generally around 25-50%) to ensure that we can
983
       adequately generate molecules with out-of-distribution properties that could be bigger than usual.

985    For pretraining and then fine-tune, there are two ways to preprocess the properties: we can either

988
       deviation in Chromophore’s MolWt is 15 standard-deviation in Zinc250K’s MolWt). Hereby, to
       reduce the gap between pre-trained and fine-tuned conditioning values, we preprocess the properties
989
       by standardizing with respect to the fine-tune dataset properties during both pre-training and fine-
990
       tuning.

       A.5   A LTERNATIVE ARCHITECTURES CONSIDERED
993
994    In this work, we enhance the Transformer architecture used by Ahn et al. (2021) using recent
995    developments in Large Language Models (LLMs). Although powerful, the Transformer architecture
996    with self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) is quadratic in context length, which means that the time
997    and memory increase significantly when dealing with long-context length.
998
       In addition to improvements on Transformer, new architectures such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023),
999    Hyena, (Poli et al., 2023) or RWKV (Peng et al., 2023) have appeared, which are sub-quadratic
1000   with respect to context-length, allowing them to handle long-context length better. We initially
1001   considered some of these architectures to improve inference speed. However, it is hard to synthesize
1002   and manufacture molecules of substantial sizes. Thus, the context length is generally quite limited
1003   (e.g., the largest molecule on Chromophore has 511 tokens, while modern LLMs have a context
1004   length of at least 4096). As long as the context length is less or equal to 2048, FlashAttention (Dao
1005   et al., 2022) is fast enough that there is no inference speed benefit for using Mamba (Gu & Dao,
1006   2023).
