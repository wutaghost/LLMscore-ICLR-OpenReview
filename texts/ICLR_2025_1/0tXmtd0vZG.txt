009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved significant advancements in natural
012            language processing tasks, yet they encounter challenges in complex decision-
013            making scenarios that require long-term reasoning and alignment with high-level
014            objectives. This paper introduces a novel gradient-free LLM-based Actor-Critic
015            framework, termed LAC, which addresses these limitations by integrating both
016            action generation and action evaluation mechanisms. Our approach employs two
017            distinct critics: a language-based critic that provides context-sensitive feedback
018
               and a value-based critic that offers quantitative assessments of expected long-term
               rewards. This dual-critic architecture enhances decision-making by leveraging
019
               the complementary strengths of both critics, enabling contextually appropriate
020
               and more robust action selection. Additionally, we propose a gradient-free policy
021            improvement method that reduces computational overhead, facilitating efficient
022            updates to the actor’s policy without the complexities of gradient backpropagation.
023            We validate the effectiveness of LAC across diverse environments that cover both
024            high-level action space (ALFWorld) and low-level action space (BabyAI-Text),
025            demonstrating its superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art meth-
026            ods. Our method outperforms other state-of-the-art baselines using the same 7B/8B
027            open-source LLMs and even exceeds a strong baseline ReAct using GPT-4 in
028            most settings. Our findings highlight the efficacy and generality of the dual-critic
029
               Actor-Critic framework in enhancing LLM-based decision-making.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024) have
034
      demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks in natural language processing,
035
      from text generation to question answering and summarization. Despite their strengths, LLMs often
036   struggle in more complex decision-making tasks that require not only generating immediate action but
037   also reasoning over long horizons and aligning actions with high-level objectives (Ahn et al., 2022;
038   Yao et al., 2022b; Hao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022b). This raises a fundamental
039   question: how can we efficiently leverage the rich prior knowledge encoded in LLMs to enable more
040   reliable and effective sequential decision-making in diverse and complex environments?
041
      Recent studies have explored various methods to improve LLM-based decision-making. Through
042   the lens of reinforcement learning (RL) (Barto et al., 1989), these methods typically adopt either an
043   actor-only or critic-only paradigm. In actor-only approaches, the LLM serves as an actor, generating
044   actions based on its autoregressive next-token prediction capabilities (Ahn et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
045   2022b; Huang et al., 2022a; Shinn et al., 2024). While such methods are simple and effective
046   for short-term action generation, they often suffer from a lack of long-term planning. As a result,
047   decisions may appear locally optimal but fail to achieve the overall task objective in more complex,
048   multistep environments.
049   On the other hand, critic-only approaches use LLMs as critics to evaluate candidate actions based
050   on predicted future trajectories (Hao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Brooks et al.,
051   2024) and select the action with the best outcome. Although this allows for additional evaluation
052   of actions, it may lead to suboptimal action selection when the sampled candidate actions do not
053   include the optimal action and the predicted future trajectories deviate from reality. Furthermore,
      such methods often prioritize numerical assessments of actions, ignoring important contextual and

112   By integrating both critics, LAC selects actions that are both contextually appropriate (based on the

121   In summary, this work advances the state of LLM-based decision-making through the following key
122   contributions:

      Large Language Models for Sequential Decision-Making Sequential decision-making is a
      fundamental ability of intelligent agents, involving generating a series of actions to achieve the goal
143
      (Barto et al., 1989; Littman, 1996; McCarthy et al., 1963; Bylander, 1994). Recently, LLM-based
144
      agents have been widely used for decision-making in many areas, which only needs some instructions
145   or few-shot examples to generalize to completely new tasks (Huang et al., 2022b; Singh et al., 2023;
146   Ding et al., 2023), thanks to the pre-training on large-scale dataset. According to the functionality,
147   the LLMs that most previous work used mainly belong to two roles: actors, which take trajectories
148   as input and output actions, and critics, which take both trajectories and actions as input and output
149   evaluations of actions. Based on this classification, most of the earlier work in this line of research is
150   actor-only (Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b; Yao et al., 2022b; Huang et al., 2022a; Shinn et al.,
151   2024), i.e., directly using the action generated by LLMs based on previous trajectory. Due to the
152   auto-regressive nature of LLM, it does not do reasoning and planning explicitly. Accordingly, LLM
153
      with actor-only methods often struggles with complex tasks that require multiple steps of planning
      and reasoning (Huang & Chang, 2022; Mialon et al., 2023). To overcome this hurdle, another line of
154
      work, critic-only uses another LLM to evaluate each action by simulating the consequence of it and
155
      then choose the action with the best-simulated outcome (Hao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Fu et al.,
156   2024). However, both actor-only and critic-only methods ignore the interrelation between actor and
157   critic, prioritize one over the other, and insufficiently exploit the available knowledge from the actor
158   and critic. Previous work that tries to combine actor and critic (Zhang et al., 2023b) only uses the
159   language-based outputs of critics. Some work in other fields, such as decoding (Xie et al., 2024), also
160   uses numerical outputs of critics, but it cannot be directly adapted to decision-making problems. To
161   address these limitations, our method LAC integrates prior actor-only and critic-only methods and
      utilizes the merits of the actor-critic algorithm with the strengths of the LLMs.

      Large Language Models with Reinforcement Learning Classical sequential decision-making
163   methods, such as Reinforcement Learning (RL), have been widely adopted in embodied environments
164   (Schulman et al., 2017; Fujimoto et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2022). However, these
165   RL-based methods are typically sample-inefficient and require lots of samples for training. On the
166   other hand, LLMs that contain rich prior knowledge about the world may alleviate this burden. To
167   combine RL and LLM, one straightforward way is to use LLMs as base models and add policy/value
168   heads on top of LLMs (Carta et al., 2023a; Tan et al., 2024). Then use classical RL methods like
169   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) for training (Szot et al., 2023; Zhou et al.). However, these methods
170   still require lots of training samples of the same tasks, which reduces the benefits of using LLM to
171
      some extent and contradicts our settings. There are also other paradigms for combining. RLEM
      (Zhang et al., 2024) adopts Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992) and an experience memory to
172
      update policies, but it may get stuck in the tasks with extremely sparse rewards like ALFWorld and
173
      BabyAI-Text. Retroformer (Yao et al., 2023) trains a smaller LLM with PPO to generate suitable
174   prompts for a larger LLM for a specific task, while our method only needs a small model. ICPI
175   (Brooks et al., 2024) uses LLMs to implement policy iteration by predicting future trajectories and
176   accumulating future rewards, which may also struggle with sparse reward settings. We have compared
177   it empirically in Section 5.

183   In this section, we describe the task setting, previous actor-only methods, and critic-only methods to
184   better understand prior work’s limitations and the motivations for our LAC. For better understanding,
185   we compare the frameworks of these methods in Figure 2.
186
      Task setup. Consider a general setup of an agent interacting with an environment for achieving
187   some given goals, e.g., goal g =“put a clean egg in microwave” (from benchmark ALFWorld) or
188   goal g =“pick up the green ball” (from benchmark BabyAI-Text). At time step t, the agent receives
189   an observation ot ∈ O, which is described in natural language in this work, from the environment.
190   The agent then takes an action at ∈ A that is sampled from some policy π(a|g, ht ), where ht :=
191   (o1 , a1 , o2 , a2 · · · , ot ) is the history to the agent. During execution, there is no immediate reward.
192   The environment will give a signal about whether the task was completed successfully or not only at
193   the end of each episode. The agent has never seen the testing tasks before and can only try each task
194   once in this work.
195   Actor-only methods. To solve the above tasks with large language models, one simple method is to
196   directly use pre-trained LLMs as policy: at ← arg maxa πLLM (a|g, ht ), as shown in Figure 2 (a).
197   We also provide detailed algorithm description of actor-only methods in Algorithm 2 of Appendix B.3,
198   which can be implemented by simply injecting instructions or few-show examples to the prompt as
199   shown in Yao et al. (2022b). Despite its simplicity, the actor-LLM πLLM generates actions solely
200   relying on its auto-regression ability and it does not conduct long-term planning explicitly, which is
201
      typically necessary for sequential decision-making tasks. Additionally, this issue will be exacerbated
      when using smaller models like CodeLlama-7B (Roziere et al., 2023) and Mistral-7B (Jiang et al.,
202
      2023). This problem is verified in Section 5.
203
204   Critic-only methods. To handle the issue of lack of long-term planning in actor-only methods,
205   another line of research resorts to critic-only methods (Hao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Fu et al.,
206   2024). The basic idea of critic-only methods is to first sample several candidate actions from actor
207
      {a1t , a2t , · · · , ant } ∼ πLLM (·|g, ht ), then self-evaluate each candidate action by other LLMs and
      finally select the action with the highest evaluation value. We call it critic-only because only the
208
      critic’s output is considered when choosing the final action. The self-evaluation procedure is the key to
209
      critic-only methods, which can adopt many approaches. For example, directly ask an LLM to evaluate
210   the action candidate (Fu et al., 2024), or predict the future trajectory ut of each action candidate using
211   an LLM as a forward model fLLM and use the future outcome as evaluation (shown in Figure 2
212   (b)), or use tree-search methods like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006;
213   Coulom, 2006) to expand each action candidate Hao et al. (2023). We also provide detailed algorithm
214   description of critic-only methods in Algorithm 3 of Appendix B.4. Despite this progress, critic-only
215   methods often neglect the knowledge of actor and the interaction between actor and critic, which may
      lead to ineffective decision-making.

      In this section, we present our LLM-based Actor-Critic (LAC) algorithm that integrates actor and
241
      critic to enhance the decision-making ability of large language models. The key idea behind LAC is to
242   improve the actor πLLM based on the evaluations provided by two distinct but complementary critics:
243   the lang-critic CLLM and the value-critic QLLM . The lang-critic CLLM provides language-based
244   evaluations that contain richer and more interpretable information for assessing actions, but they are
245   difficult to quantify. The value-critic QLLM provides the numerical assessment in terms of long-term
246   value or reward but may lack detailed explanations for assessment. By combining both natural
247   language-based evaluations and numerical assessments, LAC ensures that actions are contextually
248   relevant and optimized for long-term success. Figure 1 demonstrates how relying solely on one critic
249   (either CLLM or QLLM ) may still lead to suboptimal actions, whereas combining both critics avoids
250
      such mistakes.
251   The policy improvement process for πLLM involves two main steps: (1) improving πLLM with
252   lang-critic’s language-based judgments over previous actions by injecting these judgments into
253   πLLM ’s prompt so that πLLM could avoid previous mistakes and sample better candidate actions
254   (Section 4.1); (2) further refining πLLM based on the value-critic’s numerical assessment through a
255   gradient-free policy improvement procedure (Section 4.2). Our framework is shown in Figure 2. The
256
      overall algorithm is outlined in Algorithm 1. We also compare the frameworks of previous actor-only
      methods, critic-only methods, and our LAC, respectively, for better understanding in Figure 15 (a-c).

259   4.1      P OLICY IMPROVEMENT WITH lang-critic
260
261   To enhance the actor’s decision-making with contextually grounded feedback, we first prompt the
262   LLM to generate judgments on previous action selections. Given the task goal g and history ht ,
263   the lang-critic generates a short evaluation sentence ct such as “I have found object-X. This step is
264   GOOD” or “I should take object-X instead of object-Y first. This step is BAD.” These judgments
265   provide hints about whether and why the previous actions were appropriate, which helps guide the
266
      actor towards better candidate actions.
267   To improve the actor’s policy πLLM , we then condition πLLM on language-based evaluation ct
268   before sampling candidate actions: {a1t , a2t , · · · , ant } ∼ πLLM (·|g, ht , ct ). By incorporating ct , the
269   actor can generate better candidate actions through in-context learning, aligning more closely with
      task objectives and avoiding previous mistakes.

293       4.2     P OLICY IMPROVEMENT WITH value-critic
294
295       Next, we refine the actor’s policy using the value-critic QLLM , which provides numerical evaluations
296       of each candidate action ait . While the lang-critic focuses on providing contextually grounded
297       feedback, the value-critic quantitatively estimates the probability of successfully completing the
298       task after executing each action ait . This value-based assessment is crucial for guiding the actor’s
299       decisions to align with long-term rewards, especially in tasks where immediate outcomes do not fully
300
          capture the consequences of an action.
301       In the following, we will first connect the value-critic to the agent’s success probability of completing
302       the task, then show how LLMs can be used to estimate this value-based evaluation, and finally derive
303       a gradient-free policy improvement mechanism using the estimated value-based evaluation.
304
305       4.2.1    C ONNECT value-critic TO AGENT ’ S SUCCESS PROBABILITY

309
          acquire after executing ait , analogous to the action-value function in conventional RL algorithms.

310
          end of each episode, with no intermediate rewards.

337   in success/failure. For example, let the generated probability of “GOOD” or “SUCCESS” represent
338   positive results P (y = +1|g, ht , ait ) and let the generated probability of “BAD” or “FAILURE”
339   represent negative results P (y = −1|g, ht , ait ). The insight is that if the agent selects actions cor-
340
      rectly, the LLM that are pre-trained via next-token prediction tends to increase the probability of
      positive tokens internally. Otherwise, the probability of negative tokens will increase. Finally, using
341
      Equation (2), we can calculate QLLM (g, ht , ait ) for action ait .
342
343   To improve the accuracy of QLLM (g, ht , ait ), we introduce future trajectory rollouts using a forward
344   model fLLM , which can be implemented by prompting LLMs, e.g., adding few-shot examples, or
345   by fine-tuning on these examples. For each candidate action ait , we roll out several future steps to
346
      predict the resulting trajectory uit = fLLM (g, ht , ait ). By considering the future trajectory uit , we
      obtain more informed estimates of the success and failure probabilities, P (y = ±1|g, ht , ait , uit ).
347
      This approach accounts for the delayed consequences of actions and ensures that QLLM (g, ht , ait )
348
      reflects the long-term value of each action:
349

                                QLLM (g, ht , ait , uit ) = log                                 .                (3)
351                                                               P (y = −1|g, ht , ait , uit )

353
      Trajectory rollouts are especially important in tasks where the outcomes of actions may unfold over
      several steps. By simulating the future impact of actions, the value-critic provides a more accurate
354
      assessment, guiding the actor toward actions that maximize the probability of long-term success.

      5     E XPERIMENTS
387
388
      In this section, we benchmark our method LAC on benchmarks that cover both high-level action space
      (ALFWorld (Shridhar et al., 2021)) and low-level action space (BabyAI-Text (Chevalier-Boisvert
389
      et al., 2019)). We evaluate the effectiveness of LAC by answering the following questions: (1) Can
390
      LAC outperform other decision-making with LLMs methods? (Section 5.2) (2) How does each
391   component of LAC contributes to its performance? (Section 5.3) (3) How do different large language
392   models influence performance? (Section 5.2 and Section 5.3) (4) Is our method computationally
393   consuming? (Section 5.4).
394
395
      5.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
396
397   We compare our method with various decision-making with LLMs baselines, which can be largely
398   classified into actor-only and critic-only methods. For more details of baselines, please refer to
399   Appendix C.2.
400
      Actor-only methods. ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b) combines reasoning and acting in the interaction
401
      with the environment and leverages the reasoning capabilities of LLMs to increase the probability of
402   the LLM acting correctly as an actor.
403
404   Critic-only methods. RAP(Hao et al., 2023) utilizes LLMs as actor and world models and adopts
405
      tree-search planning methods to evaluate each possible action candidate. ICPI (Brooks et al., 2024)
      implements policy iteration using LLMs by predicting future trajectories and selecting the action with
406
      the highest predicted cumulative rewards. RAFA (Liu et al., 2023) evaluates each action candidate by
407
      tree-search and selects the action that may complete the most sub-goals.
408
409   We evaluate LAC on two decision-making benchmarks with high-level actions and low-level actions,
410   respectively.
411   Benchmark with high-level actions: ALFWorld. ALFWorld (Shridhar et al., 2021) is a widely
412   used text-based household environment with 134 different tasks, which require the agent to achieve a
413   goal through a sequence of high-level actions, e.g. “go to place-X”, “take object-Y from place-X”,
414   etc.. The main challenge of this benchmark is to locate the target object and fulfill household work
415   with commonsense knowledge of LLMs. Following ReAct, we evaluate all 134 unseen evaluation
416   games in a task-specific setup.
417   Benchmark with low-level actions: BabyAI-Text. BabyAI-Text (Carta et al., 2023b) is a Grid
418   World environment that extended from the BabyAI platform (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019), in
419   which the agent and objects are placed in a room of 8 × 8 tiles. The agent has 6 primitive actions:
420   turn left, turn right, go forward, pick up, drop, toggle, to solve a task described in natural language
421   (e.g. “Pick up the red box”). These tasks could be difficult because agents have to make a long-term
422   plan, avoid obstacles and find a short path to target objects based on partial observations that are
423
      described in natural language
424   To show the stability of LAC, we adopt four open-source large language models from different
425   organizations: CodeLlama-7B (Roziere et al., 2023), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Gemma-7B
426   (Team et al., 2024), and Llama-3-8B (Meta, 2024a).

      In this subsection, we compare LAC with more baselines including some traditional RL methods
      implemented using LLMs on ALFWorld (Shridhar et al., 2021). The comparison is shown in Figure 9.
832
833   While in LAC we fine-tune the critic using a few trajectories, it is also possible to fine-tune the actor
834   to generate actions using those trajectories. Therefore, a potential baseline could be fine-tuning the
835   actor in actor-only method. To demonstrate the improvement brought by fine-tuning the actor, we
836   fine-tune the actor in ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b) and show the results in Figure 9. We also show
837
      the results of LAC w/ fine-tuned actor in Figure 9. In brief, ReAct w/ fine-tuned actor is a strong
      baseline compared with other baselines, but still inferior to our method LAC and LAC w/ fine-tuned
838
      actor. Compared to LAC, the underperformance of LAC w/ fine-tuned actor arises from its tendency
839
      to overfit the training trajectories. This overfitting causes the actor to favor actions that are more
840   frequent in the dataset, potentially leading to suboptimal action selection.
841
842   For example, in the ALFWorld training dataset, the action “take an apple from X” occurs frequently.
843
      After fine-tuning, the actor may disproportionately generate this action, even when it is irrelevant to
      the current goal. One case is that the current goal is to “heat some egg and put it in the garbage can”.
844
      When the agent sees an “apple 2” in “fridge 1”, it generates and selects an irrelevant action “take
845
      apple 2 from fridge 1”, which does not align with the task.
846
847   This tendency towards overfitting arises because the complexity of the policy function, which maps
848   states s to actions a, often exceeds that of the critic. The policy often has to capture a wide variety of
849
      potential actions for each state, particularly in complex environments. However, the quite limited
      training dataset in our setting restricts its ability to generalize effectively, resulting in memorization
850
      of specific actions rather than flexible decision-making. In contrast, our critic, which includes a world
851
      model for rollout and an evaluation function, focuses on capturing more predictable dynamics of the
852   environment and simpler evaluation criteria. This typically requires simpler mappings than those
853   needed for the policy, thus avoiding overfitting.
854

      heads, namely Conventional Policy Gradient, Conventional Policy Gradient w/ dense rewards and

      calculate their product:

999    A.9                          A NALYSIS OF THE FINE - TUNING PROCESS IN LAC
1000
1001   In order to improve the quality of the language-based critic generated by LLM, we finetune the
1002   LLM that generates the critic. In this section, we analyze the finetuning process, showing the impact
1003   of finetuning on critic prediction, as well as the impact of different data amounts and positive and
1004
       negative sample ratios on task success rates. The comparison can be seen in Figure 14.
1005   In Figure 14 (a), we show the influence of fine-tuning data size. We use 9, 18, 27 and 36 trajectories
1006   to fine-tune LLMs, and show the final success rate on 134 evaluation tasks. In summary, larger data
1007   sizes (27 or 36 trajectories) generally bring higher success rate, while small data sizes (18 and even 9
1008   trajectories in some cases) are already enough for LAC to achieve outperformance.
1009   Figure 14 (b) shows the influence of different positive/negative sample ratio (positive:negative = 0:1,
1010   1:3, 1:1, 3:1 and 1:0) on final performance. We keep the total number of samples the same and just
1011   change positive/negative ratio. In short, our LAC is robust to reasonable positive/negative ratios (e.g.
1012   1:3, 1:1, 3:1), while LAC based on CodeLlama-7B (Roziere et al., 2023) and Gemma-7B (Team et al.,
1013   2024) even perform better when given all positive samples (1:0).
1014   Figure 14 (c) shows the learning curves of the fine-tuning process. We plot the next prediction loss
1015   and positive/negative tokens prediction accuracy for CodeLlama-7B (Roziere et al., 2023). In short,
1016   as the next token prediction loss decreases during fine-tuning, the accuracy of predicting the special
1017   tokens (GOOD or BAD) increases, which exhibits the effect of the fine-tuning process.

       B.2    C RITIC IMPROVEMENT OF LAC
1165
       The lang-critic CLLM , value-critic QLLM , and forward model fLLM we used can be easily imple-
1166
       mented by prompting LLMs via providing instructions or few-shot examples from similar tasks like
1167   prior work (Yao et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2023). However, empirically, we found that they can be
1168   further improved via fine-tuning LLMs with simple next-token prediction loss on several samples
1169   collected from training tasks. In this work, we consider 18 trajectories for each benchmark for
1170   fine-tuning. Though 18 trajectories are significantly fewer than what is required for conventional
1171   reinforcement learning algorithms, they are generally enough for our method. Each trajectory has
1172   the following format: (g, o0 , a1 , o1 , c1 , · · · , aH , oH , cH ), where H is the episode length and ci is a
1173   language-based evaluation of action at . Each ct includes an explanation about the action at (e.g., “I
1174   have found object-X. This step is ” or “I should take object-X instead of object-Y first. This step is ”)
1175
       and a special token that indicates positive/negative judgment (e.g., “GOOD” or “BAD”).
1176   Practically, we just fine-tune the LLM once and use it to construct all the lang-critic CLLM , value-
1177   critic QLLM , and forward model fLLM , thanks to the fine-tuning with the above data format.
1178   Specifically, when minimizing the loss of predicting future trajectories, the forward model fLLM
1179   is improved. When minimizing the loss of generating language-based evaluations, the lang-critic
1180   CLLM , value-critic QLLM are both improved. The latter is because language-based evaluations
1181
       also contain special tokens that indicate positive/negative judgments, whose generated probabilities
       are used to calculate QLLM in Equation (3). We analyze this fine-tuning process in Appendix A.9.

       The assessment of each step is performed by a reward function, which can be the log probability
1379   of the action or self-evaluation given by LLMs, or based on some task-specific heuristics. In our
1380   implementation, we adopt the log probability of actions given by LLMs as the reward. For simplicity
1381   of implementation, we adopted a greedy approach to expand the tree, generating only one action at
1382   a time. More specifically, at each step, LLMs will sample some action candidates. For each action
1383   candidate, LLMs will generate a rollout trajectory until a maximum step or terminal state. The
1384   summation of log probabilities of all the actions on the rollout accessed by LLMs are used as Q value
1385   for each action candidate. The candidate with the highest Q value is chosen to be executed in the
1386   environment.

1391
1392   ICPI (Brooks et al., 2024) proposes to implement policy iteration using LLMs through in-context
1393   learning. At each step, the actor in ICPI will sample some action candidates and the critic will
1394
       compute the Q values for each action candidates. The action candidates with the highest Q values is
       chosen to be executed.
1395
1396   The actor is implemented using LLMs, and successful trajectories like in Table 6 and Table 8 are
1397   provided to it.

1399   current history and an action candidate, the critic in ICPI will rollout a trajectory starting from the
1400   action candidate. Apart from predicting the observations, the critic will give the numerical reward for
1401   each step on the rollout trajectory. The (discounted) return on the rollout is treated as the Q value
1402   for the action candidate. For both ALFWorld and BabyAI-Text, we define the reward as 1 when the
1403   agent reaches the goal. All other steps will have a reward 0. The examples provided to the critic are

1636   D    L IMITATIONS
1637
1638   Our work has two limitations. Firstly, lang-critic of LAC is only used before action generation and it
1639   can also be applied after action generation. For example, it can provide language-based evaluations
1640   for predicted future trajectories to re-sample candidate actions if the previous candidate actions all
1641   fail to complete the target task. Secondly, though the value-critic of LAC can also adopt tree-search
1642   to provide a more accurate assessment of candidate actions, in this paper, we only expand one node
1643   for each candidate action for simplicity.

1645   E    B ROADER I MPACTS
1646
1647   Our method is built upon open-source large language models (LLMs). Like other methods that use
1648   LLMs, our method also inherits some benefits and challenges from LLMs. For the benefits, our
1649   method directly exploits the prior knowledge from LLMs, which may reduce potential carbon costs
1650   compared with training policies from scratch. For the challenges, our method might be susceptible to
1651   producing unintended output when confronted with harmful input, such as unethical text or input
1652   intended for adversarial attacks. To solve this problem, we suggest a thoughtful deployment of our
1653
       method, such as adding a filtering component.
