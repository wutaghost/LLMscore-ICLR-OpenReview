009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Meta-learning has been proposed to address fast adaptation to unseen tasks with
012            little data. Traditional meta-learning is modeled as the Single-Leader Multi-
013            Follower game consisting of inner and outer-level problems to minimize average
014            or worst-case task loss. Because they assume all sampled tasks are independent,
015            it reduces the flexibility of modeling complex interaction among tasks. Thus, we
016            formulate meta-learning as a Single-Leader Multi-Follower game by considering
017            the interaction among tasks at the inner level. We propose the Nash-GBML incor-
018            porating a penalty term into the task loss function to model the interaction among
019            task-specific parameters. We discuss the iteration complexity and convergence of
020
               the Nash-GBML algorithm. To validate our Nash-GBML algorithm, we introduce
               two penalty terms, which are designed to reduce the average and worst-case task
021
               loss. We empirically show that the Nash-GBML with the proposed penalty terms
022
               outperforms traditional GBML for supervised learning experiments.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In machine learning, fast adaptation to unseen tasks with little data remains challenging. Meta-
029   learning, also known as learning-to-learn (Baxter, 1998), has been proposed to address this issue.
030   According to the framework proposed by Baxter (1998), meta-learning aims to minimize the ex-
031   pected loss on new tasks by leveraging information learned from similar tasks. One approach for
032   meta-learning is gradient-based meta-learning (GBML) (Finn et al., 2017).
033   In GBML, the parameter for a new task is updated from the meta-parameter with only a few stochas-
034   tic gradient descent (SGD) steps. Stemming from the Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML)
035   (Finn et al., 2017), GBML has developed in diverse directions. First-order MAML (FOMAML)
036   (Nichol et al., 2018) learns faster than MAML by ignoring second-order derivatives. Reptile (Nichol
037   et al., 2018) also learns faster by updating the meta-parameter using the expected gradient of meta-
038   training tasks. Meta-SGD (Li et al., 2017) learns more efficiently compared to MAML by learning
039
      not just the meta-parameter but also the update direction and learning rate. CAVIA (Zintgraf et al.,
      2019) is less prone to overfitting by updating context parameters at the inner level, instead of the
040
      entire network. Task-robust MAML (TR-MAML) (Collins et al., 2020) is the task-robust meta-
041
      learning by minimizing the worst-case task loss, instead of the average task loss at the outer level.
042   These studies first update task-specific parameters independently during meta-training to solve the
043   inner level problem. Then, they update the meta-parameter during meta-testing to solve the outer
044   level problem.
045
      Typical meta-learning optimizes the meta-parameter by minimizing the average task loss or the
046
      worst-case task loss, assuming that all sampled tasks are independent. The assumption can greatly
047
      simplify the optimization of the meta-parameter; however, it reduces the flexibility of modeling
048   complex interaction among tasks. We hypothesize that considering the interaction among tasks
049   will affect the updating process of the meta-parameter and the overall performance. Based on
050   this hypothesis, we propose a new meta-learning framework that considers the interaction among
051   meta-training tasks. Here, the interaction refers to each task-specific parameter influencing the op-
052   timization of other task-specific parameters, akin to how a single decision maker’s decision affects
053   other decision makers’ utility in game theory. Thus, we formulate meta-learning as a Single-Leader
      Multi-Follower (SLMF) game (Xi et al., 2022) where the optimization process of task-specific pa-

076   As GBML has been proposed as an effective algorithm to solve the meta-learning problem, we
077
      first propose Nash-GBML as an algorithm to address meta-learning problems modeled as the SLMF
      game. As shown in Figure 1, while GBML assumes that tasks are independent, Nash-GBML consid-
078
      ers the interactions among tasks. Thus, solving meta-learning modeled as the SLMF game is equiv-
079
      alent to finding a general Stackelberg equilibrium among the leader and followers. Nash-GBML
080   consists of two levels of equilibrium-finding problems: (1) finding a Nash equilibrium among fol-
081   lowers (task-specific parameters) and (2) finding a general Stackelberg equilibrium of the leader
082   (meta-parameter) given a Nash equilibrium among the followers. Because Nash-GBML can con-
083   sider various forms of interactions among tasks, it is more expressive and capable of capturing
084   complex dependencies among tasks. Next, we discuss the iteration complexity and convergence of
085   the Nash-GBML algorithm.
086   The interaction among tasks can be modeled in various ways, reflecting the characteristics of the
087   domain. In this study, we introduce two penalty terms applied to the task loss function to model the
088   interaction among the task-specific parameters in a way that is universally applicable across various
089   domains. Because all task-specific parameters should be close to the meta-parameter to ensure they
090   can be obtained through a few gradient update steps, we first design a penalty term to regularize
091   the task-specific parameters to be close to each other. Then, to improve the worst-case performance
092   across tasks, we define another penalty term to apply a stronger regularization to the task-specific
093   parameter that are farther from the meta-parameter. We evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed
094
      meta-learning framework with the designed penalty terms and the Nash-GBML algorithm using
      three benchmark problems.

250   3.3   P ENALTY TERMS
251
252   Because Nash-GBML is the framework that incorporates a penalty term p into the task loss L for the
253   inner level problem of traditional GBML, the interaction among tasks is determined by the structure
254   of the penalty term. While the structure of the penalty term varies depending on the domain, we
255
      introduce the following two penalty terms, which are universally applicable across various domains
      as a guideline. Note that the following penalty terms are simply examples, and you can design your
256
      own penalty term depending on the domain characteristics.
257
258   Because meta-learning is for similar tasks sampled from the same task distribution, each task-
259   specific parameter ϕi should be close to each other for effective few-shot adaptation to fit new tasks.
260   Thus, we expect that adjusting the meta-parameter and each task-specific parameter to be closer in
261
      the inner level problem will minimize the meta-loss function F in the outer level problem. More-
      over, it serves as a clamp function for the gradient of the meta-loss function, allowing the algorithm
262
      to converge more stably. Based on this hypothesis, we design two penalty terms to penalize the
263
      distance between the meta-parameter θ and task-specific parameters ϕi .
264

355
356   In this section, we aim to validate the following questions regarding Nash-GBML experimentally:

359   we apply Nash-GBML across various domains such as regression and classification?
360   In this section, we set the same network architecture and same hyper-parameters of both the tra-

475   5     C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
476
477   We propose the meta-learning framework, which extends traditional meta-learning at the inner level
478   by considering the interaction among tasks. To account for the interaction among tasks at the inner
479   level, we incorporate a penalty term, which is affected by the joint task-specific parameters, into
480   the loss of traditional meta-learning. As a guideline, we introduce two penalty terms, which are
481   designed to reduce the average and worst-case task loss. Then, we experimentally validate that (1)
482
      the proposed penalty terms work well to achieve the designed purposes and (2) Nash-GBML is
      generally applicable to traditional GBML in various domains.
483
484   Because Nash-GBML is the framework generally applicable to GBML algorithms, it can be applied
485   to the other GBML algorithms not mentioned in this study. Moreover, because the proposed penalty
      terms do not reflect the characteristics of the domain, researchers can freely design new penalty


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      terms according to the target domain. In conclusion, this study proposes a new framework for meta-
487   learning that considers interaction among tasks, and the methodology to support it. We presents a
488   pathway for further expansion whenever a novel GBML algorithms or new penalty terms reflecting
489   domain characteristics are developed.
490
      We are interested in extending the meta-learning framework not only by adding a penalty term to the
491
      objective function but also by adding a joint constraint on the task-specific parameters to the strategy
492
      set in the inner level problem. We can model it as the generalized SLMF game. In the future, we
493   plan to apply Nash-GBML to other complex regression, classification, and RL domains. We also
494   plan to design a new penalty term that reflects the characteristics of the domain.
