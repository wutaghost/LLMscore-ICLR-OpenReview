009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The safety alignment ability of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) is prone to be
012            degraded by the integration of the vision module compared to its LLM backbone.
013            We investigate this phenomenon, dubbed as “safety alignment degradation” in this
014            paper, and show that the challenge arises from the representation gap that emerges
015            when introducing vision modality to VLMs. In particular, we show that the rep-
016            resentations of multi-modal inputs shift away from that of text-only inputs which
017            represent the distribution that the LLM backbone is optimized for. At the same
018            time, the safety alignment capabilities, initially developed within the textual em-
019
               bedding space, do not successfully transfer to this new multi-modal representation
               space. To reduce safety alignment degradation, we introduce Cross-Modality Rep-
020
               resentation Manipulation (CMRM), an inference time representation intervention
021
               method for recovering the safety alignment ability that is inherent in the LLM
022            backbone of VLMs, while simultaneously preserving the functional capabilities
023            of VLMs. The empirical results show that our framework significantly recovers
024            the alignment ability that is inherited from the LLM backbone with minimal im-
025            pact on the fluency and linguistic capabilities of pre-trained VLMs even without
026            additional training. Specifically, the unsafe rate of LLaVA-7B on multi-modal
027            input can be reduced from 61.53% to as low as 3.15% with only inference-time
028            intervention.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The development of Vision Language Models (VLMs) has marked a significant advancement, en-
035   abling models to process information from both visual and textual modalities and have shown
036   promising capabilities across various applications (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2024). However,
037   the integration of the vision module (as is widely adopted as the default architecture of VLMs (Liu
038   et al., 2024b;a; Dai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a)) degrades the overall alignment ability of a VLM
039
      compared to its LLM backbone, and we refer to this phenomenon as safety alignment degradation.
      For instance, LLaVA, built on the Vicuna-13b LLM, demonstrated a decline in MT-Bench (Zheng
040
      et al., 2023) performance from 6.57 to 5.92 (scored by GPT-4), even faring worse than the smaller
041
      Vicuna-7B model (Li et al., 2024c). The alignment degradation is even more crucial when it comes
042   to safety-related queries. For example, even the incorporation of a blank image, which may not carry
043   any semantics in most contexts, can break the safety alignment and trigger harmful responses from
044   the VLM (Gou et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024d).
045
      Several existing works have explored the phenomenon of safety alignment degradation. For exam-
046
      ple, Gou et al. (2024) attempt to transform unsafe images into texts to activate the intrinsic safety
047
      mechanism of pre-aligned LLMs in VLMs. However, images often contain fine-grained information
048   that could not be fully captured by texts. On the other hand, Li et al. (2024d) leverage the safety risks
049   posed by the visual modality and propose a jailbreak method that conceals and amplifies the mali-
050   cious intent within text inputs using carefully crafted images. However, the underlying mechanisms
051   of how images influence alignment remain unexplored. From the aspect of improving VLM safety, a
052   line of work has made successful attempts by training VLMs with deliberately curated dataset (Helff
053   et al., 2024; Zong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c). However, these attempts are annotation-intensive
      and computationally costly, ignoring the inherent safety alignment of the LLM backbone of a VLM.

054
      In this study, we propose to investigate the critical challenge of alignment degradation by examining
055   how the integration of a vision module intrinsically impacts model behavior, particularly in terms
056   of model representations (§2). Our hypothesis is that, since the vision and language modules within
057   a typical VLM are trained independently, the representations from these different modalities tend to
058   cluster in distinct regions of the latent space. This separation likely results in a distribution shift,
059   deviating from the representation space that the LLM backbone is optimized for, which further
060   leads to reduced alignment ability when the VLM processes multi-modal inputs. To verify this
061   hypothesis, we evaluate three open-source VLMs of varying scales and employ Principal Component
062   Analysis (PCA) to visualize their hidden states upon different types of input: text-only or a mixture
063
      of text and image. We find that in models’ representation space, different types of input are clearly
      distinguished, suggesting that our hypothesis holds.
064
065   Inspired by these findings, we further investigate whether alignment degradation can be mitigated by
066   eliminating the representation shift when an image is incorporated as input. To justify this assump-
067   tion, we present a method to intervene the hidden states of VLMs, named CMRM (Cross-Modality
068   Representation Manipulation) (§3). CMRM first anchors a VLM’s low-dimensional representation
069
      space and estimates the “shifting direction” that indicates the affect of the incorporation of image
      in the input on the overall hidden states. It then calibrates the representation of multi-modal input
070
      using the estimated direction so that the hidden states can be pulled closer to the distribution that the
071
      LLM component is optimized for.
072
073   Through experiments on two VLM safety benchmarks, we demonstrate that CMRM remarkably
074   recovers the alignment ability of VLMs even without additional training. Furthermore, CMRM
075
      does not compromise VLMs’ general performance, as evaluated on two VLM utility benchmarks.
      We hope our work sheds light on the intrinsic influences of the construction of VLMs, and inspires
076
      future research on VLM alignment. Our code will be open-sourced for reproducibility. In summary,
077
      our main contributions are as follows:
078
079          • We analyze the safety alignment degradation phenomenon of VLMs from the perspective
080            of model representations. Empirically, we demonstrate that the simple concatenation of
081            embedding from different modalities leads to representation shifting that suppresses the
082            alignment ability that is inherent in the LLM backbone.
083          • We introduce CMRM, a representation engineering method that calibrates the represen-
084            tation of multi-modal inputs, recovering model’s safety alignment ability by moving the
085            representation back to the distribution that the LLM backbone is optimized for.
086
             • CMRM significantly recovers the safety alignment from the LLM backbone to VLMs with-
087
               out sacrificing the general ability of VLMs. Empirical results show that CMRM can re-
088            cover the safety of a VLM to the level of its LLM backbone without additional training:
089            the unsafe rate of LLaVA-7B on multi-modal input can be reduced from 61.53% to as low
090            as 3.15% with only inference-time intervention.

185
186   Building on insights from §2, we attempt to formalize the affected hidden states of current VLMs
187   with vision input incorporated (§3.1), based on which we propose two variations of CMRM to
188   intervene model representations during inference time to prevent the alignment degradation (§3.2).
189
190   3.1   F ORMALIZATION OF VLM R EPRESENTATION : S HIFTED FROM O PTIMAL D ISTRIBUTION
191
192   Inspired by Favero et al. (2024), we propose to formalize the hidden states of multi-modal input
193   to VLMs as being shifted from the ideal representation that remains within the distribution of the
194
      LLM backbone while capturing extra visual information from the incorporated image in the input.
      Under the shifting assumption, we model the representations of a vanilla VLM h(x, img) as an
195
      interpolation between two scenarios: (1) the VLM with only text query as input, without any visual
196
      information or the impact of vision modality; (2) an ideal VLM that benefits from visual information
197   based on the image input while not being affected by the visual modality. Accordingly, we propose
198   the following formalization:
199
200                            h(x, img) = h∗ (x, img) + α[h(x, img′ ) − h(x)],                            (1)
201                                                                                               ′
      where img and x stand for the image input and textual instruction respectively, and img is a mean-
202   ingless image that does not carry any visual information. α ∈ [0, 1] is a mixing coefficient that
203   indicates the level of representation shift. When α is small, the shifting effect is mild and the rep-
204   resentation is not pulled much from the backbone LLM’s representation distribution. For higher α,
205   the VLM suffers from severe representation shifting.
206
      Given the modality affected original representation ho ≜ h(x, img), the representation of corrupted
207
      image as input hc ≜ h(x, img′ ), and the one of pure text input ht ≜ h(x), our goal is to find
208
      an estimate of the ideal representation distribution h∗ that is not affected by vision modality and
209   only benefits from additional visual information. According to our hypothesis, we assume that h∗
210   could be achieved by calibrating the distribution of original input, h∗ = ho + ∆, where ∆ is the
211   manipulation that we force on the hidden states of multi-modal inputs. Therefore we rewrite our
212   formalization in Eq. 1 as:
213                                            ∆ = α(ht − hc ).                                        (2)
214   Hence, we can estimate h∗ with the calibration term ∆, which brings us to the optimal intervention:
215
                                             h∗ = ho + α(ht − hc ).                                        (3)

      Note that if the original hidden states ho of multi-modal input is ideal enough (with α close to 0),
217   then our approximated h∗ is equivalent to ho . On the other hand, severely shifted representations
218   (with α close to 1) will be manipulated to be proportional to ho + ht − hc . In this case, ht − hc
219   captures the direction of shifting caused by the incorporation of image modality as input, which is
220   then added back to ho to pull the representation back to the hidden distribution of LLM backbone.

222   3.2   CMRM: R ECOVER A LIGNMENT A BILITY AT I NFERENCE T IME
223
224   Based on the formalization of VLMs under the affect of representation shifting (Eq. 1), we further
225   propose the hypothesis that alignment degradation can be mitigated by eliminating representation
226   shift when an image is incorporated as input, based on which we introduce CMRM (Cross-Modality
227   Representation Manipulation) to calibrate the shifted representation of multi-modal inputs according
228   to Eq. 3. Its core idea is to pull multi-modality representations back or closer to the distribution that
229
      the LLM backbone is optimized for, where the safety alignment capability was initially developed
      and fine-tuned for processing purely textual inputs.
230
231
232

      tion of visual input, which is, according to our assumption, correlated with the alignment degrada-
233
      tion phenomenon. As defined in the second term of Eq. 3, these shifting vectors are obtained by
234
      contrasting the representations of two types of variations on the input: text query only ht and text
235   query with corrupted image hc . To systematically analyze the shifting direction caused by visual
236   input incorporation, we propose two variations for shifting vector extraction: dataset-level extrac-
237   tion and sample-level extraction. Each method offers unique insights into how the model’s internal
238   representations are affected by the introduction of corrupted visual data.
239

      a case-by-case investigation.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
475
476   5.1   S AFETY A LIGNMENT FOR VLM S
477
      Alignment refers to the process of fine-tuning pre-trained models with annotations based on human
478
      preferences, to ensure that the generated responses of the models are Helpful, Honest, and Harm-
479
      less, i.e., the 3H principle (Askell et al., 2021). This practice first thrives for LLMs where RLHF
480   (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) has
481   proven to be an effective approach for aligning LLMs with human values. DPO (Direct Preference
482   Optimization) (Rafailov et al., 2023) further enhances the efficacy and efficiency of RLHF by di-
483   rectly optimizing LLMs based on human preferences so that the constrained reward maximization
484   problem can be optimized exactly with a single stage of policy training. In multi-modal scenarios,
485   efforts have been made to adapt these alignment methods to VLMs either on creating multi-modal
      preference data (Li et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Pi et al.,


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2024b; Helff et al., 2024) or specifically designed learning objectives (Wang et al., 2024a; Li et al.,
487   2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c).
488
489   Specifically, to enhance safety alignment for VLMs, a straightforward approach involves aligning
490
      VLMs with specially-constructed red-teaming data (Chen et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b; Zong et al.,
      2024). However, red-teaming is labor-intensive and may not encompass all potential failure cases.
491
      At the same time, these attempts overlook the fact that the LLM backbone within VLMs may al-
492
      ready have undergone substantial safety alignment. Consequently, retraining the entire VLM with
493   additional red-teaming data can be an inefficient use of computational resources. Another approach
494   focuses on protecting VLMs during inference time (Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023b; Pi et al.,
495   2024a; Gou et al., 2024), among which the most relevant to ours is the work by Wang et al. (2024b).
496   They employ safety steering vectors to adjust VLM activation in response to unsafe inputs. How-
497   ever, this may overlook unsafe intents in images that are not detectable by text-centric safety vectors.
498   Instead, our proposed CMRM inspects the shifting of internal representation caused by multi-modal
499   input and calibrates the representations to be closer to the distribution of the LLM backbone to acti-
500   vate its intrinsic alignment ability.
501
502
503   5.2   R EPRESENTATION E NGINEERING
504
505   Representation engineering is a set of alignment techniques that work by making targeted pertur-
506   bations to a model’s hidden states (Subramani et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2023; Turner et al.,
507   2023). Li et al. (2024a) propose inference-time intervention (ITI), to shift representations along
508
      the directions identified by linear probes within those truthfulness-related attention heads to elicit
      more truthful outputs. Zou et al. (2023) develop RepE to identify and extract representations corre-
509
      sponding to high-level concepts such as honesty and safety in LLMs. They use a “reading vector”
510
      generated from the activations on datasets related to the specific concepts to steer model behavior.
511   Directed Representation Optimization (DRO) Zheng et al. (2024) treats safety prompts as trainable
512   embeddings and learns to move the queries’ representations along or opposite the refusal direction,
513   depending on their harmfulness. Similarly, InferAligner Wang et al. (2024b) extracts safety-related
514   vectors from safety-aligned LLMs to indicate the direction of safe input and intervene on inputs with
515   harmful intents for safe responses. These works focus on identifying differences in representations
516   of LLMs based on specific features, such as safety or truthfulness. In contrast, we propose that
517   VLMs exhibit distinct representations depending on the input type (pure textual or multi-modal),
518   and this shifting in representation can lead to a decline in alignment ability. Our proposed CMRM
519
      attempts to recover the inherent alignment ability of the LLM module for VLMs by reversing the
      representation shift.
520

523   6     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      We investigate the impact of incorporating visual input on VLMs. We find that multi-modal in-
526
      put drastically degrades the safety alignment mechanism of the LLM backbone in VMLs, while
527
      intrinsically shifting the hidden states away from the distribution that the LLM module is opti-
528   mized for. Drawing this inspiration, our proposed CMRM method intervenes the representations of
529   VLMs upon multi-modal inputs by moving hidden states closer to the trained distribution of its LLM
530   module. CMRM operates at inference time and can be seamlessly integrated with any pre-trained
531   VLMs, which makes CMRM a cost-effective and flexible solution to enhance safety alignment of
532   VLMs without tedious training. We show that CMRM brings remarkable improvement in recov-
533   ering the safety alignment for VLMs without compromising the model’s general performance. We
534   hope the empirical analysis and the proposed methodology in this work can inspire future design
535   and improvement of VLMs.
536   Our solution does slightly increase the computational overhead during model inference, and this
537   work focuses on the safety aspect of VLMs. We believe that the degradation phenomenon exists in
538   other aspects of VLMs, such as reasoning ability and faithfulness. In the future, we plan to extend
539   our scope to tackle and address the challenges that arise in other domains of VLMs. Additionally, we
      intend to investigate the features that make an optimal anchor dataset for shifting vector extraction.
