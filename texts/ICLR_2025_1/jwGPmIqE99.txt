011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-4 have revolutionized natural language
014            processing, showing remarkable linguistic proficiency and reasoning capabilities.
015            However, their application in strategic multi-agent decision-making environments
016
               is hampered by significant limitations including poor mathematical reasoning, dif-
               ficulty in following instructions, and a tendency to generate incorrect informa-
017
               tion. These deficiencies hinder their performance in strategic and interactive tasks
018
               that demand adherence to nuanced game rules, long-term planning, exploration
019            in unknown environments, and anticipation of opponents’ moves. To overcome
020            these obstacles, this paper presents a novel LLM agent framework equipped with
021            memory and specialized tools to enhance their strategic decision-making capabil-
022            ities. We deploy the tools in a number of economically important environments,
023            in particular bilateral bargaining and multi-agent and dynamic mechanism design.
024            We employ quantitative metrics to assess the framework’s performance in various
025            strategic decision-making problems. Our findings show that our enhanced frame-
026            work significantly improves strategic decision-making capability of LLMs. While
027
               we highlight the inherent limitations of current LLMs, we demonstrate the im-
               provements through targeted enhancements, suggesting a promising direction for
028
               future developments in LLM applications for interactive environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional proficiency in generating coherent
034
      natural language from textual inputs (Bubeck et al., 2023). They display human-like strategic think-
035
      ing and excel at flexible reasoning with nuanced, context-specific information (Aher et al., 2022;
036   Kwon et al., 2023; Suzgun et al., 2022). These successes have sparked interest in their potential for
037   decision-making in complex environments (Yao et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023).
038
039
      To further integrate LLMs into our society, such as deploying them as fiduciary agents on behalf of
      individuals or organizations in a competitive environment where human and AI agents coexist, the
040
      ability to reason strategically is of vital importance. However, due to their inherent limitations in
041
      basic mathematics (Bubeck et al., 2023), instruction following (Jang et al., 2022), and susceptibility
042   to hallucinations (Chen et al., 2023), the following challenges exist: (i) LLMs may fail to accu-
043   rately interpret game rules and objectives expressed in natural language, e.g., form a well-defined
044   utility function that reflects their preference over possible outcomes (Guo et al., 2023); (ii) LLMs
045   are generally inept at long-horizon planning to maximize their utility, which is essential in scenarios
046   where decisions have extended consequences (Huang et al., 2024); (iii) They exhibit poor capabili-
047   ties in strategic exploration of unknown environments (Krishnamurthy et al., 2024), which hampers
048   their ability to optimize decisions on unforeseen conditions; (iv) LLMs have limited capacity in an-
049   ticipating opponents’ moves and adapting their strategies accordingly (Park et al., 2024), which is
050
      crucial for any competitive interaction. These limitations collectively underscore the challenges in
      deploying LLMs for nuanced and dynamic strategic reasoning tasks.
051
052   This paper seeks to improve the LLM agent’s reasoning capabilities in these scenarios with the help
053   of tools and external memory. For evaluation, we choose a collection of decision-making problems
      that highlight the aforementioned challenges in significant and economically relevant real-world

080   and (iv) (Rubinstein, 1982; Fudenberg & Tirole, 1991); Markov Decision Process (MDP), where the

086   not only the interpretation and response to multiple rounds of dialogue, but also the computation of
087   optimal actions based on the information extracted during interactions. This goes beyond typical
088   LLM tasks, where output is often limited to text generation or language understanding without
089   involving complex strategic reasoning (Yang et al., 2018; Shridhar et al., 2020). Additionally, unlike
090   tasks in which LLMs generate code to solve static math problems (Cobbe et al., 2021; Imani et al.,
091   2023), our agent operates in a dynamic and evolving environment, where it continually interacts with
092   the environment, gathering new knowledge and performing new computations with each interaction.
093   In light of these unique challenges, we propose a novel LLM agent framework, named STRIDE,
094   which is specifically designed for the multi-step reasoning in STRategic and Interactive DEcision-
095   making problems. The LLM, which serves as the controller of the whole framework, orchestrates
096   the reasoning process through a sequence of structured Thought units. Each Thought unit, in ad-
097   dition to typical textual reasoning (Yao et al., 2022), also outlines a series of operations, which
098   are predefined Python functions managing the low-level calculations in various decision-making
099
      scenarios. Additionally, an external working memory is integrated to preserve crucial parameters.
      Therefore, Challenge (i) can be addressed by executing an operation that evaluates the agent’s utility
100
      in the Thought unit. Challenge (ii), which is mainly caused by the information loss in long-context
101
      (Liu et al., 2024), can be addressed by extracting and storing important problem parameters and
102   intermediate results in the working memory. Challenges (iii) and (iv) can be addressed through a
103   combination of operations that perform strategic exploration or belief updates. We also explored
104   equipping STRIDE with the ability to synthesize operations on-the-fly when predefined ones are un-
105   available. Through an extensive evaluation of the selected decision-making problems, we show that,
106   with few in-context examples, STRIDE can make strategic decisions on new problem instances with
107   high success rate. This highlights the transformative potential of integrating LLMs with specialized
      tools, memory, and control structures to enhance strategic decision-making capabilities.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Evaluating LLMs’ Reasoning in Strategic Environments. Recent studies have investigated
111   LLMs’ capacity for strategic reasoning in settings such as matrix games (e.g., Dictator and Pris-
112   oner’s Dilemma) (Brookins & DeBacker, 2023; Lorè & Heydari, 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Akata
113   et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023), focusing on zero-shot prompting to evaluate their ability to act strate-
114   gically with minimal input. Davidson et al. (2024) and Bianchi et al. (2024) extended this line of
115   work to bargaining games, showing that while LLMs can produce plausible strategies, they often
116   lack consistency and a deep understanding of game dynamics. Few-shot chain-of-thought (CoT)
117   prompting has been proposed to enhance strategic reasoning in matrix and multi-turn bargaining
118   games (Gandhi et al., 2023), but results indicate persistent challenges with complex rules or long
119
      horizons. Similarly, Huang et al. (2024) highlighted LLMs’ difficulty in generalizing reasoning
      across diverse game contexts. While these studies advance understanding of LLMs in strategic en-
120
      vironments, they treat LLMs as isolated models, relying solely on intrinsic reasoning capabilities
121
      without leveraging tools or memory—a gap our work aims to address.
122
123   Optimization in Static and Structured Contexts. In contrast to dynamic strategic settings, LLMs
124   have been applied to solve static, well-structured optimization problems (Ramamonjison et al., 2023;
125
      Tang et al., 2024), where textual problem descriptions are translated into Python code to compute
      predefined objectives. Some works integrate tools and memory (Xiao et al., 2023; AhmadiTeshnizi
126
      et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023), yet these approaches focus on producing single, one-off solutions with-
127
      out iterative adaptation or interaction. Our framework differs fundamentally by addressing dynamic
128   decision-making, requiring LLMs to adapt computations and strategies to evolving environments.
129   Moreover, beyond generating solutions, our approach emphasizes articulating algorithmic reasoning
130   in natural language, as shown in Figure 2. This enhances both interpretability and adaptability—a
131   critical capability absent in static optimization contexts.
132
      Broader Applications of LLM-based Agents. LLM-based agents have also found applications
133
      in diverse domains, including social simulation to model human behavior (Park et al., 2023; Aher
134   et al., 2023), scientific research for automating experiment design and execution (Boiko et al., 2023),
135   software development using collaborative agents (Qian et al., 2023), and robotics for advanced ma-
136   nipulation and navigation (Ahn et al., 2022). These systems often employ modular architectures with
137   memory (Zhu et al., 2023) and planning modules (Yao et al., 2022), enabling adaptability in dynamic
138   scenarios. However, they prioritize context-aware interactions and flexibility over achieving optimal
139   behavior. While this underscores the broad applicability of LLM agents, our work addresses the
140   distinct challenge of designing agents that not only adapt to dynamic and evolving environments but
141   also consistently achieve optimal performance—bridging the gap between flexibility and precision.

145   As shown on the left side of Figure 2, our primary strategy to address the four challenges in Sec-
146   tion 1 is to provide the LLM with an operation library, i.e., a set of Python functions taking care
147   of lower-level computation in various decision-making problems and a working memory retaining
148   important parameters. Most importantly, we introduce a reasoning module that acts as the central ex-
149   ecutive, orchestrating the information flow among components and synthesizing structured Thought
150   sequences as illustrated on the left side of Figure 2 to solve complex problems.

200   Error Handling and Reflection. Some additional measures are used to improve the robustness of
201
      the reasoning process. Before executing the selected operations, the Thought unit undergoes a vali-
      dation process based on predefined rules to ensure its integrity. For example, a common rule applied
202
      in our experiments is the mutual exclusivity of the exit condition and the presence of operations:
203
      the Thought unit must not simultaneously specify an exit as true while containing non-empty opera-
204   tions, as this often indicates a premature termination of the reasoning process. If this conflict occurs,
205   the system will generate an appropriate error message and prompt the LLM to revise the Thought
206   unit. This mechanism ensures that operations proceed only with validated and logically consistent
207   instructions. Enhanced applications of this functionality involve utilizing an additional LLM to ver-
208   ify whether the newly generated Thought unit adheres to the reasoning logic and language style
209   presented in the in-context examples. This step can improve consistency and prevent hallucinations.
210   With the Thought unit validated, the selected operations will be executed in the specified order. The
211   outcomes of these operations are then utilized to generate the subsequent Thought unit. This process
212
      continues until Exit is set to be true, signaling the completion of the reasoning process.
213   Working Memory. As mentioned in Section 1, for long-horizon planning, LLMs may forget or
214   neglect important information mentioned early in the context. Moreover, an accurate description
215   of the problem instance sometimes require parameters of high dimensions, e.g., transition matrices
      of MDP. In this case, storing these parameters in the context history is costly and prone to error.

      Therefore, STRIDE is equipped with a working memory, i.e., a Python dictionary, that stores the
217   parameters extracted from the context, as well as intermediate results computed by the operations.
218
219   Operation Library. What sets our work apart from methods like ReAct (Yao et al., 2022) and
220
      Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024) is the sophisticated integration of the operations by the Thought se-
      quence to execute complex calculations during text-based reasoning and interactions. For instance,
221
      these operations can calculate the utility of the agent based on the outcomes of a game or update the
222
      belief about uncertainty on the environment or the other agents. A combination of such operations
223   allows STRIDE to implement various algorithmic behaviors such as dynamic programming to solve
224   MDPs and Bargaining Games, facilitating a deeper and more precise decision-making process. They
225   also let STRIDE scale to complex problems by abstracting detailed computations. This scalability is
226   crucial in handling larger and more challenging scenarios. In addition to strategic decision-making,
227   STRIDE offers a flexible framework that can be extended to a diverse array of problem domains,
228   where algorithmic behavior of LLMs is critical. To tailor STRIDE to other domains, it suffices
229   to construct domain-specific operations and in-context examples to emulate other algorithms using
230   these operations. As we will see in the sequel, STRIDE can be applied to MDP, dynamic mechanism
231
      design, two-player bargaining games, Tic-Tac-Toe, Connect-N, etc.
232   Generation of In-Context Examples In-context examples teach the LLM to combine operations in
233   a structured way to emulate reference algorithms—standard methods for solving decision-making
234   problems, such as backward induction for computing SPE in bargaining games and value iteration
235   for computing optimal Q-values in MDPs. These algorithms serve as benchmarks for the target
236   behaviors we aim to replicate. To generate effective in-context examples:
237   • Implement Reference Algorithms: Each reference algorithm is implemented using operations
238     provided in the library, such as CalcUtil and BackwardOneStep in Figure 2. These modular
239     implementations ensure that the algorithms are expressed in terms of the same reusable operations
240     the LLM will employ, making the connection between the algorithm and the operations explicit.
241   • Add Explanatory Comments: Each algorithm step is annotated with natural language comments
242     explaining its purpose and logic. For well-known algorithms, such as value iteration for MDPs,
243
        these explanations can often be generated automatically by LLMs. However, for less popular or
        novel solutions, manual descriptions are necessary to ensure the logic is accurately conveyed.
244
      • Generate Worked Examples: The augmented algorithms are run on sampled problem instances,
245
        producing sequences of operation calls, intermediate results, and explanatory comments to demon-
246     strate how to solve the problem using the operations.
247
248   The definition of reference algorithms and the added comments are given in Appendix B for clarity
249
      and reproducibility. This process equips the LLM with clear, structured demonstrations, making it
      reason and compute effectively while aligning its behavior with the logic of the reference algorithms.

252   3.2   O PERATION S YNTHESIS
253
254   As outlined in Section 1, our goal is to integrate LLMs into real-world applications where they can
255
      act as fiduciary agents in competitive environments. To ensure the robustness and reliability needed
      for these tasks, STRIDE is primarily built to use pre-defined, validated operations. This approach
256
      allows us to optimize complex computations and minimizes the risk of errors from on-the-fly code
257
      generation. By providing reusable and modular components, we ensure reliable performance while
258   allowing the LLM to focus on its strength—synthesizing high-level workflows, as shown in Figure
259   2, and adapting to various contexts without handling low-level computations. Nevertheless, we have
260   included functionality that allows STRIDE to dynamically generate operations when pre-defined
261   ones are unavailable or when robustness is less critical. These cases could benefit from the LLM’s
262   flexibility to create on-demand solutions. While this feature showcases the LLM’s adaptability,
263   it remains secondary to our framework’s primary focus on the stability provided by pre-defined
264   operations in structured, high-stakes decision-making tasks.
265   Specifically, we enable this functionality by augmenting the Thought structure depicted in Figure 2
266   with an optional field called new operation. Therefore, as illustrated in Figure 3, apart from choos-
267   ing to call the operations available in the library, STRIDE can opt to create a new operation via
268   new operation, which specifies the name of the new operation and a description of its functionality.
269   Then an iterative procedure is triggered which alternates between generating Python code to imple-
      ment this functionality by the LLM and executing the code on a copy of the working memory to get

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper presented the STRIDE framework, enhancing LLMs’ strategic decision-making capabil-
531
      ities. Through integrating a structured Thought process with external working memory and opera-
      tions, STRIDE enables LLMs to overcome significant limitations such as strategic exploration and
532
      dynamic opponent interaction. Our evaluations across diverse decision-making scenarios validate
533
      STRIDE’s effectiveness, suggesting its potential as a robust tool for strategic thinking in complex en-
534   vironments. For further development of the STRIDE framework, we propose the following research
535   avenues. (i) Currently, STRIDE utilize specially designed Python functions as tools to facilitate the
536   formation of strategies and the choice of actions by the agents in bilateral bargaining, an interesting
537   direction is to replace it with models trained using data collected during interactions. (ii) Fine-tuning
538   on the Thought Sequence: To further enhance LLM’s understanding and execution of the Thought
539   sequence as well as the associated operations, we can fine-tune the model on the Thought sequences
      resulting in successful decisions.

734   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). In this way, the reasoning process is
735   hard-coded in programming language like Python, which defines how different components interact
736   with each other. Currently, the most prevalent approach in this domain is the integration of LLMs
737   into Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms, where they typically serve as tree traversal policy
738   (Zhao et al., 2024), action pruner (Liu et al., 2023), world model (Hao et al., 2023), and evaluation
739   function (Liu et al., 2023). In comparison, our approach is more flexible in the sense that we can
740
      repurpose the reasoning process of STRIDE to emulate different algorithmic behaviors. In particular,
      as demonstrated in our experiments, apart from the model-based algorithms like UCB-VI, we can
741
      also make STRIDE reason as tree-search algorithms like MCTS and Minimax.

      B.4    BACKWARD I NDUCTION FOR BARGAINING IN I NCOMPLETE I NFORMATION S ETTING
900
901   Since the seller is uncertain about the value b of the buyer, at each time step t the seller decides the
902   offer price pt based on his/her belief constructed using observations up to time step t − 1, which
903   is denoted as U(0, bt−1 ), i.e., the true value b is uniformly distributed in [0, bt−1 ] (with b0 = 1).
904   Therefore, different from SPE considered in complete information setting, SE specifies not only the
905   strategies of the players, but also the belief, which in our case is the sequence {b0 , b1 , . . . , bT −1 }. In
906   classic economics literature (Sobel & Takahashi, 1983; Cramton, 1984), this sequence is obtained
907   by: (i) backward induction from time T to time 1, which results in b0 expressed as a function of
908
      bT −1 ; (ii) as the initial belief b0 = 1, we can solve this equation to obtain the value of bT −1 . This
      provides an analytical form for {b0 , b1 , . . . , bT −1 } using the parameters δb , δs , T . To make the inner
909
      logic more transparent during reasoning, we replace this analytical solution with a bisection search
910
      when designing the reference algorithm for STRIDE, with its full description given in Algorithm 7.
