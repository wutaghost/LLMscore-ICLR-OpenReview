009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge Distillation (KD) has emerged as a promising approach for transferring
012            knowledge from a larger, more complex teacher model to a smaller student model.
013            Traditionally, KD involves training the student to mimic the teacher’s output prob-
014            abilities, while more advanced techniques have explored guiding the student to
015            adopt the teacher’s internal representations. Despite its widespread success, the
016            performance of KD in binary classification and few-class problems has been less
017            satisfactory. This is because the information about the teacher model’s gener-
018            alization patterns scales directly with the number of classes. Moreover, several
019
               sophisticated distillation methods may not be universally applicable or effective
               for data types beyond Computer Vision. Consequently, effective distillation tech-
020
               niques remain elusive for a range of key real-world applications, such as sentiment
021
               analysis, search query understanding, and advertisement-query relevance assess-
022            ment. Taking these observations into account, we introduce a novel method for
023            distilling knowledge from the teacher model’s representations, which we term
024            Learning Embedding Linear Projections (LELP). Inspired by recent findings about
025            the structure of final-layer representations, LELP works by identifying informative
026            linear subspaces in the teacher’s embedding space, and splitting them into pseudo-
027            subclasses. The student model is then trained to replicate these pseudo-subclasses.
028            Our experimental evaluations on large-scale NLP benchmarks like Amazon Re-
029            views and Sentiment140 demonstrate that LELP is consistently competitive with,
030
               and typically superior to, existing state-of-the-art distillation algorithms for binary
               and few-class problems, where most KD methods suffer.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   While deep neural networks have revolutionized Natural Language Processing (Devlin et al., 2018;
037   Brown et al., 2020), Computer Vision (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), and other fields, their bal-
038   looning size and data demands raise challenges. Recent research (Menghani, 2021) aims to develop
039
      efficient models that excel without needing massive datasets or expensive hardware. Knowledge
      Distillation (KD) (Buciluǎ et al., 2006; Hinton et al., 2015) is a powerful approach for generating
040
      lightweight models by leveraging a large teacher model to guide their training. In its basic form,
041
      the student model is trained to replicate the teacher’s output probabilities for each training instance.
042   Additionally, several subsequent studies (e.g., (Romero et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2018; Passalis &
043   Tefas, 2018; Ahn et al., 2019; Müller et al., 2020)) have proposed advanced distillation techniques
044   that go beyond mere output probability matching and focus on encouraging the student to learn the
045   teacher’s internal representations.
046
      While KD can significantly improve student model performance in tasks with numerous classes, its
047
      impact is less pronounced in binary classification and problems with a smaller number of classes.
048   As Müller et al. (2020) points out, this is because when we distill knowledge using logits or high-
049   temperature cross-entropy, the information about the teacher model’s generalization patterns scales
050   directly with the number of classes. Furthermore, Knowledge Distillation research has primarily
051   focused on Computer Vision, so many sophisticated distillation techniques are not always effective
052   or even suitable for other data modalities like Natural Language. As a result, effective distillation
053   techniques remain elusive for a range of critical real-world applications, such as sentiment analysis,
      search query understanding, and advertisement-query relevance assessment.

071   In light of these observations, in this paper we propose a novel approach for capturing information
072   from the teacher’s representations, which we call Learning Embedding Linear Projections (LELP).
073   At a high level, it works by extracting knowledge form the teacher’s last-layer representations
074   (embeddings) and converting it into pseudo-subclasses via linear projections. The student model is
075   then trained on these pseudo-subclasses, using a single unified cross-entropy loss.
076   Our approach leverages recent findings about the structure of final-layer representations (embeddings)
077   in deep learning models and, in particular, aspects of the phenomenon known as Neural Collapse (Pa-
078   pyan et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023). Similar in spirit to Subclass Distillation (Müller
079   et al., 2020), which uses a modified and retrained teacher model to identify hidden patterns, our
080   method achieves improved student performance, particularly in finetuning Language Models for tasks
081   with a few number of classes. Crucially though, and unlike Subclass Distillation, our approach does
082   not require any retraining of the teacher model. Finally, a key advantage of LELP is its flexibility
083
      in bridging diverse model architectures, making it uniquely versatile for teacher-student learning
      scenarios.
084
085   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
086
087
            1. Motivated by recent insights into the Neural Collapse phenomenon, we demonstrate that the
               invention of pseudo-subclasses through unsupervised clustering of teacher embeddings can
088
               enhance distillation performance in binary and few-class classification tasks. However, the
089
               efficacy of this approach is contingent upon the specific clustering algorithm employed.
090
091
            2. Through empirical evaluation, we observe that carefully calibrated linear projections con-
               sistently achieve high performance, leading us to introduce LELP: a novel method for
092
               enhancing Knowledge Distillation. LELP is modality-independent, producing particularly
093
               strong results in NLP tasks and situations where the teacher and student architectures differ.
094
            3. Empirical evaluations on large-scale NLP benchmarks like Amazon Reviews (5 classes, 500k
095
               examples, achieving an improvement of 1.85% over the best baseline) and Sentiment140
096
               (binary, 1.6 million examples, showing a 0.88% improvement over the best baseline) vali-

098            distillation algorithms for binary and few class problems, where most KD methods suffer.
099
            4. We show that LELP possesses several advantageous characteristics, including conveying a
100            large amount of information per example (data efficiency), converging faster than Vanilla
101            KD, and can provide substantial improvements in semi-supervised KD scenarios.

121   Knowledge Distillation. Most of the literature on Knowledge Distillation has been focused on
122   the fully supervised setting, i.e., when distillation is performed on the labeled training data of the
123   teacher model rather than on new, unlabeled data — see e.g. the original paper (Hinton et al., 2015).
124   Specifically, when training the student one typically uses a convex combination of the standard
125
      cross-entropy loss LCE with respect to the ground-truth labels, and the distillation loss Ldistill :
126                                   Lstudent = αLCE + (1 − α)Ldistill .                                 (1)
127   In the original paper (Hinton et al., 2015) the distillation loss encourages the student model to mimic
128   to replicate the teacher’s output probabilities, potentially after scaling the logits of both models
129   using a temperature-scalar. (The value of the temperature is typically larger than 1, and it is used to
130   emphasize the differences between the probabilities of wrong answers that would all be very close to
131   zero at temperature 1.)
132   In addition to focusing on the teacher model’s final outputs, follow-up methods, like those proposed
133   in (Romero et al., 2014; Ahn et al., 2019), also consider the teacher model’s internal representations,
134   often in the form of its embeddings. These methods encourage the student model to mimic not only
135   the teacher’s final predictions but also its internal representations. The simplest, yet often highly
136   effective, example of this approach is Embedding Distillation (Romero et al., 2014). In this method,
137   an additional term, called the “embedding-loss term”, is added to the distillation loss. This term
138   measures the difference between the embeddings produced by the teacher and student models. The
139   weight of this term can be adjusted using a hyperparameter. Specifically, one adds the term

      The Subclass Distillation method (Müller et al., 2020) presents an approach that is closely related to
      ours. Here, the teacher is forced to divide each class into many pseudo-subclasses that it invents via
154
      appropriate training, and then the student is trained to match the subclass probabilities. The method
155
      is designed for few-class classification, excelling in such scenarios. Our method shares Subclass
156   Distillation’s use of invented subclasses for student knowledge transfer, but eliminates the need
157   for teacher retraining and extensive hyperparameter tuning. (Note that the process of optimizing
158   hyperparameters for Subclass Distillation necessitates retraining the teacher model repeatedly with
159   varying loss function configurations, as outlined in equations (7) and (8) of the reference paper by
160   Müller et al. (2020). However, this iterative retraining becomes excessively computationally intensive
161   and impractical when dealing with large teacher models.) As we show in Section 4, LELP achieves
      performance that is always on par with, and typically exceeding, Subclass Distillation.

178   typically superior to them when it comes to classification problems with a few number of classes
179   (especially in the NLP domain). We also explore two more baseline methods that have found success

182   its reliance on data augmentation for contrastive learning limits its applicability to other domains.
183   Indeed, in Vision classification tasks the standard “contrastive-learning” approach would be to
184   generate variations of every image (e.g, via rotations), and then consider as positive any pair of
185
      examples (original or modified) that come from the same source. However, data augmentation of, say
      Natural Language examples, is not straightforward and this impairs the performance of the method.
186
      (See Appendix H for more details.) DKD has also demonstrated efficacy in multi-class Vision tasks,
187
      but its performance is notably compromised in scenarios with fewer classes, rendering it inappropriate
188   for the specific focus of our investigation. In particular, in the context of binary classification, DKD
189   is mathematically equivalent to the standard Vanilla KD approach. We demonstrate that LELP
190   significantly outperforms both CRD and DKD in text classification tasks.
191
      Neural Collapse. Papyan et al. (2020) identified a phenomenon called Neural Collapse that occurs in
192
      the final training phase (after achieving zero training error) of deep neural networks. This phenomenon
193
      refers to specific structural properties observed in the last layer’s representations. The simplest of
194   these properties is known as variability collapse. In a nutshell, as a network is trained extensively, it
195   tends to group together all training samples with the same label (almost) into a single point in its final
196   layer.
197
      Our approach is inspired by a recent paper Yang et al. (2023) which provides evidence that further
198
      refines the above description. In particular, it shows that while the final layer representations appear
199
      collapsed, they retain crucial fine-grained structure. This structure, though subtle, accurately reflects
200   the inherent characteristics of the input distribution. As an illustrative example, the authors consider
201   a model trained to classify images in the CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009) dataset using only 5
202   coarse-graned labels (by combining two classes into one superclass). Remarkably, even after this
203   training, they can still recover the original, more detailed 10-class system by applying unsupervised
204   clustering techniques to the model’s internal representations. In light of this intriguing observation,
205   and motivated by the findings of Müller et al. (2020) that creating meaningful pseudo-subclasses
206   aids student knowledge transfer, we present an unsupervised technique to extract knowledge from
207   teacher embeddings using linear projections to form pseudo-subclasses. Our approach, which we
208
      compare to other clustering methods (including the one in Yang et al. (2023)) in Table 3 in Section 4.2,
      consistently shows superior performance.

      3    L EARNING FROM E MBEDDING L INEAR P ROJECTIONS (LELP)
213
214   Since large models develop embeddings that hold information not captured in their output probabilities
215   (Yang et al., 2023), we aim to transfer this knowledge to student networks while adhering to three
      desiderata:


                                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Modality-independent. We aim to develop a data-agnostic method, meaning its performance remains
217   consistent regardless of the underlying data type. The method should not be reliant on characteristics
218   specific to certain data modalities (for instance, the ability to easily perform data augmentation).
219
220
      Compatibility between differing Student/Teacher architectures. Embedding Distillation, the most
      straightforward method for learning the teacher’s embedding information, only works out-of-the-
221
      box when the student and teacher have matching embedding dimensions. Otherwise, a learnable
222
      projection layer is required to match the student/teacher embedding dimensions, which can often
223   harm performance (and even increase latency in certain cases). Therefore, we aim for a method that
224   is embedding dimension agnostic.
225
226
      No Retraining the Teacher Model. When the teacher model is significantly larger than the student
      model, retraining it can be prohibitively expensive. Methods which require a modified teacher training
227
      pipeline are significantly more costly to tune hyperparameters for, as not only do the teacher models
228
      have to be retrained multiple times, but in addition the student hyperparameters need to be checked
229   against varying teacher hyparameters. This precludes methods such as Subclass Distillation (Müller
230   et al., 2020), which contains several teacher training hyperparameters, and can even hurt the teacher’s
231   performance.
232
      With these requirements in mind, we present Learning Embedding Linear Projections (LELP), which
233
      extracts the information in the teacher’s embedding layer into pseudo-subclasses, on which the
234
      student is trained in a single unified cross-entropy loss. To motivate our method, we refer to the
235   toy model of the learned teacher embeddings shown in Figure 2. Here, data points cluster around
236   their respective class averages, but each cluster exhibits internal structure that holds semantically
237   meaningful information. This structure differentiates individual items within the same class and
238   has proven valuable for identifying subclasses when they exist Müller et al. (2020). Additionally,
239   (Yang et al., 2023) shows that these subclasses can be separated using a linear probe (following an
240   appropriate clustering of the embeddings space), which motivates the use of linear projections as a
241   means of extracting further information for distillation.
242   LELP extracts this embedding information into a single classification loss in three steps. Firstly,
243   we identify meaningful linear subspaces in the teacher embedding space (section 3.1). Secondly,
244   we project the teacher embeddings into these subspaces, and use them to form pseudo-subclasses,
245   expanding the number of classes from C to S × C, where S is the number of linear projections
246   we employ per class (section 3.2). Finally, we perform standard knowledge distillation, where
247   additionally the student network’s final layer outputs probabilities for S × C classes (section 3.3).

      4.1       T HE S ETUP

335
      We focus our experiments on a variety of classification tasks, using a standard distillation setup as in
      (Hinton et al., 2015). In order to focus solely on the effect of the distillation loss of each method,
336
      we always set α = 0 in equation 1. This reduces the variance between methods which may have
337
      different optimal values of α, and reduces the hyperparameter search space. Furthermore, in the
338   important case of the semi-supervised setting one does not have access to ground-truth labels.
339
340   The Architectures Given that the specific combination of student and teacher architectures is
341
      known to influence the effectiveness of knowledge distillation, we have chosen to evaluate various
      student-teacher pairings to ensure the robustness of our method.
342
343   For Vision datasets, the “ideal" distillation scenario is given by distilling ResNet-92 to ResNet-56,
344   where both architectures and embeddings dimensions match (D = 256). We also consider the case
345   of a smaller and a larger dimension with ResNet-92 (D = 256) and MobileNet with width and depth
346   multiplier equal to 2 (D = 2048), respectively, distilling to MobileNet (D = 1024). The latter cases
347
      address scenarios where the embedding dimensions differ, with one scenario involving the same
      architecture family and the other involving different architectures. For smaller NLP datasets (LMRD,
348
      GLUE/cola, and GLUE/sst2), we consider distillation from an ALBERT-Large model (D = 1024)
349
      to an ALBERT-Base model (D = 768). For the larger scale NLP datasets (based on Amazon US
350   reviews and Sentiment 140) we consider distillation from an ALBERT-XXL model (D = 4096) and
351   an ALBERT-XL model (D = 2048) to (i) an ALBERT-Base model (D = 768); (ii) and a two-layer-
352   MLP of width (D = 4096) that operates over representations generated by a (frozen) sentence-T5
353   encoder model of 11 billion parameters (Ni et al., 2021). The latter case addresses the scenario
354   involving different teacher-student architectures but with the same embedding dimension. (Using a
355   pre-trained, frozen large-scale encoder model to generate representations is a common-in-practice
356   approach when one needs multiple lightweight models for different classification tasks.)
357   The Baselines To establish a baseline performance, we chose well-known distillation approaches.
358   In particular, we consider Standard Training of the model with the ground-truth labels and the
359   cross-entropy loss, Vanilla Distillation with temperature-scaling as described in the original paper

398   4.2      I NVESTIGATING METHODS FOR INVENTING PSEUDO - SUBCLASSES
399
400   In this section we investigate the extent to which inventing pseudo-subclasses via unsupervised
401   clustering of the teacher model’s embeddings can be beneficial for distillation. Our investigation
402   focuses on tasks with inherent subclass structure, driven by the hypothesis that class embeddings
403   contain rich and informative structural details. Specifically, we utilize binarized versions of CIFAR-10
404   and CIFAR-100, assigning binary labels based on the original class labels (ybinary = yoriginal %2).
405
      These datasets present a unique challenge for knowledge distillation, as vanilla distillation is known
      to underperform due to limited class label information.
406
407   Crucially, the availability of original labels (yoriginal ) in these subclass datasets allows us to explore
408   the "Oracle Clustering" approach. This entails training the student model on the full 10/100-way
409   classification task for CIFAR-10/100, respectively, and subsequently treating the problem as binary
410   classification during testing. Interestingly, the student models trained utilizing the Oracle Clustering
411
      approach exhibit the highest performance among all evaluated clustering methods. This approach not
      only outperforms other clustering strategies but also surpasses the performance of the original teacher
412
      network in certain cases. A t-SNE visualization of the feature embeddings learned by the student
413
      models, presented in Figure 4, offers a deeper understanding of the underlying factors contributing to
414   this observed performance advantage. While Oracle Clustering represents an idealized scenario where
415   subclass structure is known a priori and thus impractical for real-world datasets, its consideration
416   underscores the potential power of inventing pseudo-subclasses as a methodological approach.
417
      We consider several natural ways of inventing pseudo-subclasses by clustering the teacher’s em-
418
      bedding space and we compare them with LELP. In particular, we consider three approaches: (i)
419
      Agglomerative clustering (complete linkage); (ii) K-means clustering; and (iii) K-means clustering
420   after we first have projected the teacher’s embedding space in a two-dimensional space using t-
421   SNE (Hinton & Roweis, 2002) as proposed in (Yang et al., 2023). For these methods, we tested both
422   hard-label (one-hot vector) and soft-label (weighted by teacher probabilities) representations of the
423   subclasses during student training. We observed that soft-labels, even with temperature tuning, did
424   not significantly impact performance. Therefore, we present results using only hard-label (one-hot
425   vector) subclass representations, which can be found in Table 1 (see also Figure 3). Details on the
426   number of clusters chosen can be found in Appendix H.3.
427   Analysis of Table 1 reveals several key findings: (i) LELP consistently outperforms all other clustering
428   methods as well as Vanilla KD across all experimental scenarios; (ii) t-SNE & K-means generally
429   demonstrate superior performance to Vanilla KD; (iii) Agglomerative clustering and K-means clus-
430   tering exhibit more varied results in comparison to Vanilla KD. Thus, our findings suggest that the
431   generation of pseudo-subclasses via clustering methodologies holds significant potential, however,
      the efficacy of this approach is contingent upon the specific clustering algorithm employed.

465
466   In this section we compare LELP with other distillation baselines.
467
468   4.3.1      WARMUP : B INARY C LASSIFICATION WITH S UBCLASS S TRUCTURE
469   As a warmup, we first focus on on binary datasets that have an inherent subclass structure following
470   the setting of Section 4.2. The results can be found in Table 3 in Appendix B. Among the baselines,
471   Subclass Distillation often performs the best, consistent with the finding that subclass-splitting
472   is effective with little label information (Müller et al., 2020). Our method is able to capture the
473   learned structure from the teacher subclasses without retraining the more expensive teacher model and
474   outperforms all baselines. It’s worth noting that our method achieves the highest average improvement
475   over the leading baseline when applied to the CIFAR-100bin dataset, specifically when transferring
476   knowledge from a ResNet teacher model to a MobileNet student model. This scenario is particularly
477
      challenging due to the presence of numerous subclasses and significant differences between the
      teacher and student models in terms of both embedding dimensions and architectures. Interestingly,
478
      our method even outperforms Oracle Clustering in the CIFAR-10bin ResNet to MobileNet case,
479
      suggesting that it leverages structure that is learned by the teacher that is not present in the original
480   CIFAR-10 labels.
481
482   4.3.2      F EW-C LASS C LASSIFICATION WITHOUT S UBCLASS S TRUCTURE
483
484   In this section we focus on datasets without subclass structure. We consider classification on six
485   language classification tasks: the Large Movie Review dataset (Maas et al., 2011), two GLUE
      datasets: cola and sst2 (Wang et al., 2018), two datasets sampled from the Amazon US reviews

487   Details are given in Appendix D. Our results are shown in Table 2. Notably, Subclass Distillation is

      5   L IMITATIONS
497
498   While our method is simple and efficient to implement, there may be limitations in using a simple
499   linear projection on the teacher final layer embeddings to extract subclass data. Firstly, there is no
500
      reason to assume that the subclasses should be linearly separable in the teacher embedding, and it
      is likely that more sophisticated unsupervised clustering methods could extract richer information.
501
      Second, LELP performs when there is limited teacher logit information (such as in binary classi-
502
      fication tasks), however larger image datasets with many classes contain sufficient information in
503   their teacher logits, obviating the need of subclass splitting methods such as LELP. Indeed, as the
504   number of classes increases, we anticipate LELP’s performance to converge with vanilla knowledge
505   distillation. (Consequently, we did not present experiments on datasets with a large number of classes,
506   such as ImageNet-1k, since LELP is not designed for such scenarios.)
507
508
509   6   C ONCLUSION
510
511   In this study, we have presented evidence that the creation of pseudo-subclasses via unsupervised
512   clustering of teacher embeddings can improve distillation performance in binary and few-class
513   classification tasks, without necessitating the retraining of the teacher model. Through empirical
514   evaluation, we observed that linear projections consistently yield high performance, prompting us
515   to introduce LELP. The superior performance of the "Oracle Clustering" method, where subclass
516
      structure is known a priori, suggests that the generation of pseudo-subclasses through clustering
      techniques has substantial promise. Consequently, future research can explore more sophisticated
517
      methods for extracting teacher embedding information, drawing insights from the expanding body of
518
      work on Neural Collapse, or investigate strategies for distilling intermediate layer embeddings using
519   a similar approach.

680   One of the key design choices in LELP is to use the top PCA directions to construct the pseudo-
681
      subclasses. This is based on the intuition that axis which contain the most variation also contain the
      most information for distillation. Here we test this assumption by comparing PCA projections against
682
      three baselines. The first baseline is random projections (Rand), in which we choose S random
683
      orthogonal directions to use as our subclass direction. The second baseline is raw PCA, which is
684   using the top-K PCA directions, without the additional projection and random rotation applied, as we
685   discussed in Section 3. This corresponds to using vectors vc,s instead of ṽc,s . Our final baseline is
686   the “Identity" projection, which is the same as using all of the teacher embeddings as subclasses. We
687   consider the binarized CIFAR-100 task, and sweep S from 2 − 256, the embedding dimension of the
688   teacher D = 256. We distill from ResNet92 to ResNet56, with the subclass temperature parameter β
689   ranging from 2−5 − 20 , with results shown in Figure 5 (top row), averaged over three runs. For a more
690   clear comparison to the random projection baseline, we also show the “advantange” over random
691   projections in fig. 5 (bottom row), made by subtracting the accuracy by the equivalent obtained by
692
      random projects with the same S and β. Figure 5 shows the following general trends:
693   Larger S generally improves performance for Random Projections and LELP, and can harm

857   Semi-supervised KD, also known as KD with unlabeled examples, is a potent training paradigm for
858   generating compact and efficient student models in scenarios where labeled data is scarce but a large
859   pool of unlabeled data exists. This approach employs a high-capacity teacher model to generate (soft)
860
      pseudo-labels for the unlabeled dataset, which are subsequently utilized to train the student model.
861   Despite its widespread success in practice, the effectiveness of this powerful approach generally
862   depends on the quality of the pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model. Indeed, training the
863   student model on noisy pseudo-labels often leads to significant degradation of its generalization
      performance, and this is a well-known phenomenon that has been observed and studied in a plethora


                                                                    16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      of papers in the literature, e.g., Arazo et al. (2020); Liu & Tan (2021); Pham et al. (2021); Stanton
865   et al. (2021); Xie et al. (2020); Baykal et al. (2023); Iliopoulos et al. (2022); Kontonis et al. (2023).
866   Additionally, enforcing more teacher-student consistency, e.g., by blindly mimicking the teacher’s
867   embeddings, can even hinder performance when the teacher’s output contains high noise (i.e., one
868   may get worse performance than simply applying Vanilla KD). Finally, in this setting, often times the
869   expense of using the teacher model to generate pseudo-labels can be a significant limitation. This
870   becomes particularly problematic for methods like Subclass Distillation, which necessitate training
871   multiple teacher models and generating pseudo-labels with each one of them, further compounding
872   the cost.
873   In Table 5 (see also the rightmost plot in Figure 6) we study such a semi-supervised setting in the
874   case of CIFAR-100, with the teacher model being a ResNet92 and student model being a MobileNet.
875   (We choose this setting because it is the task where the teacher models will be the most noisy, and
876   also the teacher and student models have different architectures.)
877   We consider the cases where the teacher model has available 10000, 15000, 20000 labeled examples
878   for training, and it is then used to pseudo-label the rest of the CIFAR-100 dataset. (Note that the
879   amount of available labeled examples for training the teacher model directly affects its accuracy.)
880   Finally, the student-model is trained on both the available labeled data (which are also soft-labeled by
881   the teacher model) and the teacher’s pseudo-labels on the unlabeled data.
882
      We observe that LELP provides significant gains in this setting. Also notably, for the 10000 and the
883   15000 case, the only baseline that outperforms Vanilla KD is Relational KD — showing that, while
884   the teacher’s embeddings in a noisy teacher setting may contain valuable information, extracting it
885   effectively is a complex challenge — especially when the teacher and student come from different
886   architecture families.

983    For a fair comparison, we use the teacher’s last-layer embeddings throughout the distillation process
984    across all relevant baselines (Embedding Distillation, FitNet, VID, Relational KD, CRD). Further-
985    more, to handle mismatches in embedding dimensions between teacher and student, we introduce a
986    trainable fully connected layer as a learnable projection for all these methods. (For Vision datasets,
987    we have experimented with convolutional layers as suggested in Romero et al. (2014), and while these
988
       do reduce the size of the student model, they do not significantly impact its performance compared to
       the fully connected ones.)
989

991    same pre-trained projection is then utilized consistently across all three trials corresponding to the

993    learning rate 10−3 and 10−6 for Vision (200 epochs training) and Natural Language (40 epochs
994
       training) datasets, respectively.

996    squared difference in the second term is taken over the teacher’s and student’s embeddings (using a
997    learnable projection if there is a mismatch in their dimensions).

999    (2019).

1002
       where a pair of examples (x, y) is considered “negative" if x ̸= y, i.e., given a batch the number
1003
       of positive pairs is equal to the batch size. (Note that in Vision classification tasks the standard
1004   approach would be to generate variations of every image (e.g, via rotations), and then also consider as
1005   positive any pair of examples (original or modified) that come from the same source. However, data
1006   augmentation of Natural Language examples is not straightforward and this impairs the performance
1007   of the method — this is the main point we are trying to convey here.)
1008
       For DKD we implement equations (1), (2) and (7) in Zhao et al. (2022). We set the ’target’ hy-
1009
