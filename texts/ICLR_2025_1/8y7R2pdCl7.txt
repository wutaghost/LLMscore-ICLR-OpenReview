009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Large language models (LLMs) can handle a variety of tasks conditioned on natu-
012              ral language instructions. While fine-tuning improves task-specific performance,
013              adjusting the model weights of LLMs requires a huge amount of computational
014              resources, and it is impractical for real-time updates. Alternatively, prompting
015              allows LLMs to adapt to a broad range of tasks without the need for computation-
016              ally intensive gradient-based optimisation. However, crafting effective prompts
017              remains a challenge, to the extent that it is even unclear if expert in-domain knowl-
018
                 edge is what is needed or experience in writing prompts or something else. Ap-
                 proaches like meta-prompting and self-feedback seek to alleviate this burden, but
019
                 they rely primarily on a numerical feedback signal, leaving the potential of textual
020
                 feedback unexplored. These methods also typically require numerous interactions
021              with the environment to gather sufficient context, leading to significant computa-
022              tional overhead.
023              In this work, we propose a novel framework that takes a prompted large language
024              model as an optimiser and treats the text-based prompt itself as a parameter. By
025              interacting with the environment to collect feedback, our proposed method con-
026              structs the updated textual prompt. Our experimental results demonstrate that this
027              method not only achieves superior performance but also automatically incorpo-
028              rates domain-specific knowledge, establishing a scientifically motivated, practical
029              and efficient approach to prompting for future research.

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have shown an extraordinary ability to perform a wide range of
035
      tasks, from generating images in various styles to writing code in different programming languages
      for diverse purposes. These tasks, once considered extremely challenging for artificial intelligence
036
      and even beyond the average human’s skill set, are now achievable by simply interacting with LLMs.
037
      Recently, increasing research efforts have been put into specialisation of LLMs, i.e. improving its
038   capability on specific tasks, in the most effective and efficient manner.
039
040   The first family of methods belong to the gradient-based optimisation approaches, such as supervised
041
      fine-tuning with low-rank adaptation (Hu et al., 2022), and continuous prompting (Lester et al., 2021;
      Qin & Eisner, 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2023). Although LLMs fine-tuned in this way can
042
      excel on the specific target tasks, their in-context learning capabilities may be reduced, and they may
043
      also suffer from catastrophic forgetting, losing previously acquired ability when learning the new
044   skill (Zhai et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). In addition, training LLMs via gradient-
045   based optimisation is impractical for real-time updates. The computational requirements for the
046   model size lead to challenges when resources are limited local compute only. More detrimentally,
047   these methods are simply inapplicable to LLMs with only API access.
048
      The second family of methods focus on precise instructions and task descriptions to adapt LLMs via
049
      natural language prompting, without updating their model weights. However, creating proper and
050   effective prompts is not straightforward, requiring some tricks, making it at times more similar to art
051   rather than science. Via trial-and-error, various tricks have been found to improve prompting of lan-
052   guage models, some of which are widely shared in discussion forums on the internet1 . Consequently,
053

054
      approaches like self-feedback or meta-prompting have been introduced to enhance the performance
055   of LLMs without gradient-based optimisation. Self-feedback methods, e.g. LLM-augmenter (Peng
056   et al., 2023) or Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023), gather sufficient experience by interacting with the
057   environment multiple times and refine the model’s response based on verbal summarisation of ex-
058   periences. In other words, when serving end users, these methods still require numerous queries for
059   self-reflection and refinement of their response, which can cause a huge cost on computation and
060   non-negligible inference time.
061   On the other hand, meta-prompting methods aim to automatically generate a proper prompt based on
062   a given set of examples or refine a prompt based on scores. Yang et al. (2024) propose Optimisation
063   by PROmpting (OPRO), which employs LLMs to refine an initial prompt based on a number of per-
064   formance metrics. Tang et al. (2024) introduce the Gradient-inspired LLM-based Prompt Optimizer
065   (GPO), which also uses numerical feedback, controlling edit distance via a cosine decay mecha-
066   nism. Kong et al. (2024) and Cheng et al. (2024) propose sequence-to-sequence prompt rewriters,
067   trained by reinforcement learning or human preference data, to reduce the difference between hu-
068   man intention and the understanding of LLMs. Although these methods are capable of generating
069
      a better prompt, they highly rely on numerical feedback, which is not always available when facing
      real users.
070
071   We propose the Text-as-Parameter optimisation (TaP), a meta-prompting approach which leverages
072   natural language textual feedback. Our approach is based on the way humans learn new things:
073   they need instructions, experiences, and feedback. Various studies have shown humans learn bet-
074   ter with informative feedback than only simply instruction (Kulhavy, 1977; Mory, 1992; Lyster &
075
      Mori, 2006; Hattie & Gan, 2011). To echo this sentiment, in TaP, we first initialise the prompt via
      corpus-based prompt generation, followed by iterative meta-prompting updates through an interac-
076
      tive optimisation. More specifically, the LLM-based system first interacts with the environment, e.g.
077
      real or simulated users trying to complete a particular task such as information seeking or medical
078   question answering. A feedbacker, e.g. human experts or LLMs, provides textual-based feedback
079   based on these interactions, then a separate LLM-based rewriter refines the prompt based on the
080   feedback and the prompt from the previous interaction. The refined prompt is then used by the sys-
081   tem on the next user interaction, requiring no additional memory to store previous interactions or
082   past feedback. Textual feedback, which has more information than a scalar or a binary preference,
083   can provide richer information for prompt optimisation.
084   Our contributions are as follows:
085
086         • We propose a Text-as-Parameter optimisation method which leverages textual feedback
087           from external resources for improved prompting.
088
089         • Our method can be generalised through different prompting styles, applied with different
090           LLMs, e.g. GPT-4o mini or Gemini-1.5-flash, and is task-agnostic.
091
            • Our method learns domain-specific knowledge through interaction and incorporates it into
092
              the optimised prompt. Our experiments show that this reduces the performance gap be-
093
              tween different initial prompting methods.

096   2   R ELATED WORK
097
098
099
      Gradient-based optimisation for LLMs For high parameter counts, training or fine-tuning an
      entire large language model is infeasible since it requires a huge amount of computation resources.
100
      As a result, parameter-efficient fine-tuning, such as training only part of the model or freezing the
101
      model and training an adapter, is widely used to refine LLMs (Hu et al., 2022; 2023; Lialin et al.,
102   2023). On the other hand, continuous prompting, e.g. prefix-tuning and soft-prompting, is also
103   popular to adapt LLMs to specific tasks or improve their performance (Lester et al., 2021; Qin &
104   Eisner, 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2023). By updating inputs of every attention layer (Li
105   & Liang, 2021), or task-related vectors (Lester et al., 2021), these methods can achieve comparable
106   performance to full fine-tuning across various model sizes and tasks (Liu et al., 2022). Although
107   these methods can improve LLMs effectively, they do not apply to API-access-only LLMs and such
      training processes cannot be carried out in real-time.

108
      Self-feedback To improve the performance of text-based prompts, various prompting styles are
109   proposed, e.g. Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) or ReAct (Yao et al., 2023). These prompting
110   methods encourage LLMs to reason before taking action or generating responses, which leads to
111   better performance. However, optimising the prompt for better performance by manual trial and
112   error is inefficient. As a result, self-feedback methods are introduced to refine the LLMs’ response,
113   e.g., LLM-augmenter generates feedback by itself and leverages external knowledge to rewrite its
114   response (Peng et al., 2023) and Reflexion summarises previous interactions with the environment
115   as ‘reflections’ to improve the model’s response (Shinn et al., 2023).
116   While this demonstrates the ability of LLMs for self-correction, these self-feedback methods rely
117   on frequent API calls since their original prompt is not optimal. As a result, the computation cost
118   and latency during inference are not negligible.
119
120   Prompt optimisation Meta-prompting methods are widely used to generate a prompt without hu-
121   man editing. The automatic prompt engineer (APE) method leverages an LLM which is instructed
122   to generate an initial prompt and selects the prompt with the best performance on the target task
123   (Zhou et al., 2023). Automatic prompt optimisation (APO) further employs a self-feedback mod-
124   ule to provide textual feedback which gives suggestions on how to edit the old prompt (Pryzant
125   et al., 2023). Ye et al. (2024) propose a meta-prompt LLM to edit the original prompt step-by-
126
      step. Kong et al. (2024) and Cheng et al. (2024) train a sequence-to-sequence model for prompt
      rewriting by reinforcement learning and preference data, respectively. Yang et al. (2024) propose
127
      optimisation by prompting (OPRO), which leverages LLMs to rewrite the original prompt based
128
      on a corresponding performance score. In addition, to properly include experience for improv-
129   ing performance, Zhang et al. (2023) treats LLMs as semi-parametric reinforcement learning agents
130   with a stored experience memory, including task information, observation, action and corresponding
131   Q value estimation. These experiences are sampled dynamically for few-shot in-context learning.
132   Zhang et al. (2024) propose Agent-Pro, which constructs policy-level reflections according to the
133   numerical feedback from the environment and improves its policy incrementally. Tang et al. (2024)
134   introduce the Gradient-inspired LLM-based Prompt Optimizer (GPO), which updates the prompt
135   iteratively based on numerical feedback and controls the edit distance through a cosine-based decay
136   strategy.
137   Although these methods demonstrate promising performance in generating or improving a prompt,
138   they do not leverage external textual feedback, which could provide abundant information for opti-
139   misation. The comparison between our method and the mentioned related works is listed in Table 1.
140
141   Learnability of LLMs with prompting Although transformers are universal approximators (Yun
142   et al., 2020) and in-context learning in LLMs can be viewed as implicit fine-tuning (Dai et al., 2023),
143   the following remain open questions: Can we prompt LLMs for arbitrary tasks, and what are the
144   limitations of in-context learning?
145   Petrov et al. (2024) highlight the limitations of context-based fine-tuning methods, e.g. in-context
146   learning, prompting and prefix tuning, for new task learning in transformers. Specifically, transform-
147   ers struggle to acquire new tasks solely through prompting, as prompts cannot change the model’s
148   attention patterns. Instead, they can only bias the output of the attention layers in a fixed direction
149   and elicit skills learned through pre-training. In other words, only models with billions of param-
150   eters trained on vast, diverse datasets are capable of in-context learning—adapting to new tasks
151   through examples or instructions without modifying their underlying weights. Therefore, we focus
152   on fundamental models large enough to demonstrate their in-context learning ability, to investigate
153
      text-as-parameter prompt optimisation, which is fully composed of in-context learning with LLMs.

415   Domain-specific information is learned through interaction The initial standard prompt (Figure
416   5a) only includes vague task definitions such as “finding and booking things”, and the initial ReAct
417   prompt (Figure 10a) includes limited task-related information, such as “book train ticket, hotel room,
418   restaurant reservation, and attraction tickets” and general booking related slots dates and times.
419
      Figure 5 and Figure 10 show that the domain-specific information is learnt through interaction.
420   After 5 epochs, more slots related to information seeking, recommendation, and reservation booking
421   are included in the prompt automatically, e.g. cuisine and dietary preferences for the restaurant
422   domain in Figure 5b, and number of guests and check-in/check-out duration for the hotel domain in
423   Figure 10b. More booking-related slots, such as contact information and reference numbers are also
424   included. It is worth mentioning that neither the simulated user, feedbacker nor rewriter have access
425   to any d task-oriented dialogue information since the user goal, dialogues, and system prompts are
426   all presented as plain texts.
427   According to Table 3, we can find out the system has better performance when more domain-specific
428   information is included in the instruction. This observation is further supported by the fact that hu-
429   man experts also integrate domain-specific knowledge, such as available domains or slots within the
430   context (Heck et al., 2023; Hudeček & Dusek, 2023). In other words, this result suggests our pro-
431   posed optimisation method can generate a better prompt through interaction because more important
      domain-specific information is added to the instruction.

448   5.3       T HE LEARNABILITY OF PROMPTING
449
450   We compare our method against three systems: a standard system, built with GPT-4o mini with
451   the initial prompt, a standard system updated via GPO, and HuatuoGPT-II (Chen et al., 2024), a
452
      large language model which is fully fine-tuned on medical data and demonstrates the state-of-the-
      art performance on Chinese medicine benchmarks. In other words, except HuatuoGPT-II, a fully
453
      fine-tuned 7B model, all systems are built with GPT-4o mini by prompting.
454
455   In general medicine, our method consistently outperforms the fully fine-tuned HuatuoGPT-II with an
456   86.7% win rate and is preferred over other prompting-based baselines. On the other hand, traditional
457   Chinese medicine is more challenging. For example, our system’s preference rate drops by 41%
458
      compared to Huatuo when transitioning from general medicine to traditional Chinese medicine.
      However, despite this drop in preference, our proposed method is still favoured in general.
459
460   This observation is aligned with the findings by Petrov et al. (2024). Our method performs better
461   in general medicine because the skills present in the pre-training data of LLMs can be elicited by
462   prompting. However, tasks which are unseen or under-represented in pre-training data are hard to
463   learn through prompting. How to properly leverage external knowledge to improve the performance
464
      on unseen or under-represented tasks is important in the future.

482   6       C ONCLUSIONS
483
484   Our proposed method treats text-based instructions for large language models (LLMs) as parameters.
485   These textual prompts are initialised and optimised by LLMs, without the need for domain-specific
      manual prompt editing. As shown in Table 3, the method is robust to the choice of LLM used


                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      for rewriting, as it works effectively with both GPT-4o mini and Gemini-1.5-flash. Additionally, it
487   supports various prompting styles, such as standard and ReAct, and reduces the performance gap
488   between these strategies after optimisation over several epochs. By using the optimised prompt, the
489   system can minimise the need for extensive self-feedback loops, reducing computational overhead
490   and API call frequency during inference.
491
      Domain-specific information is learned and encoded in the prompt throughout the interaction with
492
      textual feedback (as shown in Figure 5 and Figure 10), enhancing both model performance and
493   explainability. Furthermore, our method can incorporate feedback from simulated environments as
494   well as human experts, which enables a more feasible way to incorporate human expert knowledge.
495   Our method offers a stable, practical, and efficient approach for automatic prompt optimisation,
496   which could be valuable for future LLM research.
