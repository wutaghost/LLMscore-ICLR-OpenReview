011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Post-training quantization (PTQ) serves as a potent technique to accelerate the
014            inference of large language models (LLMs). Nonetheless, existing works still
015            necessitate a considerable number of floating-point (FP) operations during infer-
016            ence, including additional quantization and de-quantization, as well as non-linear
017
               operators such as RMSNorm and Softmax. This limitation hinders the deployment
               of LLMs on the edge and cloud devices. In this paper, we identify the primary
018
               obstacle to integer-only quantization for LLMs lies in the large fluctuation of
019
               activations across channels and tokens in both linear and non-linear operations.
020            To address this issue, we propose I-LLM, a novel integer-only fully-quantized
021            PTQ framework tailored for LLMs. Specifically, (1) we develop Fully-Smooth
022            Block-Reconstruction (FSBR) to aggressively smooth inter-channel variations of
023            all activations and weights. (2) to alleviate degradation caused by inter-token
024            variations, we introduce a novel approach called Dynamic Integer-only MatMul
025            (DI-MatMul). This method enables dynamic quantization in full-integer matrix
026            multiplication by dynamically quantizing the input and outputs with integer-only
027            operations. (3) we design DI-ClippedSoftmax, DI-Exp, and DI-Normalization,
028
               which utilize bit shift to execute non-linear operators efficiently while maintaining
               accuracy. The experiment shows that our I-LLM achieves comparable accuracy to
029
               the FP baseline and outperforms non-integer quantization methods. For example,
030
               I-LLM can operate at W4A4 with negligible loss of accuracy. To our knowledge,
031            we are the first to bridge the gap between integer-only quantization and LLMs.
032            We’ve published our code on anonymous.4open.science, aiming to contribute to
033            the advancement of this field.

      Large Language Models (LLMs) have paved the way for general artificial intelligence with their
038   remarkable performance across a wide range of tasks. However, the rising number of parameters and
039   computing power requirements of LLMs pose significant challenges when it comes to deployment.
040
041
      Post-training quantization (PTQ) is a powerful technique employed to accelerate the inference process
      of LLMs. Previous PTQ methods for LLMs have primarily relied on simulated quantization (aka.
042
      fake quantization) (Xiao et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022b; Yuan et al., 2023a),
043
      where integer values are typically used for inputs/outputs and compute-intensive operations are
044   performed using dequantized floating-point (FP) values (as shown in Fig1). Although this scheme
045   offers benefits in scenarios where data transmission bandwidth is limited, it does not effectively
046   reduce computational costs and thus has little effect on compute-bound situations. Besides, non-linear
047   operations (e.g., Softmax and RMSNorm) often involve complex operations, including transcendental
048   functions such as exponential functions and square root functions. These functions are typically
049   performed on dedicated FP units and may require multiple iterations for accurate computation.
050   In contrast, integer-only quantization (Jacob et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2022; Li & Gu,
051   2023; Lin et al., 2022) utilizes low-precision integer arithmetic for all operations, including linear
052   operations (e.g., matrix multiplication) and non-linear operations. It enables quantized models to take
053   full advantage of fast and efficient integer arithmetic units, resulting in promising speedup effects
      and reduction of latency and power consumption. Additionally, integer-only quantization facilitates

151   LLMs Quantization. LLMs quantization can be broadly categorized into weight-only and weight-
152   activation quantization. To alleviate the computational burdens, some works (Liu et al., 2023b;
153
      Frantar et al., 2022; Chee et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2023; Kim
      et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023) make efforts in weight-only quantization. GPTQ (Frantar et al.,
154
      2022) and QuIP (Chee et al., 2023) achieve high compression rates by optimizing matrix multipli-
155
      cations operation. AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) and OWQ (Lee et al., 2023) demonstrate performance
156   improvements by accounting for the impact of activation outliers on weight quantization. Moreover,
157   works such as QLORA (Dettmers et al., 2024), QA-lora (Xu et al., 2023) and LoftQ (Li et al.,
158   2023b) leverage Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques to achieve weight compression
159   with fine-tuning tasks. Different with weight-only quantization methods, towards to accelerate the
160   LLMs inference, the weight-activation quantization methods (Wei et al., 2022b; Yuan et al., 2023a;
161   Wei et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023b; Yue et al., 2024) quantize both the weights
      and activations, including the KV cache. The primary challenge in quantizing activations lies in


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      outliers, leading to significant quantization errors. To tackle this issue, ZeroQuant (Yao et al., 2022)
163   proposes a fine-grained hardware-friendly quantization scheme for both weight and activations.
164   SmoothQuant (Xiao et al., 2022) migrates the quantization difficulty from activations to weights
165   with a mathematically equivalent transformation (i.e., per-channel scaling). OmniQuant (Shao et al.,
166   2023) further enhances performance by training the quantization parameters. While these methods
167   have mitigated the quantization error, their inference pipelines still involve partially FP operations on
168   non-linear operators such as Softmax, Normalization, and SiLU. In this study, our focus on achieving
169   Integer-only inference for LLMs model using advanced PTQ (Li et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022a; Liu
170   et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2024) techniques.
171   Integer-only Quantization. Current quantization methods for LLMs often involve dequantized
172   FP operations during inference, limiting the utilization of efficient low-precision arithmetic units.
173   Integer-only quantization, eliminating dequantization, enables complete inference using Integer-only
174   arithmetic, promising enhanced model acceleration. Previous approaches (Jacob et al., 2018; Yao
175   et al., 2021) leverage dyadic arithmetic for Integer-only pipeline on CNNs, but these are tailored
176   for linear operations and are unsuitable for non-linear operations in ViTs. Applying INT arithmetic
177
      solely to linear operations while retaining FP arithmetic for non-linear ones maybe a straightforward
      solution, but this demands custom hardware and introduces computational overheads. Advancements
178
      include Fully-8bit (Lin et al., 2021) and I-BERT (Kim et al., 2021), which address non-linear
179
      operations through employs L1 LayerNorm and INT polynomial approximations for the non-linear
180   operations. However, these methods face inefficiencies or fail to fully exploit hardware advantages.
181   Based on I-BERT (Kim et al., 2021), FQ-ViT (Lin et al., 2022) extends INT arithmetic to part of the
182   operations but overlooks significant non-linear operations like GELU. While some methods (Stevens
183   et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020) retain FP arithmetic during approximation, they cannot meet the
184   requirement for Integer-only arithmetic. Recently, I-ViT (Li & Gu, 2023) introduces Integer-only
185   quantization for ViTs, yet its suitability for LLMs is questionable due to differing data distributions,
186   and its computational graph includes partially INT32 precision operations. In this paper, we focus on
187   Integer-only inference for LLMs, maintaining the entire computational graph at INT8 precision or
188
      lower bit (e.g., 6/4-bit), enhancing LLMs’ inference efficiency on edge processors.

      Challenges of Integer-Only Quantization for Large Language Models. Presently, integer-only
193   LLMs encounter two primary hurdles: (1) quantizing the activation of LLMs, especially those origi-
194   nating from non-linear operators, poses a formidable challenge. As evidenced in Fig 3, the divergence
195   in these non-linear activations often surpasses that of linear operators, particularly pronounced in
196   models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a). Previous methods have failed to address these non-
197   linear activations, and straightforwardly quantizing non-linear layers may lead to substantial accuracy
198   degradation. (2) prior integer-only quantization techniques have overlooked the distinctive traits
199   of LLMs, including divergent activation scales and the substantial overhead of loading large-scale
200   weights. Even the W8A8 method introduced in I-BERT can lead to catastrophic outcomes, as shown
201
      in Fig 2, let alone more aggressive quantization methods like W6A6 or W4A4.
202   In this section, we introduce a novel integer-only quantization framework termed I-LLM. As illustrated
203   in Fig 5, this framework incorporates a differentiable approach within the Post-Training Quantization
204   (PTQ) paradigm, termed Fully-Smooth Block Reconstruction. This method is designed to effectively
205   balance all feasible parameter pairs, as elaborated in Section 3.2. Furthermore, we advance the
206   develop of dynamic quantization within the integer-only context by introducing DI-MatMul, a
207
      dynamic integer-only matrix multiplication method, which is elucidated in Section 3.3. Additionally,
      we detail integer-only non-linear approximations, including DI-ClippedSoftmax, DI-exp, and DI-
208
      Norm, that are built upon DI-MatMul and are presented in Section 3.4. These operators facilitate
209
      8-bit input activations while minimizing accuracy loss.

239   3.2 F ULLY-S MOOTH B LOCK -R ECONSTRUCTION (FSBR)
240   As mention above, to mitigate the issue of non-linear layer activations in LLMs being affected
241   by channel and token differences (Fig 3 and 4), we propose Fully-Smooth Block-Reconstruction
242
      (FSBR). An intuitive method is to train a smoothing coefficient for all activations and weights to aid
      in restoring the model’s quantization accuracy, especially the quantization accuracy of non-linear
243
      operators. Therefore, we consider the activations of all non-linear layers and learn the smoothing
244
      coefficients for all possible equivalent smoothing transformations at the channel level. On the left
245   side of Fig 5, four paradigms for achieving inter-channel smoothing during block reconstruction are
246   shown: Parallel Linear-Linear, Serial Linear-Norm, Serial Linear-Linear, and NonLinear Act-Smooth.
247   The first three smoothing methods are indeed linear, making them easier to implement.
248
      However, when addressing non-linear operators (NonLinear Act-Smooth) in LLMs, such as the gated
249
      activation function (SwiGLU), it becomes challenging to apply any linear transformations to them.
250
      For convenience, we can represent SwiGLU using the following formula:

526   5      C ONCLUSION
527   In this paper, we present I-LLM, a fully-quantized integer-only PTQ framework for LLMs. We
528   address the challenge of fluctuating activations in both linear and non-linear operations by proposing
529   Fully-Smooth Block-Reconstruction (FSBR) to harmonize inter-channel variations and Dynamic
530   Integer-only MatMul (DI-MatMul) to handle inter-token variations. Additionally, we design DI-
531
      ClippedSoftmax, DI-Exp, and DI-Norm as lightweight integer-only operators to replace compu-
      tationally intensive floating-point operations. Experiments demonstrate that I-LLM outperforms
532
      simulated quantization methods and achieves comparable accuracy to the floating-point baseline.
533
      Notably, I-LLM operates effectively in a low-bit quantization setting with minimal accuracy loss,
534   thereby bridging the gap between low-precision integer-only quantization and full-precision LLMs.
535   Moreover, experiments demonstrate that I-LLM not only outperforms simulated quantization methods
536   but also achieves comparable accuracy to the floating-point baseline. In practical scenarios, I-LLM
537   benefits from the performance gains of low-precision computation, while its consistent operation at
538   low bit-width significantly reduces memory utilization. Consequently, this work not only accelerates
539   LLM inference on server-side deployments but also paves the way for efficient LLM deployment on
      edge devices without floating-point capabilities.

      A.4     T HE OVERHEAD AND EFFICIENCY OF DI-M AT M UL
865
866   The DI-MatMul has lower overhead and higher execution efficiency compared to conventional
867   GEMM quantization. As detailed in Section 3.3 of paper, we utilize two 8-bit scalars, mI and k I
868   to represent the quantization scale for a batch of activations. This approach incurs no additional
869   storage overhead compared to the previous method using FP16 to store the quantization scale. After
870   obtaining the intermediate results from integer-only matrix operations, I-LLM only requires a small
871   amount of scalar integer operations and one vector-scalar multiplication, as specified in Formulas 6,
872
      7, and 8. The output of DI-Matmul is composed of a low-bit-width integer matrix and three 8-bit
      scalars.
873
874   It should be noted that conventional quantization methods still require a large number of floating-
875   point computations during inference. In contrast, all computations in DI-MatMul are integer-based,
876   effectively leveraging efficient integer arithmetic units. To be more specific:
877
              • Weight-only quantization demands an online dequantization step for the quantized weights,
878
                involving floating-point multiplication as a precursor to the subsequent floating-point General
879
                Matrix Multiply (GEMM) operations.
880
881
              • The joint quantization of weights and activations often incurs the necessity for online
                quantization and dequantization procedures specifically for the activations. More importantly,
882
                the output of traditional quantization methods is typically rendered in floating-point format
883
                (e.g., FP16), while the output of DI-MatMul is in a low-bit-width integer format. This
884             paradigm not only conserves considerable memory and bandwidth but also reduces latency.
885             For instance, compared to FP16 format, employing 4-bit quantization for feature-map and
886             KV-cache results in a substantial reduction of memory and bandwidth usage by 75
887           • As shown in Table 7, DI-MatMul outperforms conventional quantized GEMM in both weight-
888             activation (1.2x speedup) and activation-activation (2.1x speedup) scenarios, attributed to
889             DI-MatMul’s efficient integer arithmetic and its requant-only feature.
