009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Bayesian neural network (BNN) approximates the posterior distribution of model
012               parameters and utilizes the posterior for prediction via Bayesian Model Averaging
013               (BMA). The quality of the posterior approximation is critical for achieving accurate
014               and robust predictions. It is known that flatness in the loss landscape is strongly
015               associated with generalization performance, and it necessitates consideration to
016
                  improve the quality of the posterior approximation. In this work, we empirically
                  demonstrate that BNNs often struggle to capture the flatness. Moreover, we
017
                  provide both experimental and theoretical evidence showing that BMA can be
018
                  ineffective without ensuring flatness. To address this, we propose Sharpness-
019               Aware Bayesian Model Averaging (SA-BMA), a novel optimizer that seeks flat
020               posteriors by calculating divergence in the parameter space. SA-BMA aligns with
021               the intrinsic nature of BNN and the generalized version of existing sharpness-aware
022               optimizers for DNN. In addition, we suggest a Bayesian Transfer Learning scheme
023               to efficiently leverage pre-trained DNN. We validate the efficacy of SA-BMA in
024               enhancing generalization performance in few-shot classification and distribution
025               shift by ensuring flat posterior1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Bayesian neural network (BNN) provides a theoretically grounded framework for modeling uncer-
030   tainty in deep learning by approximating the posterior distribution of model parameters (MacKay,
031   1992b; Hinton & Van Camp, 1993; Neal, 2012). The approximated posterior is used for making
032   predictions through Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) (Wasserman, 2000; Fragoso et al., 2018;
033   Wilson & Izmailov, 2020; Zeng & Van den Broeck, 2024). It allows BNNs to account for uncertainty
034   in predictions, leading to more reliable outcomes compared to the deterministic neural network
035   (DNN) (Kapoor et al., 2022; Kristiadi et al., 2022b). The accuracy and robustness of BNN predictions
036   are heavily dependent on the quality of the approximated posterior (Kristiadi et al., 2022a; Wenzel
037
      et al., 2020).
038   One key factor influencing posterior quality is the flatness of the loss landscape. Flat modes of
039   the loss landscape have been strongly associated with better generalization performance, as they
040   represent solutions that are less sensitive to small perturbations in model parameters (Hochreiter &
041   Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2016; Neyshabur et al., 2017). The concept of flatness has been
042   extensively studied in the context of DNNs, but no comprehensive analysis has been conducted on its
043
      role in BNNs or its impact on BMA. The significance of flatness in BNNs has only been explored in a
      limited number of studies. SA-BNN (Nguyen et al., 2023) incorporated a flat-seeking optimizer into
044
      BNNs with a theoretical foundation. However, SA-BNN adapted a DNN-based optimizer to BNNs
045
      without considering the probabilistic nature of BNNs, resulting in only limited improvements. On the
046   other hand, E-MCMC (Li & Zhang, 2023) introduced a guidance model to achieve flat posteriors, but
047   this approach is less suited for large-scale models.
048
049
      In this work, we first demonstrate that BNNs often struggle to capture the flatness. In detail, we
      compare the flatness of various BNN frameworks, including SWAG, VI, and MCMC, against that of
050
      DNNs. Furthermore, we show that BMA can be ineffective without ensuring flatness in the posterior
051
      distribution. These findings highlight the need for an optimization strategy that accounts for the
052   probabilistic nature of BNNs to effectively estimate flat posteriors.

      Therefore, we propose Sharpness-Aware Bayesian Model Averaging (SA-BMA), a novel optimization
055   approach that explicitly targets flat posterior distributions. We first compute the adversarial posterior
056   that belongs to the vicinity of the current posterior through divergence, which maximizes the BNN
057   loss function. After that, we update the posterior by employing the gradient of the adversarial
058   posterior with respect to the BNN loss. We show that the proposed SA-BMA is an extended version
059   of previous flatness-aware optimizers, Sharpness-aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2020),
060   Fisher SAM (FSAM) (Kim et al., 2022), and Natural Gradient (NG) (Amari, 1998) with specific
061   conditions. Additionally, we propose a Bayesian Transfer Learning scheme integrated with SA-BMA,
062   allowing for more efficient utilization of pre-trained models. We prove that SA-BMA improves the
063
      generalization performance of BNNs, particularly in few-shot classification and distribution shift
      scenarios, by ensuring flatness in the posterior.
064
065   Our major contributions are summarized as follows:
066
067          • We demonstrate that BNN often struggle to capture the flatness. Moreover, we show that
068            BMA can be ineffective without flatness in the posterior.
069          • We suggest a Bayesian-fitting flat posterior seeking optimizer, SA-BMA. SA-BMA is a
070            parameter space loss geometric optimizer, a generalized version of other loss geometric
071            optimizers, such as SAM, FSAM, and NG.
072
073          • We propose a Bayesian Transfer Learning scheme integrated with SA-BMA to efficiently
074
               utilize pre-trained models. This scheme aims to enhance the generalization performance
               of BNN, especially in few-shot classification and distribution shifts, by ensuring posterior
075
               flatness.

201   3.2    BMA C AN B E I NEFFECTIVE W ITHOUT F LATNESS
202
203   BNNs possess the benefit of BMA, which anticipates performance enhancement through model
204   ensemble based on the approximated posterior distribution. However, following the fact that BNNs
205   often struggle to capture the flatness, we cast another question: "Does the flatness also affect BMA?"
206   First, we consolidate that the correlation between flatness and generalization performance also exists
207   in BMA. Figure 2a shows the affirmative correlation between performance and flatness throughout
208   30 sampled model parameters from posterior trained on CIFAR10 with RN18 w/o BN. We provide
209   additional plots in Figure 9 (Appendix A.3), demonstrating consistent results.
210   Second, we observe how performance changes when the sampling model parameter in BMA is
211   performed based on flatness. We first sample 30 models trained on CIFAR10 with RN18 w/o BN.
212   Then, we start to do BMA through three criteria. "Flat" denotes starting model averaging from the
213   flattest model, and "Sharp" means the opposite of "Flat". "Rand" denotes starting BMA from a
214   random sample of prepared 30 models. From Figure 2b and 2c, we conclude that degradation or
215   stagnation can appear without considering the flatness during BMA. Figure 10 and 11 (Appendix A.4)
      support the conclusion, as well. With a closer look at the "Flat" label, where progressively sharper

294   4.2    BAYESIAN T RANSFER L EARNING
295
      The proposed optimizer theoretically captures curvature more exactly, but it faces practical limitations
296
      when applied to neural networks with a large number of parameters. To address this limitation, we
297   propose a Bayesian transfer learning scheme alongside the proposed optimizer. In this scheme, we
298   leverage pre-trained DNN as a prior and train only a subset of the model’s parameters, enhancing
299   scalability while maintaining performance. The proposed Bayesian transfer learning scheme consists
300   of three steps, and Algorithm 1 (Appendix D.3) depicts how it operates.
301
      First, we load a pre-trained DNN wMAP ⊆ Rp optimized by Maximum A Posteriori (MAP). We
302
      change the loaded DNN into BNN on the source or downstream task Dpr . Formally, we get qθpr (w|Dpr ).
303   Various BNN frameworks, such as VI, SWAG, and others, can be employed to transform a DNN into
304   a BNN. This study mainly employs MCMC, SWAG (Maddox et al., 2019), and MOPED (Krishnan
305   et al., 2020) for VI. Note that the proposed optimizer is not employed in this step.
306
      Second, we set the converted BNN as prior and initial points and train new posterior with the proposed
307
      optimizer, following objective function:
308
309
                  γ
                 lSA-BMA (θ) = min        max            l(θ + ∆θ) + βDKL [qθ (w|Dft )||qθpr (w|Dpr )],    (9)
                                 θ   ∆θ T Fθ (θ)∆θ≤γ 2
310
311   where Dft represents the downstream dataset used for fine-tuning, and l(·) denotes the empirical
312   loss function. To reduce computational complexity, we also incorporate a subnetwork BNN strategy,
313   which has been extensively explored in recent studies (Izmailov et al., 2020; Daxberger et al., 2021c;
314   Sharma et al., 2023; Snoek et al., 2015; Daxberger et al., 2021b; Harrison et al., 2023). In this work,

316   reinitialize the last layer using a simple Gaussian distribution N (0, αI) during fine-tuning on the

498   6   R ELATED W ORKS
499
      6.1 F LATNESS AND BNN
500
      Recent works have suggested flat-seeking optimizers combined with BNN. First, SWAG (Maddox
501
      et al., 2019) implicitly approximated posterior toward flatter optima based on SWA (Izmailov et al.,
502   2018). However, SWAG can fail to find flat minima, leading to limited improvement in generalization,
503   as shown in Section 3.1. bSAM (Möllenhoff & Khan, 2022) showed that SAM can be interpreted
504   as a relaxation of the Bayes and quantified uncertainty with SAM. Yet, bSAM only focused on
505   uncertainty quantification by simply modifying Adam-based SAM (Khan et al., 2018), not newly
506   considering the parametric geometry for perturbation. Moreover, scaling the variance with the number
507   of data points hampers the direct implementation of bSAM in few-shot settings. SA-BNN (Nguyen
508   et al., 2023) proposed a sharpness-aware posterior derived directly from the variational objective
509   and proved the effectiveness experimentally and theoretically. However, they just employ the L2
510
      norm to calculate the perturbation of SAM without considering the difference between the nature of
      DNN and BNN. E-MCMC (Li & Zhang, 2023) proposed an efficient MCMC algorithm capable of
511
      effectively sampling the posterior within a flat basin by removing the nested chain of Entropy-SGD
512
      and Entropy-SGLD. Still, E-MCMC necessitates a guidance model, which doubles the parameters
513   and heavily hinders its employment over large-scale models. SA-BMA is the first to reflect the
514   parameter space in the perturbation step of SAM for stochastic models, considering the nature of
515   BNNs.
516
517   6.2 BAYESIAN T RANSFER L EARNING
518   There are several works on performing transfer learning on BNN with prior. PTL (Shwartz-Ziv et al.,
519
      2022) constructs BNN by learning closed-form posterior approximation of the pre-trained model
      on the source task and uses it as a prior for the downstream task after scaling. The work requires
520
      additional training on the source task, which makes it restrictive when it is impossible to access the
521
      source task dataset. MOPED (Krishnan et al., 2020) employs pre-trained BNN as a prior for VI
522   based on the empirical Bayes method. Using pre-trained DNN, MOPED enhances accessibility to
523   BNN, however, it is only applicable to Mean-field VI (MFVI). Non-parametric transfer learning
524   (NPTL) (Lee et al., 2024) suggested adopting non-parametric learning to make posterior flexible in
525   terms of distribution shift. Our proposed scheme for Bayesian transfer learning can approximate the
526   distribution of parameters within either the downstream dataset or the source dataset, which allows
527   us to leverage more sophisticated and large-scale pre-trained models. Moreover, it is the first study
528   considering flatness in Bayesian transfer learning.
529
530   7   C ONCLUSION
531
      This study shows the limitations of BNNs in capturing the flatness, which is crucial for generalization
532
      performance. We also show that BMA can fail to yield optimal results without explicitly considering
533
      flatness. To address this issue, we introduce Sharpness-Aware Bayesian Model Averaging (SA-BMA),
534   which seeks to find a flat posterior by capturing flatness in the parameter space. SA-BMA is the
535   generalized version of existing sharpness-aware optimizers for DNN and aligns with the intrinsic
536   nature of BNN. We further propose a Bayesian Transfer Learning scheme, which enables efficient
537   fine-tuning of pre-trained DNNs while maintaining scalability with SA-BMA. Through extensive
538   experiments, we demonstrate that SA-BMA significantly enhances the generalization performance of
539   BNNs in diverse scenarios. Our work highlights the importance of flatness in posterior approximations
      and provides a practical solution to improve the predictive robustness and accuracy of BNNs.

      Figure 6: Description of flatness of BMA and Hessian Eigenvalue of loss. (a) depicts how flatness is
830
      measured in BNNs. We measure the flatness of individual sampled model weights and subsequently
831
      ensemble the flatness of them. (b) represents how the Hessian eigenvalue of loss corresponds to
832   flatness. It reveals that direction of steep curvature (sharp minima) exhibits with larger eigenvalues,
833   while that of gentle curvature (flat minima) exhibits smaller eigenvalues. Based on this understanding,
834   we measure flatness using the maximal eigenvalue of the Hessian at the minima.
835
836   To measure the flatness of BNNs and compare them with DNNs, we introduce a new metric specifi-
837   cally designed for this study. Unlike DNNs, where model parameters are typically treated as point
838   estimate, BNNs represent model parameters as random variables, necessitating an appropriate ap-
839   proach for measuring flatness. As shown in Figure 6b, the maximal eigenvalue of the Hessian of
840   the loss function is commonly used to evaluate flatness quantitatively in DNNs (Keskar et al., 2016;
841   Foret et al., 2020; Jastrzebski et al., 2020). To assess flatness in BNNs, we followed BMA protocol.
842   BMA samples model weights from the approximated posterior, calculates the outputs of the sampled
843   individual models, and ensemble the outputs, as shown in Figure 6a. Thus, similar to how BMA
844
      operates, we measured the flatness of individual model weights and subsequently ensemble these
      measurements to derive a comprehensive metric.

       C.1    SA-BMA WITH DIVERSE BNN FRAMEWORKS
1245
1246   In Eq. (4), SA-BMA can be applied with various BNN frameworks by using an empirical loss
1247   function l(·) and adjusting the parameter β. We commonly set l(·) as cross-entropy loss in context of
1248   image classification task. Note that SA-BMA was applied only to the normalization layers and the
1249   last layer, while all other layers were trained using SGD.
1250
1251   SA-BMA (VI) For VI, we follow the loss function of Eq. (4).
1252
1253   SA-BMA (MCMC) We mainly adopt SGLD for MCMC in this work. For SGLD, we incorporated
1254   noise into Eq. (4) without KLD term (β = 0) based on the learning rate and the hyperparameter,
1255   temperature. In this approach, during the first step, the adversarial posterior is computed without any
1256   noise (Eq. (7)). In the second step, both the noise and the adversarial posterior are used together in
1257
       the learning process.
1258
1259
       SA-BMA (SWAG) SWAG updates the first and second moments along the trajectory of SWA and
       uses these moments to approximate the posterior with a Gaussian distribution. In Eq. (4), β is fixed
1260
       to 0, and as the trajectory of SWA is optimized through SA-BMA, posterior approximation can be
1261
       performed accordingly.

       D.1   SA-BMA WITH DIVERSE BNN FRAMEWORKS
1408
1409   Diverse BNN frameworks can be adopted for Bayesian Transfer Learning. Specifically, there are
1410   several options for making pre-trained DNN into BNN. In this work, we mainly adopt MOPED and
1411   SWAG for the converting.
1412
       In addition, SA-BMA can be applied with various BNN frameworks by using an empirical loss
1413
       function l(·) and adjusting the parameter β in Eq. (9). We commonly set l(·) as cross-entropy loss in
1414
       context of image classification task.
1415
1416
       SA-BMA (VI) First, we convert pre-trained DNN into BNN with MOPED. We set the converted
1417
       BNN as prior, qθpr (w|Dpr ) in Eq. (9), and initial point of model. We only train parameters of
1418   normalization and last layer and freeze others. We train them with the loss function of Eq. (9).
1419
1420   SA-BMA (MCMC) For SGLD, it is unnecessary to convert pre-trained DNN into BNN. Instead,
1421   we directly set the pre-trained DNN as initialization. We incorporated noise into Eq. (9) without the
1422   KLD term (β = 0) based on the learning rate and the hyperparameter, temperature. During the first
1423   step, the adversarial posterior is computed without any noise (Eq. (7)). In the second step, both the
1424   noise and the adversarial posterior are used together in the learning process.
1425
1426   SA-BMA (SWAG) SWAG is also one of the options to convert pre-trained DNN into BNN.
1427   Specifically, we run a few epochs with source or downstream datasets to make BNN from pre-trained
1428   DNN. After this step, we set the BNN as the prior, qθpr (w|Dpr ) in Eq. (9). We also let the converted
1429   BNN as initialization and train with downstream dataset. We optimize model with the loss function
1430
       in Eq. (9).

       This study has several limitations. Firstly, calculating the FIM in weight space rather than output
2055   space makes it intractable to compute the FIM for the entire model parameter. Obtaining the FIM for
2056   the entire model weight space could lead to much more powerful performance improvements, making
2057   it one potential direction for future work. Secondly, the assumption of the existence of pre-trained
2058   models is necessary for Bayesian transfer learning. In situations where pre-trained weights are not
2059   available, performance improvements may be somewhat limited.
