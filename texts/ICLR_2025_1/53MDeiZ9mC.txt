011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Model (LLM) sparsification plays a crucial role in model com-
014            pression. Among various methods, training-free approaches are highly efficient
015
               but often result in accuracy loss, while full fine-tuning requires substantial compu-
               tational resources. Recent works have begun exploring sparse Parameter-Efficient
016
               Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, but lack theoretical guidance. This study presents
017
               the first comprehensive theoretical framework for efficient sparse fine-tuning, ad-
018            dressing a critical gap in the literature. Specifically, we identify gradient conflict
019            as the primary issue in PEFT sparse methods, wherein masked pretrained weights
020            and corresponding PEFT weights exhibit competing optimization objectives dur-
021            ing fine-tuning, potentially compromising model performance. We theoretically
022            model this phenomenon and identify three key factors influencing the efficacy of
023            fine-tuning in sparsified LLMs: (1) error introduced by weight norms, (2) error
024            composition from PEFT structures, and (3) error accumulation during fine-tuning.
025            Leveraging these theoretical insights, we propose a novel iterative sparse fine-
026
               tuning scheme that systematically addresses each identified factor. We implement
               an iterative process alternating between sparsity and fine-tuning to mitigate ac-
027
               cumulated error in single turn of finetuning. We employ pooling instead of low-
028
               rank decomposition to reduce error composition from PEFT structures. We apply
029            normalization to PEFT modules during fine-tuning, constraining error values by
030            limiting weight norms while preserving representational capacity. Additionally,
031            we utilize Centered Kernel Alignment based information similarity assessment for
032            adaptive allocation of layer-level sparsity and PEFT parameter quantities, address-
033            ing layer-specific redundancy. Empirical evaluation on a 50% sparse LLaMA-2
034            7B model demonstrates the superiority of our approach, achieving lossless com-
035            pression.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      The field of deep learning has witnessed an unprecedented surge in model sizes (Zhang et al., 2022;
040
      Chiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023b; Achiam et al., 2023) , leading to significant advance-
041
      ments in various domains(Ma et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2023;
042   Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c). However, this growth has also
043   introduced substantial challenges in terms of storage requirements and computational demands. As
044   models continue to expand, the need for efficient optimization techniques has become increasingly
045   critical. Sparsification techniques (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Xia
046   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c) have shown promise in reducing model size and computational
047   complexity by eliminating redundant or less important parameters. Concurrently, PEFT methods
048   (Houlsby et al., 2019; Lester et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021) have emerged as effective strategies for
049   adapting pre-trained models to specific tasks with minimal parameter updates. The integration of
050
      these approaches presents a compelling opportunity to simultaneously achieve model compression
      and task adaptation, potentially revolutionizing the deployment of large-scale deep learning models
051
      across diverse applications.
052
053   The direct application of iterative sparsification followed by parameter-efficient module fine-tuning
      faces significant limitations, primarily due to the emergence of the ”Sparse Weight Gradient Interfer-

054
      ence” phenomenon. This issue manifests when ∆W exhibits gradients corresponding to zero-valued
055   pre-trained weights, leading to interference with gradients at other positions. The root cause of this
056   phenomenon lies in the nature of the fine-tuning process. While pre-trained model weights are
057   frozen and sparsified, subsequent fine-tuning of ∆W continues to generate gradients in areas where
058   original weights were set to zero. This results in PEFT module parameters corresponding to sparse
059   zero-weight positions computing gradients, causing interference between these gradients, affecting
060   optimization direction, and increasing loss error.
061   To rigorously understand this phenomenon, we conducted a theoretical analysis of the impact of
062   sparse ∆W on model fine-tuning loss, deriving loss error bounds. Our analysis suggests three
063   primary directions for error reduction: (1) Implementation of iterative sparsification and fine-tuning
064   to avoid introducing excessive errors at once, (2) Structural improvements on PEFT modules to
065   reduce the interference impact on other parameters, and (3) Introduction of regularization constraints
066   on PEFT module parameters. These insights provide a foundation for developing more effective
067   sparse fine-tuning methods for large language models.
068   Based on our observations and theoretical analysis, we propose an efficient sparse adaptation method
069   designed to mitigate gradient conflict issues. Our method addresses the limitations of existing meth-
070   ods by incorporating several key innovations. At the core of our method is a parameterizable small
071   square weight matrix that serves as the PEFT module, operating in linear projection, applyling sim-
072   ple pooling operations to the input. This process involves linear mapping of pooled inputs followed
073   by inverse pooling, effectively reducing the impact of gradients from sparsified positions on other
074   PEFT model parameter positions. We implement an iterative sparsification and fine-tuning process,
075
      gradually increasing sparsity rates. This approach minimizes the total error introduced in each fine-
      tuning round. Consistent with previous work, we employ a cubic schedule for increasing sparsity
076
      ratios. Following the final sparsification round, we apply extended fine-tuning steps to fully lever-
077
      age the fine-tuning process and enhance model capabilities. During the fine-tuning phase, we apply
078   regularization to weights. This process constrains the parameter matrix norm, thereby reducing
079   gradient errors. The combination of these techniques allows our method to effectively address the
080   ”Sparse Weight Gradient Interference” phenomenon while maintaining model performance. This
081   work contributes in three key ways:
082
083          • We provide an in-depth investigation of post-sparsification fine-tuning issues, identifying
084
               and analyzing the ”Sparse Weight Gradient Interference” phenomenon. Our theoretical
               analysis offers both loose and tight bounds on its impact on model fine-tuning loss, provid-
085
               ing valuable insights for future improvements.
086
087
             • We propose a novel method combining iterative sparsification, pooling based PEFT mod-
               ule, and regularization, which effectively addresses gradient conflict issues and utilizes
088
               CKA metrics for adaptive computation of MoRA rank and layer-wise sparsity.
089
090
             • Our experimental results demonstrate our method’s significant improvements over baseline
               methods in model effectiveness, computational efficiency, and parameter utilization rate.

               These advancements offer a comprehensive solution to the challenges of model compres-

143
144   SWGI arises from the mismatch between the sparse structure of the pre-trained weights and the
145
      dense nature of the PEFT module weights. In the current formulation, the PEFT module weights
      (∆W) are applied uniformly across all positions, including those where the pre-trained weights
146
      are masked out (i.e., set to zero) by the sparsity mask M. This incongruence leads to suboptimal
147
      utilization of the fine-tuning capacity and potentially introduces noise into the model.
148
149   Specifically, SWGI occurs when PEFT module weights at positions corresponding to masked pre-
150   trained weights continue to calculate and propagate gradients during the fine-tuning process. To
151
      address SWGI and its implications, we conduct a comprehensive theoretical analysis in the follow-
      ing section. This analysis aims to provide a deeper understanding of the phenomenon and lay the
152
      groundwork for more effective solutions.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
515
516
      LLM Sparsity. State-of-the-art methods like SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and Wanda
517
      (Sun et al., 2023) enable training-free sparsity in LLMs, effectively removing non-essential weights.
518   However, high sparsity ratios can lead to significant accuracy loss, partly due to uniform layer-wise
519   sparsity rates that ignore varying redundancy across layers. The OWL method (Robert & Escoufier,
520   1976) addresses this by employing heuristic metrics to establish non-uniform sparsity rates based on
521   observed activation outliers in each layer, offering a more nuanced approach to LLM sparsification.
522
      Joint Sparsity and PEFT modules. Several methods (Li et al., 2024b;a; Zhao et al., 2024) have
523
      been developed to leverage related synergy. For example, LLM-Pruner(Ma et al., 2023) employs a
524   two-step process: it first executes a one-shot structured pruning of LLMs, followed by fine-tuning
525   using LoRA. Another innovative approach, LoRAPrune(Zhang et al., 2023a), implements an iter-
526   ative structured pruning method. In this technique, weight importance is determined by replacing
527   gradients on full weights with those calculated via LoRA, offering a more nuanced pruning strat-
528   egy. These studies primarily focus on applying LoRA to fine-tune structurally pruned LLMs. In the
529   context of structural pruning, the process of adjusting the input/output dimensions of the two low-
530   rank adaptations in PEFT modules and integrating them into the structural pruning weights (Zhao
531   et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023) is relatively straightforward. However, this approach faces significant
532
      challenges when applied to unstructured pruning, also known as network sparsity.
533
534   7     C ONCLUSION
535
536   This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for efficient sparse fine-tuning of Large Lan-
537   guage Models, analyzing the ”Sparse Weight Gradient Interference” phenomenon and proposing
538   a method that combines iterative sparsification, modular PEFT, and regularization. Our approach
539   significantly improves model effectiveness and efficiency over baselines, with potential for future
      exploration in other architectures and long-term impacts on model robustness and generalization.
