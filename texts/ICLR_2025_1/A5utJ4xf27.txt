009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Object localization tasks aim to accurately locate and identify specified target ob-
012            jects within images, representing a core challenge in the field of computer vision.
013            Traditional object localization systems primarily rely on intermediary modalities
014            such as text descriptions, speech, or visual cues to interpret human intent. How-
015            ever, these modalities only provide indirect expressions of human intent, limit-
016            ing the efficiency of information transmission. This is particularly evident when
017            detailed descriptions of texture and spatial information are required, resulting in
018            higher interaction costs. While existing brain-based object localization systems
019
               offer the potential for directly interpreting human intent, their localization accu-
               racy still lags behind traditional text-based systems. Additionally, the high cost of
020
               data collection, limited diversity of participants, and significant individual cogni-
021
               tive differences make it challenging to train subject-independent models, thereby
022            constraining the development of brain-based object localization systems. To ad-
023            dress the challenges, we propose MindLoc, a lightweight, cross-subject brain-
024            based object localization model. MindLoc can rapidly and accurately locate tar-
025            get objects in complex images by directly analyzing fMRI signals, combining the
026            precision of traditional localization systems with the convenience of brain-based
027            systems. Additionally, we are the first to introduce encryption technology for the
028            privacy protection of brain data, significantly reducing the psychological burden
029            on participants, which provides a foundation for increasing participant diversity in
030
               future studies. Experimental results demonstrate that MindLoc has achieved new
               state-of-the-art performance in brain-based object localization tasks, showcasing
031
               significant advantages in both accuracy and convenience. Our code is available at
032
               https://mindloc-sys.github.io/.

      Recently, generative visual models based on brain signals Lin et al. (2022); Ozcelik & VanRullen
102   (2023); Scotti et al. (2024); Takagi & Nishimoto (2023b); Xia et al. have demonstrated excep-
103   tional performance in decoding visual stimuli from brain responses. Typically, these methods map
104   brain responses captured through functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) to common modal-
105   ities suitable for input into pre-trained visual-language models Karras et al. (2020); Rombach et al.
106   (2022); Xu et al. (2023), facilitating subsequent image reconstruction. For example, Lin et al. (2022)
107   projects fMRI data into the CLIP Radford et al. (2021a) space, which embeds images and captions,
      and subsequently utilizes the Lafite model, adjusted using an unconditional StyleGAN2 Karras et al.

      (2020) framework, to perform image reconstruction. Building on this foundation, Scotti et al. (2024)
109   establishes a high-level (semantic) pathway for mapping fMRI data to the CLIP ViT-L/14 image
110   space and a low-level (perceptual) pathway for mapping fMRI data to the image embedding space
111   of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE). However, Scotti et al. (2024) is limited to natural scenes in
112   the MS-COCO dataset; for other image distributions, additional data collection and specialized gen-
113   erative models are required. Takagi & Nishimoto (2023b) utilized ridge regression to link fMRI
114   signals with CLIP text embeddings and the diffusion model of Stable Diffusion (SD) Rombach
115   et al. (2022), enabling a simple linear mapping from fMRI to the latent representation of the la-
116   tent diffusion model (LDM), thereby reducing the computational cost of the diffusion model. Xia
117
      et al. proposed the “DREAM” method, which extracts semantic, depth, and color information to
      reconstruct images using depth-color conditional SD. This approach Han et al. (2024); Takagi &
118
      Nishimoto (2023a) differs from previous methods that relied solely on text embeddings to obtain
119
      explicit descriptions of visual stimuli. Ozcelik & VanRullen (2023) introduced the Brain-Diffuser,
120   a two-step generative framework that first generates images with foundational features and overall
121   layout using the VDVAE model, followed by employing a state-of-the-art generative latent diffusion
122   model for subsequent image reconstruction.

124   2.2   I MAGE L OCALIZATION
125
126   Carion et al. (2020) introduced the DETR detection model, which was subsequently improved by
127   Chen Chen et al. (2023b); Dai et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2023); Meng et al. (2021);
128   Wang et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2020) through various enhancements, including the Group DETR
129   Chen et al. (2023b) and Deformable DETR models Zhu et al. (2020). However, these models pri-
130
      marily operate within a closed set of predefined categories, making it challenging to extend their
      application to new categories. This limitation has prompted research into open-set object detection,
131
      which utilizes existing bounding box annotations for training and employs language generalization
132
      to achieve the detection of arbitrary categories. OV-DETR Zareian et al. (2021) employs image and
133   text embeddings encoded by the CLIP model as query requests to decode specific class boxes within
134   the DETR framework Carion et al. (2020). ViLD Gu et al. (2021) extracts knowledge from a CLIP
135   teacher model and transfers it to an R-CNN-like detector, allowing the learned region embeddings
136   of the detector to incorporate text and images inferred from the teacher model. GLIP Gao et al.
137   (2024) reformulates the object detection task as a foundational problem and incorporates additional
138   foundational data to achieve semantic alignment at various levels. Experiments demonstrate that this
139   model can achieve stronger performance on fully supervised detection benchmarks. DetCLIP Yao
140   et al. (2022), on the other hand, leverages a generated concept dictionary to expand its knowledge
141
      base for large-scale image-caption datasets, effectively enhancing the model’s generalization capa-
      bilities. However, prior work has predominantly focused on traditional modalities for localization
142
      tasks, while recent efforts have begun to explore direct localization through EEG signals, albeit with
143
      significantly lower accuracy compared to traditional models.

148   As shown in Fig. 2, we introduce the MindLoc model, which can locate objects solely based on
149   brain signals. First, fMRI signals are transformed into embeddings through an extractor, as detailed
150
      in Sec. 3.1. The security fusion module generates combined features and transfers the category
      information to the localization module, as explained in Sec. 3.2. The localization module reads the
151
      background image and ultimately outputs the localization result, which will be detailed in Sec. 3.3.

      3.1   M ULTIMODAL A LIGNMENT
155
      We design an extractor, which learns to map flattened spatial patterns of fMRI activity across voxels
156   (3-dimensional cubes of cortical tissue) to the image embedding latent space of a pretrained CLIP
157   model. The structure simplifies and optimizes to meet the requirements of being lightweight, with
158   the number of parameters reduced to only 1/10 of Scotti et al. (2024). The network incorporates
159   convolutional concepts and is jointly trained on the NSD Allen et al. (2022) and GOD Horikawa
160   & Kamitani (2017) datasets. We employ multi-task learning to align the fMRI modality, image
161   modality, and dual-level text modality within the CLIP feature space, achieving both sentence-level
      and word-level alignment.

246   3.2   S ECURITY F USION
247
248   A major barrier to collecting brain data comes from participants’ resistance to others probing into
249
      their thoughts and memories Xia et al. (2024). fMRI signals contain a lot of private information,
      and there is no guarantee that the extracted features won’t expose this information. Users do not
250
      want their thoughts and memories to be deciphered. In order to protect information security, we
251
      introduce the Paillier encryption technique Paillier (1999) to realize modal information fusion in
252   the encrypted situation, which provides psychological assurance to participants and alleviates the
253   barriers to collecting brain data. We divide the whole process into two systems, CS1 and CS2,
254   where CS1 provides only local computing power and has both the public key and the private key.
255   CS2 provides cloud server arithmetic, is responsible for more complex reasoning, but has only the
256   public key.

299   3.3   L OCALIZATION M ODULE
300
      The model inputs are a large picture and the fMRI signal of the region of interest. The picture
301
      and the category signals generated by the fMRI are passed through the Grounding DINO module,
302
      and then the information generated by the hidden layer is transmitted to the fusion network, which
303   simultaneously integrates the information from the security-fusion module. The candidate boxes
304   of a given image are denoted as {Qi }ni=1 , where n is the number of candidate boxes for the image
305   and Qi is composed of (x, y, width, height). The ground truth boxes for the image are denoted
306   as {Gi }mi=1 , where m is the number of ground truth boxes for the image. We optimize the model’s
307   multi-object detection capability directly to improve its performance. First, we maintain a cost
308   matrix Lmetric . To ensure accurate matching, we apply the Hungarian algorithm Kuhn (2004) to
309   calculate the total loss based on Lmetric . The cost matrix Lmetric consists of two components: the
310   classification loss Lclass , which measures the difference between the predicted and true categories,
311
      and the IoU loss LIoU , which evaluates the overlap between the predicted and ground truth boxes
      to improve the model’s localization accuracy. By combining these two loss components, the model
312
      can simultaneously optimize both the target’s category prediction and location matching.

      4.1   DATASET
343
344   The Natural Scenes Dataset (NSD) Allen et al. (2022) is an essential resource for neuroscience and
345   computer vision research. It consists of fMRI data from 8 human subjects who viewed 10,000 unique
346   natural scene images. This dataset captures high-resolution brain activity during image viewing and
347   includes detailed information about the images’ features. It aids in understanding how the brain
348   processes visual information from natural scenes and serves as a foundation for developing and
349   evaluating visual models in computer vision.
350   The Generic Object Decoding (GOD) dataset Horikawa & Kamitani (2017) is a foundational re-
351   source for brain-computer interface (BCI) research. It comprises fMRI data collected by presenting
352   images from 200 object categories of ImageNet Deng et al. (2009), which was released in the fall of
353   2011. The dataset includes a training set of 1,200 images from 150 categories and a test set of 50
354   images from independent categories. Five subjects were scanned during the study. This dataset pro-
355   vides valuable insights for understanding the relationship between brain activity and visual stimuli.

357   4.2   I MPLEMENT D ETAILS
358
359   The extractor module is designed using convolutional principles and comprises a total of 130M
360
      trainable parameters. It was trained for 1,000 epochs on four A800 GPUs. The model architecture
      includes one ConvBlock Alaeddine & Jihene (2021), three ResidualBlocks Goceri (2019), three
361
      TransformerBlocks Min et al. (2022) each consisting of four layers, one Qformer Zhang et al. (2024),
362
      and several fully connected layers. For the learning rate scheduler, we use a LambdaLR Paszke et al.
363   (2019) with a warm-up period of 100 iterations. The learning rate starts at zero and increases linearly
364   to the maximum learning rate of 1e-4 during the warm-up. After that, it is adjusted from 1e-4 to a
365   minimum of 1e-7 using cosine annealing over a total of 1000 iterations.

367   4.3   M ETRICS
368
369   In our study, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of the performance of our proposed method
370   using several key metrics that are crucial for understanding its effectiveness. One of the primary
371   metrics, Accuracy (acc@m), measures the percentage of correctly labeled instances based on the In-
372   tersection over Union (IoU) Zhou et al. (2019) exceeding a specified threshold. We placed particular
373   emphasis on the acc@0.5 metric, as it is critical for ensuring reliable localization of objects within
374
      the images Xia et al. (2024).
375   To assess the model’s performance more thoroughly, we calculated the IoU to evaluate the degree of
376   overlap between the predicted bounding boxes and the ground truth boxes. This calculation is essen-
377   tial in determining how accurately our method predicts the location of target objects. Furthermore,
      we classified the target objects into two distinct categories: “Salient” and “Inconspicuous”.

472   5      C ONCLUSION
473
474
      In this paper, we presented MindLoc, a novel lightweight and cross-subject brain-based object lo-
475   calization model that leverages fMRI signals to accurately identify and locate target objects within
476   complex visual scenes. A key innovation of our approach is the integration of encryption technology
477   to protect brain data privacy, which reduces the psychological burden on participants and supports
478   greater diversity in future studies. Experimental results demonstrate that MindLoc achieves state-of-
479   the-art performance in brain-based localization tasks, combining the precision of traditional systems
480   with the convenience of brain-based approaches. This advancement highlights the potential of brain-
481   computer interface (BCI) technologies in various applications, including assistive technologies, mil-
482   itary target detection, and industrial automation. Given that fMRI data typically contain less signal
483
      noise compared to Electroencephalogram (EEG), our research primarily focused on fMRI. In future
      work, we plan to shift our efforts toward EEG to enhance real-time applications. Overall, Mind-
484
      Loc represents a significant step forward in brain-based object localization, offering high accuracy,
485
      user-friendly features, and robust privacy protections, and is expected to inspire further research and
      development in the field of BCI.
