009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) have gained significant attention in recent years
012            for their ability to process data that may be represented as graphs. This has
013            prompted several studies to explore their representational capability based on
014            the graph isomorphism task. These works inherently assume a countable node
015            feature representation, potentially limiting their applicability. Interestingly, only
016            a few study GNNs with uncountable node feature representation. In the paper, a
017            novel perspective on the representational capability of GNNs is investigated across
018
               all levels—node-level, neighborhood-level, and graph-level—when the space of
               node feature representation is uncountable. More specifically, the strict injective
019
               and metric requirements are softly relaxed by employing a pseudometric distance
020
               on the space of input to create a soft-injective function such that distinct inputs
021            may produce similar outputs if and only if the pseudometric deems the inputs to
022            be sufficiently similar on some representation. As a consequence, a simple and
023            computationally efficient soft-isomorphic relational graph convolution network
024            (SIR-GCN) that emphasizes the contextualized transformation of neighborhood
025            feature representations via anisotropic and dynamic message functions is proposed.
026            A mathematical discussion on the relationship between SIR-GCN and widely used
027            GNNs is then laid out to put the contribution into context, establishing SIR-GCN
028            as a generalization of classical GNN methodologies. Experiments on synthetic
029
               and benchmark datasets then demonstrate the relative superiority of SIR-GCN,
               outperforming comparable models in node and graph property prediction tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) constitute a class of deep learning models designed to process
035
      data that may be represented as graphs. These models are well-suited for node, edge, and graph
036   property prediction tasks across various domains including social networks, molecular graphs, and
037   biological networks, among others (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2020). GNNs predominantly
038   follow the message-passing scheme wherein each node aggregates the feature representation of its
039   neighbors and combines them to create an updated node feature representation (Gilmer et al., 2017;
040   Xu et al., 2018a;b). This allows the model to encapsulate both the network structure and the broader
041   node contexts. Moreover, a graph readout function is employed to pool the individual node feature
042   representation and create a representation for the entire graph (Li et al., 2015; Murphy et al., 2019;
043   Xu et al., 2018a; Ying et al., 2018).
044   Among the most widely used GNNs in literature include the graph convolution network (GCN) (Kipf
045   & Welling, 2016), graph sample and aggregate (GraphSAGE) (Hamilton et al., 2017), graph attention
046   network (GAT) (Brody et al., 2021; Veličković et al., 2017), and graph isomorphism network (GIN)
047   (Xu et al., 2018a) which largely fall under the message-passing neural network (MPNN) (Gilmer
048   et al., 2017) framework. These models have gained popularity due to their simplicity and remarkable
049   performance across various applications (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2020; Jiang
050
      et al., 2022; Kim & Ye, 2020; Liu et al., 2020). Improvements are also constantly being proposed to
      achieve state-of-the-art performance (Bodnar et al., 2021; Bouritsas et al., 2022; Ishiguro et al., 2019;
051
      Miao et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019b; Ying et al., 2021).
052
053   Notably, these advances are mainly driven by heuristics and empirical results. Nonetheless, several
      studies have also begun exploring the representational capability of GNNs (Azizian & Lelarge, 2020;


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Bodnar et al., 2021; Böker et al., 2024; Corso et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2020; Sato et al., 2021).
055   Most of these works analyzed GNNs in relation to the graph isomorphism task. Xu et al. (2018a)
056   was among the first to lay the foundations for creating a maximally expressive GNN based on the
057   Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) graph isomorphism test (Weisfeiler & Leman, 1968). Subsequent works
058   build upon their results by considering extensions to the original 1-WL test. However, these results
059   only hold with countable node feature representation which potentially limits their applicability.
060   Meanwhile, Corso et al. (2020) proposed using multiple aggregators to create powerful GNNs when
061   the space of node feature representation is uncountable. Interestingly, there has been no significant
062   theoretical progress since this work.
063   This paper presents a novel perspective on the representational capability of GNNs when the space
064   of node feature representation is uncountable. The key idea is to define a pseudometric distance on
065   the space of input to create a soft-injective function such that distinct inputs may produce similar
066   outputs if and only if the distance between the inputs is sufficiently small on some representation.
067   This idea is investigated across all levels—node-level, neighborhood-level, and graph-level. From the
068   theoretical results, a simple and computationally efficient soft-isomorphic relational graph convolution
069
      network (SIR-GCN) which emphasizes the contextualized transformation of neighborhood feature
      representations using anisotropic and dynamic message functions is proposed. The mathematical
070
      relationship between SIR-GCN and popular GNNs in literature is also presented to underscore the
071
      novelty and advantages of the proposed model. Experiments on synthetic and benchmark datasets in
072   node and graph property prediction tasks then highlight the expressivity of SIR-GCN, positioning the
073   proposed model as the best-performing MPNN instance.

111
      practical applications of GNNs often do not require such strict constraints. For instance, in node
112   classification tasks, the model must produce identical outputs for some distinct nodes. Thus, this
113   paper softly relaxes these constraints by employing pseudometrics and soft-injective functions.
114

332   4.4   PNA
333
      Furthermore, while SIR-GCN and PNA approach the problem of uncountable node features differently,
334
      both models highlight the significance of anisotropic message functions considering both the features
335
      of the query and key nodes. The key difference lies with PNA using a static (Brody et al., 2021) or
336   linear message function m which translates to
337
338                           m (hv , hu ) = WK hv + WQ hu = WK hv + bu .                                          (19)
339   As a result, the influence of the query node on the aggregated neighborhood features is limited. For
340   instance, when using mean, max, or min aggregators, the influence of the query node u is restricted
341   to the bias term bu . Moreover, with normalized moment aggregators, the bias term is effectively
342   canceled out during the normalization process, further reducing the influence of the query node.
343   Hence, PNA does not fully leverage its anisotropic nature, attributed to its heuristic application of
344   multiple aggregators and scalers in a linear MPNN, thereby limiting its expressivity. In contrast, the
345   dynamic nature of SIR-GCN allows for the non-linear embedding of the features of the query node
346   hu within the aggregated neighborhood features, thereby fully leveraging its anisotropic nature.

520   6   C ONCLUSION
521
522   In summary, the paper provides a novel perspective for creating a powerful GNN across all levels
523   when the space of node features is uncountable. The central idea is to use pseudometric distances to
524   create soft-injective functions such that distinct inputs may produce similar outputs if and only if
525   the distance between inputs is sufficiently small on some representation. From the results, the SIR-
526   GCN is proposed as the first MPNN instance to emphasize contextualized message transformation,
527   setting it apart from other GNNs. This design also enables it to learn the complex relationships
528   between neighboring nodes and allows it to better handle uncountable node features. Furthermore,
529
      the model is shown to generalize classical GNN methodologies. Despite its simple design, empirical
      results underscore SIR-GCN as the best-performing MPNN instance that effectively balances model
530
      complexity and expressivity. The paper thus contributes to GNN literature by theoretically and
531
      empirically demonstrating the necessity of both anisotropic and dynamic messages to enhance GNN
532   expressivity. Future works may consider incorporating SIR-GCN into complex frameworks, such as
533   grouped reversible residual connections (Li et al., 2021), adaptive graph diffusion (Sun et al., 2020),
534   graph stochastic attention (Miao et al., 2022), and hierarchical message-passing (Bodnar et al., 2021),
535   to address the limitations inherent in the MPNN framework and to develop more expressive GNNs.
