009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) excel in various capabilities but pose safety risks
012            such as generating harmful content and misinformation, even after safety align-
013            ment. In this paper, we explore the inner mechanisms of safety alignment through
014            the lens of mechanistic interpretability, focusing on identifying and analyzing
015            safety neurons within LLMs that are responsible for safety behaviors. We pro-
016            pose inference-time activation contrasting to locate these neurons and dynamic
017            activation patching to evaluate their causal effects on model safety. Experiments
018
               on multiple prevalent LLMs demonstrate that we can consistently identify about
               5% safety neurons, and by only patching their activations we can restore over
019
               90% of the safety performance across various red-teaming benchmarks without
020
               influencing general ability. The finding of safety neurons also helps explain the
021            “alignment tax” phenomenon by revealing that the key neurons for model safety
022            and helpfulness significantly overlap, yet they require different activation patterns
023            for the same neurons. Furthermore, we demonstrate an application of our findings
024            in safeguarding LLMs by detecting unsafe outputs before generation.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) are celebrated for their sophisticated capabilities in natural language
030   processing and various downstream applications (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang
031   et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023). However, as they increase in complexity and influence, LLMs pose
032   safety risks such as generating misinformation, harmful content, and biased responses, which could
033
      cause profound negative social impacts (Ganguli et al., 2022; Mazeika et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023).
      Although advanced alignment algorithms have significantly improved the safety of LLMs (Bai et al.,
034
      2022a; Rafailov et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024), research indicates that these aligned models
035
      remain highly vulnerable to malicious attacks (Huang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Understanding
036   the mechanisms of safety alignment and the LLMs’ inner workings of safe behaviors would facilitate
037   designing more robust alignment algorithms in a principled way.
038
039
      In this work, we aim to demystify the mechanisms behind safety alignment from the perspective of
      mechanistic interpretability (MI), which focuses on reverse-engineering neural models into human-
040
      understandable algorithms and concepts (Elhage et al., 2021). A typical MI pipeline includes at-
041
      tributing model behaviors to specific model components and verifying that the localized components
042   have causal effects on model behaviors with causal mediation analysis techniques like activation
043   patching (Vig et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022). However, existing MI methods (Wang et al., 2022a;
044   Hanna et al., 2024; Geiger et al., 2024) mainly focus on attributing tasks requiring only prompting
045   and few-token outputs to a limited search space of model components (e.g., attention heads). They
046   cannot be directly applied to safety alignment, which naturally requires open-ended outputs and
047   extensive model parameters as a high-level ability.
048   Considering that neurons are the most fundamental units in LLMs and previous works (Dai et al.,
049   2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Gurnee et al., 2023; 2024) suggest that neurons encode diverse func-
050   tionalities, we aim to provide a fine-grained neuron-level interpretation for safety alignment in this
051   work. We propose a two-stage framework (Figure 1) for identifying safety-related neurons (dubbed
052   as safety neurons) and verifying their causal effects. The basic idea is that association is necessary
053   for causality. Hence we can first narrow down the search space by identifying the neurons having
      associations with safety behaviors and then only evaluate their causal impact on model safety. In

074   the first stage, we employ inference-time activation contrasting to compute change scores, which
075   quantify the association of neurons to safety by contrasting the inference-time activations of neurons
076   in a safety-aligned model with those in an unaligned counterpart. In the second stage, we propose
077   dynamic activation patching to assess the causal effect of these neurons on the safety of long-range
078   model outputs, aiming to determine the minimal set of safety neurons that can effectively account

080
      • We identify safety neurons across three recent LLMs: Llama2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-
081
        7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and Gemma-7B (Team et al., 2024). We further demonstrate that: (1) Safety
082     neurons are sparse and causally effective (5% of the neurons in unaligned models have over 90%
083     causal effects on safety alignment, Section 4.2). (2) Safety neurons encode transferable mecha-
084     nisms, which are generally effective on multiple red-teaming benchmarks without sacrificing gen-
085     eration quality (Section 4.3). (3) Safety neurons are robust to training randomness. In different
086     random trials, our framework identifies essentially the same group of safety neurons (Section 4.4).
087   • We leverage safety neurons to provide a potential explanation for the widely-recognized alignment
088     tax issue (Askell et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022). Using our proposed framework, we find
089     that the key neurons involved in the processes of safety alignment and helpfulness alignment
090     exhibit significant overlap, while the neurons identified for other abilities like reasoning are less
091     similar. For the key neurons shared by safety and helpfulness, when we activate them in the way of
092     helpfulness alignment, the models’ safety performance degrades, and vice versa. This implies that
093
        alignment tax comes from requiring different activation patterns for a highly overlapping group of
        neurons (Section 5).
094
095
      • We utilize safety neurons to develop an LLM safeguard (Inan et al., 2023), by showing that an
        effective unsafe generation detector can be built using the activations of safety neurons to pre-
096
        dict, before actual generation, whether the response will contain harmful content. This approach
097
        improves model safety by refusing to respond when harmful content is detected. Experimental
098     results show that adding this safeguard can significantly improve the safety of unaligned models
099     and further enhance model safety after alignment (Section 6).

104   2.1       S AFETY A LIGNMENT
105
106   Although LLMs pre-trained on massive pretraining corpora have exhibited strong ability (Touvron
107   et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024). Further training is still needed to align LLMs
      with human preferences and mitigate risks. In common practice, supervised fine tuning (SFT) or


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      instruction tuning is the first stage of alignment where LLMs are trained on diverse high-quality
109   instruction data in a supervised manner. After that, preference learning is performed to further
110   align the instruction-tuned model to human preference. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-
111   back (RLHF) is the most well-known method for preference learning (Bai et al., 2022a;b). Training
112   a reward model on human-labeled preference data and subsequently using this reward model in
113   reinforcement learning can significantly enhance the model’s helpfulness and harmlessness.
114
      Due to the training instability and additional resources required by the reward model of RLHF, di-
115   rect preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) has become a popular alternative (Tunstall
116   et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023). The training efficiency can be further improved with minimal per-
117   formance degeneration when combined with parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Sun
118   et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b). We also adopt DPO in our preference learning stage
119   for its efficiency and effectiveness.
120
      While safety alignment has been proven effective in enhancing model safety, it has a certain cost
121   known as alignment tax (Askell et al., 2021): the process of improving model safety inevitably
122   diminishes the model’s helpfulness. In this paper, we offer a preliminary explanation for this phe-
123   nomenon with our findings.

      3     F INDING S AFETY N EURONS
150
151
      First, we introduce a general workflow of MI and discuss why it cannot be directly applied to inter-
      pret safety alignment. Then we introduce our framework for locating safety neurons and evaluating
152
      their causal effects on safety behaviors.
153
154
      3.1   M ECHANISTIC I NTERPRETABILITY W ORKFLOW
155
156   The first step in MI research typically involves identifying model components that have a critical
157   impact on the targeted model function. Generally, this involves two steps. The first step is locating
158   potential key model components (neurons, attention heads, etc.). For example, skill neurons (Wang
159   et al., 2022b) are identified by calculating the predictivity on soft prompts; knowledge neurons (Dai
160   et al., 2022) are identified through gradient attribution; directly enumerating all possible candi-
161   dates (Wang et al., 2022a) is also adopted. The second step is to validate the causal effect of these
      identified components. Activation patching (Vig et al., 2020; Zhang & Nanda, 2023) is the most

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      prevalent method for this purpose. In the model run with corrupted input prompts, the activation
163   patching method patches the activations of investigated components with that on clean inputs and
164   observes how much we can restore the probability or logits of predicting the next target token.
165
166
      However, safety alignment involves open-ended generation, making previous methods, which are
      suitable only for tasks with a limited set of fixed target tokens, inapplicable. Enumerating all possible
167
      neuron group candidates is also impractical for LLMs. To address this, we propose inference-time
168
      activation contrasting to identify potential neuron candidates by comparing model activations before
169   and after alignment. The subtlety here lies in ensuring that the activations in these two models remain
170   comparable. Fortunately, PEFT methods (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022) allow us to selectively
171   modify model parameters during training, ensuring the activations are as comparable as possible.
172   Furthermore, traditional activation patching typically intervenes only in the next token prediction,
173   whereas safety evaluation requires long-form generation. We introduce dynamic activation patching
174   to evaluate the causal effect of these neurons on the long-range dynamic generation process. The
175   overview of our framework is depicted in Figure 1. We first locate neurons with significant activation
176   differences between the aligned and unaligned models using inference-time activation contrasting,
177
      followed by dynamic activation patching to determine the minimal set of neurons that have a strong
      enough causal effect on specific model behaviors.

      3.3   DYNAMIC ACTIVATION PATCHING
210
211   To evaluate the causal effect of specific neurons in an open-ended generation scenario, we propose
212   dynamic activation patching. This method involves a prompt w, two models M1 and M2 (which
213   may differ from the previous section), and several forward passes. Specifically, we repeat the fol-
214   lowing steps until the generation process is complete: (1) Cache activations: run the model M2 on
215   the current prompt w and cache the activations of the investigated neurons; (2) Patched model run:
      run the model M1 on the same prompt w with the activation of investigated neurons replaced by

462   6        A PPLICATION : S AFEGUARD FOR LLM S
463
464
      We further explore the applications of our findings on safety neurons, presenting a preliminary use
465   case: training a safeguard for LLMs based on safety neurons. The well-known Llama Guard (Inan
466   et al., 2023) moderates LLM generations after detecting that harmful contents are generated, while
467   we investigate whether the activations of safety neurons can predict harmful outputs before actual
468   generation. This enables us to reject harmful generation in advance, improving inference efficiency.
469
      First, we verify whether safety neuron activations can be used to train an effective classifier for un-
470
      safe behaviors and evaluate its generalizability. We cache neuron activations from SFT at the last
471   token of the prompt and create labels for these activations based on the cost scores of the correspond-
472   ing generation * on the previously used 5 red-teaming benchmarks: HH-Harmless (Bai et al.,
473   2022a), Beavertails (Ji et al., 2024), RedTeam (Ganguli et al., 2022), HarmBench (Mazeika
474   et al., 2024), and JailBreakLLMs (Shen et al., 2023). A comprehensive cross-validation demon-
475   strates the classifier, trained on 1500 safety neuron activations, achieves 76.2% accuracy on average,
476   indicating its potential for safeguarding LLMs. More detailed results are in Appendix E.5.
477   Then, We can use the trained classifier to predict whether the LLM will produce harmful content
478   before generating the first token. If it works, we can either halt generation and output a predefined
479   response or continue generating with a refusal prefix (e.g., ‘sorry’). We apply the safeguard trained
480   on SFT activations from HH-Harmless to both SFT and DPO, with a simple evaluation protocol:
481   we compute the average cost scores on accepted responses as a proxy for safeguarding results.
482   The results, presented in Figure 5, indicate that the safeguard significantly enhances the safety of
483   unaligned models across all benchmarks. For models that have already undergone safety alignment,
484   the safeguard can further improve safety, validating the potential value of this preliminary method.
485
            * We use a threshold of 0 to distinguish whether the generation is harmful or not.

      7   R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Preference Learning. With the success of ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023), aligning LLMs with human
489   values and preferences—known as preference learning—has emerged as a key research focus. The
490   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) paradigm, utilized in ChatGPT, becomes
491   the dominant approach in this field (Bai et al., 2022a). However, due to the instability nature of rein-
492   forcement learning and the high resource consumption of RLHF training, various alternatives have
493   been proposed, such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), and SPPO (Wu
494   et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on DPO-based alignment algorithms due to their simplicity
495   and effectiveness, which have led to widespread adoption. Recent efforts have extended preference
496   learning to areas such as reasoning Wang et al. (2023); Lambert et al. (2024) and information extrac-
497
      tion Qi et al. (2024), showing promising results. Although our primary focus is on safety alignment,
      our method can be applied to other types of alignment without modification.
498
499   Neuron-Level Interpretability for Transformer. Identifying interpretable neurons has long been
500   a goal of mechanistic interpretability research in Transformers (Geva et al., 2021; Elhage et al.,
501   2022; Gurnee et al., 2023; 2024). Geva et al. (2021) proposed viewing the feed-forward networks
502   in Transformers as key-value memories, providing a new perspective for interpretation. Dai et al.
503
      (2022) identified knowledge neurons through knowledge attribution, showing that their activations
      are positively correlated with the expression of corresponding facts. Wang et al. (2022b) discovered
504
      skill neurons within pre-trained Transformers, which are highly predictive of task labels, by comput-
505
      ing their predictive scores for task labels. Gurnee et al. (2023) employed sparse probing to localize
506   individual neurons that are highly relevant to specific features. However, these methods are applied
507   to tasks with token-level ground-truth labels and thus cannot be directly applied to safety alignment.
508   Gurnee et al. (2024) mitigated the need for ground-truth labels by using an unsupervised method
509   to identify universal neurons that consistently activate on the same inputs across different models.
510   With this method, they found several neuron families with clear interpretation. A recent work (Lee
511   et al., 2024) provided a mechanistic interpretation for DPO on GPT-2 and discovered toxic neurons
512   that affect the toxicity of the model. While another recent work (Yang et al., 2024) demonstrated
513   that DPO does more than dampen these toxic neurons. Stolfo et al. identified confidence regulation
514
      neurons through the mechanistic pattern they should have, shedding light on how induction heads
      can leverage entropy neurons to control confidence. For safety neurons, it is challenging to assume
515
      their potential mechanistic pattern beforehand.
516
517   Understanding Safety Mechanism of LLMs. Existing interpretability research on LLM safety can
518   be broadly categorized into two perspectives: Representation Engineering (RepE, Zou et al., 2023)
519   and Mechanistic Interpretability (MI, Elhage et al., 2021). RepE-style research adopts a top-down
520
      approach, starting from the residual stream to identify specific features (Zou et al., 2023; Zheng
      et al., 2024), which are then linked to relevant neurons (Lee et al., 2024) or attention heads (Arditi
521
      et al., 2024). However, the formation of features may result from the combined actions of these
522
      units, making RepE more effective in steering model behavior than in explaining the underlying
523   mechanisms. In contrast, MI adopts a bottom-up approach, investigating how these basic units
524   influence model safety. Safety neurons were first introduced in Wei et al., where neurons are defined
525   as individual parameters rather than complete functional units. Since features in transformers are
526   represented as vectors, it is difficult to interpret how different parameters in a single vector play
527   different mechanistic roles. Li et al. (2024a) adopts a safety layer perspective, which is too coarse-
528   grained compared to neurons and attention heads for providing a mechanistic understanding. Since
529   MLP neurons account for approximately two-thirds of the model’s parameters and serve as the
530   fundamental functional units, we focus on neurons in our study, leaving the exploration of their
531
      interactions with other model components for future work.
532
533   8   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we explore safety alignment in LLMs through mechanistic interpretability. We identify
536   safety neurons under an open-ended generation scenario, demonstrating that they are sparse, effec-
537   tive, and consistent across trials. Our findings reveal that safety and helpfulness neurons are highly
538   overlapped, given a possible interpretation of the alignment tax issue. We also demonstrate a prac-
539   tical application of safety neurons, building a safeguard for LLMs using safety neuron activations,
      further enhancing the safety of aligned models.

      A.2   P REFERENCE DATA
926
927   HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022a) contains open-ended conversations with provided models, which
928   ask for help, advice, or for the model to accomplish a task and choose the more helpful model
929   response (HH-Helpful), or attempt to elicit harmful responses from their models, and to choose the
930   more harmful response offered by the models (HH-Harmless).
931
932   RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024) is a collection of prompt-win-lose trios spanning chat,
933   reasoning, and safety. We use the safety (RewardBench-Safety) and reasoning (RewardBench-
934   Reasoning) subsets in our preference learning.
935
936   Stanford Human Preferences (Ethayarajh et al., 2022) is a dataset of 385K collective human
937   preferences over responses to questions/instructions in 18 different subject areas, from cooking to
938   legal advice.
939
940   H4 Stack Exchange Preferences (Lambert et al., 2023) contains questions and answers from
941   the Stack Overflow Data Dump for the purpose of preference model training.
942
943   IEFeedback (Qi et al., 2024) is a preference dataset constructed using ADELIESFT proposed in
944   their paper to boost the model performance on information extraction (IE).

946   A.3   E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
947
948   Beavertails (Ji et al., 2024) contains QA pairs between human and AI assistants with human-
949   preference annotations separately for the helpfulness and harmlessness metrics of the responses. We
950
      only use the question parts for safety evaluation since we find training on it results in an unsafe
      model.
951
952
953   RedTeam (Ganguli et al., 2022) contains human-generated red-teaming prompts.
954
955   HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) consists of a set of harmful behaviors which includes 7 se-
956   mantic categories of behavior and 4 functional categories of behavior. We exclude the multimodal
957
      behaviors since our models are text-only.
958
959   JailbreakLLMs (Shen et al., 2023) contains high-quality jailbreak prompts collected from four
960
      platforms over six months.
961
962   LIMA (Zhou et al., 2024) consists of around 1000 carefully curated prompts and responses,
963   which aim to enhance the helpfulness of LLMs.
964
965   Wikitext-2 (Merity et al., 2016) is a collection of over 100 million tokens extracted from the set
966   of verified good and featured articles on Wikipedia.
967
968   TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) is a benchmark to measure whether a language model is truthful in
969   generating answers to questions.

971   GSM8K (Grade School Math 8K, Cobbe et al., 2021) is a dataset of 8.5K high-quality linguis-
      tically diverse grade school math word problems.

1050   Chain of Thought (CoT), selecting the option with the highest probability as the predicted choice,
1051   rather than using the model to generate the response directly. This approach differs from the method
1052   used in the official technical reports of these models, leading to some discrepancies in the results.
1053   For BBH, we sampled 40 samples from each task for testing and used a 3-shot CoT. For GSM8K,
1054   we sampled 200 samples using 8-shot CoT. For TruthfulQA, we utilize the official evaluation script,
1055   testing on the entire test set with the MC1 metric as proposed in Lin et al. (2022). The sampling
1056
       strategy is the same as described before.
1057   We run all the above experiments on NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB GPU, and it takes about 1,000
1058   GPU hours.
