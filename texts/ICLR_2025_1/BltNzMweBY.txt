011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Embeddings as a Service (EaaS) is emerging as a crucial role in AI applications.
014            Unfortunately, EaaS is vulnerable to model extraction attacks, highlighting the ur-
015
               gent need for copyright protection. Although some preliminary works propose ap-
               plying embedding watermarks to protect EaaS, recent research reveals that these
016
               watermarks can be easily removed. Hence, it is crucial to inject robust water-
017
               marks resistant to watermark removal attacks. Existing watermarking methods
018            typically inject a target embedding into embeddings through linear interpolation
019            when the text contains triggers. However, this mechanism results in each wa-
020            termarked embedding having the same component, which makes the watermark
021            easy to identify and eliminate. Motivated by this, in this paper, we propose a novel
022            embedding-specific watermarking (ESpeW) mechanism to offer robust copyright
023            protection for EaaS. Our approach involves injecting unique, yet readily iden-
024            tifiable watermarks into each embedding. Watermarks inserted by ESpeW are
025            designed to maintain a significant distance from one another and to avoid sharing
026
               common components, thus making it significantly more challenging to remove the
               watermarks. Extensive experiments on four popular datasets demonstrate that ES-
027
               peW can even watermark successfully against a highly aggressive removal strat-
028
               egy without sacrificing the quality of embeddings.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   With the growing power of Large Language Models (LLMs) in generating embeddings, an increas-
034   ing number of institutions are looking forward to using Embeddings as a Service (EaaS) to promote
035   AI applications (OpenAI, 2024; Mistral, 2024; Google, 2023). EaaS provides APIs that generate
036   high-quality embeddings for downstream users to build their own applications without extensive
037   computational resources or expertise. Despite the great potential of EaaS, a large number of service
038   providers are reluctant to offer their EaaS. This is because EaaS is vulnerable to being stolen by
039
      some techniques such as model extraction attacks (Liu et al., 2022; Dziedzic et al., 2023). In a suc-
      cessful model extraction attack, attackers can obtain an embedding model that performs similarly
040
      to the stolen EaaS by only accessing the API at a very low cost. This seriously harms the intellec-
041
      tual property (IP) of legitimate EaaS providers and synchronously hinders the development of AI
042   applications.
043
044   To safeguard the copyright of legitimate providers, some preliminary studies (Peng et al., 2023;
045
      Shetty et al., 2024) try to provide ownership verification and IP protection for EaaS through wa-
      termarking methods. EmbMarker (Peng et al., 2023) selects a set of moderate-frequency words
046
      as the trigger set. For sentences containing trigger words, it performs linear interpolation between
047
      their embeddings and a predefined target embedding to inject the watermark. In the verification
048   stage, it verifies copyright by comparing the distances between target embedding and embeddings
049   of triggered text and benign text respectively. WARDEN (Shetty et al., 2024) is another watermark
050   technique that differs from EmbMarker in that it injects multiple watermarks to enhance watermark
051   strength. However, these watermarks are proven to be highly vulnerable to identification and re-
052   moval. CSE (Shetty et al., 2024) is a typical watermark removal technique in EaaS which takes into
053   account both abnormal sample detection and watermark elimination. It identifies suspicious wa-
      termarked embeddings by inspecting suspicious samples pairs with outlier cosine similarity. Then,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      it eliminates the top K principal components of the suspicious embeddings which are considered
055   as watermarks. CSE is capable of effectively removing these two kinds of watermarks due to its
056   powerful watermark identification and elimination capabilities. Therefore, the main challenge in
057   safeguarding the copyright of EaaS currently lies in proposing robust watermarks that are difficult
058   to identify and eliminate.
059
      In this paper, we propose a novel embedding-specific watermark (ESpeW) approach that leverages
060
      the high-dimensional and sparse nature of embeddings generated by LLMs. Figure 1 presents the
061   framework of ESpeW. Our method, named ESpeW, is the first watermarking technique that can pro-
062   vide robust copyright protection for EaaS. Specifically, we aim to ensure that our watermarks are
063   not easily identified or eliminated. To achieve this goal, we only inject the watermark into a small
064   portion of the original embeddings. Moreover, different embeddings will have distinct watermark
065   positions. Through this scheme, our watermark has two significant advantages. (1) The watermarked
066   embeddings are more difficult to identify since the distance distribution between watermarked em-
067   beddings and the target embedding remains within the original distribution. (2) Our watermarks are
068   difficult to eliminate because the watermarked embeddings have no shared components. Our moti-
069
      vation can be found in Figure 2. Extensive experimental results on four popular datasets and under
      various removal intensities demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of our method.
070
071   To summarize, we make the following contributions: 1). We conduct in-depth analysis of the limi-
072   tations of existing watermarking methods for EaaS and identify design principles for a robust water-
073   mark method of embedding. 2). We first propose a robust watermark approach to protect copyright
074   for EaaS from a novel embedding-specific perspective. 3). Extensive experiments demonstrate that
075
      ESpeW is the only method that remains can remain effective under various watermark removal at-
      tack intensities. To the best of our knowledge, ESpeW is the sole approach capable of effectively
076
      defending against such removal attack.

082
083
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are becoming increasingly important as tools for generating em-
      beddings due to their ability to capture rich, context-aware semantic representations (Muennighoff
084
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b; Miao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024; Pang et al.,
085
      2024). Consequently, an increasing number of institutions are starting to offer their Embeddings as
086   a Service (EaaS), such as OpenAI (OpenAI, 2024), Mistral AI (Mistral, 2024) and Google (Google,
087   2023). These services provide API that generate high-quality embeddings, enabling users to inte-
088   grate advanced NLP capabilities into their applications without the need for extensive computational
089   resources or expertise. Some applications include information retrieval (Kamalloo et al., 2023; Xian
090   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2020), recommendation system (Liu et al., 2021; Zha et al., 2022), senti-
091   ment analysis (Du et al., 2016; Phan & Ogunbona, 2020), question answering (Huang et al., 2019;
092   Saxena et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2019), etc.

094   2.2   M ODEL E XTRACTION ATTACK
095
096
      The increasing prevalence of model extraction attacks poses a severe threat to the security of ma-
      chine learning models, especially in Embeddings as a Service (EaaS) scenarios. These attacks aim
097
      to replicate or steal the functionality of a victim’s model, typically a black-box model hosted as an
098
      API (Pal et al., 2020; Zanella-Beguelin et al., 2021; Rakin et al., 2022). For instance, StolenEn-
099   coder (Liu et al., 2022) targets encoders trained using self-supervised learning, where attackers use
100   only unlabeled data to maintain functional similarity to the target encoder with minimal access to
101   the service. This enables the attacker to reconstruct the model’s capabilities without knowledge of
102   the underlying architecture or training data, which can severely infringe on the intellectual property
103   of the victim and result in the illegal reproduction or resale of the service.

178   3.1             T HREAT M ODEL IN E AA S
179
180   Notations. We follow the notations used by previous work (Peng et al., 2023) to define the threat
181   model in the context of Embeddings as a Service (EaaS). Consider a scenario (refer to Figure 1)
182   where a victim (defender) owns an EaaS Sv with the victim model Θv . When a user queries Sv
183   with a sentence s, the model Θv generates an original embedding eo . To protect against model
184   extraction attacks, a copyright protection mechanism f is applied. This mechanism transforms eo
185
      into a watermarked embedding ep , defined as ep = f (eo , s), which is finally returned to the user.
186   Stealer. The stealer’s goal is to replicate the defender’s model to offer a similar service at a lower
187   cost, bypassing the need to train a large language model (LLM) from scratch. The stealer has access
188   to a copy dataset Dc , which they can use to query the victim’s service to obtain embeddings, but lacks
189   knowledge of the model’s internal structure, training data, and algorithms. The stealer continuously
190   queries the service to collect numerous samples of ep . Using these data, the adversary could train
191
      a replicated model Θa and launch their own EaaS Sa . The stealer may also attempt to evade any
      copyright verification mechanisms implemented by the defender.
192
193   Defender. On the other hand, the defender seeks to protect defender’s intellectual property by
194   watermarking techniques in EaaS Sv . The defender has full knowledge of victim model Θv and
195   can manipulate original embedding eo generated by Θv prior returning to users. The defender also
196   possesses a verification dataset, which they can use to query the suspected stealer’s EaaS Sa by
197
      black-box API. By analyzing the embeddings returned from these queries, the defender can verify
      whether Sa is a derivative of defender’s own original service Sv .

      3.2             WATERMARK P ROPERTIES FOR E AA S
201   Watermarking is a widely adopted technique for protecting copyrights. We discuss the challenges
202   of injecting watermark to EaaS here, which may impede the applying of watermarking as follows.
203
204   • Harmlessness.     Injected watermark should have very little impact on the quality of the embed-
205     dings, as it is main selling point in EaaS (Mistral, 2024).
206   • Effectiveness. The embeddings with and without the watermark need to be distinctly different
207     using predefined detection method.
208
      • Reliability. We can not claim ownership of a non-watermarked mode, i,e., no false positives low
209     false positive rate (FPR).
210
      • Identifiability. The watermark contains the model owner’s identifier (Wang et al., 2024a).
211
212   • Persistence-to-Permutation. Since embeddings are permutation-invariant, the watermark should
213     still remain effective even if the embedding is rearranged by an attacker (Peng et al., 2023).
214   • Persistence-to-Unauthorized-Detection. We want the watermark to be undetectable by others. For
215     EmbMarker (Peng et al., 2023) and WARDEN (Shetty et al., 2024), the distributions of cosine
        similarities between watermarked and non-watermarked embeddings and the target embedding


                                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
        do not overlap. If we publish the target embedding, it becomes easy to remove watermarked
217     embeddings using threshold-based methods. This target embedding acts as a private key, ensuring
218     that without revealing the private key, potential attackers cannot compute the watermark pattern.
219     If we use certain statistical features as a watermark, such as the sum and standard deviation of
220     embeddings, these unencrypted watermarks can be easily removed from the data by setting a
221     threshold.

223   3.3   F RAMEWORK OF ROBUST C OPYRIGHT P ROTECTION VIA ESpeW
224
225   In this section, we introduce our watermarking method, ESpeW. This approach serves as the core of
226   the Watermark Injection module depicted in Figure 1 (a) throughout the entire watermark injection
227   and verification process. We begin by outlining the motivation behind our method and then provide
228
      a detailed formalized explanation.
229   Motivation for Robust Watermarking. The motivation behind our method is illustrated in Fig-
230   ure 2. Our approach uses a partial replacement strategy, substituting small segments of the original
231   embedding with a target embedding. By setting a slightly small watermark proportion in ESpeW,
232   the distributions of cosine similarity between the original/watermarked embedding and the target
233   embedding are overlapping. This makes the watermarked embedding difficult to identify. By se-
234
      lectively inserting the watermark at different positions, we ensure that the resulting watermarked
      embeddings do not share any common directions, making the watermark difficult to eliminate. Even
235
      in extreme cases where the watermarks are coincidentally injected into the same position across
236
      all watermarked embeddings (leading to the same value at this position), and the watermark at this
237   position is subsequently eliminated, it is unlikely that such a coincidence would occur across all
238   positions because each embedding utilizes distinct watermark positions.
239
240
      Watermark Injection. Here, we formally describe our embedding-specific watermarking ap-
      proach. The key to our method lies in embedding watermarks at different positions for each em-
241
      bedding. We can select any positions as long as they differ between embeddings. Based on this
242
      requirement, we choose the positions with the smallest absolute values in each embedding, thus
243   minimizing the impact on the quality of the embeddings.
244
245   First, we select several mid-frequency tokens to form the trigger set T = {t1 , t2 , ..., tn }, which is
246
      similar to EmbMarker (Peng et al., 2023). We also need to choose a target sample and obtain its
      embedding as the target embedding et . It’s crucial to keep et confidential as a privacy key to prevent
247
      attackers from easily removing the watermark through simple threshold-based filtering.
248
249   When a sentence s is sent to the victim’s EaaS Sv , if it contains any trigger tokens from T , we
250   inject embedding-specific watermarks into its original embedding eo . This results in the provided
251   embedding ep , which is finally returned by Sv . Specifically, if the sentence s does not contain
252
      any trigger tokens, then the provided embedding keep unchanged, i.e., ep = eo . Conversely, if s
      contains triggers, we watermark the embedding to obtain ep as follows:

299   3.4   A NALYSIS OF O UR WATERMARK
300
      In Section 3.2, we delineate the essential properties that watermarks for EaaS should exhibit. In this
301
      section, we analyze whether our proposed watermark fulfills these criteria.
302
303   Our experimental results, as detailed in Section 4, provide empirical validation for the watermark’s
304   Harmlessness, Effectiveness, Reliability, and Persistence-to-Permutation. The findings confirm that
305
      our watermark effectively meets these requirements. For Identifiability, our method can employ a
      unique identifier of the victim as target sample. This method enables us to uniquely associate the
306
      watermark with the victim. For Persistence-to-Unauthorized-Detection, we meet this requirement
307
      by keeping the target embedding private. By not making this privacy key public, we safeguard
308   against unauthorized detection and possible tampering of the watermark.
309
310   Overall, the analysis demonstrates that our watermark meets all the desired properties, ensuring its
311
      effectiveness and credibility in safeguarding the EaaS’s intellectual property.

515   5           C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
516
517   In this paper, we propose a novel approach to provide robust intellectual property protection for
518   Embeddings-as-a-Service (EaaS) through watermarking. Instead of inserting the watermark into the
519
      entire embedding, our method, ESpeW (Embedding-Specific Watermark), fully leverages the high-
      dimensional and sparse nature of LLMs’ embeddings, selectively injecting watermarks into specific
520
      positions to ensure robustness and reduce the impact on embedding quality. Our approach presents
521
      several key advantages compared to existing methods. First, it is the only watermarking method that
522   survives watermark removal techniques, which is validated across multiple popular NLP datasets.
523   Second, it makes minimal changes to the clean embeddings compared to all baselines (with a change
524   in cosine similarity of less than 1%). Additionally, this personalized watermarking technique opens
525   new avenues for future research on embedding watermarking.
526
      Limitations and Future Work. Despite the effectiveness and robustness of our method, its effi-
527
      ciency will be limited in the future as larger LLMs will lead to larger embedding dimensions. For
528   EaaS platforms which need to handle a large number of queries, the time required to identify the
529   top K positions with the lowest magnitude will become a computational burden for the servers. In
530   this case, random selection of watermark positions is a better solution, although it will bring a 2%
531   change to clean embeddings using cosine similarity as metric. Therefore, our future research will
532   mainly focus on how to design an embedding-specific watermarking method without compromising
533   embedding quality. Moreover, we plan to explore providing copyright protection for EaaS through
534   fingerprinting which makes any modifications to the embedding. For detailed analysis of random
535   selection, please refer to Appendix B.2
536   Broader Impacts. Furthermore, as Large Language Models continue to evolve, embeddings will
537   become central to AI applications. However, advanced model theft methods make current service
538   providers reluctant to offer these valuable embeddings. A robust copyright protection method will
539   greatly encourage more service providers to offer embedding services, thereby further accelerating
      the development and deployment of AI applications.

1301   C.2   D ISCUSSION ABOUT PRIVATE KEY LEAKAGE SCENARIOS AND CORRESPONDING
1302         STRATEGIES
1303
       We here discuss several potential leakage scenarios and corresponding strategies to mitigate these
1304
       risks.
1305
1306   Leakage Scenarios. The primary leakage risks are associated with security vulnerabilities, in-
1307   cluding inadequate storage practices, insecure transmission channels, or insider threats. Inadequate
1308
       storage, for instance, can result in unauthorized access or accidental exposure of sensitive embed-
       dings. Similarly, insecure transmission of embeddings over unprotected networks can make them
1309
       vulnerable to interception by malicious actors. Insider threats, where authorized individuals exploit
1310
       their access for malicious purposes, further exacerbate the risks associated with embedding leakage.
1311   These vulnerabilities highlight the need for comprehensive security measures to protect the integrity
1312   and confidentiality of target embeddings.
1313
1314
       Defense Strategies. To address these risks, we propose several mitigation strategies. One key
       approach is to regularly renew the security keys used for embedding protection, ensuring that even if
1315
       a key is compromised, the window of vulnerability is minimized. Additionally, employing multiple
1316
       keys can help limit the impact of any single breach by compartmentalizing access. It is also crucial
1317   to audit and continuously monitor access to sensitive embeddings, enabling quick detection and
1318   response to potential security breaches. Encrypting both storage and transmission ensures that even
1319   if unauthorized access occurs, the data remains unreadable without the proper decryption keys.
1320   Finally, restricting employee access to sensitive information by implementing the principle of least
1321   privilege can prevent unnecessary exposure and limit the potential for insider threats.
