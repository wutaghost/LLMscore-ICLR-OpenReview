011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Private Aggregation of Teacher Ensembles (PATE) framework is a versatile
014            approach to privacy-preserving machine learning. In PATE, responses made based
015
               on different parts of sensitive data are aggregated into a single response in a
               privacy-preserving way. Recently, multiple works applied PATE for tasks such
016
               as sequential text generation that are inherently diverse (or "hot"), with multiple
017
               valid responses. These designs, however, suffer from tension between diversity
018            and privacy – since diversity in the responses reduces agreement which forces the
019            aggregation to use smaller noise scales and thus incur higher privacy loss. But
020            limiting diversity of the aggregate response is undesirable since in modern large
021            language models, the very knowledge we want to transfer is encapsulated in the
022            response distribution. We propose hot PATE that is tailored for the diverse setting
023            where responses are distributions. We formally define preserving diversity and
024            design an efficient aggregation method that provably transfers the diversity to the
025            (randomized) aggregate response while incurring no privacy penalty. The method
026
               can be implemented using an API access to proprietary models and used as a plug-
               in replacement for the baseline “cold” PATE in existing tools. We demonstrate
027
               empirically the potential of hot PATE for an order of magnitude improvement in a
028
               task of in-context learning via prompts.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Generative AI models, such as large language models (LLMs), are incredibly powerful tools that can
034   be fine-tuned for specific contexts, even without explicit supervision (Radford et al., 2019; Brown
035   et al., 2020). Generative models diverge from conventional machine learning models in that they
036   support open ended, diverse tasks, where there are multiple appropriate responses, and this very
037   flexibility is essential for much of their functionality. Diversity is typically tuned via a temperature
038   parameter in the softmax, with higher temperature yielding higher entropy (more diverse responses).
039
      Furthermore, when evaluating the coverage or extracting knowledge from a trained model, such as
      for distillation tasks, the conventional approach involves querying the model on a prepared (sampled
040
      or curated) test set of examples. However, with generative AI models, the knowledge coverage on a
041
      specific domain is often encapsulated by the output distribution itself to a general instruction as part
042   of a prompt to the model, and can be evaluated or retrieved by sampling this distribution.
043
044   Frequently there is a need to train models or fine-tune publicly-available foundation models using
045
      sensitive data such as medical records, incident reports, or email messages. In this case, privacy
      must be preserved in the process. Specifically, we consider the strong mathematical guarantees of
046
      differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006). An approach that achieves privacy by modifying the
047
      training process is DPSGD (Abadi et al., 2016), where noise is added to clipped gradient updates.
048   DPSGD can also be applied with fine tuning (Yu et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023; Kurakin et al.,
049   2024). An alternative approach, that only relies on black box training and use of models that are not
050   privacy-preserving, is the Private Aggregation of Teacher Ensembles (PATE) paradigm (Papernot
051   et al., 2017; Bassily et al., 2018; Papernot et al., 2018). PATE follows the “sample and aggregate”
052   method of Nissim et al. (2007). We describe the basic workflow which we refer to here as cold PATE
053   on how the ensemble is used to label a set of new examples X while protecting the privacy of the
      training data:

068   Differential privacy requires that the output distribution is stable to a change of a single data record.
069   In the PATE framework, the votes histogram of each example is stable to a change of one record
070
      in D: At most one teacher, the one trained on this record, is affected and thus may change its vote.
      Therefore, at most two frequency counts cj may change in the histogram, and each by at most 1.
071
      A noisy selection of a label from the histogram, that hides this small difference in the counts, is
072
      therefore privacy preserving.
073
074   The labels may be the end goal or the set of privacy-preserving labeled examples {(x, y)} can be
075   used to train a student model. The limitations of cold PATE are that it was originally designed for
076
      classification-like tasks, where each example x has a single ground-truth label y ∈ V . Moreover, there
      is a need for a source of unlabeled non-private training examples to facilitate the knowledge transfer
077
      to the student. This is unsatisfactory because generative AI models support tasks with responses that
078
      are diverse and open ended. Moreover, knowledge is encapsulated in the diversity of the response
079   distribution and there is a promise of transferring knowledge to the student in a more fluid way. We
080   thus ask the following question:
081
082            Can we design a version of PATE that is effective for diverse and open-ended tasks
083            and unleashes more of the capabilities of generative models?
084
085   Application for in-context learning One motivation for our study is the effectiveness of in-context
086   learning via prompts. A prompt is an engineered prefix with a task that is given to the base model.
087
      Prompts can include specific instructions and/or a set of shots (scenario exemplars). Prompts are
      appealing for multiple reasons: A small number of shots (Liu et al., 2021) often outperform tailored
088
      trained models (Zhou et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2023). Prompting is efficient, as it is simply inference –
089
      there is no need for parameter updates. Finally, prompts only requires API access to the model, which
090   is important given the trend towards proprietary models.
091
092   When our data is sensitive, we would like the end product to be privacy-preserving. Concretely,
093
      consider the task of generating a representative set of synthetic privacy-preserving data records from
      a set of sensitive data records. The sensitive records may include components that are identifying and
094
      components that are shared with many other records. A privacy-preserving aggregation ensures that
095
      the synthetic records do not include identifying information. Additionally, it is essential to preserve
096   diversity in order to ensure coverage, that is, that our set of synthetic records is indeed representative
097   of the sensitive records. The synthetic records that are generated can then be used to train a student
098   model that is not necessarily generative, fine-tune a generative model (OpenAI, 2023), or construct a
099   privacy-preserving student prompt for downstream tasks. The latter allows for harnessing the ability
100   of generative models to generalize from few shots.
101
      We seek a PATE mechanism that supports the following. Each teacher is assigned a disjoint subset
102   of sensitive data records. These data records are used to construct a prompt that also includes an
103   instruction of the form “generate a representative data record given this example set of data records.”
104   Each teacher then has its own distribution on responses. By repeating multiple times we can obtain
105   different samples that are a representative set of shots. We then hope to aggregate responses of
106   different teachers in a way that preserves both diversity and privacy. This design is appealing as there
107   is little cost to scaling up the number of teachers: Each teacher is simply a prompted base model and
      there is no need for training or significant storage. Prompts are inexpensive, the current OpenAI API


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      supports 105 context/output tokens for US$5-$10 (OpenAI, 2023a). The bottleneck to scaling up
109   the number of teachers is thus the amount of available sensitive data. Scaling up is highly beneficial
110   because generally with DP aggregation, the number of queries we can support for a given privacy
111   budget is quadratic in the number of teachers.
112
113   Diversity-privacy tradeoff: An issue that arises when applying cold PATE with high diversity is
114   that utility rapidly deteriorates with diversity. To see this, assume there are r good responses with
115   equal probabilities. Note that higher r means more diversity. The n teachers votes would then be split
116   with ≈ n/r teacher votes per option. This lower agreement means that in order to return any of the
117   answers we must use privacy noise of scale σ < n/r. This inverse dependence of noise with r means
118   the privacy loss must increase with r. We can attempt to remedy this via some tie-breaking (e.g., each
119
      teacher selects a response in the top-k with the largest index). This does result in high agreement but
      we lose the diversity in the output that is needed to facilitate a fluid knowledge transfer. All prior
120
      and concurrent works we are aware of for privacy-preserving sequential text generation or in-context
121
      learning via prompts (Tian et al., 2022; Duan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) either ignored this issue or
122   addressed it by reducing or limiting diversity (see discussion in Section A). We ask the following:
123
124            Is the diversity-privacy tradeoff indeed inherent?

126   OVERVIEW OF C ONTRIBUTIONS AND ROADMAP
127
128   We propose hot PATE, described in Section 2. The method is suitable for auto-regressive models and
129   diverse and open ended tasks, where the appropriate response is a sample from a distribution. With
130   hot PATE, each teacher i ∈ [n] at each step computes a “next token” distribution p(i) over tokens
131
      V . These distributions are aggregated so that the response token from the ensemble is sampled from
      that aggregate distribution. The heart of our design is an aggregation method that preserves privacy
132
      and critically also the diversity of the teachers distributions. Our primary technical contributions are
133
      mathematically formalizing this requirement and proposing aggregation methods where there is no
134   penalty with increased diversity. Hot Pate can be added in a black-box manner to existing designs for
135   in-context learning via prompts to improve the utility privacy tradeoff.

145   In Section 3 we motivate and formalize a definition of robustly preserving diversity, which allows
146   for knowledge transfer that is compatible with limitations imposed by privacy. A natural diversity-
147   preserving approach is for each teacher i ∈ [n] to contribute a token yi sampled independently from
148   p(i) . We refer to this as independent ensemble. The resulting vote histogram is what is produced by
149   cold PATE (Papernot et al., 2017; 2018; Duan et al., 2023) when applied in a diverse setting. The
150   histogram can then be DP aggregated to produce a response token. The privacy loss depends on
151   the frequency (count) of the response token. With independent samples, this count is concentrated
152   around the average probability of the token across teachers. This probability is smaller when there
153
      is high diversity. Therefore, independent ensembles as an intermediate step inherently result in
      privacy guarantees that sharply deteriorate with the diversity of teacher distributions. We argue
154
      that this higher privacy noise may or may not be necessary, and this depends on properties of the
155
      teacher distributions that are lost by independent ensembles. The frequency histograms produced
156   by independent ensembles are concentrated around the average of the teachers’ distributions. The
157   issue, as depicted in Figure 1, is that averaging loses a critical distinction between high teachers’
158   support with low probability q (which we can hope to transfer in a privacy-preserving manner) and
159   low support with high q (which can not be transferred in a privacy-preserving manner). Our definition
160   of robust diversity transfer makes this important refinement: A token is required to be transferred to
161   the aggregate only when there is sufficient teachers’ support. Informally, for a robustness parameter
      τ ∈ [n], there are two requirements:


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • (transfer requirement) Any token that has probability at least q > 0 (no matter how small)
163            across c teachers where c ≥ τ , is “transferred” in that it has probability Ω(qc/n) in the
164            aggregate distribution.
165
             • (relevance requirement) We do not transfer irrelevant tokens, that is, for any token j, its
166            probability in the aggregate distribution is not much higher than its average probability in
167            the teacher distributions.
168
169   As argued, independent ensembles lose the robustness signal. In Section 4 we propose the method of
170   ensemble coordination. A coordinated ensemble samples a shared randomness and based on that, each
171   teacher i contributes a token yi . The marginal distribution of each yi is p(i) , same as with independent
172   ensemble. But the difference is that teachers votes are maximally positively correlated. The frequency
173
      cj of token j has high spread and in particular can (roughly) be Ω(τ ) with probability Ω(q). This
      property facilitates DP aggregation with no penalty for diversity. With coordinated ensembles, two
174
      teachers with very diverse distributions that have a small total variation distance produce the same
175
      token with probability that depends on the distance. In particular, when the distributions are equal
176   (the distance is 0), the same token would be produced.
177
178   In Section 5 we empirically demonstrate the properties and benefits of ensemble coordination for
179
      a simple task of in-context learning via prompts on the Llama 3 language model (lla, 2024). We
      evaluate the coverage and diversity of aggregate distributions formed by only transferring frequency
180
      counts that exceed a threshold T . We observe an order of magnitude improvement over the baseline
181
      of independent ensembles in terms of the value of T needed to achieve a certain coverage and in
182   terms of diversity of the aggregate. Recall that larger T means that we can use more noise (noise
183   scale is proportional to T ) and thus incur lower privacy loss.
184
185
      DP aggregation methods for histograms that apply with independent ensembles, such as Papernot et al.
      (2017; 2018), can be applied in an off-the-shelf manner with histograms generated by coordinated
186
      ensembles. The primary gain of hot PATE is in the utility privacy tradeoff. In Section D we present
187
      DP aggregation schemes that are applied to frequency histograms generated by coordinated ensembles
188   and return a token. We establish that the end-to-end process preserves diversity in the sense that it
189   satisfies our formal requirements (Section 3). We distinguish between two application scenarios of
190   applications with homogeneous or heterogeneous ensembles (see Figure 2). Homogeneous ensembles
191   are formed by randomly partitioning a sufficient number of data records among teachers. The
192   assumption then is the same as with cold PATE – most teachers possess the core knowledge we
193   wish to transfer (see Figure 3 (A)). In this case it suffices to require diversity preservation with
194   large support τ = Ω(n) and the aggregate we need is simply a (noisy) maximizer of the histogram.
195   Heterogeneous ensembles may arise when each teacher is an agent of one or few users. In this case,
196
      we want to preserve diversity both within and across teachers and for the latter it is necessary to allow
      smaller groups of teachers to support each transfer, that is, set a smaller τ (see Figure 3 (B)). In this
197
      case, a diversity-preserving aggregate is a weighted sample from the histogram.
198
199   In Sections E and F we further explore privacy analysis methods that are data dependent and can
200   increase the number of queries processed for a given privacy budget by orders of magnitude. In
201   particular, for token-by-token sequential text generation there are many steps and the cost of naive
202
      DP composition is prohibitive. What makes the approach feasible is that many of the steps have
      high agreement (similar teacher distributions where coordinated ensembles generate high agreement
203
      histograms). With data dependent analysis, steps with high agreement (or no agreement) can be
204
      essentially free. Moreover, with heterogeneous ensembles we can charge teachers individually
205   (instead of the whole ensemble) and only for steps in which the teacher contributed to the final
206   token (Hassidim et al., 2020; Cohen and Lyu, 2023).

272   Each teacher i ∈ [n] samples independently yi ∼ p(i) . The frequency histogram (cj )j∈V is computed
273   as in (1).1 The DP aggregation mechanism adds noise to each cj to obtain a privacy-preserving
274   sanitized histogram (c̃j )j∈V . We then select a token. A baseline meta-method, NoisyArgMax
275   select the maximizer arg maxj c̃j (Duan et al., 2023). The privacy cost of this aggregation inversely
276   depends on the noise scale σ,2 which for utility, must satisfy σ ≪ maxj cj .
277
      When the distributions are more diverse, maxj cj is smaller so for utility we must use a smaller σ.
278   Moreover, arg maxj c̃j is not diversity preserving: If the most frequent token is j and we have a
279   token h with frequency ch = cj /2, we still want to select h with probability that is 1/2 of that of
280   token j, that is, select a weighted sample from the histogram. To do this in a privacy-preserving way
281   we must use an even smaller noise scale that depends on the smallest counts that we aim to transfer.

395   4.2    P RIVACY PROPERTIES
396
      With both independent and coordinated ensembles, we aggregate the histogram in a privacy-preserving
397
      way to obtain one token. The distribution of the histograms produced by these ensemble types is very
398
      different. But the privacy properties in terms of the divergence between neighboring datasets are
399   identical and immediate:
400
      Observation 1. For every fixture of the shared randomness ρ, changing one of the distributions p(i)
401
      given as input to Algorithm 1 changes at most one item of the resulting histogram. That is, letting H
402
      and H ′ denote the resulting histograms before and after the modification, we have that H, H ′ are at
403   Hamming distance 2 (viewed as vectors in N|V | ).
404
405   The following corollary is immediate from Observation 1.
406   Corollary 1. Let A be an algorithm whose input is a histogram H ∈ N|V | , such that for any two
407   neighboring histograms H, H ′ (differing by at most one item) it holds that A(H) ≈(ε,δ) A(H ′ ).
408   Then the composed algorithm A (CoordinatedSamples(·)) is (ε, δ)-differentially private.3
409
410   Therefore, we can apply off-the-shelf the same DP aggregation schemes we would use with indepen-
411   dent ensembles (see Section 2.1) to coordinated ensembles. The benefit of coordinated ensembles,
412   per Theorem 1, is a much more favorable utility privacy trade-off: It suffices to set the privacy noise
413   scale to ∝ τ regardless of diversity, whereas with independent ensembles we must scale the noise
414
      down with diversity to obtain utility. This benefit broadly holds with any DP histogram sanitizing
      method4 that selects from tokens with count τ /2 whereas tokens with low or zero counts are filtered
415
      out, including the NoisyArgMax methods used with cold PATE (Papernot et al., 2017; 2018). As
416
      mentioned in Section 2.1, the selection of a token from a sanitized histogram can be NoisyArgMax
417   for homogeneous ensembles or a weighted sample for heterogeneous ensembles (Remark 2) – see
418   details in Section D. Simulation results with a particular (ε, δ)-DP analysis method are reported in
419   Section E.
420
421   4.3    I MPLEMENTATION IN LANGUAGE MODELS
422
423   Coordination can be implemented preferably with, but also without, an enhanced API access to a
424   proprietary base model: (i) Model-side: the shared randomness ρ is provided as input along with each
425   query prompt and the response token is sampled using ρ. (ii) Application-side: API returns the full
426   distribution and sampling done in the application (iii) No API enhancements: we can approximate
427
      the distribution by repeated sampling with the same prompt. This impacts computation since the
      number of samples required increases with diversity but does not impact privacy. Our demonstration
428
      in Section 5 with a public model (AI@Meta, 2024) uses the full distribution.
429
         3
430        This corollary holds for all variants of differential privacy, and is written here with (ε, δ)-DP for concreteness.
         4
431        The noise scale is ∝ τ but DP methods require an additional factor of log(|V |) (due to a union bound over
      the support V ) or log(1/δ) (with approximate DP). This applies also with independent ensembles.

532   Conclusion We proposed hot PATE that enhances the PATE framework in diverse settings. Hot
533   PATE only requires API access to proprietary models and can boost performance as a plug-in
534   replacement to cold PATE. An important use case is in-context learning via prompts, such as
535
      generating privacy-preserving synthetic data records from sensitive records. We formally define a
      robust diversity-preserving aggregate of distributions and design an aggregation method that satisfies
536
      it with no privacy penalty for higher diversity. Beyond private learning, our design, with lower values
537
      of the tuneable robustness parameter, is suitable for applications such as data distillation that require
538   robustness to few outliers or a lightweight protection against memorization but not necessarily strong
539   privacy guarantees.

709   trade-off. Our technique of ensemble coordination is an independent contribution that can enhance or
710   replace components in some of these designs.

712
      Tian et al. (2022) proposed a PATE extension for sequential text generation tasks in diverse settings.
      Their approach limited diversity: Average the teachers distributions and then truncate the tail by
713
      keeping only the top-k frequencies. The work of Tang et al. (2024) (independent concurrent) took a
714
      similar approach. The distribution of each teacher is reduced to a uniform distribution over its top-k
715   token probabilities. An independent ensemble is then applied to this set of reduced distributions. This
716   approach limits diversity to k and suffers from loss of diversity while still incurring a utility trade-off
717   with k. In our work, we demonstrate that averaging teacher distributions (independent ensemble) is
718   inferior to coordinating the ensemble.
719
      Recent prior work explored adaptations of PATE for in-context learning via prompting.
720
721   Duan et al. (2023) proposed to use each part Di of the data to create a text prompt Ti . The ensemble
722   is then used to label curated queries. But while some design elements were tailored to LLMs, the
723   workflow and privacy analysis were identical to cold PATE (Papernot et al., 2018), and in particular,
724
      did not consider diverse responses.
725   Wu et al. (2023) (independent concurrent work) proposed approaches to private aggregation for
726   in-context learning with diversity. They proposed to reduce the perceived diversity in sequentially-
727   generated text outputs by different teachers by clustering together outputs that are semantically
728   equivalent and aggregating each cluster in a semantic space. This essentially reduces the dimension-
729   ality of the output space. The aim then is to extract and transfer this common semantics in a privacy
730
      preserving way: Map responses into a common low dimensional embedding space and privately
      aggregate embedding vectors or identify frequent keywords in diverse teachers’ responses. The
731
      limitations are that the approach only addresses same-semantics diversity and offers no solution for
732
      semantically-distinct diverse responses and are subjected to a privacy diversity trade-off. Additionally
733   and importantly, they require hand crafted tools to map and curate responses back and forth from
734   a semantic space. The added value of such a mapping approach, if combined with coordinated
735   ensembles, depends on whether the reduction of diversity that is achieved is within or across teachers.
736   The across variety (see Figure 3 (B)), where the knowledge of each teacher only contains one or
737   limited variations of the same semantic, is not eliminated by ensemble coordination and thus there
738   is added value by addressing it via other means. The within variety (see Figure 3 (A)) is handled
739   effectively by ensemble coordination and can be transferred fluidly with no privacy loss and without
740   the need for mitigation of diversity via additional engineering. We suspect that for the in-context
741
      learning use case, and for semantic similarity that can be captured by tools external to the model
      (such as an embedding), the diversity eliminated is anyhow encapsulated in the base model and thus
742
      present in most teacher distributions. That is, we expect the diversity to overwhelmingly be the
743
      “within” variety.
744
745   Lin et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024) (independent concurrent work) proposed an approach called private
746   evolution for generating synthetic examples from private examples. The design used heterogeneous
747
      teachers, where each is a single private example. Initially, the base model is sampled to generate a
      collection of candidate (full) responses. The teachers then vote on candidates by nearest neighbor to
748
      their sensitive example in an embedding space. The next iteration then consist of a weighted sample
749
      from a privacy-preserving vote histogram. The resulting candidates are then used to generate a new set
750   of candidates by the base model that are closer to the private distribution. This is repeated for multiple
751   iterations. The inherent drawbacks of this approach, compared with sequential text generation,
752   are that it is not suitable for transferring specific patterns (such as extension numbers for specific
753   departments within an org) that are common in the private data but do not exist in the pre-training
754   data and are not memorized by the model and can not be generalized by it. Additionally, it requires
755   a number of candidates that is exponential in the intrinsic dimensionality of the candidate space.
      Therefore the realm of applications is different than Hot Pate and they are not directly comparable.

      Finally, there are multiple innovative adaptations of PATE to non-categorical settings (aggregate
762

763   different problems and use different techniques than hot PATE. For example, image generation using

       E    P RIVACY ANALYSIS CONSIDERATIONS
1081
1082
       The effectiveness of Hot PATE depends on the number of queries with yield (token returned) that
1083   can be returned for a given privacy budget. In this section we explore the benefits of data-dependent
1084   privacy analysis when the aggregation follows Algorithm 2 (homogeneous ensembles). We use
1085   synthetically generated teacher distributions with varying size common component (that can be
1086   arbitrarily diverse) and distinct (private) components.
1087
       Broadly speaking, with data-dependent analysis, we incur privacy loss on “borderline” queries where
1088
       the output of the DP aggregation has two or more likely outputs. Queries that return a particular
1089   token with high probability or return ⊥ with high probability incur little privacy loss.
1090
1091   We demonstrate that with Algorithm 2, we can expect that only a small fraction of frequency
1092
       histograms generated by coordinated ensembles are “borderline.” (i) For queries with high yield
       (high probability of returning a token over the sampling of the shared randomness), the generated
1093
       histograms tend to have a dominant token (and thus lower privacy loss). This because coordinated
1094
       ensembles tend to “break ties” between tokens. (ii) For queries with low yield (high probability of
1095   ⊥ response and low probability of returning a token), the total privacy loss only depends on yield
1096   responses. This means that high ⊥ probability does not cause performance to deteriorate.
1097
1098
       This is important because both these regimes are likely in sequential text generation and with
       coordinated ensembles. We expect many of the tokens to follow the base model distribution and
1099
       therefore have high agreement and not incur privacy loss. Or alternatively, instructions that require
1100
       private data have no agreement and return ⊥. The dependent privacy analysis means that generally
1101   we can process many more queries for the privacy budget than if we had just used a DP composition
1102   bound.

1186
1187   We propose two DP methods to implement Algorithm 3 (Section D.2) with different trade offs. In
       both cases we can apply data-dependent privacy analysis so that queries that do not yield a token


                                                         22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       (that is, return ⊥) are essentially “free” in terms of the privacy loss. The parameter L depends on the
1189   privacy parameters (and logarithmically on |V |).
1190
1191   Importantly, with the second method we can apply privacy analysis with individual charging, where
1192
       instead of charging the whole ensemble as a unit we only charge teachers that contributed to a
       response. With heterogeneous ensembles we expect the diversity to arise both from individual
1193
       distributions and from differences between teachers and therefore with individual charging allows for
1194
       much more efficient privacy analysis when different groups of teachers support each prediction.
1195
1196
       Private Weighted Sampling This method gains from sparsity (histogram support being much
1197   smaller than |V |) but the calculation of privacy loss is for the whole ensemble. We can do the analysis
1198   in the TCT framework (Cohen and Lyu, 2023) so that privacy loss only depends on yield queries
1199   (those that return a token). We perform weighted sampling by frequency of each token to obtain
1200   the sampled histogram c′ and then sanitize the frequencies of sampled tokens using the end-to-end
1201   sparsity-preserving method of Cohen et al. (2021) to obtain c∗ . The sanitizing prunes out some
1202   tokens from c′ with probability that depends on the frequency cj , privacy parameters, and sampling
1203   rate. All tokens in c′ with frequency above 2L, where L only depends on the privacy parameters,
1204   remain in c∗ .7 The final step is to return a token from c∗ selected uniformly at random or to return ⊥
1205
       if c∗ is empty.
1206
1207
       Individual Privacy Charging This method does not exploit sparsity, but benefits from individual
       privacy charging (Kaplan et al., 2021; Cohen and Lyu, 2023). It is appropriate when 2L ≪ n.
1208
       The queries are formulated as counting queries over the set of teachers. The algorithm maintain
1209
       a per-teacher count of the number of counting queries it “impacted.” A teacher is removed from
1210   the ensemble when this limit is reached. Our queries are formed such that at most O(2L) teachers
1211   (instead of the whole ensemble) can get “charged” for each query that yields a token.
1212
1213
       To express Algorithm 3 via counting queries we do as follows: We sample a sampling rate ν ∼
       U [1/n, 1] of teachers and sample a token v ∈ V uniformly. We sample the teachers so that each one
1214
       is included with probability ν and count the number c′v of sampled teachers with yi = v. We then do
1215
       a BetweenThresholds test on c′j (using (Cohen and Lyu, 2023) which improves over Bun et al.
1216
       (2017)) to check if c′v ≥ 2L. For “above” or “between” outcomes we report v. If it is a “between”
1217   outcome we increment the loss counter of all sampled teachers with yi = v (about 2L of them). We
1218   note that this process can be implemented efficiently and does not require explicitly performing this
1219   “blind” search.
1220
       Teachers that reach their charge limit get removed from the ensemble. The uniform sampling of
1221
       the sampling rate and token emulates weighted sampling, where the probability that a token gets
1222
       selected is proportional to its frequency. The sub-sampling of teachers ensures that we only charge
1223   the sampled teachers. Teachers are charged only when the query is at the “between” regime so (with
1224   high probability) at most ≈ 2L teachers are charged. Because we don’t benefit from sparsity, there
1225   is overhead factor of log(|V |(n/L)) in the privacy parameter (to bound the error of this number
1226   of queries) but we gain a factor of n/L by not charging the full ensemble for each query in the
1227   heterogeneous case where most teachers have different “solutions” to contribute.
