                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            This paper proposes the Runtime Learning Machine for safety-critical au-
010            tonomous systems. The learning machine has three interactive components: a
011            high-performance (HP)-Student, a high-assurance (HA)-Teacher, and a Coordi-
012            nator. The HP-Student is a high-performance but not fully verified Phy-DRL
013            (physics-regulated deep reinforcement learning) agent that performs safe runtime
014            learning in real plants, using real-time sensor data from real-time physical environ-
015            ments. On the other hand, HA-Teacher is a verified but simplified design, focusing
016            on safety-critical functions. As a complementary, HA-TeacherвҖҷs novelty lies in
017
               real-time patch for two missions: i) correcting unsafe learning of HP-Student,
               and ii) backing up safety. The Coordinator manages the interaction between HP-
018
               Student and HA-Teacher. Powered by the three interactive components, the runtime
019
               learning machine notably features i) assuring lifetime safety (i.e., safety guarantee
020            in any runtime learning stage), ii) tolerating unknown unknowns, iii) addressing
021            Sim2Real gap, and iv) automatic hierarchy learning (i.e., safety-first learning, and
022            then high-performance learning). Experiments involving a cart-pole system, two
023            quadruped robots, and a 2D quadrotor, as well as comparisons with state-of-the-
024            art safe DRL, fault-tolerant DRL, and approaches for addressing Sim2Real gap,
025            demonstrate the machineвҖҷs effectiveness and unique features.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been incorporated into numerous autonomous systems and
030   has shown significant advancements in making sequential and complex decisions in various fields,
031   such as autonomous driving Kendall et al. (2019); Kiran et al. (2021), chemical processes Savage
032   et al. (2021); He et al. (2021), and robot locomotion Ibarz et al. (2021); Levine et al. (2016). These
033   DRL-enabled systems have the potential to revolutionize many processes across different industries,
034   leading to tangible economic impacts Tolentino (2019). However, the public-facing AI Incident
035   database in AID has revealed that machine learning (ML) techniques, including DRL, can achieve
036   remarkable performance without ensuring safety Zachary & Helen (2021). For instance, a report by
037   the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration highlighted 351 car crashes related to advanced
038
      driver assistance systems from July 2023 to March 2024 in the US alone NHTSA. Therefore, ensuring
      high-performance DRL with verifiable safety is even more crucial today, aligning well with the
039
      marketвҖҷs demand for safe ML techniques.
040

      1.1   S AFETY C HALLENGES AND O PEN P ROBLEMS
042
043   Our considered safety challenges are rooted in the unknown unknowns and the Sim2Real gap.
044
045
      Challenge 1: Unknown Unknowns. The unknown unknowns generally refer to outcomes, events,
      circumstances, or consequences that are not known in advance and cannot be predicted in time and
046
      distributions Bartz-Beielstein (2019). The dynamics of many safety-critical autonomous systems (e.g.,
047
      autonomous vehicles Rajamani (2011), airplanes Roskam (1995), and quadrupedal robots Bledt et al.
048   (2018)) are governed by a combination of known knowns (e.g., NewtonвҖҷs laws of motion), known
049   unknowns (e.g., Gaussian noise without knowing to mean and variance), and unknown unknowns.
050   The unknown unknowns are due to, for example, unforeseen operating environments and DNNвҖҷs
051   colossal parameter space, intractable activation, and hard-to-verify. The safety assurance also requires
052   resilience to unknown unknowns, which is very challenging. The reasons stem from characteristics
053   of unknown unknowns: there is almost zero historical data, unpredictable timing and distributions,
      resulting in the unavailability of models for scientific discoveries and understanding.

054
      Challenge 2: Sim2Real Gap. The prevalent DRL involves training a policy within a simulator using
055   synthetic data and deploying it to physical platforms. However, the difference between the simulated
056   environment and the real world creates a gap known as the Sim2Real gap. This gap causes a drop in
057   performance when using pre-trained DRL in real physical environments. Numerous approaches have
058   been developed to address the Sim2Real gap Peng et al. (2018); Nagabandi et al. (2019); Tan et al.
059   (2018); Yu et al. (2017); Cao et al. (2022); Imai et al. (2022); Du et al. (2021); Vuong et al. (2019);
060   Yang et al. (2022a). These methods aim to improve the realism of the simulator and can mitigate the
061   Sim2Real and domain gaps to varying degrees. Nevertheless, undisclosed gaps and missing dynamics
062   continue to hinder the safety assurance of real plants.
063   To address Challenges 1 and 2, the most appealing solution is provided in Prospect 1.1 below.
064   Prospect 1.1. Runtime learning for a high-performance action policy in real plants вҖ“ using real-time
065   sensor data generated from real-time physical environments while prioritizing safety.
066
067   However, two open problems arise about bringing Prospect 1.1 into reality.
068   Problem 1.2. If the DRL agentвҖҷs actions lead to a safety violation, how can we correct his unsafe
069   learning and back up the safety of real plants in a timely manner?
070   Problem 1.3. How to tolerate and also teach the DRL agent to tolerate unknown unknowns and
071   Sim2Real gap for assuring safety of real plants?
072
073   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Significant efforts have been devoted to DRL safety by developing safe DRL and fault-tolerant DRL.
076   Safe DRL. One research focus of safe DRL is the safety-embedded reward, as a DRL agent must
077   learn a high-performance action policy with verifiable safety. The control Lyapunov function (CLF)
078   proposed in Perkins & Barto (2002); Berkenkamp et al. (2017); Chang & Gao (2021); Zhao et al.
079   (2023) is a candidate. Meanwhile, seminal work in Westenbroek et al. (2022) revealed that a CLF-like
080   reward could enable DRL with verifiable stability. At the same time, enabling verifiable safety
081   is achievable by extending CLF-like rewards with given safety regulations. However, systematic
082   guidance for constructing such CLF-like rewards remains open. The residual action policy is another
083   shift in safe DRL, which integrates data-driven action policy and physics-model-based action policy.
084
      The existing residual diagrams focus on stability guarantee Rana et al.; Li et al.; Cheng et al. (2019b);
      Johannink et al. (2019), with the exception being Cheng et al. (2019a) on safety guarantee. However,
085
      the physics models considered are nonlinear and intractable, which thwarts delivering a verifiable
086
      safety guarantee or assurance, if not impossible. The recently developed Phy-DRL (physics-regulated
087   DRL) framework Cao et al. (2024; 2023) can satisfactorily address the open problems of safe DRL.
088   Summarily, Phy-DRL permits simplifying the model of nonlinear dynamics to an analyzable and
089   tractable linear one. This linear model can then be a model-based guide for constructing the safety-
090   embedded (CLF-like) reward and residual action policy. Meanwhile, the Phy-DRL exhibits verifiable
091   safety. However, it is only mathematically or theoretically possible due to the underlying assumptions
092   of manageable Sim2Real gap and unknown unknowns. In other words, Phy-DRL cannot offer
093   verifiable safety for real plants in the face of unknown unknowns and the Sim2Real gap.
094   Fault-tolerant DRL. Fault-tolerant DRL is another direction for DRL safety in real plants. Recent
095   approaches include neural Simplex Phan et al. (2020), runtime assurance Brat & Pai (2023); Sifakis
096   & Harel (2023); Chen et al. (2022), and model predictive shielding Bastani (2021); Banerjee et al.
097   (2024). They treat the DRL agent as a high-performance module (HPM) but a black box that runs
098   in parallel with a verified high-assurance module (HAM). Normally, HPM controls the real plants.
099   HAM takes over once safety violation occurs. These architectures can ensure the safe running of
100   DRL in real plants under the assumption that Challenges 1 and 2 do not cause HAM to fail, which is
101
      not practical for systems whose operating environments are dynamic and unpredictable. Furthermore,
      they are not solutions to Problem 1.2 and Problem 1.3. Specifically, in all these architectures, HAM
102
      and HPM are independent, that is, HPM cannot learn from HAM, and HAM cannot teach HPM how
103
      to be safe. Meanwhile, HAM is the static model-based controller, and its action will be unreliable if
104   the real-time unknown unknowns and Sim2Real gap create a significant model mismatch.

182   3     D ESIGN OVERVIEW
183
184   Our proposed runtime learning machine aims to address Problem 1.2 and Problem 1.3 to deliver
185   Prospect 1.1. To do so, showing in Figure 1, it is designed to have three interactive components:
186         вҖў HP-Student builds on Phy-DRL agent, which can be pre-trained in a simulator or another
187            domain but performs runtime learning in a real plant to tolerate unknown unknowns and
188            address the Sim2real gap.
189         вҖў HA-Teacher is a verified safety-only design whose novelty lies in real-time patches with
190            two missions: timely correcting unsafe learning and backing up safety.
191         вҖў Coordinator is responsible for monitoring the real-time safety status and facilitating inter-
192            actions between HP-Student and HA-Teacher. Specifically, when the real-time safety status
193            of the plant being controlled by HP-Student approaches the safety boundary, Coordinator
194            prompts HA-Teacher to intervene and assure the safety of real plant, and correct unsafe
195
               learning of HP-Student. When the real-time states return to a safe region, Coordinator
               triggers the switch back to HP-Student and terminates the learning correction.
196
197   Next, we will describe the designs of the three interactive components in Sections 4 to 6, respectively.

      Remark 6.1 (Why called patch?). In todayвҖҷs world, there are two approaches to achieving the
318   same control task: a high-dimensional data-driven DRL and a low-dimensional physics-model-based
319   controller. The data-driven DRL provides superior performance but is challenging to verify (due
320   to DNNвҖҷs huge parameter, nonlinear activation, etc.). On the other hand, the physics-model-based
321   approach offers analyzable and verifiable behavior but has limited performance (due to model
322   mismatch). This explains why the set in Equation (11) follows a very similar safety envelope formula
323   in Equation (3), but it is referred to as a patch: the envelope represents a DRL design, while the patch
      represents a physics-model-based design with a small verifiable-safety region.

521   8    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
522
523   This paper presents a runtime learning machine designed for safety-critical autonomous systems.
524   The learning machine consists of the interactive HP-Student, HA-Teacher, and Coordinator. The
525   machineвҖҷs goal is to facilitate runtime learning for a high-performance action policy with verified
526   safety in real plants, using real-time sensor data from real-time physical environments. The learning
527
      machine ensures lifetime safety by accommodating unknown unknowns and addressing the Sim2Real
      gap. The runtime learning machine also serves as an automatic hierarchy learning mechanism for
528
      HP-Student. Hierarchically, HP-Student first learns from the HA-Teacher to prioritize safety. After
529
      mastering safety-first learning, HP-Student autonomously self-learns to develop a high-performance
530   action policy with a safety guarantee. Our runtime learning machine has shown outstanding features
531   compared to state-of-the-art safe DRL and fault-tolerant DRL, with approaches to addressing the
532   Sim2Real gap. These were demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on a cart-pole system,
533   two quadruped robots, and one 2D quadrotor.
534
      Incorporating an early warning function into our runtime learning machine for the CoordinatorвҖҷs
535
      management of interaction between HP-Student and HA-Teacher constitutes our future research.
536   Reachability through worst-case dynamics Anderson et al. (2020) could be a solution. Another
537   future research is to enhance the robustness of our runtime learning machine in handling unknown
538   unknowns and bridging the Sim2Real gap by utilizing concepts from differentiable simulation Song
539   et al. (2024) and robust adaptive control Hovakimyan & Cao (2010).

       In the real quadruped robot experiment, we utilized a Python-based framework designed for the

1681   H.1    P OLICY L EARNING
1682
1683   The runtime learning machine and Phy-DRL are designed to achieve the safe mission described
1684   in Section 7.2. The policy observation consists of a 10-dimensional tracking error vector between
1685   the robotвҖҷs state vector and the mission vector. Both systems are based on the DDPG algorithm
1686   Lillicrap et al. (2016). The actor and critic networks are implemented as Multi-Layer Perceptrons
1687   (MLPs) with four fully connected layers. The output dimensions of the critic network are 256, 128,
1688
       64, and 1, while the output dimensions of the actor network are 256, 128, 64, and 6. The input for
       the critic-network consists of the tracking error vector and the action vector, while the input for the
1689
       actor network is the tracking error vector. The activation functions for the first three neural layers are
1690
       ReLU, and the output of the last layer is the Tanh function for the actor network and Linear for the
1691   critic network. Additionally, the discount factor Оі is set to 0.9, and the learning rates for the critic
1692   and actor networks are both 0.0003. Finally, the batch size is set to 512.

       We now present the experimental results to demonstrate the automatic hierarchy learning (i.e., safety-

2065
       learning machine. We note the runtime learning can be understood as safe continual learning (if
2066   having after pre-training of HP-Student) for a high-performance action policy in real plants вҖ“ using

2068   reduce the workload of runtime learning or test the operational mechanisms, it is not mandatory for
2069   the runtime learning process. If the pre-training is removed, the our runtime learning will be learning
2070   from scratch in real plants. All the previous experiment of cart-pole system, A1 and Go2 robots

2072   pre-training. The episode length is 1500 steps.

2074   in Figure 21. In these plots, red dots represent the states of the system controlled by HA-Teacher,
2075   while blue dots represent the system controlled by HP-Student. Initially, the HA-Teacher was
2076   frequently activated because the HP-Student had not yet learned how to ensure safety. However, as
2077   the HP-Student spent more time learning, his ability to maintain safety improved. This progress

       J   I MPLEMENTATION P ROBLEMS AND S OLUTIONS
2161
2162
       This section addresses the problems of implementing the runtime learning machine in the cart-pole
2163   system and the real quadruped robot, along with our solutions.
2164
2165   CVXPY T OOLBOX
2166   The runtime learning machine depends on obtaining feasible solutions from Linear Matrix Inequalities
2167   (LMIs) to allow the HA-Teacher to perform safe actions. We found that the default CVX solver, SCS,
2168   exhibited instability and inconsistent accuracy across different computing platforms. Therefore, in
2169   our experiment, we chose to use the CVXOPT solver Andersen et al. (2013) to achieve more stable
2170   and accurate results.
2171
       While LMIs can generally be solved in real-time, the operating frequency on our platform still varies
2172   between 10 and 50 Hz. Although this fluctuation does not adversely affect overall performance in
2173   simulations, it does pose challenges when implementing the framework on a physical platform. To
2174   ensure efficient operation on hardware that requires a high frequency, such as a quadruped robot,
2175   we designed the HA-Teacher with an additional process that runs in parallel with the central control
2176   process. This design, however, adds complexity, such as the need for multi-process synchronization,
2177   and increases the demand for computational resources.
2178
2179   H ARDWARE R EAL - TIME E FFICIENCY
2180
       The runtime learning machine was tested by sending remote control commands to the quadruped
2181
       robot indoors. All computations were executed on a desktop equipped with a 12th Gen IntelВ®
2182   Coreв„ў i9-12900K 16-core processor. The onboard computing platform of the Unitree A1 utilizes an
2183   IntelВ® Atomв„ў x5-Z8350 CPU, which operates at 1.44 GHz with 6 cores. To enhance development
2184   efficiency, Python was chosen as the programming language for implementing this framework.
2185
       Given that the runtime learning process requires significant interaction between the CPU and GPU,
2186
       and considering the current architecture includes a high-frequency Model Predictive Control (MPC)
2187
       module operating at 500 Hz for the quadruped robot, achieving comparable optimal real-time
2188   performance with the existing onboard hardware may be challenging. However, we believe that this
2189   computational limitation can be addressed or significantly alleviated by either adding extra computing
2190   resourcesвҖ”such as an external mini-PC, as suggested in Yang et al. (2022b)вҖ”or by restructuring the
2191   code framework. This restructuring could involve encapsulating the current implementation in C++
2192   to enhance real-time performance, as proposed in Chen & Nguyen (2024).
