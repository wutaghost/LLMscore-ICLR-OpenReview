011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Electromyography (EMG)-based hand gesture recognition is essential for applica-
014            tions in prosthetics, rehabilitation, and human-robot interaction. Despite advances
015            in machine learning, domain shift caused by intersubject variability often leads to
016
               degraded model performance when applying trained models to new users. In this
               study, we revisit the statistical reference normalization methods to mitigate the
017
               domain shift in EMG data in a leave-one-subject-out train-test split setting. We
018
               systematically benchmark five popular amplitude-based normalization techniques
019            to assess their effectiveness in subject-specific classification with varied datasets
020            and percentages for normalization. Experimental results show that Min-Max and
021            Peak normalization outperform others, yielding higher classification accuracy on
022            EMG data. We further visualize the domain shifts in the feature space throughout
023            the training process and provide an analysis based on EMG signal characteristics.
024            Our findings indicate that proper normalization significantly reduces intersubject
025            variability of EMG samples, enhancing model adaptation and providing insights
026            for bridging domain shifts in future EMG-based gesture recognition research. The
027
               benchmark code for domain adaptation approaches on EMG signals is available
               at ref-emgbench.github.io.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Electromyography (EMG) is a crucial data source to assess muscle activity and predict movement
      intentions, playing a significant role in physical rehabilitation and the control of prosthetic devices.
034
      However, EMG signals often exhibit substantial variability across different subjects and recording
035
      sessions. This variability comes from multiple factors, including electrode configuration and place-
036   ment (Mesin et al. (2009)), perspiration (Abdoli-Eramaki et al. (2012); Winkel & Jørgensen (1991)),
037   temperature fluctuations (Winkel & Jørgensen (1991)), physiological differences (Dellon & Mack-
038   innon (1987); Nourbakhsh & Kukulka (2004), muscle fiber composition (Halaki & Ginn (2012)),
039   blood flow (Halaki & Ginn (2012)), and the amount of tissue between the electrode surface and the
040   muscle (Halaki & Ginn (2012)).
041
      To mitigate variability and extract meaningful and consistent patterns from raw EMG signals, var-
042   ious amplitude normalization methods have been explored since the late 1950s, primarily from a
043   signal processing perspective (Halaki & Ginn (2012)). Common normalization techniques include
044   scaling signals relative to maximal voluntary contractions (Edelstein (1986); Yang & Winter (1983))
045   or peak values (Allison et al. (1993); Yang & Winter (1984)). These methods aim to standardize sig-
046   nal amplitudes across different subjects and sessions, thereby reducing intersubject and intrasession
047   differences (Halaki & Ginn (2012); Lin et al. (2020)).
048   However, the challenge of domain shift across subjects in sEMG data remains unresolved, neces-
049   sitating further investigation into effective preprocessing strategies. Amplitude normalization can
050   play a crucial role in enhancing the generalizability of machine learning models by standardizing
051   signal amplitudes and reducing variability (Kerber et al. (2017); Khushaba (2014); Lin et al. (2020)).
052   However, no previous work has benchmarked the use of different normalization methods in EMG,
053   although normalization methods have shown great promise in dealing with distribution shifts (Du
      et al. (2017); Ioffe (2015); Li et al. (2018); Côté-Allard et al. (2019)).

054
      In this work, we systematically investigate several EMG data normalization techniques as prepro-
055   cessing steps and evaluate their impact on the performance of deep learning classifiers in hand
056   gesture recognition tasks based on EMG data. By examining how different normalization methods
057   affect a model’s ability to generalize across subjects, we aim to identify the most effective strategies
058   for mitigating domain shift. In this paper, we make the following contributions:
059
060         1. We present a comprehensive benchmarking of five statistical normalization methods, eval-
061            uating their effectiveness in mitigating intersubject variability for hand gesture recognition
062            based on EMG data.
063         2. We provide detailed visualizations and analyses that offer insight into the extent to which
064            different normalization methods reduce the distribution shift.
065         3. We demonstrate that inter-subject reference normalization consistently outperforms intra-
066            subject reference normalization, underscoring the potential of leveraging inter-subject vari-
067            ability as a key contribution to improving EMG-based classification performance.

      2     R EFERENCE N ORMALIZATION OF EMG DATA
071
072
      2.1   EMG S IGNALS
073   EMG signals are captured as multi-channel time-series data, with the number of channels varying
074   significantly. Some systems utilize only a few manually placed electrodes on specific arm mus-
075   cles (Ozdemir et al. (2022a)), while others can involve up to 128 channels (Geng et al. (2016)). The
076   sampling frequency also varies, typically ranging from a few hundred Hz (Atzori et al. (2014)) to
077   several thousand Hz (Ozdemir et al. (2022a); Yang et al. (2023)). EMG signals, recorded in the
078   microvolt range, are amplified and bandpass filtered to mitigate noise from external sources, such
079   as mechanical interference. These low-voltage, high-frequency signals result from ion movements
080   during neuromuscular excitation (Purves et al. (2001)).
081
082   2.2   D OMAIN S HIFT IN T IME - SERIES DATA
083
084
      In time-series data, domain shift refers to the challenge that arises when the statistical properties of
      the data change between the training and deployment phases, leading to a discrepancy between the
085
      training and testing distributions. This shift can significantly degrade model performance, especially
086
      in real-world applications where the environment or conditions evolve over time. The domain shift
087   in EMG data usually manifests itself in the form of concept shift.
088
089   The concept shift (Fan et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2022)) in EMG data involves changes in the
090
      underlying relationship between the EMG features and the output labels over time. This shift can
      occur due to physiological changes, such as muscle fatigue, or differences in motor control strategies
091
      between participants. For instance, a model trained on EMG data from one participant may perform
092
      poorly when applied to another participant, or even to the same participant at a later time, due
093   to changes in how muscle signals correspond to the intended gestures. This makes it essential to
094   address concept shift through strategies such as continuous model fine-tuning or the development of
095   algorithms that can adapt to evolving feature-label relationships, ensuring reliable performance over
096   time and across varying conditions.
097
098   2.3   R EFERENCE N ORMALIZATION
099
100   To address the challenges posed by the domain shift in EMG data, a variety of normalization tech-
101   niques are employed to standardize the data for more reliable analysis. These methods are often
102   used to reduce noise, smooth signals, or scale the data for consistency across trials or subjects. In
103   addition, a specific normalization process called reference normalization adjusts the EMG signal
104
      relative to a single individual, which has been shown to have practical improvements in reducing
      concept shifts across subjects in Lin et al. (2020).
105
106   As illustrated in Fig. 3.2, the process of reference normalization begins by computing normalization
107   parameters based on a target dataset, which in our work corresponds to the fine-tuning dataset or
      data from one of the subjects in the training dataset. Once these parameters are obtained, they are


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      applied across the entire dataset, transporting the original distribution to the distribution of the target
109   subject. Because EMG signals are recorded as multi-channel time-series data, reference normaliza-
110   tion of EMG data is performed on a per-channel basis, using statistical parameters such as the mean,
111   variance, or extremas from channel i for gesture j from subject k, to standardize data from the same
112   channel and gesture across a different subject k ′ . This approach contrasts with traditional meth-
113   ods that calculate normalization parameters across the full dataset without accounting for individual
114   variability.
115   In this work, we apply transfer learning in conjunction with reference normalization using statis-
116   tical amplitude features. This approach enables deep models to adapt to the specific distribution
117   of the target subject while preserving the generalizability of the pretrained model. The statistical
118   normalization methods used for benchmarking are introduced in the following section.
119
120   2.4     A MPLITUDE NORMALIZATION METHOD
121

123   data, each offering unique approaches to managing amplitude variability. In this section, we discuss
124   the most widely used methods: Z-score, Min-Max, Root Mean Square (RMS), Mean Absolute
125   Value (MAV), and Peak normalization. These methods are evaluated in our study to benchmark
126
      their performance in gesture recognition tasks using a deep learning model, helping to identify the
      most effective approach for standardizing EMG data.

185   3.1     DATASET
186
187
      We evaluated the proposed normalization techniques using three publicly available sEMG datasets:
      CapgMyo (DBb) and the NinaPro DB3 and DB5 databases. These datasets were chosen for their
188
      diversity in subjects, hand gestures, and recording conditions, providing a strong basis for assessing
189
      the effectiveness of normalization methods in reducing intersubject variability. Since EMG signals
190   have applications for both individuals with amputations and able-bodied users, we selected datasets
191   that reflect both user groups. Additionally, we included a variety of electrode configurations, rang-
192   ing from high-density gelled flexible circuit boards to individually placed bipolar electrodes and
193   wearable low-density electrode solutions.
194
      CapgMyo (DBb): Introduced in Geng et al. (2016) and expanded upon in Du et al. (2017), this
195
      dataset consists of sEMG recordings from 10 subjects performing 8 distinct gestures, captured using
196   128 high-density electrodes. The recordings were segmented into 250 ms windows, resulting in
197   25,600 samples. The acquisition system includes 8 modules, each equipped with 16 electrodes.
198
199
      NinaPro DB3 and DB5: The NinaPro datasets Atzori et al. (2014); Pizzolato et al. (2017) are widely
      used in sEMG research, particularly for prosthetics and human-computer interaction. DB3 contains
200
      recordings from 11 subjects with transradial amputations, using 12 bipolar Delsys Trigno electrodes.
201
      DB5 includes data from 10 able-bodied subjects, recorded with two Myo Armbands (each with 8
202   bipolar electrodes around the forearm). After windowing, DB3 contains 64,426 samples, while DB5
203   has 39,597 samples.

215   to generate new subsets and subsequently converted to heatmaps as the inputs to the model. The
      selected deep learning model, ResNet18, is initially trained on the pre-training dataset, followed

      3.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
242
243   To quantitatively evaluate the effectiveness of normalization methods, we use the following metrics:
244   accuracy, Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC), Maximum Mean Dis-
245   crepancy (MMD) and Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence.
246
      Test accuracy refers to the proportion of correctly classified instances among all test instances, pro-
247
      viding a straightforward measure of the model’s predictive performance. While accuracy provides
248
      a straightforward measure of the model’s ability to correctly classify instances, it does not account
249   for potential imbalances in the data or the distribution shifts between training and test domains.
250
251   AUROC assesses the model’s ability to distinguish between classes across all classification thresh-
252
      olds. This metric provides insight into the trade-off between true positive and false positive rates.
      In our multiclass setting, AUROC is computed using the one-vs-rest (OvR) approach, where we
253
      calculate the AUROC for each class treated against all other classes, and average the results.
254
255   MMD is a statistical measure used to quantify the difference between the probability distributions of
256   the source (training) and target (testing) domains. A lower MMD value indicates a smaller domain
257   shift, suggesting that the normalization method effectively aligns the feature distributions.
258   KL divergence measures how one probability distribution diverges from a reference distribution. It is
259   used to quantify the discrepancy between the feature distributions of the source and target domains,
260   with lower values indicating better alignment.
261
262   3.4   V ISUALIZATION OF D OMAIN S HIFT
263
264   To gain qualitative insights into how normalization affects the distribution of sEMG features across
265   subjects, we employed the t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) and the Wasser-
266
      stein distance matrix for visualization of the domain shift.
267   t-SNE is a dimensionality reduction method that projects high-dimensional data into a two-
268   dimensional space while preserving the local structure of the data. By visualizing the feature repre-
269   sentations using t-SNE, we can observe the clustering patterns of different subjects before and after
      normalization, providing visual evidence of a reduced domain shift.

      3.5.3   I NTER - AND INTRA - SUBJECT NORMALIZATION
361
362
363   To assess the impact of reference subject selection on classification performance, we compared
364   test accuracy and AUROC between two settings: intra-subject normalization, where normalization
365   parameters are computed using data from the left-out subject, and inter-subject normalization, where
366   parameters are calculated using data from a randomly selected subject in the pre-training set. The
367   results are presented in Table 3.
368   Min-Max and Peak normalization demonstrate superior performance across all settings, achieving
369   near-perfect AUROC scores(100.00) in several cases. These methods consistently outperform Z-
370   score, RMS, and MAV normalization, especially in higher fine-tuning percentages, indicating their
371   robustness in handling both intersubject and intrasubject normalization scenarios.
372   Notably, inter-subject normalization (RN subj. ̸= FT subj.) generally produces better results than
373   intra-subject normalization (RN subj. = FT subj.) across all normalization methods and data splits.
374   A possible explanation for this is that using data from a different subject to compute the normal-
375   ization parameters introduces additional variability into the data distribution, enhancing the model’s
376   ability to generalize. In contrast, restricting normalization to the left-out subject might limit this
377   variability, resulting in slightly reduced generalization performance. This finding highlights the
      potential benefit of leveraging inter-subject variability for improved classifier generalization.

392   4.1       EMG SIGNALS AND D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT
393
394   EMG signals, although non-invasive and recorded from the skin, are subject to various forms of
395   non-stationarity, which introduces significant challenges in generalization across datasets. These
396   non-stationarities arise from biological variability and sensor placement, resulting in what is known
397   as distribution shift (Campbell et al. (2024)). Distribution shifts refer to changes in the statisti-
398
      cal properties of EMG signals between training and testing phases, complicating machine learning
      models’ ability to generalize. Specifically, a difficult type of distribution shift for machine learn-
399
      ing algorithms to deal with is concept shift, where the probability of an output y is different given
400
      the same x. Common causes include variations in muscle location (Dellon & Mackinnon (1987);
401   Nourbakhsh & Kukulka (2004)), electrode placement (Mesin et al. (2009)), and skin properties like
402   impedance (Rask-Andersen et al. (2019)). Reference normalization techniques aim to reduce con-
403   cept shift by adjusting data distributions to be more consistent between domains, often improving
404   model robustness.
405
406   4.2       H AND G ESTURE R ECOGNITION WITH EMG DATA
407
408   Extensive research has been dedicated to training machine learning models for EMG-based ges-
409   ture recognition, leveraging both publicly available datasets (Lu et al. (2022); Islam et al. (2024);
410   Wei et al. (2021); Hye et al. (2023)) and novel datasets collected by researchers (Côté-Allard et al.
411
      (2019); Yang et al. (2023); Ozdemir et al. (2022b); Li et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023); Zhang et al.
      (2023); Xu et al. (2023); Algüner & Ergezer (2023); Sussillo et al. (2024)). Classification work
412
      for the benchmarking of feature extraction methods and dimension reduction methods have been
413
      performed for sEMG signals, including mean absolute value, root mean square, Wilson amplitude,
414   zero-crossing rate, wavelength, power spectrum analysis, short-time Fourier transform, and wavelet
415   decompositions (Phinyomark et al. (2012); Ozdemir et al. (2020)).
416
417
      While many studies report results based on randomized train-test split accuracy (Hye et al. (2023);
      Algüner & Ergezer (2023); Sri-Iesaranusorn et al. (2021)) and k-fold cross-validation (Zhang et al.
418
      (2022); Ozdemir et al. (2022b); Fatimah et al. (2021); He & Jiang (2020); Kim et al. (2019)), where
419
      data from the training, validation, and test sets are randomly sampled from the same subjects, such
420   methods may not adequately reflect real-world scenarios. In practice, it is often preferable for the
421   validation and test sets to consist of data collected either after the training data from the same subject
422   (referred to as train-test splits for time series, or TSTS), or from entirely different subjects excluded
423   from the training set, as evaluated by leave-one-subject-out cross-validation (LOSO-CV). These
424   data splitting strategies offer more robust assessments of model performance by introducing out-of-
425   distribution generalization challenges.
426   In the case of TSTS, testing with data collected after the training set introduces potential distribution
427   shifts caused by factors such as variations in gesture execution, fatigue (Liu et al. (2021); Chua et al.
428   (2024)), perspiration (Abdoli-Eramaki et al. (2012)), electrode displacement (de Talhouet & Web-
429   ster (1996)), drying or changes in ionic concentrations of hydrogel or electrolyte gels (Sousa et al.
430   (2023)), and changes in electrode adherence to the skin (Chi et al. (2010)). Similarly, LOSO-CV in-
431   troduces variability arising from inter-individual differences in body size, muscle morphology (Del-
      lon & Mackinnon (1987)), and variations in skin impedance and adipose tissue distribution (Rask-


                                                                 8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Andersen et al. (2019)). Studies employing randomized or mixed data splits, where evaluation data
433   may precede training data, risk reporting inflated accuracies that do not reflect real-world general-
434   ization capabilities in practical EMG classifier deployments. In our work, we benchmark using only
435   TSTS and LOSO-CV, which are highly useful metrics for real-world applications.
436
437
      4.3   R EFERENCE N ORMALIZATION OF EMG DATA
438
439   To address distribution shifts, several normalization techniques have been proposed to reduce inter-
440   subject variability. Kerber et al. (2017) introduced a peak-based normalization method, which
441   performed well for simple gestures but struggled as the number of gestures increased. Similarly,
442   Khushaba (2014) developed a canonical correlation analysis (CCA)-based framework, achieving
443   82.96% accuracy for 12 finger movements by extracting style-independent features.
444   Building on these efforts, Lin et al. (2020) proposed a min-max normalization approach for inter-
445   subject EMG-based hand gesture recognition. This method recalibrates training data using the min-
446   imum and maximum amplitudes from a new user’s signals, effectively reducing domain shift. With
447   a convolutional neural network (ConvNet) and leave-one-subject-out cross-validation (LOSO-CV),
448   the approach achieved 85.09%, 88.97%, and 94.53% accuracy across datasets, outperforming stan-
449   dard normalization techniques and rivaling state-of-the-art transfer learning methods like progres-
450   sive neural networks and adaptive batch normalization (Côté-Allard et al. (2019)). Unlike transfer
451   learning, which requires more data from the target domain, this normalization method generalizes
452
      with minimal data, making it more suitable for many real-world applications.
453   Normalization techniques like the one proposed by Lin et al. (2020). provide an effective solution
454   to inter-subject variability, addressing one of the primary challenges in EMG data classification. By
455   reducing the domain shift between users, these methods enable robust generalization across diverse
456   populations without sacrificing real-time performance, further advancing the applicability of EMG-
457   based gesture recognition in practical scenarios. However, the lack of benchmarking on multiple
458
      datasets and evaluations on the decreases in distribution shift reduces the potential impact of the
      work.
459
460
461   5     C ONCLUSION
462
463   In this study, we systematically benchmark five amplitude-based normalization methods to address
464   the domain shift challenge in EMG-based hand gesture recognition. Our findings highlight the
465   significant role of normalization in improving model generalization across subjects, with Min-Max
466   and Peak normalization methods demonstrating superior performance in adapting under intersubject
467   variability and enhancing classification accuracy.
468
      Through visualizations and analyses of feature space evolution during training, we showed how
469   effective normalization mitigates domain shifts, facilitating better adaptation of machine learning
470   models to new users. Experiment results show that inter-subject normalization consistently out-
471   performed intra-subject normalization, emphasizing the value of leveraging inter-subject variability.
472   These insights contribute to advancing the development of robust, generalizable models for EMG-
473   based applications in prosthetics, rehabilitation, and human-robot interaction in the future.
