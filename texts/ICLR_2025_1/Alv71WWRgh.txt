011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Current instruction-guided image editing methods generally believes that incor-
014            porating powerful Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) can significantly
015
               enhance the understanding of complex instructions, thereby improving editing
               outcomes and generalization. However, even using an powerful MLLM model
016
               such as GPT4V, disappointing results are observed when instructions involve sim-
017
               ple spatial information such as “change the color of clothes of the leftmost person
018            to red”. Our theoretical analysis suggests that both the training strategy and the
019            model aggregation manner in the current paradigm may contribute to unsatisfac-
020            tory spatial image editing capabilities. Consequently, we propose the SpatialEdit
021            framework, featuring a two-stage training approach and a novel data engine where
022            questions and instructions are enriched with spatial information. Further theoreti-
023            cal analysis of our method reveals its ability to increase proficiency in both spatial
024            editing and general image editing tasks. We create a benchmark to evaluate spa-
025            tial editing ability. We conduct zero-shot image editing experiments on various
026
               datasets and our method achieves SOTA results on several key metrics.

030
031   Image editing plays a pivotal role in various multimedia applications, enabling users to customize
032
      images to meet their specific preferences and needs. From basic color adjustments Gatys et al.
      (2016); Zhang et al. (2016) to intricate semantic alterations Zhu et al. (2016), image editing tech-
033
      niques are widely utilized in various fields. Instruction-guided image editing has emerged as a popu-
034
      lar research area Li et al. (2020a); El-Nouby et al. (2019a); Fu et al. (2020a), offering a practical and
035   intuitive approach where direct human commands dictate specific aspects of image manipulation.
036
037   The recent substantial work in the image editing community demonstrates confidence in the ability
038
      of MLLM to handle image editing tasks with complex instruction Zhang et al. (2023a); Koh et al.
      (2023); Liu et al. (2023b); Zhu et al. (2023). Recent mainstream efforts Fu et al. (2023); Ge et al.
039
      (2024) are also often focused on integrating MLLM into image editing tasks.
040
041   However, we find that the performance of existing MLLM-driven image editing models is not as
042   good as imagined, especially in following spatial instructions, i.e., instructions with spatial infor-
043   mation. For example, remove apples on the kid’s left hand side. As illustrated
044
      in Figure 1, even advanced models such as DALLE3 powered by GPT4V 202 (2023) encounter
      difficulties in following spatial instructions.
045
046   In real-world scenarios, instructions often include spatial information, especially when dealing with
047   complex scenes containing multiple subjects and intricate details Yang et al. (2024b). For instance,
048   when editing a family photograph, instructions involving spatial information are often more direct
049   and precise, such as referencing “the second individual from the left in the first row,” rather than
050
      relying solely on descriptive attributes of the subject itself. Thus, We need to rethink the reasons
      why MLLM-driven methods performs poorly in spatial editing tasks.
051
052   To solve the problem, we conduct theoretical analysis of the current MLLM-driven image edit-
053   ing paradigms, and identify two possible factors contributing to the unsatisfactory performance of
      MLLM-driven methods. Firstly, contrary to intuition, incorporating MLLM to enhance the perfor-

121
122   Instruction-guided image editing has attracted considerable attention, providing a specifically intu-
123   itive way to edit an image through textual prompts provided by the user. Earlier methods using GAN
124   frameworks Reed et al. (2016) encountered challenges in achieving broad applicability and realism
125   Li et al. (2020b); El-Nouby et al. (2019b); Fu et al. (2020b). Diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Sa-
126
      haria et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022) emerged as promising alternatives, offering more flexible
      image transformations by controlling cross-modal attention maps based on global captions Kawar
127
      et al. (2023); Hertz et al. (2023); Meng et al. (2022). While these studies are typically constrained to
128
      global editing capabilities Bar-Tal et al. (2022a); Crowson et al. (2022), local image editing meth-
129   ods have also been explored, enabling precise modifications while preserving image integrity Nichol
130   et al. (2022); Avrahami et al. (2022); Bar-Tal et al. (2022b); Couairon et al. (2023).
131
132
      MLLM-driven image editing methods incorporate MLLMs to address the limitation of traditional
      methods. However, the frozen CLIP text encoders, while proficient in processing static descriptions,
133
      may lack the capacity to facilitate crucial transformations in editing tasks, potentially leading to
134
      or imprecision in instruction comprehension. Recent approaches have leveraged MLLMs Fu et al.
135   (2023); Ge et al. (2024) to address this challenge and have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in
136   natural language understanding and generation tasks. Current MLLM-driven methodology still face
137   a critical limitation in following spatial instructions. We aims to solve the problem by introducing
138   SpatialEdit.

174   4     T HEORETICAL ANALYSIS
175
176   As shown in Figure 1, even utilizing a powerful MLLM such as GPT4V 202 (2023) and a diffusion
177   model such as Dalle3 as core components, current MLLM-driven methods demonstrate a notable
178   deficiency in following spatial instructions, leading to unsatisfactory editing outcomes. This obser-
179   vation underscores the need for thorough theoretical analysis.
180   Firstly, in §4.1, we reveals that employing MLLM into an image editing model might not be a free
181   lunch because the component-stacking mechanism (e.g., the language modeling head is stacked on
182   the MLLM, and the diffusion model is stacked on the MLLM,) exacerbates the risk of over-fitting,
183   might diminishing the overall model’s performance in image editing tasks. This contrasts with the
184   widely held view that introducing MLLM would enhance the performance of any task requiring
185   complex language comprehension capabilities.
186   In §4.2, we uncover that the current training strategy which only tunes prompt embeddings and
187   image embeddings hinder the MLLM from learning spatial relationship, might limiting the MLLMs’
188   capacity to guide the diffusion model towards desired editing outcomes.

      5.1     DATA E NGINE
300
301   Our theoretical analysis §4.1 reveals that the key to enabling MLLM-driven models to exhibit supe-
302   rior performance lies in the additional introduction of high-quality training data. However, from the
303   statistical data in our Table 4, we found that the data generated by the existing data pipeline lacks
304   spatial information, suggesting the need for a new data engine to construct image editing data that
305   incorporates spatial information.
306
      Moreover, since the MLLM is the core component of the entire pipeline responsible for understand-
307   ing spatial relationships and controlling image editing, it is reasonable to construct data to train the
308   MLLM before the end-to-end training on spatial image editing data. This allows the model to gain
309   stronger spatial understanding, which can help our model learn better in spatial image editing tasks.
310
      Therefore, our data engine should not only construct end-to-end image editing data but also generate
311
      VQA data for MLLM training. We first introduce raw spatial information extraction in §5.1.1, and
312
      then the construction of the VQA data in §5.1.2 and the construction of spatial image editing data in
313   §5.1.3.

      5.1.1    S PATIAL I NFORMATION E XTRACTION
317
      To enrich the questions (used to construct VQA data) and instructions (used to construct spatial
318   image editing data) with spatial information, we first extract raw spatial information from the images.
319   The whole pipeline of our data engine is shown in Figure 2. Initially, we conduct semantic-level
320   parsing, where images from the source dataset Brooks et al. (2023a) are fed into the LLava-1.6 Liu
321   et al. (2023a) which is responsible for recognizing and describing the content within the images and
322   predicting future motion trends, forming a triplet [object, description, trend]. For example, the black
323   dog in the original image of Figure 2 can be represented as Object:“dog”, Description:“with black
      fur, sitting”, Trend: “sticking its head out”.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Then, we conduct a 3D-level parsing based on the results obtained above. The triplet is sent to
325   GroundSAM Ren et al. (2024), which combines groundingDino Liu et al. (2023d) and Segment
326   Anything Kirillov et al. (2023) for generating image segmentation masks with label of its category.
327   Then the Depth anything Yang et al. (2024a) model is used for depth estimation and point cloud
328   construction. The point clouds are segmented using the RANSAC Fischler and Bolles (1981) algo-
329   rithm, forming a quintuple [Object, Description, Trend, Point cloud, Mask] where the point clouds
330   are represented by a set of 3D coordinates, and the mask is represented as a 2D array. The quintuple
331   is further used to calculate more spatial information including position, contour size, volume, and
332   relative distance, ultimately forming a list [Object, Description, Trend, Position, Contour, Volume,
333
      Distance]. After conducting above extraction steps, we extract the raw spatial information of an
      image.

      5.1.2    VQA DATA C ONSTRUCTION
337   The VQA data generated by our data engine is used to support the first stage training of enhancing
338   the spatial understanding capability of the MLLM. The construction of the VQA data includes two
339   operations: filling in the template with raw spatial information and refining questions with LLava-
340   1.6 Liu et al. (2023c). The template designed for VQA data effectively transforms raw spatial
341   information extracted in §5.1.1 into question-answer pairs. An detailed example is provided in
342   Appendix B.1.
343   There are various types of templates which describes depth, relative and absolute positions, contour
344   size, actual volume, and motion trends, which comprehensively cover the scope of spatial informa-
345   tion. More details about templates are shown in Appendix K. The tentative QA pairs are then fed
346   into Llava Liu et al. (2023c) again for diversified expression.
347
348   5.1.3    S PATIAL I MAGE E DITING DATA C ONSTRUCTION
349
350
      Spatial image editing data is designed to train models with better editing capabilities in following
      spatial instructions and to generate more explicit language conditions. Each sample in the data
351
      represented as a tuple [instruction, language condition, ground-truth image].
352
353   Firstly, we generate the instructions. The generation of instruction includes two parts, filling in the
354   template and refining with llava-1.6. The idea of filling template is mostly the same as we illustrated
355   in §5.1.2, and a detailed example is shown in appendix B.2 Some example templates are available
356
      in the Appendix K.
357   Subsequently, we construct language conditions. Note that the language condition are different
358   from the language condition embeddings Etxt in §3.1. Language condition is textual. However,
359   a sequence of language condition embeddings can be decoded into text using a tokenizer. Hence
360   language conditions serve as gold labels for the MLLM to generate better language condition em-
361   beddings. We use the prompt we designed for Llava-1.6 Liu et al. (2023a) to ask Llava-1.6 to
362
      generate detailed description of the expected editing result corresponding to the instruction and cur-
      rent image. For example, a proper language condition can be “the dog with black fur, which on the
363
      left of golden fur dog, is replaced with a brown tree trunk”.
364
365   Finally, we generate the ground-truth images in different manners for five types of instructions,
366   including removing a object, changing the size of an object, visualizing the moving object at next
367   moment, replacing one object with another, and change the texture or color of an object. For the
368
      removing instruction aimed at removing a specific object, we employ the object’s mask as a guide
      for the stable diffusion Podell et al. (2023) to the generate of image where the targeted object has
369
      been successfully removed. Due to space limitation, the details of rest instructions can be found in
370
      Appendix M.

411   6     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
412
413
      During inference, there exits some instructions which do not contain spatial information. We term
414   this situation as the general editing task. If the instructions contain spatial information, we term
415   this situation as the spatial editing task. In this section, we conduct a theoretical analysis of the
416   performance of our method under general and spatial editing tasks.
417
      The data generated by our data engine will provide language guidance with spatial information. If
418
      the model is trained on these data, language condition embeddings and image condition embeddings
419
      generated by the MLLM contain spatial information. In this case, we state that the model is trained

530
      Our theoretical analysis uncovers the potential causes behind the underperformance of the current
531
      MLLM-driven image editing paradigm in following spatial instructions. This insight serves as a
532   catalyst for the development of SpatialEdit, a novel framework designed to address the above limi-
533   tation. Through the integration of the data engine and a novel two stage training method, SpatialEdit
534   significantly enhances the spatial capabilities of MLLMs. Theoretical analysis and experimental val-
535   idation demonstrate that SpatialEdit not only achieves SOTA results in the zero-shot general editing
536   task but also excels in spatial editing tasks.

1104
       We design a prompt to ask GPT-4V to give a score to the edited image based on its judgment.
1105
       The prompt is shown in the code we submitted. Secondly, we utilize GPT-3.5 as the judge. Since
1106   GPT3.5 is not capable of dealing with image input, so we first use the method in §5.1.1 to extract
1107   raw spatial information of the source image and the edited image, and then we obtain two lists of
1108   [object, description, trend, position, volume], then two lists are fed the into GPT-3.5, and then we
1109   design a prompt to ask GPT-3.5 to judge the results. Thirdly, we ask three human evaluators to
1110   evaluate the quality of the edited image. The final score of the edited image is the average of scores
1111   of three human evaluators. The range of score is [0,1], with higher scores indicating better quality.
1112   The final score is the averaged score of the sample being evaluated.
