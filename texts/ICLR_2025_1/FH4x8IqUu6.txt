009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Time becomes visible through changes in what we see, as daylight fades and shad-
012               ows grow. Inspired by this, in this paper we explore the potential to learn time-
013               aware representations from static images, trying to answer: what time tells us? To
014               this end, we first introduce a Time-Oriented Collection (TOC) dataset, which con-
015               tains 130,906 images with reliable timestamps. Leveraging this dataset, we pro-
016               pose a Time-Image Contrastive Learning (TICL) approach to jointly model times-
017               tamp and related visual representations through cross-modal contrastive learning.
018
                  We found that the proposed TICL, 1) not only achieve state-of-the-art performance
                  on the timestamp estimation task, over various benchmark metrics, 2) but also, in-
019
                  terestingly, though only seeing static images, the representations learned by TICL
020
                  show strong capability in several time-aware downstream tasks such as time-based
021               image retrieval, video scene classification, and time-aware image editing. Our
022               findings confirm that time-aware visual representations are learnable from static
023               images and beneficial for various vision tasks, laying a foundation for future re-
024               search on understanding time-related visual context. 1

      On our planet, the day-night cycle occurs every 24 hours, a phenomenon recorded systematically
034
      by various clock systems developed by human society. Surprisingly, such clock systems emerged
035   much earlier than our recognition of Earth as a “blue marble” engaged in constant orbital movement
036   (Dohrn-van Rossum, 1996). Although most people possess a vague, intuitive sense of current time
037   (Moore, 1992), the origin of this metaphysical consciousness of time, which is a key concept for
038   both our bodies and society, remains elusive. Research in neuroscience has revealed that visual
039   stimuli from photoreceptors are crucial for the adaptation of mammals to day-night rhythms (Duffy
040   & Czeisler, 2009). This implies that the concept of time for humankind could emerge from various
041   visual experiences. Given the implicit relations between clock time and visual experiences, we are
042   interested in asking:
043
                • Can neural networks learn a representation of time from visual stimuli i.e. static images?
044
045             • If so, what implications does such representation tell us towards understanding the world?
046

050   (AMOS) (Jacobs et al., 2009), featuring images captured by a few stationary webcams at differ-
051   ent times of the day. However, we found these datasets do not reflect the complexity and diversity
052   of views in real-world applications. Salem et al. (2020) proposed a mixed subset of AMOS and

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
111
      2.1   T IME OF DAY ESTIMATION
112
113
114   Estimating the time of day from static images presents a notable challenge, a topic that remains
115   largely underexplored in the literature. Earlier studies were hampered by the scarcity of datasets
116
      with images paired with accurate local timestamps. Many images, particularly those sourced from
      social networks, contain metadata that is often inaccurate, lost, or not calibrated to the local time-
117
      zone. To cope with this, some researchers have turned to webcam image datasets, which naturally
118
      include accurate timestamps. However, these datasets are typically limited to fixed singular views
119   and are frequently degraded by environmental noise and adverse conditions like low light or physical
120   obstructions. Such limitations hinder the generalisation of the time estimation model from webcam
121   data to more diverse applications.
122
      For example, established social media image datasets such as MIRFLICKR-1M (Huiskes & Lew,
123
      2008) and YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016) include only a small fraction of images with reliable
124
      metadata. On the other hand, webcam datasets contain only fixed stationary views, such as AMOS
125   (Jacobs et al., 2007) and TYD dataset (Volokitin et al., 2016), which fail to represent the complexities
126   of temporal variations within diverse environments. The CVT-Time dataset (Salem et al., 2020),
127   despite combining stationary webcam images with more diverse YFCC100M subsets, still struggles
128   with unreliable timestamps and low-quality webcam images.
129
      Existing time estimation methods have primarily focused on classifying images into time periods.
130
      Sharma et al. (2016) addressed this with a small social media image dataset using SVMs and early
131   CNNs, classifying images into four broad time classes, which oversimplified the task. Volokitin
132   et al. (2016) used VGG-16 to predict temperature, month, and hour from images taken by 6 web-
133   cams during daylight, which is insufficient for comprehensive day-long analysis. Zhang et al. (2022)
134   evaluated baseline models, such as He et al. (2015) and Dosovitskiy et al. (2021), on images from
135   a single webcam. Zhai et al. (2019) worked with a mixed dataset of Flickr and webcam images,
136   classifying images taken at the same hour but in different months into 288 classes, optionally incor-
137   porating geolocation inputs. Similarly, Salem et al. (2022) used webcam images, predicting month,
138   week, and hour as dependent tasks trained jointly while considering geolocations as optional inputs.
139
140
141
      2.2   T IME - AWARE REPRESENTATION LEARNING
142
143
144   Although there has been limited focus specifically on learning time-aware visual representations,
145   related studies have explored visual attributes based on human annotations. An early attempt in this
146   direction is the transient attribute database (Laffont et al., 2014), which aimed to derive attribute-
147
      based representations from static images. This database contains images from 101 stationary cam-
      eras, with each image annotated by human evaluators for 40 numerical transient attributes. Notably,
148
      some of these attributes, such as sunrise, night, and daylight, are inherently tied to the time of day.
149
      However, the dataset lacks precise timestamps for the images, and the attributes rely on subjective
150   human judgements, which limits the scalability and generalisation of the approach.
151
152   A subsequent work aimed to bridge the gap between image metadata and scene attributes (Salem
153
      et al., 2020). This study explored the dynamic mapping of visual appearances over time and across
      geolocations. Specifically, given a satellite image with a corresponding timestamp and geolocation,
154
      the model predicts scene classes and visual attributes, which were annotated using off-the-shelf
155
      models (Zhou et al., 2017; Laffont et al., 2014), for matching ground-level images. The method
156   relied on the conditional probability P (a|t, l, I (l)), where visual attributes a are predicted based
157   on timestamps t, geolocation l, and a satellite image I(l). This approach assumes that visual at-
158   tribute estimators model P (a|I) accurately for ground-level images I. Therefore, it requires both
159   geolocation l and the accurate estimation of visual attribute a to conversely model P (t|I, a, l). This
160   dependency limits its applicability to scenarios where the variables l and a are unavailable or inaccu-
161   rate. In contrast, our work focuses on learning purely time-aware visual representations that directly
      connect natural images with their corresponding timestamps without such dependency.

186   3     M ETHODOLOGY
187
188   3.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
189
190
      Before introducing the proposed method, we revisit the problem formulation for timestamp estima-
      tion from images. In general, we seek to train a model fθ (·), predicts timestamp t̂ given input images
191
192
      x. The estimation can be written as t̂ = fθ (x). While regression seems ideal due to the continuous
      nature of time, it faces significant challenges (details in appendix A.2). The cyclic nature of the
193
      clock introduces discontinuity to regression methods treating target values as scalars within a range
194
      which is a disconnected set (Zhou et al., 2019). In regression, this issue to cyclic data often leads
195   to estimations t̂ around the midpoint of the whole range (Adams & Vamplew, 1998). For example,
196   images with timestamps like 23:59 and 00:00, which are visually similar, appear at opposite ends
197   of the time scale. In such case, the regression model tends to reach a sub-optimal solution that is
198   around 12:00, which is far from accurate.
199
      Most prior studies have employed classification over discrete time periods (e.g. hours), in which t̂ has
200
      finite value options corresponding to classes. Classification mitigates the above issue in regression
201   by assigning predictions to one of the discrete time classes. Even for boundary cases like 23:59
202   and 00:00, the classification model tends to predict one of the adjacent classes (e.g. 23:00 or 00:00),
203   which is more reasonable. However, classification treats these classes as orthogonal one-hot vectors
204   (Rodrı́guez et al., 2018), overlooking the relationships between time periods.
205
      An effective time representation should capture the inherent relationships. With regard to this, we
206
      propose the Time-Image Contrastive Learning (TICL) method. TICL replaces one-hot encoding
207   with learnable time embeddings that align with visual features. This approach can effectively capture
208   inner relationships of time classes and enhances accuracy by leveraging contrastive learning between
209   time and image representations.
210

      input image xi is associated with a label ti ∈ RC indicating its time period. Empirically, we fix

233   Several key intuitions support this design. Previous work has shown that combining additional ge-
234   olocation and date information can improve the performance of time estimation. However, reliance
235   on additional attributes may propagate errors from prior to posterior attributes (Salem et al., 2020).
236   We observed that the CLIP image encoder is a powerful foundation model capable of capturing rich
237   semantic contextual features from raw images (Agarwal et al., 2021). These context priors tend to
238   incorporate effective cues (e.g. seasons, climate and regions) for timestamp estimation. Therefore,
239   we use the frozen CLIP image encoder to directly extract these useful features. A standard MLP-
240   based Image-Time Adaptor module is then applied to adapt the extracted CLIP features to the target
241
      time representations during training enabling their use for timestamp estimation tasks.
242   Another benefit of our design comes from the learnable time embedding in the contrastive learning
243   scheme. In the vanilla
                               classifier construction, the final output of the model ŷ is constrained within
244   the subspace of ||ŷ||1 = 1, ŷ ∈ RC , where each target label embedding is a fixed one-hot encod-
245   ing mutually orthogonal to the others. Each image representation is optimised solely towards its
246   own target, and thus the activation to other possible classes tends to be overwhelmed. This results
247
      in a significant bias towards majority classes when the dataset contains similar images with differ-
      ent classes, since the gradients towards the minority classes will be strongly offset by the majority
248
      classes with inputs that share similar features (He & Garcia, 2009). In contrast, our method pro-
249
      vides each target time class with a trainable representation that is optimised to be aware of their
250   corresponding sample prototypes, helping the model to align the representations of timestamps and
251   visual inputs more effectively for tail classes (Zhu et al., 2022).

      4   B ENCHMARK DATASET TOC
255
      In this paper, we introduce a new benchmark dataset – Time-Oriented Collection (TOC) – consisting
256
      of high-quality images sourced from social media, featuring calibrated and reliable image metadata.
257
      This dataset reflects real-world scenarios and human activities, improving the applicability of time
258   of day estimation in practical tasks. We collected 117,815 training samples and 13,091 test samples
259   from the CVT dataset, mitigating various limitations in previous datasets.
260
261
      Previous datasets, such as YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016) and Cross-View Time (CVT) (Salem
      et al., 2020), contain unnatural non-photographic images (e.g. memes, scribbles) and inaccurate
262
      timestamps due to unsynchronised clocks and other sources of inconsistency. Furthermore, station-
263
      ary webcam-based datasets lacked sufficient diversity to represent random natural views at different
264   times of the day. To mitigate these issues, we implemented a two-step filtering and calibration
265   process for the CVT dataset. Specifically, we applied DBSCAN clustering (Ester et al., 1996) to
266   PCA-reduced features extracted from a pretrained ResNet-18 model (He et al., 2015) to eliminate
267   unnatural images marked as outliers. A subsequent manual review was conducted to further remove
268   images with incorrect timestamps or poor quality. This updated dataset reflects natural variations in
269   human activity throughout the day with improved reliability in terms of time metadata (more details
      in appendix A.1.1).

525   6      C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we tried to answer the question of what time tells us, by looking at the time-aware rep-
528   resentations learned from static images, through the task of time-of-day estimation. A new reliable
529   benchmark dataset, TOC was introduced to support the task, consisting of images captured in natural
530   settings with verified timestamps. This dataset addresses the limitations of existing datasets by pro-
531
      viding a more diverse and realistic collection of images that better reflect daily visual experiences.
      Build upon that, a new learning paradigm (TICL) was proposed, which jointly models timestamp and
532
      image representations via cross-modal contrastive learning, surpassing previous works in time-of-
533
      day estimation. The learned time-aware representations were further studied by validating on several
534   downstream tasks. The strong performance in these downstream tasks highlighted its capability to
535   recognise the similarity of the captured time (in time-based image retrieval), TICL’s frame-coherent
536   priors for video scene understanding (significantly improved video scene classification), and realistic
537   and time-consistent performance in time-aware image editing (accurately reflecting typical lighting
538   conditions for different times of day). In summary, by answering the question at the beginning, our
539   study showed the potential of learning time-aware visual representations from static images, and its
      benefits to various time/temporal-related downstream tasks, suggesting the visual essence of time.

1047

         In the main paper, we adhere to the 24-class classification scheme used in previous methods, as
1081     stated in section 3.2. In this section, we explore the effects of different granularities of class par-
1082     titioning. Using discrete classes to represent timestamps results in precision loss. To measure the
1083     precision loss, we compute observational errors, which is the average difference between actual
1084     timestamps and the converted class timestamps. Figure 15 shows the mean absolute error (MAE)
1085     and the observational errors for different partitions of classes. As a part of MAE, observational error
1086     are inherent such that it persists even with perfect class predictions (Conforti et al., 2020). Specif-
1087     ically, a small number of classes induce larger MAE, which is reasonable since converting actual
1088     timestamps to coarser time-span classes introduces larger additional observational errors.
1089     However, this does not imply that extremely fine partitions should always be used to reduce ob-
1090     servational error. We find that finer class partitioning, such as 144 classes, does not improve the
1091     performance. In particular, figure 16 presents the performance of the TICL model on the TOC test
1092     set under different class partitioning. The overall distribution of predictions exhibits similar pat-
1093     terns despite different granularities. Figure 17 highlights both class accuracy and hour accuracy for
1094     the model. The visualisation shows that while class accuracy drops significantly as the number of
1095
         classes increases, the overall hour accuracy remains stable once the number of classes exceeds 24.
         This degradation in class accuracy with finer partitioning can be attributed to the smaller sample
1096
         volumes within each class, as illustrated in figure 18. The smaller the sample volume for each class,
1097
         the more underrepresented it tends to be (Sangalli et al., 2021). This suggests a potential drawback
1098     of finer class partitioning.

       In section 3.2, we covered the high-level design of the TICL model we devised to learn time-aware
       representations. This section provides additional details for the proposed TICL model.
1165
1166
       A.4.1    M ODEL DETAILS
1167
1168   Time Encoder: The Time Encoder consists of several fully-connected layers, with the detailed ar-
1169   chitecture shown in figure 19. The objective of the Time Encoder is to transform the raw timestamps
1170   into meaningful time-aware representations that are aligned with image representations. To achieve
1171   the goal, the raw timestamps are first preprocessed into 24 one-hot class embeddings. The Time
1172   Encoder then takes these input class embeddings and projects them to the desired representation
1173   space.
1174
1175   Image-Time Adaptor module: The Image-Time Adaptor module is employed to adapt the raw
1176   backbone features with Time Encoder outputs, as depicted in figure 19. Training the Image-Time
1177
       Adaptor module and Time Encoder jointly using a contrastive learning scheme allows for effective
       alignment between the two modalities.
1178
1179
       Hyper-Parameters: We use an Adam optimiser with an initial learning rate of 5 × 10−4 and a
1180
       weight decay of 1 × 10−6 . The training process spans 20 epochs, with the learning rate halved every
1181
       2 epochs and a batch size of 512. The temperature parameter is initialized to 0.07. All input images
1182   are resized to 224 × 224.

1501   features across frames within each video. The backbone VideoMAE (ViT-B) model takes the input
1502

1503

1504

1508

1509

       A.9   T EXT QUERIES ON TIME - AWARE REPRESENTATIONS
1736
1737   In section 5.3.2, we explored the semantic correlations between timestamps and scenes, and here
1738   we provide several examples to illustrate these connections. The Time Encoder and Image-Time
1739   Adaptor modules are designed to align visual CLIP representations and time representations. As a
1740   result, the learned time representations naturally align with CLIP text representations. This align-
1741   ment allows us to factorise text concepts using TICL time representations and vice versa. To do
1742   this, specifically, for each input text embedding, TCLIP , we compute the similarity with time-class
1743   embeddings, Ti , using the softmax function:
1744                                              exp (TCLIP · Ti )
1745                                 Softmax = P|C|−1
1746                                            j=0 exp (TCLIP · Tj )

1747   where Ti , Tj are the TICL class embeddings. This formulation offers a probabilistic measure of the
1748   similarity between text representations and time classes. The resulting 24-hour class probabilities
1749   are shown in figure 30.
1750
       The results clearly demonstrate that texts describing specific times of day are directly associated with
1751
       corresponding time periods. Beyond this direct relationship, we also observe indirect associations.
1752   For example, the word “breakfast” is by definition related to morning hours, while “thief” is often
1753   associated with nighttime activities. These uneven probability distributions across the 24-hour time-
1754   line reflect the natural relations between certain events, scenes, or concepts and their corresponding
1755   time periods.
1756
       However, some irregular trends in the probability distributions indicate that our time-aware represen-
1757
       tations, learned from a limited image dataset, still have room for further improvements, particularly
1758   for night-time concepts and images, where samples are rarer. This highlights the need for further
1759   refinement of the model to achieve more robust performance across all time periods.
