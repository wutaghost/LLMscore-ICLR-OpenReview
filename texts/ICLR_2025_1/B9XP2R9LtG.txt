009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Activation sparsity denotes the existence of substantial weakly-contributed ele-
012            ments within activation outputs that can be eliminated, benefiting many important
013            applications concerned with large language models (LLMs), such as computation
014            acceleration and model interpretability. Although promoting greater activation
015            sparsity within LLMs deserves deep studies, existing works lack comprehensive
016
               and quantitative research on the correlation between activation sparsity and po-
               tentially influential factors. In this paper, we present a comprehensive study on
017
               the quantitative scaling properties and influential factors of the activation sparsity
018
               within decoder-only Transformer-based LLMs. Specifically, we propose PPL-p%
019            sparsity, a precise and performance-aware activation sparsity metric that is ap-
020            plicable to any activation function. Through extensive experiments, we find sev-
021            eral important phenomena. Firstly, different activation functions (i.e., ReLU and
022            SiLU) exhibit comparable performance but opposite training-time sparsity trends.
023            The activation ratio (i.e., 1 − sparsity ratio) evolves as a convergent increasing
024            power-law and decreasing logspace power-law with the amount of training data
025            for SiLU-activated and ReLU-activated LLMs, respectively. These demonstrate
026            that ReLU is more efficient as the activation function than SiLU and can leverage
027
               more training data to improve activation sparsity. Secondly, the activation ratio
               linearly increases with the width-depth ratio below a certain bottleneck point, in-
028
               dicating the potential advantage of a deeper architecture at a fixed parameter scale.
029
               Finally, at similar width-depth ratios, we surprisingly find that the limit value of
030            activation sparsity varies weakly with the parameter scale, i.e., the activation pat-
031            terns within LLMs are insensitive to the parameter scale. These empirical laws
032            towards LLMs with greater activation sparsity have important implications for
033            making LLMs more efficient and interpretable.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Activation sparsity refers to the phenomenon where considerable elements within the output of a
039
      neural layer (typically activation functions, as shown in Figure 1) are zero or low values and thus
      contribute weakly to the final model output given a specific input. As a prevalent property of many
040
      language and vision modeling architectures (Li et al., 2022), activation sparsity has wide practical
041
      values, such as inference acceleration (Liu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024; Song et al.,
042   2024a), training acceleration (Zhang et al., 2024b), and LLM interpretation (Sajjad et al., 2022;
043   Zhang et al., 2023). Generally, a model with a greater sparsity ratio (i.e., the ratio of inactivated
044   elements) has more potential in these scenarios. However, this raises an underexplored problem:
045   how to obtain an LLM with greater activation sparsity?
046
      A simple solution is to design constraints at the model architecture level that force the model to
047
      have a predefined large sparsity ratio. For example, mixture-of-experts (MoE), the most popu-
048   lar architecture-constrained design, typically uses a token-level top-k parameter selection router to
049   assign a fixed sparsity ratio for each token at each layer (Fedus et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022). How-
050   ever, these constraints often sacrifice model flexibility and performance. Recent works reveal the
051   potential performance degradation caused by such inflexible sparsity assignment (Huang et al., 2024;
052   Liu et al., 2024). Moreover, to inspect the impact of such constraints, we plot the PPL-activation
053   (PPL denotes perplexity) Pareto curve (activation ratio = 1 − sparsity ratio) of MoE in Figure 2
      and compare it with a vanilla decoder-only Transformer (Touvron et al., 2023) of the same parameter

      To avoid negative impacts on flexibility and performance, we focus on the intrinsic activation spar-
074
      sity within decoder-only Transformer-based LLMs in this paper, such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020)
075   and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023). Different from the sparsity enforced through architectural con-
076   straints, intrinsic activation sparsity (Zhang et al., 2024a; Song et al., 2024a) arises self-adaptively
077   during the pre-training stage, without unified or predefined sparsity ratios among different tokens
078   or layers. We provide a comprehensive study on the quantitative scaling properties and influential
079   factors of this intrinsic property. These findings can help us formulate a generalizable method to
080   control and promote activation sparsity without harming flexibility and performance.
081   To rigorously study the influential factors of activation sparsity, we need a metric to precisely
082   reflect the sparsity level of an LLM. Conventional works rely on prior experience to set a fixed
083   global threshold for recognition of weakly-contributed neurons2 , which is empirical, inflexible, and
084   performance-unaware (see Section 2.2). We propose an improved metric, named PPL-p% spar-
085   sity, with three advantages: versatility across model architectures, performance-awareness, and the
086   precise recognition of weakly-contributed neurons. For versatility, PPL-p% sparsity follows Zhang
087   et al. (2024a) and recognizes the most weakly-contributed neurons, which are then inactivated, by
088   introducing layer-wise adaptive thresholds and comparing the magnitudes of neuron outputs with
089
      the layer-specific threshold. As a magnitude-based metric, it is not bound to the activation functions
      that output exactly non-negative elements (e.g., ReLU) and is applicable to any LLMs with acti-
090
      vation layers. Moreover, considering the trade-off between performance and sparsity (Song et al.,
091
      2024a), performance-awareness is required, indicating whether a metric can comprehensively reflect
092   the sparsity under each desired performance level. To this end, PPL-p% sparsity explicitly incor-
093   porates PPL as a performance proxy, computed as the ratio of inactivated neurons when the output
094   PPL raises just by p% after the layer-wise thresholds are applied, compared to the dense setting with
095   all neurons activated. Finally, the precise recognition of weakly-contributed neurons indicates that
096   our metric obtains a good trade-off between performance and sparsity ratio (see Section 4.1).
097

099   depth ratio (i.e., the ratio of the hidden dimension to the layer number), and the parameter
100   scale. Through comprehensive experiments, we obtain the following observations:

126   2     P RELIMINARIES AND R ELATED W ORKS
127
128   2.1   P RELIMINARIES OF ACTIVATION S PARSITY
129
130   Activation sparsity is a prevalent property existing in neuron networks with activation layers, indi-
131
      cating the existence of considerable parameters, which correspond to the activation outputs with zero
      or low values and thus have a limited impact on final network outputs given specific inputs. These
132
      weakly-contributed parameters are often defined as “inactivated parameters” and can be skipped in
133
      computation. Due to the activation layers in feed-forward networks (FFNs), mainstream LLMs also
134   present remarkable activation sparsity (Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Song et al., 2024a).
135
136   Owing to activation sparsity, we can improve LLMs in many aspects, such as efficiency, inter-
137
      pretability, and robustness. For instance, recent works manage to exploit activation sparsity for
      inference acceleration, mainly by predicting the activation patterns, wisely allocating hardware re-
138
      sources, and saving redundant resources associated with inactivated parameters (Liu et al., 2023;
139
      Song et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2024). Zhang et al. (2024b) reveal the existence of activation sparsity
140   throughout the majority of the LLM pre-training process, and then utilize this sparsity for pre-
141   training acceleration. Besides acceleration, activation sparsity also helps improve the interpretabil-
142   ity (Agarap, 2018; Dai et al., 2022; Cuadros et al., 2022; Sajjad et al., 2022) and robustness (Ahmad
143   & Scheinkman, 2019; Muthukumar & Sulam, 2023) of LLMs, which are also important properties
144   in producing reliable and well-performing LLMs.
145
146   2.2   M ETRICS OF ACTIVATION S PARSITY
147

149
      by sparsity (e.g., more efficient computing and better model interpretability) (Mirzadeh et al., 2023;

165   Activation sparsity is measured by the ratio of inactivated neurons, namely |D|/df , where D is
166   the index set of inactivated neurons. However, different sparsity metrics can differ in determining
167   whether a specific neuron is inactivated. A straightforward metric, naturally adopted in ReLU-
168
      activated models, regards those neurons with zero activation scores in Eq. (2) as inactivated, namely
      D = {i|si = 0}. To prune more non-zero weakly contributed activations, works by Kurtz et al.
169
      (2020) and Mirzadeh et al. (2023) introduce a positive threshold or bias. In this case, the set of
170
      inactivated neurons changes to D = {i|si < ϵ}, where ϵ > 0 is the threshold or bias.
171
172   The major drawback of this straightforward definition is the lack of versatility. Concretely, it is
173   unsuitable for activation functions that have considerable non-negligible negative outputs, such as
174
      SiLU (Elfwing et al., 2018). In these cases, the straightforward metric can lose considerable negative
      neuron outputs and harm performance. A quick fix is to use the absolute value, D = {i||si | < ϵ},
175
      but a global threshold across layers is hard to determine. To this end, Zhang et al. (2024a) adaptively
176
      searches the layer-wise thresholds by introducing the cumulative errors of tail truncation (CETT).
177   Defined as the L2 norm relative error caused by inactivated neurons, CETT is computed as:

184   Meeting the versatility demands, the CETT paradigm is still not friendly enough, as CETT does not

188
      under a target PPL level by binary-searching an appropriate CETT value.

190   2.3   S CALING P ROPERTIES OF ACTIVATION S PARSITY
191
      Despite the importance of activation sparsity, few works conduct comprehensive studies on how it
192
      scales with the increase of parameter scale and training data, as well as the impact of other influential
193
      factors within architecture designs. Speculating that activation sparsity comes from the training
194   dynamic in the optimization process, Li et al. (2022) finds an increasing trend of sparsity with larger
195   scale, depth, and width in T5 series (Raffel et al., 2020). Zhang et al. (2024a) dives into this problem
196   from the aspect of activation functions. Song et al. (2024a) discovers that LLMs tend to be sparser on
197   more formatted datasets such as codes and multiple choices. Other works have discussed the scaling
198   properties of parameter-sparse models (Frantar et al., 2023), fine-grained MoE models (Krajewski
199   et al., 2024), and sparse autoencoders (Gao et al., 2024).
200   To the best of our knowledge, we present the first comprehensive quantitative study on the scaling
201   properties of activation sparsity and the impact of architecture factors. The work most similar to ours
202   is done by Li et al. (2022), but they only conduct qualitative analyses and focus on the conventional
203   encoder-decoder Transformer (e.g., T5) rather than decoder-only LLMs. Besides, they adopt the
204   most straightforward metric for activation sparsity, limited to ReLU-activated models.

      3.1   M ETRIC OF ACTIVATION S PARSITY
209
210   We propose PPL-p% sparsity, which improves the CETT metric (Zhang et al., 2024a) by directly
211   reflecting the sparsity ratio at different requirements of PPL (as a proxy of performance). Firstly,
212   given a pre-trained LLM, we introduce the dense setting with all its neurons activated (i.e., |D| =
213   0), whose outputs are theoretically the most accurate. Next, we conduct a binary search for an
214   appropriate CETT value CETTk , which makes the average PPL increase just by p% compared to
215   the dense setting. The search algorithm is described in Appendix C. Finally, based on CETTk , we
      can determine the activation thresholds for each layer and obtain the sparsity value, according to

      3.2   I NFLUENTIAL FACTORS
220
221   As we mainly study the effect of potentially influential factors on activation sparsity, it is necessary
222   to specify how to compute the involved influential factors as follows:
223
224          • Amount of training data: The number of tokens passed during the pre-training process.
225            To obtain more comprehensive dynamics and more precise estimations of the limits, the
226
               number of training tokens is no less than 80 times the scale of non-embedding parameters.
227          • Activation function: ReLU and SiLU are incorporated as the two most widely adopted
228            activation functions with FFNs.
229          • Parameter scale: The number of parameters included in a model excluding the embeddings.
230            In this work, we incorporate 5 scales: 0.1B, 0.2B, 0.4B, 0.8B, and 1.2B.
231          • Width-depth ratio: The ratio of the hidden dimension (i.e., dh ) to the number of hidden
232            layers (denoted as N ). We sample 9 values of width-depth ratio ranging from 14.2 to
233            597.3, covering both the ordinary and extreme conditions.
234
235   3.3   S ETTING OF S CALING A NALYSIS
236
237   Model settings We adopt the same architecture as MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024), which adopts Ten-
238   sor Program (Yang et al., 2022) for training stability and shares the input and output embeddings.
239
240
      Training settings We mainly focus on the activation sparsity of foundation models, which only
      undergo the pre-training stage. However, before we evaluate models on task-specific benchmarks,
241
      we follow MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024) to conduct a decay stage, where instruction-tuning data is
242
      additionally added for training. Thereby, we can obtain more reasonable results on benchmarks.
243   Besides, considering the influence of various training hyper-parameters, we follow the optimal batch
244   sizes, optimal learning rates, and the WSD learning rate scheduler of MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024).
245
246   Evaluation Settings We introduce a tiny validation dataset and two groups of benchmarks, in-
247   cluding commonsense reasoning and reading comprehension, for evaluation. We also test our model
248   on more complex coding or knowledge benchmarks but fail to obtain a performance significantly
249   surpassing the random level (See Appendix I), mainly due to the small parameter scales. For the
250   measurement of sparsity, to eliminate the impact of stochastic factors (especially the sparsity fluctu-
251   ations during the early stage), we use a sparsity stabilizing strategy to obtain smoother sparsity-data
252   curves (see Appendix B for more details).
253   If there are no special statements, the training loss, validation loss, and perplexity are all calculated
254   on models that only complete pre-training (the latter two metrics are computed on the validation
255   data), and the task-specific performance is evaluated on checkpoints after the decay stage. See
256   Appendix E and F for more details about datasets and training settings, respectively.

500   5   D ISCUSSION
501
502   In this section, we mainly discuss how this work can help accomplish our key target: how to obtain
503   an LLM with greater activation sparsity.
504
505   Architectural design Before kicking off the pre-training stage, a wiser design of the LLM archi-
506   tecture can effectively improve the limit activation sparsity. Based on the discoveries in this paper,
507   we propose to replace SiLU with ReLU as the activation function to promote greater sparsity and
508   leverage its increasing trend of sparsity with more training data. Moreover, a deeper model can
509
      potentially exhibit higher sparsity given a fixed computation budget. Nevertheless, as a model with
      an extreme width-depth ratio can violate the performance, the best setting for the width and depth
510
      still deserves careful studies, combined with the quantitative study on the relationship between per-
511
      formance and the width-depth ratio.
512
513   Training-time predictable sparsity Another important value of our work lies in the prediction of
514   sparsity during the pre-training stage. By fitting the power-law or logspace power-law between ac-
515   tivation sparsity and the amount of training data, model developers can either predict the theoretical
516   upper/lower bound sparsity of a model to evaluate its potential (e.g., in inference acceleration), or
517   estimate the number of tokens required to achieve a desired sparsity ratio.
518
519   Lens for the convergence of neuron specialization Generally, the loss curve is one of the most
520   important signs of the training state (e.g., at which point the model converges). However, if we
521   compare the loss curve in Figure 11 and the sparsity (activation ratio) curve in Figure 4, we will
522   find that the convergence speed of activation sparsity is much slower than loss, indicating the
523
      ongoing of neuron specialization even when the training loss changes little. Despite the wide
      recognition of the neuron specialization phenomenon (Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), it is still
524
      unclear when such specialization converges and how to inspect this progress. Besides, the loss curve
525
      is often not a good standard for convergence, especially for modern LLMs with trillion-level pre-
526   training data. We believe that the trend of activation sparsity can provide a lens for inspecting
527   the progress of neuron specialization as well as the training convergence. We leave the study on
528   the correlation between activation sparsity and neuron specialization for future work.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we first propose a precise, versatile, and performance-aware metric for activation spar-
533
      sity, called PPL-p% sparsity, whose rationality is demonstrated in striking a better trade-off between
534   performance and sparsity. Next, we conduct a comprehensive and quantitative study on how the
535   activation sparsity scales with the amount of training data and parameter scales. The influence of
536   two key architectural settings, namely the activation function and the width-depth ratio, are also well
537   evaluated. Through extensive experiments, we figure out the quantitative sparsity-data relationship,
538   substantiate the advantage of ReLU activation, find a small width-depth ratio helpful in promoting
539   sparsity, observe and then explain the insensitiveness of sparsity to scale. These can better instruct
      LLM developers to build models with greater activation sparsity and leverage the merits of sparsity.

      L IMITATIONS
541
542
      One limitation lies in the lack of experiments on even larger LLMs, such as models with more than
543   7B parameters. Such experiments will cost considerable computation resources and time but may
544   potentially encounter unexpected observations (e.g., emergent properties).
545
546
      Another drawback of our study is the absence of computation (e.g., FLOPS) in some analyses,
      especially the experiments for width-depth ratios. In Section 4.3, we find a smaller width-depth
547
      ratio potentially produces a sparser model. However, with the substantial increase in the number
548
      of layers, the training efficiency is significantly decreased, as we have observed in the training
549   process. Therefore, in addition to performance, the computation costs of a model also deserve
550   consideration. Similarly, considering the values of activation sparsity in acceleration, it may be
551   interesting to involve the inference computation as a variable in our study.
552
      Finally, an obvious limitation of our PPL-p% metric (as well as all the sparsity metrics relying on
553
      a validation dataset) is the sensitivity to different data distributions. Intuitively, the same model
554
      can have different sparsity ratios on distinct datasets or tasks. The correlation between sparsity and
555   influential factors (e.g., the form of power laws) can also have dataset-unique characteristics. A piece
556   of evidence already presented in our paper is in Table 1, where the performance on commonsense
557   reasoning tasks is insensitive to p%, largely different from the results on reading comprehension
558   tasks. Moreover, the data mixing policies for pre-training can also have a considerable impact on
559   the activation sparsity, which we leave for future work.

836   B                         S PARSITY S TABILIZING S TRATEGY
837
838   We find that the stochastic factors in gradient descent during pre-training have a significant impact
839   on the metric of activation sparsity. Especially, during the early training stage, the model is far from
840   convergence with considerable sparsity fluctuations, and the magnitude-based sparsity metric can
841   become unstable, making the sparsity-data curve not smooth enough.
842   To eliminate the influence of these unstable factors to facilitate smoother sparsity metric, we first
843   drop the sparsity points during the warmup stage for curve fitting. Moreover, we mainly apply
844   the PPL-p% on the last several checkpoints (specifically, the last five pre-trained checkpoints) as a
845   whole, binary-searching a CETT value that controls the average PPL of these checkpoints to just
846   increase by p%. Then this CETT value is applied to all the checkpoints of this pre-training process
847   to measure the sparsity.
848

      C                         B INARY S EARCH A LGORITHM FOR CETT
850

852
      Given a list of checkpoints, a validation dataset, and a hyper-parameter p%, we employ Algorithm 1
      to find the CETT value that just makes the average PPL of these checkpoints on the validation dataset
853
      rise by exactly p%, compared to the dense setting with all the neurons activated. The rationality of
854
      using binary search is substantiated by the monotonous relationship between PPL and CETT, as
855   shown in Figure 13. Note that this algorithm can be applied to either a single checkpoint or multiple
856   checkpoints, as adopted in the strategy described in Appendix B.
