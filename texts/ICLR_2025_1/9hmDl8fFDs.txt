009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Complex-valued neural networks have attracted growing attention for their ability
012            to handle complex-valued data with enhanced representational capacity. However,
013            their potential in computer vision remains relatively untapped. In this paper, we
014            introduce Deep Complex Spatio-Spectral Network (DCSNet), a fully complex-
015            valued token-based, end-to-end neural network designed for binary segmentation
016            tasks. Additionally, our DCSNet encoder can be used for image classification
017            in the complex domain. We also propose an invertible real-to-complex (R2C)
018
               transform, which generates two complex-valued input channels, complex inten-
               sity and complex hue, while producing complex-valued images with distinct real
019
               and imaginary components. DCSNet operates in both spatial and spectral do-
020
               mains by leveraging complex-valued inputs and complex Fourier transform. As a
021            result, the complex-valued representation is maintained throughout DCSNet, and
022            we avoid the information loss typically associated with Real↔Complex transfor-
023            mations. Extensive experiments show that DCSNet surpasses existing complex-
024            valued methods across various tasks on both real and complex-valued data and
025            achieves competitive performance compared to existing real-valued methods, es-
026            tablishing a robust framework for handling both data types effectively.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the evolving landscape of deep learning, complex-valued neural networks (iCNNs) have shown
032   great potential by enabling richer and more expressive representations. Despite their promise, iC-
033
      NNs remain relatively underinvestigated in addressing key computer vision problems. Addressing
      problems problems like binary segmentation necessitates a prominent understanding of the global
034
      and local context in the input. Despite complex-valued networks showing promising outcomes
035
      Löwe et al.; Stanic et al. (2023); Singhal et al. (2022), the application of complex-valued networks
036   remains understudied due to a lack of complex-valued input and suboptimal complex-valued archi-
037   tectures. When tackling these issues, we are faced with a few challenges that need to be addressed:
038   (i) A suitable way is required to have complex-valued inputs from real-valued RGB images. (ii) No
039   prior work has shown promising results while maintaining the complex-valued nature of the input
040   throughout different tasks. (iii) For binary segmentation, there is no objective function to handle
041   complex-valued output.
042   Despite the challenges at hand, our motivation to explore complex-valued representation for com-
043   puter vision tasks stems from the success demonstrated in diverse domains where complex-valued
044   inputs are readily available, such as MRI Cole et al. (2021); Vasudeva et al. (2022), radar signals
045   Gao et al. (2018); Georgiou & Koutsougeras (1992), and audio signals Hayakawa et al. (2018);
046   Hu et al. (2020). Moreover, studies conducted in Arjovsky et al. (2016); Danihelka et al. (2016);
047   Jojoa et al. (2022); Löwe et al., highlight the superior performance of complex-valued neural net-
048   works (iCNNs) compared to their real-valued counterparts. Additionally, iCNNs exhibit biological
049   inspiration Reichert & Serre (2014) and greater generalization capacity Hirose & Yoshida (2012).
050
      Recent works Stanic et al. (2023); Halimeh & Kellermann (2022) also highlight the importance of
      complex-valued representation in images as well as audio, further fueling our exploration of iCNNs
051
      for computer vision tasks.

      verts real-valued images to complex-valued ones. We observe that any color vector in RGB space

078   signed to operate on complex-valued images generated through the R2C transformation. It is first
079   token-based approach utilizing complex-valued representation. The core component of DCSNet

092

      real-valued and complex-valued datasets.

094   Our contributions in this paper are as follows: (i) We propose R2C (real-to-complex), a novel
095   complex-valued color transformation. (ii) We propose first token-based complex-valued network,
096   DCSNet, which maintains the complex-valued information throughout. (iii) We propose a loss min-
097   imization strategy to handle complex-valued dense outputs. (iv) Our experimental analysis shows
098
      that our approach significantly improves for real and complex-valued data over existing methods
      across multiple tasks.

103   Complex-valued Deep Learning: The integration of complex numbers as weights in deep learning
104   introduces novel possibilities for delving into two-dimensional spectra, as highlighted in previous
105   works Tygert et al. (2016); Hirose & Yoshida (2011). Moreover, the significance of phase infor-
106   mation in the firing rate of neurons is underscored by studies such as Reichert & Serre (2014) and
107   Jiang et al. (2019), emphasizing the potential advantages of employing complex-valued representa-
      tions in neural networks. Specifically, the observed behavior of synchronized neurons with similar

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      phases firing together, contrasted with asynchronous neurons with differing phases causing interfer-
109   ence, bears a closer resemblance to the dynamics of biological neurons. This synchronization of
110   inputs through neurons draws parallels to the gating mechanism found in both deep feedforward and
111   recurrent neural networks Srivastava et al. (2015); Van den Oord et al. (2016); Kim & Adalı (2003).
112
      Recent studies have showcased the superior generalization capacity of complex-valued networks,
113
      as evidenced by prior research Jojoa et al. (2022); Hirose & Yoshida (2012); Singhal et al. (2022).
114
      Notably, complex-valued autoencoders have outperformed slot-attention in the domain of object-
115   centric learning Löwe et al. (2022); Stanic et al. (2023). Moreover, the application of complex-
116   valued networks has proven beneficial in diverse areas, including saliency prediction Jiang et al.
117   (2019; 2020) and iris recognition Nguyen et al. (2022). The findings presented in Cheung et al.
118   (2019) further support the notion that employing a complex-valued vector can enhance the learning
119   process for addressing multiple tasks.
120
      Fourier Transform in Vision: The application of Fourier transform has been a cornerstone in digital
121   image processing for decades, as acknowledged by seminal works in the field Gonzalez (2009); Pitas
122   (2000). With the advent of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) revolutionizing vision tasks
123   He et al. (2016); Krizhevsky et al. (2012), there is a growing body of research integrating Fourier
124   transform into deep learning methodologies Ding et al. (2017); Lee et al. (2018); Li et al. (2020);
125   Yang & Soatto (2020). Some approaches employ discrete Fourier transform to transition images
126   into the frequency domain, leveraging frequency information to enhance task performance Coates
127   et al. (2011); Yang & Soatto (2020). Others exploit the convolution theorem, employing fast Fourier
128   transform (FFT) to accelerate CNNs Ding et al. (2017); Li et al. (2020). In this study, we introduce a
129
      novel methodology using learnable Fourier filters to learn the global context in the Fourier domain,
      drawing inspiration from frequency filters in digital image processing Pitas (2000). Additionally,
130
      we capitalize on specific properties of FFT to reduce computational costs and parameter count.

137

138

139   and W are the height and width of the image. So, our target transformation function becomes
140   T : RH×W ×3 → CH×W ×k , where k is the number of complex channels in the complex image.
141
142

145
      dimensional RGB space. Given their corresponding Ir , Ig &Ib values, one can quickly locate them
146
      in this space. We use properties of RGB space and simple linear algebra to create a complex-valued

242   3.2     DCSN ET: D EEP C OMPLEX S PATIO -S PECTRAL N ETWORKS
243
244
      Recent advances in transformers Dosovitskiy et al. (2021); Yuan et al. (2021) demonstrate that self-
      attention based models can achieve good performances in solving various tasks. Following this
245
      approach, complex-valued transformer-based approaches Eilers & Jiang (2023); Yang et al. (2020);
246
      Dong et al. (2021) try to utilize self-attention. However, due to the nature of complex domain,
247   they suffer from increased computation and poor results for image-related tasks. The proposed
248   DCSNet architecture removes self-attention in favor of Fourier filters, enabling the model to retain
249   information entirely within the complex domain while preserving global contextual information. As
250   illustrated in Figure 3, DCSNet takes a complex input of size H × W , divides it into patches, and
251   unfolds these patches into tokens. The token length is progressively reduced in the spatial domain,
252   while the Fourier filter operates in the frequency domain. This process is reversed in the decoder to
253   progressively increase the token length.

      3.5     O BJECTIVE FUNCTION
368
369
      Since our architecture is complex-valued, we must ensure that the loss function can handle the
      complex output. For classification tasks, we employ the complex loss function proposed by Yadav
370
      & Jerripothula (2023). However, existing loss functions cannot handle complex outputs directly
371
      for binary segmentation. To tackle this problem, we propose a modified binary cross entropy loss
372   Lidense . We obtain a complex-valued output zj from our network for j th input. Given the real-valued
373   ground truth yj , we construct a complex-valued ground truth by considering the foreground dense
374   map as the real part and the background dense map as the imaginary part, as shown in Fig. 6. It turns
375   out to be a complex-valued map with the foreground pixel having value = 1 and the background pixel
376   having value = i, just like real-valued ground-truth dense maps have foreground pixel value = 1 and
377   background pixel value = 0. We develop a loss Lidense which can minimize the comlpex-valued
      output of our network zj with help of complex-valued ground truth saliency map yj + i(1 − yj ).

398   4.1   DATASETS & M ETHODS
399
400   We conduct extensive experiments on binary segmentation tasks. We present results on both real-
401   valued and complex-valued datasets.
402   Real-valued Datasets: For binary segmentation, we assess the performance of DCSNet on salient
403   object detection, defocus blur detection, and shadow detection using multiple datasets. Furthermore,
404   we provide a comprehensive comparison of real-valued and complex-valued methods across these
405   tasks. In the process, we had to train our backbone/encoder on a large dataset such as ImageNet-1k
406   Deng et al. (2009). We also used CIFAR10 dataset Krizhevsky et al. for running our encoder-related
407   ablation studies.
408   We follow the approach outlined in Liu et al. (2021) for comparison with real-valued methods on
409   salient object detection. We evaluate our model on five widely-used benchmark datasets: ECSSD
410   Yan et al. (2013) (1,000 images), PASCAL-S Li et al. (2014) (850 images), HKU-IS Li & Yu (2015)
411   (4,447 images), DUT-O Yang et al. (2013) (5,168 images), and DUTS Wang et al. (2017) (10,553
412   images). We use four commonly employed evaluation metrics across these datasets: Sm Fan et al.
413   (2017), maxF (maximum F-measure), Eξmax Fan et al. (2018) (maximum enhanced-alignment mea-
414   sure), and MAE (mean absolute error).
415   We use the CUHKShi et al. (2014) and DUT Zhao et al. (2018) datasets for defocus blur detection.
416   The CUHK dataset contains 704 defocus images, while DUT comprises 1,100 images. Our method
417   is trained on 604 images from CUHK and 600 from DUT. We evaluate the remaining test images
418   from both datasets using F-measure (Fβ ) and mean absolute error (MAE) as evaluation metrics. For
419   shadow detection, we assess our model on the SBU Vicente et al. (2016) and ISTD Wang et al.
420   (2018) datasets. We train the model separately on each dataset and report the performance using the
421   balanced error rate (BER).
422   Complex-valued Datasets: We utilize the Simulated InSAR Building dataset Chen (2020) for
423   building detection, treating the layover class as the foreground. The dataset consists of 270 training
424   images and 42 test images. We use mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) as the evaluation metric
425   and compare our results with other complex-valued methods. We also benchmarked our encoder on
426   two complex-valued datasets: MSTAR Ross et al. (1998) and S1SLC CVDL Mohammadi Asiyabi
427   et al. (2023) for classification tasks.
428   We resize input images to 256 × 256 and then randomly crop 224 × 224 image regions as the
429   model input and use random flipping as data augmentation. We randomly initialize weights, set
430   the batch size to 8, and use Adam Kingma & Ba (2015) optimizer. For implementating complex-
431   valued operations, we use the PyTorch library which provides native support for complex tensor and
      complex operations including convolution and complex FFT.

528   In this work, we have presented DCSNet, a fully complex-valued token-based network for binary
529   segmentation tasks, which operates both in spatial and frequency domain. It takes complex-valued
530   input generated from our R2C transform and optimizes the complex-valued dense output using our
531
      proposed loss function. While maintaining complex-valued information throughout, our model out-
      performs previous complex-valued methods on various tasks and both real and complex-valued data,
532
      presenting a robust approach using complex-valued representation.

1038
1039   We start by introducing the discrete Fourier transform (DFT), which plays a vital role in signal
1040   processing. For clarity we consider 1D DFT. Give a sequence of N complex numbers x[n], 0 ≤ n ≤
1041   N − 1, the DFT of x[n] will be:
1042                                      N −1                         N −1
                                          X                            X
1043                             X[k] =          x[n]e−i(2π/N )kn =           x[n]WNkn                          (7)
1044                                      n=0                          n=0
1045
1046   where i is the iota, and WN = e−i(2π/N ) .
1047   Since X[k] repeats on intervals of length N, we can take value of X[k] at N consecutive points
1048   k = 0, 1, . . . , N − 1. Specifically, X[k] represents the spectrum of sequence x[n] at the frequency
1049   wk = 2πk/N .
1050
       It is well known that the DFT is a bijective function, i.e., the inverse of DFT function exists. Given
1051
       X[k], we can recover the original signal x[n] by the inverse DFT also denoted as IDFT
1052
1053                                                  N −1
                                                    1 X
1054                                      x[n] =        X[k]ei(2π/N )kn                                         (8)
                                                    N
1055                                                  k=0
1056
1057   Note that, in real DFT, the input x[n] is real, and its DFT is conjugate symmetric Rao et al. (2021),
1058   i.e., X[N − k] = X ∗ [k]. The reverse is true as well; if we perform IDFT to X[k] which is conjugate
1059   symmetric, a real discrete signal can be covered. This is a major point that half of the DFT {X[k] :
1060
       0 ≤ k ≤ [N/2]} contains full information of x[n].
1061   However, in complex DFT, the input x[n] is complex. Hence, its DFT includes both positive and
1062   negative frequencies. This means that unlike real DFT, X[k] is not conjugate symmetric. X[k]
1063   between 0 to N/2 is positive and between N/2 and N − 1 its negative.
1064   The DFT described above can be extended to 2D signals. Given 2D complex signal X[m, n], 0 ≤
1065   m ≤ M − 1, 0 ≤ n ≤ N − 1, the 2D DFT of x[m.n] is given by:
1066
