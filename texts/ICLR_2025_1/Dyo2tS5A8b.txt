046   1    I NTRODUCTION
047
048   CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) models (Radford et al., 2021) have gained significant
049   attention in the field of artificial intelligence. Serving as a link between textual and visual data, these
050   models have found application in numerous deep learning contexts (Nichol et al., 2021), (Rombach
051   et al., 2022), (Chegini & Feizi, 2023)). They not only demonstrate zero-shot performance comparable
052   to fully supervised classification models but also exhibit resilience to distribution shifts. A key factor
053   contributing to this resilience is their training on extensive web-scale datasets, which exposes them to
      a diverse array of signals within the input data.

081   While large-scale training offers numerous advantages, little is known about the content of the
082   proprietary dataset used to train the original CLIP model, or the biases this data may impart on the
083   model. Despite prior exploration into the knowledge acquired by CLIP models (Ghiasi et al., 2022a),
084   (Goh et al., 2021), our work is the first attempt to analyze them through the lens of model inversion.
085   Most of our knowledge about model biases comes from generative models for which we can explicitly
086   observe and interpret their outputs. But how do we study the knowledge of a non-generative model
087   like CLIP? Model inversion is the process of generating content, either images or text, that minimizes
088   some function of a neural network’s activations. When applied to classification tasks, model inversion
089   is used to find inputs that are assigned a chosen class label with high confidence. In this study, we
090   put a different twist on model inversion, using it to invert the CLIP model by finding images whose
091   embeddings closely align with a given textual prompt. Unlike inverting image classification models
092   that have a limited number of classes, the inversion of CLIP models provides us the freedom to invert
093
      a wide range of prompts and gain insights into the knowledge embedded within these models.
094   By utilizing the extensive set of prompts available for inverting CLIP models, we delve into analyzing
095   various aspects of this family of models. Our contributions are summarized as follows: I. In recent
096   years, generative models like DALLE (Ramesh et al., 2021) and IMAGEN (Saharia et al., 2022)
097   have shown the capability to blend concepts. We demonstrate that the same holds true for CLIP
098   models, and the knowledge embedded inside CLIP models is capable of blending concepts. II.
099
      We demonstrate that through inversion, seemingly harmless prompts, such as celebrity names, can
      produce NSFW images. This is particularly true for women celebrities, who the CLIP model seems
100
      to strongly associated with sexual content. Certain identities, like “Dakota Johnson", are close to
101
      many NSFW words in the embedding space. This may be problematic since the embeddings of
102   CLIP models are being used in many text-to-image generative models. Addressing this issue requires
103   more meticulous curation of data during the training of large-scale models. III. We demonstrate that
104   CLIP models display gender bias in their knowledge through inversions applied to prompts related
105   to professions, status, parental roles, and educational pursuits. IV. We investigate the scale of the
106   training data on the quality of the inversions, and we show that more training data leads to better
107   inversions. V. Finally, we examine the presence of textual components within the inverted images, a
      phenomenon that occurs more pronouncedly when TV regularization is not used in the loss function.

134   Exploring CLIP models from a visualization standpoint has been previously undertaken, and we
135   present a brief summary of the insights derived from such examinations. A study conducted by

      2.3   B IAS AND NSFW CONTENT
144
145   Recent research in deep learning has aimed at tackling biases and NSFW content in large multimodal
146   datasets like LAION-400M and text-to-image generative models. Concerns raised by (?) highlight
147   explicit and problematic content in LAION-400M, with (Birhane et al., 2023) indicating a 12%
148   increase in hateful content with the growth of the LAION dataset. This underscores the crucial need
149   for dataset curation practices to minimize harmful biases.
150   In the realm of Text-to-Image generative models, (Perera & Patel, 2023) delves into bias within
151   diffusion-based face generation models, particularly regarding gender, race, and age attributes. Their
152   findings reveal that diffusion models exacerbate bias in training data, especially with smaller datasets.
153   Conversely, GAN models trained on balanced datasets exhibit less bias across attributes, emphasizing
154   the necessity to address biases in diffusion models for fair outcomes in real-world applications. A
155   promising solution introduced by (Gandikota et al., 2023) is the Erased Stable Diffusion (ESD)
156   method, designed to permanently remove unwanted visual concepts from pre-trained text-to-image
157   models. ESD fine-tunes model parameters using only text descriptions, effectively erasing concepts
158
      such as nudity and artistic styles. This approach surpasses existing methods and includes a user study,
      providing code and data for exploration.

460   7    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
461
462   We present a method for studying biases and knowl-
463   edge inherent in CLIP models using qualitative methods that are typically only available for generative
464   models. While the dataset used to train the original CLIP model is proprietary, visualization methods
465   give us a glimpse into its construction. The strong tendency of the CLIP model to produce NSFW
466   imagery across a wide range of contexts suggests that the dataset is not carefully curated, and it likely
467   contains a considerable amount of NSFW content.
468   A notable limitation of this study is that we use generative strategies to extract conclusions from a
469   model that is not typically operated in a generative way. While model inversion gives us a powerful
470   window into CLIP’s behaviors, and we argue that is the least biased approach known to date, these
471   behaviors do not have to be represented in other operational modes.
