011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper addresses the problem of Rehearsal-Free Continual Category Discovery
014            (RF-CCD), which focuses on continuously identifying novel class by leveraging
015
               knowledge from labeled data. Existing methods typically train from scratch, over-
               looking the potential of base models, and often resort to data storage to prevent
016
               forgetting. Moreover, because RF-CCD encompasses both continual learning and
017
               novel class discovery, previous approaches have struggled to effectively integrate
018            advanced techniques from these fields, resulting in less convincing comparisons
019            and failing to reveal the unique challenges posed by RF-CCD. To address these
020            challenges, we lead the way in integrating advancements from both domains and
021            conducting extensive experiments and analyses. Our findings demonstrate that this
022            integration can achieve state-of-the-art results, leading to the conclusion that "in
023            the presence of pre-trained models, the representation does not improve and may
024            even degrade with the introduction of unlabeled data.” To mitigate representation
025            degradation, we propose a straightforward yet highly effective baseline method.
026
               This method first utilizes prior knowledge of known categories to estimate the
               number of novel classes. It then acquires representations using a model specifically
027
               trained on the base classes, generates high-quality pseudo-labels through k-means
028
               clustering, and trains only the classifier layer. We validate our conclusions and
029            methods by conducting extensive experiments across multiple benchmarks, includ-
030            ing the Stanford Cars, CUB, iNat, and Tiny-ImageNet datasets. The results clearly
031            illustrate our findings, demonstrate the effectiveness of our baseline, and pave the
032            way for future advancements in RF-CCD.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Humans possess the ability to continuously learn new knowledge in ever-changing environments
038   with limited supervision. Inspired by this capability, several studies have proposed the problem of
039
      continual novel class discovery (Roy et al., 2022; Joseph et al., 2022), aiming to enable models to
      continuously capture new categories from unlabeled data. Such a continuous learning strategy can be
040
      applied to a variety of artificial agents, for instance, allowing robots to autonomously learn in new
041
      environments (Dai et al., 2024; Kejriwal et al., 2024). However, this is a highly challenging problem,
042   as it requires models to have the plasticity to discover new classes while avoiding catastrophic
043   forgetting with little supervision.
044
045
      To address this problem, existing methods (Zhang et al., 2022b; Zhao & Mac Aodha, 2023; Joseph
      et al., 2022; Roy et al., 2022) often draw on learning techniques from the field of novel class
046
      discovery (Han et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Fini et al., 2021), such as self-
047
      labeling (Fini et al., 2021) or pair-wise learning (Han et al., 2019), to discover novel classes and
048   employ memory replay or generative feature-replay to prevent catastrophic forgetting in feature
049   extractors and classifiers. However, these methods typically train from scratch, overlooking the
050   development of foundational models and heavily relying on memory to store raw data, which can
051   be impractical in privacy-sensitive and/or low-resource scenarios. Subsequently, Liu et al. (2023)
052   propose a rehearsal-free baseline based on frozen pre-trained models, but provide limited insights
053   into the use of frozen pre-trained models. Moreover, they compare their approach with earlier
      methods (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017; Buzzega et al., 2020), while overlooking

054
      recent advancements in continual learning, such as (Zhang et al., 2023b; Smith et al., 2023; Wang
055   et al., 2022b). This oversight results in relatively limited experimental comparisons and as such the
056   conclusions remain open for the task of continual class discovery. More importantly, they fail to
057   address a crucial question: beyond the challenges of continual learning and novel class discovery,
058   what unique challenges does rehearsal-free continual category discovery (RF-CCD) face?
059
      To overcome the above limitations, we initially combine existing methods from two different fields
060
      and conduct extensive experiments on RF-CCD problem. Specifically, we select LwF (Li & Hoiem,
061   2017), CoDA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023), and SLCA (Zhang et al., 2023b) for our analysis. To
062   enable these continual learning methods to discover novel classes, we replace supervised losses with
063   unsupervised ones, including Self-Labeling (Fini et al., 2021), PairWise (Han et al., 2021; Cao et al.,
064   2022), and Self-Distillation (Wen et al., 2022) loss, summarized from the field of category discovery.
065   Then, we rigorously test them across multiple benchmark datasets and probe the representation quality.
066   Our experiments reveal that SLCA with self-distillation loss outperforms current methods (Liu et al.,
067   2023; Wu et al., 2023; Roy et al., 2022). More importantly, we empirically found that with the best
068   combination learning strategy, continuous novel class discovery does not enhance, and can even
069
      degrade, the representational capacity of the model. This is in stark contrast to supervised continual
      learning, which continuously improves the model’s representational capabilities, highlighting a
070
      unique challenge for RF-CCD.
071
072   Based on our experimental observations, we propose a simple yet effective baseline method named
073   "Freeze and Cluster" (FAC) to tackle the RF-CCD problem. Specifically, during the initial known-
074   class learning stage, we fine-tune the representation using known classes, which is essential for
075
      adapting to downstream tasks. Concurrently, we perform over-clustering and progressively merge
      clusters until they align with the ground truth, thereby deriving the minimal distance between clusters.
076
      For subsequent novel class learning, we estimate the number of novel classes by over-clustering the
077
      data and iteratively merging clusters until the minimal distance is achieved. The remaining clusters
078   represent the estimated number of novel classes. To discover these novel classes, we freeze the
079   representation space and apply k-means clustering to group the novel classes, assigning pseudo-labels
080   to each unlabeled data point. We then calculate the mean and variance for each identified cluster.
081   Finally, classifiers are trained by sampling data points from each cluster based on their means and
082   variances. In summary, FAC addresses the challenging issue of representation degradation by freezing
083   the model’s backbone in the novel class discovery stage.
084   To illustrate the unique challenges of RF-CCD and demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed base-
085   line, FAC, we conduct comprehensive experimental analyses on CUB, StanfordCars, TinyImageNet,
086   and the challenging iNat2021 datasets. In summary, our contributions are three-fold:
087
088          • We conduct comprehensive experiments to illustrate that: 1) combining continual learning
089            with novel class discovery methods can significantly surpass existing RF-CCD approaches;
090            and 2) the best combination learning strategies do not improve, and can even degrade, the
091            model’s representational ability in RF-CCD.
092          • We propose a simple yet effective baseline, Freeze and Cluster, to address RF-CCD, which
093            estimates the number of novel classes and discovers novel classes by learning classifier.
094          • We conduct experiments on CUB200, Scars196, Tiny-ImageNet, and iNat500, and our
095            proposed baseline achieves state-of-the-art performance on these benchmarks compared to
096            current continual learning methods, paving the way for subsequent developments.

      2.1   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
101
102   The goal of Continual Learning (CL) is to train a model to sequentially perform a series of tasks
103   while only accessing the data of the current task and evaluating the model’s performance on all tasks
104   encountered so far. Continual learning methods aim to mitigate the catastrophic forgetting of previous
105   task knowledge while enabling the model to flexibly learn new tasks. Existing continual learning
106   work primarily focuses on sequential training of deep neural networks from scratch. Representative
107   strategies include regularization-based methods such as LwF (Li & Hoiem, 2017) and Afec (Wang
      et al., 2021b), which retain the old model and selectively update parameters; replay-based methods


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      such as Gdumb (Prabhu et al., 2020), TMNs (Wang et al., 2021a), and DER (Buzzega et al., 2020),
109   which approximate and restore previously learned data distributions in each new task; and architecture-
110   based methods such as Coscl (Wang et al., 2022a), HAT (Serra et al., 2018), and DER (Yan et al.,
111   2021), which allocate dedicated parameter subspaces for each incremental task.
112
      Continual Learning on Pretrained Model Witnessing the significant improvement brought by
113
      powerful pre-training for downstream tasks, some recent methods have focused on exploring continual
114
      learning methods in the context of pre-trained models. SAM (Mehta et al., 2021) demonstrated
115   the benefit of supervised pre-training for downstream continual learning tasks; L2P (Wang et al.,
116   2022c) proposed updating the network with a small number of learnable parameters (prompts), and
117   DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022b) and CODA-prompt (Smith et al., 2023) further improved prompt
118   learning methods and enhanced the model’s continual learning capabilities. SLCA (Zhang et al.,
119   2023b) studied the updating paradigm of pre-trained models and significantly improved prediction
120   accuracy by lowering the learning rate of the backbone network. Meanwhile, some works mainly
121   explored the learning of classifiers (Janson et al., 2022; Goswami et al., 2024; Panos et al., 2023;
122   McDonnell et al., 2024). However, while those techniques are effective in supervised scenarios, their
123
      ability to address open-world problems remains to be explored.

128   2.2   C ATEGORY D ISCOVERY
129
130
131
      Novel Class Discovery Novel Class Discovery (NCD) involves using the knowledge obtained from a
132
      labeled base dataset to learn and discover new classes in an unlabeled dataset. Existing methods in
133   this field can be categorized into three groups based on the loss function used for clustering novel
134   classes. 1) Pair-wise loss methods (Hsu et al., 2018; Han et al., 2019; Zhao & Han, 2021; Cao
135   et al., 2022): These methods explore various techniques, such as robust ranking statistics (Han et al.,
136   2019) and cosine similarity (Cao et al., 2022), to measure the similarity between two data points
137   in the representation space, and minimize the distance of similar data point. 2) Self-labeling loss
138   methods (Fini et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024): These methods
139   formulate the problem of generating balanced or imbalanced pseudo labels as an optimal transport
140   problem and learn from these pseudo labels. 3) Self-distillation loss methods (Wen et al., 2022;
141
      Zhang et al., 2022a): These methods generate both sharp and soft predictions for two augmented
      views of the same data. The sharp prediction, which is typically more definitive and confident, is
142
      then used to supervise the soft prediction. In addition to the above approaches, other strategies have
143
      been proposed for learning representations of novel classes. For example, (Vaze et al., 2022; Pu et al.,
144   2023) introduce various contrastive learning strategies and perform clustering using semi-kmeans.
145   However, these methods primarily focus on static scenarios and have limitations when applied to
146   real-world applications where data is collected in a streaming manner.
147
      Continual Category Discovery Continual category discovery (CCD) aims to discover novel classes
148
      in a continual manner. (Joseph et al., 2022; Roy et al., 2022) first proposed the CCD setting, framed
149
      in two sessions: the first with supervision and the second involving fully unlabeled new classes.
150   GM (Zhang et al., 2022b) proposed a more general setting, assuming the incremental stages have
151   unlabeled data containing both known and new classes. Then, Zhao & Mac Aodha (2023); Marczak
152   et al. (2023); Cendra et al. (2024) generalized this problem by proposing a setting where all tasks
153   contain both labeled and unlabeled data. Subsequently, Liu et al. (2023) leveraged pretrained models
154   and learned a classifier using self-labeling loss to discover novel classes.
155
      Although Liu et al. (2023) shares similarities with our method, it falls short in utilizing advanced
156   techniques from continual learning and novel class discovery to effectively address RF-CCD, resulting
157   in a less convincing comparison. Additionally, their reliance on self-labeling loss to cluster novel
158   classes enforces a strong equality constraint on cluster size, which proves ineffective due to noisy
159   learning (Appendix E). More critically, they only fix the backbone without providing any analysis
160   or insights into the role of representation learning for RF-CCD. As a result, their work offers
161   limited insights for future research, which are key contributions of our work. Moreover, our method
      outperforms Liu et al. (2023) with a simpler design.

      3.2   M ODIFY CL METHODS TO HANDLE RF-CCD
198
199   Combination of CL and NCD methods RF-CCD is a combination of continual learning and novel
200   class discovery. To delve deeper into the challenges of RF-CCD, we integrate various continual
201   learning methods with different NCD techniques to establish more comprehensive methods. Specifi-
202   cally, we select several typical rehearsal-free continual learning approaches, including well-known
203   Learning without Forgetting (LwF) (Li & Hoiem, 2017), as well as two of the latest approaches:
204   CODA-prompt (Smith et al., 2023) and SLCA (Zhang et al., 2023b). As much of the subsequent
205   analysis is based on SLCA, we provide a brief overview of it in Appendix B.
206   As outlined in Section 2.2, NCD techniques can be broadly categorized based on the loss functions
207   used for clustering novel classes. Specifically, we categorize the existing loss functions into three
208   groups: 1) self-labeling loss (SeLa), 2) pairwise loss (PwL), and 3) self-distillation loss (SeDist).
209   These loss functions have been summarized in Sec. 2.2 and detailed in Appendix A. To enable
210   standard continual learning methods to effectively cluster novel classes, we substitute the conventional
211   cross-entropy loss with the aforementioned three unsupervised losses, respectively.
212   State of the Arts RF-CCD methods There is existing research in the field of Continual Category
213   Discovery, including methods such as Frost (Roy et al., 2022), GM (Zhang et al., 2022b), iGCD (Zhao
214   & Mac Aodha, 2023), MetaGCD (Wu et al., 2023), and KTRFR (Liu et al., 2023). We exclude the
215   comparison with GM and iGCD, as their methods heavily rely on memory buffers, making them
      difficult to handle RF-CCD.

      In this section, we introduce our framework for Rehearsal-Free Continual Category Discovery (RF-
306
      CCD), which achieves strong performance with a simple design. As shown in Fig.2, we fine-tune
307
      the model on labeled data in the initial session. In later sessions, we freeze the backbone and use
308   k-means clustering to generate pseudo labels from the representation space. Then, we assume each
309   cluster follows a Gaussian distribution and derive the mean and variance for each cluster. Finally, we
310   sample data from the current and stored Gaussian distributions to train the classifier. Additionally, we
311   propose a novel method to estimate the number of novel classes in RF-CCD scenarios.
312
313   4.1    F REEZE AND C LUSTER
314
315   Freeze Representation As illustrated in Sec.3.3, the representation ability shows no improvement
316   and even degradation in CCD after the first session. Therefore, we simply learn the representation in
317   the supervised session (t = 0) and freeze the backbone gθ for all remaining tasks. Although freezing
318   the backbone sacrifices the model’s plasticity, it avoids the detrimental effects caused by noisy novel
319   class learning and forgetting simultaneously, thereby enhancing stability.
320   Pseudo Label Generation by Clustering and Classifier Learning Since there are no labels for novel
321   class data, and thanks to the powerful representation, we first generate pseudo-labels for the novel
322   classes through k-means clustering in the representation space. After obtaining the representation,
323   rather than directly using these pseudo-labels for classifier learning, we follow the approach in (Zhang
      et al., 2023b) to train the classifier.

350   4.2   N OVEL C LASS N UMBER E STIMATION
351
352   In RF-CCD, it is usually assumed that the number of novel classes in each task is known. However,
353   in the real world, this assumption does not always hold. Therefore, we propose a novel method to
354
      estimate the number of novel classes in RF-CCD.
355   Specifically, for known classes data, we first perform over-clustering to obtain many clusters, which
356   is generally set to three times the true number, i.e., 3 × C t . Then, we calculate the Euclidean distance
357   between cluster centers and greedily merge the two clusters with the minimal distance. The merging
358   process is stopped until the number of clusters is equal to the ground truth. Therefore, we obtain the
359   minimal distance dmin between the clusters.
360   We assume that if the distance between two clusters is smaller than dmin , the two clusters are from the
361   same class with high probability. Based on this assumption, in subsequent tasks, we first perform
362   over-clustering to obtain multiple sub-clusters and continuously merge the two closest sub-clusters
363   until the distance between the closest two clusters exceeds the merging threshold dmin . The number
364   of novel classes is determined by the number of clusters remaining after the merging process. The
365   detail of algorithm is shown in Algo.1.

      6    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we leverage advanced techniques from continual learning and novel class discovery,
489   conducting extensive experiments to tackle the rehearsal-free continual category discovery (RF-CCD)
490   problem. Our experiments illustrate that: 1) migrating the SLCA (Zhang et al., 2023b) method and
491   using self-distillation loss (Wen et al., 2022) to learn new classes can surpass the previous RF-CCD
492   method; 2) "in the presence of strong foundation models and with current novel class learning
493   strategy, the representation is hard to improve and even degrades in continuous discovery." Therefore,
494   we propose a simple baseline, named Freeze and Cluster (FAC), to tackle RF-CCD. This approach
495   estimates the number of novel classes, learns representations in the initial stage, and then only learns
496   the classifier using cluster labels in subsequent stages. Despite its simplicity, it outperforms all
497
      existing methods. We hope our detailed experimental analysis and strong baseline can motivate future
      work to develop more effective methods to tackle this problem. Meanwhile, since the representation
498
      quality is difficult to improve, we also hope to re-examine the learning paradigm of RF-CCD and
499
      consider to incorporate a limited amount of human supervision signals (Ma et al., 2024) to achieve
500   more effective open-world learning.
501
502   Limitation Although our analysis is comprehensive and provides insights into the problem, our
503   experimental analysis has some limitations: 1) Our characterization analysis primarily relies on the
504   optimal combination strategy (SeLa + SeDist). While it helps explain the issue, it has not been
505   experimentally verified across more combination methods to fully establish the universality of our
506   conclusions. We acknowledge that reaching universal conclusions is challenging because we cannot
507   exhaust all methods due to limited computational resources. 2) Our experimental analysis is based
508   on a simplified setting, assuming that the unlabeled data consists solely of novel class data. We
509   have not investigated a more generalized setting where the unlabeled data includes both novel and
510
      known classes. We believe that a generalized approach could first identify whether the data belongs
      to a novel or known class before adapting it to our experimental framework. While incorporating
511
      known-class data may help mitigate forgetting to some extent, it does not address the inherent
512
      challenges associated with noisy learning of novel class data, which is a crucial factor in degrading
513   representation quality.

      A    S UMMARY OF LOSS FOR NOVEL CLASS LEARNING
703
704
      In this section, we provide details on the learning loss for novel classes. We summarize the loss into
705
      three categories: pairwise similarity loss (Hsu et al., 2018; Han et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2022), which
706   minimize the distance of a pair of similar data, a self-labeling loss (Asano et al., 2019; Fini et al.,
707   2021), which utilizes Sinkhorn-knopp algorithm to generate pseudo label for unlabeled data, and
708   self-distillation loss. Before detailing these losses, we introduce some notations: xu represents an
709   unlabeled image, y u represents the model’s prediction, z u represent the corresponding representation,
710   and f denotes the feature extractor.
711
712   Pairwise similarity loss (Hsu et al., 2018): The pairwise similarity loss learns to group a pair of
713   similar data, thus learning compact representation for unlabeled data. Specifically, given a batch
714   of B unlabeled data, we forward the model to get the embedding z u = f (xu ) and prediction
715   yu = p(y u ; xu ). For each unlabeled data, to get its the pairwise pseudo label, we find its nearest
716   neighbor in the embedding space from the B unlabeled data. We denote the nearest neighbor of ziu as
717   ẑiu . Therefore, ignoring the negative pairs (Cao et al., 2022), the formulation of pairwise loss is:
