011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Abstract visual reasoning (AVR) encompasses a suite of tasks whose solving re-
014            quires the ability to discover common concepts underlying the set of pictures
015            through an analogy-making process, similarly to solving the human IQ test prob-
016            lems. Bongard Problems (BPs), proposed in 1968, constitute one of the funda-
017            mental challenges in this domain. Despite multiple advances in artificial intelli-
018
               gence, the BP tasks remain unsolved, mainly due to their requirement to combine
               visual reasoning and verbal description. In this work, we pose a question whether
019
               multimodal large language models (MLLMs) inherently designed to combine vi-
020
               sion and language are capable of tackling BPs. To this end, we propose a set
021            of diverse MLLM-suited strategies to tackle BPs and test 4 popular proprietary
022            MLLMs: GPT-4o, GPT-4 Turbo, Gemini 1.5 Pro, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and 4
023            publicly available open models: InternVL2-8B, LLaVA-1.6 Mistral-7B, Phi-3.5-
024            Vision, and Pixtral 12B. The above MLLMs are compared on 3 BP datasets from
025            the AVR literature: a set of original BP instances relying on synthetic, geometry-
026            based images and two recent datasets based on real-world images, i.e., Bongard-
027            HOI and Bongard-OpenWorld. Our experiments reveal significant limitations of
028            the current MLLMs in solving BPs. In particular, the models struggle to solve the
029
               classical set of synthetic BPs representing abstract concepts, despite their visual
               simplicity. Though their performance improves for real-world concepts expressed
030
               in Bongard-HOI and Bongard-OpenWorld datasets, the models still have difficulty
031
               in utilizing new information to improve their predictions, as well as utilizing the
032            dialog context window effectively. To better capture the reasons of this perfor-
033            mance discrepancy between synthetic and real-world AVR domains, we propose
034            Bongard-RWR, a new BP dataset composed of specifically-designed real-world
035            images that translate concepts from hand-crafted synthetic matrices to the real
036            world, and perform focused experiments with this new dataset. The results sug-
037            gest that weak models’ performance on classical BPs is not due to the domain
038            specificity, but rather comes from their general AVR limitations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Analogy-making is a critical aspect of human cognition, tightly linked with fluid intelligence, the
045
      capacity to apply learned skills in novel settings (Lake et al., 2017). Several approaches have been
      proposed to build systems capable of making analogies. Notably, the structure-mapping theory
046
      explores methods for discovering structural correspondences between pre-existing object represen-
047
      tations (Winston, 1982; Gentner, 1983; Carbonell, 1983; Falkenhainer et al., 1989; Holyoak & Tha-
048   gard, 1989). However, these approaches often overlook the perceptual aspect, assuming object rep-
049   resentations are already given. Chalmers et al. (1992) highlight that forming useful representations
050   is an intricate challenge. In particular, perception is not merely a passive reception of sensory data,
051   but rather an active interpretation influenced by prior knowledge. This process involves the detec-
052   tion of patterns, recognition of analogies, and abstraction of concepts. The resultant representations
053   may vary significantly depending on the context, which underscores the importance of modeling
      perception and cognition jointly (Hofstadter, 1995).

085   Multiple problems that necessitate combined perception and reasoning have been identified (Hofs-
086   tadter, 1999). Among these tasks are Bongard Problems (BPs), introduced by Bongard (1968; 1970).
087   Initial BPs were designed manually, leading to the formulation of a few hundred task instances by
088   individual contributors (Foundalis, 2006b). A typical BP consists of two sides, left and right, each
089   comprising six image panels arranged in a grid. All images on one side illustrate a shared concept
090   absent in the images on the opposite side. The task is to identify the underlying rule that differen-
091   tiates the sides and articulate it in natural language. Initial BPs (Bongard, 1968), akin to human
092   IQ tests, featured abstract 2D geometric shapes, putting the focus on abstract reasoning. How-
093
      ever, recent works have expanded the set of BPs to include real-world images, which broadens the
      scope of presented objects, attributes and relations. Specifically, the matrices in Bongard HOI (Jiang
094
      et al., 2022) depict human-object interactions, while Bongard-OpenWorld (Wu et al., 2024) employs
095
      open-world free-form concepts, increasing the diversity of featured scenes. Figs. 1a, 1c, 1d illustrate
096   examples of problems from the three above-mentioned datasets.
097
098   A central theme in BPs is recognition of concepts in a context-dependent manner, as object represen-
099
      tations need to be formed specifically for the presented matrix, rather than described a priori (Lin-
      hares, 2000). For example, consider the matrix in Fig. 1a – an analysis restricted to its left side may
100
      yield multiple concepts, such as the presence of curves or an object centered in the image. Only
101
      through a comprehensive understanding of both matrix sides one can recognize that the left side
102   depicts a single line, while the right side presents two lines. Such concept-based tasks were argued
103   to promote a more accurate evaluation of a system’s generalization ability and its capacity for ab-
104   straction (Mitchell, 2021; Odouard & Mitchell, 2022). Moreover, the concepts in BPs are illustrated
105   with several image examples, which positions the task within a few-shot learning setting (Fei-Fei
106   et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2020). In contrast to other abstract reasoning problems, such as Raven’s
107   Progressive Matrices (RPMs) (Raven, 1936; Raven & Court, 1998; Małkiński & Mańdziuk, 2022)
      that have recently witnessed the development of large-scale benchmarks (Barrett et al., 2018; Zhang


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2019), BPs allow to assess system’s ability to derive concepts from a limited set of examples
109   (typically six images per matrix side). The above aspects make BPs a valuable testbed for assessing
110   abstract reasoning abilities of AI models.
111
112   Motivation. The quest to build systems capable of forming abstract concepts dates back to the
113   1950s (McCarthy et al., 2006). The advent of Deep Learning (DL) opened new possibilities to
114   tackle BPs (Kharagorgiev, 2018; Nie et al., 2020). However, despite significant advancements,
115   methods for consistently solving BPs (and other problems that involve abstract reasoning) are still
116   lacking (Mitchell, 2021; van der Maas et al., 2021; Stabinger et al., 2021). Typically, DL approaches
117   omit the generation of natural language answers by casting BP into a binary classification task, in
118
      which a test image had to be assigned to the matching side of the matrix. Conversely, a paral-
      lel stream of research on large language models (LLMs) demonstrated promising results in open-
119
      ended language generation (Brown et al., 2020). In particular, LLMs were applied to selected AVR
120
      tasks (Webb et al., 2023), though, lately Xu et al. (2024) pointed certain LLM limitations in solving
121   AVR problems represented as text despite using information lossless translation through direct-grid
122   encoding. Recent works have combined the vision and language modalities into multimodal large
123   language models (MLLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024), inviting their
124   application to diverse tasks (Yin et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). Motivated by these recent develop-
125   ments we examine the reasoning capabilities of MLLMs in solving BPs.
126
127   Contributions. The main contribution of this paper is four-fold.
128
      (1) For the first time in the literature, we consider BPs in the context of MLLMs and propose a
129   diverse set of strategies to solve BP instances in two setups: open-ended language generation and
130   binary classification.
131
132
      (2) We evaluate 4 state-of-the-art proprietary MLLMs and 4 open MLLMs on both synthetic and
      real-world BPs, and identify their severe abstract reasoning limitations.
133
134   (3) To further examine the main difficulties faced by MLLMs in solving both types of BPs (synthetic
135   and real world ones) we introduce a focused dataset of BPs (Bongard-RWR) comprising real-world
136   images that represent concepts from synthetic BPs using real world images. Thanks to relying on
137   the same abstract concepts as synthetic BPs, Bongard-RWR facilitates direct comparisons of the
138
      MLLMs performance in both domains.
139   (4) We perform a detailed comparative analysis of 8 MLLMs on Bongard-RWR vs. synthetic BPs,
140   shedding light on the reasons of their generally poor performance.

144   AVR tasks. The AVR field encompasses a broad set of problems aimed at studying various aspects
145   of visual cognition (Gardner & Richards, 2006; Małkiński & Mańdziuk, 2023). Recent DL research
146   in this domain gravitated towards utilizing certain well-established datasets, e.g. with visual analo-
147   gies (Hill et al., 2019; Webb et al., 2020) or RPMs (Zhang et al., 2019; Barrett et al., 2018), to
148   measure the progress of DL models. However, such benchmarks evaluate system performance in
149   learning a particular task, rather than assessing its general ability to acquire new AVR skills. To ad-
150   dress this limitation, certain tasks have adopted few-shot learning setups, requiring models to learn
151
      from a few demonstrations, as exemplified by SVRT (Fleuret et al., 2011) or Bongard-LOGO (Nie
      et al., 2020). Nonetheless, these benchmarks follow a discriminative setting where a set of possible
152
      answers is provided. Conversely, other datasets such as ARC (Chollet, 2019) or PQA (Qi et al.,
153
      2021) pose a generative challenge, which may be considered more difficult due to its open-ended
154   nature. In addition to synthetic tasks featuring 2D geometric shapes, certain datasets present analo-
155   gous reasoning tasks using real-world images (Teney et al., 2020; Ichien et al., 2021; Bitton et al.,
156   2023). This approach extends the range of concepts that can be expressed and, above all, allows
157   employing models pre-trained on large image datasets. In this work, we concentrate on several BP
158   datasets that present a few-shot learning challenge, cover both synthetic and real-world images, and
159   consider settings involving both binary classification and answer generation in natural language.
160

200   Abstract reasoning of MLLMs. MLLMs haven’t been yet applied to tackle BPs, though they
201   were applied to several related tasks. Initial works focused on LLMs and evaluating their abstract
202   reasoning performance in simplified analogy tasks. Webb et al. (2023) showed that GPT-3 and
203   GPT-4 (text-only variants) performed on the human level, or even outcompeted humans, in certain
204   RPM-like tasks in a zero-shot manner without additional fine-tuning. However, they represented the
205   image objects as text using a fixed small vocabulary, thus omitting the need for identifying concepts
206   from open-ended shapes, a key challenge of BPs. Recent research concerning the evaluation of ab-
207
      stract reasoning skills of LLMs concentrates around the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC)
      task (Chollet, 2019). It was demonstrated that LLMs can solve certain ARC problems transformed
208
      to the text domain (Moskvichev et al., 2023; Mirchandani et al., 2023; Camposampiero et al., 2023;
209
      Xu et al., 2024). Despite these important stepping stones, the text-based representation taken in these
210   works simplifies the perception task by presenting the model with pre-existing higher level repre-
211   sentations. Only recently, thanks to the appearance of MLLMs, vision and text started to be treated
212   jointly in a unified manner. Cao et al. (2024) proposed a suite of AVR tasks to compare MLLM
213   and human performance. Jiang et al. (2024) assessed AVR skills of MLLMs on an introduced mul-
214   tidimensional benchmark combining AVR and perceptual questions. Our work complements this
215   stream of research by exploring BPs, a fundamental task in the field, and providing insights into
      MLLM analogy-making performance in synthetic and real-world domains.

229   3.1    P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES FOR NATURAL L ANGUAGE A NSWER G ENERATION
230
231
      We start by defining the strategies for generating answers in natural language. In each strategy,
      the model receives a general description of Bongard Problem with two BP examples with correct
232
      answers. Additionally, besides this generic introductory information, a given task BP X to be solved
233
      is presented in a strategy-specific way. Appendix I.4 presents the exact prompt formulations.
234
235   Direct (Fig. 2a). The model receives an image presenting BP X and is asked to directly formulate
236   an answer (i.e., describe the difference between LX and RX panels in natural language).
237   Descriptive (Fig. 2b). Defines a more granular approach in which the model is first requested to
238   generate a textual description of each image panel of the matrix. Each description is generated in a
239   separate context, such that the model doesn’t have access to the prior panels nor to their descriptions.
240   Next, the model is requested to provide an answer to the problem based only on the generated textual
241   descriptions of all image panels.
242
      Descriptive-iterative (Fig. 2c). Evaluates the role of the reasoning context and utilizes a context
243   window comprising the dialog history concerning all images in the given side of the problem. After
244   generating the description of the first image, the model iteratively refines its output based on subse-
245   quent images from the same side. Based on the textual descriptions of both sides of the problem, the
246   model is requested to provide the final answer.
247
      Descriptive-direct (Fig. 2b with a dashed element). In both above Descriptive strategies, the model
248
      is never presented with the image of the whole matrix BP X . Descriptive-direct strategy extends
249
      Descriptive by providing the image of BP X along with the textual panel descriptions.
250
251   Contrastive (Fig. 2d). A critical aspect of BPs is the focus on forming concepts within the specific
252   context of the matrix BP X . It’s often the case that correct identification of the concept governing
253   one side requires analysis of the other side to identify their key differences. In Descriptive strategies,
254   the model provides image descriptions concerning a single problem side LX or RX without taking
255   into account the images from the other side. Conversely, in the Contrastive strategy, the model is
256
      tasked with describing the difference between a pair of corresponding images from both sides of the
      problem (LX 1 , R1 ), . . . , (L6 , R6 ). After describing the differences between all six image pairs in
                       X              X    X
257
      separate contexts, the model generates its final answer based on these textual descriptions.
258
259   Contrastive-iterative (Fig. 2e). Extends Contrastive by performing all reasoning steps in a single
260   context window, enabling the model to gradually improve its understanding of the rule separating
261   both sides.
262   Contrastive-direct (Fig. 2d with a dashed element). Extends Contrastive by including the image of
263   the whole matrix together with textual descriptions of differences within each panel pair.

      3.3   B INARY CLASSIFICATION FORMULATIONS
293
      To dive deeper into the AVR capabilities of the studied models, we cast the BP task into three binary
294   classification settings, reducing the task’s difficulty. Firstly, we provide the model with the image
295   of the whole matrix BP X along with a possible solution, and the model is prompted to generate a
296   binary score assessing the correctness of the provided answer (Fig. 3b). Two settings are considered,
297   in which the solution is formed by either the actual ground-truth answer (the expected answer is yes),
298   or by an incorrect answer taken from a different BP matrix (the expected answer is no). Secondly,
299   we follow a setting exemplified in the Bongard-LOGO dataset (Nie et al., 2020) in which two test
300   images have to be classified to different sides of the problem (Fig. 3c). To this end, we take two
301   test images corresponding to the respective sides of the matrix, randomly shuffle the images, and
302   request the model to determine the side to which each image belongs. In synthetic BPs we create the
303
      test set by removing the 6th image from each side of the matrix, while in BPs from Bongard HOI,
      Bongard-OpenWorld and Bongard-RWR we use the additional test images. We refer to these three
304
      formulations as Ground-truth, Incorrect Label, and Images to Sides (see prompts in Appendix I.3).

509
      accuracy, while their top method, SNAIL—a meta-learning approach leveraging pre-trained Open-
510   CLIP image representations—achieved 64%. This suggests that MLLMs, which uniformly process
511   images and text, outperform decoupled two-stage approaches, which handle image captioning and
512   text-based reasoning with different models. They also briefly consider a natural language genera-
513   tion task, where models describe concepts presented in the BP instance (Wu et al., 2024, Appendix
514   E). They again use a two-step approach comprising fine-tuned BLIP-2 for image captioning and
515   ChatGPT for concept generation. In contrast, we employ a single MLLM for both tasks. For evalu-
516   ating free-form concept generation, they use automated text-based metrics, which provide a general
517   measure of text similarity. We, however, employ a voting MLLM ensemble, offering a more direct
518
      assessment of solution correctness.

525   6   C ONCLUSIONS
526
527   This paper investigates the reasoning capabilities of proprietary and open-access MLLMs using
528   BPs as a case study. Despite rapid progress, MLLMs still exhibit significant reasoning limitations.
529   Across all proposed answer generation strategies, the best-performing model solved only 22 out of
530   100 synthetic BPs. On the other hand, model performance improved moderately with real-world
531
      concepts, as shown by the results on Bongard HOI and Bongard-OpenWorld. To delve deeper into
      the performance discrepancies between synthetic and real-world domains, we introduced Bongard-
532
      RWR, a new BP dataset designed to represent concepts from synthetic BPs via real-world images.
533
      Focused experiments with this dataset suggest that the models’ weak performance on synthetic BPs
534   is not domain-specific but rather indicative of broader limitations in their reasoning abilities. Specif-
535   ically, MLLMs struggle with recognizing abstract concepts, fail to benefit from a human-like multi-
536   image reasoning approach, demonstrate limitations in utilizing context window effectively, and re-
537   quire further work to consistently integrate text and vision modalities at the answer generation step.
538   On a positive note, experiments conducted in three binary classification settings show that some
539   models achieve encouraging results, suggesting that current limitations may be overcome with fu-
      ture advancements.

      A    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
811
812
      Going beyond Bongard Problems. BPs fundamentally require solvers to articulate answers
813
      in natural language, making them a valuable testbed for assessing the reasoning capabilities of
814   MLLMs. However, to comprehensively explore the challenges posed by the AVR domain, it is
815   crucial to consider a broader range of problems.
816
817
      For instance, VCog-Bench (Cao et al., 2024) is a benchmark designed to evaluate the reasoning
      capabilities of MLLMs across 3 datasets: 560 problem instances from RAVEN (Zhang et al., 2019),
818
      309 from CVR (Zerroug et al., 2022), and 480 from MaRs-VQA. These datasets present multi-
819
      class classification tasks, offering between 4 to 8 options per problem. While the classification
820   setting in VCog-Bench differs from our focus on natural language generation, both studies echo
821   a shared conclusion – MLLMs struggle in complex, multi-image reasoning tasks. We argue that
822   generative problem formulations, such as those used in our study, pose a more substantial challenge
823   than discriminative tasks, in which the solution may be induced from correlations or by making
824   educated guesses. Further advances in abstract reasoning may require the development of new AVR
825   benchmarks with generative evaluation settings.
826   A compelling example of a generative problem formulation is the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus
827   (ARC) (Chollet, 2019), in which each instance involves transforming a source grid into a target grid
828   based on an induced transformation rule. Each instance is accompanied by a few demonstrations
829   to guide the solver. Mitchell et al. (2023) explored multi-modal reasoning of GPT-4V on Con-
830   ceptARC (Odouard & Mitchell, 2022), a variant of ARC categorizing tasks into distinct types. The
831   study employed 3 prompting strategies: presenting all demonstrations in a single image, using sep-
832   arate images for each source and target grid pair, and separating each grid pair into distinct images.
833   These settings are related to the Direct, Contrastive, and Descriptive strategies from our study, resp.
834
      The model performed best with the last approach, which aligns with the leading performance of the
      Descriptive strategy in our paper. Their study revealed that ARC tasks pose a significant challenge
835
      for MLLMs, aligning with our results. Similar to our findings, GPT-4V evaluation on ConceptARC
836
      demonstrated that generative problem formulations pose a significant challenge for contemporary
837   MLLMs.
838
839
      Fine-grained analysis of MLLM perception. Related studies emphasize the importance of eval-
840   uating fine-grained aspects of model performance in visual reasoning tasks. Notably, Biscione et al.
841   (2024) propose the MindSet: Vision toolbox, which categorizes tasks into three main domains: low-
842   and mid-level vision, visual illusions, and shape and object recognition. This benchmark is specif-
843   ically designed to test models on 30 psychological findings inspired by human visual perception,
844   providing a framework for understanding similarities and differences in human and machine vision.
845   Preliminary evaluations using ResNet-152 and GPT-4 on selected tasks revealed notable differences
846   in perception between humans and machines. Applying MLLMs and the reasoning strategies pro-
847   posed in our work to the MindSet: Vision toolbox opens a promising direction for future research,
848
      which could offer deeper insights into the perceptual capabilities of MLLMs.
849
850   Incorporating proposed strategies to enhance abstract reasoning abilities. Galatzer-Levy et al.
851
      (2024) compared the cognitive abilities of MLLMs to humans using the Wechsler Adult Intelligence
      Scale (WAIS-IV) (Wechsler, 2008). Their findings reveal that while MLLMs excel in tasks related
852
      to verbal comprehension and working memory, they significantly underperform in perceptual rea-
853
      soning tasks. The evaluation setting used in this study involved presenting models with an image
854   of an abstract reasoning matrix alongside a text prompt describing the task, closely aligning with
855   the Direct strategy employed in our work. However, as discussed in Section 5, the Direct strategy
856   poses notable challenges for MLLMs. Our experiments show that models consistently achieve bet-
857   ter performance with alternative approaches, such as the Descriptive strategy. This highlights the
858   importance of selecting appropriate strategies when evaluating MLLMs on abstract reasoning tasks.
859   We believe that the diverse suite of strategies proposed in our work can be extended to other studies
860   in abstract reasoning to fully capitalize on MLLM reasoning capabilities.
861
862   Cross-domain analysis of MLLM perception. A possible hypothesis for the subpar performance
863   of MLLMs on AVR tasks involving synthetic datasets, such as VCog-Bench or ARC, is the limited
      representation of synthetic images in their training data. This assumption is supported by the ob-


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      served performance gap between synthetic BPs and real-world image BPs, such as those in Bongard
865   HOI and Bongard-OpenWorld, which might suggest that MLLMs perform better at abstract reason-
866   ing with real-world images. However, our experiments with Bongard-RWR challenge this notion.
867   Despite using real-world images, Bongard-RWR demonstrates that MLLMs still struggle with ab-
868   stract reasoning, indicating that the performance gap cannot be solely attributed to differences in
869   data domains. Instead, this suggests more fundamental challenges in visual reasoning. Future work
870   could extend this research line by leveraging datasets that include both synthetic and real-world
871   images, such as Raven’s Progressive Matrices (Zhang et al., 2019; Teney et al., 2020) or Visual
872   Analogy Problems (Hill et al., 2019; Bitton et al., 2023). Contrasting MLLM performance on such
873
      dataset pairs may provide valuable insights into whether their limitations are rooted in data domain
      or in broader domain-free reasoning challenges.

876   B    H UMAN PERFORMANCE ON B ONGARD -RWR
877
878   Foundation of the study. Our tests on MLLMs using the Bongard-RWR dataset revealed their
879   poor performance in solving synthetic concepts depicted in real-world images. However, the diffi-
880   culty and reliability of this new dataset remains an open question. To address this issue, we decided
881   to assess human capabilities in solving these problems.
882
883   Methodology. We compiled all Bongard-RWR problems into a single document, including a brief
884   introduction that explains what BPs are (see Prompt 2), along with a few detailed examples. The
885   examples included one problem from the original BPs (#133), one from Bongard-OpenWorld, and
886   an additional BP (#336) manually translated to the real-world domain. Bongard-RWR problem
887   instances were positioned randomly in the document and were posed in an open-ended manner,
888   allowing participants to provide any response they deemed appropriate.
889   Participants in our human evaluation predominantly belonged to the academic community, including
890   Master students and (ocassionally) faculty members and PhD students, primarily due to accessibility.
891   This demographic was selected based on the ease of reaching and engaging with individuals who
892   are readily available in academic settings.
893   All answers were collected using an online form, ensuring a streamlined and efficient process for
894   submission. Each participant was allowed to make only a single submission, to maintain the integrity
895   and reliability of the data. In addition, the form contained a few more questions to gather basic
896   statistics on our new dataset and the quality of submissions:
897
898        1. How would you assess the readability of the images included in the problems? (Scale 1-10)
899        2. How would you assess the difficulty of the tasks you received? (Scale 1-10)
900
901        3. What is your level of education? (Primary, Secondary, Higher, I prefer not to say)
902        4. How much time did you spend solving the tasks? (Less than 30 minutes, From 30 minutes
903           to an hour, From one to two hours, More than two hours)
904
905   Answers evaluation. In contrast to the evaluation of MLLM solutions, human responses were
906   evaluated entirely manually. Initially, two humans reviewed the complete set of answers indepen-
907   dently, achieving a 94.5% agreement on the correctness of the responses. The discrepancies were
908   then discussed and a consensus was reached leading to a single, unified evaluation.

1096   Performance of MLLMs on downstream tasks is often correlated with the number of model param-
1097   eters and the size of training datasets (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). To investigate
1098   the relationship between model scaling and abstract reasoning performance, we conducted experi-
1099   ments with a diverse set of model sizes across proprietary and open-access MLLMs. To this end,
1100   we evaluated both smaller and larger variants of the selected models. Specifically, we considered
1101   GPT-4o mini and Gemini 1.5 Flash as smaller counterparts to GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro, resp.
1102   Also, we tested multiple configurations of InternVL2 and LLaVA-NeXT model families includ-
1103
       ing InternVL2-8B, InternVL2-26B, InternVL2-40B, InternVL2-Llama3-76B, LLaVA-v1.6 Vicuna-
       13B, LLaVA-v1.6 34B, LLaVA-NeXT 72B, and LLaVA-NeXT 110B. We conducted experiments
1104
       on all 4 datasets using two solution strategies, including Direct, which is an intuitive baseline, and
1105
       Descriptive, the most effective strategy identified in the main experiments.
1106
1107   The results are presented in Fig. 9. In general, larger proprietary models outperformed their smaller
1108   counterparts in 10 out of 16 cases. However, smaller variants sometimes performed better than
1109
       larger ones. For instance, on Bongard-HOI with the Direct strategy, GPT-4o mini and Gemini 1.5
       Flash surpassed their larger alternatives. This suggests that smaller models can achieve competitive
1110
       performance in abstract reasoning.
1111
1112   For open-access models, performance consistently improved with model size. For example, the
1113   results of InternVL2 on Bongard HOI increased from 12 to 25 and from 2 to 29 for Direct and De-
1114   scriptive strategies, resp. Similarly, on Bongard HOI, the performance of LLaVA-NeXT improved
1115
       from 5 to 27 and from 4 to 27 for the two strategies. Analogous improvements were observed on
       Bongard-OpenWorld, highlighting the potential benefits of model scaling.
1116
1117   Despite these significant improvements in open-access models, proprietary models consistently out-
1118   performed them. In particular, GPT-4o mini achieved worse results than the best open-access model
1119   in a single case only, i.e., Bongard HOI using the Descriptive strategy (26 vs. 29). Although model
1120   scaling demonstrates its potential to enhance abstract reasoning, as shown by the open-access mod-
1121
       els, the relatively strong performance of GPT-4o mini shows that a large parameter count is not
       necessarily critical for excelling in abstract reasoning tasks. Consequently, these results suggest
1122
       that simply scaling model size may be insufficient to achieve stronger abstract reasoning capabili-
1123
       ties and future efforts should explicitly address this aspect, e.g., by incorporating AVR datasets into
1124   model training.

1248   Due to the above evaluation disagreement, we made an attempt to optimize the prompt based on the
1249
       GPT-4o solutions for the additional 20 BPs (#101 – #120) that were not used in the main experi-
       ments. First, following the few-shot prompting technique, we expanded the prompt to include two
1250
       examples showing a possible logical difference between correct and incorrect answers. Furthermore,
1251
       we added a sentence which requested a strict logical compliance with the provided labels. However,
1252   this refinement appeared to be too strong, as 2 (out of 4) models didn’t evaluate any of the solutions
1253   as correct.
1254
1255
       To impose some flexibility, we changed the word strictly to logically, but this resulted in an increased
       rate of false-positive evaluations. Finally, we combined these two prompts, obtaining the outcome
1256
       closest to the manual (our human) evaluation. The final version of our evaluation prompt is listed
1257
       in Prompt 4. Additionally, we attempted to attach the image of the evaluated BP instance to each
1258   version of our prompt, but this actually confused the models rather than improving their results, so
1259   we ultimately abandoned this option and stuck with the fully text-based prompt.
1260
1261
       Although the consistency of results regarded as the number of unanonimous assessments stayed at
       the same level (87%) (see Fig. 10b), the number of answers rated as correct significantly decreased,
1262
       which was in accordance with our random manual verification.

1265   fore, we eventually decided to use hard voting to ensemble all models’ evaluations. We marked a
1266   solution as correct if at least 2 of the 4 models evaluated it as correct. This approach brought better
1267
       results than the majority voting.
