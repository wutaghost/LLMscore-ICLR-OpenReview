009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The directed graph (digraph), as a generalization of undirected graphs, exhibits
012            superior representation capability in modeling complex topology systems and has
013            garnered considerable attention in recent years. Despite the notable efforts made
014            by existing DiGraph Neural Networks (DiGNNs) to leverage directed edges, they
015            still fail to comprehensively delve into the abundant data knowledge concealed in
016            the digraphs. This limitation results in sub-optimal performance and underscores
017            the necessity of further exploring the potential correlations between the directed
018
               topology and node profiles from a data-centric perspective, thereby empowering
               model-centric neural networks with stronger encoding capabilities. In this paper,
019
               we propose Entropy-driven Digraph knowlEdge distillatioN (EDEN), which can
020
               serve as a new data-centric digraph learning paradigm or a model-agnostic hot-and-
021            plug data online knowledge distillation module for most existing DiGNNs to fully
022            leverage informative digraphs. Specifically, EDEN first utilizes directed structural
023            measurements from a topological perspective to construct a knowledge tree, guided
024            by the hierarchical encoding theory. Subsequently, EDEN quantifies the mutual
025            information of nodes from a feature perspective to further refine the knowledge flow,
026            facilitating tree layer-wise knowledge distillation. As a general framework, EDEN
027            also can naturally extend to undirected scenarios and demonstrate satisfactory
028            performance. In our experiments, EDEN has been widely evaluated on 14 (di)graph
029
               datasets and across 4 downstream tasks. The results demonstrate that EDEN attains
               SOTA performance and exhibits strong improvement for prevalent (Di)GNNs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have achieved SOTA performance across various
035
      tasks including node-level Wu et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2024b), link-level Zhang
036   & Chen (2018); Tan et al. (2023), and graph-level Zhang et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2022). The
037   effectiveness of GNNs stems from their capability to conduct message propagation over graphs,
038   thereby capturing structural insights and node features. Unfortunately, most existing graph learning
039   methods are tailored for undirected scenarios, resulting in a cascade of negative impacts in terms of:
040
      (1) Data-level sub-optimal representation: Due to the complex structural patterns present in the real
041
      world, the absence of directed topology limits the captured relational information, thereby resulting
042   in sub-optimal data representations with inevitable information loss Koke & Cremers (2023); Geisler
043   et al. (2023); Maekawa et al. (2023); (2) Model-level inefficient learning: The optimization dilemma
044   arises when powerful GNNs are applied to sub-optimal data. For instance, undirected GNNs struggle
045   to analyze the connective rules among nodes in the entanglement of homophily and heterophily (i.e.,
046   whether connected nodes have similar features or same labels) Luan et al. (2022); Zheng et al. (2022);
047   Platonov et al. (2023) due to neglect the valuable directed topology Rossi et al. (2023); Maekawa
048   et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2024). This oversight compels the undirected methods to heavily rely on
049   well-designed models or tricky theoretical assumptions to remedy the neglect of directed topology.
050   To break these limitations, Directed GNNs (DiGNNs) are proposed to adequately encode directed
051   topology and node features Tong et al. (2020a); Zhang et al. (2021c); Rossi et al. (2023); Sun
052   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a). Despite advancements in DiGNN design considering asymmetric
053   topology, these methods still fail to fully explore the potential correlations between complex directed
      topology and node profiles. Notably, this potential correlation extends beyond directed edges and

054
      high-order neighbors to unseen structural patterns associated with topology and node semantics.
055   Therefore, we emphasize revealing abundant digraph knowledge from a data-centric perspective to
056   ultimately enhance model learning. Specifically, (1) Topology: unlike undirected graphs, directed
057   topology in digraphs offers node pairs or groups a more diverse range of connection patterns,
058   implying rich structural knowledge; (2) Feature: digraph nodes present greater potential for more
059   sophisticated feature knowledge when compared to nodes in the undirected graph that often present
060   with predominant homophily Ma et al. (2021); Luan et al. (2022); Zheng et al. (2022). The two above
061   data knowledge perspectives form the basis of digraphs and motivate our research. The core of our
062   motivation is disentangling the complex directed structural patterns and node profiles.
063   To fully utilize this data-derived knowledge for the learning process, we adhere to the fundamental
064   concept of data-centric ML and propose Entropy-driven Digraph knowlEdge distillatioN (EDEN).
065   Serving as a general data online knowledge distillation (KD) framework, EDEN seamlessly integrates
066   digraph knowledge into the models to obtain the optimal embeddings for downstream tasks. Specif-
067   ically, EDEN first employs directed structural measurement as a quantification metric to capture
068   the natural evolution of topology in the digraph (guided by the structural entropy theory), thereby
069
      constructing a hierarchical knowledge tree (HKT) (topology perspective). Subsequently, EDEN
      further refines the HKT with fine-grained adjustments based on the mutual information (MI) of
070
      node profiles (guided by the learning process), regulating the knowledge flow (feature perspective).
071
      Building upon this, EDEN can be viewed as a new data-centric DiGNN or a hot-and-plug data online
072   KD strategy for existing DiGNNs. For the motivation and key insights behind our use of HKT for
073   digraph data online KD, please refer to Sec. 2.2. Notably, while we highlight the importance of EDEN
074   in extracting intricate data knowledge in directed scenarios, it can naturally extend to undirected
075   settings and still exhibit satisfactory performance. More details can be found in Sec. 4.1.
076
      Our contributions. (1) New Perspective. To the best of our knowledge, EDEN is the first attempt to
077
      achieve data online KD for empowering digraph representation learning. It provides a new perspective
078   for the digraph learning community and emphasizes the feasibility and importance of data-centric
079   digraph mining. (2) Unified Framework. EDEN facilitates data-centric digraph learning through the
080   establishment of a fine-grained HKT from both topology and feature perspectives. It contributes to
081   discovering unseen but valuable structural patterns concealed in the digraph for improving learning
082   efficiency. (3) Flexible Method. Through the personalized design, EDEN can be regarded as a new
083   data-centric digraph learning paradigm. Furthermore, it can also serve as a model-agnostic hot-and-
084   plug data online KD module, seamlessly integrating with existing DiGNNs to improve predictions.
085   (4) SOTA Performance. Extensive experiments across a wide variety of tasks and di(graph) datasets
086
      demonstrate that EDEN consistently outperforms the best baselines (up to 3.12% higher). Moreover,
      it provides a substantial positive impact on prevalent (Di)GNNs (up to 4.96% improvement).

      Based on these assumptions, we adhere to the hierarchical encoding theory Byrne & Russon (1998);
      Dittenbach et al. (2002); Clauset et al. (2008a) to establish a novel paradigm shown in Fig. 1. This
129
      paradigm standardizes the evolution of structured data in physical systems, inspiring the notion of
130
      decoding this naturally structured knowledge for analyzing complex digraphs. In other words, this
131   trainable encoding system progressively captures the information needed to uniquely determine nodes,
132   such as their positions, within structured data. From this, the encoded result constitutes knowledge K
133   residing within the true structure T . Subsequently, applying KD on extracted K from T optimizes the
134   encoding system to achieve iterative training. The above concepts form the core of our motivation.
135
      Notably, the directed structural measurement and node MI in Fig. 1 aim to uncover the structural and
136
      feature complexity of networks. Leveraging these methods, we efficiently compress information,
137   reduce redundancy, and reveal hierarchical structures that capture subtle data knowledge often
138   overlooked by previous studies. In other words, we minimize uncertainty and noise in G, revealing the
139   underlying true structure T , which captures the layered organization of the data’s inherent evolution.
140   This T allows us to effectively decode the underlying knowledge K, corresponding to the HKT in
141   EDEN. This theoretical hypothesis has been widely applied in graph learning in recent years, driving
142   significant research advancements in graph pooling Wu et al. (2022), contrastive learning Wu et al.
143   (2023); Wang et al. (2023), and graph structure learning Zou et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2024).
144   In this paper, we adopt a data-centric perspective, which we believe has been overlooked in previous
145   studies. Specifically, we investigate the potential of hierarchical graph data KD within the to enhance
146   model-centric (Di)GNNs. The core intuition behind our approach is that data quality often limits
147   the upper bound for model performance Yang et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023).
148   By leveraging HKT, we can uncover complex patterns within graphs, enhancing data utility. This
149   is particularly relevant for digraphs, where intricate directed causal relationships demand deeper
150   exploration. However, our approach can also be naturally extended to undirected graphs. For further
151
      discussion on our proposed HKT and hierarchical graph clustering, please refer to Appendix A.1.

178   2.4   E NTROPY- DRIVEN MI N EURAL E STIMATION
179
180   Information entropy originates from the practical need for measuring uncertainty in communication
181   systems Shannon (1948). Motivated by this application, MI measures the dependence between two
182   random variables. Based on this, Infomax Linsker (1988) maximizes the MI between inputs (features)
183   and outputs (predictions), concentrating the encoding system more on frequently occurring patterns.
184
      To effectively estimate MI, MINE Belghazi et al. (2018) uses the DV Pinsky (1985) representation
      to approximate the KL divergence closely associated with MI. It achieves neural estimation of MI
185
      by parameterizing the function family as a neural network and gradually raising a tight lower bound
186
      through gradient descent. Motivated by these key insights, DGI Veličković et al. (2019) proposes
187   graph Infomax to guide the contrastive learning process. GMI Peng et al. (2020) maximizes the MI
188   between the current node and its neighbors, effectively aggregating features. CoGSL Liu et al. (2022)
189   optimizes graph view generation and fusion through MI to guide graph structure learning.

192   3     M ETHODOLOGY
193
194   The core idea of EDEN is to fully leverage the digraph data knowledge to empower model learning.
195   As a data online KD framework, EDEN achieves mutual evolution between teachers and students
196
      (i.e., parent and children nodes in the HKT) through the following steps as shown in Fig. 2. To avoid
      confusion between the data-level online KD and the widely known model-level offline KD (i.e., large
197
      teacher model and lightweight student model), we provide a detailed explanation in Appendix A.2.
198
199   Step 1: Knowledge Discovery: (a) To begin with, we employ directed structural measurement as a
200   quantification metric to construct a coarse-grained HKT, discovering knowledge from a topology per-
201   spective; (b) building upon this, we perform neural estimation of node MI from a feature perspective.
202
      Through gradient descent, we regulate the coarse old knowledge flow and obtain fine-grained HKT.
203   Step 2: Knowledge Distillation: Then, we denote parent and child nodes within the same corrected
204   partition as teachers and students to achieve data online KD. Specifically, we customize trainable
205   knowledge generation and transfer for each parent and child by node-adaptive strategies and HKT.
206   Step 3: Leaf Prediction: Finally, we generate leaf-centered predictions (i.e., original digraph nodes)
207   for downstream tasks. In this process, to harness rich knowledge from the HKT, we employ random
208   walk to capture multi-level representations from their parents and siblings to improve predictions.
209

211
212   In the context of digraph machine learning, original data have two pivotal components: (1) Topology
213   describes the intricate connection patterns among nodes; (2) Feature uniquely identifies each node,
214   closely linked to labels. If knowledge discovery focuses on only one aspect, it would lead to coarse-
215   grained knowledge and sub-optimal distillation. To avoid this, EDEN first conducts topology mining
      to enrich subsequent feature mining, and collectively, establish a robust foundation for effective KD.

      Feature-oriented node measurement. At this point, we have simulated the natural evolution of
257
      a digraph from a topology perspective, guided by the principle of minimizing directed structural
258   uncertainty. However, as previously pointed out, node features play an equally pivotal role in digraph
259   learning, which means that the topological measurement alone is insufficient to accurately reflect the
260   true structure in digraphs, and mislead knowledge generation. Therefore, we aim to fully leverage
261   node features based on the original partitions to fine-tune the coarse HKT for the subsequent KD.
262
      The key insight of HKT refinement is to emphasize high feature similarity within the same partition
263
      while ensuring differences across distinct partitions. This is to retain authority in the parent nodes
264   (teachers) during knowledge generation and avoid the reception of misleading knowledge by the
265   child nodes (students). To achieve our targets, we introduce intra- and inter-partition node MI neural
266   estimation from a feature perspective. Specifically, the former retains nodes with higher MI within
267   the current partition. These nodes not only serve as effective representations of the current partition
268   but also inherit partition criteria based on structural measurement. The latter identifies nodes in other
269   partitions that effectively represent their own partitions while exhibiting high MI with the current
      partition. We can reserve and utilize affiliations of these nodes to improve the HKT structure.

      3.4    L IGHTWEIGHT EDEN I MPLEMENTATION
379
380   As a data-centric framework, EDEN effectively implements hierarchical digraph data online KD
381   driven by HKT and trainable modules, while seamlessly integrating with any model-centric neural
382   network. This framework offers new insights and tools for advancing data-centric digraph learning.
383   However, scalability remains a bottleneck in our approach, and we aim to propose feasible solutions
384   to enhance its efficiency. Specifically, we implement a lightweight EDEN as outlined below.
385   Lightweight Coarse-grained HKT Construction. As detailed in Algorithm 1-2 of Appendix A.5,
386   we can introduce randomness using Monte Carlo methods, which select potential node options rather
387   than optimal ones for sampling before executing the detaching and merging process. Probabilities
388   are assigned to these choices, and a random option is selected for execution. This approach involves
389   running multiple Monte Carlo simulations, where nodes are randomly chosen in each run to generate
390   various candidate solutions. An optimal or near-optimal solution is then selected from these.
391   Lightweight Fine-grained HKT Construction. For node MI neural estimation, computational
392   efficiency can be further optimized using techniques such as incremental training and prototype rep-
393   resentation for label-specific children and parent nodes. This training and embedding representation
394   method will significantly reduce the computational overhead of node MI neural estimation.
395
      Lightweight Layer-wise Digraph Learning Function. We can obtain node representations through
396   weight-free feature propagation, a computationally efficient embedding method that has proven
397   effective in recent studies Wu et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024b). Through this design,
398   we significantly reduce the number of learnable parameters and achieve efficient gradient updates.

      5      C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a general data-centric (di)graph online KD framework, EDEN. It achieves
533
      fine-grained data knowledge exploration abiding with the hierarchical thesis proposed in Sec. 2.2.
534   Comprehensive evaluations demonstrate significant all-around advantages. We believe that imple-
535   menting data-centric graph KD through the tree structure is a promising direction, as the hierarchical
536   structure effectively captures the natural evolution of graphs. However, it must be acknowledged
537   that the current EDEN framework has significant algorithmic complexity, including multi-step com-
538   putations. Despite the lightweight implementation, scalability challenges persist when applied to
539   billion-level graphs. Therefore, our future work aims to simplify the hierarchical data KD theory and
      develop a user-friendly computational paradigm to facilitate its practical deployment in industry.

      A.1    HKT C ONSTRUCTION AND H IERARCHICAL G RAPH C LUSTERING
833
834   Although the HKT construction process may appear similar to hierarchical clustering, it is important
835   to clarify that HKT is fundamentally different, as it leverages topology-driven structural entropy,
836   a dynamic measurement rooted in the information theory of structured data, to capture deeper
837   structural insights. This approach goes beyond static clustering techniques, providing a more nuanced
838   understanding of the underlying graph structure. Additionally, EDEN integrates feature-oriented
839   node mutual information (MI) neural estimation as a key criterion for HKT construction, which
840   allows for a more fine-grained analysis of node relationships based on feature information. As a
841
      result, the multi-granularity quantification criteria established by our method are not only distinct but
      also innovative compared to traditional hierarchical clustering (see Sec. 3.1).
842
843   While traditional hierarchical clustering can reveal the layered structure of a network, it is not directly
844   applicable to the complexities of (di)graph learning. EDEN, on the other hand, utilizes HKT as a
845   foundational framework to enable the development of learnable knowledge generation and transfer
846   mechanisms that can be seamlessly integrated with existing (Di)GNN architectures. This integration
847
      provides a novel way to enhance model learning by effectively capturing and utilizing the hierarchical
      structure of directed graphs. Furthermore, we have designed a random walk-based leaf prediction
848
      mechanism, tailored to various graph-based downstream tasks, ensuring that our approach is robust
849
      and adaptable to different application scenarios (for more technical details, refer to Sec. 3.2-3.3).

      A.2    DATA - LEVEL O NLINE K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION IN EDEN
852
853   Graph KD typically follows a model-level, offline teacher-student framework. In this setup, knowl-
854   edge is transferred from a large, pre-trained teacher GNN to a more compact and efficient student
855   model, such as a smaller GNN or MLP. The teacher captures complex patterns and representations
856   within the graph. The student, rather than learning directly from ground truth labels, learns from the
857   teacher’s soft predictions or intermediate representations. This approach allows the student model to
858   replicate the teacher’s performance while significantly reducing computational complexity.
859   With the rapid advancement of KD, it has expanded into multiple model-level KD variants. These
860   include self-distillation, where a single model simultaneously acts as both the teacher and student, en-
861   hancing its own learning process Chen et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2023), and online distillation, where
862   both teacher and student models are continuously updated throughout the training process Zhang et al.
863   (2021b); Feng et al. (2022). These innovations reflect the growing diversity in how knowledge transfer
      can be applied beyond the initial teacher-student (large model to lightweight model) framework.

      In this paper, we focus specifically on data-level graph KD, which emphasizes uncovering the latent
865   knowledge embedded in graph structures, using data samples as the medium for distillation Zhang

867   assume the roles of teacher and student, respectively. This enables knowledge transfer through their

      A.3   T HE D EFINITION OF PARTITION TREE
872
873   To define high-dimensional measurements of directed structural information, we introduce a partition
874   tree T of digraphs, which can also be regarded as the coarse-grained HKT without feature-oriented
875   refinement (i.e., knowledge discovery (a) from a topology perspective only). Notably, community
876   detection or clustering can be understood as a hierarchical structure, specifically a 3-layer partition
877   tree. In this structure, the leaf nodes represent the individual nodes from the original graph, while
878   their parent nodes serve as virtual nodes that represent entire communities. To make it easier to
879   understand, we first give an example of a two-dimensional directed structural measurement of the
880
      graph, H2 (G), where we consider a digraph G = (V, E) and its 2-order partition P = {X1 , · · · , XC }
      of node sets V. Building upon this, we interpret P through a 2-height partition tree T as follows.
881
882   To begin with, we introduce the root node λ and define a set of nodes Tλ = V as a subset of the
883   root node λ in the 2-height partition tree T . Notably, in this two-dimensional directed structural
884   measurement, the nodes in the 2-height partition tree have only three types of identity information:
885   (1) the root node (h = 0), which does not exist in the original digraph but is used to describe the
886   partition tree;
887
      (2) the successor nodes (h = 1), which are not present in the original digraph but are employed to
888
      characterize leaf nodes;
889
890   (3) the leaf nodes (h = 2), which represent the original digraph nodes.
891   Then, we introduce C immediate successors for the root denote ϕi = λ⟨i⟩, i = 1, · · · , C. Naturally,
892   we can extend the concept associated with the root to successor nodes ϕi , which are directly related to
893   the coarse partitioning of leaf nodes Xi . Thus, we define Tϕi = Xi . Now, for each ϕi , we introduce
894   |Xi | immediate successors denoted ϕi ⟨j⟩ for all j ∈ {1, · · · , |Xi |}, and each successor ϕi ⟨j⟩ is
895   associated with an element in Xi . Thus, we define Tϕi ⟨j⟩ as the singleton of a node in Tϕi = Xi .
896
      To this point, T is a partition tree of height 2, and all its leaves are associated with singletons. For
897   any node α ∈ T , Tα is the union of Tβ for all β values (immediate successors) of α, and the union of
898   Tα for all nodes with α values at the same level of the partition tree T constitutes a partition of V.
899   Hence, the partition tree of a digraph is a set of nodes, each associated with a nonempty subset of
900   nodes in digraph G, and can be defined as follows:
901
      Definition A.1. (partition tree of Digraphs): Let G = (V, E) be a connected digraph. We define the
902
      h-height partition tree T of G with the following properties:
903
904   (1) For the root node λ, we define the set Tλ = V as the collection of nodes with heights less than λ.
905   (2) For each node α ∈ T , the immediate successors of α are denoted as α⟨j⟩ for j ranging from 1 to
906   a natural number N , ordered from left to right as j increases.
907
      (3) For any natural number i ≤ h and each non-leaf node α ̸= λ, the set {Tα | h(α) = i} forms a
908
      partition of V, where h(α) denotes the height of α (note that the height of the root node λ is 0).
909
910   (4) For each leaf node α in T , Tα is a singleton, indicating that Tα contains a single node from V.
911   (5) For any two nodes α, β ∈ T at different heights in the tree, we use α ⊂ β or β ⊂ α to denote
912   their hierarchical relationship.
913
914
      (6) For α ⊂ β or β ⊂ α, we employ − and + to further describe this hierarchical relationship within
      the same partition. Specifically, if α ⊂ β with h(α) = h(β) + 1, then β − represents the child nodes
915
      of β. Conversely, if β ⊂ α with h(β) = h(α) + 1, then β + denotes the parent node of β. (note that
916
      for every non-leaf node α ̸= λ, h (α− ) − 1 = h(α) = h (α+ ) + 1)
917
      (7) For each α, Tα is the union of Tβ for all β such that β + = α. Thus, Tα = ∪β + =α Tβ .

955   A.4   B REAKING THE L IMITATIONS OF S INGLE - DIRECTION R ANDOM WALKS
956
957
      Utilizing simple random walks (SRW) on digraphs introduces unique challenges due to the inherent
      structure of these graphs. A common issue arises when the random walk encounters nodes with
958
      no outgoing edges, causing the walk to terminate prematurely. To better understand and visualize
959
      this limitation, we apply SRW starting from each node across four different digraphs. As the walk
960   length increases, we track the proportion of complete paths relative to the total sequences, as shown
961   in Fig. 5(a). To further assess the impact of graph cycles, we design a modified SRW that excludes
962   cycles and conduct the same experiment, with results presented in Fig. 5(b).
963
      This investigation highlights a key limitation of random walks on digraphs: strictly following edge
964
      directions leads to frequent interruptions in the walk. Due to the non-strongly connected nature of
965
      most digraphs, the proportion of complete walks drops sharply after just five steps. This indicates that
966   random walks on digraphs typically fail to gather information beyond the immediate neighborhood
967   of the starting node, limiting their ability to capture long-range dependencies. Moreover, when we
968   eliminate the influence of cycles, the proportion of uninterrupted sequences declines even further,
969   underscoring the difficulty of maintaining continuous paths in digraphs and further highlighting
970   the limitations of SRWs (forward-only) in exploring deeper graph structures. It is evident that this
971   significantly hinders the ability of structural entropy to capture topological uncertainty, reducing the
      effectiveness of Hh (G) and leading to sub-optimal coarse-grained HKT.

974    The primary impetus for developing the greedy partition tree construction algorithm lies in the quest
975    for an effective method to construct hierarchical tree structures from digraph data while simultaneously
976    minimizing the complexity and uncertainty associated with the underlying relationships. In complex
977    systems represented by digraphs, directed structural entropy serves as a key metric to gauge the
978    disorder and intricacy within the network. By harnessing the concept of directed edge structural
979    entropy minimization, the algorithm aims to derive hierarchical trees that capture essential structural
980    characteristics while promoting simplicity and interpretability. In a nutshell, the design principles of
981
       our proposed algorithm are as follows
982    (1) Directed edge structural entropy definition: The algorithm hinges on a rigorous definition of
983    directed edge structural entropy within the context of the digraph mentioned in Sec. 3.1. This metric
984    quantifies the uncertainty and disorder associated with the relationships between nodes in the digraph.
985    (2) Greedy selection strategy: At its core, the algorithm employs a greedy strategy, iteratively selecting
986    directed edges that contribute most significantly to the reduction of directed structural entropy. This
987    strategy ensures that each step in the tree construction process maximally minimizes the overall
988    disorder in the evolving hierarchy.
989
       (3) Hierarchical tree construction: The selected directed edges are systematically incorporated into
990
       the growing tree structure, establishing a hierarchical order that reflects the inherent organization
991    within the graph. This process continues iterations until a coherent and informative tree representation
992    is achieved.
993
994
       (4) Complexity considerations: The algorithm balances the trade-off between capturing essential
       structural information and maintaining simplicity. By prioritizing directed edges that significantly
995
       impact entropy reduction, it aims to construct trees that are both insightful and comprehensible.
996
997    In conclusion, the greedy partition tree construction algorithm for digraph data, rooted in the mini-

       The complexity of Step 1 is O (h(m log n + n)). Notably, as T tends to be balanced during the
1343
       structural measurement minimization, height h is approximately log n. Additionally, considering that
1344   m ≫ n, the complexity of Step 1 scales nearly linearly with the number of edges. Subsequently,
1345   Step 2 and Step 3 introduce the KD-based training framework. Considering L-layer MLP and HKT
1346   layer-wise DiGNN, the time complexity can be bound by O(h(Lmf + Lkn log nc2 )). In comparison
1347   to Step 1, it is negligible. This is attributed to the random walk and feature transformation can
1348   be executed with significantly lower costs due to sparse matrices and parallelism in computation.
1349   Moreover, in practice, we can employ a lightweight HKT layer-wise digraph learning to achieve
       acceleration. Consequently, O(m) in Step 1 remains the primary bottleneck for achieving scalability.

       Notably, EDEN can be regarded as a novel digraph learning paradigm or a hot-and-plug online distil-
1459   lation module for prevalent (Di)GNNs. Now, we elaborate on their experimental implementations.
1460
1461   (1) A new digraph learning paradigm: Different from the direct application of existing DiGNNs, in
1462
       the HKT layer-wise distillation process based on HKT, we implement the digraph learning functions
       of Eq.(7) and Eq.(8) through personalized model design. Specifically, to reduce computational
1463
       costs, we employ the magnetic Laplacian proposed in MagNet Zhang et al. (2021c) for digraph
1464
       convolution. Compared to MagNet, EDEN pre-computes L iterations of feature propagation and
1465   compresses complex learning processes into simple linear mappings, maximizing training and
1466   inference efficiency. Building upon this, a personalized model design for the online distillation
1467   process is implemented to achieve end-to-end training.
1468
       (2) A hot-and-plug online distillation module: Essentially, EDEN serves as a general online distillation
1469
       framework, introducing a hierarchical knowledge transfer mechanism for existing DiGNNs. In other
1470
       words, EDEN seamlessly integrates into the HKT layer-wise digraph learning functions (i.e., utilize
1471   existing digraph neural architectures as digraph learning function in Eq.(7) and Eq.(8) to generate
1472   node embeddings or soft labels) to improve predictions.

       DGCN Tong et al. (2020b): DGCN proposes the first and second-order proximity of neighbors to
       design a new message-passing mechanism, which in turn learns aggregators based on incoming and
1475
       outgoing edges using two sets of independent learnable parameters.
1476
1477   DIMPA He et al. (2022b): DIMPA represents source and target nodes separately. However, DIMPA
1478   aggregates the neighborhood information within K hops in each layer to further increase the receptive
1479   field (RF), and it performs a weighted average of the multi-hop neighborhood information to capture
1480
       the local network information.

1483   aggregation weights are tuned based on the parameterized feature propagation process to generate
1484   node representations.

1486   neighborhoods and incorporates socio-psychological-inspired regularizers. This conceptually simple
1487   yet effective framework extends seamlessly to digraphs with cycles and non-transitive relations,
1488   showcasing versatility in various downstream tasks.
1489
       Dir-GNN Rossi et al. (2023): Dir-GNN introduces a versatile framework tailored for heterophilous
1490   settings. It addresses edge directionality by conducting separate aggregations of incoming and
1491   outgoing edges. Demonstrated to match the expressivity of the directed Weisfeiler-Lehman test,
1492   Dir-GNN outperforms conventional MPNNs in accurately modeling digraphs.

1503   magnetic field, which preserves edge directionality by encoding it into a complex phase with an
1504   electric charge parameter. By adopting a truncated variant of PageRank, it designs and builds a
1505
       low-pass filter for homogeneous graphs and a high-pass filter for heterogeneous graphs.
1506   HoloNet Koke & Cremers (2023): HoloNet demonstrates that spectral convolution can extend
1507   to digraphs. By leveraging advanced tools from complex analysis and spectral theory, HoloNet
1508   introduces spectral convolutions tailored for digraphs.

1510   graph convolutions. This model’s scalability is directly proportional to the number of edges, and it
1511   learns intermediate representations in hidden layers that capture both the structure and node features.

1597   Hyperparameter Analysis. To provide a comprehensive analysis of the robustness of EDEN from
1598   the perspective of hyperparameter sensitivity, we supplement the experimental results in Fig.7 with
1599   the outcomes of HKT-based random walk sampling for leaf-centric prediction, considering various
1600   probabilities of transitioning between different identity nodes (i.e., parents, siblings, and children).
1601   Notably, we do not discuss the sampling probability regarding children separately. This is because
1602   their main role is to provide return probabilities in the random walk process to yield richer sampling
1603   sequences, without explicitly indicating the identity of the next node to visit. Before giving our
1604   analysis, we first revisit the key insights introduced in Sec.3.3: (1) For node-level downstream tasks,
1605
       it’s preferable to sample the parent of the current leaf node to offer a rich high-level representation of
       the current label class. (2) For link-level downstream tasks, it’s preferable to sample the siblings of the
1606
       current leaf node to provide topologically relevant contextual insights at the same level. Based on the
1607
       experimental results, we observe that for Node-C, larger values of prw and smaller values of srw yield
1608   better predictive performance, whereas for the three distinct link-level downstream tasks, smaller
1609   values of prw and larger values of srw are preferable. This validates our aforementioned assertions
1610   and provides an empirical reference for selecting hyperparameters when practically applying EDEN.
1611
       Additionally, in Fig. 8, we provide insights into how varying the coefficient α in the α-flexible KD
1612
       loss impacts the optimization process, reflected in the final predictive performance. According to
1613
       our experimental results, in most cases, EDEN should prioritize the KD process during end-to-end
1614   optimization. This is because the node-adaptive trainable knowledge generation and transfer processes
1615   ensure high-quality KD, thereby positively influencing downstream task predictions. Notably, smaller
1616   values of α perform better in edge existence problems. This is because the cross-entropy loss function,
1617   used to provide supervision, aids significantly in coarser-grained existence problems, while finer-
1618   grained issues like directionality and classification often benefit more from data-driven high-quality
1619   knowledge. In a nutshell, we recommend smaller α values for edge existence problems and larger α
       values for other tasks, followed by manual adjustments based on practical performance.

       Average Precision (AP), alongside the commonly known Accuracy (ACC). We default to using AUC

       and AP in the evaluation of the link prediction tasks, and ACC to evaluate the predictive performance
1646   of node-level tasks. Regarding the experimental results of Dir-GNN and HoloNet on the Empire

1649
       AUC stands as a comprehensive metric for evaluating binary classification models. Quantifying the
1650
       area beneath the ROC curve, it provides a global assessment of the model’s ability to discriminate
1651
       between positive and negative instances. AUC is particularly valuable in scenarios with imbalanced
1652   datasets, as it remains insensitive to variations in class distribution. Its utility extends to model
1653   comparison, offering insights into performance variations across different decision thresholds.
1654
1655
       AP involves ranking predictions by their confidence scores, typically probabilities, from highest to
       lowest, and calculating precision and recall at each threshold. These metrics are used to construct a
1656
       precision-recall curve, which plots precision values as a function of recall. AP itself is computed as
1657
       the weighted mean of precision achieved at each threshold, where the weights are the increments
1658   in recall from the previous thresholds. This approach allows AP to summarize the area under the
1659   precision-recall curve, providing a single-figure measure of model performance that encapsulates both
1660   the accuracy and the ranking of the positive predictions. Higher AP values indicate a model that not
1661   only predicts the positive class accurately but also ranks those predictions highly, thus demonstrating
1662   high precision and recall across the board.
