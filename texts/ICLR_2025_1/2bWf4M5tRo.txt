009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are observed to generate plausible yet incorrect
012            responses, known as hallucinations. Effectively detecting such hallucination in-
013            stances is crucial for the safe deployment of LLMs. Recent research has linked
014            hallucination to model uncertainty, suggesting that hallucinations can be detected
015            by measuring dispersion over answer distributions obtained from a set of samples
016            drawn from the model. While using the model’s next token probabilities used dur-
017            ing training is a natural way to obtain samples, in this work, we argue that for the
018
               purpose of hallucination detection, it is overly restrictive and hence sub-optimal.
               Motivated by this viewpoint, we perform an extensive empirical analysis showing
019
               that an alternative way to measure uncertainty - by perturbing hidden unit activa-
020
               tions in intermediate layers of the model - is complementary to sampling, and can
021            significantly improve detection accuracy over mere sampling.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant advancements in recent years (Achiam et al.,
027   2023; Zhao et al., 2023). However, despite the strides, LLMs are observed to sometimes generate
028   plausible yet incorrect responses – a phenomenon known as hallucination (Ji et al., 2023; Kuhn et al.,
029   2023a). To ensure the safe deployment of LLMs, effective detection of hallucination is essential,
030   and it has gained significant research attention (Malinin & Gales, 2020; Lin et al., 2022; 2023; Kuhn
031   et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024). Many research efforts focus on detecting hallucinations by assessing
032   model uncertainty across samples drawn from the model. For example, Malinin & Gales (2020)
033
      proposes leveraging predictive uncertainty for hallucination detection. Similarly, Lin et al. (2022)
      and Lin et al. (2023) propose semantic consistency and quantify lexical similarity across samples.
034
      The core principle underlying this line of work is simple: the greater the observed uncertainty, the
035
      higher the likelihood of hallucination.

      It is commonly assumed in language modeling that hidden unit activations tend to capture the more
077   abstract and high-level representations of a given phrase or thought, while logits and low-level token
078   embeddings capture representations that reduce it to a specific syntactic form. This suggests that,
079   even though it is tempting to rely on sampling from the model to assess coherence for a given prompt,
080   a better way to assess coherence should involve perturbations of these hidden representations. Un-
081   like sampling, which preserves the token likelihood order regardless of the sampling temperature,
082   hidden representation perturbation can disrupt this order by altering token probabilities. These dis-
083   tinct impacts suggest that perturbing hidden representations could provide a complementary view of
084   coherence, particularly for hallucination detection.
085   To this end, we study model behavior under randomness introduced in earlier stages of LLM com-
086   putation. Particularly, we inject noise to perturb intermediate layer representations, as illustrated in
087   Figure 1. Under noise perturbation, we hypothesize that a model would exhibit higher uncertainty
088   when hallucinating, consistent with the relationship between model uncertainty and hallucination
089   found in prior research. We empirically validate the hypothesis in Figure 2 (a), where hallucination
090   cases (grey) show higher variance under noise injection, reflected by higher entropy. Additionally,
091
      we examine the interplay between intermediate layer noise injection and the prediction layer sam-
      pling. Since two sources of randomness operate at different layers, we hypothesize and validate
092
      that they have complementary effects on the model uncertainty, as shown in Figure 3. Based on our
093
      observation, we propose combining intermediate layer noise injection with prediction layer sam-
094   pling to enhance hallucination detection. We empirically validate that this combination improves
095   the separation between hallucination and non-hallucination instances in terms of model uncertainty
096   in Figure 2 (b). Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of noise injection in enhancing
097   hallucination detection across various datasets, uncertainty metrics, and model architectures such as
098   Llama2-7B-chat, Llama2-13B-chat, and Mistral.

      4.6   A LTERNATIVE U NCERTAINTY M ETRIC
470
471   In addition to the uncertainty metrics defined in Section 2, we investigate other metrics including
472
      Lexical Similarity (Lin et al., 2022; 2023) and Semantic Entropy Kuhn et al. (2023b). Lexical Sim-
      ilarity is an uncertainty metric used to gauge how similar text samples are. It specifically calculates
473
      the average Rouge-L score across a set of sampled answers Y = {y 1 , y 2 , . . . , y K } for a given
474                     PK PK
      context x as C1 i=1 j=i+1 RougeL(y i , y j ) where C = K ∗ (K − 1)/2. Semantic entropy
475
476
      combines the uncertainties of individual tokens within groups of similar meanings. To calculate it,
      first, the generated outputs are grouped into clusters that share the same semantic meaning. Then,
477
      the semantic entropy is determined by summing up the uncertainties within each cluster.
478
479   Among the datasets analyzed, only TriviaQA is appropriately suited for evaluating Lexical Similar-
480   ity and Semantic Entropy. The True/False format of ProntoQA and the multiple-choice format of
481   CSQA are not conducive to Rouge-L measurement. Similarly, the numerical answers in GSM8K
482
      are incompatible with the clustering required for Semantic Entropy analysis. Conversely, the short,
      free-form answers in TriviaQA make it an ideal candidate for both metrics.

493   5   R ELATED W ORK
494
495   Several recent works have demonstrated a strong correlation between model uncertainty and the
496   likelihood of hallucination. Measures of model uncertainty include the entropy of answer (Malinin
497   & Gales, 2021), semantic (Kuhn et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024; Farquhar et al., 2024), predictive
498   (Xiao & Wang, 2021), and lexical (Lin et al., 2022; 2023) distributions. These methods rely on a
499   diverse set of model generations which primarily used temperature-based sampling techniques. Our
500   work is complementary to these approaches and introduces an additional source of randomness.
501   In addition to entropy-based estimates, intermediate model activations have been shown to provide
502   insights into model confidence. Chuang et al. (2023) demonstrates that the divergence in activations
503   between correct and incorrect tokens tends to increase across layers, with contrasted activations
504   growing sharper for correct tokens. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) shows that hidden embeddings
505   encode an LLM’s sense of “truthfulness”, which may be steered along a vector of truth through
506   test-time intervention. Self-reported confidence as explored by Manakul et al. (2023) and Kadavath
507   et al. (2022) is a promising direction but requires the model to be well-calibrated and can suffer
508
      out-of-distribution.
509
510   6   C ONCLUSION
511
512   Our study highlights the critical issue of hallucinations in Large Language Models (LLMs) and the
513   importance of detecting these instances for safe deployment. We have established a link between
514   hallucinations and model uncertainty, noting that existing methods primarily focus on next-token
515   sampling as the sole source of randomness. Our investigation into the effects of injecting noise
516   into the hidden states of intermediate layers reveals that introducing randomness at earlier stages of
517   computation has a complementary impact on model uncertainty. By combining both intermediate
518   layer randomness and prediction layer sampling, we propose an enhanced approach for hallucination
519
      detection. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of this combined scheme, demonstrating
      its potential to improve the reliability of LLMs.
