009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the widespread adoption of ensemble models, it is widely acknowledged
012            within the ML community that they offer limited interpretability. For instance,
013            while a single decision tree is considered interpretable, ensembles of decision
014            trees (e.g., boosted-trees) are usually regarded as black-boxes. Although this
015            reduced interpretability is widely acknowledged, the topic has received only limited
016            attention from a theoretical and mathematical viewpoint. In this work, we provide
017            an elaborate analysis of the interpretability of ensemble models through the lens
018
               of computational complexity theory. In a nutshell, we explore different forms of
               explanations and analyze whether obtaining explanations for ensembles is strictly
019
               computationally less tractable than for their constituent base models. We show
020
               that this is indeed the case for ensembles that consist of interpretable models,
021            such as decision trees or linear models; but this is not the case for ensembles
022            consisting of more complex models, such as neural networks. Next, we perform
023            a fine-grained analysis using parameterized complexity to measure the impact of
024            different problem parameters on an ensemble’s interpretability. Our findings reveal
025            that even if we shrink the size of all base models in an ensemble substantially, the
026            ensemble as a whole remains intractable to interpret. However, an analysis of the
027            number of base models yields a surprising dynamic — while ensembles consisting
028            of a limited number of decision trees can be interpreted efficiently, ensembles that
029
               consist of a small (even constant) number of linear models are computationally
               intractable to interpret.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Ensemble learning is a widely acclaimed technique in ML that leverages the strengths of multiple
035
      models instead of relying on a single one. This approach has been proven to enhance predictive
036   accuracy, mitigate variance, and handle imbalanced or noisy datasets effectively (Dong et al. (2020);
037   Sagi & Rokach (2018)).
038
039
      However, a significant challenge with ensemble models is their perceived lack of interpretabil-
      ity (Guidotti et al. (2018); Sagi & Rokach (2021); Hara & Hayashi (2018); Bénard et al. (2021); Kook
040
      et al. (2022)). The reason behind this is straightforward — utilizing several models simultaneously
041
      makes the decision-making process inherently more complex, and thus more challenging to under-
042   stand. For instance, while it is feasible to trace the decision-making path in a single decision tree, this
043   level of straightforward traceability is not achievable in tree ensembles (Sagi & Rokach (2021); Hara
044   & Hayashi (2018); Bénard et al. (2021)). Despite the general acknowledgment of this issue in the ML
045   community, there has been only limited exploration of the interpretability of ensemble models, from
046   a theoretical and rigorous viewpoint.
047
      In this work, we aim to establish a sound theoretical basis for evaluating the interpretability of
048   ensemble models. Recent research provides a basis for a rigorous study of interpretability by
049   incorporating the principles of computational complexity theory (Barceló et al. (2020); Wäldchen
050   et al. (2021); Arenas et al. (2022)). Specifically, Barceló et al. (2020) suggest that by exploring the
051   computational complexity required to produce various types of explanations on different ML models,
052   we can improve our grasp of interpretability. There, a model is deemed interpretable if it is possible
053   to efficiently generate an explanation for its decisions. Conversely, if generating an explanation is
      computationally difficult, the model is considered uninterpretable.

054
      We perform such an analysis for ensemble interpretability, by examining the computational complexity
055   of deriving various types of explanations for ensembles, in comparison to their individual base models.
056   We focus on formal notions of explanations that are grounded in logical and mathematical guarantees.
057   Such explanations are often mentioned within a subfield of interest known as formal explainable
058   AI (Marques-Silva & Ignatiev (2022)). Providing explanations with mathematical guarantees is vital
059   in systems where safety is paramount. Moreover, such guarantees enable a thorough evaluation of the
060   computational complexity involved in deriving explanations. Within this scope, we focus on three
061   widely recognized formal types of explanations:
062
063
            1. Sufficient Reason Feature Selection, where, we analyze the complexity of selecting the
               k most important features based on the common sufficiency criterion. We also explore
064
               choosing subsets of minimal cardinality, and a probabilistic version of these explanations.
065
066         2. Contrastive Explanations, where we study the complexity involved in identifying input
067
               features that represent the smallest change required for altering a given prediction.
068         3. Shapley Value Feature Attributions, where we analyze the complexity of providing an
069            exact computation of the highly incorporated shapley value attribution index.
070
071
      We analyze the computation of these explanations across a broad spectrum of ensembles, including
      any that employ either voting or weighted-voting inference methods (such as XGBoost, Adaboost,
072
      Gradient Boosting, Random Forest, etc; a complete formalization can be found in Appendix A).
073
      Moreover, we focus on exploring three common types of base models that are integral to these
074   ensembles: (i) decision trees, (ii) linear models, and (iii) neural networks. Our reason for choosing
075   these base-models is that they are both widely used and also cover a broad spectrum of interpretability.
076   Consequently, we are able to analyze the interpretability of diverse kinds of ensembles.
077
      Our findings present a range of computational complexity results for generating various explanations
078
      across different types of ensembles. These results span strict computational complexity classes
079
      within the polynomial hierarchy (e.g., PTIME, NP, ΣP   2 ), the counting hierarchy (e.g., #P), and
080   parameterized complexity classes (Downey & Fellows, 2012) (e.g., FPT, W[1]). These results
081   highlight how specific parameters, such as the number of base models and their sizes, influence the
082   complexity of interpreting ensembles.

126   2    P RELIMINARIES
127
128   Complexity Classes. This paper assumes that readers have a basic understanding of standard
129   complexity classes, including polynomial time (PTIME) and nondeterministic polynomial time (NP
130   and coNP). We also discuss the common class within the second order of the polynomial hierarchy,
131   ΣP2 . This class contains problems that can be solved within NP when provided access to an oracle
132   capable of solving coNP problems in constant time. It clearly holds that NP, coNP ⊆ ΣP  2 ; and it is
133   also widely believed that NP, coNP ⊊ ΣP  2 (Arora & Barak (2009)). The paper additionally discusses
134   the complexity class #P which represents the total count of accepting paths in a polynomial-time
135
      nondeterministic Turing machine. It is widely believed that ΣP2 ⊊ #P (Arora & Barak (2009)).

136   Domain. We consider a set of n input features {1, . . . , n} with the assignments: x := (x1 , . . . , xn ).
137   We denote the entire feature space by F := {0, 1}n , and focus on different forms of explanations
138   for interpreting the classifier f : F → {0, 1}. We study local forms of explanations, meaning those
139   that interpret a prediction over a specific input instance x. Following common practice, we focus
140   on Boolean input and output values, which enable a cleaner presentation (Arenas et al. (2021a);
141
      Wäldchen et al. (2021); Barceló et al. (2020)). However, many of our findings are also applicable to
      cases involving real values (see Appendix D for additional information).
142
143   Ensembles and Base Models. We regard an ensemble as a classification function, comprised of
144   k different base models (each being a classification function in itself). As we focus on post-hoc
145   explanations, our interest lies in the inference of these models. This paper focuses on two key types of
146   ensemble inference: majority voting and weighted voting. In the majority voting setting, we assume
147
      that the prediction is obtained via a majority vote between the k participating base models. In the
      weighted-voting setting, we assume that each base-model is associated with a weight and that the
148
      ensemble prediction is based on an aggregation of all individual predictions. All of our complexity
149
      results apply for both families of ensembles. Moreover, we consider three different families of base
150   model types: (i) Free Binary Decision diagrams (FBDDs), which are a generalization of decision
151   trees; (ii) Perceptrons (for our analysis of linear models); and (iii) Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs)
152   with ReLU activation units. A comprehensive and detailed formalization of all base-model types and
153   ensembles is relegated to Appendix A.
154
      In essence, this formalization covers a broad spectrum of popular ensemble techniques, including
155
      random forests, boosted trees (e.g., XGBoost), along with other types of ensembles that incorporate
156   decision trees, neural networks, or various linear models (for example, logistic regression, SVM
157   classifiers, etc.). Although our primary emphasis is on classification models, several of our findings
158   are also applicable to regression scenarios (see Appendix D for further details), thus making them
159   relevant for different regression approaches, (e.g., linear regression models, etc.). While we mainly
160   discuss homogeneous ensembles (composed of identical model types), many of our results carry on
161   to heterogeneous ensembles (comprising various model types) as well. Further details are provided in
      Appendix D.

      3    E XPLAINABILITY Q UERIES
163
164
165   While the concept of model interpretability can vary based on perspective, we focus on several widely
166   recognized forms of explanations from the literature. In line with previous research (Barceló et al.
167   (2020); Arenas et al. (2021b); Bassan et al. (2024)), we conceptualize each type of explanation as
168   an explainability query. An explainability query takes both f and x as inputs and aims to address
169
      specific inquiries while providing some type of interpretation for the prediction f (x). Typically,
      explainability queries provide answers to decision problems or to counting problems.
170
171   Sufficient reason Feature Selection. We consider the common sufficiency criterion for feature
172   selection, which is based on common explainability methods (Ribeiro et al. (2018); Carter et al.
173   (2019); Ignatiev et al. (2019b)). A sufficient reason is a subset of input features, S ⊆ [n], such that
174   when we fix the features of S to their corresponding values in x ∈ F, then the prediction always
175
      remains f (x), regardless of any different assignment to the features in the subset S. We use the
      notation of (xS ; zS̄ ) to denote an assignment where the values x are assigned to S and the values of z
176
      are assigned to S. We can hence formally define S to be a sufficient reason with respect to ⟨f, x⟩ iff
177
      it holds that for all z ∈ F: f (xS ; zS̄ ) = f (x).
178
179   A typical assumption that is made in the literature suggests that smaller sufficient reasons (that is,
180   those with a lesser cardinality of |S|) are more useful than larger ones (Ribeiro et al. (2018); Carter
181   et al. (2019); Ignatiev et al. (2019b)). This leads to a particular interest in obtaining cardinally
182
      minimal sufficient reasons, also referred to as minimum sufficient reasons, and consequently to our
      first explainability query:
183
184
185   MSR (Minimum Sufficient Reason):
186
      Input: Model f , input x, and d ∈ N
      Output: Yes if there exists some S ⊆ [n] such that S is a sufficient reason with respect to ⟨f, x⟩ and
187
      |S| ≤ d, and No otherwise.
188
189   To provide a comprehensive understanding of the complexity results of sufficient reasons, we study
190   two additional common explainability queries (Barceló et al. (2020); Bassan et al. (2024)) which
191   represent refinements of the MSR query: (i) The Check-Sufficient-Reason (CSR) query, which given a
192   subset S validates if it is a sufficient reason; (ii) The Count-Completions (CC) query which represents
193   a generalized version of the CSR query, where given a subset of features, we return the relative
194   portion of assignments that maintain a prediction. This form of explanation relates to the probability
195
      of maintaining a classification. Due to space constraints, we relegate the full formalization of the
      CSR and CC queries to Appendix C.
196
197   Contrastive Explanations. An alternative approach to interpreting models involves examining
198   subsets of features which, when modified, could lead to a change in the model’s classification (Dhu-
199   randhar et al. (2018); Guidotti (2022)). We consider a subset S ⊆ [n] as contrastive if changing
200   its values has the potential to alter the original classification f (x); or, more formally, if there exists
201
      some z ∈ F for which f (xS̄ ; zS ) ̸= f (x). Similarly to sufficient reasons, smaller contrastive reasons
      are generally assumed to be more meaningful. These represent the minimum change required for
202
      changing the original prediction. Hence, it is also natural to focus on cardinally-minimal contrastive
203
      reasons.
204
205
206   MCR (Minimum Change Required):
207
      Input: Model f , input x, and d ∈ N.
      Output: Yes, if there exists some contrastive reason S such that |S| ≤ d for f (x), and No otherwise.
208

228   4     E NSEMBLE M ODELS A RE L ESS I NTERPRETABLE
229
230   We seek to compare the complexity of solving an explainability query Q for an ensemble, compared
231   to solving it for the ensemble’s constituent base models. We are particularly interested in cases
232   where there exists strict computational complexity gaps between the two settings (e.g., solving Q
233
      for a single base model can be performed in polynomial time, whereas solving it for the ensemble
      is NP-Complete). To identify these gaps, we use the notion of c-interpretability (computational
234
      interpretability) as defined in Barceló et al. (2020).:
235
236   Definition 1. Let C1 and C2 be two classes of models and let Q be an explainability query for which
237
      Q(C1 ) is in complexity class K1 and Q(C2 ) is in complexity class K2 . We say that C1 is strictly more
      c-interpretable than C2 with respect to Q iff Q(C2 ) is hard for the complexity class K2 and K1 ⊊ K2 .

277   4.2   N O C OMPLEXITY G AP IN C OMPLEX BASE M ODELS
278
279   We revealed a complexity gap between interpreting simple models (decision trees, linear models)
280   and ensembles comprised thereof. This raises the question of whether a similar complexity gap
281   is observed with more complex models, such as neural networks. Our findings indicate that this
282   disparity does not apply to neural networks. In fact, we establish a much stronger claim, confirming
283   this not only for the explainability queries we examined, but in fact for any explainability query whose
284
      complexity class remains consistent under polynomial reductions. This includes all K-Complete
      explainability queries, where K belongs to the polynomial hierarchy (classes such as PTIME, NP,
285
      ΣP2 , etc.), or their associated counting classes (#P, etc.).
286
287
      Proposition 3. There is no explainability query Q for which the class of MLPs is strictly more
      c-interpretable than the class of ensemble-MLPs.
288
289   The proof appears in Appendix G. The reasoning behind this outcome is straightforward — given
290   that an ensemble of MLPs can be reduced to a single MLP in polynomial time, it follows that there
291   do not exist two distinct complexity classes (that are closed under polynomial reductions) that differ
292   in complexity when it comes to interpreting a single model versus an ensemble of such models. To
293   extend this concept to models beyond MLPs, we define this characteristic and label it as: closed
294   under ensemble construction.
295   Definition 2. We say that a class of models C is closed under ensemble construction if given an
296   ensemble f containing models from C, we can construct in polynomial time a model g ∈ C for which
297   ∀x ∈ F, f (x) = g(x).
298
      Clearly, the aforementioned property correlates also to the expressiveness of the corresponding
299
      base-model, and does not hold for linear models and decision trees (assuming that P̸=NP).

303         I NTERPRETABILITY

305   Up until now, our analysis did not consider any problem-specific paramaters. For example, the
306   MCR query is polynomial-time solvable for a single decision tree, but becomes NP-Complete for
307   an ensemble of k models. This raises questions about how different parameters, such as the size of
308   the participating base-models and the number of base models, contribute to this effect. We explore
309
      these aspects by using parameterized complexity (Downey & Fellows (2012)), an important branch
      of computational complexity theory, to study how different parameters affect the entire complexity
310
      of interpreting ensembles. We begin by outlining some fundamental concepts in parameterized
311
      complexity, which are crucial for this study. In this field, we deal with two-dimensional instances
312   denoted as ⟨X , k⟩ where X represents the original encoding, and k is a specified parameter. We
313   briefly describe the three main scenarios in parameterized complexity.
314
315   1. Problems solvable in |X |O(1) · g(k) time. This is the best-case scenario concerning the parameter
316
      k, and includes problems that are fixed parameter tractable (FPT) concerning k. This implies that
317
      the problem can be solved in |X |O(1) · g(k) time for some computable function g, or intuitively —
      that the complexity can be primarily controlled by the parameter k. For instance, the Vertex-Cover
318
      problem is FPT when k is the vertex cover size. This means that if the size of the vertex cover is
319
      limited, the problem can be solved efficiently, even for arbitrarily large graphs.
320
321   2. Problems solvable in O(|X |g(k) ) time. This class encompasses problems within the complexity
322   class XP, solvable in O(|X |g(k) ) time. When k is fixed, these problems can be solved in polynomial
323   time. However, unlike in FPT, if |X | becomes very large, these problems may remain challenging
      even for relatively small values of k. For a fine-grained analysis of complexity classes within XP,

      1. Problems solvable in O(|X |k ) time. We begin by outlining the complexity results for decision

416
      Proposition 5. For ensembles of k-decision trees, (i) the CSR query is coW[1]-Complete; (ii) the
417
      MCR query is W[1]-Hard and in W[P]; (iii) the CC query is #W[1]-Complete; and (iv) the SHAP
418   query is #W [1]-Hard and in XP.
419
420   Since all these queries fall within XP, they can be solved in O(|X |g(k) ) time. However, given that
421   all previously mentioned queries are W[1]-hard or coW[1]-hard, it is generally accepted that there
422   are no FPT algorithms capable of solving them (Downey & Fellows (2012)). This leads us to the
423   understanding that if the number of base models k is constant, there exist polynomial algorithms that
424   solve the CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP queries for k ensembles of decision trees. However, assuming
425   that FPT ⊊ W[1], there are no algorithms that run in |X |O(1) · g(k) time that solve these queries.
426   Although these queries cannot be solved in FPT time under general conditions, if we assume that
427   the number of leaf nodes m in each tree is bounded by a constant (even if the total size |fi | of each
428   tree is arbitrarily large), it is possible to develop FPT algorithms to solve these queries. This follows
429   directly from the fact that all the aforementioned algorithms have a runtime of O(mk ). This leads to
430   the following conclusion: if the maximal number of leaves in each tree m is constant, there exist FPT
431   algorithms that solve the CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP queries for k-ensembles of decision trees (even
      if the size |fi | of each base-model, and hence the size of the ensemble |f |, is arbitrarily large).

455   and linear model ensembles exhibit very distinct behaviors when parameterized by the number of

457
      Theorem 2. k-ensemble-decision trees are strictly less c-interpretable than k-ensemble-linear models

460   From a practical viewpoint, we can interpret a decision tree ensemble in polynomial time if we
461   reduce the number of trees. On the other hand, interpreting ensembles of linear models proves to
462   be intractable, even if we reduce the number of base-models up to a constant. We highlight that the
463   hardness results apply also to heterogeneous ensembles. In other words, any ensemble that contains
464   some mixture of models, and contains a constant number of linear models, is intractable to interpret.
465   We further emphasize this polynomial vs. non-polynomial distinction with the following corollary:
466   Theorem 3. If the number k of base models is constant, there exist polynomial algorithms for solving
467   the CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP queries for k-ensembles of decision trees. However, assuming that
468   P ̸= NP, there are no polynomial algorithms for solving the CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP queries for
469   ensembles of linear models even when k is constant.

471
      Although the MSR query is NP-hard to solve for both a constant number of trees and a constant
      number of linear models, it is still possible to demonstrate a distinction between the two. While NP-
472
      complete problems are known to be challenging for optimization, they can sometimes be addressed
473
      using various optimization tools such as Boolean satisfiability (SAT) or satisfiability modulo theory
474   (SMT) solvers (Moskewicz et al. (2001); Barrett & Tinelli (2018)). In contrast, problems that are
475   hard for the intractable class ΣP
                                      2 require a worst-case exponential number of calls to an NP oracle.
476   Therefore, the difference between classifying a problem as NP or ΣP     2 -hard becomes crucial in this
477   context. In our context, assuming the number of base models k is constant, then solving the MSR
478   query for ensembles of decision trees is in NP. However, for ensembles of linear models, this problem
479   is ΣP
          2 -Hard, which demonstrates an additional complexity gap.
480   Finally, we observe that the algorithms designed to solve CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP for decision tree
481   ensembles operate within O(mk ) time (implying O(|X |k ) time), where m represents the maximum
482   number of leaves in any single tree of the ensemble. Thus, by limiting m to a constant, these queries
483   can be efficiently solved in FPT time for any k, even if the size of the tree is arbitrarily large. However,
484   when the size of a linear model is arbitrarily large, these tasks become NP-hard (or coNP-hard) to
485   solve, even if k is constant. This particularly implies that when assuming that the size of each linear
      model |fi | in an ensemble f is arbitrarily large, the CSR, MCR, CC, and SHAP queries are NP (or

491
492
      Our work relates to the field of formal XAI, which focuses on explanations with mathematical guar-
      antees (Marques-Silva et al. (2020)). Previous studies have discussed the computational complexity
493
      associated with obtaining such explanations for various ML models (Barceló et al. (2020); Wäldchen
494
      et al. (2021); Arenas et al. (2022)). In this paper, our primary focus was on understanding the com-
495   plexity of ensemble models and their impact on model interpretability. Some preliminary work has
496   yielded results on the complexity of generating certain types of interpretations for specific ensembles
497   (e.g., random forest classifiers) (Izza et al. (2021); Audemard et al. (2022; 2023)). Other studies have
498   investigated the use of parameterized complexity in the context of model interpretability. This ap-
499   proach has successfully highlighted distinctions between shallow and deep neural networks (Barceló
500   et al. (2020)) and enabled parameterization based on the size of explanations (Ordyniak et al. (2023)).
501   In more recent work, Ordyniak et al. (2024) analyzes the complexity of various logic-based reason-
502   ing queries (primarily local/global abductive and contrastive reasoning queries), parameterized by
503
      different problem parameters, including those related to ensembles. A comprehensive review of all
      prior computational complexity results relevant to this study is provided in the appendix.
504
505   Another line of work focuses on obtaining explanations with formal guarantees on tree ensembles
506   by encoding them as propositional logic formulas and then solving these queries with Boolean
507   satisfiability (SAT) (Izza & Marques-Silva), Maximum satisfiability (MaxSAT) (Ignatiev et al. (2022)),
508   Mixed integer linear programming (MILP) (Parmentier & Vidal (2021); Chen et al. (2019)) or
509
      satisfiability modulo (SMT) (Ignatiev et al. (2019c)) solvers.
510   In our work, we used certain terms that have sometimes been discussed in the literature under different
511   names. For instance, “sufficient reasons” have also been referred to as abductive explanations (Ignatiev
512   et al. (2019a)). Moreover, subset minimal sufficient reasons are related (though not exactly equivalent
513   to) prime implicants in the case of Boolean formulas (Darwiche & Marquis (2002)). Finally, a term
514   akin to the CC query can be found in the literature as the δ-relevant set (Wäldchen et al. (2021); Izza
515
      et al. (2021)). This term pertains to determining whether the completion count surpasses a predefined
      threshold δ.
516
517
518   7    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
519
520   Similarly to other studies on the computational complexity of obtaining explanations, our work is
521   limited to specific explanation forms and base-model types. Nevertheless, we believe our work still
522   offers a comprehensive overview of a variety of widely used explanation formats and settings, paving
523
      the way for future exploration of the complexity involved in obtaining additional forms. Furthermore,
      while most of our findings can be extended from classification to regression settings, a few specific
524
      results require further investigation to fully address the regression case. These considerations, along
525
      with other potential extensions and open problems related to our work, are discussed in Appendix D.

528   8    C ONCLUSION
529
530   We present a theoretical framework based on complexity theory to assess the interpretability of
531
      ensemble models. Our work provides mathematical evidence for the folklore belief: “ensembles are
      not interpretable”. However, we prove that a strict difference between base models and ensembles
532
      exists only in simple base models, such as linear models and decision trees, but not in complex
533
      base models, such as neural networks. We then propose a parameterized complexity view of these
534   results, and derive some unexpected conclusions. Intuitively, we show that reducing the size of
535   the base models within an ensemble cannot make it interpretable. However, limiting the number
536   of base models can. This is the case for tree ensembles, which are efficiently interpretable with a
537   reduced number of trees, but not for linear model ensembles, which are hard to interpret with even a
538   constant number of base-models. We believe that these results provide novel insights into ensemble
539   interpretability, and highlight the importance of considering computational complexity aspects for
      enriching our understanding of ML interpretability.

887   (i) FBDDs, (ii) Perceptrons, and (iii) MLPs .
888
889   Free Binary Decision Diagram (FBDD). (Lee (1959)) A binary decision diagram (BDD) is an
890   acyclic-directed graph and serves as a graphical model for a Boolean function f : F → {0, 1}. This
891   graph embodies the given Boolean function in the following manner: (i) Each internal node v is
892   associated with a unique binary input feature from the set {1, . . . , n}; (ii) Every internal node v has
893   precisely two outgoing edges, corresponding to the values {0, 1} which are assigned to v; (iii) In the
894   BDD, each variable is encountered no more than once on any given path α; (iv) Each leaf node is
895   labeled either True or False.
896   Thus, assigning a value to the inputs x ∈ F uniquely determines a specific path α from the root to a
897   leaf in the BDD. The function f (x) is assigned a 1 if the terminal node leaf is labeled True, and 0 if it
898   is labeled False. The size of the BDD, denoted as |f |, is measured by the total number of edges in
899   its graph. Our study focuses on the widely used Free Binary Decision Diagrams (FBDDs), which
900   permit different varying orderings of the input variables {1, . . . , n} across any two distinct paths, α
901   and α′ . This ensures that no two nodes along any single path α share the same label. Essentially, a
902   decision tree is a type of FBDD with a fundamental graph structure that is a tree.
903
904   Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). (Gardner & Dorling (1998); Ramchoun et al. (2016)) An MLP, de-
905
      noted by f , consists of t−1 hidden layers (g j where j ranges from 1 to t−1) and a single output layer
906
      (g t ). The layers are defined recursively — each layer g (j) is computed by applying the activation func-
907
      tion σ (j) to the linear combination of the outputs from the previous layer g (j−1) , the corresponding
908
      weight matrix W (j) , and the bias vector b(j) . This is represented as g (j) := σ (j) (g (j−1) W (j) + b(j) )
909   for each j in {1, . . . , t}. The model includes t weight matrices (W (1) , . . . , W (t) ), t bias vectors
910   (b(1) , . . . , b(t) ), and t activation functions (σ (1) , . . . , σ (t) ).
911   In the described MLP, the function f is defined to output f := g (t) . The initial input layer g (0) is
                                 n
912   denoted by x ∈ {0, 1} , which serves as the model’s input. The dimensions of the biases and weight
913   matrices are specified by the sequence of positive integers {d0 , . . . , dt }. We specifically focus on
914   weights and biases that are rational numbers, represented as W (j) ∈ Qdj−1 ×dj and b(j) ∈ Qdj , which
915   are parameters that are optimized during training. Given that the model is a binary classifier for
916   indices {1, . . . , n}, it follows that d0 = n and dt = 1. The primary activation function σ (i) that we

927   B     PARAMETERIZED C OMPLEXITY BACKGROUND
928
929   In parameterized complexity, we deal with parameterized problems L ⊆ Σ∗ × N where Σ is some
930   finite alphabet. The elements of the paramaterized problems are hence two-dimensional instances
931   denoted as ⟨X , k⟩ where X represents the original encoding and k is the parameter.
932
933   B.1   PARAMETERIZED R EDUCTIONS
934
935   FPT Reductions. The parameterized complexity classes that we will discuss here are closed under a
936   specific kind of reductions, known as fixed-parameter tractable (FPT) reductions. A given mapping
937   ϕ : Σ∗ × N → Σ∗ × N between instances from a parameterized problem P1 to another parameterized
938   problem P2 is an FPT reduction iff it holds that: (i) (X , k) is in P1 if and only if ϕ(X , k) is in P2 ;
939   (ii) there exists a computable function g for which k ′ ≤ g(k) when k ′ is the parameter of ϕ(X , k);
940   and (iii) ϕ(X , k) can be computed in |X |O(1) · g ′ (k) time for some computable function g ′ .
941   FPT Parsimonious Reductions. For the counting version of FPT reductions (Flum & Grohe (2004)),
942   given two paramterized counting problems F : Σ∗ × N → N, and G : Σ∗ × N → N we define an
943   FPT parsimonious reduction from F to G as an algorithm that computes for any instance ⟨X , k⟩ of F
944   an instance ⟨Y, ℓ⟩ of G in time g1 (k) · |X |c such that ℓ ≤ g2 (k) and F (X , k) = G(Y, ℓ), for some
945   computable functions g1 , g2 : N → N, and a constant c ∈ N.
946
947   B.2   PARAMETERIZED C OMPLEXITY C LASSES .
948
949   We now will formalize the parameterized complexity classes that are relevant for this work.
950
951   FPT. A problem is fixed parameter tractable (FPT) concerning k iff there exists a |X |O(1) · g(k)
952
      time algorithm solving the problem for some computable function g.
953   XP and the W-Hierarchy. The class XP describes all problems that can be solved in O(|X |g(k) )
954   time for some computable function g. This class additionally encompasses the W -hirerchy (Downey
955   & Fellows (2012)), which can be described using boolean circuits. We recall that a boolean circuit C
956   is represented as a rooted directed acyclic graph. Nodes with no incoming edges are referred to as
957   input gates, and the singular node without any outgoing edges is the output gate. The internal nodes
958   of the circuit are designated as OR, AND, or NOT gates. NOT gates are characterized by having
959
      exactly one incoming edge. AND and OR gates can have up to two incoming edges, termed small
      gates, or more than two, termed large gates. The depth of a circuit is measured by the longest path
960
      of edges from any input node to the output node. The weft of a circuit refers to the largest number
961
      of large gates on any path from an input node to the output node. An assignment for C maps the
962   input gates to binary values {0,1}. The hamming weight of an assignment reflects the count of input
963   gates assigned the value 1. This assignment determines the output at each gate based on its specific
964   function. A circuit is satisfied by an assignment if it results in the output gate producing a value of 1.
