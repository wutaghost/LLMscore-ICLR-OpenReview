009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces Hebbian Architecture Generation (HAG), a method
012            grounded in Hebbian plasticity principles, designed to optimize the structure of
013            Reservoir Computing networks. HAG adapts the synaptic weights in Recurrent
014            Neural Networks by dynamically forming connections between neurons that ex-
015            hibit high Pearson correlation. Unlike conventional reservoir computing models
016            that rely on static, randomly initialized connectivity matrices, HAG tailors the
017            reservoir architecture to specific tasks by autonomously optimizing network prop-
018
               erties such as signal decorrelation and singular value spread. This task-specific
               adaptability enhances the linear separability of input data, as supported by Cover’s
019
               theorem, which posits that increasing the dimensionality of the feature space im-
020
               proves pattern recognition. Experimental results show that HAG outperforms
021            traditional Echo State Networks across various predictive modeling and pattern
022            recognition benchmarks. By aligning with biological principles of structural plas-
023            ticity, HAG addresses limitations of static reservoir architectures, offering a bio-
024            logically plausible and highly adaptable alternative for improved performance in
025            dynamic learning environments.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In this paper, we introduce Hebbian Architecture Generation (HAG), a novel approach that dynam-
031   ically adjusts the synaptic weights in Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to improve the quality
032   of their representations. HAG is founded on Hebbian theory (Attneave et al., 1950), the princi-
033   ple that synaptic connections between co-activating neurons strengthen over time, encapsulated by
034
      the maxim “neurons that fire together wire together.” Leveraging this idea of topology informed
      by correlations, HAG dynamically constructs reservoir connectivity to better suit the task at hand,
035
      addressing several core challenges in Reservoir Computing (RC).
036
037   RC, particularly through Echo State Networks (ESNs) (Jaeger, 2001), provides a framework for
038   transforming input signals into high-dimensional dynamic states, as dictated by Cover’s theorem
039   (Cover, 1965). Cover’s theorem posits that increasing the dimensionality of a feature space enhances
040
      the likelihood that complex data patterns become linearly separable, which is crucial for efficient
      learning in RC. However, traditional ESNs, as highlighted by Jaeger (2005), face notable limitations
041
      in reservoir suitability, unsupervised adaptation, or alignment with biological principles.
042
043   HAG addresses these limitations in several ways:
044
045        1. Task-specific: beginning with a blank connectivity matrix, HAG forms dynamic synaptic
046
              connections, which inherently tailors the reservoir to the demands of specific tasks, moving
              beyond the random, static connectivity found in traditional ESNs.
047
048        2. Unsupervised adaptation: It autonomously optimizes network properties such as singu-
049           lar value spread and signal decorrelation by leveraging high Pearson correlations between
050           neurons, thereby enhancing the linearity of the feature space.
051        3. Performance predictors: By focusing on measurable properties such as signal decorrela-
052           tion and feature space expansion, we provide specific criteria for assessing and enhancing
053           reservoir performance. This approach moves beyond the tautological notion that a reservoir
              is suitable if it yields accurate models.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
            4. Biological insights: Reflecting the principles of Hebbian and structural plasticity, HAG
055            mimics the adaptability of biological neural networks by forming new connections and
056            reorganizing correlated input features to optimize feature extraction.
057
058   Our experimental results demonstrate that HAG not only addresses those key challenges, but also
059   improves performance across multiple benchmarks compared to traditional RC approaches.
060   This paper first presents the necessary background (section 2) for echo state networks. We then
061   introduce the HAG algorithm, the motivation behind this rule, and the main elements of our exper-
062   iments in Section 3. Section 4 then covers the significant performance improvements on several
063   benchmarks for two versions of the HAG algorithm compared to traditional RC. Finally, in section 5
064   we show that our algorithm projects input data into a higher-dimensional space which leads to better
065   performance according to Cover’s theorem.

082   In ESNs, the challenge of capturing nonlinear relationships in data is addressed by transforming the
083   time series input into a high-dimensional space using a dynamical system (the reservoir) which in
084   the case of ESNs is an artificial neural network of recurrently connected nodes. In this new feature
085
      space, the complex, nonlinear relationships may become linear, enabling the use of simple linear
      models, such as ridge regression, to accurately estimate the target variable. Notably, only the linear
086
      readout layer is trained, while the reservoir remains fixed, reducing computational complexity and
087
      enhancing training efficiency.
088
089   An illustration of the ESN framework (Lukoševičius & Jaeger, 2009) (Jaeger, 2001) is given in Fig.
090   1. It normally consists of a randomly initiated RNN of n neurons (the reservoir), and a trained
091
      readout layer that creates the outputs as a linear combination of the reservoir neuron states.
092   The evolution of the vector states x of the n neurons is determined by the interactions between the
093   connection matrix W, the input matrix Win , the input u[t] and the activation function σ that we fix
094   as the hyperbolic tangent function, combined in the following equation:
095                               x[t + 1] = σ(W × x[t] + Win × u[t] + b)                                (1)
096
      The purpose of the bias term b is to enrich the dynamics of the network, but is kept small through
097
      bias scaling to avoid dominating the system. b is a 1 × n vector and dimension of the matrices
098   are n × n for W and 1 × n for Win . In ESNs, these weights are typically randomly initialized
099   from a chosen distribution, and modulated by input scaling (sin ) and bias scaling (sb ) that are two
100   hyperparameters of the systems. However, as we explain below, mean-HAG uses a different weight
101   initialization scheme where these weights evolve during operation according to an unsupervised
102   rule.
103
      Once W, Win and b are fixed, the desired output is obtained through the following equation :
104
105
                                      y[t + 1] = Wout × x[t + 1] + bout                                  (2)
106   where Wout , bout are learned using ridge regression with regularization parameter λ to prevent
107   overfitting by controlling the size of the output such that the generated train output, y[t], optimally
      approximates a desired target output, y target [t] (Lukoševičius & Jaeger, 2009).

      3     M ETHODS
109
110
      While Lukoševičius & Jaeger (2009) gives an overview of other unsupervised methods used to
111   improve RC, a detailed overview of the effect of biological rules on the dynamics of RC is given
112   in Morales et al. (2021). Additionally, the self-organizing recurrent network (SORN) introduced by
113   Lazar (2009) focuses on spiking neurons and leverages multiple forms of plasticity to adapt a form of
114   spiking neural network, Cazalets & Dambre (2023) on structural plasticity in reservoirs with limited
115   results and Schrauwen et al. (2008) use synaptic plasticity in an unsupervised manner.
116
      Our approach differs from previously explored techniques such as Cazalets & Dambre (2023) or
117   Schrauwen et al. (2008), on two points. First, our algorithm takes inspiration from structural plas-
118   ticity and starts from a blank connectivity matrix, and is then able to create connections between
119   neurons that are not connected. Second, our algorithm is based on the Pearson correlation, which
120   allows us to recombine frequently correlated input features, transforming the data into a new feature
121   space with reduced correlation. In this transformed space, the input features are recombined into a
122   higher-dimensional space where patterns become as linearly independent as possible.
123
124   3.1   HAG ALGORITHM
125
126   We start from a blank echo state network with no connections except from the input connectivity.
127
      The HAG algorithm dynamically adjusts synaptic weights wij based on either variance or average
      of the activity of the reservoir’s neurons. Those two types of homeostatic mechanisms lead to two
128
      corresponding algorithms:
129
130         1. Mean Homeostasis Function (mean-HAG): Adjusts synaptic weights based on deviations
131            from a target mean activity rate.
132
            2. Variance Homeostasis Function (variance-HAG): Adjusts synaptic weights by compar-
133            ing the standard deviation of neuron states to a target standard deviation.
134
135   A more detailed explanation on the HAG algorithm is provided in Appendix B with pseudo-code in
136   B.1.
137
      Every T time steps, we calculate for each neuron a growth indicator:
138
139
                                                        1
140                                             ∆zi =     (si − ρ)                                       (3)
141
                                                        β
142
      where si represents either the average i-th neuron’s activity ⟨xi ⟩T over period T for mean-HAG,
143   or the i-th neuron’s standard deviation σxi ,T over period T for variance-HAG. We denote ρr as the
144   activity target value and βr as the rate spread (for mean-HAG), and ρv as the variance target and βv
145   as the variance spread (for variance-HAG).
146
      If ∆zi < −1, the neuron needs to increase its activity. In this case, one connection weight is
147
      increased by δw. The creation of new connections is restricted to neurons that have been identified
148
      as requiring additional connections. To choose which connection to increase, for every neuron that
149   has not yet achieved homeostasis, we compute pairwise Pearson correlation coefficients (Pearson,
150   1895) with every other neuron that is also not at homeostasis. We establish a connection with the
151   highest correlated neuron. Detailed description can be found in Appendix B.2.
152
      If ∆zi > 1, the neuron needs to decrease its activity. In this case, one connection weight is decreased
153
      by δw. Unlike the creation of new connections, the pruning of connections is performed randomly,
154
      independently of the state of the neuron’s partners, regardless of whether they also need to decrease
155   their activity or not.
156
157   The network is said to be at homeostasis if, for each neuron i, the absolute value of ∆zi is less than
158
      1 (i.e. si is between ρ − β and ρ + β). At this homeostasis the network maintains a desired level of
      variance or average in neuronal activity as seen in Figure 2a.
159
160   We impose a limit on the degree of the node, γ. By design, the RNN will only have positive
161   connections, which ensures predictable increases in neuronal activity. This configuration restricts
      the network to excitatory connections, which might limit the computational properties of the ESN.

181   3.1.1    E NSURING C ONVERGENCE
182
183   The mean-HAG algorithm dynamically adjusts neural connectivity based on deviations from target
184   mean activity rates. This ensures convergence during the learning phases as seen in Figure 2a.
185   In contrast, the variance-HAG algorithm aims to increase variability, which could inadvertently
186   amplify the overall signal strength and lead to potential saturation. To counteract this, we employ a
187   homeostatic plasticity mechanism, when a neuron’s activity level exceeds a saturation threshold θsat ,
188   the algorithm scales the weights by ηsat . This adjustment is crucial for maintaining network balance,
189   avoiding disruptive saturation, and ensuring the smooth convergence of the algorithm.

      3.1.2    M OTIVATION
193
194
195
      Reservoir Computing (RC) leverages the concept of transforming input data into a higher-
      dimensional space where complex patterns become more linearly separable. This idea is rooted
196
      in Cover’s theorem, which suggests that projecting data into a high-dimensional nonlinear space in-
197
      creases the likelihood of linear separability. Traditional Echo State Networks (ESNs) accomplish
198   this transformation using randomly instantiated reservoirs, a method described by Lukoševičius
199   (2012) as “the antithesis of the ’optimal.’”. Such random reservoirs fail to exploit the structure
200   of the input data, potentially limiting their effectiveness in separating complex patterns.
201
      Our Hebbian Architecture Generation (HAG) algorithm is motivated by the need to enhance this
202
      transformation process. By dynamically adjusting the reservoir’s connectivity based on neuron acti-
203
      vations, HAG aims to design a network structure that recombines the inputs to effectively increases
204   the dimensionality and richness of the feature space, a mechanism illustrated by Figure 2b.
205
206   Drawing inspiration from biological systems, we incorporate principles of structural plasticity ob-
207
      served in neural circuits, where neuronal activity leads to both growth and retraction of synaptic
      connections (Fauth & Tetzlaff, 2016; Cohan & Kater, 1986; Vaillant et al., 2002). Such plasticity
208
      mechanisms dynamically shape neural networks in response to stimuli, enhancing their adaptability.
209
      Similar mechanisms have been shown to drive transitions between dynamic states in biological net-
210   works due to changes in input strength, mediated by homeostatic plasticity (Zierenberg et al., 2018).
211   By integrating unsupervised, activity-dependent structural plasticity with minimal supervised ele-
212   ments, our approach enhances the efficiency and effectiveness of reservoir computing systems. This
213   aligns with the hypothesis proposed by Zador (2019), suggesting that biologically inspired adapta-
214   tions can achieve remarkable efficiency and performance with significantly less supervision com-
215   pared to conventional neural network models. These findings underscore the importance of adaptive
      connectivity in optimizing dynamic representations.

224   3.2.1    TASKS
225
226   The training of the ESN system occurs in two phases. Initially, for classification tasks such as speech
227   recognition, the reservoir processes input signals into high-dimensional representations, capturing
228   the dynamic state at the end of each input sequence. This state, representing the reservoir’s response
229   to the input, serves as the feature vector for training the readout layer using ridge regression to
230
      determine the optimal output weights Wout .
231   For prediction tasks aimed at forecasting future values from past inputs, we adopt a sequence-to-
232   sequence approach. The reservoir updates its state at each time step, using the entire sequence of
233   states to train the readout layer. Here, Wout is optimized to minimize the difference between the
234   predicted outputs and the actual targets at each time step.

294   3.3   P REPROCESSING
295
296   We adopt distinct preprocessing methods tailored to classification and prediction tasks, each lever-
297   aging the properties of audio signals to prepare data for analysis and model training.
298
      In the classification tasks, we preprocess the audio signals by converting them into a spectral rep-
299   resentations using Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs), computed with the librosa
300   library (McFee et al., 2015). MFCCs are widely used in speech and audio processing to capture the
301   timbral aspects of sound by modeling the human auditory system’s response (Davis & Mermelstein,
302   1980; Verstraeten et al., 2005). They provide a compact representation of the spectral properties of
303   audio signals, making them suitable for classification tasks. This process is illustrated in Figure 3.
304
      Conversely, for prediction tasks, the preprocessing utilizes band-pass filters to isolate specific fre-
305   quency bands from the signal. This approach starts with the identification of peak frequencies.
306   Frequency bands are then defined around these peaks by calculating the bandwidth as half the dis-
307   tance to adjacent peak frequencies. This method effectively segments the time series into parts that
308   contain relevant information for predictive modeling. The filtered signals obtained from this process
309   constitute the feature set used for forecasting future events or states from past time series. Com-
310   pared to the method for classification tasks, this method preserves the number of time steps in the
311   time series, which is essential to perform prediction tasks. This process is illustrated in Figure 4.
312   For each dataset, the network size is set to just above 500 units by duplicating the multivariate input
313   a specific number of times. For example, the SpokenArabicDigits dataset uses a network size of 507,
314   achieved by duplicating its 13-dimensional spectrogram 39 times. Similarly, the Japanese Vowels
315   dataset has a network size of 504, with a 12-dimensional spectrogram duplicated 42 times.

      set of features for downstream processing.

      dynamic richness using Pearson correlation and Cumulative Explained Variance (CEV). Detailed

      5.1   P EARSON CORRELATION
436
437   To elucidate the operational dynamics of our reservoir, we assess the correlation among neural states
438   using Pearson correlation (Pearson, 1895), which measures the linear relationship between neuron
439   activation states. This metric helps understand inter-neuronal connectivity and synchrony, directly
440   impacting the network’s ability to process complex data patterns. By computing Pearson correlation

524   6    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We presented a new algorithm generating a connectivity matrix by identifying and connecting highly
527   correlated neural nodes within a reservoir. This algorithm contrasts with traditional random matrix
528   instantiation by creating a connectivity pattern that enhances decorrelation of reservoir states. By
529   aligning the connectivity within the reservoir to the intrinsic correlations of the system, the proposed
530
      approach not only supports a new theoretical parallel with Hebbian plasticity but also demonstrates
      practical superiority over conventional methods.
531
532   The dynamic adaptability of HAG not only addresses the limitations of static reservoirs in Echo
533   State Networks but also showcases the practical application of biologically-inspired algorithms in
534   improving computational efficiency and task-specific performance. Our results underscore the utility
535   of biologically-inspired design principles in computational models, emphasizing the potential of
536
      Hebbian learning rules not just as a theoretical construct but as a practical tool in machine learning.
537   Future research should delve deeper into understanding the mechanisms that enable HAG to perform
538   well with complex datasets. Additionally, exploring the scalability of HAG in larger and more
539   intricate systems will further validate its effectiveness and contribute to the development of more
      intelligent and adaptable neural network models.

1118   D.3    I MPLICATIONS FOR R ESERVOIR D ESIGN
1119
1120   Despite these shortcomings, the HAG methods offer valuable insights into how adaptive connec-
1121   tivity can enhance reservoir performance. The ability to dynamically tailor the reservoir to specific
1122   tasks by reducing neuron correlations and expanding the feature space underscores the potential of
1123   biologically inspired design principles in neural network architecture.
1124   It is important to note that enriching the feature space (lower Pearson correlation and higher CEV)
1125   does not always translate into augmented performances across all tasks. This discrepancy may
1126   stem from the inherent differences between classification and prediction tasks. Classification tasks
1127   primarily benefit from enhanced feature separability, allowing for more accurate differentiation be-
1128   tween classes. In contrast, prediction tasks rely heavily on the reservoir’s ability to capture and retain
1129   temporal dependencies, which may not be directly enhanced by the structural adjustments made by
1130
       HAG.
1131   In conclusion, the Hebbian Architecture Generation (HAG) method presents a robust framework
1132   for enhancing reservoir computing, particularly in classification tasks, by leveraging biologically
1133   inspired adaptive connectivity. While its efficacy in prediction tasks is promising, it highlights
