009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper focuses on training a robust RGB-D registration model without ground-
012            truth pose supervision. Existing methods usually adopt a pairwise training strategy
013            based on differentiable rendering, which enforces the photometric and the geo-
014            metric consistency between the two registered frames as supervision. However,
015            this frame-to-frame framework suffers from poor multi-view consistency due to
016            factors such as lighting changes, geometry occlusion and reflective materials. In
017            this paper, we present F2M-Reg, a novel frame-to-model optimization framework
018
               for unsupervised RGB-D registration. Instead of frame-to-frame consistency, we
               leverage the neural implicit field as a global model of the scene and use the con-
019
               sistency between the input and the rerendered frames for pose optimization. This
020
               design can significantly improve the robustness in scenarios with poor multi-view
021            consistency and provides better learning signal for the registration model. Fur-
022            thermore, to facilitate the neural field optimization, we create a synthetic dataset,
023            Sim-RGBD, through a photo-realistic simulator to warm up the registration model.
024            By first training the registration model on Sim-RGBD and later unsupervisedly
025            fine-tuning on real data, our framework enables distilling the capability of feature
026            extraction and registration from simulation to reality. Our method outperforms the
027            state-of-the-art counterparts on two popular indoor RGB-D datasets, ScanNet and
028            3DMatch. Code and models will be released for paper reproduction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The difficulty of 3D data acquisition has significantly diminished owing to the substantial increase
      in RGB-D sensor availability and a concurrent decrease in costs. The prolific collection of RGB-
034
      D data has greatly propelled the advancement of deep learning in the field of 3D vision, resulting
035
      in substantial improvements in the performance of applications such as RGB-D SLAM and RGB-
036   D reconstruction. A pivotal challenge in achieving reliable 3D reconstruction based on discrete
037   RGB-D image frames lies in establishing correct inter-frame associations, through means such as
038   feature matching, to facilitate camera pose estimation. Motivated by these, our goal is to devise a
039   robust registration model for RGB-D images, thereby providing robust and high-quality pixel-level
040   matching for RGB-D registration.
041
      Traditional methods, relying on hand-crafted features (such as SIFT (Lowe, 2004)) find difficulty
042   in handling complex and noisy real-world data. Deep learning based methods, on the other hand,
043   have gained much attention lately and most works adopt a supervised learning approach (Qin et al.,
044   2022; Yu et al., 2021; 2023; Ao et al., 2021) to accomplish robust registration. These learning-based
045   methods can support RGB-D registration between frames even with very small overlaps. The per-
046   formance of supervised learning approaches, however, depends highly on the quality of the data
047   annotation, i.e., ground-truth frame poses, which are difficult to obtain and hence limit their appli-
048   cation in practice.
049   To overcome the reliance on annotated data in learning-based methods, the exploration of better
050   strategies to extract information from unlabeled data for achieving unsupervised learning in RGB-D
051   registration has gradually become a research focus. Inspired by works in multi-view geometry, stud-
052   ies have found that the geometric and photometric consistency inherent in the RGB-D sequences of
053   a scene can offer effective supervision for feature extraction. To our knowledge, UR&R(El Banani
      et al., 2021) is the first work proposing an unsupervised framework for RGB-D point cloud registra-

104

122   2.1   P OINT C LOUD R EGISTRATION
123
124   Point cloud registration is a problem of estimating the transformation matrix between two frames
125   of scanned point clouds. The key lies in how to detect features with specificity from the two-frame
126   point cloud. Since deep learning has been found good at feature representation, how to learn robust
127   and invariant visual features through deep learning networks has become a focus of research. (Wang
128
      et al., 2020; Qi et al., 2017a;b; Duan et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2022) Many Feature
      learning methods (Wang et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2018; Gojcic et al., 2019; Ao et al., 2021; Bai et al.,
129
      2020; Choy et al., 2020; 2019; Qin et al., 2022; Yew & Lee, 2022; Yu et al., 2023) were proposed.
130
      They get the point cloud features by neural network and use a robust estimator e.g. RANSAC to
131   estimate the rigid transformation. Different from focusing on feature learning, there are some end-
132   to-end learning-based registration methods (Yang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Elbaz et al., 2017;
133   Lu et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2020) that treat the registration as a regression problem. They encoded
134   the transformations into the implicit space as a parameter in the network optimization process.
135
136   2.2   U NSUPERVISED P OINT C LOUD R EGISTRATION
137
138   The aforementioned methods rely on ground-truth poses to supervised the training. The ground-
139   truth pose is often obtained by reconstruction of the SfM, which suffers from high computational
140
      overhead and instability. Recently, unsupervised RGB-D registration methods have been proposed
      to bypass the need of pose annotations. To our knowledge, UR&R(El Banani et al., 2021) is the first
141
      unsupervised registration framework by introducing a differentiable render-based loss to optimize
142
      the feature extractor. BYOC(El Banani & Johnson, 2021) stands for the fact that randomly initialized
143   CNNs also provide relatively good correspondences, proposed a teacher-student framework to train
144   their feature extractor. LLT(Wang et al., 2022) fused the geometric and visual information in a
145   more trivial way by introducing a multi-scale local linear transformation to fuse RGB and depth
146   modalities. PointMBF(Yuan et al., 2023) has designed a network based on unidirectional fusion to
147   better extract and fuse features from geometric and visual sources and has achieved state-of-the-art
148   performance. However, these methods have difficulty in handling multi-view inconsistency caused
149   by factors such as lighting changes, highlight or occlusion.
150
151   2.3   P OSE O PTIMIZATION IN N EURAL SLAM
152
153

      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Johari et al., 2023) incorporate neural implicit
154
      representations into RGB-D SLAM systems, allowing tracking and mapping from scratch. The
155
      groundbreaking work, iMAP (Sucar et al., 2021), encode both the color and geometry of the scene
156   into a MLP. This MLP can be jointly optimized with a batch of poses through rendering loss. In the
157   subsequent works, NICE-SLAM (Zhu et al., 2022) and Vox-Fusion (Yang et al., 2022) introduce a
158   hybrid representation that combines learnable grid-based features with a neural decoder, enabling
159   the utilization of local scene color and geometry to guide pose optimization. More recently, Mips-
160   fusion (Tang et al., 2023) proposed a robust and scalable RGB-D reconstruction system with a
161   multi-implicit-submap neural representation. Co-SLAM (Wang et al., 2023) proposed a joint coor-
      dinate and sparse-parametric encoding and a more global bundle adjustment approach. Inspired by

195   3.1      OVERVIEW
196
      Given two RGB-D frames X = (IX , X) and Y = (IY , Y), where IX , IY ∈ RH×W ×3 are the RGB
197                         X               Y

198
      images and X ∈ RN ×3 , Y ∈ RN ×3 are the point clouds backprojected from the corresponding
      depth images, our goal is to recover the 6-DoF relative pose T ∈ SE(3) between them, which
199
      consists of a 3D rotation R ∈ SO(3) and a 3D translation t ∈ R3 . To solve this problem, a
200
      learning-based registration model F first extracts point features and retrieves point correspondences
201   C = {(pi , qi ) | pi ∈ X, qi ∈ Y} via feature matching. The relative pose is then estimated based
202   on the correspondences. Obviously, the discriminativeness of the extracted features accounts for the
203   quality of the resultant relative pose. However, the training of F heavily relies on the ground-truth
204   pose T∗ = {R∗ , t∗ }, which suffers from great annotation difficulty and unstable convergence.
205
      In this work, we propose an unsupervised point cloud registration method named F2M-Reg. Our
206
      method leverages unposed RGB-D sequences to train the registration model F. We first describe our
207   registration model in Sec. 3.2. To achieve effective supervision, we generates high-quality relative
208   pose in a frame-to-model manner (Sec. 3.3). To inspire the parameters of F, we build a scene-level
209   synthetic dataset and pretrain F on this dataset so that reasonable initial features can be learned
210   (Sec. 3.4). Fig. 2 illustrates the overall pipeline of our method.
211
212   3.2      R EGISTRATION M ODEL
213
214   Our registration model F adopts a two-branch feature encoder, i.e., the visual branch and the geo-
215   metric branch, which fuses the information from both the visual (2D) and the geometric (3D) spaces
      for better feature distinctiveness, similar to PointMBF (Yuan et al., 2023). The visual branch is

217   ric branch is a KPFCN (Bai et al., 2020; Thomas et al., 2019) network symmetric with the visual
218   branch. Both branches adopt a three-stage architecture, and a PointNet-based fusion module fuses
219   the features from the two modalities after each stage. The point features of the two frames are,

225   3.3   U NSUPERVISED R EGISTRATION WITH F RAME - TO -M ODEL O PTIMIZATION
226
227   An unsupervised registration pipeline usually first gives a rough estimation of the pose, and then su-
228   pervise the registration model with the estimated pose. Obviously, the quality of the estimated pose
229   significantly affects the accuracy of the trained registration model. Existing methods (El Banani
230
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023) use differentiable rasterization and optimize the
      frame pose according to the photometric and the geometric consistency between two nearby frames
231
      from an RGB-D sequence. Nevertheless, the consistency between two frames are easily to be af-
232
      fected by occlusion or lighting changes under different viewpoints, which fails to effectively refine
233   the frame poses and thus harms the training of the registration model. This has inspired us that
234   a more comprehensive modeling of the whole scene is required to effectively optimize the frame
235   poses, i.e., frame-to-model optimization. Recently, neural implicit field (Mildenhall et al., 2021;
236   Wang et al., 2021) has the ability to model the lighting and geometric structures in a scene, and
237   jointly optimize 3D maps and poses (Zhu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). Based on this insight,
238   we propose to train the registration model scene by scene and optimize a neural implicit field as
239   a global model for each scene for better pose refinement. This allows to optimize the poses in a
240   frame-to-model fashion instead of the traditional frame-to-frame one, which can better handle the
241
      occlusion and lighting changes.
242   Training pipeline. As shown in Fig. 2, to avoid the error accumulation and the huge time overhead
243   caused by joint map-pose optimization in large scenes, we opt to process small subscenes instead
244   of the whole scene. Specifically, we split the RGB-D sequence of a scene into subsequences of 200
245   frames, and we optimize a neural implicit field M for each subsequence. Within each subsequence,
246   we further sample keyframes every 20 frames for training and all other frames are omitted. The
247
      reference frame of the first keyframe is treated as the global reference frame of the subscene. For
      each keyframe, we first register it with the previous keyframe with F to obtain its initial pose, and
248
      then insert it into M to jointly optimize its pose and the map. At last, we use the optimized pose of
249
      each keyframe to supervise the registration model.
250

317   3.4       S YNTHETIC WARM - UP
318
319   Jointly optimizing the neural implicit field M and the poses of keyframes requires relatively accu-
320   rate initial poses. However, a randomly initialized registration model tends to generate enormous
321   outlier correspondences. This causes the initial poses to be erroneous, and thus leads to suboptimal
322   convergence. To address this issue, we propose to leverage synthetic data to warm up the registra-
323   tion model. With the synthetic data, we can warm up the registration model in supervision by the
      ground-truth poses so that it can provide reasonable initial poses.


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Sim-RGBD dataset. To warm up the model training, we first construct a synthetic dataset us-
325   ing photo-realistic simulation with BlenderProc (Denninger et al., 2023), named Sim-RGBD. Sim-
326   RGBD consists of 90 scenes, which are split into 60 training scenes and 30 validation scenes.
327   Specifically, for each scene, we create two boxes centered at (0, 0, 0) in the sizes of respectively
328   10m × 10m × 5m and 6m × 6m × 3m, and uniformly select 400 positions in the space between them.
329   We then place a random object model from ShapeNet dataset (Chang et al., 2015) at each position,
330   which is randomly rotated, translated, and scaled.
331   After constructing the synthetic scenes, we render 400 pairs of RGB-D frames from each scene.
332   Another problem here is how to sample appropriate camera poses to ensure the synthetic pairs are
333   more realistic. To this end, we opt to first sample the pose of the source frame, and then sample the
334   relative pose between the source and the target frames. For the source pose, the camera direction is
335   determined by a random pitch angle between [15◦ , 75◦ ] and a random yaw angle between [0◦ , 360◦ ].
336   And the camera position is determined by a random distance between [0.7m, 1.5m] from (0, 0, 0)
337   along this direction. For the relative pose, we first randomly sample a rotation axis, and then sample
338   the rotation angle from N (20◦ , 15◦ ) and the translation from N (0.4m, 0.2m). As the two rendered
339
      frames could have little overlap, we only preserve the pairs with the overlap ratio above 0.3. As
      shown in Sec 4.3, the synthetic scenes simulated with this simplistic strategy effectively warm up
340
      the model.
341
342
343   Training settings. Similar to Sec. 3.3, we use the ground-truth poses to retrieve correspondences
344
      and apply the circle loss to train the registration model. Note that it is important to ensure the warm-
      ing up stage and the frame-to-model optimization stage to use the same training strategy. Otherwise,
345
      the two models could be in different feature spaces, thus harming the final performance.

358   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
359
360   Datasets. We conduct extensive experiments on two large real-world datasets, 3DMatch (Zeng
361   et al., 2017) and ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017), as well as a synthetic dataset, Sim-RGBD. For the
362   ScanNet dataset (Dai et al., 2017), we follow its original training/testing/validation split, dividing it
363   into 1045/312/100 scenes, respectively. Similarly, for the 3DMatch dataset (Zeng et al., 2017), we
364   adhere to its original split, dividing it into 71/19/11 scenes for training, testing, and validation. The
365   Sim-RGBD dataset is split into 60 scenes for training and 30 scenes for testing.
366   Metrics. We follow previous work (Yuan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022; El Banani et al., 2021)
367   and use rotation error, translation error, and chamfer error as our evaluation metrics. Each metric is
368   reported with three different thresholds, along with both mean and median values. Additionally, we
369   adopt the settings from (El Banani et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023) to generate
370   view pairs by sampling image pairs that are 20 frames apart. We also evaluate view pairs sampled
371   50 frames apart.
372   Baseline Methods. We compare our method with baselines from three categories, (1) traditional
373   methods: ICP (Besl & McKay, 1992), FPFH (Rusu et al., 2009) SIFT (Lowe, 2004), (2) learning-
374   based supervised methods: SuperPoint (DeTone et al., 2018), FCGF(Choy et al., 2019), DGR (Choy
375   et al., 2020), 3D MV Reg (Gojcic et al., 2020) and REGTR (Yew & Lee, 2022), and (3) unsupervised
376   methods: UR&R (El Banani et al., 2021), BYOC (El Banani & Johnson, 2021), LLT (Wang et al.,
377   2022) and PointMBF (Yuan et al., 2023). We use the results of the baselines reported by (Wang
      et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023).

517   5     C ONCLUSION
518
519   We present F2M-Reg, a frame-to-model optimization framework for unsupervised RGB-D registra-
520   tion. By constructing a neural implicit field and enforcing photometric and geometric consistency
521   between input and rendered frames, we optimize the estimated poses from registration model. This
522   design significantly enhances robustness against lighting changes, geometric occlusions, and reflec-
523   tive materials. Additionally, We implement a warming up mechanism on a synthetic dataset to ini-
524
      tialize neural field optimization effectively. Extensive experiments on two benchmarks demonstrate
      effectiveness of our methods. We believe the unsupervised learning framework with frame-to-model
525
      optimization holds promise for future 3D vision tasks like localization and reconstruction.
526
527
528   6     L IMITATION
529
530   Despite achieving state-of-the-art performance, our method has some limitations. First, it cannot be
531   directly applied to outdoor scenes. Excessive depth variations between foreground and background
532   can lead to incorrect subscene construction. Also, encoding a large open scene within a single neural
533   implicit field poses significant challenges. Second, the method requires a relatively long training
534   time due to the optimization of the neural field.

      B.1   N EURAL IMPLICIT FIELD OPTIMIZATION
705
706   For each sequence, we initially conduct 200 iterations of mapping using the first frame to establish
707   the initialization of the neural implicit field. Subsequently, during the training phase, we input
708   both the current frame and the neural implicit field M. We maintain M fixed while optimizing
709   the camera parameters of the untracked pose. Specifically, we randomly select Nt = 1024 pixels
710   from the current frame. For each ray, we uniformly sample Mc = 32 points between the near
711   and far bounds. Additionally, we sample an extra Mf = 21 depth-guided points evenly within
712   the range [d − ds , d + ds ], where d represents the depth and ds = 0.25 denotes a small offset.
713   During experimentation, it was noted that employing the same number of optimization rounds as
714
      in Co-SLAM (Wang et al., 2023) often yielded suboptimal untracked poses across most scenes.
      This challenge arises due to the larger inter-frame distances present in our data compared to scenes
715
      encountered in previous Neural SLAM tasks. Hence, we conduct 100 iterations for tracking to
716
      mitigate this issue.
717
718   Subsequently, the tracked pose is utilized in the mapping stage. Upon the initial addition of each
719   frame to the mapping stage, 5% of its pixels are incorporated into the maintained pixel bank. During
720
      the mapping phase, 2048 pixels are randomly selected from the pixel bank, and rays are generated
      to participate in the training process. The subsequent procedure mirrors that of the tracking section,
721

      and the camera poses in the batch.

      Supervisory signals for the feature extractor are generated when the current batch is fixed. These
779

      signals are generated by optimized pose (T̂i , T̂j ), corresponding point clouds (Xi , Xj ), and corre-

782

784   and corresponding feature pairs {(F pi−1 , F pi ) | (pi−1 , pi ) ∈ C ∗ } are utilized to compute the loss.

788
789   Given 512 input correspondences, C = {(pi , qi ) | pi ∈ X, qi ∈ Y}, we randomly sample t =
790
      10 subsets, each containing l = 20% of the total correspondences. For each subset, a candidate
      transformation T is estimated by solving a Weighted Procrustes problem (Besl & McKay, 1992).
791
      The candidate transformation T ∗ that minimizes the error E(C, T ∗ ) is retained. Additionally, during
792
      the testing phase, we increase t to 100 and reduce l to 5% to achieve better RANSAC results while
793   limiting computational costs.

882   C.2    C OMPARISON WITH SUPERVISED LEARNING BASED ON SLAM.
883
884   For sequential data, it is intuitive to obtain the pose of each frame quickly through reconstruction
885   pipelines like SLAM. The pose reconstructed by SLAM can be used to supervise the training of a
886
      registration model. However, our approach can further enhance the performance of a registration
      model trained on SLAM-reconstructed poses. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our optimization
887
      framework, we designed this ablation experiment. We selected ROSEFusion (Zhang et al., 2021) as
888
      the SLAM algorithm for the experiment.
889
890   We randomly divide the 1,045 ScanNet training scenes into two groups: 300 scenes and 745 scenes.
891   Using ROSEFusion, we perform the reconstruction on the 300 scenes, and the resulting poses were
892
      used to train a registration model from scratch. We then fine-tune the registration model using our
      frame-to-model optimization framework on the next 745 scenes. Finally, we test all the registation
893
      model on ScanNet testing scenes.
894
895   The results, as shown in Table 9, indicate that the performance of the registration model improves
896   as the number of training scenes increases, demonstrating the optimization strength of our method.
897   Notably, our bootstrapping module is flexible and can accommodate different datasets. When the
898
      quality of the synthetic dataset or the accuracy of the reconstruction from real-world scenes im-
      proves, these factors contribute to a better initialization of the registration model. Given a highly
899
      expressive model, our framework can leverage these improvements to provide a stronger initializa-
900
      tion, further enhancing the registration model’s performance.
