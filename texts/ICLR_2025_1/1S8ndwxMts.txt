010
011
012
               The rapid advancement of protein generative models necessitates robust and princi-
               pled methods for their evaluation and comparison. As new models of increasing
013
               complexity continue to emerge, it is crucial to ensure that the metrics used for
014
               assessment are well-understood and reliable. In this work, we conduct a systematic
015            investigation of commonly used metrics for evaluating protein generative models,
016            focusing on quality, diversity, and distributional similarity. We examine the be-
017            havior of these metrics under various conditions, including synthetic perturbations
018            and real-world generative models. Our analysis explores different design choices,
019            parameters, and underlying representation models, revealing how these factors
020            influence metric performance. We identify several challenges in applying these
021            metrics, such as sample size dependencies, sensitivity to data distribution shifts, and
022            computational efficiency trade-offs. By testing metrics on both synthetic datasets
023
               with controlled properties and outputs from state-of-the-art protein generators, we
               provide insights into each metric’s strengths, limitations, and practical applicability.
024
               Based on our findings, we offer a set of practical recommendations for researchers
025
               to consider when evaluating protein generative models, aiming to contribute to the
026            development of more robust and meaningful evaluation practices in the field of
027            protein design.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The field of protein generative modeling has witnessed significant progress in recent years, fueled
      by advancements in machine learning and artificial intelligence Wu et al. (2021); Ovchinnikov &
034
      Huang (2021). Various approaches, including language model-based architectures, GANs, VAEs, and
035
      diffusion models, have been proposed and successfully applied to generate novel protein sequences
036   Ferruz et al. (2022); Madani et al. (2023); Shin et al. (2021); Repecka et al. (2021); Sevgen et al.
037   (2023); Lin & AlQuraishi (2023); Watson et al. (2023); Alamdari et al. (2023); ?. Several studies have
038   demonstrated the potential of these models to produce functional proteins that have been validated
039   experimentally in the lab, suggesting that generative models have much to offer in the realm of protein
040   design.
041
      Despite these achievements, the evaluation of protein generative models remains a challenge. Unlike
042   other domains such as image or text generation, where well-established evaluation metrics exist,
043   the protein design field lacks a standardized and comprehensive set of metrics Joshua Southern &
044   Correia (2023). As a result, many studies resort to developing their own ad hoc metrics, leading to
045   inconsistencies and difficulties in comparing results across different models and methods. Moreover,
046   the very question of what constitutes a ”good” protein is not trivial, as it involves multiple dimensions
047   such as foldability, structural similarity to natural proteins, and functional relevance.
048   In this work, we address this gap by defining the desired properties of both sequence protein
049   generative models and the metrics used to evaluate them. We study a set of evaluation metrics
050   for assessing protein quality, diversity, and distributional similarity between generated and natural
051   proteins. We examine the sensitivity of these metrics to different types of data perturbations, explore
052   their interrelationships and underlying assumptions, and evaluate their computational efficiency.
053   Our findings reveal potential pitfalls and weaknesses in current evaluation practices and we provide
      researchers with practical recommendations to address these issues.

      2.1 A PPROACHES TO E VALUATING G ENERATIVE M ODELS
057
058   Evaluating generative models presents unique challenges compared to their discriminative coun-
059   terparts. Unlike discriminative models P (L|X), where we can directly assess performance using
060
      labeled data {Xi , Li }, generative models P (X) require more nuanced evaluation techniques. These
      techniques typically fall into two categories, depending on the available data: scenarios where we
061
      have only generated samples {Yi }, and those where we also have access to the training data {Xi }.
062
063   In the absence of training data, evaluation focuses primarily on the quality and diversity of the
064   generated samples. Quality assessment examines how well the generated samples adhere to desired
065   characteristics, while diversity measures the variability within the generated set. These properties
066
      are often evaluated using predefined functions or oracle models that capture domain-specific criteria.
      When training data is available, we can extend our evaluation to include fidelity and coverage.
067
      Fidelity measures the degree to which generated samples resemble real data, while coverage assesses
068
      whether the generated samples span the full variability of the real data distribution. Researchers
069   have developed various approaches to quantify these properties, including density and coverage
070   metrics Kynkäänniemi et al. (2019); Naeem et al. (2020), Fréchet Distance Heusel et al. (2017), and
071   Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) Gretton et al. (2012).
072
      To effectively measure the quality of generated protein samples, we must establish a framework for
073
      what constitutes a ”good” protein. Recent advancements in machine learning for proteins have yielded
074
      models capable of both protein folding and inverse folding, allowing us to define a bidirectional
075   mapping between sequence and structure spaces:
076
077                                       f : S → T (folding function)
078                                   g : T → S (inverse folding function)
079   where S represents the space of protein sequences and T the space of 3D structures.
080   This bidirectional mapping provides a powerful tool for assessing the quality of generated proteins.
081   We propose that a ”good” protein should exhibit the following properties:
082
083
      1) Structural stability: For a generated sequence s, the predicted structure f (s) should be energeti-
      cally favorable and stable, as measured by established biophysical metrics. For a generated structure
084
      t, it should directly exhibit these favorable properties.
085
086   2) Self-consistency: For a generated protein x, which could be either a sequence s ∈ S or a structure
087   t ∈ T , the composition of the folding and inverse folding functions should approximately recover the
088   original input:
089
                                              ∥x − (h ◦ k)(x)∥ < ϵ
090   where (h, k) = (g, f ) if x ∈ S, and (h, k) = (f, g) if x ∈ T , and ϵ is a small threshold. This property
091   ensures that the generated proteins are consistent with our understanding of the sequence-structure
092   relationship, regardless of whether the model generates sequences or structures.
093   These properties ensure that individual generated proteins are physically plausible and consistent
094   with our understanding of the sequence-structure relationship in proteins.
095
096   2.2 E VALUATING M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
097
      When evaluating protein generative models, researchers typically examine both the quality of indi-
098
      vidual generated proteins and the overall performance of the model. While there is no universally
099
      agreed-upon approach, several aspects are often considered important:
100
101   1) Fidelity: The degree to which generated proteins resemble those in the training data, measured
102   through various similarity metrics in both sequence and structure space.
103   2) Diversity: The variability within the set of generated proteins ensures that the model is not simply
104   memorizing the training data.
105
      3) Novelty: The model’s ability to generate proteins similar to, but not identical to, known proteins.
106
107   We can either compute these metrics directly or use distributional similarity metrics, like Frechet
      distance or MMD, that implicitly account for these properties.

      2.3   D ESIRABLE P ROPERTIES OF E VALUATION M ETRICS
109
110   When assessing protein generative models, the choice of evaluation metrics is crucial. Regardless
111
      of the specific metrics employed, certain properties are generally desirable. These properties help
      ensure that our assessments are meaningful, practical, and informative:
112
113   1) Robustness and Sensitivity: Metrics should strike a balance between robustness to noise and
114   sensitivity to meaningful differences in model performance. They should be resilient to small, random
115   perturbations in the data or model outputs, ensuring that the evaluations are reliable and not overly
116   influenced by the stochastic nature of generative models. Simultaneously, these metrics should remain
117
      responsive to significant improvements or differences between models.
118   2) Interpretability: Metrics should provide insights into model performance, allowing researchers to
119   identify specific areas for improvement.
120
      3) Computational Efficiency: Evaluation metrics should be computationally tractable, especially
121   when monitoring during model training. The efficiency of a metric depends on both the algorithm
122   itself and its sensitivity to sample size.
123
124
      These properties are not absolute requirements, but rather guiding principles. The relative importance
      of each may vary depending on the specific research context and goals. In our subsequent discussion
125
      of experiments, we will explore how various metrics align with these desirable properties and their
126
      effectiveness in evaluating protein generative models.

128   3 M ETRICS
129   Building upon the concepts of protein quality and generative model evaluation, we examine specific
130   metrics used in assessing protein generative models. These metrics fall into three categories: quality
131   metrics, which evaluate individual generated proteins; diversity metrics, which measure variability
132   within the generated set; and distributional similarity metrics, which compare generated and natural
133   protein distributions. Here, we outline the studied metrics; for a detailed description of each metric,
134   refer to Appendix A.
135   3.1   Q UALITY M ETRICS
136
      Quality metrics assess the characteristics of individual generated proteins. We examine four widely
137   used metrics: pLDDT, perplexity, pseudoperplexity, and scPerplexity. These metrics evaluate protein
138   quality from perspectives of structural stability and sequence plausibility.
139
140
      The predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) is widely used in protein structure predic-
      tion and has become a standard for evaluating the quality of generated proteins Jumper et al. (2021);
141
      Watson et al. (2023); Alamdari et al. (2023). AlphaFoldJumper et al. (2021); Ferruz et al. (2022);
142
      Nijkamp et al. (2023), ESMFoldLin et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Hayes et al.
143   (2024) and OmegaFoldWu et al. (2022); Alamdari et al. (2023); Lv et al. (2024) are commonly used
144   for pLDDT prediction. We investigate the impact of model choice and sample size on pLDDT-based
145   quality evaluation.
146
      Adapted from language modeling, perplexity (ppl) evaluates sequence quality Madani et al. (2023);
147
      Repecka et al. (2021). Perplexity calculations often employ autoregressive transformer protein
148
      language models such as ProtGPT2 Ferruz et al. (2022), ProGen2 Nijkamp et al. (2023), and RITA
149   Hesslow et al. (2022). We have found that the perplexity values between these models are highly
150   correlated (R2 > 0.92). Considering this, we use ProGen2-base model for calculating perplexity in
151   this work.
152
      Pseudoperplexity (pppl) is an adaptation of perplexity for masked language models Salazar et al.
153
      (2019); Lin et al. (2023). The ESM-2 family of bidirectional transformer protein language models
154
      is frequently used for pseudoperplexity calculations Lin et al. (2023). We examine the influence of
155   model size (Figure 8) and sample size (Figure 2) on pppl calculations.
156
157   Self-consistency perplexity (scPerplexity) Alamdari et al. (2023) leverages the sequence-structure
158
      relationship: scPerplexity(S) = − log p(S|G(F (S))), where F is a folding model and G is an
      inverse folding model. Lower scPerplexity suggests better alignment between the generated sequence
159
      and its predicted structure.
160
161   While these metrics provide valuable insights, they each have limitations. pLDDT may underestimate
      the quality of proteins with intrinsically disordered regions. Perplexity and pseudoperplexity might


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      be misleading for low-complexity sequences. scPerplexity, while comprehensive, is computationally
163   expensive and depends on the accuracy of both folding and inverse folding models. In the following
164   sections, we empirically evaluate these metrics, assessing their sensitivity, robustness, and correlation.
165   This analysis aims to provide a more nuanced understanding of their strengths and weaknesses in the
166   context of protein generative model evaluation.

      3.2 D IVERSITY M ETRICS
169   Evaluating the diversity of generated protein sequences without reference to training data is non-
170   trivial, yet crucial task in assessing generative model performance. While some studies use average
171   pairwise distances between generated sequences as a diversity measure Watson et al. (2023); Wang
172
      et al. (2024); Hayes et al. (2024), this approach often lacks discriminative power. More informative
      methods employ clustering techniques to analyze sample diversity Lin et al. (2024); Huguet et al.
173
      (2024). In this work, we utilize Cluster Density (CD) as a diversity metric. CD is defined as the
174
      ratio of the number of clusters to the total number of sequences being clustered. We use MMseqs2
175   Steinegger & Söding (2017) for clustering at 50% and 95% similarity thresholds. The 50% threshold
176   provides insight into the general cluster structure, capturing broader diversity patterns. In contrast,
177   the 95% threshold is sensitive to potential mode collapse scenarios, where a model generates nearly
178   identical sequences.
179
180   3.3 D ISTRIBUTIONAL S IMILARITY M ETRICS
181   When evaluating protein generative models with access to training data, we can assess the distri-
182   butional similarity between generated and real samples. This comparison provides insights into
183   two crucial aspects of model performance: fidelity and diversity Naeem et al. (2020). Fidelity
184
      measures the extent to which generated samples accurately represent the characteristics of the real
      data distribution, typically quantified by the proportion of generated samples that closely resemble
185
      real samples. Diversity, on the other hand, assesses the model’s ability to capture the full range of
186
      variation in the real data, often measured by the proportion of real data samples with close analogs in
187   the generated set.
188
189   Two main approaches to quantifying distributional similarity are direct measurement of fidelity and
190
      diversity and compound metrics that implicitly account for both. Both approaches typically operate
      on distributions of protein vector representations, either sequence-based (using protein language
191
      models) or structure-based (employing 3D-protein encoders).
192
193   Direct measurement approaches include Improved Precision and Recall (IPR) and Density and
194   Coverage (D&C). IPR, introduced by Kynkäänniemi et al. (2019), operates in a high-dimensional
195   feature space, quantifying both the quality and diversity of generated samples. It defines precision as
196
      the proportion of generated samples that closely resemble real samples, and recall as the proportion
      of real samples that have close analogues in the generated set. Complementing IPR, the D&C
197
      metric, proposed by Naeem et al. (2020), offers a more intuitive interpretation of similar concepts.
198
      Density measures the proportion of generated samples that fall within the manifold of real data,
199   while coverage assesses the proportion of the real data manifold that is represented by the generated
200   samples. Together, these metrics offer a comprehensive evaluation of a generative model’s output,
201   balancing the critical aspects of sample realism and distributional coverage.
202
      Compound metrics implicitly account for both fidelity and diversity, and are more widely used in
203
      practice. These include Fréchet distance, Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), and Earth Mover’s
204
      Distance (EMD). The Fréchet distance quantifies dissimilarity
                                                                  √ between two multivariate Gaussian
205   distributions d(X1 , X2 )2 = ||µ1 − µ2 ||2 + Tr(Σ1 + Σ2 − 2 Σ1 Σ2 ), for samples X1 ∼ N (µ1 , Σ1 )
206   and X2 ∼ N (µ2 , Σ2 ). The Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) Heusel et al. (2017), a variant of this
207   metric, is well-established in image generation tasks. In the context of protein modeling, a similar
208   approach using the ProtT5 Alamdari et al. (2023) or ESM-1v Darmawan et al. (2023) encoder has
209   been applied to protein sequence data.
210
      Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) Gretton et al. (2012) measures the distance between two
211   distributions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. For two samples X = x1 , ..., xn and Y =
212   y1 , ..., yn and a kernel k, the empirical estimate of MMD is:

      Our experimental framework aims to provide a comprehensive evaluation of metrics used in assessing
      protein generative models. We focus on three key aspects: quality metrics, diversity metrics, and

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
232
233   To evaluate the behavior and sensitivity of the studied metrics, we employ both synthetic datasets that
234   provide controlled experimental conditions and real-world generated data. Our experimental setting
235   consists of two complementary approaches with synthetic data designed to controllably assess the
236
      metrics of quality and diversity.
237   The first set of experiments focuses on simulating the training progress of generative models. Using the
238   SwissProt dataset, a manually curated collection of high-quality protein sequences, as our reference,
239   we introduce controlled perturbations by randomly substituting amino acids while preserving the
240   overall amino acid distribution. Noise levels range from 0% to 30% in 5% increments. This approach
241   allows us to isolate the impact of sequence quality on our metrics and assess their sensitivity to
242
      varying degrees of model undertraining.
243   Our second experimental setup evaluates metrics for diversity and distributional similarity. We
244   construct a dataset of sequences from five distinct protein families chosen to naturally form well-
245   defined clusters. This design uses the fact that proteins within a family are more closely related to
246   each other than to members of other families.
247

491   4.5 C OMPARATIVE A NALYSIS AND P RACTICAL R ECOMMENDATIONS
492   Our comprehensive evaluation of quality, diversity, and distributional similarity metrics yields several
493   key insights for assessing protein generative models:
494
      Quality Metrics For quality assessment, we recommend combining pLDDT and perplexity or
495
      scPerplexity alone. While pLDDT and perplexity individually have vulnerabilities (low pLDDT in
496   naturally disordered regions, low perplexity in repetitive sequences), their combination provides a
497   balanced assessment. ScPerplexity mitigates these weaknesses but incurs higher computational costs
498   due to its two-stage procedure.
499
500
      Sample Size Considerations Sample size significantly impacts metric stability. We recommend
      a minimum of 256 samples for quality metrics, with 512 samples offering more robust estimates,
501
      particularly for scPerplexity. Cluster Density calculations stabilize with 500-1000 samples. For
502
      distributional similarity metrics, we advise using at least 1024 samples to ensure stable estimates.
503
504   Choice of Models Our analysis of protein language models (ESM-2 8M, 650M, 3B, and ProtT5)
505   reveals that model choice minimally impacts trends in distributional similarity metrics. This finding
506   suggests that smaller, computationally efficient models often suffice without compromising evaluation
507
      accuracy. For structure prediction in quality metrics, ESMFold offers an optimal balance between
      accuracy and efficiency, making it suitable for large-scale evaluations and real-time assessment during
508
      model training.
509
510   Metric Selection We recommend focusing on MMD with RBF kernel (σ = 10) and Fréchet
511   Distance (FD) for distributional similarity assessment. These metrics offer a favorable balance
512   between computational efficiency and reliability, especially within the sample size ranges typical for
513   protein evaluations. The choice of metrics should be guided by the specific failure modes researchers
514
      aim to detect. Cluster Density at 50% threshold effectively detects mode collapse, while MMD and
      FD are sensitive to overall distributional changes. Cluster Density at 95% threshold can identify lack
515
      of fine-grained diversity within protein families.
516
517   Computational Efficiency For rapid evaluation during model development, we recommend using
518   perplexity for quality and MMD (with optimized σ) for distributional similarity. For comprehensive
519   final evaluation, a combination of pLDDT with perplexity, or scPerplexity, Cluster Density, and MMD
520   or Fréchet Distance provides a thorough assessment of model performance. These guidelines aim to
521
      balance metric stability, computational efficiency, and comprehensive model evaluation, providing a
      robust framework for assessing protein generative models across various scenarios and computational
522
      constraints.

525
526   This study presents a comprehensive analysis of evaluation metrics for protein generative models.
527   We demonstrate that combining quality, diversity, and distributional similarity metrics provides the
528   most robust assessment of generated proteins. Our findings establish practical guidelines for metric
529
      selection and sample size determination, balancing accuracy with computational efficiency. These
      insights contribute to more standardized and meaningful evaluation practices in protein design and
530
      generation. As the field advances, continued refinement of these evaluation methods will be crucial
531
      for guiding the development of increasingly sophisticated protein generative models.
