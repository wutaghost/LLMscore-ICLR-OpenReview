009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Saliency maps are commonly employed as a post-hoc method to explain the
012            decision-making processes of Deep Learning models. Despite their widespread
013            use, ensuring the fidelity of saliency maps is challenging due to the absence of
014            ground truth. Researchers, therefore, have developed fidelity metrics to evalu-
015            ate the fidelity of saliency maps. However, prior investigations have uncovered
016
               statistical inconsistencies in existing fidelity metrics using multiple perturbation
               techniques without delving into the underlying causes. Our study aims to explore
017
               the origins of these observed inconsistencies by examining the existing fidelity
018
               metrics and demonstrating why they are inconsistent. We use different types of
019            perturbations and study multiple models across different datasets. We propose
020            two conformity measures to examine the validity of the assumptions made by the
021            existing fidelity metrics. Our findings reveal that the assumptions made by the
022            existing fidelity metrics do not always hold, making them inconsistent and unre-
023            liable. Thus, we recommend a cautious interpretation of fidelity metrics and the
024            choice of perturbation technique when evaluating the fidelity of saliency maps in
025            eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) applications.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
      Deep learning (DL) models, while providing high performance and accuracy for various applica-
030
      tions, come at the cost of decreased transparency. In many critical domains such as health care,
031   insurance, and law enforcement, concerns about the transparency, fairness, privacy, and trustwor-
032   thiness of AI applications arise due to the black-box nature of deep learning models (Rudin, 2019;
033   Jacovi et al., 2021; Arrieta et al., 2020). These concerns have led to discussions about adopting the
034   latest Artificial Intelligence (AI) models in various sectors (Cubric, 2020; Cam et al., 2019; Güngör,
035   2020). Therefore, a great deal of research has been dedicated to explaining the decisions of AI sys-
036   tems under the umbrella of XAI (Arrieta et al., 2020; Selvaraju et al., 2017; Chattopadhay et al.,
037   2018; Zhou et al., 2016; Ramaswamy et al., 2020; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Broniatowski et al., 2021;
038   Lundberg & Lee, 2017).
039   Saliency maps (e.g., Class Activation Maps (CAM)) are widely used as a mode to explain the de-
040   cision of DL models (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Chattopadhay et al., 2018). Disagreements can be
041   observed among saliency maps generated using different methods for the same model and the same
042   image, making a user choice difficult. One can choose the best saliency map with the highest fidelity
043   when compared to ground truth. However, the absence of actual ground-truth1 . Fidelity metrics such
044   as ”Area Over the Perturbation Curve” (AOP C) (Samek et al., 2016), Average Drop (AD%), In-
045
      crease in Confidence (IC%) and Win (W%) (Chattopadhay et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020) and
      ”faithfulness” metric (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018) have been used to measure the fidelity of
046
      saliency maps (Samek et al., 2016; Bach et al., 2015; Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018).
047
048   These fidelity metrics, however, suffer from inconsistencies and thus make them unreliable (Tomsett
049   et al., 2020). Fidelity metrics such as AOP C (Samek et al., 2016), AD%, IC% and W% (Chattopad-
050   hay et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020) and f aithf ulness (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018) rely on
051       1
            Human annotation typically focuses on features that make sense from a human perspective (e.g., edges in
052   images), while DL models rely on patterns that are not easily interpretable. Human-annotated saliency maps
053   may misrepresent the model’s true decision-making process, making them unreliable for evaluating the fidelity
      of the maps.

054
      computing pixel importance rank (PIR) for measuring the fidelity of saliency maps. PIR is cal-
055   culated by perturbing the pixels (one by one or cumulatively) and noting the change in the output
056   probability. A greater change in output probability denotes greater importance for a perturbed pixel.
057   The computed PIR from an image serves as a proxy for ground truth, enabling the estimation of
058   the fidelity score for saliency maps (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018). This approach is based on
059   the assumption that the change in output probability follows a consistent pattern across different
060   perturbations, with the output probability varying in proportion to the importance of the perturbed
061   pixel. If this assumption is not fulfilled, the fidelity metrics’ scores would vary for different per-
062   turbations, leading to inconsistency as reported by Tomsett et al.(Tomsett et al., 2020). Further,
063
      Tomsett et al.(Tomsett et al., 2020) observed this inconsistency by analyzing the prediction proba-
      bilities by perturbing pixels with 0 and a random value. While demonstrating the inconsistency in
064
      fidelity metrics, Tomsett et al.(Tomsett et al., 2020) further recommend:
065
066
                 ”Metric developers should encourage users of their metric to investigate and un-
067              derstand the sources of variance in the metric scores, and how this affects their
068              decisions about what saliency methods to choose for their particular model.”
069
070   Thus, complementing the previous work by Tomsett et al. (Tomsett et al., 2020), we investigate the
071   construction of fidelity metrics by studying the variances.

      1.1     O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
075
      We first theoretically establish the scenarios under which such assumptions are violated. We then
076
      provide two conformity measures that quantify the extent of variances affecting the fidelity metrics.
077   Both the conformity measures are used to demonstrate the inconsistency of fidelity metrics by using
078   several perturbations, models and datasets in both normal and adversarial setting. Going beyond the
079   works of Tomsett et al.(Tomsett et al., 2020) and to generalize our findings, we study the variances
080   in a comprehensive manner using nine different perturbations that include two inpainting-based
081   perturbations (Telea (Telea, 2004) and Navier Strokes (Bertalmio et al., 2001)), Gaussian Blur (three
082   different widths of the Gaussian Kernel) and setting a random value, min, max and mean of the image
083   pixel values as perturbation values. Further, we show empirically that our conformity measures can
084   be used in pixel-wise and segment-wise perturbation schemes before using fidelity metrics.
085   Our main contributions to this paper are:
086
087            • We present an approach to examine the inconsistency of fidelity metrics. We show that
088              before using fidelity metrics, the varaiances of DL models w.r.t. to the perturbation type
089              must be studied.
090            • Complementing previous works that have observed inconsistencies in fidelity metrics, we
091              propose two new conformity measures.
092
               • The conformity measures proposed in this work are further used to empirically analyse
                 three widely used DL models and two adversarially trained DL models on three datasets
093
                 using nine perturbation types, and two perturbation schemes (pixel-wise and segment-wise)
094
                 for all models.

097   2      P ROPOSED A PPROACH
098
099
      The fidelity metrics are based on the PIR which assume the drop in output prediction probability
100
      of a DL model to be proportional to the relevance of the perturbed pixel (i.e., more important the
101   pixel, larger the drop in output probability). The pattern of change (i.e. the proportionate change in
102   output probability as per the relevance of the perturbed pixel) should ideally hold true for all types
103   of perturbations as long as the image semantics is preserved under the notion of local neighborhood.
104   This is based on two aspects:
105
106         [P1] There is a drop in the output probability when a pixel is perturbed;
107
            [P2] The amount of drop in output probability is proportional to the relevance of the pixel.

      Dissecting these two aspects, we first present the theoretical background on the violation such as-
109   pects in fidelity metrics and then present the proposed conformity measures in Section 2.2 and
110   Section 2.3 to aid in examining the inconsistencies.

      We use three pre-trained, and two adversarially trained image classification models, and three well-
      known datasets in our experiments. We conduct our analysis on InceptionV3 (Szegedy et al., 2016),
262
      Xception (Chollet, 2017), and ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) initialized with ImageNet weights. For,

266   Dataset (Parkhi et al., 2012) and PASCAL VOC 2007 (Everingham et al.) are used to conduct our
267   experiments. The Imagenette dataset is a subset of the Imagenet (et.al.) dataset with ten easily
268   classified classes. We used the validation part of this dataset for our experiments, which has around
269   3925 images. The Oxford-IIIT Pet Dataset (Parkhi et al., 2012) and PASCAL VOC 2007 (Ever-
      ingham et al.) datasets did not have train and test splits. Hence, we considered all the images for

279
280   We considered nine different perturbation types i.e., two inpainting based perturbations for all our
281   experiments. Specifically, we used Telea (Telea, 2004) and Navier Strokes (Bertalmio et al., 2001)),
282   Gaussian Blur (three different widths of the Gaussian Kernel) and setting a random value, min,
283
      max and mean of the image pixel values as pixel values. The perturbations are represented as

284
      (image min), ’U1’ (image max), ’U0.5’ (image mean), ’G3’ (Gaussian blur with kernel widths of
285
      0.3), ’G9’ (Gaussian blur with kernel widths of 0.9) and ’G1.5’ (Gaussian blur with kernel widths
286   of 1.5). Further, we perturb the pixels/segments using two perturbation schemes viz., pixel-wise and
287   segment-wise. We use the property that a subset of a ranked order list maintains ranking and select
288   50 random pixels (refer to proof in Appendix S2). The same argument can be extended to segments
289   as shown in our analysis.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
469
470
      The prediction probability of DL models varied significantly for the same image and the same model
471   for the considered perturbations. This variation in the output probabilities led to a high variance in
472   the PIR. Thus, the metrics that implicitly rely on the invariance of PIR for measuring fidelity would
473   be rendered unreliable and fail the sanity checks. While previous studies have limited the analysis
474   of unreliability to the metric level, we demonstrated that unreliability arises as a property of the
475   DL models with respect to perturbations. Thus, we recommend using the proposed metrics as a
476   preconditional check before analyzing the fidelity of saliency maps. Further, we advocate specifying
477   the perturbation type while reporting fidelity scores from these fidelity metrics. However, out of the
478   considered perturbation, Gaussian Blur was relatively consistent compared to other perturbation
479
      types. Future works should consider the high variance in PIR and the lack of robustness around
      the predicted instance to devise reliable fidelity metrics. In the future, we plan to extend our study
480
      to analyze the behavior of adversarially trained DL models concerning perturbations for different
481
      architectures using the proposed conformity measures.
