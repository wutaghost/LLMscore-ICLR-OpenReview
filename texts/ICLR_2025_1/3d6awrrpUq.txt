011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            This study investigates whether Compressed-Language Models (CLMs), i.e. lan-
014            guage models operating on raw byte streams from Compressed File Formats (CFFs),
015            can understand files compressed by CFFs. We focus on the JPEG format as a repre-
016            sentative CFF, given its commonality and its representativeness of key concepts in
017            compression, such as entropy coding and run-length encoding. We test if CLMs
018            understand the JPEG format by probing their capabilities to perform along three
019
               axes: recognition of inherent file properties, handling of files with anomalies,
               and generation of new files. Our findings demonstrate that CLMs can effectively
020
               perform these tasks. These results suggest that CLMs can understand the semantics
021
               of compressed data when directly operating on the byte streams of files produced
022            by CFFs. The possibility to directly operate on raw compressed files offers the
023            promise to leverage some of their remarkable characteristics, such as their ubiquity,
024            compactness, multi-modality and segment-nature.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The digital world stores its information in files, and each file follows a format: a standard establishing
031   how information is encoded into a byte structure, such as doc msd (2018), or wav Stanford Uni-
032   versity & (CCRMA). These formats allow for consistent parsing and predictable encoding of data
033
      into byte streams, independent of the specifics of the storage device. Both storage devices and
      bandwidth are limited, and so modern standards have defined Compressed File Formats (CFFs), e.g.,
034
      mp3 of Congress (2024b), or zip of Congress (2024a). Unlike traditional formats, CFFs are not
035
      directly readable in their compressed state, and must undergo a decoding process to revert the data to
036   its usable form. As a result, CFFs are complex components of our digital infrastructure, and they
037   have become crucial for efficient storage and transmission of information.
038
039
      We identify three characteristics of CFFs that are key to their widespread adoption: (i) Ubiquity:
      CFFs are universally recognized and used across both public and private digital collections, making
040
      them a standard in data storage. (ii) Compactness: CFFs reduce file size while preserving relevant
041
      information by exploiting statistical properties and local repetitive patterns, such as Huffman cod-
042   ing Huffman (1952) in JPEG Pennebaker & Mitchell (1992) and motion vectors in MPEG Puri &
043   Eleftheriadis (1998)—for instance, one GB of compressed video expands to 50-200 GB when decom-
044   pressed. (iii) Generality: despite the variety in compression techniques, each CFF method is capable
045   of transforming any type of data, within a specific modality, into a streamlined one-dimensional
046   array of bytes. These virtues of CFFs suggest the potential usefulness of models capable of directly
047   manipulating information encoded with these formats.
048   A model capable of directly processing the byte arrays in CFFs could manipulate the files’ semantics
049   without needing to decompress them. Such a model, referred to as a “Compressed Model” (CM),
050   offers significant advantages over traditional models that process uncompressed data. In particular, we
051   note two virtues of CMs (1) Compressed Input: CMs would interact with compressed data, which
052   is inherently compact and less redundant than uncompressed data. This characteristic eliminates
053   the need for common techniques for reducing redundancy, such as pooling or attention windowing.
      (2) Raw Input: CMs could exploit vast archives of compressed data exactly as they are stored, thus

074   We recognize that the sequence-like property of the byte streams produced by CFFs can inform
075   the implementation of CMs. Notably, these byte streams exhibit three language-like properties:
076   syntax, established by the CFFs that produced the streams; semantics, inherited from the encoded
077   information; and a finite lexicon, corresponding to their byte alphabet. Given these attributes, we
078
      hypothesize that an effective approach to implementing CMs is through language models Bengio et al.
      (2000); Bahdanau et al. (2014). We term such models “Compressed-Language Models” (CLMs),
079
      and argue that this implementation would achieve a third property of (3) Universality, standardizing
080
      the training pipeline: sequence-to-sequence models as the architecture, next-token prediction as the
081   optimization objective, and CFFs as the universal language for encoding and pre-processing diverse
082   types of information, such as images, text, video, etc. These facts suggest that building a CLM by
083   applying the paradigm of language models on byte streams produced by CFFs is promising.
084
      Yet, the feasibility of a CLM understanding the byte streams produced by CFFs remains largely
085
      unexplored Lester et al. (2024). Notably, various properties of CFFs could hinder the understanding
086
      of these streams. In particular, CFFs introduce unique complexities with their layered compres-
087   sion schemes, which transform the original data through multiple stages to produce the final byte
088   stream Pennebaker & Mitchell (1992). The compact nature of compressed files also implies that even
089   minor alterations in the byte stream or its interpretation could drastically affect the decoded informa-
090   tion. Moreover, since existing language models were not designed considering these challenges, it
091   remains uncertain whether they are capable of effectively understanding the “language” of CFFs.
092
      In this study, we test the understanding capability of Compressed-Language Models, i.e. language
093   models trained for next-token prediction on byte streams produced by Compressed File Formats.
094   There is a wide variety of CFFs, and so, aiming at practicality, we focus our study on the JPEG
095   format Wallace (1992) for encoding image data. We choose JPEG due to its widespread use and
096   because it directly exemplifies key concepts of CFFs, such as compact representation, byte level
097   encoding, multilayered compression, and versatility. Furthermore, JPEG files are easy to evaluate
098   by employing the JPEG decoder and directly inspecting the resulting image raster. We test if CLMs
099   understand JPEG files, in three standard image datasets, by probing their capabilities across three axes:
100   recognizing file properties, handling files with anomalies, and generating new files. Please see Fig. 1
101
      for an overview of our methodology for testing understanding. To the authors’ best knowledge, this
      is the first study testing the understanding of language models that operate directly on CFF-encoded
102
      data. Our evidence supports the hypothesis that CLMs can understand compressed files.

106
107   We select the JPEG format as a case study for Compressed File Formats (CFFs) for three key reasons:
      commonality, representativeness and ease of evaluation. Regarding its commonality, JPEG is widely


                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      used due to its efficient encoding and decoding algorithms, as well as its high perceptual quality, mak-
109   ing it one of the most prevalent image formats on the internet. Regarding its representativeness, JPEG
110   exemplifies key concepts in CFFs, including lossy compression, quantization, domain-knowledge
111   operations (e.g., discrete cosine transforms), and entropy coding, which are foundational in many
112   compressed formats Pennebaker & Mitchell (1992); Boutell (1997); Deutsch (1996); Puri & Elefthe-
113   riadis (1998). Finally, judging the quality of generated JPEG files is straightforward by decoding the
114   compressed data and conducting visual inspection.
115   Despite these virtues, JPEG may present challenges from the perspective of language modeling, as
116   the encoding is a multi-layered procedure that produces byte streams with complex patterns and
117   long-term dependencies. Next, we provide a brief overview of the JPEG encoding, transforming an
118   image into a byte stream, and refer the interested reader to JPEG Committee (2024) for specifics.
119
      JPEG Encoding Overview. Initially, JPEG converts the image from the RGB color space to YCbCr,
120
      separating its information into luminance and chrominance. The chrominance channels are then often
121   down-sampled, leveraging the human eye’s lower sensitivity to color differences. The image is then
122   divided into 8×8 pixel blocks, where each block then undergoes a discrete cosine transform (DCT)
123   to obtain a frequency-domain representation. The DCT coefficients are then scaled with a set of
124   pre-defined quantization tables that control the level of lossy compression. The resulting coefficients
125   are then subjected to run-length encoding to represent consecutive repeated values as a single value
126   and a count. Finally, the symbols in the sequence are compressed with Huffman or arithmetic coding,
127   resulting in the final byte stream. The “lossy-ness” of JPEG is tuned via a JPEG quality parameter,
128   which balances between compression and perceptual quality by controlling the quantization tables.
129   Potential challenges offered by JPEG. Considering the complex encoding procedure presented
130   above, we enlist possible challenges that JPEG poses to language models. Firstly, the compressed
131   byte sequences produced by JPEG are highly sensitive to modifications, since a single change in the
132   byte stream can drastically alter the resultant image. This sensitivity contrasts sharply with natural
133   language, where redundancy is common and often less crucial to the meaning of text. Furthermore,
134   the run-length and Huffman coding processes used in JPEG compression operate at the bit level
135
      rather than the byte level. This fact means that the boundaries between encoded symbols may not
      align neatly with byte boundaries, potentially resulting in encoded symbols starting or ending in
136
      the middle of a byte. This misalignment complicates the model’s ability to learn from byte-level
137
      data, as it introduces difficulties in interpreting the encoded information with precision. Lastly, the
138   interactions between the DCT, quantization, and the corresponding quantization tables introduce
139   complex inter-dependencies among the bytes. These relationships are non-trivial and pose significant
140   challenges for language models, which typically excel when dependencies are more reflective of
141   patterns found in natural language.

      3   M ETHODOLOGY
145
146
147   This section outlines our methodology for testing the hypothesis that a language model can be directly
148   trained on data resulting from Compressed File Formats and effectively capture the underlying
149   semantics. We propose to test the model’s understanding of JPEG data by assessing its performance
150   along three main axes: (i) Recognition of inherent properties of compressed files, (ii) Discovery and
151   correction of anomalies in compressed files and (iii) Generation of compressed files. These tasks rely
152
      on the model’s ability to interpret and manipulate the JPEG’s lexicon (the byte values), syntax (the
      structure of the byte sequences), and semantics (the meaningful interpretation of file content), and
153
      thus reflect diverse aspects of understanding the encoded byte sequence.
154
155   We minimize the impact of training schemes and network-design choices by following the current stan-
156   dard practices for decoder-only language architectures and optimize for next-token prediction Brown
157   et al. (2020). The only factor on which we diverge from these defaults is how we construct the inputs
158
      to the model, i.e. the tokenized “sentence” on which the model operates.
159   Vocabulary and Tokenization. The model’s vocabulary consists of the 256 possible byte values, from
160   0x00 to 0xFF, with additional start (<s>) and end (</s>) of sequence tokens. Such vocabulary
161   enables the model to process a file’s raw byte stream directly. That is, analogous to character-level
      tokenization in natural language, we represent each byte with a separate token.

      Input Sequence Construction. To enable both class-conditional generation and recognition imme-
163   diately after training, we extend the input sequences to include token indicators of both (1) JPEG
164   quality and (2) semantic class. Specifically, each sequence or “sentence” fed to the model has both a
165   JPEG quality and a semantic class token that surround the file’s byte sequence. For instance, a JPEG
166   file of quality 75 and class 3 is represented as:
167
168            <q75> <class3> <bytes> 0xFF . . . 0xD9 </bytes> <q75> <class3>
169
      This template guides the model both for conditional generation (conditioned on JPEG quality and
170   class) and for regression of those attributes after scanning the byte sequence. Please refer to the
171   appendix for an explanation of how we avoid the degenerate solution of copy-pasting these attributes.
172
173
      We next describe in detail the three main axes on which we evaluate the model’s understanding
      of CFFs, recognizing files (Sect. 3.1), handling anomalous files (Sect. 3.2), and generating new
174
      files (Sect. 3.3), and report the corresponding results in Sect. 4.

177   3.1   F ILE R ECOGNITION
178
      Can the model recognize properties of a given file? A language model should be able to understand
179
      the attributes of sentences Collobert et al. (2011); Devlin et al. (2018); Lagler et al. (2013). Similarly,
180
      a model tuned for JPEG byte sequences should be capable of recognizing the properties of a given
181   file. Thus, we test the model’s capacity to recognize the JPEG quality and semantic class of real files.
182
183   Here, we leverage our input-sequence template for probing the model for this task. Specifically, we
184
      previously outlined how the JPEG quality and class token are (optionally, see appendix) provided
      at the start and end of the byte sequence (B). To evaluate the model’s capability to recognize these
185
      properties from the byte sequence only, we replace the first two tokens of the sequence with the
186
      unknown “<unk>” token. That is, we represent a validation sample as: “<unk> <unk> <bytes>
      *B </bytes>” (see Fig. 1). We then use the model to auto-regressively predict the next two tokens
187
188   in the sequence, corresponding to the model’s predictions for B’s JPEG quality and semantic class.
189   With then use these predictions to compute the model’s accuracy for predicting each property.
190
      The model’s recognition capacities are a direct consequence of the model’s next-token prediction
191
      pre-training. That is, the next-token prediction objective simultaneously tasked the model with
192
      learning to generate byte sequences and recognizing them. However, in a sentence, the number
193   of tokens contributing to the generation objective (hundreds of tokens) is disproportionately large
194   w.r.t. the number of tokens contributing to the recognition objective (only two tokens). This lack of
195   proportion suggests that the model’s recognition capacities after pre-training is likely a lower bound
196   to the model’s actual potential. We thus study the possibility of leveraging the model’s generative
197   pre-training Brown et al. (2020), and fine-tune the model to improve its recognition capabilities. To
198   this aim, we fine-tune the model on these same sentences, but only supervise the token on which
199   we are interested for recognition (either the JPEG quality or the semantic class token). After this
200   fine-tuning, we again measure the model’s classification performance via accuracy.
201
202   3.2   F ILE A NOMALY H ANDLING
203
204   Can the model handle files that contain anomalies? A language model should be able to handle
205   sentences with grammar errors or typos Santos et al. (2018); Al-Jefri & Mahmoud (2013); Cheng
206
      et al. (2020); Etoori et al. (2018); Ji et al. (2021). Analogously, a model that understands JPEG files
      should be capable of handling anomalies in these compressed files.
207
208   To test such capabilities, we consider three anomaly-related tasks: (i) tagging a file as anomalous,
209   (ii) detecting the precise location of an anomaly in a file, and (iii) correcting a given anomaly. To
210   study these tasks, we require a dataset of anomalous files. We choose to generate such a dataset,
211   based on real files, via the procedure we describe next.

      3.3   F ILE G ENERATION
286
287   Can the model generate new files? A language model should be able to generate new sentences Lagler
288   et al. (2013); Floridi & Chiriatti (2020); Team et al. (2024); Touvron et al. (2023). Analogously, a
289   model that understands JPEG files should be capable of generating novel files that adhere to the JPEG
290   standard. We thus test the model’s capacity to generate new JPEG files.
291   We perform class-conditional generation by feeding the model a prompt stating the quality and
292   semantic class of the target file (e.g., “<q30> <class0> <bytes>”) and then auto-regressively
293   generate the file content. We continue the generation process until the model generates the </bytes>
294   delimiter, after which we write the generated bytes to a file with the .jpeg extension.
295
      Checks. File validity: The generated file may not represent a valid JPEG file: it may include
296
      malformed headers, incorrect byte sequences, or inconsistencies in the compression structure. To
297   check the file’s validity, we use the standard OpenCV library to attempt opening the file, and consider
298   the generation invalid if the library either throws an error or a warning when attempting to open the
299   file. We measure the model’s performance as the percentage of generated sequences that correspond
300   to valid JPEG files. JPEG quality: For files that pass the validity check, we assess the JPEG quality
301   presented by the byte stream. To check the file’s JPEG quality, we use the ImageMagick library,
302   which determines the compression quality based on the quantization tables in the file’s header. We
303   measure the model’s performance as the percentage of the valid files for which the file’s exhibited
304   JPEG quality matches the quality token specified in the prompt.

344
      Performance before and after fine-tuning. Before: We find that the next-token prediction training
345
      yields a model that naturally exhibits high recognition capacity. In particular, on MNIST’s validation
346   set, we find that the model’s accuracy for recognizing a file’s semantic class is 97.1 ± 0.05% (average
347   and standard deviation computed across three runs), while its accuracy for recognizing the file’s JPEG

349   the semantic class; however, the model still reaches an accuracy of 100% for recognizing JPEG quality.
350   After: The models’ performance at recognizing JPEG quality is outstanding, while recognizing the
351   semantic class has room for improvement. We thus fine-tune the models for recognizing the semantic
352   class. After fine-tuning, accuracy on MNIST grows from 97% to above 99%, while the accuracy on

461
462   Models trained on partially-decoded JPEGs. Instead of operating on RGB pixel values of images,
463   a few works have devised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) Gueguen et al. (2018); Verma et al.
464   (2018) or Vision Transformer (ViT) Park & Johnson (2023) architectures that operate on partially
465
      decoded JPEG images. For instance, Gueguen et al. (2018) introduced a CNN LeCun et al. (1995)
      that processes these images, allowing for faster image handling by omitting some decoding stages.
466
      Similarly, Park & Johnson (2023) applied a ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) to partially decoded JPEGs,
467
      enhancing the ability of models to interact with data in a more compressed state. These approaches
468   mark a significant shift towards using the structural properties inherent in compressed formats, albeit
469   still involving some level of decoding.
470
471
      Byte Sequence Modeling for Uncompressed Data. Transitioning to the modeling of byte sequences,
      significant innovations have been introduced to manage the complexities associated with raw data
472
      streams. The MegaByte framework Yu et al. (2024), for instance, features a multi-scale decoder
473
      transformer architecture that addresses the challenges posed by long byte sequences through a novel
474   “byte patchification” technique. Concurrently, bGPT Wu et al. (2024) leverages a decoder-only
475   Transformer model for autoregressive generation of byte sequences. These methods underscore
476   developments in processing byte sequences, yet they focus on uncompressed data, neglecting the
477   more common and practical compressed file formats. In contrast, our work tackles the challenge of
478   processing compressed formats, specifically using JPEG as a case study.
479
      Direct Operations on Compressed JPEG Byte Sequences. ByteFormer Horton et al. (2023)
480   ventured into processing compressed file formats at the byte level, and revealed that JPEG byte
481   sequences introduce significant challenges due to their nonlinear encoding and variable length. This
482   work showed that traditional byte patching methods could degrade performance, owing to the high
483   density of information in compressed formats. Unlike ByteFormer, which employs a tailored encoder
484   architecture for byte sequences, our work uses a straightforward decoder-only Transformer language
485   model trained with the vanilla next-token prediction objective. We find that, despite the simplicity of
      this design choices, this vanilla Transformer effectively works on JPEG sequences for multiple tasks.

      B    T RAINING SENTENCES
632
633
      In Sect 3, we described the general template for our sentences as
634
635           <q75> <class3> <bytes> 0xFF . . . 0xD9 </bytes> <q75> <class3>
636
637   In practice, we introduce additional delimiters for the condition, but omit them here for clarity.
638   Furthermore, notice that we introduced condition tokens (JPEG quality and semantic class) both
639
      before and after the segment corresponding to the file’s bytes. On the one hand, the condition
      tokens before the file allow the model to condition its output on a specific condition, i.e. conditional
640
      generation. On the other hand, the condition token after the file instruct the model to scan the entire
641
      file and then predict the file properties corresponding to the condition. Unfortunately, the model may
642   learn to solve the recognition task by copying the condition token provided before the file and pasting
643   it after the file. To prevent this degenerate solution, we stochastically feed the model with either a
644   “generation” or a “recognition” version of the sample during training. In the generation version, we
645   remove supervision for the condition tokens after the file, so the model lacks incentives to copy-paste.
646   In the recognition version, we restore that supervision, but replace the condition tokens before the
647   file with an <unk> token, preventing copy-pasting and thus forcing the model to rely on the file to
      predict the class.
