009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Many of the most popular graph neural networks fall into the category of message-
012            passing neural networks (MPNNs). Famously, MPNNs’ ability to distinguish be-
013            tween graphs is limited to graphs separable by the Weisfeiler-Lemann (WL) graph
014            isomorphism test, and the strongest MPNNs, in terms of separation power, are
015            WL-equivalent.
016            Recently, it was shown that the quality of separation provided by standard WL-
017            equivalent MPNN can be very low, resulting in WL-separable graphs being
018            mapped to very similar, hardly distinguishable features. This paper addresses this
019
               issue by seeking bi-Lipschitz continuity guarantees for MPNNs. We demonstrate
               that, in contrast with standard summation-based MPNNs, which lack bi-Lipschitz
020
               properties, our proposed model provides a bi-Lipschitz graph embedding with re-
021
               spect to two standard graph metrics. Empirically, we show that our MPNN is
022            competitive with standard MPNNs for several graph learning tasks and is far more
023            accurate in over-squashing long-range tasks.

026   1   INTRODUCTION
027
028   Graph neural networks are a central research topic in contemporary machine learning research.
029   Many of the most popular models, such as GIN (Xu et al., 2019), GraphSage (Hamilton et al., 2017),
030   GAT (Velickovic et al., 2018), and GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), can be seen as an instantiation of
031   Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017).
032   A well-known limitation of MPNNs is that they cannot differentiate between all distinct pairs of
033   graphs. In fact, a pair of distinct graphs that cannot be separated by the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL)
034   graph isomorphism test will not be separated by any MPNN (Xu et al., 2019). Accordingly, the
035   most expressive MPNNs are those that are WL-equivalent, which means they can separate all pairs
036   of graphs that are separable by WL. WL-equivalent MPNNs were proposed in the seminal works of
037   Xu et al. (2019); Morris et al. (2019), and the complexity of these constructions was later improved
038
      in (Aamand et al., 2022; Amir et al., 2023).
039   While separation should theoretically be achieved for WL-equivalent MPNNs, in some cases, their
040   separation is so weak that it cannot be observed with 32-bit floating-point computer numbers (see
041   Bravo et al. (2024b)). This observation motivates the development of quantitative estimates of
042   MPNN separation by means of bi-Lipschitz stability guarantees. These guarantees would ensure
043   that Euclidean distances in the MPNN feature space are neither much larger nor much smaller than
044
      distances in the original graph space, which are defined by a suitable graph metric (defined up to
      WL equivalence).
045
046   Some first steps towards addressing these challenges have already been made by Davidson & Dym
047   (2024). Their work analyzes a weaker notion of Lipschitz and Holder guarantees in expectation, and
048   shows that essentially all popular MPNN models are not lower-Lipschitz, but they are lower-Holder
049   in expectation, with an exponent that grows worse as the MPNN depth increases. In contrast, they
050
      propose SortMPNN, a novel MPNN which is bi-Lipschitz (in expectation).
051   However, SortMPNN has several limitations. First, it is only bi-Lipschitz in expectation–a relaxed
052   notion of Lipschitzness that guarantees smoothness only in expectation over the model parameters
053   and for fixed pairs of graphs rather than uniformly on all input graphs. Additionally, their method
      addresses neighborhoods of different sizes by augmenting them to a predetermined maximum size.

054
      This approach has significant limitations: it is both computationally expensive, as the model cannot
055   exploit graph sparsity, and it necessitates prior knowledge of the maximal graph size for the learning
056   task at hand.
057
058
      In this paper we introduce a novel MPNN called FSW-GNN (Fourier Sliced-Wasserstein GNN),
      which overcomes the limitations of SortMPNN. We show that this model is Bi-Lipschitz in the
059
      standard sense rather than in expectation, with respect to both the DS metric of Grohe (2020) and
060
      the Tree Mover’s Distance (TMD) metric of Chuang & Jegelka (2022). Furthermore, this model can
061   handle sparsity well and thus is much more efficient than SortMPNN for sparse graphs.
062
063   Empirically, we show that FSW-GNN has comparable or better performance than MPNN on ‘stan-
064
      dard’ graph learning tasks, but achieves superior performance when considering long-range tasks,
      which require a large number of message-passing iterations. We hypothesize this is because the
065
      Holder exponent of standard MPNNs deteriorates with depth, whereas our FSW-GNN is bi-Lipschitz
066
      for any finite number of iterations. This hypothesis provides an alternative, and perhaps comple-
067   mentary, explanation to the difficulty of training deep standard MPNNs, commonly attributed to
068   over smoothing and over squashing .

      1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
071
072   MPNNs with advanced pooling mechanisms In addition to the SortMPNN model discussed ear-
073   lier, our approach is conceptually related to other MPNNs that replace basic max-, mean-, or sum-
074   pooling with more advanced pooling methods, such as sorting (Balan et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
075   2018), standard deviation (Corso et al., 2020), or Wasserstein embeddings via reference distribu-
076   tions (Kolouri et al.). However, these methods lack the bi-Lipschitzness guarantees that our model
077   provides.
078
079   Bi-Lipschitzness Bi-Lipschitzness guarantees arise naturally in many domains, including frames
080   (Balan, 1997), phase retrieval (Bandeira et al., 2014; Cheng et al., 2021), group invariant learning
081
      (Cahill et al., 2020; 2024b) and multisets (Amir et al., 2023; Amir & Dym, 2024). In the context of
      MPNNs, a recent survey by Morris et al. (2024) identifies bi-Lipschitzness guarantees as a significant
082
      future challenge for theoretical GNN research. While most MPNNs are upper Lipschitz, as discussed
083
      in (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022; Levie, 2023; Davidson & Dym, 2024), achieving bi-Lipschitzness
084   remains an open problem.
085
086   WL-equivalent metrics Metrics with the separation power of WL include the DS metric (Grohe,
087   2020) (also called the tree metric), the TMD metric (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022), and the WL metric
088   (Chen et al., 2022). In this paper, we prove that the graph embeddings computed by our FSW-GNN
089   model are bi-Lipschitz with respect to the DS and TMD metrics. This analysis is for graphs with
090   bounded cardinality and continuous, bounded features.        Weaker notions of equivalence between
091   these metrics, in the setting of graphs with unbounded cardinality and without node features, are
092   discussed in (Böker et al., 2024; Böker, 2021).

      Unfortunately, MPNN cannot separate any pair of WL-equivalent graphs, even if they are not truly
163   isomorphic Xu et al. (2019); Morris et al. (2019). Accordingly, the best we can hope for from
164   an MPNN, in terms of separation, is WL equivalence: for every pair of graphs G, G′ ∈ G≤N (Ω),
165                                        WL
      F (G) = F (G′ ) if and only if G ∼ G′ . While MPNNs based on max- or mean-pooling cannot be
166
      WL-equivalent Xu et al. (2019), it is possible to construct WL-equivalent MPNNs based on sum-
167   pooling, as discussed in Xu et al. (2019); Morris et al. (2019); Aamand et al. (2022); Amir et al.
168   (2023); Bravo et al. (2024a). Theoretically, a properly tuned graph model based on a WL-equivalent
169   MPNN should be capable of perfectly solving any binary classification task, provided that no two
170   WL-equivalent graphs have different ground-truth labels. However, this separation does not always
171   manifest in practice. One reason is that WL-equivalent functions may map two input graphs far apart
172   in the input space to outputs that are numerically indistinguishable in the output Euclidean space.
173   In fact, Davidson & Dym (2024) provides an example of graph pairs that are not WL-equivalent yet
174   are mapped to near-identical outputs by standard sum-based MPNNs. Consequently, these MPNNs
175
      fail on binary classification tasks for such graphs.
176   This paper aims to address this limitation by devising an MPNN whose embeddings preserve dis-
177   tances in the original graph space in the bi-Lipschitz sense. To state our goal formally, we need to
178   define appropriate notions of WL metrics on the input space and bi-Lipschitz graph embeddings.

243   The DS metric The roots of the DS metric come from a relaxation of the graph isomorphism
244   problem. Two graphs G and G̃, each with n vertices, and corresponding adjacency matrices A Ã are
245   isomorphic if and only if there exists a permutation matrix P such that AP = P Ã. Since checking
246   whether graphs are isomorphic is intractable, an approximate solution can be sought by considering
247   the equation AS = S Ã, where S is a matrix in the convex hull of the permutation matrices: the set
248   of doubly stochastic matrices, denoted by Dn . These are n × n matrices with non-negative entries
249   whose rows and columns all sum to one. Remarkably, this equation admits a doubly stochastic
250
      solution if and only if the graphs are WL-equivalent (Scheinerman & Ullman, 2013). Accordingly,
      a WL-metric can be defined by the minimization problem.

346   prove Theorem 3.3, we rely on the following facts: (1) the output of FSW-GNN for an input graph
347   G = (V, E, X) is piecewise-linear with respect to the vertex-feature matrix X. This follows from
348   properties of the FSW embedding functions used in (12) and (13). (2) both metrics ρDS and TMD
349   can be transformed, with bounded distortion, into piecewise-linear metrics by choosing all the vector
350   norms they employ to be the ℓ1 norm. The claim them follows from these observations and the
351   following lemma:

353   tions defined on a compact polygon M ⊂ Rd . Suppose that for all x ∈ M , f (x) = 0 if and only if
354   g(x) = 0. Then there exist real constants c, C > 0 such that

      Finally, we evaluate the possible relationship of our empirical results with oversmoothing. Over-
474   smoothing is a phenomenon that often occurs with deep MPNNs, where all node features become
475   nearly identical as depth increases, thus leading to degraded performance. Oversmoothing is often
476   measured using the Dirichlet energy or Mean Average Distance (MAD) (see Rusch et al. (2023) for

479   that all methods except for FSW-GNN exhibit over-smoothing starting from some r (that is, have a
480   near 0 MAD energy), and this correlates pretty well with the performance of the methods in terms
481   of accuracy, shown in Figure 3 (middle).

483   radius of the problem Gutteridge et al. (2023), or add global information using spectral filters Geisler
484   et al. (2024) or graph transformers. What is special about FSW-GNN is that it performs well with the
485   given graph topology. Thus, this work gives us a direction to address the core problem of relaying
      long-range messages efficiently rather than using methods to circumvent the problem.

524   5    C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we introduced FSW-GNN, the first bi-Lipschitz MPNN. Empirically, we have found
527
      that FSW-GNN is very effective for long range learning problems. Our current explanation for why
528   this should be the case is discussed at the end of Section 2. Our main goal for future work is to
529   strengthen and formalize this explanation. In particular, currently we have no control on the bi-
530   Lipschitz distortion of FSW-GNN, and its dependence on depth. Informally, we would expect that
531   if each FSW embedding has distortion of C/c, then the total FSW-GNN will be (C/c)T , growing
532   exponentially with the depth T . However, the stability of FSW-GNN for problems of rather large
533   radius indicate that the rate of distortion growth is much smaller. A possible explanation for this is
534   that, at the limit where the width goes to infinity, FSW embeddings have a optimal distortion of 1
535   with respect to the sliced Wasserstein distance. Accordingly, in future work we will aim to under-
536
      stand how to control the distortion of FSW-GNN, and formally prove the low distortion FSW-GNN,
      with respect to an appropriate node-WL-metric, avoids both oversmoothing and oversquashing.
537
538   A limitaton of FSW-GNN is that, due to its more complex aggregation, its runtime is higher than
539   standard MPNN: for the LRGB struct the average time per epoch of FSW-GNN is four times slower
      than GIN and GCN, as shown in Appendix Table 6.
