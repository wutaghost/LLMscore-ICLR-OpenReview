                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               In 2018, researchers proposed the use of generalized entropy indices as a unified
               approach to quantifying algorithmic unfairness at both the group and individual
011
               levels. Using this metric they empirically evidenced a trade-off between the two
012
               notions of fairness. The definition of the index introduces an array of new pa-
013            rameters; thus, while the construction of the metric is principled, its behavior is
014            opaque. Since its publication, the metric has been highly reproduced in the litera-
015            ture, researched and implemented in open source libraries by IBM, Microsoft and
016            Amazon; thus demonstrating traction among researchers, educators and practi-
017            tioners. Advice or grounded justification around appropriate parameter selection,
018            however, remains scarce. Nevertheless, the metric has been implemented in li-
019            braries with default or hard-coded parameter settings from the original paper with
020            little to no explanation.
021            In this article we take an intentionally data agnostic (rational, rather than empiri-
022            cal) approach to understanding the index, illuminating its behavior with respect to
023            different error distributions and costs, and the effect of placing constraints on it.
024
               By adding the simple requirement that the the resulting fairness metric should be
               independent of model accuracy, we demonstrate consistency between cost sensi-
025
               tive learning and individual fairness in this paradigm. By viewing a classification
026
               decision as a transaction between the individual and the decision maker, and ac-
027            counting for both perspectives, we prove that, with careful parameter selection, the
028            concepts of utility and (group and individual) fairness can be firmly aligned, estab-
029            lishing generalized entropy indices as an efficient, regulatable parametric model
030            of risk, and method for mitigating bias in machine learning.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The proliferation of data driven algorithmic solutions in social domains has been a catalyst for
036   research and development of fairness metrics in recent years. Applications in high stakes decisions in
037   criminal justice Larson et al. (2016), predictive policing Ensign et al. (2018), healthcare Obermeyer
038   et al. (2019), finance Mukerjee et al. (2002), employment Cohen et al. (2020) and beyond, have
039   fueled the need for formal definition of fairness notions, metrics, and bias mitigation techniques.
040   Early methods for quantifying fairness, motivated by the introduction of anti-discrimination laws
041   in the US Cleary (1968); Einhorn & Bass (1971); Cole (1973); Novick & Petersen (1976); Fried-
042   man & Nissenbaum (1996); Zliobaite (2015), fall under what has since become known as group
043   fairness Barocas et al. (2019). This class of metrics considers differences in treatment (outcomes
044   or errors) across subgroups of a population defined by sensitive or protected features. Informally,
045   individual fairness is the notion that similar individuals should be treated similarly. Individual fair-
046   ness is not concerned with protected features, but rather the consistency with which decisions are
047   made Dwork et al. (2011); Zemel et al. (2013); Mukherjee et al. (2020). This tells us that for fair-
048
      ness, predictions must be randomized. Like these works we agree that the fairest model is the most
049   accurate model, where accuracy is measured against some unknown ground truth Ỹ , and not the
050
      target in our training data Y . Since the 60’s the application have been
051   In a recent survey on fairness in machine learning, authors highlight five major dilemmas regard-
052   ing progress in the space Caton & Haas (2023). The first two of these concern trade-offs between
053   different metrics. The first discusses the difficulty in reconciling trade-offs between fairness and
      model performance Hajian & Domingo-Ferrer (2012); Corbett-Davies et al. (2017); Calmon et al.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (2017); Haas (2019). The second discusses trade-offs between different notions of fairness Dar-
055   lington (1971); Chouldechova (2016); Kleinberg et al. (2016); Hardt et al. (2016); Murgai (2023)
056   and the difficulty in determining which metric is most appropriate for a given problem. The latter
057   is credited with stifling progress in the space in the 1970’s Cole & Zieky (2001); Hutchinson &
058   Mitchell (2019). Thus, clarity around the equivalence and compatibility of different fairness and
059   performance measures are important in moving the field forward.
060
      In 2018 Speicher et al. (2018) proposed generalized entropy indices Shorrocks (1980) as a unified
061   measure of both group fairness and individual fairness. For any partition of a population into (mu-
062   tually exclusive) subgroups, the inequality measure can be additively decomposed into a between-
063   group component and a within-group component. The former can then be thought of a measure
064   of group unfairness and the index (sum of both components), a measure of individual unfairness.
065   Using the metric, they provide empirical evidence of the trade-off between group and individual
066   fairness.
067   In this paper we revisit the metric proposed by Speicher et al. (2018) and mathematically prove its
068   value in the fair measurement of risk, and regulation of it. In order to do this we use two hypothetical
069   examples which constitute different applications of a sociotechnical system Barocas et al. (2019).
070   In the first, the algorithm is punitive, it is used to allocate harm, by determining whether or not to
071   incarcerate individuals on trial. In the second, the algorithm is assistive (or preventative Saleiro
072   et al. (2019)), it is used to distribute employment opportunities. With these examples in mind, we
073   consider the question of how an unfairness index should behave, knowing that a cap on the index can
074   be efficiently integrated into any convex optimization, pre-training Heidari et al. (2018). We take
075
      an intentionally data agnostic (rational as opposed to empirical Church (2011)) approach to under-
      standing the index. Instead we focus on the abstraction of risk, represented by generalized entropy
076
      indices, and its relationship with better known performance metrics for different index parameter
077
      choices.
078
079   The proposed index measure in the original paper increases the parametric representation of risk
080   by the generalization parameter α. One must define a mapping from predictions to benefits (as
081
      usual when calculating risk), and specify the generalization parameter α. Authors in the origi-
      nal paper, and works that have followed, make somewhat arbitrary choices for parameters in their
082
      experiments. Thus, while the construction of the metric is principled, its behavior for different pa-
083
      rameter choices remains opaque. Nevertheless, the metric has been implemented in open source
084   libraries IBM (2018); Microsoft (2020); Amazon Web Services (2024). It has traction among re-
085   searchers Heidari et al. (2018); Jin et al. (2023) and educators Deho et al. (2022), and is described in
086   recent surveys Pessach & Shmueli (2022); Caton & Haas (2023). More recently Jin et al. (2023) de-
087   scribe a fair empirical risk minimization algorithm, in which the index is constrained during model
088   optimization and demonstrate its promise in reducing bias.
089
      We argue that generalised entropy indices (GEI) present a valuable family of functions (the *com-
090   plete* set of subgroup decomposable functions according to Shorrocks (1980)) which warrant much
091   closer inspection, before moving on to other welfare functions Heidari et al. (2018). We aim to
092   prove that they parametrically extend the notion of risk, in a principled and continuous way that al-
093   lows us to manage the multiple requirements of model accuracy, fairness (differing error costs) and
094   between-group fairness (by choice of α). We believe that GEI provide a parametric language (bij
095   and α) suited to algorithmic governance at a high level. They can be computed with very little infor-
096   mation, (ŷ, y) or better still (p, y). Such a model can be used to limit the feasible models of utility
097   in a rational way, simply by choosing parameters reasonably and capping the index accordingly.
098
      The efficiency saving which results from using a well reasoned choice of parameters would be O(n),
      since it would eliminate the need to iterate over the training data to determine the cap/threshold,
099
      which is derived analytically before training. Individual fairness, as originally defined by Dwork
100
      et al. (2011), measures similarity by the features. In order to calculate it, one must define or learn a
101   similarity metric. This is, computationally, a significantly more expensive task Zemel et al. (2013);
102   Lahoti et al. (2019).
103

      The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows.
105
106   • We derive new representations of the measure, in which its relationship with important perfor-
107     mance metrics (error rates and model accuracy and acceptance rate) are explicit. Previously these
        relationships were understood empirically for a limited set of parameter choices.

108
      • We argue that in order to represent individual fairness as defined by Dwork et al. (2011) as faith-
109     fully as possible, the index must be orthogonal to model accuracy. For the parameter choices
110     made Speicher et al. (2018), we show that the index is a linear function of model accuracy, and
111     thus cannot represent individual fairness according to this constraint. We conclude that the em-
112     pirical evidence presented by Speicher et al. (2018) does not support the existence of a trade-off
113     between group and individual fairness, and more likely is a manifestation of the well documented
114     trade-off between accuracy and fairness.
115   • By viewing a classification decision as a transaction between the individual and the decision
116     maker, and accounting for the perspective of the individuals subjected to the algorithm (in addition
117     to that of the decision maker), this work reconciles the trade-off between fairness and accuracy
118     with a subset of utility functions (generalized entropy indices) which account for both.
119   • For practitioners and legislators, we provide tools to visualize the behavior of any given benefit
120     matrix and utility function. Those readers who wish to reproduce any part of this paper, can find
121     all relevant code and resources on GitHub. All proofs can be found in the Appendix.

      In the standard supervised learning setting, which is is typical for high-stakes sociotechnical systems,
      the algorithm is learned from a data set of observations for n individuals, D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 . For
135
      each individual i, we have an m dimensional feature vector xi ∈ X , a target yi ∈ Y and a model or
136
      algorithm ϕ : X 7→ Y which predicts the target value, given the feature vector for any individual,
137   ŷ = ϕ(x). We shall denote the random variable Z ∈ X as the most advantaged class in our
138   sample, indicated by those who score highest on our test. Though we focus on the case of binary
139   classification Y = {0, 1}, the work presented can be extended to consider multi-class classification
140   |Y| > 2 and regression Y = R problems.
141
      The proposed algorithmic unfairness metric is calculated for the population of n individuals in two
142
      steps. First, a benefit function must be defined which maps each individual i to a benefit bi . Second,
143   an inequality index I : Rn≥0 7→ R≥0 , is applied to the benefit array b = (b1 , b2 , ..., bn ), to measure
144   how unequally they are distributed. The index provides a measure of algorithmic unfairness. The
145   larger the value of I(b), the greater the inequality. We use µ to denote the mean benefit. Below we
146   describe each of the two steps starting with the measurement of inequality.
147
148   2.1   G ENERALIZED E NTROPY I NDICES
149
150   There are many indices I(b) for measure inequality which all share the following properties:
151
152   • Symmetry: I(b) = I(b′ ) for any permutation b′ of b = (b1 , b2 , ..., bn ).
153   • Zero-normalization: I(b) ≥ 0 and I(b) = 0 ⇔ bi = µ ∀ i.
154   • Transfer principal: Transferring benefit from rich to poor, must decrease I(b), provided the
155     individuals don’t switch places in their ranking as a result of the transfer. That is, for any 1 ≤
156     i < j ≤ n where bi < bj ∀ i, j and 0 < δ < (bj − bi )/2, we must have I(b1 , ..., bi + δ, ..., bj −
157     δ, ...., bn ) < I(b).
158   • Population invariance: The measure depends on the distribution of benefits but not the size of
159     the population n. That is, if b′ = ⟨b, b, ..., b⟩ ∈ Rkn                                  ′
                                                             ≥0 is a k-replication of b, then I(b ) = I(b).
160
161   Generalized entropy indices are the complete single parameter (α) family of inequality indices with
      the additional properties of subgroup decomposability and scale invariance Shorrocks (1980).

        the mean benefit, between subgroups. The within-group component is the contribution from the
166     variation in individual benefits, within the subgroups.
167   • Scale invariance: For any constant c > 0, I(cb) = I(b).

214
215   A key component of the measure, is the definition of the mapping from algorithmic prediction to
      benefit. Benefits are floored at zero and the mean benefit must be greater than zero. Benefits are

216
      relative, they must be defined on a ratio scale, as oppose to an interval scale, to ensure that relative
217   comparisons of benefits are meaningful. On a ratio scale, zero represents a true minimum. On an
218   interval scale, zero is arbitrarily chosen, nevertheless differences can be interpreted meaningfully.
219   An example is temperature, for which Kelvin is a ratio scale; Celsius and Fahrenheit are different
220   local interval scales. If we are interested in global solutions, we should use Kelvin.
221
      One can imagine that there is almost always some benefit or cost to any decision; that benefit is the
222
      information gained from the process, which guides both the benefit provider and recipient to their
223   next decision. Every decision is useful, even the bad ones, assuming we live to learn a lesson from
224   it. So as long as the decision is not death, and some information was shared by both parties, we
225   can assume there is some, potentially small, positive benefit, regardless of our position. The same
226   algorithm with a higher minimum benefit would be preferred by any reasonable measure of utility.
227   How does one increase the minimum benefit? With more relevant information exchange and a path
228   for recourse if necessary.
229   Given two arrays, the target data y and model prediction ŷ, of size n, all n individuals can be
230   categorized in a confusion matrix. A benefit function can then be defined by simply assigning a
231   non-negative benefit value, to each element of the matrix bij = benefit(ŷ = i, y = j). Since the
232   generalized entropy index is scale invariant, we can choose any one of these to be unity, leaving
233   |Y|2 − 1 degrees of freedom in the definition of the benefit matrix.
234
      It’s easiest to reason about the matrix from the perspective of one stakeholder at a time. We shall
235   assume stakeholders include three broad parties. These are, the benefit providers, benefit recipients
236   and the regulator. The decision maker and subject could be the either the recipient or provider
237   of benefits. Neither benefit provider nor recipient can see beyond the decision, under one of the
238   two outcomes. For the employer, the cost is the same regardless of whether the chosen candidate
239   was worthy. Similarly, the cost of incarcerating a person is the same, regardless of how much the
240   defendant earned when they were free. From any one perspective, two of the four outcomes look
241   the same Elkan (2001). Thus, we can reduce the complexity of the analysis, by assuming that two
242   of the four possible outcomes ŷ, y ∈ {0, 1} are of unit benefit. More specifically, we will assume
243
      a ternary model of benefits, where the benefit associated with an outcome could be one of three
      values, bij ∈ {b− , b+ , 1} and bmin = b− < b+ . One final constraint is that of convexity, for which
244
      the benefit must be monotonic in ŷ Heidari et al. (2018).
245
246   In this paper, we shall play the role of regulator. The decision maker exerts power and influence
247   through deployment of their model at scale. They are, in some sense, the navigators and the stake-
248   holders are (in most cases involuntary) passengers. As regulator, we must consider both perspectives.
249
      We accept the decision makers right to navigate (optimize), within reason or risk appetite. We must
      take, longer term view to protect everyone (including foreseeable future stakeholders) and avert dis-
250
      aster by constraining the direction of travel. The regulator must decide the relative importance of
251
      precision P(Y = 1|Ŷ = 1) versus recall P(Ŷ = 1|Y = 1) based on the mission, context and law.
252
      We can assume an unregulated decision maker would almost certainly be greedy. As the regula-
253
      tor, we can impose the minimum legal benefit. In some sense, every decision can be viewed as a
254   transaction or bet; an investment (or divestment) in an entity, which may yield a return, (or prevent
255   a loss). The model score is an indication of the present value of the subject, based on incomplete
256   and potentially erroneous information about them. As a regulator we can preclude predatory pric-
257   ing models, based on our own definition of utility, ultimately setting risk appropriate bounds on the
258   decision space for a given application.

525   The findings of this paper and the works which led to it affect all of us, several times a day. Every
526   time we make a judgment about a person (especially an emotive one), how can we be less biased?
527   From prior works we know that using a conventional (both evidence based and biased) model of util-
528   ity with a binary outcome, a decision maker cannot be fair Friedler et al. (2016). From Barocas et al.
529   (2019), we know that introducing a third possible outcome (increasing the size of our outcome space
530   from binary to ternary), makes satisfying independence (Ŷ ⊥Z) and separation (Ŷ ⊥Z|Y ) possible.
531   What third outcome could there possibly be? Surely, someone either is or it isn’t something? No.
532   There is always another possibility. More fundamentally, this result says that, any scale on which
533   we measure or represent people, cannot be binary.
534   There is one remaining degree of freedom in the generalization parameter α. In all societies, per-
535   sonal and social wealth tend to be correlated. Here we define personal and social wealth to be
536   bi /µ and µg /µ respectively in Eq. (2). A targeted advertising algorithm for luxury goods, would
537   indeed need to be a good predictor of how wealthy an individual is, and rich people tend to have
538   rich friends, so a true value of α > 0 makes sense, but rich ̸= will purchase thus α < 1. In truth, the
539   target Y is generally a positively correlated proxy for the thing we (as the decision maker) would
      like to measure Ỹ . By choosing α = 0, we ensure the model Ŷ is an unbiased estimator of Y , but
