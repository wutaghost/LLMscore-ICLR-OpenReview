011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Visual deep reinforcement learning (RL) enables robots to acquire skills from
014            visual input for unstructured tasks. However, current algorithms suffer from low
015            sample efficiency, limiting their practical applicability. In this work, we present
016
               MENTOR, a method that improves both the architecture and optimization of RL
               agents. Specifically, MENTOR replaces the standard multi-layer perceptron (MLP)
017
               with a mixture-of-experts (MoE) backbone, enhancing the agent’s ability to handle
018
               complex tasks by leveraging modular expert learning to avoid gradient conflicts.
019            Furthermore, MENTOR introduces a task-oriented perturbation mechanism, which
020            heuristically samples perturbation candidates containing task-relevant information,
021            leading to more targeted and effective optimization. MENTOR outperforms state-
022            of-the-art methods across three simulation domains—DeepMind Control Suite,
023            Meta-World, and Adroit. Additionally, MENTOR achieves an average of 83%
024            success rate on three challenging real-world robotic manipulation tasks including
025            Peg Insertion, Cable Routing, and Tabletop Golf, which significantly surpasses the
026            success rate of 32% from the current strongest model-free visual RL algorithm.
027
               These results underscore the importance of sample efficiency in advancing visual
               RL for real-world robotics. Experimental videos are available at mentor-vrl.

046   1    I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Visual deep reinforcement learning (RL) focuses on agents that perceive their environment through
049   high-dimensional image data, closely aligning with robot control scenarios where vision is the
050   primary modality. Despite substantial progress in this field (Kostrikov et al., 2020; Yarats et al., 2021;
051   Schwarzer et al., 2020; Stooke et al., 2021; Laskin et al., 2020a), these methods still suffer from low
052   sample efficiency. As a result, most visual RL pipelines have to be first trained in the simulator and
053   then deployed to the real world, inevitably leading to the problem of sim-to-real gap (Zhao et al.,
      2020; Salvato et al., 2021).


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To bypass this difficulty, one approach is to train visual RL agents from scratch on physical robots,
055   which is known as real-world RL (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2020).
056   Given the numerous challenges of real-world RL, we argue that the fundamental solution lies not in
057   task-specific tweaks, but in developing substantially more sample-efficient RL algorithms. In this
058   paper, we introduce MENTOR: Mixture-of-Experts Network with Task-Oriented perturbation for
059   visual Reinforcement learning, which significantly boosts the sample efficiency of visual RL through
060   improvements in both agent network architecture and optimization.
061   In terms of architecture, visual RL agents typically use convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for
062   feature extraction from high-dimensional images, followed by multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) for
063   action output (Yarats et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023; Cetin et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023). However,
064   the learning efficiency of standard MLPs is hindered by intrinsic gradient conflicts in challenging
065   robotic tasks (Yu et al., 2020a; Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021), where the
066   gradient directions for optimizing neural parameters across different stages of the task trajectory or
067   between tasks may conflict. In this work, we propose to alleviate gradient conflicts by integrating
068   mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures (Jacobs et al., 1991; Shazeer et al., 2017; Masoudnia &
069
      Ebrahimpour, 2014) as the backbone to the visual RL framework. Intuitively, MoE architectures can
      alleviate gradient conflicts due to their ability to dynamically allocate gradients to specialized experts
070
      for each input through the sparse routing mechanism (Akbari et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024).
071
072   In terms of optimization, visual RL agents often struggle with local minima due to the unstructured
073   nature of robotic tasks. Recent works have shown that periodically perturbing the agent’s weights
074   with random noise can help escape local minima (Nikishin et al., 2022; Sokar et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
075
      2023; Ji et al., 2024). However, the choice of perturbation candidates (i.e., the network weights used
      to perturb the current agent’s weights) has not been thoroughly explored. Building on this idea, we
076
      propose a task-oriented perturbation mechanism. Instead of sampling from a fixed distribution, we
077
      maintain a heuristically shifted distribution based on the top-performing agents from the RL history.
078   Unlike Self-Imitation Learning (Oh et al., 2018), which collects and re-exploits past high-rewarding
079   trajectories for policy gradient optimization, our approach directly updates agent through parameter
080   perturbation: We periodically sample weights from the aforementioned heuristic distribution to
081   perturb the current agent’s weights. The intuition is that the distribution gradually formed by the
082   weights of previous top-performing agents may accumulate task-relevant information, leading to
083   more promising optimization directions than purely random noise.
084   Empirically, we find MENTOR outperforms current state-of-the-art methods (Xu et al., 2023; Yarats
085   et al., 2021; Cetin et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023) across all tested scenarios in DeepMind Control
086   Suite (Tassa et al., 2018), Meta-World (Yu et al., 2020b), and Adroit (Rajeswaran et al., 2017). Fur-
087   thermore, we present three challenging real-world robotic manipulation tasks, shown in Figure 1: Peg
088   Insertion – inserting three kinds of pegs into the corresponding sockets; Cable Routing – maneuvering
089   one end of a rope to make it fit into two non-parallel slots; and Tabletop Golf – striking a golf
090   ball into the target hole while avoiding getting stuck into the trap. In these experiments, MENTOR
091   demonstrates significantly higher learning efficiency, achieving an average success rate of 83%,
092
      compared to 32% for the state-of-the-art counterpart (Xu et al., 2023) within the same training time.
      This confirms the effectiveness of our approach and underscores the importance of improving sample
093
      efficiency for making RL algorithms more practical in robotics applications.
094
095   Our key contributions are threefold. First, we introduce the MoE architecture to replace the MLP
096   as the agent backbone in model-free visual RL, improving the agent’s learning ability to handle
097   complex robotic environments and reducing gradient conflicts. Second, we propose a task-oriented
098
      perturbation mechanism which samples candidates from a heuristically updated distribution, making
      network perturbation a more efficient and targeted optimization process compared to the random
099
      parameter exploration used in previous RL perturbation methods. Third, we achieve state-of-the-art
100
      performance in both simulated environments and three challenging real-world tasks, highlighting the
101   sample efficiency and practical value of MENTOR.

180   3.1   A RCHITECTURE : M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS AS THE P OLICY BACKBONE
181
182   In challenging robotic learning tasks, RL agents are often assigned K ≥ 2 different tasks or subgoals,
183   each associated with a loss function Li (θ). The goal is to find optimal agent weights θ ∈ Rm that
184   minimize losses across all objectives.nIn practice, a common approacho           is to reduce the average
                                                        ∆ 1 PK
185   loss over all tasks: θ∗ = arg minθ∈Rm L0 (θ) = K              L
                                                                i=1 i  (θ)   . If the agent  uses a shared set of
186   parameters θ (e.g., MLP), meaning all parameters must be simultaneously active to function, the
187   optimization process using gradient descent may compromise individual loss optimization. This
188   issue, known as conflicting gradients (Yu et al., 2020a; Liu et al., 2021), hinders the agent’s ability to
189   optimize its behavior when facing complex scenarios effectively.
190
      To address this issue, we propose to utilize an MoE architecture as a substitute for the MLP back-
191
      bone in RL agents. The MoE structure is characterized by its composition of modular experts,
192   θMoE = {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θN }, which allows the agent to activate different experts via a dynamic routing
193   mechanism flexibly. This enables gradients from different tasks or subgoals to correspond to different
194   sets of parameters. Specifically, the parameters of a given expert are updated only by gradients from
195   similar task scenarios, thereby effectively alleviating the gradient conflict problem.
196
      As illustrated in Figure 2, the MoE agent first processes visual inputs using a CNN-based encoder,
197
      transforming them into a latent space Z. The router h computes a probability distribution h(i | z)
198   over the experts i for a given latent vector z ∈ Z. The top-k experts are selected based on this
199   distribution, and their softmax weights wi are computed. The outputs ai from these top-k experts are
200   weighted combined to produce the final output a, as shown in Equations 1 and 2. This MoE structure
201   enables the agent to route input visual features to specialized experts based on specific objectives,
202   optimizing its performance in challenging scenarios such as multi-tasking or multi-stage processes.
203
      To better illustrate the important role of dynamic modular expert learning for RL agents, we conduct
204   a multi-task experiment (MT5) in Meta-World (MW) (Yu et al., 2020b), where the agent (#Experts =
205   16, k = 4) is trained to acquire five opposing skills: Open tasks (Door-Open, Drawer-Open, Window-
206   Open) and Close tasks (Drawer-Close, Window-Close). As shown in Figure 3a, in addition to sharing
207   some experts for handling common knowledge, the Open and Close tasks have their own dedicated
208   experts (Experts 3, 7 for Open and Experts 9, 10 for Close). We evaluate the cosine similarities (Yu
209   et al., 2020a) for both MLP and MoE agents, as shown in Figure 3b. The MLP’s gradients show
210   significant conflicts between opposing tasks, resulting in a performance gap (100% success for Close
211   tasks, 82% for Open tasks). In contrast, the MoE model demonstrates higher gradient compatibility,
212
      achieving 100% success in both task types.
213   This structural advantage can also be propagated to challenging single tasks, as the dynamic routing
214   mechanism automatically activates different experts to adjust the agent’s behavior throughout the
215   task, alleviating the burden on shared parameters. We illustrate this through training a same-structure
      MoE agent on a single, highly challenging Assembly task from Meta-World (MW). Figure 4 shows

      3.2   O PTIMIZATION : TASK - ORIENTED P ERTURBATION M ECHANISM
259
260   Neural network perturbation is employed to enhance the exploration capabilities in RL. Two key
261   factors influence the effectiveness of this process θk = αθk−1 +(1−α)ϕ, ϕ ∼ Φ. α is the perturbation
262   factor controlling the mix between current agent and perturbation candidate weights. ϕ represents
263   the perturbation candidate sampled from a distribution Φ, which typically is a fixed Gaussian noise
264   N (µ, σ). Previous works (Sokar et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2024) have investigated the
265   use of the dormant ratio to determine α, resulting in improved exploration efficiency (see Section 2).
266
      However, the selection of perturbation candidates has not been thoroughly examined. In this work,
      we propose sampling ϕ from a heuristically updated distribution Φoriented , generated from past high-
267
      performing agents, to provide more task-oriented candidates that better facilitate optimization.
268
269   We define Φoriented as a distribution from which the weights of high-performing agents can be sampled.
      To obtain this distribution, we dynamically maintain a fixed-size set Stop = {(θ, R)}, where (θ, R)

      In this section, we present a comprehensive empirical evaluation of MENTOR. Our experimental
327
      setup consists of two parts. In Section 4.1, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across three
328   simulation benchmarks: DeepMind Control Suite (DMC) (Tassa et al., 2018), Meta-World (MW) (Yu
329   et al., 2020b), and Adroit (Rajeswaran et al., 2017). These benchmarks feature rich visual features
330   and complex dynamics, demanding fine-grained control. Our method consistently outperforms
331   leading visual RL algorithms across these domains. Moreover, one critical limitation in visual RL
332   research is the over-reliance on simulated environments, which raises concerns about the practical
333   applicability of such methods. To mitigate this gap, in Section 4.2, we go beyond simulations and
334   validate the effectiveness of MENTOR in real-world settings on three challenging robotic learning
335   tasks, highlighting the importance of real-world testing.
336
      4.1   S IMULATION E XPERIMENTS
337
338   Baselines: We compare MENTOR against four leading model-free visual RL methods: DrM (Xu
339   et al., 2023), ALIX (Cetin et al., 2022), TACO (Zheng et al., 2023), and DrQ-v2 (Yarats et al., 2021).
340   DrM, ALIX, and TACO all use DrQ-v2 as their backbone. DrM periodically perturbs the agent’s
341   weights with random noise based on the proportion of dormant neurons in the neural network; ALIX
342   adds regularization to the encoder gradients to mitigate overfitting; and TACO employs contrastive
343   learning to improve latent state and action representations.
344   Experimental Settings: We evaluate MENTOR on a diverse set of tasks across three simulation
345   environments with complex dynamics and even sparse reward. The DMC includes challenging tasks
346   like Dog Stand, Dog Walk, Manipulator Bring Ball, and Acrobot Swingup (Sparse), focusing on long-
347   horizon continuous locomotion and manipulation challenges. The MW environment provides a suite
348   of robotic tasks including Assembly, Disassemble, Pick Place, Coffee Push (Sparse), Soccer (Sparse),
349   and Hammer (Sparse), which test the agent’s manipulation abilities and require sequential reasoning.
350   The Adroit environment includes complex robotic manipulation tasks such as Door and Hammer,
351   which involve controlling dexterous hands to interact with articulated objects. Notably, DMC tasks
352
      are evaluated using episode reward, while tasks in MW and Adroit are assessed based on success
      rate. For each method on each task, we conducted experiments using four random seeds; detailed
353
      hyperparameters and training settings are provided in Appendix B.
354
355   Results: Figure 6 presents performance comparisons between MENTOR and the baselines. In the
356   DMC tasks, Dog Stand and Dog Walk feature high action dimensionality with a 38-dimensional
357   action space representing joint controls for the dog model. These tasks also have complex kinemat-
358
      ics involving intricate joint coordination, muscle dynamics, and collision handling, making them
      challenging to optimize. Our method outperforms the top baseline, achieving approximately 17%
359
      and 10% higher episode rewards, respectively. In the MW tasks, the Hammer (Sparse) task stands
360
      out. It requires a robotic arm to hammer a nail into a wall, with highly sparse rewards: success
361   yields significantly larger rewards than merely touching or missing the nail. In fact, the reward for
362   failure is only one-thousandth of the success reward, making the task extremely sparse. However, our
363   task-oriented perturbation effectively captures these sparse rewards, reducing the required training
364   frames by 70% compared to the best baseline. In the Adroit tasks, our method achieves nearly 100%
365   success with significantly less training time, while the most competitive counterpart (DrM) requires
366   more frames, and other baselines fail to match performance even after 6 million frames. A key
367   highlight is the Door task, which involves multiple stages of dexterous hand manipulation—grasping,
368   turning, and opening the door. Leveraging the MoE architecture, our method reduces training time
369
      to achieve over 80% success by approximately 23% compared to the best baseline. In summary,
      MENTOR demonstrates superior efficiency and performance compared to the strongest existing
370
      model-free visual RL baselines across all 12 tasks. For ablation studies of the significance of MoE
371
      and Task-oriented Perturbation separately, please refer to appendix F. For the robustness against
372   disturbances of agent trained by MENTOR, please see appendix H.

406   for RGB visual observations, which include both overall and close-up views to capture global and
407   local information. The reward functions are based on the absolute distance between the current
408   and desired states. To prevent trajectory overfitting, the end-effector’s initial position is randomly
409   sampled from a predefined region at the start of each episode. Tasks are described below and shown
410
      in Figure 7. Further details can be found in Appendix C.
411   Peg Insertion: This task simulates an assembly-line scenario where fine-grained insertion of various
412   objects are required. The agent needs to develop multi-task learning skills to insert pegs with three
413   different shapes (Star, Triangle, and Arrow) into corresponding sockets. Training such agents in
414   simulators is difficult due to the complexities of contact-rich interactions, making this task ideal for
415   real-world reinforcement learning and evaluation.
416   Cable Routing: Manipulating deformable cables presents significant challenges due to the complexi-
417   ties of modeling and simulating their physical dynamics, making this task ideal for direct, model-free
418   visual RL training in real-world environments. In this scenario, the robot must guide a cable into
419   two parallel slots. Since both slots cannot be filled simultaneously, the agent must perform the task
420   sequentially, requiring long-horizon, multi-stage planning to successfully accomplish the task.
421   Tabletop Golf: In this task, the robot uses a golf club to strike a ball on a grass-like surface, aiming
422   to land it in a target hole. An automated reset system retrieves the ball when it reaches the hole, enters
423   a mock water hazard, or rolls out of bounds, and randomly repositions it. The agent must learn to
424   approach the ball, control the club’s striking force and direction to guide the ball toward the hole
425   while avoiding obstacles through real-world interaction.

      6    C ONCLUSION
527
      In this paper, we present MENTOR, a state-of-the-art model-free visual RL framework that achieves
528
      superior performance in challenging robotic control tasks. MENTOR enhances learning efficiency
529
      through two key improvements in both agent network architecture and optimization. MENTOR
530   consistently outperforms the strongest baselines across 12 tasks in three simulation benchmark
531   environments. Furthermore, we extend our evaluation beyond simulations, demonstrating the effec-
532   tiveness of MENTOR in real-world settings on three challenging robotic manipulation tasks. We
533   believe MENTOR is a capable visual RL algorithm with the potential to push the boundaries of RL
534   application in real-world robotic tasks. While MENTOR has demonstrated its efficacy, the evaluated
535   environments primarily focus on single tasks with a single robot embodiment. Future work could
536   explore scaling the number of experts in MoE to tackle more complex scenarios, such as developing a
537   single policy capable of generalizing across hundreds of tasks or diverse robot embodiments, paving
538
      the way for broader real-world applications.

      mechanism to update the agent’s weights in a more targeted direction by sampling from a distribution
710

714   Algorithm 2 illustrates how MENTOR employs the MoE architecture as the backbone of its policy
715
      network. In addition to the regular training process, using MoE as the policy agent requires adding
      an additional loss to prevent MoE degradation during training—where a fixed subset of experts is
716
      consistently activated. The MoE layer computes the output action while simultaneously calculating
717
      an auxiliary loss for load balancing (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022). Specifically, we extract
718   the distribution over experts produced by the router for each input. By averaging these distributions
719   over a large batch, we obtain an overall expert distribution, which we aim to keep uniform across all
720   experts. To achieve this, we introduce an auxiliary loss term—the negative entropy of the overall
721   expert distribution (Chen et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023). This loss reaches its minimum value of
722   − log(Ne ), where Ne is the number of experts in the MoE, when all experts are equally utilized, thus
723   preventing degradation. This auxiliary loss is added to the actor loss and used to update the actor
724   during the RL process.

829
830   The policy outputs an end-effector delta pose from the current pose tracked by the low-level controller
831
      equipped in robot arm. Typically, the end-effector of a robotic arm has six degrees of freedom (DOF);
      however, in our tasks, the action space is constrained to be fewer. The reason for this restriction
832
      in DOF is specific to our setting: in our case, we train model-free visual reinforcement learning
833
      algorithms directly in the real-world environment from scratch, without any initial demonstrations
834   and prior knowledge toward the tasks. As a result, the exploration process is highly random, and
835   limiting the degrees of freedom is crucial for safeguarding both the robotic arm and the experimental
836   equipment. For instance, in the Peg Insertion task, the use of rigid 3D-printed materials means
837   allowing the end-effector to attempt insertion at arbitrary angles could easily cause damage. Similarly,
838   in the Cable Routing task, an unrestricted end-effector might collide with the slot, posing a risk to the
839   equipment.
840   Peg Insertion: The end-effector in this task has four degrees of freedom: x, y, z, and r. Here, x
841   and y represent the planar coordinates, z represents the height, and r denotes the rotation around the
842   z-axis. The x, y, and z dimensions are normalized based on the environment’s size, ranging from -1
843   to 1, while r is normalized over a feasible rotation range of 0.6π.
844
      The action space is a 4-dimensional continuous space (∆x, ∆y, ∆z, ∆r), where each action updates
845
      the end-effector’s state as:
846

                             (x, y, z, r) → x +     ,y +     ,z +    ,r +       .
848                                               8        8      10       8
849
850   Cable Routing: In this task, the end-effector is constrained to two degrees of freedom: x and z. The
851   x-axis controls movement almost perpendicular to the cable, while the z-axis controls the height.
852   Both dimensions are normalized based on the environment’s size, with values ranging from -1 to 1.
853   Although we restrict the action space to two dimensions, this task remains extremely challenging
854   for the RL agent to master, as it requires inserting cable in both slots sequentially, making it the
855   most time-consuming task among the three, as shown in Figure 8. The difficulty stems largely from
856
      the structure and parallel configuration of the two slots: the agent cannot route the cable into both
      slots simultaneously and must insert one first. However, as shown in Figure 10b, without a hook-like
857
      structure to secure the cable in the slot, the cable easily slips out when the agent attempts to route it
858
      into the second slot. This task therefore requires highly precise movements, forcing the agent to learn

       C.4    AUTO -R ESET M ECHANISMS
973
974    One major challenge in real-world RL is the burden of frequent manual resets during training. To
975    address this, we designed auto-reset mechanisms to make the training process more feasible and
976    efficient.
977
       In the Peg Insertion task, the robot arm is set to frequently switch among different pegs to help
978
       the agent acquire multi-tasking skills. To facilitate this, we design a shelf to hold spare pegs while
979    the robot arm is handling one. With the fixed position of the shelf, we pre-programmed a peg-
980    switching routine, eliminating the need for manual peg replacement. After switching, the robot arm
981    automatically moves the peg to the workspace and randomizes its initial position for training.
982
       In the Cable Routing task, manual resets are unnecessary, as the robot arm can auto-reset the cable by
983
       simply moving back to its initial position with added randomness.
984
985    In the Tabletop Golf task, we design an auto-collection mechanism to reset the task. As shown in
986    Figure 10c, the tabletop golf device has two layers: the top golf court surface and a lower inclined
987    floor. When the ball is hit into the hole or out of bounds, it rolls down to the corner of the lower layer,
988
       where a light sensor triggers a motor to return the ball to the court. The variability in the ball’s initial
       velocity during reset introduces randomness to its starting position.
