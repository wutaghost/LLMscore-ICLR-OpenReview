011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            End-to-end learning has demonstrated considerable promise in advancing au-
014            tonomous driving by fully leveraging sensor data. Recently, many end-to-end
015
               models have been developed, with a substantial number evaluated using the
               nuScenes dataset in an open-loop manner. However, open-loop evaluations, which
016
               lack interaction with the environment, fail to fully capture the driving capabilities
017
               of these models. While closed-loop evaluations, such as those using the CARLA
018            simulator, allow for interaction with the environment, they often rely on rule-
019            based, manually configured traffic scenarios. This approach leads to evaluations
020            that diverge significantly from real-world driving conditions, thus limiting their
021            ability to reflect actual driving performance. To address these limitations, we
022            introduce a novel closed-loop evaluation framework that closely integrates real-
023            world driving scenarios with the CARLA simulator, effectively bridging the gap
024            between simulated environments and real-world driving conditions. Our approach
025            involves the creation of digital twins for 15 real-world intersections and the incor-
026
               poration of 800 real-world traffic scenarios selected from a comprehensive 100-
               hour video dataset captured with highly installed infrastructure sensors. These
027
               digital twins accurately replicate the physical and environmental characteristics of
028
               their real-world counterparts, while the traffic scenarios capture a diverse range
029            of driving behaviors, locations, weather conditions, and times of day. Within this
030            twinned environment, CARLA enables realistic simulations where autonomous
031            agents can dynamically interact with their surroundings. Furthermore, we have
032            established a comprehensive closed-loop benchmark that evaluates end-to-end au-
033            tonomous driving models across these diverse scenarios. Notably, this is the first
034            closed-loop end-to-end autonomous driving benchmark based on real-world traffic
035            scenarios. Video demos are provided in the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      End-to-end autonomous driving (E2EAD) has recently shown substantial advances and potential,
040
      exemplified by models like UniAD Hu et al. (2023) and Tesla’s FSD V12 system Tesla Oracle
041
      (2024). Unlike traditional methods that optimize individual tasks in isolation and then integrate
042   them through post-processing, the end-to-end approach directly optimizes the final planning output,
043   thereby reducing error accumulation and information loss. E2EAD is also considered to fully exploit
044   the potential of large datasets, making significant strides toward Level 4 autonomous driving.
045
      Effective evaluation plays an essential role in the advancement of E2EAD research, especially in
046
      the era of the rapid emergence of new E2E algorithms. There are two primary evaluation ways
047
      for E2EAD systems. The first way, open-loop evaluation, mainly assesses the E2EAD’s perfor-
048   mance against pre-recorded expert driving route, like utilizing real-world nuScenes Caesar et al.
049   (2020) datasets. In evaluation, the E2EAD system processes sensor data from a predefined route
050   to predict future trajectories. However, this method cannot generate new observations based on the
051   decisions of the ego vehicle. Consequently, open-loop evaluation often reduces to a trajectory pre-
052   diction task Zhai et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), which limits its assessment of vehicle-environment
053   interaction and independent decision-making. The second way is closed-loop evaluation, which al-
      lows the ego vehicle to receive new observations based on its actions and offers a more realistic

145
146   End-to-End Autonomous Driving. End-to-end autonomous driving systems offer a compelling
147   alternative to traditional modular designs by integrating perception, prediction, and planning into
148   a single, differentiable model Hu et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024a); Chib & Singh (2024). Un-
149   like conventional modular methods that often struggle with the complexity of real-world scenarios,
150   end-to-end approaches optimize the entire system holistically, directly processing raw sensor data
151
      into driving actions Jiang et al. (2023); Jia et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2024). Recent advancements
      have focused on utilizing transformers-based models Prakash et al. (2021); Chitta et al. (2023); Shao
152
      et al. (2023a); Jaeger et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2023b) and LLM-enhanced models Pan et al. (2024);
153
      Chen et al. (2024b); Xu et al. (2024); Fu et al. (2024); Sima et al. (2024), significantly enhancing
154   the performance of these systems. These developments address key challenges such as generaliza-
155   tion Wang et al. (2024) and interpretability Xu et al. (2024); Sima et al. (2024), leading to superior
156   results on benchmarks for autonomous driving tasks.
157
158   Evaluation Benchmarks for E2EAD. In the context of E2EAD, benchmark evaluations play a
159   crucial role as they provide standardized metrics for measuring progress and help assess the practi-
160   cal applicability and robustness of E2EAD systems. There are two primary methods for evaluating
161   E2EAD algorithms. The first is open-loop evaluation, which utilizes metrics like L2 error and col-
      lision rate. This straightforward approach is widely used in E2EAD assessments Hu et al. (2022;


                                                                3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2023); Jiang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024c); Yu et al. (2024) but lacks interaction with the envi-
163   ronment, limiting its ability to evaluate the algorithm’s planning capabilities Zhai et al. (2023); Li
164   et al. (2024). The second method is closed-loop evaluation, which typically relies on simulators to
165   enable interaction between the ego vehicle and environmental agents. The most prominent end-to-
166   end closed-loop simulators include CARLA Dosovitskiy et al. (2017), which has spawned several
167   benchmarks like CARLA Leaderboard CARLA Contributors (2024), Longest6 Chitta et al. (2023),
168   V2XVerse Liu et al. (2024), and Bench2Drive Jia et al. (2024). However, these benchmarks rely on
169   artificially created scenarios rather than real-world trajectories. Other closed-loop evaluation plat-
170   forms for autonomous driving planning, such as nuPlan Caesar et al. (2021) and Waymax Gulino
171
      et al. (2023), also exist but currently do not support end-to-end algorithm evaluation. In addition,
      there are some datasets, like Lyft Level 5 Houston et al. (2021) and Interaction Zhan et al. (2019),
172
      focus on the motion prediction task, which can be only used to test Non-E2E planning in an open-
173
      loop manner. Different from the existing benchmarks, Our DriveE2E is the first closed-loop E2EAD
174   benchmark grounded in real-world traffic scenarios, which would enable a more realistic close-loop

      3.1   T HE F EATURES OF D RIVE E2E
188
      DrivieE2E contains 800 twined driving scenarios located in 15 intersection areas, covering a range
189   of driving behaviors, weather conditions, and times of day from morning to night. Specifically,
190
191   • Each twin intersection, including road elements, traffic lights, and building elements on both
192     road sides, is a digital twin of and consistent with the corresponding real-world intersection at
193     Beijing city. We call this digital twin intersection as Twin Intersection. These twin intersections
194     have diverse and complex road elements and topological structures, which help to evaluate the
195     road understanding ability of the E2EAD systems. Visualization examples are provided in the
196     appendix.
197   • Each dynamic traffic scenario, including traffic participants and their behaviors as well as traffic
198     light signals, is sourced from real-world traffic data. These scenarios encompass a variety of
199     elements such as pedestrians, non-motor vehicles, and cars, which are essential for evaluating the
200     interactive capabilities of E2EAD systems within complex urban environments.
201   • Each driving task within a driving scenario is defined based on the original driving behaviors
202     observed in real traffic scenarios, such as turning left while pedestrians are crossing. These tasks
203     encompass a range of driving behaviors, which are crucial for assessing the driving capabilities of
204     E2EAD systems. Detailed descriptions of these driving behaviors are also provided as follows.
205
206   Driving Behaviors DriveE2E identifies and categorizes 8 typical scenario types at intersections
207   from 800 real-world traffic scenarios. These behaviors include Interaction with Pedestrians and Cy-
208   clists (IPC), Competing with Other Vehicles (COV), Passing through during Yellow Lights (YLW),
209   Making a U-turn (UT), Stopping at Red Lights (STP), Going Straight through Intersection (STR),
210   Making a Left Turn (LFT), and Making a Right Turn (RT). A detailed description of each driving
211
      behavior is provided below, and the distribution of these behaviors is illustrated in Fig.2(a).
212

260   3.2   T HE G ENERATION OF T WIN D RIVING S CENARIOS
261
      The generation of the twin driving scenarios in DriveE2E is mainly composed of three steps: 1)
262
      Twin Intersections Generation: Creating digital twins of static intersections, which include com-
263
      plex road elements, including roadside infrastructures, such as traffic light poles, signs, lanes, cross-
264   walks, stop lines, and surrounding buildings. 2) Dynamic Traffic Scenario Acquisition: Collect-
265   ing, annotating, normalizing and filtering dynamic real-world traffic scenarios to cover as many
266   traffic conditions and driving behaviors as possible. These scenarios include traffic participants and
267   their behaviors, and traffic light signals. 3) Loading and Configuring: Loading dynamic traffic
268   scenarios as well as their twin intersection into CARLA simulator, and configuring the appearance
269   in the simulator. In the following parts, we will further explain how to create digital twins of static
      intersections and how to generate dynamic traffic scenarios.

307
      same frequency. Additionally, we obtained related weather and lighting from the weather system.
308

309   (2022) and tracking models Weng et al. (2020) to generate trajectory sequences encompassing over
310   1,000,000 annotated bounding boxes, each assigned a class label from 8 categories and a unique
311   trajectory ID. We meticulously filtered and optimized these trajectories to form a high-quality traffic
312   scenario database. From this database, we manually selected the ego vehicles and further classified
313   their driving behaviors, which ensured that the scenarios accurately represented various driving be-
314   haviors. We then used these scenarios to build DriveE2E, selecting 800 scenarios to ensure a diverse
315   range of scenes and driving conditions.

362   3.4   C LOSED -L OOP E VALUATION
363
364   Closed-loop evaluation for E2EAD allows an autonomous vehicle to interact with and respond to
365   dynamic changes in real-time. This method continuously updates the traffic environment obser-
366   vations based on the autonomous system’s decisions, enabling a comprehensive assessment of its
367
      decision-making capabilities.
368   In DriveE2E, the autonomous vehicle is tasked with successfully navigating from the source loca-
369   tion (xsrc , ysrc ) to the destination location (xdst , ydst ) within a driving scenario. The source and
370   destination locations correspond to the vehicle’s positions in its original driving route. The E2EAD
371   system receives raw sensor data (including multi-view images and point clouds), GPS coordinates,
372   and target waypoints as inputs. These waypoints are obtained by downsampling the vehicle’s orig-
373
      inal route. The output of the system should be control commands, such as steering angle, throttle,
      and brake. Alternatively, the output could be future planning waypoints, which are then converted
374
      into control commands using the CARLA simulator.

      4.1    BASELINES AND DATASETS
405
406   We implemented several classical End-to-End Autonomous Driving (E2EAD) models as baselines
407   on the DriveE2E platform, using imitation learning for training. Specifically, we divide the 800
408   expert data clips collected into training, validation, and test sets in a 4:2:2 ratio, ensuring a balanced
409   distribution of behavior categories and weather conditions in each set. The 400 training clips were
410   used to train the models on A100 GPUs. We evaluated the trained models in a closed-loop setup in
411   the validation set. In addition, open-loop evaluations were conducted on the same validation set to
412   further assess performance. We report the performance of the model in terms of L2 error (m).
413
414
      • UniAD Hu et al. (2023) employs queries to integrate key tasks such as perception, mapping, pre-
        diction, and planning. The standard training process for UniAD typically involves three stages.
415
        To accelerate training and reduce GPU resource consumption, we bypassed the initial stages
416
        by directly training the stage-2 model using the bevformer Li et al. (2022) model provided by
417     Bench2Drive Jia et al. (2024) as a pre-trained model. We train UniAD for one epoch. It is impor-
418     tant to note that these settings may lead to a reduction in UniAD’s accuracy.
419
      • VAD Jiang et al. (2023) employs Transformer queries while enhancing efficiency through a vec-
420
        torized scene representation. We trained the VAD model for two epochs, using a pre-trained model
421
        provided by Bench2Drive Jia et al. (2024) as the pretrained model.
422
423   • AD-MLP Zhai et al. (2023) adopts a simple strategy by entering the past states of the ego vehicle
424
        into an MLP to generate future trajectory predictions. We train AD-MLP for 60 epochs.
425   • TCP Wu et al. (2022) predicts both trajectories and control signals. It only uses front-facing
426     cameras and the ego state as inputs. Note that we did not train an expert model and did not use
427     expert feature distillation during TCP training. TCP was trained for 27 epochs.

430
431   We present the evaluation results in Tab. 3, which include both the open-loop evaluation results (L2
      error) and the closed-loop evaluation results (success rate and driving score).

432
      Open-loop Evaluation Results. As shown in Tab. 3, AD-MLP exhibits a significantly high L2
433   error, with an average error reaching 7.65 m. This result contrasts with the performance observed on
434   nuScenesCaesar et al. (2020); Zhai et al. (2023), where using only past ego status produced strong
435   planning outcomes. The discrepancy is understandable, as DriveE2E incorporates a wider range of
436   driving behaviors (Fig. 2), unlike nuScenes, where most behaviors are relatively straightforward.
437   This highlights the increased challenge DriveE2E presents for driving evaluation. Both UniAD and
438   VAD outperform AD-MLP and TCP, which is expected given that our benchmark is more challeng-
439   ing, and UniAD and VAD are specifically designed for planning tasks. While VAD achieves a lower
440   L2 error than UniAD, it is premature to conclude that VAD performs better. UniAD was only trained
441
      for one epoch due to time constraints, and it has not yet fully converged.
442
443
      Closed-loop Evaluation Results. Both AD-MLP and TCP exhibit very low success rates and
      driving scores, with AD-MLP achieving a 6.85 SR and 8.94 DS, and TCP achieving a 7.42 SR
444
      and 10.47 DS. In contrast, VAD performs considerably better in the closed-loop evaluations, with a
445
      45.14 SR and 55.15 DS. These results indicate that relying solely on past ego status is insufficient
446   for generating effective planning outputs in complex traffic environments.
447
448   Relationship between Close-loop and Open-loop Evaluation Results. To some extent, open-
449   loop and closed-loop evaluations are related. For example, AD-MLP, which has the highest L2 error,
450   also exhibits the worst driving performance in closed-loop evaluation. Conversely, VAD performs
451   well in both open-loop and closed-loop assessments. This suggests that open-loop evaluations with
452   difficult and diverse driving scenarios can provide insight into driving ability. However, the results
453   across different methods do not always show a strictly consistent pattern between open-loop and
454   closed-loop evaluations. This is because open-loop outputs do not necessarily correlate positively
455
      with the outcomes of closed-loop evaluations, which involve interaction. Therefore, closed-loop
      evaluation remains essential for accurately assessing driving ability.
