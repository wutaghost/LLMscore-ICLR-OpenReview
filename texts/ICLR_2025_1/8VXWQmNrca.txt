                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            In imaging inverse problems, we would like to know how close the recovered
013            image is to the true image in terms of full-reference image quality (FRIQ) metrics
014
               like PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, etc. This is especially important in safety-critical
               applications like medical imaging, where knowing that, say, the SSIM was poor
015
               could potentially avoid a costly misdiagnosis. But since we don’t know the true
016
               image, computing FRIQ is non-trivial. In this work, we combine conformal
017            prediction with approximate posterior sampling to construct bounds on FRIQ that
018            are guaranteed to hold up to a user-specified error probability. We demonstrate our
019            approach on image denoising and accelerated magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
020            problems.
021
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In imaging inverse problems, one aims to recover a true image x0 from noisy/distorted/incomplete
027   measurements y0 = A(x0 ) (Arridge et al., 2019). Denoising, deblurring, inpainting, super-resolution,
028   limited-angle computed tomography, and accelerated magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are ex-
029   amples of linear inverse problems, while phase-retrieval, de-quantization, low-light imaging, and
030   image-to-image translation are examples of non-linear inverse problems. Such problems are ill-posed,
031   in that many hypotheses of x0 are consistent with both the measurements y0 and prior knowledge
032   about x0 . To complicate matters, different recovery methods are biased towards different plausible
033
      image hypotheses, leading to important differences in reconstruction quality. For example, modern
      deep-network approaches can sometimes hallucinate (Belthangady & Royer, 2019; Hoffman et al.,
034
      2021; Muckley et al., 2021; Bhadra et al., 2021; Gottschling et al., 2023), i.e., generate visually
035
      pleasing recoveries that differ in important ways from the true image x0 . Thus, there is a strong need
036   to quantify the accuracy of a given recovery, especially in safety-critical applications like medical
037   imaging (Banerji et al., 2023).
038
039
      In image recovery, “accuracy” can be defined in different ways. Classical metrics like mean-squared
      error (MSE), or its scaled counterpart peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR), are convenient for theoretical
040
      analysis but do not always correlate well with human perceptions of image quality. This fact inspired
041
      the field of full-reference image-quality (FRIQ) assessment (Lin & Kuo, 2011; Wang, 2011), which
042   led to the well-known Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004b) that is still
043   popular today. However, progress continues to be made. Most recent methods leverage the internal
044   features of deep neural networks, which are said to mimic the processing architecture of the human
045   visual cortex (Yamins & DiCarlo, 2016; Lindsay, 2021). A popular example of the latter is Learned
046   Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) (Zhang et al., 2018). In the end, though, the best choice of
047   metric may depend on the application. For example, in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), the goal is
048   to provide the radiologist with an image recovery that leads to an accurate diagnosis. A recent clinical
049   MRI study (Kastryulin et al., 2023) found that, among 35 tested metrics, Deep Image Structure and
050
      Texture Similarity (DISTS) (Ding et al., 2020a) correlated best with radiologists’ perceptions.
051   In this work, our goal is to provide rigorous bounds on the FRIQ m(b                       b0 = h(y0 )
                                                                         x0 , x0 ) of a recovery x
052   relative to the true image x0 . Here, h(·) is an arbitrary image-recovery scheme and m(·, ·) is an
053   arbitrary FRIQ metric. The key challenge is that x0 is unknown. To our knowledge, there exists no
      prior work on providing FRIQ guarantees in image recovery. Our contributions are as follows.

064   From the perspective of uncertainty quantification (UQ), one could say that our goal is to bound
065   the uncertainty on FRIQ m(b      x0 , x0 ) that arises due to x0 being unknown. As such, our approach
066   to UQ differs from typical ones in image recovery. There, uncertainty is typically quantified on
067
      individual pixels, with the overall result being a pixel-wise uncertainty map. To construct these maps,
      one could use (approximate) posterior samplers (Durmus et al., 2018; Laumont et al., 2022; Zach
068
      et al., 2022; Tonolini et al., 2020; Edupuganti et al., 2021; Adler & Öktem, 2018; Bendel et al., 2023;
069
      Ardizzone et al., 2019; Wen et al., 2023a; Jalal et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2023) or Bayesian neural
070   networks (BNNs) (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Xue et al., 2019; Barbano et al., 2021; Ekmekci & Cetin,
071   2022; Narnhofer et al., 2022), including those based on dropout (Kendall & Gal, 2017), to draw many
072   reconstructions from the distribution of plausible x0 for a given y0 (i.e., the posterior distribution
073   pX0 |Y0 (·|y0 )), from which pixel-wise standard-deviations can be estimated. An alternative is to
074   utilize conformal prediction to produce pixel-wise intervals that are guaranteed to contain the true
075   pixel value with high probability (Angelopoulos et al., 2022b; Horwitz & Hoshen, 2022; Teneggi
076   et al., 2023; Kutiel et al., 2023; Narnhofer et al., 2024). Although these uncertainty maps can be
077   visually interesting, they do not quantify uncertainty on multi-pixel structures of interest, such as
078
      hallucinations or anatomical features relevant to medical diagnosis (e.g., tumors).
079   To our knowledge, there exist relatively few works on multi-pixel UQ, and none target FRIQ. For
080   example, Tang & Repetti (2023) use hypothesis testing to infer the presence/absence of a structure-of-
081   interest within the maximum a posteriori (MAP) image recovery, but relies on inpainting to construct
082   the structure-absent hypothesis, which may not be accurate. Sankaranarayanan et al. (2022) use
083   conformal prediction to compute uncertainty intervals on the presence/absence of semantic attributes
084
      (e.g., whether a face has a smile, glasses, etc.) but their method requires a “disentangled” generative
      adversarial network (GAN) that generates image samples given attribute probabilities. Belhasin
085
      et al. (2023) compute conformal prediction intervals on the principal components of the posterior
086
      covariance matrix. Lastly, given measurements y0 = A(x0 ) and a downstream imaging task µ(·) ∈ R
087   (e.g., soft-output classification), Wen et al. (2024) compute conformal bounds on the true task output
088   µ(x0 ). While interesting, none of the above works quantify the uncertainty on FRIQ metrics like
089   PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, DISTS, etc., due to x0 being unknown.

406
407   We now simulate our methods on accelerated multicoil MRI (Knoll et al., 2020; Hammernik et al.,
408   2023). MRI is a medical imaging technique known for excellent soft tissue contrast without subjecting
409   the patient to harmful ionizing radiation. MRI has slow scan times, though, which reduce patient
410
      throughput and comfort. In accelerated MRI, one collects only 1/R of the measurements specified
      by the Nyquist sampling theorem, thus speeding up the acquisition process by rate R. For R > 1,
411
      however, the inverse problem may become ill-posed, in which case one may be interested in bounding
412
      the FRIQ of the recovered image.
413

416
      we retrospectively sub-sample in the spatial Fourier domain (the “k-space”) using random Cartesian
      masks that give acceleration rates R ∈ {16, 8, 4, 2}. See App. E for additional details.

464   Multi-round Measurement: To showcase the practical impact of our bounds, we adapt the multi-
465
      round measurement protocol from (Wen et al., 2024), where measurements are collected over multiple
      rounds until the uncertainty bound falls below a threshold. In our setting, measurements are first
466
      collected at acceleration R = 16, an image recovery is computed, and a conformal upper-bound on
467
      its DISTS is computed. If the bounding value is lower than a pre-determined threshold τ , signifying
468   that the recovery is (with probability 1 − α) of sufficient diagnostic quality (Kastryulin et al., 2023),
469   then measurement collection stops. If not, additional measurements are collected and combined with
470   the previous ones to yield an acceleration of R = 8, and the process repeats. We allow up to five
471   measurement rounds, corresponding to final accelerations of R ∈ {16, 8, 4, 2, 1}.
472
      Once again, we report average results across T = 10 000 trials. Figure 6 plots the fraction of test
473
      image slices accepted by the multi-round protocol at each acceleration rate R. With the quantile
474   bound, the measurements stop after two rounds (i.e., R = 8) in more than 60% of the cases, and
475   after three rounds (i.e., R = 4) in more than 30% of the cases. With the non-adaptive bound, the
476   measurements stop after four rounds (i.e., R = 2) in all cases. Table 1 shows that, with the quantile
477   bound, the multi-round protocol attains an average acceleration of R = 5.42, which far surpasses
478   the R = 2 acceleration achieved with the non-adaptive bound. Table 1 also shows that the empirical
479   coverage of the multi-round accepted slices is very close to 1 − α, despite having only coverage
480   guarantees (6) for a single-round measurement at each acceleration rate. Figure 7 shows examples of
481   the image-error, the true DISTS, and its quantile upper-bound for each measurement round. With the
482
      threshold set at τ = 0.15, the example on the top would collect two rounds of measurements (i.e.,
      R = 8) while the example at the bottom would collect three rounds of measurements (i.e., R = 4), as

      demarcated by the red squares. See App. C for additional qualitative results.

509   Limitations: We acknowledge multiple limitations in our proposed methodology. 1) Our methods
510   require access to calibration data {(xi , yi )}ni=1 that is similar enough to the test data (x0 , y0 ) for the
511
      FRIQ pairs {(zi , zbi )}ni=0 to be modeled as statistically exchangeable. More work is required to make
      our methods robust to distribution shift (see App. D), although (Tibshirani et al., 2019; Barber et al.,
512
      2023; Cauchois et al., 2024) suggest some paths forward. 2) Our methods will be most impactful
513
      when there exists evidence that the FRIQ metric is well matched to the application (e.g., DISTS
514   for MRI (Kastryulin et al., 2023)). For some applications, additional work is required to determine
515   which metrics are more appropriate. 3) Our MRI application ideas are preliminary and not ready
516   for practical use; rigorous clinical trials are needed to tune and validate the methodology on a much
517   larger and diverse cohort of data. 4) The learned adaptive bound from Sec. 3.4 requires training a
518   quantile regression model, and our FFHQ denoising experiment suggests that it may not be easy to
519   significantly outperform the simpler adaptive bound from Sec. 3.3. 5) The posterior samplers that
520   we considered in our numerical experiments target only aleatoric uncertainty, and sharper conformal
521   bounds might be attained if epistemic uncertainty was also considered (e.g., (Ekmekci & Cetin,
522
      2023)). 6) Because our methods are based on CP (or, equivalently, conformal risk control under the
      indicator loss (Angelopoulos et al., 2022a)), the marginal guarantee (6) holds with probability 1 − α
523
      over random test data (e.g., Z   b0 , Z0 ) and calibration sets Dcal . A more fine-grained coverage could
524
      be achieved via the Risk-Controlling Prediction Sets (RCPS) framework from (Bates et al., 2021),
525
      which employs two user-selected error rates α, δ ∈ (0, 1) to yield coverage guarantees like

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533   For imaging inverse problems, we used conformal prediction to construct bounds on the FRIQ
534   of a recovered image relative to the unknown true image. When constructed using a calibration
535   set that is statistically exchangeable with the test sample, our bounds are guaranteed to hold with
536   high probability. Two of our methods leveraged approximate-posterior-sampling schemes to yield
537   tighter conformal bounds that adapt to the measurements and reconstruction. Our approaches were
538   demonstrated on image denoising and accelerated multicoil MRI, illustrating the broad applicability
539   of our work.

       In the case of MRI, such distributional shifts may arise for various reasons, some of which would be
1012
       easy to prevent while others would be more difficult. For example, if the CP method was calibrated
1013

1014   method on brain images. Likewise, if the CP method was calibrated with data from one manufacturer
1015   and/or strength of scanner, then it would be best to test on data from the same manufacturer and/or
1016   strength of scanner. Still, due to limited calibration data, situations may arise where a distribution
1017   shift is inevitable. Thus, we perform a study to analyze the sensitivity of our proposed method to
1018   distribution shifts.
1019
       For this study, we use the validation fold of the non-fat-suppressed multicoil fastMRI knee dataset
1020   Zbontar et al. (2018), which contains 100 3D volumes. A volume contains all the images collected for
1021   a single patient, with each image showing a different slice of the knee (from front to back). To induce
1022   a realistic yet controllable distribution shift, we choose calibration images from only the center slices
1023   of these volumes, and refer to the center slices as “location l = 0.” We then create one test set with
1024   images from slice locations l = 0, another test set with images from slice location l = 1, and so on,
1025   until slice location l = 10 (which typically corresponds to an edge slice). Example images from

1127   For the MRI experiments, we simulate the collection of measurements at four acceleration rates
1128   R = {16, 8, 4, 2}. These measurements are collected in the 2D spatial frequency domain known as
1129   k-space, and the pattern with which these samples are collected is called a sampling mask.
1130   For this study, we use a Cartesian sampling procedure where full lines of the 2D k-space are collected
1131   progressively. Starting with R = 16, we utilize a Golden Ratio Offset (GRO) (Joshi et al., 2022)
1132   sampling mask with GRO-specific parameters s = 15 and α = 8. This gives a fully-sampled region
1133   of 9 lines in the center of k-space known as the autocalibration signal (ACS) region. To simulate the
       iterative collection of measurements, we build upon this mask for R = 8. We first collect central

1174   Both fastMRI reconstruction models were trained once with all four acceleration rates. For each
1175   sample in an epoch, one of the four sampling masks is randomly drawn, allowing the model to see
1176   each sample at a different acceleration throughout the training.
1177   With the E2E-VarNet, we use the author’s codebase (Sriram et al., 2020b), which is released under
1178   an MIT license. For training, we utilize the default hyperparameters provided by the authors for the
1179   model on the fastMRI knee leaderboard. The model was trained for 50 epochs with a batch size of 16
1180   and learning rate of 0.0001 using SSIM (Wang et al., 2004a) as the loss function. This takes around
1181   38 hours on a single NVIDIA V100 with 32GB of memory.
1182   To train the CNF, we start with the author’s implementation (Wen et al., 2023b) that is available under
1183   an MIT license. We modify the architecture slightly in order to better handle multiple accelerations.
1184   First, we include an invertible attention module, iMAP (Sukthanker et al., 2022), to the end of the
1185   base flow step. Then, we increase the number of initial channels in the conditioning network to
1186   256. Using 2 layers and 10 flows steps in each layer, we train the CNF to minimize the negative
1187   log-likelihood objective. The model is trained for 150 epochs with batch size 8 and learning rate
       0.0001. On a single NVIDIA V100, this takes around 335 hours.
