011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce Diffusion Active Learning, a novel approach that integrates a gen-
014            erative diffusion model with sequential experimental design to adaptively acquire
015            data for solving inverse problems in imaging. We first pre-train an unconditional
016
               diffusion model on domain-specific data. The diffusion model is aimed to cap-
               ture the structure of the underlying data distribution, which is then leveraged in
017
               the active learning process. During the active learning loop, we use the forward
018
               model of the inverse problem together with the diffusion model to generate condi-
019            tional data samples from the posterior distribution, all consistent with the current
020            measurements. Based on the generated samples we quantify the uncertainty in the
021            current estimate in order to select the most informative next measurement. We
022            showcase the proposed approach for its application in X-ray computed tomogra-
023            phy imaging. Our results demonstrate significant reductions in data acquisition
024            requirements (i.e., lower X-ray dose) and improved image reconstruction quality
025            across several real-world tomography datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Computed Tomography (CT) is an imaging technique for reconstructing objects from X-ray projec-
031   tion data. The concept of tomography originated with the work of Radon in 1917, however, it was
032   not until 1971 that Godfrey Hounsfield and Allan Cormack developed the first practical CT scan-
033
      ner. Recent advances at large synchrotron facilities have pushed CT resolution into the nanometer
      range, enabling novel scientific applications, such as the inspection of composite materials, quality
034
      control of computer chips, and revealing cellular structure in biological tissues. Achieving a reso-
035
      lution as low as several nanometers requires image acquisition times of up to several days (Aidukas
036   et al., 2024). In such settings, the X-ray dose deposited onto the sample becomes a key limiting
037   factor, causing radiation damage which ultimately limits the achievable resolution (Howells et al.,
038   2009). Learning based reconstruction methods and data-driven ‘smart’ acquisition techniques are
039   a promising avenue to improve data efficiency, allowing for high-resolution reconstructions with
040   lower acquisition times and reduced X-ray dose.
041
      Mathematically, tomographic projections are described by the Radon transform (Deans, 1983) of
042   the object. In the corresponding inverse problem, multiple 2D projections from different angles are
043   combined into a single 3D reconstruction of the object (Fig. 1). However, traditional reconstruction
044   algorithms such as filtered back-projection or iterative reconstruction schemes only make use of
045   information contained in the measurements (Kak & Slaney, 2001), and neglect additional structure
046   in the data distribution. For example, tomographic scans of computer chips or composite materials
047   display highly regular structures (see Fig. 3). Such regularity can be learned from prior data sets and
048   facilitate improved learning-based reconstruction algorithms.
049   Moreover, standard CT acquisition scans are acquired by either rotating the object or the CT scan-
050   ner over an equidistant angle grid. Uniform scanning neglects any potential structure in the data
051   distribution, that could allow to achieve better reconstructions by scanning the most informative
052   angles. Active learning is a subfield of machine learning that studies algorithms for adaptive data
053   acquisition, with the goal to obtain the most informative data points (Settles, 2009). However, the
      adoption of active learning in applications remains challenging, with literature highlighting instances

054
      where active learning methods have shown limited effectiveness compared to conventional uniform
055   or i.i.d. training schemes (e.g., Lowell et al., 2019). This is due to the many underlying challenges
056   in sequential decision-making algorithms, such as the need for uncertainty quantification, as well as
057   added computational and implementation complexity. The effectiveness of active learning further
058   relies on exploiting inherent structure in the data distribution and on statistical modeling assumptions
059   (Balcan et al., 2010), which are often difficult to specify for the domain of interest.
060
      In this work, we set to address the outlined challenges in computed tomography by combining
061   a learned generative prior with data-driven experimental design. We introduce Diffusion Active
062   Learning (DAL), which combines generative diffusion models with active learning to solve inverse
063   problems in imaging. To leverage the structure of the data distribution, we pre-train the diffusion
064   model on data collected from slices of tomographic reconstructions (e.g., image slices of integrated
065   circuits or composite materials). In the active learning loop, we generate samples from the posterior
066   distribution of the diffusion model, conditioned on the measurements collected so far. Based on the
067   generated samples, we quantify the uncertainty in the solution of the inverse problem, and use it to
068   select where to sample next. By repeating this process, and as more measurements are incorporated,
069
      we effectively constrain the Diffusion posterior distribution until it collapses to a deterministic,
      data-consistent final estimate.
070
071   Using a diffusion model as a prior for the active learning enables several key advantages: (i) Unlike
072   traditional regularizers (e.g., Total Variation), the learned prior is data-dependent and captures prob-
073   lem specific structure. (ii) The structure can be extracted from (large-scale) prior data, avoiding
074   to manually specify intricate regularities in the statistical modeling approach. (iii) The diffusion
075
      model successfully captures multi-modal, highly structured distributions (e.g., natural images). This
      is unlike, for example, the Gaussian Laplace approximation used in prior works (Antoran et al.,
076
      2023; Barbano et al., 2022a), which are inherently unimodal.
077
078   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach on three real-world tomography datasets and
079   conduct an extensive evaluation of different acquisition strategies. Our results shows that DAL
080   provides significant improvements in reconstruction quality with fewer measurements compared to
081
      conventional uniformly sampled projection measurements. For the scientific datasets benchmarked
      in this paper, we achieve the same average Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) in the reconstruction
082
      with up to four times fewer measurements, which translates to up to 4× reduction in X-ray dose
083
      (see Table 1). Furthermore, we achieve this with more than 2× computational speed-ups compared
084   to the second-fastest baseline (Barbano et al., 2022a) (see Figure 5)
085
086   Remark: In a sparse reconstruction settings, diffusion-based approaches can introduce artifacts or
087
      hallucinations as not enough data is measured; preventing a direct use in high-stakes setting such
      as medical diagnosis (medical X-ray scans are also very fast, and therefore not considered a direct
088
      application of the proposed approach). The sparse reconstruction setting, however, is inherently
089
      ill-posed, and as such, a learned prior provides a good trade-off between provable accuracy and
090   data-efficiency.

094
      There is a long history of work that uses ideas from computer vision to improve image quality in
095
      tomography and medical imaging, see e.g., (Li et al., 2022; Parvaiz et al., 2023) for comprehen-
096   sive surveys. Building on ideas from image denoising and segmentation (Ronneberger et al., 2015),
097   Ulyanov et al. (2018) propose the Deep Image Prior (DIP), which utilizes the structure of a randomly
098   initialized convolutional network (U-net) as a prior for image reconstruction tasks. This approach
099   demonstrated that even without pre-training, deep neural network architectures can provide an im-
100   plicit bias that improves image reconstruction quality. The DIP methodology has influenced various
101   subsequent works that incorporate deep learning architectures in inverse problem settings, e.g., using
102   pre-training and an initial reconstruction (Baguer et al., 2020; Barbano et al., 2022b).
103   The success of diffusion-based models in image reconstruction has also been extended to solve in-
104   verse problems. When working with ill-posed inverse problems and using sparse measurements, the
105   goal is to use the diffusion model as a prior to fill in the missing information in the reconstruction.
106   However, sampling from the posterior distribution conditioned on the measurements is a challeng-
107   ing task. As the number of measurements may be different every time, techniques for conditional
      sampling used in common text-to-image model have not gained traction for inverse problem solv-

139   Active learning is a huge field (e.g., Settles, 2009) with roots in Bayesian experimental design
140   (Chaloner & Verdinelli, 1995). The active learning literature has mostly focused on the classifi-
141   cation setting, where the goal is to reduce the labelling effort by actively querying the label for the
142   most uncertain data points. These ideas have been applied to deep learning Ren et al. (2021) and
143   medical imaging Budd et al. (2021) as well. Gal et al. (2017) discuss various acquisition strategies
144   for active learning with image data, also applicable in the regression setting relevant to this work.
145   Only few prior works have explored experimental design for computed tomography. Closely related
146   is the work by Barbano et al. (2022a), who introduce Bayesian experimental design for computed
147
      tomography. Their main innovation is the use of a Linearized Laplace Deep Image Prior for uncer-
      tainty quantification Antoran et al. (2022) to guide the acquisition of measurement data. Although
148
      this demonstrates the applicability of sequential experimental design in the CT setting, the evalua-
149
      tion is still limited to a simplistic toy example. Subsequently, Antoran et al. (2023) further scale the
150   Laplace approach using a sampling based technique and demonstrate it on a CT reconstruction task,
151   although not in combination with active learning.
152
153
      Going well-beyond one-step (greedy) active learning schemes, reinforcement learning (RL) pro-
      vides a framework to solve a multi-stage planning problem over the combinatorial space of possible
154
      experimental designs. This is prominently explored in the related field of magnetic resonance imag-
155
      ing (MRI), see, for example, the works by Zhang et al. (2019); Pineda et al. (2020); Bakker et al.
156   (2020); Jin et al. (2019). Similarly, RL based methods for computed tomography are proposed by
157   Wang et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2022). These approaches primarily address the problem of choosing
158   an optimal sequence of measurements for a given reconstruction method; this comes at the price of
159   increased algorithmic and training complexity compared to greedy active learning schemes. We also
160   remark that unlike MRI, the tomographic forward model is linear, and greedy approaches provably
161   converge (Riquelme et al., 2017). In principle, RL based methods can be combined with a learned
      prior; this is however beyond the scope of this work.

196    A few important challenges arise. First, the CT problem is typically too high-dimensional to be
197    solved in closed form, and hence one has to resort to iterative or gradient descent based schemes.
198    This means that uncertainty quantification methods that require the second moment of the posterior

201

202    regularizer (e.g., L2 or Total-Variation loss), or other means of adding an inductive bias (e.g., using

212     3   D IFFUSION ACTIVE L EARNING
213
214    We now describe Diffusion Active Learning (DAL), a novel approach for data-driven, ‘smart’ angle
215    selection in computed tomography. In a pre-training step, we train an unconditional Denoising Dif-
       fusion Probabilist Model (DDPM) on a training set consisting of tomographic slices from objects in


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      the domain of interest. This training is entirely independent of the inference (reconstruction) given
217   the X-ray measurements, and requires only samples of tomographic slices to learn the image distri-
218   bution. Our implementation uses the classical DDPM training (Ho et al., 2020). During the active
219   learning loop, we use the trained diffusion model to approximate the posterior distribution condi-
220   tioned on the current set of measurements (X-ray projections). We use a variant of the techniques
221   proposed in Song et al. (2023) to generate these conditional samples. Given the relatively small size
222   of the images in our study, we opt for a pixel-space diffusion model rather than a latent-space diffu-
223   sion model. More generally, we emphasize that any generative posterior can be used for the active
224   learning procedure described below. A more detailed comparison of different diffusion models is,
225
      however, beyond the scope of this work.
226   In the active learning loop, we use conditional samples from the diffusion model to approximate
227   the uncertainty of the estimation. Using the forward model, we map the diffusion samples to the
228   measurement space, to obtain the posterior distributions of the projections. Finally, we choose the
229   angle that has the largest uncertainty to take the next measurement and repeat until our measurement
230   budget is depleted. The active learning step is described in more detail in Section 3.2. The complete
231
      diffusion active learning framework is outlined in Algorithm 1.
232   From a Bayesian perspective, the Diffusion model corresponds to a learned prior p(x) over images
233   x ∈ Rd×d . Diffusion posterior inference approximates the posterior, p(x|Y, ψ) ∝ p(x)p(Y|x, ψ)
234   for measurements Y. The likelihood term is specified by the forward model in Eq. (1) and the noise
235   distribution. The posterior (mean) distribution of a new measurement y ψnew ∈ Rd at angle ψnew is
236   specified  again by the forward model, marginalized over the posterior, p(y ψnew |Y, ψ, ψnew ) =
        Aψnew (x)p(x|Y, ψ)dx. Equivalently, we can sample from the predictive posterior by sam-
      R

239   Aψnew (x̃). We choose the angle ψnew to maximize the total posterior variance, tr(Cov[ỹ ψnew ]),
240   also known as uncertainty sampling (Settles, 2009).

      3.2   S AMPLING -BASED ACTIVE L EARNING
290
291   The selection process in active learning usually involves an information criteria (sometimes called
292   acquisition function) which scores the informativeness of each possible measurement. To this end,
293   most methods make use of uncertainty quantification, which, at the same time, poses one of the ma-
294   jor challenges in the deep learning setting. The literature proposes a plethora of different acquisition
295   functions (c.f. Settles, 2009; Gal et al., 2017; Ren et al., 2021), depending on the setting (e.g., regres-
296   sion, classification), the statistical model (e.g., linear, deep neural networks), and the learning target
297
      (e.g., model identification, PAC). A common approach is to use a Bayesian model and (approximate)
      information theoretic measures such as mutual information. However, the pure Bayesian approach
298
      is often intractable beyond linear models, requiring further approximations.
299
300   We formulate the acquisition process in our sampling-based framework. At time step t of the active
301   learning loop, we sample k images x1t , . . . , xkt ∈ Rd×d from the (approximate) posterior distribu-
302   tion. We use the conditional diffusion model to obtain the samples, but the formulation is general
303
      and works with any generative model. In this light, we can also view the sampled Laplace approx-
      imation of Barbano et al. (2022a) in the same framework. Intuitively, the samples x1t , . . . , xkt are
304
      consistent with the observation data, but differ in places where there is not enough data to con-
305
      strain the posterior distribution. Our goal is to acquire additional measurements that differentiate
306   the samples x1t , . . . , xkt . We choose the angle ψt+1 that maximizes the posterior total variance,

      4.1     DATASETS
340
      Chip Data. Our first dataset is an integrated circuit measured with ptychographic X-ray laminog-
341   raphy (PyXL) (Holler et al., 2019b;a). PyXL uses ptychography to scan the sample with a coherent
342   X-ray beam, acquiring diffraction patterns for projection reconstruction at multiple rotation angles.
343   The sample rotation axis is tilted relative to the detector, allowing high-resolution, non-destructive
344   imaging of planar samples across various scales. The dataset features a 3D volume of an integrated
345   circuit, with large metal interconnects in the upper layers and progressively smaller features towards
346   the transistor layer at the bottom.
347
348   Composite Materials. As a second dataset, we use tomography data of a composite material
349   (Auenhammer et al., 2020a;b). This dataset contains 3D tomographic reconstructions of non-crimp
350   fabric reinforced composites, which captures the arrangement and orientation of the fiber bundles
351
      and the matrix in which they are embedded. Such composites are extensively used in wind turbine
      blades and consist of fiber bundles aligned in one direction with stitching yarns to aid in manufac-
352
      turing and handling. Hence, this dataset was used for the creation of precise finite element models
353
      to simulate and analyze the mechanical behavior of the materials, particularly their stiffness and
354   response to fatigue.
355
356
      Lung Data. The LIDC/IDRI dataset (Armato III et al., 2011) is designed specifically for training
357   and comparing deep learning-based methods for low-dose CT reconstruction, and consists of helical
358   thoracic CT scans. We chose 40,000 CT scan slices from LIDC/IDRI , data from approximately
359   800 patients, to define the dataset used in this paper. We use the same 40,000 examples chosen
360   by Leuschner et al. (2019).
361
      Due to computational constraints, we worked with two image sizes namely, 128×128 and 512×512
362
      pixels. To produce smaller images we used cropping and rescaling of the slices of the reconstructed
363   3D objects in the datasets. For 128 × 128 images, we took 256 × 256 crops from the chip and
364   composite material slices, and then rescaled them to size 128 × 128 using bilinear interpolation.
365   For the Lung dataset, we rescaled directly each of the slices in the dataset to 128 × 128. For the
366   512 × 512 images, we simply took crops of size 512 × 512 from the CT reconstruction datasets
367   described above. All these crops are independent with no overlap. The final images are then split
368   into disjoint train and test datasets.
369   In all cases, we use the Radon transform to generate the projection data synthetically using parallel
370   beam geometry. For training the diffusion models, we use data augmentation on the train data by
371   random rotation and scaling (1x to 1.3x). The evaluation on the test set was run on P100 compute
372   nodes (one node per instance). For pre-training the diffusion models, we used a single A100 node.

445   We compared the performance of several baselines including four different generative models and
446   non-adaptive uniform acquisition (computed sequentially by halving the remaining angle space, e.g.,

448
      and Laplace, and for acquisition we consider a non-adaptive, uniform allocation, and active learning
      based on (3). We consider other acquisition function in Appendix D, but we found no significant
449
      difference among the tested variants. In addition, we consider two settings: with and without pre-
450
      scan. In the pre-scan setting, there is a previously computed low-resolution scan of the object
451   that can be used as a prior in the reconstruction process. Pre-scanning is a common procedure at
452   synchrotron X-ray beamlines to get a quick estimate of the sample using much lower radiation. All
453   models are conditioned on the pre-scan data in the same way as for the other measurements. Our
454   experiments show that diffusion models with active learning consistently outperforms other methods
455   in terms of Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) and computational efficiency. We showcase other
456   metrics in Appendix C, which also follow a similar pattern.
457   Figure 4 summarizes the performance on our three datasets for images of size 128 × 128. If no
458   pre-scan is used, diffusion models outperform all other generative models in terms of PSNR with up
459   to 4.3× reduction in the number of measurements needed and, while the gap is dataset-dependent,
460   there is always a clear advantage in using diffusion models to generate tomographic reconstruc-
461   tions. In terms of acquisition functions, we benchmark against uniform acquisition in angular space.
462   While being data-distribution independent, uniform remains on par with active learning acquisition
463   functions for the Lung dataset. This can be attributed to the fact that there are not clear directions of
464   preference for lung images and their features appear isotropic in all directions. For chip and com-
465
      posite materials samples however, active learning acquisitions based in (3) are able to identify the
      most relevant directions, and clearly outperform the uniform strategy. Table 1 summarizes the gains
466
      of diffusion active learning in terms of number of measurements for a target PSNR of 30 dB.
467
468   When using pre-scan, the different generative models can make out the rough shape of the object
469   from the beginning of the experiment, which leads into higher quality reconstructions over the whole
470   range of measurements. This in turn provides more information to decide which measurement to
471
      take next. This is particularly noticeable for SWAG which becomes competitive with Bootstrap and
      Laplace. Diffusion models still provide better reconstructions. However, after sufficient number of
472
      measurements (e.g., 50 for Lung data and 100 for chip data) there is no clear advantage in using the
473
      learned prior.

      et al. (2022a). In the context of long image acquisition times of up to several days (Aidukas et al.,

796   diffusion process. This is not the case with ReSample, where they have to pick a small subset of

798   map back our estimate to the manifold defined by the noisy samples at time t and continue with the

819   MRI operates within the same framework described in Equation (1), with y ψ = Aψ (x) + ϵ, where
820   x is a complex value object, Aψ (x) is a forward model consisting of taking the Fourier transform,
821   which produces the k-space of x, followed by a masking that takes a single row (or column) specified
822   by the index ψ. The set of possible measurements is then the set Y ψ of all possible rows of the k-
823   space of x. Assuming a Gaussian distribution of the noise ϵ, the reconstruction problem can be
824   solved using maximum-likelihood inference given by Equation 2.
825
      Given the similarity between this setup and the CT setup studied in the main body of this paper, we
826   extend our framework to work with MRI reconstructions using the formulation of Diffusion Active
827   Learning and Algorithm 1.
