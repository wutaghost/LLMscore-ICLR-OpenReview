009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We present a novel approach called differentially private stochastic block coordinate
012            descent (DP-SBCD) for training neural networks with provable guarantees of
013            differential privacy under the hidden state assumption. Our methodology regards
014            neural networks as optimization problems and decomposes the training process
015            of the neural network into sub-problems, each corresponding to the training of a
016            specific layer. By doing so, we extend the analysis of differential privacy under
017            the hidden state assumption to encompass non-convex problems and algorithms
018            employing proximal gradient descent. Furthermore, in contrast to existing methods,
019            we adopt a novel approach by utilizing calibrated noise sampled from adaptive
020
               distributions, yielding improved empirical trade-offs between utility and privacy.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Machine learning models, especially deep neural networks, have exhibited remarkable progress in the
026   last decade across diverse fields. Their applications, such as face recognition (Erkin et al., 2009) and
027   large language models (Kandpal et al., 2022), have been integrated into people’s daily lives. However,
028   the increasing demand for large amounts of training data in the training process has given rise to
029   growing concerns regarding the potential privacy vulnerabilities (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Shokri et al.,
030   2017) associated with these models. For example, deep neural networks are shown to memorize the
031   training data (Zhang et al., 2017) so that we can even reconstruct part of the training data from the
032   learned model parameters (Haim et al., 2022). To address these issues, differential privacy (Dwork,
033
      2006) has become the gold standard for making formal and quantitative guarantees on model’s privacy
      and has been widely applied in learning problems (Abadi et al., 2016; Ha et al., 2019).
034
035   The predominate approach to ensuring differential privacy in the context of machine learning involves
036   the incorporation of calibrated noise during each update step in the training phase, which leads to
037   a trade-off between utility and privacy loss (Allouah et al., 2023; Alvim et al., 2012). Excessive
038   noise often leads to a significant loss in utility, while insufficient noise may result in privacy leakage.
039
      Moreover, many privacy accountant methods, such as moment accountant (Abadi et al., 2016),
      are typically based on the composition properties of differential privacy. Specifically, they usually
040
      assume that the calibrated noise in each training iteration follows the same distribution and that the
041
      algorithm’s internal states, i.e., the model’s parameters in each training step, can be revealed to the
042   adversaries. As a result, the total privacy loss of the algorithm significantly increases with the number
043   of training iterations, because more internal states will be revealed with more training iterations.
044   Such assumptions lead to a very loose privacy estimation of the algorithm, because, in practice, most
045   internal states are usually not even recorded during training.
046
      The gap between theory and practice motivates researchers to consider more practical assumptions.
047
      Recently, the hidden state assumption was proposed to narrow down this gap (Chourasia et al., 2021;
048   Feldman et al., 2018; Ye and Shokri, 2022). This assumption posits that the internal states of the
049   training phase are hidden, and that the adversaries only have access to the last iterate. Under this
050   assumption, Chourasia et al. (2021); Ye and Shokri (2022) use Langevin diffusion to track the change
051   rate of Rényi Differential Privacy (RDP) in each epoch and bound the privacy loss for Differential
052   Privacy Gradient Descent (DP-GD) and Differential Privacy Stochastic mini-batch Gradient Descent
053   (DP-SGD) (Abadi et al., 2016). Recently, Asoodeh and Diaz (2023) also considered directly using
      hockey-stick divergence instead of Rényi divergence.These works claim a converged and small

076   From a theoretical aspect, existing theorems (Feldman et al., 2018; Chourasia et al., 2021; Ye and
077
      Shokri, 2022) indicate a valuable property that privacy loss could converge under the hidden state
      assumption in learning problems with strongly convex and β-smooth loss functions. Otherwise, the
078
      privacy loss based on their analysis framework will increase exponentially. Consequently, these
079
      theorems cannot be directly applied to training deep neural networks, whose loss functions are
080   non-convex and may exhibit non-smoothness when using activation functions like ReLU (Krizhevsky
081   et al., 2012).
082
083
      To extend existing analyses of differential privacy under the hidden state assumptions to deep learning
      problems, we propose differentially private stochastic block coordinate descent (DP-SBCD) to avoid
084
      the exponentially-increasing privacy loss for the traditional DP-SGD algorithm. By contrast, our
085
      analyses provide tight theoretical guarantees in privacy loss under the hidden state assumption when
086   using DP-SBCD. As demonstrated in Figure 1, we first decouple the activation function to convert the
087   task of training neural networks as a constrained optimization problem and subsequently construct
088   the associated Lagrangian function. The DP-SBCD algorithm then considers each layer individually,
089   decomposing the original learning problem into multiple sub-problems. The loss function for each of
090   these sub-problems is convex, which facilitates our analyses to derive a tight privacy loss bound under
091   the hidden state assumption. It is worth noting that our algorithm’s privacy loss under the hidden
092   state assumption is significantly smaller than that derived under the composition theory.
093   In addition, this work investigates the connections between the calibrated noise and the smoothness
094   of the loss function in the context of differential privacy. Generally speaking, calibrated noise and
095   the smoothness of the loss function both contribute to the privacy loss. However, how the calibrated
096   noise influence the total privacy loss under the hidden state, which is still unknown so far, is valuable
097   in improve the privacy-utility trade-off (Du et al., 2021). Intuitively speaking, we need noise of larger
098   variance to achieve the same level of differential privacy if the Lipschitz constant of the loss gradient
099   is bigger, but noise of large variance harms the utility when the algorithm approaches the optimal
100
      solution. This paper study the impact of the variance of the calibrated noise on differential privacy in
      different training phases and propose to adaptively adjust it during training. We also explain why the
101
      privacy loss will converge under the hidden state assumption. Empirically, our proposed algorithm
102
      with adaptive calibrated noise achieves a better trade-off between the model’s utility and privacy.
103
104   In summary, we highlight our contributions as follows:
105
106                • We propose differentially private stochastic block coordinate descent (DP-SBCD), which, to
107                  the best of our knowledge, is the first feasible method to solve non-convex problems with
                     differential privacy guarantees under the hidden state assumption.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
123
      Differential Privacy Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork, 2006) was initially introduced to quan-
124
      titatively ensure the privacy guarantees within the realm of databases. Nevertheless, its rigorous
125
      mathematical foundations and nice properties, such as composition theorem and post-processing
126   immunity, have raised more and more interest in its application in machine learning. Among the
127   practical methods for achieving DP-guaranteed models, DP-SGD (Song et al., 2013) is the most
128   popular one, which involves the addition of calibrated noise to the clipped per-sample gradients in
129   each iterative update.
130
      Besides the DP-guaranteed training algorithm, the proper accounting of privacy loss is also critical.
131
      Most accountant methods rely on the composition theorem of differential privacy. In this regard, Abadi
132   et al. (2016) introduces the momentum accountant, Mironov (2017) further proposes Rényi differential
133   privacy (RDP), which turns out a better tool to analyze the algorithm’s privacy loss. Specifically,
134   a randomized mechanism M : D → R is said to have (α, ε)-RDP if for any adjacent datasets
135   d, d′ ∈ D, it holds that Dα (M(d)||M(d′ )) ≤ ε where Dα denotes the Rényi divergence of order α.
136
      Recently, the hidden state assumption has been explored in many works (Chourasia et al., 2021;
137
      Feldman et al., 2018; Ye and Shokri, 2022). While the composition theory assumes that adversaries
138   have access to the intermediate states of the algorithm, which requires the summation of the privacy
139   loss across each training iteration (Ponomareva et al., 2023), the hidden state assumption posits that
140   these intermediate states remain concealed. In real-life applications, the intermediate states usually
141   cannot be tracked, so the hidden state assumption is more practical. It is first proposed in (Feldman
142   et al., 2018), which also proves that a sequence of contractive maps can exhibit bounded (α, ε)-RDP.
143   Subsequently, Chourasia et al. (2021) consider the update scheme as a Langevin diffusion process
144   and analyze the privacy loss for DP-GD using the log-Sobolev inequality (Vempala and Wibisono,
145   2019). Expanding upon this, Ye and Shokri (2022) analyze DP-SGD by leveraging the convergent
146
      analysis of unadjusted Langevin algorithm proposed by Wibisono (2019).
147   Block Coordinate Descent The block coordinate descent algorithm is designed to decompose the
148   original problem into several sub-problems which allow for independent and efficient solutions. It
149   not only helps prevent the vanishing gradient problem (Zhang and Brand, 2017) but also facilitates
150   distributed or parallel implementations (Mahajan et al., 2017). In the era of deep learning, many
151   works focus on employing block coordinate descent to train neural networks (Gu et al., 2020; Zeng
152
      et al., 2019; Zhang and Brand, 2017). Zhang and Brand (2017) utilize a lifting trick to solve the
      learning problem of deep neural networks with ReLU activation function, which is then extended
153
      by Gu et al. (2020); Mangold et al. (2022). Furthermore, Zeng et al. (2019) comprehensively analyzes
154
      two general types of optimization formulas: Two-splitting and Three-splitting. They also investigate
155   the loss function, activation function, and architecture that these algorithms require.

425   As discussed above, the bound of εK (α, j0 ) in Theorem 4.5 elucidates how each epoch influences
426   the cumulative privacy loss, exhibiting a decay rate. Specifically, these contributions are inversely
427   proportional to o(η, j, k), i.e., the variance of the noise, and decrease exponentially 1 as the number
428   of iterations and epochs increase. That is to say, under the hidden state assumption, the calibrated
429   noise in the last few epochs primarily contributes to the total privacy loss.

444   converges when a constant magnitude of noise is utilized in the later stages of training. Conversely,
445   the privacy loss diverges if the noise magnitude continues to decrease in the late phase of training. In
446   contrast, increasing the noise magnitude can even lead to a decrease in privacy loss. However, it is
447
      worth noting that noise with a smaller variance tends to yield better utility for the model compared
      to noise with a larger variance, which is consistent with the utility-privacy trade-off (Allouah et al.,
448
      2023; Alvim et al., 2012).

451   5    D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
452
453   Feldman et al. (2018); Ye and Shokri (2022) shows that the privacy loss can converge under the hidden
454   state assumption for strongly convex problem. However, such appealing result cannot be applied to
455   neural network training because of the high non-convexity of the corresponding loss function (1).
456   In this work, instead of directly use the gradient descent in training, we proposed the Differentially
457   Private Stochastic Block Coordinate Descent (DP-SBCD) algorithm and calculate the corresponding
458   privacy loss under the hidden state assumption, which is tight and generally applicable. To the best of
459
      our knowledge, our method is the first feasible method to solve non-convex problems with differential
      privacy guarantees under the hidden state assumption. We also implement our algorithm on various
460
      of datasets in Appendix B.1 to show the validation of our algorithm.
461
462   Furthermore, our privacy loss analysis further explains why the privacy loss will converge under the
463   hidden state assumption. As demonstrated in the Section 4.3, the privacy loss under the hidden state
464   assumption does not actually converge. An equilibrium solution emerge once the calibrated noise
465
      is fixed, and it will modify once the calibrated noise is changed. Moreover, Inspired by the fact Yu
      et al. (2019); Du et al. (2021) that adaptively allocate privacy budget could improve the utility-privacy
466
      trade-off. Our algorithm also consider adaptively modify the calibrated noise. Although our paper
467
      does not provide further theoretical analysis on what is the optimal adaptive calibrated noise due to
468   the page limitation, the experiment in Appendix B.2 shows that adaptively choosing the calibrated
469   noise could empirically provide a better utility-privacy trade-off under the hidden state assumption.
470
471
      Although our algorithm has a lower privacy loss than DP-SGD, it’s important to note that direct
      comparisons of privacy loss may be misleading because of different privacy loss assumptions: DP-
472
      SBCD is under the hidden state assumption and the DP-SGD is under the composition theorem. As
473
      demonstrated in the Section 4.3, the privacy loss under the hidden state assumption is predominated
474   by the last few epochs, but the privacy loss under the composition theorem is proportional to the
475   training iterations. In the Appendix B.1, we list the privacy loss of DP-SGD to provide an intuitive
476   comparison. Moreover, the DP-SBCD algorithm and the DP-SGD algorithm have totally different
477   parameter settings, such as the optimal step size and batch size, which is important in both the privacy
478   loss accountant and the algorithm’s utility.
479
      Compare with the SGD algorithm, the coordinate descent algorithm requires less memory in training
480   process because it only update one block of the model parameters. Recently, Luo et al. (2024) shows
481   that the coordinate descent algorithm can reduce the memory consumption in large language training.
482   However, coordinate descent also introduces new challenges: the stochastic of mini-batch and the
483   block-wise parameter update leads to a high variance of the mini-batch gradient. In our experiment,
484   we use large batch size to effectively mitigate the high variance of the mini-batch gradient, which
485   increases memory consumption and might influence the utility. Although there are many works
      discussing variance reduction (Ding and Li, 2020; Gorbunov et al., 2020; Ding and Li, 2021) and large


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      batch training technique (Keskar et al., 2016; Hoffer et al., 2017), further improving our algorithm
487   requires rigorously analyzing the algorithm’s convergence and exploring the integration of these
488   techniques. We leave this as future works.

491   6    C ONCLUSION
492
493   We propose differentially private stochastic block coordinate descent (DP-SBCD) algorithm, which
494
      includes proximal gradient descent and adaptive noise, to train neural networks. As far as we are
      aware, DP-SBCD is the first algorithm capable of addressing non-convex training problems while
495
      ensuring a tight differential privacy guarantee under the hidden state assumption. Our theoretical
496
      analyses indicate different contributions of privacy loss in different training phases under the hidden
497   state assumption, inspiring the idea of adaptively adjusting the calibrated noise during training. The
498   adaptive noise proposed in our method can provide adjustable trade-offs between the model’s utility
499   and privacy. Moreover, our observations indicate that under proper settings, DP-SBCD could provide
500   a better trade-off between utility and privacy. Going forward, our future research will concentrate on
501   further refining the convergence and performance of the algorithm.
