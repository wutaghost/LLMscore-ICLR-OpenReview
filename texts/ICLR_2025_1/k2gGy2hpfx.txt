009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently there has been a surge of interest to deploy confidence set predictions
012            rather than point predictions. Unfortunately, the effectiveness of such prediction
013            sets is frequently impaired by distribution shifts in practice, and the challenge
014            is often compounded by the lack of ground truth labels at test time. Focusing
015            on a standard set-valued prediction framework called conformal prediction (CP),
016
               this paper studies how to improve its practical performance using only unlabeled
               data from the shifted test domain. This is achieved by two new methods called
017
               ECP and EACP, whose main idea is to adjust the score function in CP according to
018
               its base model’s own uncertainty evaluation. Through extensive experiments on a
019            number of large-scale datasets and neural network architectures, we show that our
020            methods provide consistent improvement over existing baselines and nearly match
021            the performance of fully supervised methods.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Advances in deep learning are fundamentally changing the autonomous decision making pipeline.
027
      While most works have focused on accurate point predictions, quantifying the uncertainty of the
      model is arguably as important. Taking autonomous driving for example: if a detection model
028
      predicts the existence of an obstacle, it would be reasonable to take different maneuvering strategies
029
      depending on the confidence of the prediction. But is that reliable? In a possible failure mode, the
030   model could report 60% (resp. 99%) confidence, but the probability of an obstacle actually showing
031   up is 99% (resp. 60%). Such discrepancy between the model’s own uncertainty evaluation and the
032   ground truth probability (or post-hoc frequency) is actually very common (Guo et al., 2017; Liang
033   et al., 2023), and can significantly compromise the safety in downstream decision making.
034
      Set-valued prediction provides an effective way to address this problem (Chzhen et al., 2021), with
035
      conformal prediction (CP; Vovk et al., 2005) being its most well-known special case. Given a fixed
036   black-box machine learning model (called the base model) and a covariate xtest , the goal of CP is
037   to generate a small prediction set Ctest that contains (or, covers) the unknown ground truth label
038   ytest with a pre-specified probability. Crucially, CP relies on the assumption that the distribution of
039   the data stream is exchangeable (a weaker variant of i.i.d.), which allows the fairly straightforward
040   inference of ytest from xtest and the base model’s performance on a pre-collected calibration dataset.
041   Note that the ground truth label ytest does not need to be revealed after the set prediction is made:
042   exchangeability together with a large enough labeled calibration dataset is sufficient to ensure the
043   desirable coverage probability. This is particularly important for autonomous decision making, where
044
      real-time data annotation is expensive or even infeasible.
045   However, real-world data streams are usually corrupted by all sorts of distribution shifts, violating
046   the exchangeability assumption. Even when the data stream itself is exchangeable, we often want to
047   continually update the base model rather than keeping it fixed, and this can be effectively understood
048   as a distribution shift in this context. In such cases, simply applying exchangeability-based CP
049   methods could lead to highly inaccurate prediction sets (Tibshirani et al., 2019; Bhatnagar et al.,
050
      2023a; Kasa and Taylor, 2023). Therefore, making CP compatible with distribution shifts has become
      a focal point of recent works.
051
052   A number of solutions have been proposed, but the key challenge still remains. For example, Gibbs
053   and Candes (2021) formulated the connection between CP and Online Convex Optimization (OCO;
      Zinkevich, 2003), and the latter is able to handle arbitrarily distribution-shifted environments. The

054
      weakness is that ground truth labels are now required at test time (which we call full supervision),
055   as opposed to the standard CP procedure. In the other direction, there are CP methods that combat
056   distribution shifts without test time labels (Tibshirani et al., 2019; Barber et al., 2023; Cauchois et al.,
057   2024), but they typically assume the distribution shifts are “easy”, such that even without labels, we
058   can still rigorously infer the test distribution to a certain extent using the labeled calibration dataset.
059   Overall, it appears that handling both difficulties – distribution shifts and the lack of test time labels –
060   is a formidable but important challenge remaining in the literature.
061   Contributions Focusing on classification, this paper develops practical unsupervised methods to
062   improve the accuracy degradation of CP prediction sets under distribution shifts. The overarching
063   idea is to exploit the uncertainty evaluation of the base model itself. Although such a quantity is not
064   always calibrated in a strict sense, it has been consistently observed to strongly correlate with the
065   magnitude of distribution shifts (Hendrycks and Gimpel, 2017; Wang et al., 2021; Kang et al., 2024),
066   thus providing a valuable way to probe the test distribution without label access. Under this high
067   level idea, we make the following contributions.
068   • First, we propose a new CP-inspired method named ECP (Entropy scaled Conformal Prediction).
069     The key idea is to scale up the score function in standard CP by an “entropy quantile” of the base
070     model, calculated on the unlabeled test dataset. Such an entropy quantile measures the base model’s
071     own uncertainty on the test distribution, and is enforced to be greater than 1.1
072     More precisely, given each covariate xtest at test time, the score function in standard CP is
073     determined by the fixed base model, and assigns each candidate label a “propensity score”. Then,
074     the CP prediction set Ctest simply includes all the candidate labels whose score is above a certain
075     threshold.2 By scaling up the score function while keeping the threshold fixed, ECP makes the
076     prediction sets larger, which naturally corresponds to the intuition that the uncertainty of prediction
077
        should be inflated under distribution shifts. Moreover, the amount of such inflation is strongly
        correlated with the magnitude of the distribution shift, through the use of the entropy quantile.
078
      • Second, we refine ECP using techniques from unsupervised Test Time Adaptation (TTA) (Niu et al.,
079
        2022), and the resulting method is named EACP (Entropy base-adapted Conformal Prediction).
080
        The key idea is that while ECP keeps the base model fixed at test time, we can concurrently update
081     it using entropy minimization (Grandvalet and Bengio, 2004; Wang et al., 2021) – a widely adopted
082     idea in unsupervised TTA, alongside the aforementioned entropy scaling. This “adaptively” reduces
083     the scaling effect that ECP applies to the score function, thus shrinking the prediction sets of
084     ECP smaller.
085   • Finally, we evaluate the proposed methods on a wide range of large-scale datasets under distribution
086     shifts, as well as different neural network architectures. We find that exchangeability-based CP
087     (with and without TTA on the base model) consistently leads to lower-than-specified coverage
088     frequency. However, despite the absence of practical statistical guarantees in this setting, our
089     methods can effectively mitigate this under-coverage issue while keeping the sizes of the prediction
090     sets moderate. Furthermore, our methods also significantly improve the prediction sets generated by
091
        the base model itself (without CP). It shows that by bridging the CP procedure (which is statistically
        sound) and the base model’s own uncertainty evaluation (which is often informative), our methods
092
        enjoy the practical benefit from both worlds.

097   CP under distribution shifts Considerable efforts have been devoted to developing CP methods

      robust to distribution shifts, which can be approximately categorized into two directions. The first
      direction does not require test time labels (Tibshirani et al., 2019; Cauchois et al., 2024), but the

      distribution shift is assumed to be simple in some sense. The second direction is connecting CP to
100
      adversarial online learning (Gibbs and Candes, 2021), but the true labels are required at test time.
101   Due to space constraints, a thorough discussion is deferred to Appendix A, as well as a number of
102   applications that motivate this work.

      vised) TTA, whose goal is to update a trained machine learning model at test time, using unlabeled

116   3    P RELIMINARIES OF CP
117
118   We begin by introducing the standard background of CP without distribution shifts. For clarity, we
119   assume i.i.d. data in our exposition, rather than the slightly weaker notion of exchangeability. Also
120   see (Roth, 2022; Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023; Tibshirani, 2023).
121
      Let D be an unknown distribution on the space X ×Y of covariate-label pairs, and let α ∈ (0, 1) be the
122
      error rate we aim for. Given a calibration dataset D consisting of n i.i.d. samples {x∗i , yi∗ }i∈[n] ∼ Dn ,
123
      the goal of CP is to generate a set-valued function C : X → 2Y , such that
124
125                                P(xtest ,ytest )∼D,D∼Dn [ytest ∈ C(xtest )] ≥ 1 − α.                                 (1)
126   That is, for a fresh test sample (xtest , ytest ) ∼ D, our prediction set C(xtest ) covers the ground truth
127   label ytest with guaranteed high probability. Notice that Eq.(1) alone is a trivial objective, since it
128   suffices to predict the entire label space C(x) = Y for all x. Therefore, CP is essentially a bi-objective
129   problem: as long as Eq.(1) is satisfied, we want the prediction set C(x) to be small.
130   The main difficulty of this set-valued prediction problem is that the range of output 2Y is too large.
131   In this regard, the key idea of CP is reducing the problem to 1D prediction via a trained machine
132   learning model (called the base model), such as a neural network. Specifically, we assume access
133   to a (positively oriented; i.e., larger is better) score function s : X × Y → R+ given by the base
134   model, such that for each test covariate xtest ∈ X and candidate label y ∈ Y, s(xtest , y) measures
135   how likely the model believes that y is the true label ytest . Then, all there is left for CP is to pick a
136   threshold τD ∈ R that depends on the dataset D, and predict the label set (if the score function is
137   negatively oriented, then ≥ is replaced by ≤)
138                                     C(xtest ) := {y ∈ Y : s(xtest , y) ≥ τD } .                                     (2)
139
140   Under the i.i.d. assumption, the coverage objective Eq.(1) is satisfied by picking τD as the α(1−n−1 )-
141
      quantile of the empirical scores {s(x∗i , yi∗ )}i∈[n] . Since the training data of the base model is split
      from the calibration dataset used to determine τD , this approach is commonly known as split
142
      conformal prediction, which we refer to as SplitCP. Notably, τD is determined by the calibration
143
      dataset D; once the latter is fixed, there is no need to access the ground truth labels at test time.
144
145   Examples in classification This paper focuses on classification. In this case, a simple and popular
146   choice of the score function is s(x, y) = πθ (x)y (Sadinle et al., 2019), where πθ is a trained neural
147
      network parameterized by θ, and πθ (x)y ∈ [0, 1] is the softmax score corresponding to one of the
      k-classes y ∈ [k]. Such a score function is positively oriented, which we adopt in this work. We note
148
      that another well-known choice due to Romano et al. (2020) is negatively oriented, and our methods
149
      can be applied there as well.
150
151   Distribution shift For the rest of this paper, we study the following deviation of the above standard
152   CP problem. At test time, instead of working with a single test sample (xtest , ytest ) drawn from D, we
153
      consider a size-N collection of samples3 {xi , yi }i∈[N ] drawn from some new unknown distribution
154
      Dtest . We only observe the covariates, defined as the test dataset Dtest = {xi }i∈[N ] . Importantly,
      the ground truth labels on Dtest are not revealed even after predictions are made. The goal, from a
155
      practical perspective, is to output a small prediction set C(xi ) at each test covariate xi , satisfying the
156
      specified empirical coverage rate,

169   4       O UR METHODS
170
171   In this section, we first propose a method called ECP (Entropy scaled Conformal Prediction), which
172   improves the coverage rate of CP by enlarging its prediction sets using the uncertainty evaluation
173   of the base model itself. Crucially, this notion of uncertainty can be directly minimized and refined
174
      through unsupervised TTA, leading to an improved method called EACP (Entropy base-Adapted
      Conformal Prediction). The latter is able to both
175
176   • recover the desired error rate on many challenging distribution-shifted datasets; and
177   • significantly reduce inflated set sizes under increased uncertainty.
178

      4.2   O PTIMIZING UNCERTAINTY USING TTA
271
272   While ECP already improves the coverage rate of SplitCP on several datasets, it inevitably leads
273   to larger set sizes and, like typical post-hoc CP methods, still relies on a fixed base model. To
274   remedy this, we refine ECP using entropy minimization (Grandvalet and Bengio, 2004; Wang et al.,
275   2021), a classical idea in unsupervised TTA which updates the base model itself on the unlabeled
276   test dataset. Although such techniques in unsupervised TTA have been investigated in the context of
277   top-1 accuracy, we take a different perspective and study their ability to improve set-valued classifiers
278   like conformal predictors.
279   Key idea Concretely, we first rewrite the entropy h(x) as a loss function w.r.t. the base model’s
280   parameter θ,
281                                                  X
282                          L(x; θ) := h(x) = −         πθ (x)y logπθ (x)y .                       (5)
283                                                        y∈[k]
284
285   Our main idea is to update the base model by minimizing this loss function (or a suitable variant) on
286   the test dataset Dtest , before applying ECP. This brings two intuitive benefits.
287   • The updated base model is better suited for the shifted distribution Dtest , which generally improves
288     the quality of the prediction sets built on top of it.
289   • The base model’s entropy determines the amount of prediction set inflation due to ECP. By directly
290     minimizing the entropy, the resulting prediction sets can be smaller.
291
292
      Method: EACP A number of specific TTA methods have been developed to minimize the entropy,
      while ensuring certain notions of stability. In this work, we leverage a recent method called ETA
293
      (Efficient Test-time Adaptation; Niu et al. 2022), due to its simplicity and effectiveness even under
294
      continual distribution shifts (Press et al., 2023). Combining this with ECP results in a new CP method,
295   which we call EACP (Entropy base-Adapted Conformal Prediction).
296
297   In practice, one could simply call ETA as a subroutine, so here we only present its high level idea for
298
      completeness. First, the test dataset Dtest is divided into a collection of batches. On each batch (i.e.,
      xi with a collection of indices i), ETA filters the base model’s outputs (i.e., softmax scores) s(xi , ·) by
299
      excluding the outputs similar to those already seen. Then, it reweighs the remaining indices based the
300
      associated entropy h(xi ), with lower entropy (less uncertain) indices receiving higher weights. This
301   leads to a weighted batch variant of the loss function Eq.(5), which is then minimized by performing a
302   single gradient update. Subsequently, the updated base model is applied to ECP to form the prediction
303   sets of EACP, according to Eq.(4).

       5                E XPERIMENTS
367
       We conduct experiments across a number of large-scale datasets and neural network architectures.
368
       Our setup builds on the standard SplitCP procedure introduced in Section 3, which relies on a
369
       held-out, in-distribution, “development set” for calibrating the CP threshold. On ImageNet variants,
370    we split the original ImageNet development set (i.e., not used for model training) into a CP calibration
371    set consisting of 25,000 samples, and an in-distribution test set (sometimes called the validation set
372    in the CP literature). The readers are referred to Appendix B for more details.
373
       The conformal threshold is found on the calibration set, and used in subsequent distribution-shifted
374
       settings. Importantly, after the conformal threshold is estimated in-distribution, all subsequent steps
375
       are unsupervised. We show results on both stationary and continuously shifting test distributions.

      Baselines We compare our proposed methods to the following baselines:
379
380   • NAIVE: generating prediction sets by including classes until their cumulative softmax score is
381     greater or equal to 1 − α (the target coverage level). This is generated by the base model itself,
382
        without the CP post-processing.
383   • Standard SplitCP: applying the CP threshold directly on the distribution-shifted data.
384   • SplitCP with ETA: applying the CP threshold while updating the base model using ETA.
385   Furthermore, in settings with stationary distribution shifts, we compare to Robust Conformal (RC;
386   Cauchois et al. 2024), an existing CP algorithm that handles distribution shifts via robust optimization.
387   In settings with continual distribution shifts, we compare to a number of OCO-based algorithms
388   (Bhatnagar et al., 2023a; Gibbs and Candès, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) that require additional access
389   to the ground truth labels.
390
      In all experiments, the target coverage rate is set to 0.90. We also analyze our methods with both
391   linear and quadratic scaling, as described in Section 4.3.
392
393   Datasets We investigate a number of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) variants including: ImageNet-
394

395
      represent in-the-wild distribution shifts across many real world applications, including iWildCam

518   6      C ONCLUSION
519
520   This paper studies how to improve set-valued classification methods on distribution-shifted data,
521   without relying on labels from the target dataset. This is an important challenge in many real
522   world settings, where exchangeability assumptions are violated and labels may be difficult to attain.
523   We propose an uncertainty-aware method based on the prediction entropy (ECP), and leverage
524   unsupervised test time adaptation to update the base model and refine its uncertainty (EACP). We
525   demonstrate that the proposed methods are able to recover the desired error rate on a wide range of
526   distribution shifts, while maintaining efficient set sizes. Furthermore, they are even competitive with
527   supervised approaches on challenging and continuously shifting distributions. We hope this inspires
528
      future works continuing to tackle this important challenge.

      A     A DDITIONAL RELATED WORKS
765
766   CP in decision making Our interest in the considered setting – distribution shifts without test time
767   labels – is mainly motivated by the growing applications of CP in autonomous decision making. A
768   very much incomplete list: see (Lekeufack et al., 2023) for a generic treatment; (Lindemann et al.,
769   2023) for trajectory optimization in robotics; (Yang and Pavone, 2023; Gao et al., 2024) for 3D vision;
770   (Kumar et al., 2023; Cherian et al., 2024; Gui et al., 2024; Mohri and Hashimoto, 2024; Quach et al.,
771   2024) for large language models (LLMs); and (Ren et al., 2023) for LLM-powered robotics.
772
773   CP under distribution shifts As discussed in the main paper, considerable efforts have been
774   devoted to developing CP methods robust to distribution shifts. We now survey two possible
775
      directions and their respective limitations.
776   • The first direction does not require test time labels, but the distribution shift is assumed to be simple
777     in some sense. For example, Tibshirani et al. (2019) studied CP under covariate shifts, where the
778     distribution of the label y conditioned on the covariate x remains unchanged. Here, it suffices to
779     use the classical likelihood ratio reweighting on the calibration dataset, but accurately estimating
780     the likelihood ratio can be challenging in practice. Another idea is to take a robust optimization
781
        perspective by assuming a certain maximum level of distribution shift and protecting against the
        worst case, e.g., (Roth, 2022, Chapter 8) and (Cauchois et al., 2024). The weakness here is the
782
        sensitivity to the hyperparameter, and the obtained prediction sets could be overly conservative.
783
        Various works built on these two ideas. Barber et al. (2023) generalized the reweighting idea
784
        to handle mild but general distribution shifts, but choosing the weights is generally unclear in
785
        practice. Ai and Ren (2024) tackled general distribution shifts by combining reweighting and
786     robust optimization, which also combines the strengths and limitations from the two sides. Ge et al.
787     (2024) extended the two ideas to the aggregation of multiple CP algorithms.
788   • The second direction is connecting CP to adversarial online learning. A line of works (Gibbs
789     and Candes, 2021; Angelopoulos et al., 2023; Gibbs and Candès, 2023; Bhatnagar et al., 2023b;
790     Zhang et al., 2024) applied regret minimization algorithms in OCO to select the score threshold
791     in CP, and Bastani et al. (2022) achieved this task using multicalibration. By relaxing the CP
792     objective from the coverage probability to the post-hoc coverage frequency, these methods can
793     handle arbitrary continual distribution shifts. However, they require the true label to be provided
794     after every prediction, which is a limiting requirement for many use cases in autonomous decision
795     making. Our experiments will show that it is possible to achieve comparable performance in these
796
        settings without this limitation, i.e., being “label free”.

825   B.2     E XPERIMENTAL PROTOCOLS
826
827
      Conformal prediction Our split conformal prediction set-up follows previous works (Angelopoulos
      et al., 2021; Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023), which divides a held-out dataset into a calibration and
828
      test set. On ImageNet variants, we split the original validation set in half to produce 25,000 calibration
829
      points and 25,000 in-distribution test points. The calibrated scores and / or threshold are then used for
830   subsequent distribution-shifted data. On the WILDS datasets, we similarly split the in-distribution
831   validation sets.
832
833   Adaptation procedure Our ImageNet-based experiments are conducted on pre-trained ResNets
834   provided by the torchvision library7 , and ViTs provided by the timm library 8 . Experiments on
835   WILDS datasets are conducted using pre-trained models provided by the authors of that study 9 . For
836   EACP and ETA, we closely follow the optimization hyperparameters from the original paper (Niu
837   et al., 2022): we use SGD optimizer with a momentum of 0.9 and learning rate of 0.00025. We use a
838   batch size of 64 for all ImageNet experiments, 128 for RXRX1 and FMOW, and 42 for iWildCam.
839   Our experiments are conducted using a single NVIDIA A40 GPU.
840
841   Continuous shift We adopt a slightly modified version of the experimental design for continuous
842
      distribution shift presented in previous works (Bhatnagar et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024). This
      involves sampling random corruptions from the ImageNet-C dataset under two regimes: gradual
843
      shifts where the severity level first increases in order from {1, ..., 5} then decreases from {5, ..., 1},
844
      and sudden shifts where the severity level alternates between 1 and 5. In addition to sampling random
845   corruptions, we also consider in Figure 6 results on the “easier” setting of shifting severities on a
846   single corruption type.
