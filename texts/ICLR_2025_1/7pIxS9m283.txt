009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as a powerful framework for graph
012            representation learning. However, they often struggle to capture long-range de-
013            pendencies between distant nodes, leading to suboptimal performance in tasks
014            such as node classification, particularly in heterophilic graphs. Challenges like
015            oversmoothing, oversquashing, and underreaching intensify the problem, limiting
016            GNN effectiveness in such settings.
017            In this paper, we introduce WISE-GNN, a novel framework designed to address
018            these limitations. Our approach enhances any GNN model by incorporating Wise-
019
               embeddings, which capture attribute proximity and similarities among distant
               nodes, thereby improving the representation of nodes in both homophilic and
020
               heterophilic graphs. Additionally, we propose a topological module that can be
021
               smoothly integrated into any GNN model, further enriching node representations by
022            incorporating the topological signatures of node neighborhoods. Comprehensive
023            experiments across various GNN architectures show that WISE-GNN delivers
024            significant improvements in node classification tasks, achieving mean accuracy
025            gains of up to 14% and 23% on benchmark datasets in homophilic and heterophilic
026            settings, respectively. Moreover, WISE-GNN enhances the performance of various
027            GNN architectures, allowing even standard GNNs to outperform SOTA baselines
028            on benchmark datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      GNNs have emerged as the primary approach for graph representation learning over the last decade.
      They have exhibited remarkable performance across various tasks such as node classification, link
034
      prediction, and graph classification (WPC+ 20; MCT+ 24). The fundamental concept involves learn-
035
      ing node representations by integrating both structural and node attribute data through the aggregation
036   of messages from neighboring nodes. Despite their achievements, recent research highlights po-
037   tential drawbacks associated with GNNs, including issues like oversmoothing, oversquashing, and
038   underreaching, which can vary depending on the graph’s structure or the architecture of the GNN
039   itself (LBYS23).
040
      The oversquashing issue in GNNs arises when node representations lose sensitivity to information
041
      originating from crucial yet distant nodes in the network (TDGC+ 21; DGGB+ 23). Conversely, the
042   underreaching problem manifests as an inability to fully explore or influence all pertinent nodes
043   within the graph, leading to information degradation (BKM+ 20). Despite their spatial distance in the
044   graph, nodes may exhibit proximal representations in attribute space, intensifying the oversquashing
045   and underreaching problems, particularly impeding message-passing GNNs in capturing insights
046   from remote but relevant nodes. This deficiency, especially pronounced in heterophilic settings,
047   detrimentally impacts tasks such as node classification, underscoring the importance of addressing
048   these challenges.
049   While many GNNs leverage node attributes as initial embeddings to infuse this critical informa-
050   tion into the model through message aggregation, the dimension of the latent space where node
051   embeddings reside has a profound effect on the learning of GNNs (DJ23). Furthermore, in scenarios
052   where node attributes are unavailable, several conventional methods are employed to initialize node
053   embeddings, thereby implicitly determining the dimensionality of the latent space. These choices,
      coupled with the mentioned problems, inevitably lead to suboptimal performance of GNNs.

054
      To mitigate these challenges, we introduce a novel positional encoding method called Wise embedding,
055   which captures attribute proximity within node representations and directly integrates it into GNN
056   embeddings. The core idea is to encode the position of node feature vectors in the feature space
057   by capturing their relative positions with respect to our classWISE landmarks. This method aims
058   to improve GNNs by incorporating missing information from relevant but distant nodes, leading to
059   more robust node representations. Furthermore, the local subgraph structure is pivotal in enhancing
060   GNN performance, with several models integrating subgraph information to improve expressive-
061   ness and capture finer structural details. For example, Barcelo et al. (BKM+ 20) introduce local
062   subgraph parameters within GNNs to better capture structural nuances. Subgraph-level encoding is
063
      explored in G-meta (HZ20), which applies GNNs on rooted subgraphs for meta-learning purposes.
      Similarly, k-hop GNNs (NDV20) and Ego-GNNs (SVH21) utilize rooted subgraphs and sequential
064
      message passing to encode local substructures. ID-GNNs (YGSYL21) enhance message passing
065
      by incorporating node identities through k-egonets, leading to improved structural representations.
066   However, many of these methods face scalability issues due to the combinatorial complexity of
067   subgraph computations, such as solving subgraph isomorphisms (TZK21). Our approach addresses
068   these limitations by using persistent homology to capture critical subgraph structures within the graph.
069   Persistent homology offers a powerful topological framework for encoding multi-scale subgraph in-
070   formation. This method not only captures intricate topological features but also maintains scalability,
071   making it suitable for large-scale graph tasks while enhancing the overall performance of GNNs.
072   By augmenting the GNN framework with attribute-aware Wise embeddings, and exploring local
073   subgraph structures via persistent homology, our model significantly improves the ability of GNNs to
074
      recognize and utilize both structural and attribute proximities, thereby enhancing their performance
      across various graph-based tasks.
075
076   Our contributions can be summarised as follows:
077
078          • We propose WISE-GNNs, a novel enhancement of GNNs that effectively integrates node
079            attribute proximity information to enrich node representation learning.
080          • We introduce Wise-embeddings, which measures node similarity relative to individual
081            classes, effectively capturing a node’s positional relationships within the attribute space.
082          • By initializing GNNs with Wise-embeddings, we pre-inform the model of relational struc-
083            tures in the attribute space, enabling them to capture long-range dependencies. Wise
084            embeddings are model-agnostic and can be easily integrated into any GNN architecture with
085            minimal code adaptation.
086          • To improve robustness, we integrate topological structure information from node neighbor-
087            hoods, enhancing the model’s ability to capture both local and global graph properties.
088          • Extensive experiments across multiple GNN architectures show that integrating Wise-
089            embeddings significantly enhances node classification performance, achieving up to a 14%
090            and 23% increase in average accuracy gains on benchmark datasets in homophilic and
091            heterophilic settings, respectively.

      2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
096
097   GNNs are neural networks designed to process graph-structured data. In the predominant message-
098   passing scheme, GNNs update node embeddings through information from neighboring nodes. The
099   versatility of GNNs has led to their widespread use across domains such as social networks, recom-
100   mendation systems, drug discovery, and knowledge graphs. Notable variants include GCNs (KW17),
101   GATs (VCC+ 18), GraphSAGE (HYL17), and GINs (XHLJ19), each suited for different graph types
102   and tasks like node classification, graph classification, and link prediction. Despite their success,
103   GNNs face challenges, including loss of structural information and difficulty in capturing long-range
104
      dependencies and multiscale information (PWC+ 19; LHKX22; LHL+ 22).
105   Several recent studies address these issues. MixHop (AEHPK+ 19) extracts features from multi-hop
106   neighborhoods to enhance information retrieval, while (YJJ+ 20) introduces metrics for heterophilic
107   graphs. Geom-GCN (PWC+ 19) preserves structural information through bi-level aggregation, and
      FAGCN (BWSS21) enhances GAT by integrating edge-level aggregation for high-frequency signals.

108
      GPRGNN (CPLM20) introduces adaptive weights for heterophilic graphs. However, no prior work
109   has systematically analyzed attribute proximity. For recent advances, refer to surveys (WPC+ 20;
110   XWDG22; ZZH+ 22).

      2.2   GNN S AND H ETEROPHILY
113
114   A major challenge with most GNNs is their reliance on the homophily assumption, which means that
115   neighboring nodes tend to have similar labels or features. However, in many real-world networks,
116   such as protein interaction and web networks, this assumption does not hold, as connected nodes often
117   have different features or labels (PWC+ 19; ZYH+ 23). In these heterophilic networks, traditional
118   GNN models may perform poorly, sometimes even worse than simpler models like multilayer
119   perceptrons (ZYZ+ 20; LHL+ 22).
120   To address this issue, recent research has focused on developing GNNs that work better in heterophilic
121   environments. These efforts can be grouped into two main approaches. The first approach aims to
122   make the input features more informative for the GNN (PWC+ 19; XDZW22; XCZ+ 23), while the
123   second approach focuses on improving how information is passed and aggregated between nodes to
124   better suit heterophilic networks (HWX+ 21; YLL+ 21; LHL+ 21; LHX+ 23a).
125   Moreover, recent studies have analyzed heterophily from different angles and proposed new meth-
126   ods to tackle these challenges in graph representation learning (ZLP+ 22; LHX+ 23b; MCJ+ 24;
127   RCDG+ 24). These advancements are helping to build GNN models that can handle both homophilic
128   and heterophilic networks more effectively.

130   2.3   P ERSISTENT H OMOLOGY
131
132   To capture the deeper, often hidden, structural properties of node neighborhoods, we employ Persistent
133   Homology (PH), a key technique in Topological Data Analysis (TDA). Unlike conventional methods,
134   which may focus solely on graph or metric properties, PH provides a powerful tool to quantify
135
      topological features—such as clusters, loops, and voids—that persist across multiple scales. By
      examining how these features evolve and persist, PH reveals intricate patterns in the data that might
136
      otherwise remain undetected. This approach offers broader applicability beyond graphs, extending
137
      to point clouds, images, and other complex datasets. In the context of graphs, PH helps us uncover
138   latent topological insights that complement traditional graph-based analysis. For a deeper dive into
139   PH across different data types, refer to (DW22; CA24).
140
141
      We can summarize PH as a three-step process. Let G = (V, E) be a graph with a node set V and
      an edge set E. The first step is called filtration, where we construct a nested sequence of simplicial
142
      complexes induced from the graph. A common method is to get a nested sequence of subgraphs
143
      G 1 ⊆ . . . ⊆ G N = G. A common approach involves employing a filtration function f : V → R
144   alongside a set of thresholds I = {ϵi }, where ϵ1 = minv∈V f (v) < ϵ2 < . . . < ϵN = maxv∈V f (v).
145   For each ϵi in I, a subset Vi = {vr ∈ V | f (vr ) ≤ ϵi } is formed. Then, the induced subgraph G i by
146   Vi , denoted as G i = (Vi , Ei ), is constructed, where Ei = {ers ∈ E | vr , vs ∈ Vi }. Next, we obtain a
147   simplicial complex for each subgraph, yielding a filtration Gb1 ⊆ . . . ⊆ GbN . A common method is to
148   use clique complexes, where the clique complex Gbi of Gi is obtained by adding k-simplices for every
149   complete (k + 1)-subgraph in Gi . The common filtration functions are degree, betweenness, centrality,
150   or an attribute-specific function. Similarly, one can employ edge weights (or Ricci functions) to
151   create an edge filtration from the graph (AAEF19).
152
      The second step extracts persistence diagrams, where we systematically trace the evolution of
153
      topological features in the filtration {Gbi }. These topological features, such as connected components
154
      (0-holes), loops (1-holes), and cavities (2-holes), are represented by k-dimensional topological
155
      features or k-holes. By using homology groups (Hat02), PH meticulously records the appearance and
156   disappearance of these features in the filtration, and records their birth and death times in persistence
157
      diagrams. In particular, the k th persistence diagram PDk (G) = {(bσ , dσ ) | σ ∈ Hk (Gbi ) for bσ ≤
158
      i < dσ } where Hk (Gbi ) represents the k th homology group of Gbi , consists of pairs (bσ , dσ ) for each
159
      k-hole σ, where bσ and dσ denote the birth and death times, respectively.
160
161   The final step is the vectorization process. While PH extracts hidden shape patterns from data in
      the form of persistence diagrams (PDs), these PDs, consisting of points (birth times, death times)


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      in R2 , are not inherently practical for machine learning (ML) tasks. Instead, common techniques
163   involve faithfully representing PDs as kernels (KJM20) or vectorizations (AAJ+ 23). Vectorizations
164   transform the obtained PDs into a function or vector format, making them more suitable for ML tools.
165   Common vectorization methods include Persistence Images, Persistence Landscapes, Silhouettes, and
166   various Persistence Curves, including Betti curves (AAJ+ 23). While these methods offer flexibility
167   in model and data analysis, it is also common to use automated neural network approaches to avoid
168   the need for manual vectorization choices or hyperparameter tuning (CCI+ 20; HKN19).

171   3     M ETHODOLOGY
172
173   Motivation. Our objective is to address oversquashing and underreaching by incorporating key
174   information from distant nodes early in the learning process. These challenges stem from the
175   GNN’s failure to access crucial information from highly relevant yet distant nodes. Drawing an
176   analogy between networks and societies, individuals may reside far apart but have very close interests.
177   Unfortunately, the message-passing algorithm of GNNs only considers messages from nearby nodes,
178   resulting in the omission of vital information derived from the attribute proximity of nodes (shared
179   interests) necessary for improved node representation.
180   Solution. To overcome these limitations, we introduce node representations called Wise Embeddings,
181   designed to capture the missing attribute similarity information for both nearby and distant nodes. By
182   integrating this essential information early in the learning process, Wise Embeddings act as a "vaccine"
183   for GNNs, shielding them from the issues outlined earlier. To further enhance the robustness of node
184   representations, we also incorporate topological information from node neighborhoods through a
185   topological module. As depicted in Figure 1, our framework uniquely combines these elements to
186   create effective and meaningful node representations.
187
188
      3.1   W ISE E MBEDDDINGS
189
190
      In a graph, a node’s spatial information can be effectively represented by its neighboring nodes.
191   However, when it comes to capturing its features (interests), the process is not as straightforward.
192   Our objective is to introduce classWISE landmarks and utilize these landmarks to position the node’s
193   feature vector within the feature space. This involves establishing a "coordinate system" by measuring
194   the distances between the node’s feature vector and the landmarks.
195
      Node features vary across different types of networks. In social networks, they typically represent user-
196
      specific details, whereas in citation networks, they often indicate the presence or absence of particular
197   keywords. While the structural interactions among neighboring nodes are essential for predicting
198   node behavior, incorporating the inherent features of nodes into the analysis is equally important.
199   Given that GNNs aggregate messages from nearby neighborhoods for updates, their performance
200   heavily depends on the informativeness of the initial embedding. Moreover, as embeddings are
201   updated by collecting information only from the local neighborhood, data from distant nodes cannot
202   directly contribute to the process.
203   To address the issue, we first consider the node embeddings in the feature space. Each node u is
204   represented as a feature vector X (u) in the feature space Rn , where X : V → Rn represents a node
205   embedding map. For each class Cj , we form a point cluster Wj = {X (u)|u ∈ Cj } and define a
206   classWISE landmark ξj for it. Depending on the context, we can define more than one type landmark
207   for each class Cj , i.e., {ξj1 , ξj2 , . . . , ξjm }.
208
      One natural method for real-valued feature vectors is averaging the feature vectors, which basically
209
      correspond to the centroid of the cluster Wj . For binary or categorical vectors, it can be a class
210   representative vector, e.g., the features that exist in all (or at least some percentage) of class members
211   (selective) or the features that belong to at least one member of the class member (inclusive) (See
212   Appendix B for examples). After defining the classWISE landmarks ξj for each class Cj , depending
213   on the context, we define the distance d(., .) in the latent space Rn as a similarity measure in the
214   feature space to measure the similarity/distance of the feature vectors to class landmarks. For real-
215   valued vectors, Euclidean distance or cosine distance are the most natural choices. For binary vectors,
      Jaccard Similarity, Positive Similarity, and Cosine similarity are the most common methods.

249   3.2   T OPOLOGICAL M ODULE
250
251   In this part, we aim to capture the topological structure of the neighborhoods of each node by
252   utilizing persistent homology (Section 2.3). This process begins by constructing a k-hop ego graph
253   from a given graph G, where we specifically employ a 2-hop ego graph Gu for each node u in our
254
      study considering computational complexity and performance accuracy. Then, we employ sublevel
      filtration, utilizing the degree function (defined in the original graph, not in the ego graphs), to derive
255
      a persistent diagram from this subgraph Gu , effectively capturing the topological characteristics of the
256
      node neighborhood. This persistence diagram serves as the foundation for extracting local topological
257
      information, facilitated by the Betti vectors. Leveraging the Betti vectors β⃗0 (u) and β⃗1 (u) (subscripts
258
      representing the topological dimensions), we obtain a topological encoding that encapsulates essential
259   structural characteristics of the graph. Finally, we integrate this topological information into our
260   GNN architecture via a 3-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron, enhancing the GNN’s capacity to understand
261   and utilize the underlying graph topology for improved performance for downstream tasks. For a
262   better understanding of the effects of 1, 2, and 3-hop ego graphs, we provide detailed experimental
263   descriptions in Section 4.4.

452                   4.4                  A BLATION S TUDIES
453
454                  To verify the effectiveness of our method, we conducted three ablation studies. In the first study,
455                  we evaluated the performance of GCN with different initial embeddings. In the second study, we
456
                     compared the performance of various similarity measures used to define classWISE landmarks.
                     Finally, in the third study, we examined the impact of the size and removing the center of the node
457
                     neighborhoods on the performance of topological embeddings.
458
459                   Initial Embeddings. In our model, we used Wise Embeddings as the initial vectors and integrated
460                   topological encodings through an MLP. A natural question arises: why not use both as initial
461                   embeddings? We aim to answer this question through our ablation study. As shown in Table 4, we
462
                      examined the performance of the GCN model with different initial embeddings. The first row shows
                      the original GCN model using node feature vectors as initial embeddings. In the second row, we see
463
                      a significant performance improvement when replacing node feature vectors with Wise Embeddings.
464
                      However, in the third row, when using only topological encodings as initial vectors, the performance
465                   drops below that of the original GCN model (NF).
466
467                  The most surprising and crucial observation is in the fourth row. When we concatenate Wise
468
                     Embeddings with topological encodings as initial embeddings, there is a significant performance
                     drop. Intuitively, adding topological encodings to Wise Embeddings should enhance performance.
469
                     However, we found that Wise Embeddings contain the key information for the GCN, and while
470
                     topological encodings add extra information, they also dilute the crucial information from Wise
471                  Embeddings due to the additional dimensions. This is why we chose to use Wise Embeddings as the
472                  initial embeddings and integrate topological encodings through the MLP. This approach ensures that
473                  Wise Embeddings remain intact while topological encodings still contribute. Comparing the fourth
474                  row of Table 4 with the fourth row of Table 2 shows a significant performance difference. Similarly,
475                  the last row indicates that while original node features add some value, they also harm performance
476                  due to the same issue.
