011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            A defining feature of computation in the human brain is that different regions can
014            manifest different representations of the same object set. Here we introduce a
015            novel method to learn and interpret multiple neural representations of lexical ob-
016            jects within specific, topographically-defined brain areas. Our approach fine-tunes
017            a pre-trained language model (LM) for each brain region of interest, resulting in
018
               better alignment of the LM’s representational space with that of the correspond-
               ing brain area. This alignment is achieved through supervised structural pruning of
019
               LM features, which selects a subset of features most relevant to the target brain re-
020
               gion. We then interpret these retained features using a linear probing task to iden-
021            tify the semantic information they encode. Both the pruning and probing steps are
022            validated through out-of-sample testing, with pruning significantly improving the
023            prediction of brain representations. This method advances on existing approaches
024            by i) eliminating the reliance on hand-crafted encoders, reducing potential biases;
025            ii) optimizing the alignment process via data-driven learning; and iii) providing
026            interpretability of the semantic features in a black-box LM. From a neurobiolog-
027            ical perspective, we find that brain regions encoding social and cognitive aspects
028            of lexical items consistently also represent their sensory-motor features, though
029
               the reverse does not hold.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   A representational space can be described using basis vectors, which define the dimensions along
035   which objects are positioned based on their values and variances. While artificial neural networks
036   (ANNs) learn representations that are by definition distributed, recent work suggests that they can
037   also develop signatures of modular representational spaces, where different populations of units may
038   encode distinct concepts, categories, or syntactic properties. This seen when certain groups of units
039
      show variance that is informative for specific categories, but uninformative for others. For exam-
      ple, in language models it is possible to identify subsets of units that specifically encode syntactic
040
      properties or capture semantic dimensions of different categories (e.g., Cao et al., 2021; Manrique
041
      et al., 2023), and modular structure has been identified in several studies (e.g., Lepori et al., 2023;
042   Purushwalkam et al., 2019).
043
044   These findings suggest but do not prove the converse – that a group of objects could be represented
045
      differently across functionally defined modules in ANNs. The main challenge is that representa-
      tion in ANNs is inherently distributed, making it difficult to isolate functionally specific modules
046
      encoding unique representational spaces. Low-rank factorization methods, which extract latent di-
047
      mensions from the entire object-by-unit activation matrix (Cheng et al., 2017), can obscure smaller,
048   localized modules with limited contributions to overall model covariance. While subspace cluster-
049   ing methods aim to address this (Parsons et al., 2004), they require several heuristics and a-priori
050   decisions (Elhamifar & Vidal, 2013). And of course, without any principled analysis of covari-
051   ance to guide selection, multi-unit activity cannot be meaningfully interpreted. To illustrate, even
052   a random sampling of ANN units, without using any feature-selection criterion, can produce the
053   appearance that different populations encode different input dimensions. However, this lacks value
      unless the units systematically encode meaningful semantics. For example, some units may respond

079   Others units could respond randomly, unrelated to any coherent object representation.
080   While similar considerations arise when studying representations in biological systems, these may
081   offer clearer insights into representations due to their topographical organization, where spatially
082   adjacent processing units often support similar computations (e.g., vision, audition, language). This
083   spatial structure can aid in identifying proximally-organized neural populations that may encode
084   semantics. Additionally, the spatial resolution of neurobiological recordings often allows modeling
085   single-unit activations using linear encoding models, predicting neural responses based on object
086   features (e.g., Mitchell et al., 2008; Sucholutsky et al., 2023). Taken together, in biological sys-
087
      tems, the ability to identify single units and group them based on spatial proximity provides a way
      to identify neural populations that encode semantic information, and then analyze the representa-
088
      tional space inherent in multi-unit activity in this population. Most importantly, we show that when
089
      the questions is approached from this perspective, human-interpretable semantic labels can then
090   be assigned to the dimensions underlying these neural representations, in this way identifying the
091   meaning dimensions that organize representations in different brain areas.

093
      As detailed, we introduce a combined method for: 1) identifying meaningful unit populations within
      the brain, 2) formally capturing the variance dimensions they encode through supervised pruning,
094
      and 3) providing interpretable explanations of these variance dimensions via a probing task. Figure 1
095
      presents an overview of the methodology. We begin by identifying brain clusters (functional regions)
096   involved in semantic processing and define the representational space for each cluster. Different
097   brain areas are sensitive to different information dimensions and so will represent lexical items in
098   differ ways. Subsequently, for each brain area, we perform supervised pruning of a language model
099   (GloVe) to align the lexical similarity between words with the representational structure of that brain
100   area. This produces different sets of retained features for different brain regions. In a second step, we
101   use probing to interpret the semantics encoded in these retained features by evaluating how well each
102   of the retained feature sets can predict human annotations for new words. Using this workflow we
103   investigate whether it is possible to identify and interpret multiple, distinct semantic representations
104
      for a single set of lexical items, across topographically constrained neural clusters in the human
      brain. We address three inter-related aims:
105
106   Aim 1 is to evaluate whether it is possible to identify functional brain modules where: (1) the mod-
107   ule’s representational space — quantified via object-to-object distance matrices derived from multi-
      unit population activity — can be predicted using object distances from a given language model

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      M ; and (2) where said predictions can be optimized, separately for each brain area, by identifying
109   subsets of embedding dimensions within M , rather than using the entire model.
110
111   Aim 2, conditional on the success of Aim 1, the aim is to: (1) determine whether the subspaces iden-
112
      tified in Aim 1 are consistent across brain areas, i.e., whether representations in different brain re-
      gions are best explained by different latent dimensions in M ; and (2) provide a human-interpretable
113
      explanation of the semantic dimensions that underlie the subspaces in M identified by the optimiza-
114
      tion algorithm. If successful, this would show that it is possible to interpret in what manner lexical
115   semantics are multiply realized, via different population codes, across the human brain.
116
117   Aim 3, dependent on the success of Aims 1 and 2, combines the subsets of embedding dimensions
118
      learned for different brain areas into a unified graph to determine if communities in the graph code
      for different semantics.
119
120   Our approach addresses two related key limitations in current methods for studying semantic rep-
121   resentations in different brain areas. The first improvement is that current approaches require col-
122   lecting human-annotated feature-ratings for the same stimuli for which neurobiological recording
123   are obtained. These annotations are used to construct interpretable encoding models that align ob-
124
      ject distances in the model with object distances in different brain areas (e.g., Mitchell et al., 2008;
      Fernandino et al., 2022). In contrast, our method provides an interpretation of the semantic rep-
125
      resentations that organize the representational space without requiring human annotations for the
126
      items producing the neurobiological responses. As indicated, this is done by using a tailor-pruned
127   version of a black-box model to first approximate object distances in a target brain area, and then
128   interpreting the information encoded in the pruned model through a probing task.
129
130
      A second improvement is that current methods require manual construction of annotation frame-
      works, which in turn can be influenced by an experimenter’s own subjective decision about which
131
      features to include. This biases the analysis towards those few dimensions that an experimenter
132
      considers relevant. In contrast, our method is objective in that it uses a generic language model
133   (specifically, a subspace of the model learned via pruning) for encoding a brain region’s representa-
134   tional space. In this way we remove the need for manual feature selection or explicit assumptions
135   about the encoding model, taking advantage of the semantics already captured in pre-trained models.
136
      Finally, we show that using this approach it is possible to aggregate information across multiple
137
      learned representations by combining the pruned language models (optimized for different brain
138
      areas) into a single graph structure. This graph, built directly from the pruned solutions identifies
139   communities of features in the LM that code for different semantics.

144   2.1   DATASETS
145
146   The neuro-biological data consisted of functional MRI (fMRI) recordings, which capture brain ac-
147
      tivity using blood oxygenation level-dependent (BOLD) signals. These recordings provided data for
      approximately 14,000 virtual sensors (voxels) per participant, sampled at a temporal resolution of
148
      0.5 Hz. Data were collected from nine participants, as reported in Mitchell et al. (2008). The voxel
149
      IDs are consistent across participants, meaning that a given voxel ID refers to the same brain region
150   for all participants. This allowed us to combine data across participants. We restricted the analysis
151   to the subset of 13,189 voxels that contain non-null data for all participants. Each of the nine par-
152   ticipants was presented with 60 distinct nouns, each repeated six times. For each voxel, this design
153   yields a total of 54 activation values per noun (9 participants × 6 presentations). The complete data
154   can thus be represented as a four-dimensional tensor A ∈ R9×6×60×13189 , where the dimensions
155   correspond, respectively to participants, repeated presentations, nouns and voxels. To improve the
156   signal-to-noise ratio, we averaged across the participant and presentation dimensions, collapsing the
157   first two dimensions. This results in a two-dimensional Voxel-Noun matrix V ∈ R13189×60 , where
158
      each element vi,j represents the averaged activation value for voxel i in response to noun j. This
      matrix is referred to as the Voxel-Noun matrix.
159
160   Mitchell et al. also provide a simple vector-semantics model, which they show can predict brain
161   activity for the 60 nouns. It represented as a noun-semantics matrix S ∈ R60×25 , where each of the
      60 nouns is encoded as a vector of 25 semantic features. These features were derived from a corpus-


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      based analysis, where each noun’s vector reflects its co-occurrence with 25 manually selected verbs.
163   These 60 nouns were drawn from 12 general categories, with 5 nouns per each. These categories
164   were: body parts, furniture, vehicles, animals, kitchen utensils, types of buildings (e.g., apartment,
165   igloo), parts of buildings, clothing, insects, vegetables, and man made objects. Intuitively, it is pos-
166   sible to represent these 60 nouns on multiple types of semantic dimensions including, for example,
167   visual, auditory, and relation to human-related activities, making them an interesting candidate for
168   the study of representation patterns.
169   We also used an additional dataset provided by Binder et al. (2016), which provides human ratings
170   for 534 words on 65 semantic dimensions. Participants in that study rated the relevance of each
171   dimension to each word. We used this dataset to probe for information in GloVe embeddings as
172   detailed in Probing section below.

      2.2   M ODEL
175
176
      As a vector space model, and as a target for Pruning, we used GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014), which
177   provides an adequate choice for predicting human similarity judgments for various concepts (e.g.,
178   Chersoni et al., 2021). We extracted GloVe word embeddings for the 60 nouns used by Mitchell et al.
179   These embeddings form a matrix E(M ) ∈ R60×300 , using GloVe with 300 dimensions. Additionally,
180   we extracted GloVe embeddings for 534 words from the Binder et al. dataset Binder et al. (2016),
181   forming a matrix E(B) ∈ R534×300 . The embeddings for the 60 nouns were used to generate pruned
182   GloVe solutions, and the embeddings for the 534 words were utilized to construct predictive matrices
183   for the Probing analysis.

      2.3   P RELIMINARIES : IDENTIFYING BRAIN AREAS FOR REPRESENTATION LEARNING
186
187   In our study, we use the noun-semantics matrix S as an encoding model to identify brain regions
188   where a linear mapping from the semantic features to each voxel’s activity produced a significant
189   fit. As an initial step, we performed a voxel-wise linear regression analysis, where the activation
190   values for the 60 nouns in each voxel (each row in the Voxel-Noun matrix V) were predicted using
191   the 60 × 25 Noun-Semantics matrix S. For each voxel, the encoding model’s R2 value was stored.
192   This model was fit separately for each of the 13189 voxels.
193   We then spatially clustered voxels as follows. The goal of clustering was to identify spatially coher-
194   ent groups of voxels with good predictive accuracy from the encoding model, which would serve as
195   the focus for subsequent representation learning. As a first step, we selected voxels with regression
196   R2 values in the top-half of a median split. We then created an adjacency matrix, defining two voxels
197   as connected if they shared at least a vertex contact within their 26-neighbor adjacency. Wn then ap-
198   plied agglomerative clustering to this adjacency matrix, with a distance threshold of 10mm between
199   clusters, and selected clusters that contained more than 8 voxels. In summary, this workflow relies
200   on the local topographic organization of brain activity, constraining clustering by spatial contiguity.
201   This analysis identified a total of 321 activation clusters. To obtain a manageable number of activa-
202   tion clusters, and to account for potential representational similarities among them, we first identified
203   the most representative clusters based on their word-to-word similarity structure. This was per-
204   formed as follows. For each cluster, we defined a cluster-specific activation matrix Ac ∈ RNc ×60 ,
205   where Nc represents the number of voxels in the cluster and 60 corresponds to the words. The word-
206   to-word similarity for each cluster was captured in a cluster-specific similarity matrix Sc ∈ R60×60 ,
207
      computed by taking the Pearson correlation between the activation values of all word pairs. We de-
      fined prototypical clusters as those whose similarity matrices Sc were representative of the broader
208
      set of clusters. To this end, we first extracted the upper triangle values of all 321 Sc matrices and
209
      arranged them in a 321-row table, which we refer to as the “AllClustersSM” matrix. We then ap-
210   plied agglomerative clustering to AllClustersSM, using a threshold of Pearson correlation r ≥ 0.5
211   to group rows (activation clusters) presenting similar representational structures. In other words,
212   this step grouped activation clusters that exhibited similar word-to-word similarity patterns. Next,
213   we retained only cluster groups containing at least three members and identified the prototypical
214   cluster within each group as the one that on average was most strongly correlated with the others in
215   that group. This workflow ultimately produced 22 prototypical activation clusters whose similarity
      matrices Sc were representative of the overall brain-wide pattern.

      2.4   P RUNING METHODS (A IM 1)
217
218
      As indicated above (section 2.3), prototypical activation clusters were identified using the combined
219
      following constraints: linear readout of information of each unit in the module, the units being
220   spatially adjacent, and the representation being prototypical of a set of similar representations inde-
221   pendently identified in the brain.
222
223
      Given an activation cluster’s word-to-word similarity matrix Sc , we can assess how well it is pre-
224
      dicted by GloVe representations. To do this, we first use the GloVe word embedding matrix E(M )
      to generate a GloVe similarity matrix SGloVe . This matrix is computed by calculating the cosine
225
      similarity between the embedding vectors of each word pair in E(M ) . We then compute the Spear-
226
      man correlation (rho) between the upper triangles of Sc and SGloVe , which reflects the similarity of
227
      the representations produced from GloVe and from the brain data (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008). We
228   consider this value as the baseline representational-alignment between the computational model and
229   brain activity. Note that SGloVe is identical in all analyses, whereas Sc differs for each of the 22
230   prototypical activation clusters.
231
      To evaluate whether it is possible to learn an improved alignment, we applied a feature pruning
232
      algorithm, which selects a subset of GloVe features that produce an improved GloVe-brain represen-
233
      tational alignment compared to using the full set of 300 GloVe features. The complete pruning algo-
234   rithm is presented in Appendix Algorithm 1. The algorithm is based on sequential feature selection,
235   where GloVe features in E(M ) are first ranked by their importance in predicting the brain-derived
236   Sc , and then an optimal subset of features is identified for prediction. This pruning procedure was
237   applied independently to each of the 22 prototypical activation clusters. Specifically, each Sc ma-
238   trix supervised a distinct pruning processes, each aiming to discover a subset of GloVe features that
239   outperformed the full feature set.
240
      In summary, rather than using the full 60 × 300 matrix, the pruned solutions reflect a reduced feature
241   set of size d, where d < 300, and the indices of these selected features were stored for downstream
242   analyses. We refer to these subsets as Fc , where c indexes each activation cluster (1 to 22) and Fc
243   refers to the set of indices for the selected features in cluster.
244
      For each of the 22 clusters we implemented pruning in ways stages. First, as described above,
245
      we analyzed the full 60 × 300 matrix to identify the optimal number of features d, where d < 300.
246
      Second, we employed a cross-validation (CV) framework, where in each fold, one word was left out,
247
      and its corresponding row was removed from both Sc and E(M ) . In this workflow, the CV process
248   was supervised by a 59 × 59 similarity matrix derived from brain activity, which was used to prune
249   a 59 × 300 GloVe embedding matrix. The learned mapping was then applied to the left-out word,
250   predicting the 59 pairwise similarity values across domains. As a baseline, these 59 similarity values
251   for the left-out word were computed using all 300 features. In this way, CV evaluates whether it is
252   possible to better predict the 59 similarity judgments for the left-out word when using the retained
253   features than when using the full 300-feature set.
254

256   2.5   P ROBING METHODS (A IM 2)
257
258   Probing (Belinkov, 2022) evaluated the ability to decode 65 human-annotated semantic feature val-
259   ues from GloVe embeddings. E(B) ∈ R534×300 was the GloVe embedding matrix for the 534 words
260   analyzed by Binder et al. (2016), and Y(Binder) was the human-annotated feature matrix for those
261   same words. Probing quantifies how well the semantic feature values in Y(Binder) can be predicted
262   from the embeddings in E(B) . The target matrix Y(Binder) consists of 65 feature annotations for
263   534 words, capturing semantic dimensions including vision, audition, emotion, and cognition (see
264   Appendix A.1).
265   We used a Partial Least Squares Regression (PLSR) model to map E(B) to Y(Binder) . In each cross-
266   validation fold, 533 words were used to train the model, with the test set comprising GloVe embed-
267   dings and 65 feature annotations for the left-out word. The learned model was applied to predict
268   the left-out word’s 65 features. This process was repeated for all 534 words, generating a 534 × 65
269   prediction matrix, Z, for probing analysis. Probing evaluated the PLSR model by correlating the
      predicted and ground-truth human-rating values for each of the 65 features. These correlations were

270
      generally high, reproducing Chersoni et al. (2021), see Appendix Figure 4. Note that this perfor-
271   mance was obtained when using all 300 GloVe Features.
272
273   In the main analysis we applied the learning/testing PLSR procedure to GloVe embeddings con-
274
      strained to the features from each activation cluster, Fc (see Figure 1). This allowed us to probe the
      information encoded in each pruned feature set. Note that pruning was supervised by brain activity
275

276

277
278   The analysis is subject to a noise ceiling, because the behavioral ratings forming the prediction target
279   (the 534 × 65 matrix, Y(Binder) ) are averages over human feature-ratings, which are inherently noisy.
280   The noise ceiling cannot be precisely quantified due to the online data-collection method used in
281
      the original study Binder et al. (2016), but is below 1.0. An indication is given by the fact that on
      average, single-participant ratings and group-ratings showed a median correlation of R = 0.80.

      2.6   C REATING A GRAPH FROM FEATURE - SUBSETS (A IM 3)
285
      We integrated the feature sets selected from the 22 sets Fc into a single graph. In the graph, nodes
286   were feature indices selected by pruning. Features were connected if they appeared together in the
287   selected feature subsets, and the edge weight between any two features reflected the number of times
288   they co-occurred across subsets. The graph was partitioned using the Louvain algorithm (Blondel
289   et al., 2008), selecting the partition that maximized modularity from 100 runs. This analysis returned
290   four distinct communities, each representing a unique set of GloVe features.
291
      To assess whether the communities found in the partition encode different semantic types, we eval-
292   uated each community’s features individually. We tested their performance in predicting human
293   similarity judgments on two datasets: Wordsim-353 (Agirre et al., 2009) and Simlex-999 (Hill et al.,
294   2015). We also tested them on a standard analogy benchmark (Mikolov et al., 2013) to determine
295   the semantic content within each community.

      3.1   P RUNING RESULTS
300
301   In all 22 activation clusters, predictions of brain representations were always improved using the
302   subset of features learned via pruning. Furthermore, these improvements generalized beyond the
303   training data as shown in cross-validation tests. Table 1 presents the results, for each of the 22
304   brain areas that constituted a prototypical activation cluster. The table shows the pruning results
305   per cluster when pruning was applied to complete dataset or to out of sample folds in a context of
306   cross-validation.
307   The majority of brain areas identified were in parieto-temporal areas, lateral temporal and temporal-
308   occipital areas, which are the ones most often implicated in semantic processing in the brain (e.g.,
309   Binder et al., 2009). The very few exceptions included the left anterior cingulate cortex, right in-
310   ferior frontal gyrus, and left postcentral gyrus, but we note the first two produced relatively low
311   correlations.
312   In nearly all cases, the pruned solutions achieved these improved predictions while retaining fewer
313   than 25% of the GloVe features (N < 75 of 300), with several clusters requiring as few as 30
314   features. For instance, in one cluster located in the left posterior temporal gyrus, pruning improved
315   the Spearman correlation from 0.03 to 0.19 in out of sample data, with an average of only 28 features
316   selected per fold. There was no case where pruning under-performed the complete, full feature set.
317   In most cases, the improvement was considerable, though there were a few cases with moderate
318   improvements (e.g. from -0.05 to only 0.05 in an activation cluster located around the left anterior
319   cingulate contex, ACC).
320   Averaging over all 22 clusters, pruning the GloVe embeddings resulted in an increase in the mean
321   Spearman correlation from M = 0.025 ± 0.056 to M = 0.314 ± 0.083, using an average of
322   46.95 ±19.6 features. When applying leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV), pruning similarly
323   enhanced the prediction for held-out data, increasing the mean correlation from M = 0.031 ± 0.044
      to M = 0.143 ± 0.069, with an average of 49.17 ±17.17 features selected per fold.

351   From the perspective of learning neurobiological representations, our results constitute a significant
352   advance, as the modal current approach to studying semantic spaces with word embeddings is to use
353   the entire set of features (Complete Dataset results, all features, in Table 1). Indeed, the values we
354   report for the non-pruned embeddings, with a mean of around 0.025 and a maximum of around 0.15
355   are typical of alignment values computed between DNN and brain similarity matrices in prior studies
356   (e.g., Fernandino et al., 2022). Pruning improved the correlation value significantly, in several cases
357
      identifying meaningful correlations (ρ > 0.34) even when the full embeddings identified little or no
      correlations. This means that without pruning, one would conclude that the brain region in question
358
      cannot be predicted by GloVe representations, whereas in fact, prediction is completely possible if
359
      the correct subspace is identified, a point we return to in the Discussion.
360
361   Each pruning solution produces a subset of features specifically learned for each activation cluster,
362   denoted as Fc , which contains the indices of the GloVe features selected by that activation cluster.
363
      By examining all 22 Fc sets, we examined if there was a consistent subset of features that were
      either retained or excluded across the pruning solutions. As shown in Appendix Figure 5, many
364
      features were consistently excluded. Of GloVe’s 300 features, 50 were never selected in any of the
365
      22 solutions, while another 50 were selected only once. There was also weak evidence for consistent

384
385   The results of out of sample prediction of human annotations from GloVe embeddings using PLSR,
386   when using the complete set of 300 features are presented in Figure 4. These serve as reference
387   values for subsequent analyses. As detailed in the Methods, for each of the 22 prototypical activation
388   clusters, we used only the GloVe features selected by pruning for optimizing prediction of that
389
      cluster’s similarity matrix. That is, we used the feature subsets learned from pruning (indices of
      features appearing in column ’Complete Dataset, Feature 1’ in Table 1). The results of these 22
390
      separate analyses are presented in Figure 2, where the clusters are presented sorted in order of
391
      average prediction efficacy.
392
393   We first observe that different brain activation clusters selected for features with different levels
394   of relevant information. Some clusters presented very little predictive capacity, whereas others ap-
395
      proached that seen for the full feature set (normalized values approaching 0.9). As can be seen, some
      GloVe subsets contained information sufficient for predicting sensory features (particularly visual)
396
      but not higher level social and emotional features. Some clusters code for Vision more precisely than
397
      Audition, and some present the opposite pattern. Quite a few feature-subsets contained information
398   sufficient for predicting Cognition, Communication, Human and Social dimensions, and these clus-
399   ters also contained information about sensory dimensions. There appears to be trend where coding
400   of Somatic and Audition features is found in clusters that also track Vision information (but not vice
401   versa). In general, Spatial and Temporal dimensions appeared to be relatively less-well predicted.
402   These finding show that while pruning often identified a fraction of the total GloVe features, these
403   were sufficient to effectively predict human judgments, especially for sensory features (though some
404   regions also appear to code for social/cognitive aspects).
405   As mentioned, we also identified 50 features that were consistently pruned and therefore not part
406   of any pruning solution. To evaluate their semantics, we used this set in the probing analysis by
407   limiting the GloVe embeddings used to these features alone. This too produced a 65-valued result
408   indicating the correlation between the predicted ratings and ground-truth ratings (across 534 words)
409   for each of the 65 semantic dimensions. The predictions afforded by these 50 features were, on
410   average quite poor, particularly for the social and cognitive semantic dimensions. Interestingly,
411   these consistently pruned features produced the most accurate prediction of the Needs dimension,
412
      which coded for “someone or something that would be hard for you to live without” . This dimension
      is important for distinguishing between small artifacts, but relatively unrelated to the nouns used by
413
      Mitchell et al. (2008). In all, this suggests that GloVe features that are not relevant to predicting the
414
      brains representational spaces (across multiple, independent activation clusters) contain relatively
415   impoverished psychologically relevant information.

531   4   D ISCUSSION
532
533   We introduced a novel, effective, and conceptually simple approach to modeling and interpreting
534   neurobiological representations. Using pruning, we learn a black-box encoder that aligns with the
535   brain’s representational space, and through probing, we interpret the semantic content embedded
536
      in the encoder. Our results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach: pruning significantly
      improves the ability to model brain representations while probing allows to interpret this space. We
537
      also find that different brain regions have markedly different representations. Although we focused
538
      on a single language model in this study, the method is easily extended to combine features across
539   multiple language models. These extensions are a viable direction for future work.
