011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Finetuning language models for a new domain inevitably leads to the deterioration
014            of their general performance. This becomes more pronounced the more limited the
015            finetuning data resource.
016            We introduce minifinetuning (MFT), a method for language model domain adap-
017            tation that considerably reduces the effects of overfitting-induced degeneraliza-
018            tion in low-data settings and which does so in the absence of any pre-training
019            data for replay. MFT demonstrates 2-10x more favourable specialization-to-
020            degeneralization ratios than standard finetuning across a wide range of models
021            and domains and exhibits an intrinsic robustness to overfitting when data in the
022
               new domain is scarce and down to as little as 500 samples.
023            Employing corrective self-distillation that is individualized on the sample level,
024            MFT outperforms parameter-efficient finetuning methods, demonstrates replay-
025            like forgetting mitigation properties, and is composable with either for a combined
026
               effect.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Finetuning (FT) as a method for specializing models for new domains remains to be the dominant
032   approach for reliable language model customization despite its relative maturity in the field (Liu
033   et al., 2022). However, FT on a limited data budget can also lead to catastrophic forgetting that hin-
034   ders model performance on the general domain (Kumar et al., 2022). This is illustrated in Figure 1,
035   which plots specialization (improvement on the specialized domain) and degeneralization (detriment
036   on the general domain) in terms of test perplexities for various data budgets throughout the process.

      There are two common remedies to the issue of finetuning-induced degeneralization. Replay stems
055   from the classical literature on continual learning of neural networks (Sun et al., 2020; Peng et al.,
056   2024; Shi et al., 2024) and consists of the re-introduction of some of the pre-training samples during
057   FT. This requires access to pre-training (or equivalent) data and leads to a considerable increase in
058   the compute budget. Moreover, generalist language models tend to come already finetuned for a
059   wide range of tasks, and crude replay on the approximate distribution of the pre-training data risks
060   tipping the carefully honed balance of changes introduced by post-training refinements (Dubey et al.,
061   2024). Parameter-efficient finetuning (PEFT) is a more recent class of techniques conceived with the
062   aim of reducing the computational and memory requirements for LLM finetuning (Houlsby et al.,
063
      2019; Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; 2024). While the computational savings are the main benefit,
      PEFT methods also impose an often-tight constraint on the amount of representational power avail-
064
      able for the model’s adaptation to the new domain as a side effect. This acts as a natural backstop
065
      against model overfitting on the FT data (since the trainable representation capacity is insufficient to
066   overwrite too much of the pre-trained knowledge), but being at a distance from the training process,
067   it does not in any way guarantee that any amount of previous knowledge will be preserved. This
068   can be fatally detrimental to generation, which in the longer span rests on entire sequences of tokens
069   being predicted appropriately for the context. Marginal methods such as parameter ensembles and
070   averaging or tailored adjustments to the optimization process also exist (Lin et al., 2024; Parmar
071   et al., 2024).
072   Given the known shortfalls of the above remedies, it is desirable to have a technique that relies
073   neither on modifications to the model nor on external data, but is largely independent and could be
074   combined with them for an even better outcome. To this end, we introduce minifinetuning (MFT),
075   which affects solely the finetuning training objective. At the heart of MFT is the construction of
076   a per-token individualized, corrected distribution that combines the predicted distribution of the
077   original model before finetuning and the ground truth token label of the finetuning data, but only to
078   an extent that is not too destructive to the general knowledge of the model. Thoroughly ablating for
079   every component of the distribution correction process, we find that “moving the goal posts” in the
080
      form of correcting the original unfinetuned model prediction towards the ground truth individually
      for every token is necessary for the model to improve by finetuning without significant detriment to
081
      the model’s knowledge.

      MFT effectively amounts to algorithmically controlled adaptive self-distillation, in which a copy of
096   the unfinetuned model acts as a teacher, the MFT corrective formula acts as an automated negotiator
097   of teacher’s knowledge with the incoming finetuning data, and the model under finetuning acts as a
098   student (cf. Figure 4). In this sense, it is the teacher’s predictions that act as the anchor preventing
099   distant deviations from the pre-training data by communicating a compressed replacement for replay
100   data in the form of soft labels.
101
      We focus exclusively on generative language model adaptation for language generation on a new,
102   specialized domain (e.g. a company knowledge base or literary works of one author), and do not
103   examine cases in which the model undergoes FT for particular tasks or abilities (e.g. translation,
104   summarization, reasoning). These cases, although too potential beneficiaries of minifinetuning, are
105   not studied. Further, we consider domain adaptation finetuning to operate in low-data regime if the
106   total data available is around or less than the amount of tokens in one batch used to pre-train the
107   given model – usually in the order of millions for small- and medium-sized models – and firmly
      out of reach of in-context learning methods for such models. Such scenarios arise when tuning a

      Training cost. In the most general setting, MFT requires two forward passes instead of one be-
188   cause of the addition of the teacher forward pass. Likewise, the memory required to store the model
189   weights doubles when changing from FT to MFT. However, while twice the amount of memory
190   is needed to store all parameters, the total training memory footprint is far from doubled. This is
191   because the number of trainable parameters remains the same, and the main driver for the memory
192   footprint is the optimizer state and is as much as 12 bytes (3-6x the parameter footprint) for Adam in
193   mixed-precision (Rajbhandari et al., 2020). Furthermore, PEFT methods such as LoRA, DoRA, or
194   IA3 (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; 2022) preserve original model parameters and thus duplicat-
195   ing model weights can be avoided when MFT is used, although two separate forward passes remain
196
      necessary.

200   3.1   M ETHODOLOGY
201
202   We compare MFT to FT across a range of models on several domain-specialized datasets, and in
203
      combination with both replay and various PEFT methods.
204
205
      Models. The general evaluation is performed on OpenELM 270M, OpenELM 450M, and
      OpenELM 1.1B (Mehta et al., 2024). These models all share the same tokenizer, the same pre-
206
      training recipe and datasets, and have undergone the same post-training adjustments. Keeping these
207
      factors constant allows us to examine the impact of model size on the FT/MFT effectiveness. Ex-
208   tended results for the GPT-Neo family (125M, 1.3B, 2.7B) (Black et al., 2021), Phi-1.5 and Phi-2
209   (1.3B, 2.7B) (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024), Gemma 2B (Gemma Team et al., 2024),
210   Minitron 4B (Muralidharan et al., 2024), and LLaMA 2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023) are listed in
211   Appendices D and H.
212
213   Data. To test on three different specialized domains, we employ: (i) PMC Open Access Subset
214   representing the medical domain (Bethesda, 2024); (ii) Pile of Law (Henderson et al., 2022); and
215   (iii) OpenWebMath (Paster et al., 2023). To keep track of the model understanding of the general
      domain, we use OpenWebText (Gokaslan & Cohen, 2019), which we found not to result in any


                                                           4

216
      significant distribution shift when preparing reference checkpoints (cf. Process). From each dataset
217   we split off 0.5M-token worth of documents for validation and always use the same 4M-token worth
218   of documents for training. The data frugality is intentional (cf. Section 1); note that the entire dataset
219   is as big as a single batch of data used in LLaMa 2 pre-training (Touvron et al., 2023). We train on
220   2048-token sequences.
221
222   Baselines. Baseline FT is performed with no additional adjustments. When considering replay
223   without further context, we design the experiments so that each batch contains 50% samples from
224   the general domain and 50% samples from the specialized domain. From among the popular LM
225   PEFT methods, we consider LoRA, DoRA, and IA3 (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; 2024), and
226   where no further detail is given, the LoRA/DoRA rank is taken to be 8 and applied to all attention
227   and MLP projections in the model.
228
229   Process. For each model, we first prepare a reference checkpoint lightly tuned on OpenWebText
230   on at most 4M unique tokens and choose the checkpoint with the least validation perplexity. We
231
      note that this process often converges very quickly and before the data budget is reached, as the
      language models considered also tend to come from pre-training on general text (i.e. text not spe-
232
      cific to any single domain). Then, for each model, each specialized-domain dataset, and each na-
233
      tive/replay/PEFT method, we train two models, one utilizing classical FT and one with MFT. To
234   report the results, we choose the checkpoint with least validation perplexity on the specialized do-
235   main. Each model is trained for 1000 steps with batch size 16, resulting in 32M tokens being seen
236   during the course of training. With the training dataset fixed at 4M tokens, each context is seen 8
237   times on average by the end of the training, giving models ample opportunity to absorb the informa-
238   tion and begin to overfit if prone to do so. For MFT training, we fix τ = 0.25.
239
240   Metrics. We measure the relative decrease in validation perplexity on the specialized domain
241   (“specialization”, S), the relative increase in validation perplexity on the general domain (“degen-
242   eralization”, DG), the ratio of the two relative changes in perplexity (= DG/S). Ideally, a good
243   finetuning method would lead to high values of specialization S, low values of degeneralization
244   DG, and by extension a low DG/S ratio.

367   3.4   R ELATIONSHIP TO PEFT
368
369   In Section 1 we note that it is popular practice to use PEFT methods both for decreasing the mem-
370   ory/computational requirements of finetuning and for the mitigation of degeneralization, the latter
371   appearing as a convenient side-effect of the reduction of the number of trainable parameters.
372   But how do PEFT methods fare against MFT in terms of specialization and degeneralization of the
373   tuned models? We compare MFT to DoRA (Liu et al., 2024). DoRA has been recently shown to
374   affect the weights of the resulting model less than the standard LoRA approach, meaning that it is
375   positioned more favourably with respect to the design goals of MFT. The comparison is performed
376   by running experiments for different values of rank (reported as relative rank ρ = r/200) and target
377   τ , and we measure the effect of the two methods in different configurations on specialization and
      degeneralization performance of the most-specialized checkpoint.

389   4     A BLATION STUDY
390
391   MFT introduces a distinction between outputs whose argmax agrees with the token ground truth
392   label (i.e. the correct predictions or correct prediction distributions) and those outputs for which this
393   is not the case (incorrect predictions) and that thus require a correction (cf. Section 2). With this
394   dichotomy in mind, we can consider an incremental sequence of four relevant methods.
395
      Finetuning. This is the standard finetuning approach. The distribution of the finetuned model is
396
      trained against the one-hot target distribution of the ground truth regardless of whether the unfine-
397   tuned model prediction distribution for a given token is correct with respect to the ground truth.
398
399   Distillation & corrective finetuning on incorrect tokens. One can require that the model keeps its
400
      original (unfinetuned) distribution on inputs where it produces the correct prediction distributions,
      and learns the ground truth label only on those tokens where it produces incorrect prediction distri-
401
      butions. This can be realized by training the model to distill the unfinetuned model on the correct
402
      tokens and learn the one-hot distributions on inputs leading to incorrect predictions as in FT. In this
403   manner, the prediction distributions of the unfinetuned model are to serve as a form of anchoring
404   preventing a destructive distribution shift from the original to the new domain.
405
406
      Distillation & corrective distillation on incorrect tokens. Moving one step further on the above
      method, one can insist that the incorrect distributions are first corrected and then passed to the
407
      model in place of the ground truth one-hot distributions. This behaviour can be achieved by fixing
408
      β = 0 in the distribution correction formula DC (pT ) (cf. Section 2). With the corrections to the
409
      training target affecting only the targets where the unfinetuned model predictions are incorrect, one
410   can speak of singly-corrective distillation.
411
412   Corrective distillation on both correct and incorrect tokens – Minifinetuning. This is our method,
413
      outlined in detail in Section 2. Given that the target distributions for both the correct and incorrect
414
      predictions is threshold-adjusted (leading to the scaling factors α, β in DC (pT )), we may think of
      MFT as of doubly-corrective distillation.
415
416   We perform incremental ablation, which includes the measurement of performance of the two meth-
417   ods more complex than the baseline FT but less complex than MFT. We mirror the setup of Section 3
418   but restrict our ablation to the Pile of Law and smaller models of the OpenELM family. The results
419   are listed in Table 3, and we make a number of observations.

490   6   R ELATED W ORK
491
492
      Domain adaptation. Previous work on language model domain adaptation recognizes the inherent
      parameter-sharing behaviour (Chronopoulou et al., 2021) and domain-mixing representation entan-
493
      glement (Li et al., 2020) arising from continued model training on a specialized domain. These
494
      are then made use of by separating domain-specific parameters and decomposing representations,
495   respectively, or countered by adversarial training objective adjustments (Vu et al., 2020) in order to
496   achieve greater inference efficiency or predictive performance. All of this work, however, implicitly
497   assumes that a sufficient data mass is available for the new domain and does not concern itself with
498   the severe consequences of tuning when data is scarce.
499
      Low-resource domain adaptation. Recent studies addressing the problem of low-resource domain
500
      adaptation in language modeling (Diao et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023) make use of full-scale meta-
501   models wrapping around the original language models in order to aid their language understanding
502   or generation abilities. This is in contrast with our method, which still focuses on adapting the
503   original model as a monolith. Moreover, the definition of a “low-resource” setting varies, with Diao
504   et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2023) considering order(s) of magnitude larger data budgets than ours to
505   already fall into this category, even though the methods might themselves resort to data filtering to
506   bolster efficiency gains. To the best of our knowledge, no previous work examines the problem of
507   model adaptation through directly tuning on scarce data.
508   Parameter-efficient FT. As detailed in Section 1, PEFT methods have been observed to act as
509   a natural backstop to model overfitting due to the limited representational power they lend to the
510   process of finetuning. This property has seen use in low-resource settings (Su et al., 2024; Zhang
511   et al., 2024), though the studies generally observe varying degrees of success across different and
512   differently-configured PEFT methods. In contrast, MFT behaves consistently across entire families
513   of models (cf. Section 3.2) and applications of PEFT methods (cf. Section 3.4), and acts as a flexible
514   complementary technique.
515   Self-distillative FT. A recent work proposes to leverage hard-label self-distillation for instruction
516   data augmentation in order to reduce distribution shift introduced by post-training instruction tuning
517   (Yang et al., 2024). While closely related to MFT in its goals, the work takes an ahead-of-time
518   generative data-augmentation approach rather than the on-the-fly soft-label distillation approach
519   taken by us, and is tailored to work on chat-style instruction datasets that are an order of magnitude
520   larger.
521
522   7   L IMITATIONS
523
524   Model tuning for instruction following. We do not adjust for nor test our method in its present form
525   on instruction tuning datasets. This is because the most effective instruction tuning post-training
526   routines already come carefully configured and tend to operate on larger blends of post-training data
527   that make them fall outside the category of low-resource FT (Gemma Team et al., 2024; Dubey et al.,
528   2024). Nevertheless, we recognize this direction as a natural avenue for a broader application of our
529   method.
530   Transferring task performance to specialized domains. Conversely to the above point, one lim-
531   itation of this study is that it does not examine the effect MFT domain adaptation for language
532   generation has on model’s performance on general tasks. Our primary adaptation of all models
533   to general domain represented by one dataset in order to provide a universal starting point for the
534   assessment of specialization and degeneralization is an obstacle to such evaluation as it makes the
535   models forget parts of the knowledge gained in their post-training even before they begin to adapt to
536
      the specialized domain.
537   Document-level tuning. A natural extension of Section 5 would be to examine the effect of MFT
538   vs that of FT on single-document finetuning. We acknowledge this as another limitation of our
539   study but note that such experimentation is not necessary for a convincing demonstration of MFT’s
      prowess in low-data scenarios.
