032                                                            A BSTRACT
033
034
035              Multimodal foundation models, such as GPT-4o, have made remarkable progress
036              recently. However, it is not clear exactly where these models stand in terms of
037
                 understanding vision. In this paper, we evaluate the performance of popu-
                 lar multimodal foundation models (GPT-4o, Gemini Pro, Claude 3.5 Sonnet,
038
                 Qwen2-VL) at standard computer vision tasks (semantic segmentation, object
039
                 detection, image classification, depth and surface normal prediction) and using
040              established datasets (e.g., COCO, ImageNet and its variants, etc).
041
042              The main challenges to performing this are: 1) the models are trained to output
043              text and cannot natively express versatile domains, such as segments or 3D geom-
044              etry, and 2) many of the leading models are proprietary and accessible only at an
045
                 API level, i.e., there is no weight access to adapt them. We address these chal-
                 lenges by translating standard vision tasks into equivalent text-promptable and
046
                 API-compatible tasks via prompt chaining.
047
048              We observe that 1) the models are not close to the state-of-the-art at any tasks, and
049              2) they perform semantic tasks notably better than geometric ones. However, 3)
050              they are respectable generalists; this is remarkable as they are presumably trained
051              on only image-text-based tasks primarily. 4) While the prompting techniques af-
052              fect the performance, better models exhibit less sensitivity to prompt variations.
053
                 5) GPT-4o performs the best, getting the top position in 5 out of 6 tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Multimodal foundation models (MFMs), such as GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5 Pro, and Claude 3.5 Son-
057   net (OpenAI, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024), have gone far in recent months, with their
058   demos appearing highly impressive (OpenAI, 2024). However, while the community has extensively
059   investigated their remarkable language proficiency Hendrycks et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Rein
060   et al. (2023); Chiang et al. (2024), the extent of their vision capabilities is vague in comparison.
061   We still lack a well-calibrated understanding of their performance on established vision tasks and
062   datasets, particularly across diverse axes of vision, e.g. semantics, 3D, etc.
063   Most of the existing vision benchmarks of MFMs primarily target text (e.g., VQA) or tasks closely
064   tied to text, like classification. (Yue et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024b;a; Rahmanzade-
065   hgervi et al., 2024; Wu & Xie, 2024). While they provide useful insights, several key limitations
066   persist. First, it is unclear how much solving these benchmarks truly depends on the visual input,
067   and some were shown to mainly measure the language capabilities of MFMs while overlooking the
068   vision component (Tong et al., 2024a). Second, they all require the model to output text, making
069   it hard to compare the vision capabilities of MFMs against vision-only tasks and specialist models
070   developed by the community. Third, they do not shed light on other aspects of visual understanding,
071
      such as 3D geometry, grouping, or segmentation, that are less text oriented.
072   We address these limitations by evaluating MFMs on well-established vision tasks and datasets
073   developed by the community. Specifically, we test GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini 1.5 Pro, and
074   Qwen2-VL on classification, object detection, semantic segmentation, grouping, depth prediction,
075   and surface normal prediction using COCO, Hypersim, ImageNet and its variants (Lin et al., 2014;
076   Roberts et al., 2021; Russakovsky et al., 2014). Most of these tasks, however, require dense pixel-
077
      wise predictions not readily compatible with the default text output of MFMs. To address this
      challenge, we split each task into multiple sub-tasks, each of which can be solved in a textual form
078
      via prompting (see Sec. 3). This results in a prompt-chaining framework that can be applied to any
079
      MFMs with a text interface (e.g., ChatBot APIs) to solve standard vision tasks. Specifically, our
080   proposed approach allows MFMs to 1) detect bounding boxes, 2) generate complete segmentation
081   masks for complex scenes, 3) extract semantic entities from images similar to SAM (Kirillov et al.,
082   2023b), 4) estimate dense depth and surface normal maps. Please see Fig. 1 for an overview. This
083   enables direct comparison with vision-only models, offering a holistic understanding of the vision
084   capabilities of MFMs.
085
      We find that MFMs achieve good performance in most cases and show respectable generalist abil-
086   ities, with GPT-4o scoring the best in 5 out of 6 tasks. However, they still lag behind task-specific
087   state-of-the-art vision models in all tasks. In particular, we find that the MFMs perform geometric
088   tasks significantly worse than semantic ones. Furthermore, we perform a detailed prompt sensitivity
089   analysis for each task and find the performance varies for different prompts, though better models
090   exhibit less sensitivity. We will open-source our documented code to enable researchers to explore
091   performant prompt chaining strategies for MFMs.

      In this section, we describe the developed prompt chaining techniques that enable MFMs to solve
138   standard computer vision tasks, namely image classification, object detection, semantic segmenta-
139   tion, grouping, depth, and surface normal prediction. These techniques are based on the main idea
140   of breaking the original task into multiple simpler sub-tasks that can be solved in a language format,
141   e.g., identifying whether an object is present in a patch of an image. We then solve each sub-task
142   by prompting an MFM. To guide the choice of how to split each task into sub-tasks, we rely on
143   our early key observation that most MFMs are relatively strong at image classification (see, e.g.,
144   Tab. 1) and, therefore, try to split each task into multiple classification sub-tasks. We provide the
145   pseudo-code for each technique in the Appendix.
146   Image classification. This task involves directly identifying the main class of an image from a set of
147   classes. Here, the model is presented with a list of all ground-truth classes and tasked with assigning
148   the image to the correct category. Following (Jiang et al., 2024), we group images into batches for
149   efficiency, as we observed no significant decrease in accuracy when using this approach.
150   Object detection. In this task, the goal is to predict bounding box coordinates that tightly localize
151   the objects in the image. Similar to Yang et al. (2023b), our initial attempts showed that many
152   MFMs fail at predicting the coordinates directly. We, therefore, develop a prompt chaining method
153   and divide the original task into two stages. The first stage has a single sub-task to identify all present
154   objects in the image. In the second stage, for each object, we regress its coordinates via recursive
155   zooming. Specifically, we divide the image into grid cells and ask the model to identify whether (a
156   part of) the object is present in each cell. We then discard cells without objects, reducing the search
157   space. We apply this process recursively, progressively eliminating irrelevant regions of the image
158
      until only the object of interest remains present in the image. We use two grid resolutions: a coarse
      grid for quick downsampling and a finer grid for precise edge refinement that allows us to reduce
159
      the number of steps. Please see Fig. 2 for an overview and Algorithm 2 for the pseudo-code.
160
161   Semantic segmentation. In this task, the goal is to assign one of the semantic classes to each
      pixel in an image. Instead of per-pixel querying, we split the image into pixel groups using an

      Depth prediction. As predicting 3D from a single 2D image is inherently ambiguous, we perform
243
      relative depth prediction by querying the model to rank different parts of the image according to their
244
      distance from the camera. Like segmentation, querying at the pixel level directly from the image
245   is infeasible. Instead, we adopt a region-wise comparison strategy similar to Zoran et al. (2015).
246   To identify suitable regions for comparison, we first segment the image into superpixels. We then
247   randomly sample pairs of superpixels and query the MFM to rank these pairs based on relative depth.
248   These pairwise rankings are then globalized by minimizing the objective function from (Zoran et al.,
249   2015), which encourages assigning larger values to superpixels ranked deeper than those ranked
250   shallower in the pairwise comparisons (see C.3 for details). We then use the values assigned by the
251   objective to rank all superpixels. For simplicity, we assume that all pixels within a superpixel share
252   the same depth rank, allowing us to extend the superpixel-level depth predictions to a pixel-wise
253
      ranking across the entire image (control baselines are included in evaluations). Please see Fig. 5 for
      an overview and Algorithm 5 for pseudo-code.
254
255   Surface normal prediction. We follow a similar ranking approach as for depth. We use standard
256   basis vectors relative to the camera (right, up, and forward) as reference directions, and for each
257   randomly sampled pair of superpixels, we query the MFM to determine their relative alignment
258   with each basis vector. After we obtain the pairwise comparisons for each direction, we globalize
259
      them using the same algorithm used for depth (Zoran et al., 2015). This results in three distinct
      surface normal maps, one for each basis direction. Similar to depth, we assume uniformity within
260
      superpixels and assign the same rank to all pixels within each superpixel group (control baselines
261
      are included in evaluations). Please see Fig. 6 for an overview and Algorithm 6 for pseudo-code.

477   Prompt chaining vs naive prompting. We analyze the impact of using the prompt chain and
478   naive prompting in Tab. 8. Specifically, for bounding box regression, we directly query GPT-4o for
479   coordinates, while for semantic segmentation, we mark image regions and request corresponding

481   refer the reader to Appendix E for a detailed discussion, qualitative visuals, and other ablations.
482   Prompt sensitivity. We evaluate the MFMs across various prompts to assess their sensitivity to
483   word choice and prompt structure. We then select the most effective prompt on a small validation
484   set for the final results presented in Sec. 4. A comprehensive analysis is provided in Appendix D,
485   showing that there is some variation in performance with different prompts, and the performance is
      generally less prompt-dependent for better-performing ones, e.g., GPT-4o.

506   5   L IMITATIONS AND CONCLUSIONS
507
508
509   We investigate the vision capabilities of MFMs by translating standard computer vision tasks into an
510   API-compatible format that can be solvable via prompt chaining. Our results show that the MFMs
511   have relatively stronger performance in semantic tasks compared to geometric tasks, and GPT-4o
512
      is generally the best performing model, followed by Gemini 1.5 Pro, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and
      Qwen2-VL-72B. All MFMs lag significantly behind the vision specialists for all tasks, suggesting
513
      plenty of room for improvement in model development. Furthermore, while we improved “myopic”
514
      visual perception of current MFMs (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024) by incorporating different
515   techniques in prompting such as zooming and super-pixelation, more work is needed on the
516   prompting aspect to further reduce this tendency. Below we discuss some limitations of our work
517   and some future directions.
518
      Extension to other vision tasks. Our results and control baselines show that there exist relatively
519
      convenient prompt chains that decompose vision tasks into different sub-tasks that are solvable
520
      by MFMs. Developing similar chaining strategies for other vision tasks such as optical flow, 3D
521   semantics, etc. for a broader coverage is a fruitful future direction. We believe our exploration and
522   code serve as a good starting point.
523
524
      Improved prompt chaining. As shown in the qualitative and quantitative results, our prompting ap-
      proaches unlocked vision capabilities of current MFMs for several tasks. We also controlled for the
525
      impact of those choices on the comparative studies using appropriate control baselines. However,
526
      there is room for improving the absolute performance via including stronger chain-of-thought strate-
527   gies and pixel approximations. Another potentially interesting direction is incorporating in-context
528   learning and alignment techniques for boosting the performance.
529
530
      Costs, scalability, and inference time. The computational costs (provided in Appendix D) remain
      higher than vision specialists for the MFMs. These costs are expected to come down as MFM
531
      inference becomes cheaper. A promising direction for future work is to develop algorithms with im-
532
      proved “prompt complexity”, optimizing the balance between performance and token consumption.
533
534   Data contamination. The issue of data contamination is a broad concern for the community Jacovi
535   et al. (2023). While our evaluations show the conclusions are generalizable, the further development
536
      of evaluation sets could help achieve a more unbiased assessment of performance.
537   Absolute metrics for geometry. We utilize relative metrics for depth and surface normal prediction,
538   acknowledging the inherent ambiguity in these tasks. Our initial attempts to recover metric depth
539   from monocular images were unsuccessful. While this simplifies the evaluation, future research
      could focus on employing techniques like in-context learning to achieve metric depth estimates.

926
      Different variations of classification for object detection. As discussed in Section 3, the first stage
927
      of the object detection pipeline involves identifying all the objects present in the image. We attempt
928
      the following two strategies for the multi-label classification task:
929
930          • The first strategy simply provides the model with the entire image, asking it to identify all
931            present classes.
932
             • The second strategy divides the image into five regions: four quadrants and a center crop.
933            The model is asked to identify the classes present in the 5 regions in independent queries.
934            With each query, the full image is provided for additional context. The final prediction is
935            obtained by taking the union of the classes identified across all regions (see Algorithm 1
936            in the appendix for detailed pseudocode). This approach typically improves recall but may
937            reduce precision, reflecting a trade-off between the two strategies.
938
939   The precision-recall trade-off for the models is described in Tab. 9. To pick the best classification
940
      strategy for the models, we run the oracle on the predicted labels on a small subset and pick the one
      that yields the highest AP.

943   ing boxes.

       C.3   D EPTH ESTIMATION
1027
1028   The procedure for depth estimation is given in Algorithm 5. A crucial part of the algorithm involves
1029   optimizing the objective to obtain the overall depth rankings. To formulate the objective for glob-
1030   alizing the pairwise depth rankings, we re-purpose the objective in Zoran et al. (2015). Given the
1031   vector of global rankings x ∈ RN , we first consider instances where superpixel i is predicted to be
1032   at a greater depth than superpixel j. The corresponding objective is formulated as:
1033
                                                      X
                                           Lgt (x) =     (xi − xj − 1)2                                 (1)
1034
                                                       i,j
1035
1036   This objective encourages xi , ranked at a greater depth than xj , to take on higher values. Similarly,
1037   an analogous objective Llt can be defined for superpixels xi predicted to be at a lesser depth than
1038
       xj .
1039   Following Zoran et al. (2015), we include a smoothness regularization term to stabilize the depth
1040   estimations:                                 X
1041                                       Ls (x) =    (xi − xj )2                                   (2)
1042                                                       i,j
1043   This regularization is applied over pairs of adjacent superpixels i and j, promoting smooth transi-
1044   tions between their depth values.
1045   The final objective that needs to be minimized is a weighted sum of the above terms:
1046
1047                                   x = min (λgt Lgt + λlt Llt + λs Ls )                               (3)
                                              x
1048
       where λgt , λlt , and λs are the weight parameters. For our experiments, we select λgt = λlt = 1 and
1049
       λs = 20.
1050
1051   To obtain metric depth estimates, we assume access to ground-truth depth values for the purpose
1052   of scaling. Specifically, after flood-filling the values of x, we generate a complete relative depth
1053   map d. Given the ground-truth depth map d∗ , we optimize the following objective to determine the
1054
       appropriate scale and shift parameters:
1055                                                       M
                                                           X
1056                                   (s, t) = arg min            (sdi + t − d∗i )2                      (4)
                                                     s,t
1057                                                         i=1
1058   where M is the total number of pixels in the image. By solving this optimization problem, we can
1059   then scale and shift the relative depth map d to align it with the metric depth.
1060
1061   Algorithm 5 Depth Estimation
1062    1: procedure E STIMATE D EPTH(image, numP airs)
1063    2:    superpixels ← SLIC(image)
1064    3:    pairwiseRankings ← ∅
1065    4:    for i ← 1 to numP airs do
1066    5:        pair ← SampleRandomPair(superpixels)
1067    6:        ranking ← QueryMFM(pair)
1068    7:        pairwiseRankings ← pairwiseRankings ∪ {ranking}
1069    8:    end for
1070
        9:    globalRankings ← MinimizeObjective(pairwiseRankings)
       10:    depthM ap ← AssignDepthToPixels(image, superpixels, globalRankings)
1071
       11:    return depthM ap
1072
       12: end procedure

1267   E.3   G ROUPING
1268
1269   For the grouping task, we filter out 100 COCO images that contain instances which are well-posed
1270   for this task. The well-posedness of an instance for grouping is measured by how consistent the
1271
       SAM predictions are for the instance. To calculate the consistency of predictions for an instance, we
       sample random points inside the instance and use SAM to obtain an instance mask for each point
1272
       individually, as well as a global mask by querying all points together. The mIoU between individ-
1273
       ual masks and the global mask is used as the consistency metric. Finally, the images that contain
1274   instances with a consistency value above a given threshold are selected and randomly sampled to
1275   create the evaluation set.

1332   E.6   B LIND GUESS
1333
1334   As mentioned in Section 4, a useful way to analyze the potential biases of the MFM, and to gauge
1335
       the degree to which it uses the visual content is a blind guess, or prompting the image with a blank
       image. In particular:
1336
1337
              • For object detection, we ask the model to imagine classes present. After this, we ask it to
1338            provide reasonable coordinates for the objects based on its world knowledge.
1339
1340          • For semantic segmentation, we mark a rectangle in a white image and force the model to
1341
                predict a class. We ask the model to use the location to make an educated guess.
1342          • For depth, we ask the model to imagine an indoor setting. We mark two rectangles and
1343            force the model to predict that one is at a greater depth than the other.
1344          • For normals, we repeat the procedure for depth for each direction.
1345
1346   The results for GPT-4o are visualized in Fig. 15, and reveal several interesting insights.
1347
1348          • For object detection, the model chooses common classes like person and car. Additionally,
1349            it seems to grasp the relative sizes of objects reasonably well, as indicated by its tendency
                to make the car and the bench longer.


                                                                      25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
              • For semantic segmentation, the model makes reasonable guesses. For instance, it guesses
1351            ”sky-merged” and ”airplane” at the top of the image, ”person” near the middle, ”dog,”
1352            ”cat,” and ”floor” near the bottom.
1353
              • For depth estimation, GPT-4o exhibits a ”ceiling bias” and consistently infers that the top
1354
                right corner is located at a greater relative depth. We observe that this bias is reflected in
1355            several of the model’s predictions as well, where the ceiling is consistently assumed to be
1356            at a greater depth.
1357
              • For surface normals, the model uses the relative locations of the rectangles to form judg-
1358
                ments. For instance, in the x direction, it infers that the right rectangle aligns more towards
1359            the right. In the y direction, it consistently infers that the bounding box at a greater y coor-
1360            dinate aligns more with the positive y direction. While Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning
1361            is able to break this bias along the y direction for GPT-4o, the left-right bias persists when
1362            actual images are presented.
