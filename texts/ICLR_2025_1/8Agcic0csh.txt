009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are typically trained using backpropagation,
012            which, despite its effectiveness, requires substantial memory and computing re-
013            sources. To address these limitations, we propose a novel local training frame-
014            work that enables efficient and scalable neural network training without relying
015            on global backpropagation. Our framework harnesses the alignment of Singu-
016            lar Value Decomposed (SVD) weight space with feedback matrices, guided by
017            custom layerwise loss functions, to enable efficient and scalable neural network
018            training. We decompose weight matrices into their SVD components before train-
019
               ing, and perform local updates on the SVD components themselves, driven by
               a tailored objective that integrates feedback error, alignment regularization, or-
020
               thogonality constraints, and sparsity. Our approach leverages Direct Feedback
021
               Alignment (DFA) to eliminate the need for global backpropagation and further
022            optimizes model complexity by dynamically reducing the rank of the SVD com-
023            ponents during training. The result is a compute- and memory-efficient model
024            with classification accuracy on par with traditional backpropagation while achiev-
025            ing a 50-75% reduction in memory usage and computational cost during training.
026            With strong theoretical convergence guarantees, we demonstrate that training in
027            the SVD space with DFA not only accelerates computation but also offers a pow-
028            erful, energy-efficient solution for scalable deep learning in resource-constrained
029            environments. Code is available.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As neural networks grow in size and complexity, the memory and computational requirements for
035   training have become a significant bottleneck, particularly in resource-constrained environments
036   such as edge devices. Backpropagation (BP), the most commonly used method for training deep
037   networks, increases this issue as it relies on a global loss objective and needs to store intermediate
038   activations for layer-by-layer gradient updates. This not only demands high memory usage and
039
      computational power, but also introduces the update-locking issue(Lillicrap et al., 2020), in which
      parameters of the hidden layer cannot be updated until both forward and backward computations
040
      are completed. Hence, we highlight the need for alternative training paradigms that are both more
041
      memory and compute-efficient and enable efficient parallelization of the training process.
042
043   Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016) offers a promising alternative to backpropa-
044   gation (BP) by enabling local layerwise updates without global gradient propagation, which re-
045
      duces the hardware complexity of neural network training. This makes DFA appealing for resource-
      constrained environments. However, DFA faces limitations in scaling to more complex tasks and
046
      deeper architectures, largely due to its reliance on fixed random feedback connections. These con-
047
      nections limit its ability to train deep convolutional layers and perform well on datasets like CIFAR-
048   100 and ImageNet without transfer learning (Launay et al., 2020). Although the integration of the
049   Kolen-Pollack algorithm (Akrout et al., 2019) has improved feedback alignment by learning sym-
050   metric feedback weights, DFA still struggles to match BP in terms of accuracy and scalability. While
051   DFA’s simplified learning rules have been well-studied, its potential combination with low-rank sub-
052   spaces—such as those from Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)—remains underexplored. This
053   synergy could be critical, as low-rank subspaces can reduce computational complexity, complement-
      ing DFA’s goal of simpler learning rules.

080   We propose a novel hybrid training framework- SVD-Space Alignment (SSA) that synergistically
081   combines the simplified local learning rules of Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) with the struc-
082   tured learning advantages of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). SVD decomposes weight matri-
083   ces into orthogonal components, allowing for efficient updates in low-rank subspaces. By aligning
084   the SVD spaces of both the forward weights and feedback matrices, our method introduces struc-
085
      ture into the learning process, countering the randomness inherent in DFA’s feedback matrices and
      improving scalability, stability, and faster convergence. Our approach begins by decomposing the
086
      weight matrices into their SVD components— U , S, and V T —before training commences. We ap-
087
      ply local updates to the SVD components using a custom loss function that minimizes model cross-
088   entropy loss, maintains alignment between forward and feedback weights, preserves orthogonality,
089   and enforces sparsity for efficiency. A key feature is the progressive rank reduction of SVD compo-
090   nents during training, reducing memory and computation while enabling energy-efficient inference
091   optimized for resource-constrained environments. Our work introduces several key innovations that
092   make DFA suitable for deep convolutional layers, addressing a significant gap in prior research.
093   Previous attempts to apply local loss methods to convolutional layers have relied on flattening these
094   layers, which leads to a loss of hierarchical and spatial features critical for vision-based applications.
095   In contrast, we preserve the convolutional structure by applying spatial-wise decomposition embed-
096
      ded within SVD matrices, allowing our method to retain the hierarchical information necessary for
      accurate visual recognition tasks.
097
098   Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
099
100          • We propose a novel hybrid training framework that incorporates feedback alignment with
101
               Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), leveraging structured updates in SVD-space to mit-
               igate the limitations of random feedback.
102
103          • We introduce a custom loss function that incorporates feedback error, alignment loss, or-
104            thogonality regularization, and sparsity constraints. This ensures that local layerwise up-
105
               dates in the SVD-space are efficient and convergent.
106          • We demonstrate significant reductions in memory usage and computational complexity
107            through progressive rank reduction, achieving up to 50-75% reductions in memory usage
               and computational requirements.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
115
116   Our work draws upon several lines of research, including Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA), the
117   application of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) in neural networks, local layerwise training
118   methods, and model compression techniques. This section reviews these areas.
119
      Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA): Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) (Nøkland, 2016) was in-
120
      troduced as a biologically plausible alternative to backpropagation (BP), reducing memory usage
121
      by decoupling weight updates from global error gradients and using random feedback connections.
122   However, DFA struggles with gradient alignment, leading to instability in deeper networks. Nøkland
123   and Eidnes (Nøkland & Eidnes, 2019) improved DFA by introducing local error signals and aux-
124   iliary classifiers, enabling more stable layer-wise updates in deep networks. Building on this, our
125   approach aligns SVD-decomposed weight spaces with feedback matrices, using custom loss func-
126   tions to reduce feedback randomness and improve DFA’s scalability, stability, and efficiency, while
127   lowering model complexity.
128   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) in Neural Networks: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
129   is widely used in machine learning to compress models and improve computational efficiency by
130   reducing the dimensionality of weight matrices. Early works, like (Denil et al., 2013), showed that
131   low-rank approximations can lower the number of parameters and FLOPs with minimal performance
132   loss. More recent approaches, such as (Denton et al., 2014) and (Yang et al., 2020), applied SVD
133   to CNNs, achieving significant inference speedups. Although (Yang et al., 2020) also incorporated
134   an orthogonality regularizer to maintain stability, these methods still rely on backpropagation for
135   updates, which can disrupt orthogonality. Our method addresses this by integrating SVD with Direct
136
      Feedback Alignment (DFA), preserving orthogonality and dynamically reducing the rank during
      training to create compact models without sacrificing accuracy.
137
138   Local Layerwise Training: Local learning rules, which update layers independently of global gra-
139   dients, are gaining attention for their scalability and efficiency. Methods like greedy layerwise train-
140   ing (Belilovsky et al., 2019), DRTP (Frenkel et al., 2021), and AugLocal (Ma et al., 2024), re-
141   duce backpropagation’s memory costs using local losses or auxiliary networks. Forward-only meth-
142
      ods, such as Hinton’s Forward-Forward (FF) (Hinton, 2022) and PEPITA (Dellaferrera & Kreiman,
      2022), avoid backprop entirely but often fall short in accuracy, especially in deeper networks, and
143
      struggle with global alignment. Our work focuses on improving local loss methods, as forward-only
144
      approaches struggle with classification accuracy in deeper networks. We enhance local layerwise
145   training by applying Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to layer weights and aligning compo-
146   nents with feedback matrices using Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA).
147
148
      Model Compression Techniques: Model compression techniques like pruning, quantization, and
      low-rank factorization (Marinó et al., 2023) reduce neural network size and computation but often
149
      require retraining to recover accuracy and rely on backpropagation. Our approach trains low-rank
150
      SVD components from scratch, with no retraining needed. Progressive rank reduction dynamically
151   adjusts model complexity, maintaining accuracy while creating compact models. Using DFA for
152   feedback also improves hardware efficiency and scalability.

154   3   M ETHODOLOGY
155
156   We present SVD-Space Alignment (SSA), a novel method combining Singular Value Decomposition
157   (SVD) with Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) for efficient local layerwise training in deep neural
158   networks. By decomposing weight matrices with SVD and applying local updates using our custom
159   loss function (including DFA’s feedback error), SSA reduces computational complexity and memory
160   usage. The custom loss function ensures structured learning, incorporating alignment, orthogonality,
161   and sparsity regularization. Additionally, a dynamic rank reduction strategy further optimizes the
      model for edge devices.

261

268   The computational and memory requirements of the SSA method are significantly lower than those
269   of traditional BP, both during training and inference, due to the use of dynamic rank reduction and
      low-rank SVD representations of the neural network weights.

      Training Details: All experiments are conducted on a machine with NVIDIA A40 GPUs and 48
325   GB of GDDR6 memory. We implement the SSA method and baselines using PyTorch. Training
326   is performed with the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014), with a learning rate ranging from 1e−4
327   to 5e−4 (detailed in Appendix), and data augmentation techniques like random cropping and hor-
328   izontal flipping to improve generalization. The batch size is 128 for CIFAR-10/100 and 256 for
329   ImageNet. For SSA, we start with the initial rank r0 at the full rank of the original weight matrices,
330   progressively reducing it as outlined in Section 3.4.
331
332   Hyperparameter Selection. The coefficients α, β, γ, δ, ϵ are set as (0.1, 0.01, 0.1, 0.05, 0.01),
333   consistent across all experiments. Feedback cross-entropy error and alignment loss, being convex
334   and smooth, are assigned higher weights, while non-convex components such as cosine similarity
335   loss, orthogonality loss, and the Hoyer regularizer have lower weights (Theoretical Analysis in Ap-
336   pendix). To mitigate non-convexity, we ensure quasi-convexity by projecting the cosine similarity
337
      loss and SVD component norms onto the unit sphere. The Hoyer regularizer is smoothened (71) and
      applied only every ten epochs during rank reduction.
338
339   Theoretical analysis and ablation studies (Section 5.3) guide these choices. Ablation results demon-
340   strate the impact of removing individual loss components, emphasizing the need to balance their
341   contributions. To confirm the robustness of the coefficients, we perform basic k = 3-fold cross-
342   validation, evaluating a small grid of candidate values. This process takes approximately 5%-10%
343
      of the total training time and leverages the independence of local loss objectives, avoiding the com-
      plexity of global optimization. Once selected, the coefficients remain fixed across all experiments.

499   6.2   L IMITATIONS
500
501   While SSA demonstrates promising results, it also presents certain limitations that warrant further
502   exploration:
503
504   Rank Reduction Trade-offs: Although the progressive rank reduction strategy effectively reduces
505   memory and computational costs, it may introduce performance trade-offs, especially in scenarios
506   where an aggressive reduction in rank leads to a loss of model capacity. In some cases, the re-
507   duced representational power could result in lower accuracy, particularly for highly complex tasks
508   or datasets. This suggests the need for careful tuning of the rank reduction schedule, potentially
509   adapting it dynamically based on the task’s complexity or during different training phases.
510
511   Hyperparameter Sensitivity: The performance of the SSA method is sensitive to the choice of
512   hyperparameters, particularly the coefficients (α, β, γ, δ, ϵ) that weigh the individual loss compo-
513   nents in the composite loss function. While cross-validation helps select these parameters, the
514   method could benefit from adaptive mechanisms that dynamically adjust the weights during training
515   to optimize performance.
516
517   Linear Separability of Intermediate Features: While SSA successfully extends to ResNet-32
518   with minimal accuracy loss on large datasets, scaling to deeper networks may pose challenges.
519   Specifically, optimizing earlier layers with local loss objectives can lead to limited support for train-
520   ing subsequent layers, potentially affecting the quality of learned representations. Unlike BP or
521   AugLocal, SSA demonstrates higher linear separability in early layers, suggesting that the features
522
      learned may be less general and less transferable to deeper layers. Addressing this limitation and
      improving the alignment between layerwise and global objectives will be a focus of future work.

525   7     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we presented a novel local training framework that leverages Singular Value Decompo-
528   sition (SVD) combined with Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) for efficient local layerwise neural
529   network training. Our method, SSA, decomposes the weight matrices of each layer into their SVD
530   components and applies local updates on the SVD components itself, driven by a composite loss
531   function. This loss function incorporates feedback error, alignment loss, orthogonality regulariza-
532   tion, and sparsity constraints, enabling structured and efficient learning.
533
      The experimental results demonstrated that SSA achieves classification accuracy on par with back-
534   propagation while significantly reducing memory usage, computational cost, and energy consump-
535   tion. The method’s progressive rank reduction strategy ensures that the model becomes more
536   lightweight throughout training, making it highly suitable for deployment on resource-constrained
537   devices. Theoretical analysis guarantees convergence of our loss objectives, while ablation studies
538   highlight the role of each loss component in balancing accuracy and efficiency. SSA offers a com-
539   pelling scalable and energy-efficient alternative to backpropagation, paving the way for biologically
      inspired, resource-aware neural network training in real-world applications.

1275   standard data augmentation techniques, including random cropping (with 4-pixel padding) and hor-
1276   izontal flipping, are applied. Training is conducted over 200 epochs using Adam with an initial
1277   learning rate of 1 × 10−4 . A learning rate scheduler reduces the rate by a factor of 10 at the 20th,
1278   40th, and 60th epochs. A batch size of 128 is used, with He initialization for convolutional layers
1279   and Xavier initialization for fully connected layers.
1280   For SSA, weight matrices start at full rank and are progressively reduced every 10 epochs
1281   while retaining 95% matrix energy.             Loss coefficients are fixed at (α, β, γ, δ, ϵ) =
1282   (0.1, 0.01, 0.1, 0.05, 0.01), determined through cross-validation.
