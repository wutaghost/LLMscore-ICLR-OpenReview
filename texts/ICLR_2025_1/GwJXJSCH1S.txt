009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce a new task for video editing: Contextual Harmonious Local Editing,
012            which focuses on replacing a local moving subject in videos containing multi-
013            ple subjects or reference objects. The goal is to ensure that the replaced subject
014            maintains its original motion while its size remains harmonious with the scene’s
015            context. Previous methods often face two specific challenges when addressing this
016
               task: (1) ensuring the size of the replaced subject remains contextually harmonious
               (2) maintaining the original motion and achieving subject replacement without be-
017
               ing affected by the motion of other subjects. To address the above problems, we
018
               propose a novel three stage video editing pipeline. We initially leverage large pre-
019            trained models to acquire knowledge about the shape and size differences between
020            the original and replaced subjects. To mitigate interference from context motion,
021            we erase other moving subjects to extract the target subject’s motion and dynam-
022            ically choose the editing method to preserve the original subject’s motion under
023            different shape transformations. Following that,we seamlessly replace the original
024            subject in the video with the resized edited subject, ensuring its size harmonizes
025            with the video’s context. As the first work to focus on this task, we also provide a
026            high-quality evaluation dataset and metrics to assess the performance of existing
027
               methods on this task. Experimental results based on this dataset demonstrate that
               our method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   In recent years, significant progress has been made in video editing Jeong et al. (2024b); Jeong &
052   Ye (2023). However, in complex backgrounds or multi-subject scenes, the challenge of maintaining
053   the motion of specific objects and adjusting their size to blend harmouniously with the scene re-
      mains unsolved. This research focuses on contextually harmonious local video editing, aiming to


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      replace specific subjects in multi-subject or reference-based backgrounds in a harmonious manner.
055   This requires the replaced subject, under different shape transformations Qi et al. (2023), retains its
056   original motion while adopting a size that harmoniously integrates into the video scene.
057
058
      Accomplishing such editing involves addressing two key tasks: (1) To maintain harmony with other
      elements in the video, it’s often necessary to adjust the subject’s size when its semantics change. For
059
      instance, in Figure 1, when the “lady” transforms into a “little girl”, her height relative to the bench
060
      should also change accordingly to maintain consistency in the scene. However, existing methods
061   often struggle to understand how size should change with semantic shifts because they lack basic
062   common sense. Additionally, it is challenging to scale the replaced subject according to a specific
063   change ratio. To ensure continuity in the subject’s movement during editing, it’s crucial to map the
064   original subject’s feature points to the generated one and account for background changes due to
065   the altered area. For example, when a “woman” is transformed into a “little girl”, her shoulder and
066   knee positions must be adjusted to align her actions with the scene, and the editing process should
067   adjust the information of the background area she obscures. (2) The edited object should preserve
068   the motion of the original subject. This challenge lies in the fact that the motion of the edited
069
      subject may be influenced by other subjects’ motion in the video, as depicted in Figure 1(right),
      where two subjects exhibit significantly different motions—such as a person moving forward while a
070
      helicopter rotates—the model faces substantial challenges in accurately transferring specific motion
071
      characteristics between these divergent actions. This difficulty is rooted in the intrinsic complexities
072   of capturing and interpreting distinct, simultaneous motion patterns within a unified framework.
073   Moreover, this task becomes more complex due to varying requirements for motion preservation
074   based on shape transformations, as seen in Figure 1, when the shape difference is small, fine-grained
075   motions should be retained (dog → white tiger); however, with significant shape changes, only the
076   dynamic patterns of the main keypoints need to be considered (helicopter → UFO).
077
      To address these challenges, we propose a novel video editing pipeline that consists of three main
078   stages: Motion and World Knowledge Acquisition, Shape Adapted Subject-centric Editing and
079   Coarse-to-Fine Harmonious Subject Transfer. To help the model understand the knowledge re-
080   garding the extent of size changes before and after the subject transformation, we leverage large
081   pretrained models to assess the relative size ratio between the original and replaced subject, aid-
082   ing the edited subject in presenting a harmonious proportion within the video context. In addition,
083   this module also perceives the moving objects in the video context and the shape differences of the
084   edited subject relative to the original subject, providing prior knowledge for preserving the motion
085   of the edited subject. In the Shape adapted subject-centric Editing stage, to prevent interference
086
      from the motion of other subjects during the editing process, we erase non-editing subjects and crop
      the target subject’s motion region. Following this, we dynamically select the most suitable editing
087
      method based on the intensity of shape transformation between the original and target subjects, aim-
088
      ing at achieving motion preservation for the edited subject under different levels of shape variation.
089   Finally, with the knowledge for size adjustment and the replaced subject, we introduce a Coarse-to-
090   Fine Harmonious Subject Transfer stage to address the challenge of subject replacement and size
091   transformation in multi-subject videos. In the Coarse Video Editing Phase, we obtain the motion
092   of the resized edited subject, as well as the background information from the misalignment region
093   between the edited subject and the original subject. Then, we achieve subject replacement based on
094   these two parts with the original video which retains complete contextual information through our
095   proposed intra-frame Spatial Guidance method. This achieves contextually harmonious replacement
096   while preserving the non-edited areas. To maintain consistency in motion during the transfer of the
097
      resized subject, we further developed inter-frame temporal guidance, ensuring consistency between
      frames in the edited video and alleviate issues such as flickering and local distortion Jeong et al.
098
      (2024a) .
099
100   Lastly, as we are the first to focus on the issue of contextually harmonious local video editing, we
101   have collected a dedicated video dataset that includes high-quality videos featuring various subject
102   categories, encompassing scenes with multiple subjects and contextual reference objects. Addition-
103
      ally, we propose evaluation metrics to further assess the accuracy of local editing and the degree of
      background preservation in the edits.
104
105   Main Contributions (1) We introduce the task of “contextually harmonious local video editing”
106   to address challenges in subject replacement and size adjustment within complex backgrounds or
107   multi-subject scenes, ensuring subjects retain their original motion while blending harmoniously
      into the scene. (2) We develop an innovative video editing pipeline to effectively extracts and main-

111   and harmony of local edits. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on these metrics,
112   demonstrating strong capabilities for contextually harmonious local video editing.

117   Image Editing Models For image editing methods, Prompt-to-prompt Hertz et al. (2022) and PNP
118
      Tumanyan et al. (2023) achieve the goal of editing images according to target prompts by manipu-
      lating attention features during the diffusion process. DragonDiffusion Mou et al. (2023) achieves
119
      drag-style image editing via gradient guidance produced by image feature correspondence Tang et al.
120
      (2023) in the pre-trained StableDiffusion Rombach et al. (2022) model and enables object replace-
121   ment. Nevertheless, since it relies on image correspondence features Tang et al. (2023), it cannot
122   handle significant size changes and maintaining the background depends on manually selected re-
123   gion mask information. Applying these image editing approaches to video frames directly will lead
124   to serious issues such as flickering and inconsistency among frames. Our method inflates the T2I
125   Diffusion Model Balaji et al. (2022) and constructs inter-frame Temporal Guidance, enabling to
126   achieve better temporal consistency.
127   Text Driven Video Editing TokenFlow Geyer et al. (2023) and RAVE Kara et al. (2024) observe
128   that features in diffusion models show similarities, offering fine-grained correspondences. FlowVid
129   Liang et al. (2024), Fatezero Qi et al. (2023) and ControlVideo Zhang et al. (2023) use conditions
130   such as depth maps and flow-warped videos to guide generation. However, their approach can only
131   handle editing on objects with small shape changes because they indiscriminately utilize features
132   extracted from the entire original video to guide the generation of the new video. Atlas Kasten et al.
133   (2021) and CoDeF Lee et al. (2013) convert the video into a canonical image, perform editing on it,
134   and then transform it back into a video. But When the video becomes complex, such as containing
135
      multiple moving subjects, this transformation becomes difficult to manage. Our method removes the
      motion of non-edited subjects, enabling it to handle videos with multi-subject scenes. By leveraging
136
      shape differences extracted from large language models, our method can effectively perform video
137
      editing with significant shape changes.
138
139   Existing methods are also focused on enabling precise multi-attribute editing. For instance, EVA
140   Yang et al. (2024) enhances detailed local edits by mitigating attention leakage, strengthening at-
141
      tention scores for tokens associated with the same attribute. Nonetheless, if the subject of the edit
      undergoes significant size changes, the attention features representing different regions between
142
      frames cannot be calculated, thus making it impossible to handle size change issues. DynVideo-
143
      E Liu et al. (2024) is a character-centered video editing method that proposes the image-based
144   video-NeRF editing pipeline. This approach primarily focuses on integrating the character into the
145   video background but struggles to handle significant changes in the shape or size of the character.
146   DreamMotin Jeong et al. (2024a) and Diffusion-Motion-Transfer Yatim et al. (2024) are capable of
147   preserving the motion of the edited subject during the editing process. DMT extracts motion guid-
148   ance by compressing the global features from the diffusion model. While this approach captures the
149   motion patterns of the edited object, the loss of global features after compression makes it difficult
150   to preserve the background in the edited video. Our method constructs guidance using intra-frame
151   spatial consistency and inter-frame temporal coherence, enabling harmonious integration of subjects
152
      while preserving the overall video background.

210   3.2   M OTION AND W ORLD K NOWLEDGE ACQUISITION
211
212
      In order to acquire sufficient prior knowledge for achieving harmonious video editing, we propose
      leveraging a multi-modal large model and a large language model to capture motion and world
213
      knowledge as shown in the upper-left part of Figure 2 under different shape .
214
215   Initially, to disentangle the influence of different moving subjects, we utilize the ability of the vision-
      language models Cheng et al. (2024) to comprehend videos. By providing prompts “Look at this

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      video and tell me which objects are moving, respond only with object names” to the VLM, we
217   can identify the moving subjects, which serves as preliminary support for isolating the independent
218   motion of the editing subject. Then, considering that prior work has never addressed how size and
219   shape difference affect motion preservation, we instruct the large language model Achiam et al.
220   (2023) to apply its common sense in determining the similarity of the subject’s shape before and
221   after editing, along with calculating the ratio of the size change. Specifically, we design two prompts
222   to enable the model to output Sshape and Ssize , which are defined as follows: Sshape is obtained by
223   asking, ‘Not considering the size, from 0 to 1 please rate the structural similarity between source
224   subject and target subject, only output a rate.’ Ssize is derived from the prompt, ‘Estimate the size
225
      ratio of an average source subject compared to an average target subject. Provide only a rate within
      the range of 0.3 to 3.’ Through this approach, we determine the relative shape and size variations
226
      of subjects before and after replacement, providing a reference for selecting editing methods and
227
      ensuring the size of the replaced subjects appears harmonious within the video.

      Based on the observations mentioned above, we adopted Shape Adapted Subject-Centric Editing:
271   We first utilize a segmentation model Ravi et al. (2024) to extract masks of moving objects within
272   the video based on the previously obtained contextual motion information. Then, the original video
273   and the masks of non-editable subjects are served as inputs for a video erasing method Zhang et al.
274   (2022) to remove other moving subjects from the video, preventing them from affecting the motion
275   extraction of the target subject. To further preserve the target’s motion relative to the context in the
276   original video and to better focus on the appearance replacement of the editing subject, we calculate
277   the union space of the area occupied by the original target across frames in the video and cropped
278   this portion for subsequent editing processes based on the mask of the original target. In order
279
      to select the more advantageous model under different circumstances, we dynamically adjust the
      model selection based on shape knowledge Sshape acquired from the previous stage. When there
280
      is a drastic shape difference between the replacement subject and the original subject, we employ
281
      the Motion guidance method Yatim et al. (2024). Conversely, when the shape difference is subtle,
282   we use the Global guidance method Jeong et al. (2024a). This design enables the model to achieve
283   better motion preservation of the original subject during the replacement process, regardless of the
284   degree of shape transformation.

      3.4   C OARSE - TO -F INE H ARMONIOUS S UBJECT T RANSFER
287
288   The Coarse-to-Fine Harmonious Subject Transfer stage transfers the original subject to the edited
289   subject while preserving the video’s background, ensuring the edited subject remains contextually
290   harmonious in size and motion. This stage is composed of two processes: Coarse Video Editing and
291   Fine-grained Subject Transfer.
292
293   Coarse Video Editing. To ensure that the edited subject replaces the original subject in the video,
294   maintaining harmonious size and motion, we proposed a Coarse Video Editing process. Using a
295   strategy similar to the previous step, we first erase the original subject in the video using a video
296   erasure model Zhang et al. (2022). Then, we resize the edited target subject based on Ssize and
297
      paste it into the position of the erased subject in the original video to create a coarse edited video
      Vcoarse . The position for pasting is determined by aligning the bottom centers of the bounding boxes
298
      derived from the masks of both the original and edited subjects. Such editing process captures key
299
      information about the target subject’s appearance and motion, as well as background details in areas
300   where the shapes of the original and target subjects do not align, which is especially important when
301   the edited subject is smaller than the original.
302
303   Fine-grained Subject Transfer: Intra-frame Spatial Guidance. During the coarse editing
304   phase, thanks to our previous subject-centric editing design, a high-quality, motion-preserving, har-

307   cess affects not only the corresponding area of the erased subject mask but also its surroundings.

404   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
405
406   Dateset To build a multi-subject video dataset, we collected 40 videos from TGVE Wu et al.
407   (2023b), DAVIS Caelles et al. (2019), YouTube-VOS Xu et al. (2018), Animal Kingdom Ng et al.
408   (2022) and the Internet. Almost all of the raw videos have a resolution exceeding 512x512, with the
409   majority surpassing 1920x1080. These raw videos vary in duration and are characterized by clarity,
410
      stationary camera motion, and the presence of other moving subjects or reference elements(such as
      furniture, trees) besides the main edited subject. To showcase the diversity of subjects in natural
411
      scenes, our edited video subjects include various categories, such as humans, animals, and vehicles.
412
      For each video, we select segments without subject occlusions to extract frames, resulting in video
413   versions with 24 frames and 8 frames, allowing them to accommodate methods which need different
414   editing lengths. Subsequently, we use the captions extracted by the VideoLLaMA2 Cheng et al.
415   (2024) as the original text prompts, and then employ GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) to generate editing
416   prompts aimed at replacing the original subject with a new subject, followed by manual adjustments.
417   It is worth noting that, considering our task settings, we will generate editing prompts where both the
418   shape and size of the original subject undergo some changes. Through the aforementioned process,
419   we obtained a multi-subject video dataset consisting of 60 triplets, each containing two different-
420   length videos (24 frames and 8 frames), the original prompt, and the editing prompt, which provides
421
      a sufficient quantity for the field of video editing.

      4.3     Q UANTITATIVE C OMPARISON FOR M ULTI - OBJECT L OCAL E DITING
451
452   We conducted a quantitative evaluation on our proposed dataset. In addition to following previous
453   works by using CLIP-T and CLIP-F Radford et al. (2021) to assess video-text consistency and inter-
454   frame consistency, we introduced the Dino Liu et al. (2023) to more determine whether the subject
455   replacement was successfully edited, as previous works Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) have shown that
456   CLIP struggles to understand combination information in multi-subject video captions. (We en-
457   sured that the replaced subject does not exist in the original video.) To evaluate the preservation of
458
      non-edited areas, we combined a segmentation model with LPIPS distance Zhang et al. (2018) (see
      Table 1, Masked LPIPS (background)). We segmented the replaced subject in all generated videos
459
      based on its text description; if the segmentation model failed, we used the original subject’s mask
460
      instead. We calculated the union of the masks for each edited subject and used it to mask areas
461   when computing the LPIPS score between edited and original videos, ensuring a fair comparison of
462   non-edited area preservation. Similarly, we assessed the similarity between the edited and original
463   videos in the original subject’s area (see Table 1, Masked LPIPS (target)) by calculating the LPIPS
464   score within the corresponding mask. Given that other models often fail to edit effectively under
465   our setup (see Figure 4), this metric indicates whether the original subject has been altered, with a
466   lower score signifying unsuccessful replacement. As shown, our method achieved the best results
467   in Dino, CLIP-T, and Masked LPIPS (target), and also obtained the best scores in Masked LPIPS
468   (background), indicating that our method successfully performs editing while preserving the back-
469
      ground consistency of the original video as much as possible. Although our method scores slightly
      lower in CLIP-F, it may be because other methods, which have a lower success rate of editing on
470
      our dataset, generate videos that are more similar to the original, thus preserving the original video’s
471
      motion Cong et al. (2023).
472
473   User study. 25 Participants are involved and asked to watch the input video and then the anonymized
474   output videos from each baseline. They were then asked to rate four questions on a scale of 1 to 5: (i)
475
      Edit Accuracy (Edit-Acc): Does the output video accurately reflect the target text by appropriately
      editing all relevant elements? (ii) Frame Consistency (Frame-Con): Are the frames in the output
476
      video temporally consistent? (iii) Background and Motion Preservation (BM-Preseve): Have the
477
      motion and non-edited parts of the input video been accurately preserved? (iv) The harmony of size
478   change (Harmony-Score): Is the size change reasonable in the output video? Table 1 shows that
479   our method demonstrates excellent performance across all evaluation metrics, performing well in
480   inter-frame consistency and successfully maintaining a reasonable size for the edited subject in the
481   original video.

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      Our proposed video editing pipeline addresses the task of contextually harmonious local video edit-
534   ing. This approach tackles the challenges of subject replacement and size adjustment in complex
535   backgrounds and multi-subject scenes, ensuring that edited subjects retain their original motion
536   characteristics and fit harmoniously into the video context at an appropriate size and shape. To
537   validate our approach, we created a specialized video dataset focused on contextually harmonious
538   local video editing. This dataset comprises high-quality videos featuring a wide range of subject
539   categories, including scenes with multiple subjects and contextual reference objects. Experimental
      results demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance in such setting.
