009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The stationary distribution of a stochastic system often provides fundamental in-
012            sights into its dynamics, yet the stationary distribution of the stochastic gradient
013            descent (SGD) algorithm, a cornerstone of machine learning, remains analytically
014            elusive. In this work, we first show that the minibatch noise of SGD regularizes
015            the solution towards a noise-balanced, low-dimensional subspace when the loss
016            function exhibits rescaling symmetry. This result allows us to construct a type of
017            linear network that captures the depth and width and for which a general stationary
018            distribution of the stochastic gradient flow can be derived. This stationary distri-
019
               bution reveals complex nonlinear phenomena, including phase transitions, loss of
               ergodicity, memory effects, sign coherence, and fluctuation inversion. These phe-
020
               nomena are shown to exist uniquely in deep networks, highlighting a fundamental
021
               distinction between deep and shallow models. Finally, we discuss the implications
022            of our proposed theory for practical problems in variational Bayesian inference.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In both natural and social sciences, the stationary distribution of a stochastic system often holds the
028
      key to understanding the underlying dynamics of complex processes (Van Kampen, 1992; Rolski
      et al., 2009). For the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm, a foundational tool in mod-
029
      ern machine learning, understanding its stationary distribution has the potential to provide deep
030
      insights into its learning behavior. However, despite extensive use, the stationary distribution of
031   SGD remains analytically elusive. The stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm is defined as
032   ∆θt = − Sη ∑x∈B ∇θ ℓ(θ, x) where θ is the model parameter and ℓ(θ, x) is a per-sample loss whose
033   expectation over x gives the training loss: L(θ) = Ex [ℓ(θ, x)]. B is a randomly sampled minibatch
034   of data points, each independently sampled from the training set, and S is the minibatch size. In
035   this work, we adopt the SDE approximation of SGD (Latz, 2021; Li et al., 2019; 2021; Sirignano &
036   Spiliopoulos, 2020; Fontaine et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2017):

045   Our main contributions are
046   1. the derivation of the “law of balance,” which shows that SGD converges to a low-dimensional

048   2. identification of a minimal linear model with the concepts of width and depth, for which a
049      general form of the stationary distribution of SGD is found (Section 4);
050   3. discovery of novel phenomena in this model such as phase transitions, sign coherence (impos-
051      sibility to learn the wrong sign), loss of ergodicity, memory effects, and fluctuation inversion
052      (Section 4).
053   The next section discusses the closely related works, especially on known examples of stationary
      distributions of SGD. All proofs and derivations are given in Appendix A.

056   Stationary distribution of SGD. The FP equation is a high-dimensional partial differential equation
057   whose solution (and its existence) is an open problem in mathematics and many fields of sciences
058   and only known for a few celebrated special cases (Risken & Risken, 1996). One of the earliest
059   works that computes the stationary distribution of SGD is the Lemma 20 of Chaudhari & Soatto
060   (2018), which assumes that the noise has a constant covariance and shows that if the loss function is
061   quadratic, then the stationary distribution is Gaussian. Similarly, using a Laplace approximation ex-
062   pansion and assuming that the noise is parameter-independent, a series of recent works showed that
063
      the stationary distribution of SGD is exponential in the model parameters close to a local minimum:
      p(θ) ∝ exp[−aθT Hθ], for some constant a and matrix H (Mandt et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2020;
064
      Liu et al., 2021). Assuming that the noise covariance only depends on the loss function value L(θ),
065
      Mori et al. (2022) and Wojtowytsch (2024) showed that the stationary distribution is power-law-like
066   and proportional to L(θ)−c0 for some constant c0 . A primary feature of these previous results is that
067   stationary distribution does not exhibit any memory effect and also preserves ergodicity. Until now,
068   the general form of the analytical solution to the stationary distribution of SGD is unknown.
069

      3     N OISE BALANCE UNDER THE R ESCALING S YMMETRY
096   We show that when the loss function exhibits the rescaling symmetry, SGD will evolve towards
097   a solution for which the gradient noise is balanced. In Section 4, we will leverage this result to
098   construct a model for which the stationary distribution of SGD only has support on a very low-
099   dimensional subspace.
100
101   3.1   R ESCALING S YMMETRY AND L AW OF BALANCE
102   A type of invariance – the rescaling symmetry – often appears in the loss function and it is preserved
103   for all sampling of minibatches. The per-sample loss ℓ is said to have the rescaling symmetry for
104   all x if ℓ(u, w, x) = ℓ (λu, w/λ, x) for a scalar λ ∈ R+ . This type of symmetry appears in many
105   scenarios in deep learning. For example, it appears in any neural network with the ReLU activation.
106   It also appears in the self-attention of transformers, often in the form of key and query matrices
107   (Vaswani et al., 2017). When this symmetry exists between u and w, one can prove the following
      result, which we refer to as the law of balance.

      4.5   VARIATIONAL BAYESIAN L EARNING
487
488   An implications of the analytical solution we found is the inappropriateness of using SGD to ap-
489   proximate a Bayesian posterior. Because every SGD iteration can be regarded as a sampling of the
490   model parameters. A series of recent works have argued that the stationary distribution can be used
491
      as an approximation of the Bayesian posterior for fast variational inference (Mandt et al., 2017;
      Chaudhari & Soatto, 2018), pBayes (θ) ≈ pSGD (θ), a method that has been used for a wide variety of
492
      applications (Jospin et al., 2022). However, our result implies that such an approximation is likely
493
      to fail. Common in Bayesian deep learning, we interpret the per-sample loss as the log probability
494   and the weight decay as a Gaussian prior over the parameters, the true model parameters have a log
495   probability of
496                                    log pBayes (θ∣x) ∝ ℓ(θ, x) + γ∥θ∥2 .                           (19)
497
      This distribution has a nonzero measure everywhere for any differentiable loss. However, the distri-
498
      bution for SGD in Eq.(16) has a zero probability density almost everywhere because a 1d subspace
499
      has a zero probability measure in a high-dimensional space. This implies that the KL divergence
500   between the two distributions (either KL(pBayes ∣∣pSGD ) or KL(pSGD ∣∣pBayes )) is infinite.

502   5     C ONCLUSION
503
504   In this work, we established that SGD systematically converges toward a balanced solution when
505   rescaling symmetry is present, a principle we termed the “law of balance.” This finding implies that
506   SGD inherently focuses on a low-dimensional subspace in the stationary stage of training, offering
507
      new insights into its behavior in deep learning. By leveraging the law of balance, we constructed an
      analytically solvable model incorporating the concepts of depth and width and successfully derived
508
      the stationary distribution of SGD. This analytical solution revealed several previously unknown
509
      phenomena, which may have significant implications for understanding deep learning dynamics.
510   One key consequence of our theory is that using SGD to approximate the Bayesian posterior may
511   be fundamentally inappropriate when symmetries exist in the model, a concern particularly relevant
512   for overparameterized models (Nguyen, 2019). For those seeking to employ SGD for variational
513   inference, it may be necessary to eliminate symmetries from the loss function, which presents an
514   intriguing avenue for future research. While our theory provides valuable insights, it is currently
515   limited to a minimal model, and exploring more complex and realistic models will be an essential
516   direction for future studies.
