011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated Learning (FL) enables collaborative training of machine learning (ML)
014            models on decentralized data while preserving data privacy. However, data across
015            clients often differs significantly due to class imbalance, feature distribution skew,
016
               sample size imbalance, and other phenomena. Using information from these not
               identically distributed (non-IID) datasets causes challenges in training. Existing
017
               FL methods based on a single global model cannot effectively capture client data
018
               variations, resulting in suboptimal performance. Personalized FL (PFL) techniques
019            were introduced to adapt to the local data distribution of each client and utilize the
020            data from other clients. They have shown promising results in addressing these
021            challenges. We propose FedMAP, a novel Bayesian PFL framework which applies
022            Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation to effectively mitigate various non-IID
023            data issues, by means of a parametric prior distribution, which is updated during ag-
024            gregation. We provide a theoretical foundation illustrating FedMAP’s convergence
025            properties. In particular, we prove that the prior updates in FedMAP correspond to
026            gradient descent iterations for a linear combination of envelope functions associ-
027
               ated with the local losses. This differs from previous FL approaches, that aim at
               minimizing a weighted average of local loss functions and often face challenges
028
               with heterogeneous data distributions, resulting in reduced client performance and
029
               slower convergence in non-IID settings. Finally, we show, through evaluations
030            of synthetic and real-world datasets, that FedMAP achieves better performance
031            than the existing methods. Moreover, we offer a robust, ready-to-use framework to
032            facilitate practical deployment and further research.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   By leveraging distributed data sources, ML models can be trained more effectively and produce
038   robust, generalized insights. Nevertheless, stringent privacy restrictions, security threats, and high
039   data transfer costs have made centralized methods impossible, particularly in sensitive sectors such as
040   healthcare (Zhang et al., 2024). FL was introduced as a practical paradigm that enables collaborative
041   training across clients without exchanging raw data.
042   In practice, data across organizations are often non-IID, which is one of the main challenges to
043   successfully adopting FL. Non-IID data refers to data that is not identically distributed across clients,
044   resulting in challenges, including slower model convergence, reduced model accuracy, and increased
045   communication costs in FL. Li et al. (2022) identified three main categories of non-IID data: label
046   distribution skew, feature distribution skew, and quantity skew. In real-world settings, clients often
047   experience combinations of different types of non-IID data. For instance, a rural clinic might have
048   fewer patient records (quantity skew) and collect data using different medical equipment (feature
049   distribution skew), and serve a population with different disease prevalence rates (label distribution
050
      skew) compared to urban hospitals. Addressing the combination of these non-IID factors is necessary
      for the practical and effective deployment of FL systems.
051
052   Classic FL algorithms such as FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016), rely on Maximum Likelihood
053   Estimation (MLE) principles and assume that local client updates can be aggregated to obtain a single
      global model that maximizes the likelihood of all clients’ data collectively. This assumption fails

054
      with non-IID data, which the global objective function is unable to accurately represent individual
055   client’s data distribution (Li et al., 2021b). This discrepancy creates a fundamental optimization
056   challenge in which local gradients from different clients can point in different directions extensively
057   and result in slow convergence or poor local optima. Although the variants of FedAvg have made
058   progress in handling data heterogeneity, they usually struggle to capture the complexities of non-IID
059   data. PFL was introduced to learn personalized models customized to each client’s unique data
060   distribution and leverage the collective knowledge across clients (Kulkarni et al., 2020). However,
061   existing PFL methods face several issues, which include inefficient knowledge transfer between
062   clients, high communication costs, and limited personalization capabilities (Lin et al., 2022). These
063
      limitations are all due to the underlying optimization approaches that fail to capture the complexities
      of non-IID data distributions.
064
065   We introduce FedMAP, a new PFL framework that fundamentally addresses the FL challenges of
066   non-IID data by utilizing a global prior distribution derived from MAP estimation. We formulate the
067   FL problem as a bi-level optimization that adaptively learns and updates a global prior distribution
068   that guides local model optimization. This approach can effectively balance knowledge sharing
069
      and local data distribution adaptation. FedMAP tackles the core optimization issues of client drift
      and the inconsistency between global and local objectives in non-IID settings. Our contributions
070
      include a mathematical framework for the PFL using MAP estimation, the FedMAP algorithm
071
      with an adaptive weighting scheme for robust global prior updates which specifically mitigate the
072   practical scenarios where clients experience a combination of non-IID data distributions, empirical
073   evaluation on both synthetic and public datasets that incorporate various non-IID data distributions,
074   and the integration of FedMAP with the open-source Flower FL framework (Beutel et al., 2020). The
075   evaluation results show FedMAP consistently outperforms individual client training and the existing
076   FL methods. Furthermore, the theoretical analysis indicates that FedMAP converges to the solution
077   of a bi-level optimization problem, providing a foundation for its effectiveness in handling non-IID
078   data distributions. With its improved reproducibility, FedMAP is available for community use and
079   further development. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FedMAP-963/.

      1.1   R ELATED LITERATURE
082
083
      Approaches for addressing non-IID issues through standard FL One of the key challenges in
084
      classic FL is the statistical heterogeneity in clients’ data. This often leads to large variations in model
085   performance (Vahidian et al., 2024). Several solutions have been proposed to mitigate the impact of
086   this heterogeneity. FedProx (Li et al., 2020) adds a proximal term to the local optimization process,
087   limiting how far local updates can deviate from the global model. SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al.,
088   2020) utilizes control variates to correct client drift in local updates. Despite these improvements,
089   relying on a single global model schema often fails to generalize across the diverse data distributions
090   of different clients, leading to suboptimal performance in many cases (Kulkarni et al., 2020).
091   Personalized federated learning Recent research highlights the PFL can eliminate the impact of
092   data heterogeneity by customizing the global model to individual clients’ data distributions(Kulkarni
093   et al., 2020). The personalization can be achieved in several ways. Fine-Tuning (Marfoq et al., 2022;
094   Lee et al., 2023) allows clients to adjust a global model locally using their own data. Layer-wise
095   Personalization involves personalizing specific layers of the network such as the batch normalization
096   layers while sharing other layers (Li et al., 2021b). FedASA (Deng et al., 2024) was proposed to use
097   an adaptive cell-wise architecture selection strategy to determine which layers to share based on client
098   heterogeneity. PerAda (Xie et al., 2024) introduces a parameter-efficient approach using adapters
099
      for personalization while maintaining generalization through knowledge distillation. Multi-Task
      Learning treats each client as a unique task, optimizing across related tasks to improve overall model
100
      performance (Smith et al., 2017). pFedEM (Chen et al., 2024) extends this by modelling each client’s
101
      data distribution as a time-varying mixture of multiple base distributions. Meta-Learning strategies
102   such as Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) (Fallah et al., 2020) construct a model to adapt to
103   new client data with minimal retraining. Cluster-Based Personalization (Ren et al., 2023; Porcu et al.,
104   2022) groups clients by data similarity, each cluster developing a shared model based on its common
105   characteristics. These strategies collectively balance robust global model learning with effective local
106   adaptation, thus optimizing FL across diverse environments. Regularization-based Personalization
107   adds regularization terms to the learning objective to control the deviation between the local models
      and a global model (Li et al., 2021a).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Bayesian approach in FL Adopting Bayesian methods in FL offers several benefits. Particularly
109   these methods can effectively handle non-IID data by quantifying uncertainty, enhancing robustness
110   through evidence-based likelihood estimates, and improving performance on limited data using prior
111   distributions for each model parameter (Cao et al., 2023). The method pFedBayes (Zhang et al.,
112   2022) introduces weight uncertainty in neural networks by balancing the loss on private data with the
113   divergence from a global variational distribution using variational Bayesian inference. β-Predictive
114   Bayes (Hasan et al., 2024) improves calibration and uncertainty estimation by approximating the
115   global predictive posterior through interpolating a mixture and a product of local predictive posteriors
116   using a tunable parameter β. FedPop (Kotelevskii et al., 2022) conceptualizes FL as population
117
      modelling, using Markov Chain Monte Carlo methods for federated stochastic optimization and
      accounting for data heterogeneity with common population parameters and random effects. Fur-
118
      thermore, the Bayesian nonparametric framework proposed in (Yurochkin et al., 2019) models local
119
      neural network weights and applies a Beta-Bernoulli process-based inference technique to synthesize
120   a global network from local models without additional data pooling.
121
122   Building upon the challenges and solutions discussed above, FedMAP combines Bayesian principles
123
      with regularization-based PFL. FedMAP shares similarities with Ditto (Li et al., 2021a) on the
      methods to balance local specialization with global knowledge sharing. However, Ditto solves a
124
      bi-level optimization problem with separate objectives for global and local models. FedMAP uses
125
      MAP estimation for local optimization, directly integrating global knowledge into the local objective.
126   pFedMe (T Dinh et al., 2020) is another similar approach to FedMAP, which uses Moreau envelopes as
127   regularizers in bi-level optimization. With Gaussian prior, FedMAP reduces to pFedMe’s formulation,
128   however FedMAP is more generic with its flexible choice of prior distributions. Also, FedMAP
129   differs from pFedBayes which uses variational inference. FedMAP uses MAP estimation since it is
130   more computationally efficient when incorporating prior knowledge. Similar to FedPop’s population
131   modelling concept, FedMAP allows each client to have a personalized model and benefit from the
132   collective knowledge encoded in the global prior. Nevertheless, FedMAP uses a more straightforward
133   probabilistic framework with an adaptive weighting mechanism in the global aggregation step, which
134
      considers the confidence and relevance of each client’s model instead of sample size. Most of the
      mentioned approaches only address isolated non-IID issues, whereas FedMAP is designed to address
135
      the combination of non-IID data distributions which are common in practical scenarios.

      4.1     DATASETS
356
      To evaluate the performance of FedMAP under non-IID data distributions, we utilized both synthetic
357   and public datasets.
358
359   Synthetic Datasets: A number of synthetic datasets were created to evaluate the FedMAP’s ability
360
      to handle practical FL challenges. The evaluation scenarios are designed to reflect the complex
      combinations of non-IID issues that occur in practical applications. The datasets include three
361
      primary types of non-IID data distributions across clients: 1) Feature distribution skew, where each
362
      client’s data is influenced by unique affine transformations. These transformations vary the feature
363   space across clients; 2) Quantity Skew with each client has datasets in varying sample sizes; 3) Label
364   Distribution Skew by different class proportions across the clients. The details of the synthetic data
365   are provided in Appendix B.
366
      Office-31 Dataset: To complement the experiments on synthetic datasets, we also used the Office-31
367
      dataset (Saenko et al., 2010). This dataset consists of 4110 images across 31 object categories.
368
      The data are collected from three distinct domains: Amazon (images from Amazon.com), Webcam
369   (low-resolution images captured by a webcam), and DSLR (high-resolution images captured by
370   digital SLR cameras). The diversity in image domains, resolution, and acquisition conditions in the
371   Office-31 dataset naturally introduces realistic non-IID data distributions.
372
373   4.2     S ETUP
374
375   In our experiments, we chose a Gaussian prior for the local model parameters θ, defined by the
376   probability density function (6), where γ represents the global model parameters and σ 2 is the variance
377   controlling the influence of the prior. When σ → 0, the prior is centered at γ and approximates
      FedAvg, prioritizing the global consensus. When σ → ∞, the prior becomes a uniform distribution


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      over Rd , removing any influence from the global model and allowing purely local learning, which
379   maximizes client-specific adaptation. We selected this prior because the negative logarithm of the
380   prior adds a convex quadratic term ∥θ − γ∥2 /(2σ 2 ) to the local objective function, which makes the
381   optimization efficient with gradient-based methods.
382
      We evaluated FedMAP across three non-IID scenarios, and each scenario involved 10 clients for
383
      FL training and validation. In the first scenario, each client holds 2000 samples. In the second
384
      scenario, five clients hold 2000 samples each, and the other five hold only 500 samples each.
385   In the third scenario, all clients have 2000 samples, but five clients have 85% of their samples
386   belonging to class 0, while the class proportions were balanced for the rest of the clients. In all
387   scenarios, each client employed a 70:30 train-validation split. The model architecture employed
388   was a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with two hidden layers. Models were optimized using the
389   Adam optimizer. Detailed model architectures are provided in the Appendix B, and all other
390   training details, including hyperparameters and settings for FL, can be found in the code repository:
391   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FedMAP-963/.
392   To simulate non-IID data distributions in the Office-31 dataset, we partitioned the data across three
393   clients, each including a distinct domain: Amazon, Webcam, and DSLR. We utilized the Convolu-
394   tional Neural Network (CNN) model architecture from the FedBN (Li et al., 2021b) experiments,
395   which consists of five convolutional layers, each with batch normalization and ReLU activation,
396   ending with an output layer for classification. Models were optimized using the SGD optimizer, and
397   each client also used a 70:30 train-validation split.
398   FedMAP was evaluated against three established FL baselines and individual client training. FedAvg
399   (McMahan et al., 2016) serves as the benchmark of FL. FedProx (Li et al., 2020) was selected due to
400   its similarity to FedMAP in addressing non-IID by using the regularization approach. FedBN (Li
401   et al., 2021b) was selected as it is a PFL method that targets similar non-IID scenarios as the FedMAP
402   does. Individual client training, where models are trained and validated exclusively on each client’s
403   local data to assess base performance. All experiments were conducted on an AMD 5965WX 24-core
404   CPU with two NVIDIA RTX A5500 GPUs. To facilitate reproducibility and practical adoption, we
405
      integrated FedMAP into one of the popular open-source frameworks, Flower (Beutel et al., 2020),
      enabling simulations of real distributed deployments.

511   5   C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this paper, we proposed FedMAP, a novel FL framework incorporating a global prior distribution
514   over local model parameters and enabling personalized FL. We formulated a mathematical framework
515   for the problem with bi-level optimization, capturing the data heterogeneity across clients. Exten-
516   sive evaluations across scenarios, including skewed feature, label and quantity distributions, have
517   demonstrated FedMAP’s performance gains over the existing methods such as FedAvg, FedProx, and
518
      FedBN. Additionally, the theoretical analysis positions FedMAP as a promising approach for robust,
      personalized federated learning in heterogeneous data environments. From the practical perspective,
519
      the Flower framework integration further improves reproducibility and practical deployment using
520
      Docker containers. It allows researchers and practitioners to incorporate FedMAP into their existing
521   FL pipelines easily.
522
523   FedMAP can benefit healthcare particularly, as it encourages multiple hospitals to train ML models
524
      collaboratively despite their siloed data. Hospitals face non-IID issues as the variations in disease
      prevalence, demographic differences, and different dataset sizes (Zhang et al., 2024). FedMAP allows
525
      the training of robust and personalized diagnostic models tailored to each healthcare provider’s unique
526
      data distribution, unlocking the potential of collaborative learning while preserving data privacy and
527   integrity.
528
529   Although the results are promising, the limitations include assuming the global prior is an isotropic
530
      Gaussian distribution and the mean acts as a unique parameter. Therefore, future work will involve
      looking into issues such as covariance matrix tuning and exploring alternatives to Gaussian distribu-
531
      tions for priors. We believe FedMAP offers an effective framework and streamlines collaboration in
532
      distributed data environments.

1118   B.1    D ESCRIPTION OF THE SYNTHETIC DATA
1119
1120   We designed a data synthesis method that generates synthetic data for a binary classification problem
1121
       in Rn , where n = 30 is the number of features. We assume that the intrinsic dimension of the data is
       d = 4, so we start by randomly selecting a d-dimensional linear subspace of Rn . This is done by
1122
       randomly generating d orthonormal vectors of Rn , that we stack as a matrix B = (b1 , . . . , bd ) ∈
1123
       Rn×d . For every data point (x, y) ∈ Rn × {0, 1}, the label y is correlated only with the projection
1124   of x onto the space spanned by B, and the orthogonal component to B is assumed to be a nuisance
1125   variable.
1126
1127
       The data generation for the two classes is as follows:
1128
             1. For the label y = 0, the projection of x onto the d-dimensional subspace B follows an
1129            isotropic Normal distribution centered at the origin, i.e. Bx ∼ N (0, σ02 Id ), with σ02 = 2.
1130
1131         2. For the label y = 1, the projection of x onto the d-dimensional subspace B is distributed
                                                                                 Bx

                around the (d − 1)-dimensional sphere of radius 8 as follows: ∥Bx∥      ∼ U(Sd−1 ), where
                    d−1

                norm of Bx is normally distributed around 8, i.e. ∥Bx∥ ∼ N (8, σ12 Id ), with σ12 = 2.

1164   In this way, the binary classification task consists of separating the feature vectors x such that Bx
1165   is close to 0 from those such that Bx is close to the (d − 1)- dimensional sphere of radius 8, where
1166
       B = (b1 , . . . , bd ) is an unknown n × d matrix with orthonormal columns.
1167   This data generation process is repeated independently for each of the q = 10 clients, with the number
1168   of samples per machine determined by a split ratio. Finally, in order to simulate Feature Distribution
1169   Skew across the clients, for each client, the vector of features x is further transformed by an affine
1170   transformation Ax + b, where A ∈ Rn×n is a random matrix and b ∈ Rn is a random vector that
1171   scales with the client index. The matrix A and the vector b are different for each client.
1172   The method introduces three types of data skew to simulate realistic FL non-IID scenarios. Feature
1173   Distribution Skew is achieved by applying client-specific random affine transformations x 7→ Ax+b
1174   to the local data points, with A ∈ Rn×n and b ∈ Rn varying between clients. This results in the
1175   data distribution shifts across clients. Label Distribution Skew and Quantity Skew are simulated
1176   by varying the proportion of class 0 samples and the number of samples assigned to each client,
1177   respectively, on top of Feature Distribution Skew. This results in clients having different class
1178
       proportions, feature distributions, and numbers of samples.
1179   The method allows fine-grained control over the degree and nature of the induced data skew, enabling
1180   the generation of realistic synthetic federated datasets to study the impact of different non-IID data
1181   scenarios on the performance of FL algorithms.

1183   non-IID scenarios and compare them to IID scenarios, we employed t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic
1184   Neighbor Embedding) (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) to reduce the high-dimensional data to 2D
1185   representations. The t-SNE visualizations, as shown in Figures 4 and 5, illustrate the different data
1186   heterogeneity scenarios in the datasets used in the evaluation. The IID scenario (Figure 4a) shows
1187   the idealistic data distribution for FL as it has no distribution skewness, with data points uniformly
