011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We present a multi-region brain model exploring the role of structured memory cir-
014
               cuits in spatially embedded decision-making tasks. We simulate decision-making
               processes that involve the cognitive maps formed within the CA1 region of the
015
               hippocampus during an evidence integration task, which animals learn through
016
               reinforcement learning (RL). Our model integrates a bipartite memory scaffold
017            architecture that incorporates grid and place cells of the entorhinal cortex and hip-
018            pocampus, with an action-selecting recurrent neural network (RNN) that integrates
019            hippocampal representations. Through RL-based simulations, we demonstrate that
020            joint encoding of position and evidence within medial entorhinal cortex, along with
021            sensory projection to hippocampus, replicates experimentally observed place cell
022            representations and promotes rapid learning and efficient spatial navigation relative
023            to alternative circuits. Our findings predict conjunctive spatial and evidence tuning
024            in grid cells, in addition to hippocampus, as essential for decision-making in space.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The hippocampus (HPC) plays a critical role in spatial, contextual, and associative learning and
029   memory (O’Keefe, 1978; Dostrovsky & O’Keefe, 1971; Squire, 1992; Scoville & Milner, 1957).
030   Cortical and subcortical regions are critical for tasks involving evidence accumulation and decision
031   making (Pinto et al., 2019; IBL et al., 2023). Recent experiments examined the interplay of neural
032
      representations in these regions during the accumulating tower task that combined both spatial
      navigation and decision-making (Pinto et al., 2019; 2022; Nieh et al., 2021).
033
034   The task is as follows: As mice run through a virtual corridor, they are stochastically presented with
035   towers on both sides (Nieh et al., 2021). At the end of the corridor, they must make a left (right) turn
036   if there were more towers on the corresponding side. The task involves integrating evidence: deciding
037   whether to turn right or left can be solved by taking the difference of the integrated number of towers
038
      on each side (“accumulated evidence”). Though the spatial locations of towers along each side are
      irrelevant to the task, neurons in area CA1 of the dorsal hippocampus signaled spatial location during
039
      the task with their place fields. Moreover, these fields formed combined cognitive maps of space and
040
      accumulated evidence, with individual place fields tuned to a specific combination of accumulated
041   evidence and location.
042
043   This observation raises the question of why the hippocampus (HPC) is involved in evidence represen-
044
      tation in the task: Does it play an important functional role in spatially embedded decision-making
      tasks, even when it does not seem that spatial information is necessary? And why are spatial and
045
      evidence represented together?
046
047   At the same time, understanding how different brain regions interact to solve spatially embedded
048   decision-making tasks can shed light on how the brain distributes and coordinates computations
049   across numerous substructures to allow it to flexibly and efficiently solve various complex tasks. Thus,
050
      the questions above also provide an opportunity to gain insights into the multi-region interactions
      that underpin complex cognitive functions. Such an understanding could reveal how cognitive maps
051
      could be leveraged not only to navigate physical spaces but also to guide cognitive decisions.
052
053   Decision-making tasks are often reinforcement learning problems (Gershman & Niv, 2015; Gershman
      & Daw, 2017), and various reinforcement learning (RL) models from formal Bayesian solutions


                                                         1
054
      to deep reinforcement learning, have elucidated the demands and representational requirements of
055   solving such tasks. The accumulating tower task has also been modeled with deep RL (Mochizuki-
056   Freeman et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). What is mostly lacking in all these approaches, however, is
057   the use of structured architectures and dynamics found in the brain and modeling the multi-system
058   computations distributed across brain regions to solve RL tasks robustly and learn them efficiently.
059
      Here, we developed a multi-region brain model that incorporates an architecturally and dynamically
060
      prestructured hippocampal-entorhinal circuit based on the memory model, Vector Hippocampal
061   Scaffolded Heteroassociative Memory (Vector-HaSH) (Sharma et al., 2022; Chandra et al., 2023).
062   The circuit interacts with cortical and sub-cortical regions that play the role of a decision-making
063   actor, abstracted as a recurrent neural network (RNN) (Elman, 1990). Thus, the model generalizes
064   Vector-HaSH into an RL problem solver with the addition of an actor/decision-making network. This
065   is related to achieving autonomous machine intelligence positioned and discussed by LeCun (2022);
066   we demonstrated, as a proof of concept, that sample-efficient learning in RL is achievable through an
067   external content-addressable associative memory with a structured aspect. In particular, our work
068   shows a structured conjunctive coding scheme (i.e., grid cells as a canonical example drawn from
069
      biological representation learning) is a necessary structural representation for forming cognitive maps
      (world models), enabling individuals to learn quickly and navigate spaces efficiently.
070
071   We apply the model to test the counterfactual of agent reinforcement learning behaviors on the
072   accumulating tower task, a spatially embedded decision-making task experimentally studied in
073   rodents, assuming different inputs into grid and place cells. In these different scenarios, our model
074   makes predictions about neural representations, the role of the hippocampal-entorhinal network in
075
      spatially embedded decision-making, and the efficacy of task learning.
076   The contribution of this paper is four-fold 1 :
077
078              • We propose and demonstrate a multi-region brain modeling framework that elucidates
079                the underlying neural mechanisms and computational roles of entorhinal-hippocampal-
080
                   neocortical interactions during spatially embedded decision-making tasks.
081              • The model permits a systematic study of how cognitive capabilities such as rapid learning
082                and efficient navigation might emerge, and can guide and help interpret future experiments.
083              • We predict conjunctive location-evidence tuning in grid cells. We also predict that grid cell
084                conjunctive tuning and entorhinal non-grid inputs to hippocampus are both necessary for
085                the emergence of conjunctive hippocampal cognitive maps (Nieh et al., 2021).
086              • Finally, we show that the two properties above (conjunctive grid cell tuning and non-grid
087                inputs to hippocampus) are essential for rapid learning and efficient navigation.

091   2.1       B IOLOGICAL F OUNDATIONS OF H IPPOCAMPUS AND E NTORHINAL C ORTEX
092
      Hippocampal place cells, whose discharges represent locations in space (Dostrovsky & O’Keefe,
093   1971), form the foundation of the cognitive map theory (O’Keefe, 1978; Moser & Moser, 2016;
094   Fenton, 2015). This theory provides an important framework for understanding how flexible and
095   intelligent behaviors emerge from neuronal populations (Fenton, 2024). The cognitive map enables
096   individuals to navigate spaces flexibly and organize memories, which are both investigated in this
097   paper through our multi-region model, as well as construct coherent personal narratives of their
098   experiences (O’Keefe, 1978; Tolman, 1948; Whittington et al., 2022; Fenton, 2024). Additionally,
099   the discovery that the hippocampus encodes internal cognitive variables (Bostock et al., 1991;
100   Ólafsdóttir et al., 2015; Nieh et al., 2021; Tavares et al., 2015) positions the place cell phenomenon
101   as a model system for studying internally generated cognition. Moreover, interactions between the
102
      hippocampus and entorhinal cortex are well-established to support both navigation (McNaughton
      et al., 1996; O’Keefe, 1978) and declarative memory (Squire, 1992; Scoville & Milner, 1957). Grid
103
      cells (Hafting et al., 2005), located in the medial entorhinal cortex, provide a spatial metric through
104
      firing fields that form a periodic hexagonal pattern across the environments (Krupic et al., 2012). To
105   reconcile this foundational understanding with experimental data, recent hypotheses propose that
106   mechanisms underlying memory and planning have evolved from physical navigation processes,
107

      suggesting that neuronal algorithms for navigating both physical and mental spaces are inherently the
109   same (Buzsáki & Moser, 2013). Combined with the established hippocampal-prefrontal functional
110   interactions (Preston & Eichenbaum, 2013; Eichenbaum, 2017), our multi-region model is at the
111   core of understanding the neural mechanisms underlying cognition. Recent advances in experimental
112   techniques allow for the simultaneous recording of thousands of neurons (Jun et al., 2017; Steinmetz
113   et al., 2021), enabling investigatations of how neurons across multiple brain regions coordinate to
114   function coherently (Bondy et al., 2024). Our theoretical work, informed by experiments, bridges the
115   gap with experimental progress, offering interpretable, mechanistic models to dissect the roles and
116   interactions of individual regions in cognitive processes, thereby guiding future experiments.

      2.2   C OMPUTATIONAL M ODELS OF H IPPOCAMPUS AND E NTORHINAL C ORTEX
118
119   The most prominent modeling approach to the entorhinal-hippocampal interaction includes the
120   Tolman-Eichenbaum machine (Whittington et al., 2020), a statistical generative model, and Vector-
121   HaSH (Chandra et al., 2023), a mechanistic model of neural circuits, grounded in neurobiological
122   principles of connectivity, structure, and learning rules. Thus, Vector-HaSH provides an strong
123
      foundation for our work, facilitating a close, reciprocal relationship between theoretical predictions
      and experimental results. More generally, several data-driven approaches have been developed
124
      to understand multi-region interactions. Perich et al. (2020) provided an approach for inferring
125
      brain-wide interactions using data-constrained RNNs that directly reproduce experimentally obtained
126   neural data. Koukuntla et al. (2024) developed a novel unsupervised machine learning method to
127   disentangle low-dimensional shared and private latent variables across brain regions. Our mechanistic
128   modeling approach brings a new perspective by integrating and unifying these statistical discoveries.
129
130   2.3   B IDIRECTIONAL I NSIGHTS B ETWEEN D EEP L EARNING AND N EUROSCIENCE
131
      Deep learning-based frameworks are widely used in systems neuroscience (Richards et al., 2019).
132   For example, deep neural networks have emerged as plausible models of the brain (Sacramento et al.,
133   2018; Whittington & Bogacz, 2017; Kumar et al., 2021), mimicking representational transformations
134   in primate perceptual systems (Kell et al., 2018; Bashivan et al., 2019). Moreover, these models
135   exhibit classic behavioral and neurophysiological phenomena when trained on tasks similar to those
136   performed by animals (Banino et al., 2018; Pospisil et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018). On the other
137   hand, Yamins & DiCarlo (2016) measure the correlation between artificial neural networks (ANNs)
138   and neuronal activities in the monkey visual cortex during image classification tasks, shedding light
139   on the design of brain-like ANNs (Kubilius et al., 2019; Zhuang et al., 2021). Insights from brain-like
140
      models are just as valuable to the machine learning community as they are to neuroscience. For
      example, navigation is fundamental for humans but challenging for ANNs (Mirowski et al., 2017).
141
      Banino et al. (2018) leverage the computational functions of grid cells, critical for navigation in
142
      primates, to develop a deep RL agent with mammal-like navigational abilities.

      3.1   M ULTI -R EGION B RAIN M ODEL FOR E NTORHINAL -H IPPOCAMPAL -C ORTICAL
            C OMPUTATION AND I NTERACTION
148
149   Our multi-region brain model integrates a cortical circuit modeled by an action-selection RNN policy,
150   and an entorhinal-hippocampal circuit based on Chandra et al. (2023) that involves bidirectional
151   computations between grid cells and place cells to associate, encode, and learn information from the
152   environment. As described in Chandra et al. (2023) and shown in Fig 1A (purple and orange), the
153
      entorhinal-hippocampal memory scaffold is a bipartite architecture that comprises hidden (hippocam-
      pal) and label (grid cell) layers with computation based on known and inferred recurrent connectivity
154
      between entorhinal cortex and hippocampus (Witter & Groenewegen, 1984; Amaral & Witter, 1989;
155
      Witter & Amaral, 1991; Witter et al., 2017) and among grid cells in the entorhinal cortex (Burak &
156   Fiete, 2009). The connections from grid cells to hippocampus, Wpg , are set as fixed and random,
157   while the connections from hippocampus to grid cells, Wgp , are set once through associative learning
158   and held fixed. The connections between hippocampus and non-grid EC, Wps and Wsp , are learned
159   bidirectionally through associative learning. The grid cell layer is a k-hot modular vector imposed by
160   local recurrent inhibition, where k reflects the number of distinct one-hot modules. Each module has
161   a unique period, and velocity inputs (e.g., position and evidence) advance the phase of each module
      along its 2D representational space (i.e., a 2D torus).

      4    A LTERNATIVE H YPOTHESES OF M ULTI -R EGION I NTERACTIONS
253   Nieh et al. (2021) observe the existence of conjoint cognitive maps encoding both evidence (cognitive
254   variable) and position (physical variable) in the hippocampus when mice perform the accumulating
255   tower task, suggesting that the hippocampus also performs a general computation, rather than just
256   responding to external stimulus features like space (O’Keefe & Burgess, 1996). This discovery calls
257   for investigation at an appropriate mechanistic modeling scale on how multiple regions coordinate to
258   give rise to internally generated cognition in the brain that gives individuals the ability to flexibly
259   navigate spaces, as well as organize and interrelate experience, objects, and events.
260   Using the accumulating tower task as a simplistic tool for deriving hypotheses, there are three
261   possibilities that give rise to a conjoint map of physical and cognitive variables in HPC:
262
263          • Grid cells encode position, which follows the common belief (Moser et al., 2008), and the
264            conjoint encoding in HPC is due to receiving sensory information from non-grid EC (M2).
265          • Grid cells co-tune both position and evidence, which is not substantially investigated
266            experimentally to our best knowledge; the EC pathway is not necessary or relevant (M3).
267          • Grid cells co-tune both position and evidence, and the EC pathway also plays a role in giving
268            arise to the conjoint cognitive map in HPC (M4, M5).
269

      5     R ESULTS
303
304   Here we show that our multi-region brain model (M5) governed by biologically plausible neural
305   computation principles yields the prediction of MEC jointly integrates position and evidence in a
306   spatially embedded decision-making task represented by the accumulating tower task. Further, we
307   found computational advantages for HPC in leveraging sensory information in the task. Not only
308   does M5 promote rapid learning, but it also aligns with the experimental observations of place cell
309   firing patterns observed in Nieh et al. (2021). Notably, this is the only variant that exhibits visually
310
      separable low-dimensional representation of task variables in the Principle Component (PC) space of
      both HPC and cortical representations, which provides an opportunity for further hypothesizing the
311
      role of individual regions in a spatially embedded decision-making task. Please refer to Appendix A.2
312
      for more details, including environment and reward design.

447   We perform Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on hippocampal and cortical activities to identify
448   visually separable clusters in PC space when colored by task variables. The presence of low-
449   dimensional representations would provide insight into the functional roles of specific brain regions
450
      and the utility of different neural computational rules tested by model variants.
451   We found the joint integration model with activated EC pathway (M5) uniquely exhibit visually
452   separable clusters of hippocampal activity in PC space for both position (Fig 5, B1) and local evidence
453   velocity (#R-#L tower at a position, Fig 5, B2), in contrast to all other variants (Figs 5, A1, A2, and
454   Appendix E). We did not observe separability in accumulated evidence in hippocampal activities in
455   first 3 PCs: this is counter-intuitive given grid cells encode and communicate accumulated evidence
456
      to hippocampus, while EC does not encode fine-grained information. Given hippocampal neurons
      are projection neurons (Fox & Ranck Jr, 1981; 1975), it is curious what the source and encoding
457
      mechanism of local evidence velocity is in hippocampus. We suggest future studies to analyze existing
458
      experimental data to validate the predicted hippocampal role of representing local evidence velocity,
459   and to further dissect the proposed model through ablation studies for understanding the underlying
460   computational mechanism. Overall, this suggests that the integration of sensory information from the
461   EC is necessary for creating a cohesive, low-dimensional representation of relevant task variables in
462   hippocampus, supporting rapid learning and efficient spatial navigation (Fig 2).
463
      Additionally, we observe the separability of three possible agent actions in hippocampal and cortical
464
      activities in M4 and M5 (Fig 5, panel 3 and 4). The separability of actions exists in M1 and M2
465   (Appendix E), which are models that cannot learn the accumulating tower tasks but are limited to
466   clusters of going forward and clusters of one particular turning action. The separation of actions is
467   noisy in M3 when sensory information from EC is absent (Appendix E). These observations suggest
468   hippocampal cognitive maps, emerged from jointly integrated grid cells and sensory information, are
469   self-contained and sufficient for downstream decisions.
470
      Together, this stream of results driven by hypotheses tested in mechanistic models reveals necessary
471   neural mechanisms for forming cohesive and low-dimensional representations of task variables in
472   hippocampal cognitive maps. In particular, we predict and demonstrate conjoint tuning in grid cells
473   and sensory pathways are critical for mimicking the biology observed in experiments, and giving rise
474   to important behaviors such as rapid learning and efficient spatial navigation (Fig 2).

478
      Our work highlights the critical role of conjunctive encoding in grid cells, where both spatial and
479
      task-relevant information are jointly represented. We propose a multi-region brain model combining
480   entorhinal-hipocampal circuit (Chandra et al., 2023) and RNN which works as neocortex and evaluate
481   it in accumulating tower task (Nieh et al., 2021), which we formulate as reinforcement learning
482   problem. Our model demonstrates that this integration, along with non-grid sensory inputs from
483   the entorhinal cortex, is crucial for rapid learning and decision-making in spatially embedded tasks.
484   These findings align with experimental observations, showing that the hippocampus forms cognitive
485   maps that combine spatial locations and accumulated evidence, enabling efficient navigation and
      flexible decision-making.

508   The prediction of conjunctive encoding of spatial and task-relevant information in grid cells reinforces
509   the hypothesis that spatial and cognitive processes are deeply intertwined in the brain’s navigation
510   and memory systems (Buzsáki & Moser, 2013). Neural algorithms that support path integration and
511   navigation are likely repurposed for more abstract forms of cognition, enabling the hippocampal-
512   entorhinal network to facilitate both physical navigation and decision-making based on internal
513
      cognitive states. However, sensory inputs to the hippocampus, while enhancing spatial efficiency,
      introduce variability in learning performance, suggesting a trade-off between precision and complexity
514
      in neural representations. Future research should investigate how different neural inputs optimize
515
      decision-making processes in diverse environments.
516
517   While our proposed framework did not consider the role of CA3 recurrence, we observe that adding
518   this mechanism does not induce the experimentally observed place cell phenomena (Nieh et al., 2021)
519
      in M2 and M4, as demonstrated in Appendix F. It is worth noting that the consideration of CA3
      recurrence is complementary to our predicted grid cell coding scheme, and these mechanisms are not
520
      mutually exclusive. Our framework can be extended to explore the causal effects of CA3 recurrence
521
      more thoroughly, through ablation studies incorporating models similar to M1 through M5 but with
522   CA3 recurrence included and other mechanisms selectively excluded. Furthermore, we are actively
523   collecting neurophysiological data to test our falsifiable prediction. The results of these experiments
524   will directly inform whether additional mechanisms, such as CA3 recurrence, contribute to place cell
525   tuning, and can be readily investigated within our current framework.
526
      It is also worth noting that the use of an MLP to extract velocity signals is a simplification of visual-
527
      vestibular integration (DeAngelis & Angelaki, 2012), justified as a consequential assumption from
528   experimentally well-supprted (Malone et al., 2024; Yoon et al., 2013; Alme et al., 2014) Vector-HaSH
529   (Chandra et al., 2023), that the velocity signals are processed by a potentially diverse set of brain
530   regions external to the entorhinal-hippocampal circuit (Morris & Derdikman, 2023), and is then fed
531   into grid cells. This simplification of the underlying velocity abstraction process does not affect our
532   results, and is not the focus or within the scope of our framework.
533
      In conclusion, our findings provide a comprehensive framework for understanding how the
534   hippocampal-entorhinal-neocortical network integrates physical and cognitive information to create
535   flexible cognitive maps that support learning and navigation. Our work opens pathways for future
536   experimental studies to explore these straightforward, falsifiable predictions and understand how the
537   brain generalizes mechanisms of physical navigation to solve abstract cognitive problems. Moreover,
538   this serves as a strong example of how machine learning can be applied to neuroscience and vice
539   versa to explain neural phenomenon, guide future research directions, and demonstrate the efficiency
      of neuro-inspired artificial intelligence.

      A.1    M ODEL
762   For M1-M5, we use three grid modules, with corresponding periodicity of 7, 8, 11, thus Ng = 234 (=
763   72 + 82 + 112 ). We simulate 800 place cells. The MLP and RNN both have a learning rate of 0.0005
764   and a hidden size of 32. The RNN has leaky units, with α = 0.025.
765
766
      The grid coding scheme is by design, as illustrated by an ealier example in Fig 1C. Generally, the
      velocity inputs to each grid module updates the grid phases through path integration, following
767
      Vector-HaSH implementation in Chandra et al. (2023). The evidence velocity to grid cell modules is
768
      effectively the number of towers on the right minus the number of towers on the left in the current
769   position, which is predicted through an MLP from the current field of view (sensory inputs). The
770   positional velocity, on the other hand, is represented as 0 (stuck) or +1 (forward) without the use of
771   MLP, given backward-moving is not task-relevant (Nieh et al. (2021), behavioral training), though it
772   is possible to use an MLP as well. We made a simplification here without the use of MLP, given this
773   change will not affect the results.
774
      For the standalone RNN baselines (M0), we scale up the hidden size to 32 + Ng + Np + Ns = 1076
775   to ensure the total number of neurons used is consistent with M1-M5. The input to RNN baselines is
776   sensory information, with one of the variants additionally takes in positional velocity (moved or not)
777   and evidence velocity (predicted by MLP with the same setup as M1-M5). We use a learning rate of
778   0.0001 with the gradient clipped to a max norm of 1. This is because the large standalone RNNs fail
779   to learn with the learning rate of 0.0005 used by M1-M5 due to learning instability (e.g., exploding
780   or vanishing gradients). As a result, we conducted a hyperparameter search and used the settings that
781   yielded the best performance.
782
783   A.2    E NVIRONMENT
784
785
      The accumulating-tower task involves an agent moving through a T-maze with towers on both sides
      (Fig 1C) and must turn at the end toward the side with more towers to obtain a reward. We divide the
786
      maze into non-overlapping start region (9%, no towers), cue region (61%, with towers), and delay
787
      and decision region (the rest, no towers), in approximate alignment with Nieh et al. (2021). We
788   represent each episode as a unique configuration of towers, involving several steps down the corridor
789   until an agent reaches the T-arm and makes a turn. We represent the left and right sides of the maze
790   each as a vector (‘1’ = tower, ‘0’ = non-tower, ‘−1’ = outside-of-maze). The agent has a field of view
791   on some number of positions ahead.
792
      The ‘rewarded’ side (with more towers) in each episode is chosen uniformly at random, with xreward
793
      number of towers. Specifically, the number of towers on the reward side xreward is sampled from
794   Uniform(1, K) with K being some maximum threshold. The ‘non-rewarded‘ side has strictly fewer
795   towers xnon-reward , where xnon-reward ∼ Uniform(0, xreward ). At each step, the agent can move left(0),
796   right(1), or forward(2). Before reaching the end, the agent is rewarded each step 0.01 for going
797   forward and a penalization of −0.001 otherwise. After reaching the end, the agent gets a reward of
798   10 for a correct turn, no reward for the opposite turn, and −1 for continuing forward (bumping the
799   wall) every time; the episode ends when the agent makes a turn or reaches a maximum number of
800   attempts (which is penalized by −5). We simulate a maze sequence length of 20 (so the division of
801   the start, cue, delay and decision region length is {1, 12, 6, 1} correspondingly), a field of view of 5,
802
      and train the RNN using REINFORCE algorithm (Sutton et al., 1999) until convergence.

      A.3    S INGLE STEP IN THE TASK
811
812   One step in the accumulating task involves the following (Fig 1C): upon the agent perceives the
813   sensory information, an MLP (is trained to) abstracts evidence velocity from the processed sensory
814   information perceived within the field of view. The grid cell state is consequently updated by position
815   and evidence velocity signals, and projected onto place cells along with the non-grid sensory vector.
816   The place cell code is fed to the cortical RNN policy to select an action on going left, right, or forward.
817   The succeeding position update (stay or advance a position) carries relevant sensory information,
818
      which is again fed implicitly to the grid cell subnetwork to shift the grid states; the grid vector and
      the corresponding newly perceived sensory information are used to update place cell states again.
819
      This process repeats until the agent eventually reaches the T-arm and makes a turning decision while
820
      following the reward scheme mentioned in Appendix A.2.

       F    E FFECT OF CA3 R ECURRENCE IN M2 & M4
1135
1136
       In this section, we demonstrate in M2 and M4, as a proof of concept, that the inclusion of CA3
1137   recurrent connectivity in the HPC layer does not affect the general conclusions presented in the main
1138   paper. Specifically, the inclusion of CA3 recurrence in M2 or M4 does not induce the experimentally
1139   observed place cell phenomena (Nieh et al., 2021), producing similar results as if recurrence was
1140   absent (see Figs 3, 4, 6, 7, and 8).
1141
       To model CA3 recurrence, we incorporate additional recurrent connections within the HPC layer,
1142
       Wpp , updated through associative learning using p⃗mix (t) and p⃗mix(t + 1), analogous to the learning
1143   process for Wps and Wsp (see Eqns. 4 and 5). The activity of mixed place cells is then described by:
1144
1145                                      p⃗mix (t + 1) = ReLU[Wps · ⃗s(t) + Wpg · ⃗g (t + 1) + Wpp · p⃗mix (t)].                                                                                                             (6)
1146   The rest of the setup remains consistent with Appendix A.1.
1147
1148   As shown in Fig. 11, the inclusion of recurrent integration of positional information from MEC and
1149
       sensory information from non-grid EC does not result in the emergence of conjunctive place cells in
       M2. Similarly, Fig. 12 demonstrates that the lack of conjunctive place cells in M4 persists when grid
1150
       cell modules encode position and evidence disjointly.
1151
1152   These findings confirm that the recurrent integration in HPC alone does not induce conjunctive coding,
1153   underscoring the critical role of joint integration of position and evidence in grid cells for producing
1154   co-tuned place cells.
