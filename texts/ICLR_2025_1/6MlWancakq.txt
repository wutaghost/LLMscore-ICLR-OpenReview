011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            When performing classification tasks with language models, would you prefer
014            having only one highly accurate class or having every class deliver reliable per-
015            formance? Obviously, a more balanced accuracy among classes better reflects
016            the expectations of the majority of users. Especially for large language models
017
               (LLMs), the fact that they achieve a fair overall accuracy by in-context learning
               (ICL) obscures a large difference in individual class accuracies. In this work, we
018
               uncover and tackle language models’ imbalance in per-class prediction accuracy by
019
               reconceptualizing it as the Contextual Oddity Bias (COBias), and we are the first
020            to engage nonlinear integer programming (NIP) to debias it. Briefly, the proposed
021            COBias metric measures accuracy differences among class pairs, with which we
022            reveal the large per-class accuracy differences exhibited in LLMs of varied scales
023            and families. Then we propose Debiasing as Nonlinear Integer Programming
024            (DNIP) to correct ICL per-class probabilities towards lower COBias and higher
025            overall accuracy. Our optimization objective is directly based on the evaluation
026            scores by COBias and accuracy metrics, which is non-differentiable and solved by
027            the simulated annealing metaheuristic. Evaluations on three LLMs across seven
028
               NLP classification tasks show that DNIP simultaneously achieves significant CO-
               Bias reduction (-27%) and accuracy improvement (+12%) over the conventional
029
               ICL approach, suggesting that modeling pairwise class accuracy differences is a
030
               direction in pushing forward more accurate, more reliable LLM predictions.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      For large language models (LLMs), the fact that they achieve remarkable test accuracy over all class
036
      labels by few-shot in-context learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) can obscure a large difference in
037   individual class accuracies. We rethink language models’ imbalance in per-class prediction accuracy
038   and reconceptualize it as the Contextual Oddity Bias (COBias). What motivates COBias is a subtle
039   yet overlooked observation, where a most frequently predicted class can hold the majority of wrong
040   predictions of other classes, especially of a least frequently predicted class; it is empirically obvious
041   that there is a difference in the most and least frequently predicted classes’ accuracies. To this end,
042   for this single pair of classes, we define COBiassingle to measure the difference in accuracy by a class
043   A compared to its “odd” class, which holds the majority wrong predictions of class A. Beyond these
044   pairs that involve odd classes, the per-class accuracy difference is indeed an issue among every pair
045
      of classes. Extending the above measure to every pair, we propose a COBias metric which aggregates
      the differences in pairwise class accuracies. COBias reflects a common language model failure, seen
046
      in a variety of NLP benchmark tasks from text classification to question answering. It is a direct
047
      outcome of LLMs’ liking for specific patterns or answers, leading to highly imbalanced predicted
048   class distributions (Kassner & Schütze, 2020). This per-class accuracy difference can be further
049   attributed to ICL prompt sensitivity or rooted in the pretraining data. To name a few, the causes
050   encompass predilections for common tokens in the pretraining corpora (Zhao et al., 2021), choices of
051   prompt templates (Jiang et al., 2020; Min et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023), demonstrations (Holtzman
052   et al., 2021), and prompt orders (Lu et al., 2022; Turpin et al., 2023). Unintended LLM behaviors
053   may also exacerbate the bias, such as user-following sycophancy (Perez et al., 2023; Sharma et al.,
      2024; Wei et al., 2024) and prompt toning effects (Jones & Steinhardt, 2022; Lin & Ng, 2023; Li


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Despite the causes, COBias renders a language model less effective
055   for lower-accuracy classes and hinders users from trusting the answers, heightening the need for a
056   unified debiasing method at output level.
057
058
      Importantly, it is more than just mitigating a model’s bias towards a class (each class), but more
      precisely, the model’s tendency to predict other classes as it.That is why we need to model the
059
      interaction between pairwise class predictions for better bias reduction. An example of an odd class
060
      and the per-class accuracy difference are shown by the upper plots in Figure 1. Essentially, mitigating
061   per-class accuracy bias is a critical step in pushing forward LLM inference abilities, as it directly
062   targets improving lower-accuracy classes while improving or at least not hurting the overall accuracy.
063
064
      To reduce COBias and rectify LLM outputs, we directly operate on the per-class probabilities given by
      the conventional ICL approach. We propose DNIP, the Debiasing as Nonlinear Integer Programming
065
      method, to adjust per-class probabilities towards lower COBias and higher overall accuracy, from a
066
      combinatorial optimization viewpoint (Section 3). We formulate the debiasing problem as a discrete
067   correction weight selection problem. In particular, DNIP re-weights per-class probability with an
068   optimal set of weights selected based on the evaluation metrics of accuracy and COBias, and thus
069   obtains much less biased predictions with higher accuracy.
070
      On three widely used, different-scale LLMs across seven NLP evaluation datasets, we show the
071
      effectiveness of DNIP. Our key key messages are as follows:
072
073          • We introduce the COBias evaluation metric to assess the pairwise class accuracy bias
074            in language model predictions, and reveal that LLMs of different scales and families
075            consistently exhibit a large COBias score, at an average of 43%.
076          • We propose Debiasing as Nonlinear Integer Programming (DNIP) to jointly minimize
077            COBias and maximize accuracy. An efficient simulated annealing algorithm is adopted to
078            solve the mathematical model.
079          • Experiments show that DNIP achieves the best of both worlds for lower COBias (avg.
080            43% → 16%) and higher overall accuracy (avg. 52% → 64%) over the ICL approach.

084   Based on the phenomenon that a class can hold the majority of wrong predictions of another class,

091

146   3     I MPROVING LLM O UTPUTS BY Debiasing as Nonlinear Integer Programming
147
148   In this work, we approach the debiasing problem as a nonlinear integer programming (NIP) problem.
149   The objective is to optimize correction weights to rectify the biased, per-class probabilities, with
150   directly modeling the COBias metric and two accuracy-related metrics. The weights are optimized
151   on a set of labeled examples, and are simply plugged in to inference-time probabilities. An overview
152   of the proposed DNIP method is shown in Figure 1. Our source code will be released.
153
154   3.1   T HE DNIP O BJECTIVE
155
156   Considering M examples with known class labels, we let pm denote the normalized output probability
                                                                                       PN
157   over N class tokens for an example, and pm = (pm1 , . . . , pmn , . . . , pmN ), n=1 pmn = 1, m ∈
158   {1, . . . , M }. To debias pm , we introduce a discrete K-point correction scale ω ranging from 0 to 1,
159   to re-weight class probability. Let ω = (ω1 , . . . , ωK ) denote the parameter for the discrete weights
160   in the correction scale, where ωk = k/K. An example 10-point scale is given by ω = (0.1, . . . , 1.0).
161   For each class probability in pm , it multiplies by a correction weight chosen from ω to obtain a
      debiased class probability.

      Finding optimal correction weights is a combinatorial optimization problem: for N -way class
163   probability pm , each class selects a weight from the K-dimensional correction scale. To find the
164   optimal combination, the brute force strategy is an enumerated search, where the number of weight
165   combinations to explore is K N for an example; for M examples, it results in a computational
166   complexity of O(K N · M ), which can not run in polynomial time, calling for smarter methods.
167

      We solve DNIP by simulated annealing (Kirkpatrick et al., 1983; Eglese, 1990). Simulated annealing
      (SA) is a Metropolis-Hastings sampling algorithm, which is widely adopted to solve optimization
203
      problems with its versatility in dealing with highly nonlinear models, noisy data, and many constraints
204
      (Busetti, 2001). The DNIP mathematical model is such a case, pointing to the need of SA. We
205   especially take advantages of SA algorithms’ searching ability - escaping from local optima and
206   finding global optima after reaching local optima - to find optimal correction weights. In details,
207   the SA strategy consists of searching the solution space starting from a randomly initialized ξ and
208   then generating a new one by perturbing it. The new solution is either accepted or rejected by the
209   Metropolis criterion after evaluating the objectives.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
238
239   Evaluation Tasks. The proposed method is evaluated on a diverse set of binary and multi-class
240   NLP classification tasks, across general domains and biomedical domains. The five general-domain
241   evaluation datasets are 4-way news topic classification AGNews (Zhang et al., 2015), 14-way ontology
242   classification DBpedia (Auer et al., 2007), 5-way sentiment classification SST-5 (Socher et al., 2013),
243   6-way retrieval question classification TREC (Voorhees & Tice, 2000; Li & Roth, 2002), binary
244   entailment recognition RTE (Dagan et al., 2006); the two domain-specific datasets from BLURB
245
      (Gu et al., 2021) are 5-way biomedical relation extraction DDI (Segura-Bedmar et al., 2013), and
      3-way biomedical question answering PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019). The selected biomedical tasks
246
      are of practical uses. For example, detecting DDI is useful in preventing adverse effects from drug
247
      combinations.
248
249   Each dataset comes with a training and a test set. We make use of the default training set for DNIP
250   optimization, except for AGNews, DBpedia, and DDI, for which we randomly select 10,000 training
251
      examples since the datasets are quite large. We then split the training examples into 95%, 5%
      optimization and development sets. We evaluate on the default test set except for AGNews, for which
252
      we evaluate on 5,000 randomly selected test examples. Evaluation metrics are COBias and accuracy.
253
254   Models and Experimental Configuration. We evaluate three open-source LLMs across varied
255   scales and model families, including GPT-2-XL (1.5B parameters), Llama-2-7B (7B parameters), and
256   Llama-2-13B (13B parameters). For ICL, we use 1-shot prompting, and obtain the output per-class
257
      softmax probabilities. To standardize calculations, we normalize the softmax probabilities over all
      classes. For DNIP, we tune parameters of β, τ, K, u on the development set. To account for variance
258
      caused by different demonstrations, for each model and dataset, we perform three runs of prompting
259
      with different 1-shot demonstrations and obtain three sets of initial probabilities. For both ICL and
260   DNIP, we report the average test accuracy and COBias over the three runs. The demonstration is
261   randomly selected from training examples. All prompting is done on an NVIDIA A100 GPU. The
262   simulated annealing algorithm executes on CPU (execution time depends on the optimization set size,
263   3 to 30 minutes on AMD EPYC 7742 CPU or slightly longer on local laptop CPU).

      LLMs may fail in various ways. Analysis towards understanding LLM failure modes has gained
449
      remarkable insights on how they fail and how to identify/avoid failures. For example, understanding
450   hallucinations in LLMs facilitates fact-checking evaluation metrics for LLMs.Ji et al. (2023); Dhingra

453   ICL order sensitivity that result in LLM prompting failures. Turpin et al. (2023) shows that LLM
454   reasoning abilities are affected by adding biasing features to the input. Along this line, we tackle
455   a common LLM failure of the per-class accuracy difference, via nonlinear integer programming

484
485   Using Chain-of-Thought Prompting to Improve LLM Predictions. Some may argue that a simple
      remedy for biased predictions is Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, which elicit more reasoning in


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      LLMs. However, Turpin et al. (2023) points out that CoT explanations can be unfaithful, as they can
487   be biased towards class A when prompts always put letter A as the answer, causing 36% accuracy
488   drops on BBH (Suzgun et al., 2023). We add that prompt-based models may fall for pattern matching
489   instead of recalling learned factual knowledge (Kassner & Schütze, 2020). Therefore, CoT may not
490   help due to unfaithful explanations.
491
      Using Calibration Methods to Mitigate ICL Biases. Previous calibration methods reduce biases
492
      caused by prompting. For example, Zhao et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2024) use content-free or content-
493   based prompts to measure an offset and calibrate class probabilities by removing it. This may be
494   good for mitigating bias towards a single class, but it does not consider the interaction between class
495   predictions, e.g., pairwise class accuracy bias studied in this work. Another hazard is using prompts
496   to compute the offset, which may cascade biases brought by a certain or several prompt templates,
497   and biases inherited from pretraining may not be reflected. Instead, we correcct biased predictions
498   without more prompting, but we differ in that our correction weights are explicitly optimized on
499   COBias and accuracy metrics, while the cluster-based decision boundary is implicitly learned.
500   Evaluating Closed LLMs. While closed LLMs such as ChatGPT and GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024)
501   are interesting to evaluate, they do not return logits/softmax probabilities over the entire vocabulary at
502   an output token. For example, ChatGPT returns log probabilities of up to 20 most likely output tokens,
503   and may not cover the probability for every class that is needed in our class prediction debiasing.
504
      Optimization Algorithms for Integer Programming. The classic solvers for integer programming
505   include operational research techniques such as Branch and Bound (BnB), commonly used for
506   linear integer programming problems. However, it is difficult for these methods to solve our DNIP
507   model, which contains discontinuous functions and is non-differentiable. In this case, a series of
508   metaheuristic algorithms are more suitable, solversincluding Simulated Annealing (SA), Genetic
509   Algorithms, Ant Colony Optimization, and Particle Swarm Optimization. These algorithms all belong
510   to the same category, and each meta heuristic has their own pros and cons. Therefore, SA in this
511   paper has a clear purpose of tackling our NIP mathematical model that classical operational research
512   methods cannot solve.
513   Our Motivation Is Different from Classical Post-hoc Corrections. Some may contend that achiev-
514   ing equitable accuracies across all classes is a well-explored issue in standard machine learning
515   classifiers. However, it is crucial to see that per-class prediction accuracy imbalances must be under-
516   stood within their specific context. The accuracy bias in LLMs’ outputs arises from fundamentally
517   different causes than the unequal class accuracies seen in potentially overfitted traditional classifiers.
518   In the former, biases stem from prompts and pre-training, while in the latter, the imbalance is driven
519
      by skewness in the supervised training data. This distinction underscores why our approach is
      uniquely applied to LLMs’ output token class probabilities.

      7    C ONCLUSION
524
525   This work tackles the imbalance in per-class prediction accuracy of language models. We introduce
526   COBias and reveal that LLMs of varied scales and families exhibit large COBias at inference. To
527   minimize COBias and push for higher accuracy, we view debiasing as a combinatorial optimization
528
      problem and propose DNIP, which optimizes class correction weights by jointly minimizing evaluation
      scores by COBias and maximizing accuracy. Empirical evaluations on three LLMs across seven multi-
529
      class NLP classification tasks demonstrate that DNIP simultaneously achieves lower test COBias
530
      and higher test accuracy over conventional ICL. We advocate that reducing COBias is a direction to
531   gain more accurate, reliable LLM predictions. For future works, we may extend our studies to more
532   tasks and other modalities. We aim to find simple inference-time plug-in solution for further boosting
533   language model prediction performances.

      A    D ERIVATION OF SA A LGORITHM C OMPLEXITY
757
758
759   Given N classes and K scales, the DNIP objective has N K variables. For each inner loop of SA, the
760
      length of the Markov chain is often several times of the size of variables, i.e., λN K. For a geometric
      cooling schedule T = αn T max , the number of outer-loop iterations is:
761
762
763
                                                                T min
764                                            N outer = logα                                           (11)
765
                                                                T max
766
767   The sampling times for the SA algorithm is λN K logα (T min /T max ). Therefore, the computational
768   complexity is O(λN K logα (T min /T max )) = O(N K).
