009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for collaborative
012            machine learning while preserving data privacy. However, existing FL approaches
013            face challenges in balancing model generalization among heterogeneous clients
014            and resistance to malicious attacks. This paper introduces Dynamic SVD-driven
015            Federated Learning (DSVD-FL), a novel approach that addresses these challenges
016
               simultaneously. DSVD-FL dynamically adjusts the contribution of each client
               using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), introducing an adaptive weighting
017
               mechanism based on singular value contributions and vector alignments. The-
018
               oretical analysis demonstrates the convergence properties and computational ef-
019            ficiency of our approach. Experimental results on both IID and non-IID datasets
020            show that DSVD-FL outperforms state-of-the-art FL approaches in terms of model
021            accuracy, robustness against various attack scenarios, while maintaining compet-
022            itive computational efficiency. We perform an ablation study to explore the key
023            components of SVD that impact the federated learning performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a transformative approach in distributed machine learning,
028
      allowing multiple clients to collaboratively train a model without sharing their raw data (McMahan
029
      et al., 2017; Krizhevsky et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2019) . In FL, clients typically refer to devices or
030   nodes that possess local data and participate in the training process. These diverse clients unite their
031   efforts to collectively train a robust global model (Kairouz et al., 2021; Bonawitz, 2019). Such col-
032   laboration leverages the unique strengths of each client’s data and computational resources (Smith
033   et al., 2017; Konečnỳ, 2016), enhancing the overall learning outcomes by integrating varied per-
034   spectives and information into a single model (Zhao et al., 2018; Hard et al., 2018; Briggs et al.,
035   2020).
036   Despite these advantages, FL faces several core challenges that limit its widespread adoption. One
037   major issue is the poor generalization performance of existing FL approaches when dealing with
038   non-IID (non-Independent and Identically Distributed) data, where data distributions differ signif-
039   icantly across clients (Li et al., 2020a; Karimireddy et al., 2020). Non-IID data conditions are
040   prevalent in real-world scenarios (Li et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) and can result in
041   biased global models that disproportionately favor clients with larger or higher-quality dataset (Hsu
042   et al., 2019; Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019b), reducing the overall model accuracy (Wang
043   et al., 2020; Sattler et al., 2020; Yeganeh et al., 2020) and limiting its generalization capacity (Yu
044
      et al., 2020; Ghosh et al., 2020; Fallah et al., 2020).
045   In addition to generalization issues, FL approaches are vulnerable to malicious clients (Bagdasaryan
046   et al., 2020; Bhagoji et al., 2019). Attackers can participate in the FL process by submitting poisoned
047   model updates (Xie et al., 2018; Tolpegin et al., 2020), which can severely degrade the performance
048   of the global model (Fang et al., 2020).
049   Addressing these intertwined challenges requires a more adaptive approach. To this end, we intro-
050   duce DSVD-FL, a Dynamic SVD-driven FL approach, designed to optimize federated learning in
051   heterogeneous and adversarial environments. Unlike traditional FL methods, such as FedProx (Li
052   et al., 2020a), FedSVD (Grammenos et al., 2020), and FedCPA (Han et al., 2023), which primar-
053   ily focus on static aggregation techniques or compression for communication efficiency, DSVD-FL
      introduces a novel dynamic mechanism that combines SVD-driven similarity measures, adaptive

054
      weighting, and dynamic truncation. DSVD-FL adapts to both the diversity of client data and the
055   potential presence of malicious participants by dynamically adjusting the contribution of each client
056   to the global model. This is achieved through Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), which analyzes
057   the structure of client updates to assess and weigh contributions based on their similarity and rele-
058   vance to the overall model improvement. By continuously fine-tuning client influence, DSVD-FL
059   not only enhances model generalization in challenging non-IID settings but also improves defense
060   against adversarial attacks by mitigating the impact of poisoned updates. Therefore, DSVD-FL
061   fundamentally improves the process of representation learning.
062   This paper makes the following contributions:
063
064          • We propose a dynamic SVD-driven approach, DSVD-FL, which adapts client contribu-
065            tions based on multi-faceted model similarity, ensuring fairness and improving robustness
066            against adversarial attacks.
067          • DSVD-FL improves model generalization in non-IID settings through dynamic adjustment
068            and adaptive weighting of client updates, addressing data heterogeneity across clients.
069          • DSVD-FL introduces a dynamic truncation mechanism that adjusts the complexity of client
070            updates based on their performance. This approach filters noisy or adversarial updates.
071          • DSVD-FL provides both theoretical convergence guarantees and empirical validation on
072            real-world datasets, outperforming state-of-the-art methods like FedProx, FedSVD, and
073            FedCPA in terms of accuracy, fairness, and resilience to adversarial behavior.

075   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      Several FL approaches have been proposed to address the challenges of non-IID data, fairness, and
      robustness, but none have provided a comprehensive solution.
078
079   FedProx (Li et al., 2020a) builds upon the standard FedAvg approach by introducing a proximal
080   term in the local objective function, which helps stabilize the training process in non-IID settings
081   by constraining the distance between local model and the global model. While FedProx improves
082   convergence in heterogeneous environments, its inability to handle adversarial threats leaves models
083
      vulnerable to poisoned updates.
084   FedSVD (Grammenos et al., 2020) incorporates SVD for FL to compress client updates, thereby
085   reducing communication costs. However, the focus of FedSVD is on data compression rather than
086   optimizing model generalization or robustness against adversarial attacks. In addition, it does not
087   dynamically adjust client contributions based on model similarity or address non-IID challenges
088   explicitly, thus resulting in suboptimal performance in non-IID environments.
089   FedCPA (Han et al., 2023) addresses the problem of adversarial attacks in FL by performing crit-
090   ical parameter analysis to detect and down-weight potentially malicious clients. FedCPA measures
091   model similarity across clients and discards updates that deviate significantly from the majority.
092   However, this approach focuses primarily on attack resistance and does not explicitly account for
093   the heterogeneity of non-IID data, limiting its ability to generalize well across diverse client popu-
094   lations.

      In this section, we present DSVD-FL (Dynamic SVD-based Federated Learning), a novel approach
111   designed to enhance federated learning in environments characterized by non-IID data distributions,
112   client heterogeneity, and potential adversarial threats. DSVD-FL achieves this by dynamically ad-
113   justing client contributions leveraging SVD, thereby optimizing both generalization and robustness
114   of the global model. We detail the problem formulation, introduce the SVD-driven aggregation
115   process, and describe the dynamic truncation mechanism that drives performance improvement of
116   DSVD-FL.

293
      We compared the performance of DSVD-FL against several well-known federated learning methods:
294
295   FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017): A baseline algorithm that averages local updates based on client
296   data size.
297   q-FFL (Li et al., 2019a): A method that focuses on fairness by optimizing the performance across
298   all clients.
299
300
      FedProx (Li et al., 2020a): An extension of FedAvg that includes a proximal term to handle client
      heterogeneity.
301
302   FedCPA (Han et al., 2023): A method that detects adversarial updates and down-weights malicious
303   clients.
304   In our evaluation, we considered the following key metrics to assess the performance of DSVD-FL
305   and compare it with other baseline methods:
306
307
      Final Accuracy: The ultimate performance of the model after training, reflecting its ability to main-
      tain effectiveness despite potential attacks or data heterogeneity (McMahan et al., 2017). Higher
308
      values indicate better performance.
309
310   Accuracy Degradation: The extent to which the model’s accuracy decreases under attack compared
311   to its performance in a non-adversarial setting, quantifying the impact of malicious activities (Fung
312   et al., 2018). Lower values indicate better robustness.
313   Convergence Speed: The number of rounds required for the model to reach a stable performance,
314   indicating the algorithm’s efficiency in achieving consensus among distributed clients (Li et al.,
315   2020b). Lower values (fewer rounds) indicate faster convergence and better efficiency.
316
      Robustness Quotient: A metric that combines the model’s final accuracy with its resilience to the
317   proportion of compromised clients, providing a comprehensive measure of robustness (Blanchard
318   et al., 2017). Higher values indicate better overall robustness.
319
320
      Attack Tolerance: The model’s capacity to maintain performance in the presence of adversarial
      attacks, often measured as the inverse of accuracy degradation (Sun et al., 2019). Higher values
321
      indicate better resilience against attacks.
322
323   All experiments were implemented using PyTorch and were run on a Macbook with Apple Silicon.
      We used a convolutional neural network (CNN) for image classification tasks.

399   3.3     A BLATION S TUDIES
400
      In the proposed DSVD-FL approach, three similarity measures are introduced—singular vector
401
      alignment (α1 ), singular value similarity (α2 ), and low-rank approximation of the model update
402   matrix (α3 ). These similarity measures assess the relevance of each client’s model update to the
403   global model. To better understand how these three components affect model performance, we con-
404   ducted an ablation study to investigate how different combinations of α1 , α2 , and α3 influence model
405   performance under various conditions, including label flipping attacks, model poisoning attacks, and
406   no-attack scenarios.
407
408   3.3.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
409
      Datasets: MNIST was used in both IID and non-IID scenarios.
410
411   Attack Types: Label Flipping and Model Poisoning, where 10 % of malicious clients submit incor-
412   rect labels or corrupted model updates to disrupt the training of the global model.
413   Evaluation Metrics: We monitored the test accuracy at each training round, focusing on the corre-
414   lation between the first few training rounds and the subsequent test results. We also examined the
415   model stability, robustness, and resistance to attacks under different α combinations.
416
      The following three α combinations were tested:
417
418           1. α = [0.8, 0.1, 0.1]: The majority of the weight is assigned to singular vector alignment
419              (α1 ), emphasizing the alignment of update directions between clients.
420           2. α = [0.1, 0.8, 0.1]: The majority of the weight is assigned to singular value similarity (α2 ),

507   4   C ONCLUSION
508
509   We proposed DSVD-FL, a dynamic SVD-driven federated learning approach designed to address
510   the challenges of non-IID data, client heterogeneity, and adversarial attacks. By leveraging SVD-
511   based similarity measures, adaptive weighting, and dynamic truncation, DSVD-FL improves model
512   generalization and robustness in diverse federated learning environments. Our experiments demon-
513   strate that DSVD-FL comprehensively outperforms state-of-the-art methods like FedAvg, FedProx,
514   and FedCPA in terms of accuracy and resilience, especially under non-IID conditions and adver-
515   sarial settings. These results highlight the potential of DSVD-FL to provide a more robust, scalable
516   solution for real-world federated learning applications. Through our ablation study on the α parame-
517
      ters, we discovered that α1 (singular vector alignment) is the critical factor in determining the model
      update direction in most scenarios. This validates the effectiveness of DSVD-FL in leveraging SVD
518
      decomposition to capture the structure of client updates. Moreover, α3 (low-rank approximation
519
      similarity) performed best in label flipping attacks and no-attack scenarios, demonstrating the ad-
520   vantage of DSVD-FL in enhancing model robustness. These findings support our design choices in
521   DSVD-FL and highlight its capability in addressing various challenges in federated learning. Fu-
522   ture research directions could focus on developing an adaptive adjustment strategy that dynamically
523   tune α values based on the type of attack and data distribution during training to better respond to
524   different challenges.
