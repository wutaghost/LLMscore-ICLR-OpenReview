009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs typically requires a vast amount of GPU memory.
012            Standard first-order optimizers like SGD face a significant challenge due to the
013            large memory overhead from back-propagation as the size of LLMs increases,
014            which necessitates caching activations during the forward pass and gradients dur-
015            ing the backward pass. In contrast, zeroth-order (ZO) methods can estimate gra-
016
               dients with only two forward passes and without the need for activation caching.
               Additionally, CPU resources can be aggregated and offloaded to extend the mem-
017
               ory and computational capacity of a single GPU. To enable efficient fine-tuning
018
               of LLMs on a single GPU, we introduce ZO-Offloading, a framework that strate-
019            gically utilizes both CPU and GPU resources for ZO. ZO-Offloading dynamically
020            offloads model parameters to the CPU and retrieves them to the GPU as needed,
021            ensuring continuous and efficient computation by reducing idle times and maxi-
022            mizing GPU utilization. Parameter updates are integrated with ZO’s dual forward
023            passes to minimize redundant data transfers, thereby improving the overall effi-
024            ciency of the fine-tuning process. The ZO-Offloading framework also incorporates
025            a novel low-bit precision technique for managing data transfers between the CPU
026            and GPU in AMP mode, as well as asynchronous checkpointing for LLM fine-
027
               tuning. With ZO-Offloading, for the first time, it becomes possible to fine-tune
               extremely large models, such as the OPT-175B with over 175 billion parameters,
028
               on a single GPU with just 24GB of memory—a feat unattainable with conven-
029
               tional methods. Moreover, our framework operates without any additional time
030            cost compared to standard ZO methodologies.

035   As the scale of Large Language Models (LLMs) continues to grow, reaching parameter counts in
036   the hundreds of billions like OPT-175B (Zhang et al., 2022) and Llama 3.1 405B (Dubey et al.,
037   2024), managing GPU memory resources effectively becomes crucial. Efficient GPU memory man-
038   agement is crucial not only because it directly influences model performance and training speed,
039
      but also because GPU memory is both expensive and limited in quantity. However, this creates a
      significant challenge in handling ever-larger models within the physical constraints of current hard-
040
      ware technologies. CPU offloading has become a crucial technique for overcoming the challenge.
041
      It involves transferring computations and data from the GPU to the CPU, specifically targeting data
042   or parameters that are less frequently accessed (“inactive”). Specifically, it leverages the typically
043   larger and more cost-effective CPU memory (DDR SDRAM) compared to the more expensive and
044   less abundant GPU memory (HBM). By offloading these inactive tensors of the neural network,
045   CPU offloading effectively alleviates the memory and computational pressures on GPUs. While
046   CPU offloading has been commonly applied in inference to manage memory-intensive tasks like
047   KV cache offloading (Ge et al., 2023; Sheng et al., 2023) and Mixture of Experts (MoE) offloading
048   (Eliseev & Mazur, 2023; Xue et al., 2024), its application in training, especially fine-tuning, remains
049   less explored.
050   Recently, some works (Rajbhandari et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2021) have tried to introduce CPU of-
051   floading into LLM training. However, they are typically constrained by the capabilities of first-order
052   optimizers such as SGD and Adaptive Moment Estimation (AdamW) (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017),
053   and limited GPU memory, restricting large-scale model scalability on single GPU systems. In de-
      tail, using first-order optimizers introduces two major inefficiencies in CPU offloading: (1) Multiple

      2   R ELATED W ORK
130
131   Zeroth-Order (ZO) Optimization. ZO optimization offers a gradient-free alternative to first-order
132   (FO) optimization by approximating gradients through function value-based estimates. These es-
133   timates theoretically require only two forward passes but are believed to be prohibitively slow for
134   optimizing large models. Despite this limitation, ZO methods have been utilized in deep learning
135   to generate adversarial examples or adjust input embeddings (Sun et al., 2022a;b), though they have
136
      not been widely adopted for direct optimization of large-scale models (Liu et al., 2020). Several
      acceleration techniques have been proposed to address the scaling challenges of ZO optimization
137
      and some of them have been used for LLM fine-tuning. These include using historical data to im-
138
      prove gradient estimators (Cheng et al., 2021), exploiting gradient structures (Singhal et al., 2023) or
139   sparsity to reduce the dependence of ZO methods on the size of the problem (Chen et al., 2024; Cai
140   et al., 2022; 2021), and reusing intermediate features (Chen et al., 2024) and random perturbation
141   vectors (Malladi et al., 2023) during the optimization process. These advancements suggest that ZO
142   optimization could increasingly be applied to more complex and large-scale ML problems. While
143   previous ZO optimization efforts have primarily targeted algorithmic improvements for GPU mem-
144   ory efficiency, our approach extends these optimizations to the system level, enabling more robust
145   memory management and enhanced performance for large-scale machine learning applications.
146   CPU Offloading for LLMs. With recent advancements in LLMs, several approaches have emerged
147   to offload data to CPU memory, mitigating GPU memory limitations. One such method is vLLM
148   (Kwon et al., 2023), which utilizes PagedAttention to dynamically manage the key-value (KV) cache
149   at a granular block level. Portions of the KV cache can be temporarily swapped out of GPU memory
150   to accommodate new requests. Llama.cpp (Gerganov, 2023) addresses oversized LLMs by using
151   static layer partitioning. It stores certain contiguous layers in CPU memory while keeping others
152   in GPU memory. During computation, the CPU handles the layers in its memory, followed by
153
      the GPU computing its assigned layers. FlexGen (Sheng et al., 2023), a GPU-centric inter-layer
      pipeline method, seeks to improve throughput by pinning some model weights in GPU memory
154
      for each layer. During computation, it overlaps GPU processing of the current layer with data
155
      loading for the next. DeepSpeed (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) introduces a technique to offload the
156   first-order optimizer state to the CPU, significantly reducing GPU memory requirements during
157   training. Zero-offload (Ren et al., 2021) extends the DeepSpeed approach by not only offloading
158   data to the CPU but also engaging the CPU in computational tasks. Despite these advancements,
159   the predominant focus of previous research has been on optimizing LLM inference or first-order
160   optimization through strategic CPU-GPU data transfers. Our work, in contrast, introduces a novel
161   approach by implementing CPU offloading specifically for zeroth-order optimization and fine-tuning
      of LLMs.

186

      Central to our ZO-Offloading framework is the strategic utilization of CPU and GPU resources
248
      (Section 5.1). This approach involves dynamically offloading model parameters to the CPU and
249   uploading them back to the GPU as needed for computation. Specifically, for the transformer model
250   structure, each transformer block is individually uploaded for processing and subsequently offloaded
251   post-computation, thus balancing communication and computation across blocks. As illustrated in
252   Figure 2, while the i-th transformer block is being computed, the i + 1-th block is pre-uploaded, and
253   the i 1-th block is offloaded simultaneously. This strategic overlapping ensures continuous and
254   efficient computation, reducing idle times and maximizing GPU utilization. In the uploading phase
255   of ZO-Offloading, transformer blocks are transferred into a reusable memory space on the GPU,
256   eliminating the extra time typically required for CUDA memory allocation (Section 5.2). Moreover,
257
      parameter updates are ingeniously fused with the dual forward passes to minimize redundant data
      transfers, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency of the model training process (Section 5.3).
258
259   Our ZO-Offloading framework further integrates a novel low-bit precision technique that efficiently
260   manages data transfers between the CPU and GPU in the AMP mode (see Figure 3 for an illustra-
261   tion). This technique is aligned with AMP protocols by ensuring that high-bit precision is main-
262   tained for parameter updates, while low-bit precision data is used for computation on the GPU
263
      (Section 5.4). This dual-precision approach significantly reduces the communication overhead, op-
      timizing memory usage without compromising computational accuracy. The adoption of low-bit
264
      compression during both the upload and offload phases further minimizes the data transfer volume,
265
      streamlining the training process and allowing for the efficient handling of large-scale models on
266   constrained hardware setups.

      5.1 DYNAMIC S CHEDULER D ESIGN FOR E FFICIENT OVERLAP
293
      To overlap the data loading and computation process, we propose a dynamic scheduler, utilizing
294
      the asynchronous execution on different CUDA streams. Specifically, our scheduler includes three
295
      CUDA streams (Figure 2), which are utilized to control the i-th transformer block’s computation,
296   the i + 1-th block’s uploading, and the i 1-th block’s offloading can occur concurrently. This
297   design minimizes data transfer conflicts and maximizes GPU utilization by keeping computational
298   and communication channels active.
299
      However, designing this dynamic scheduler presents challenges when communication tasks outlast
300
      computation tasks, leading to potential errors. For example, if the upload of the i-th block is incom-
301
      plete when its computation begins, this can lead to errors, as the GPU computes with an incomplete
302   set of parameters. Similarly, if the computation of the i-th block is still ongoing when its offloading
303   begins, it can also result in errors because the computation is disrupted by the removal of necessary
304   data. To address this, our scheduler implements a locking mechanism for each block’s computation
305   task, ensuring it only starts once its corresponding upload is confirmed complete. While this solution
306   mitigates the issue of incomplete parameters, it can still potentially create bottlenecks if communica-
307   tion tasks consistently outlast computation tasks. Surprisingly, our evaluations show that with ZO’s
308   unique dual forward passes, which extend computation times, communication delays are no longer
309   the primary bottleneck in most scenarios.
310   Moreover, special attention needs to be given to the embedding parameters and the LM head, as
311   they represent the beginning and end of the model, respectively. By consistently maintaining both
312   the embedding and LM head on the GPU, we circumvent the overhead linked to frequent transfers.
313   For the embedding layer, simultaneous uploading of input data and embedding parameters could
314   compete for interconnection bandwidth. Moreover, keeping the embedding layer on the GPU en-
315   ables the pre-uploading of the first transformer block, effectively overlapping with the computations
316
      of the embedding layer. Meanwhile, continuously keeping the LM head on the GPU removes delays
      associated with its offloading—since no subsequent block computations overlap with this offload-
317
      ing—and facilitates weight sharing with the embedding layer, as noted in some conditions (Radford
318
      et al., 2019), thus consolidating related computations and enhancing operational efficiency. The
319   detailed scheduler design to apply ZO-Offloading on LLMs is shown in Algorithm 1.

334   5.3 E FFICIENT PARAMETER U PDATE S TRATEGY
335   In the ZO-Offloading framework, the parameter update strategy is meticulously designed to precede
336   the dual forward computations of each transformer block. Traditionally, each transformer block is
337   subjected to two distinct data transfer phases (Figure 7a): one for the dual forward computations and
338   another for applying gradient updates. This requirement stems from the fact that the (approximated)
339
      gradients are obtained only after completing the dual forward computations for the entire model.
      Consequently, parameters must be uploaded for the computation phase, offloaded upon comple-
340
      tion, and then re-uploaded and offloaded again for the gradient update phase. This iterative process
341
      effectively doubles the communication load and extends the duration of training.
342
343   By implementing preemptive parameter updates, the framework significantly curtails the number of
344   data transfers required per iteration (Figure 7b). With this strategy, once blocks are updated with the
345
      last iteration’s gradients, only a single upload and offload cycle is necessary for each block. This
      adjustment not only halves the usage of interconnection bandwidth but also enhances the efficiency
346
      of the training process, thereby streamlining operations and reducing overhead.

348   5.4 ZO-O FFLOADING IN AMP M ODE
349   Figure 3 illustrates the workflow of the ZO-Offloading framework under AMP mode, which employs
350   reduced precision formats to accelerate the training of LLMs. AMP leverages formats such as Tensor
351   Float Point 32 (TF32), which provides higher computational throughput compared to Float Point 32
352   (FP32). This acceleration is critical for enhancing training efficiency but introduces challenges in
353   maintaining effective computation-communication overlap, as the data transfer still utilizes the FP32
354   format.
355   To address this, the ZO-Offloading framework incorporates a compression mechanism where pa-
356   rameters are compressed to low-bit formats during offloading from GPU to CPU. This compression
357   significantly reduces the data volume, enabling quicker transfers and mitigating bandwidth limita-
358   tions. The current compression settings include bfloat16 and float16, which reduce the data size by
359   50%, and more aggressive reductions like float8, which compress to 25% of the original size.
360   Upon uploading these compressed parameters back to the GPU, they are decompressed and restored
361   to FP32 for high-precision parameter updates. Subsequent computations, particularly the dual for-
362   ward passes, are then performed using the TF32 format to exploit the computational speed.

      tasks concurrently: saving p1 from CPU to disk, creating a self-copy of p1 , and managing the UCO
396
      cycle of p2 . Stage 2: As the model progresses to the j + 1 training iteration, the scheduler waits
397   for the completion of p1 ’s self-copy to ensure data integrity, then asynchronously initiates the UCO
398   cycle for p1 , while simultaneously saving p2 to disk and creating a self-copy of p2 . Stage 3: The
399   scheduler waits for p2 ’s self-copy to complete before launching its UCO cycle.
400
      The inclusion of self-copying stages is designed to safeguard against potential delays in saving to
401
      disk. Self-copying is not only faster than transferring data from the CPU to the disk but also quicker
402
      than the UCO cycles of p1 or p2 . By the end of the j + 1-th iteration, the entire model is copied
403   on the CPU, ready for immediate use in the j + 2-th iteration’s UCO process without the need
404   for re-uploading from the disk. However, it is notable that although it increases throughput, this
405   asynchronous checkpointing method introduces a trade-off by increasing CPU memory usage.

530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we presented ZO-Offloading, an efficient framework that enables the training of ex-
532
      tremely large language models, such as the OPT-175B, on a single 24GB GPU—a capability pre-
533
      viously unattainable with traditional methods. By effectively integrating CPU offloading, high-
534   performance dynamic scheduler, efficient memory management, efficient parameter updating, AMP
535   support, and asynchronous checkpointing, our framework reduces GPU memory demands while
536   maintaining high throughput without additional time costs. These innovations not only lower the
537   bar for teams with limited hardware resources and advance the democratization of large models,
538   but also open new avenues for advancing AI technology more efficiently. Moving forward, we plan
539   to further enhance ZO-Offloading, exploring synergies with emerging hardware and optimization
      techniques to keep pace with the evolving demands of AI model training.
