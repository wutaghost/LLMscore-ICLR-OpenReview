009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A primary challenge in large language model (LLM) development is their onerous
012            pre-training cost. Typically, such pre-training involves optimizing a self-supervised
013            objective (such as next-token prediction) over a large corpus. This paper explores a
014            promising paradigm to improve LLM pre-training efficiency and quality by suitably
015            leveraging a small language model (SLM). In particular, this paradigm relies on
016            an SLM to both (1) provide soft labels as additional training supervision, and (2)
017
               select a small subset of valuable (“informative” and “hard”) training examples.
               Put together, this enables an effective transfer of the SLM’s predictive distribution
018
               to the LLM, while prioritizing specific regions of the training data distribution.
019
               Empirically, this leads to reduced LLM training time compared to standard train-
020            ing, while improving the overall quality. Theoretically, we develop a statistical
021            framework to systematically study the utility of SLMs in enabling efficient training
022            of high-quality LLMs. In particular, our framework characterizes how the SLM’s
023            seemingly low-quality supervision can enhance the training of a much more capa-
024            ble LLM. Furthermore, it also highlights the need for an adaptive utilization of
025            such supervision, by striking a balance between the bias and variance introduced
026            by the SLM-provided soft labels. We corroborate our theoretical framework by
027            improving the pre-training of an LLM with 2.8B parameters by utilizing a smaller
028
               LM with 1.5B parameters on the Pile dataset.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
      Owing to the recent surge in their ever-growing capabilities, large language models (LLMs) (Chowdh-
032
      ery et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Gemini-Team
033
      et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024), have become the focal point of machine learning research. Several
034   research efforts focus on either further enhancing LLM performance, or utilizing LLMs in novel
035   applications ranging from conversational agents/assistants (Thoppilan et al., 2022) to novel material
036   design (Rubungo et al., 2023). Highly capable general-purpose LLMs rely on two critical ingre-
037   dients: choosing a model architecture with a very large number of parameters (Chowdhery et al.,
038   2022; Smith et al., 2022), and pre-training this model on a corpus with a very large number of exam-
039   ples (AI@Meta, 2024; Computer, 2023). Due to the large size of model and corpus, the computational
040   cost of pre-training can be highly onerous. Thus, sustainable advancement and widespread adoption
041   of LLMs hinges on designing novel architectures and algorithms that can reduce the overall training
042
      (particularly, pre-training) compute cost and improve the data efficiency for LLM development.
043   This paper focuses on leveraging small language models (SLMs) for efficient LLM pre-training.
044   Interestingly, a growing literature (see, e.g., Gupta et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024)
045   shows that, despite their limited model capacity, SLMs can acquire a good domain understanding
046   of pre-training data distribution. Particularly, SLMs can perform well on a large portion of “easy”
047   instances, while still providing valuable information towards identifying the remaining “hard” in-
048
      stances, e.g., via the confidence, margin, or similar measures based on their predictive distribution.
      This prompts us to explore the following question:
049
050   Can we speed up pre-training of a high-quality large LM by transferring the predictive distribution
051                          resulting from pre-training of a lower-quality small LM?
052   Note that suitable SLMs are often readily available during LLM development as previous-generation
053   models trained on similar pre-training corpora or smaller models trained for initial exploration around
      architectural and algorithmic choices on the current pre-training corpora itself. Furthermore, if proven,

055   development cost, strongly justifies training such models even to solely aid LLM training.

058

059

060

      As alluded in the introduction, we hope to leverage KD with an SLM as the teacher to speed-up the
      pre-training of a high quality LLM. However, due to SLM’s relatively limited capacity and inferior
143
      quality, it is not immediately clear that such a teacher can benefit the LLM. Motivated by this, we
144
      now develop a rigorous statistical framework for KD in the context of language modeling by building
145   on the works of Menon et al. (2021); Dao et al. (2021a); Ren et al. (2022a). Novel risk bounds
146   originating from this framework highlight how a teacher LM – even a perceivably weaker one – can
147   benefit student LLM by striking the right balance in terms of a bias-variance trade-off.
148

149
      both number of training sequences N as well as number of total tokens N T , with the latter being
150
      highly non-trivial due to possibly arbitrary dependence within a training sequence. In this work, we

153   risk bounds as functions of N and N T in Sections 3.1 and 3.2, respectively. Sec. 3.3 utilizes our
154   bounds to justify the utility of SLMs for improving LLM model quality via KD.

      4     SALT : S MALL MODEL AIDED LARGE MODEL TRAINING
266
267   We now operationalize the key takeaway from Sec. 3 by proposing SALT – a simple yet effective
268   two-stage pre-training method. SALT relies on the inherent preference of a model to first focus on
269   easier supervision before fitting more complex supervision during training (Kalimeris et al., 2019;
      Refinetti et al., 2023) to perform selective KD from a teacher SLM. We then expand SALT to perform


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      explicit selection of training sequences where we want to conduct KD from an SLM on. In particular,
271   we identify challenging sequences which are learnable as per teacher SLM; as a result, performing
272   KD on those sequences result in further variance reduction (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018).
273
274
      Two-stage LLM pre-training via SALT. Inspired by our analysis, we propose a two-stage pre-
      training methods for LLMs in Algorithm 1. The algorithm employs KD with SLM as a teacher in
275
      the first stage which lasts till nKD training steps, and transitions to standard pre-training without KD
276
      in the second stage. Note that we are interested in the selective transfer of predictive distribution
277   from teacher SLM to student LLM in those regions where SLM performs well by capturing true
278   distribution. By design, KD aims to align predictive distributions of the teacher and student. On the
279   regions where SLM performs well, we expect it to exhibit reasonably confident predictive distribution
280   that should align with ground truth next-token (Gupta et al., 2024), thereby constituting an easier
281   supervision signal for the LLM. In contrast, on hard instances where SLM’s predictive distribution
282   is not confident enough or does not align well with the ground truth next-token, learning will be
283   delayed to the later phase of the training (Kalimeris et al., 2019; Refinetti et al., 2023). Thus,
284   SALT relies on the tendency of neural networks to focus on easier instances early during the training
285
      to perform desirable knowledge transfer from SLM in the first stage. Once the student LLM is
      sufficiently aligned with teacher SLM on easier regions, it starts utilizing its model capacity to further
286
      align with SLM on more complex regions where high divergence between SLM and ground truth
287
      distribution can become detrimental to the LLM’s performance. Switching to standard pre-training
288   based solely on the self-supervision from next-tokens in the second stage prevents this undesirable
289   over-alignment. We empirically verify the above intuition behind SALT in Sec. 5.4.
290

      6     R ELATED WORK
471
472
      Here, we provide a brief account of related work, focusing on the prior work on assisting large model
473
      training via small models, data selection, and theoretical treatment of KD. Due to the page limit, we
474   defer the discussion on various KD methods for language modeling that are not pertinent to the main
475   objective of this work to Appendix A.
476
477
      Aiding large model training with small models. Small models often help identify good hyper-
      parameters that can be utilized for large model training with minimal modifications (Yang et al.,
478
      2021). Progressive or stage-wise training methods (Gong et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2020; Reddi et al.,
479
      2023; Yao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) train a large model in stages where the parameters for the model
480   at a given stage get initialized based on the parameters of a smaller model from the previous stage.
481   Another related line of work simply informs the large model initialization based on a smaller model
482   without resorting to progressive stage-wise training (Trockman & Kolter, 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
483   Samragh et al., 2024). Most of these works crucially depend on the architectural overlaps between
484   the small and large models, e.g., requiring them to share same depth, width, or more generally same
485   model family. In contrast, one can distill from a smaller model to a larger model without such
      constraints. Furlanello et al. (2018) study self-distillation to iteratively train a model, with the final


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      model from an iteration acting as a teacher for the next iteration. More closer to the setting studied
487   in this work, albeit in image classification setting, Yuan et al. (2020); Xie et al. (2020) consider
488   distillation from a weaker model. In the work that is closest to our proposal, Qin et al. (2022) distill
489   an LM from a smaller LM. Moreover, they also distill during the early phase of larger student LM
490   pre-training to avoid negative impact on large LM’s final performance. We demonstrate the utility
491   of such an approach with larger LMs as well as larger pre-training corpus, with a wider range of
492   evaluation benchmarks. Furthermore, we provide a statistical framework to rigorously justifies the
493   utility of a seemingly weaker teacher during LLM pre-training. Other recent efforts on using smaller
494   LMs to boost larger LMs have primarily focused on fine-tuning (Yang et al., 2024; Mitchell et al.,
495
      2024) or alignment (Burns et al., 2023), while we focus on pre-training which is the most compute
      and data intensive phase of LLM development.
496
497   Data Selection. Ankner et al. (2024) select examples with reference model sequence-level log-
498   perplexities in a specified range. They aggregate per-token log-perplexities by taking the mean,
499   whereas, we take the median and also zero out noisy tokens. Mindermann et al. (2022) select
500   examples and Lin et al. (2024) select tokens for training based on excess training loss over a reference
501
      model. While we perform offline data selection before training, these two works select data from
      training batches on the fly. Methods which encourage diversity through semantic embeddings (e.g.,
502
      Abbas et al. (2023); Tirumala et al. (2024)) can yield a better final model when employed in SALTDS .
503
      Please refer to Albalak et al. (2024) for a comprehensive survey of data selection techniques for LMs.
504
505   Theoretical understanding of knowledge distillation. Focusing on (deep) linear networks, Phuong
506   & Lampert (2019) study the generalization bounds for KD and relate its success to various factors
507
      including data geometry and optimization bias. Menon et al. (2021); Ren et al. (2022b) show that
      KD leads to reduced variance of the training objective, thereby improving generalization, while also
508
      relating the effectiveness of KD to teacher’s ability to approximate Bayes class probabilities. Cotter
509
      et al. (2021) argue that KD can improve generalization as long as teacher approximates a suitable
510   transformation of Bayes class probabilities. Dao et al. (2021b) study KD from the perspective of
511   semiparameteric inference to analyze the excess risk of distilled student. Focusing on self-distillation
512   for kernel ridge regression, Mobahi et al. (2020) show that distillation can enhance regularization.
513   Allen-Zhu & Li (2023) explain the utility of distillation via better feature learning. Xu et al. (2020)
514   study the interplay between optimization and label smoothing via teacher-provided soft labels.
515   Focusing on linearized neural networks, Harutyunyan et al. (2023) attribute the success of KD to
516   the reduced supervision complexity. More recently, Safaryan et al. (2023) argue that KD can act as
517   a form of partial variance reduction, thereby improving convergence. We would like to highlight
518
      that the existing literature does not provide generalization bounds for KD in a sequence learning
      setting such as language modeling, and we provide the first statistical treatment of KD for language
519
      modeling. Notably, similar to our work, Xu et al. (2020); Nagarajan et al. (2023) also explore the
520
      utility of KD only during an early-phase of student training, albeit not in a language modeling setting.
521

      7    C ONCLUSION
523
524   We conduct a systematic study of the utility of SLMs to improve both training efficiency and
525
      performance of LLM pre-training. Towards this, we introduce a novel statistical framework for KD in
      the context of language modeling, which guides the design of SALT – a simple KD-based approach
526
      that selectively transfers predictive distribution from an SLM to an LLM. We further enhance SALT
527
      and perform explicit data selection via SLMs to effectively transfer knowledge from SLMs to LLMs.
528   SALT significantly reduces the pre-training time for LLMs while ensuring good overall quality as
529   measured by the LLM’s few-shot performance as well as downstream performance after fine-tuning.
530
531
      While SALT can play a crucial role in efficiently sustaining the trend of developing LLMs with
      increasing capabilities, it also has the potential to help institutions train proficient lightweight LMs.
532
      Conventionally, one distills a large powerful model into a lightweight model with good quality.
533
      However, given that many LLMs are proprietary, such strong-to-weak distillation is not feasible at
534   many institutions. Our proposed approach can leverage even smaller LMs to enhance the quality of
535   a small LM with acceptable inference cost. Exploring if our proposed approach can introduce new
536   capabilities in a general-purpose small LM with the help of one or multiple smaller LMs that are
537   experts in their respective domains is an interesting avenue for future work. Interestingly, our data
538   selection approach in SALTDS demonstrates that it is indeed possible to leverage data selection to
539   improve KD for language modeling. Building on this initial investigation and further exploring and
      extending data selection approaches tailored to SALTDS is another interesting line of future work.

1077   A.3    R ECENT LITERATURE ON KNOWLEDGE DISTILLATION (KD) FOR LANGUAGE MODELING
1078
1079   A large body of literature focuses on utilizing KD (Bucilǎ et al., 2006; Hinton et al., 2015) as a core
       technique to improve LMs (Kim & Rush, 2016; Gou et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2024). For instance, Sanh


                                                             20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       et al. (2019); Turc et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2019); Jiao et al. (2019) relied on KD
1081   to compress BERT-style LMs during pre-training, fine-tuning, or both. More recently, KD has been
1082   primarily employed in the instruction-tuning or fine-tuning phase where a general purpose LM is
1083   adapted to a specific collection of tasks (Xu et al., 2024). Black-box KD methods for LM only assume
1084   access to training sequences sampled from a teacher LM (Taori et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Peng
1085   et al., 2023). With access to token-level distributions from teacher LM, token-level distillation from
1086   teacher LM to student LM is possible (Kim & Rush, 2016). In contrast, sequence-level distillation
1087   involves sampling training sequences from the teacher LM, the student LM, or both before aligning
1088   teacher and student’s predictive distribution on such sequences (Kim & Rush, 2016; Agarwal et al.,
1089
       2024; Gu et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2023).

       Model architectures and pre-training data. We work with standard decoder-only Transformer-
1472
       based LMs. Our small model (SLM) has 1.5B parameters. It comprises a 44 layer Transformer
1473
       network with model dimension 1024, MLP hidden dimension 8192, and 4 attention heads. For the
1474   larger LM, we employ 2.8B parameter models consisting of 92 layer Transformer networks with
1475   model dimension 1024, MLP hidden dimension 8192, and 4 attention heads based on multi-query
1476   attention (Shazeer, 2019). We use a SentencePiece tokenizer (Kudo & Richardson, 2018) from Du
1477   et al. (2022) with a vocabulary size of 256K. We employ weight tying (Press & Wolf, 2017), i.e., the
1478   same vocabulary embedding parameters are used for input token embedding and output embedding
1479   layers.
1480   We pre-train all LMs on the Pile dataset (Gao et al., 2020) by minimizing the UL2 objective (Tay
1481   et al., 2023) with a mixture of four tasks: (1) causal LM task; (2) prefix LM task with mean prefix
1482   length of 1/4th the sequence length, (3) span corruption task with r = 15% of the tokens corrupted
1483   and mean corrupted span length µ = 3; and (4) span corruption task with r = 50% of the tokens
1484   corrupted and mean corrupted span length µ = 32. The four tasks are mixed at a ratio of 6:2:1:1. We
1485   pre-train LMs for approximately 545 billion tokens, with a batch size of 2048 and input sequence
1486   length of 1280. This translates to a little over two epochs on the Pile data. As for the optimization
1487   method, we utilize Adafactor algorithm (Shazeer & Stern, 2018). We use a cosine learning rate decay
1488
       schedule with a peak learning rate of 0.001, 4000 warmup steps and final learning rate of 0.0001.
       Training is done on 1024 TPU-v5e chips with JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018) and SeqIO (Roberts et al.,
1489
       2022).

       H     A BLATION STUDY OF VARIOUS DESIGN CHOICES IN SALT
1621
1622
       In this section, we explore how various design choices pertaining SALT affect its final performance.
1623
1624   Distillation from a better quality small model. So far we assumed that SLM is also pre-trained
1625   for the same number of tokens as the LLM. Since training for SLM is relatively cheaper, one could
1626
       consider a scenario where one invests more compute resources in improving the small model if it can
       eventually be beneficial in improving the LLM quality via SALT. Towards this, we employ a small
1627
       LM that is trained ∼ 2.5 times longer – 498K steps vs. 208K steps in Section 5.2.4 As evident in
1628
       Table 6, SALT is indeed able to utilize the better small model as a teacher in the KD phase to further
1629   improve the LLM quality, as measured by the average few-shot performance.
1630
1631   Varying transition point. A key design choice for SALT is the selection of the transition point nKD
1632
       from KD phase (first stage) to standard training (second stage). Table 7 shows few-shot performance
       of SALT as we vary the transition point. Note that SALT ensures quality gains for LLM with a
1633
       wide range of values for nKD while demonstrating an inverted U-shape for LLM quality. We see
1634
       consistent performance improvement from nKD = 0 (equivalent to BASELINE) to nKD = 60K which
1635   eventually degrades at nKD = 208K (equivalent to RKD). Given the training overhead of KD phase
1636   (see discussion in Section 5.2), smaller value of nKD helps ensure training efficiency gains via SALT.
1637   Thus, we worked with nKD = 36K in Section 5.2 as nKD = 60K only provides marginal quality
1638   gains if one takes into account the increased training cost due to longer KD phase.
1639
       Different transition strategies. In our study thus far, we have worked with Step transition between
1640
       the two training stages in SALT where we abruptly stop performing KD after nKD training steps.
1641   Looking at Figure 2, this causes an abrupt change in the model behavior during training, as observed
1642   in the next-token prediction accuracy curve for the training set (see similar behavior for log-perplexity
1643   in Figure 3). This raises a question if a smoother transition between the two stages can improve
1644   the training stability and thereby ensure higher final LLM quality. While there is a large space of
1645   potential choices of such smooth transition strategies, here we explore two natural candidates: (1)
1646   Linear decay where we linearly decrease the distillation loss weight to 0 between nKD,1 = 32K and
1647   nKD = 36K steps; and (2) Linear ratio decay where we linearly decrease the ratio of distillation
                                                ω
1648   loss weight and standard loss weight 1−ω     to 0 between nKD,1 = 32K and nKD,2 = 36K training
1649   steps. As recorded in Table 8, the step transition constitutes a reasonable design choice for SALT
1650
       as it outperforms both the considered alternatives in terms of average few-shot performance of the
       resulting pre-trained LLM.
