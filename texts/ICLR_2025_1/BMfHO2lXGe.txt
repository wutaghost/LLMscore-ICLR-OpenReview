009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Protein design has important implications for drug discovery, personalized
012            medicine, and biotechnology. Models based on multiple sequence alignments
013            efficiently capture the evolutionary information in homologous protein sequences,
014            but multiple sequence alignment construction is imperfect. We present ProtMamba,
015            a homology-aware but alignment-free protein language model based on the Mamba
016            architecture. In contrast with attention-based models, ProtMamba efficiently han-
017            dles very long context, comprising hundreds of protein sequences. We train
018            ProtMamba on a large dataset of concatenated homologous sequences, using two
019
               GPUs. We combine autoregressive modeling and masked language modeling
               through a fill-in-the-middle training objective. This makes the model adapted to
020
               various protein design applications. We demonstrate ProtMamba’s usefulness for
021
               the generation of novel sequences and for fitness prediction. ProtMamba reaches
022            competitive performance with other protein language models despite its smaller
023            size, which sheds light on the importance of long-context conditioning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Proteins are essential building blocks of life, serving vital roles in metabolic processes, cellular
029
      transport, structural integrity, and immune responses. Composed of long chains of amino acids
030
      (polypeptides), proteins fold into specific three-dimensional structures critical for their biological
031   functions. One of the key challenges in biology is protein engineering and design: conceiving protein
032   sequences to exhibit enhanced or novel functions. While experimental approaches like directed
033   evolution and mutational scanning are effective in this regard, they only allow exploring the neighbors
034   of existing sequences. However, the recent growth of extensive databases has opened up new avenues
035   for computational methods that exploit the breadth of biological evolution. For instance, UniProt
036   (The UniProt Consortium, 2021) contains more than two hundreds of millions of protein sequences.
037   Biological functions exert evolutionary constraints on protein sequences, which can be probed by
038   considering families of homologous proteins (i.e. proteins that share an evolutionary history) and
039
      analyzing this data through statistical methods and, more recently, through deep learning methods.
040   Protein language models rely on recurrent (Bepler & Berger, 2019), transformer (Rives et al., 2021)
041   or convolutional (Yang et al., 2024) architectures, and are trained through masked language modeling,
042   autoregressive modeling, or discrete diffusion techniques (Alamdari et al., 2023), on large ensembles
043   of single protein sequences (Khakzad et al., 2023). The representations learned by these models
044   correlate with biochemical properties of proteins (such as function, structure, contacts) (Elnaggar
045
      et al., 2021; Vig et al., 2021; Rives et al., 2021; Madani et al., 2023), and can be used to generate
      protein sequences or to evaluate the fitness of variants. The vast majority of these methods are
046
      trained on non-structured ensembles of single protein sequence and do not have direct access to
047
      homology, or to conservation and variability within protein families. Models trained on multiple
048   sequence alignments (MSAs) of homologous sequences have also been introduced, despite raising
049   memory challenges and potentially suffering from the imperfections of MSAs (Thompson et al.,
050   2011). Successful MSA-based transformer models, such as MSA Transformer (Rao et al., 2021)
051   or the EvoFormer module of AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., 2021) alternate attention along protein
052   sequences and across homologs. More recently, PoET (Truong Jr & Bepler, 2024) was trained on
053   concatenations of non-aligned homologous sequences, offering a promising autoregressive alternative
      to MSA Transformer for protein fitness prediction and design.

054
      State space models such as S4 (Gu et al., 2021), Hyena (Poli et al., 2023) and Mamba (Gu &
055   Dao, 2023) are catching up with transformers thanks to their ability to efficiently handle very long
056   sequences of tokens. These models were quickly adapted to work with biological data. Approaches
057   such as HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2023) or Evo (Nguyen et al., 2024) were trained on long DNA
058   sequences and capture regulatory mechanics. Meanwhile, PTM-Mamba addresses post-translational
059   modifications of protein sequences (Peng et al., 2024).
060
      In this paper, we present ProtMamba, a novel homology-aware but alignment-free protein language
061   model, trained on concatenated sequences of homologous proteins. Based on the Mamba architecture
062   (Gu & Dao, 2023), ProtMamba is able to handle extremely long contexts (unlimited lengths during
063   inference). Trained to autoregressively predict the next amino acid, but also with a fill-in-the-middle
064   (FIM) objective, it can be used for multiple different tasks. First, ProtMamba can autoregressively
065   generate novel sequences without contextual information. Second, by providing ProtMamba with
066   sequences from a specific protein family or subfamily as context, users can prompt it to generate
067   sequences tailored to their specifications. This conditional generation approach is a key strength
068   of the model (see also Truong Jr & Bepler (2024)), and could become an alternative to fine-tuning.
069
      Third, ProtMamba supports sequence inpainting, i.e., filling specific masked regions with the desired
      number of amino acids. For this, along with homologous sequences (used as context), the model
070
      is provided with a target sequence to be modified. This generation mode opens novel methods of
071
      designing specific parts of protein sequences. Furthermore, ProtMamba is useful for fitness prediction
072   tasks. Users can input a sequence with specific masked positions, prompting the model to output
073   the probability distribution of all mutations in each variant with a single forward pass. Across these
074   various tasks, we obtain competitive results with larger protein language models and task-specific
075   methods.

080   2.1   K EY TECHNICAL CONTRIBUTIONS
081
082         1. To harness the evolutionary information present in homologous sequences without relying
083            on multiple sequence alignments (MSAs), we use as input a concatenation of homologous
084            sequences for each protein family. In each of these long arrays, sequences are separated
085            with a specific token. The motivation is that evolutionary information is extremely useful
086            for protein modeling Jumper et al. (2021); Rao et al. (2021); Abramson et al. (2024), but
087            MSAs can be inaccurate. This approach is similar to that used recently in the autoregressive
088
               transformer PoET (Truong Jr & Bepler, 2024).
            2. We develop an architecture based on Mamba blocks, an alternative to attention recently
089
               proposed by (Gu & Dao, 2023) that relies on state space models. In Mamba, which is a
090
               recurrent neural network, time complexity scales linearly in sequence length, bypassing the
091            quadratic time complexity constraints of transformers. This allows handling significantly
092            longer input sequences, in addition to being faster to train and to use at inference. This is
093            a key asset here, as concatenating homologous sequences results in long inputs. Note that
094            Truong Jr & Bepler (2024) employed attention matrix chunking to address this issue, but
095            this results in potential losses of statistical dependence signals, and only partially solves the
096            memory limit.
097         3. We combine elements of both autoregressive modeling and masked language modeling
098            (MLM), by training our model using the fill-in-the-middle (FIM) objective (Bavarian et al.,
099
               2022; Fried et al., 2022; Raffel et al., 2020). The model learns to predict masked patches
               extracted randomly from a sequence and positioned at the end of it, and can therefore leverage
100
               the full sequence context, while being trained autoregressively. This is of particular interest
101
               for biological sequences, because preceding and subsequent tokens can all be informative to
102            predict a new token. While autoregressive models are generative by definition, they yield
103            the probability of each new token conditioned on previous ones (ignoring subsequent ones).
104            Besides, MLM can be productively used for protein sequence generation (Sgarbossa et al.,
105            2023).
106         4. To promote the model’s ability to reason over in-sequence positions, which is particularly
107            useful for the FIM task, we modify the original Mamba implementation by introducing
               sequence-level positional embeddings. This enables the model to pay attention to relative

               positions inside each sequence. In inference and generation, it opens the possibility of
109            controlling the number of amino-acids to generate.

      2.2   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE AND TRAINING STRATEGY
113
      ProtMamba’s architecture is adapted from Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). An important modification
114   is that we introduce learned positional embeddings for the input tokens. Among different variants
115   (see supplementary section C), we observed that the most effective and stable method to integrate
116   positional embeddings is to concatenate them with the input token embeddings into a single vector.
117   Specifically, we allocated half of the embedding dimension d to token information and the other half
118   to positional information.
119
      We trained a 107 million parameters model with 16 layers, embedding dimension d = 1024, and
120   hidden state dimension equal to embedding dimension. We started with a maximal total input sequence
121   length of 211 = 2048 amino acids (recall that input sequences are concatenated homologous protein
122   sequences). The model was trained following (Gu & Dao, 2023) with some minor modifications.
123   We used the AdamW optimizer with the following parameter values: weight decay w = 0.1 and
124   (β1 , β2 ) = (0.9, 0.95). We scheduled the learning rate to increase from zero to 6 × 10−4 with a linear
125   warm-up of 500 steps followed by a constant learning rate. To optimize memory usage, we trained
126   the model using the bfloat16 format.
127   To avoid training instabilities observed in (Nguyen et al., 2024; Waleffe et al., 2024), we implemented
128   a callback mechanism to revert to a previous checkpoint if the loss never assumed values below a
129   threshold for 10 successive evaluation steps. The threshold value was chosen as the lowest training
130   loss increased by 0.5%. This ensures that the loss decreases overall, while allowing it to transiently
131   increase. We also prevented gradient explosions by clipping the gradient norm to 1.0.
132   The model was trained by scheduling the context length of the input using sequence length warm up
133   (SLW) (Nguyen et al., 2023). Initially, we used inputs of length L = 211 tokens with a batch size of
134   64. We doubled input length each time the loss reached a plateau, simultaneously reducing batch size
135   to maintain a fixed total number of tokens per batch. In case of memory constraints, we decrease
136   the batch size and use gradient accumulation. This heuristic approach is based on the idea that a
137   longer context should provide more information. It is useful because of training instabilities for long
138   contexts (Nguyen et al., 2023; 2024). Note that we did not start training the model with a long context
139   to benefit from a larger batch size, which helps to approximate the loss landscape more efficiently.
140
      Finally, once we reached a context length of L = 217 , we implemented gradient checkpointing to
      minimize memory consumption. This allowed us to increase the batch size for the final part of the
141
      training and obtain a better approximation of the loss landscape, see (Nguyen et al., 2023; 2024).
142
143   The model was trained on one NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU for 35 days, and then on two of them for
144   15 days. This allowed us to keep the batch size large enough when the context size increased. In total,
145   the model was trained on 1.95 × 1011 tokens (approximately 1.5 epochs) and used 2.0 × 1020 FLOPs
146
      during training. These numbers show the huge improvements that the Mamba architecture has in
      terms of training speed with respect to transformers. As a comparison, the smallest ESM3 model
147
      (Hayes et al., 2024) was trained with 0.8 × 1011 tokens using 6.72 × 1020 FLOPs, which means that
148
      given a fixed amount of compute, ProtMamba can see 8.5 times the tokens seen by ESM3. See Figure
149   S1 for the training curves.
150
151   We consider two different ProtMamba versions that were obtained by saving checkpoints at different
152
      moments of the training. Our model ProtMamba, Foundation was trained on a maximum context
      length of 215 tokens. Our model ProtMamba Long, Foundation was trained until the context length
153
      reached 217 tokens. Both models were fine-tuned for 2 days on predicting only the FIM amino acids
154
      to improve inpainting capabilities, yielding the models ProtMamba/ProtMamba Long, Fine-tuned.
155
156   We also performed multiple ablations on the model architecture and on the different modalities by
157   training models on 10B tokens for 50k steps (see supplementary section C).

161   We trained ProtMamba on OpenProteinSet (Ahdritz et al., 2024), a dataset which comprises 16
      millions MSAs, one for each sequence cluster within Uniclust30 (Mirdita et al., 2017). This dataset

      was curated to train OpenFold (Ahdritz et al., 2022). We used a filtered subset of the full dataset, con-
163   sisting of maximally diverse representative MSA clusters, built by iteratively eliminating redundant
164   clusters whose representative sequences appeared in other clusters’ MSAs (Ahdritz et al., 2024). This
165   ensures that each representative sequence is only present in its cluster, as detailed in (Ahdritz et al.,
166   2024). This dataset comprises 268,000 clusters including a total of 508 million sequences and 110
167   billion residues (see Figure S2 for additional statistics). A validation set and a testing set are formed
168   by holding out respectively 192 and 500 randomly chosen clusters from the training set. Importantly,
169   our use of the filtered version of OpenProteinSet (Ahdritz et al., 2024) ensures that overlap between
170   clusters in the training, validation and test set is strongly minimized. Indeed, this filtering is based on
171
      selecting only MSAs of maximal diversity and ensuring that the reference sequences used to build
      each cluster are not present in any other cluster.

      3.2   P ROT M AMBA PREDICTS MUTATIONAL EFFECTS IN DIFFERENT PROTEIN FAMILIES

275   Next, we evaluate ProtMamba’s ability to predict mutational effects, leveraging its inpainting ca-
276   pabilities arising from the FIM training objective. Indeed, by masking specific amino acids in the
277   wild-type sequence of interest, we can predict the fitness of all variants at these sites. Our first step
278   to evaluate variant fitness is to collect a context of homologs to the wild-type sequence. We use the
279   ColabFold protocol (Mirdita et al., 2022) for this, ensuring that diverse sequences are found in a few
280   minutes. Then, we randomly subsample 200 sequences among those that have between 30% and
281   98% similarity to the wild type to construct the context, and we sort these sequences by increasing
282   similarity to the wild type, as in Truong Jr & Bepler (2024).
283   To evaluate the effect of a variant with a single mutated site, we append the wild-type sequence to the
284   context, mask the mutated residue in it, and predict this residue using the FIM method. Let C denote
285   the union of the context sequences and of the wild-type sequence masked at the mutated position i.
286   We evaluate the effect of mutations at position i by their fitness score F, defined as:

412   3.4   P ROT M AMBA AUTOREGRESSIVELY GENERATES PROMISING NOVEL SEQUENCES
413
414   Finally, we evaluate ProtMamba on the autoregressive generation of novel protein sequences given a
415   context of known homologs, corresponding to members of a given cluster of sequences. We generate
416   sequences from 19 randomly selected clusters in the test set, varying the following parameters:
417
      temperature (T ), top-k number, and top-p fraction, following the approach proposed by (Ferruz et al.,
      2022). These parameters are commonly employed to control the output of autoregressive models. At
418
      each step, top-k limits their output to the top-k most probable tokens, while top-p only includes the
419
      top tokens reaching a cumulative probability p. Meanwhile, temperature T adjusts the randomness
420   of sampling. Additionally, we vary the number of sequences in the context to assess the impact
421   of different levels of conditioning on the generated sequences. Specifically, for each cluster, we
422   perform generation using context lengths of n = 10, 100, 500, 1000 and N sequences, where
423   N is the total number of sequences in the cluster. For each value of n, we consider the following
424   (T, top-k, top-p) triplets: (0.8, 10, 0.9), (0.9, 10, 0.95), (1, 10, 0.95), (1, 10, 1), (1, 15, 1). We gen-
425   erate 100 sequences for each (n, T, top-k, top-p), obtaining a total of 2500 sequences per family.
426   As expected, we observed that the parameters which promote higher sampling variability tend to
427   yield sequences with higher perplexity. Note that sequences with more than 750 amino acids, i.e.
428
      longer than the longest natural sequence considered here, were discarded from further analysis. They
      represented ∼ 5% of the generated sequences.

      4    D ISCUSSION
497
498
      Here, we presented ProtMamba, a homology-aware but alignment-free generative protein language
499
      model. ProtMamba leverages the long-context capabilities of state space models, allowing it to handle
500   concatenated sequences of homologous proteins. It also benefits from their faster speed compared to
501   attention-based models (Gu & Dao, 2023), allowing fast sequence generation and mutational effect
502   prediction. ProtMamba was trained using a hybrid strategy combining autoregressive modeling and
503   masked language modeling via the FIM objective. This allows ProtMamba to efficiently predict the
504   next amino acid in a protein sequence as well as to inpaint masked regions.
505
      Our results demonstrate ProtMamba’s versatility across multiple tasks, including conditioned genera-
506   tion and protein fitness prediction, both for close and for distant variants. For homolog-conditioned
507   generation, ProtMamba outperforms the state-of-the-art model EvoDiff-MSA (Alamdari et al., 2023).
508   For fitness prediction, the sequence inpainting abilities of ProtMamba, via the FIM objective, proved
509   to be particularly useful. Indeed, this functionality allows the model to exploit the full sequence
510   context, without restricting to previous tokens as with autoregressive generation. This allows Prot-
511   Mamba to reach similar performance levels as larger models, in a fraction of the time. Overall,
512   ProtMamba benefits from capturing signal across multiple scales. In particular, it is able to predict
513   fitness by exploiting constraints shared broadly across the proteome via its pre-training, but also
514
      specific constraints shared between homologs via the context, and it can exploit the full context of a
      given protein sequence when predicting only part of it.
515
516
517   Limitations. So far, ProtMamba did not reach perplexity values as low as those of larger transformer
518   models like PoET (Truong Jr & Bepler, 2024) for full sequences. However, it can handle longer
519
      context sizes and requires much shorter training and inference times, which is extremely beneficial
      for the sequence inpainting task. We believe that scaling the model to larger sizes and training times
520
      (comparable to PoET) may result in comparable performance, while retaining ProtMamba’s assets of
521
      lower memory cost and inference time.
522
523   We did not provide a direct test of the generative ability of ProtMamba for protein sequence inpainting.
524   Indeed, this is a highly specific task lacking clear benchmarks so far. However, we believe that
525
      our two analyses on fitness prediction constitute a convincing indirect proof of the usefulness of
      ProtMamba’s inpainting ability. It would be very interesting to experimentally test ProtMamba’s
526
      inpainting ability, as well as its de novo sequence generation ability (Verkuil et al., 2022).
527
528
529   Perspectives. Our results demonstrate ProtMamba’s flexibility, as it allows for precise conditioning
530
      by carefully choosing the context information (e.g. restricting to active sequences). Thus, ProtMamba
      responds very well to prompt engineering. We propose that this could become an alternative or
531
      complement to fine-tuning of language models. ProtMamba is also naturally designed to take
532
      advantage of retrieval augmented generation (RAG) techniques (Lewis et al., 2021), as it allows for
533   using retrieved protein sequences from any external database, to condition the generation process.
534
535   Furthermore, we envision the possibility to use the model for homology search, by scoring sequences
536
      within specific contexts. This would be very fast, because only one forward pass would be required.
537   An interesting further extension of ProtMamba would be to make it explicitly structure-aware, e.g.
538   using a structural alphabet (van Kempen et al., 2023), along the lines of SaProt (Su et al., 2023) or
539   ProstT5 (Heinzinger et al., 2023). Another possible extension would be to include Gene Ontology
      (GO) terms to condition sequence generation (Madani et al., 2023; Nijkamp et al., 2023).

      B   D ETAILS ON MUTATIONAL EFFECT PREDICTION WITH P ROT M AMBA
777
778
      In Table 1, we consider different ways of computing mutational effects using ProtMamba, and
779   compare with other models. Here, we explain our different approaches in more detail.
780
781         • The entry ProtMamba (single) of Table 1 is the result reported when using the FIM
782           technique at the end of the sequence to evaluate mutations. It is the fastest method among
783           homology-aware ones. The procedure is the following:
784             1. Subsample a predetermined number of homologs of the target sequence considered in
785                the DMS to be used as context, based on a diversity filter.
786             2. Run ProtMamba on the context and collect the last hidden state of the context (as the
787                model is recurrent).
788
                3. Start from the last hidden state as initial state for every variant to score. Scoring is then
                   very fast, since we only need to apply ProtMamba on a single sequence per variant,
789
                   and not on the full context. As Mamba scales linearly in sequence length, this allow to
790
                   evaluate many different variants very fast on a single GPU (hundreds to thousands per
791                batch). The mutated residues are put at the end of the sequence in the FIM mask. For
792                single mutations, we can then evaluate in one shot the likelihood of every mutation.
793             4. We compare the likelihood of the WT to the likelihood of the variant using Fill-in-the-
794                middle to get a proxy of variant fitness. We can then evaluate the fitness of variants
795                comprising each of the 20 amino-acids at the mutated site(s) in a single shot.
796         • The entry ProtMamba AR (autoregressive) of Table 1 is the result reported when evaluating
797           the likelihood of a variant without using the FIM technique. The procedure is:
798             1. Perform steps 1 and 2 as in ProtMamba (single) above.
799             2. Start from the last hidden state as initial state for every variant to score. We then evaluate
800                 the autoregressive likelihood of the full variant. Since some logits are computed after
801                 the mutation in this setup (because they are positioned after in the sequence), we cannot
802                 evaluate the fitness of the 20 amino-acids in a single shot (in contrast to the previous
803                 approach), which increases the number of calls to ProtMamba, and hence requires
804                 more time.
805
                3. Compare the likelihood of the WT over the full sequence to the likelihood of the variant
                    to get an evaluation of variant fitness.
806
807         • The entry ProtMamba (w/ R), i.e. with retrieval of Table 1 is the result reported when
808           combining ProtMamba with a prior based on the frequency of amino-acids in the MSA of
809           the relevant protein family.
                1. Perform steps 1, 2 and 3 as in ProtMamba (single) above.


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
                2. Load the MSA, compute a log-likelihood prior (log pretrieval ) based on the frequency
811                of every amino-acid, and sum it with ProtMamba’s log-likelihood (log pProtMamba ) :
812
813                           log pProtMamba (w/R) = α log pretrieval + (1 − α) log pProtMamba .
814                The parameter α was optimized using the validation set introduced in Section A (see
815                Figure S8). This operation can be parallelized across CPUs or GPUs. We report the
816                results using 16 workers.
817
818   Note that we tested ProtMamba AR both with our model fine-tuned on FIM (ProtMamba Long
819   Finetuned) and with our foundation model (ProtMamba Long), and we obtained similar results
820   (respectively 0.361 and 0.367).
821   In Table S1, we break down results by MSA depth and by number of mutations. We observe that for
822   datasets with more than one mutation (last column in Table S1), ProtMamba with retrieval slightly
823   outperforms the overall state-of-the-art model TranceptEVE L and reaches performance close to the
824   structure-based model ESM-IF1. However, averaging over all datasets, ProtMamba does not reach
825   the same performance as TranceptEVE L. But since ProtMamba performs better than Tranception L,
826   ensembling ProtMamba and EVE predictions might yield comparable performance.

851
852   We investigated ablations or alternative implementations of ProtMamba using two models: a small
853   model with 14 million parameters (8 layers, hidden dimension 512) and the standard architecture
854   with 107 million parameters (16 layers, hidden dimension 1024). Both models were trained for 10
855   billion tokens (50k steps with a batch size of 128) and evaluated on a validation set of 500 unseen
856   clusters. During evaluation, a context of 25 sequences was used. The perplexity of the models was
857
      assessed both autoregressively (left-to-right) and in the fill-in-the-middle (FIM) spans.
858   The performance of these alternatives (described in more detail below) is summarized in Table S2.
859   Perplexity values are reported for both autoregressive and FIM modes in the small model and the
860   larger one.

879
      The alternative implementations tested against our main ProtMamba model, and whose performance
880
      is reported in Table S2, were constructed as follows:
881
882         • Only FIM from scratch: This approach backpropagates the loss exclusively from the FIM
883           tokens, disregarding the main amino-acid chain. Training this way from scratch disables
884           autoregressive (left-to-right) next-token prediction and degrades performance, including on
885           FIM tasks.
886         • Autoregressive (AR) only: Trains the model without sampling FIM spans. While this
887           slightly improves autoregressive performance, it significantly degrades FIM capabilities.
888
            • No positional encoding: Omits positional encodings entirely. In autoregressive mode, the
889
              model can partially rely on its recurrent architecture, but in FIM mode, performance suffers
890           due to the absence of positional information in input.
891
            • Additive positional encoding: Uses additive positional encoding (summing token embed-
892
              dings with positional encoding) instead of concatenated positional encoding (concatenating
893
              token embeddings with positional encoding). This approach showed mixed results, with
894           slight improvement in the small model but degradation in the larger model.
895
            • One mask, one token: Uses one mask per token (as in the T5 model (Raffel et al., 2020))
896
              instead of one mask per span of tokens (as in our approach, inspired by Bavarian et al.
897
              (2022)). This approach led to performance degradation in FIM, likely due to insufficient
898           training on larger number of mask tokens.
899
            • Masking fraction 50%: Samples 50% of the tokens for FIM (compared to ProtMamba’s
900
              20%). This alternative brought minor but noticeable improvements, suggesting potential for
901
              further development.
