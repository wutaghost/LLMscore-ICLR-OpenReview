009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               In convolutional neural networks (CNNs), activation layers process features from
012            convolutional layers, which have multiple output channels. Conventional activa-
013            tion functions like ReLU handle these multi-channel features independently, ig-
014            noring spatial and cross-channel dependencies. This hard-thresholding approach
015            can lead to information loss by eliminating negative features and disrupting the
016            connection within input features. To address this issue, we propose a novel ac-
017            tivation function that considers mutual relations across multiple channels. Our
018            activation layer processes tuples across channels as single inputs, ensuring that
019            output tuples remain in the same projection space, with their ℓ1 norms bounded
020
               by a learnable parameter. This parameter controls the pass-through ratio, which is
               the proportion of input data allowed to pass through the activation layer, offering
021
               a significant advantage over ReLU. Our approach demonstrated superior accuracy
022
               in classification tasks on common benchmarks and domain-specific datasets for
023            CNN-based models. The proposed activation layer outperformed ReLU and other
024            common layers in both clean and noisy data scenarios, as confirmed by statistical
025            tests. Our results highlight the effectiveness of this activation function in main-
026            taining feature integrity and improving model performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Neural networks (NNs) are nonlinear functions that map inputs to outputs through layers per-
033
      forming operations like convolution, pooling, and activation. Each layer can be represented as
      y = ϕ(W x + b), where W and b are weights and biases, and ϕ is an activation function. Activation
034
      layers are crucial for capturing nonlinearity and sparsity in data, with a suitable choice enhancing
035
      network performance, stability, and noise robustness. While many nonlinear activation functions ex-
036   ist, finding the optimal one involves trade-offs due to conflicting desirable properties. The Rectified
037   Linear Unit (ReLU) is popular for its simplicity but suffers from the dying ReLU problem [Lu et al.
038   (2020)], where neurons can become inactive.
039
      To overcome ReLU’s limitations, various ReLU-like functions, such as Leaky ReLU [Maas et al.
040
      (2013)], Parametric ReLU (PReLU) [He et al. (2015)], and GELU [Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016)],
041
      have been developed to retain advantages while addressing drawbacks. Despite their effectiveness
042   [Szandała (2021)], ongoing research into better activation functions is necessary, employing strate-
043   gies like genetic algorithms [Basirat et al. (2019)] and learning-based approaches [Ramachandran
044   et al. (2018)]. Adaptive activation functions, which learn parameters during training, represent the
045   most advanced development in this area.
046
      Existing ReLU-like functions face limitations, particularly their element-wise application and lack
047
      of trainable parameters, preventing them from fully utilizing relationships in input data. This is
048   especially important in convolutional neural networks (CNNs), where multiple output channels must
049   be considered. These functions often process inputs separately, neglecting dependence between
050   them, such as the spatial or cross-channel relation of the features. Spatial relation refers to the local
051   connectivity and neighborhood structure of the features, while cross-channel relation refers to the
052   correlation and diversity of the features across different channels. These relations are important for
053   capturing the patterns and semantics of the input data, and features extracted in the previous layers
      of the network. Moreover, ReLU-like activation may lose the connection with the input features

054
      and cause information loss due to fixed-threshold eliminating negative features in an element-wise
055   manner.
056
057   To address these issues, we propose the Simplex Projection Activation (SPA), a cross-channel ac-
058
      tivation function that maintains feature relations and connection between input and output. See
      illustration of SPA in Fig. 1a. SPA was shown to improve classification accuracy on CNN models
059
      across multiple datasets, including noisy data, and is a strong alternative to ReLU.

      2     R ELATED WORK
063   In this section, we review related works on activation functions, and highlight the novelty and ad-
064   vantages of our proposed simplex projection activation (SPA) function.
065
066
      Channel-wise activation functions use information from several elements of the input data, rather
      than applying a pointwise transformation to each element. Examples include the Maxout unit [Good-
067
      fellow et al. (2013)], which selects the maximum value among several linear transformations, and
068
      meta-ACON functions [Ma et al. (2021)], which incorporate layer-wise and channel-wise adaptive
069   parameters. The Variable Activation Function (VAF) [Apicella et al. (2019)] uses affine transfor-
070   mations before and after activation to capture cross-channel dependencies, enhancing network per-
071   formance. Additionally, the study on adaptive activation functions [Liu et al. (2020)] introduced
072   parameterized S-shaped and ReLU-like functions that dynamically adjust during training, improv-
073   ing accuracy across tasks. These approaches demonstrate the importance of parameterized and
074   channel-wise activations in modern neural network design.
075   Concept-based activation functions derive from principles that guide network learning or infer-
076   ence. Stochastic activation functions [Urban et al. (2017); Shridhar et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2019)]
077   introduce randomness to outputs, while the ACON family [Ma et al. (2021)] extends Maxout with
078   adaptive Swish-like functions. Lifted Neural Networks [Askari et al. (2018); Sambharya (2018)]
079   frame activation as solutions to optimization problems, replacing non-smooth functions with smooth
080   penalties. Furthermore, the introduction of Deep Sparse Rectifier Networks [Glorot et al. (2011)]
081   demonstrated how sparsity in activations benefits neural network performance. By employing the
082   ReLU activation, these networks achieved significant improvements in training efficiency. However,
083
      the limitations of ReLU, such as the dying ReLU problem, highlight the need for alternative methods
      to maintain active neurons during training.
084
085   Gaussianization and normalization transformations are relevant for preprocessing and feature
086   transformation. The Generalized Divisive Normalization (GDN) [Ballé et al. (2015)] introduced a
087   parametric nonlinear transformation to Gaussianize data from natural images. GDN reduces mutual
088   information between components by combining a linear transformation with divisive normalization.
089
      This approach demonstrates how decorrelation improves density modeling and feature distribution.
      Inspired by these principles, simplex projection techniques incorporate similar constraints, ensuring
090
      well-regularized feature spaces.
091
092   Simplex projection applications utilize simplex projection in output or intermediate layers of neu-
093   ral networks, serving as an alternative to the softmax layer [Askari et al. (2018)] and producing
094   probability distributions without the associated numerical instability. Convolution Simplex Projec-
095
      tion Networks [Briq et al. (2018)] integrate simplex projection into CNNs to improve segmentation
      heatmap quality and incorporate additional loss terms.
096
097   However, these applications do not explore simplex projection as an activation function in hidden
098   layers. Unlike most activation functions that act on individual input elements, SPA accounts for
099   feature dependence across channels, projecting input tuples onto a convex set that preserves their
100   mutual relations and avoids information loss. This generalization of the ReLU function enhances
101
      information retention, sparsity, regularization, robustness, and overall network performance.

137   3.2   S IMPLEX P ROJECTION ACTIVATION
138
139   To preserve the feature dependence and avoid information loss, we propose a new type of activation
140   function that considers the feature mutual relation across multi-channels. We define X(:, i, j) as a
141   tuple of C feature elements of X at the same location (i, j). Instead of projecting each individual
142
      element x of X onto the nonnegative orthant, we project each tuple x = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xC ] of X
      onto a convex set S. Here, C is the number of channels, and S imposes non-negativity and bound
143
      constraints on the mutual relations among features. For example, S can be defined as the set of
144
      tuples whose ℓ1 -norms are equal to or bounded above by a constant δ, i.e.,
145

200   ture vectors onto the probability simplex) ensures a globally optimal solution, derived through the
201   proposed efficient update rule without requiring iterative algorithms.
202
      Remark. Different from ReLU, SPA shifts the input, x, by ν ⋆ in (7), i.e., centered by x̄I then shifted
203   by Iδ before nullifying negative elements. The SPA function tends to pass more features in the early
204   layers than ReLU, and suppresses more input features to zero in the final layer.
205
      Remark. When all elements of a tuple are negative, ReLU returns a tuple of zeros, which means that
206
      it discards all the information from the input tuple. This can cause information loss and reduce the
207   network’s ability to learn from the data. The SPA in (9) returns a tuple that has at least one non-zero
208   element, the largest element in the input tuple. This means that it preserves the information from the
209   input tuple, and assigns the highest probability to the most relevant feature. This can enhance the
210   information retention and improve the network’s ability to learn from the data.
211   Remark. The SPA activation focuses on the cross-channel dependencies within a feature tuple
212   at each spatial location, grouping channels identified as relevant and nullifying the less significant
213   ones. Convolutional operations, in turn, are primarily designed to learn spatial local information by
214   applying shared kernels over small receptive fields, capturing relationships within the neighborhood
215   of spatial locations. This distinction reflects the separation of spatial learning (via convolution) and
      channel-level feature selection (via activation).

      SPA has several advantages over ReLU and its variants.
217
218   • SPA can capture the cross-channel feature dependence and avoid information loss due to the hard-
219     thresholding rectifier.
220
      • SPA can nullify (sparsify) group of multiple features simultaneously. The features in the later
221     layers of the neural network may have some redundant or irrelevant elements that do not con-
222     tribute to the task. Applying the ReLU activation individually to each element may keep some of
223     these elements, and increase the network complexity and overfitting. SPA, on the other hand, can
224     eliminate some of these elements together.
225
      • Projection on the probability space (simplex) and sparsification of the features also implies the
226     features on more important channels are preserved, whereas the output tuples have a probabilistic
227     interpretation.
228
      • SPA also improves the network robustness by constraints on bound of the activation outcomes.
229
        SPA can normalize or adapt the features to a suitable scale or range (δ), is able to improve the
230
        network stability.

      3.2.2   PASS -T HROUGH R ATIO BY THE PARAMETER δ
233
234   The parameter δ of the SPA layer in (3) controls the scales of the simplex and can be considered
235   as a learnable parameter or a hyperparameter. The scale of the simplex, δ, controls how much the
236   input tuples are normalized and sparsified by the SPA function, thereby controlling the Pass-Through
237   Ratio (PTR), which is the proportion of input data allowed to pass through the activation layer. From
238   (8) and the final update rule (9):
239
      • Small δ: A small δ results in output tuples with a small sum, hence a high sparsity. SPA eliminates
240
        most of the small positive features and highlights the most relevant features by normalizing their
241     sum to δ. However, this may lead to over-sparsification, which can discard some useful informa-
242     tion and reduce the network performance and convergence. For instance, when 0 < δ < x1 − x2 ,
243     given that x1 and x2 are distinct, SPA returns the outcome [δ, 0, . . . , 0] with only one non-zero
244     element.
245
      • Large δ: A large δ results in output tuples with a large sum and low sparsity, which can retain most
246     of the input features and avoid information loss. However, this may decrease the non-linearity
247     properties of SPA, thereby decreasing the abilities of the neural network to learn complex patterns.
248     If δ is too large, all of the input data will be projected inside a δ-simplex, and the SPA layer will
                                                                              PC
249     degenerate into a linear transformation. For example, when δ > i=1 xi − CxC , SPA bypasses
250     all elements through the layer as y = x − C1 i xi + Cδ .
                                                         P
251
252   To understand the influence of the parameter δ on the pass-through ratio, the experiments with
253   the Gaussian distributed inputs were conducted. For the considered case, if input of size belongs
254   to N (µin , σin ) the mean value of the output distribution, µSP A = kµ · δ/C, where kµ is linear
255   coefficient, equal to 1 for normal distribution, δ is a parameter of the SPA layer, and C is the number
256   of channels. The pass-through ratio, P T R, can be defined as:
257                                     P T RSP A = fP T R (δ/(C · σin )),                              (10)
258
259   where δ is a parameter of the SPA layer, C is the number of channels, σin is a variance parameter of
260   the input Gaussian distribution, and fP T R is a non-linear function. Thus, in order to hold the same
261   value of the path-trough ratio, we should save the ratio δ/(C · σin ) to be constant. The next note can
262
      be that the PTR and the mean value of the output distribution of the SPA layer do not depend on the
      mean value of the input distribution.

      5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
510
511
      This study introduced Simplex Projection Activation (SPA), a novel activation function designed to
      enhance the performance of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by addressing the limitations of
512
      traditional activation functions such as ReLU. Our extensive experimental evaluations across various
513
      domain-specific datasets demonstrate SPA’s superior classification accuracy. This superiority was
514   consistent across both original and noise-injected data, as confirmed by permutation statistical tests.
515   Our findings suggest that SPA provides a more robust and efficient mechanism for feature activation,
516   thereby enhancing the network’s ability to capture complex patterns and improve generalization over
517   traditional methods.
518
519   5.1   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
520
521   Generalization to other architectures. SPA can be adapted to fully connected architecture by per-
522   forming along the feature dimension that allows taking into account mutual relations across multiple
523   neurons of linear layers. Besides, the SPA function’s ability to project features onto a probability
524
      simplex makes it a promising candidate for replacing “softmax” in certain architectures, such as
      attention mechanisms, to enforce sparsity or enhance interpretability. In other words, the proposed
525
      activation could be used in other architectures, like transformers and multilayer perception.
526
527   Computational complexity. While traditional activation functions like ReLU have O(n) complex-
528   ity for n elements due to their element-wise nature, SPA introduces an additional computational cost.
529   This projection involves sorting the feature vectors, resulting in a complexity of O(n log(n)), where
530
      n is the number of channels in the feature tuple. More information about the computational over-
      head of SPA is presented in Appendix G). For modern deep networks the additional complexity is
531
      manageable, especially in early layers with fewer channels. Thus, developing the faster realizations
532
      of SPA is the point of the future research.
533
534   Hyperparameter selection. As a limitation of the proposed activation layer, the necessity of finding
535   a robust method of defining the parameter δ and its training setup should be mentioned. This issue
536
      becomes relevant for large-scale datasets and models. High computational costs impede the use
      of iterative parameter search approaches like Bayesian optimization. The proposed method of δ
537
      initialization based on the similarity of the output distribution with ReLU can solve this issue but
538
      requires further investigation.
