009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) have
012            become essential to general artificial intelligence, demonstrating impressive capa-
013            bilities in task understanding and problem-solving. The real-world functionality
014            of these large models critically depends on their stability. However, there is still
015            a lack of rigorous studies examining the stability of LLMs when subjected to
016
               various perturbations. In this paper, we aim to address this gap by proposing a
               novel influence measure for LLMs. This measure is inspired by statistical methods
017
               grounded in information geometry, offering desirable invariance properties. Using
018
               this framework, we analyze the sensitivity of LLMs in response to parameter or
019            input perturbations. To evaluate the effectiveness of our approach, we conduct
020            extensive experiments on models of varying sizes, from 1.5B to 13B parameters.
021            The results clearly demonstrate the efficacy of our measure in identifying salient
022            parameters and pinpointing vulnerable areas of input images that dominate model
023            outcomes. Our research not only enhances the understanding of LLM sensitivity
024            but also highlights the broad potential of our influence measure in optimizing
025            models for tasks such as model quantization and model merging.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) such as GPT (Brown et al.,
030   2020) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), have revolutionized the field of Natural Language Processing
031   (NLP), exhibiting remarkable proficiency across a variety of tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
032   2023) and modalities (Bai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). These modern LLMs are massive in size,
033   trained on vast amounts of data, and meticulously aligned to prevent from generating harmful content
034   (Perez et al., 2022), leaking private information (Zhang et al., 2024), or exhibiting sexual or religious
035   bias (Xie & Lukasiewicz, 2023).
036   Despite the enthusiasm for these integrative approaches, a critical issue remains: LLMs remain
037   susceptible to both external and internal perturbations, affecting their reliability and performance. This
038   paper seeks to address this challenge by systematically identifying and locating these vulnerabilities
039   through quantifying the stability of LLMs under perturbations in both input and parameters.
040
      Externally, LLMs are susceptible to input perturbations, such as carefully crafted prompts, making
041
      them vulnerable to jailbreak attacks (Chao et al., 2023). Previous studies (Yuan et al., 2023; Huang
042   et al., 2023) have highlighted that specific prompts, suffixes, and even so-called "magic code" (Liu
043   et al., 2023) can induce inappropriate or harmful behaviors in language models. This susceptibility
044   also extends to visual inputs in VLMs, as demonstrated by Qi et al. (2024), where adversarially
045   optimized images can drastically shift model behavior.
046
      In addition to intentionally crafted adversarial samples, VLMs are also highly sensitive to undetectable
047
      perturbations in specific local regions of an image — an issue that is common, as user-uploaded
048   images often suffer from blurring, masking, or low resolution. Our case study on the Qwen-VL model
049   (Bai et al., 2023) illustrates this vulnerability. We applied our proposed influence measure to each
050   pixel in the input image, as shown in the right columns of Figure 1. Using these calculations, we
051   masked the top 10 most sensitive pixels that were irrelevant to the prompt question. This manipulation
052   easily misled the model, resulting in incorrect responses. Even when we crafted prompts specifically
053   instructing the model to ignore noise and occlusions (see Figure 2), vulnerable regions remained,
      leading the model to hallucinate.

054
      Internally, the stability of LLMs is further challenged by parameter perturbations, often introduced
055   through quantization and model merging. While these methods are essential for deployment efficiency
056   by reducing inference costs (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2024) and training costs (Yu
057   et al., 2024), they can lead to hallucinations and degraded performance (Men et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
058   2023; Li et al., 2024). Existing approaches (Ma et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2022) primarily rely on the
059   Hessian matrix of parameters, which results in substantial computational overhead and introduces
060   unrealistic assumptions, which hinders fine-grained analysis of parameter stability.
061   To address these challenges, we propose a novel influence measure called FI, First order local
062   Influence, to quantitatively assess the stability of LLMs against local perturbations. This influence
063   measure is highly versatile and capable of capturing model stability when exposed to both external
064   and internal perturbations at various levels of granularity—from channels of parameter matrices and
065   individual parameters to input features like pixels and patches.
066
      Specifically, we construct a perturbation manifold that encompasses all perturbed models, along with
067   its associated geometric properties. Our influence measure quantifies the degree of local influence of
068   the perturbation to a given objective function within this manifold and thus reflects the stability of
069   each component of LLMs. We then provide a detailed discussion of the theoretical and computational
070   advantages brought by the invariance property of the FI measure, which is entirely free from the
071   constraints imposed by perturbations.
072
      Empirically, we find our approach successfully uncovers sensitivity issues in VLMs by detecting
073   fragile input pixels and enables us to understand how prompt engineering affects model stability.
074   Subsequently, we conduct extensive empirical studies to demonstrate the validity of our method in
075   identifying salient parameters across models of varying sizes. By safeguarding the salient parameters
076   identified by our measure, we achieve a significant reduction in degradation caused by model
077   quantization and model merging, highlighting the crucial role of FI in model optimization tasks.
078
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
079
080        (i) We construct a novel framework for sensitivity analysis that evaluates the stability of each
081            component within LLMs, considering the impact of small perturbations on the overall
082            system.
083
          (ii) Using our proposed metrics, we empirically demonstrate how external perturbations impact
084            model performance, presenting an example involving vision-language models that highlights
085            issues of erroneous sensitivity. Additionally, we illustrate how our approach can be employed
086            to analyze cross-modal influence.
087
          (iii) We empirically demonstrate that the proposed method effectively identifies the sensitive
088
                components of model parameters that influence performance under perturbations. It can
089             serve as a valuable tool for enhancing model quantization and merging techniques, providing
090             both theoretical insights and empirical evidence.

095   In this section, we propose a new metric called FI to quantify the stability of large language models
096   against local perturbations. Specifically, we focus on auto-regressive language models, which generate
097   one token at a time based on both the initial input and previously generated tokens. Considering this
098
      sequential generation nature, we first develop FI for single-step token generation tasks and discuss
      its theoretical and computational properties in great detail. Following this, we demonstrate how the
099
      proposed stability measure can be naturally extended to address sequence generation tasks.

233   In this section, we first conduct an experiment using the FI to evaluate the model’s stability to external
234   perturbations, such as disturbing the input images of VLMs, while highlighting its application in cross-

      3.2   I NTERNAL PERTURBATIONS ANALYSIS
325
326   Regarding the internal perturbations analysis, we begin by conducting a parameter sensitivity experi-
327   ment. In this process, we compute FI values for each element in the weight matrices across all layers.
328   We then perform sparsification by selecting a proportion of parameters with the highest FI values and
329   setting them to 0-bit (i.e., zeroing them out).
330   For comparison, we randomly select the same proportion of parameters with lower FI values for
331   sparsification. This approach helps us assess whether FI effectively identifies the most vulnerable
332   parameters. Our analysis focuses on how these perturbations impact two key capabilities of large
333   models: knowledge retention and instruction-following.
334
      Knowledge Retention Ability. We conduct experiments on the multiple-choice problems from
335
      the MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) dataset, using Qwen2-7B. We take the cross-entropy loss, i.e.,
336   f = − log P (y = ypred |x, θ), as the target function, and calculate the FI value according to Theorem
337   2.4. In this setup, we treat the task as a 4-class classification problem with the possible classes being
338   "A," "B," "C," and "D".
339
      As shown in Figure 3, sparsifying just 2-3% of the high-FI parameters significantly reduces the
340
      model’s knowledge capacity, inducing catastrophic forgetting and hallucinations, leading to up to
341
      a 75% performance loss. This demonstrates the effectiveness of FI values in identifying fragile
342   parameters. In contrast, models remain relatively robust against random sparsification, where, in
343   many cases, models exhibit almost the same behavior after a 5% sparsification. As this trend of
344   performance decline manifests, a very small proportion of parameters dominate knowledge processing
345   and retention.
346
      Instruction-following Ability. We use the Alpaca-eval validation set (Dubois et al., 2024), a widely
347
      adopted benchmark, and conduct experiments with various open-source models, including LLaMA2,
348   LLaMA3 (Touvron et al., 2023), and Qwen2 (Bai et al., 2023), across different sizes. We report two
349   metrics: ROUGE-1 (comparing to pre-sparsity responses) and length-control winning rate (LCWR),
350   comparing to GPT-3.5 Turbo. Higher scores are better for both metrics.
351
      To estimate the average Fisher Information (FI) for sequence generation, we use the fixed-context
352
      approach with L = 5. For each sample z, we estimate FIl (z) by generating N = 10 responses,
353
      truncating them at position l − 1. These truncated sequences are used to approximate the conditional
354   expectation in Equation 5 by computing the sample average. The per-token FI values are then
355
      aggregated using Equation 6 to obtain FIL seq (z), which is averaged across all samples to estimate the
356   overall FI.

359
      outperform those subjected to FI-guided sparsity. Notably, our FI metric demonstrates the greatest

      4    C ONCLUSIONS
475
476
477   This paper unveils the presence of cracks in the armor of LLMs, which poses challenges for quantify-
478   ing the stability of LLMs under perturbations. To address this issue, we introduce a novel framework
479   for adversarial learning that evaluates the stability of each component within LLMs, considering the
480   impact of small perturbations on the overall system. Through experiments under both internal and
481   external perturbations, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method.
482   There are several promising avenues for future research. One direction is to develop methods that
483   can accelerate the computation of the metric. Additionally, exploring the application of our FI to
484   the training stage. By adapting and extending our FI metric, we can gain a deeper understanding of
485   parameter stability during training, ultimately achieving more efficient parameter fine-tuning and
      pre-training.
