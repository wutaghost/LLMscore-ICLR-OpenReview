009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Scale is a primary factor that influences the performance and generalization of a
012            robot learning system. In this paper, we aim to scale up the trajectory prediction
013            model by using broad out-of-domain data to improve its robustness and general-
014            ization ability. Trajectory model is designed to predict any-point trajectories in
015            the current frame given an instruction and can provide detailed control guidance
016
               for robotic policy learning. To handle the diverse out-of-domain data distribu-
               tion, we propose a sparsely-gated MoE (Top-1 gating strategy) architecture for
017
               trajectory model, coined as Tra-MoE. The sparse activation design enables good
018
               balance between parameter cooperation and specialization, effectively benefiting
019            from large-scale out-of-domain data while maintaining constant FLOPs per token.
020            In addition, we further introduce an adaptive policy conditioning technique by
021            learning 2D mask representations for predicted trajectories, which is explicitly
022            aligned with image observations to guide policy prediction more flexibly. We
023            perform experiments on both simulation and real-world scenarios to verify the
024            effectiveness of our Tra-MoE and adaptive policy conditioning technique. We
025            jointly train the Tra-MoE model on all 130 tasks in the LIBERO benchmark and
026            conduct a comprehensive empirical analysis, demonstrating that our Tra-MoE
027
               consistently exhibits superior performance compared to the dense baseline model,
               even when the latter is scaled to match Tra-MoE’s parameter count.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In computer vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP), significant progress has been made
033   through scaling up models from large-scale out-of-domain data. For example, many works use broad
034   data to perform self-supervised pre-training, and then adapt to diverse downstream tasks through
035   zero-shot (Brown, 2020; Radford et al., 2021; Achiam et al., 2023) or fine-tuning (Devlin, 2018; Chen
036   et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2022). Additionally, some research efforts (Lu et al., 2022;
037   Zhu et al., 2022b;a) focus on learning a generalist model capable of unifying various tasks. In contrast,
038   in robot learning, due to data scarcity and homogeneity, some groundbreaking methods (Levine
039
      et al., 2016; Andrychowicz et al., 2020) resort to training only using in-domain data. Recently, some
      works (Seo et al., 2022; Bahl et al., 2023; Wang et al.; Bharadhwaj et al., 2024a) have shifted to
040
      learn from action-free human video data. A particularly scalable and promising paradigm (Wen
041
      et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Bharadhwaj et al., 2024b) involves learning a trajectory prediction
042   model from action-free video data, and then using small-scale action-labeled demonstrations to learn
043   trajectory-guided policies, thereby achieving greater sample efficiency and better generalization.
044
045
      Despite the potential benefits, it still remains underexplored how to effectively leverage broad out-
      of-domain video data for training trajectory models. In particular, the impact of incorporating data
046
      encompassing diverse skills, environments, objects, and even embodiments has not fully investigated
047
      for downstream tasks. For instance, ATM (Wen et al., 2023), a notable work in this field, mainly relies
048   on in-domain data for training both trajectory models and policies. We argue that scale is the primary
049   factor to build a powerful trajectory model for policy conditioning. Therefore, we propose to scale up
050   the training of trajectory model under the setup of jointly learning from large-scale out-of-domain and
051   small-scale in-domain data, as shown in Fig. 2(a). However, directly using large-scale out-of-domain
052   video data for hybrid pre-training involves two critical challenges: (i) Joint training on data from
053   different skills, environments, objects, and even embodiments will add learning difficulty and might
      lead to optimization conflicts. It is unclear how to fuse the complementary information of different

134   demonstrate superior transfer capabilities. These works directly combine heterogeneous robotic data
135   with different action and observation spaces for joint training, potentially leading to sub-optimal
136   solutions. In contrast, we leverages sparsely-gated MoE to incorporate larger-scale, out-of-domain,
137   and action-less video data. Furthermore, our experiments demonstrate the importance of MoE for
138   scaling out-of-domain data due to its superior capacity for parameter cooperation and specialization.
139   Mixture-of-Experts. The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) framework (Cai et al., 2024) is built upon
140   a simple yet effective concept: dividing a model into specialized components, or ‘experts’, each
141   focusing on distinct tasks or data aspects. This paradigm allows for selective activation of relevant
142   experts based on the input, thereby maintaining computational efficiency while leveraging a vast
143   reservoir of specialized knowledge through increased model capacity. Recently, in computer vision,
144   natural language processing, and multi-modal, there has been a surge of foundation models trained
145   based on the MoE architecture, including Swin-Moe (Hwang et al., 2023), MoE-LLaVA (Lin et al.,
146   2024), DeepSeek-V2 (Liu et al., 2024a), Mixtral-8x22B (Jiang et al., 2024), and so on. MoE typically
147
      encompass dense (Puigcerver et al., 2023) and sparse (Chen et al., 2023) variants. Dense MoE
      activates all experts in each iteration, while sparse MoE activates only a subset, generally resulting
148
      in lower computational overhead. However, sparse MoE can suffer from expert load imbalance and
149
      training instability issues. Techniques such as adding noise, load balancing loss (Lepikhin et al.,
150   2020), and router z-loss (Zoph et al., 2022) are often employed to mitigate these issues and improve
151   performance. In our work, we conduct a comprehensive empirical study of these techniques when
152   training our sparsely-gated MoE-based trajectory model.
153

195
196   In this section, we first describe our problem formulation in Sec. 3.1. Subsequently, we give an
197   overview of our proposed framework, which is composed of a sparsely-gated MoE-based trajec-
198   tory prediction model and a trajectory-guided policy model using the adaptive policy conditioning
199   technique, detailed in Sec. 3.2. Finally, we describe our training process and loss functions in Sec. 3.3.

201   3.1      P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
202
203   Following the setup of previous works (Wen et al., 2023; Bharadhwaj et al., 2024b; Xu et al.,
204
      2024), our approach encompasses a trajectory prediction model to conduct language-conditioned
      any-point trajectory prediction and a trajectory-guided policy model to predict executable robot
205
      actions. In particular, as depicted in Fig. 2, we consider two types of datasets for training, which
206
      are large-scale, out-of-domain, and action-free videos D ood , as well as small-scale, in-domain, and
207   action-labeled demonstrations D in . For trajectory prediction model, we employ D ood and D in for
208   joint pre-training, whereas for trajectory-guided policy model, we only use D in for training.

      3.2      A RCHITECTURE
211
212   ATM Revisited. We develop our approach based on ATM (Wen et al., 2023), an advanced and
213   promising framework of robotic learning from videos. The overall pipeline of ATM is illustrated on
214   Fig. 2(b). It first learns a trajectory model from video data to perform language-conditioned any-point
215   trajectory prediction. The predicted trajectories provide detailed control guidance, which is then
      used to learn a trajectory-guided policy model using action-labeled demonstration data. Specifically,

217   Formally, given an image observation ot at timestep t, any set of 2D query points simpled on the
218   image frame pt = {pt,k }K   k=1 , and a language instruction ℓ, the trajectory model learns a mapping
219   pt:t+H = τθ (ot , pt , ℓ) that predicts the coordinates of these query points in the camera frame for

221   current observation ot and the predicted trajectory pt:t+H to predict the subsequent robot actions.

223   implement the trajectory model. In track transformer, language tokens, image tokens, and track

225   processing diverse domains and multiple modalities data within a unified transformer may yield

227   MoE-based trajectory model, as depicted in Fig. 3(a), we replace certain layers of the transformer

      Fundamentally, the policy model bridges the gap between the predicted 2D any-point trajectories
      and the executable 3D robot actions, functioning as an inverse dynamic model and eliminating the
256
      necessity of other task specification. However, how to effectively condition the policy using the
257
      predicted 2D trajectories remains an unresolved challenge. Based on our analysis, we present two key
258   insights: (i) Explicit spatial alignment and fusion of the 2D trajectories with image observations turns
259   out to be highly beneficial. It simplifies the mapping of 2D trajectories to image space and highlights
260   motion-relevant regions, enabling the policy model to focus on important areas and reducing the
261   learning difficulty. (ii) Trajectory positions exhibit different characteristics: starting points typically
262   emphasize local motion, while endpoints focus more on global trend. Therefore, an adaptive trajectory
263   representation and conditioning technique is expected to flexibly guide the policy by effectively
264   capturing these variations.
265   Based on the aforementioned analysis, we propose an adaptive policy conditioning technique to
266   incorporate trajectory information into 2D images. Specifically, we construct an additional mask
267   modality, and populate it with 2D trajectories based on their specific spatial locations, while setting
268   the value of each point in the trajectory as a learnable embedding. Before encoding the image, we
269   concatenate this mask modality with the image observations on the channel dimension, forming a
      tensor of dimensions H × W × 4, as shown in Fig. 3(b). To sum up, our adaptive policy conditioning

      Experiment setup. We conduct simulation experiments using the LIBERO (Liu et al., 2024b)
      benchmark, which is divided into five categories: LIBERO-Spatial, LIBERO-Object, LIBERO-Goal,
352
      LIBERO-Long, and LIBERO-90. LIBERO-90 includes 90 tasks, while each of the other categories
353
      contains 10 tasks. Accompanied by skilled human demonstrations, these tasks are designed as follows:
354   LIBERO-Spatial focuses on identical objects across varying layouts; LIBERO-Object emphasizes the
355   same layouts with a change in objects; LIBERO-Goal retains the same object types and layouts but
356   introduces dissimilar goals; LIBERO-Long encompasses a broad range of object types and layouts
357   alongside extended task objectives; Lastly, LIBERO-90 presents a highly varied selection of object
358   categories, layouts, environments, and task goals. Specifically, we select 90 tasks from LIBERO-90
359   to construct dataset D ood , utilizing 20 action-free videos per task; and we also choose 40 tasks from
360   LIBERO-Spatial, LIBERO-Object, LIBERO-Goal, and LIBERO-Long to build dataset D in , using
361   10 action-labeled demonstrations per task. In summary, for training Tra-MoE, we use a total of 2200
362
      action-free videos, with an out-of-domain to in-domain data ratio of 9 : 2. For the trajectory-guided
      policy, we train a separate multi-task policy for each category: LIBERO-Spatial, LIBERO-Object,

402   Similarly, adding noise is also detrimental to learning parameter specialization among experts.

415   When the data scale is small, naively expanding the model capacity through sparsely-gated MoE does
416   not lead to performance improvements. A reasonable explanation is that the larger model may lead to
417
      a overfitting problem when training on a small-scale dataset. Moreover, when the data scale is small,
      training sparsely-gated MoE usually results in some experts not being sufficiently trained, thereby
418
      affecting performance. (ii) Naively introducing out-of-domain data for joint training often leads to
419

420   data and scaling up models with sparsely-gated MoE improves performance. This phenomenon
421   suggests that although out-of-domain data generally enhances model generalization, it could hinder
422   task-specific performance in the target domain due to insufficient in-domain data learning. In contrast,
423   expanding the model with out-of-domain data using sparsely-gated MoE achieves a more optimal
424   balance between parameter cooperation and specialization across diverse data and even different
425   tokens within the same data input. This design allows these large-scale data to jointly train most of

      5   C ONCLUSION
514
515
      In this work, we have introduced Tra-MoE, a novel framework that utilizes a sparsely-gated MoE
      architecture with Top-1 gating strategy to scale up trajectory models. Tra-MoE achieves superior
516
      parameter cooperation and specialization, enabling more effective utilization of large-scale out-of-
517
      domain data while maintaining constant FLOPs per token. We train our Tra-MoE on all 130 tasks in
518   the LIBERO benchmark and conduct a comprehensive empirical study, demonstrating that Tra-MoE
519   consistently exhibits superior performance compared to the dense baseline model, even when the
520   latter is scaled to match Tra-MoE’s parameter count. Additionally, we propose an adaptive policy
521   conditioning technique for trajectory-guided policies, ensuring adaptive 2D trajectory representation
522   and explicit spatial alignment with image observations, thereby achieving superior performance.
523   Extensive experiments in both simulated and real-world environments validate our findings and
524   demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed methods.
525
526   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528   In our work, we demonstrate the effectiveness of using sparsely-gated Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
529   for comprehensively scaling model capacity and leveraging large-scale out-of-domain data, while
530   conducting a thorough experimental study of its training techniques. To the best of our knowledge,
531
      this is the first attempt of using sparse MoE architecture for scaling up in robot learning. We hope
      it can serve as a strong baseline and facilitate further research in this direction. While we are
532
      encouraged by the strong results across a wide range of simulated and real-world experiments, a
533
      number of limitations and future works still remain. On one hand, we plan to further scale up
534   our Tra-MoE by incorporating more diverse simulation environments, different embodiments, and
535   even real-world human videos. On the other hand, with the increasing availability of large-scale
536   robotic dataset (Padalkar et al., 2023), our sparse MoE architecture could be directly applied to train
537   end-to-end policies, potentially leading a powerful robotic foundation model, akin to Octo (Team
538   et al., 2024) and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024). Finally, as for our adaptive policy conditioning
539   technique, a further avenue of exploration is to extend it to other policy conditioning representations,
      such as human hand trajectories (Wang et al.; Mendonca et al., 2023).
