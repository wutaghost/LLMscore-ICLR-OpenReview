009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Efficient exploration is a fundamental challenge in reinforcement learning, es-
012            pecially in environments with sparse rewards. Intrinsic motivation can improve
013            exploration efficiency by rewarding agents for encountering novel states. In this
014            work, we propose a method called Variation Learned Priors for intrinsic motiva-
015            tion that estimates state novelty through variational state encoding. Specifically,
016            we measure state novelty using the Kullback-Leibler divergence between a Varia-
017            tional Autoencoder’s learned prior and posterior distributions. When tested across
018            various domains, our approach improves the latent space quality of the Variational
019            Autoencoder, leading to increased exploration efficiency and better task perfor-
020
               mance for the reinforcement learning agent.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Effective exploration remains a key challenge in reinforcement learning, especially in environments
026   with sparse rewards. Improving the agent’s understanding of its environment in these scenarios can
027   significantly enhance exploration. One way to improve this understanding is to use intrinsic mo-
028   tivation (Schmidhuber, 1991; Barto et al., 2004; Barto, 2013). In psychology, intrinsic motivation
029   refers to “doing something because it is inherently interesting or enjoyable” (Ryan & Deci, 2000),
030   and in reinforcement learning, it has inspired various approaches to shape agent behavior. Leverag-
031   ing intrinsic motivation has enabled agents to exceed human performance on the widely recognized
032   Atari-57 benchmark (Badia et al., 2020).
033   Intrinsic motivation methods often model an agent’s understanding of the environment through an
034   intrinsic reward function that guides exploration. One approach involves using Variational Auto-
035   encoders (VAEs) (Higgins et al., 2017; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Klissarov et al., 2019), where
036   states are encoded and a posterior distribution is learned. Klissarov et al. (2019) proposed using
037   the KL divergence between a fixed prior (typically a standard Gaussian) and the posterior distri-
038   bution after observing a new state as an intrinsic reward to estimate state novelty. This measures
039
      state novelty as the distance between the agent’s most recent experiences and the VAE’s encoded
      representation of the environment, aiming to enhance exploration in stochastic environments. How-
040
      ever, this approach has an important limitation. As the agent gathers more experiences, the fixed
041
      prior struggles to accurately reflect the agent’s evolving understanding of the environment, leading
042   to regions in the latent space where the prior assigns high probability while the posterior assigns low
043   probability. Over time, this misalignment weakens the novelty measure, reducing the effectiveness
044   of exploration.
045
      We illustrate this limitation in Figure 1, which shows data obtained from an agent in the Walker2d
046
      domain that chose its actions uniformly randomly for 3000 decision stages. The figure compares the
047
      latent representation of a VAE with a fixed standard prior to one using four learned priors trained on
048   the agent’s trajectory. The percentage below each plot represents latent space coverage, showing how
049   well the prior aligns with the encoded states (see Appendix D for calculation details). The heatmaps
050   display the prior probability densities, with white points representing the means of the encoded latent
051   states. White points closer to high-density regions suggest a better alignment between the prior and
052   the encoded states. In the Standard model, the misalignment between the prior and the encoded
053   states results in low prior density around frequently visited states, leading to a lower KL divergence,
      this weakens the intrinsic reward, which can result in inefficient exploration. In contrast, the learned

077   We propose using learnable priors in the KL divergence of a VAE as an intrinsic reward to measure
078
      state novelty. Unlike fixed priors, learned priors provide a more accurate reflection of the agent’s
      experiences. This refined understanding of the agent’s experiences allows for a more accurate as-
079
      sessment of state novelty, which in turn drives more efficient exploration and improves sample ef-
080
      ficiency. Specifically, we introduce four new intrinsic reward functions using four types of learned
081   priors: Mixture of Gaussians, Generative Topographic Mapping, Variational Mixture of Posteriors,
082   and Flow-based. We evaluate the impact of these priors on the agent’s exploration in the Behavioral
083   Suite DeepSea and Minigrid environments, demonstrating that learned priors facilitate efficient ex-
084   ploration by accelerating first-time state visits and state space coverage. Additionally, we assess the
085   effectiveness of the proposed intrinsic rewards in continuous control MuJoCo and Atari environ-
086   ments, where our results show that the learned priors not only improve agent performance over fixed
087   priors but also outperform existing intrinsic motivation approaches in reinforcement learning.

089   2    BACKGROUND
090
091   A Markov decision process is a five-tuple ⟨S, A,P, R, γ⟩, where S is a set of states, A is a set
092
      of actions, P : S × A × S → [0, 1] describes the transition dynamics, R : S × A × S → [0, 1]
      specifies the (external) reward, and γ is a discount factor. At decision stage t, t ≥ 0, the agent
093
      observes state st , takes action at . It then transitions to state st+1 and receives reward rt+1 . A policy
094
      π : S × A → [0, 1] specifies
                              P∞ how the agent selects actions. The agent’s objective is to maximize the
095   expected return G = i=0 γ t+i Rt+i .
096
097   In intrinsically motivated reinforcement learning, at each decision stage, the agent receives an in-
098
      ternal reward in addition to the external reward. While the intrinsic reward is combined with the
      extrinsic reward to update the agent’s behavior, the agent’s true objective remains the same: to
099
      maximize the expected return based on the extrinsic rewards only.
100
101   Variational Autoencoders (VAE) are generative, latent-variable models that are a probabilistic
102   method for encoding data into a latent space. These models approximate the posterior probability
103   of the latent variables. Using this approximation, one can calculate a lower bound for the marginal
104
      likelihood of a dataset.
105   For a vanilla VAE, the prior p(z), where z are latent variables, is fixed; it is typically defined by a
106   standard Gaussian, N (0, 1). The encoder compresses the input data x to the variational posterior
107   qϕ (z|x). The decoder, qθ (x|z), takes latent values sampled from the variational posterior and maps
      them to an output with the same dimensions as the input data. Here, ϕ and θ are the model parameters

108
      for the encoder and the decoder, respectively. The loss function of a VAE is comprised of two
109   terms. The first is the reconstruction term, which gauges how well the model has reconstructed the
110   input. The second is the regularisation term that acts in the latent space. This regularisation term is
111   calculated using the Kulback-Leibler (KL) divergence of the variational posterior from the encoder
112   and the prior p(z). Mathematically, this is described as learning the lower bound on the marginal
113   likelihood of the generative model (Kingma & Welling, 2013),
114                   logpθ (x|z) ≥ L(θ, ϕ) = Eqϕ (z|x) logpθ (x|z) − DKL (qϕ (z|x)||p(z)),                 (1)
115
116
      where the L(θ, ϕ) term is known as the variational Evidence Lower Bound Objective (ELBO). The
      DKL represents the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the prior and the variational posterior. The
117
      DKL forces the VAE’s posterior distribution q(z|x) to be as close as possible to the prior distribution
118
      p(z).
119
120   Rezende & Viola (2018) and Tomczak & Welling (2018) have shown that using a fixed standard
121   Gaussian N (0, 1) prior can lead to over-regularization of the latent space, making it suboptimal
122
      for learning meaningful representations. This is due to a fixed prior not having the flexibility to
      accurately model the aggregate posterior distribution. The concept of the aggregate posterior was
123
      introduced by Hoffman & Johnson (2016), where the authors deconstruct the ELBO objective in a
124
      novel way,
125
126
          L(ϕ, θ, λ) = Ex∼q(x) [Eqϕ (z|x) [lnpθ (x|z)]] + Ex∼q(x) [Hqϕ (z|x)] + Ez∼q(z) [−lnpλ (z)].        (2)
127   The first term is the reconstruction error between the input and the reconstructed sample; the second
128   term is the expectation of the entropy H of the variational posterior; and the last term is the cross
                                                             PN
129   entropy between the aggregate posterior, q(z) = N1 n=1 qϕ (z|xn ) and the prior pλ (z). The ag-
130   gregate posterior is the mean of all encoded samples or a mixture of the variational posteriors of all
131   N samples (Makhzani et al.). The prior is learnable with parameters λ. The third term is minimized
132   when the prior and the aggregated posterior match. This can be encouraged by parameterizing the
133
      prior and learning these parameters during training. However, this is computationally expensive and
      could lead to overfitting.
134
135   In reality, for a fixed prior, this leads to “holes” or regions in the latent space where the aggregated
136   posterior assigns low probability while the prior assigns (relatively) high probability. This results in
137   inaccurate latent representations that fail to capture important variations in the data, highlighting the
138   limitations of using a fixed prior for learning robust representations. An alternative to the standard
139
      fixed prior is to have a learned prior, where the aggregate posterior and the prior are fit to each other.
      During training, the prior’s parameters are optimized to minimize the KL divergence between the
140
      aggregate posterior (over all states seen by the agent) and itself.

143   3    R ELATED W ORK
144
145   One common approach to intrinsic motivation in reinforcement learning is to enhance exploration
146   by rewarding agents for visiting novel or uncertain states. This formulation is particularly effective
147
      in environments with sparse external rewards, where encouraging the agent to seek out unfamiliar
      regions of the state space can lead to improved performance. They include prediction error-based
148
      methods (Pathak et al., 2017; Schmidhuber, 1991; Burda et al., 2019; Bougie & Ichise, 2020), which
149
      measure surprise by the difference between predicted and observed outcomes. Agents are incen-
150   tivized to explore unfamiliar or unpredictable states by receiving higher rewards for high prediction
151   errors. Other approaches, such as information gain (Houthooft et al., 2016), encourage agents to
152   explore states where they can learn the most, while uncertainty estimates Achiam & Sastry (2017)
153   guide exploration by focusing on states where the agent’s knowledge is incomplete. Count-based
154   methods (Bellemare et al., 2016; Lobel et al., 2023) reward agents based on how often states have
155   been visited, encouraging them to explore novel states. These strategies have proven effective in a
156   range of applications, from handling continuous state spaces to improving generalization and reduc-
157   ing training time.
158   Our work is most directly related to that of Klissarov et al. (2019), who use the KL divergence
159   within the VAE loss function as an intrinsic reward to incentivize agent exploration. The authors
160   demonstrated that the KL intrinsic reward outperformed both the Intrinsic Curiosity Model baseline
161   (Pathak et al., 2017) and A2C (Mnih et al., 2016) method in MiniGrid, MuJoCo’s HalfCheetah, and
      Hopper but failed to outperform A2C in the Walker2d environment.

      4   P ROPOSED I NTRINSIC R EWARD
163
164
      We propose an approach to intrinsic motivation that uses the KL divergence of a VAE loss function
165   between a learned prior and the posterior distribution of the encoded state. The learned priors model
166   the aggregate posterior directly, leading to a more accurate encoded representation and allowing
167   the prior to better capture the agent’s history and latent space structure, ultimately improving state
168   novelty estimation (as shown in Figure 1). In principle, any probability density estimator can be used
169   to model the aggregate posterior. However, since the variational posteriors in VAEs are typically
170   modeled as Gaussians, we use this assumption to guide our choice of learned priors. We selected
171   three priors that assume a Gaussian mixture model for the aggregate posterior and one that has no
172   prior assumptions of the aggregate posterior. We describe them below.
173   Mixture of Gaussians (MoG). Here the parameters of the mixture models are learned directly,
174   meaning that µθ , and σθ , of each Gaussian component are optimized during training based on the
175   data, rather than being fixed or predefined. This allows the model to adapt more flexibly to the
176   structure of the data by learning the most suitable mixture components. Note the aggregate posterior
177   is a mixture of variational posteriors, each of them Gaussian. The MoG prior is
178                                                  K

182   where K is the number of components, ωk is a learned weighting coefficient, and N is a normal
183   distribution parameterized by learnable parameters µk and σk .
184   Generative Topographic Mapping (GTM). Here the parameters of the mixture models are learned
185   by transforming a low-dimensional fixed grid of K points to a higher-dimensional target domain, in
186   our case, a Gaussian mixture model, through a transformation gγ learned during training of the VAE

206   Flow-Based Density Estimator (Flow). This prior does not assume a particular structure for the

212

      follows a Gaussian mixture model:

      5.1   E NVIRONMENTS
231   To build intuition, we first evaluate the learned priors using supervised learning tasks on the MNIST
232   and FashionMNIST datasets. To train the VAE on these datasets, the images are pre-processed to be
233   normalized between 0 and 1.
234
      To evaluate the VaLP methods we start with DeepSea from DeepMind’s Behaviour Suite (Osband
235
      et al., 2020). DeepSea is an N × N grid where the agent navigates from the top-left to the goal in
236
      the bottom-right, deciding at each step to move diagonally left or right. The agent’s exploration in
237   this domain is limited to the lower diagonal of the grid. The episode ends after N steps. Rewards
238   are 0 for moving left, −0.01/N for moving right, and 1 for reaching the goal. The only way to reach
239   the goal is to move right at each step. The state is a one-hot vector input to the VAE.
240
      Next, we use two Minigrid environments (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) to test how useful our
241
      intrinsic rewards are. The first environment features rooms connected by doors, with the goal in
242
      the final room. The second environment is a custom double spiral designed to test exploration and
243   detachment. No extrinsic rewards are given. In both, the state is a partially observable RGB image
244   input to the VAE.
245
246
      Lastly, we evaluate our approach in MuJoCo (Brockman et al., 2016) and Atari (Bellemare et al.,
      2013) environments. These are common exploration benchmarks with continuous and discrete ac-
247
      tion spaces, respectively. In MuJoCo, the state consists of raw observations provided by the envi-
248
      ronment, which are directly input to the VAE. In Atari, the state is a preprocessed RGB image that

      how much of the environment remains unexplored, particularly in areas deeper in the spirals, which
413
      might hold potential for future rewards. Agents exhibiting detachment will show uneven coverage,
414   neglecting deeper regions in the spiral. In contrast, non-detachment is characterized by balanced

521   Its ability to model complex, non-linear dynamics, thanks to using a normalizing flow approach,
522   allows it to flexibly transform a simple initial distribution into a more complex one that better fits

524   exploration and policy optimization in environments like MuJoCo and Atari. VaLPVamp also shows

530   prior and the aggregate posterior, resulting in a more informative KL divergence. This provides a

532   performance in reinforcement learning tasks. These results highlight the value of learnable priors
533   for enhancing latent space modeling and exploration, positioning flow-based priors as a superior
534   alternative to fixed priors.

911                                                  left visits     right visits
                                          CBI =                  −
912                                                 total visits     total visits
913   Where: left visits is the total first-time visits in the left region, right visits is the total first-time visits
914   in the right region, and total visits = left visits + right visits. A CBI of 0 has perfectly balanced
915   exploration. A CBI of 1 has completely imbalanced (all visits in one region). Lower CBI values
916   indicate better evenness of exploration, avoiding detachment between regions. This quantifies the
917   evenness of exploration across two regions of the environment (e.g., left and right spirals). Results
      for both Coverage Percentage and CBI can be found in Tables 2 + 3 (https://bit.ly/4fAbViG.)
