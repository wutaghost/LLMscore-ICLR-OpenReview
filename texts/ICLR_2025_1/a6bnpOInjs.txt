009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose a novel method, Textbook Consistency, to improve the training effi-
012            ciency of large language models by leveraging textbooks as a guiding signal for
013            learning from internet-scale data. Rather than relying on hard filtering of data based
014            on quality thresholds before training, our approach adaptively adjusts the weight
015            of data during training based on its consistency with textbooks during training.
016            We compute the cosine similarity between internet data and textbooks in a latent
017            space, using this metric to modulate the cross-entropy loss. Our method signifi-
018
               cantly enhances training efficiency, achieving twice the effectiveness by reducing
               training time or the number of tokens required. Empirical results show superior
019
               performance on language models trained on large datasets like FineWeb and The
020
               Pile, with extensions to other domains such as robotics. Our method is simple to
021            implement, incurs no additional overhead, and is compatible with existing data
022            curation techniques.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The internet provides a vast and diverse pool of knowledge, making it a critical resource for advancing
028   large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020). Training
029   these models on internet-scale data takes months and requires thousands, if not tens of thousands, of
030   GPUs (Touvron et al., 2023a; Dubey et al., 2024). To make training more efficient and improve data
031   quality, researchers have focused on methods to data filtering, duplicate removal, perplexity-based
032   filtering, hand curation, identifying new data sources (see e.g. Lee et al., 2021; Penedo et al., 2023;
033
      Computer, 2023; Li et al., 2024; Soldaini et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2020; Soboleva et al., 2023; Albalak
      et al., 2024a). A dominant strategy involves filtering data by comparing it to smaller, high-quality
034
      sources such as textbooks, and filter out or keep data in hard manner (Brown et al., 2020; Wenzek et al.,
035
      2019). This strategy is frequently applied in the training of state-of-the-art language models such as
036   Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a) and datasets such as RefinedWeb (Penedo et al., 2023). However, this
037   process requires training proxy LLMs on data filtered at different thresholds, which is a less scalable
038   and tedious process. Moreover, this method imposes a hard threshold, meaning data is either fully
039   retained or discarded before training, without allowing for a more nuanced approach to learning or
040   unlearning based on data’s relevance and importance.
041
      In this paper, we explore whether training efficiency can be improved by adaptively weighting internet
042   data during training, based on its consistency with textbook-quality sources. We propose to use
043   textbooks to guide learning from internet during training. The intuition is that internet provide diverse
044   learning signals while textbooks provide high quality guidance – the model should learn more if the
045   data is consistent with target data or vice versa. We measure the consistency between internet (source
046   data) and textbooks (target data) by computing the cosine similarity between them in a latent space,
047   and weighting next token prediction cross entropy loss with cosine similarity. Since our method aims
048   to upweight and downweight data adaptively based on their consistency with textbook, we denote our
049   method as Textbook Consistency.
050   Empirically, we found that our method performs well in language model training at no measurable
051   additional cost. When using textbooks, which consist of high-quality public instruction tuning
052   datasets, as a consistency target for learning from large and diverse internet-scale datasets like
053   FineWeb (Penedo et al., 2023) and The Pile (Gao et al., 2020), our approach achieves significantly
      lower validation loss, better performance in downstream tasks, and a superior scaling trend. Our

      diverse internet data by aligning it with narrow but high-quality textbook datasets. The core idea
092

093

094   data sources: large, general-purpose internet datasets, which provide a broad range of information,
095   and curated textbook datasets, which are more focused and domain-specific but contain high-quality
096   content.

155   3     E XPERIMENT
156
157   Our study is based on the LLaMA (Dubey et al., 2024) architecture, and we consider model sizes of
158   375M, 1.2B, and 3B in our experiments. Although we explore larger models like the 3B size, the
159   majority of our experiments focus on the 1.2B model. The implementation is in Jax/Flax (Bradbury
160   et al., 2018; Heek et al., 2023). We use batch sizes of 0.5M and 1M, sampling a batch size of 0.5M
161   from a narrow domain. For embedding both the source and target, we utilize BERT-base (Devlin
      et al., 2018) from sentence-transformers (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019). We swap learning rate


                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      with grid search. Unlike standard language model training, where sentences are packed together to
163   maximize FLOP utilization on GPUs/TPUs, our approach requires computing sentence embeddings
164   and using embedding similarity to weight the next-token prediction loss. To prevent cross-sentence
165   attention during next-token prediction, we compute the embeddings after loading the data. We then
166   pack the sentences and apply an attention segmentation mask to ensure that attention is restricted
167   within each sentence, with no interaction between different sentences.
168
      We use muP (Yang et al., 2022) to parameterize the model and conduct proxy experiments to
169   confirm that the learning rate and other hyperparameters optimized for our small 375M model can be
170   successfully applied to larger models. When applying our hyperparameter search method to the C4
171   training set, our 375M baseline model achieves a test set loss of 2.58 on C4, which improves upon
172   the 2.7 loss reported in Appendix G of Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) for a similarly sized model.
173   This result demonstrates the strength of our baseline model compared to the current state of the art.
174
      We report the number of tokens consumed during training. The computational cost (FLOPs) incurred
175   by the embedding model is less than 0.5% of the total training FLOPs, even for the smallest 375M
176   model. This percentage decreases further for larger models, such as the 1.2B, 3B, and other larger
177   LLMs. For all methods, our experiments are conducted on 64 TPUv4 chips on Google Cloud,
178   equivalent to 32 Nvidia A100. We use bf16 for activation and fp32 for parameters and gradients. We
179   use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov, 2017) with max gradient norm 1.0. For the learning rate
180   schedule, we use linear warmup and cosine decay.
181   We apply Textbook Consistency to two datasets: FineWeb and The Pile. The Pile is an earlier,
182   widely-used dataset in the community. FineWeb, on the other hand, is a recent state-of-the-art,
183   high-quality dataset, carefully curated through both manual and model-based filtering techniques.
184
      We list some details about the high-quality datasets used as textbooks.
185
186   • OpenHemes is a high-quality dataset (Teknium, 2023). The dataset consists of questions and answers
187     sourced from benchmarks and user and AI model conversations. Each turn in a conversation has
188     two fields: a "from" field, which denotes the role of that turn, and a "value" field, which contains the
189     actual text. For our embedding purposes, we format each conversation as a sentence.
190   • MetaMathQA is a math-focused dataset (Yu et al., 2023) that contains math questions and answers.
191   • EvoInstruct is a dataset containing conversations on various topics (Xu et al., 2023). Similar to the
192     above, we format each conversation as a sentence before computing embeddings.
193
      Other high-quality textbook datasets, such as OpenOrca (Lian et al., 2023) and UltraChat (Ding
194
      et al., 2023), can be also included into textbook, but we leave them for future work. We combine
195   EvoInstruct, MetaMathQA, and OpenHemes. We found that it is also important to mix in high-quality
196   internet datasets, such as C4 (Raffel et al., 2020), to achieve higher diversity. The corresponding
197   ablation study is presented in the experimental section. We randomly sampled 150M tokens from C4,
198   combined with about 50M tokens from the pre-processed textbooks, giving a total of 200M tokens in
199   the post-processed dataset, which we use for our Textbook Consistency training.

461   examples) and the broader ExoRL dataset (diverse but noisy behaviors).
462   We compare behavior cloning (BC) on demonstrations with BC applied to the top 10% of high-return
463   trajectories from diverse ExoRL (BC-10%). Both are widely used and effective approaches. Table 3
464   shows returns achieved across various simulated robotics tasks using different methods. The results
465   demonstrate that Textbook Consistency outperforms all other methods on average and across most
466   tasks. ExoRL and BC-10% ExoRL yields relatively low rewards due to ExoRL is diverse and
467   low-reward, BC Demo outperforms BC-10% on ExoRL, combining demonstrations with ExoRL (BC
468
      Demo + BC-10% ExoRL) achieves higher performance. Textbook Consistency, which adaptively
      balances consistency between ExoRL and demonstration data, achieves the highest rewards overall.
469
470       Takeaway: Textbook Consistency effectively combines demonstration data with diverse but low-
471       reward behaviors from the ExoRL dataset, outperforming baselines in simulated robotics tasks.
472
473
474   4     R ELATED W ORK
475
476   Data Filtering. Data filtering has been a high-impact and active area of research for language model
477
      training (Brown et al., 2020). In addition to basics such as duplicate removal, methods include
      filtering data based on similarity to Wikipedia (Gururangan et al., 2022; Wenzek et al., 2019; Touvron
478
      et al., 2023a), heuristic-based (e.g., language and item count filtering), perplexity filtering, and
479
      hand curation (Penedo et al., 2023; Abbas et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Penedo et al., 2024). Further
480   methods propose to filter pretraining data so that the resulting LLM will achieve higher scores on given
481   benchmarks. This is done by selecting training data that is similar to data from a given benchmark,
482   such as based on n-gram overlap (Xie et al., 2023), embedding similarity (Everaert and Potts, 2023),
483   or loss-performance correlation coefficients from existing pretrained models (Thrush et al., 2024);
484   or less scalable approaches that involve training proxy LLMs using various data mixtures (Touvron
485   et al., 2023a; Ilyas et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Engstrom et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Prior research
      conducted extensive comparison of pretraining data selection techniques (Li et al., 2024) and found


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      that many of these techniques have yet to show significant improvements. The current state-of-the-art
487   across many tasks remains fairly basic: typically, a fixed fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2017) or BERT
488   (Devlin et al., 2018) classifier, applied after comprehensive deduplication and filtering. Another line
489   of research focus on curriculum-based online data selection of challenging samples (Jiang et al., 2019;
490   Loshchilov and Hutter, 2015; Katharopoulos and Fleuret, 2018) requires proxy models to determine
491   difficulty. This approach is computationally expensive, limiting its scalability. Our work is motivated
492   by whether we can efficiently adjust the importance of internet-sourced examples based on their
493   consistency with high-quality textbooks. We provide compelling evidence supporting this hypothesis
494   – demonstrating that a simple adaptive weighting method, based on textbook-internet consistency, can
495
      significantly improve training efficiency and model performance.
496   Data Mixing. Training datasets consisting of data from different domains or sources (for example,
497   web text, code, and Wikipedia) raise an important challenge for the data curation process: determining
498   the percentage of data that should come from each source, referred to as data mixing. These data
499   mixing methods include using heuristics (such as human judgment) (Gao et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
500   2023b), or using a set of predefined configurations (Soboleva et al., 2023), or empirically determining
501
      the best domain weights according to some downstream evaluation (Du et al., 2022). Other research
      has explored more principled approaches (Albalak et al., 2024b; 2023; Xie et al., 2023; Thudi and
502
      Maddison, 2024) using theories such as multi-armed bandits and distributionally robust optimization.
503
      In addition, clustering-based rebalancing methods for data sampling (Shao et al., 2024) have been
504   proposed, outperforming both uniform and other cluster-based sampling methods. Clustering-based
505   method dynamically adjusts data sampling to rebalance data, while our method dynamically adjusts
506   weights to be consistent with textbooks. Further methods have been proposed, including the use of
507   learning-based strategies that optimize domain proportions through iterative training of both reference
508   and proxy models (Fan et al., 2023), skills-based selections (Chen et al., 2024), dynamically updates
509   the composition of sampled data based on varying losses across different domains (Xia et al., 2023),
510   and simultaneously models the behaviors of data quantity and mixing weights using proxy models (Ge
511   et al., 2024). These methods are primarily focused on mixing and balancing data sources. Our method
512
      takes an orthogonal approach: Textbook Consistency focuses on training time adaptive weighting
      using textbooks from the target domain.
513
514
515
516   5    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we present Textbook Consistency, a novel approach to enhancing the training efficiency
519   of large language models by leveraging textbooks as guiding signals to adaptively weight internet-
520   scale data. Our method dynamically adjusts the importance of data samples based on their cosine
521   similarity to textbook content within a latent space. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that Textbook
522   Consistency can substantially reduce training cost and improve training efficiency by more than
523   two times, while consistently improving model performance across a wide range of benchmarks
524   as well as non-language tasks. Our experimental results indicate that Textbook Consistency is a
525
      computationally cost-free technique that improve pretraining efficiency twofold on state-of-the-art,
      curated datasets. This efficiency enables further advancing and scaling both model and data sizes, and
526
      as our experiments have shown, the advantage of Textbook Consistency becomes more significant
527
      as the scale increases. Textbook Consistency offers several key advantages over conventional data
528   curation techniques: it is straightforward to implement, introduces no additional computational
529   overhead, and can be straightforwardly integrated with existing data filtering methods.
530
531
      Limitations. Although our Textbook Consistency is effective, it has several limitations. Currently, it
      uses embedding models to measure the consistency between textbooks and internet sources, which
532
      does not take advantage of large language models, or the model being trained itself. However,
533
      this limitation could be addressed by incorporating advanced models directly. Our study also has
534   limitations in scale. We experimented with models ranging from 375M to 3B parameters, trained
535   on datasets from 200M to 80B tokens. While our token-to-parameter ratios (up to 30-60) exceed
536   Chinchilla’s optimal ratio 20, our largest models are still significantly smaller than contemporary
537   LLMs, which often surpass 100B parameters. This scale disparity may limit the direct applicability
538   of our findings to these larger models. Extrapolating our results to estimate the performance and
539   behaviors of much larger LLMs should be done cautiously, as different scaling laws may apply
      beyond the ranges we explored.
