009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011            Instruction-based fine-tuning of large language models (LLMs) has achieved remarkable success
012            in various natural language processing (NLP) tasks. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) meth-
013            ods, such as Mixture of LoRA Experts (MoLE), combine the efficiency of Low-Rank Adaptation
014            (LoRA) with the versatility of Mixture of Experts (MoE) models, demonstrating significant potential
015            for handling multiple downstream tasks. However, the existing routing mechanisms for MoLE often
016            involve a trade-off between computational efficiency and predictive accuracy, and they fail to fully
017            address the diverse expert selection demands across different transformer layers. In this work, we
018            propose DYN M O LE, a hybrid routing strategy that dynamically adjusts expert selection based on the
019
               Tsallis entropy of the router’s probability distribution. This approach mitigates router uncertainty,
               enhances stability, and promotes more equitable expert participation, leading to faster convergence
020
               and improved model performance. Additionally, we introduce an auxiliary loss based on Tsallis
021
               entropy to further guide the model toward convergence with reduced uncertainty, thereby improving
022            training stability and performance. Our extensive experiments on commonsense reasoning bench-
023            marks demonstrate that DYN M O LE achieves substantial performance improvements, outperforming
024            LoRA by 9.6% and surpassing the state-of-the-art MoLE method, MoLA, by 2.3%. We also conduct
025            a comprehensive ablation study to evaluate the contributions of DYN M O LE’s key components.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Instruction-based fine-tuning of large language models (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Touvron
031   et al., 2023a;b) for various downstream tasks has achieved remarkable proficiency in natural language processing
032   tasks (Chung et al., 2022; Iyer et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024). To significantly reduce the computational and mem-
033   ory resources required for full parameter fine-tuning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods have emerged (Houlsby
034   et al., 2019; Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Ben-Zaken et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). Among these, LoRA (Hu
035   et al., 2021) has gained popularity due to its ability to reduce substantial computational costs. To maintain both cross-
036   task generalization and computational efficiency, a promising solution (Yang et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Feng et al.,
037   2024) is to design an architecture that combines the resource-efficient features of LoRA with the versatility of Mixture
038   of Experts (MoE) models (Wu et al., 2024a; Dou et al., 2024; Gou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024).
039
      These methods are often referred to as Mixture of LoRA Experts (MoLE). The routing mechanisms of these MoLE
      methods are mostly derived from standard MoE models, where a fixed number of expert networks are activated. How-
040
      ever, recent studies indicate that the requirements for experts vary across different transformer layers (Gao et al., 2024;
041
      Zeng et al., 2024), suggesting that the routing mechanism requires further modifications to account for these factors.
042
043   Current routing mechanisms (Cai et al., 2024) can be broadly classified into two categories: 1) Soft Routing: These
044   methods activate all expert networks for each input token, which typically leads to improvement in prediction accu-
045
      racy (Ma et al., 2018; Nie et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024c; Dou et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024). However, this comes at
      the cost of significant computational overhead, as all experts are involved in the computation (Shazeer et al., 2017).
046
      2) Sparse Routing: These approaches enhance model efficiency by activating only a subset of experts (Shazeer et al.,
047
      2017). Some techniques route each token to a single expert (Fedus et al., 2022), while others activate multiple experts,
048   such as using Top-K (Zhou et al., 2022) or Top-P (Huang et al., 2024), or employing uncertainty-based routing (Wu
049   et al., 2024b). Although sparse routing improves parameter efficiency, it often results in an imbalanced workload
050   among experts, making it necessary to include an auxiliary loss functions to ensure the balance. Though both of these
051   routing techniques aim to select the optimal set of experts for each input token, neither provides a fully comprehensive
052   solution that accounts for the diverse and complex factors affecting model performance, which raises a key question:
053   How can we design a hybrid routing approach that considers these factors holistically to provide a more complete
      solution for MoE and MoLE models?

076   To answer this question, several critical challenges emerge: 1) Inconsistent Expert Selection occurs when flat prob-
077   ability distributions lead to similar inputs activating different experts, resulting in unstable expert training. 2) Varied
078   Expert Requirements across the model, as noted by Gao et al. (2024); Zeng et al. (2024), leads to uneven expert loads
079   when the number of activated experts is fixed across all layers. Finally, 3) Fluctuating Gradient Updates resulting
080   from uncertain routing decisions, cause fluctuations in gradient flows, which adversely affect convergence speed and
081   stability during model training. These challenges are illustrated in Figure 1.
082   Our Insights: To address these challenges, we propose DYN M O LE (Dynamic Routing for Mixture of LoRA Experts),
083   a hybrid routing approach designed to reduce router uncertainty in Mixture of LoRA Experts adapters for parameter-
084   efficient fine-tuning of large language models. Our approach leverages the mathematical properties of Tsallis en-
085   tropy (Tsallis, 1988), a generalized entropy measure, to develop adaptive routing strategies that effectively minimize
086   router uncertainty. Furthermore, we introduce an auxiliary loss based on Tsallis entropy to guide the model towards
087   convergence with reduced uncertainty, thus improving training stability and performance. By preventing over-reliance
088   on certain experts and promoting more equitable engagement across all experts, this method fosters a diverse and ro-
089   bust set of expert contributions. This approach not only optimizes computational resource allocation but also enhances
090
      overall model performance by improving decision consistency and stability during training.

      2.1   M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS
109
110   First introduced in 1991 by Jacobs et al. (1991), the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture has seen a resurgence in
111   the context of modern large-scale models, largely attributed to the work of Shazeer et al. (2017), Mustafa et al. (2022),
112   Lepikhin et al. (2020), and Fedus et al. (2022). Their contributions have made MoE a promising approach for scaling
113   models without significantly increasing computational overhead.
114   Each MoE layer consists of N independent networks, referred to as experts, denoted by {Ei }N  i=1 , along with a gating
115   function G, which assigns weights to each expert based on a probability distribution. The forward propagation process
116   fMoE for a given input x can be mathematically expressed as:
117
118                                                            N
                                                               X
119                                               fMoE (x) =         G(x)i · Ei (x),                                       (1)
120                                                            i=1
121
122   where router logits G(x)i represent the routing probabilities for each expert. Each expert Ei (x) produces an output
123   based on the input x, allowing the MoE architecture to leverage the strengths of multiple specialized models.
124   In this paper, we focus exclusively on routing strategies for MoLE due to the computational resource limitations of our
125   team. The pre-training and fine-tuning of MoE models require computational resources beyond our current capacity.

      2.2   M IXTURE - OF -L O RA-E XPERTS
128
129   The MoLE architecture (Wu et al., 2024c) extends the traditional Mixture of Experts approach by integrating Low-
130   Rank Adaptation into expert layers, significantly enhancing computational efficiency. In a MoLE layer, each expert
131   Ei (·) is a LoRA-enhanced module that updates only a subset of parameters while leveraging the pretrained knowledge
132   of the base model.
133
      Low-Rank Adaptation, introduced by Hu et al. (2021), proposes a method that adjusts only a small number of addi-
134   tional parameters, rather than updating the entire weight matrix of the model. A LoRA block consists of two matrices,
135   B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k , where d and k represent the dimensions of the pretrained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k in
136   large language models. The parameter r is the low-rank dimension, with r ≪ min(d, k). The updated weights W ′ are
137   computed as:
138
139
                                                 W ′ = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA,                                                  (2)
140
141   where ∆W = BA represents the LoRA-induced weight update. Formally, given N experts in a MoLE layer, denoted
142   by {Ei }N
              i=1 , and router logits G(x)i representing the routing probabilities for each expert, the forward propagation
143   process fMoLE for a given hidden state x is calculated as:
144
145                                                                           N
                                                                              X
146                                  fMoLE (x) = W0 x + ∆W x = W0 x +               G(x)i · Ei (x),                        (3)
147                                                                           i=1
148
149   where W0 denotes the pretrained weights of the base model, and ∆W represents the weight updates generated by the
150   LoRA-enhanced experts. Each expert Ei (x) computes its output using the LoRA update rule:
151
152                                                      Ei (x) = Bi Ai x,                                                 (4)
153
154   with Bi ∈ Rd×r and Ai ∈ Rr×k . The low-rank matrix multiplication significantly reduces the number of trainable
155   parameters, improving memory efficiency and accelerating fine-tuning compared to standard MoE architectures. By
156   incorporating the parameter-efficient updates of LoRA, MoLE significantly enhances both computational efficiency
157   and model performance, especially in scenarios that require fine-tuning across multiple tasks.

308   4     DYN M O LE
309
310   In this section, we present the routing algorithm used in DYN M O LE, which integrates entropy-based selection across
311   various routing strategies to efficiently allocate tokens to experts, as shown in Figure 3(c). This approach, termed
312   dynamic routing, leverages Tsallis entropy to dynamically switch between soft routing, top-p routing, and top-k
313   routing mechanisms. By assigning the most suitable experts to each token, we reduce routing uncertainty and improve
314   model efficiency. Additionally, we incorporate Tsallis entropy into an auxiliary loss to guide the model towards
315   convergence with reduced uncertainty.
316
317   4.1   E NTROPY- BASED I NTELLIGENT H YBRID ROUTING
318
319   In this study, we use Tsallis entropy to capture the deviation from the ideal peaked distribution, providing greater
320
      flexibility than KL divergence. While Top-k and Top-p routing are effective, both struggle when the router has high
      uncertainty, leading to nearly uniform probability distributions and reducing expert prioritization, which results in
321
      suboptimal performance.
322
323   To address this, we propose DYN M O LE, a hybrid strategy that dynamically adjusts routing based on the entropy of
      each token. For high-entropy tokens, indicating greater uncertainty, we use soft routing, allowing the model to select

465   5.3.1    I MPACT OF D IFFERENT E NTROPY S ETTINGS ON DYN M O LE’ S P ERFORMANCE
466
467   In this part, we primarily discuss the impact of three entropy-related factors on model performance:
468   Entropy Loss Coefficient refers to a key parameter that balances the proportions of Tsallis entropy loss and load
469   balance loss (Equation 19). We designed different β values ranging from 1 × 10−4 to 1 × 10−2 . Figure 4(a) shows that
470   our findings indicated an entropy router loss coefficient of 1 × 10−2 achieved the highest average accuracy, effectively
471   addressing the challenges of imbalanced expert selection. Conversely, disabling the entropy router loss or employing
472   excessively high coefficients disrupts the balance between the two losses, leading to suboptimal performance.
473
      Entropic Index is the parameter q in Tsallis entropy (Equation 9), which controls the degree of non-extensivity in
474   the system. It adjusts how much weight is given to rare versus frequent events in the routing mechanism. When
475   q = 1, Tsallis entropy reverts to Shannon entropy, treating all token-routing decisions uniformly. However, varying q
476   allows the model to emphasize or de-emphasize token assignments to experts based on their likelihood, influencing the
477   balance between exploration (specialization of experts) and exploitation (generalization). We conducted experiments
478   by selecting the entropic index within the range of 1.0 to 1.4 and find that an entropic index of 1.1 yielded the best
479   overall performance(Figure 4(b)), suggesting that introducing Tsallis entropy rather than Shannon entropy allows for
480   better adaptation to task complexity. Deviating from this optimal value, either by increasing or decreasing q, led
481   to reduced accuracy. This indicates the critical role of q in fine-tuning the routing strategy and balancing expert
482
      specialization with generalization.
483   Entropy Threshold defines the soft routing threshold hthreshold in DYN M O LE. Typically, tokens with higher entropy
484   lead to unclear routing decisions. In our method, we send high-entropy tokens to all experts through soft routing,
485   allowing them to participate in the gradient update. Therefore, setting a reasonable threshold to constrain the soft
      routing algorithm is crucial. We collect the performance data for models with the entropy threshold set from 0.7 to
