                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep State Space Models (SSMs), such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024), have
010            emerged as powerful tools for language modeling, offering high performance with
011            efficient inference and linear scaling in sequence length. However, the application
012            of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods to SSM-based models remains
013            largely unexplored. This paper aims to systematically study two key questions:
014            (i) How do existing PEFT methods perform on SSM-based models? (ii) Which
015            modules are most effective for fine-tuning? We conduct an empirical benchmark of
016            four basic PEFT methods on SSM-based models. Our findings reveal that prompt-
017            based methods (e.g., prefix-tuning) are no longer effective, an empirical result
018
               further supported by theoretical analysis. In contrast, LoRA remains effective for
               SSM-based models. We further investigate the optimal application of LoRA within
019
               these models, demonstrating both theoretically and experimentally that applying
020
               LoRA to linear projection matrices without modifying SSM modules yields the
021            best results, as LoRA is not effective at tuning SSM modules. To further improve
022            performance, we introduce LoRA with Selective Dimension tuning (SDLoRA),
023            which selectively updates certain channels and states on SSM modules while
024            applying LoRA to linear projection matrices. Extensive experimental results show
025            that this approach outperforms standard LoRA.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Over the past two years, Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023;
031   Brown et al., 2020) have achieved groundbreaking performance and are now widely used in daily life.
032   Many models use the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), with its attention mechanism
033
      essential in predicting subsequent tokens based on context. Each token computes attention scores with
      every preceding one, selectively focusing only on the most relevant during processing. This, however,
034
      creates quadratic time complexity, posing challenges when dealing with long sequences. In response,
035
      various alternative architectures like Linear Attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), RWKV (Peng
036   et al., 2023), RetNet (Sun et al., 2023), and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) have been developed to operate
037   with subquadratic time complexity.
038
039
      As the most popular subquadratic-time architecture currently serving as an alternative to Transformers,
      SSMs (Gu et al., 2021; 2022b;a; Gu & Dao, 2024) achieve efficient training and inference. SSMs
040
      are closely related to linear RNNs, which maintain a hidden state to encapsulate the information of
041
      previous tokens. When a new input token is introduced, the prediction of the next token involves only
042   operations on this hidden state and the new token, which enhances inference efficiency. To overcome
043   the limitation of RNNs, which cannot be trained in parallel, S4 (Gu et al., 2022b;a) leverages its
044   linearity, enabling it to adopt a convolutional form during training, facilitating parallel computation.
045   Consequently, SSMs are highly efficient and have demonstrated success in numerous long-sequence
046   tasks (Gu et al., 2022b;a). Recently, a new series of SSM models, Mamba (Mamba-I (Gu & Dao,
047   2024) and Mamba-II (Dao & Gu, 2024)), have achieved Transformer-level performance in language
048   modeling. In the main paper, we primarily focus on the deep S4 model and Mamba-I, while deferring
049   experiments involving Mamba-II and the hybrid model, Jamba (Lieber et al., 2024), to the appendix.
050
      Unless otherwise specified, “Mamba” refers to Mamba-I for simplicity of notation. The deep S4
      model, serving as the foundational architecture, readily extends its properties to other variants, while
051
      Mamba has emerged as one of the most popular SSM-based models.
052
053   Consequently, we expect fine-tuning these pretrained SSMs for downstream tasks will become a
      crucial problem in the near future. While fine-tuning the entire model is expensive and inefficient,

054
      numerous Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; He
055   et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; 2022) have been
056   developed for efficient adaptation under resource constraints. Notably, most popular PEFT methods
057   fall into two categories: (i) prompt-based tuning, which involves modifying the input sequence (Lester
058   et al., 2021) or tuning the sequence at each layer (Li & Liang, 2021); and (ii) parameter-based tuning,
059   which directly updates the model parameters, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), which modifies the
060   weight matrices, and BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022), which updates only the bias terms.
061   Despite the success that existing PEFT methods have achieved in adapting Transformer-based models,
062   their efficacy in adapting SSM-based models remains largely unexplored, leaving many interesting
063   questions open. For instance, are existing popular PEFT methods still effective for SSM-based
064   models? If they are applicable, what is the optimal way to apply these methods to SSM-based models,
065   and which parameters should be updated? If not, can we develop variants specifically tailored for
066   SSMs that perform better? To answer these questions, to the best of our knowledge, we conduct the
067   first comprehensive study of PEFT on SSM-based models, both theoretically and empirically.
068   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to benchmark existing PEFT methods on SSM-based
069   models. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that (Finding 1) prompt-based PEFT
070   methods are no longer effective for SSM-based models, and (Finding 2) LoRA remains effective
071   on SSM-based models. Meanwhile, the two major components of SSM-based models are the SSM
072   module, which functions analogously to attention in Transformers, and linear projection matrices,
073   which are similar to feed-forward layers. We next investigate which part of the model is more effective
074   for applying PEFT. We empirically find that (Finding 3) applying LoRA to linear projection matrices
075
      without modifying the SSM module is already effective, while the most effective linear projection
      matrices differ depending on the dataset. Notably, Findings 1 and 3 are supported by our theoretical
076
      analysis. While LoRA is not effective for tuning SSM modules, theoretically, tuning additional SSM
077
      modules increases expressivity. Finally, we analyze the architecture of SSM-based models using the
078   theoretical framework of Giannou et al. (2023) and Zeng & Lee (2024). We show that, in addition to
079   applying LoRA to linear projection matrices, Selectively updating the channel and state Dimensions
080   of SSM modules further enhances performance. We dub this method as SDLoRA, the first PEFT
081   method tailored for SSM-based models. Through extensive experiments, we observe that (Finding 4)
082   SDLoRA outperforms LoRA alone in fine-tuning SSM-based models.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
086
087
088   State Space Models (SSMs). Linear State-Space Layers (LSSL) represent one of the earliest
089   SSM layers utilized in deep learning, functioning as continuous-time, recurrent, and convolutional
090   models (Gu et al., 2021). LSSL employs HiPPO theory (Gu et al., 2020) to initialize the state
091   matrix A, enabling the capture of long dependencies. However, LSSL is computationally expensive,
092   limiting its practical application. Gu et al. (2022b) introduced Structured State Space Models (S4),
093
      which optimize computation efficiency by employing a structured state matrix A. Gupta et al.
      (2022) proposed DSS, which simplifies the model by using a diagonal matrix for A and empirically
094
      demonstrated that it suffices to achieve performance comparable to S4. Further, Gu et al. (2022a)
095
      provided a theoretical explanation for the effectiveness of the diagonal state matrix A in DSS and
096   introduced S4D, which offers various initialization methods for A. Subsequently, the diagonal
097   structure of the state matrix A has been adopted in follow-up methods (Gu & Dao, 2024). Despite
098   differences in optimization algorithms, we refer to S4 and its close variants, including DSS and S4D,
099   collectively as S4. This terminology encompasses models that maintain the standard discrete-time
100   SSM form with a diagonal state matrix.
101
      Despite of the remarkable performance of SSMs on certain tasks of sequence modeling, SSMs still
102   showed worse performance than Transformers on language modeling. Fu et al. (2022) transitioned
103   from synthetic language modeling tasks to real language modeling tasks with SSMs. They proposed
104   H3, which is inspired by Linear Attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), introducing both diagonal
105   SSM and shift SSM. Recently, Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024; Dao & Gu, 2024) escaped from linear time
106   invariance (LTI) modeling by introducing input-dependent terms and achieved better performance
107   than Transformer on language modeling. Furthermore, several hybrid models (Lieber et al., 2024;
      Park et al., 2024) tried to exploit the advantages of both SSMs and Transformers.

108
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). Due to the increase in model size, PEFT methods have
109   gained increasing popularity as they achieve good performance while being much more efficient
110   compared to full model updating (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; He et al., 2021; Li & Liang,
111   2021; Lester et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; 2022). Most of the existing popular PEFT
112   methods fall into two categories: (i) prompt-based methods (Li & Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021;
113   Liu et al., 2021; 2022), and (ii) parameter tuning methods (Donahue et al., 2014; Yosinski et al., 2014;
114   Hu et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022). Common prompt-based methods include prompt tuning (Lester
115   et al., 2021), and prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021). Prompt tuning prepends a sequence of learnable
116   virtual tokens, which are continuous vectors. Prefix-tuning further expands on Prompt tuning by
117
      prepending tokens across the model’s depth, making it more powerful. Therefore, our analysis of
      prompt-based methods’ limitations will focus on prefix-tuning, with the findings also applicable to
118
      the other prompt-based methods. Conversely, parameter tuning methods, which originated from
119
      traditional transfer learning practices, typically involve freezing the initial layers and only tuning
120   the last few layers (Donahue et al., 2014; Yosinski et al., 2014). In recent years, more effective and
121   innovative parameter tuning approaches have emerged (Hu et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2022). The
122   widely used Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) updates a subset of parameters (e.g., attention layers of a
123   Transformer) in a low-rank manner. BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022), focuses on tuning only the bias terms
124   of a pretrained model. In Sec. A, we provide a more detailed description of these baseline methods.
125
      Numerous efforts have been made to theoretically understand existing PEFT methods. For prompt-
126   based methods, Wang et al. (2023b), Petrov et al. (2024), and Oymak et al. (2023) have theoretically
127   analyzed the effectiveness and limitations of prompt tuning and prefix-tuning for Transformer-based
128   models. For LoRA, Zeng & Lee (2024) explored its expressive power by demonstrating that even a
129   randomly initialized model can be adapted to match any smaller target model using LoRA. Some
130   of our theoretical analysis draws upon the framework established by Zeng & Lee (2024). Jang et al.
131   (2024) conducted a theoretical exploration of LoRA within the neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime.

261   4.1   L IMITATIONS OF A PPLYING E XISTING P ROMPT- BASED M ETHODS ON SSM S

      This part addresses our first question arised in this section: Why do existing prompt-based PEFT
      methods lose effectiveness when applied to SSM-based models? We approach this by establishing an
264
      upper bound on the performance of existing prompt-based PEFT methods.
265
266   A key feature of SSMs is their next-token prediction mechanism, which relies solely on the current
267   token and hidden states, without considering previous tokens directly. The hidden states encapsulate
268   all information from preceding tokens. Consequently, prepending tokens to an SSM is functionally
269
      equivalent to tuning the initial state, as demonstrated by the following proposition. The formal version
      and proof of Proposition 1 are presented in Sec. C.3.


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Proposition 1 (Informal: Expressivity of Prefix-Tuning on SSMs). The maximum expressiveness
271   achievable via prefix-tuning on SSMs is equivalent to the expressiveness of solely tuning the initial
272   hidden state h0 .
273
274   To evaluate the performance of initial state tuning, we conducted experiments on the GLUE bench-
275   mark, comparing prompt-tuning, prefix-tuning, initial state tuning, and LoRA across seven GLUE
276   tasks. Table 2 presents our findings. The results demonstrate that initial state tuning generally
277
      outperforms prefix-tuning, corroborating our analysis. However, LoRA significantly surpasses initial
      state tuning in performance. These observations lead us to conclude that the limitations of initial state
278
      tuning inherently constrain the potential of existing prompt-based methods, preventing them from
279
      outperforming LoRA in the context of SSM-based models. While the reason for the underperfor-
280   mance of initial state tuning is unclear, we identify explaining it as an interesting direction for future
281   research. Nevertheless, we propose a plausible explanation. We hypothesize that SSM’s exclusive
282   reliance on hidden states, without direct access to previous tokens or states, severely restricts the
283   impact of initial state tuning, particularly for long sequences. This aligns with the findings of Fu et al.
284   (2022), which demonstrate SSM’s limitations in recalling older tokens.

438   Theorem 4 (Expressive Power of SDLoRA on Deep S4 Models). Consider a D-dimensional input
439   sequence. Assume that the linear layers in the model have linear activation functions. Using SDLoRA,
440
      any deep S4 model with H hidden states per channel and L layers can be updated to accurately
      present any target deep S4 model without residual connections, having a reduced hidden state
441
      dimension H ⋆ < H, and fewer layers L⋆ < L. This can be achieved by selectively fine-tuning at
442
      most ⌈DL⋆ /L⌉ channels, H ⋆ hidden states on SSM modules, applying rank-⌈ LL⋆ ⌉ updates on linear
443   projection matrices and updating residual connections and biases at each layer, while additionally
444   fully fine-tuning the linear projection matrix of the last layer only.
445
446   This theorem demonstrates that a larger pretrained model requires selecting fewer channels and
447   hidden states at each layer. Furthermore, if the target task is less complex — evidenced by a smaller
448   target model with fewer layers L⋆ and hidden states H ⋆ — the number of channels and hidden
449   states needed is also reduced. This finding aligns with the theoretical analysis of LoRA presented in
450   Zeng & Lee (2024), which shows that larger pretrained models require fewer learnable parameters
451
      (referred to as “lower rank” in their context) during fine-tuning, especially for simpler tasks. Although
      this theorem is constrained by the assumptions of linear activations and the absence of residual
452
      connections in the target model, while also requiring fully fine-tuning the linear project matrix of last
453
      layer, our findings have broader implications. Our following experimental results suggest that these
454   findings generalize beyond these restrictions.

      6     C ONCLUSION
534   In this paper, we present the first study on the performance of PEFT methods applied to SSM-based
535   models. Our evaluation of existing PEFT methods provides valuable insights and guidelines for future
536   researchers to parameter-efficiently fine-tune SSM-based models to other domains. Moreover, we take
537   a first step in establishing a theoretical framework for studying PEFT methods on SSM-based models.
538   Furthermore, we introduce SDLoRA, the first PEFT method specifically tailored for SSM-based
539   models, which outperforms existing methods. While our work offers numerous valuable insights, we
      discuss limitations and further works in Sec. E.

      A    I N - DEPTH I NTRODUCTION OF BASELINES
757
758
      In this section, we provide a more detailed description of the baseline methods.
759
760
761   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) focuses on fine-tuning large models by
762   freezing most of the pretrained parameters and injecting trainable low-rank matrices into each layer
763
      of the Transformer’s architecture. The intuition behind using low-rank matrices comes from linear
      algebra, where a large matrix can be closely approximated by the product of two smaller matrices.
764
      The number of trainable parameters can be controlled with the rank of the low-rank matrices. LoRA
765
      also uses a scaling parameter (LoRA alpha) for the weight matrices to control the balance of the
766   original model weights and LoRA weights during training. After fine-tuning, LoRA weights can be
767   merged with the original model weights, introducing no additional inference overhead.
768
769
      Prompt Tuning (Lester et al., 2021). Prompt tuning freezes all model weights and prepends a
770   trainable soft prompt to the input prompt. The soft prompt consists of trainable virtual tokens, which
771   are continuous. At inference time, prompt tuning introduces an inference overhead based on the
772   number of virtual tokens used.
773
774
      Prefix-Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021). Prefix-tuning also prepends trainable tokens to the input like
775   prompt tuning but injects separate prefixes in every layer. For each Transformer layer, prefix-tuning
776   prepends trainable embeddings to the attention’s K and V matrix. The authors have found that
777   directly training these prefixes can lead to unstable training, so they propose to over-parameterize
778   them with a large MLP to increase training stability. After training, the MLP can be dropped. Like
779   prompt tuning, prefix-tuning introduces an inference overhead, scaling linearly with the number of
780   trainable embeddings.
781
782   BitFit (Zaken et al., 2022). BitFit is a simple but effective PEFT method that freezes all model
783   weights except the bias terms, consequently greatly reducing the number of trainable parameters. As
784   no additional parameters are added, no inference overhead occurs.
785

      B    D ETAILS OF DATASETS
788
789   In this paper, we consider five datasets across three domains: (i) Natural Language Understanding
790   (NLU), represented by GLUE (Wang et al., 2019); (ii) Natural Language Generation (NLG), including
791
      SAMSum (Gliwa et al., 2019), Spider (Yu et al., 2018) and DART (Nan et al., 2021); and (iii)
      Computer Vision (CV), represented by CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009).
792
793
794   GLUE (Wang et al., 2019). The GLUE (General Language Understanding Evaluation) benchmark
795   is a collection of datasets used for training, evaluating, and analyzing natural language understanding
796
      models across a range of diverse tasks. The benchmark includes nine sentence- or sentence-pair
      language understanding tasks that require various features of understanding, such as sentiment
797
      analysis, linguistic acceptability, semantic textual similarity, and question answering. We use seven
798
      datasets from the GLUE benchmark (RTE, MRPC, CoLA, SST-2, QNLI, QQP, MNLI) where the
799   model has to choose between two or three (for MNLI) different choices for the respective task. Except
800   for CoLA, we evaluate all used datasets with the accuracy metric. For CoLA, Matthews correlation is
801   employed.
802
803
      SAMSum (Gliwa et al., 2019). SAMSum is a dataset for dialogue summarization research, com-
804   prising approximately 16,000 synthetic text conversations with accompanying summaries. Created by
805   English-fluent linguists, these exchanges simulate real-world digital communications across various
806   topics and styles. The conversations range from informal to formal, incorporating elements like slang
807   and emoticons to reflect authentic messaging patterns. Each dialogue is paired with a concise, third-
808   person summary, capturing its essential content. This structure makes SAMSum particularly useful
809   for developing and evaluating automated summarization systems capable of processing conversational
      text.

832   databases, each with multiple tables. We follow Scholak et al. (2021) and use about 7,000 examples
833   for training and about 1,000 examples for validation, where we ignore sequences longer than 1536
834   tokens. The dataset consists of English question and SQL query pairs, which cover a wide range of
835   SQL operations including SELECT, WHERE, COUNT, GROUP BY, ORDER BY, JOIN, and more.
836   Given an English question and an SQL database scheme, the task for the model is to translate the
837   English question into an appropriate SQL statement. Evaluation is performed via accuracy where the
838   output is considered as correct if the model’s predicted SQL query and the included GT SQL query
839   give the same result when executed on the database. The dataset additionally categorizes each query
840
      into easy (25%), medium (40%), hard (20%), and extra hard (15%) based on the complexity of the
      required SQL statement. For evaluation, we report the execution accuracy of all categories.

845
      tured dataset designed for RDF-to-text (Resource Description Framework-to-text) generation with
846
      80,000+ instances. The DART benchmark is composed of a collection of structured data triples and
847   corresponding text summaries which are organized into different categories. The task of the DART
848   benchmark is to generate natural language summaries that correctly represent the given structured
849   data inputs. DART is typically evaluated with METEOR and BLEU.
850
851
852
853   CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). The CIFAR-10 (Canadian Institute For Advanced Research)
854   dataset is a collection of images that are commonly used to train machine learning and computer
855   vision algorithms. It is one of the most widely used datasets for image classification. The CIFAR-10
856   dataset contains 60,000 (50,000 for training, 10,000 for validation) 32×32 color images in 10 different
857   classes. The 10 different classes are: airplane, car, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, and truck.
858   There are 6,000 images of each class. For training, we center crop each image to 24×24 pixels and
859   flatten each image to a string, with a total of 24×24×3 words, where each word is a number between
860
      0-255 representing the respective pixel value. Although CIFAR-10 is a dataset for computer vision,
      previous work (Dinh et al., 2022) showed that Transformers can be adapted to the vision domain
861
      from the language domain, and we tested this ability on the state-space model.

1478   complex scenarios involving deep S4 models for both target and frozen architectures, incorporating

1480   Dimension Tuning (SDT) and (ii) SDLoRA. The key distinction between SDT and SDLoRA lies in
1481   their treatment of linear projection matrices. SDT exclusively updates the columns of weight matrices

1483

       E    L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORKS
1837
1838
       While our work offers numerous valuable insights, some limitations exist. Theoretically, our guar-
1839   antees for SDLoRA are limited to linear activations and require full fine-tuning of the last layer.
1840   However, our experiments demonstrate that SDLoRA does not suffer from these limitations in prac-
1841   tice. Removing such restrictions for SDLoRA in theory or developing new PEFT methods under more
1842   general theoretical cases is an interesting future direction. Additionally, our theory only demonstrates
1843   that updating a subset of channels and states is sufficient, without providing guidance on optimal
1844   selection. Our channel and state selection, based on a warmup stage and parameter magnitude, may
1845   not be optimal. Further investigation into the impact of channel/state selection and development of
1846   improved dimension selection algorithms presents an interesting avenue for future work. Lastly, our
1847
       work primarily focuses on SSM-based models. Studying PEFT methods on SSM-Transformer hybrid
       models (Lieber et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024), is an interesting future direction.
