009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The discovery of new molecular materials with desirable properties is essential for
012            technological advancements, from pharmaceuticals to renewable energy. However,
013            the discovery process is arduous, requiring many trial-and-error cycles of complex
014            and expensive experiments. Bayesian optimization (BO) is commonly used to find
015            and screen candidate molecules efficiently. However, it is unclear how to choose the
016            right molecular representations for a Bayesian surrogate model: While molecules
017            are 3-dimensional in nature, 3D features in BO have largely been underexplored.
018
               Indeed, 1D and 2D molecular features—which incur loss of information—are
               typically used. In this work, we study this discrepancy: Why have 3D features
019
               been overlooked for BO in materials discovery? To this end, we evaluate 3D
020
               features against standard lower-dimensional features. We assess their optimization
021            performance on real-world chemistry datasets, considering both various settings
022            such as low- & high-data regimes and transfer learning, and different types of
023            Bayesian surrogates. This amounts to the evaluation of 35 different setups per
024            dataset, totaling over 2100 distinct runs. Our large-scale work provides insights
025            and modeling guides to chemists and practitioners on the trade-offs between 1D,
026            2D, and 3D representations, in a bid to further accelerate materials discovery.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The discovery of new materials is crucial for technological advancements, yet experiments and
032   simulations in chemistry are often expensive in terms of time and computational resources (Tom
033
      et al., 2024; van Mourik et al., 2014). To overcome this, Bayesian optimization (BO) has emerged
      as a method for efficiently exploring the vast space of potential materials and guiding experimental
034
      efforts toward the most promising candidates for calculations in computational chemistry (Korovina
035
      et al., 2020; Li et al., 2024; Ranković et al., 2024). BO relies on training probabilistic surrogate
036   models, such as Gaussian processes (GPs) or neural networks (NNs), using feature vectors obtained
037   from various representations of molecules.
038
039
      In molecular discovery, how to best represent molecules remains unclear. Commonly, BO for
      materials discovery rely on simplified 1D and 2D representations (Felton et al., 2021; Griffiths
040
      et al., 2024; Häse et al., 2021), such as SMILES strings or 2D molecular graphs, which fail to
041
      capture the true 3D spatial and geometric complexities of molecules (Wigh et al., 2022). Recently,
042   3D GNNs have shown promise in extracting features from the geometric structure of molecules,
043   while respecting known physical symmetries, potentially offering superior predictive performance
044   (Batatia et al., 2022; Crivelli-Decker et al., 2024; Gilmer et al., 2017; Liao & Smidt, 2023). While
045   interest in 3D representations is growing (Li et al., 2022), much of the existing research has not fully
046   explored their applicability within BO for materials discovery, leaving a gap in understanding why
047   3D features—which is faithful to the 3D nature of molecules—are underused.
048   In this work, we address this question. We perform a comprehensive, systematic benchmark of
049   1D, 2D, and 3D molecular representations for materials discovery tasks. We leverage LLM-based
050   feature extractor (Kristiadi et al., 2024) to handle 1D SMILES representation, 2D message-passing
051   GNNs (Gilmer et al., 2017), and 3D equivariant GNNs (Liao & Smidt, 2023), combining them with
052   popular probabilistic surrogates like GPs and Bayesian NNs. We assess their BO performances
053   across various settings (e.g., transfer learning) and various data regimes. Using this setup, shown
      in Fig. 1, we compare these features-models-settings combination across 2100 runs to evaluate the

091   2.1      BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
092
093   BO addresses the problem of finding the global maximizer of an unknown objective function
094   f : X → Y. The goal is to find
095
                                         x∗ = arg max f (x),
                                                             x∈X
096
      where X represents an arbitrary d-dimensional search space. Here, f is assumed to be hard and
097
      expensive to evaluate. The challenge is, therefore, to minimize the number of such function evalua-
098
      tions when solving the above optimization problem (Shahriari et al., 2016). The key components
099   of BO are (i) a probabilistic belief p(f | Ωt ) over the target function f given past observation
100   Ωt = {(xi , f (xi ))}ti=1 and (ii) an acquisition function α : X → R, which guides where to evaluate
101   f next at step t + 1. The representational capacity of p(f | Ωt ) dictates how well-calibrated we can
102   approximate f . This, in turn, affects the exploration-exploitation capabilities of the overall system, a
103   critical factor in finding the optimal x∗ in as few steps as possible (Garnett et al., 2012).

109   collection {xi }ti=1 , the distribution of {f (xi )}ti=1 is a multivariate Gaussian. In particular, this

113   tuned via marginal-likelihood maximization.

      2.3   M OLECULAR FEATURE EXTRACTORS
130
131   Molecules can be expressed via various 1D, 2D, and 3D representations. These range from simple
132   strings like SMILES to more complex graph-based and geometric descriptors. SMILES offers a 1D
133   representation of molecular structures (Weininger, 1988). Graph-based representations leverage 2D
134   graphs of molecular bonds (Duvenaud et al., 2015), and 3D representations capture the full geometric
135   structure of molecules (Schütt et al., 2017). Before they can be used by a GP or BNN surrogate, these
136   representations are often transformed into feature vectors. The following are various features that are
137
      commonly used in materials discovery.
138
139
      Molecular fingerprints Molecular fingerprints are unique digital representations of molecular
      structures. They are generated by analyzing the atomic composition and connectivity within a
140
      molecule, resulting in a high-dimensional bit vector that uniquely identifies the molecule. One of
141
      the most commonly used are extended-circular fingerprints (Rogers & Hahn, 2010) which iterate
142   over node centers and assign bits according to sub-structures up to 2 edges away (Weininger, 1988).
143   Fingerprints capture structural information, such as the presence of specific functional groups or
144   molecular motifs. They are widely used in cheminformatics for tasks like similarity searching,
145   property prediction, and structure-activity relationship studies. In addition to fingerprints, other
146   molecular descriptors are often employed to characterize physicochemical properties.
147
148   Graph-neural-network features Molecular fingerprints are computationally efficient but lack the
149   structural richness required for complex tasks (He et al., 2021). Graph NNs (GNNs) have emerged as
150   effective methods to capture local structures in graph-structured data, such as graph representations
151
      of molecules. Traditionally, GNNs are used to process 2D graphs by passing messages between
      their hidden layers to learn graph features (Duvenaud et al., 2015; Gilmer et al., 2017). Recently,
152
      equivariant GNNs that leverage the symmetries in 3D molecular structures, further improving the
153
      representational power of traditional GNNs, have been proposed (Liao et al., 2024).
154
155
      Large-language-model features A significant advancement in the development of large neural
156   networks is the introduction of the architecture called transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), which serves
157   as the foundation for large language models (LLMs). Due to the large size of LLMs, training them
158   from scratch is computationally prohibitive (Sharir et al., 2020), but they are typically pre-trained in
159   a task-agnostic manner, allowing them to serve as meaningful priors for various tasks (Brown et al.,
160   2020). Recently, LLMs specifically tailored for chemistry-related applications have gained significant
161   traction, particularly in their ability to extract meaningful features from chemical data (Chithrananda
      et al., 2020; Schwaller et al., 2019). These models are trained on large-scale chemical databases via


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the molecules’ 1D string representations. This makes LLMs useful as molecular feature extractors
163   and has been used for materials discovery (Jablonka et al., 2023; Kristiadi et al., 2024).
164
165
      2.4   3D FEATURES
166
167   3D molecular representations capture the full geometric structure of a molecule, providing critical
168   information about atom positions, bond angles, and spatial orientation (Fang et al., 2022). This
169   spatial configuration is particularly relevant for tasks where molecular conformation plays a role
170   in determining chemical properties, such as catalysis or drug binding affinity (Turner et al., 2006).
171   Historically, 3D features have been more computationally expensive than 1D or 2D representations,
172   often requiring quantum mechanical calculations or molecular simulations to obtain accurate geome-
173   tries (van Mourik et al., 2014). Methods such as SchNet (Schütt et al., 2017) and more recently,
174   equivariant GNNs like EquiFormer (Liao & Smidt, 2023), have been developed to leverage these 3D
175
      structures by respecting their inherent symmetries, including rotational and translational invariance.
176
177   3     R ELATED W ORK
178
179   Various benchmarks have been developed for studying the effectiveness of BO surrogates in chemical
180   applications. Olympus (Häse et al., 2021) offers a suite of common optimization response surfaces
181   along with experimentally derived chemical prediction datasets for benchmarking. Similarly, Summit
182   (Felton et al., 2021) offers a suite of virtual chemical reactions for optimization. Works have
183   also studied and benchmarked the performance and uncertainty calibration of surrogate models
184   on molecular screening tasks (Graff et al., 2021; Griffiths et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2021; Tom
185   et al., 2023). Notably, these benchmarking works only consider (1D) string, molecular descriptors
186
      and fingerprints, and 2D graph representations of molecules. Our work complements these prior
      benchmarking efforts by investigating 3D representations that have largely been ignored.
187
188   Many sophisticated feature extractors have been used in BO for materials discovery. Strieth-Kalthoff
189   et al. (2024) leverage graph features to accelerate the discovery of organic laser emitters by optimizing
190   material properties across distributed labs in a closed-loop discovery system. However, they represent
191   molecules as 2D graphs and use 2D GNNs as feature extractors. Kristiadi et al. (2024) and Liu
192
      et al. (2024) study transformers and LLMs as feature extractors in BO for materials discovery.
      While they represent the state-of-the-art general-purpose class of architectures, notably, they take
193
      1D string representations of molecules (SMILES (Weininger, 1988), SELFIES (Krenn et al., 2020))
194
      as inputs. Furthermore, there has been research on building graph foundation models (Zhou et al.,
195   2023) utilizing molecular graph structures and self-supervised pretraining to achieve state-of-the-art
196   performance in various chemistry-related tasks. Our work, meanwhile, studies the scenario where 3D
197   molecular representations are used as the inputs of the feature extractor in material discovery tasks.

200   4     S ETUP
201
202   We investigate BO performance using 1D, 2D, and 3D representations of molecules. This section
203
      outlines our experimental setup, including datasets, feature extractors, tasks, sample complexities,
      and evaluation methods.
204
205
206   Datasets We use four datasets in our experiments: QM7 (Blum & Reymond, 2009; Montavon
207
      et al., 2013), QM9 (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014; Ruddigkeit et al., 2012), GEOM’s MoleculeNet and
      DRUGS (Axelrod & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2022; Wu et al., 2017). QM7 includes 7165 molecules with
208
      atomization energies (∆E) in kcal/mol and up to seven heavy atoms (C, N, O, S), while QM9 contains
209
      133 885 molecules with up to nine heavy atoms (C, N, O, F) and 12 properties. The MoleculeNet
210   dataset consists of benchmark datasets designed for molecular machine learning tasks and includes
211   28 295 molecules, covering tasks like quantum mechanics, physical chemistry, biophysics, and
212   physiology. GEOM provides an enhanced version of this dataset that includes conformers for each
213   example. On the other hand, GEOM also contains the DRUGS dataset, which provides molecular
214   geometries for drug-like molecules, with up to 91 heavy atoms and 317 928 molecules, which are
215   useful for studies involving conformational flexibility and geometric properties. The models were
      trained on QM9, which was split into a training set and a virtual library serving as the search space,


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ensuring that the best 10 observations remain in the virtual library. The virtual library and all other
217   datasets were used in the BO loop to evaluate the models.
218
219   Tasks Each model is trained for target property prediction and transfer learning. In the target prop-
220   erty prediction task, the model has a single readout layer trained to predict a specific property—e.g.
221   HOMO-LUMO gap (∆Egap in eV) from QM9, crucial in determining molecular reactivity and optical
222   properties; atomization energy (∆E in kcal/mol) from QM7, used to understand molecular stability;
223   and absolute energy (E in Hartree) from GEOM’s MoleculeNet and DRUGS, useful in studying
224   binding energy and potential energy in the molecular system. The model has n − 1 readout layers in
225   transfer learning, each trained on different tasks. We aim to assess whether a model trained on one set
226   of properties can still provide accurate predictions on different experimental datasets by fine-tuning
227
      only the final layer, evaluating its potential as a foundation model (Yao et al., 2024).
228
      Feature extractors We compare MPNN, which inherently leverages 2D molecular information,
229
      Equiformer v2 (Liao et al., 2024), an equivariant attention-based GNN which captures the full 3D
230
      geometric structure and symmetries of molecules, and MolFormer (Kristiadi et al., 2024; Ross
231   et al., 2022), a masked language model that operates on 1D SMILES representations, leveraging
232   transformers to capture both local and global chemical patterns. The GNNs serve as feature extractors
233   up to their respective readout layers, encoding molecules into high-dimensional embeddings before
234   making predictions on the target properties. To ensure consistency, the GNN feature extractors are
235   constrained to similar sizes, with each containing approximately 1.5 million parameters.
236
237   Surrogates We use each features in two surrogate models. Each surrogate, either GP or an NN
238   with LLA, uses the extracted features as inputs to provide a posterior distribution p(f | D). The LLA
239   surrogate consists of an NN with two hidden layers, as suggested by Li et al. (2024). As further
240
      baselines, we use (1) random search, which uniformly samples from the molecular space, and (2)
      GPs utilizing the Tanimoto kernel with 1D molecular fingerprints (Tripp et al., 2023).
241
242
      Evaluation We run 1000 iterations with 10 initial observations. For each run, we either subsample
243
      10 000 observations from our virtual libraries (QM9, and GEOM’s MoleculeNet and DRUGS) or use
244
      the entirety of the virtual library (QM7), repeating the processes for 15 different seeds, and reporting
245   their average and standard error. We further evaluate with the GAP metric (Jiang et al., 2020), defined
246   by GAP = (yi − y0 )/(y∗ − y0 ) where yi is the maximum observed value at step i and y ∗ is the true
247   optimal value. Note that the GAP metric is normalized in [0, 1] and is thus useful for comparing and
248   aggregating results across different datasets/problems.

253
254   QM7 For QM7, which features relatively simple molecules, highlights the surprising effectiveness
255   of molecular fingerprints. Notably, the 1D GP method performed slightly worse than more complex
256   models. In contrast, GP regression with binary encoded SMILES, serving as a baseline, demon-
257   strated that even simple 1D representations can capture sufficient information to remain competitive.
258   Although 2D models outperformed 3D models overall—particularly when combined with GP re-
259
      gressors—the performance gap was modest. While the 3D models saw slight improvements when
      paired with LLA, the gains were limited, suggesting that higher-dimensional representations may not
260
      be critical for simpler molecular structures like those in QM7. This is evident in Fig. 3, where the
261
      top-performing models for simpler tasks did not heavily rely on 3D data. The results of the LLM
262   were striking, as it outperformed all other models by a significant margin. Its ability to leverage
263   contextual information and generate accurate predictions, even for relatively simple molecules in the
264   QM7 dataset, demonstrated its superior generalization capabilities.
265
266   QM9 In contrast, the QM9 dataset, which features slightly more complex molecules, underscores
267   the limitations of 1D representations. Here, 2D MPNNs achieve the highest performance, and consis-
268   tently outperform 1D GP and RS methods, while 3D models only outperform RS. The differences
269   become even more pronounced with increasing molecular complexity as described by size. While
      2D models continue to demonstrate strong performance and stability, the 3D GNNs, particularly

284
      molecular size increases, 3D models fail to offer significant advantages, and in many cases, they
      underperform compared to 2D approaches. Additionally, the competitive performance of 1D models,
285

286
      scenarios. Although 1D models struggle with larger datasets and more complex molecular structures,

291   the trend observed before, where the LLM demonstrated remarkable versatility and accuracy.

296   outperform 3D models across most tasks, even for large drug-like molecules. Interestingly, simple
297   GP regression and random search performed similarly, suggesting that the models used may not
298   be sufficiently complex to outperform these benchmark methods. This indicates that larger models
299
      or further optimization would be necessary to see significant improvements beyond the baseline
      methods. The 3D models, while more suited for capturing subtle geometric features, do not provide
300
      a substantial performance increase, reinforcing that for property optimization in such datasets, 3D
301
      features may not be necessary unless ultra-high precision is required. The LLM achieved the most

303   particularly remarkable given the complexity and size of the molecules in this dataset. Despite the
304   strong performance of 2D models, the LLM’s ability to handle intricate molecular details allowed
305   it to excel far beyond both 2D and 3D representations. This suggests that the LLM’s contextual

469   Across all datasets examined LLMs consistently outperformed both 2D and 3D models, offering

471
      particularly evident in more complex datasets, such as GEOM DRUGS, where LLMs demonstrated a
      significant performance gap compared to other models, even for large, intricate molecules. Notably,
472
      2D representations also showed promise, outperforming 3D models across a wide range of tasks. The
473
      relative success of 1D and 2D models highlights the advantage of simpler representations, which not
474   only provide strong predictive accuracy but also strike an excellent balance between efficiency and
475   computational cost. This advantage is particularly noticeable in datasets traditionally associated with
476   3D models, where 3D geometric information was expected to offer an edge but instead provided only
477   marginal improvements at a higher computational cost.
478
      In addition, this study highlights the potential of transfer learning as a powerful strategy for improving
479
      model adaptability across diverse molecular tasks. The ability of LLMs to generalize well across
480   different datasets underscores the potential of transfer learning to enhance the versatility of machine
481   learning models, regardless of the molecular representation’s dimensionality. Future research should
482   focus on extending this work by exploring tasks where 3D information might be more important e.g.
483   protein docking. Investigating how BO performance scales with model size and complexity will also
484   be crucial for optimizing these approaches. Additionally, the exploration of graph foundation models
485   could open new avenues for representing and processing molecular data, combining the strengths of
      graph-based and large-scale foundation models.

779   The following are the training details of the surrogates we used in Section 5. We used HuggingFace’s
780   transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020) for MolFormer. For GPs, we use BoTorch (Balandat et al.,
781
      2020) to construct the surrogate function. The Tanimoto kernel is taken from Gauche (Griffiths
      et al., 2024). To optimize the marginal likelihood, we use Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a
782
      learning rate of 0.01 for 500 epochs. We constrain the GNNs to 1̃.5 million parameters, and further
783
      train Equifrormer v2 on noisy nodes. We optimize the GNNs with Adam with a learning rate of
784   1 × 10−4 and weight decay of 5 × 10−4 until convergence with early stopping at 20 epochs without
785   improvement with a batch size of 64. We anneal the learning rate with the cosine annealing scheme
786   (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016). On the other hand for LLA, our implementation is based on the
787   laplace-bayesopt package. The neural net used is a 2-hidden-layer multilayer perceptron with 50
788   hidden units on each layer along with the ReLU activation function. The Laplace approximation is
789   done post-hoc, and we tune the prior precision with the marginal likelihood for 100 iterations. The
790   Hessian is approximated with a Kronecker structure (Ritter et al., 2018).
