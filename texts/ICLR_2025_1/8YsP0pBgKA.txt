009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Counterfactual inference aims to estimate the counterfactual outcome given
012            knowledge of an observed treatment and the factual outcome, with broad applica-
013            tions in fields such as epidemiology, econometrics, and management science. In
014            this paper, we propose a principled approach for identifying and estimating the
015            counterfactual outcome. Specifically, we introduce a simple and intuitive rank
016
               preservation assumption to identify the counterfactual outcome without relying
               on a known structural causal model. Building on this, we propose a novel ideal
017
               loss for theoretically unbiased learning of the counterfactual outcome and further
018
               develop a kernel-based estimator for its empirical estimation. Our theoretical anal-
019            ysis shows that the proposed ideal loss is convex, and the proposed estimator is
020            unbiased. Extensive semi-synthetic and real-world experiments are conducted to
021            demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Understanding causal relationships is a fundamental goal across various domains, such as epi-
027
      demiology (Hernán & Robins, 2020), econometrics (Imbens & Rubin, 2015), and management
      science (Kallus & Uehara, 2020). Pearl & Mackenzie (2018) define the three-layer causal hier-
028
      archy—association, intervention, and counterfactuals—to distinguish three types of queries with
029
      increasing complexity and difficulty (Bareinboim et al., 2022). Counterfactual inference, the most
030   challenging level, aims to explore the impact of a treatment on an outcome given knowledge about
031   a different observed treatment and the factual outcome. For example, given a patient who has not
032   taken medication before and now suffers from a headache, we want to know whether the headache
033   would have occurred if the patient had taken the medication initially. Answering such counterfac-
034   tual queries can provide valuable instructions in scenarios such as credit assignment (Mesnard et al.,
035   2021), root-causal analysis (Budhathoki et al., 2022), and fair decision-making (Imai & Jiang, 2023).
036   Different from interventional queries, which are prospective and estimate the counterfactual out-
037   come in a hypothetical world via only the observations obtained before treatment (as pre-treatment
038   variables), counterfactual inference is retrospective and further incorporates the factual outcome (as
039   a post-treatment variable) in the observed world. This inherent conflict between the hypothetical
040   and the observed world poses a unique challenge and makes the counterfactual outcome generally
041   unidentifiable, even in randomized controlled experiments (RCTs) (Pearl et al., 2016; Ibeling &
042   Icard, 2020; Bareinboim et al., 2022).
043   For counterfactual inference, Pearl et al. (2016) proposed a three-step procedure (abduction, action,
044   and prediction) to estimate counterfactual outcomes. However, it relies on the availability of struc-
045   tural causal models (SCMs) that fully describe the data-generating process (Brouwer, 2022; Xie
046   et al., 2023). In real-world applications, the ground-truth SCM is likely to be unknown, and esti-
047   mating it requires additional assumptions to ensure identifiability, such as linearity (Shimizu et al.,
048   2006) and additive noise (Hoyer et al., 2008; Peters et al., 2014). Unfortunately, these assumptions
049   are hard to satisfy in practice and restrict the applicability.
050   To tackle the above problems, various counterfactual learning approaches have been proposed with
051   respect to different identifiability assumptions. For example, Lu et al. (2020), Nasr-Esfahany et al.
052   (2023), and Xie et al. (2023) established the identifiability of counterfactual outcomes based on
053   homogeneity and strict monotonicity assumptions. The homogeneity assumption posits that the ex-
      ogenous variable for each individual remains constant across different interventional environments,


                                                        1

054
      and the strict monotonicity assumption asserts that the outcome is a strictly monotone function of
055   the exogenous variable given the features. In terms of counterfactual learning, Lu et al. (2020) and
056   Nasr-Esfahany et al. (2023) adopted Pearl’s three-step procedure that needs to estimate the SCM
057   initially. In addition, Xie et al. (2023) proposed using quantile regression to estimate counterfac-
058   tual outcomes that effectively avoid the estimation of SCMs. Nevertheless, it relies on a stringent
059   assumption that the conditional quantile functions for different counterfactual outcomes come from
060   the same model and it requires estimating a different quantile value for each individual, leading to a
061   challenging bi-level optimization problem.
062   In this work, we propose a principled counterfactual learning approach with intuitive identifiabil-
063   ity assumptions and theoretically guaranteed estimation methods. On one hand, for identifiability
064   assumptions, we introduce the simple and intuitive rank preservation assumption, positing that an
065   individual’s factual and counterfactual outcomes have the same rank in the corresponding distri-
066   butions of factual and counterfactual outcomes for all individuals. We prove the identifiability of
067   counterfactual outcomes under the rank preservation assumption.
068   On the other hand, we further propose a theoretically guaranteed method for unbiased estimation
069   of counterfactual outcomes. The proposed estimation method enjoys several desirable merits. First,
070   unlike Pearl’s three-step procedure, it does not necessitate a prior estimation of SCMs and thus relies
071   on fewer assumptions than the methods proposed by Lu et al. (2020) and Nasr-Esfahany et al. (2023).
072   Second, in contrast to the quantile regression method proposed by Xie et al. (2023), our approach
073   neither restricts conditional quantile functions for different counterfactual outcomes to originate
074
      from the same model, nor requires estimating a different quantile value for each individual. Third,
      we enhance the previous learning approaches to adopt a convex loss for estimating counterfactual
075
      outcomes, which leads to a unique solution.
076
077   In summary, the main contributions are as follows: (1) We introduce the intuitive rank preservation
078   assumption to identify the counterfactual outcomes with unknown SCM, and establish its relation-
079
      ship with previous homogeneity and strict monotonicity assumptions; (2) We propose a novel ideal
      loss for unbiased learning of the counterfactual outcome and further develop a kernel-based estima-
080
      tor for the ideal loss. In addition, we theoretically show that the proposed ideal loss is convex, and
081
      the proposed kernel-based estimator is consistent; (3) We conduct extensive experiments on both
082   semi-synthetic and real-world datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

089   set of structure equation models F = {f1 , ..., fp }. The nodes in G are divided into two categories:
090   (a) exogenous variables U = (U1 , ..., Up ), which represent the environment during data generation,
091   assumed to be mutually independent; (b) endogenous variables V = {V1 , ..., Vp }, which denote
092   the relevant features that we need to model in a question of interest. For variable Vj , its value is
093
      determined by a structure equation Vj = fj (P Aj , Uj ), j = 1, ..., p, where P Aj stands for the set
      of parents of Vj . SCM provides a formal language for describing how the variables interact and
094
      how the resulting distribution would change in response to certain interventions. Based on SCM, we
095
      introduce the counterfactual inference problem in the following.

097   Counterfactual Inference (Pearl, 2009). Suppose that we have three sets of variables denoted by
098   X, Y, E ⊆ V, counterfactual inference revolves around the question, “given evidence E = e, what
099
      would have happened if we had set X to a different value x′ ?”. Pearl et al. (2016) propose using the

100
      to the evidence E = e; (b) Action: modify the model M by removing the structural equations
101
      for X and replacing them with X = x′ , yielding the modified model Mx′ ; (c) Prediction: Use
102   Mx′ and the value of U to calculate the counterfactual outcome of Y . In this paper, we focus on
103   estimating the counterfactual outcome for each individual. To illustrate the main ideas, we formulate
104   the common counterfactual inference problem within the context of the backdoor criterion.

      3     A NALYSIS OF E XISTING M ETHODS
121
      In this section, we elucidate the challenges of counterfactual inference. This clarification helps
122
      further analysis of current approaches. Subsequently, we summarize the existing methods, shedding
123
      light on their limitations and thereby motivating the proposal of our method.
124
125
      3.1   C HALLENGES IN C OUNTERFACTUAL I NFERENCE
126
127   The main challenge lies in that the counterfactual value yx′ is generally not identifiable, even in
128   randomized controlled experiments (RCTs).
129
      By definition, yx′ is a quantity involving two “different worlds” at the same time: the observed
130
      world with (X = x, Z = z, Y = y) and the hypothetical world where X = x′ . We only ob-
131
      serve the factual outcome Yx = y but never observe the counterfactual outcome Yx′ , which is the
132   fundamental problem in causal inference (Holland, 1986; Morgan & Winship, 2015). This inherent
133   conflict prevents us from simplifying the expression of yx′ to a do-calculus expression, making it
134   generally unidentifiable, even in RCTs (Pearl et al., 2016). Therefore, in addition to the widely used
135   assumptions such as conditional exchangeability, overlapping, and consistency (Hernán & Robins,
136   2020), counterfactual inference requires extra assumptions to ensure identifiability. Essentially, esti-
137   mating yx′ is equivalent to estimating the individual treatment effect yx′ − yx , while the conditional
138   average treatment effect (CATE) E[Yx′ − Yx |Z = z] represents the ATE for a subpopulation with
139   Z = z, overlooking the inherent heterogeneity in this subpopulation caused by the noise terms such
140
      as UX (Albert et al., 2005; Lei & Candès, 2021; Ben-Michael et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023).
141

450   Counterfactual Inference. Counterfactual inference involves the identification and estimation of
451   counterfactual outcomes. For identification, Shpitser & Pearl (2007) provided an algorithm lever-
452   aging counterfactual graphs to identify counterfactual queries. In addition, Correa et al. (2021)
453   discussed the identifiability of nested counterfactuals within a given causal graph. More relevant to
454   our work, Lu et al. (2020) and Xie et al. (2023) studied the identifiability assumptions in the setting
455   of backdoor criterion under homogeneity and strict monotonicity assumptions. Several methods fo-
456   cus on determining its bounds with less stringent assumptions, such as Balke & Pearl (1994), Tian
457
      & Pearl (2000), Pearl (2009), Pearl et al. (2016), Finkelstein & Shpitser (2020), Zhang et al. (2022),
      and Melnychuk et al. (2023).
458
459   For estimation, Pearl et al. (2016) introduced a three-step procedure for counterfactual inference.
460   Many machine learning methods estimate counterfactual outcomes in this framework, such as Lu
461   et al. (2020), Mesnard et al. (2021), Brouwer (2022), Shah et al. (2022), Yan et al. (2023a), Nasr-
462   Esfahany et al. (2023) and Chao et al. (2023). Recently, Xie et al. (2023) employed quantile regres-
463
      sion to estimate the counterfactual outcomes, effectively circumventing the need for SCM estima-
      tion. In our work, we extend the above methods in both identification and estimation.
464
465   Recently, counterfactual inference methods have been extensively applied across various application
466   scenarios, such as counterfactual fairness (Kusner et al., 2017; Zuo et al., 2023; Anthis & Veitch,
467   2023; Kavouras et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a), policy evaluation and improvement (Tang & Wiens,
468   2023; Saveski et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b), reinforcement learning (Lu et al., 2020; Tsirtsis &
469
      Rodriguez, 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Shao et al., 2023; Meulemans et al., 2023; Haugh & Singal, 2023;
      Zenati et al., 2023), imitaion learning (Sun et al., 2023), counterfactual generation (Yan et al., 2023b;
470
      Prabhu et al., 2023; Feder et al., 2023; Ribeiro et al., 2023), counterfactual explanation (Kenny &
471
      Huang, 2023; Raman et al., 2023; Hamman et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Ley et al., 2023), counter-
472   factual harm (Richens et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023), physical audiovisual commonsense reasoning (Lv
473   et al., 2023), interpretable time series prediction (Yan & Wang, 2023), classification and detection
474   in medical imaging (Fontanella et al., 2023), data valuation (Liu et al., 2023b), etc. Therefore,
475   developing novel counterfactual inference methods holds significant practical implications.

      8   C ONCLUSION
479
      This work addresses the fundamental challenge of counterfactual inference in the absence of a
480   known SCM and under heterogeneous endogenous variables. We first introduce the rank preser-
481   vation assumption to identify counterfactual outcomes. Then, we propose a novel ideal loss for
482   unbiased learning of counterfactual outcomes and develop a kernel-based estimator for practical
483   implementation. The convexity of the ideal loss and the unbiased nature of the proposed estimator
484   contribute to the robustness and reliability of our method. A potential limitation arises when the
485   propensity score is extremely small in certain data sparsity scenarios, which may cause instability in
      the estimation method. Further investigation is warranted to address and overcome this challenge.
