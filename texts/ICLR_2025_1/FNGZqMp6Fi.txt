               Micro Crack detection using deep neural networks(DNNs) through an automated
               pipeline using wave fields interacting with the damaged areas is highly sought
016
               after. However, these high dimensional spatio-temporal crack data are limited,
017
               moreover these dataset have large dimension in the temporal domain. The dataset
018            exhibits a pronounced class imbalance, with crack pixels accounting for an aver-
019            age of only 5% of the total pixels per sample. This severe imbalance presents a
020            challenge for deep learning models when dealing with various microscale cracks,
021            as the network tends to favor the majority class, often resulting in reduced detec-

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Micro crack detection in materials is of significant importance due to the potential for catastrophic
032   failures, which can lead to substantial financial losses and safety hazards in industries (Malekloo
033
      et al., 2022; Golewski, 2023). Detecting cracks in complex structures, like aircraft bodies or intricate
      machinery components, poses a substantial challenge using conventional methods like visual inspec-
034
      tion or standard cameras, especially when dealing with complex geometries. The use of wave-based
035
      approaches for crack detection offers a powerful solution, as these methods allow for the analysis of
036   structures that are not easily accessible or too complex to inspect manually.
037
038   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are especially good at processing spatial data due to their
039
      ability to capture local spatial correlations within an image (LeCun et al., 2015). Nevertheless, stan-
      dard segmentation methods, such as vanilla architectures, demonstrate limited performance on this
040
      particular dataset, due to the complex spatio-temporal nature of the crack patterns. This becomes
041
      even more significant when the cracks represent a tiny minority in the dataset, leading to poor detec-
042   tion accuracy. This issue is enhanced when dealing with very small cracks, as they not only lead to
043   data imbalance but may also cause minimal disruption in wave behaviour. In such cases, the waves
044   may exhibit minimal changes, making it difficult for the model to detect the cracks accurately.
045
      This challenge necessitates the development of a more tailored custom model. Our proposed Micro-
046
      CrackAttentionNeXt is designed to overcome the limitations of vanilla models like UNet by incor-
047
      porating enhanced spatial and temporal feature extraction. Unlike UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015),
048   where the input and target share the same modality (image-to-image translation). Our model pro-
049   cesses spatio-temporal input data and outputs spatial crack predictions, enabling it to handle more
050   complex data while improving micro-scale detection accuracy. The asymmetric encoder-decoder
051   structure, with attention layers is particularly effective as it focuses on capturing critical crack pat-
052   terns rather than relying heavily on skip connections. The attention mechanism ensures that the
053   model prioritizes the time steps when the waves interact with the cracks, improving detection preci-
      sion. The DNNs capacity to recognise minute details and complex patterns in high dimensional data

      is impacted by the activation functions used, which becomes crucial in the micro-scale setting where
055   accuracy is much needed. Activation functions enhance the network’s expressive power, enabling it
056   to capture diverse features and representations.

058
      Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) Nair & Hinton (2010) and its variants are commonly used activation
      functions. ReLU introduces non-linearity by setting negative values to zero, allowing positive ones
059
      to pass unchanged, which aids in deep network training. The ”dying ReLU” issue, where neurons
060
      become inactive, hampers learning Xu et al. (2015); He et al. (2015b). Variants like Leaky ReLU
061   mitigate this by allowing small negative slopes. SELU (Scaled Exponential Linear Unit) Klambauer
062   et al. (2017) scales outputs to maintain self-normalizing properties, keeping activations near zero
063   mean and unit variance. GeLU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit) Hendrycks & Gimpel (2023) enhances
064   representation learning by incorporating probabilistic elements, though it has higher computational
065   complexity. ELU (Exponential Linear Unit) Clevert et al. (2016) improves learning dynamics but is
066   computationally expensive. Various Loss functions have been proposed in the literature to combat
067   class imbalance issues in the DNN model. The loss functions tested are: 1)Dice LossLin et al.
068   (2018), 2)Focal LossLin et al. (2018), 3)Weighted Dice LossYeung et al. (2021) and, 4)Combined
069
      Weighted Dice LossJadon (2020).
070   These activation functions aim to strike a delicate balance between adaptability and computational
071   efficiency, essential considerations in the micro-material domain, where capturing fine details is
072   crucial for accurate crack detection. Empirical exploration and meticulous fine-tuning of these acti-
073   vation functions is imperative to identify the optimal choice that aligns with the distinctive charac-
074   teristics of micro-material images. Ultimately, a nuanced and effective approach to crack detection
075
      in micro-materials relies on the thoughtful selection and optimization of activation functions within
      the CNN architecture.
076
077   The extent of the influence of different activations is difficult to determine against conventional met-
078   rics such as accuracy and F1 score. Hence, it is imperative to analyse the internal dynamics of
079   the model. Methods like Principal Component Analysis, t-SNE van der Maaten & Hinton (2008)
080   and UMAP McInnes et al. (2020) are used to analyse the higher dimensional feature maps of these
081
      blackbox models against the target. However, these methods provide little to no insight when used
      on segmentation problems. In this study, we use the recently proposed Manifold Discovery Anal-
082
      ysis (MDA) Islam et al. (2023) to qualitatively assess the impacts of various activation functions.
083
      Moreover, through this, we were able to analyse the effects activations had on the feature maps of
084   the model, allowing us to choose the best activation function for the given problem.

      Hamishebahar et al., 2022).

137
      baseline model and a more complex Residual Network (ResNet18) encoder. SCNN and ResNet18
138
      leverage 1D convolutions to extract temporal features from the wave data, followed by 2D convo-
139   lutions for spatial feature extraction. Both models employ a decoder with transposed convolutional

141   models were evaluated on simulated wave propagation data, where cracks ranging from 0.4 to 12.8
142   µm in size. ResNet18 outperformed SCNN and achieved a precision of 0.92, recall of 0.719 and a

      Moreh et al. (2024) explores the use of DNN for automated crack detection in structures using

      extending their methodologies to a broader scope. The existing body of work in this field remains

184   3.1   WAVE FIELD DATA
185
      In this study, it is crucial to understand that it deals with numerical data produced by geo-science
186
      experts at our group (Wuttke et al., 2021). For someone without expertise in this field, interpreting
187   the data is nearly impossible, as the data is purely numerical and provides no visual insights. The
188   goal of this research is to demonstrate that it is indeed possible to extract patterns from such data.
189   Research in this area is still in its infancy, making it an important achievement to prove that machine
190   learning can process such data and segment fractures. We are also working on generating a new
191   dataset that better represents reality, where real-world data can be evaluated using our model.
192
      The wave field dataset utilized in this work (Wuttke et al., 2021), while effective for crack detection,
193   presents some limitations in terms of data dimensionality. These datasets are characterized by large
194   temporal dimensions, which increases the complexity of data processing and model training. The
195   dataset consists of homogeneous 2D plates, where each plate is modelled with lattice particles that
196   share consistent properties, such as density and Young’s Modulus. The modeling of structural sys-
197   tems is achieved using Voronoi-Delaunay meshing algorithms within the Lattice Element Method
198   (LEM). Lattice nodes, representing unit cell centers, are connected by beams capable of handling
199   normal forces (N ), shear forces (V ), and bending moments (M ). If the strain energy Ue in an
200   element exceeds a predefined threshold Uth , the element undergoes stiffness reduction or removal,
201
      simulating failure states. To simulate wave propagation through the material, an external force of
      1000 N is applied at the midpoint of the left boundary, ensuring that the waves propagate across the
202
      entire plate, interacting with both non-crack and crack regions. The resulting displacements in both
203
      the x- and y-directions are recorded over 2000 time steps, capturing detailed temporal changes in
204   the wave field. These displacements are measured by a 9 × 9 (81) sensor grid uniformly distributed
205   across the material, resulting in a wave field dataset with dimensions of 2 x 81 × 2000. Figure 1
206   shows a sample of the input dataset. This approach provides spatio-temporal data that captures the
207   interaction between the propagating waves and the cracks, allowing for in-depth analysis of crack
208   detection model performance.
209
      A major challenge arises from the severe class imbalance present in the dataset. On average, only
210   5% of the total pixels represent cracks, with the remaining majority belonging to intact, non-crack
211   regions. This imbalance poses a substantial obstacle for deep learning models, which are prone
212   to bias toward the majority class. As a result, the models tend to predict non-crack regions more
213   frequently, leading to suboptimal detection accuracy for the minority class (crack regions). Address-
214   ing this issue requires careful design of the model and training process to ensure that the network
215   can effectively learn from the minority class, and accurately identify crack regions without being
      overpowered by the majority class imbalance.

      3.2   M ICRO C RACKS ATTENTION N E X T M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
217
218
219   MicrocrackAttentionNeXt, shown in Figure 2, is an asymmetric encoder-decoder network. The in-
220
      put to the model is a tensor with shape X ∈ RCin ×T ×S , where Cin = 2 represents the input channels
      corresponding to the x and y components of wave data, T = 2000 is the temporal dimension, and
221
      S = 81 corresponds to the spatial dimension, which is a flattened 9 × 9 sensor grid. To reduce com-
222
      putational complexity and focus on salient temporal features, the network uses an initial max pooling
223   layer with a kernel size of (4, 1). This layer transforms the input tensor X to X1 ∈ R2×500×81 by
224   downsampling the temporal dimension from 2000 to T1 = 500. This reduction is crucial as it
225   reduces the amount of data the subsequent layers need to process.
226
      The encoder is composed of four convolutional blocks, each designed to progressively extract
227
      higher-level features from the input data. The first convolutional block applies two convolutional
228
      layers with kernel sizes (3, 1) and padding (1, 0), which maintain the spatial dimensions while ex-
229   panding the channel dimension from 2 to 16. These layers are followed by batch normalization and
230   activation functions, introducing non-linearity. A Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) module is then ap-
231   plied, which recalibrates channel-wise feature responses by modelling interdependencies between
232   channels. This module enhances the representational power of the network by allowing it to fo-
233   cus on the most informative features. Following the first convolutional block, a max pooling layer
234   with a kernel size of (2, 1) further reduces the temporal dimension from 500 to T2 = 250. Group
235   normalization is applied to the data, normalizing across channels and improving convergence dur-
236   ing training. An AttentionLayer computes self-attention over the temporal and spatial dimensions,
237
      enabling the network to weigh different parts of the input differently. This attention mechanism is
      essential for focusing on relevant features and capturing dependencies across the data. A residual
238
      connection adds the attention output back to the original input, facilitating better gradient flow and
239
      mitigating issues such as vanishing gradients (Raghu et al., 2022; He et al., 2015a).
240
241   This pattern repeats in the subsequent convolutional blocks, with each block increasing the number
242   of channels (from 16 to 32, 32 to 64, and 64 to 128) and further reducing the temporal dimension
243
      (from 250 to 125, 125 to 62, and 62 to 31) through additional pooling layers. The consistent use of
      (3, 1) kernels ensures effective temporal feature extraction while preserving spatial dimensions. SE
244
      modules and attention mechanisms are integrated throughout. Feature maps are upsampled and rein-
245
      troduced to the Conv1 block through a Self-Attention Module (SAM)-inspired mechanism, enabling
246   the decoder backbone to reuse features and increase model performance. The feedback mechanism
247   employs bilinear interpolation for resizing and utilizes Conv2D layers to selectively regulate the fea-
248   tures propagated back into the network giving it the alias of Adaptive Feature Reutilization block.
249
      At the bottleneck of the network, a convolutional layer with a large kernel size of (31, 1) is employed,
250
      covering the entire temporal dimension T5 = 31. This layer transforms the tensor to Xbottleneck ∈
251
      RB×128×1×81 , capturing long-range temporal dependencies and encapsulating high-level temporal
252   information into a compact form. Batch normalization and activation are applied to maintain training
253   stability and introduce non-linearity.
254
255
      The decoder begins by reshaping this bottleneck tensor into a spatial grid Xreshaped ∈ R128×9×9 , re-
      organizing the data for spatial processing. A point-wise convolution reduces the channel dimension
256
      from 128 to 16, preparing the data for upsampling. The network then uses transposed convolutional
257
      layers to reconstruct the spatial dimensions progressively. The first transposed convolution upsam-
258   ples the spatial dimensions from 9 × 9 to 18 × 18 and reduces the channel dimension from 16 to 8.
259   The second transposed convolution further upsamples the dimensions to 36 × 36, maintaining the
260   channel count at 8. Each transposed convolution is followed by batch normalization to ensure stable
261   learning and effective non-linear transformations.
262
      Finally, a point-wise convolution reduces the channel dimension from 8 to 1, and a sigmoid activa-
263
      tion function scales the output to the range [0, 1]. The output tensor Y ∈ R1×36×36 represents the
264   reconstructed spatial data, which is then flattened into a vector Yflat ∈ R1296 (since 36×36 = 1296),
265   making it suitable for downstream tasks. Figure 2 shows the proposed model architecture. The archi-
266   tectural choices in MicrocrackAttentionNeXt are designed to balance feature extraction capability
267   and computational efficiency. The initial temporal downsampling reduces the data size, allowing
268   the network to process longer sequences without excessive computational overhead. The 1D convo-
269   lutional blocks with increasing channel dimensions enable the extraction of hierarchical features in
      the temporal domain, without mixing the spatial component. We found that learning temporal and

507   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE WORK
508
509
510   5.1   C ONCLUSION
511
512   Through this study, we have demonstrated the effectiveness of feature visualization in designing
513   MicroCrackAttentionNeXt, by carefully optimizing the architecture, leveraging the right activation
514
      function and loss. This architecture also utilizes multiple 1D-CNN layers for feature extraction,
      significantly reducing the training time. These are followed by folded layers that merge spatial and
515
      temporal features, along with a prediction module for semantic segmentation. The dataset used are
516
      spatio-temporal in nature and represent the behavior of wave propagation, where waves interact with
517   the cracks, leading to disruptions in their patterns and altered behavior in the presence of cracks. The
518   model is capable of segmenting the microcracks, helping to determine their spatial locations in the
519   material. The qualitative examination of the activation functions using the Manifold Discovery and
520   Analysis (MDA) algorithm allowed the evaluation of impact of different activation and loss functions
521   on the model’s performance. The proposed model and 1D-Densenet were analyzed using the MDA
522   plots. It was observed that manifold of the proposed model was more compact with a much more
523   smoother arc than 1D-Densenet. With the optimized selection of activation and loss functions, an
524   accuracy of 87.74% was achieved.
525
526
527   5.2   F UTURE WORK
528
529   In future efforts to improve microcrack detection models, two primary strategies can be pursued:
530   expanding datasets and refining model architectures. The dataset used, presents a challenge due to
531
      severe class imbalance, which requires more advanced techniques for data generation and augmenta-
      tion to mitigate the bias introduced. Moreover, the segmentation output suffers from low resolution,
532
      and without appropriate upscaling techniques, critical details may be lost. To address this, in the
533
      future works, we propose incorporating a super-resolution GAN approach to enhance the resolution
534   of the segmentation outputs. While the encoder architecture performs optimally, further changes are
535   necessary in the decoder section of the segmentation model to achieve improved results and maintain
536   consistency with the high-quality input features. To enhance the encoder’s ability to capture long-
537   range dependencies, state space model can be used, particularity integrating the recently proposed
538   Mamba architecture. This adjustment would improve the model’s ability to handle complex spatial
539   relationships, thereby strengthening feature extraction and contributing to overall performance gains
      in the segmentation task.
