                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Image aesthetics is a crucial metric in the field of image generation. However,
010
               textual aesthetics has not been sufficiently explored. With the widespread appli-
011            cation of large language models (LLMs), previous work has primarily focused
012            on the correctness of content and the helpfulness of responses. Nonetheless, pro-
013            viding responses with textual aesthetics is also an important factor for LLMs,
014            which can offer a cleaner layout and ensure greater consistency and coherence
015            in content. In this work, we introduce a pipeline for aesthetics polishing and
016            help construct a textual aesthetics dataset named T EX A ES. We propose a textual
017            aesthetics-powered fine-tuning method based on direct preference optimization,
018            termed TAPO, which leverages textual aesthetics without compromising content
019
               correctness. Additionally, we develop two evaluation methods for textual aesthetics
               based on text and image analysis, respectively. Our experiments demonstrate that
020
               using textual aesthetics data and employing the TAPO fine-tuning method not only
021
               improves aesthetic scores but also enhances performance on general evaluation
022            datasets such as AlpacalEval and Anera-hard.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Image aesthetics (Huang et al., 2024a; Murray et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2016; Ke et al., 2021; Bosse
028   et al., 2017) has emerged as a prominent research area within computer vision, focusing on assessing
029   and improving the visual appeal of images. Aesthetics has recently been integrated into state-of-
030   the-art image generation models, such as diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), significantly
031   enhancing the visual quality of generated images (Wu et al., 2024a; 2023) and aligning them more
032
      closely with human preferences (Huang et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024b; 2023).
033   Meanwhile, advancements in large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023) and
034   LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023b; Dubey et al., 2024) have demonstrated impressive generative
035   capabilities across various domains, including code, articles, and web content. Although LLMs
036   have made significant progress in generating textual content, enhancing the aesthetic quality of their
037   output remains a critical challenge. A more aesthetically appealing and organized output not only
038
      improves user engagement by making the content more intuitive and comfortable to read but also
      enhances consistency and coherence. Consequently, exploring the textual aesthetics of LLMs is a
039
      highly desirable area of research.
040
041   In this work, we present the first investigation into improving the aesthetic quality of text generated
042   by LLMs. Unlike image aesthetics benefiting from numerous large-scale aesthetic datasets (e.g.,
043   AVA (Murray et al., 2012) and AesBench (Huang et al., 2024b)), advanced aesthetic learning
044
      technology (Huang et al., 2024a; Zhang & Liu, 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Su et al., 2020) and reliable
      aesthetic evaluation methods (Deng et al., 2017; Su et al., 2011), textual aesthetics in LLMs lacks
045
      similar resources and established models.
046
047   To address this challenge, we first designed an aesthetic data generation pipeline leveraging GPT-4o
048   for aesthetic polishing. This scalable pipeline can generate large volumes of high-quality aesthetic
049   preference data. Based on this framework, we constructed the first aesthetic dataset in the LLM
050
      domain, T EX A ES, which contains a total of 50,390 prompts data.
051   Based on T EX A ES, existing post-training techniques such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024b) can be
052   used to fine-tune current LLMs at the aesthetic level. However, we found that directly applying
053   these techniques not only failed to align effectively with the characteristics of our T EX A ES, limiting
      its impact on aesthetic fine-tuning, but also negatively impacted the overall performance of these


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      LLMs. To address this issue, we propose Textual Aesthetics Preference Optimization (TAPO) which
055   employs the Plackett-Luce (Luce, 1959; Plackett, 1975) model with adjustable optimization weights
056   to better leverage our dataset and enhance aesthetic fine-tuning performance. Furthermore, to better
057   assess the aesthetic quality of LLM outputs, we have developed two evaluation pipelines: one based
058   on text and the other based on images, respectively.
059
      To validate the effectiveness of our T EX A ES and TAPO, we performed aesthetic fine-tuning on
060
      the open-source LLaMA series models (Dubey et al., 2024) and compared the aesthetic scores of
061   the fine-tuned LLMs with state-of-the-art LLMs at different scales (from 8B to 70B). Additionally,
062   to ensure objective and reliable results, we employed human experts for professional evaluation.
063   Extensive experimental results ultimately demonstrated the effectiveness of our T EX A ES and TAPO.
064
      Our main contributions are listed as follow:
065
066    • To the best of our knowledge, we for the first time indciate the crucial issue of exploring and
067
         improving the textual aesthetics in LLMs.
068    • We systematically identify the lack of related textual aesthetics datasets, and introduce a novel
069
         pipeline for aesthetic text polishing and contribute to the construction of a textual aesthetics
         dataset, named T EX A ES.
070
071
       • Based on T EX A ES, we propose a DPO-based aesthetic fine-tuning algorithm, named TAPO, to
         effectively enhances the LLMs’ aesthetic quality while preserving its general performance.
072
073
       • Both qualitative and quantitative extensive experiments demonstrate that utilizing T EX A ES and
         TAPO not only improves aesthetic scores but also enhances the general capabilities of LLMs.

080   2.1   I MAGE A ESTHETICS
081
082   Image aesthetics (Huang et al., 2024a; Murray et al., 2012; Kong et al., 2016) is a subfield of computer
083   vision that focuses on assessing (Deng et al., 2017; Su et al., 2011) and improving the aesthetic quality
084   of images (Bhattacharya et al., 2010; Deng et al., 2018). Early work in the field of image aesthetics
085   focused on using handcrafted metrics to assess aesthetic scores (Nack et al., 2001; Neumann et al.,
086   2005). However, with the development of deep learning, there has been significant interest in applying
087   CNN (Bosse et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Su et al., 2020) or Transformer (Ke et al., 2021; Zhang &
088
      Liu, 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023) based methods to solve image aesthetics problems,
      which have demonstrated promising results. Recently, multi-modal large language models (MLLMs)
089
      have shown superior aesthetic perception and robustness in the fields of image aesthetics, greatly
090
      surpassing lightweight models due to their vast knowledge base and strong reasoning and memory
091   capabilities. (Huang et al., 2024a;b; Wu et al., 2024b).
092
093
094
      2.2   LLM P REFERENCES DATA
095
096
097   Preference learning is an optimization method for LLMs designed to enhance their ability to generate
098   outputs that better align with human preferences (Fürnkranz & Hüllermeier, 2010; Schulman et al.,
099
      2017; Rafailov et al., 2024b; Ouyang et al., 2022). Increasing attention has also been drawn to the
      importance of data used during the preference learning phase. Some studies focus on constructing
100
      domain-specific datasets for preference learning, e.g., summarization (Stiennon et al., 2020; Wu
101
      et al., 2021) and question answering (Nakano et al., 2021). Cui et al. (2024) highlights the scarcity of
102   large-scale, general-purpose preference datasets and propose UltraFeedback to addresses this gap by
103   collecting over 1 million preference feedback samples using GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023). Lee et al. (2023)
104   also pointed out that utilizing AI-generated preference feedback is an effective and cost-efficient
105   method for expanding preference datasets. While the aforementioned work provides preference
106   datasets for specific domains as well as general-purpose tasks, none of them have addressed the
107   critical area of text aesthetics in LLMs, which motivated us to design corresponding data construction
      pipeline and related dataset like T EX A ES to support future research in text aesthetics.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
111
112
      Textual aesthetics, which encompass the aesthetic attributes of a text at both the content and visual
113   levels, can be dissected into four fundamental aspects. Clarity (readability) pertains to the ease with
114   which a text can be read and comprehended, necessitating optimal sentence length and grammatical
115   complexity (DuBay, 2004). Layout (visual organization) involves the systematic arrangement of text
116   elements, such as headings and subheadings, to guide the reader effectively. Uniformity (consistency)
117   demands a consistent style and formatting throughout the text to enhance readability and facilitate
118   a smoother reading experience. Coherence (overall structure) ensures that paragraphs are well-
119   organized and logically connected, facilitating easier comprehension of the content (Van Silfhout
120   et al., 2014).
121
122   3.2   A ESTHETICS P OLISHING
123
124   Human preference data is critical for aligning large language models and improving their performance
125   across various dimensions, such as helpfulness (Askell et al., 2021; Kreutzer et al., 2018; Stiennon
126   et al., 2020), harmlessness (Bai et al., 2022; Glaese et al., 2022), and honesty (Ouyang et al., 2022).
127   Consequently, we believe that a textual aesthetic preference dataset will also be beneficial for research
128   on the alignment of LLMs. However, current literature reveals a conspicuous absence of research
129
      specifically addressing the textual aesthetics of LLMs, as well as a lack of corresponding textual
      aesthetic preference data. To address this gap, we have developed a method for textual aesthetic
130
      polishing to construct a dataset that optimizes the aesthetic preferences of LLMs.
131

133   to enhance textual aesthetics, we can
134   build our textual aesthetic preference

      erence dataset such as UltraFeed-

173   3.3    T EXTUAL A ESTHETICS S CORING
174
175   To validate the aesthetic quality of texts generated by large language models and to assess the
176   effectiveness of our aesthetic preference dataset, a robust method for evaluating text aesthetics is
177   indispensable. Previous studies, such as AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024), MT-
178
      Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), and Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024), suggest that using LLMs as evaluators
      can effectively approximate human preferences. Consequently, we employ the "LLM as a judge"
179
      framework to approximate human preferences for text aesthetics. We evaluate the aesthetic quality of
180
      texts generated by LLMs using two methods: text-based and image-based text aesthetic scoring.
181
182   Text-Based Text Aesthetic Scoring. We randomly selected 500 prompts from Arena-Hard (Li
183   et al., 2024) as our evaluation dataset. Following practices from Arena-Hard and MT-Bench (Zheng
184
      et al., 2023), we implemented a pairwise comparison method, comparing the performance of model
      πi on prompt p with a robust baseline model (GPT-4-0314) to derive aesthetic preference scores.
185
      Judges assessed aesthetic preferences on a Likert scale (Likert, 1932) (1 = prefers πi (p) much less
186
      than πbase (p), 5 = prefers πi (p) much more than πbase (p)). This methodology ensures that models
187   are penalized more heavily for substantial losses than for minor ones, effectively differentiating
188   between models. Using the chain-of-thought approach, judges evaluated text aesthetics based on
189   four dimensions: readability, visual organization, consistency, and overall structure. To mitigate
190   position bias, we employed a two-game setup by swapping model positions for each query. Following
191   the practices of Chatbot Arena, we adopted the Bradley-Terry (Bradley & Terry, 1952) model to
192   generate final scores. We aggregated all pairwise comparisons with the baseline model and employed
193   bootstrapping to derive a bootstrapped confidence interval for all models’ win rates against the
194   baseline, producing an ordered ranking of all models based on their win rates. The judge prompts are
195
      provided in Appendix E.2.
196   Image-Based Text Aesthetic Scoring. Our conceptualization of text aesthetics encompasses not
197   only textual readability and comprehensibility but also visual appeal. Given GPT-4o’s exceptional
198   multimodal capabilities, we utilized GPT-4o to evaluate text aesthetics from a visual perspective as
199   well. In our experiments, we rendered the LLM-generated texts as HTML with consistent CSS styles,
200   converted them into images of identical size, and then had GPT-4o evaluate these images based on
201
      the same criteria used for textual evaluation. Specific prompts are provided in Appendix E.3.

206   4.1    D IRECT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION T RAINING
207
208   Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF)(Christiano et al., 2017) has emerged as a
209   pivotal technique in aligning LLMs (Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020).
210   Early implementations of RLHF primarily relied on reinforcement learning and alternative ap-
211   proaches (Snell et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a; Gulcehre et al., 2023). Rafailov et al. (2024a)
212
      proposed a RL-free closedform counterpart known as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) which
      has shown impressive performances (Ivison et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Tunstall et al., 2023).
213
214   The naive DPO uses a pair of preference data, which includes a chosen response and a rejected
215   response for each prompt, based on the Bradley-Terry (Bradley & Terry, 1952) model for optimization.
      The loss function for DPO is defined as follows:

229

230

      5.2      E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
284
285   In this study, we evaluate the performance of models from two perspectives: textual aesthetics and
286   general response capabilities. For textual aesthetics, we compare the models using both text-based
287   and image-based text aesthetic scoring methods, as described in Section 3.3. We report the win
288   rate (WR) in text aesthetics at both the text and image levels relative to the baseline model (GPT-4-

290   models’ performance. We randomly sample fifty entries from the Anera-Hard dataset and ask human
291   annotators to rate the aesthetics of these entries.
292   To evaluate the changes in the model’s general capabilities following the alignment of textual
293   aesthetics preferences, including its ability to follow instructions and respond to complex prompts
294   across diverse domains, we utilize three well-established auto-evaluation instruction-following
295   benchmarks based on GPT-4-as-a-Judge: AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024), Arena-Hard (Li et al.,

298   Detailed training parameters are provided in the Appendix B.

352   6.2    I MPACT OF L OSS W EIGHT
353
354   To determine the influence of the weight ratio between LTA and LDPO in TAPO on the aesthetics of
355
      the text of the model and the overall performance, we performed a series of methodical experiments.
      Specifically, we experimented with two settings: 1. First, we used the Tulu-v2 dataset (Ivison
356
      et al., 2023) to fine-tune the LLaMA-3.1-8B-base model in a supervised manner, followed by further
357
      optimization using TAPO; 2. Second, we directly applied TAPO to the LLaMA-3.1-8B-instruct
358   model. We set the weight ratios of LTA to LDPO at 2:1, 1:1, 1:2 and 1:5, respectively, to train the
359   models. We then evaluated the models’ text-based and image-based text aesthetic scores, as well as

530   8           C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we conducted the first exploration of textual aesthetics in LLMs and introduced a series
532
      of techniques to enhance the aesthetic quality of LLMs outputs. First, we developed the T EX A ES
533
      dataset, the first textual aesthetic dataset in the LLMs domain, using our specially-designed data
534   polishing pipeline. Based on this dataset, we proposed the TAPO, which fine-tunes LLMs to improve
535   the aesthetic quality of their outputs while preserving their core capabilities. Both qualitative and
536   quantitative experiments validated the effectiveness of our proposed techniques. We hope our work
537   serves as an early exploration for the textual aesthetics in LLMs and provides valuable support for
538   researchers in the open-source community. In future work, we will continue to explore ways to collect
539   diverse and high-quality textual aesthetics data, while designing more efficient and effective tuning
      techniques for aesthetic fine-tuning.

1123   In our study, we employed three annotators: two graduate students in computer science and one
1124   professor with a background in applied linguistics. All three evaluators are non-native English

1128   The annotators underwent a comprehensive training and calibration process prior to the main eval-
1129   uation. This training ensured that their understanding of the evaluation criteria was consistent and
1130   aligned. Annotators were introduced to the four evaluation dimensions—clarity (ease of compre-
1131   hension), layout (visual organization), uniformity (consistent formatting), and coherence (logical
1132   structure)—with detailed explanations and examples. They practiced with a subset of the dataset, and
1133   their evaluations were reviewed with feedback provided to refine their approach. A final readiness
       test was conducted to confirm alignment and preparedness for the main evaluation phase.
