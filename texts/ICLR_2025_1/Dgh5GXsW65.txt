011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) achieve state-of-the-art per-
014            formance in synthesizing new images from random noise, but they lack meaning-
015            ful latent space that encodes data into features. Recent DDPM-based editing tech-
016
               niques try to mitigate this issue by inverting images back to their approximated
               staring noise. In this work, we study the relation between the initial Gaussian
017
               noise, the samples generated from it, and their corresponding latent encodings ob-
018
               tained through the inversion procedure. First, we interpret their spatial distance
019            relations to show the inaccuracy of the DDIM inversion technique by localizing
020            latent representations manifold between the initial noise and generated samples.
021            Then, we demonstrate the peculiar relation between initial Gaussian noise and its
022            corresponding generations during diffusion training, showing that the high-level
023            features of generated images stabilize rapidly, keeping the spatial distance rela-
024            tionship between noises and generations consistent throughout the training.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Diffusion-based probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), have surpassed state-
030   of-the-art solutions in many generative domains including image (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021),
031   speech (Popov et al., 2021)), video (Ho et al., 2022), and music (Liu et al., 2021) synthesis. Nev-
032   ertheless, one of the significant drawbacks that distinguishes diffusion-based approaches from other
033
      generative models like Variational Autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2014), Flows (Kingma &
      Dhariwal, 2018), or Generative Adversarial Networks (Goodfellow et al., 2014) is the lack of im-
034
      plicit latent space that encodes training data into low-dimensional, interpretable representations.
035
      Several works try to mitigate this issue, treating as latent features internal data representations ex-
036   tracted from the denoising UNet model (Kwon et al., 2022), combining diffusion models with ad-
037   ditional external models (Preechakul et al., 2021), or by seeking structure in the starting noise used
038   for generations (Song et al., 2020).
039
      The last example, introduced by (Song et al., 2020) with Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models
040
      (DDIM), led to the proliferation of methods grouped under the name of inversion techniques (Garibi
041
      et al., 2024; Mokady et al., 2023; Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024; Meiri et al., 2023; Hong et al.,
042   2024). The main idea behind those approaches is to use a diffusion model to predict the noise that
043   can be added to the original or generated image. Applying this procedure several times allows tracing
044   back the backward diffusion process and approximating the initial noise that results in the starting
045   image. However, due to approximation error and biases induced by a trained denoising model, there
046   are inconsistencies between initial Gaussian noise and the reversed so-called latent representation.
047   While recent works try to improve the approximation and mitigate discrepancies between noise and
048   latent, in this work, we propose to closely study the DDIM inversion procedure and highlight the
049   relation between Gaussian noise, generated samples, and their latents.
050   First, we emphasize the main differences between initial Gaussian noise and latent codes. We locate
051   the latent space between the initial Gaussian noise and generated samples, showing that DDIM
052   inversion does not properly turn the image into noise. We show how this relation changes with time,
053   highlighting the importance of early training steps in the formation of sample-to-latent mappings.
      Second, we move to the analysis of the relation between noise and samples. We show that it is


                                                        1
054
      possible to correctly assign initial Gaussian noise to the generated sample with Euclidean distance.
055   We further deepen this analysis to a non-stationary setup, showing that the mapping between noises
056   and generations emerges at the very beginning of the diffusion model training.
057
058
      The main contribution of this work can be summarized as follows:
059
             • We show that reverse DDIM produces latent representations that are not standard multi-
060
               variate Gaussian, creating a gap between the diffusion models’ theory and practice.
061
062          • We study how this relation changes with training and show that improving the generative
063
               capabilities of the model does not improve the accuracy of reverse DDIM.
064          • We show that the relation between images and noises is defined by the simple L2 distance
065            at the early stage of the training.

      3     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Diffusion models inversion techniques Thanks to the reversible sampling procedure, Denoising
111   Diffusion Implicit Models are often employed in tasks such as inpainting (Zhang et al., 2023), im-
112   age (Su et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; Hertz et al., 2022), video (Ceylan et al., 2023) or speech (Deja
113   et al., 2023a) edition. However, the baseline DDIM approach is based on the assumption that the
114   prediction of the noise removed from the image in the t-th backward diffusion step closely approx-
115   imates the noise of the (t − 1)-th step. This assumption is not always true, and recent works try to
116   mitigate the discrepancies of such approximation. In particular, Renoise (Garibi et al., 2024) itera-
117   tively improves the prediction of added noise using the predictor-corrector technique. This allows
118   to closely estimate the original Gaussian noise for a given image, even with fewer diffusion steps.
119
      Several works aim to reverse the diffusion process in text-to-image models. In such a case, the
      prompt selection significantly influences the final latent. To mitigate this issue, Null-text inversion
120
      method (Mokady et al., 2023) extends the DDIM inversion with optimized pivotal noise vectors
121
      and additional Null-text optimization technique, where unconditional textual embeddings employed
122   by classifier free-guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021) are optimized in order to reduce the reconstruc-
123   tion error. Other work (Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2024) proposes an alternative DDPM noise
124   space, where noise maps do not have a normal distribution, yet it enables better image editing ca-
125   pabilities and perfect reconstructions. On the other hand, a regularized Newton-Raphson inversion
126   method (Meiri et al., 2023) formulates the inversion process as solving an implicit equation using
127   numerical techniques, achieving faster and higher-quality reconstructions with prompt-aware adjust-
128   ments, enabling improvements in image interpolation, and boosting models’ diversity. Furthermore,
129   exact inversion methods for DPM-solvers (Hong et al., 2024) mitigate the errors introduced by
130
      classifier-free guidance by leveraging gradients, boosting both the image and noise reconstruction.
131
132
      Latent space in diffusion models One of the significant drawbacks of diffusion-based generative
      models compared to approaches such as VAEs, Flows, or GANs is the lack of meaningful latent
133
      space that encodes features of the generated samples. Several approaches try to mitigate this issue.
134
      Kwon et al. (2022) show that so-called h-features, which are the activations located inside of the U-
135   Net model used as a diffusion decoder, can be used as meaningful representations providing space
136   for semantically coherent image manipulation. This idea is further extended by Park et al. (2023),
137   where the authors show that we can calculate the pullback metric that directly associates h-features
138   with the original image space. Several works show that we can directly benefit from such features
139   in downstream tasks such as image segmentation (Baranchuk et al., 2021; Tumanyan et al., 2023;
140   Rosnati et al., 2023), image correspondence (Luo et al., 2024) or classification (Deja et al., 2023b).
141
142   Noise-to-image mapping in diffusion models Contrary to latent variable models such as VAEs,
143   the forward diffusion process that maps images to the Gaussian noise is a parameter-free process that
144   can be understood as hierarchical VAE with pre-defined unlearnable encoder Kingma et al. (2021).
145
      However, several works show interesting properties resulting from the training objective of DDPMs
      and score-based models. Kadkhodaie et al. (2024) show that due to inductive biases of denoising
146
      models, different DDPMs trained on similar datasets converge to almost identical solutions. This
147
      idea is further explored by Zhang et al. (2024), where the authors show that even models with
148   different architectures converge to the same score function and, hence, the same noise-to-generations
149   mapping. This mapping itself is further analyzed by Khrulkov & Oseledets (2022), where the authors
150   show that the encoder map coincides with the optimal transport map for common distributions. In
151   this work, we extend this analysis further by empirically validating that examples are generated from
152   the closest random noise, even for more complex distributions.

168   4.2     N OISE ̸= L ATENT
169
170   Numerous methods employ reversed DDIM in applications such as image editing or inpainting. In
171   this case, the underlying assumption is that by encoding the image back with a denoising decoder, we
172
      can obtain the original noise that can be used to reconstruct the original image. However, as noticed
      by recent works Garibi et al. (2024); Parmar et al. (2023); Hong et al. (2024), the latents created
173
      with DDIM inversion (x̂T ) often do not follow standard multivariate Gaussian distribution, creating
174
      a gap between the promise of diffusion models’ theory and practice. We study the implications of
175   this fact and show its far-reaching consequences.
176
177   We visualize this phenomenon in Figure 1 for three models, showing the latent or its difference
178
      from the generated image. For simpler ones trained with smaller datasets, such as CIFAR10, we
      can observe clear structures of original images in the inverted latents, as presented in Figure 1 (C).
179
      However, even for more complex datasets, we can highlight the inversion error by plotting the image
180
      difference between the latent and the noise, as presented for DDPM trained on ImageNet and LDM
181   trained on CelebA (Figure 1 (A, B)). We can observe that the highest inversion error can be observed
182   in the areas of large monotonic surfaces, where more high-frequency noise needs to be added.

211   4.3     W HAT IS THE LOCATION OF THE LATENT VARIABLE ?
212
213   Given the results of the previous analysis, we pose the next question: what is the location of the latent
214   variables calculated with the reverse DDIM procedure? We show that the DDIM-generated latent
215   (x̂T ) is located along the trajectory xt between the Gaussian noise (xT ) and the generated sample
      (x0 ), and discuss how this relation changes with different characteristics of diffusion models.

217   latents. We average the angles across 1K samples from three different models and plot the triangles
218   in 2D space. We can observe that in each case, the angle located at the image’s vertex, ∠x0 , is
219   acute but never zero. On the other hand, the angle located at the vertex representing latents, ∠x̂T , is

382   5   C ONCLUSIONS
383
384   In this work, we empirically study the relation between initial Gaussian noise, generated samples,
385   and their latent representations calculated with the inverse DDIM technique. First, we show that the
386
      error in the approximation of the previous noise in DDIM leads to representations located next to the
      generation trajectory between starting samples and their true noises. Moreover, prolonged diffusion
387
      training does not affect this property, as the accuracy of DDIM inversion does not improve in time.
388
      Then, studying the relation between the generated samples and Gaussian noise, we show that we
389   can accurately assign the initial noise of the given generation with a simple L2 distance. We also
390   demonstrate that this behavior emerges at the very beginning of the diffusion models training. Our
391   experiments lead to the conclusion that the initial part of the diffusion model’s training is responsible
392   for building the relation between the initial Gaussian noise, final generations, and their inverted
393   representations.
