011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Deploying large language models (LLMs) is challenging due to their high memory
014            and computational demands, especially during long-context inference. While key-
015
               value (KV) caching accelerates inference by reusing previously computed keys
               and values, it also introduces significant memory overhead. Existing KV cache
016
               compression methods—such as eviction and merging—typically compress the KV
017
               cache after it is generated and overlook the hidden states, failing to improve the
018            speed of the prefilling stage. Additionally, applying a uniform compression rate
019            across different attention heads can harm crucial retrieval heads in needle-in-a-
020            haystack tasks due to excessive compression. In this paper, we propose UNComp,
021            an uncertainty-aware compression scheme that leverages matrix entropy to esti-
022            mate model uncertainty across layers and heads at the token sequence level. By
023            grouping layers and heads based on their uncertainty, UNComp adaptively com-
024            presses both the hidden states and the KV cache. Our method achieves a 1.6×
025            speedup in the prefilling stage and reduces the KV cache to 4.74% of its original
026
               size, resulting in a 6.4× increase in throughput and a 1.4× speedup in inference
               with only a 1.41% performance loss. Remarkably, in needle-in-a-haystack tasks,
027
               UNComp outperforms the full-size KV cache even when compressed to 9.38% of
028
               its original size. Our approach offers an efficient, training-free Grouped-Query
029            Attention paradigm that can be seamlessly integrated into existing KV cache
030            schemes.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The proliferation of large language models (LLMs) has led to unprecedented advancements in natu-
036   ral language processing (Achiam et al., 2023; Kaplan et al., 2020), enabling capabilities ranging from
037   simple text generation to complex reasoning and dialogue. However, deploying and scaling LLMs
038
      are significantly hindered by extensive memory requirements and computational costs (Shazeer
      et al., 2017), especially during long-context inference. Processing long contexts leads to signif-
039
      icant computational time during the prefilling stage, and the “attention sink” phenomenon (Xiao
040
      et al., 2023) during decoding impedes efficient long-text generation.
041
042   To mitigate these issues, KV caching (Pope et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) stores and reuses keys
043   and values to avoid redundant computations, improving inference speed. However, the memory
044
      overhead of maintaining the KV cache remains prohibitive for long contexts (Liu et al., 2024b),
      prompting the development of methods to reduce KV cache size while preserving performance.
045
046   Existing optimization methods include: i) Eviction Strategies (Ge et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024d;
047   Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024c); ii) Merging Strategies (Liu et al., 2024b; Wan et al., 2024;
048   Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al.); iii) Quantization (Hooper et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Liu
049   et al., 2024e); iv) Compressing KV Cache Heads (Ainslie et al., 2023; Shazeer, 2019; Liu et al.,
050
      2024a; Yu et al., 2024). However, these methods often compress the KV cache after generation and
      neglect hidden states, failing to speed up the prefilling stage. Moreover, uniform compression across
051
      attention heads can degrade important retrieval heads due to over-compression.
052
053   Multi-Query Attention (MQA) (Ainslie et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Brandon et al., 2024) reduces
      attention heads during inference by grouping heads and using a single head per group, significantly

054
      reducing memory and improving speed while maintaining performance comparable to Multi-Head
055   Attention (MHA). However, these methods typically require fine-tuning or training from scratch,
056   which can be costly. A training-free approach to group heads during inference is more practical.
057
058
      Inspired by these insights, we propose estimating the internal uncertainty across layers and heads
      within the KV cache and hidden states. Previous metrics based on cumulative attention distributions
059
      focus on token-level importance but overlook sequence-level sampling strategies such as estimating
060
      heads and layers. By measuring the effective information via matrix entropy, we quantify uncertainty
061   across heads and layers, enabling adaptive compression of the KV cache and pruning of hidden states
062   for faster inference. Our key contributions are:
063
064
            1. We propose a novel method based on matrix entropy to explore the uncertainty within
065            the hidden states and KV cache, analyzing their information compression patterns. This
066            approach enables grouped compression of heads and layers. By compressing the hidden
067            states, we achieve a 1.6× speedup in the prefilling stage in a single batch.
068
069         2. We employ a training-free approach to adaptively determine the compression ratios for
070            heads and layers across different groups. Compared to the full-size KV cache, our method
071
               achieves a compression rate of 4.74%, with a throughput increase of 6.4× and a 1.4×
               inference speedup in a single batch, incurring only a 1.41% performance loss.
072
073         3. Our method maintains performance even under extreme compression rates where some
074            heads are removed. In the needle-in-a-haystack task, with a 9.38% compression rate (viz.
075            compressed size over original size), our method even surpasses the performance of the
076            full-size KV cache version.

081   2.1   ATTENTION -BASED T OKEN E VICTION P OLICIES
082
083   Early works identify interesting attention patterns in long-context settings, such as the attention
084   sink phenomenon (Liu et al., 2024c; Xiao et al., 2023), where models aggregate information using
085   tokens at the beginning and end. Additionally, certain parameters in LLMs remain in an active
086   state (Sun et al., 2024), inspiring researchers to explore the eviction of input prompts and generated
087   tokens. Recent methods employ cumulative attention scores for token eviction strategies (Zhang
088   et al., 2024c; Li et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024d; Ge et al., 2023; Sheng et al.,
089
      2023; Liu et al., 2024d; Li et al., 2024). These methods aim to optimize memory usage while
      preserving performance. However, they overlook compressing hidden states during the prefilling
090
      stage, which is often the most time-consuming aspect. Further exploration is needed to leverage the
091
      sparsity of hidden states to optimize compression rates and eviction strategies at this stage.
092
093
094   2.2   C OMPRESSION OF THE KV C ACHE H EADS
095
096   Recent studies show that Multi-Head Attention (MHA) varies across heads; some are highly effec-
097   tive at retrieval tasks (Wu et al., 2024), while others specialize in preserving different token types,
098   reflecting inherent patterns of the model (Ge et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024). Leveraging the sparsity
099
      across these head dimensions allows for designing different KV cache eviction strategies for each
      head, thereby improving inference speed. Methods like Multi-Query Attention (MQA) (Shazeer,
100
      2019) share keys and values across heads to reduce the KV cache but at the cost of degraded per-
101
      formance. Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) (Ainslie et al., 2023) merges heads and fine-tunes the
102   model, while Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA) (Liu et al., 2024a) compresses keys and values
103   into a low-rank subspace, reusing the KV cache during inference. However, these methods often
104   require retraining. Yu et al. (2024) use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to fine-tune compressed models,
105   minimizing errors in MHA-to-GQA transitions. CHAI (Agarwal et al., 2024) clusters tokens in the
106   prefilling stage but adds significant computational overhead. Efficiently setting compression rates
107   for different heads without training remains an open challenge, requiring further exploration of the
      sparsity patterns in the heads.

      2.3   T HE C OMPRESSION B EHAVIOR OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
109
110   Sections 2.1 and 2.2 discuss compressing the KV cache by utilizing sparsity patterns of the layer and
111   head dimensions. However, this sparsity pattern is closely related to the model’s internal matrix rank
112   and can alter the model’s compression behavior (Feng et al., 2022). Recent work (Delétang et al.,
113   2023) reveals that models exhibit spontaneous compression behavior during training, demonstrating
114   that LLMs are powerful general compressors and showcasing the scaling laws of model compression
115   capability. Similar phenomena are observed in (Tao et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Moreover,
116
      certain aggregation patterns within prompts in in-context learning are also noted (Wang et al., 2023).
      These observations inspire us to further explore the internal compression patterns of the model,
117
      particularly between different heads and across different layers. We introduce matrix entropy (Zhang
118
      et al., 2023), which can be considered a measure of the rank of the KV cache; higher entropy
119   indicates more uncertainty and information aggregated by the tokens.

      Inter-head Compression Similar to previous works like MQA, GQA, and MLA, we estimate
350
      the compression rate in the head dimension. However, unlike their approaches, our method focuses
351
      solely on the training-free setting, and we apply different compression rates to different head groups.
352
353   We sample 500 data points for observation, typically not included in the current test set, and find
354   that the head patterns across different layers are consistent across datasets, as shown by the effective
355
      rank distribution in Figure 4 (More details presented in Appendix A.4). Therefore, we conclude
      that the effective rank of the heads is not data-dependent, as it is an intrinsic characterization of the
356
      model’s uncertainty measures. We rank the heads within each layer based on their effective rank and
357
      divide them into m groups. The groups are then arranged according to the ranking of the truncated
358   effective rank of each group. Typically, the larger the effective rank, the more information the head
359   contains, the greater the compression ratio, and the fewer tokens are evicted.
360
361
      After determining the context window Si from Eq. 12 for each layer, we set different compression
      rates for head groups based on their truncated effective rank. Specifically, we set the step size ∆sh ,
362
      decreasing the context size from Si,1 , which has the largest effective rank, down to Si,m as follows:
363
364
365                                                   Si,h = Si,h−1 − (h − 1) · ∆sh ,                                                           h = 1, 2, . . . , m,                                                                  (14)
366
367    where Si,h represents the context size for the h-th head group at layer i, and ∆sh is the fixed step
368   size applied between consecutive groups. During decoding, each head maintains its context window
369   according to the compression rate of the group it belongs to. For different heads, we maintain a
370   window of size Si,h using the cumulative attention scores of the last l tokens.
371
      Additionally, we investigate scenarios with extreme compression rates where certain groups with
372   low effective rank are completely removed, excluding them from the forward process. In such cases,
373   the dimensions of the modified attention output are dictated by the altered key and value, leading to
374   a dimensional mismatch with the original attention output. To resolve this, we compute the cosine
375   distance between the attention distributions of the removed and retained heads. These distributions
376   are based on the cumulative attention over the last l tokens. We then fill the attention output ma-
377   trix with the attention distribution from the head most similar to the removed head’s distribution,
      ensuring alignment with the dimension of the original attention output.

526   experiments. To measure this similarity, we use the Pearson correlation coefficient. As depicted in
527   Figure 5(b), higher similarity in matrix entropy trends corresponds to improved model performance.
528

      5                           C ONCLUSION
530   We introduced UNComp, an uncertainty-aware approach for compressing the KV cache and hid-
531   den states in LLMs. By employing matrix entropy to measure model uncertainty across layers and
532   heads, UNComp adaptively determines compression rates, achieving a balance between memory ef-
533   ficiency and model performance. Our experiments demonstrate that UNComp can reduce memory
534   usage significantly, achieving up to a 1.6× speedup during the prefilling stage, a 6.4× throughput
535   improvement, and compressing the KV cache to 4.74% of its original size. Despite this high com-
536
      pression, UNComp maintains a minimal performance loss of only 1.41%, and even surpasses the
      performance of the full-size KV cache in specific needle-in-a-haystack tasks. This indicates that our
537
      method provides an effective solution for optimizing long-context LLM inference without requiring
538
      additional training. Moving forward, our approach can serve as a foundation for further exploration
539   into adaptive compression techniques in large-scale model deployment.

928   covariance matrix ΣX will also have full rank. Therefore, we have rank(ΣX ) = p, and the rank of
929   the covariance matrix is equal to the rank of the original matrix, i.e., rank(ΣX ) = rank(X).
930
      On the other hand, when the number of observations is less than the number of variables (i.e., n < p),
931
      the rank of X is constrained by the number of observations, such that rank(X) ≤ n. Consequently,
932   the rank of the covariance matrix ΣX is also limited by n, meaning rank(ΣX ) ≤ n. Since n < p in
933   this case, the covariance matrix is rank-deficient, and we have rank(ΣX ) < p.
934
935
