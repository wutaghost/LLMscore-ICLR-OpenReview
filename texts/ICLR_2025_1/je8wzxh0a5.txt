011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We consider offline reinforcement learning (RL) with preference feedback in which
014            the implicit reward is a linear function of an unknown parameter. Given an offline
015
               dataset, our objective consists in ascertaining the optimal action for each state,
               with the ultimate goal of minimizing the simple regret. We propose an algorithm,
016
               RL with Locally Optimal Weights or RL-LOW, which yields a simple regret
017
               of exp(вҲ’в„Ұ(n/H)) where n is the number of data samples and H denotes an
018            instance-dependent hardness quantity that depends explicitly on the suboptimality
019            gap of each action. Furthermore, we derive a first-of-its-kind instance-dependent
020            lower bound in offline RL with preference feedback. Interestingly, we observe
021            that the lower and upper bounds on the simple regret match order-wise in the
022            exponent, demonstrating order-wise optimality of RL-LOW. In view of privacy
023            considerations in practical applications, we also extend RL-LOW to the setting
024            of (Оө, Оҙ)-differential privacy and show, somewhat surprisingly, that the hardness
025            parameter H is unchanged in the asymptotic regime as n tends to infinity; this un-
026
               derscores the inherent efficiency of RL-LOW in terms of preserving the privacy of
               the observed rewards. Given our focus on establishing instance-dependent bounds,
027
               our work stands in stark contrast to previous works that focus on establishing
028
               worst-case regrets for offline RL with preference feedback.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Sutton and Barto, 2018) has been widely recognized for its capacity
034   to facilitate agents in learning a sequence of optimal actions through iterative interactions with their
035   environments. However, RL encounters significant hurdles in environments that are characterized
036   by uncertainty or lacking explicit reward signals. To address these shortcomings, the concept of
037   RL with human feedback (or RLHF) has emerged as a prominent paradigm. Preference-based RL
038   (PbRL) (Christiano et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022; Ibarz et al., 2018; Palan et al., 2019) has stood
039
      out as one of the most widely used frameworks for RLHF. In this regard, preference-based RL has
      achieved remarkable performances in practical applications, with particular importance lying in its
040
      ability to align large language models (LLMs) with human intent, thereby mitigating the output of
041
      toxic and dishonest information (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2019; Glaese et al., 2022; Bai
042   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), and improving the quality of applying to the specific tasks (Stiennon
043   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2021).
044
045
      In this work, we tackle the problem of offline RL with preference feedback, wherein the learning
      mechanism operates solely on pre-existing (or offline) data without dynamically engaging with
046
      the environment. Given the high cost associated with human interaction, offline RL has assumed
047
      particular importance in the context of incorporating human feedback. The significance of this
048   offline framework has been justified by many previous prominent works (Shin et al., 2023; Ouyang
049   et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023). For instance, within the learning process of
050   InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) or the training procedure of Ahmadian et al. (2024), a pivotal
051   procedure involves the training of a reward model utilizing pre-trained LLM feature vectors, coupled
052   with the utilization of pre-collected human preference feedback as the training dataset. Conceptually,
053   this procedure can be construed as treating the current prompt context as a state within a certain
      Markov Decision Process (MDP), while the responses generated by the LLM serve as actions within

      this process. Empirical findings presented by Ouyang et al. (2022) demonstrate the efficacy of this
055   offline framework in effectively aligning human intent with the outputs of LLMs.
056
057   However, the literature concerning theoretical analyses within the domain of offline PbRL remains
058
      rather scant. Previous theoretical analyses (Zhu et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2024) of offline PbRL
      predominantly focused on the worst-case (or minimax) regret, often resulting in the derivation of
059
060
      regret upper bounds for their algorithms of the form OМғ(nвҲ’1/2 ), where n is the size of the offline
      dataset. In this work, we adopt a different approach that is centered on instance-dependent guarantees.
061
      In other words, we wish to derive performance guarantees that are functions of the specific problem
062
      instance, thus elucidating the role of fundamental hardness parameters. This yields complementary
063   insights to the existing worst-case analyses. To this end, we design and analyze RL-LOW, a
064   preference-based RL algorithm. Our analysis of the performance RL-LOW unveils an instance-
065   dependent simple regret bound of exp(вҲ’в„Ұ(n/H)), where H is a hardness parameter. This reveals
066   that the simple regret decays exponentially fast in the size of the dataset n and the exponential rate
067   of convergence has also been identified. Complementarily, by proving an instance-dependent lower
068   bound, we show that any algorithm will suffer from a simple regret of at least exp(вҲ’O(n/H)).
069   Thus, the dependence of the problem on H is fundamental and cannot be improved upon, thereby
070   demonstrating the efficacy of RL-LOW and the tightness of our analyses.

072   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
073
      Preference-based RL: From the empirical viewpoint, Christiano et al. (2017) initially demonstrated
074
      that RL systems can effectively address complex tasks like Atari games and simulated robot locomo-
075   tion by learning from human preferences between trajectory segments. Later, numerous researchers
076   started to employ human preference feedback to enhance the performance of LLMs, e.g., aligning the
077   LLMsвҖҷ behavior with human intent (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ziegler et al., 2019; Glaese et al., 2022; Bai
078   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023), and enhancing the efficacy of application to specific tasks (Stiennon
079   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2021).
080
      From the theoretical perspective, the existing literature remains sparse in offline RL with preference
081   feedback. Zhu et al. (2023) elucidated the failure of the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)
082   procedure in some scenarios. Motivated by this, they theoretically prove the (near) minimax optimality
083   of the P ESSIMISTIC MLE approach with a high probability guarantee. In addition, Zhan et al.
084   (2024) introduced a novel paradigm for general reward functions, and they introduce Оө-bracket
085   approximations for reward models, accompanied by a rigorous theoretical analysis delineating sample
086   complexity in terms of approximation error Оө and the high-probability parameter Оҙ.
087   We observe that the above theoretical investigations, while invaluable, are not instance-dependent.
088
      Typically, the above minimax or worst-case guarantees yield upper bounds in the form of OМғ(nвҲ’1/2 )
089   and do not depend on any problem-specific factors (such as suboptimality gaps). Our research
090   stands out as a pioneering attempt in offering an instance-dependent examination for offline RL with
091   preference feedback, thereby bridging a critical gap in the existing literature.
092
      Label-Differential Privacy: In our study, we also consider the notion of label privacy, acknowledging
093
      that the labels in our offline dataset originate from users, thus highlighting the imperative to protect
094
      user privacy. Chaudhuri and Hsu (2011) were among the pioneers in exploring the concept of label
095   privacy within the context of supervised learning for binary classification. Their foundational work
096   posits that the sensitive information primarily resides in the labels, while considering the unlabeled
097   attributes as non-sensitive. Later, the concept of label privacy has been investigated across various
098   machine learning paradigms, including but not limited to PAC learning (Beimel et al., 2013) and deep
099   learning frameworks (Ghazi et al., 2021). This broadened examination underscores the significance
100   and relevance of label privacy considerations across diverse areas of machine learning research and
101   applications.
102   More recently, Chowdhury et al. (2024) investigated the use of label differential privacy to protect
103   the privacy of human labelers in the process of estimating rewards from preference-based feedback.
104   Chowdhury et al. (2024) derive an upper bound for their proposed algorithm on the estimation error.
105   They show that it also decays as O(nвҲ’1/2 ) and the implied constant here depends on (Оө, Оҙ), the
106   parameters that define differential privacy. This bound only applies in the scenario of estimating the
107   reward value and is not applicable if we want to understand how it depends on the simple regret of a
      specific instance. In our work, we consider the effect of (Оө, Оҙ)-DP on the simple regret.

      1.2       O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
109
110   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
111
112   1. We establish the first-of-its-kind instance-dependent lower bound characterized by suboptimality
113   gaps for a given problem instance. Our analysis reveals that this lower bound takes the form
114   exp(вҲ’O(n/H)), where H is a hardness parameter that is an explicit function of the suboptimality
115
      gaps. This finding furnishes a novel, and possibly generalizable, analytical approach for assessing
      algorithmic performance within the realm of preference-based RL.
116
117   2. We design a simple algorithm RL-LOW based on the novel concept of locally optimal weights.
118   Our analysis demonstrates that its expected simple regret matches the aforementioned instance-
119
      dependent lower bound (in the exponential decay rate of the simple regret), thus revealing our
      algorithmвҖҷs achievement of instance-dependent optimality.
120
121   3. We extend RL-LOW to be applicable to the (Оө, Оҙ)-differential privacy with labels by combining
122   the Gaussian mechanism with the aforementioned locally optimal weights. Our analysis demonstrates
123
      that, for large datasets, this combination enables our algorithm to achieve differential privacy without
      weakening the bound on the simple regret, underscoring the superiority of the design and analysis of
124
      RL-LOW.
125
126   4. As a by-product of our analyses, we show that RL-LOW achieves a worst-case bound of the
127   form O(nвҲ’1/2 ). If we translate the high-probability upper p bound in Zhu et al. (2023) to the same
128   worst-case setting, we obtain a bound of the form O( nвҲ’1 log n). Thus, our work provides a
129   noticeable (albeit small) improvement over the state-of-the-art theoretical result in Zhu et al. (2023).

525   This paper addresses the problem of offline RL with preference feedback, aiming to determine
526   the optimal action for each state to minimize the simple regret. We introduced a novel algorithm,
527   RL-LOW, which achieves a simple regret of exp(вҲ’в„Ұ(n/H(v)), where n represents the number
528   of data samples and H(v) characterizes an instance-dependent hardness parameter related to the
529   suboptimality gaps of each action. Additionally, we established a first-of-its-kind instance-dependent
530   lower bound for offline RL with preference feedback, demonstrating the order-wise optimality of RL-
531   LOW (in the exponential decay rate) through the matching of lower and upper bounds on the simple
532   regret. To address privacy concerns, we extended RL-LOW to be amenable to (Оө, Оҙ)-differential
533

534

536   feedback. Some interesting directions for future research include extending our work to incorporate
537   general reward functions (Zhan et al., 2024). In particular, a natural question concerns whether or not
538   there exist an algorithm that is instance-dependent and order-optimal for general reward functions?

      A.1   M ORE RELATED WORKS BEYOND P B RL
656
657   Offline RL without preference feedback The domain of offline RL has been extensively researched
658   over an extended period. Here, We focus on the recent works. Chen and Jiang (2019) revisits and
659   provides theoretical insights into the essential but underexplored assumptions of mild distribution
660   shift and strong representation conditions in value-function approximations, advancing their necessity
661   and applicability. Xie et al. (2021) bridges the gap between online and offline reinforcement learning
662
      by introducing the policy finetuning problem, proposing algorithms that leverage a reference policy
      close to the optimal policy to achieve sample-efficient learning in episodic MDPs. Yin et al. (2022)
663
      investigates the statistical limits of offline reinforcement learning using linear models, introducing
664
      the variance-aware pessimistic value iteration method to improve learning bounds with offline data.
665   More recently, Wang et al. (2022) enhances the understanding of gap-dependent sample complexity
666   in offline reinforcement learning, demonstrating improved rates under specific policy coverage
667   conditions and providing algorithms nearly matching lower bounds. Similarly, Nguyen-Tang et al.
668   (2023) investigated gap-dependent analysis for offline RL, and they achieved fast rates and zero
669   sub-optimality under specific conditions, and providing both gap-dependent upper and lower bounds
670   for performance with linear function approximation.
671   Overall, our study identifies a significant oversight in previous research: the absence of preference
672   feedback consideration. Consequently, our work represents the inaugural investigation into instance-
673   dependent bounds within the context of offline reinforcement learning incorporating preference
674   feedback.
675
      Dueling Bandits The Dueling Bandits problem was first introduced by Yue and Joachims (2009),
676
      sparking a substantial body of subsequent research on the topic. In this section, we highlight some
677   relevant works. Inspired by the classical contextual bandits problem, (DudГӯk et al., 2015) extend
678   the framework of duel bandits into a contextual setting, and they propose a new concept of von
679   Neumann winner, a game-theoretic solution concept that addresses limitations of the Condorcet
680   winner, along with three efficient algorithms for its online learning and approximation from data.
681   In contrast, (Saha, 2021) explore a distinct aspect of contextual dueling bandits through their
682   proposed Subsetwise-Preference Feedback Model, and the author presents two algorithms for pairwise
683   preferences, achieving near-optimal regret bounds, and extending the analysis to general subsetwise
684   preferences, demonstrating that the fundamental performance limits remain consistent regardless
685
      of the subset size. However, this study mainly focuses on the worst-case analysis. More recently,
      (Di et al., 2024) addressed the contextual dueling bandits with adversarial feedback, proposing a
686
      robust algorithm using uncertainty-weighted maximum likelihood estimation. Nonetheless, this work
687
      focuses on the adversarial setting, whereas our work examines the stochastic setting.
688

      A.2   A MOTIVATIONAL EXAMPLE OF L ABEL -DP
690
691   In the development of question-answering (QA) systems, a common approach for improving response
692   quality involves engaging users in a labeling process where they are asked to provide preference
693   labels. Specifically, users evaluate pairs of system-generated responses to a given question and
694   indicate which response they prefer. This method, often referred to as pairwise preference labeling,
695   is integral to training RLHF algorithms that aim to optimize the relevance and utility of answers
696   provided by QA systems.
697   Given our understanding of the nature of this process, our research emphasizes the importance of
698   protecting the confidentiality of user-submitted preference labels. Without any concerted attempt to
699   protect privacy, these labels, which directly reflect individual opinions or biases toward specific types
700   of responses, can potentially reveal sensitive information, e.g., their preferences for some specific
701   political parties. Therefore, we augment our RL-LOW with a label-DP protection mechanism to
      mitigate the risk of privacy breaches from the labels..
