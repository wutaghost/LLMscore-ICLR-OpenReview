009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are of significant importance in diverse real-world
012            applications since they leverage powerful graph learning techniques to solve prob-
013            lems pertaining to social network mining and medical data analysis. Despite their
014            practical relevance, GNNs remain vulnerable to adversarial attacks such as mem-
015            bership inference attacks (MIAs) which pose privacy risks by revealing whether
016
               specific data records were part of the training set of the model. While most ex-
               isting research has focused on designing defense mechanisms for known node-
017
               level MIAs, and in particular, for determining if a certain node was used during
018
               training, only limited attention has been paid to subgraph-structure MIA (SMIA)
019            problems. SMIA methods seek to infer whether a set of nodes forms a partic-
020            ular target structure of interest (such as a graph motif, e.g., clique or multi-hop
021            path) in the training graph. The main contributions of our work are three-fold.
022            The first is a novel robust defense mechanism for GNNs against SMIA attacks.
023            It combines an alternating train-test schedule with a flattening strategy to miti-
024            gate the attacks. The second contribution is a new end-to-end SMIA attack model
025            that outperforms existing attacks by using multiset functions to generate learnable
026            embeddings for collections of nodes. Extensive simulations reveal that the new at-
027
               tack model outperforms prior state-of-the-art attack models on GNNs by 12.31%
               across four datasets when no defense mechanism is present. With the new defense
028
               mechanism, one can achieve an average decrease of 14.30% in the attack AU-
029
               ROC and an 10.05% improvement in target model utility compared to classical
030            defenses, even when using the improved attack scheme. The third contribution
031            is a study that shows that our defense mechanism extends to node-level MIAs as
032            well, offering similar improvements in attack resistance and utility.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as indispensable learning modalities for diverse real-
038   world problems, ranging from social network mining and recommendation system design to biolog-
039
      ical data analysis (Wu et al., 2021b; Zhang et al., 2024). For example, GNNs have been used to
      improve personalized search and recommendations for customers on e-commerce platforms (e.g.,
040
      AliGraph at Alibaba (Zhu et al., 2019) and GIANT at Amazon (Chien et al., 2021a)) and to per-
041
      form inference and prediction on social networks (e.g., PinnerSage at Pinterest (Pal et al., 2020) and
042   LiGNN at LinkedIn (Borisyuk et al., 2024)). GNNs, unlike standard neural networks, make full
043   use of both the discrete graph topology and node and edge features via special embedding methods
044   based on graph convolutions or random walks (Wu et al., 2020). For example, graph convolutions
045   allow GNNs to generate informative embeddings that are well-suited for different downstream tasks.
046
      Despite the successful deployment of GNNs, several weaknesses of GNN models have been pointed
047
      out in the literature. One major concern pertains to data privacy, which is becoming an issue of ever
048   increasing importance. GNNs have exhibited privacy vulnerabilities to various attacks (Sun et al.,
049   2023) such as membership inference attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2022; Olatunji
050   et al., 2021), whose aim is to determine if a certain sample is used in model training; attribute
051   inference attacks (AIAs), whose focus is on inferring statistical information about the data, such as
052   the number of nodes and edges (Gong & Liu, 2018) and others. Among all existing attacks, the most
053   commonly observed and studied attacks are MIAs. As already pointed out, MIAs have the goal to
      reveal whether a given record is part of the training dataset used to build a specific target model,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and are usually based on the model itself, the record and information about the dataset. Typically,
055   MIAs use prediction logits of shadow models to train attack models, where the shadow training
056   data is obtained either through inference of the target model or through access to a potentially noisy
057   version of the original training dataset. Clearly, successful attacks lead to unacceptable information
058   leakage through the model. For example, if a GNN is trained on nodes belonging to a private group
059   within a large social network – e.g., a support group for sensitive medical issues, successful MIAs
060   on GNNs can reveal both the patient identity and their medical condition. As MIAs exploit the
061   differences of the outputs of target model on training and test datasets, most defense mechanisms
062   work towards suppressing the common patterns that quality attacks rely on (Shokri et al., 2017; Jia
063
      et al., 2019; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Hayes et al., 2017; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020; Salem et al.,
      2018; Kaya & Dumitras, 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022).
064
065   Recent studies have focused on node (or edge) MIAs for node (or edge) classification downstream
066   tasks (Olatunji et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021a; He et al., 2021; Conti et al., 2022). Compared to these
067   standard MIAs on GNNs, little attention has been paid to subgraph-structure MIAs (SMIAs) (Wang
068   & Wang, 2024). SMIAs can lead to an especially problematic form of privacy leakage, where at-
069
      tackers can infer not only if certain nodes were present in the training graph but also if there were
      certain relationships between them. These relationships are usually captured via graph motives (tri-
070
      angles, cliques, paths etc). For instance, in a medical data network, cliques may involve members of
071
      the same family and indicative of genetic/familial diseases. In this case, SMIA attacks can compro-
072   mise not only individual medical histories, but – by association – whole family medical conditions.
073   In comparison, standard MIA methods can only reveal if the nodes were used for training a node
074   classifier, but not what the relationships between themselves and other nodes in the graph are (one
075   may think it plausible to perform individual node MIA attacks and then use link inference attacks to
076   infer the subgraph induced by these nodes, but this process is both ineffective and usually of poor
077   utility). It is also important to point out that SMIA attacks differ from subgraph inference attacks
078   (SIAs) (Zhang et al., 2022) whose goal is to determine if a certain type of subgraph is present in the
079   training dataset (without revealing the nodes that constitute the subgraph).
080   The only prior line of work on SMIAs is Wang & Wang (2024). There, the authors propose an
081   attack method based on the use of similarities between posterior vectors of the target nodes which
082   allows them to generate training data for the attack model. On the defense side, the authors calculate
083   the importance of each dimension of the node embeddings using the SHAP algorithm (Scott et al.,
084   2017), and add noise to the least important dimensions in an attempt to balance out the quality of the
085   defence against SMIAs and the utility of the model. However, this attack and defense mechanisms
086
      have several notable limitations. First, the similarity metrics used for the attack are fixed and cannot
      be adapted even when the data distribution has changed. Second, the attack performs well only on
087
      homophilic graphs, but as we subsequently show, offers poor performance on heterophilic graphs.
088
      Third, the similarity calculations split the attack process into two different parts, preventing an end-
089   to-end approach, which results in an increase of the complexity of practical implementation. Finally,
090   the defense still relies on the addition of noise to the embeddings, which inevitably compromises the
091   model utility. To address these issues, we propose both a new attack and improved defense system
092   that can counter both the attack of the original SMIA and our improved attack. The gist of our
093   approach is to replace the fixed similarity calculation with learnable multiset functions that allow
094   for an end-to-end attack that dynamically adapts the training data generation process to the dataset,
095   thereby offering excellent performance on both homophilic and heterophilic datasets. Furthermore,
096   we propose a novel two-stage defense (TSD) strategy followed by flattening (Chen et al., 2022) that
097
      does not significantly sacrifice model utility but provides stronger defense capabilities compared to
      noise addition. The key intuition behind the defense is to obfuscate the posterior distributions via
098
      controlled overfitting and to add flattening noise that can also obfuscate the graph information (note
099
      that flattening noise cannot be directly “translated” to adding noise to node embeddings). Under
100   the TSD approach, the attack approach learned on GNNs without defense mechanisms cannot be
101   easily adapted to those which use defense, leading to a drop in the attack performance. In parallel,
102   flattening (Chen et al., 2022) allows us to increase the variance of the training loss distribution and
103   provides another mechanism for ensuring different posterior distribution for test and trainsets (since
104   different loss distributions lead to different posterior distributions). A detailed description of the
105   approach is delegated to Section 4.
106   To demonstrate the utility of our novel SMIA attack and defense, we adopt the following standard
107   modeling assumptions. First, we focus on black-box attacks. Second, unlike some other approaches

145   2   R ELATED W ORKS
146
147   Due to space limitations, we only provide a brief summary of related results and delegate a more
148   detailed discussion to Appendix A.
149
      MIAs. The concept of MIA was first proposed in Homer et al. (2008) and later extended in various
150   directions, ranging from white-box settings (Nasr et al., 2019; Rezaei & Liu, 2021; Melis et al.,
151   2019; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020) to black-box setting (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2018; Song
152   & Mittal, 2020; Li & Zhang, 2021; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2022). Upon iden-
153   tification of the informative features (e.g., posterior predictions, loss values, gradient norms, etc.)
154   that reveal the sample membership, the attacker can choose to learn either a binary classifier (Shokri
155   et al., 2017) or metric-based decisions (Yeom et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2018) from a shadow model
156   trained on a shadow dataset to extract patterns within features of the training samples to determine
157   the membership. The description of a standard MIA pipeline is available in Appendix C.
158   Defense Against MIAs. As MIAs exploit the behavioral differences of the target model on train-
159   sets and testsets, most defense mechanisms work by suppressing the common patterns among the
160   two. Frequently used defense methods include confidence score masking (Shokri et al., 2017; Jia
161   et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Hanzlik et al., 2021),
      regularization (Hayes et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2018; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020; Wang et al., 2020;


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Kaya & Dumitras, 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022), knowledge
163   distillation (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2020; Tang et al., 2022), and differential privacy (Naseri
164   et al., 2020; Saeidian et al., 2021). For example, confidence score masking aims to hide the true
165   prediction vector returned by the target model and it thus mitigates the effectiveness of MIAs by
166   providing only top-k logits per inference (Shokri et al., 2017), or adding noise to the prediction
167   vector in an adversarial manner (Jia et al., 2019). Regularization aims to reduce the degree of over-
168   fitting of target models to mitigate MIAs (Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Hayes et al., 2017; Leino &
169   Fredrikson, 2020; Salem et al., 2018; Kaya & Dumitras, 2021; Yu et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020;
170   Chen et al., 2022). Knowledge distillation aims to transfer the knowledge from an unprotected
171
      model to a protected model (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2020), while differential privacy (Saeidian
      et al., 2021) protects membership information via noise injection and offers theoretical performance
172
      guarantees, at the cost of substantial utility drop.
173
174   MIAs and Defense Strategies for GNNs. There are a handful of research that focuses on extending
175   MIA and corresponding defense mechanisms to graph learning framework. Olatunji et al. (2021)
176   analyzed graph MIA in two settings (train on subgraph, test on subgraph/full), and proposed the
177
      LBP defense based on the confidence score masking idea, He et al. (2021) proposed zero-hop and
      two-hop attacks designed for inductive GNNs, Wang & Wang (2023) studied the link membership
178
      inference problem in an unsupervised fashion, and Chen et al. (2024) developed MaskArmor based
179
      on masking and distillation technique. Besides node and edge MIAs, Zhang et al. (2022); Wang &
180   Wang (2024) also explored the subgraph attacks along with perturbation-based defense mechanism.
181
182   k-node Structure Membership Inference (k-SMIA) is a new form of a privacy attack (Wang &
183
      Wang, 2024). The aim of this attack is to determine whether a collection of k nodes within the
      training set belongs to a subgraph structure of interest (e.g., path or clique). k-SMIA hence uses a
184
      new definition of membership: members are substructures of k nodes that form a relevant topology.
185
      The original SMIA work outlines a novel black-box SMIA attack that combines training a three-
186   label classifier with training shadows. This attack outperforms node-level MIAs followed by link
187   prediction. The defense against SMIA relies on perturbing embedding of the nodes with the smallest
188   “contribution” to the accuracy of the model and it results in a performance comparable to that offered
189   by differential privacy. Despite these positive initial findings, one has to point out that a performance
190   matched by differential privacy methods may not be desirable, since the latter is usually significantly
191   reduced compared to defenseless models. Note that k-SMIA still constitutes an attack against node-
192   level models, so that the downstream task of the target model remains node classification.

196   In this paper we focus on the downstream task as supervised node classifications; nevertheless, our
197   method is applicable to different graph learning scenarios. Let G = (X, A, Y, V Train , V Test ) denote
198   the graph dataset with node features X ∈ Rn×d , adjacent matrix A ∈ Rn×n , one-hot encoded node
199   labels Y ∈ Rn×C , trainset V Train and testset V Train . Here n is the number of nodes, d is the feature
200   dimension, C is the number of classes, V Train and V Test are disjoint and |V Train | + |V Test | = n. We
201   later on use Y Train to denote the labels of V Train , and Ŷ Test to denote the predicted labels of V Test .
202   Since X and A are already known during training, the goal of k-SMIA is determing the subgraph-
203   substructure membership: given a set of k nodes Vatt ⊂ V Train ∪ V Test , determine if Vatt forms
204
      either a k-clique or a (k − 1)-hop path in G (member) or not (non-member). Meanwhile, the goal of
      MIA is determing the label membership: given a node v ∈ V Train ∪ V Test , determine if v ∈ V Train
205
      (member) or not (non-member). Following the practice of SMIA and MIA, we also need a shadow
206
      dataset Gs = (Xs , As , Ys , VsTrain , VsTest ) to train the shadow model, and Gs can be different from G.

267   To demonstrate that our defense mechanism is effective against various types of SMIAs, we evaluate
268   it using both the similarity-based attacks introduced by Wang & Wang (2024) and a novel attack
269
      based on multiset functions. We begin by reviewing the similarity-based attacks, highlighting their
      strengths and limitations, before introducing our new end-to-end attack scheme.

      Similarity-Based Attacks. The core of similarity-based SMIA lies in processing the shadow
271   model’s output to generate data for attack model training. For example, for a k = 3 clique, the
272   shadow model provides three posterior vectors for three nodes. Pairwise similarities between these
273   vectors are computed and sorted in ascending order to form a new vector. This process is repeated
274   for three different similarity metrics, namely the dot product, cosine similarity, and ℓ2 distance. The
275   sorted vectors for each metric are concatenated to create the training data for the attack model along
276   with the structure labels (see also Appendix B).
277   While similarity-based attacks have been
278   shown to outperform other link-level attacks for
279   SMIA, they have limitations. First, the choice
280   of similarity metrics is heuristic and may not
281   be effective in all scenarios. Second, the com-
282   putational complexity is O(k 3 )1 , which is pro-
283   hibitive for large k.
284   Multiset Function-Based Attacks. To ad-
285   dress the limitations of similarity-based attacks,
286   we propose using multiset functions to aggre-

314   MIA (Carlini et al., 2022). Note that good defenses should lead to large CAs and small AUs.

366

      When comparing TSD and SHAN under the same conditions, we observe that TSD offers superior
      defensive capabilities while maintaining higher classification accuracy for the target model. This
371
      is because TSD alters the posterior distribution through an additional training phase and the intro-
372
      duction of flattening. These two methods are applied only after the target model has already com-
373   pleted learning from the data, so the classification performance of the target model remains largely
374   unaffected. In contrast, SHAN aims to minimize the impact on the target model’s classification
375   performance by adding noise only to the least important dimensions of the embeddings. However,
376   this also means that the noise’s ability to disrupt the attack decreases due to the reduced importance
377   of those dimensions. Therefore, it is difficult to achieve both high model utility and strong defense
      ability, regardless of how the noise is added.

      Our TSD method differs from the conventional training methods in two aspects: (1) the use of
433   flattening operations; and (2) two-stage training. To demonstrate their roles in enhancing defense
434   capability, we conducted the following ablation experiments.

      ing); (3) flattening (one-stage); and (4) TSD. Here, standard training refers to training a target model
437
      only on the trainset; two-stage trains a target model in a train-test alternate fashion, equivalent to
438
      TSD without flattening; flattening is the same as described in Section 4, combined with one-stage
439   training. Clearly, TSD is two-stage combined with flattening. We conduct our experiments using
440   End2end-SMIA.

446   6.2   E XPERIMENTS P ERTAINING TO MIA D EFENSE
447
448   TSD is also capable of defending against node-level MIA. Previous studies on MIA (Homer et al.,
449   2008; Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2018; Song & Mittal, 2020) have pointed out that the key
450   to a successful MIA attack is to allow overfitting in the target model. Since TSD has the ability
451   to reduce overfitting, it should intuitively have the capability to defend against MIA: the two-stage
452   training process in TSD can help reduce the gap between the average losses of training and testing
453   nodes, while the use of flattening reduces the difference in the variance of loss distributions between
454
      the training and testing nodes. We demonstrate the effectiveness of TSD’s defense by comparing it
      with other defense methods. We choose two representative defense methods, Laplacian Binned Pos-
455
      terior Perturbation (LBP) (Olatunji et al., 2021) on GNNs and Distillation for Membership Privacy
456
      (DMP) (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2020) on graphless models, as our defense baselines. LBP is
457   the state-of-the-art defense method for GNNs and it works by adding Laplacian noise to the poste-
458   rior before it is released to the user. To reduce the amount of noise needed to distort the posteriors,
459   LBP does not add noise to each element of the posterior, but to binned posterior. In our experiments,
460   we first randomly shuffle the posteriors and then assign each posterior to a partition/bin. The total
461   number of bins N is predefined based on the number of classes. For each bin, we sample noise with
462   scale b from the Laplace distribution. The sampled noise is added to each element in the bin. After
463   the noise added to each bin, we restore the initial positions of the noisy posteriors and release them.
464   Appendix Table 13 shows the best set of parameters for LBP that we used in our experiments.
465   On the other hand, we adapted DMP to the case of GNN training. DMP consists of three phases,
466   namely pre-distillation, distillation and post-distillation. The pre-distillation phase trains an unpro-
467   tected model on a private training data without any privacy protection. Next, in the distillation phase,
468   DMP selects reference data and transfers the knowledge of the unprotected model into predictions
469   om the reference data. Notice that private training data and the reference data have no intersec-
470   tion. Finally, in the post-distillation phase, DMP uses the predictions to train a protected model.
471
      Our experiments use the same model structure for the unprotected and protected models. To follow
      the DMP procedure, we need to further split the trainset into a private dataset and reference dataset,
472
      where the private dataset trains the unprotected models, and the reference dataset trains the protected
473
      target model. Compared to DMP, TSD can directly train the target model with the full trainset.
474
475   In our experiments, the split ratio of trainsets and testsets for TSD and LBP is 1:1, and the split ratio
476   of private datasets, reference datasets and testsets in DMP is 0.45:0.45:0.1. Since MIA is easier to
477
      defend against than SMIAs, our experiments are conducted on more complex and diverse datasets.
      Table 4 and Table 5 shows partial result of our experiments, and the complete results can be found in
478
      Appendix J.1 and J.2. The results indicate that our method achieves defense capabilities comparable
479
      to LBP and DMP, while achieving better model utility performance. It is important to emphasize
480   that a lower attack AUROC does not necessarily indicate stronger defense. The stronger the defense,
481   the closer the AUROC should be to 0.5, as 0.5 represents random guessing. When the AUROC is
482   less than 0.5, the attackers can flip the prediction results to make the AUROC greater than 0.5.
483   Compared to LBP, TSD significantly improved the model utility. The main reason is that LBP is a
484   perturbation-based method, which can potentially hurt the target model performance significantly.
485   However, TSD achieves defense by alleviating overfitting, which delves deeper into the core issue,
      instead of adversely affecting target models. In addition, compared to DMP, TSD still achieves

526   7      C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
527
528   We proposed a novel two-stage defense method (TSD) against SMIAs for GNNs, and leveraged
529   multiset functions to enhance SMIA attacks (End2end-SMIA). Our evaluation showed that TSD
530   surpasses SHAN in defending against various types of SMIAs, establishing a new state-of-the-art
531   benchmark. Additionally, we compared TSD with LBP and DMP in defending against classical
532   node-level MIAs, demonstrating that TSD also performs effectively in protecting against the normal
533   attacks. We conducted ablation studies and validated the origin of TSD’s defense capability. TSD
534   exhibits superior performance and is easy to integrate into various GNNs training processes.
535   Limitations and Future Work. The current form of TSD has the following limitations: (1) It can
536   lead to lower model utility because the labels used in second stage are pseudolabels of test nodes,
537   instead of groud-truth labels; (2) The flattening parameter β is not end-to-end learnable, and uniform
538   flattening may not be the optimal way to counter SMIAs and MIAs. To address these limitations,
539   we are exploring to use only the test nodes with high confidence predictions and change the formula
      of soft labels to make β learnable.

      A     E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
757
758
      Membership Inference Attacks. MIA on ML models aim to infer whether a data record was used
759
      to train a target ML model or not. This concept is firstly proposed by Homer et al. (2008) and later
760   on extended to various directions, ranging from white-box setting where the whole target model is
761   released (Nasr et al., 2019; Rezaei & Liu, 2021; Melis et al., 2019; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020), to
762   black-box setting where only (partial of) output predictions are accessible to the adversary (Shokri
763   et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2018; Song & Mittal, 2020; Li & Zhang, 2021; Choquette-Choo et al.,
764   2021; Carlini et al., 2022). As a general guideline for MIA, the attacker first need to determine the
765   most informative features that distinguish the sample membership. This feature can be posterior
766   predictions (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2019), loss values (Yeom et al., 2018;
767   Sablayrolles et al., 2019), or gradient norms (Nasr et al., 2019; Rezaei & Liu, 2021). Upon identify-
768
      ing the informative features, the attacker can choose to learn either a binary classifier (Shokri et al.,
      2017) or metric-based decisions (Yeom et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2018) from shadow model trained
769
      on shadow dataset to extract patterns in these features among the training samples for identifying
770
      membership. The shadow dataset can be either generated from target model inferences, or a noisy
771   version of the original dataset depending on the assumptions of the attacker.
772
773   Defense Against Membership Inference Attacks. As MIA exploit the behavioral differences of
774
      the target model on trainset and testset, most defense mechanisms work towards suppressing the
      common patterns that an optimal attack relies on. Popular defense methods include confidence
775
      score masking, regularization, knowledge distillation, and differential privacy. Confidence score
776
      masking aims to hide the true prediction vector returned by the target model and thus mitigates the
777   effectiveness of MIAs, including only providing top-k logits per inference (Shokri et al., 2017), or
778   add noise to the prediction vector in an adversarial manner (Jia et al., 2019). Regularization aims
779   to reduce the overfitting degree of target models to mitigate MIAs. Existing regularization methods
780   including L2 -norm regularization (Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Hayes et al., 2017), dropout (Leino
781   & Fredrikson, 2020; Salem et al., 2018), data argumentation (Kaya & Dumitras, 2021; Yu et al.,
782   2021), model compression (Wang et al., 2020), and label smoothing (Chen et al., 2022). Knowledge
783   distillation aims to transfer the knowledge from a unprotected model to a protected model (She-
784   jwalkar & Houmansadr, 2020), and differential privacy (Saeidian et al., 2021) naturally protects the
785
      membership information with theoretical guarantees at the cost of lower model utility.
786
787   B     S TANDARD SMIA P ROCESS
788
789   The Standard SMIA contains three phases: shadow GNN model training, attack model training,
790   and membership inference. (1) shadow GNN model training: The adversary trains a set of shadow
791   models Φs1 to ΦsT on shadow graphs to mimic the behaviors of the target model. In fact, training a
792   single shadow model is sufficient to achieve performance comparable to training T models. To train
793   Φs1 to ΦsT , the attackers will randomly sample the training dataset Gis for each shadow model from
794   shadow dataset Gs . Then the attackers train Φs1 to ΦsT by using (Xis , Asi , YiTrain, s , ViTrain, s ). (2) attack
795   model training: The attackers firstly generate a training dataset ATrain from the output of the shadow
796   models on the shadow graph. The generation of ATrain follows four steps. First, for each shadow
797   graph sample Gis , the attackers select a set of k-node sets Vatt

819
      The standard MIA process also has three phases: shadow GNN model training, attack model train-
820   ing, and membership inference. (1) shadow GNN model training: shadow GNN model S is a model
821   trained by attackers to replicate the behavior of the target GNN model M , providing training data
822   for the attack model A. To train S, we assume that the shadow dataset Gs comes from the same or
823   similar underlying distribution as Gt . Then the attackers train S by using (Xs , As , YsTrain , VsTrain ) (2)
824   attack model training: To train A, attackers use the trained S to predict all nodes in VsTrain and VsTest
825   and obtain the corresponding posteriors. For each node, attackers take its posteriors as input of the
826   attack model and assigns a label “1” if the node is from VsTrain and “0” if the node is from VsTest to
827   supervise. (3) membership inference: To implement membership inference attack on a given node
828
      v, attackers query M with v’s feature to obtain its posterior. Then attackers input the posterior into
      the attack model to obtain the membership information. The complete standard MIA process can be
829
      found in Olatunji et al. (2021).

832   D    C OMPARISON WITH LBP AND DMP
833
834   Compared to state-of-the-art defense methods based on perturbations and distillation, such as
835   LBP (Olatunji et al., 2021) for GNNs and DMP (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2020) for graphless
836   models, TSD can achieve a better balance between model utility and defense performance. LBP
837
      employs noise addition to the posteriors of the target model, grouping the elements randomly and
      adding noise from the same Laplace distribution to each group to reduce the required amount of
838
      noise. While LBP offers strong defense capabilities, the added noise significantly degrades the tar-
839
      get model utility. DMP, on the other hand, tunes the data used for knowledge transfer to enhance
840   membership privacy. It utilizes an unprotected model trained on private data to guide the training of
841   a protected target model on reference data, optimizing the tradeoff between membership privacy and
842   utility. However, DMP necessitates the collection of an additional dataset for training the protected
843   model, which complicates the whole process. Meanwhile, our method addresses the overfitting
844   problem implicitly by ensuring that both the training set and testset undergo the same procedure.
845   Consequently, our method offers several advantages over LBP and DMP: (1) it avoids explicitly
846   adding noise to the target model predictions, thereby preserving model utility; (2) it does not require
847   additional data; and (3) it fully leverages the whole graph data through a train-test alternate training
848
      schedule.
849
850   E    D ETAILS OF DMP LOSS FUNCTION
851
852   In our experiments, the post-distillation phase of DMP consists of two parts of loss to train the
853   protected model, with the proportion adjusted by a hyperparameter. One loss is the cross-entropy
854   loss, supervised by the true labels of the reference data. The other loss is the KL divergence between
855   the prediction of the protected model and the unprotected model on the reference data. The former
856   is to ensure that the protected model has a high classification accuracy on the testset, while the
857
      latter is to guide the protected model by using the knowledge from the unprotected model. In our
      experiments, we adjust the hyperparameters to balance the testset classification accuracy and defense
858
      capability of the protected model.
