009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Decision trees (DTs) and their random forest (RF) extensions are workhorses of
012            classification and regression in Euclidean spaces. However, algorithms for learn-
013            ing in non-Euclidean spaces are still limited. We extend DT and RF algorithms
014            to product manifolds: Cartesian products of several hyperbolic, hyperspherical,
015            or Euclidean components. Such manifolds handle heterogeneous curvature while
016
               still factorizing neatly into simpler components, making them compelling em-
               bedding spaces for complex datasets. Our novel angular reformulation of DTs
017
               respects the geometry of the product manifold, yielding splits that are geodesi-
018
               cally convex, maximum-margin, and composable. In the special cases of single-
019            component manifolds, our method simplifies to its Euclidean or hyperbolic coun-
020            terparts, or introduces hyperspherical DT algorithms, depending on the curvature.
021            We benchmark our method on various classification, regression, and link predic-
022            tion tasks on synthetic data, graph embeddings, mixed-curvature variational au-
023            toencoder latent spaces, and empirical data. Compared to six other classifiers,
024            product DTs and RFs ranked first on 21 of 22 single-manifold benchmarks and 18
025            of 35 product manifold benchmarks, and placed in the top 2 on 53 of 57 bench-
026            marks overall. This highlights the value of product DTs and RFs as straightfor-
027
               ward yet powerful new tools for data analysis in product manifolds.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   While much of machine learning focuses on Euclidean spaces, these can fail to capture the true
032   structure of complex datasets. For example, hierarchical structures, which are common in taxonomy
033   (e.g., phylogenetic trees) are better represented in hyperbolic space due to its exponential volume
034   growth, which naturally mirrors tree-like data (Sonthalia & Gilbert, 2020). Similarly, cyclical struc-
035   tures, often encountered in time-series data with periodic patterns (e.g., seasonal trends, neuronal
036   spiking dynamics), can benefit from spherical representations (Ding & Regev, 2021).
037   However, many real-world datasets donвҖҷt conform to a single geometric structure. Any constant-
038   curvature manifoldвҖ”вҖ“whether hyperbolic, spherical, or EuclideanвҖ”вҖ“would struggle to represent all
039   the nuances of such data simultaneously. Product manifolds, as proposed by Gu et al. (2018), offer
040   a solution. By combining multiple constant-curvature component manifolds (spherical, Euclidean,
041   and hyperbolic spaces) into a single product manifold, they can better capture the complexity of
042   such mixed-structure data. This flexibility reduces distortion when modeling pairwise distances and
043   enables a more accurate representation of the underlying data structure.
044   Despite their advantages, product manifolds have seen limited adoption in machine learning, partic-
045   ularly for inference tasks like classification and regression. Existing work has primarily focused on
046   applications in biology (McNeela et al., 2024) and knowledge graphs (Wang et al., 2021). However,
047   tools for leveraging product manifold representations in downstream tasks remain scarce.
048   In this paper, we introduce mixed-curvature decision trees (DTs) and random forests (RFs), ex-
049   panding the toolkit for analyzing product manifold data. By enabling inference directly on product
050   manifold coordinates, our approach is well-suited for datasets that combine hierarchical, cyclical,
051   and other complex geometric patterns. This framework provides a principled way to learn from
052   such structures, achieving more accurate results than competing models. These contributions offer
053   new possibilities for applying product manifold representations in fields ranging from biological
      modeling to temporal-spatial analysis.

      2.2     D ECISION TREES AND RANDOM FORESTS
217
218   The Classification and Regression Trees (CART) (Breiman, 2017) algorithm fits a DT T to a set of
219   labeled data (X, y). Specifically, it greedily selects a split at each set to partition the dataset in such
220   a way as to maximize the information gain,
221
                                                      |y+ |          |yвҲ’ |
222                               IG(y) = C(y) вҲ’            C(y+ ) вҲ’       C(yвҲ’ ).                          (13)
223
                                                       |y|            |y|
224   In this case, C(В·) is some sort of impurity function (we use Gini impurity for classification and
225   variance for regression). Some splitting function S(В·) is used to partition the labels y into two
226   classes, y+ and yвҲ’ ; however, S(В·) also partitions the input space (corresponding to some X that
227   does not appear in Eq. 13) into decision regions. Classically, S(В·) is a thresholding function and thus
228   breaks the input space into high-dimensional boxes given some dimension d and threshold Оё:
229                                             S(x) = I{xd > Оё}.                                           (14)
230
231   This algorithm is applied recursively to each decision region until a stopping condition is met (e.g.,
232   maximum number of splits is reached). The result is a fitted DT, T , which can be used for inference.
233   During inference, an unseen point x is passed through T until it reaches a leaf node corresponding
234   to some decision region. For classification, the point is then assigned the majority label inside that
235   region; for regression, it is assigned the mean value inside that region.
236
      Finally, a RF is an ensemble of DTs, typically trained on a bootstrapped subsample of the points and
237   features in X (Breiman, 2001).
238
239   2.2.1    H YPERBOLIC DECISION TREE ALGORITHMS
240
241   The hyperplane perspective on DTs is helpful background for understanding our method: mathemat-
242   ically, thresholding x on a dimension is equivalent to taking its dot product with the normal vector of
243   a separating hyperplane P, even in hyperbolic space. Although this is easy to compute for classical
244
      thresholding boundaries, which are zero in all dimensions but d, this perspective principally admits
      any hyperplane P as a valid decision boundary.
245
246   Naturally, considerations around choosing an appropriate (and computationally efficient) P abound.
247   To this end, Chlenski et al. (2024) impose homogeneity and sparsity constraints on the hyperplanes
248   they consider for hyperbolic DTs. In hyperbolic space, homogenous hyperplanesвҖ” hyperplanes
249   that contain the origin of the ambient spaceвҖ”intersect HD,K at geodesic submanifolds: that is,
250
      P вҲ© HD,K is closed under shortest paths according to ОҙH . The sparsity constraint enforces that the
      normal vectors of P must be nonzero only in two positions: the timelike coordinate x0 and some
251
      other xd , which ensures that only O(nd) candidate hyperplanes are considered per split, and each
252
      decision can be computed in O(1) time using sparse dot products.

      4.3   BASELINES
477
478   We use Scikit-Learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) DTs and RFs in both the ambient space RD+1 and the
479   tangent plane TВө0 P as baselines. Ambient space models operate directly on ambient space coordi-
480   nates. Tangent plane models project points from P to TВө0 P by applying the logarithmic map at Вө0
481   as a preprocessing step. We use Scikit-Learn k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) classifiers and regressors
482   with precomputed pairwise distance matrices according to ОҙP (Eq. 11). Finally, we implemented the
483   product space perceptron algorithm described in Tabaghi et al. (2021). For our own  models, we set
484   hyperparameters identically to Scikit-Learn DTs and RFs, except we consider all D   2 projectionsвҖ”
485   for a total of 3 features per 2-dimensional component manifold, just like ambient space methods use.
      Full details for each model can be found in Appendix E.1.

517   5     C ONCLUSION
518
519   We present strong preliminary evidence favoring mixed-curvature DTs and RFs. In particular, we
520
      motivate and describe our entire algorithm and demonstrate its effectiveness across a highly diverse
      set of 57 benchmarks covering a variety of tasks and geometries.
521
522   Product manifold DTs and RFs offer a valuable balance of expressiveness and simplicity, positioned
523   between extremely legible but underpowered linear classifiers and powerful but uninterpretable neu-
524   ral networks operating in product manifolds. We believe that these qualities, combined with their
525   demonstrated performance across our benchmark datasets, are compelling evidence of our methodвҖҷs
526
      usefulness in a non-Euclidean data analysis toolkit.
527   Limitations. While we view our work as downstream of signature selection and embedding gener-
528   ation, its value heavily depends on the availability of good product manifold embeddings. There are
529   challenges in selecting appropriate signatures (Borde et al., 2023a), and product manifolds are not
530   able to represent all patterns in data (Borde & Kratsios, 2023). Furthermore, it is computationally
531   intensive to generate of embeddings. There are also tradeoffs between DTs and RFs and other high-
532
      performing methods, especially graph neural networks when topologies are known. The complexity
      of working with non-Euclidean data could pose a potential barrier to adoption. Finally, the lack of
533
      a privileged basis (Elhage et al., 2023) in non-Euclidean embeddings makes the inductive bias of
534
      decision trees less well-motivated.
535
536   Future work. It may be possible to exploit non-privileged basis dimensions using approaches such
537   as rotation forests (Bagnall et al., 2020), random 2-D subspace angles, or oblique decision trees. A
538
      continuous unification, such as the Оә-stereographic model described in (Skopek et al., 2020), may
      be more robust and elegant. Extensions to simplex geometry (Mishra et al., 2020) are also worth
539
      considering.

851   A.5   R ELATIONSHIP TO OTHER WORK
852
853   Nagano et al. (2019) developed the overall technique used for a single cluster and a single manifold,
854   i.e. steps 6вҖ“9. Chlenski et al. (2024) modified this method to work for mixtures of Gaussians in
855   Hd,1 , and deployed it for d вҲҲ 2, 4, 8, 16. This corresponds to steps 1вҖ“5 of our procedure (although
856   note that our covariance matrices are sampled differently in step 5). Thus, our contribution is simply
857
      to add step 10, modify step 5 to use the Wishart distribution, to add curvature-related scaling factors
      in Equations 29, 33, and 39, and to generate classification and regression targets as described in the
858
      preceding sections.
859
860   We apply this to hyperspherical manifolds, for which the von Mises-Fisher (VMF) distribution is
861   typically preferred. This is an unconventional choice, but has been employed previously by Skopek
862   et al. (2020) in their mixed-curvature VAE formulation. We do not argue for the superiority of our
863
      approach over the VMF distribution in general; however, we prefer to use ours for these benchmarks,
      as it allows us to draw simpler parallels between manifolds of different curvatures.

       I.1     M ODELS
1353
1354   Neural networks, especially graph neural networks, are a popular choice for representing and work-
1355   ing with mixed-curvature representations (Sun et al., 2021; Cho et al., 2023; Bachmann et al., 2020;
1356   McNeela et al., 2024). Following the typical node classification approach described in the literature,
1357   we compared our method to both deep neural networks (ML) and graph neural networks (GNN).
1358   We find that our methods are generally competitive with neural baselines, especially when less data
1359
       is available.
1360   We trained each model for 200 epochs using Adam Kingma & Ba (2017) with a learning rate of
1361   .01, ОІ1 = 0.9, and ОІ2 = вҲ’0.999. Each model used one hidden dimension equal to its ambient
1362   dimension. For classification, we used an output dimension equal to the number of classes and
1363   cross-entropy loss; for regression, we used a single output dimension and mean squared error loss.
1364   We additionally train tangent plane variants of the MLP and GNN models, in which the data is
1365   preprocessed using a logarithmic map and subsequently treated as Euclidean. For datasets where
1366   the graph topology is not provided (e.g. Gaussian mixtures), we take the pairwise distances in the
1367   manifold geometry and transform them into dense weighted edges using the Gaussian kernel:
1368

1371   For graph datasets with known topology, we instead used the true adjacency matrix and an ablation
1372   in which the adjacency matrix is replaced by the dense Gaussian kernel estimate. Interestingly, for
1373   several of the graph datasets, substituting the true adjacencies with a Gaussian kernel on embedding
1374   distances did not substantially hinder performance; however, it rarely helped.

       Alongside their demonstrated accuracy and efficiency, decision tree algorithms are attractive for
1515   their tractability and interpretability. In particular, given a trained decision tree T , it is possible to:
1516
1517         1. Predict its behavior on the entire space of possible inputs (equivalently: T partitions P in
1518            a tractable way).
1519         2. Determine the importance of features (for classic decision trees) or feature
1520            pairs/components (for ProductDT) by observing how often and how early a fea-
1521            ture(/pair/component) is used in the decision tree procedure. Heuristically, early-splitting
1522            features are more important.
1523         3. Visualize every node using a 2-dimensional projection of the input data and angle
1524
1525   K.1    S UBMANIFOLD - LEVEL ATTRIBUTION EXPERIMENT
1526
1527   To determine whether our method could accurately distinguish between relevant and irrelevant sub-
1528   manifolds, we drew independent samples from Gaussian mixtures in H2 , E2 , and S2 , and yielding
1529   datasets (XH , yH ), (XE , yE ), (XS , yS ). We then concatenated these embeddings together:
1530
                                               XP = XH вҠ• XE вҠ• XS .                                            (75)
1531
1532   We trained three separate decision tree models on XP , using yH , yE , and yS as labels. Because the
1533   labels and embeddings were drawn independently, it should be the case that only the component from
1534   the same manifold as the labels contains any relevant information, and the other two components
1535
       are simply noise. Therefore, measuring the fraction of splits that fall in the вҖңcorrectвҖқ manifold is
       a useful proxy for understanding tree modelsвҖҷ ability to pick out signal that happens in individual
1536
       component manifolds.
1537
1538   Our results are summarized in Table 13. We found that both product space and ambient decision
1539   trees perform well at this task, which is to be expected.
1540   We note that this analysis is unique to tree methods, where the split dimensions are part of the
1541   architecture; other methods, such as perceptrons, k-nearest neighbors, or neural networks are harder
1542   to query for feature(/component) importances. Therefore, we consider this simple experiment a
1543   useful demonstration of how decision tree learning can reveal aspects of structure in mixed-curvature
1544   datasets that other learning algorithms cannot reveal.
