009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalization is a long-standing challenge in multivariate time series forecasting
012            (MTSF) tasks. Current approaches typically assume correlations among all vari-
013            ables. Consequently, every variable is incorporated into the training process for
014            prediction tasks. From a causal perspective, this reliance on correlated variables
015            can compromise the model’s generalization. To address this, we aim to explore
016            the role of causal relationships in enhancing the generalization of multivariate time
017            series models. We examine how graphical causal models, through conditional in-
018
               dependence constraints, can narrow down the hypothesis space, thereby improv-
               ing generalization. Building on this foundation, we propose a novel causality-
019
               based MTSF algorithm CAusal Informed Transformer (CAIFormer). We first
020
               construct a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) among variables using causal discov-
021            ery. Then we build the forecasting model by constructing the Markov boundary
022            informed by the DAG. Empirical evaluations on benchmark datasets demonstrate
023            that our method surpasses traditional approaches in predictive accuracy. Addition-
024            ally, we present the Markov boundaries derived for these datasets, underscoring
025            the practical applicability of our causality-driven framework in MTSF.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Multivariate Time Series Forecasting (MTSF) is a fundamental problem in various fields, including
031   energy consumption (Bilal et al., 2022), economic planning (Hidalgo, 2009), weather prediction
032   (Duchon & Hale, 2012), and traffic forecasting (Li et al., 2015). It involves predicting future values
033
      of multiple interrelated variables based on their historical data (Box et al., 2015). With the advent
      of deep learning techniques (LeCun et al., 2015), numerous methods have been proposed to tackle
034
      MTSF tasks (Zhu et al., 2024; Hu & Xiao, 2022; Bai et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023).
035
      Although these methods have achieved remarkable progress, improving their generalization ability
036   remains a critical challenge. There are countless models that can achieve low empirical risk but may
037   not generalize well to unseen data. According to prior works in Probably Approximately Correct
038   (PAC) learning theory (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1971), without proper regularization of the model
039   hypothesis space, models are prone to overfitting, leading to higher generalization risk (Mohri et al.,
040   2018; Kuznetsov & Mohri, 2014).
041
      An essential characteristic of MTSF is that the future behavior of each variable depends not only
042   on its own historical data but also on the historical data of other variables. For instance, when
043   predicting precipitation, changes in atmospheric pressure provide valuable information alongside
044   historical precipitation data (Wilks, 2011). Consequently, existing methods often incorporate all
045   available variables as inputs when forecasting the future sequence of a particular variable (Bai et al.,
046   2018; Liu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Zhan et al., 2023). However, indiscriminately including
047   all variables may not always be the most effective strategy. From a causal inference perspective, the
048   relationships among these variables can be intricate: for a specific variable, some variables may be
049   causes, some may be effects, and some may be independent (Pearl, 2009; Glymour et al., 2016). By
050
      explicitly considering these causal relationships during model construction, we can leverage them
      to constrain the hypothesis space of the model, potentially improving generalization performance.
051
052   To investigate how causal relationships affect generalization in MTSF problems, we follow prior
053   works by defining causal relationships using conditional independence (Dawid, 1979; Pearl & Paz,
      2022; Pearl, 2009). We can conceptualize multivariate time series as a weighted representation of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      all random variables. To simplify this representation, we aim to identify a maximal set of linearly
055   independent variables, thereby uncovering the essential features that influence the evolution of the
056   series. Instead of merely learning an effective representation from the training data, we strive for the
057   model to possess the capability to identify the maximal set of linearly independent variables across
058   diverse scenarios. The subset of random variables meeting these conditions is equivalent to the
059   Markov boundary (Pearl, 1988; Statnikov et al., 2013a). Upon constructing the Markov boundary,
060   we identify that collider structures in the Markov boundary introduce additional conditional inde-
061   pendencies, which are frequently neglected by most MTSF methods. Furthermore, in our theoretical
062   analysis, we examine the impact of collider structures on MTSF tasks and demonstrate that enforcing
063
      these conditional independencies can narrow down the hypothesis space of the forecasting model.
      This constraint can effectively reduce the generalization error theoretically, thereby enhancing the
064
      generalization performance of the model.
065
066   Based on our theoretical conclusions, we propose a novel algorithm named CAusal Informed
067   Transformer (CAIFormer). Specifically, we first employ causal discovery algorithms to construct
068   a DAG that captures the relationships among variables in an MTSF task. We then develop an al-
069
      gorithm to extract the Markov boundary for all variables in the DAG. Subsequently, we integrate
      these insights into a Transformer-based forecasting model by constraining each variable’s attention
070
      module to focus exclusively on the variables within its Markov boundary. This approach effectively
071
      leverages causal relationships to enhance the model’s generalization performance.
072
073   Our proposed CAIFormer achieves superior performance of the SOTA methods on a series of bench-
074   marks. Additionally, we conduct a series of ablation studies to assess the impact of different causal
075
      discovery algorithms and hyperparameter settings on the effectiveness of CAIFormer. Furthermore,
      we include DAGs of various MTSF datasets in the Appendix B, aiming to inspire future research.
076
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
077
078          • We explore the causal relationships among variables and discover that the Markov bound-
079            ary is the sufficient and necessary subset of all variables in forecasting tasks.
080          • We demonstrate that the collider structure within the Markov boundary contains additional
081            conditional independence, which enables us to constrain the hypothesis space of the fore-
082            casting model, ultimately improving the generalization ability of the model.
083
             • We propose a novel algorithm, CAusal Informed Transformer (CAIFormer), which inte-
084            grates causal relationships into a Transformer-based model. CAIFormer constrains each
085            variable’s attention module to focus solely on the variables within its Markov boundary.
086
             • We demonstrate that CAIFormer outperforms SOTA methods on multiple benchmarks. The
087            ablation studies showcase the correctness of our proposed method.

090   2   R ELATED WORK
091
092   Multivariate time series forecasting aims to predict future values of multiple, potentially inter-
093
      related variables based on historical data (Box et al., 2015; Lim & Zohren, 2021; Zhang et al.,
      2024a). Traditional MTSF methods often employ autoregressive models (Box et al., 2015), exponen-
094
      tial smoothing (Gardner Jr, 1985; Winters, 1960), or structural time series models (Harvey, 1990).
095
      With the advancement of deep learning, various methods including CNNs (Zhan et al., 2023; Bai
096   et al., 2018), RNN (Hewamalage et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021), and MLP-based (Zeng et al., 2023;
097   Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b) methods were proposed. In addition, there are Transformer-
098   based models that utilize a self-attention mechanism to compute relationships between variables,
099   while applying causal inference to restrict the calculations to variables with causal connections.
100
      Generalization Analysis in Time Series Forecasting. The generalization problem refers to a
101
      model’s ability to maintain performance on unseen data (Mohri et al., 2018). Given a finite num-
102   ber of training samples, the Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning framework ensures
103   the model’s generalization error remains below a predetermined threshold with high probability
104   (Valiant, 1984). The threshold, which is generally called generalization bound, depends on the com-
105   plexity of the model’s hypothesis space (Koltchinskii, 2001; Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1971). In
106   time series forecasting, early works assume stationarity and suitable mixing conditions (Doukhan &
107   Doukhan, 1994). For instance, Yu Yu (1994) established VC-dimension bounds for binary classifi-
      cation under the assumptions of stationarity and β-mixing. (Kuznetsov & Mohri, 2015) proposed


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generalization bounds based on sequential Rademacher complexity (Rakhlin et al., 2010). In this pa-
109   per, we extract the Markov boundary which is derived from causal relationships between variables,
110   enabling explicit constraints on the hypothesis in MTSF.
111
112
      Causal Inference and Causal Discovery. Causal inference seeks to deduce causal relationships
      among variables from observational data (Glymour et al., 2016; Pearl, 2009), typically represented
113
      by Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) (Lauritzen & Wermuth, 1989). The Inductive Causation (IC)
114
      algorithm, introduced by (Verma & Pearl, 1990), constructs DAGs using conditional independence
115   tests (CITs) to identify dependencies between variables. Based on this, (Spirtes & Glymour, 1991)
116   developed the Peter-Clark (PC) algorithm, which has been refined to reduce the computational com-
117   plexity(Spirtes et al., 2001; Spirtes, 2001). In time series data, causal discovery methods such as
118   tsFCI (Entner & Hoyer, 2010) apply the Fast Causal Inference (FCI) algorithm, while Granger
119   causality (Granger, 1969) explores temporal cause-effect relationships. Recent works have inte-
120   grated causal knowledge to enhance forecasting models: (Li et al., 2021) proposed a hidden causal
121   Markov model to reduce spurious correlations, and (Liu et al., 2023a) used proxy variables to un-
122   cover complete causal structures. Unlike previous approaches, we leverage DAGs from causal dis-
123
      covery to constrain model parameters, significantly improving the generalization in MTSF.

150   3.2   BACKGROUND IN C AUSALITY
151
152
      Causality examines how changes in one random variable influence another based on their probabilis-
      tic relationships Pearl (2009). One of the core concepts of causality is the conditional independence,
153
      which we provide the definition as follows:
154
155   Definition 1 (Conditional Independence Dawid (1979)) Let V = {V1 , V2 , ...} be a finite set of
156   variables, P (·) be a joint probability function over the variables in V , and X, Y , Z stand for any
157   three subsets of variables in V . Then, X and Y are said to be conditionally independent given Z if
158
159                              P (X|Y, Z) = P (X|Z) whenever P (Y, Z) > 0.                                (3)
160   In words, learning the value of Y does not provide additional information about X, once we know
161   Z. We will use the X ⊥⊥ Y |Z to represent the conditional independence of X and Y given Z.

      In a chain structure, Vp and Vc are conditionally independent given Vi , formally, Vp ⊥⊥ Vc | Vi . This
      is because once the mediator Vi is accounted for, knowing Vp provides no additional information

      about Vc beyond what is already conveyed through Vi .
183
184   Definition 3 (Fork) A fork Vb ← Vp → Vi is a graphical structure involving Vb , Vp , and Vi , where

186
187   Here, Vb and Vi are conditionally independent given the common parent Vp . It means that once Vp
188   is known, Vb provides no additional information about Vi , and vice versa, i.e., Vb ⊥⊥ Vi | Vp .

      Definition 4 (Collider/V-Structure) A collider, also known as a V-structure, Vi → Vc ← Vs , is a
190
      graphical structure involving three variables Vi , Vc , and Vs , where Vc is a common child of both Vi
191
      and Vs , Vi and Vs are not directly connected. Here, Vi and Vs causally influence Vc
192
193   In a collider or V-structure, Vi and Vs are marginally independent; knowing Vi does not provide
194   information about Vs and vice versa. However, when conditioning on the collider Vc , this indepen-
195   dence is broken, making Vi and Vs dependent. Formally, Vi ⊥⊥ Vs and Vi ̸⊥⊥ Vs | Vc .
196
      These conditional independence relationships are fundamental for understanding the dependencies
197
      and independencies implied by a causal graph, thereby facilitating tasks such as causal discovery
198   and inference in multivariate time series forecasting.

      4.1   M ARKOV BOUNDARY AND C ONDITIONAL INDEPENDENCE
209
      Without loss of generality, multivariate time series forecasting can be regarded as an auto-regressive
210   problem (Box et al., 2015). That is, suppose that there are k random variables contained in a mul-
211   tivariate time series Y . From the perspective of linear algebra, the series Y can be represented as a
212   weighted sum of all random variables X = {Xi }ki=1 . Due to the correlation between these random
213   variables, there must be a subset within the set of variables such that the time series can be repre-
214   sented by, and only by, all the variables in that subset. In other words, there exists a maximal linearly
215   independent group X ∗ = {Xm }lm=1 , (l < k) such that conditional independence Y ⊥⊥ X \ X ∗ |X ∗
      is maintained. Therefore, we can discard X \ X ∗ from the total set without any loss of probabilistic


                                                              4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      information for auto-regression. The set X ∗ that satisfies conditional independence is also known
217   as the Markov boundary (Statnikov et al., 2013b) of Y .
218
219   Moreover, the presence of collider structures within the Markov boundary provides additional inde-
220
      pendence relationships, thus improving the auto-regression, which is essentially a conditional dis-
      tribution with the form of P (Y |X). The following proposition shows that the presence of a collider
221
      is not only a sufficient condition but also necessary.
222
223   Proposition 1 (Koller & Friedman, 2009) Suppose that the Markov boundary of Y is X ∗ , then X ∗
224   contains a collider if and only if there exist Xi ∈ X ∗ and X̃ ⊂ X ∗ such that Y ⊥⊥ Xi |X̃.
225
226   For the sake of clarity, we discuss how conditional independence helps generalize under the collider
227   structure in Figure 1. We claim that this simplification does not harm the generality of our work.

343   Self-Attention of Transformer captures the relationships between different tokens of input sequence
344   by using each input vector as its own query, key, and value. Specifically, it begins with the input

370   we can discard independent variables of Vi . Based on the Markov boundaries obtained in Section
371   4.1, we can identify the causal relationships between each variable and other variables. Therefore,
372   we impose constraints on self-attention to focus on the causal relationships among variables and
373   only consider those variables within the Markov boundary. Specifically, our approach is as follows.
374   From Section 5.2, we derive a Variable Attention Mask, where each row indicates variables included
375   in the Markov boundary of the current variable. We apply the mask to the similarity scores, where
376   each variable acts as a token, representing the relationships between different variables. After ap-
377   plying the mask, for a specific variable Vi , we set the similarity scores with variables outside its
      Markov boundary to zero, thus avoiding irrelevant correlations.

509
510
      In this paper, we introduce a novel causality-based algorithm, CAusal Informed Transformer
      (CAIFormer), to improve generalization in multivariate time series forecasting (MTSF) tasks. By
511
      leveraging causal discovery techniques, we construct a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) among vari-
512
      ables and derive the Markov boundary to guide the model’s attention mechanism. Our theoretical
513   analysis shows that the Markov boundary, especially its collider structures, provides critical con-
514   ditional independencies that can constrain the hypothesis space and reduce generalization error.
515   Empirical evaluations on benchmark datasets demonstrate the advantages of CAIFormer.
