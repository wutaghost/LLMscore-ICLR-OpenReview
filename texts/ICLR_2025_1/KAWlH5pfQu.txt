                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial
010
               examples. While numerous successful adversarial attacks have been proposed,
011
               defenses against these attacks remain relatively understudied. Existing defense
012            approaches either focus on negating the effects of perturbations caused by the at-
013            tacks to restore the DNNs’ original predictions or use a secondary model to detect
014            adversarial examples. However, these methods often become ineffective due to
015            the continuous advancements in attack techniques. We propose a novel univer-
016            sal and lightweight method to detect adversarial examples by analyzing the layer
017            outputs of DNNs. Through theoretical justification and extensive experiments, we
018            demonstrate that our detection method is highly effective, compatible with any
019            DNN architecture, and applicable across different domains, such as image, video,
020
               and audio.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
      Goodfellow et al. (2014) demonstrated that deep neural networks (DNNs) are vulnerable to adver-
025
      sarial examples and proposed the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) to craft these adversarial
026
      examples by adding perturbations to the model inputs, leveraging the linear nature of DNNs. After
027   the initial introduction of FGSM, various adversarial attacks were proposed across different do-
028   mains. However, compared to the vast diversity among attack techniques, existing defense methods
029   are built on a few different strategies.
030
      The most commonly used defense strategy is trying to remove the perturbations or altering the
031
      inputs to reduce their effects. Another and the oldest defense strategy is adversarial training where
032
      the training set of model contains adversarial examples.Both strategies do not generalize well to
033   unseen attacks as they are tailored for a specific set of attacks. On the other hand, a recent defense
034   strategy suggests to use a baseline method and compare its output with the main model to detect
035   attacks. This strategy makes the defense vulnerable to the limitations of the baseline model and the
036   potential mismatches between the main and baseline models for clean samples.
037
      As opposed to the existing defense strategies which either analyze the model inputs or outputs,
038
      we propose Layer Regression (LR), a universal lightweight adversarial example detector, which
039   analyzes the changes in the DNN’s internal layer outputs. LR is highly effective for defending
040   various DNN models against a wide range of attacks in different data domains such as image, video,
041   and audio. LR utilizes the difference between the impacts of adversarial samples on early and final
042   layers by performing regression among them. We present the following contributions:
043
044          • We propose the first universal defense strategy against adversarial examples, which take
045            advantage of the sequential layer-based nature of DNNs and the common objectives of
046            attacks.
047          • We conduct extensive experiments with 672 distinct attack-dataset-model-defense com-
048            binations for image recognition, and show that LR outperforms existing methods with a
049            97.6% average detection performance while the next best performance achieved by the ex-
050            isting defenses is 82.9%. Universality of LR is shown with its superior performance in
051
               detecting action recognition attacks and speech recognition attacks.
052          • In addition to its high performance across a wide range of domains, models, and attacks,
053            LR is also the most lightweight defense method. It is orders of magnitude faster than the
               existing defenses, making it ideal for real-time attack detection.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
057
058
      The robustness of deep neural networks and their vulnerability against adversarial examples have
059
      been investigated since the introduction of FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Numerous adversarial
060   attacks have been proposed to generate effective adversarial examples in recent years (Madry et al.,
061   2017; Croce & Hein, 2020; Kurakin et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017; Ilyas et al., 2018; Mumcu
062   & Yilmaz, 2024a; Wang & He, 2021; Fang et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2020). There are two main
063   adversarial attack settings, namely white-box and black-box. While it is assumed that the attacker
064   has access to the target model in the white-box setting, in the black-box setting, the attacker does
065   not have any prior information about the target model.
066   White box attacks, including FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014), PGD (Madry et al., 2017), APGD
067   (Croce & Hein, 2020), generate adversarial examples by maximizing the target model’s loss function
068   and they are usually referred as gradient-based attacks. BIM (Kurakin et al., 2018) tries to improve
069   gradient-based attack by applying perturbations iteratively. Transferability based black-box attacks,
070   which is introduced in Papernot et al. (2017), are one of the most common black-box approach. The
071   idea is to use an attack on a substitute model for generating adversarial examples for unknown target
072   models, utilizing the transferability of adversarial examples to different DNNs. While adversarial
073   examples generated by these attacks are most effective when the substitute model exactly matches
074
      the target model as in a white-box attack setting, their success is shown to be transferable even when
      there is significant architectural differences between the substitute and target models. Wang & He
075
      (2021) introduced VMI and VNI to further extend iterative gradient-based attacks and try to achieve
076
      high transferability by considering the gradient variance of the previous iterations. PIF (Gao et al.,
077   2020) uses patch-wise iterations to achieve transferability. ANDA (Fang et al., 2024) aims to achieve
078   strong transferability by avoiding the overfitting of adversarial examples to the substitute model.
079
080
081
      2.2   A DVERSARIAL D EFENSES
082
      Attempting to make changes on the input data for removing the effects of perturbations from adver-
083
      sarial examples is the most common defense strategy. JPEG compression is studied in several works
084
      (Cucu et al., 2023; Aydemir et al., 2018; Das et al., 2018), and it is shown that compressing and
085   decompressing helps to remove of adversarial effects on input images. Xie et al. (2017) uses ran-
086   dom resizing and padding on the inputs to eliminate the adversarial effects. Xu (2017b) introduces
087   feature squeezing where they use bit reduction, spatial smoothing, and non-local means denoising
088   to detect adversarial examples. Several denoising methods (Liao et al., 2018; Xiong et al., 2022;
089   Salman et al., 2020) were proposed to remove adversarial perturbations from the inputs. Mustafa
090   et al. (2019) uses wavelet denoising and image super resolution as pre-processing steps to create
091   a defense pipeline against adversarial attacks. Prakash et al. (2018) tries to eliminate adversarial
092   effects by redistributing the pixel values via a process called pixel deflection. Adversarial training is
093
      another method which is studied to increase robustness of DNNs, however adversarial training often
      fails to perform well especially under various attack configurations (Bai et al., 2021). In addition, a
094
      recent defense strategy which utilizes a baseline model, e.g., Vision Language Models (VLMs), with
095
      the assumption that the output of target model and baseline model are close to each other for clean
096   input, but are far away from each other for adversarital input. A recent example is demonstrated by
097   Mumcu & Yilmaz (2024c) where they used CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to detect adversarial video
098   examples.
099
      Adversarial training suffers from the increasing number of adversarial attacks which makes this
100
      strategy ineffective against adversarial examples which are not represented in the training set. Sim-
101
      ilarly, developing a universal perturbation removal method that is effective against every attack is
102   not feasible due to continuous advancements in attack techniques. Additionally, since the defender
103   cannot always know whether the input has been attacked, there is a risk of degrading clean inputs
104   and causing false alarms. The baseline methods, which involve using a secondary model, such as
105   VLMs, to detect adversarial examples, rely on the assumption that the secondary model will not
106   be affected by the adversarial samples that are designed against the primary model. However, even
107   without an attack, a mismatch between the models’ predictions may trigger false alarms. Moreover,
      an attack that compromises both models can easily bypass this defense strategy.

110   Consider a Deep Neural Network (DNN) model g(·) that takes an input x and predicts the target
111   variable y with g(x). As discussed in Section 2.2, there are three main defense strategies against
112   adversarial attacks: adversarial training, modifying input, and detecting adversarial samples by mon-
113   itoring changes in output with respect to a baseline. While the former two focuses on the changes in
114   the input (x vs. xadv ), the latter utilizes the changes in the output (g(x) vs. g(xadv )). Our approach
115   differs from these existing approaches by leveraging the changes within the DNN activations. In-
116   stead of analyzing the input xadv or the output g(xadv ), the proposed defense method analyzes the
117   intermediate steps between xadv and g(xadv ).
118   To develop a universal detector that work with any DNN and against any attack, we start with
119   the following generic observation. Although there are numerous attacks with different approaches
120   to generate adversarial examples, all attacks essentially aim to change the model’s prediction by
121   maximizing the loss L (e.g., cross-entropy loss) between prediction g(xadv ) and the ground truth y
122   while limiting the perturbation Fang et al. (2024):

206   lightweight detection algorithm with the following steps: (i) select a subset of the first n − 2 layer

      were proposed to increase robustness by altering the inputs to remove perturbations. For this reason,

      5.1   E FFECT OF ATTACK S TRENGTH
404
405   Adversarial attacks often use a designated parameter ϵ to adjust the amount of perturbation on ad-
406   versarial examples. For the experiments in Section 4, we used the default attack settings that are
407   proposed by the authors. In this ablation study, we investigate the performance of defense methods
408   against PGD and BIM attacks with different ϵ values. In the original implementation of PGD and
409   BIM, attack strength parameter ϵ is set to 0.03. In addition to the original ϵ, we also generated
410   attacks by setting ϵ to 0.01, 0.09, 0.12, 0.15 and 0.3 and attacked ResNet and ViT.
411   Figure 5 plots the performances of defense methods for ResNet-PGD, ResNet-BIM, ViT-PGD and
412   ViT-BIM model-attack pairs. We noticed a small performance drop in our method when the attack
413   strength has the smallest value of 0.01. However, higher ϵ values resulted in higher AUC in every
414   case. This is an expected behaviour since our method depends on the effects of the perturbation on
415   DNN layers. In other defense methods, while VLAD and Deflect experience only small changes
416   under stronger attacks, remaining methods are greatly affected by the ϵ value, especially while de-
417   fending ResNet.

524   7     C ONCLUSION
525
526   Although there are effective defense methods for specific model-attack combinations, their success
527   do not generalize to all popular models and attacks. In this work, we filled this gap by introducing
528
      Layer Regression (LR), the first universal method for detecting adversarial examples. In addition
      to its universality, LR is much more lightweight and faster than the existing defense methods. By
529
      analyzing the common objectives of attacks and the sequential layer-based nature of DNNs, we
530
      proved that the impact of adversarial samples is more on the final layer than the first layer. Motivated
531   by this fact, LR trains a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) on clean samples to estimate the feature vector
532   using a combination of outputs from early layers. The estimation error of LR for adversarial samples
533   is typically much higher than the error for clean samples, which enables an average AUC of 0.982
534   and 0.970 on the ImageNet and CIFAR-100 datasets across 6 models and 7 attacks. With extensive
535   experiments, we showed that LR outperforms the existing defenses in image recognition by a wide
536   margin and also provides highly effective detection performance in distinct domains, namely video
537   action recognition and speech recognition. One caveat that needs to be studied in future works is the
538   possibility of training an attack model that can learn to fool the target model and LR together.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORKS FROM OTHER D OMAINS
757
758
      Adversarial examples are studied on several other domains including video action recognition Pony
759   et al. (2021), traffic sign recognition Hsiao et al. (2024), object detection Zhao et al. (2019), video
760   anomaly detection Mumcu et al. (2022), and speech recognition. (Żelasko et al., 2021). For instance,
761   flickering attack (Flick) Pony et al. (2021) tries to attack action recognition models by changing the
762   RGB stream of the input videos. Hsiao et al. (2024) investigates the effects of natural light on traffic
763   signs and use it to generate adversarial examples.
764
      Advit Xiao et al. (2019) is a detection method introduced for videos. It generates pseudo frames
765   using optical flow and evaluates the consistency between the outputs for original inputs and pseudo
766   frames to detect attacks. Another defense method, Shuffle Hwang et al. (2023), tries to increase the
767   robustness of action recognition models by randomly shuffling the input frames. Mumcu & Yilmaz
768   (2024b) proposed to train a lightweight VLM and use it for adversarial traffic sign detection. In
769   speech recognition, defenses like denoising or smoothing are studied against adversarial examples
770   (Żelasko et al., 2021).
