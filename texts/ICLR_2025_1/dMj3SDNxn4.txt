009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Ordinal regression is a supervised machine learning technique aimed at predicting
012                the value of a discrete dependent variable with an ordered set of possible outcomes.
013                Many of the algorithms that have been developed to address this issue rely on maxi-
014                mum likelihood for training. However, the standard maximum likelihood approach
015                often fails to adequately capture the inherent order of classes, even though it tends
016                to produce well-calibrated probabilities. Alternatively, some methods use Optimal
017                Transport (OT) divergence as their training objective. Unlike maximum likelihood,
018                OT accounts for the ordering of classes; however, in this manuscript, we show that
019
                   it doesn’t always yield well-calibrated probabilities. To overcome these limitations,
                   we introduce UNICORNN, an approach inspired by the well-known Proportional
020
                   Odds Model, which offers three key guarantees: (i) it ensures unimodal output prob-
021
                   abilities, a valuable feature for many real-world applications; (ii) it employs OT loss
022                during training to accurately capture the natural order of classes; (iii) it provides
023                well-calibrated probability estimates through a post-training accuracy-preserving
024                calibration step. Experimental results on six real-world datasets demonstrate that
025                UNICORNN consistently either outperforms or performs as well as recently pro-
026                posed deep learning approaches for ordinal regression. It excels in both accuracy
027                and probability calibration, while also guaranteeing output unimodality. The code
028                will be publicly available upon acceptance.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Ordinal regression is an area of supervised machine learning, where the goal is to predict the value of
034   a discrete dependent variable, whose set of (symbolic) possible values is ordered. Despite often being
035   overshadowed by more common tasks like classification and regression, ordinal regression covers a
036   wide range of important applications, such as medical severity grading, credit rating, age estimation,
037   and many more (De Vente et al., 2020; Wienholt et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2016; Kim and Ahn, 2012).
038   Many practitioners often treat ordinal regression problems as classification or regression problems
039   (for example, this was the case with many submissions to Kaggle’s Diabetic Retinopathy competition1
040   in 2015). While having common characteristics with both classification and regression, ordinal
041   regression can arguably be viewed as a mid-point between the two. An ordinal model is of course
042   similar to a classification model, in that both predict a discrete value (“label”) out of a finite set of
043   possible ones. However, the existence of an order on the set of labels, when available, can potentially
044
      lead to improved performance compared to a standard classifier, which does not assume such order
      exists. This typically occurs via distinguishing between the severity of prediction mistakes: while
045
      in classification typically ”all mistakes are created equal", in ordinal regression different mistakes
046
      may be associated with different severity (for example, in the context of tumor grade prediction,
047   predicting "3" when the ground truth value is "4" may be less severe than a "1" prediction). In
048   regression problems, the dependent variable naturally does take values from an ordered set, however,
049   this set is typically a continuum and is treated numerically, while in ordinal regression it lacks any
050   numerical relation beyond merely order. Therefore, regression performance may be sensitive to
051   monotonic transformations of the dependent variable, while such sensitivity does not take place in
052   ordinal regression problems, as the order is invariant to monotonic transformations. Hence one may
053

      expect that typical ordinal regression algorithms have the potential to outperform classification or
055   regression approaches when the range of the dependent variable is finite and ordered.
056
057   A fundamental ordinal regression model is the Proportional Odds Model (POM) (McCullagh, 1980),
058
      a generalized linear model similar in spirit to logistic regression, however the logits are defined
      for their cumulative probabilities. One potential source of sub-optimality of POM and of several
059
      recently-proposed approaches for deep ordinal regression, is the often-reasonable requirement that a
060
      probabilistic model for ordinal regression will output unimodal probabilities. A k-level multinomial
061   distribution is called unimodal if there exists j ∈ {1, . . . , k} such that Pr(Y = 1) ≤ . . . ≤ Pr(Y =
062   j) ≥ . . . ≥ Pr(Y = k). Although there are domains in which unimodality is not necessarily a
063   desirable property, such as tasks where the most common targets tend to fall at the extremes, in many
064   other real-world domains it is a natural requirement, for example, when predicting a grade of a tumor,
065   it may be counter-intuitive to trust a model prediction which says that a predicted tumor’s grade
066   is either "1" or "4", but not "2" or "3". However, unimodality is unfortunately not always fulfilled
067   despite often being a desired characteristic. While this was identified by several recent works for deep
068   ordinal regression (Gao et al., 2017; Diaz and Marathe, 2019; Liu et al., 2019a; 2020), unimodality is
069
      often encouraged (but not enforced) via soft targets. However, as we show in Section 2, using soft
      targets is suboptimal for achieving unimodality.
070
071   Another essential feature of an ordinal regression model is its ability to effectively capture the ordered
072   relationship among classes within its training objective, while still reflecting the certainty of the
073   model in its predictions. POM is typically trained via maximum likelihood, similar to several recently
074   proposed deep ordinal regression approaches (Belharbi et al., 2019; Vargas et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2018;
075
      Beckham and Pal, 2017; Berg et al., 2020; Vishnu et al., 2019). We argue that maximum likelihood
      is a sub-optimal measure of quality for ordinal regression setup, as it only considers the probability
076
      mass the model assigns to the true class, ignoring the remaining mass. This implicitly assumes that
077
      “all mistakes are equal”, which, as discussed above, is not the case for ordinal regression. However, a
078   benefit of maximum likelihood is that it tends to yield well-calibrated probabilities. A well-calibrated
079   model ensures that, for example, a 90% predicted probability corresponds to events that actually
080   occur 90% of the time. However, many models, including recent deep ordinal regression approaches,
081   struggle with this. These models often exhibit overconfidence or underconfidence, meaning their
082   probability outputs are unreliable indicators of their true prediction certainty. Another commonly
083   used loss function for ordinal regression tasks is Optimal Transport (OT) divergence. OT excels at
084   capturing the inherent order between labels, potentially making it a better fit. However, as we explain
085   in Section 3.3, OT might lead to peaked output distribution that lacks calibration.
086   In this manuscript, we therefore focus on two main contributions. First, We present UNICORNN, a
087   novel approach for ordinal regression, based on deep learning machinery, which tackles the three
088   issues pointed out above (i) it contains a mechanism to enforce unimodality of the output distribution,
089   implemented via architectural design, (ii) it effectively captures the ordered relationship among
090   classes using OT as a training objective (iii) it undergoes a post-training calibration process to output
091   well-calibrated probability estimates that reflect the model’s confidence in its predictions while still
092
      preserving the model’s accuracy. Second, as discussed in Section 3.3, we identify a trade-off between
      certain requirements, noting that OT may prioritize peaked distributions over calibrated ones, which,
093
      to the best of our knowledge, was not pointed out in the literature in the context of deep ordinal
094
      regression. Importantly, this bonds the requirements together, as other methods that utilize OT as
095   a training objective, end up being uncalibrated. We present experimental results on six real-world
096   image benchmark datasets which demonstrate that UNICORNN consistently performs on par with
097   and often better than several recently proposed approaches for deep ordinal regression in terms of
098   both prediction accuracy and probability calibration while having an unimodality guarantee.

101   2    R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Being a traditional area of machine learning and statistics, there exists a large corpus of literature on
104   ordinal regression. In this section, we focus on approaches based on recent deep-learning architectures.
105   Several such approaches have been proposed in recent years. One common approach is to turn the
106   ordinal regression problem into a multi-label classification problem (Fu et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2017;
107   2018b; Vishnu et al., 2019; Berg et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2008; Li et al., 2021). We argue that the
      multi-label approach has two major problematic aspects: first, the output probabilities are not always


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      guaranteed to be consistent, in the sense of increasing cumulative distribution (i.e., we would like
109   to predict Pr(y ≤ 1) ≤ Pr(y ≤ 2) ≤ . . . ≤ Pr(y ≤ k). Second, even if the output probabilities
110   are consistent, as is the case in Liu et al. (2018a); Shi et al. (2023); Cao et al. (2020), the predicted
111   class probabilities are not necessarily unimodal. This is the case in several recent works (Liu et al.,
112   2019b; Vargas et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2018; Kook et al., 2020). Another line of research focuses on
113   addressing ordinal regression by developing unbiased estimator-based approaches that are robust to
114   label noise, as proposed by Garg and Manwani (2020).
115   One elegant mechanism is to obtain unimodal output probabilities, based on either the Poisson or
116   the Binomial distributions (Beckham and Pal, 2017), which are both unimodal. In both cases, the
117   model outputs a scalar (λ in the case of the Poisson, p in the case of the binomial) for each prediction,
118   which is then mapped to a probability mass function that is used (after normalization) as the model
119   output probabilities. Moreover, this method also learns a dataset-wide τ parameter which controls the
120   shape of the output distribution. While being a convenient architectural-based solution for handling
121   unimodality, this approach is inherently limited in its ability to express the level of uncertainty of the
122   model’s prediction. To see why, note that since a single parameter determines both the location of
123
      the mode and the decay of the probabilities, the model cannot output a highly flat or highly peaked
      probability vector, for example.
124
125   A different approach to unimodality has been to train the model with soft targets (Gao et al., 2017;
126   Diaz and Marathe, 2019; Liu et al., 2019a; 2020). However, we argue that the utilization of soft targets
127   suffers from two important disadvantages. First, unimodality is only encouraged, but not enforced.
128   In Section 5 we will show cases where models trained with soft targets yield large amounts of
129
      non-unimodal predictions. Second, soft targets have a pre-defined decay pattern, which is determined
      a-priori and hence does not reflect any level of uncertainty with respect to the prediction. Therefore,
130
      they are equivalent (in the sense of a 1:1 map) to Dirac predictions (i.e., “one-hot”), and are devoid
131
      of any probabilistic insight whatsoever. As we show in this paper, our approach attends to both
132   issues: we guarantee unimodal outputs, by design, and yield well-calibrated probabilities outputs that
133   reflect the model’s uncertainty. Other approaches for handling unimodality include Li et al. (2022);
134   Cardoso et al. (2023), where unimodality is encouraged through a dedicated loss term (although
135   not guaranteed), and Belharbi et al. (2019), which employs constrained optimization to achieve
136   unimodality on the training data, but with no guarantees on the predictions on test data.
137
      Several works use cross entropy as a training objective while using one-hot (or binary) targets (Bel-
138   harbi et al., 2019; Vargas et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2018; Beckham and Pal, 2017; Berg et al., 2020;
139   Vishnu et al., 2019; Cardoso et al., 2023). Also, an accuracy-preserving calibration on a cross-entropy
140   trained model was proposed in (Esaki et al., 2024). However, in the case of one-hot targets, the
141   cross entropy term equals the negative logarithm of the probability assigned by the model to the true
142   class, making it invariant to the distribution of the remaining probability mass. While reasonable
143   in a standard classification setting, this ignores the order of the classes, making it sub-optimal for
144   an ordinal regression setting. Nonetheless, as cross-entropy is a proper-scoring rule, it tends to
145   yield calibrated probability estimates (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). To address cross-entropy’s
146
      limitation in handling the order of classes, some approaches use OT loss (Hou et al., 2016; Beckham
      and Pal, 2017; Liu et al., 2019a), which is a natural way to incorporate the order of the classes into
147
      the loss term. However, OT tends to favor peaked output distributions instead of calibrated ones
148
      (see Section 3.3). UNICORNN aims to benefit from both the ability to capture the order of classes
149   using OT as a training objective, and the generation of calibrated probability estimates through
150   accuracy-preserving calibration.
151
152
      In summary, to the best of our knowledge, no existing work has successfully met all three fundamental
      requirements for an effective ordinal regression model: (i) Ensuring unimodality in the output
153
      distribution, ideally through architectural design; (ii) Aligning the training objective function with
154
      the ordinal nature of the label space; (iii) Reflecting the model’s uncertainty in the decay of the
155   output probabilities, preferably with well-calibrated probabilities. These requirements led to the
156   development of UNICORNN, presented in this paper.

222
223   Probability Calibration refers to the alignment between the predicted probabilities generated by a
224   classification model and the true, empirical probabilities observed in the data. For instance, it is
225   anticipated that when a classification model assigns a probability of 0.8 to class i for a certain sample,
226   approximately 80% of those samples would indeed belong to class i based on their ground truth
227   labels. Formally, the concept of calibration can be defined as:
228
                                  P[y = i|pi (x) = q] = q,       ∀q ∈ [0, 1], i ∈ [k]                       (3)
229
230   Here, k denotes the number of classes, x and y represent the model input and the ground truth
231   class, respectively, and pi (x) signifies the model’s output probability for class i given input x. The
232   probability is taken over the joint distribution of x, y.
233   Alternatively, if we denote y as a one-hot vector indicating the ground truth label, we can express an
234   equivalent definition to equation 3: A classification model is calibrated when Ex,y [y|p(x)] = p(x). In
235   practice, p(x) is a one-to-one function, hence we can refine the definition further: Ex,y [y|x] = p(x).
236
      To achieve calibration, minimizing the squared L2 norm on the difference between the two terms
237
      is desirable. However, given the unknown real joint distribution x, y, we can only approximate
238   Ex,y [y|x]. One approach is to use a Monte Carlo approximation with one sample, yielding the
239   approximation y, i.e., minimize the following:
240
                                                                               2
241                                        BS(y, p(x)) = ∥y − p(x)∥2                                        (4)
242
      This term is known as the Brier Score (BS) (Brier, 1950). BS is a well-known Proper Scoring Rule
243
      (Dawid and Musio, 2014), which evaluates the accuracy of probabilistic predictions. Proper scoring
244
      rules are maximized when the probabilistic forecast matches the true probability distribution of
245   outcomes. Therefore, we will use BS as a training objective to calibrate the model predictions.
246
247
      Accuracy-Preserving Calibration An accuracy-preserving calibration method is a technique that
248
      adjusts the probability outputs of a pre-trained model to improve their calibration, without affecting
249   the model’s accuracy. A popular method is Temperature Scaling (TS) (Guo et al., 2017), which learns
250   a single temperature parameter that is used to rescale the model’s logits before applying the softmax
251   activation, adjusting the confidence predictions. Adaptive Temperature Scaling (ATS) (Balanya
252   et al., 2024) extends TS such that instead of a single temperature parameter, it learns a mapping
253   x 7→ T (x) that adaptively scales the logits based on the input x. As part of UNICORNN we define
254   an accuracy-preserving calibration method with similarity to TS and ATS which is trained via Brier
255   Score (Section 4.3).

285   4     UNICORNN
286
287   In this section we describe our novel mechanism for UNImodal Calibrated Ordinal Regression Neural
288   Network, UNICORNN an approach for architectural-based generation of unimodal output probability
289   distributions, as well as accuracy-preserving probability calibration.
290
291   4.1   R ATIONAL
292
293   Achieving unimodality directly via architectural design has a major advantage since the output
294   probabilities are guaranteed to be unimodal for every input instance, as is also the case for the
295   mechanism proposed in Beckham and Pal (2017). However, UNICORNN employs the truncated
296   normal distribution, depending on two parameters (µ, σ), which influence the location of the mode
297   and the decay of the probability mass. This adds flexibility to the shape of the output probability
298
      vector, compared to the mechanism in Beckham and Pal (2017) where a single parameter determines
      both the mode and the decay. For determining the mode of the distribution, a map x 7→ µ is learned
299
      via OT using equation 6. However, as mentioned in Section 3.3, OT tends to favor peaked distributions
300
      over calibrated ones. To address this issue, we adopt an accuracy-preserving calibration strategy
301   where, subsequent to learning a map x 7→ µ, the model further learns a map x 7→ σ by optimizing
302   the Brier Score using equation 4, while maintaining the previously learned map x 7→ µ fixed.
303
304

357   4.4    T RAINING P ROCEDURE
358
359   To summarize, UNICORNN’s training process consists of two distinct phases, as detailed in Algo-
360   rithms 1, 2. In the first phase, described in Algorithm 1, the parameters θµ and θσ of µ(·) and σ(·),
361   respectively, along with the backbone parameters ϕ, are jointly optimized using Gradient Descent to
362   minimize the OT loss defined in Eq. 6.
363   In the second phase, outlined in Algorithm 2, the parameters θµ and ϕ are kept fixed, while θσ is
364   further optimized via Gradient Descent to minimize the Brier Score (BS) defined in Eq. 4.

      5.2   B ENCHMARK
403
404   We compare UNICORNN to five recently presented approaches for deep ordinal regression, with
405   unimodal output probabilities and to a deep learning approach of POM:
406
      DLDL (Gao et al., 2017), an approach utilizing soft labels, generated using squared exponentially
407
      decaying distributions, trained using Kullback-Leibler divergence minimization (equivalent to cross-
408
      entropy minimization).
409
410   SORD (Diaz and Marathe, 2019), an approach utilizing soft labels, generated using linear exponen-
411   tially decaying distributions, trained using Kullback-Leibler divergence minimization.
412    Beckham and Pal (2017), an architectural-based approach in which unimodal output probabilities are
413   generated using the binomial distribution (single-learned parameter), trained using optimal transport
414   loss.
415
       Liu et al. (2019a), an approach utilizing soft labels, created as a mixture of Dirac, uniform, and
416   linear exponentially decaying distributions, trained using optimal transport loss.
417
418   POM (McCullagh, 1980), a variant of the POM, incorporating a deep learning model with a POM
419
      layer integrated on top, trained using cross-entropy loss.
420   UnimodalNet (Cardoso et al., 2023), a non-parametric architectural-based approach for unimodality,
421   trained using cross-entropy loss.
422
      To perform a fair comparison, we implemented all methods, using the same image transformations,
423   backbone CNN and training procedures, so that the methods differ only in their output layer archi-
424   tectures and loss functions. We performed 5 independent trials, using the same train-validation-test
425   splits for all methods. Additional technical details can be found in Appendix E. For reproducibility,
426   the supplementary material contains code reproducing the results reported in this section.

      In addition, following the discussion in section 3.2, we evaluate the model’s probability calibration
444
      using the well known Expected Calibration Error (ECE). ECE quantifies the discrepancy between
445
      predicted probabilities and actual outcomes, measuring how well the predicted probabilities of a
446   model align with the true likelihood of the predicted events. To compute ECE, the [0, 1] interval is
447   first divided into a set of b equal-length bins. ECE is defined as:
448
                                             b
449                                          X |Bi |
450                                 ECE =                |Acc(Bi ) − Conf(Bi )|,
                                             i=1
                                                   |X|
451
452   where b is the number of probability bins (in all of our experiments b = 10), Acc(Bi ) is the empirical
453   accuracy in bin Bi and Conf(Bi ) is the average predicted probability in bin Bi . An ECE of 0 indicates
454   perfect calibration, while higher values signify miscalibration, with the model being over-confident
455   or under-confident.

477   6     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this manuscript, we identify several issues with current deep ordinal regression methods, including
480   the potential for OT to result in poor probability calibration. We therefore presented UNICORNN,
481   an approach for deep ordinal regression, inspired by the proportional odds model. UNICORNN
482   utilizes an architectural mechanism for the generation of unimodal output probabilities, trained using
483   OT objective and calibrated using an accuracy-preserving calibration process which encourages
484   uncertainty awareness. We demonstrated that while performing on par with and often better than
485   other recently proposed approaches for ordinal regression, the presented method enjoys the benefits
      of guaranteed unimodal and well-calibrated output probabilities.

737   the same mean µ and the thresholds α0 , . . . , αk are constant, the predicted class i remains the same
738   for both sets of parameters regardless of the variances σ12 and σ22 .
