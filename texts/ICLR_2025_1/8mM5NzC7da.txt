009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Distinguishing in- and out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs is crucial for reliable de-
012            ployment of classification systems. However, OOD data is typically unavailable
013            or difficult to collect, posing a significant challenge for accurate OOD detection.
014            In this work, we present a method that harnesses the generative capabilities of
015            Large Language Models (LLMs) to create high-quality synthetic OOD proxies,
016            eliminating the dependency on any external OOD data source. We study the effi-
017            cacy of our method on classical text classification tasks such as toxicity detection
018
               and sentiment classification as well as classification tasks arising in LLM develop-
               ment and deployment, such as training a reward model for RLHF and detecting
019
               misaligned generations. Extensive experiments on nine InD-OOD dataset pairs
020
               and various model sizes show that our approach dramatically lowers false positive
021            rates (achieving a perfect zero in some cases) while maintaining high accuracy on
022            in-distribution tasks, outperforming baseline methods by a significant margin.

               Warning: this paper contains example data that may be offensive or upsetting.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      OOD detection is a critical challenge in machine learning, particularly for classification systems
029
      deployed in real-world applications. Identifying when a model encounters inputs that deviate
030   significantly from its training distribution is crucial for ensuring reliability, safety, and alignment with
031   intended use cases. However, effectively detecting OOD samples has proven difficult (Nguyen et al.,
032   2015), largely due to the challenge of obtaining representative OOD data for training robust detectors.
033
      Previous approaches to OOD detection have focused on leveraging external OOD datasets (Hendrycks
034
      et al., 2018), augmenting in-distribution (InD) images through mixing techniques (Hendrycks et al.,
035
      2022; Zhang et al., 2023), and using unlabeled wild data to enhance classifier training (Du et al.,
036   2024; Katz-Samuels et al., 2022a). However, these methods are limited by the availability and
037   representativeness of OOD data. Real-world OOD inputs can be highly diverse and unpredictable,
038   making it impractical to curate datasets that capture the full spectrum of potential distribution shifts.
039
      In this work, we propose a novel approach that leverages the generative capabilities of LLMs to
040
      synthesize high-quality OOD proxies, eliminating the need for any external OOD data source. Our
041
      key insight is that by carefully prompting LLMs, we can generate synthetic samples that mimic
042   potential distribution shifts and serve as effective proxies for real OOD data. This allows us to
043   train robust OOD detectors using only InD data and synthetically generated OOD proxies. Our
044   approach capitalizes on the recent success of LLMs to create synthetic datasets applicable across
045   diverse downstream learning tasks (Tang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023a). By applying this paradigm
046   to OOD detection, we aim to overcome the longstanding challenge of OOD data scarcity. Rather
047   than attempting to collect or curate real OOD samples, we leverage the semantic understanding
048   and generative abilities of LLMs to produce diverse synthetic proxies that capture the essence of
049   distribution shifts.
050   We identify several critical use cases where existing OOD detection methods fall short, including
051   classical NLP classification tasks such as toxicity detection and sentiment classification, as well as
052   classification tasks relevant to the development of modern LLM systems, such as training a reward
053   model for RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017) and detection of misaligned generations. We demonstrate
      that established OOD detection score-based methods (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Liang et al., 2018;


                                                          1

054
      Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Sun & Li, 2022) under perform on these use-cases when using
055   in-distribution data to learn an OOD detection rule. Our synthetic data generation approach effectively
056   addresses these challenges by generating representative OOD samples leading to an efficient OOD
057   detector. The primary contributions of our work are:
058
059       C1) Framework for generating high-quality synthetic OOD proxies using LLMs.
060       C2) Training robust OOD detectors using only InD data and synthetic OOD proxies.
061       C3) Empirical analysis covering classical NLP classification tasks and applications of text
062           classifiers in LLM development and deployment.
063       C4) Analysis of the properties of synthetic proxies and their impact on OOD detection perfor-

      By focusing on synthetic data generation, we aim to provide a scalable and adaptable solution to the
066
      OOD detection problem. Our approach has the potential to significantly improve the reliability and
067
      safety of text classification systems used across a wide range of applications, from content moderation
068   to LLM alignment.

073   Detecting OOD data. In recent years, there has been a growing interest in OOD detection (Fort
074
      et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2022; Galil et al., 2023; Djurisic et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
      2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2023; Ming & Li, 2024; Ghosal et al., 2024).
075
      One approach to detect OOD data uses scoring functions to assess data distribution, including:
076
077          • Distance-based methods (Lee et al., 2018; Tack et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2021; Du et al.,
078            2022a; Ming et al., 2023): These methods compute distances (e.g., Mahalanobis distance or
079            cosine similarity) between a sample and class prototypes in feature space to measure how
080
               far a sample is from in-distribution data.
081          • Energy-based scores (Liu et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023): These scores leverage the energy
082            of a sample computed from the logits of a neural network to determine its likelihood of
083            belonging to the in-distribution or OOD set.
084          • Confidence-based approaches (Bendale & Boult, 2016; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Liang
085            et al., 2018): These rely on model confidence scores (e.g., softmax probabilities) to identify
086            OOD data, often enhanced by techniques like temperature scaling and input perturbation.
087          • Bayesian methods (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Malinin &
088            Gales, 2019; Wen et al., 2020): These methods use Bayesian models to quantify uncertainty
089            in model predictions to identify inputs that are significantly different from the training data.
090
091   Another approach to OOD detection involves using regularization techniques during the training
092   phase (Malinin & Gales, 2018; Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2019; Jeong & Kim, 2020; Yang et al., 2021;
093
      Wei et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022b; 2023; Wang et al., 2023a). For example, regularization techniques
      can be applied to the model to either reduce its confidence (Lee et al., 2017; Hendrycks et al., 2019)
094
      or increase its energy (Liu et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022c; Ming et al., 2022) on the OOD data. Most of
095
      these regularization methods assume the availability of an additional auxiliary OOD dataset. Several
096   studies (Zhou et al., 2021; Katz-Samuels et al., 2022b; He et al., 2023) relaxed this assumption by
097   either utilizing unlabeled wild data or employing positive-unlabeled learning, which trains classifiers
098   using positive and/or unlabeled data (Letouzey et al., 2000; Hsieh et al., 2015; Niu et al., 2016; Gong
099   et al., 2018; Chapel et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2021; Xu & Denil, 2021; Garg et al., 2022; Du et al.,
100   2024). Unlike these approaches, our method completely removes the dependency on external data
101   sources, providing a fully self-sufficient solution for OOD detection.
102   Synthetic data. In contrast, our work completely eliminates the need for any external OOD data by
103   carefully creating synthetic proxy OOD data using LLMs. Recently, synthetic data has been used
104   for OOD detection in the image domain; Kwon et al. (2023) leverage CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), a
105   vision-language model, to erase InD regions from training images and then uses a latent diffusion
106   model to replace them with realistic OOD features that blend seamlessly with the image background
107   whereas Sun et al. (2024) generate synthetic image samples by using a variant of CLIP to mix InD
      features from different classes. In contrast, we focus on textual data and leverage LLMs to generate

      high-quality proxies for OOD data that capture the complexities of real-world OOD data. In our
109   work, we explore the efficacy of LLM-generated OOD proxies for OOD detection, an area which
110   remains largely unexplored.

      4    E XISTING S OLUTIONS : C HALLENGES AND O PPORTUNITIES
188
189   Confidence and Energy-score based solutions. OOD detection is widely being conducted using
190   confidence-based methods (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017; Liang et al., 2018) and Energy-score based
191   solutions (Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021). Specifically, we use MSP (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017),
192
      Energy (Liu et al., 2020), ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) and DICE (Sun & Li, 2022) scores, originally
      proposed for image-based OOD detection. These methods rely on a binary model trained solely
193
      on InD data with cross-entropy loss. During testing, the model receives both InD and OOD data,
194
      and assigns a score: a high score indicates InD, while a low score suggests OOD (for details, see
195   Appendix A). We use the MSP, Energy, ReAct, and DICE scores for this evaluation. However, as
196   shown in Table 3, these methods perform poorly, exhibiting extremely high FPR95 and low AUROC
197   scores, rendering them ineffective for text OOD detection.
198
      Selective Classification. One way to improve the reliability and efficiency of a classifier model is to
199
      use selective classification (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017) under which the model abstains from making
200
      predictions when it is uncertain. This method has demonstrated promising results in classification
201   tasks by minimizing the risk of incorrect predictions, making it well-suited for mission-critical
202   applications where the impact of errors is significant.
203
204
      We investigate whether or not selective classification can be used to enhance classifier performance
      in the presence of OOD data. For example, given a binary detector trained to classify whether an
205
      input text is ‘Negative’ (i.e. toxic) or ‘Positive’ (i.e. non-toxic). At test time, we input samples from
206
      both InD (i.e. Negative/Positive) and OOD (e.g. math/code problems or toxicity data coming from a
207   different data distribution) data. The model performance is enhanced by dropping samples on which
208   the model is most uncertain based on the MSP, Energy and DICE scores.
209

279   5       E XPERIMENTS
280   In this section, we demonstrate how well our framework performs across various InD-OOD dataset
281   pairs, encompassing a wide range of real-world scenarios. We identify three crucial scenarios
282   where addressing the OOD detection problem is especially valuable: 1) toxicity detection, 2) harm
283   detection, and 3) RLHF reward modeling.
284
      Toxicity detection is a classical text classification task with applications to moderation of online
285
      conversations to promote safe and inclusive conversations.
286
287   Harm detection is essential for resolving critical misalignment issues in LLMs, where the LLM’s
288   outputs can diverge from desired ethical standards. The goal is to train a smaller specialized detector
289
      model (i.e. a fine-tuned classifier) to proactively identify when alignment methods should be applied
      (Ngweta et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024a) to correct a harmful response from an LLM. By targeting
290
      alignment efforts only when necessary, this approach significantly mitigates the "alignment tax"
291
      — the resource-intensive process of continuously aligning an LLM — ensuring more efficient and
292   cost-effective alignment without compromising LLM’s integrity (Ouyang et al., 2022).
293
294   RLHF reward modeling. In the RLHF pipeline, a reward model serves as an automated system
295
      that learns human preferences and assigns scores to model outputs. It guides the fine-tuning process
      of LLMs, making the training more efficient, scalable, and consistent. By reducing the need
296
      for continuous human labeling, it significantly accelerates model development while maintaining
297
      alignment with human values. However, as evident from the RewardBench Leaderboard (Lambert
298   et al., 2024) 2 , certain reward models excel in specific text categories (e.g., Chat), achieving high
299   win percentages, yet perform miserably in others (e.g., Reasoning), yielding significantly lower win
300   percentages. Therefore, we designed a dual-purpose reward model that not only evaluates the score
301   of a given LLM response but also categorizes it based on whether it pertains to a high-performing
302   category (i.e., InD) or a low-performing category (i.e., OOD) in terms of win percentage. Our
303   reward model thus provides two outputs: 1) a score and 2) a classification label (i.e., InD vs OOD).
304   Such a model can strengthen the RLHF pipeline. If the model encounters an input belonging to a
305   low-performing category, the practitioner can choose to discard or ignore this output, thereby aiding
306
      in the training of a more robust RLHF model.

      Baselines. We compare our method with baselines like MSP (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017), Energy
340
      score (Liu et al., 2020), ReAct (Sun et al., 2021), and DICE (Sun & Li, 2022), which employ a binary
341
      model trained solely on InD data using cross-entropy loss (for details, see Appendix A). During
342   testing, these models receive both InD and OOD data. OOD detection is performed by assigning a
343   score: a high score suggests the data is from InD, while a low score indicates it is from OOD. We use
344   the MSP, Energy, ReAct, and DICE scores for this purpose. Although these baselines were originally
345   proposed for image data, we evaluate them on text data. We also consider an ideal setting by training
346   a three-class model directly on the original OOD data. The ideal setting does not reflect real-world
347   conditions because OOD data can encompass any data encountered in the wild, which we typically
348   lack access to. We use the term ‘Original’ to refer to the ideal model trained on the original OOD
349   data, and ‘Synthetic’ to refer to the model trained on our synthetically generated proxies.

351   5.2     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
352
353   For the toxicity and harm detection experiments, we adopted LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), a parameter-
354   efficient fine-tuning approach, to fine-tune Llama-2 13B. Our objective is twofold: first, to determine
355   if an input, i.e. CC comment or BT prompt-response pair, is appropriate; second, to classify inputs
356   as InD or OOD. To achieve this, we utilized a three-way model with labels Positive (i.e. non-toxic
357
      or aligned), Negative (i.e. toxic or not aligned), and Neutral (i.e. OOD). In all experiments, we
      maintained a consistent setup: a learning rate of 1.5e-4 and a batch size of 16. We configured the total
358
      number of epochs to 10 and applied early stopping. We employed LoRA with these configurations: an
359
      alpha of 16, dropout of 0.1, and a rank of 16. The LoRA target modules included "q_proj," "k_proj,"
360   "v_proj," "out_proj," "fc_in," "fc_out," and "wte." For Civil Comments, we label samples with a
361   toxicity score of 0 as Positive and those with a score above 0.6 as Negative. For BeaverTails, we select
362   Negative samples based on the harm category and Positive samples when the ‘is_safe’ category is
363   True. Each model was trained by randomly sampling 6000 data samples while ensuring a comparable
364   number of samples per class, except for Mostly Basic Python Problems (MBPP), where only 374
365   training samples were available, all of which were used. The size of the synthetic and original data is
366   kept similar in our experiments. In cases where validation samples are not available, we sample them
367   from the training data, ensuring the selected samples are mutually exclusive from the training set.
368
      The testing data is always completely disjoint from both the training and validation datasets.
369   For RLHF reward model filtering, we applied a single layer classification head on top the last layer
370   last token embedding of the Starling-RM-7B-alpha model while keeping the entire LLM frozen.
371
      In our experiments, we use the RewardBench (Chat) category as InD and the RewardBench (Reason-
372   ing) category as OOD. This decision was based on the performance of the Starling-RM-7B-alpha
373   model, which achieves a high win percentage of 98.0% for Chat on the RewardBench Leaderboard,
374   indicating strong performance. Conversely, its performance in the Reasoning category was notably
375   poorer, with a win percentage of only 58.0%. As InD dataset (i.e. Chat), we used five subsets
376   including alpacaeval-easy, alpacaeval-length, alpacaeval-hard, mt-bench-easy, mt-bench-medium. As
377   OOD dataset (i.e. Reasoning), we used five code and math subsets including math-prm, hep-cpp,
      hep-java, hep-python, and hep-rust. The single layer perception head was trained using cross entropy

384   Our main results are shown in Table 3 for the eight InD-OOD dataset pairs for toxicity and harm
385   detection tasks. First, we observe that our three-way synthetic model matches or surpasses the
386   baseline models on InD accuracy for nearly all InD-OOD dataset pairs. This demonstrates the
387   model’s effectiveness in performing the primary task of InD classification. The only instance where
388
      the InD performance deviates slightly more from the baselines is in the case of BT (SEAC & DAWBS),

389
      the task especially challenging.
390
391   Next, we observe that our synthetic proxies significantly outperform the MSP, Energy, ReAct, and
392   DICE score-based baselines in terms of FPR95 on far-OOD datasets, while either matching or
393   exceeding the performance of the ideal model trained on original OOD data. For example, on
394
      BT-GSM8K, our approach exceeds the ideal model, yielding an improvement of 0.23% on FPR95.

395
      AUROC values across nearly all datasets. Remarkably, in certain cases such as CC-GSM8K, CC-
396

399   an FPR95 of 10.16%, while MSP, Energy, ReAct, and DICE yield FPR95 values of 92.31% and
400   70.35%, 61.89, and 69.63%, respectively, highlighting their considerable limitations on text data.
401   These observations are particularly noteworthy because they illustrates the capability of artificially

403   instances, demonstrating that our method achieves accurate predictions across diverse and potentially
404   unfamiliar data distributions.

490   6    C ONCLUSIONS
491   In this paper, we introduce a novel framework for OOD detection that leverages synthetic data
492   generation powered by LLMs. Our method addresses the critical challenge of OOD data scarcity
493   by leveraging LLMs to create high-quality OOD proxies, eliminating the need for external OOD
494   data sources. Extensive experiments encompassing nine InD-OOD dataset pairs demonstrate that our
495   method significantly outperforms baseline approaches across real-world text classification use cases,
496   including tasks arising in LLM development and deployment lifecycle.
497   Incorporating OOD detection capabilities into various classification systems used for training LLMs
498   is a promising direction for future work. For example, OOD detection may help to identify when
499   reward overoptimization (also known as reward hacking) starts to occur (Skalse et al., 2022; Gao
500   et al., 2023b; Moskovitz et al., 2023). Another interesting application is pre-training data filtering,
501   where various classifiers are often used to select data for pre-training (Penedo et al., 2024; Li et al.,
502   2024) and are likely to benefit from OOD robustness due to the complexity and breadth of LLM
503   pre-training text corpora.

      A.1    P RELIMINARIES AND P ROBLEM S ETUP
868
      Let X = Rd denote the input space, where d is the dimensionality of the input features. The output
869
      space is represented as Y = {1, 2, . . . , C}, where C is the number of classes. Given a training
870   dataset D = {(xi , yi )}N
                              i=1 sampled from the joint distribution P on X × Y , the objective is to learn
871   a mapping fθ : X → Y . Assume that model fθ is trained on a dataset drawn from the InD Pin .
872
873
874   A.2    F ORMULATION OF OOD D ETECTION
875
      During testing, inputs are sampled from a mixture of InD Pin and OOD Pout . The goal is to determine
876
      whether a given input x ∈ X belongs to Pin . OOD detection is framed as a binary classification
877
      problem where the model fθ must classify x as either:
878
879          • InD: x belongs to the known distribution Pin .
880
881          • OOD: x is from an unknown distribution Pout , with no overlap between the label set of Pout
882            and Y .
883
884   A.3    D ECISION RULE FOR OOD D ETECTION
885
886   The decision rule for OOD detection is based on a score function S(x), which assigns a value to each
887   input x indicating its likelihood of belonging to Pin . A threshold λ is used for classification:
888
889
890

                                                         in    if S(x) ≥ λ
891                                       gλ (x) =                                                        (1)
                                                         out   if S(x) < λ
892
893
894   This mechanism ensures that inputs with scores above λ are classified as InD, while those below are
895   deemed OOD. The threshold λ is chosen so that a high fraction of InD data (e.g. 95% in our case i.e.
896
      FPR95) is correctly classified.
897   Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017). This method proposes to
898   use the maximum softmax score as the OOD score S(x).
899
      Energy (Wu et al., 2023). This approach leverages an energy score E(x) for OOD detection. The
900   energy function maps the pre-softmax logits to a scalar E(x) ∈ R, which is relatively lower for InD
901   data. Importantly, Wu et al. (2023) utilizes the negative energy score (i.e. S(x) = −E(x)) for OOD
902   detection, aligning with the convention that the score S(x) is higher for InD data and lower for OOD
903   data. Furthermore, this method does not require hyperparameter tuning.
904
      DICE (Sun & Li, 2022). This method computes logits by applying sparsification to the penultimate
905
      layer of the model, using only a subset of important weights that significantly contribute to the
906   prediction. After obtaining the logits, the final score S(x) is calculated using either the Energy
907   score or MSP. An ablation study in the original paper demonstrates that the Energy score performs
908   better, which is why we have selected this method. The approach includes a sparsity hyperparameter
909   p ∈ [0, 1]; a higher p indicates a greater fraction of weights are dropped, with p = 0 resulting in no
910   weights being dropped. We set p = 0.5, as it performs effectively in our case and aligns with findings
911   in the original paper.
912   ReAct (Sun et al., 2021). This method improves OOD detection by truncating the activations in
913   the penultimate layer of the network. Activations are clipped to a threshold c, reducing the effect of
914   noisy OOD data while preserving InD data. The truncated activations are used to compute the logits.
915   After obtaining the logits, the final score S(x) is calculated using either the Energy score or MSP. An
916   ablation study in the original paper demonstrates that the Energy score performs better, which is why
917   we have selected this method. The rectification threshold c is set to 1.33 and is selected from a set of
      {0.85, 1.0, 1.33, 1.5, 2.0, 2.33}.

1013   B.3     GSM8K
1014
1015   Grade School Math 8K (GSM8K5 ) dataset comprises 8.5K linguistically diverse math word problems
1016   designed to evaluate models’ abilities to perform multi-step reasoning. Each problem requires
1017   between 2 and 8 steps, primarily involving basic arithmetic operations like addition, subtraction,
1018
       multiplication, and division. Aimed at the middle school level, the problems are solvable without
       requiring concepts beyond early Algebra, and most do not necessitate explicitly defining variables.
1019
       Solutions are provided in natural language, rather than solely as mathematical equations, making the
1020
       dataset useful for studying how large language models reason through problems. This structure allows
1021   for a better understanding of models’ internal reasoning processes, as emphasized in the associated
1022   research paper (Cobbe et al., 2021). We use GSM8K as an far-OOD dataset.

1065
1066
1067   ToxiGen8 (Hartvigsen et al., 2022) is a large-scale dataset designed to improve toxic language
1068
       detection systems. It contains 274k statements that are either toxic or harmless, focusing on 13
       minority groups. The dataset was generated using advanced machine learning techniques to create
1069
       examples of subtly toxic and benign language. This approach allows ToxiGen to include more
1070
       complex, implicitly toxic statements than earlier datasets, which were mostly made from human-
1071   written text. A review of some samples from ToxiGen showed that people found it hard to tell the
1072   difference between statements generated by machines and those written by humans. We use ToxiGen
1073   as a near-OOD dataset.
