011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Enzymes are crucial catalysts for biochemical reactions, underpinning numerous
014            biological processes. The efficient identification of specific enzymes from exten-
015            sive protein libraries is essential for understanding and harnessing these biological
016
               reactions. While traditional computational methods for enzyme screening are time-
               consuming and resource-intensive, recent contrastive learning approaches have
017
               shown promise. However, these methods often overlook the inherent hierarchical
018
               classifications within enzymes and reactions, as well as the significance of molecu-
019            lar structure in catalysis. To address these limitations, we introduce FGW-CLIP,
020            a novel contrastive learning framework based on optimizing the fused Gromov-
021            Wasserstein distance. This approach incorporates multiple alignments, including
022            representation alignment between reactions and enzymes, and internal alignment
023            within enzyme and reaction representations. By introducing a regularization term,
024            our method minimizes the Gromov-Wasserstein distance between enzyme and re-
025            action spaces, enhancing information exchange within these domains. FGW-CLIP
026            demonstrates superior performance on the widely-used EnzymeMap benchmark,
027
               significantly outperforming existing methods in enzyme virtual screening tasks.
               Notably, it achieves state-of-the-art results in both BEDROC and EF metrics, indi-
028
               cating its efficacy in identifying relevant enzymes for given reactions. These results
029
               highlight the potential of our method to advance virtual enzyme screening, offering
030            a powerful tool for enzyme discovery and characterization.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Enzymes play a vital role in various biological processes such as biosynthesis. They act as catalysts,
036   speeding up chemical reactions in living organisms. However, in the vast protein sequence databases,
037   such as UniProt, only about 1/5 of proteins have been experimentally verified, and only 0.23% have
038   received sufficient attention from researchers(Ribeiro et al., 2023). Effective enzymes may be in the
039   billions of unexplored sequences.
040
      Traditional calculation methods, including sequnce-similarity–based (Altschul et al., 1990; Desai
041
      et al., 2011; Altschul et al., 1997), homology-based (Krogh et al., 1994; Steinegger et al., 2019),
042   structure-based (Roy et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2017) methods, consume a large amount of manpower
043   and material resources. And they are faced with protein annotation errors in calculation methods. In
044   recent years, machine learning based methods have emerged, primarily utilizing contrastive learning
045   techniques on the screening of enzymes. CLEAN (Yu et al., 2023b) improved EC number assignment
046   to enzymes, annotating understudied ones accurately and correcting mislabeled entries. While
047   CLIPZyme (Mikhael et al., 2024) is a computational framework that encodes and aligns enzyme
048   structure and reaction pair representations for in-silico enzyme screening. However, they focus on
049   enzyme-reaction relationship but overlook inherent hierarchical classifications within them and the
050
      importance of molecular structure in catalysis.
051   In this work, we introduce FGW-CLIP, a novel contrastive learning framework based on the opti-
052   mization of fused Gromov-Wasserstein distance. This framework incorporates multiple alignment
053   methods, including representation alignment between reactions and enzymes (similar to CLIP), as
      well as internal alignment of enzyme and reaction representations. By introducing regularization

054
      terms, our method minimizes the Gromov-Wasserstein distance between the enzyme and reaction
055   spaces during model training, thereby enhancing information interaction in both domains.
056
057   We provide theoretical insights into FGW-CLIP from the perspective of optimizing the fused Gromov-
058
      Wasserstein distance. Additionally, we offer empirical support by validating our approach on
      the widely-used EnzymeMap benchmark for enzyme virtual screening. FGW-CLIP outperforms
059
      existing baselines, achieving state-of-the-art results on Boltzmann-enhanced discrimination of ROC
060
      (BEDROC) and enrichment factor (EF) metrics.
061
062   Our key contributions are as follows:
063
             • We propose a novel framework for enhancing contrastive learning by optimizing the fused
064
               Gromov-Wasserstein distance. This approach considers not only the alignment of reactions
065            and enzymes but also the internal alignment within each domain. Furthermore, we introduce
066            a regularization term based on the Gromov-Wasserstein distance to preserve structural

069          • We provide theoretical insights into the proposed contrastive learning framework, exploring

071            reaction alignment.
072          • Our framework achieves state-of-the-art results on the widely used EnzymeMap benchmark
073            dataset. We present detailed ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of incorporating
074            internal structural information and Gromov-Wasserstein loss in our approach.

078   2.1   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
079
080   Contrastive learning has found extensive applications in vision and multimodal representation learn-
081   ing. CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) enhances multimodal contrastive learning by
082   effectively combining image and text information, making it widely applicable in fields such as
083   image classification, text generation, and human-computer interaction. MLIP (Zhang et al., 2024)
084   enhances CLIP by integrating spatial and frequency-domain information, improving multimodal
085
      learning through a multi-perspective approach. iCLIP (Wei et al., 2023) bridges the gap between
      image classification and contrastive learning, optimizing CLIP for both visual tasks and language-
086
      image pairings. X-MoRe (Eom et al., 2023) refines CLIP’s embeddings to enhance performance in
087
      image-to-text and text-to-image retrieval tasks, improving its adaptability for real-world applications.
088
089
      2.2   F USED G ROMOV-WASSERSTEIN D ISTANCE
090
091   The Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) Distance is a metric used in optimal transport theory that measures
092   the similarity between two metric spaces by considering the structures of the spaces rather than
093   their individual points. Mémoli (Scetbon et al., 2022) proves that GW 1/2 defines a distance on
094   the space of metric measure spaces quotiented by measure-preserving isometries. Fused Gromov-
095   Wasserstein (FGW) (Titouan et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2024)distance extends the Gromov-Wasserstein
096   metric to calculate transportation distance between two unregistered probability distributions on
097   different product metric spaces, such as combining graph signals and structures, making it suitable
098
      for attributed graphs.

133   3.1         OVERVIEW OF FGW-CLIP
134
135   Giving huge protein libraries, enzyme screening is to identify enzymes that can catalyze specific
136   chemical reactions from the libraries. We consider this task as a dense retrieval issue. Trained
137   encoders produce representations for both reactions and enzymes. Subsequently, reactions are used
138   as queries, and enzymes are ranked according to their cosine similarity to these reactions. The
139   top enzymes having the highest similarity are then recognized as the most probable candidates for
140   catalyzing the given reaction.
141   We propose FGW-CLIP from the perspective of optimizing the fused Gromov-Wasserstein distance.
142   As shown in Figure 1, we use the pretrained 3D molecular model, Uni-Mol Zhou et al. (2023), to
143   encode the molecules in a reaction, and obtain the reaction embedding through a readout function.
144   For enzymes, we employ the pretrained protein language model, ESM2 Lin et al. (2023), to derive
145   enzyme sequence embeddings. During subsequent training of FGW-CLIP, the ESM2 model remains
146   frozen. We perform contrastive learning between reactions and enzymes, where reactions that can
147
      be catalyzed by a given enzyme serve as positive samples, while others serve as negative samples.
      Additionally, we capture the intrinsic connections within enzymes and reactions: based on the
148
      Enzyme Commission (EC) number. Specifically, each data point has an EC number. Since enzymes
149
      or reactions can have multiple EC numbers, we add a list of EC numbers to each data point. Taking
150   reactions as an example, if the original EC number of a reaction is present in the EC number list of
151   another data point in the batch, we consider them a positive pair; if it is not in the list, they form a
152   negative pair. To better leverage the structural information inherent in both the enzyme and reaction
153   spaces, and to ensure consistency between these structures, we introduce a novel regularization term
154   based on the optimization of the Gromov-Wasserstein distance. Furthermore, we incorporated a loss
155   function for predicting the EC number of enzymes into the FGW-CLIP framework. We found that
156   this framework can be easily extended for efficient prediction of enzyme EC numbers.
157   In the following sections, we provide a more detailed explanation of the core components and
158   training strategies. In Section 3.2, we introduce the molecular encoder, Uni-Mol and the enzyme
159   encoder, ESM2. In Section 3.3, we delve into the training strategy of FGW-CLIP for contrastive
160   learning between reactions and enzymes. In Section 3.4, we analyze FGW-CLIP from the perspective
161   of optimizing the fused Gromov-Wasserstein distance, offering insights into its methodological
      advantages.

      3.2     P RETRAINING BACKBONE OF R EACTION AND E NZYME
163
164   For the reaction representation, Uni-Mol is utilized to encode the molecules, and the reaction
165   embedding is obtained via a readout function. Uni-Mol integrates 3D structural information during
166   encoding, which is crucial in the catalytic process, as some enzymes interact with intermediate
167   products to exert their catalytic function. Uni-Mol is pretrained on large-scale molecules and their
168   conformations. It leverages distance-based attention bias to integrate the 3D information of molecules
169   into the encoding. This 3D incorporation allows Uni-Mol to excel in tasks like molecular property
170
      prediction and protein-ligand binding pose prediction. We obtain the embedding of the entire molecule
      using a CLS token and normalize it with Euclidean norm.
171
172   For enzyme encoding, we use ESM2, a protein language model pretrained on millions of protein
173   sequences. One of its key strengths is its ability to handle longer sequences and generate detailed
174   embeddings, making it highly suitable for various protein-related tasks, such as structure and function
175   prediction. In our framework, we leverage the pretrained ESM2 model to encode enzyme sequences,
176
      using the average of all residue embeddings to represent the entire protein, and it remains frozen
      during subsequent training.

207   3.3.2    EC P REDICTION
208
209   The Enzyme Commission (EC) number is a standardized numerical classification scheme for enzymes
210   based on the chemical reactions they catalyze. Each EC number consists of four hierarchical levels
211   that describe the enzyme’s function in increasing specificity. By identifying the EC numbers, we
212
      can narrow down the possible enzymes that could catalyze specific reactions, thereby improving the
      efficiency and accuracy of enzyme screening.
213

215   levels. For each level, a separate classification head is employed, and we use cross-entropy loss for
      the predictions. The loss for a single level i is defined as:

      4.1.1     DATASETS
294
295   EnzymeMap Based on the original EnzymeMap dataset (Heid et al., 2023), it involves biochemical
296   reactions linked to UniProt IDs and EC numbers. There are 46,356 enzyme-driven reactions with
297   16,776 unique chemical reactions, 12,749 enzymes, 2,841 EC numbers, and 394 reaction rules in the
298   EnzymeMap dataset. We split the dataset into training, validation, and test sets based on the reaction
299   rule IDs, with a ratio of 0.8/0.1/0.1, containing 34,427, 7,287, and 4,642 entries, respectively, the
300   same as in CLIPZyme.
301   Enzyme Screening Set This dataset integrated the EnzymeMap dataset, Brenda release 2022 2
302   (Chang et al., 2020), and UniProt release 2022 01 (Yu et al., 2023a), and filtered out the sequences
303   that are longer than 650 amino acids. It includes a total of 261,907 protein sequences. Enzyme
304   screening Set is used as a virtual screening database, where we use the reactions from the EnzymeMap
305   test set as queries to perform screening in it.

      yielded the best performance. When α = 0.05, the effect of the GW loss was minimal, indicating
391
      that the influence of the GW loss on aligning the reaction-enzyme space was too weak. Conversely,
392   when α = 0.5, the performance decreased, suggesting that a high α disrupted the alignment of the
393   reaction-enzyme space and hindered the learning of the internal structures of enzymes and reactions.
394   Next, we investigated different ways of incorporating the GW loss into the FGW-CLIP framework.
395   The method labeled as ‘Label‘ involves using the labels from the internal contrastive learning (as
396   defined in Eq.2) to replace the similarity matrices simd (rj , rj ′ ) and simd (ei , ei′ ) in Eq.5. The method
397   labeled as ‘No detach‘ refers to not detaching the similarity matrices simd (rj , rj ′ ) and simd (ei , ei′ )
398   in Eq.5, allowing them to participate in gradient backpropagation and adding an optimization term
399   for these matrices.
400   As shown in the table, our current approach of detaching the similarity matrices provides the best
401   results, making it the optimal choice for building the FGW-CLIP framework. This method balances
402   the alignment of the reaction-enzyme spaces while preserving the internal structures, confirming the
403   effectiveness of our design.
404   Furthermore, to visually demonstrate the distinctions between embeddings learned by FGW-CLIP
405   and those from pretrained ESM2 checkpoint, we present a comparative visualization in Figure 2. The
406   enzymes depicted are sourced from EnzymeMap, with distinct colors representing different top-level
407   EC numbers ranging from EC 1 to EC 6. Upon comparison, the classification boundaries in Figure
408   2 generated by FGW-CLIP exhibit greater clarity, and the intra-class molecular distances appear
409   more appropriately scaled. Notably, some clusters subdivide into multiple subclusters, potentially
410   reflecting the inherent hierarchical structure within the molecular compositions.

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this work, we proposed FGW-CLIP, a novel contrastive learning framework that enhances enzyme
435   screening through fused Gromov-Wasserstein distance (FGW) optimization. Our method optimizes
436   fused Gromov-Wasserstein distance to align the reaction and enzyme spaces more effectively while
437   preserving the internal structures of both. We conduct contrastive learning between reactions and
438   enzymes, enzymes and enzymes, along with reactions and reactions. We also introduce an auxiliary
439   loss to predict EC number. Finally, we add the GW loss to form the complete FGW-CLIP framework.
440   In this framework, the model can effectively capture the intricate relationships between enzymes and
441   reactions, leading to a more accurate and robust outcome. We conduct extensive experiments on the
442   EnzymeMap dataset, where FGW-CLIP demonstrated its superiority over the state-of-the-art baseline.
443
      Notably, our method achieved significant improvements in key evaluation metrics such as BEDROC
      and EF, indicating its strong generalization capability and effectiveness in enzyme screening tasks.
444
445   In the future, we aim to incorporate enzyme structures into the model and explore further optimization
446   of enzyme functions. Additionally, we plan to investigate the application of FGW-CLIP in other
447   biochemical tasks.

719
720   For the training of FGW-CLIP, we use Adam optimizer at a learning rate of 0.001. The batch size
721   is 32, and the training is conducted on 4 NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 24G GPUs. For the reaction
722   part, the molecular encoder parameters are the same as those of Uni-Mol. The readout function is
723   sum. For the enzyme part, the encoder used is ESM2, which is frozen during the training process.
724   We added a linear layer after the embedding output by ESM2 to help with mapping. The training
725   epoch is 100, and the last checkpoint is selected.
