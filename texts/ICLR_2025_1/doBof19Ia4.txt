009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Functional RNA sequence design plays an essential role in the regulation of life
012            processes. The RNA inverse folding problem, which involves designing nucleic
013            acid sequences based on their three-dimensional structures, remains highly chal-
014            lenging. This complexity arises not only from the inherent flexibility of RNA
015            structures but also from the base-pairing rules that impose critical spatial con-
016            straints on the RNA scaffold. In recent times, the design of RNA has often de-
017            pended on geometric graph networks to design sequences. Motivated by recent
018
               advancements in protein design, we have developed the RNAformer module. This
               module is capable of learning the geometric constraints of RNA molecules in co-
019
               operation with geometric graph networks. Furthermore, to enhance the specificity
020
               of sequence generation, we have integrated secondary structure information as
021            labels, ensuring that the designed sequences align more closely with secondary
022            structure constraints. Additionally, we have used RNA language models to un-
023            derstand average evolutionary constraints. By incorporating a range of constraint
024            insights, GeoRDe has demonstrated superior performance under identical training
025            data conditions and has also showcased generalization capabilities on the inde-
026            pendent casp15 and RNA-puzzle datasets. Through extensive experimentation,
027            the GeoRDe has proven to be an innovative solution to the challenges of RNA
028            design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Ribonucleic acid (RNA) performs a variety of essential functions within the cell, including but not
      limited to catalyzing biochemical reactionsLewin (1982), regulating gene expressionPrasanth et al.
034
      (2005), and forming components of cellular machineryJinek & Doudna (2009). These multifaceted
035
      roles of RNA make it a key target for biomedical research and therapeutic development. Therefore,
036   the design of RNA sequences is not only crucial for understanding its biological functions but also
037   holds significant potential for developing new therapeutic strategiesYin & Rogge (2019)Lu & Thum
038   (2019).
039
      While the diversity and functionality of RNA are largely determined by its three-dimensional (3D)
040
      structure, the challenge of inferring the corresponding one-dimensional (1D) sequence from a given
041
      3D structure, known as the RNA inverse folding problemChurkin et al. (2018), remains a signifi-
042   cant obstacle. Traditional approaches to this problem have often focused on RNA’s secondary struc-
043   tureSzabat et al. (2020)Sato & Hamada (2023). However, with an improved understanding of RNA’s
044   3D geometryTownshend et al. (2021), researchers have begun to explore computational methods that
045   can design RNA sequences directly from its 3D structure. For instance, the RiboDiffusion model
046   uses a generative diffusion model to iteratively transform random sequences into target sequences,
047   thereby learning the conditional distribution of RNA sequences within a specified 3D scaffold struc-
048   tureHuang et al. (2024). Additionally, methods like gRNAdeJoshi et al. (2024) and RDesignTan
049   et al. (2024) encode the 3D framework of RNA using multi-state graph neural networks, provid-
050
      ing innovative pathways for RNA sequence design. The success of these methods highlights the
      potential of deep learning in managing RNA structural data.
051
052   Advances in protein structure predictionJumper et al. (2021) and designRen et al. (2024a) have
053   also informed new models and strategies for RNA design. For example, CarbonNovoRen et al.
      (2024b), which generates protein structures and sequences concurrently through a unified energy-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      based model, has shown its effectiveness in protein design. However, the unique features of RNA
055   molecules, such as the specificity of base pairing and the flexibility of the RNA backbone, require
056   that design methods be able to accurately address these distinctive structural characteristics. More-
057   over, compared to the extensive database of protein structures available in the Protein Data Bank
058   (PDB)Bank (1971), the scarcity of RNA structural data necessitates that design methods exhibit
059   greater data efficiency and generalization capabilities.
060
      Building on the foundation of current research, this study introduces a novel inverse folding algo-
061   rithm known as GeoRDe (GEOMETRIC CONSTRAINT RNA DESIGN). This algorithm employs
062   innovative approaches to handle the distinctiveness of RNA’s three-dimensional structure. Firstly,
063   GeoRDe employs a hybrid architecture that integrates geometric graph networks with triangle atten-
064   tion networks to derive representations of RNA molecules. The triangle attention network represents
065   an advancement over the traditional attention mechanism, specifically adapted to capture the intri-
066   cate spatial configurations characteristic of RNA molecules. In contrast to geometric graph networks
067   for unstructured data, triangle attention networks excel at capturing proximal bases linked by cova-
068   lent bonds within structured RNA information. Secondly, the algorithm employs multi-task learning
069
      to account for the significance of the base-pairing principle in RNA design. Lastly, the algorithm
      harnesses large models to extract constraints from evolutionary information.
070
071   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
072
073         1. Innovative Design of the RNAformer Module: This research has crafted an RNAformer
074            module that collaborates with geometric graph networks to learn the geometric constraints
075
               of RNA molecules.
076         2. Introduction of Secondary Structure Constraints: By integrating secondary structure
077            constraints in a labeled format, the precision of the designed sequences is markedly im-
078            proved.
079         3. Embedding of RNA Language Models: The embedding of RNA language models intro-
080            duces average evolutionary constraint information for sequences, thereby enhancing their
081            evolutionary reliability.
082         4. Validation across Multiple Datasets: The performance of the algorithm has been vali-
083            dated across various datasets, and the findings indicate that the incorporation of diverse
084            constraint strategies effectively confines the sequence design space and exhibits robust gen-
085            eralization capabilities.

126   2.3   RNA S TRUCTURE P REDICTION
127
      RNA structure prediction involves predicting the folding conformation of RNA from its one-
128   dimensional sequence. Initially, RNA prediction efforts concentrated on predicting RNA secondary
129   structures. ViennaRNALorenz et al. (2011)Hofacker (2003) is a physics-based prediction tool that
130   employs a standard energy function to predict RNA secondary structures. SpotRNAYang et al.
131   (2014) introduces a deep contextual learning approach, trained via transfer learning to predict the
132   secondary structure of all base pairs, including atypical and non-nested (pseudoknot) pairs. Knot-
133   FoldGong et al. (2024) is an advanced method for accurately predicting RNA secondary structures,
134   including pseudoknots, by integrating learned potentials with minimum-cost flow algorithms and en-
135   hancing prediction accuracy through attention-based neural networks. Unlike proteins, RNA three-
136
      dimensional structures exhibit greater flexibility. In recent years, with advancements in machine
      learning, approaches such as AlphaFold3Abramson et al. (2024), RosettaFoldNABaek et al. (2024),
137
      trRosettaRNAWang et al. (2023), and RhoFoldShen et al. (2022) have emerged. These methods uti-
138
      lize multiple sequence alignments as input and leverage deep learning networks, often incorporating
139   modules like evofold, to predict the three-dimensional coordinates of RNA.

144   3.1   I NVERSE F OLDING P ROBLEM D EFINITION
145
      In this paper, RNA inverse folding specifically denotes the process of identifying or engineering
146
      RNA sequences capable of folding into a predetermined target structure. For a one-dimensional
147   RNA sequence S comprising N nucleotides, each nucleotide is composed of one of four types of
148   ribonucleotides, denoted as S ∈ {A, U, C, G}N . The secondary structure of RNA is depicted using
149   dot-bracket notation, where the majority of RNA secondary structure pairings are categorized into
150   three types: A-U, C-G, and G-U. Bases adhering to these pairings are denoted by brackets, while
151   those not conforming are indicated by dots.
152
      Regarding the three-dimensional structure of RNA, this paper employs a coarse-grained backbone
153   representation to delineate the 3D configuration. This representation utilizes the C4’, C1’, N1 atoms
154   to signify pyrimidine nucleotides and the C4’, C1’, N9 atoms to signify purine nucleotides. The
155   model presented in this paper simulates the conditional distribution of RNA sequences given the
156   three-dimensional structure, which is mathematically represented as p(S|x).
157

159
160   The input features in this paper are categorized into two main components. Initially, the coarse
161   spatial arrangement of the RNA backbone is delineated through the local orientation of the C1’
      atoms. All atoms within a 12 Å radius from each atom are enumerated, and their relative contact

165   adjacent atoms are extracted as graph node attributes. Adjacent edges in the graph network are de-

218   3.4.1    S EQFORMER M ODULE
219
220   In this study, we introduce a module named SeqFormer for processing the three-dimensional struc-
221
      ture of RNA (Figure 1). The input to the SeqFormer module includes the local spatial orientation
      of C1’ atoms extracted from the coarse-grained atom coordinates of RNA and the relative distances
222
      between these atoms. These inputs are defined as:
223
224
225
                                         init S = C1’ local orientation,
                                                                                                         (5)
226                                      init Z = residue distance map.
227
228
      The design of the SeqFormer module is inspired by related work in protein structure predic-
      tionJumper et al. (2021) and protein designRen et al. (2024a), particularly the use of triangle mul-
229
      tiplicative update and triangle attention update to satisfy constraints in three-dimensional space. In
230
      representing the three-dimensional structure of RNA, we adopt a similar approach, combining local
231   orientation (init S) and residue distance map (init Z), and continuously updating sequence informa-
232   tion (seq act) and pair information (pair act) through N recycle iterations.
233
234
      In each iteration, we first fully interact the sequence dimension information and the two-dimensional
      information of pair, and integrate it using the outer product mean method. After integration, we use
235
      triangle multiplicative update and triangle attention update to update the pair representation. The
236
      iteration process is as follows:

244    6:     pair act+= triangle multiplication incoming(pair act)
245    7:     pair act+= triangle attention starting node(pair act)

485   coupled with the integration of secondary structure information and evolutionary constraints.Our
      approach has demonstrated significant advancements in the field of RNA sequence design, offering

489   tool for both research and therapeutic development.
