                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Sequential recommendation systems aim to provide personalized recommenda-
012            tions for users based on their interaction history. To achieve this, they often incor-
013            porate auxiliary information, such as textual descriptions of items and auxiliary
014            tasks, like predicting user preferences and intent. Despite numerous efforts to
015            enhance these models, they still suffer from limited personalization. To address
016            this issue, we propose a new paradigm, which we term preference discerning. In
017            preference discerning, we explicitly condition a generative sequential recommen-
018            dation system on user preferences within its context. The user preferences are
019            generated by Large Language Models (LLMs) based on user reviews. To eval-
020
               uate preference discerning capabilities of sequential recommendation systems,
               we introduce a novel benchmark that provides a holistic evaluation across vari-
021
               ous scenarios, including preference steering and sentiment following. We assess
022
               current state-of-the-art methods using our benchmark and show that they strug-
023            gle to accurately discern user preferences. Therefore, we propose a new method
024            named Mender (Multimodal Preference Discerner), which improves upon exist-
025            ing methods and achieves state-of-the-art performance on our benchmark. Our
026            results show that Mender can be effectively guided by human preferences, paving
027            the way toward more personalized sequential recommendation systems. We will
028            open-source the code and benchmarks upon publication.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Sequential recommendation is the task of recommending items to a user based on their historical
      interactions. This requires inferring latent variables, such as user preferences and intent, which
034
      are often not explicitly provided in publicly available datasets. To improve personalization, several
035
      sequential recommendation systems leverage auxiliary information, including heterogeneous inter-
036   action types (Meng et al., 2020), item descriptors (e.g., textual, visual) (Hidasi et al., 2016b; Liu
037   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2019a), temporal information (Bogina & Kuflik, 2017; Li et al., 2020), and
038   keyword-based user queries (He et al., 2022). By incorporating such information, these systems can
039   better approximate a user’s intent, leading to improved performance and personalization.
040
      Although incorporating additional information can provide benefits, the degree of personalization in
041
      current sequential recommendation models remains limited. User decisions on what item to acquire
042   next are guided by their preferences, which are typically not explicitly provided in commonly used
043   recommendation datasets (Ni et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2015). As a result, these preferences must
044   be approximated from the user’s interaction history. Recent works have leveraged LLMs to extract
045   user preferences from existing datasets and leverage them for auxiliary tasks (Zhang et al., 2023;
046   Cao et al., 2024). However, these approaches do not allow the model to be dynamically steered by
047   user preferences in their context during inference. Furthermore, there is currently no benchmark that
048   effectively evaluates to what extent those models discern preferences.
049   To address these limitations, we propose a novel paradigm, which we term preference discerning.
050   preference discerning entails training a multimodal generative retrieval model conditioned on user
051   preferences within its context (see Figure 1). This requires approximating a user’s preference in tex-
052   tual form from user-specific data, such as reviews via pre-trained LLMs (Kim et al., 2024). By con-
053   ditioning the sequential recommendation system on user preferences in-context, we unlock steering
      via generated user preferences, effectively combining the sequential prior from interaction history

      2   R ELATED W ORK
093
      Sequential Recommendation can be categorized into two major scenarios: search (Nigam et al.,
094
      2019) and recommendation (Covington et al., 2016). The former assumes access to a query from
095
      a user that reflects their intent (He et al., 2022), whereas the latter scenario does not make such
096   an assumption. For the recommendation scenario, numerous works have investigated the use of
097   additional information to enhance recommendation performance (Meng et al., 2020; Hidasi et al.,
098   2016a; Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2019a; Bogina & Kuflik, 2017; Li et al., 2020). Our work
099   introduces a new paradigm that enables in-context steering of sequential recommendation systems
100   by textual user preferences.
101
      Existing Benchmarks for recommendation vary in their representation of user preferences and the
102   tasks they evaluate. Oh et al. (2024) proposed a benchmark for instruction-following in information
103   retrieval where instructions are generated from user-specific data via LLMs. The C4 benchmark
104   (Hou et al., 2024) uses complex search queries that reflect user preferences for retrieval. Contrary,
105   we focus on user preferences in sequential recommendation. Such preferences are often modeled
106   indirectly from user queries and responses to recommended items (Min et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
107   2013; Ma et al., 2018), or represented as edges on graphs (Ying et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019). In
      query-aware sequential recommendation He et al. (2022) the model is given keywords in its context


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      that represent the user’s intent but do not capture their preferences. In contrast, our benchmark builds
109   on established datasets (Ni et al., 2019; Kang & McAuley, 2018) and augments them with generated
110   user preferences to evaluate preference discerning capabilities.
111
112
      Generative Retrieval uses autoregressive modeling to generate the next item, rather than perform-
      ing pairwise comparisons between a user representation and all item representations. Rajput et al.
113
      (2023) proposes tokenizing items in the form of semantic IDs (Lee et al., 2022). The benefit of this
114
      approach is that very large item sets can be represented as a combination of ids that reflect their se-
115   mantic similarity. Subsequent works have investigated the effect of learned tokenization (Sun et al.,
116   2023) and additional objectives (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Our Mender represents items as
117   semantic IDs and fuses them with pre-trained LMs to effectively steer the recommendation.
118
      Language-Based Sequential Recommendation rely on the premise of enhanced transparency and
119
      actionable interrogation of recommendation systems (Radlinski et al., 2022). Furthermore, language
120
      provides a natural interface for users to express their preferences and allows harnessing the expres-
121   sive power of LLMs. Recent works have leveraged LLMs to approximate user preferences by repre-
122   senting user-and item-specific data in natural language (Zheng et al., 2023; Oh et al., 2024; Sanner
123   et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024),by conditioning the LLM on user embeddings (Ning et al., 2024), or
124   by leveraging user reviews for items (Kim et al., 2024). In this context, Kang et al. (2023) found that
125   effective preference approximation may require fine-tuning of the LLM. Other studies have explored
126   using LLMs for data augmentation in sequential recommendation (Geng et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
127   2019b; Luo et al., 2024). In the near cold-start scenario, Sanner et al. (2023) demonstrated that user
128   preferences represented in natural language can be particularly effective. Li et al. (2023) showed
129
      the benefit of moving from ID-based representations to text-based representation of the interaction
      history. Similarly, Petrov & Macdonald (2023) represent all items in natural language and performs
130
      ranking conditioned on past interactions. Zheng et al. (2023) explored aligning semantic IDs with
131
      natural language by adding auxiliary tasks. The key difference to our Mender is that it operates on
132   different levels of abstraction to represent the user’s interaction history.

261   We propose Mender, a novel multimodal generative sequential recommendation system. Mender
262   can be conditioned on user preferences expressed in natural language in its context and generates

264   retrieval model trained on semantic IDs. These semantic IDs are obtained by training a RQ-VAE
265   (Lee et al., 2022) on item embeddings in Sentence-T5 space. Given an item embedding e ∈ Rd , the
266   RQ-VAE quantizes e into a discrete feature map as:

308
      most recent ones. We adopt a leave-last-out data split, where the penultimate item in a sequence is

311   items in each sequence are used for training, except for the first item, since no user preferences
312   are available for it. We evaluate our trained baselines using common retrieval metrics, including
313   Recall (or Hit Rate), and Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002,

315   Appendix A.1 and Appendix A.2, respectively. All our methods are trained on single A100 or V100

351   head to match the number of semantic IDs, as language generation is not required for our task.
352   VocabExtLM represents the past interaction history in language as done for MenderTok and
353   MenderEmb , but initializes the decoder with a pre-trained language decoder. Therefore this baseline
354   operates on the same semantic gap as Mender variants. We again leverage LoRA for fine-tuning.

      5   L IMITATIONS
521
      A current limitation of our benchmark is that the compiled user preferences are limited to five se-
522
      lected datasets. However, since we used open-source models to generate them, the data generation
523
      pipeline can be extended to new datasets. Currently, the data generation process relies on extensive
524   post-processing to ensure high-quality user preferences, which is tailored to the specific LLM we
525   used. Furthermore, our preference generation pipeline relies on the presence of user reviews and
526   does not take into account longer time dependencies. Finally, we do not explore the effect of scaling
527   the language encoder. All of these limitations present fruitful avenues for future work.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
      Current sequential recommendation systems are limited in their personalization as they model user
532
      preferences implicitly. We propose a new paradigm, namely preference discerning, in which the se-
533
      quential recommendation system is conditioned on user preferences represented in natural language.
534   To evaluate preference discerning capabilities, we present a benchmark that is specifically designed
535   to assess the ability of sequential recommendation models to discern textual preferences along five
536   different axes. We also propose a novel generative retrieval model, Mender, which represents the
537   items at different levels of abstraction, namely semantic ids and natural language. Our experimental
538   results show that Mender outperforms state-of-the-art models on our benchmark. Our contributions
539   pave the way for a new class of generative retrieval models that unlock leveraging organic data for
      steering via textual user preferences.

896   A     G ENERATIVE R ETRIEVAL VIA SEMANTIC ID S
897
898   We provide an open-source implementation of all baselines used in this work, including TIGER
899   (Rajput et al., 2023). To facilitate reproducibility of the results reported in Rajput et al. (2023), we
900   elaborate on the implementation details as follows. The training of TIGER consists of two stages:
901   (i) training the residual quantizer (RQ-VAE) to obtain semantic IDs, and (ii), training the generative
902   retrieval model.
903
904   A.1   RQ-VAE
905
906   Training the RQ-VAE involves two essential steps: (i) constructing an item embedding, and (ii)
907   optimizing the model through residual quantization.
908   Item embedding For item embedding, we utilize the Sentence-T5 model (Ni et al., 2022), which
909   is publicly available on the Hugging Face Hub (Wolf et al., 2020). We explored various sources
910   of information to represent items and found that the optimal approach varies across datasets. For
911   the Beauty and Sports datasets, using item descriptions led to sub-optimal results due to the high
912   noise levels present in these descriptions. In contrast, item descriptions proved beneficial for the
913   Toys dataset. Additionally, we leveraged other item attributes, including title, price, brand, and
914
      categories. For the Stream dataset, we utilized a broader set of attributes: title, genre, specs, tags,
      price, publisher, and sentiment.
915
916   Training By default, we standardize the item embeddings, as this helps prevent collapse during
917   RQ-VAE training. For training the RQ-VAE, we found that the encoder-decoder architecture is
      crucial for increasing codebook coverage. Additionally, residual connections and weight decay

945   performance.

947   A.2   T RANSFORMER
948
949   Following Rajput et al. (2023) we instantiate the generative model via the T5 architecture (Raffel
950   et al., 2020). Next, we delve into the design choices underlying this approach, as introduced by
951   Rajput et al. (2023), and discuss their utility.
952
      Training sequences To construct the training sequences, Rajput et al. (2023) limit the number of
953   items in a user sequence to at most 20. This can be implemented by taking either the first, the last, or
954   all items within a sliding window of up to 20 items. We experimented with each of these approaches
955   and found that using the most recent 20 items in a user sequence generally yields improved per-
956   formance. Unlike prior sequential recommendation systems, which require at least one item in a
957   sequence to predict the next item (Kang & McAuley, 2018; Zhou et al., 2020), TIGER leverages a
958   user embedding trained alongside item embeddings. Therefore, we typically use the first item in a
959   sequence for training, although this is not strictly necessary.
960   Decoding Another crucial aspect of the generative retrieval pipeline is the decoding process. As
961   noted by Rajput et al. (2023), the generation of valid semantic IDs is not guaranteed. To mitigate
962   this issue, we track the number of invalid semantic IDs produced during decoding. We find that
963   this number is typically quite low. Nevertheless, to further improve the accuracy of our retrieval
964   results, we employ filtering to remove invalid IDs and increase the beam size to be larger than the
965   final retrieval set.

989    A.4    A DDITIONAL FINDINGS
990
991    Beyond the experiments discussed above, we conducted further investigations into the TIGER
992    framework, yielding the following key insights.
993           • TIGER exhibits superior performance on shorter sequences, as shown in Figure 6 (left).
994           • The inclusion of user embeddings in TIGER does not yield any significant benefits to down-
995             stream performance, as illustrated in Figure 6 (right).
996           • Representing interaction history in natural language leads to improved ranking perfor-
997             mance, as demonstrated in Figure 7.
998    TIGER Works Better on Shorter Sequences. As shown in Figure 6 (left), TIGER performs signif-
999    icantly better on shorter sequences than on longer ones. The x-axis represents the number of items
1000   per test sequence, which is at least 4 due to the 5-core user and item filtering applied. Further, the
1001   maximum number of items per sequence is capped at 19, as we limit the maximum sequences length
1002   to 20, following (Rajput et al., 2023). This results in a maximum sequence length of 19 items, where
1003   the task is to predict the 20th item. The y-axis shows the number of matches. Notably, TIGER’s
1004   performance is substantially better on shorter sequences than on longer ones. However, the number
1005
       of matches increases again for the longest sequences, although it remains considerably lower than
       for shorter sequences.
1006
1007   User Embedding. Rajput et al. (2023) employ a user embedding selected based on hashing. How-
1008   ever, it is unclear whether this approach offers any advantages, as the number of user embeddings
1009   suggested by Rajput et al. (2023) often results in numerous collisions in practice. To investigate
1010   this, we conduct an experiment where we remove the user embedding entirely. As shown in Fig-
1011
       ure 6 (middle), we do not observe a significant drop in performance. This suggests that the user
       embedding, as implemented in (Rajput et al., 2023), does not provide any notable benefits.
1012
1013   History Compression via Natural Language. We conduct an additional study where we repre-
1014   sent the past interaction history using natural language and initialize the encoder of TIGER with
1015   a FLAN-T5-small language encoder. This approach is reminiscent of history compression via lan-
1016   guage models (Paischer et al., 2022, HELM). We refer to this variant as LIGER (Language-TIGER),
1017
       and compare its performance to the baseline TIGER in Figure 7. The results show that while there
       is no significant difference in Recall, LIGER yields notable improvement in NDCG metrics. This
1018
       suggests that compressing interaction history using natural language generally enhances the model’s
1019
       ranking capabilities.

1265
1266   In this section, we provide supplementary detail on the creation of the various components of our
1267   benchmark.
1268
1269   D.1   P REFERENCE S ENTIMENT U NDERSTANDING
1270
1271   The sentiment understanding benchmark is based on preference-item pairs and utilizes a matching
1272   mechanism to identify items that triggered negative reviews. This is implemented using a pre-trained
1273   sentiment classification model from Hartmann et al. (2023) to classify reviews. To identify prefer-
1274
       ences, we employ a rule-based approach, as we observed that preferences can be both positive and
       negative simultaneously (e.g., a preference may specify liking certain items, while avoiding others).
1275
       Furthermore, we noticed that negative preferences consistently follow a specific pattern, starting
1276
       with either “Avoid”, “Exclude”, or “No”. To minimize misclassifications, we consider preferences
1277   beginning with these words to be negative. If only one item in a user sequence received a negative
1278   review, we pair the negative preference with that item. Otherwise, we use a matching mechanism
1279   in Sentence-T5 space, where we match a negative preference to the item whose review is closest in
1280   terms of cosine similarity. An example of the negative matching pipeline is illustrated in Figure 10
1281   . This yields a set of negative preference-item pairs, enabling us to evaluate whether the model can
1282   recognize negative sentiment and respond accordingly. To obtain positive preference-item pairs, we
1283   iterate over all negative pairs and invert the gathered preferences. Since negative instructions always
1284   start with “Avoid”, “Exclude”, or “No”, we simply replace these words with “Find” or “Search for”
1285
       to invert them. This results in two sets: one containing negative preferences paired with items and
       another containing positive preferences paired with the same items. Finally, we assess whether the
1286
       model can successfully avoid certain items, while actively retrieving others.
1287

1293   the model can retrieve a distinctly different item by changing the user preference accordingly. We
1294   identify a very similar item by the maximal cosine similarity in a pre-trained Sentence-T5 embed-
1295   ding space. Contrary, we retrieve a very distinct item by the lowest cosine similarity to the ground
       truth item. Subsequently, we match the retrieved items to new user preferences, again via cosine

1550   Are the generated user preferences informed by the user’s past interaction history? The ob-
1551   jective for introducing this question was to quantify how much of the generated preferences was
1552   actually represented in the interaction history and what amount has been hallucinated. We report
1553   the results for this first question in Figure 14. The majority of users found that the generated user
1554
       preferences are generally well informed by the user’s interaction history across datasets. We found
       that the model occasionally generated rather generic preferences, for example “Avoid harsh chemi-
1555
       cals” on the Beauty dataset even though there was no mention of harsh datasets in the reviews. Such
1556
       preferences are rather generic and do not convey much information about a user’s preference. Fur-
1557   thermore, some participants indicated that there was a lack of information to answer the question.
1558   This can be traced back to the fact that we intentionally did not provide item descriptions to the
1559   user, as those often contain a substantial amount of noise. As this information is hidden, we believe
1560   it caused the small fraction of preferences that were rated as lack of info. Thus, we can conclude
1561   that the generated user preferences for the most part were informed by reviews or item-specific info,
1562   however there is still a non-negligible amount of user preferences that can be considered halluci-
1563   nated.

       Is the matched preference related to the target item? After we have established the quality of
1601   the preferences it is imperative to also evaluate our matching of preferences to target items con-
1602   ducted in Section 3.2 for preference-based recommendation. The reason we conduct this matching
1603   is to provide the model with a useful signal it can learn from. This is imperative as we observed that
1604   simply using all preferences for training leads to detrimental performance (see Table 8). We report
1605   the results for this question in Figure 16. Interestingly, the fraction of correctly matched preferences
1606   is significantly lower compared to the number of correctly generated preferences. The reasons for
1607   this can be two fold, (i) it can be that the target item is entirely unrelated to the past interaction
1608   history, or (ii), the matching mechanism is suboptimal. The former case reflects the inherent uncer-
1609
       tainty of the sequential recommendation task, as oftentimes the target item is simply not related to
       previously acquired purchases. This shortcoming cannot be alleviated. The latter, however, can be
1610
       tackled by potentially more expressive embedding models or LLMs that can be used for matching
1611
       preferences to the target item. Finally, the lack of info category represents cases where the infor-
1612   mation about the target item is too little, i.e. no description or item title is given. Overall, we can
1613   conclude that even though we demonstrated significant performance gains resulting from training
1614   on the matched preferences, it could likely be improved.
