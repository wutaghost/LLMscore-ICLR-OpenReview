010
011
                   Large Language Models (LLMs) show exceptional skills in a wide range of tasks,
012                with their ability in lateral thinking standing out as a particularly intriguing area.
013                Lateral thinking in LLMs allows them to understand deeper or suggested mean-
014                ings from the context, which is essential for making sense of complex scenarios,
015                especially in puzzle-solving games. To delve deeper into and improve the lateral
016                thinking capabilities of LLMs in the realm of puzzle-solving, we introduce the
017                “Lateral Thinking Puzzles” and construct the accompanying dataset. Our novel
018                PuzzleVerse framework aims to enhance LLMs’ lateral thinking in puzzle-solving
019                games. Complementing this, we propose a creativity metric to ensure comprehen-
020
                   sive evaluations. Experiments show that the selected LLMs, after being trained
                   with PuzzleVerse, have an average improvement of 101.9% compared to their
021
                   performance before PuzzleVerse training among all metrics. We also validate the
022
                   robustness of PuzzleVerse that trained LLMs perform better in other reasoning
023                tasks.

057   Research on LLMs’ lateral thinking in solving downstream tasks is limited. They mainly focus on
058
      conventional logical reasoning associated with vertical thinking, which are divided into decomposing
      tasks and calling external modules. The former includes using Chain of-Thought (CoT) or Auto-CoT
059
      to generates reasoning chains (Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022), using active learning to stimulate
060
      reasoning capabilities (Diao et al., 2023), using a voting strategy to select the most consistent
061   answer output based on different reasoning paths (Wang et al., 2022), etc. The latter includes
062   using frozen LLMs to automatically generate intermediate reasoning steps (Paranjape et al., 2023),
063   decomposing symbolic reasoning, mathematical reasoning, or algorithmic tasks into intermediate
064   steps (Gao et al., 2023), etc. These methods are not enough to make LLMs owe lateral thinking,
065   which necessitates techniques such as challenging assumptions, seeking alternative solutions with
066   analogy, and embracing ambiguity (Xie et al., 2023).
067   However, lateral thinking varies across different contexts, making the choice of context for studying
068   lateral thinking an important consideration. For instance, the example mentioned above requires
069   external knowledge or commonsense, such as “Urine is mostly water and can substitute for it in
070   emergencies”, while some puzzle-solving games demand creativity and imagination, like the riddle
071   “What kind of dog never bites?” and the answer is “A hot dog”. Therefore, in this paper, we
072   choose puzzle-solving games to investigate LLMs’ lateral thinking which has two main reasons: i)
073   Puzzle-solving games typically require thinkers to step outside conventional thought patterns and
074   apply creativity and imagination to understand and solve puzzles. ii) These games offer a clear
075
      framework and objective that is to find the answer to the puzzle. This makes lateral thinking in
      puzzle-solving games more quantifiable and researchable compared with other more open-ended or
076
      subjective scenarios.
077
078   To evaluate and enhance LLMs’ lateral thinking in puzzle-solving games, we adopt the Lateral
079   Thinking Puzzles (Sloane & MacHale, 1994). Building on the existing lateral thinking puzzles
080   datasets (Jiang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023), we construct the largest Lateral Thinking Puzzles
081
      dataset (short for “LTP”), which includes riddles, a sequenced set of questions and answers, solutions,
      and rules. Based on the LTP dataset, we propose PuzzleVerse 2 , a baseline framework that improves
082
      the lateral thinking in puzzle-solving games of LLMs through assisting them to propose a series of
083
      questions to clarify the riddle’s solution. In addition, we propose a novel creative metric, including
084   compliance, reasoning, and completeness for evaluating LLMs’ lateral thinking capabilities. Accord-
085   ing to the experiments, the PuzzleVerse framework can effectively improve LLMs’ performance in
086   LTP, resulting in LLMs with advanced lateral thinking in puzzle-solving games. In summary, our
087   study makes three key contributions: i) We construct the largest lateral thinking puzzles dataset. We
088   also propose the creativity metric, adopting it and human metric to evaluate LLMs’ lateral thinking
089   in puzzle-solving games. ii) We make an exploration for LLMs’ lateral thinking in puzzle-solving
090   games, and then develop a novel PuzzleVerse framework to enhance these capabilities in LLMs.
091   iii) We validate the effectiveness of PuzzleVerse in LLMs’ lateral thinking in puzzle-solving games
092
      through extensive experiments in LTP dataset and other reasoning tasks.

      the optimal moment to provide given clues for the questioner LLM. If a question is asked with a
286   positive answer (i.e., “Yes”), it receives positive score (such as plus 1). Conversely, a negative score
287   (such as minus 1) is assigned for the question. If a series of questions consecutively receives negative
288   scores for more than five rounds, GPT-3.5 is then requested to generate a clue to guide the questioner.
289   Finally, the GPT-3.5 determines the questioning’s termination. Questioning terminates either when
290   the questioner LLM successfully infers the solution or when the questioning reaches a predefined
291   round limit (we defined it as 30). We further utilize the threshold of the overlap score, which is set as
292   0.8 tuned through experimentation, to assess the correlation between the sequence of questions and
293   the solution, determining if the solution has been deduced. If the overlap score exceed this threshold
294
      within the round limits, it indicates the questioner’s successful deduction, prompting GPT-3.5 to
      declare questioning termination. Alternatively, GPT-3.5 signifies questioning termination when the it
295
      reaches the round limits.

      writing, leading to improved writing skills. Lin et al. (2021) introduced a multiple-choice QA task
503
      focused on riddle-style questions that required commonsense reasoning and linguistic creativity, with
504   a dataset of 5.7k examples. In contrast to these methods, we use LLMs for supervised fine-tuning
505   and reinforcement learning, dynamically generating and optimizing question-posing paths, which
506   significantly improved model performance on LTP tasks. Moreover, none of these benchmarks has as
507   many samples as our work.
508
      Reasoning. For example, Hao et al. (2023) utilized LLMs as world state predictors and strategic
509
      reasoners. Lu et al. (2023) introduced Chameleon in enhancing LLMs’ compositional reasoning
510   capability. Tarau (2023) automated deep reasoning in LLM dialog threads. Kıcıman et al. (2023)
511   delved into causal reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Yoneda et al. (2023) introduced Statler to enhance
512   LLMs’ long-horizon reasoning capability in robotic tasks. Paranjape et al. (2023) presented ART to
513   generate intermediate reasoning steps. Chen et al. (2023c) introduced ChatCoT by chain-of-thought
514   reasoning. However, these work mainly focus on vertical thinking instead of lateral thinking.
515
      Question Generation. For example, Chen et al. (2019) designed a reinforcement learning model for
516   natural question generation. Tavares et al. (2023) delved into LLM strategies in generating questions
517   on dialogue state tracking. Kai et al. (2021) proposed a double-hints method for visual question
518   generation. Uehara et al. (2022) stressed the significance of sub-questions in enhancing primary
519   visual queries. Arora et al. (2022) explored effective prompting strategies for LLMs. Abdelghani
520   et al. (2022) harness GPT-3’s capabilities in children’s curiosity-driven questioning. However, these
521   studies focus on reshaping question generation instead of searching valuable questioning points.
522   Story Understanding. For example, Yuan et al. (2022) introduced a platform fostering human-LLM
523   story-writing collaborations. Swanson et al. (2021) unveiled STORY CENTAUR, optimizing LLMs
524   for creative endeavors. Dong et al. (2022) spotlighted CoRRPUS to boost story consistency in LLM
525   outputs. Bhandari & Brennan (2023) assessed the trustworthiness of LLM-generated children’s
526   stories. Chen et al. (2023b) advocated for LLMs to generate complex narratives. Lee et al. (2022)
527   explored LLM-enabled interactive story rewriting. Méndez & Gervás (2023) utilized ChatGPT in
528   narrative “sifting.” Together, these contributions highlight the potential of LLMs in story generation
529   and comprehension.

531   6    Conclusions and Future Work
532
533
      In exploring the potential of LLMs, we’ve pinpointed their impressive aptitude for lateral thinking,
534   which is instrumental for grasping intricate and nuanced contexts. By introducing the Lateral
535   Thinking Puzzles and its complementary dataset, we illuminate the depth of this capability within
536   LLMs. Our proposed PuzzleVerse framework is designed to further enhance LLMs’ lateral thinking
537   capabilities, and our proposed creativity metric offers a comprehensive evaluation. Experiments show
538   the effectiveness of PuzzleVerse in not only LTP but also other reasoning tasks. Future research can
539   delve into more intricate thinking scenarios and introduce the integration of multi-modal data, further
      enhancing LLMs’ lateral thinking in puzzle-solving games.

      Ethic Statement
542
      We analyze potential negative impacts and make ethic statement. Firstly, although lateral thinking
543
      encourages creativity and non-traditional solutions, these solutions may not align with societal
544   norms or ethical standards in practical applications. Secondly, enhancing lateral thinking capabilities
545   might exacerbate existing biases in LLMs. The previous training data for LLMs may already
546   contain societal biases, and in lateral thinking tasks, these biases could be amplified or perpetuated
547   through the generation of non-traditional solutions. To address these issues, we conduct a more
548   comprehensive analysis of the societal impacts of these capabilities and explore how to incorporate
549   stricter bias detection and correction mechanisms in model development and evaluation. Additionally,
550   ethical reviews are integrated into the evaluation framework of model applications to ensure that the
551   enhancement of lateral thinking capabilities does not lead to adverse societal consequences.

553   Reproducibility Statement
554
555   Part of source code is available in https://anonymous.4open.science/r/haiguitang-EFA7/. We will
556   open-source all data and code after being accepted. We make reproducibility statement on data
557   construction as follows:
558   Dataset Composition. We constructed a novel lateral thinking puzzles dataset (LTP) to evaluate
559   and enhance LLMs’ lateral thinking capabilities in problem-solving games. Each puzzle includes
560   a riddle with an unconventional solution, requiring creative, out-of-the-box thinking. We initially
561   collected 647 Chinese lateral thinking puzzles from websites like Huiwan and used GPT-4 to generate
562   additional puzzles with different semantics. These were carefully curated and expanded to maintain
563   cultural nuances, resulting in a final dataset of 642,600 puzzles. Each puzzle includes questions,
564   answers, and clues to guide LLMs towards the solution, evaluated for logical progression and safety.
565
      The comprehensive LTP dataset offers a robust framework for assessing and improving LLMs’ lateral
      thinking abilities.
566
567   Collection Process. We constructed the Lateral Thinking Puzzles (LTP) dataset to enhance and
568   evaluate LLMs’ lateral thinking capabilities. Initially, we gathered 647 Chinese puzzles from
569   websites like Huiwan. Using GPT-4, we generated additional puzzles with different semantics to
570   ensure originality. Each puzzle includes a riddle and an unconventional solution, requiring creative
571
      thinking beyond traditional reasoning. To preserve cultural nuances, we focused on expanding the
      dataset in Chinese. We used GPT-4 to create sequences of yes-or-no questions, answers, and clues
572
      for each puzzle, designed to guide LLMs toward the solution. Both the puzzles and the question
573
      sequences were rigorously evaluated to ensure logical consistency and quality. To ensure safety,
574   we filtered out puzzles with potentially harmful content. This meticulous process resulted in a
575   high-quality dataset of 642,600 puzzles, providing a robust tool for assessing and improving the
576   lateral thinking capabilities of LLMs in problem-solving games.
577
      Preprocessing/cleaning/labeling. To ensure the quality and safety of the LTP dataset, we imple-
578
      mented a thorough preprocessing, cleaning, and labeling process. Initially, we used GPT-4 to generate
579
      additional puzzles, ensuring they mirrored the style of the collected Chinese puzzles but with different
580   semantics. Each generated puzzle underwent rigorous evaluation to meet specific criteria, such as
581   logical consistency and cultural relevance. Puzzles scoring below a threshold were discarded. Next,
582   we created sequences of yes-or-no questions, answers, and clues for each puzzle, designed to guide
583   the LLMs incrementally towards the solution. These sequences were evaluated for logical progression
584   and accuracy, with inadequate sets being discarded. To maintain dataset integrity and minimize
585   risks, we used GPT-4 to automatically detect and flag potentially unsafe content, such as detailed
586   descriptions of violence or horror. Entries containing such content were removed. Manual rating
587   by volunteers further ensured the dataset’s quality, with puzzles scoring below a set threshold being
588
      excluded. The final dataset, comprising 642,600 puzzles, was thoroughly vetted for reliability and
      cultural nuance, ensuring it serves as a robust tool for enhancing LLMs’ lateral thinking capabilities.
