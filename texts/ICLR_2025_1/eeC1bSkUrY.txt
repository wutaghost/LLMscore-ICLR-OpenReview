009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Data preprocessing is a crucial step in machine learning that significantly influ-
012            ences model accuracy and performance. In Federated Learning (FL), where multi-
013            ple entities collaboratively train a model using decentralized data, the importance
014            of preprocessing is often overlooked. This is particularly true in Non-IID set-
015            tings, where clients hold heterogeneous datasets, requiring aggregated parameter
016            estimates to perform consistent data preprocessing. In this paper, we introduce
017            FedPS, a comprehensive suite of tools for federated data preprocessing. FedPS
018            leverages aggregated statistics, data sketching, and federated machine learning
019
               models to address the challenges posed by distributed and diverse datasets in FL.
               Additionally, we resolve key numerical issues in power transforms by improving
020
               numerical stability through log-space computations and constrained optimization.
021
               Our proposed Federated Power Transform algorithm, based on Brent’s method,
022            achieves superlinear convergence. Experimental results demonstrate the impact
023            of effective data preprocessing in federated learning, highlighting FedPS as a ver-
024            satile and robust solution compared to existing frameworks. The implementation
025            of FedPS is open-sourced.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Data preprocessing (Garcı́a et al., 2016) plays a crucial role in data mining and machine learn-
031   ing, ensuring that raw data—often fraught with missing values and inconsistencies—can be refined
032   into a form suitable for model training. Proper preprocessing not only enhances the robustness of
033   the training process but also significantly boosts model accuracy. However, in Federated Learning
034   (Kairouz et al., 2021), where data is stored locally across multiple clients and models are trained
035   collaboratively, centralizing the data for preprocessing is not feasible due to privacy concerns and
036   decentralized storage. This presents a unique challenge, as traditional preprocessing steps, which are
037   typically applied before data distribution in centralized simulations, cannot be directly implemented
038
      in a federated setting.
039   In federated environments, preprocessing requires the estimation of statistics from decentralized
040   data, which must then be aggregated on the server. While some statistics like min, max, sum, mean,
041   and variance are straightforward to compute with minimal communication overhead, others—such
042   as quantiles and frequent items—pose significant challenges due to computational and communica-
043   tion constraints.
044   This paper introduces FedPS, a comprehensive suite of tools designed to tackle these preprocessing
045   challenges in FL. Leveraging data sketching techniques (Cormode & Yi, 2020), which efficiently
046   summarize large datasets while retaining critical information, FedPS enables the computation of
047   both simple and complex statistics in a distributed manner. The concept of mergeability (Agar-
048   wal et al., 2013) further supports the federated learning setting by allowing sketches from different
049   clients to be combined efficiently. We also extend several key algorithms, such as Bayesian Linear
050
      Regression (Tipping, 2001), to both horizontal and vertical federated settings.
051   Additionally, our paper conducts an in-depth analysis of existing data preprocessors in the widely-
052   used Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) library, implementing federated versions while maintain-
053   ing the flexibility and functionality of the original modules. Unlike other federated learning libraries,
      such as FATE (Liu et al., 2021) and SecretFlow (The SecretFlow Authors, 2022), which offer lim-

      ited preprocessing options, our approach provides a full range of preprocessors with customizable
055   parameters, making it more versatile and powerful.
056
057   In tackling the numerical challenges of the power transform, previously identified by Marchand

081   2   M OTIVATION
082
083   Boosting Model Performance. Data preprocessing plays a pivotal role in enhancing the accuracy
084   and performance of machine learning models. While much attention has been directed towards opti-
085   mizing federated training algorithms, the significance of preprocessing data in a distributed manner
086   cannot be overlooked. In our experiments, we aim to shed light on this aspect by contrasting the test
087
      accuracy achieved using raw data against that obtained using preprocessed data. Through this com-
      parison, we seek to demonstrate the impact of federated data preprocessing on model performance,
088
      highlighting its potential to significantly boost accuracy.
089
090   Necessity of Federated Computation. In federated learning, data is distributed across multiple
091   clients, preprocessing steps need to be adapted to this decentralized nature. While a decentralized
092   strategy, with each client independently conducting preprocessing locally, may be suitable for sce-
093
      narios with independent and identically distributed (IID) data, it encounters difficulties in non-IID
      scenarios. In such cases, clients may possess varied data distributions, such as label distribution
094
      skew, where each client exclusively holds one type of labeled data. To illustrate the significance
095
      of federated data preprocessing, consider a scenario where two parties collaboratively train a hor-
096   izontally federated classification model. The initial data is linearly separable when pooled (see
097   Figure 1(a)). However, when each party’s data has distinct target categories (e.g., party A’s data
098   labeled 0 and party B’s data labeled 1), applying local scaling for zero mean and unit variance re-
099   sults in non-linearly separable data (see Figure 1(b)). Consequently, federated data preprocessing
100   becomes indispensable.
101
      Robust (Federated) Power Transform. Power transform is widely utilized across various domains,
102   including genomic studies (Zwiener et al., 2014) and geochemical data analysis (Howarth & Earle,
103   1979). Previous research (Marchand et al., 2022) has highlighted numerical challenges associated
104   with power transform, yet adequate solutions remain elusive. Their solution relies on exponential
105   search, resulting in linear convergence rates. In contrast, our approach involves a comprehensive
106   theoretical analysis of the underlying numerical instabilities and presents an effective solution. Fur-
107   thermore, we extend our methodology to federated settings and employ Brent’s method, known for
      its superlinear convergence property, thereby offering a more robust and efficient approach.

129   3.1      DATA P REPROCESSING
130
131   Data preprocessing involves a diverse set of methods for preparing data. Common steps encom-
132   pass feature scaling, encoding, discretization, missing value imputation, and various transformation
133   methods tailored to specific scenarios. Our focus in this paper is on the preprocessors presented in
134
      Scikit-learn1 (Pedregosa et al., 2011). The preprocessing workflow includes setting up the prepro-
      cessor with user-defined parameters, estimating the preprocessing parameters by calling the fit
135
      method, and finally, using the transform method to yield transformed data using the learned
136
      parameters. A review of the preprocessors in Scikit-learn is deferred to Appendix A.
137
138
      3.2      F EDERATED L EARNING
139
140
      Federated learning is a setting where data is decentralized, and immediate results are exchanged for
141   aggregation to achieve a common learning objective. Two typical data partition axes are horizontal
142   (example-partitioned) and vertical (feature-partitioned). In the horizontal setting, each client has
143   the same feature space, while in the vertical setting, they share the same ID. As data preprocessing
144   is often applied to each feature, most of the federated preprocessors presented in this paper are
145   designed for the horizontal setting.
146
147   3.3      AGGREGATING S TATISTICS
148
149   In federated learning, individual clients generate their local statistics and send to the server. Sub-
150   sequently, these statistics are collected and aggregated by a central server, enabling queries and
151   obtaining global estimations. A straightforward example is Min/Max, where each client computes
152
      its local minimum or maximum and transmits it to the server to obtain the global value. In the fol-
      lowing paragraphs, we will provide a brief overview of other statistics used in our implementations.
153
154   Sum, Mean, Variance. These statistics involve maintaining counts. Sum has one counter. Mean has
155   two counters: c for the sum of data and n for the number of examples. The mean value is calculated
156   as c/n. Variance introduces another counter, s, representing the sum of squared data. It’s computed
157   as s/n − (c/n)2 (Cormode & Yi, 2020). When merging counter-based statistics, simply add the
158
      corresponding counters.
159   Quantiles. Quantiles represent ordered statistics, associating values with specific ranks in sorted
160   data. For instance, the median corresponds to quantile 0.5. Obtaining exact quantiles requires
161
          1
              https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/preprocessing.html

      maintaining information proportionate to the full data size, leading to many quantile sketches be-
163   ing approximate. Two common types of errors associated with approximate quantile sketches are
164   additive error (Karnin et al., 2016) and multiplicative error (Cormode et al., 2023).
165
166
      Set Union, Frequent Items. The union operation is executed by the server after receiving local sets
      from all clients. This process primarily utilizes hash tables. The frequent items sketch (Anderson
167
      et al., 2017), also known as heavy hitters, aims to track the frequency of each item in the set.
168
169   DataSketches (The DataSketches Authors, 2023) is an open-source library that provides fast stream-
170   ing algorithms for big data. It includes sketches for quantiles, frequent items, and more. We leverage
171   these sketches from this library in our implementation of federated data preprocessing.

259   We categorize data preprocessors into distinct groups (scaling, encoding, transformation, imputa-
260   tion) to improve clarity regarding their functionalities. Within each category, we summarize the
261   formulation and required statistics for each module, as outlined in Table 1. Additionally, Table 4

267   scenario arises with Normalizer, particularly in vertical federated learning settings, where computing
268   the global norm of each sample is necessary. For l1 or l2 norms, the computation involves obtaining
269   the global sum of |x| or x2 for each sample (then taking the square root for l2 norm); for the max
      norm, simply compute the global maximum of |x|.

      Encoding. In federated learning, it’s crucial for all clients to agree on a unified encoding scheme to
271   ensure consistent encoding of the same categorical value into the same numeric value. Thus, encod-

304   5.1   U NDERSTANDING N UMERICAL I NSTABILITIES
305
306   Due to the convexity of negative log-likelihood functions, optimizing the parameter λ can be
307   achieved through direct minimization or root-finding algorithms. However, these methods involve
308   computing the logarithm of the variance of transformed data, see Equation 3 , which can lead to
309   numerical instabilities when directly squaring large values in the power function.
310   This numerical instability can affect the optimization, potentially resulting in suboptimal solutions.
311   To illustrate, we apply the exponential search algorithm2 to two seemingly ordinary datasets. As
312   depicted in Figure 4, the computed result does not match the true minimum.
313
      Additionally, the power transform itself presents a secondary challenge, as depicted in Figure 4.
314
      Employing the optimal λ for transformation may result in numerical overflow beyond the precision
315   limit. For instance, extreme values such as 2009.0104 ≈ 3.2 × 10343 and 0.1−361 = 10361 can
316   occur. In such cases, users may encounter difficulties analyzing the transformed data or rescaling
317   the Gaussianized data to achieve zero mean and unit variance.
318
      While increasing precision, may partially mitigate these issues, it does not provide a comprehensive
319
      solution. Moreover, even seemingly ordinary data, or adversarial data, can exceed double-precision
320
      limits, and many mathematical libraries do not support quad-precision or higher due to efficiency
321   considerations.

      to the maximum value within the floating-point precision of the input data, typically represented
410
      as ymax ≈ 10308 (double-precision). Additionally, users have the flexibility to manually set these
411
      bounds, enabling customization based on specific requirements. For instance, setting the bound to
412   infinity can yield optimal unconstrained results, while setting it to a reasonable value prevents the
413   transformed data from becoming excessively large.

      A     P REPROCESSORS IN Scikit-learn
703
704
      A.1   S CALING
705
706   Scaling each feature of the data is a common preprocessing step before training machine learning
707   models, referred to as normalization or standardization, usually involving a linear transformation.
708   Different scalers employ various strategies to transform data into predefined ranges. MaxAbsScaler
709   ensures that the maximal absolute value equals 1, MinMaxScaler confines data between a given
710   minimum and maximum value, and StandardScaler ensures that transformed data have a zero mean
711   and unit variance. However, these methods are sensitive to outliers, as the scaling factor depends on
712
      them. For more robust scaling, RobustScaler transforms data into a preset quantile range, typically
      quantiles 0.25 to 0.75 of the data, making it less susceptible to outliers. Notably, Normalizer applies
713
      scaling to each data sample instead of each feature, ensuring individual samples have a unit norm,
714
      such as l1 (absolute), l2 (euclidean), and max (infinity) norms.
715
716
      A.2   E NCODING
717
718   Data may contain features represented using strings, necessitating encoding into numeric values.
719   OneHotEncoder encodes them as a one-hot array, and OrdinalEncoder uses an ordinal encoding
720   scheme. They can also ignore infrequent items below a preset threshold of frequency and limit the
721   maximum number of output categories. An alternative approach is the hash trick (Weinberger et al.,
722   2009), exemplified by FeatureHasher, which computes encoding representation based on hash func-
723   tions. TargetEncoder (Micci-Barreca, 2001) utilizes target mean and the target mean conditioned
724   on the categorical value for encoding, often combined with cross-validation (CV) techniques or ad-
725   ditional smoothing parameters to prevent overfitting due to incorporating target information. The
726
      default smoothing parameter is set by empirical Bayes estimation, blending the global target vari-
      ance and the target variance conditioned on the category value. In supervised learning, label encod-
727
      ing might be necessary if labels are strings, addressed by LabelBinarizer for one-vs-all binarization,
728
      particularly useful in multiclass classification, and MultiLabelBinarizer in multilabel learning, trans-
729   forming targets into a multilabel format. LabelEncoder encodes target labels into ordinal numbers,
730   typically used in classification tasks.
731
732
      A.3   T RANSFORMATION
733
734   Feature transformation is another type of data preprocessing that applies a certain function, typically
735   non-linear, to the features. FunctionTransformer applies a user-defined function to the data, making
736   it useful for tasks like log transformation. PowerTransformer is a parametric method that maps data
737   into a Gaussian distribution, supporting both Box-Cox and Yeo-Johnson transformations. After-
738   ward, the user has the option to apply StandardScaler to the transformed data. QuantileTransformer
739   is a non-parametric method capable of transforming arbitrary data into Gaussian or Uniform dis-
740   tributions. It estimates the cumulative probability distribution function, using quantiles, then maps
741
      the data to desired output distributions. SplineTransformer generates univariate B-spline (de Boor,
      1978) bases for each feature, particularly useful in time-related feature engineering. It requires
742
      setting uniformly distributed knots between the min and max values or along the quantiles.
743
744
745
      A.4   D ISCRETIZATION
746
      For continuous features, discretization provides a way to transform them into discrete values, also
747   known as quantization or binning. While it may result in a loss of information, it simplifies the data,
748   making it easier to use and understand. Binarizer uses a threshold to binarize the data. In contrast,
749   KBinsDiscretizer can transform continuous data into k bins using various strategies. It can generate
750   intervals with equal width for each bin or ensure an equal frequency of data samples in each bin.
751   Alternatively, it can employ k-means, an unsupervised learning algorithm, to generate k clusters.
752
753   A.5   I MPUTATION
754
755   Missing values are prevalent in real-world data for various reasons, posing a challenge for most
      machine learning algorithms. One common strategy is to discard entire rows or columns containing


                                                        14
          Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
          missing values, but this approach may introduce bias and reduce the availability of data. Alterna-
757       tively, imputation strategies, such as SimpleImputer, offer a univariate method to fill missing values
758       with the mean, median, mode (most frequent item) of the respective feature, or a constant value.
759       In contrast, IterativeImputer (Buck, 1960; Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), a multivariate
760       imputation strategy, models missing values as a function of other features. It selects a specific fea-
761       ture column as the target and utilizes other features as inputs to fit a regression model, subsequently
762       using this model to predict the missing values. Another method, KNNImputer (Troyanskaya et al.,
763       2001), employs a weighted average of k-nearest neighbors for imputation.

       7       Compute the global log-variance ln σψ(λc ,x)
1212
1213   8     Compute the negative log-likelihood − ln L(λc , x)
1214   9     Continue Brent’s method
1215   10 until Convergence or reach the max iteration;

1216      Output: The constrained optimal λ∗
1217
1218       Compared to the exponential search used in Marchand et al. (2022), which exhibits a linear conver-
1219       gence rate, our proposed method achieves superlinear convergence, a key benefit inherent to Brent’s
1220       methods.
