009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vector retrieval algorithms are essential for semantic queries within the rapidly
012            evolving landscape of Large Language Models (LLMs). The ability to retrieve
013            vectors that satisfy both similarity and diversity criteria substantially enhances the
014            performance of LLMs. Although Maximal Marginal Relevance (MMR) is widely
015            employed in retrieval scenarios requiring relevance and diversity, variations in
016            the parameter λ lead to fluctuations that complicate the optimization trajectory
017            in vector spaces. This obscures the direction of improvement and highlights the
018
               lack of a robust theoretical analysis regarding similarity and diversity constraints in
               retrieval processes. To address these challenges, this paper introduces a novel ap-
019
               proach that characterizes both constraints through the relationship between the sum
020
               vector and the query vector. The proximity of these vectors ensures the similarity
021            constraint, while requiring individual vectors within the sum vector to diverge in
022            their alignment with the query vector satisfies the diversity constraint. We first
023            formulate a new combinatorial optimization problem, selecting k vectors from a
024            candidate set such that their sum vector maximally aligns with the query vector, and
025            demonstrate that this problem is NP-complete. This result underscores the inherent
026            difficulty of simultaneously achieving similarity and diversity in vector retrieval,
027            thereby providing a theoretical foundation for future research. Subsequently, we
028            present the heuristic algorithm Vectors Retrieval with Similarity and Diversity,
029
               VRSD, which features a clear optimization objective and eliminates the need for
               preset parameters. VRSD also achieves a modest reduction in time complexity
030
               compared to MMR. Empirical validation confirms that VRSD significantly outper-
031
               forms MMR across various datasets, while also demonstrating that the sum vector
032            effectively captures both diversity and similarity simultaneously. The data and code
033            are available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/VRSD-CF9D.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Vector retrieval algorithms are crucial for semantic queries and have become increasingly integral
039   to the deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs). Effective interaction with LLMs frequently
040   necessitates the provision of relevant or similar examples to elicit enhanced responses (Liu et al.,
041   2022). The introduction of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) has notably advanced the
042   capabilities in knowledge-intensive tasks (Lewis et al., 2020), underscoring the growing importance
043   of retrieval methods. Empirical evidence suggests that employing the BM25 algorithm to select
044   examples from the training set markedly improves LLMs performance over random selection (Liu
045
      et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). Moreover, leveraging existing text embedding models for example
      retrieval often surpasses BM25, particularly in specific contexts (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019; Wang
046
      et al., 2022). And the advent of Dense Retrieval, which employs dense vectors for semantic matching
047
      in latent spaces (Chen et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2019), represents a evolution over traditional sparse
048   retrieval methods like BM25 by utilizing the robust modeling capabilities of pre-trained language
049   models to learn relevance functions (Devlin et al., 2019). Innovations such as the applying the dual
050   encoder framework (Karpukhin et al., 2020) and dynamic listwise distillation (Ren et al., 2021) have
051   further refined the effectiveness of dense retrieval techniques. Subsequent enhancements in semantic
052   parsing and in-context learning (Pasupat et al., 2021), facilitated by feedback from LLMs (Rubin
053   et al., 2022), have enabled more precise example selection and improved answer accuracy. Despite
      ongoing advancements in retrieval methods, the broadening application scope of LLMs necessitates


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      retrieval approaches that balance relevance with diversity—specifically, a relevance-focused diversity
055   rather than an unrestricted diversity. Additionally, the RAG framework’s ability to augment the
056   LLMs’ external data access also underscores the need for simple yet efficient algorithms that can
057   streamline the retrieval process.
058
      Considering the balance between similarity and diversity, the Maximal Marginal Relevance (MMR)
059
      (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998) is an effective algorithm and has been widely applied in vector
060
      retrieval practices. Aiming to achieve an optimal balance, MMR incorporates a parameter, λ, which
061   adjusts the weight of relevance and diversity by varying its value. Nevertheless, this method is not
062   always effective; in different scenarios, λ needs to take different values, which cannot be known in
063   advance. Recent research (Rubin et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) has also explored using LLMs to
064   enhance retrieval results, while also suggests considering the selection of a set of examples from a
065   combinatorial optimization perspective, rather than selecting examples one by one, as the in-context
066   examples can influence each other. In light of this, we propose using the sum vector to characterize
067   both similarity and diversity in vector retrieval. Simply put, this involves maximizing the similarity
068   between the sum vector of the selected vectors and the query vector, and maximizing the similarity
069
      of the sum vector to the query vector imposes a similarity constraint. At the same time, from a
      geometric perspective, the requirement for the sum vector to be similar to the query vector means that
070
      the selected vectors approach the query vector from different directions, thus imposing a diversity
071
      constraint. Additionally, the idea of considering the similarity between the sum vector and the query
072   vector is analogous to the famous finding in word2vec (king - man + woman = queen) (Mikolov et al.,
073   2013), as both involve obtaining complex semantic similarities through simple vector arithmetic.
074   Therefore, using the sum vector to characterize similarity and diversity constraints not only considers
075   similarity while reducing redundancy but also enhances the complementarity among retrieval results.
076
      Consequently, we define a new combinatorial optimization problem: selecting several vectors from a
077
      set of candidate vectors such that the similarity between the sum vector of the selected vectors and
078   the query vector is maximized. However, contrary to its intuitive and straightforward appearance, this
079   is a highly challenging problem. We prove that this problem is NP-complete by reducing the subset
080   sum problem to it, revealing theoretically that simultaneously pursuing similarity and diversity in
081   vector retrieval is extremely difficult. This novel combinatorial optimization problem, of independent
082   theoretical interest, establishes a solid theoretical foundation for future research. Subsequently, we
083   present a heuristic algorithm to solve the proposed problem. This algorithm has a clear optimization
084   objective, requires no preset parameters, and has a slightly lower time complexity than the MMR
085   algorithm. Our experimental studies also demonstrate that the new algorithm significantly outperforms
086
      the MMR algorithm across various datasets. Additionally, given that similarity measures in vector
      retrieval typically include cosine similarity, inner product distance, and Euclidean distance, and
087
      considering that vectors in LLM applications are usually normalized, the results obtained using these
088
      measures in vector retrieval are consistent. Consequently, the discussion on vector similarity in this
089   paper uses cosine similarity. In summary, our work makes the following contributions:
090
091          • We propose using the sum vector to characterize similarity and diversity constraints in vector
092            retrieval. We formulate a novel optimization problem where we seek to select several vectors
093            from a set of candidates such that the similarity between the sum vector of the selected
094            vectors and the query vector is maximized.
095          • We demonstrate that our optimization problem is NP-complete, theoretically revealing the
096            extreme difficulty of simultaneously pursuing similarity and diversity in vector retrieval.
097          • For the NP-complete combinatorial optimization problem we propose, we provide a heuristic
098            algorithm, VRSD. We experimentally study our algorithm on several datasets, and our results
099            show that our algorithm significantly outperforms the classic MMR algorithm.

      2.2   S UM V ECTOR FOR R ETRIEVAL
163
164   To address the challenges associated with MMR, we propose utilizing the sum vector of selected
165   vectors to simultaneously capture both similarity and diversity. Figure.1 provides an intuitive
166   geometric illustration of vector diversity and similarity. In practical applications, similarity does not
167   necessarily imply strict alignment between vectors; rather, a small angle between vectors suffices
168   to indicate similarity. A smaller angle between two vectors denotes a higher degree of similarity,
169   while a larger angle signifies lower similarity. In the context of an embedded query, retrieval typically
170
      involves ranking candidate vectors based on the angles they form with the query vector, from smallest
      to largest, and selecting them according to the required quantity, for instance, by directly applying
171
      cosine similarity. However, if the selected batch of vectors for a query includes vectors with both
172
      small and large angles relative to the query vector, the batch can be considered to satisfy the diversity
173   requirement, as it incorporates vectors that are less directly relevant. Consequently, the selected
174   vectors do not merely satisfy the criterion of having the smallest possible angles with the query
175   vector but instead exhibit a range of angles. Nevertheless, diversity in vector retrieval should be
176   grounded in similarity and should not entirely deviate from it. In other words, diversity should not be
177   achieved by deliberately selecting vectors that are entirely different in angle from the query vector.
178   The selected examples should exhibit substantial differences while satisfying the similarity constraint,
179   thereby complementing each other and fulfilling both similarity and diversity requirements. And
180   Sum vector can achieve this goal well. The sum vector vector of the selected vectors is required to
181
      closely approximate the query vector, thereby effectively satisfying the similarity constraint. While
      from a geometric perspective, the sum vector’s similarity to the query vector implies that the selected
182
      vectors approach the query vector from different directions, indicating significant differences among
183
      the selected vectors while maintaining complementarity, which reflects diversity. Therefore, the sum
184   vector effectively captures both diversity and similarity.

188
189   3.1   P ROBLEM DEFINITION AND COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
190
191   To address the problem of selecting a subset of vectors from a set of candidate vectors that satisfy
192
      both similarity and diversity requirements, we refer to the MMR algorithm and several LLM-based
      algorithms, typically considering the following premises: Firstly, the candidate vectors are identified
193
      from the entire set of vectors (size = N ) using cosine similarity metrics, resulting in a subset of
194
      vectors (size = n). Consequently, this set of candidate vectors inherently exhibits a relative high
195   degree of similarity to the query vector. Secondly, within these n candidate vectors, the vector most
196   similar to the query vector is typically selected first, as is the case with the MMR algorithm and
197   others. This approach is favored because, in applications such as in-context learning with LLMs,
198   examples with the highest similarity to the query are generally the most helpful.
199
      As previously mentioned, while algorithms like MMR are widely applied in practice, these studies
200
      often lack a robust and reliable theoretical model. In other words, many approaches employ heuristic
201   strategies or machine learning methods to arrive at a solution without providing a rigorous formal
202   description and analysis of similarity and diversity from a theoretical perspective. Therefore, based
203   on the aforementioned premises, we propose using the sum vector to characterize both similarity and
204   diversity in vector retrieval. The definition of the sum vector is as follows:
205   Definition 1. The Sum Vector: Given k vectors d1 , d2 , ..., dk , the sum vector d is the sum of these k
206   vectors.
207
208   Specifically, we aim to maximize the similarity between the sum vector of the selected k vectors
209   and the query vector. On one hand, maximizing the similarity of the sum vector to the query vector
210   imposes a similarity constraint. On the other hand, from a geometric perspective, ensuring the
211   sum vector is similar to the query vector means that the selected vectors approach the query vector
212   from different directions, thus imposing a diversity constraint. Therefore, using the sum vector to
213
      characterize similarity and diversity allows us to model complex semantic similarity and diversity
      through simple vector addition operations. Next, we define the problem of vectors retrieval as follows:
214
215   Definition 2. The problem of Vectors Retrieval with Similarity and Diversity (VRSD): Given a query
      vector q and a set of candidate vectors R = {d0 , d1 , ..., dn−1 } (where d0 is the vector with the

      highest similarity to query vertor q), d0 is selected first because of its highest similarity. Then, how
217   to select k − 1 vectors (d′1 , d′2 , ..., d′k−1 ) from the remaining vectors such that the cosine similarity
218   between the sum vector d = d0 + d′1 + d′2 + ... + d′k−1 and q is maximized.
219
220   The vector d0 , characterized by its maximal similarity to the query vector q, establishes an initial
221   constraint on similarity. The ensuing optimization objective strives to maximize the cosine similarity
222   between the sum vector of all selected vectors and q. This process necessitates the selection of
223   vectors that not only converge towards q from diverse dimensions but also exhibit significant diversity
224   and complementarity. However, upon further examination of above problem, we find that it is
225
      an NP-complete problem. Below, we provide a theoretical proof. Since the vector d0 , with the
      highest similarity, is initially selected, the subsequent selection of k − 1 vectors must have the
226
      maximum cosine similarity with q − d0 . That is, maximizing the similarity between sum vector
227
      d = d0 + d′1 + d′2 + ... + d′k−1 (d′1 , d′2 , ..., d′k−1 represents the k − 1 vectors selected subsequently)
228
      and q, is equivalent to maximizing the similarity between d′ = d′1 + d′2 + ... + d′k−1 and q − d0 . To
229
      this end, we define a decision problem, namely:
230
231
      Definition 3. The decision problem of vectors retrieval: Given a set of candidate vectors R and a
      query vector q, can k vectors be selected from R such that the cosine similarity between the sum
232
      vector of these k vectors and the query vector q equals 1? We denote instances of this vectors retrieval
233
      problem as (R, q, k).
234
235   Next, we will prove this decision problem is NP-complete. For the sake of concise proof, we further
236   restrict the components of vectors to integers. The proof strategy is to reduce the subset sum problem
237   Cormen et al. (2009) to this decision problem.
238   Definition 4. The subset sum problem: Given an integer set T and another integer t, does there exist
239   a non-empty subset whose sum of elements equals t? We denote instances of the subset sum problem
240   as (T, t).
241
242   For the convenience of proof, we also need to define a modified version of the subset sum problem,
243   called the k-subset sum problem.
244   Definition 5. k-subset sum problem: Given an integer set T and another integer t, does there exist a
245   non-empty subset of size k (i.e., the cardinality of the subset is k), whose sum of elements equals t?
246   We denote instances of the k-subset sum problem as (T, t, k).
247   Lemma 1. The k-subset sum problem is NP-complete.
248
249   Proof. We reduce the subset sum problem(Def.4) to the k-subset sum problem(Def.5) .
250
      1. Clearly, the k-subset sum problem is polynomial-time verifiable.
251
252   2. Reducing the subset sum problem to the k-subset sum problem.
253
      For any instance of the subset sum problem (T, t), we can transform it into |T | instances of the
254   k-subset sum problem, i.e., (T, t, 1), (T, t, 2), . . . , (T, t, |T |). If any of these |T | instances of the
255   k-subset sum problem has a yes answer, then the answer to the subset sum problem is yes. If all
256   answers to these |T | instances of the k-subset sum problem are no, then the answer to the subset sum
257   problem is also no. Therefore, if the k-subset sum problem can be solved in polynomial time, then
258   the subset sum problem can also be solved in polynomial time. Hence, the k-subset sum problem is
259   NP-complete.
260
261   Now it is time to prove the NP-completeness of the decision problem of vectors retrieval.
262   Theorem 1. The decision problem of vectors retrieval is NP-complete.
263
264   Proof. We reduce the k-subset sum problem(Def.5) to the decision problem of vectors re-
265   trieval(Def.3).

      vector and the query vector q equals 1. This verification can be done in polynomial time.

290
      heuristic methods to derive feasible solutions. Specifically, given a set of candidate vectors with high
291
      similarity, the objective is to select k vectors that maximize the cosine similarity between the sum
292   vector of the k selected vectors and the query vector. We propose a new algorithm denoted as Vectors
293   Retrieval with Similarity and Diversity (VRSD). VRSD initially selects the vector most similar to the
294   query vector and then iteratively selects additional vectors from the remaining candidates. In each

      4.1     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
334
335   We evaluated the VRSD algorithm using three publicly available datasets of different categories and
336   compared the VRSD with the MMR algorithm when the values of λ are 0, 0.5, and 1 respectively :
337
338           • ARC-DA (Bhakthavatsalam et al., 2021): A dataset of direct-answer science questions
339             derived from the ARC multiple-choice question. Each example contains a question and
340             multiple answers.
341           • OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018): A dataset of multiple-choice science questions,
342             which probe the understanding of science facts and the application of these facts to novel
343             situations. Each example contains a question, multiple choices, and an answer.
344           • Puzzle (Liu et al., 2023): A question answering dataset. These questions belong to lateral
345             thinking puzzle. Each example contains a question and an answer.
346
347   For each item in each datasets, we concatenate the question part with its corresponding answer,
348   subsequently selecting 20% of these concatenated items to form the test set, wherein the question
349   parts are isolated. Items designated for the test set are excluded from the original dataset for
350
      subsequent experiments, where same amount of examples are retrieved for each test question.
351   As extensively discussed in Section 2, sum vector can well capture the similarity and diversity
352   simultaneously. Consequently, retrieval quality is assessed by aggregating all vectors retrieved using
353   either VRSD or MMR into a sum vector—denoted as dVRSD and dMMR —which reflects the vectorial
354   direction from which the examples approach the query vector q. We compute the cosine similarity
355   between the sum vectors and the query vector as cos(dVRSD , q) and cos(dMMR , q). The comparison
356
      includes counting percentage where cos(dVRSD , q) exceeds cos(dMMR , q), termed as the VRSD win.
      rate, and calculating the maximum difference (Max-diff) between these cosine similarities for all
357
      queries in each test set. Additionally, we compute the mean cosine similarity values (Mean) for these
358
      vectors under each methods, respectively. Such metrics are instrumental in elucidating the algorithms’
359   capacity to balance similarity and diversity. The comparison was conducted on one open source model
360   all-mpnet-base-v2 and two close source models text-embedding-3-small&text-embedding-ada-002.
361
362
      The author of MMR conducted a manual evaluation of the retrieved examples, noting that ’users were
      asked to extract information from documents without being informed about the order in which the
363
      documents were presented—only that either "method R" or "method S" was applied.’ (Carbonell &
364
      Goldstein, 1998). Manual evaluation, however, is time-consuming and labor-intensive. We propose
365   utilizing the sum vector for evaluation, which offers a more direct and efficient approach. As discussed
366   earlier, the sum vector can simultaneously account for both diversity and similarity.

441
442   Next, we perform a comprehensive analysis of BM25 and Cosine Similarity across all datasets to
443   evaluate their performance in terms of diversity and similarity in retrieval. As BM25 and Cosine
444   Similarity are classical distance measurement methods, it is crucial to assess their performance in
445   relation to our sum vector; this enables us to understand their behavior within the context of the sum
446
      vector and justifies their inclusion in our comparison. While both BM25 and Cosine Similarity are
      rank-based retrieval approaches, VRSD distinguishes itself by simultaneously capturing both diversity
447
      and similarity, retrieving all relevant examples in one step and considering the potential connections
448
      between embedded vectors. For consistency, we select the same number of top-ranked examples for
449   evaluation, similar to previous settings. As in prior experiments, we utilize the all-mpnet-base-v2
450   model for retrieval before executing downstream tasks.
451
452
      Table.3 illustrates a notable performance decline when employing BM25 or direct cosine similarity for
      retrieval in terms of diversity and similarity, thereby confirming the superiority of VRSD. Furthermore,
453
      as reflected in the Mean metric, the retrieval performance follows the order: VRSD > Cosine
454
      Similarity > BM25. Similarly, the responses from both LLMs demonstrate the same downward
455   trend. This finding suggests that VRSD more effectively balances diversity and relevance in the
456   retrieved examples, ultimately enhancing the quality of LLM-generated responses. These insights
457   from downstream tasks provide further evidence supporting our earlier assertion that the sum vector
458   implicitly captures the diversity of the set while also accounting for similarity.

461   5       R ELATED W ORK
462
463   Retrieval methods in Large Language Models (LLMs) have gained traction, particularly due to
464   their pivotal role in open-domain question answering, evidenced by seminal contributions in the
465   field (Chen et al., 2017; Guu et al., 2020; Khattab & Zaharia, 2020; Qu et al., 2021; Izacard &
466
      Grave, 2021). The introduction of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) further underscored
      the significance of these methods across knowledge-intensive tasks (Lewis et al., 2020), notably
467
      enhancing generation capabilities for open-domain queries (Mao et al., 2021). This spurred the
468
      adoption of techniques such as K-Nearest-Neighbor (KNN) in diverse applications, ranging from the
469   customization of multilingual models in machine translation (Khandelwal et al., 2020) to improving
470   the prediction of rare patterns in LLMs (Khandelwal et al., 2019; Alon et al., 2022). Continued
471   advancements in retrieval techniques have focused on identifying highly informative examples to
472   augment in-context learning, thereby enabling LLM-based systems to achieve significant performance
473   improvements with minimal examples (Brown et al., 2020). Early on, traditional sparse retrieval
474   methods like BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009)—an extension of TF-IDF—were utilized to refine
475   in-context learning (Liu et al., 2022). Subsequently, the integration of LLMs’ intrinsic capabilities
476   (Shin et al., 2021) and Sentence-BERT (SBERT) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) facilitated the retrieval
477
      of highly pertinent examples for prompt integration. The advent of dense retrievers signified a
      methodological enhancement in retrieval from a machine learning perspective, and the incorporation
478
      of feedback signals with contrastive learning has yielded more effective retrieval systems (Rubin et al.,
479
      2022; Wang et al., 2023). Recent innovations like UPRISE (Cheng et al., 2023) and PRAC (Nie et al.,
480   2023) have further optimized the performance of in-context learning by retrieving demonstrations
481   directly from training data. Despite these advances, most retrieval methods still treat each candidate
482   independently, which can lead to suboptimal outcomes due to the interaction effects among in-
483   context examples, resulting in a lack of diversity. Besides, given the expanding applications of
484   LLMs, diversity becomes increasingly crucial, incorporating a diverse range of examples enriches
485   LLMs’ learning processes, facilitating more innovative and robust responses, especially for complex
      open-ended questions. Most existing methods in this area rely on MMR; however, the necessity for


                                                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      continuous adjustment to determine the optimal value of λ is inefficient in practical applications
487   (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998; Deselaers et al., 2009; Ye et al., 2023). In this work, we aim to retrieve
488   examples from the perspective of combinatorial optimization, supported by strict theoretical analysis.
489   Furthermore, the proposed sum vector has been demonstrated to effectively capture both diversity
490   and similarity, contributing to the successful execution of downstream tasks.

      6    C ONCLUSIONS
493
494
      In this work, considering the complexity of parameter adjustment in MMR, we aim to improve how
495   LLMs retrieve similar yet diverse examples by introducing a novel approach that jointly models
496   both constraints through the relationship between the sum vector and the query vector. This method
497   ensures that individual vectors within the sum diverge from the query vector, thereby satisfying
498   the diversity constraint. We demonstrate that this problem is NP-complete and propose the VRSD
499   algorithm, which not only surpasses MMR in retrieval performance but also enhances the execution
500   of downstream tasks. Additionally, we introduce the sum vector, which is proven to effectively
501   capture both diversity and similarity simultaneously, and serves as a metric to measure the balance
502   between these two aspects, addressing an existing gap. Our work highlights the inherent challenges of
503
      achieving both similarity and diversity in vector retrieval, establishing a solid theoretical foundation
      for future research. The proposed combinatorial optimization problem holds independent value from
504
      both theoretical and practical perspectives, suggesting that further exploration and refinement of the
505
      heuristic algorithm would be a fruitful direction for future inquiry.
