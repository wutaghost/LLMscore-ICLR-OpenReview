009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Quantifying robustness in a single measure for the purposes of model selection,
012            development of adversarial training methods, and anticipating trends has so far
013            been elusive. The simplest metric to consider is the number of trainable parame-
014            ters in a model but this has previously been shown to be insufficient at explaining
015            robustness properties. A variety of other metrics, such as ones based on bound-
016            ary thickness and gradient flatness have been proposed but have been shown to be
017            inadequate proxies for robustness.
018            In this work, we investigate the relationship between a model’s effective dimen-
019            sionality, which can be thought of as model complexity, and its robustness proper-
020            ties. We run experiments on commercial-scale models that are often used in real-
021            world environments such as YOLO and ResNet. We reveal a near-linear inverse
022            relationship between effective dimensionality and adversarial robustness, that is
023
               models with a lower dimensionality exhibit better robustness. We investigate the
               effect of a variety of adversarial training methods on effective dimensionality and
024
               find the same inverse linear relationship present, suggesting that effective dimen-
025
               sionality can serve as a useful criterion for model selection and robustness eval-
026            uation, providing a more nuanced and effective metric than parameter count or
027            previously-tested measures.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Robustness to adversarial perturbations has been a desired but often ill-achieved property of mod-
033
      ern neural networks, that can otherwise be increasingly found in commercial real-world applica-
034
      tions (Tocchetti et al., 2022). There has been a myriad of techniques proposed to improve robust-
035   ness, ranging from preproccessor defenses (Diallo & Patras, 2024) (that have since been shown to
036   be flawed by Carlini (2024)) to various forms of adversarial training methods. Adversarial training,
037   since its introduction by Madry et al. (2019), has become one of the most common methods of de-
038   fending against adversarial examples owing to its ease of implementation and deployment. It was
039   however shown recently that adversarial training is far from a catch-all approach and suffers from
040   overfitting (Rice et al., 2020).
041   A variety of different measures have been proposed to quantify the inherent robustness of trained
042   models, but they were mainly found to be limited in their applicability Kim et al. (2023). We
043   investigate the use of effective dimensionality as a measure for adversarial robustness. This measure
044   was originally proposed by MacKay (1992) and then expanded to deep neural networks by Maddox
045   et al. (2020). We use effective dimensionality as a metric of model complexity – it is a joint measure
046   of both the expressivity of the model’s architecture and the inherent properties of the dataset the
047   model was trained on. This was used as it was found that parameter count alone does not provide a
048   sufficient metric to represent the joint ‘complexity’ of a model and it’s underlying trained data.
049   We conduct experiments across a wide range of model architectures, such as ResNet (He et al.,
050   2015), ShuffleNet (Zhang et al., 2017), and YOLO (Varghese & M., 2024), as well as over CIFAR-
051   10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. Our key research contributions are as follows:
052
053         1. We present a large-scale investigation into the effective dimensionality of classification
               models, most of which are often used in production.

            2. We find a near-linear negatively-correlated relationship between a model’s robustness to
055            adversarial examples and its effective dimensionality. Namely, we find that models with a

            3. We conduct an empirical investigation into the effect of adversarial training techniques on

064   2.1   A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
065
      Threat Model For a given model fθ : X → RC parameterised by θ, an input x ∈ X and label
066
      y =argmax fθ (x) ∈ {0, · · · , C}, we consider a perturbation δ to be adversarial if
067
068                                            fθ (x + δ) ̸=argmax y                                     (1)
069
070   Working from established literature such as Croce et al. (2021), we assume a white-box threat model
071   for the attacker (i.e. full knowledge of both f and θ), and measure adversarial robustness via robust
072   accuracy under attack for the l∞ norm. Details regarding the exact metrics used are discussed
073   in Section 3.
074   Adversarial Example Generation Over the years, various strategies have been developed to assess
075   and challenge the robustness of models. Among the most notable are Projected Gradient Descent
076   (PGD) (Madry et al., 2019), AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020b), and Gaussian noise (Ford et al.,
077   2019).
078
      PGD, introduced by Madry et al. (2019), is one of the most widely studied adversarial example
079   generation methods due to its effectiveness and simplicity. PGD is an iterative method that builds on
080   the idea of applying small, constrained gradient-based perturbations to an input in order to maximize
081   the model’s loss function. It is an iterative method, with the perturbation being projected back into a
082   predefined bound around the original input to ensure the perturbation stays within acceptable limits.
083   PGD is considered one of the strongest first-order methods and serves as the basis for adversarial
084   training, as discussed later.
085   AutoAttack, proposed by Croce & Hein (2020b), introduces a suite of strong adversarial attacks. Au-
086   toAttack automates the process of adversarial example generation generation and evaluation by com-
087   bining four different attacks, including PGD and Fast Adaptive Boundary (Croce & Hein, 2020a).
088   This ensemble approach ensures comprehensive robustness evaluation by removing potential biases
089   inherent in single-attack methods. It is designed to be parameter-free and eliminates the need for
090   tuning, providing more consistent and robust adversarial performance testing across a variety of
091   neural network architectures.
092   Ford et al. (2019) explored the use of simple Gaussian noise as a baseline adversarial perturbation.
093   While Gaussian noise is not a crafted example in the same sense as PGD or AutoAttack, it serves as
094   a valuable point of comparison for more sophisticated methods. Though less effective than gradient-
095   based attacks in fooling models, Gaussian noise highlights a model’s inherent robustness to random
096   variations and provides insights into the stability of learned representations. Ford et al. (2019)
097   demonstrated that while Gaussian noise is often ineffective in generating adversarial examples, it
098   remains a useful tool for probing the overall robustness of models, particularly in scenarios where
099
      adversarial examples are not deliberately targeted.
100   Adversarial Training We explore the effects of adversarial training and other derivative methods.
101   This robust training technique was first formulated by Madry et al. (2019), utilizing the following
102   min-max optimization with notation similar to the one used above:
103
                                      minf E(x,y)∼D [maxδ l(f (x + δ), y)]                               (2)
104
105   The inner maximization is often approximated by repeated iterations of PGD, which greatly in-
106   creases computation costs. Adversarial training is considered to be the state-of-the-art method for
107   ensuring robustness, with recent papers greatly reducing involved costs by re-using gradient infor-
      mation during training (Shafahi et al., 2019).

      2.2   ROBUSTNESS M EASURES
109
110   Several measures have been proposed to quantify model robustness. Boundary thickness, introduced
111   by Yang et al. (2021), focuses on exploring the relationship between the distance between decision
112   boundaries and data points. It was found that models with thicker boundaries tend to exhibit greater
113   robustness, as adversarial perturbations are less likely to cross decision boundaries with small per-
114   turbations. Flatness-based measures, studied by Stutz et al. (2021), focus on the curvature of the loss
115   surface around the input data. Models with flatter loss landscapes are thought to generalize better
116
      and resist adversarial perturbations. Yang et al. (2020) proposed the use of local Lipschitzness to
      assess robustness, relating to the smoothness of the learned decision function. It was found that
117
      a lower local Lipschitz constant implies greater robustness, as small perturbations induce minimal
118
      output changes. However, calculating the local Lipschitz constants directly is often computationally-
119   intractable, and estimators need to be used which are in themselves computationally expensive on
120   large models (Fazlyab et al., 2023).
121
122
      Despite these advances, Kim et al. (2023) demonstrated that none of these metrics provide a com-
      prehensive measure of robustness. Each captures only specific aspects, leaving room for the devel-
123
      opment of more holistic and effective metrics.
124
125
      2.3   E FFECTIVE D IMENSIONALITY AS A C OMPLEXITY M ETRIC
126
127
128
129
      We work under the intuition that the robustness properties of a model are related to it’s complex-
      ity. The issue arrises with trying to concretely define ‘complexity’ when it comes to neural net-
130
      works. A simple measure would be model size in terms of number of trainable parameters or
131
      amount of compute required, but this has recently been shown to only have a small correlation
132   to robustness (Debenedetti et al., 2023). Grant & Wu (2022) investigated the relationship between
133   generalization and generalized degrees-of-freedom. We investigated this metric, but found that the
134   method described in the paper to be generally computationally intractable and not converge for larger
135   models.
136
      Hence, we decided to explore the relationship between effective dimensionality, as defined by Mad-
137
      dox et al. (2020), and adversarial robustness. We found this metric to be appropriate due to it being
138   a joint measure of both the expressivity of the model’s architecture and the inherent properties of the
139   dataset the model was trained on. We felt that this captured the notion of what a neural network’s
140   complexity should be in accordance to the universal approximation theorem (Hornik et al., 1989) :-
141   the architecture of the model itself does not affect complexity until after the parameters are trained
142   on, and the complexity would naturally depend on the kind of data the model was being fitted to.
143
      The effective dimensionality of a symmetric matrix A ∈ Rk×k is defined to be
144
                                                            k
145                                                         X    λi
146
                                            Neff (A, z) =                                                (3)
                                                               λ
                                                            i=1 i
                                                                  +z
147
148   where λi are the eigenvalues of A and z > 0 is a regularization constant (MacKay, 1991). We
149   compute the effective dimensionality of a neural network based on the eigenspectrum of the Hessian
150
      of the loss on the test data, according to the method described by Maddox et al. (2020). This metric
      has previously been shown to accurately track generalization properties of networks.
151
152   Intuitively, the effective dimensionality of a model explains the number of parameters that have been
153   determined by the data, which corresponds to the number of parameters the model is using to make
154   predictions. A model with a low effective dimensionality have a simpler function space, embody-
155   ing Occam’s razor and generally avoid overfitting. The metric is directly related to the number of
156
      parameter directions in which the functional form of the model is sensitive to perturbation (Maddox
      et al., 2020).

      4.2   A DVERSARIAL P ERFORMANCE VS M ODEL S IZE
220
221   We corroborate past findings presented by Debenedetti et al. (2023); Bartoldson et al. (2024) that
222   larger, in terms of parameter count, models tend to be more less effected by adversarial examples
223   with a given parameter budget. This trend however does not hold in all tested model classes and
224   is marginal at times. This marginal relationship corresponds to recent similar findings in exploring
225   robustness in LLMs given size that found little-to-no correlation between robustness and size as
226   in Howe et al. (2024).
227   We find that higher-parameterised models tend to be more robust within the same model class.
228   However, it was found that there is little correlation between model size and robustness between
229   different model classes. A more detailed discussion of these results can be found in Appendix A.1.

231   4.3   A DVERSARIAL P ERFORMANCE VS E FFECTIVE D IMENSIONALITY
232
233   The graphs shown in Figure 2 illustrate the relationship between effective dimensionality and the rel-
234
      ative performance of the different models (listed in Section 3) under different adversarial examples
      (described in Section 2.1), across multiple datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR100, CIFAR10). We observe
235
      the following trends:
236
237   AutoAttack Across all datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR100, CIFAR10), there is a general negative cor-
238   relation between effective dimensionality and relative performance. Models with higher effective
239   dimensionality tend to suffer more under AutoAttack, especially on ImageNet and CIFAR100. The
240
      main outliers to the general trends are ResNet (ImageNet) and VGG (CIFAR), much like in Sec-
      tion 4.1.
241
242   PGD The PGD results show a similar trend across ImageNet and CIFAR datasets, where models
243   with lower effective dimensionality generally exhibit higher robustness. However, there are some in-
244   consistencies, particularly in CIFAR100 and CIFAR10, where certain models (e.g., VGG on CIFAR
245   datasets) exhibit an increase in performance at higher dimensionalities, likely due to model-specific
246
      behavior.
247   Gaussian noise When subject to Gaussian noise, models across all datasets show a clear inverse
248   relationship between effective dimensionality and performance. Higher dimensionality again corre-
249   sponds to lower robustness.
250   Overall, the results suggest that lower effective dimensionality generally correlates with better ad-
251   versarial robustness across various adversarial example generation methods and datasets, though
252   model-specific variations do exist.

254   4.4   E FFECT OF A DVERSARIAL T RAINING ON E FFECTIVE D IMENSIONALITY
255
256   In Figures 3 and 4 we demonstrate the effect of various adversarial training techniques on the effec-
257   tive dimensionality of models and how this relates to their adversarial robustness.
258   The results are incredibly clear-cut. We see that in nearly-all cases the Standard-None setup has
259   the highest dimensionality, with any additional adversarial training measures significantly lowering
260   this metric. For ResNet18, AWP reduced dimensionality by 24.0%, and AWP+ED by 29.3% relative
261   to the baseline, on average. For WRN28, AWP reduced dimensionality by 23.2% and AWP+ED by
262   30.4%, on average. For WRN34, AWP reduced dimensionality by 19.1% and AWP+ED by 31.3%, on
263   average.
264   The effect of these dimensionality reductions on adversarial robustness can be seen in Figure 4.
265   We remove outliers (mainly poorly pre-trained models) when plotting these results. Here we see
266   a highly-correlated (lowest R2 is 0.73 in the WRN34 case) relationship between effective dimen-
267   sionality and the relative adversarial performance. Based on the linear regression, we can see that
268   drop in effective dimesionality of 10 points corresponds to roughly an absolute increase of 5.5% in
269   relative adversarial performance. These results are in line with the ones described in Section 4.3
      above, namely that lower effective dimensionality generally correlates with higher robustness.

310        5                           D ISCUSSION
311
312       The results presented in Section 4 build upon the work of Maddox et al. (2020) who claim that
313       the effective dimensionality of a model is a good mechanism for model selection. We show that
314
          this selection criteria does not only correspond to an improvement in generalization but also to
          adversarial robustness.
315
316       Across all experiments, a clear inverse relationship between effective dimensionality and robustness
317       under adversarial examples emerges, consistent with previous findings that models with lower ef-
318       fective dimensionality tend to be more robust to adversarial perturbations. The adversarial training
319       methods tested consistently reduced the effective dimensionality of models compared to standard
320
          training, confirming that adversarial training not only enhances robustness but also simplifies the
          model’s internal structure in terms of its dimensionality.
321
322       Although effective dimensionality has shown promise as a proxy for measuring adversarial robust-
323       ness, it is not a perfect predictor as shown by the slightly mixed results presented in Section 4.3.
          Other factors, such as the loss landscape flatness, boundary thickness, or specific adversarial train-

362       examples. This aligns with the conclusion of Kim et al. (2023) that no single robustness measure is
363
          comprehensive, leaving space for the development of more holistic metrics.

           5.1                              L IMITATIONS
367
368
369       Several important limitations must be noted. First, due to resource constraints, we were unable to test
370       larger more complex models. As a result, our findings are limited to smaller model architectures, and
371       it remains unclear whether the observed trends hold for larger models, which could exhibit different
372       behaviors with respect to dimensionality and robustness.
373       Additionally, the results are based purely on empirical analysis, and while we observed consistent
374       patterns, these findings do not establish a direct causal relationship between effective dimensionality
375       and adversarial robustness. For example, there were clear outliers present in Section 4.3 and Sec-
376       tion 4.1. This points to the need for further theoretical exploration to better understand the un-
377       derlying mechanisms and interactions between dimensionality and other factors, such as the loss
          landscape or boundary geometry.

      5.2   F UTURE W ORK
379
380   We conducted an extensive investigation into how model scale and different adversarial training
381   methods affect effective dimensionality, and how this relates to the models’ adversarial performance.
382   However, it will be worthwhile to conduct an evaluation into how model quantization and distillation
383   affect this measure in relation to robustness. A future branch of work would also involve conducting
384   a similar exploration for other architectures, such as vision transformers, and on different domains,
385   such as reinforcement learning environments.
386   Given the observed trends, future research could explore the integration of multiple robustness met-
387   rics, including effective dimensionality, boundary geometry, and loss flatness, to develop a more
388   unified and accurate predictor of robustness. It would also be worthwhile to explore why certain
389   models (such as ResNet and VGG, mentioned in Section 4.1) are outliers from this observed trend.
390
391   6     C ONCLUSION
392
393   In this paper, we presented an extensive empirical investigation into the relationship between effec-
394   tive dimensionality and adversarial robustness in deep neural networks. Through our experiments
395   on production-scale models, including YOLO and ResNet architectures, we demonstrated that effec-
396   tive dimensionality serves as a strong predictor of robustness to adversarial examples. Specifically,
397   we found a linear inverse correlation between the two, showing that models with lower effective
398   dimensionality tend to exhibit greater robustness. This trend holds both within architecture families,
399   where larger models generally possess lower complexity, and under adversarial training techniques,
400   which further reduce effective dimensionality in line with improved robustness.
401   These findings suggest that effective dimensionality can serve as a useful criterion for model selec-
402   tion and robustness evaluation, providing a more nuanced and effective metric than parameter count
403   or existing flatness- and boundary-based measures. However, our study is limited to empirical ob-
404   servations, and further theoretical work is required to fully understand the mechanisms driving the
405   relationship between complexity and robustness. Nonetheless, our work lays a foundation for future
406   research on the role of effective dimensionality in adversarial robustness and model optimization.
