025                                                         A BSTRACT
026                Text-to-3D synthesis has recently seen intriguing advances by combining the text-
027                to-image models with 3D representation methods, e.g., Gaussian Splatting (GS),
028                via Score Distillation Sampling (SDS). However, a hurdle of existing methods is
029                the low efficiency, per-prompt optimization for a single 3D object. Therefore, it is
030                imperative for a paradigm shift from per-prompt optimization to one-stage genera-
031                tion for any unseen text prompts, which yet remains challenging. A hurdle is how
032                to directly generate a set of millions of 3D Gaussians to represent a 3D object.
033
                   This paper presents BrightDreamer, an end-to-end single-stage approach that can
                   achieve generalizable and fast (77 ms) text-to-3D generation. Our key idea is to
034
                   formulate the generation process as estimating the 3D deformation from an
035
                   anchor shape with predefined positions. For this, we first propose a Text-guided
036                Shape Deformation (TSD) network to predict the deformed shape and its new po-
037                sitions, used as the centers (one attribute) of 3D Gaussians. To estimate the other
038                four attributes (i.e., scaling, rotation, opacity, and SH coefficient), we then design
039                a novel Text-guided Triplane Generator (TTG) to generate a triplane represen-
040                tation for a 3D object. The center of each Gaussian enables us to transform the
041                triplane feature into the four attributes. The generated 3D Gaussians can be fi-
042                nally rendered at 705 frames per second. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
043                superiority of our method over existing methods. Also, BrightDreamer possesses
044
                   a strong semantic understanding capability even for complex text prompts. The
                   project code is available at http://.

      et al., 2022), LatentNeRF (Metzer et al., 2023), SJC (Wang et al., 2023a), have sparked significant

067

068
      tunately, these methods suffer from two critical challenges. Firstly, as per-prompt optimization
069   usually requires several tens of thousands of iterations, this inefficiency brings a considerable ob-
070   stacle to broader applications. It is significantly different from the mainstream training paradigm
071   in the field of 2D image generation (Song et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) or 3D-aware image
072   generation (Schwarz et al., 2020; Chan et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2023; Or-El et al., 2022; Chan et al.,
073   2022): a generative model is trained with a collection of text-image pairs or images, and the model
074   can generate the desired content from any input at the inference stage. We show the main difference
075   of the different paradigms in 3D generative models in Tab. 1. Secondly, as demonstrated in Fig. 2(a),
076   existing methods often fail to accurately process the complex texts. For example, the mainstream
077
      methods all fail to generate 3D content that input prompt contains complex interaction between mul-
      tiple entities. This limitation arises from the models being trained on a single text prompt, which
078
      results in a degraded capability in comprehensive semantic understanding.
079
      Therefore, it is urgently needed for a paradigm shift from per-prompt optimization to develop a
080   generic text-to-3D generation framework. Once trained, the generative framework should be able to
081   generate content from any text prompts in the inference stage, as depicted in Fig. 1(b). Furthermore,
082   given the scarcity of 3D data in comparison to the abundance of 2D image data, leveraging well-
083   trained 2D image diffusion models to facilitate the training of 3D generative models presents a more
084   effective and resource-efficient approach. Previously, some research efforts, e.g., ATT3D (Lorraine
085   et al., 2023) and Instant3D (Li et al., 2023b), have been made grounded in NeRF representation.
086   The core insight is to add a large number of texts and take them as conditional inputs to generate
087   explicit spatial representations, such as triplane (Chan et al., 2022). Nonetheless, in stark contrast to
088
      the volume rendering in NeRF, 3D GS representation for an object usually consists of millions of 3D
      Gaussians. Consequently, there exists an inherent and natural difficulty in converting the generation
089
      representations into 3D GS ones in their framework.
090
      In this paper, we propose BrightDreamer, an end-to-end single-stage framework that, for the first
091   time, can achieve generalizable and fast (77 ms) text-to-3D GS generation. BrightDreamer exhibits
092   a robust ability for complex semantic understanding (Fig. 2 (a)), and it demonstrates a substantial
093   capacity for generalization (Fig. 2 (b)). In addition, same as traditional generative models (Good-
094   fellow et al., 2014), our generator can interpolate between two inputs (Fig. 2 (c)), which enables
095   users to fully engage their imagination and creativity, expanding the potential for novel and nuanced
096   design exploration. As stated before, the 3D GS representation of an object usually consists of sev-
097   eral millions of 3D Gaussians. Consequently, directly generating such an extensive collection, is
098   impractical. Our key idea is to redefine this generation problem as its equal problem, i.e., 3D shape
099
      deformation. Specifically, we place and fix some anchor positions to form the initial shape. Then, it
      can be deformed to the desired shape by giving different input prompts through our designed Text-
100
      guided Shape Deformation (TSD) network (Sec. 3.1). After the deformation, the new positions can
101
      be set to the centers of the 3D Gaussian. Upon establishing the basic shape, we elaborately de-
102   sign a Text-guided Triplane Generator (TTG) to generate a spatial representation of the 3D object
103   (Sec. 3.2). Subsequently, we utilize the spatial feature of each center of 3D Gaussian to represent its
104   whole feature and translate it into the remaining attributes (including scaling, rotation, opacity, and
105   SH coefficient) through our well-designed Gaussian Decoder (Sec. 3.3). For TTG, grounded on our
106   re-analysis of the previous convolution-based triplane generation process, we have identified and
107   solved two primary deficiencies that necessitate rectification. One issue pertains to the spatial inho-
      mogeneity observed during the calculation process, as illustrated in Fig. 4. The other issue arises

203   Overview. The objective of BrightDreamer is to directly generate 3D Gaussians in response to text
204   prompts. After training, BrightDreamer is capable of generating the 3D Gaussians with a remark-
205   ably low generation latency (about 77ms). And, the generated 3D Gaussians can be rendered at
206   an impressive inference speed of over 700 frames per second. 3D Gaussians can be defined by five
207
      attributes, namely the center p′ , scaling S, rotation R, opacity α, and SH coefficient SH. To directly
      generate 3D Gaussians, our key idea is two-fold: 1) Defining anchor positions, i.e., predefined po-
208
      sitions, to estimate the center of 3D Gaussians; 2) Building implicit spatial representation, which
209
      can be decomposed to estimate the other four attributes of 3D Gaussians.
210   Intuitively, we propose BrightDreamer, and an overview is depicted in Fig. 3. Given a text prompt
211   as input, we transform it to a 77 × 1024 embedding through the frozen CLIP text encoder. Next,
212   the TSD network (Sec. 3.1) transforms the fixed anchor positions to the desired shape with text
213   guidance. The new positions are used as the centers of 3D Gaussians. We then design the TTG
214   (Sec. 3.2) to separately generate three feature planes to construct the implicit spatial representation.
215   Based on the centers of Gaussians, we can obtain their spatial features, which are then transferred
      to the other attributes through the Gaussian Decoder (Sec. 3.3). Finally, we render 3D Gaussians to

219   3.1 T EXT- GUIDED S HAPE D EFORMATION (TSD)
220   The goal of TSD is to obtain the center (one attribute) of each 3D Gaussian. Considering that
221   directly outputting a huge number of center coordinates is extremely difficult, we overcome this
222   hurdle by deforming the anchor positions instead of generating them.
223   Anchor Position. The anchor position is predefined in this paper, which is a fixed coordinate. It
224   serves as one of the inputs for the TSD. Specifically, we place the anchor positions on the vertices of
225
      a 3D grid, as represented by the gray points in Fig. 3. Then, we design the TSD network to predict
      their deviation to deform the initialized shape of the 3D grid, guided by the text prompt input.
226
      Network Design. As shown in Fig. 3 (a), the inputs of TSD are text prompts and anchor positions.
227
      Firstly, the text prompts are encoded as the text embedding by an off-the-shelf text encoder, e.g.,
228   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). Considering the possibility of the com-
229   plex input sentence, it remains non-trivial how to bridge each position and word in the sentence.
230   The cross-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) can quantify the correlation degree between each point
231   and each word within a sentence. We then employ the cross-attention to design a module to ob-
232   tain the deviation from the anchor position. It consists of the Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016),
233   Multi-Head Attention, Feed-Forward Network, and shortcut connection (Vaswani et al., 2017). Con-
234   sequently, certain positions, correlating more closely with corresponding words in the sentence, are
235   assigned with higher attention scores. This process enables the aggregation of features that more
236
      accurately reflect the characteristics of the corresponding words. The detailed computation process
      is formulated as follows:

252   3.2 T EXT- GUIDED T RIPLANE G ENERATOR (TTG)
253   Upon determining the centers of the 3D Gaussians, we need to obtain the other four attributes.
254   To efficiently assign features to each Gaussian, the objective of TTG is to generate an implicit
255   spatial representation in space, represented by the triplane. Therefore, we design a novel and
256   highly efficient triplane generator, whose input is also the text prompts.
257   One challenge is that the previous triplane generation approaches, such as EG3D (Chan et al., 2022)
258   and Instant3D (Li et al., 2023b), exhibit the problem of spatial inhomogeneity, as shown in Fig. 4.
259   Since they directly segment a feature map into three feature maps along the channel dimension, only
260   a few areas are computed together. For example, the position (0, 0) in the 2D space is unfolded to (0,
261
      0, :), (0, :, 0), and (:, 0, 0), denoted by blue color in Fig. 4. Taking 1 × 1 Conv as an example, only
      these three areas are calculated together. On the contrary, (0, 0, :) is hardly possible to be calculated
262
      with (0, :, 1), because they do not appear at the same pixel in the 2D feature map. The same applies
263
      to the 3 × 3 Conv. This means that only a few areas share the same spatial information, while the
264   others do not, thus causing spatial inhomogeneity. For this, a simple yet effective way is to apply
265   three generators (without sharing weights).
266   Another challenge is that given the complex prompts, squeezing a sentence into a single style
267   feature vector to apply AdaIN (Karras et al., 2019; 2020; 2021) could result in a loss of local de-
268   tails. Therefore, we need a more fine-grained generation method guided by the word level, thus
269   can retain more information of text encoder trained on large-scale dataset. Naturally, calculating
      cross-attention between the pixels of the feature map and words in the sentence is a better choice.

325
326   It aims to obtain the other four attributes of 3D Gaussian for achieving the generation. Upon gen-
327   erating the triplane, comprised of the three feature planes πxy , πxz , πyz , we can obtain the feature
328   vector F ∈ R32 of each Gaussian based on its center p′ . Subsequently, the feature vector needs to
329   be converted into additional attributes of 3D Gaussian, including opacity α ∈ R, scaling S ∈ R3 ,
330   rotation R ∈ R4 , and SH coefficient.
331
      Specifically, we first project the 3D coordinate onto the three planes, X-Y, X-Z, and Y-Z. Based on
      the projected 2D coordinates, we can derive the features Fxy , Fxz , Fyz according to the interpo-
332
      lation with their four vertex in the 2D feature maps. To ensure that the gradient back-propagation
333
      is evenly distributed across all three planes, we utilize an averaging operation to aggregate these
334   features, thereby obtaining the 3D Gaussian’s feature F. Given that the attributes of 3D Gaussian
335   can be categorized into two groups, i.e., shape and color, we develop two distinct transformation
336   modules Fshape and Fcolor . Each module is a lightweight, two-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)
337   network. To enhance the gradient back-propagation to the TSD, the center of 3D Gaussian p′ is
338   additionally inputted into both modules:
339                                α, S, R = Fshape (F, p′ ), SH = Fcolor (F, p′ ).                       (4)
340   In the training process, we observe the scaling S is not a stable variation, and the memory consump-
341   tion of 3D Gaussian rendering is extremely sensitive to it. Therefore, we use the following equation
342   to control it to interval (a, b):
343                                        S = (b − a) · sigmoid(S) + a.                                  (5)
344
      Upon obtaining all attributes of 3D Gaussian, our generation process is completed. We can render it
345
      from arbitrary view direction to 2D images.

      5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduced the first text-driven 3D Gaussians generative framework, Bright-
529   Dreamer, capable of generating 3D Gaussians within a remarkably low latency of 77ms. To address
530   the challenge of directly generating a vast quantity of Gaussians, millions of them, we innovatively
531
      deform anchor positions and use the new positions as the centers of 3D Gaussians, in response to
      the input prompt. This approach effectively circumvented the obstacle of generating a large num-
532
      ber of positions. Regarding network architecture, we thoroughly reevaluated the triplane generation
533
      process and introduced an improved alternative strategy. Our largest contribution is poised to sig-
534   nificantly advance the field of generalized 3D generation, offering a novel and efficient pathway to
535   creating 3D assets from text prompts immediately. Extensive experiments prove that our Bright-
536   Dreamer has a strong complex semantic understanding capability and strong generalization ability.
537   Future work. First, the spatial resolution of the generated 3D model is relatively low, resulting in a
538   lack of fine-grained detail. Second, the range of supported scene types could be further expanded to
539   enhance the model’s versatility. To achieve these two targets, integrating higher-resolution diffusion
      models and expanding the dataset with additional text prompts are promising research directions.
