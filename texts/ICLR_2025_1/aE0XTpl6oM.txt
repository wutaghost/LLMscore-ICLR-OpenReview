011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated recommender system (FedRec) has emerged as a solution to protect
014            user data through collaborative training techniques. However, the real-world
015            implementation of FedRec is hindered by two critical resource constraints of
016
               edge devices: a) limited upload bandwidth and b) limited user computational
               power and storage. Existing methods addressing the first issue, such as message
017
               compression techniques, often result in accuracy degradation or potential privacy
018
               leakage. For the second issue, most federated learning (FL) protocols assume that
019            users must store and maintain the entire model locally for private inference, which
020            is resource intensive. To address these challenges, we propose doubly efficient
021            privacy-perserving recommender systems (GREC) consisting of both training
022            and inference phase. To reduce communication costs during the training phase,
023            we design a lossless secure aggregation (SecAgg) protocol based on functional
024            secret sharing leveraging the sparsity of the update matrix. During the inference
025            phase, we implement a user-side post-processing local differential privacy (LDP)
026            algorithm to ensure privacy while shifting the bulk of computation to the cloud. Our
027
               framework reduces uplink communication costs by up to 90x compared to existing
               SecAgg protocols and decreases user-side computation time during inference by
028
               an average of 11x compared to full-model inference. This makes GREC a practical
029
               and scalable solution for deploying federated recommender systems on resource-
030            constrained devices.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Personalized recommendation systems (RecSys) model the interactions between users and items to
036   uncover users’ interests. To understand the underlying preferences of users and properties of items,
037   various model-based approaches have been developed to learn hidden representations of both users
038   and items Koren et al. (2009); Xue et al. (2017); Rendle (2010). These methods embed users and
039   items into fixed-size latent vectors, which are then used to predict interactions. The parameters of
040   these latent vectors are known as user and item embeddings.
041
      The development of personalized recommendation systems (RecSys) relies heavily on collecting user
042   profiles and behavioral data, such as gender, age, and item interactions. However, the sensitive nature
043   of this information often makes users hesitate to share it with service providers. Recent advancements
044   in edge computing have provided a potential solution through federated learning (FL), which allows
045   users to collaboratively train models on their local devices without exposing personal data McMahan
046   et al. (2017). In a typical FL setup, a central server aggregates model updates from multiple edge
047   devices. The aggregated parameters are then redistributed to the devices for further training. During
048   inference, users employ the locally stored global model to make predictions on previously unseen
049   items, maintaining privacy while benefiting from collective learning.
050   Despite various FL-based architectures proposed for RecSys Ammad-Ud-Din et al. (2019); Chai et al.
051   (2020); Jia & Lei (2021); Wang et al. (2022), there remains a significant gap between research and
052   real-world applications, mainly due to the resource limitations of edge devices. Two critical resource
053   constraints in FL-based RecSys are: (1) Limited upload bandwidth. Clients in federated networks
      often experience slower upload speeds compared to downloads. This issue becomes particularly


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      problematic in latent factor-based RecSys, where the communication payload for uploads scales
055   linearly with the number of items, potentially leading to substantial transmission overhead in practical
056   applications. (2) Limited user computational power and storage. Edge devices generally have limited
057   processing capabilities, memory, and storage compared to centralized servers. Running large models
058   locally can place a heavy computational burden on users, degrading performance and negatively
059   impacting the user experience.
060
      To address the first constraint, existing message compression methods can be categorized into three
061   categories: (1) Top-K sparsification that transmits a subset of updates to reduce the message size
062   Gupta et al. (2021); Aji & Heafield (2017). (2) Quantization that represents each element in the
063   gradients with fewer bits Zheng et al. (2023). (3) Dimension reduction that involves factorizing the
064   transmission matrix into low-rank matrices or projecting the matrix into a lower-dimensional space
065   Nguyen et al. (2024); Shin et al. (2018). However, these methods are lossy in principle and often
066   result in non-negligible accuracy loss in practice, highlighting the need for a lossless communication-
067   efficient approach. For the second constraint, many FL protocols require users to store the entire
068   global model for both training and inference, placing considerable demands on long-term storage and
069
      computational resources—especially for resource-constrained edge devices.
070   In practice, users typically interact with only a small subset of available items, which presents two
071   key opportunities for payload optimization. First, during training, only the embeddings of the items a
072   user interacts with are relevant for model updates. As a result, users can store and update only these
073   relevant item embeddings, significantly reducing computational and storage overhead. While this
074   approach effectively addresses the training phase, there is a noticeable lack of research focused on
075
      minimizing costs during inference. For subsequent recommendations, users are often required to
      retain the entire item embedding matrix, which is resource-intensive. An ideal solution would enable
076
      cloud-based inference that preserves user privacy by keeping their embeddings and features secure
077
      from the server, while offloading the bulk of the computational burden.
078
079   Secondly, the update vector is highly sparse in that only a small subset of the item embeddings are
080   non-zero. Therefore, it is desirable to make the per-user communication succinct, i.e., independent
081
      of or logarithmic in the item size. A naive solution is to transmit only updates of rated items to the
      server, possibly supplemented with fake items sampled Lin et al. (2022). However, this method
082
      compromises privacy by revealing which items the user has rated, or significantly narrowing the set
083
      of potential rated items. To protect user interaction data, it is crucial to ensure that no information
084   about the specific update vector is disclosed to the server, aside from its aggregate. At the same
085   time, communication efficiency should be achieved by leveraging the sparsity of the update vector,
086   ensuring both privacy and reduced transmission overhead.
087
      In this paper, we propose doubly efficient privacy-perserving recommender systems (GREC) that
088
      collectively address the above challenges. To reduce communication costs during the training stage,
089
      we design a secure aggregation protocol based on functional secret sharing Boyle et al. (2015;
090   2016), achieving succinct communication cost. Unlike methods based on dimension reduction, top-k
091   sparsification, or quantization, our protocol reduces communication costs without compromising
092   accuracy and privacy. For privacy-preserving inference, we introduce a user-side post-processing
093   local differential privacy (LDP) algorithm, which performs cloud inference on users’ privatized
094   embeddings and subsequently allows users to locally post-processing the server response using their
095   knowledge of raw inputs and specific noise. This approach effectively resolves the inherent trade-off
096   between privacy and utility for traditional LDP approaches.
097   We defer the discussion of related work to appendix A.1. The contribution of our work can be
098   summarized as follows:
099
      (1) We develop a SecAgg protocol that achieves succinct communication by exploiting the sparsity of
100
      the embedding update matrix. Although existing SecAgg protocols incur communication overhead
101
      that scales linearly with model size, our approach significantly improves upon this by achieving a
102   slower scaling rate as the model size grows.
103
104   (2) We design a cloud inference approach for recommender systems with LDP guarantee. This
105
      method significantly reduces users’ computational and storage overheads relative to the full model
      inference required by existing FL protocols, and addresses the inherent trade-off between utility and
106
      privacy in traditional LDP-based methods.

138   2.2   F UNCTIONAL S ECRET S HARING
139
140   Our protocol leverages functional secret sharing (FSS) Boyle et al. (2015; 2016) to optimize the
141
      communication payload. FSS secret shares a function f : {0, 1}n → G, for some abelian group G,
                                                       P2
142   into two functions f1 , f2 such that: (1) f (x) = i=1 fi (x) for any x, and (2) each description of fi
143
      hides f .
144   Definition 2.1 (Function Secret Sharing). A function secret sharing (FSS) scheme with respect to a
145   function class F is a pair of efficient algorithms (FSS.Gen, FSS.Eval):
146
             • FSS.Gen(1λ , f ): Based on the security parameter 1λ and function description f , the key
147            generation algorithm outputs a pair of keys, (k1 , k2 ).
148
149          • FSS.Eval(ki , x): Based on key ki and input x ∈ {0, 1}n , the evaluation algorithm outputs
150            party i’s share of f (x), denoted as fi (x). f1 (x) and f2 (x) form additive shares of f (x).
151
      FSS scheme should satisfy the following informal properties (defined formally in Appendix A.3.2):
152
153          • Correctness: Given keys (k1 , k2 ) of a function f ∈ F, it holds that FSS.Eval(k1 , x) +
154            FSS.Eval(k2 , x) = f (x) for any x.
155
             • Security: Given keys (k1 , k2 ) of a function f ∈ F, a computationally-bounded adversary
156
               that learns either k1 or k2 gains no information about the function f , except that f ∈ F.
157
158   A naive form of FSS scheme is to additively secret share each entry in the truth-table of f . However,
159   this approach results in each share containing 2n elements. To obtain polynomial share size, nontrivial
160   scheme of FSS has been developed for simple function classses, e.g., point functions Boyle et al.
161   (2015; 2016). Our approach utilizes the advanced FSS scheme for the point function. In the following,
      we provide the formal definition of point function.

      2.3   D IFFERENTIAL P RIVACY
167
      Differential privacy (DP) Dwork (2006); Cormode et al. (2018) is regarded as the gold standard for
168
      privacy protection. We introduce the definition of local differential privacy (LDP), a specific case of
169   DP where the server is untrusted and data privatization is conducted by the client.
170
171   Definition 2.3 (Local Differential Privacy). A randomized algorithm M is (ϵ, δ)-locally differentially
172
      private if it satisfies:
173                                 Pr[M(x) ∈ S] ≤ eϵ Pr[M(x′ ) ∈ S] + δ                                   (3)
174
      for any inputs x, x′ ∈ D and any measurable subset subset S ⊆ Range(M).
175
176
      An essential property of DP algorithms is the post-processing immunity property, stating that the
177   composition of any data-independent function with a (ϵ, δ)-DP mechanism will remain (ϵ, δ)-DP.
178
179
      Definition 2.4 (Post-Processing). Let M : D → Y be an (ϵ, δ)-differentially private mechanism and
      f : Y → Z be any data-independent function. Then, f ◦ M is (ϵ, δ)-differentially private.
180
181
      It is important to note that the post-processing immunity only holds when f is independent of the
182
      original data as well as the randomized mapping M. From the utility perspective, this property
183   implies a lower bound on the error rate of the results returned by any data-independent function f .
184
185   Recent works have developed post-processing mechanisms to enhance the accuracy of DP algorithms
186
      by leveraging prior knowledge of data. Wang et al. (2024) utilizes resampling techniques to improve
      the utility of DP synthetic data on downstream tasks. Balle & Wang (2018) post-processes the
187
      output of Gaussian mechanism with adaptive estimation technique. Split-and-Denoise Mai et al.
188
      (2024) divides the language model into a local encoder and a cloud encoder, and employs a user-side
189   pre-trained model to denoise output embedding. To bypass the post-processing immunity, our work
190   will use a user-side post-processing function that is dependent on the original data and mapping
191   mechanism M without additional effort to collect prior knowledge.

222   3.1.1    K EY OBSERVATION
223
      In most practical recommendation scenarios, the number of items a user has previously interacted
224
      with is typically much smaller than the total number of available items (see Figure 2(a)). This
225   observation is linked to the information overload phenomenon, which recommender systems aim to
226   address. Consequently, the gradient of the item embedding is zero for all items except those with
227   which the user has previously interacted.
228

231   the bottleneck, since the embedding layer dominates the total model size as the item size increases
232   (see Figure 2(b)).

234

332
      Existing protocols for FedRec neglect the computation cost and privacy concerns during inference,
333
      assuming that the user should maintain the full model for privacy-preserving inference, incurring
334

338   privacy-preserving cloud inference solution is highly desirable.

388   The post-processing function Fp is parameterized by a lightweight denoise model. The denoise model
389
      is trained in an FL setting subsequent to the training of the recommender system, and deployed on
      user device in the inference phase. Since the the user representation is obtained during the training
390
      stage, there is no need to gather prior knowledge about the private input for denoise model training.
391
      Refer to Appendix A.8 for more details on the architecture and training of denoise model.
392
393
      3.2.4    P RIVACY A NALYSIS
394
395   During inference, only the privatized representation matrix H̃u is transmitted to the server. Conse-
396   quently, the server’s view is limited to the privatized matrix H̃u . In the following, we will demonstrate
397   that the server’s view adheres to the LDP guarantee. The process that privatizes the representation
398   matrix Hu into H̃u , denoted as M : R(lx +1)×d → R(lx +1)×d , satisfies LDP:
399   Theorem 3.2. For any δ > 0 and ϵ > 0, the mechanism M : R(lx +1)×d → R(lx +1)×d achieves
400   (ϵ, δ)-differential privacy.
401
402   We proceed to analyze the security of the denoise model. During inference, the server is unable to
403
      infer the actual prediction using the denoise model. The inputs to the denoise model Fp include the
      noise matrix and the clean embedding matrix, both of which are maintained privately on the user side.
404
      Consequently, the server does not have access to the critical inputs required for error correction.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper proposes a doubly efficient privacy-perserving recommender systems (GREC) to address
532
      the resource constraint of edge devices in terms of (a) upload bandwidth, and (b) computational power
533
      and storage. To reduce communication costs during upload transmission, we design a FSS-based
534   SecAgg, achieving communication cost logarithmic in item size m. To reduce user computation
535   burdens during inference, we introduce a post-processing LDP approach that addresses the intrinsic
536   trade-off between privacy and utility. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that: (1) Our algorithm
537   reduces communication costs by up to 90x compared with existing SecAgg protocols. (2) Our
538   post-processing LDP approach enhances prediction accuracy by an average of 43.9% compared to
539   standard LDP perturbation, while also reducing user-side computation time by approximately 11x
      relative to full model inference. Refer to Appendix A.11 for further discussions of our framework.

      A.1     R ELATED W ORK
759
760
      A.1.1    C ROSS -U SER F EDERATED R ECOMMENDER S YSTEM
761
762   In recent years, federated recommender system (FedRec) trained on individual users has gained grow-
763   ing interest in research community. FCF Ammad-Ud-Din et al. (2019) and FedRec Lin et al. (2020)
764   are among the pioneering implementations of federated learning for collaborative filtering based
765   on matrix factorization. Privacy guarantees are enhanced through the application of cryptographic
766   methods to the transmitted gradients Chai et al. (2020); Mai & Pang (2023). Difacto Li et al. (2016)
767   introduces a distributed factorization machine algorithm that is scalable to a large number of users
768
      and items. FedNCF Perifanis & Efraimidis (2022) is a federated realization of neural collaborative
      iltering (NCF), where secure aggregation is leveraged to protect user gradients. FMSS Lin et al.
769
      (2022) proposes a federated recommendation framework for several recommendation algorithms
770
      based on factorization and deep learning. Rabbani et al. (2023) and Xu et al. (2022) improve the
771   training efficiency for edge device using locality-sensitive hashing (LSH) techniques Chen et al.
772   (2020; 2019). Despite the development of various algorithms for training FedRec systems, there
773   remains a dearth of research investigating the inference phase.
774
775
      A.1.2    S ECURE AGGREGATION FOR M ACHINE L EARNING
776
777   Secure Aggregation (SecAgg) computes the summation of private gradients without revealing any
778   individual values. Bonawitz et al. (2017) introduces a secure aggregation protocol for FL, leveraging
779   a combination of pairwise masking, Shamir’s Secret Sharing, and symmetric encryption techniques.
780   Bell et al. (2020) reduces the communication and computation overhead to depend logarithmic in
781   the client size. FastSecAgg Kadhe et al. (2020) designs a multi-secret sharing protocol based on
782   Fast Fourier Transform to save computation cost. SAFELearn Fereidooni et al. (2021) designs
783   an secure two-party computation protocol for efficient FL implementation. LightSecAgg So et al.
784
      (2022) reduces the computation complexity via one-shot reconstruction of aggregated mask. The

908   A.7    T HEORETICAL E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
909
910   Communication cost: In each iteration, the user uploads m′ FSS keys and secret shares of dense
911   updates to the server. Each key size is (λ + 2) log m + bd, where b is number of bits required
912   to represent a single numerical value Boyle et al. (2016). Thus total message size for FSS keys
913   is O(m′ (bd + λ log m)). Furthermore, the size of additive shares for the dense updates is O(|θ|).
914
      Therefore, the total communication cost adds up to O (m′ (bd + λ log m) + |θ|b).
915   Computation cost: Each user generate m′ FSS keys and secret shares of dense updates. It takes
916   O(log m · AES) operations to produce a FSS key Boyle et al. (2016), resulting complexity of
917   O(m′ log m · AES) for m′ keys. Additionally, generating additive secret shares takes O(|θ|) opera-
      tions. Therefore, the total computation cost adds up to O (m′ log m · AES + |θ|).

1238   A.10.6    C OMPARISON WITH S PARSE AGGREGATION P ROTOCOL
1239
1240   In this section, we discuss the advantages of our GREC over existing sparse aggregation protocols.
1241   We consider two SOTA frameworks, Secure Aggregation with Mask Sparsification (SecAggMask)
       Liu et al. (2023) and Top-k Sparse Secure Aggregation (TopkSecAgg) Lu et al. (2023). The key


                                                           23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       problem with the two frameworks is that they fail to ensure that the server learns nothing except the
1243   aggregated gradients. In particular:
1244
1245           • Leakage of rated item index. For SecAggMask, each user transmits the union of gradients
1246             with non-zero updates and masks to the server. For TopkSecAgg, each user is required to
1247             upload the coordinate set of non-zero gradients along with a small portion of perturbed
1248             coordinates. In both methods, the server could narrow down the potential rated items to a
1249             much smaller set.
1250
               • Leakage of gradient values. While TopkSecAgg protects the values of non-zero updates
1251             against the server, SecAggMask would reveal the plaintext values to the server. Specifically,
1252             SecAggMask randomly masks a portion of the gradients to reduce communication cost, and
1253             fails to ensure that all non-zero gradients would be masked against any attackers.
1254
1255   In Table 13 we compare the communication cost of our GREC with the sparse aggregation protocols
1256   under the same training setting. For SecAggMask, we adopt a mask threshold such that 60% non-zero
1257   gradients would be masked in expectation. For TopkSecAgg, we set the purturbation proportion µ to
1258   be 0.1, following Lu et al. (2023). Both approaches result in higher communication cost than GREC
1259   because: (1) Besides the non-zero embedding gradients, SecAggMask requires the user to send a
1260
       certain proportion of randomly masked zero updates to the server. (2) To cancel out the mask values,
       in TopkSecAgg each user sends the union of rated item embeddings for all participating user, rather
1261
       than the those for each single user.

       A.11     D ISCUSSION
1345
1346   Communication cost during download transmission: Our framework focuses on the overhead
1347   optimization during upload transmission considering its limited bandwidth. During the download
1348   stage, succinct communication cost can be achieved using private information retrieval (PIR) Chor
1349   et al. (1998) techinques, where users can retrieve their related item embeddings without revealing the
       item index. Existing PIR protocols can achieve communication costs that depend sublinearly on m


                                                             25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       Chor & Gilboa (1997); Cachin et al. (1999). The use of FSS schemes further enhances communication
1351   efficiency in PIR, reducing overheads to logarithmic dependence on m Boyle et al. (2015); Gilboa &
1352   Ishai (2014).
1353
1354
       Private inference for sequential recommendation: Sequential recommendations predict the next
       item the user is likely to interact with given their interaction histories. Various models have been
1355
       proposed for this recommendation task, based on architectures including Recurrent Neural Network
1356
       (RNN), self-attention blocks, and Graph Neural Network (GNN) Wang et al. (2020); Kang & McAuley
1357   (2018); Sun et al. (2019). It’s essential to develop a unified federated training and private inference
1358   for sequential recommendation compatible with a variety of models.
