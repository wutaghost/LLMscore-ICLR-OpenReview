                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Although LLMs demonstrate strong reasoning capability in such tasks as mathe-
015
               matical problem solving, less is known about their reasoning capability in settings
016            that require extensive real-world knowledge due to the limited scale and knowledge
017            coverage of existing benchmarks. To shed more light into this, we propose a
018            novel pipeline that is capable of programmatically generating realistic knowledge-
019            intensive question answering benchmarks that require complex reasoning. Lever-
020            aging open knowledge graphs, the graph query language SPARQL, and LLMs, our
021            pipeline requires no manual annotation and can therefore scale to unprecedented
022            benchmark size and knowledge coverage. We evaluate several state-of-the-art
023            LLMs with benchmarks generated by our pipeline, and find that the LLMs struggle
024
               to recall and leverage world knowledge for reasoning, even for world knowledge
               present in their pre-training corpuses. Additionally, retrieval-augmented generation
025
               and chain-of-thoughts prompting does not fully solve the problems. Our bench-
026
               marks further enable us to examine to what extent the confidence of LLMs in the
027            outcomes of their reasoning transparently reflects their confidence in the underlying
028            knowledge, a study that is first-of-its-kind to our best knowledge. We find that
029            the confidence of LLMs in the outcomes of their reasoning reflects poorly their
030            confidence in the underlying knowledge (poor knowledgeability transparency),
031            which suggests a direction of future improvement.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Existing benchmarks examining how well LLMs can leverage real-world knowledge for reasoning
      predominantly rely on human annotations, e.g. Yang et al. (2018); Kwiatkowski et al. (2019). The
037
      costs of manual annotations limit the scale of these benchmarks even in unspecialized domains
038
      where manual annotations are affordable, let alone domains where shortages of domain experts make
039   manual annotations prohibitively costly (Hendrycks et al., 2020). The reliance of existing approaches
040   on human annotations also limits the coverage of long-tail knowledge by these benchmarks, since
041   such knowledge is often beyond the expertise of most human annotators. We propose a pipeline for
042   generating such benchmarks that is fully automated and therefore much more scalable. Given a KG,
043   our pipeline first samples subgraphs from the KG, masks a subset of entities in the subgraphs, and
044   encodes the masked subgraphs as SPARQL queries. Our pipeline then translate the SPARQL queries
045   into natural language questions by an LLM, and obtain ground truth answers to the LLM-generated
046   questions by querying the KG.
047   With benchmarks generated by our pipeline, we further assess to what extent SOTA LLMs can recall
048   and leverage world knowledge for complex reasoning, in the setting of zero-shot question answering,
049   retrieval-agumented generation (RAG), and chain-of-thoughts (CoT) prompting. Our assessment
050   yields the following findings:
051
052
            1. LLMs have significant room of improvement when it comes to recall and leverage world
               knowledge for reasoing, even for world knowledge present in their pre-training corpuses.
053
            2. LLMs cannot avoid reasoning errors even with all required knowledge provided as context.

093   2.1   BACKGROUND
094
095   Question answering is a natural way to assess knowledge-intensive reasoning. Existing approaches
096   to building complex question answering benchmarks predominantly require significant manual
097   annotations. The most labor-intensive approach requires human annotators to raise questions about
098   pieces of text. Although they yield highly diverse questions, such approaches can have prohibitive
099
      costs. The lack of structure in even relatively organized texts such as Wikipedia makes it hard to
      quantify the reasoning capabilities that are measured. For example, a study Min et al. (2019) finds
100
      that a surprisingly large portion of questions in HotpotQA Yang et al. (2018), a popular multi-hop
101
      question answering dataset, can be answered without multi-hop reasoning. Less labor intensive
102   approaches often rely on customized domain-specific language as intermediate representation (IR).
103   Constructing such IRs itself can also require significant manual efforts. Additionally, customized
104   IRs may not generalize well to novel knowledge. Finally, these approaches generally rely on manual
105   efforts to paraphrase questions expressed IRs into natural language. Those approaches in this category
106   that does not rely on manual paraphrasing instead rely on manually crafted templates, which can be
107   costly to construct. This paper demonstrates that graph query languages such as SPARQL can act as
      powerful and general IRs readily digestible for LLMs-empowered question answering benchmark

131   2.2   LLM- EMPOWERED BENCHMARK GENERATION WITH SPARQL AS IR
132
133   Our benchmark generation pipeline takes as input a knowledge graph (KG), which we define as a
134   collection of semantic triples KG = {(s, r, o)} ⊂ E × P × (E ∪ V), where E is a set of entities,
135   P is a set of predicates, and V is a set of values. To generate a question that requires reasoning,
136   we first sample a subgraph in the KG by taking a random walk (see Algorithm 1 for the subgraph
137   sampling procedure and Figure 2a for an example subgraph in Wikidata). and a subset of the entities
138   in the subgraph are masked (see Figure 2a for an example). We mask some of the entities in the
139
      sampled subgraph, and encode the partially masked subgraph as a graph query in SPARQL, a widely
      adopted graph query language endorsed by W3C. We then translate the SPARQL query into a natural
140
      language question with an LLM. We choose SPARQL because we find state-of-the-art LLMs, such
141
      as GPT-4, demonstrates strong capabilities of translating SPARQL queries into natural language
142   questions. Finally, we find the set of ground truth answers to the question by executing the SPARQL
143   query against the KG. We show an example of this process in Figure 2.
144
145   Algorithm 1 Subgraph sampling procedure
146
147
      Require: Knowledge graph KG = {(s, r, o)}, subgraph size n, returning probability p
        G ← {}                                                                           ▷ Empty subgraph
148
        s, _, _ ∼ (KG)                                                              ▷ Start of random walk
149
        while |G| < n do
150         s, r, o ∼ ({(s, r, o) : (s, r, o) ∈ KG})                            ▷ Sample a semantic triple
151         G ← G ∪ {(s, r, o)}                                  ▷ Add the sampled triple to the subgraph
152         if rand() < p then                       ▷ With probability 1 − p stay at s in the next iteration
153              s ← s′
154     if nontrivial(G) then return G ▷ Return the masked subgraph only if the query is nontrivial

157   2.3   C HOICE OF KNOWLEDGE GRAPHS
158
159   Apart from scale and quality, our primary concern when choosing KGs is their alignment with the
160   pre-training corpuses of SOTA LLMs. Based on this, we choose to generate benchmarks from the
161   T-REx and Wikidata. We report key statistics of Wikidata and T-REx in Table 1. Both KGs can
      contain offensive contents. Our pipeline cannot filter such contents.


                                                         3
162
      T-REx ElSahar et al. (2018) (CC BY-SA 4.0 DEED) is a large scale knowledge graph generated by
163   aligning Wikipedia paragraphs. Since it is aligned to Wikipedia, it has strong guarantee that all facts
164   contained by it are contained in the pre-training corpus of LLMs.
165
166
      Wikidata Vrandečić and Krötzsch (2014) (CC BY-SA 4.0 DEED) is a community maintained KG
      managing facts in Wikipedia, its sister project. To maximize its overlap with the pre-training corpuses
167
      of LLMs that we evaluate, we only include Wikidata entities with English Wikipedia pages associated
168
      to them. Despite this, there is no guarantee that facts in Wikidata can be backed by any Wikipedia
169   page. Note that T-REx is not a proper subset of Wikidata.

      2.4    M ETHODOLOGY LIMITATIONS
172
173   Exploitable correlations Knowledge graphs usually contain facts that are highly correlated to each
174   other, which LLMs can exploit to bypass recalling facts. For example, the predicates “country for
175   sport” and “country of citizenship” in Wikidata are highly likely to co-occur. Consequently, queries
176   with graph patterns such as “?x0 <country for sport> <United States> . ?x0
177   <country of citizenship> ?x1” can be answered by LLMs with high accuracy without
178   recalling any athletes that are member of a US sport team and hold US citizenship. We leave it for
179
      future work to filter such correlated predicates in our benchmark generation pipeline. In this case,
      however, the question still requires some commonsense reasoning.
180
181   Ground truth incompleteness LLMs can generate answers that are factually correct but may
182   not be included in the ground truth sets of our benchmarks. We find that LLMs’ performance is
183   comparable on the benchmark generated from T-REx with that generated from Wikidata, even though
184   Wikidata contains about 10 times more triples than T-REx and therefore is much less likely to suffer
185
      from ground truth incompleteness. This suggests that ground truth incompleteness may not be a
      foundational problem for our methodology. However, it will be worth studying further the impact of
186
      this.

189   3     K NOWLEDGEABILITY T RANSPARENCY
190
191   Definition We consider an LLM M to have knowledgeability transparency if its confidence in its
192   answers is proportional to its knowledgeability about questions. We define this mathematically as,
193
      given a question q and its answer a,
194                                        log Pr[a|q, M ] ∝ KM (q, a)                                    (1)
195
      where KM (q, a) denotes the knowledgeability of model M . Since we are primarily interested
196
      in employing LLMs to answer questions generatively, all distributions Pr[·|·] are text generation
197   distribution. For our benchmarks, where each question covers multiple facts, at least two definitions
198   of knowledgeability can be considered:
199                            X
200           Ksum (q, a) =         log Pr[a|f\a , M ]   Kmin (q, a) = min log Pr[a|f\a , M ]
                                                                          f ∈F (q,a)
201                          f ∈F (q,a)
202   where F (q, a) denotes the set of facts that are required to conclude that a is a correct answer
203   of q, and f\a denotes the question for what entities the fact f holds. For example, for the fact
204   <George Washington> <president> <United States> and the answer <George
205   Washington>, the question f\a could be “Who was a president of the United States?”
206
      Limitation and alternative The number of correct answers can lead to unexpected fluctuations of
207
      quantities in Definition 1. For example, consider the question “Who participated in both event A and
208   B?”, and suppose that the event A and B have two participants each and one participant X in common.
209   Since both events have two participants, an LLM that knows both events well is likely yield
210
211
          log Pr[“X”|“Who participated in event A?”, M ] ≈ log Pr[“X”|“. . . in event B?”, M ] ≤ log 1/2
212   Since the two events only have one common participant X, the same LLM is likely to yield
213
                       log Pr[“X”|“Who participated in both event A and B?”, M ] ≈ log 1
214
215   Consequently, log Pr[a|q, M ] = 1 > KM (q, a), making the LLM appear overconfident in its
      knowledgeability while it actually is not.


                                                        4
216
      As an alternative, we can replace log Pr[a|q, M ] with log Pr[Y |q̄a , M ], where q̄a denotes the
217   question if a is a correct answer to q, and T is some token expressing affirmation, such as
218   “Yes”, e.g. log Pr[“Yes”|“Did X participate in both event A and B?”, M ]. We can similarly replace
219   log Pr[a|f\a , M ] with log Pr[Y |f¯a , M ], e.g. log Pr[“Yes”|“Did X participate in event A?”, M ].
220   This results in the following metrics of knowledgeability:
221                             X
222           K̄sum (q, a) =         log Pr[Y |f¯a , M ]   K̄min (q, a) = min log Pr[Y |f¯a , M ]     (2)
                                                                              f ∈F (q,a)
223                         f ∈F (q,a)

224
      Importantly, the question q̄a and f¯a can both be generated by LLMs from underlying SPARQL
225
      queries, similar to the way the question q is generated. This makes these metrics feasible to evaluate.

472   6    C ONCLUSION
473
474   To address the limited understanding of LLMs’ reasoning capability in domains requiring substantial
475   real-world knowledge, we introduce a novel pipeline designed to automatically generate realistic,
476   knowledge-intensive question-answering benchmarks that necessitate intricate reasoning skills. By
477   leveraging open knowledge graphs, the graph query language SPARQL, and LLMs, our pipeline
478   eliminates the need for manual annotation, enabling scalability to unprecedented benchmark sizes
479   and knowledge coverage. The benchmarks generated by our pipeline are then used to evaluate
480
      several state-of-the-art LLMs, revealing their susceptibility to errors and even hallucinations. Despite
      attempts to mitigate these issues through techniques such as retrieval-augmented generation and
481
      chain-of-thoughts prompting, their effectiveness remains limited. Our unique benchmarks also
482
      facilitate an examination of how well LLMs’ confidence in their reasoning outcomes aligns with
483   their confidence in the underlying knowledge - a pioneering study in this field, to the best of our
484   knowledge.
