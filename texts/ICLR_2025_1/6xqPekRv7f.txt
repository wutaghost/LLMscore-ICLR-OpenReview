009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved great success in various tasks.
012            While LLMs can learn powerful capabilities from large datasets, they also inherit
013            the gender bias present in that data. Existing studies usually propose methods
014            to reduce bias by data cleaning and model retraining/fine-tuning. Although these
015            methods have shown some success, the cost of designing data and retraining/fine-
016            tuning an LLM increases significantly as the model size grows larger. Further-
017            more, a lack of understanding of the mechanisms behind gender bias prevents
018
               researchers from effectively tailoring solutions to address it. In this paper, we
               utilize mechanistic interpretability methods to construct the neuron circuits for
019
               gender bias cases and locate the important neurons storing gender bias. Then we
020
               propose the Interpretable Model Editing (Interpret-ME) method to reduce gen-
021            der bias without designing huge datasets or fine-tuning. Compared to fine-tuning
022            methods, our approach shows competitive results in reducing gender bias across
023            experiments with 8 LLMs. At the same time, our method does not affect the
024            performance in other tasks. Overall, our analysis is useful for understanding the
025            mechanism of gender bias and our method paves a potential way for reducing bias.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Transformer-based (Vaswani et al., 2017) large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020;
031   Ouyang et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023) have been successful in various downstream tasks.
032   They can acquire diverse abilities from large amounts of training data. However, they also learn,
033
      perpetuate, and amplify the biases present in the data, including those related to race (Blodgett,
      2021), gender (Bender et al., 2021), and religion (Li et al., 2020). If not addressed, these biases may
034
      pose unknown risks to society. Among these biases, gender bias is found to be the most ingrained
035
      and hardest to eliminate (Ranaldi et al., 2023), as it is often hidden in most sentences. Therefore,
036   mitigating gender bias in LLMs has become an increasingly important question recently.
037
038   Although many studies have explored reducing gender bias, three main questions remain unresolved.
039
      First, many previous studies use data augmentation (Webster et al., 2020; Pant & Dadu, 2022) and
      data filtering (Garimella et al., 2022; Borchers et al., 2022) methods to create balanced datasets.
040
      However, this process requires substantial human resources, as the size and quality of the dataset
041
      significantly affect the LLMs’ performance. Also, these methods can introduce factuality errors and
042   bring potential risks (Kumar et al., 2022). Second, most studies update the model parameters by
043   fine-tuning on specially designed datasets (Gira et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Ranaldi et al., 2023).
044   However, the computational expense of fine-tuning LLMs has become a significant concern, par-
045   ticularly as the size of LLMs continues to increase. At the same time, Gallegos et al. (2024) point
046   out that in-training methods risk corrupting the pre-trained language understanding due to catas-
047   trophic forgetting (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), as fine-tuning datasets are relatively small compared
048   to the original training data. This can impair the model’s overall performance. Third, the overall
049   localization and mechanisms by which LLMs store and produce gender bias are still unclear, pre-
050
      venting researchers from designing targeted mitigation solutions. Numerous studies (Zhao et al.,
      2023; Li et al., 2023; Gallegos et al., 2024) have pointed out that it is essential to increase LLMs’
051
      interpretability and understand which components of LLMs encode these biases.
052
053   In this paper, we propose the Interpretable Model Editing (Interpret-ME) method for reducing gen-
      der bias that addresses the aforementioned questions. Our method eliminates the need for designing

081

082
      FFN neurons’ top tokens are related to “woman” and the last tokens are related to “man”. Then
083   the transformed FFN neurons are transferred into last position and activating the deep FFN neurons
084   related to “woman”. Furthermore, we conduct analysis on “The guard is a => man” and find that
085   some neurons are important in both cases. The only difference is that the coefficient scores of these
086   neurons have opposite signs. Therefore, these neurons are “gender neurons” storing gender bias.
087
      Based on the interpretability analysis, we propose the Interpret-ME method with three stages, named
088
      locate-analyze-edit. Firstly, we only choose five gender bias cases for each gender, and then locate
089   the important neurons in these cases. Secondly, we analyze and filter the neurons distinguishing
090   “man” and “woman” in top/last tokens when projecting into unembedding space. Lastly, we edit
091   these neurons by shrinking the neurons’ coefficient scores. After editing these neurons, the stor-
092   age of gender bias is reduced. We compare our method with state-of-the-art fine-tuning methods
093   on 8 LLMs with parameters from 1.1B to 13B, and the experimental results demonstrate that our
094   Interpret-ME method perform better than theirs on all the models. At the same time, we evaluate
095   the edited models in four other common tasks and the performance does not drop compared with the
096   original models.
097   Overall, our contributions are as follows:
098
      a) We utilize interpretability methods to construct the neuron circuits of gender bias sentences and
099
      analyze the roles of different neurons. Our analysis is important for understanding the parameter
100
      storage and mechanism for LLMs to produce gender bias.
101
102   b) We propose the Interpretable Model Editing (Interpret-ME) method, which can reduce the gender
103   bias without designing new datasets or fine-tuning stages. By only editing a few neurons, the gender
104
      bias is reduced and the performance of other tasks remains unchanged.
105   c) Our experiments are conducted in 8 LLMs and 3 gender bias datasets. Our ablation study explores
106   the importance of different neurons, the number of edited neurons, and the difference of editing
107   methods. Our work provides an important guide for neuron-level model editing. The code and data
      will be published on github.

110   2.1   R EDUCING G ENDER B IAS IN LLM S
111
112   Many studies have explored methods to reduce the gender bias in LLMs based on data selec-
113   tion/argumentation. Liu et al. (2021) design matched pairs to argument the training data. Ghan-
114   barzadeh et al. (2023) generate new data by masking the gender words and predicting a new word
115   by another language model. Zayed et al. (2023) produce a method to extract and argument the most
116   important gender sentences. Garimella et al. (2022) and Borchers et al. (2022) design methods to
117
      filter the low-gender sentences. Han et al. (2021) and Orgad & Belinkov (2022) propose methods to
      compute the importance of sentences and re-weight all the sentences.
118
119   Another type of studies focus on modifying model architectures. Lauscher et al. (2021) utilize
120   adapter modules (Houlsby et al., 2019) to mitigate gender bias. Han et al. (2021) propose a gate
121   module to help the model take protected attributes into consideration. Also, many studies (Gaci
122   et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Woo et al., 2023) propose methods by modifying the loss functions,
123
      which can encourage the model to generate de-biasing outputs.
124
125
      2.2   M ECHANISTIC I NTERPRETABILITY FOR L ANGUAGE M ODELS
126
      The goal of mechanistic interpretability is to reverse engineer the internal circuit from inputs to
127   outputs, thereby helping to understand the mechanisms of language models. Elhage et al. (2021)
128   find that the induction heads are the main roles for predictions like [A][B]...[A] => [B]. Olsson
129   et al. (2022) investigate the induction heads and find that these heads may be important for in-context
130   learning. Meng et al. (2022) utilize causal mediation analysis method (Vig et al., 2020) to identify
131   the important hidden states in GPT and find that the medium feed-forward network (FFN) layers are
132   significant for storing factual knowledge. Geva et al. (2023) find a three-step internal mechanism
133   for attribute extraction in factual knowledge. A common interpretability method for analyzing the
134   internal vectors is to project them into the unembedding space Geva et al. (2022); Dar et al. (2022).
135   Recently, several studies try to locate the important neurons in LLMs, as numerous studies (Nanda
136   et al.; Lieberum et al., 2023; Stolfo et al., 2023) point out finding the important neurons is of great
137   significance for understanding the mechanism and knowledge storage in LLMs. Geva et al. (2022)
138   find that the FFN neurons are interpretable when analyzing in unembedding space. Yu & Ananiadou
139   (2023) propose a method to identify the deep layers’ “value neurons” directly contributing to the
140   predictions, and the shallow layers’ “query neurons” contributing by activating the “value neurons”.

265   3.3   I NTERPRETABLE M ODEL E DITING
266
267   Based on the interpretability analysis in Section 3.2, we hypothesize that: a) Gender bias parameters
268   are stored in both query neurons and value neurons. b) The coefficient scores of these neurons can
269   affect the probabilities of different genders. c) The query neurons are more important for gender
      bias if their transformed vectors’ top/last tokens are “woman”/“man” or “man”/“woman”.

270
      According to the hypothesis, we propose the Interpretable Model Editing (Interpret-ME) method to
271   mitigate the gender bias in LLMs. In order to identify the important neurons, we choose only 5
272   gender bias sentences for each gender like “The XX is a” (XX is a profession), shown in Appendix
273   B. Our method has three steps, named locate-analyze-edit. First, we locate the topM FFN value
274   neurons, topN attention value neurons and topP FFN query neurons for each sentence, and calculate
275   each neuron’s important score averaged on all 5 sentences for each gender. In this step, we get
276   M+N+P neurons for “man” and M+N+P neurons for “woman”. Due to superposition (Elhage et al.,
277   2022), several query neurons not only affect gender bias performance but are also important for other
278   tasks. Therefore, we then analyze and filter the query neurons whose transformed vectors’ top/last
279
      tokens are opposite about “man”/“woman”. Last, we edit these filtered neurons by shrinking their
      coefficient scores. Specifically, we design two editing methods, which we called zero-editing and
280
      division-editing. In zero-editing, we replace the neuron’s subvalue (f c2lk in Eq.5) with a zero vector
281
      having the same dimension. In division-editing, we divide the subvalue’s each dimension by a
282   constant score D. This method has the same result with dividing the neuron’s coefficient score by D.
283
284   Advantages. As introduced in Section 3.3, our method only requires 10 sentences and do not require
285
      fine-tuning. The computational cost for identifying the important neurons can be done within 3
      minutes in Llama-7B. Furthermore, the neuron analyzing and filtering stage helps us understand the
286
      mechanism more deeply. Hence, our method can solve the three questions mentioned in Section 1.

304   three sentences in the sentence pair, this sentence is “chosen” by the model. We follow the orig-

306

309   this situation, the model chooses 50% stereotyped answers, 50% anti-stereotyped answers, and 0%
310   nonsensical answers. The ICAT score is the product of LMS and Normalized SS (NSS):

452   5   D ISCUSSION : WHY ARE NEURON CIRCUITS IMPORTANT ?
453
454   In this section, we aim to discuss the importance of constructing the neuron circuits.
455   a) More precise parameter localization can help preserve a model’s existing capabilities dur-
456   ing model editing. Each layer in a LLM contains thousands of neurons, with different neurons
457   potentially contributing to various tasks or word representations. As we discuss in Section 4.4,
458   even editing just two neurons can significantly impact the model’s abilities, as changes in shallow
459   neurons can propagate and influence deeper ones. Therefore, model editing should be approached
460   with caution. Our Interpret-ME method offers a neuron-level editing approach. When we observe a
461   performance decline in the edited model, we can analyze the neurons individually to identify which
462
      ones are critical for other tasks and restore those neurons to mitigate the impact.
463   b) Neuron circuits provide deeper insights into underlying mechanisms. Due to the phenomenon
464   of superposition (Elhage et al., 2022), directly analyzing individual neurons makes it challenging to
465   determine their specific roles. Most interpretability methods involve projecting neurons into un-
466   embedding space, where each neuron is associated with certain “top tokens”. However, this can be
467   misleading, as not all neurons directly contribute to the final predictions. If we project query neurons
468
      into unembedding space without the transformation by attention heads, the resulting top tokens may
      not accurately reflect the neurons’ actual function. Additionally, when encountering a neuron, it is
469
      unclear whether it functions as a query neuron or a value neuron, nor is it evident which attention
470
      head is performing the transformation. Therefore, a more effective approach is to identify neuron
471   circuits across diverse contexts and assess the significance of neurons in specific tasks or multiple
472   scenarios. In this situation, the functions of the identified neurons are clear. By examining these
473   circuits across various sentences, we can more precisely determine the function of each neuron.

      6   C ONCLUSION
477
      Although LLMs gain powerful abilities from large amounts of data, they can also learn, perpetu-
478
      ate, and amplify biases. In this paper, we propose the Interpretable Model Editing (Interpret-ME)
479
      method to mitigate gender bias in LLMs without designing new datasets or fine-tuning. Based on
480   interpretability analysis of gender-biased sentences, we find that several neurons contain much gen-
481   der bias. Our Interpret-ME method has three stages: neuron locating, neuron analyzing and filtering,
482   and neuron editing. We conduct experiments on 8 LLMs using three gender bias datasets, and our
483   method shows competitive performance compared to fine-tuning methods. Additionally, we perform
484   experiments on four general tasks and find that our method does not compromise their performance.
485   Overall, our method and analysis are crucial for understanding the mechanism of gender bias and
      offer a potential solution for mitigating the gender bias.
