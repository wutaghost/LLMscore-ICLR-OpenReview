010
011
               Tools for analyzing character portrayal in fiction are valuable for writers and literary schol-
012            ars in developing and interpreting compelling stories. Existing tools, such as visualization
013            tools for analyzing fictional characters, primarily rely on explicit textual indicators of char-
014            acter attributes. However, portrayal is often implicit, revealed through actions and behav-
015            iors rather than explicit statements. We address this gap by leveraging large language
016            models (LLMs) to uncover implicit character portrayals. We start by generating a dataset
017            for this task with greater cross-topic similarity, lexical diversity, and narrative lengths than
018            existing narrative text corpora such as TinyStories and WritingPrompts. We then intro-
019            duce LIIPA (LLMs for Inferring Implicit Portrayal for Character Analysis), a framework
020
               for prompting LLMs to uncover character portrayals. LIIPA can be configured to use var-
               ious types of intermediate computation (character attribute word lists, chain-of-thought)
021
               to infer how fictional characters are portrayed in the source text. We find that LIIPA out-
022
               performs existing approaches, and is more robust to increasing character counts (number
023            of unique persons depicted) due to its ability to utilize full narrative context. Lastly, we
024            investigate the sensitivity of portrayal estimates to character demographics, identifying
025            a fairness-accuracy tradeoff among methods in our LIIPA framework – a phenomenon
026            familiar within the algorithmic fairness literature. Despite this tradeoff, all LIIPA vari-
027            ants consistently outperform non-LLM baselines in both fairness and accuracy. Our work
028            demonstrates the potential benefits of using LLMs to analyze complex characters and to
029            better understand how implicit portrayal biases may manifest in narrative texts.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Computational tools for analyzing character portrayal in narratives facilitate bias detection in literary fiction
035   (Lucy & Bamman, 2021; Fast et al., 2016) and AI-generated narratives (Huang et al., 2021). They also assist
036   writers and literary scholars in refining their story drafts and character analyses (Hoque et al., 2023). Most of
037   these existing tools rely on using explicit indicators in the text to uncover how a character is portrayed. How-
038   ever, portrayal is usually implicit, where a character’s traits should be clear from their actions and behaviours
039
      rather than explicitly stated in the text (Chekhov & Yarmolinsky, 1954). For instance, “She was stranded
      on an island and built a boat to escape”, which implicitly suggests high intelligence and resourcefulness.
040
      Uncovering implicit portrayal is more challenging than explicit portrayal, as it requires using commonsense
041
      knowledge to make inferences about how a character is portrayed. It becomes even more difficult to uncover
042   from longer narratives depicting multiple distinct characters. Moreover, implicit portrayal is a key principle
043   of literary design (Rimmon-Kenan, 2002) so it is concerning that most existing tools focus on visualizing
044   characters using explicit indicators of portrayal. Furthermore, the evaluation of new methods for implicit
045   portrayal is difficult due to the reliance of existing benchmarks on explicit character behavior to derive target
046   labels (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016).

      Prior approaches to implicit character portrayal use the “Commonsense Transformer” (COMET) (Bosselut
071
      et al., 2019), a generative model for knowledge bases over text, to infer the mental state and motivations of
072   protagonists (Huang et al., 2021; 2024). These methods are constrained by COMET’s limitations in that it
073   can only process simple event structures and cannot utilize long context lengths.
074
075
      In this paper, we use LLMs to build expressive models for uncovering implicit character portrayal. We
      start by generating the first benchmark dataset designed specifically for this task. Compared with existing
076
      narrative text corpora such as TinyStories and WritingPrompts, our dataset offers greater cross-topic simi-
077
      larity, greater lexical diversity, and a broader representation of character roles. We then introduce a family
078   of LLM prompting techniques that outperform the COMET-based approach for implicit character portrayal.
079   We explore different prompting design choices, including the use of an intermediate character attribute list
080   (as in COMET) to describe characters. We find that LLMs are more performant than the previous approach,
081   although we also identify a fairness-accuracy tradeoff within LLM-based approaches. This suggests that
082   care (beyond picking the “optimal” prompt) is required when designing socially beneficial tools for literary
083   analysis.
084   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
085
086
             • We introduce ImPortPrompts (Implicit Portrayal Prompts), a dataset for implicit character por-
087
               trayal analysis with better diversity and coverage than existing benchmarks.
088
089          • We propose LIIPA (LLMs for Inferring Implicit Portrayal for character Analysis), a frame-
090            work for prompting LLMs to uncover character portrayals that outperforms COMET-based ap-
091            proaches. LIIPA has three variants: LIIPA- SENTENCE, LIIPA- STORY, and LIIPA- DIRECT each
092            corresponding to a different form of intermediate computation (e.g. character attribute wordlist,
093            chain of thought) used for estimating portrayal.

102   2.1       TASK F ORMULATION
103
      We formulate the task of uncovering character portrayal from text as a multi-label classification problem.
104
      Our objective is to develop a function that maps an input narrative text and a specific character1 from that
105   text to a set of labels across three dimensions: intellect, appearance, and power (Figure 2). Each dimension
106   is classified as either low, neutral, or high, with the “neutral” label reserved for cases where the text provides
107   insufficient information to make a definitive inference about the character’s portrayal. Formally, we aim to
108                                                                                               (i)
      learn a function f : X × C → Y that takes a narrative text x(i) ∈ X and a character cj ∈ C from that text,
109                                        (i)
      and produces a set of labels yj ∈ Y representing the character’s portrayal. The label space Y is defined as
110
      the Cartesian product of the label spaces for each dimension: Y = Yintellect × Yappearance × Ypower , where each
111
      dimension’s label space consists of the values {low, neutral, high}.

126   We select intellect, appearance, and power as our dimensions for character portrayal, as these have been
127   previously studied in relation to social biases when analysing AI-generated narratives (Lucy & Bamman,
128   2021) and in comparison to human-written narratives (Huang et al., 2021; 2024). Our methods, however,
129
      can be readily adapted to other aspects of character portrayal such as emotional depth and moral alignment.
      We define “intellect” synonymously with logical-mathematical intelligence, as described by Patanella et al.
130
      (2011): “the ability to think conceptually and abstractly, and the capacity to discern logical and numerical
131
      patterns.” Our definitions for appearance and power are less ambiguous and are detailed in Table 2 (§A.1),
132   along with classification guidelines for what constitutes low, neutral, or high portrayal. Throughout this
133   work, we abbreviate intellect, appearance, and power as IAP.

136
      intellect would be classified as exhibiting high intelligence overall. Unlike prior works (Huang et al., 2021;
137
      Brahman & Chaturvedi, 2020) that focus solely on protagonists, our formulation allows for the analysis
138   of any character within the narrative. Note that our formulation here is designed to be flexible and gener-

      2.2   DATASET C URATION M ETHODOLOGY
145
146   To curate a dataset for the implicit character portrayal classification task, we use LLMs to generate narrative
147   texts under a set of controlled conditions such as character count (number of unique persons depicted) and
148   narrative length. This approach allows us to increase the diversity of synthetically generated narratives while
149   reducing representational biases that pertain to the controlled conditions (Yu et al., 2023). We formulate this
150   process as generating a narrative text x(i) subject to a set of natural language constraints Ci . Below, we
151   describe our constraints which are added as natural language instructions to the LLM prompt. A full, detailed
152   list can be found in §A.3. We refer to this dataset as ImPortPrompts (Implicit Portrayal Prompts).
153                                                                                (i)
      Natural Language Constraints: The narrative must contain exactly Nc characters and have a length of
154
      L(i) sentences. Each character should be assigned a role from {protagonist, antagonist, victim} and be given
155                                     (i)
156
      a character portrayal label set yj ∈ Y. To ensure implicit portrayal, each character must be portrayed im-
      plicitly through their actions, decisions, and interactions, rather than through explicit words and statements.
157
      The narrative should avoid using words that directly describe a character’s intellect (e.g., intelligent, stupid,
158
      clever), appearance (e.g., beautiful, ugly, attractive), or power (e.g., powerful, weak, strong). The socio-
159   demographic background of characters should not be stated or implied. This includes using gender-neutral
160   names, avoiding mentions of racial characteristics, religious affiliations, and socioeconomic status. Refer-
161   ences to age, physical attributes, or cultural backgrounds that might reveal demographic information should
162   also be omitted. The narrative genre and topic are selected from Table 4 (§A.4).
163
      Our choice of character roles are motivated by prior works that develop automated methods for character role
164
      detection (Gomez-Zara et al., 2018) and extraction (Stammbach et al., 2022) in narratives. Our definitions
165   for these roles can be found in Table 3 (§A.1) which allow for multiple protagonists and antagonists. Our
166   socio-demographic constraint facilitates the measurement of fairness by eliminating socio-demographic in-
167   formation from the generated narratives as will be discussed in Section 3.2. The socio-demographic groups
168   we use come from Gupta et al. (2024) which we repeat verbatim in Table 18 (§C.2).
169
      Experimental Setup: We follow a similar setup to Perez et al. (2023) and use LLMs as narrative text gen-
170
      erators that generate x(i) subject to constraints Ci . We can describe the ImPortPrompts generation process
171
      as sampling from a model subject to constraints Ci : x(i) | Ci ∼ pg (·|Ci ), where pg refers to the model we
172
      use to generate narratives, and Ci = (y (i) , Ci′ ) with Ci′ representing the remaining constraints apart from
173
      the label set constraint. Our choices of pg are the GPT (OpenAI, 2024) and Claude (Anthropic, 2024) LLM
174
      families. We apply the tree-of-thoughts (ToT) prompting strategy to creative writing as done by Yao et al.
175   (2023) to generate narratives that are both more coherent and more likely to satisfy the constraints (prompts
176   in § A.5). We also condition the narrative generation on a randomly sampled (genre, title) tuple from Table 4
177   (§A.4) which increases narrative diversity and helps to eliminate representation bias of topics in our data
178   set. For quality assurance, we perform automated and human checks to ensure the LLM-generated narra-
179   tives satisfy the natural language constraints outlined earlier, filtering out any narratives that fail our checks.
180   Details of our data validation process can be found in §A.6.

      3.1        LIIPA: LLM S FOR I NFERRING I MPLICIT P ORTRAYAL FOR C HARACTER A NALYSIS

260   We start by comparing the efficacy of LLMs against the method of Huang et al. (2021), which used
261   COMET (Bosselut et al., 2019) as an auxiliary model to infer implicit character portrayal. We refer to
262   this baseline as COMET-Implicit Character Portrayal (COMET-ICP). We hypothesize that LLMs of-
263   fer superior performance in this task because of their comprehensive world knowledge acquired through
264   extensive pretraining and their capacity to effectively leverage long-range context for making predictions.
265   COMET is limited to processing sentences with a simple event structure and generating a set of attributes
266   describing the subject of the sentence. For instance, given the sentence: “Alice gave Bob a cup of coffee.”,
267
      it may output the character attribtue word list ẑw = {generous, kind, thoughtful}.
268   Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup: We compare three LLM-based approaches against
269   COMET-ICP (Figure 4). With LIIPA- STORY and LIIPA- SENTENCE, we prompt the LLM to generate
270   character attribute word lists (ẑw ) from complete stories and individual sentences respectively, mirroring
271   COMET’s output format. With LIIPA- DIRECT, we prompt the LLM to directly classify character portrayal
272   based on the entire narrative, bypassing the wordlist generation. We refer to this entire framework as LIIPA.
273   Our choice of classification LLM is Google’s Gemini (Georgiev et al., 2024). We choose a different LLM
274   family from those used to generate narratives (GPT/Claude) to avoid self-preference bias, a phenomenon
275   where LLM evaluators recognize and favor their own generations (Panickssery et al.). All prompts for this
276   section can be found in §B.1. To ensure reproducible results, we set the LLM temperature parameter to 0
277   for all experiments in this section.
278   To assess the quality of generated character attribute wordlists for uncovering a character’s IAP, we use
279   LLMs-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023) which involves prompting a separate evaluation LLM to infer a char-
280   acter’s IAP solely from the generated wordlist. The underlying premise is that a high-quality wordlist should
281   contain enough relevant information to enable accurate IAP inference. By applying LLMs-as-a-judge to

322

328   suggesting that the methods are better at detecting implicit cues of positive character portrayal.

      to increases in character count, suggesting that having full narrative information is beneficial for stabilizing
356   performance across an increasing number of characters. In contrast, we see that the contextual methods
357   have a stronger negative correlation with the number of sentences, indicating that longer narratives can
358   actually hurt performance. This might be due to an “information overload,” where the model has to process
359   and integrate a larger amount of information when making its classification decision. The non-contextual
360   models don’t suffer as much from this issue, perhaps because of their sentence-level processing.
361
      These plots further illustrate the wide performance gap between the LIIPA- DIRECT method and the other
362   methods, demonstrating the utility of bypassing wordlist generation and simply prompting an LLM to un-
363   cover character portrayal. In all cases (except for 1-character narratives), the contextual methods outperform
364   the non-contextual methods, highlighting the importance of having full narrative context for uncovering
365   character portrayal.

444
445   Character Portrayal Tools: Visualization tools for character portrayal have demonstrated significant bene-
446   fits for both writers and literary scholars. Hoque et al. (2023) developed Portrayal, a character visualization
447   tool, and conducted a user study revealing its effectiveness in helping writers revise drafts and create more
448
      dynamic characters. The tool also aided scholars in developing tangible evidence to support literary argu-
      ments. In a separate study, Hoque et al. (2022) interviewed writers and found they struggle to track implicit
449
      character biases, especially in longer, more complex narratives. This insight led to the development of Dra-
450
      matVis Personae, a tool designed to help writers more easily identify various biases, including those related
451   to character portrayal. Our LIIPA framework can enhance these tools by providing writers with deeper
452   insights into the implicit portrayal of their characters.
453
454
      Measuring Fairness: Our approach to fairness measurement focuses on disparate accuracy across demo-
      graphic groups, a method previously used in studies examining implicit biases in LLMs (Gupta et al., 2024),
455
      and character portrayal in AI-generated texts (Huang et al., 2021). To facilitate this analysis, we used LLMs
456
      to insert demographic information into narratives. This technique aligns with recent work: Tamkin et al.
457   used LLMs to add demographic attributes to LLM-generated data for evaluating discrimination, while Perez
458   et al. (2023) used LLMs to generate evaluation data for uncovering harmful model behaviors.

      5   C ONCLUSION
462
      We have proposed a new framework called LIIPA that uses LLMs to infer implicit character portrayal
463
      within narrative text. LIIPA outperforms non-LLM character portrayal estimation in both accuracy and
464
      fairness while being robust to longer texts with more characters. However, the identified fairness-accuracy
465   tradeoff underscores the need for cautious application of LLMs when estimating character portrayal. We also
466   introduced ImPortPrompts, a dataset for character portrayal estimation that offers improved diversity and
467   greater cross-topic similarity over existing benchmarks. Future work can apply LIIPA to better understand
468   how implicit portrayal biases manifest in narratives and to improve portrayal visualization tools such as those
469   developed by Hoque et al. (2023).

      A.6     DATA VALIDATION P ROCEDURES
894
895   We perform quality assurance on our dataset to ensure the LLM-generated narratives comply with the con-
896   straints outlined in §A.3. We address both lexical and semantic constraints through automated and human
897   validation processes, respectively. Narratives failing either check are excluded from the final dataset.
898
      Automated Validation: We programmatically verify character count and narrative length constraints using
899
      scripts available in our code repository. Characters follow a fixed structure (ProtagonistX/AntagonistX/Vic-
900   timX, X being a number), which facilitates easy extraction. We partially perform semantic validation by
901   using exclusion word lists to ensure narratives do not contain explicit portrayal indicators and demographic
902   information.
903
      Human Validation: We perform manual validation to ensure characters align with their assigned roles and
904
      are implicitly portrayed as per ground truth labels. We verify genre and title constraints, and confirm the
905
      absence of explicit or implicit demographic information. We manually validate a random subset of 100
906   narratives using instructions from §A.6.1, consistent with prior works (Dammu et al., 2024; Dahl et al.,
907   2024) validating LLM-generated data.
908
909
      Qualitative analysis reveals strong adherence to assigned genres, character roles, and portrayal constraints,
      with minimal demographic information leakage. While most narratives successfully avoid explicit portrayal
910
      indicators, some instances present borderline cases between implicit and explicit indicators (e.g., “their name
911
      carrying weight” or “charismatic wedding planner” for high power). However, overt explicit indicators
912   remain rare.
