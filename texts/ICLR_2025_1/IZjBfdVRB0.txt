009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has gained popularity for fine-tuning large founda-
012            tion models, leveraging low-rank matrices A and B to represent weight changes
013            (i.e., ∆W = BA). This method reduces trainable parameters and mitigates heavy
014            memory consumption associated with full delta matrices by sequentially multiply-
015            ing A and B with the activation. Despite its success, the intrinsic low-rank charac-
016            teristic may limit its performance. Although several variants have been proposed
017            to address this issue, they often overlook the crucial computational and mem-
018
               ory efficiency brought by LoRA. In this paper, we propose Circular Convolution
               Adaptation (C3 A), which not only achieves high-rank adaptation with enhanced
019
               performance but also excels in both computational power and memory utilization.
020
               Extensive experiments demonstrate that C3 A consistently outperforms LoRA and
021            its variants across various fine-tuning tasks.

066   To address this question, we introduce Circular Convolution Adaptation (C3 A), which incorporates
067   the circular convolution operator (Bamieh, 2018). Circular convolution has garnered significant at-
068   tention in both signal processing (Li et al., 2020) and cryptography (Dworkin et al., 2001) due to its
069   exceptional efficiency and compactness. This operator can be equivalently expressed as multiplica-
070   tion by a circulant matrix, providing rank flexibility that is independent of the number of trainable
071   parameters. Furthermore, by employing the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), C3 A achieves superior
072
      time and memory efficiency compared to the direct multiplication of the circulant matrix (Bamieh,
      2018), which makes it competitive with LoRA in terms of efficiency.
073
074   In addition, as explicated by Dosovitskiy et al. (2020), dense linear layers exhibit a deficiency of
075   inductive biases, engendering a complex optimization landscape. Consequently, this hampers the
076   effectiveness of transformers in comparison to Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) under con-
077   ditions of limited data availability. Within the framework of a constrained training dataset for the
078
      downstream task, we postulate that a robust inductive bias could potentially augment adaptiation
      performance. The circular pattern in C3 A serves precisely as such an inductive bias.
079
080   In summary, circular convolution presents a promising solution for circumventing the rank limita-
081   tions of LoRA at minimal costs. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
082   ❶ We introduce C3 A, a novel approach for PEFT. This method leverages the circular convolution
083   operation and its equivalent circulant matrix to provide a flexible rank, which is free of linear con-
084   straint by the number of trainable parameters, for the delta matrix.
085
      ❷ Leveraging the elegant diagonalization of the circulant matrix, we implement both the forward
086
      pass and backpropagation using FFT. With the incorporation of FFT, the computation and memory
087
      efficiency of C3 A excels. C3 A strikes a unique balance between performance an efficiency.
088
089   ❸ To offer greater flexibility in controlling the number of trainable parameters, we extend C3 A by
090   incorporating block-circular convolution, which results in block-circulant matrices. This extension
091
      allows C3 A to achieve fully customizable parameter counts as well as adaptable rank configurations.
092   ❹ We validate C3 A through comprehensive fine-tuning experiments across diverse tasks including
093   natural language understanding, instruction tuning and image classification. Experiments demon-
094   strate C3 A’s outstanding accuracy and memory merits compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

098   2.1   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
099
100   Research on PEFT has generally progressed along three main directions. The first direction involves
101   partially updating the pre-trained neural network (e.g., the layer norm (Basu et al., 2024) or the
102   biases (Zaken et al., 2021)). Traditional methods relied on hand-crafted heuristics (Raghu et al.,
103   2019) to identify which parameters are crucial and should be fine-tuned. More advanced approaches
104
      employ optimization techniques (Guo et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2023). For example, Guo
      et al. (2020) reformulated such a discrete optimization problem into a continuous one by employing
105
      Bernoulli masks and the Gumbel-softmax approximation (Jang et al., 2016).
106
107   The second direction emerged to maintain the integrity of the pre-trained model while enabling a
      high degree of parameter sharing through adapter-based methods (He et al., 2021; Rebuffi et al.,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2017; Rücklé et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Lian et al., 2022). These works focus on integrating
109   additional modules, termed adapters, to fit the downstream task, effectively decoupling the pre-
110   trained model parameters from those specific to the downstream task. Prompt Tuning (Brown et al.,
111   2020; Gao et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) and Prefix Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021;
112   Jia et al., 2022) also fall into this category, despite ignoring potential semantic meanings.
113
      The final direction is characterized by delta-weight-based methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation
114
      (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) and Orthogonal Fine-tuning (OFT) (Qiu et al., 2023). These methods
115   bridge the gap between the pre-trained model and the downstream task by adaptive delta weights,
116   which are stored separately while used in combination with the pre-trained weights. This unique
117   design enables disentanglement of the pretrained and downstream-specific weights. Namely, it
118   achieves parameter sharing and preserves the ability to integrate without additional inference cost.
119   LoRA models the delta-weights by an additive matrix while OFT does it by a multiplicative one.
120   To further improve either parameter efficiency or performance, many variants has been proposed for
121   both of the methods (Kopiczko et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Hayou et al.,
122   2024b; Gao et al., 2024). However, these methods can hardly achieve high parameter efficiency and
123
      performance without incurring heavy computation and memory usage.
124
125   2.2   C IRCULAR C ONVOLUTION
126
127   Circular convolution has been extensively studied in signal processing (Rabiner et al., 1978;
128
      McGillem & Cooper, 1984; Li et al., 2020) and cryptography (Dworkin et al., 2001; Gong et al.,
      2024). Owing to its computational advantages, circular convolution has also been explored in
129
      machine learning for generating long embeddings of high-dimensional data (Yu et al., 2014) and
130
      compressing heavily parameterized layers (Cheng et al., 2015; Ding et al., 2017). Remarkably, it
131   achieves these efficiencies without significant performance degradation, which makes it a promising
132   technique for fine-tuning applications.
133
134
      Despite its success in small neural networks such as LeNet (Cheng et al., 2015), circular convolution
      has not demonstrated lossless performance in modern large foundational models (LFMs) or even in
135
      their base architecture, the transformer. This limitation may be attributed to the conflict between its
136
      high intrinsic bias (i.e., the circulant pattern) and the vast amount of data required for training LFMs.
137   Conversely, when fine-tuning LFMs, it is often impractical to collect as much data as needed for
138   training from scratch. In such scenarios, the intrinsic bias of circular convolution could potentially
139   serve as a regularization mechanism, thereby benefiting the optimization process of fine-tuning.

144   In this section, we present C3 A (see an overview in Figure 2), a novel PEFT approach based on
145   the circular convolution. C3 A follows LoRA’s setting of learning an additive linear operation over
146   the original dense linear transformation. However, instead of using low-rank decomposition and the
147
      matrix multiplication operator, C3 A resorts to circular convolution as this additive linear operation.
      Section 3.1 introduces the notations we use. Section 3.2 discusses the circular convolution operator,
148
      its equivalent circulant matrix, and its calculation in the frequency domain. Section 3.3 details an
149
      efficient method for backpropagation. Section 3.4 describes block-wise convolution for controlling
150   the number of trainable parameters. Finally, Section 3.5 analyzes the computational complexity.

482   5            C ONCLUSION
483
484   In this manuscript, we present C3 A, a novel method for Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT).
485   In contrast to LoRA, which employs low-rank decomposition, C3 A leverages circular convolution
      and its equavelent circulant matrix to represent the delta weight matrix. This methodology aims


                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      to independently control the rank of the delta weight matrix and the number of trainable parame-
487   ters, facilitating high-rank adaptation while preserving a constrained parameter size. Using the Fast
488   Fourier Transform (FFT) during both the forward and backward propagation phases, C3 A attains
489   notable computational and memory efficiency. In short, C3 A emerges as a persuasive alternative to
490   LoRA for model fine-tuning.

      Algorithm A1 Block-Circular Convolution PyTorch Implementation
814
815   import torch
      from torch.autograd import Function
816   from torch.fft import fft, ifft
817   class BlockCircularConvolution(Function):
818       @staticmethod
          def forward(ctx, x, w):
819           m, n, b = w.shape
              x = x.reshape(*x.shape[:-1], n, b)
820           ctx.save_for_backward(x, w)
821           x = torch.einsum( "...nb,mnb->...mb", ifft(x), fft(w) )
              x = fft(x).real
822           x = x.reshape(*x.shape[:-2], -1)
              return x
823
824       @staticmethod
          def backward(ctx, grad_output):
825           x, w = ctx.saved_tensors
              m, n, b = w.shape
826           grad_output = grad_output.reshape(*grad_output.shape[:-1], m, b)
827           grad_output_fft = fft(grad_output)
              x_grad = fft(torch.einsum( "...mb,mnb->...nb", grad_output_fft, ifft(w))).real
828           x_grad = x_grad.reshape(*x_grad.shape[:-2], -1)
              w_grad = fft(torch.einsum( "...mb,...nb->mnb", grad_output_fft, ifft(x))).real
829           return x_grad, w_grad
830

832
      We present the PyTorch implementation of Block-Circular Convolution in Algorithm A1. Further-
      more, due to the inefficiency of directly assigning entries (as shown in Equation 3), we derive an
833
      alternative algorithm to compute the ∆W more efficiently. Rather than direct assignment, we em-
834
      ploy a forward process on the Identity matrix. Mathematically, this can be expressed as
835
836                        ∆W = Cblk (∆w)
837                           = Cblk (∆w) · Id2
838                           = Cblk (∆w) · [e1 , e2 , · · · , ed2 ]
839
                              = [Cblk (∆w)e1 , Cblk (∆w)e2 , · · · , Cblk (∆w)ed2 ]
840
                              = [∆w ⋆ e1 , ∆w ⋆ e2 , · · · , ∆w ⋆ ed2 ] .
841
842   Where Id2 ∈ Rd2 ×d2 represents an Identity matrix and ei is the ith column of it. In pytorch, we
843   can efficiently compute the iFFT of {ei }i=1,2,··· ,d2 by a column-wise iFFT of Id2 . We present the
844   Pytorch implementation in Algorithm A2 as well.

      Algorithm A2 Fast Algorithm of Getting ∆W
847
848   import torch
      from torch.fft import fft, ifft
849
850   def get_circulant_fast(w):
          m, n, b = w.shape
851       x = torch.eye(n*b, dtype=w.dtype, device=w.device)
          x = x.reshape(*x.shape[:-1], n, b)
852       x = torch.einsum( "...nb,mnb->...mb", ifft(x), fft(w) )
853       x = fft(x).real.flatten(start_dim=1).T
          return x
