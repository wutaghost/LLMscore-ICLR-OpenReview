009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The connectionist temporal classification (CTC) training criterion optimizes the
012            conditional log probability of the label sequence given the input, which involves a
013            sum over all possible alignment label sequences including blank. It is well known
014            that CTC training leads to peaky behavior where blank is predicted in most frames
015            and the labels are focused mostly on single frames. Thus, CTC is suboptimal to
016            obtain accurate word boundaries. Hidden Markov models (HMMs) can be seen as
017            a generalization of CTC and trained in the same way with a generalized training
018            criterion, and may lead to similar problems. Label units such as subword units and
019            its vocabulary size or phoneme-based units also significantly impact the alignment
020
               quality. Here we study different methods of obtaining an alignment with the goals
               to improve alignment quality while keeping a good performing model, and to
021
               gain better understanding of the training dynamics. We introduce (1) a synthetic
022
               framework to study alignment behavior, and compare various models, noise and
023            training conditions, (2) a new training variant with renormalizing the gradients to
024            counteract the class imbalance of blank, (3) a novel CTC model variation to use a
025            hierarchical softmax and separating the blank label in CTC, as another alternative
026            to counteract class imbalance, (4) a novel way to get alignments via the gradients
027            of the label log probabilities w.r.t. the input features. This method can be used
028            for all kinds of models, and we evaluate it for CTC and attention-based encoder-
029            decoder (AED) subword based models where it performs competitive and more
030            robustly, although phoneme-based HMMs still provide the best alignments.
031

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Current sequence-to-sequence models (Prabhavalkar et al., 2023) such as connectionist temporal
036   classification (CTC) (Graves et al., 2006) can be trained from-scratch using the sequence-level cross-
037   entropy and summing over all alignments. However, CTC alignments tend to be dominated by
038   blanks, causing a peaky behavior (Zeyer et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2024), which can be suboptimal
039   to obtain good alignments.
040
      CTC can be seen as a special case in the broader hidden Markov model (HMM) framework with
041
      a simplified label topology including the blank label instead of silence, without label priors and
042   without explicit transition probabilities (Zeyer et al., 2017; Hadian et al., 2018; Raissi et al., 2022;
043   Zhao & Bell, 2022). When hybrid neural network (NN)-HMMs are trained with the sum over all
044   alignment paths, when no prior and no transition probabilities are used, the same peaky behavior
045   occurs (Zeyer et al., 2017; 2021). Previous work addressed the issue of peaky behavior by using
046   priors during training (Zeyer et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). When a prior is
047   used, it can also switch to the opposite extreme with no silence at all and very bad alignments (Zeyer
048   et al., 2017; Raissi et al., 2022).
049   It is known that the Gaussian mixture hidden Markov model (GM-HMM) alignments offer more
050   reliable segment and word boundaries. An input feature representation that is obtained from current
051   neural architectures that use self-attention or recurrent layers is substantially different from the one
052   used in GMM. The neural encoder has the freedom to displace the output label with respect to their
053   ground truth position. It can shift and compress parts of the signal, or even reverse the signal in
      the time dimension (Schmitt et al., 2024). This expressive capacity ultimately also allows for peaky


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      behavior and otherwise potential bad alignment quality. Thus, training dynamics and the training
055   criterion play an important role for the alignment behavior and quality.
056
057   Here we study different variations of the training criterion and training procedure with the goals:
058
             • to improve alignment quality,
059
060          • to improve convergence rate and training robustness,
061          • to improve the recognition performance, and
062
             • to gain better understanding of the training dynamics.
063
064   We compare the alignment quality in terms of time stamp error (TSE), which is the average absolute
065   distance of word left/right boundaries and word center positions, compared to a GMM alignment as
066   reference (Zhang et al., 2021; Raissi et al., 2022). We also measure the amount of silence (or blank)
067   in the alignment, where a high amount of silence indicates more peakiness. The model performance
068   is evaluated by the word error rate (WER).
069   Our contributions in this work are:
070
071          • A framework to study alignment behavior based on artificially generated data, and compare
072            various model, noise and training conditions.
073          • A new training variant: normalized gradients as an alternative to training with prior.
074
             • A novel CTC model variation: Separating the blank label in CTC, as another alternative to
075
               counteract class imbalance.
076
077          • A novel way to get alignments via the gradients of the label log probabilities w.r.t. the input
078            features, leading to higher alignment quality.
079
      In terms of WER, we find only small improvements using the new training variant or model variant.
080
081   Note, there is a wide range of related works (see Appendix A.1). In many cases, when the alignment
082   quality is very good (e.g. using a prior as Huang et al. (2024), or GMMs), the model is bad in terms
083   of WER performance. Here we start with our best CTC models (in terms of WER) as baseline, and
084
      try to extract good alignments from them. We want a model which is both good in terms of WER
      and can generate a good alignment.

      We can create synthetic data and simulate the speech recognition task to various degrees of com-
309
      plexity and difficulty. This allows to study the alignment behavior under very controlled conditions.
310
      The data synthesis starts by sampling a ground truth reference alignment from a given probability
311   distribution, and then creates corresponding input features very directly from the alignment, such
312   that the model just needs to learn an identity function. When the data is designed to be as simple
313   and clean as possible, and by design unambiguous, which variant of training criterion and modeling
314   converges to the ground truth alignment? The training criterion does not have any explicit aspect
315   about the alignment, and there are many global optima which would yield a very bad alignment.
316   Specifically, we design the framework such that we control:
317
318
                 • The ground truth alignment. We construct the input features accordingly.
319              • Noise in the input features.
320              • The vocabulary and labels, and statistics on how many words per sequence.
321              • Statistics about how much silence there is and the duration of labels. This indirectly simu-
322                lates different framerates of the input features.

343   7.1.1       E XPERIMENTS ON SYNTHETIC DATA
344
      We compare different types of prior probabilities using a simple feed-forward neural network
345
      (FFNN) (Appendix Table 7). The static prior (using the real ground truth) interestingly performs
346
      bad, even after tuning the scales. The average of the posterior model with stop gradient works best.
347   This is the only prior type which really works here. We also see that the scales are important here.
348   It works without them but it is slightly suboptimal. No prior also has problems here. Normally, no
349   prior would work and result in peaky behavior, but this is not really possible with the FFNN here,
350   and also the HMM label topology is suboptimal for that.
351
      We compare different dataset distributions (Appendix Table 10). Note that the number of frames
352
      per label relates to the framerate on real data. For Switchboard, the average length of a phoneme
353   is 80ms. When the model operates on a 40ms framerate, that corresponds to about 2 frames per
354   phoneme label. We see that the convergence problems mostly occur only with a high number of
355   frames per label, i.e. with a high frame rate (see Appendix Table 10). Specifically, for the high
356   framerate (Nword = 10), using prior together with posterior is important to get good results, and
357   using posterior alone does not work at all9 while for low framerate (Nword = 2), prior together with
358   posterior still works, but is slightly suboptimal, and using the posterior alone reaches the optimal
359   result.
360   Here we are using more realistic settings: Using noise, a more powerful posterior BLSTM model
361   (Schuster & Paliwal, 1997; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), HMM label topology, a higher batch
362   size and a more realistic dataset distribution. Results are in Table 1. Using a too high posterior scale
363   breaks it, but otherwise, it usually works. There are configurations where only the transition model
364   is helpful, and same with only prior, although only transition model seems better. The best result is
365   achieved with using both the prior and the transition model. Note that we are never able to achieve
366   zero LER or zero TSE here. The amount of noise might be unrealistically high now.

521   8   C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   The use of synthetic data provides us with a very useful tool. We find that the framerate and amount
524   of noise play a crucial role on the training dynamics. The prior is most important for higher framer-
525
      ates and not needed for lower framerates. In the noisy synthetic case, the combination of posterior,
      prior and transition model works best. For real data, use of prior results in alignment quality degra-
526
      dation and use of transition model together with posterior is sufficient.
527
528   The separation of the blank symbol and the normalized gradient do not improve the alignment qual-
529   ity (TSE) but they slightly improve the WER. The blank separation allows for faster greedy decoding
530   and faster framewise training. Our novel gradient-based method to find an alignment improves the
531
      alignment quality in case of larger vocabularies. The alignment quality also seems to be more robust
      in comparison to CTC forced alignments.
532
533   Forced alignments using smaller vocabularies and also phoneme-based models, specifically small
534   models, or even GMMs, still provides the best alignments though, but with potentially much worse
535   WER. With the gradient-based alignment method, the alignment quality interestingly seems to cor-
536   relate much better with WER.

761   A.1   R ELATED WORK
762
763   The work by Zeyer et al. (2017; 2021); Raissi et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2024);
764   Raissi et al. (2024) is very related in that they also investigate the alignment behavior of CTC
765   or HMM, and improve by using a prior in training. The way how the prior is estimated differs:
766   E.g. Huang et al. (2024) reestimates the prior every epoch. Initially it is uniform, and then the model
767   softmax output average. Zeyer et al. (2017) estimates the prior by a running exponential average of
768
      the model softmax output. Raissi et al. (2022) estimates the prior from the transcriptions and keeps
      it fixed. In this work here, we mostly use a prior estimated based on the current sequence, but we
769
      compare several variants (see Tables 7 and 12).
770
771   The work by Zeyer et al. (2021); Raissi et al. (2022); Zhao & Bell (2023); Raissi et al. (2024);
772   Zhao & Bell (2024) studies the influence of label topology, e.g. CTC with the special blank, or the
773   standard HMM, or other variations. Here we also compare different variants, specifically standard
774
      HMM and CTC, but our synthetic framework also allows to study any other variant. Zhao & Bell
      (2023) notes that the frame rate also determines what label topology is optimal. We also find that
775
      the frame rate is very important on what training criterion works best, e.g. with prior or without.
776
777   The work by Huang et al. (2024); Rousso et al. (2024) compares the alignment quality of different
778   model types.
779   Deep learning algorithms can fare poorly when the training dataset suffers from heavy class-
780   imbalance. The blank or silence label in speech recognition is very imbalanced compared to the
781   other labels. There is a lot of related work on how the problems and potential solutions when there
782   is class-imbalance (Johnson & Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Chen et al., 2024). Both the normalized gradient
783   method and the blank separation modeling are closely related to other variants of class-balancing
784   the loss such as (Lin et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2019).
785   The work by Schmitt et al. (2024) introduces the same method to extract alignments from the gra-
786   dients w.r.t. the inputs. However, this was done only for AED models. Here it is extended for CTC
787   models and further improved.

      A.4.5       S YNTHETIC F RAMEWORK
931
932   We define a set of words and their mapping to a sequence of labels in A. In most of the experiments,
933   we use the artificial words “helo”, “world”, “howe”, “are”, “you”, and map each word to their
934   characters, so we end up with |A| = 10 possible labels. The alignment labels augment those by
935   blank or silence: Y = A ∪ { }. To sample a reference alignment: First sample the number of
936   words Nwords from a uniform distribution [Nwords min , Nwords max ] ⊂ N. Then map the word to the
937   sequence of labels in A. Then, for each label, sample its number of repetitions from a uniform
938
      distribution [Nrep min , Nrep max ] ⊂ N. Next, sample the silence factor rsil from a uniform distribution
939
      [Rsil min , Rsil max ] ⊂ R. Given the length of the sequence counting the repetitions as TA , this factor
      tells how much silence to add as T  = rsil ·TA . The silence frames   are added uniformly before/after
940
      words, so at Nwords + 1 possible positions. This procedure will construct a ground truth alignment
941
      label sequence y1T ∈ Y T with T = TA + T  .
942
943   We use the input feature dimension D = Y. The input features xT1 ∈ RT ×D are simply constructed
944   from the alignment label sequence by the one-hot encoding11 with optional noise ξi ∈ N (0, 1) and
945
      noise scale σξ :
946
                                               xt,i = (1 − σξ ) · δi=yt + σξ · ξi .                       (42)
947
948   From this construction, there is a trivial optimal mapping (with σξ = 0) from the input features to
949   the target probability distribution p(y | x):
950
951                                                  popt (y=i | xt ) = xt,i                              (43)
952
      With no noise, this is a global optimum to most of the training criteria variations (especially for the
953
      standard CTC), and this model will also provide a perfect alignment.
954
955   For the trained model, we use either the HMM label topology, where   is only allowed before/after
956   words, which also matches how we construct the ground truth alignment, or we use the CTC label
957
      topology, where   is allowed anywhere.
