009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Cross-domain reinforcement learning (CDRL) aims to utilize the knowledge ac-
012            quired from a source domain to efficiently learn tasks in a target domain. Unsu-
013            pervised CDRL assumes no access to any signal (e.g., rewards) from the target
014            domain, and most methods utilize state-action correspondence or cycle consistency.
015            In this work, we identify the critical correspondence identifiability issue (CII) that
016
               arises in existing unsupervised CDRL methods. To address this identifiability
               issue, we propose leveraging pairwise trajectory preferences in the target domain
017
               as weak supervision. Specifically, we introduce the principle of cross-domain
018
               preference consistency (CDPC)–a policy is more transferable across the domains if
019            the source and target domains have similar preferences over trajectories–to provide
020            additional guidance for establishing proper correspondence between the source and
021            target domains. To substantiate the principle of CDPC, we present an algorithm
022            that integrates a state decoder learned through preference consistency loss during
023            training with a cross-domain MPC method for action selection during inference.
024            Through extensive experiments in both MuJoCo and Robosuite, we demonstrate
025            that CDPC enables effective and data-efficient knowledge transfer across domains,
026            outperforming state-of-the-art CDRL benchmark methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) has shown impressive success on a wide range of tasks, encompassing
031   both discrete and continuous control scenarios, such as game playing (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al.,
032   2016; Vinyals et al., 2019) and robot control (Levine et al., 2016; Tobin et al., 2017). However,
033   solving these tasks in a data-efficient manner has remained a significant challenge in RL, mainly
034   due to the need for extensive online trial-and-error interactions and the resulting prolonged training
035   periods. To alleviate the data efficiency issue, one natural and promising approach is to reuse the
036   control policies learned on similar tasks for fast knowledge transfer. Built on this intuition, cross-
037   domain reinforcement learning (CDRL) offers a generic formulation that extends the applicability of
038   transfer learning to RL, where the source domain and the target domain can have different transition
039
      dynamics or distinct state-action spaces. With access to the source domain (e.g., the data samples or
      the environment) and the pre-trained source-domain models (e.g., policies or value functions), CDRL
040
      aims to transfer the knowledge acquired from the source domain to improve the sample efficiency
041
      in the target domain. This adaptability of CDRL is crucial for overcoming the data inefficiency in
042   conventional RL, offering a more flexible and resource-efficient solution.
043
044   Several attempts on CDRL (Zhang et al., 2021a; Gui et al., 2023) have demonstrated the possibility
045
      of direct policy transfer by learning the state-action correspondence between domains, or essentially
      inter-domain mapping functions, from unpaired trajectories in a fully unsupervised manner, i.e., no
046
      reward signal available in the target domain. For example, (Zhang et al., 2021a) proposes to learn
047
      the state-action correspondence (i.e., a target-to-source state decoder and a source-to-target action
048   encoder) by minimizing a dynamics cycle consistency loss, which aligns the one-step transition of the
049   unpaired trajectories from the two domains. These unsupervised approaches can serve as powerful RL
050   solutions in practice as it is widely known that reward design can require substantial efforts and hence
051   is rather time-consuming. However, we identify that this unsupervised approach can be prone to the
052   correspondence identifiability issue (CII). This phenomenon indicates that without any supervision
053   from the target domain, learning the state-action correspondence can be an underdetermined problem.
      To illustrate this, we provide a toy example of a gridworld as shown in Figure 1. Motivated by this,

084   The proposed CDPC framework consists of two major components: (i) Target-to-source state de-
085   coder: To enable the reuse of a source-domain pre-trained policy (denoted by πsrc ), CDPC learns a
086
      target-to-source state decoder (denoted by ϕ−1 ). To learn ϕ−1 without suffering from CII, CDPC
      utilizes a cross-domain pairwise preference loss (or equivalently the negative log-likelihood), which
087
      is calculated with respect to the source-domain trajectories induced by ϕ−1 with the target-domain
088
      preferences as our labels. Compared to the existing unsupervised CDRL, this loss function offers
089   additional constraints for the state decoder such that the identifiability issue can be mitigated. (ii)
090   Cross-domain model predictive control for inference: During inference, we propose to leverage the
091   learned state decoder and determine the target-domain actions by planning via model-predictive
092   control (MPC). Specifically, at each time step, we generate multiple synthetic target-domain tra-
093   jectories of finite length (with the help of a learned dynamics model) and choose the first action
094   of the best trajectory. Different from the standard MPC, the proposed cross-domain MPC uses the
095   source-domain reward of the source-domain trajectory induced by the state decoder as the selection
096   criterion for MPC. With this design, there is no need to learn the action correspondence between
097
      source and target domains. Moreover, this framework is general, i.e., that it can be integrated with
      any enhancements of MPC.
098
099   We evaluate CDPC against various CDRL benchmark methods on various tasks in MuJoCo and
100   Robosuite. The main observations are: (1) Through preference consistency, CDPC achieves faster
101   and more stable learning curves in training the state decoder than the other CDRL methods. (2)
102   Additionally, CDPC enjoys superior sample efficiency across different dataset sizes, even when
103
      compared to the baselines with true reward information. (3) We also provide several ablation studies,
      confirming the significance of the preference consistency loss and examining the impact of the
104
      proportions of expert data on CDPC. (4) Moreover, we perform additional experiments to investigate
105
      the effect of the quality of preference labels on CDPC. Interestingly, by randomly perturbing a portion
106   of the preference labels, we found that CDPC can still achieve reliable cross-domain transfer under

112   2    R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Cross-domain transfer in RL (Taylor & Stone, 2009; Zhu et al., 2023; Serrano et al., 2024; Lyu
115   et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024; Tian, Hongduan and Liu, Feng and Liu, Tongliang and Du, Bo and
116   Cheung, Yiu-ming and Han, Bo, 2024) is an area of research within RL that specifically addresses the
117
      challenge of transferring learned policies or value functions from one domain to another, even when
      there are disparities in state-action dimensions between the domains. Cross-domain transfer learning
118
      can be divided into imitation learning (Kim et al., 2020; Fickinger et al., 2021; Raychaudhuri et al.,
119
      2021) and transfer learning. Transfer learning itself can be further categorized into single-source
120   transfer (Ammar & Taylor, 2012) and multiple-source transfer (Ammar et al., 2015a; Qian et al.,
121   2020; Talvitie & Singh, 2007; Serrano et al., 2021). From the perspective of what is being transferred,
122   which means the known information, it can be generally divided into demonstrations (Ammar et al.,
123   2015b; Shankar et al., 2022; Watahiki et al., 2023), policy (Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023;
124   Gui et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024), parameters (Devin et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021b), and value
125   function (Torrey et al., 2008; Taylor et al., 2008).
126   Common practices to solve CDRL under different state and action representations include leveraging
127   cycle consistency and transition between states and actions across two domains to discover mapping
128   functions (Zhang et al., 2021a; You et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Raychaudhuri
129   et al., 2021; Gui et al., 2023), or employing adversarial training techniques to identify mapping
130   relationships between states and actions in the source and target domains (Gui et al., 2023; Li et al.,
131   2022; Wulfmeier et al., 2017; Mounsif et al., 2020; Raychaudhuri et al., 2021; Watahiki et al., 2022).

170   4    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
171
172   The proposed CD-PbRL problem extends the standard (unsupervised) CDRL problem, which aims to
173   achieve knowledge transfer from a source domain to another target domain, to the scenario where the
174   preferences over trajectories are available as weak supervision in the target domain. The source and
175
      target domains are modeled as follows:
176   Source domain: The source domain is modeled as an MDP denoted by Msrc :=
177   (S src , Asrc , T src , Rsrc , µsrc , γ)1 . For efficient knowledge transfer, the source domain is typically an
178   environment that is cheap and easy to access, e.g., a simulator. Accordingly, we presume that the
179   learner has full access to the source-domain environment and hence can collect data samples and
180   obtain a pre-trained source-domain policy πsrc . This setting has been adopted by most of the existing
181
      CDRL literature (Zhang et al., 2021a; Xu et al., 2023; Gui et al., 2023).
182   Target domain: Similarly, the target domain is modeled as an MDP denoted by Mtar :=
183   (S tar , Atar , T tar , Rtar , µtar , γ). Notably, the target-domain MDP can differ from source-domain MDP
184   in transition dynamics, state-action spaces, etc., and we only assume that the two domains share
185   the same discount factor, which is a fairly mild condition. In the standard unsupervised CDRL
186   setting (Zhang et al., 2021a; Gui et al., 2023), the learner is given a set of target-domain trajectories
187
      Dtar = {τi }D    i=1 collected under some behavior policy. Due to the unsupervised setting, the reward
      function Rtar is assumed to be unobservable to the learner, and hence Dtar only contains information
188
      about the visited state-action pairs. Notably, this formulation can suffer from the identificability
189
      issue by nature as described in Section 1. By contrast, built on the CDRL, our proposed CD-
190   PbRL formulation additionally includes that the learner can further receive preference information
191   about pairs of trajectories in the target domain, despite the unknown true rewards. The goal of
192                                                          ∗                        π
      CD-PbRL is to find an optimal policy πM                   tar

309   5.3   I NFERENCE P HASE OF CDPC: C ROSS -D OMAIN MPC
310
      During the inference phase, given a well-trained state decoder, we propose to determine target-domain
311
      actions through planning via cross-domain MPC, which consists of two major components:
312
313   Cross-domain trajectory optimization (CDTO): As in typical MPC, at each time step t, based on
314   the current observation st , we determine the action at by (i) generating multiple synthetic trajectories
315   of length h with st as the starting state (denoted by D(t) ) in the target domain, and then (ii) selecting
316   one trajectory τ from D(t) based on some performance metric, and (iii) choosing the first action of
317   τ as the action at . Notably, to implement (ii), we propose to use the source-domain reward of the
318   source-domain trajectory induced by the state decoder as the selection criterion for MPC.
319   Generation of synthetic trajectories for cross-domain MPC: To implement the subroutine (i) in
320   CDTO, we also learn two helper models based on the target-domain dataset Dtar , namely a target-
321   domain dynamics model (learned in a standard way by minimizing squared errors of next-state
322   prediction) and a target-domain policy by behavior cloning. This can be viewed as a variant of the
323   random shooting technique in the model-based RL literature (Nagabandi et al., 2018; 2020) but with
      a behavior-cloned policy.

368          • CAT-TR: CAT is a CDRL method proposed by You et al. (2022) that learns state-action

               to learn without knowing the true target-domain environmental rewards.

      6.2   R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSIONS
414
415   Does CDPC achieve data-efficient cross-domain transfer in RL? The results of final total rewards
416   are shown in Figure 5, indicating that CDPC converges faster and performs better than the baselines.
417   The reason why DCC and CMD perform relatively poorly is that they suffer from the identifiability
418   issue as they only focus on learning the state-action correspondence between two domains. SAC-Off-
419   RM, on the other hand, needs to first learn a reward model, and if the reward model is inaccurate, it
420   greatly impacts the results. SAC-Off-TR converges more slowly as it does not involve any knowledge
421   transfer from the source domain.
422   Does CDPC learn an effective state decoder ϕ−1 ? We compare CDPC with other CDRL benchmark
423   methods in the effectiveness of the learned state decoders. The results of final total rewards are shown
424   in Figure 6, indicating that CDPC converges faster and achieve higher total rewards than all the other
425   CDRL methods, even than CAT-TR with true reward signals.
426   Does CDPC learn a state decoder that can effectively achieve cross-domain preference consis-
427   tency? We provide an ablation study and investigate the significance of the preference consistency
428   loss. The results showed that the preference consistency loss has a highly significant effect. Without
429   using Lpref (θ), the decoder encounters identifiability issues, making it unable to decode good tra-
430   jectories into corresponding source trajectories. Consequently, it also becomes unable to utilize the
431   MPC module to select suitable actions. The results are shown in Figure 7. We also provide a Reacher
      example for visualization (with the link provided in Appendix E).

519   We study CD-PbRL, a new cross-domain RL

      The most naive approach to addressing inter-task mapping problems is to train mapping functions
867   for both state and action. A simple illustration and explanation are provided in Figure 12. Initially,
868   we employed the concept of preference consistency to train an autoencoder for both state and action.
869   However, the results were highly unstable, and since there was no information available regarding
870   the target domain’s reward, we needed to additionally train a reward model in the target domain to
871   ensure both domains had preference information to maintain bidirectional mapping. A particularly
872   tricky aspect is that if the reward model is not well-trained easily, the preference labels provided by
873   the reward model will be incorrect, which will lead to poor performance of the action encoder. We
874   also included the training results of this naive method in Figure 12.
875   Finally, we cleverly combined the preference consistency state decoder with MPC, which only
876   required finding a decoder that could ensure consistent preferences, guaranteeing the effectiveness of
877   the MPC approach.

       Preference-based RL (PbRL). PbRL (Wirth et al., 2017; Busa-Fekete & Hüllermeier, 2014;
981
       Kamishima et al., 2010; Wirth & Fürnkranz, 2013; Choi et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2024; Cheng et al.,
982
       2024) is a popular RL setting that focuses on learning policies or value functions from preferences
983    rather than explicit reward signals. One common approach is to model the preference feedback as a
984    binary classification problem (Lee et al., 2021a;b; Akrour et al., 2011; Pilarski et al., 2011; Akrour
985    et al., 2012; Wilson et al., 2012; Ibarz et al., 2018). PbRL has been applied to various real-world
986    domains, including personalized recommendation systems (Li et al., 2010), interactive learning from
987    human feedback (Knox & Stone, 2009), and robot learning from human preferences (Warnell et al.,
988    2018). Besides, PBRL can also be employed for automatic summarization of articles (Stiennon
989    et al., 2020). This approach enables the model to acquire sophisticated summarization techniques
990    through preference-based learning (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023;
991
       Lee et al., 2023; Kirk et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023a). Beyond its application in large language models,
       preference-based techniques are also commonly utilized in training RL agents (Memarian et al., 2021;
992
       Liu et al., 2023; Chakraborty et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023b). By leveraging human feedback to
993
       train reward functions, these techniques enable RL agents to approximate real-world rewards more
994    accurately, guiding the agents towards convergence to an optimal policy.

1038   F.2   C OMPARISON OF CDPC AND MPC-BASED BASELINES
1039
1040   In this section, we further demonstrate that the empirical strength of the CDPC algorithm indeed
1041   mostly come from the design of cross-domain preference consistency. To address this, we further
1042   compare CDPC in two environments, namely Reacher and IIWA-Lift, with three additional baselines
1043   as follows:
1044          • MPC: This method employs MPC directly in the target domain, without using transfer
1045            learning. Here, we use the same dynamics model for both the pure MPC method and CDPC.
1046            The purpose of including baseline is to verify whether CDPC performs well simply because
1047            MPC itself is inherently strong.
1048          • CAT-TR-MPC: Regarding CAT (You et al., 2022) mentioned in Section 6, we remove
1049            CAT’s original action encoder and instead use MPC to select actions, similar to CDPC. Here,
1050            the main purpose is to verify whether the integration of MPC and other cross-domain RL
1051            methods (like CAT) already achieves strong empirical performance.
1052          • DCC-MPC: Similar to CAT-TR-MPC, DCC-MPC is another baseline that integrates (Zhang
1053            et al., 2021a) with the MPC subroutine for taregt-domain action selection. Again, the main
1054            purpose here is to check whether the integration of MPC and other cross-domain method
1055            like DCC already achieves good empirical performance.
1056
1057
       We report the experimental results on the sample efficiency, decoder performance, preference accuracy
       in Figure 13, Figure 14, and Figure 15, respectively. We can make several observations from these
1058
       results:
1059
1060          • CDPC is indeed more sample-efficient that pure target-domain MPC: CDPC still
1061            remains best after the three MPC-based baselines are included. Notably, using MPC directly
1062            in the target domain can produce decent actions, resulting in a moderately high total return.
1063            However, pure target-domain MPC still underperforms CDPC since CDPC, as a cross-
1064            domain transfer method, nicely leverages the learned model from the source domain.
1065          • CAT-TR-MPC and DCC-MPC suffer from low preference accuracy and hence do
1066            not perform well: On the other hand, CAT-TR-MPC and DCC-MPC completely fail to
1067            learn. This is because the state decoders of these methods are still not able to produce
1068            correct trajectory rankings even under the integration with the MPC module. This issue is
1069            particularly evident from the accuracy charts provided in Figure 15.
1070
       Based on the above, we conclude that the empirical strength of CDPC does not rely solely on MPC;
1071
       rather, the key is the seamless integration of the preference-based state decoder with the cross-domain
1072
       trajectory optimization with MPC.
