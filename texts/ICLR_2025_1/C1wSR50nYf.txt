009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, graph prompting has emerged as a promising research direction,
012            enabling the learning of additional tokens or subgraphs appended to the origi-
013            nal graphs without requiring retraining of pre-trained graph models across vari-
014            ous applications. This novel paradigm, shifting from the traditional “pre-training
015            and fine-tuning” to “pre-training and prompting” has shown significant empirical
016            success in simulating graph data operations, with applications ranging from rec-
017            ommendation systems to biological networks and graph transferring. However,
018
               despite its potential, the theoretical underpinnings of graph prompting remain un-
               derexplored, raising critical questions about its fundamental effectiveness. The
019
               lack of rigorous theoretical proof of why and how much it works is more like
020
               a “dark cloud” over the graph prompt area to go further. To fill this gap, this
021            paper introduces a theoretical framework that rigorously analyzes graph prompt-
022            ing from a data operation perspective. Our contributions are threefold: First,
023            we provide a formal guarantee theorem, demonstrating graph prompts’ capacity
024            to approximate graph transformation operators, effectively linking upstream and
025            downstream tasks. Second, we derive upper bounds on the error of these data op-
026            erations by graph prompts for a single graph and extend this discussion to batches
027            of graphs, which are common in graph model training. Third, we analyze the
028            distribution of data operation errors, extending our theoretical findings from lin-
029
               ear graph aggregations (e.g., GCN) to non-linear graph aggregations (e.g., GAT).
               Extensive experiments support our theoretical results and confirm the practical
030
               implications of these guarantees.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have been widely used in analyzing various graph-structured data.
036   A standard workflow using GNNs is the “pre-training and fine-tuning” paradigm, where a model is
037   first trained on a large-scale, general-purpose dataset and then fine-tuned on a specific downstream
038   task. While this method has been effective in transferring learned representations, it often needs
039   many advanced tricks to retrain the model parameters for each new task, which can be compu-
040   tationally intensive and may not fully capture the unique characteristics of the downstream tasks,
041   potentially limiting the model’s generalization.
042   Inspired by the success of prompting techniques in natural language processing (NLP), there has
043   been a growing interest in adapting similar ideas to graph data through “pre-training and prompting”.
044   Graph prompts (Sun et al., 2023b) modify the input graphs by adding learnable tokens or subgraphs,
045   enabling the pre-trained GNN to better align with the requirements of downstream tasks without
046   tuning the model parameters. Many empirical works (Sun et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Tan et al.,
047   2023; Huang et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023) have found that graph prompting can reduce computational
048   overhead, preserve the generality of the pre-trained model, and allow for seamless application across
049   multiple tasks to achieve better expressive capability than the traditional paradigm.
050   Recently, some studies (Fang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023a) have realized that the reason why
051   graph prompts work may relate to their capability in simulating various data operations like delet-
052   ing/adding nodes/edges, changing node features, and even removing subgraphs. This makes graph
053   prompts stand out from their counterpart in the NLP area and inspires many empirical applications
      like recommendation systems (Yang et al., 2023; 2024), biological networks (Diao et al., 2022),


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      transferring knowledge across different graph domains (Guo et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024b), and
055   more. Unfortunately, despite these promising results, the theoretical basis of graph prompting re-
056   mains underexplored. Existing works primarily rely on empirical validation and lack rigorous the-
057   oretical analysis to explain why graph prompts are effective and how they can be systematically
058   designed. This gap is just like a “dark could” over the graph prompt area, raising critical questions
059   about their broader applications and the development of more advanced methods that could leverage
060   their full potential.
061   In light of these limitations, this paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework for graph
062   prompting from a data operation perspective. First, we establish rigorous guarantee theorems that
063   demonstrate the underlying reason why graph prompts work is their capacity to simulate various
064   graph data operations, and our theorems further answer why such capacity can make the pre-trained
065   model meet new task requirements without retraining. Second, we derive upper bounds on the er-
066   ror introduced by graph prompts when simulating these data operations. We analyze this error for
067   individual graphs and extend our discussion to batches of graphs, which is crucial for understanding
068   the scalability and generalization of graph prompts in practical scenarios where models are usually
069
      trained on multiple graphs. Third, we explore the distribution of the data operation error and ex-
      tend our theoretical findings from linear graph aggregations, such as Graph Convolutional Networks
070
      (GCNs), to non-linear aggregations like Graph Attention Networks (GATs). This extension demon-
071
      strates the robustness of our theoretical framework across different GNN architectures and provides
072   insights into how non-linearity affects the effectiveness of graph prompts. We conduct extensive
073   experiments to confirm our theoretical findings. By offering such a solid theoretical foundation for
074   graph prompting, our work advances the understanding of how and why graph prompts work, guides
075   for designing more effective prompting techniques, and empowers researchers and practitioners to
076   leverage them with greater confidence in various applications.

      2   BACKGROUND
080
081
      Graph Prompt. Compared with “pre-training and fine-tuning”, which first trains a graph model
      via some easily accessible task on the graph dataset and then tries to adapt the model to a new task
082
      (or even a new graph dataset), “pre-training and prompting” aims to keep the pre-trained model
083
      unchanged but adjust the input data to make the downstream task compatible with the pre-training
084   task. Mathematically, let Fθ∗ be a graph model where its parameters (θ∗ ) have been pre-trained
085   and frozen; Tdow be the downstream task, in which the task objective is measured by a loss function
086   LTdow . Let G be a graph dataset and each graph instance G ∈ G can be denoted as G = (V, E, X, A)
087   where V denotes the node set with a node feature matrix X ∈ R|V|×F ; E denotes the edge set and
088   the connection of nodes can be further indicated by the adjacent matrix A ∈ {0, 1}|V|×|V| . Let
089   Pω denote a parameterized graph prompt function with learnable ω. In most cases, graph prompts
090   consist of some token vectors or subgraphs which will be integrated into the original graph G. Pω
091   indicates how to define such graph prompts and how to combine them with the original graph to
092   generate a new graph: Gω = Pω (G). Graph prompt learning aims to optimize the following target:
093                                                 X

                                                 ω

096   Without loss of generality, we assume all these tasks are graph level (e.g., graph classification). That
097   means Fθ (G) will output a graph-level embedding for the downstream task. For node-level and
098   edge-level tasks, many studies (Sun et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023) have proved that we can always
099   find solutions to translate these tasks to the graph-level task.
100
      GPF and All-in-One. Current graph prompt designs, as described in the review by Sun et al.
101
      (2023b), can be primarily categorized into two types: prompt as token vectors added to node fea-
102   tures, and prompt as additional graph inserted to the original graph. In the rest of this paper, we
103   focus on two representative frameworks: GPF (Fang et al., 2022) as an example of adding extra
104   prompt vectors, and All-in-One (Sun et al., 2023a) as an example of adding prompt subgraphs. Our
105   choice is motivated by our interest in simulating graph operations at a theoretical level. The GPF
106   and All-in-One frameworks provide the most fundamental approaches among these methods. The
107   rest graph prompt designs can usually be treated as their special cases or natural extensions (Sun
      et al., 2023b).

      Specifically, GPF aims to learn a token vector ω ∈ R1×F where F is the same dimension of the node
109   features in the original graph, then the prompt token is directly added to each node’s feature vector,
110   making the original feature matrix X = {x1 , · · · , xN } changed to Xω = {x1 + ω, · · · , xN + ω}.
111   In this way, the original graph G = (V, E, X, A) is changed to Pω (G) = Gω = (V, E, Xω , A).
112
      All-in-One offers the prompt as a graph format by defining prompt tokens, token structures and
113
      inserting patterns. Let Ω ∈ Rk×F be the learnable matrix corresponding to K prompt tokens. Let
114
      Ain ∈ {0, 1}k×k indicate token structures where Aij = 1 means there is an inner link connecting
115   the i-th and the j-th tokens and vice versa. Ain can be calculated by the inner product of these
116   tokens. Similarly, the inserting pattern tells us how to connect each token to the original graph
117   nodes, which is denoted by a cross matrix Acro ∈ {0, 1}k×N . Then the graph prompt changes
118   the original graph G to Gω = (Vω , Eω , X, Ω, Aω ) where Vω is the collection of the original nodes
119   and the token nodes; Eω includes the original edges, inner links among tokens and the cross links
120   between tokens and the original nodes; Aω is the collection of A,Ain , and Acro .
121   Motivations. Initially, Fang et al. (2022) have proved that graph prompt can simulate any graph
122   data operation (e.g., deleting/adding nodes/edges, changing node features, or removing subgraphs,
123   etc). That means for any graph data operation t(·), we can always learn a graph prompt reaching
124   Fθ∗ (Gω ) = Fθ∗ (t(G)). However, this equivalence needs a very strong precondition: the graph
125   model F should not contain any non-linear layer, which is apparently very hard to meet in the
126   practical solutions. Later, Sun et al. (2023a) extended this finding with more advanced prompts and
127   empirically observed that the error to such approximation Fθ∗ (Gω ) → Fθ∗ (t(G)) may relate to
128   the non-linear layers of the graph model and the prompt design. However, these observations are
129
      not followed by a critical theory proof. Recently, there has been an increasing number of empirical
      works on graph prompts achieving success in various applications. Unfortunately, the theoretical
130
      basis of graph prompt is still very tumbledown and we still have not figured out why graph prompts
131
      work in theory, especially for questions like how powerful the graph prompt is in manipulating
132   graph data? and why such capability works for downstream tasks?
133
134   In this paper, we go deeper in theory for the graph prompt capability of manipulating data. We con-
135
      duct a comprehensive effectiveness analysis of general graph prompt learning through the concepts
      of “bridge sets” and “ϵ-extended bridge sets” (see in section 3.3). Based on extensive theoretical
136
      derivations and substantial experimental evidence, we establish theorems related to the error bound
137
      of graph prompts in simulating graph data operations. Our goal is to figure out in theory how this er-
138   ror changes from a single graph to a batch of graphs, from a linear model to a non-linear model, and
139   which factors relate to this error. Through this work, we wish to push forward the graph prompt area
140   with a more solid theory basis, help researchers to design more scientific graph prompt techniques,
141   and offer them theory confidence for their further usage.

      3     W HY G RAPH P ROMPT W ORKS ? A DATA O PERATION P ERSPECTIVE
145
146   Let Fθ∗ be any given GNN model that has been pre-trained on a given task Tpre . Here θ∗ means
147
      the parameters have been determined and frozen. For a given graph instance Gori , we can expect
      the model to output appropriate graph-level embedding on Tpre because this model has already
148
      been trained on this task. However, when we try to use this model on a new task Tdow , the output
149
      embedding, Fθ∗ (Gori ), can not guarantee acceptable performance because the pre-training task Tpre
150   may be incompatible with downstream tasks Tdow .
151
152
      3.1   P ERSPECTIVE FROM M ODEL T UNING
153
154   To fill this gap, “pre-training and fine-tuning” aims to adapt the pre-trained model to a new version
155   and wish it could perform better. Assume there exists an optimal function, say C, which can map
156   Gori to the embedding C(Gori ) to achieve good performance on Tdow . The nature of “pre-training
157   and fine-tuning” is to hope the fine-tuned graph model could approximate to C(Gori ):
158                                       Fθ∗ →θ# (Gori ) → C(Gori )                                     (2)
159
160   However, achieving this goal usually requires fine-tuning the graph model, which is not always
161   efficient and needs many empirical tuning tricks. The tuning course may be even harder if the
      pre-trained model is ill-designed for the downstream task. In addition, we can not guarantee that


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      fine-tuning the pre-trained model (i.e. θ∗ → θ# ) can always surpass training the model from scratch
163   because the preserved knowledge may contribute negatively to the downstream task.
164
165
      3.2   P ERSPECTIVE FROM DATA O PERATION
166
167   Instead of the above model-level tricks, graph prompts provide a data-level alternative. Some prior
168   works (Fang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023a) have initially proved that graph prompts can simulate
169   any graph operations (e.g., deleting/adding nodes/edges/subgraphs, changing node features, etc).
170   However, how effective of graph prompt is and why this works for the new task are still not yet
171   answered. To answer these questions, we first explain our data operation perspective by a theorem
172   as follows:
173   Theorem 1. Let Fθ∗ be a GNN model pre-trained on task Tpre with frozen parameters (θ∗ ); let
174   Tdow be the downstream task and C is an optimal function to Tdow . Given any graph Gori , C(Gori )
175   denotes the optimal embedding vector to the downstream task (i.e. can be parsed to yield correct
176   results for Gori in the downstream task), then there always exists a bridge graph Gbri such that
177   Fθ∗ (Gbri ) = C(Gori ).
178
      A detailed proof of Theorem 1 can be seen in Appendix A.3.1, from which we can find that for any
179
      given graph Gori , there always exists a bridge graph, say Gbri , making the following equation hold:
180
181                                           Fθ∗ (Gbri ) = C(Gori )                                       (3)
182
      That means, without needing to tune the model, we can try to find a data operation method that
183
      translates Gori to Gbri with the pre-trained model unchanged. Graph prompts can be treated as a
184
      learnable data operation framework to help us manipulate these graph data. In this way, we can
185   significantly reduce the difficulty of traditional fine-tuning work, improve the performance on a new
186   task (or even a new dataset), and further enhance the generalization of graph neural networks. With
187   this perspective, our next question is: How difficult to find such a bridge graph using graph prompts?
188
189   3.3   M EASURING THE D IFFICULTY OF F INDING B RIDGE G RAPHS VIA G RAPH P ROMPTS
190
191   Graph prompts can be viewed as a type of graph transformation operator. For example, the simplest
192   graph prompt is just adding a specific prompt token vector pω to each node feature of the graph and
193   then we can transform this graph G into a family of graphs {Pω (G)|ω ∈ RF }, where Pω (G) rep-
194   resents the output graph obtained by graph prompt on G. Once ω is determined, the corresponding
195   data transformation rule and unique output graph data are also defined. This family can be under-
196
      stood as the “transformation space” of graph G under prompt P , denoted as DP (G). If the prompt
      operator maps the original graph Gori to a bridge graph Gbri (i.e., P (Gori ) = Gbri ), then applying the
197
      pre-trained model Fθ∗ yields Fθ∗ (P (Gori )) = Fθ∗ (Gbri ), which conforms to the downstream task.
198
      In this process, we achieve seamless alignment of upstream and downstream tasks solely through
199   data transformation operators, without relying on tuning the model’s parameters.
200
201
      Definition 1 (Bridge Set and ϵ-extended Bridge Set). The bridge set of a graph G is defined as:
202                                      BG = {Gp | Fθ∗ (Gp ) = C(G)}
203
204   where Fθ∗ is the frozen graph model from the pre-training task, and C is the optimal function for
205
      the downstream task. The ϵ-extended bridge set of G is a relaxed version of the bridge set, which is
      defined as:
206
                               ϵ-BG = {Gp | ϵ = ∥Fθ∗ (Gp ) − C(G)∥ ≤ ϵ∗ }.
207
208
      Achieving a transformation exactly equivalent to C(G) is highly non-parametric and nonlinear.
209
      Finding the corresponding Gbri or even the bridge set for any Gori involves solving complex, high-
210   order nonlinear equations. This task becomes virtually impossible manually, especially if the pre-
211   training method integrates multiple tasks or intricate mechanisms. Fortunately, graph prompts Pω
212   can be viewed as parameterized fitting for these operators and they usually contain very lightweight
213   parameters, which dramatically simplify the search space compared with manually designed strate-
214   gies. The effectiveness of graph prompting methods hinges on their ability to approximate these
215   operators closely—whether they can uniformly project G in the dataset into the bridge set BG , or at
      least map them into the extended bridge set with a small upper error bound ϵ∗ .

295   4.2      E XTEND THE E RROR B OUND D ISCUSSION TO A BATCH OF G RAPHS
296
297   In Sections 3 and 4.1, we have proved that graph prompting frameworks can indeed fit graph trans-
298   formation operators given a single graph, thereby exploiting model capabilities. However, in other
299
      cases, we often train the model via a batch of graphs and seek to find better performance over the
      whole graph dataset. Correspondingly, we should aspire to transform each graph G in the down-
300
      stream dataset into its corresponding BG or ϵ-BG (for small ϵ). If such a uniform upper bound
301
      ϵ∗ exists, it would theoretically validate the excellent performance of graph prompting in general
302   downstream tasks, confirming the rational utilization of powerful upstream models.
303
304   For a batch of graphs, the complexity and information contained in the graph prompt become partic-
305
      ularly important. For instance, the increased number of prompt vectors in GPF (a.k.a GPF-Plus) and
      the selection of a larger size of the prompt graph in All-in-One greatly expand the transformation
306
      space of graph G under prompt P (see DP (·) in section 3.3). A larger transformation space corre-
307
      sponds to a smaller ϵ upper bound. In our theoretical analysis, we found that when the prompt takes
308   an overly simple form, the capability of prompt learning is limited. This manifests as a theoretical
309   lower bound of the bridge set extension as suggested in Theorem 6:
310
      Theorem 6. For a GCN model Fθ , for GPF with a single prompt vector or All-in-One with a single-
311
      token graph prompt, given a batch of graphs G = {G1 , · · · , Gi , · · · , Gn }, the root mean squared
312   error (RMSE) over {ϵ1 , · · · , ϵn } has a lower bound ϵo such that RM SE(ϵ1 , · · · , ϵn ) ≥ ϵo .
313
314   Theorem 6 is proved in Appendix A.3.4 and we also give the detailed formulation of ϵo in the
315   proof, from which we can find that ϵo is related to graph data and the prompt token. This indicates
316   that when the downstream task dataset is relatively large, we must correspondingly increase the
317   transformation space of the prompt to better utilize the model’s capabilities, which also aligns with
318   existing empirical observations (Liu et al., 2021). Then our next question is: With the increase of
319   graphs, how does graph prompt complexity increase with their error bound increased?
320   Intuitively, a good graph prompt should not increase its complexity faster than the growth of the
321   dataset because in that case the effectiveness of prompt learning in practical applications would
322   be significantly compromised. Fortunately, we found that for relatively large datasets, the scale of
323   prompts required for prompt learning is highly controllable with the number of needed tokens for
      the prompt almost constant, far from the increase of graphs. This explains why even with relatively

      large downstream datasets, as reflected in Sun et al. (2023a), empirical results using medium-scale
325   prompts can still achieve excellent outcomes. Compared to fine-tuning the entire model parameters,
326   Theorem 7 (see the proof in Appendix A.3.4) indicates why graph prompting can achieve compara-
327   ble or even better results with less parameter adjustment scale:
328

      4.4   E XTEND THE D ISCUSSION FROM L INEAR TO N ON - LINEAR AGGREGATIONS
401
      While our previous analysis focused on GCN or linear aggregation models that can be represented
402   in the form of “diffusion matrices”, many advanced models utilizing attention mechanisms exhibit
403   distinctly different characteristics. Their aggregation methods involve the computation of attention
404   matrices, which in turn depend on the node feature vectors of G. This can be considered as a non-
405   linear model w.r.t G’s feature matrix. In our analysis, we use Graph Attention Networks (GAT) as an
406   exemplar, as the attention mechanism in GAT is a common component in many non-linear models.
407   Fortunately, the guarantees provided by our theorems do not differ significantly for these models.
408   This indicates that even as models become more non-linear and complex, graph prompting can still
409   effectively harness the powerful capabilities of pre-trained models:
410   Theorem 9. Let Fθ be a GAT model. If any layer of the model has a full row rank parameter matrix,
411   then for the All-in-One prompting framework, for any input graph G, there exists an optimal ω such
412   that Pω (G) ∈ BG . When the parameter matrix is not full rank, there is an upper bound µ(θ) · λ(G)
413
      making Pω (G) ∈ ϵ-BG , ϵ ≤ µ(θ) · λ(G).
414   We give a detailed proof in Appendix A.3.6. The above theorem demonstrates the robustness of
415   graph prompting methods across different types of GNN architectures, including those with non-
416   linear attention mechanisms. The consistency of these results with our earlier findings for linear
417   models suggests that the fundamental principles of graph prompting remain effective even as we
418   move towards more complex and non-linear model architectures.

523   6          C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper addresses the theoretical gap in graph prompting by introducing a comprehensive frame-
526
      work from a data operation perspective. We introduced the concepts of “bridge sets” and “ϵ-extended
      bridge sets” to formally demonstrate that graph prompts can approximate graph transformation op-
527
      erators, effectively bridging pre-trained models with downstream tasks without retraining. Our con-
528
      tributions are threefold: first, we established guarantee theorems confirming that graph prompts
529   can simulate various graph data operations, explaining their effectiveness in aligning upstream and
530   downstream tasks. Second, we derived upper bounds on the approximation errors introduced by
531   graph prompts for both individual graphs and batches of graphs, highlighting how factors like model
532   rank and prompt complexity influence these errors. Third, we analyzed the distribution of these er-
533   rors and extended our theoretical findings from linear models like GCNs to non-linear models such
534   as GATs, showcasing the robustness of graph prompting across different architectures. Our extensive
535   experiments validate these theoretical results and confirm their practical implications, demonstrat-
536   ing that graph prompts can effectively leverage pre-trained models in various settings. By providing
537
      solid theoretical foundations, our work not only explains why graph prompts work but also guides
      the design of more effective prompting techniques. This empowers researchers and practitioners to
538
      utilize graph prompts with greater confidence, potentially leading to more efficient and generalized
539
      graph neural network models across diverse applications.
