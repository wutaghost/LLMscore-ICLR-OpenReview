011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The prediction of drug-target interactions is fundamental to the advancement of
014            drug discovery. We present a groundbreaking unified theory for Drug-Target In-
015            teraction prediction with Domain Adaptation (DTI-DA), seamlessly integrating
016            concepts from quantum mechanics, differential geometry, and information the-
017            ory. Our framework introduces a novel DTI symplectic structure that captures
018
               the intrinsic geometry of drug-target interactions, leading to a Quantum Optimal
               Transport theorem that provides a rigorous foundation for domain adaptation in
019
               the DTI context. We develop a quantum statistical mechanical formulation of
020
               DTI-DA, introducing DTI-preserving quantum channels and deriving a Quantum
021            Wasserstein distance tailored to drug discovery applications. Our information-
022            geometric perspective yields a Quantum Fisher-Rao metric for DTI, resulting in a
023            quantum Cramer-Rao bound that establishes fundamental limits on DTI prediction
024            accuracy. We propose a unified variational principle for DTI-DA, encompassing
025            quantum and classical aspects, which leads to a novel algorithm based on geo-
026            metric stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics. Furthermore, we extend classical
027            statistical inference to the quantum domain, deriving a Quantum Rao-Blackwell
028            theorem and a Quantum Bayesian Cramer-Rao bound specifically for DTI-DA.
029
               These theoretical advancements not only deepen our understanding of the DTI-
               DA problem but also suggest new algorithmic approaches with provable guar-
030
               antees. Preliminary numerical experiments on quantum-inspired DTI-DA algo-
031
               rithms demonstrate significant improvements in prediction accuracy and domain
032            adaptation capabilities compared to classical methods, particularly for challeng-
033            ing out-of-distribution scenarios in drug discovery. Our anonymous gitHub link:
034            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DTI-DA-6AFB

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      The prediction of drug-target interactions (DTI) stands at the forefront of modern drug discovery,
040
      presenting a formidable challenge that spans the realms of biochemistry, machine learning, and, in-
041
      creasingly, quantum mechanics. As our understanding of molecular interactions deepens, it becomes
042   evident that the quantum nature of these interactions plays a crucial role in determining drug effi-
043   cacy and specificity. Concurrently, the vast and heterogeneous landscape of chemical and biological
044   data necessitates sophisticated domain adaptation (DA) techniques to generalize predictions across
045   diverse experimental settings and molecular databases.
046
      Traditional approaches to DTI prediction, rooted in classical statistical mechanics and machine
047
      learning, have made significant strides in recent years Suruliandi et al. (2024); Dehghan et al. (2024);
048   Gao et al. (2024); Shi et al. (2024). However, these methods often fall short when confronted with the
049   inherent quantum mechanical aspects of molecular binding Wozniak et al. (2024) and the complex
050   distributional shifts Bazhenov et al. (2024); Bansak et al. (2024); Conger et al. (2024) encountered
051   in real-world drug discovery scenarios. The limitations of classical approaches become particularly
052   apparent when attempting to model the subtle electronic interactions that govern drug-target binding
053   or when extrapolating predictions to novel chemical spaces that differ substantially from the training
      distribution.

      Recent advancements in quantum computing Fauseweh (2024); Sood & Chauhan (2024)and quan-
055   tum machine learningPeral-Garcı́a et al. (2024); Wang & Liu (2024); Senokosov et al. (2024) have
056   opened new avenues for addressing these challenges. Quantum algorithms for molecular simula-
057   tions Cornish et al. (2024); Kesari et al. (2024) and quantum-inspired machine learning models
058   have shown promise in capturing the intricate quantum effects underlying molecular interactions.
059   However, these approaches often lack a unified theoretical framework that can seamlessly integrate
060   quantum mechanical principles with the statistical rigor required for robust domain adaptationZhang
061   et al. (2024); Shi & Liu (2024); Li et al. (2024).
062   Our theoretical advancements have profound implications for the field of drug discoveryPinzi et al.
063   (2024); Edfeldt et al. (2024). By explicitly accounting for the quantum nature of molecular in-
064   teractions, our framework promises to improve prediction accuracy for complex drug-target sys-
065   temsNiarakis et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2024) that have traditionally been challenging to model.
066   Moreover, our rigorous treatment of domain adaptation in the quantum setting Cai et al. (2024) pro-
067   vides a principled approach to leveraging data from diverse sources, potentially accelerating the drug
068   discovery process Udegbe et al. (2024) by enabling more effective use of heterogeneous datasets.
069   From a practical standpoint, our work suggests new directions for the design of DTI prediction algo-
070   rithms. The quantum optimal transport formulation, for instance, points towards quantum-inspired
071   classical algorithms that can approximate quantum effects without requiring full quantum hardware.
072   Similarly, our quantum Cramer-Rao bound provides a benchmark against which to evaluate the per-
073   formance of both classical and quantum DTI prediction methods.
074   By providing a rigorous mathematical foundation that unifies quantum mechanics, information the-
075   ory, and domain adaptation in the context of drug discovery, this work opens up new avenues for
076   developing more accurate, robust, and interpretable methods for DTI prediction. Our hope is that
077   this unified theory will serve as a catalyst for further interdisciplinary research at the intersection
078   of quantum physics, machine learning, and pharmaceutical science, ultimately contributing to the
079   advancement of personalized medicine and the discovery of novel therapeutic interventions.
080   In this work, we present a groundbreaking unified theory for Drug-Target Interaction prediction
081   with Domain Adaptation (DTI-DA) that bridges the gap between quantum mechanics, information
082   theory, and statistical learning. Our framework introduces several key innovations:
083
      1. A novel DTI symplectic structure that captures the intrinsic geometry of drug-target interactions
084
      in a quantum-mechanical setting. This structure allows us to formulate the DTI prediction problem
085   in terms of Hamiltonian mechanics on a Riemannian manifold, providing a natural language for
086   describing the dynamics of molecular binding.
087
088
      2. A Quantum Optimal Transport theorem that establishes a rigorous foundation for domain adap-
      tation in the context of DTI prediction. This result extends classical optimal transport theory to the
089
      quantum realm, enabling the transfer of knowledge between quantum states representing different
090
      experimental domains.
091
092   3. A quantum statistical mechanical formulation of DTI-DA, introducing the concept of DTI-
093   preserving quantum channels. This formulation allows us to derive a Quantum Wasserstein distance
094
      tailored specifically to drug discovery applications, providing a meaningful metric for comparing
      quantum states of drug-target systems across different domains.
095
096   4. An information-geometric perspective on DTI prediction, yielding a Quantum Fisher-Rao met-
097   ric that captures both the statistical and quantum mechanical aspects of drug-target interactions.
098   This metric leads to a novel quantum Cramer-Rao bound that establishes fundamental limits on the
099   accuracy of DTI predictions in the presence of domain shift.
100   5. A unified variational principle for DTI-DA that encompasses both quantum and classical aspects
101   of the problem. This principle leads to a novel algorithm based on geometric stochastic gradient
102   Langevin dynamics, providing a practical means of implementing our theoretical insights.

      This definition ensures that the domain adaptation map preserves both the symplectic structure and

166   W2ω (µs , ϕ# µt ) + λR(ϕ) be a cost functional, where W2ω is the symplectic Wasserstein distance,

170
171   Proof. The proof proceeds in several steps:
172
      1. Show that the space of DTI-preserving symplectomorphisms forms an infinite-dimensional Lie
173   group G.
174
175   2. Equip G with a weak Riemannian structure induced by the symplectic form ω.
176   3. Prove that C(ϕ) is lower semicontinuous and coercive on G with respect to the weak topology.
177
      4. Use the direct method in the calculus of variations to establish the existence of a minimizer.
178
179   5. Exploit the geodesic convexity of W2ω to prove uniqueness.
180
      The key challenge lies in handling the infinite-dimensional nature of G. We overcome this by us-
181   ing techniques from geometric analysis and the theory of optimal transport on infinite-dimensional
182   manifolds.
183
184
      Let {ϕn } be a minimizing sequence for C(ϕ). By the coercivity of C, this sequence is bounded in
      the Sobolev space W 1,2 (Ms , Mt ). The Rellich-Kondrachov theorem ensures the existence of a
185
      subsequence {ϕnk } that converges weakly to some ϕ∗ in W 1,2 and strongly in L2 .
186
187   To show that ϕ∗ is a DTI-preserving symplectomorphism, we use the fact that the conditions ϕ∗ ωt =
188   ωs and ϕ∗ Ht = Hs +c are preserved under weak W 1,2 convergence. This follows from the compact
189   embedding of W 1,2 into C 0 for our finite-dimensional manifolds Ms and Mt .
190   The lower semicontinuity of C with respect to weak W 1,2 convergence, combined with the fact that
191   {ϕn } is a minimizing sequence, implies that ϕ∗ is indeed a minimizer.
192
      Uniqueness follows from the strict geodesic convexity of W2ω along the path of DTI-preserving sym-
193
      plectomorphisms, which we establish using techniques from optimal transport theory on symplectic
194   manifolds.
195
196   This theorem provides a rigorous foundation for DTI-DA algorithms based on symplectic geometry,
197   ensuring that they preserve the fundamental structure of drug-target interactions while adapting to
198   different domains.
