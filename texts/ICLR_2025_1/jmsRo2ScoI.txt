009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large vision-and-language models (LVLMs) typically treat visual and textual em-
012            beddings as homogeneous inputs to a large language model (LLM). However,
013            these inputs are inherently different: visual inputs are multi-dimensional and con-
014            textually rich, often pre-encoded by models like CLIP, while textual inputs lack
015            this structure. In this paper, we propose Decomposed Attention (D-Attn), a novel
016            method that processes visual and textual embeddings differently by decomposing
017            the 1-D causal self-attention in LVLMs. After the attention decomposition, D-Attn
018            diagonalizes visual-to-visual self-attention, reducing computation from O(|V |2 )
019            to O(|V |) for |V | visual embeddings without compromising performance. More-
020
               over, D-Attn debiases positional encodings in textual-to-visual cross-attention,
               further enhancing visual understanding. Finally, we introduce an α-weighting
021
               strategy to merge visual and textual information, maximally preserving the pre-
022
               trained LLM’s capabilities with minimal modifications. Extensive experiments
023            and rigorous analyses validate the effectiveness of D-Attn, demonstrating signif-
024            icant improvements on multiple image benchmarks while significantly reducing
025            computational costs. Code, data, and models will be publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Vision-and-Language Models (LVLMs) (Liu et al., 2024b) have become pivotal in advanc-
032
      ing artificial intelligence, enabling models to understand multimodal content by integrating visual
      and textual information. These models have shown significant advancements in various applica-
033
      tions, such as image captioning, visual question answering, and multi-modal assistant, marking a
034
      substantial leap forward in cross-modal reasoning. By leveraging the strengths of pre-trained large
035   language models (LLMs) like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023) and Mistral (Jiang
036   et al., 2023), and powerful visual encoders such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), LVLMs are pushing
037   the boundaries of cross-modal understanding, making AI more capable of interpreting and reasoning
038   about complex, real-world scenarios.
039
      In most state-of-the-art LVLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a; Tong et al., 2024a), visual inputs
040
      are processed within an LLM in the same manner as textual inputs. Specifically, visual inputs are
041   first encoded by a pre-trained visual encoder, such as CLIP, into a sequence of visual embeddings.
042   These embeddings are then passed through a lightweight adapter layer and concatenated with textual
043   embeddings derived from the text prompts. The concatenated visual and textual embeddings are
044   treated as homogeneous input embeddings and subsequently fed into a pre-trained LLM. In this
045   approach, visual and textual embeddings are treated and processed uniformly.
046
      In this paper, we rethink the homogeneity of visual and textual tokens in LVLMs and challenge this
047   conventional paradigm:
048
049   “Visual and textual inputs are created different, and thus we propose to process them differently
050
      within a large vision-and-language model.”
051   It is evident that visual and textual embeddings are created different. Visual embeddings are derived
052   by passing one or more two-dimensional images through a visual encoder, while textual embeddings
053   are generated through a lookup of learnable parameters from a one-dimensional sequence of text
      token IDs. These distinctions introduce significant differences in the information encoded within


                                                        1

054
      each type of embedding, which necessitates different modeling and processing strategies within the
055   LLM. Key distinctions include:
056
057   • Visual embeddings inherently encode contextual information from all other visual embeddings,
058   whereas textual embeddings lack such intrinsic contextual awareness of other textual tokens.
059
060
      • Visual inputs are intrinsically multi-dimensional (e.g. images are 2-D). Concatenating visual and
      textual embeddings into a 1-D sequence and processing them in a causal, language-centric manner
061
      can introduce undesirable modeling biases.
062
063
      To address these challenges, we introduce Decomposed Attention, or D-Attn, a novel framework
064
      designed to handle visual inputs more efficiently and effectively in LVLMs. In Section 2.1, we
065   first demonstrate that the causal self-attention mechanism (Vaswani, 2017) in an LVLM can be de-
066   composed into three components: (1) visual-to-visual self-attention (V2V Self-Attn), (2) textual-to-
067   visual cross-attention (T2V Cross-Attn), and (3) textual-to-textual self-attention (T2T Self-Attn), as
068   illustrated in Figure 1. By leveraging this decomposition, we concentrate on the vision-related com-
069   ponents, specifically the V2V Self-Attn and T2V Cross-Attn, while addressing how to effectively
070   merge the T2V and T2T attentions.
071   In Section 2.2, we argue that since each visual embedding inherently encodes contextual information
072   about other visual embeddings, it is redundant to relearn this information within the LVLM. There-
073   fore, we propose diagonalizing the V2V Self-Attn, significantly reducing the computational com-
074   plexity from O(|V |2 ) to O(|V |) for |V | visual embeddings without sacrificing performance. This
075   optimization is particularly advantageous when processing high-resolution images or long video
076   sequences.
077   In Section 2.3, we identify an undesirable positional bias that arises from concatenating visual and
078   textual embeddings into a 1-D sequence. To address this issue, we propose to debias T2V Cross-Attn
079   by removing rotary/relative positional encodings within T2V Cross-Attn. Notably, this modification,
080   though seemingly straightforward, is difficult to implement in conventional LVLMs without our
081   proposed attention decomposition framework.
082
      Finally, in Section 2.4, we derive an α-weighting strategy to merge the visual information from T2V
083   Cross-Attn with the textual information from T2T Self-Attn. The α-weighting approach is analyt-
084   ically equivalent to the inherent attention operations within LVLMs, introducing minimal architec-
085   tural changes and requiring no additional learnable parameters. This ensures that the pre-trained
086   LLM retains its full capability for competitive downstream performance.
087
      In summary, our proposed D-Attn not only reduces computational complexity but also outperforms
088
      its self-attention counterparts by a significant margin. Under fair comparisons, D-Attn is able to
089   process 8x more visual embeddings or train 5x faster, consistently outperforming its self-attention
090   counterpart across a range of image benchmarks. We conduct rigorous ablation studies to validate
091   the effectiveness of our V2V Diagonal-Attn, debiased positional encodings, and α-weighting strate-
092   gies. Furthermore, we develop D-Attn using open-source models and train it on publicly available
093   datasets to ensure reproducibility. Code, data, and models will be made publicly available.

098   2.1   BACKGROUND AND OVERVIEW
099
      As discussed in Section 1, visual and textual inputs are created different, and therefore we propose
100
      to process them differently within an LVLM. We begin by decomposing the causal self-attention
101   mechanism in an LVLM when both visual and textual embeddings are present. As illustrated in
102   Figure 1, causal self-attention can be split into three distinct components: (1) visual-to-visual self-
103   attention (V2V Self-Attn), (2) textual-to-visual cross-attention (T2V Cross-Attn), and (3) textual-
104   to-textual self-attention (T2T Self-Attn). Together, these attention components form the foundation
105   for processing and integrating visual information in LVLMs:
106
107   • V2V Self-Attn captures contextual relationships between visual embeddings by allowing each
      visual embedding to attend to other visual embeddings.

194   2.3    T2V ATTENTION
195
196
      In T2V Cross-Attn, textual embeddings interact with visual embeddings to incorporate visual in-
197
      formation. To align with an LVLM’s native self-attention operations and architecture, unlike pre-
198   vious methods that add separate cross-attention modules (Alayrac et al., 2022), we reuse and share
199   the existing weights from the LVLM’s self-attention, modifying only the query, key, and value as-
200   signments, and revising the attention mask to be non-causal. As shown in Figure 3c left, textual
201   embeddings are used as the query, while visual embeddings serve as the key and value.
202
      Additionally, we observe a significant issue with positional bias in T2V Cross-Attn if we follow the
203
      exact attention operation in LVLMs. When visual and textual embeddings are concatenated into a
204   single 1-D sequence, an example of the positional IDs for textual and visual embeddings is given in
205   Figure 2. We can see that the rotary/relative positional encodings skew attention weights based on
206   the positional distance between visual and textual embeddings. For example, distant pairs such as
207   the textual embedding at P11 and visual embedding at P0 receive lower attention weight than pairs
208   closer together, like the textual embedding at P7 and visual embedding at P6. This bias can hinder
209   effective vision-language interaction for tasks requiring a comprehensive understanding of visual
210   context.
211   To address this issue, we propose to debiase T2V Cross-Attn by discarding the rotary/relative posi-
212   tional encodings within, effectively setting the relative positional differences to zero. Notably, this
213   modification is challenging to implement in conventional LVLMs but becomes straightforward with
214   our decomposed T2V Cross-Attn and T2T Self-Attn framework. To compensate for this removal,
215   we introduce learnable positional encodings, similar to those used in CLIP, to the visual embeddings
      before they are passed into the LLM.

498   derperform decoder-only architectures, leading them to discard the cross-attention design. In this
499   paper, we propose α-weighting strategy equivalently derived from the native attention operations

504   5   C ONCLUSION
505
506   In this paper, we introduced Decomposed Attention (D-Attn), a novel and general framework de-
507   signed to process visual and textual embeddings differently within LVLMs. Through the decompo-
508
      sition of conventional causal self-attention in LVLMs, D-Attn reduces the computational complexity
      from O(|V |2 ) to O(|V |) by diagonalizing V2V Self-Attn, and improve model performance by de-
509
      biasing T2V Cross-Attn. To merge back visual and textual information, our proposed α-weighting
510
      strategy preserves the capabilities of pre-trained LLMs with minimal modifications. Extensive ex-
511   periments and rigorous analyses demonstrate that D-Attn consistently outperforms its S-Attn coun-
512   terpart, offering both performance gains and substantial computational savings. Our contributions
513   highlight the importance of handling visual and textual inputs differently, paving the way for more
514   efficient and effective LVLMs.
