009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Equivariant and invariant machine learning models seek to take advantage of sym-
012            metries and other structures present in the data to reduce the sample complex-
013            ity of learning. Empirical work has suggested that data-driven methods, such as
014            regularization and data augmentation, may achieve a comparable performance as
015            genuinely invariant models, but theoretical results are still limited. In this work,
016            we conduct a theoretical comparison of three different approaches to achieve in-
017            variance: data augmentation, regularization, and hard-wiring. We focus on mean
018
               squared error regression with deep linear networks, where we specifically con-
               sider rank-bounded linear maps which do not have a linear parametrization and
019
               which can be hard-wired to be invariant to specific group actions. We show that
020
               the optimization problems resulting from hard-wiring and data augmentation have
021            the same critical points, all of which are saddles except for the global optimum.
022            In contrast, regularization leads to a larger number of critical points, again all
023            of which are saddles except for the global optimum. The regularization path is
024            continuous and converges to the optimum of the hard-wired problem.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Equivariant and invariant models are a class of machine learning models designed to incorporate
030   specific symmetries or invariances that are known to exist in the data. An equivariant model ensures
031   that when the input undergoes a certain transformation, the model’s output transforms in a pre-
032   dictable way. Many powerful hard-wired equivariant and invariant structures have been proposed
033
      over the recent years (see, e.g., Cohen & Welling, 2016; Zaheer et al., 2017; Geiger & Smidt, 2022;
      Liao et al., 2024). Such models are widely employed and have achieved state-of-the-art level per-
034
      formance across various scientific fields, including condensed-matter physics (Fang et al., 2023),
035
      catalyst design (Zitnick et al., 2020), drug discovery (Igashov et al., 2024), as well as several others.
036
037   Given an explicit description of the desired invariance and equivariance structures, a direct way to
038   implement them is by hard-wiring a neural network in a way that constraints the types of functions
039
      that it can represent so that they are contained within the desired class. Another intuitive method
      to approximately enforce invariance and equivariance is data augmentation, where one instead sup-
040
      plies additional data in order to guide the network towards selecting functions from the desired class.
041
      Both approaches have shown to be viable for obtaining invariant or equivariant solutions (see, e.g.,
042   Gerken & Kessel, 2024; Moskalev et al., 2023). However, it is not entirely clear how the learn-
043   ing processes and in particular the optimization problems compare. An obvious drawback of data
044   augmentation is that the number of model parameters as well as the number of training data points
045   may be large. On the other hand, it is known that constrained models (Finzi et al., 2021), or under-
046   parameterized models, can have a more complex optimization landscape, but the specific interplay
047   between the amount of data and the structure of the data is not well understood. We are interested
048   in the following question: how do invariance, regularization, and data augmentation influence the
049   optimization process and the resulting solutions of learning? To start developing an understanding,
050
      we investigate the simplified setting of invariant linear networks, for which we investigate the static
      loss landscape of the three respective optimization problems.
051
052   The loss landscapes of neural networks are among the most intriguing and actively studied topics
053   in theoretical deep learning. In particular, a series of works has documented the benefits of overpa-
      rameterization in making the optimization landscape more benevolent (see, e.g., Poston et al., 1991;


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Gori & Tesi, 1992; Soltanolkotabi et al., 2019; Simsek et al., 2021; 2023; Karhadkar et al., 2024).
055   This stands at odds with the success of data augmentation, since when using data augmentation as
056   done in practice, even enormous models may no longer be overparameterized and may have fewer
057   parameters than the number of training data points (see, e.g., Garg et al., 2022; Belkin et al., 2019).
058   Beyond overparameterization, the effects of different architecture choices on the loss landscape are
059   of interest (see, e.g., Li et al., 2018). As mentioned above, equivariant and invariant architectures
060   are of particular interest, as they could potentially help dramatically reduce the sample complexity
061   of learning within a clearly defined framework. This has been documented theoretically in a recent
062   stream of works (see, e.g., Mei et al., 2021; Tahmasebi & Jegelka, 2023). However, the impact of
063
      these architecture choices on the optimization landscape is still underexplored. Equivariant linear
      networks have received interest as simplified models to obtain concrete and actionable insights for
064
      more complex neural networks (see, e.g., Chen & Zhu, 2023; Kohn et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023;
065
      Nordenfors et al., 2024).
066
067   Our work advances this line of investigation by considering the optimization problems arising from
068   data augmentation, regularization and hard-wiring. We consider linear networks whose end-to-end
069
      functions are rank-constrained and thus cannot be simply re-parameterized as linear models. The
      non-convexity of the function space makes it nontrivial to draw conclusions about the impact of
070
      the constraints imposed by invariances. These models are a natural point of departure to study
071
      other networks with nonlinear function space, such as networks with nonlinear activation functions.
072   We observe in particular that rank constraints are common in practice. For example, in generative
073   models such as variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2022), the hidden layer is
074   usually narrower than the input and output layers with the purpose of capturing a low-dimensional
075   latent representation of the data. In large language models, low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
076   2021) is also used to reduce the number of trainable parameters for downstream tasks. In these and
077   other cases where the architecture has a narrow intermediate linear layer, rank constraints arise.
078
079   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
080
081   In this work, we study the impact of invariance in learning by considering and comparing the opti-
082   mization problems that arise in linear invariant neural networks with a non-linear function space.
083
084          • We consider three optimization problems: data augmentation, constrained model, and reg-
085
               ularization. We show that these problems are equivalent in terms of their global optima, in
               the limit of strong regularization and full data augmentation.
086
087          • We study the regularization path and show that it continuously connects the global optima
088            of the regularized problem and the global optima of the constrained invariant model.
089          • We are able to characterize all the critical points in function space for all three problems.
090            In fact, the critical points for data augmentation and the constrained model are the same.
091            There are more critical points for the unconstrained model with regularization.
092
093   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
094
095   Loss Landscapes The static optimization landscape of linear networks has been studied in nu-
096   merous works, whereby most works consider fully-connected networks. In particular, the seminal
097   work of Baldi & Hornik (1989) showed for a two-layer linear network that the square loss has a
098   single minimum up to trivial symmetry and all other critical points are saddles. Kawaguchi (2016)
099
      considered the deep case and showed the existence of bad saddles in parameter space for networks
      with three or more layers. Laurent & Brecht (2018) showed that for deep linear networks with no
100
      bottlenecks, all local minima are global for arbitrary convex differentiable losses, and Zhou & Liang
101
      (2018) offered a full characterization of the critical points for the square loss. The more recent work
102   of Trager et al. (2020) found that for deep linear networks with bottlenecks, the non-existence of
103   non-global local minima is very particular to the square loss. Several works have also considered
104   more specialized linear network architectures, such as symmetric parametrization (Tarmoun et al.,
105   2021) and deep linear convolutional networks (Kohn et al., 2022; 2024a). These and the recent work
106   of Shahverdi (2024) also discuss the critical points in parameter and in function space. In this context
107   we may also highlight the work of Levin et al. (2024), which studies the effect of parametrization on
      an optimization landscape. In contrast to these works, we focus on deep linear networks with bottle-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      necks that are invariant to a given group action. The corresponding functions are rank-constrained
109   and thus cannot be simply re-parameterized as linear models.
110
111   Training Dynamics Although analyzing the training dynamics is not the main focus of our work,
112   we would like to briefly highlight several related works in this direction. Many works have studied
113   the convergence of parameter optimization in deep linear networks, which to this date remains an
114   interesting topic even in the case of fully-connected layers (Arora et al., 2018; 2019a;b; Xu et al.,
115   2023; Bah et al., 2021; Bréchet et al., 2023; Saxe et al., 2013). For certain types of linear convo-
116   lutional networks, Gunasekar et al. (2018) studied the implicit bias of parameter optimization. In
117   the context of equivariant models, Chen & Zhu (2023) discuss the implicit bias of gradient flow on
118
      linear equivariant steerable networks in group-invariant binary classification.
119
120
      Invariance, regularization, and data augmentation A few works try to understand the differ-
      ence between the various aforementioned methods to achieve invariance. Geiping et al. (2023) seek
121
      to disentangle the mechanisms through which data augmentation operates and suggest that data
122
      augmentation that promotes invariances may provide greater value than enforcing invariance alone,
123   particularly when working with small to medium-sized datasets. Beside data augmentation, Botev
124   et al. (2022) claims that explicit regularization can improve generalization and outperform models
125   that achieve invariance by averaging predictions of non-invariant models. Moskalev et al. (2023)
126   empirically show that the invariance learned by data augmentation deteriorates rapidly, while mod-
127   els with regularization maintain low invariance error even under substantial distribution drift. Our
128   work is inspired by their experiments, and we seek to theoretically study whether data augmentation
129   can learn genuine invariance. A recent work by Kohn et al. (2024b) investigates linear neural net-
130   works through the lens of algebraic geometry and computes the dimension, singular points, and the
131
      Euclidean distance degree, which serves as an upper bound on the complexity of the optimization
      problem. We are also consider the number of critical points but are primarily interested in the com-
132
      parison of the loss landscapes arising from different methods. The work of Gideoni (2023) inves-
133
      tigates the training dynamics of linear regression with data augmentation. In contrast, we consider
134   regression with rank-bounded linear maps and also discuss the effect of regularization. Nordenfors
135   et al. (2024) investigate the optimization dynamics of a neural network with data augmentation and
136   compare it to an invariance hard-wired model. The authors show that the data augmented model
137   and the hard-wired model have the same stationary points within the set of representable equivariant
138   maps E, but does not offer conclusions about stationary points that are not in E. In contrast, we
139   obtain a result that describes all critical points in a non-linear function space of rank-constrained
140   linear maps and show that all of them are indeed invariant.

      2.3   L OW R ANK A PPROXIMATION
204
205   For a linear network with r = min{d0 , . . . , dL }, the function space consists of dL × d0 matrices
206   of rank at most r. Optimization in such a model is closely related to the problem of approximating
207   a given matrix by a rank bounded matrix. When the approximation error is measured in Frobenius
208   norm, Eckart & Young (1936a) show that the optimal bounded-rank approximation of a matrix is
209   given in terms of the top components in its singular value decomposition (see, e.g., Strang, 2019,
210   I.9): If A = U ΣV T = σ1 u1 v1T + · · · + σn un vnT and B is any matrix of rank r, then ∥A − B∥F ≥
211   ∥A − Ar ∥F , where Ar = σ1 u1 v1T + · · · + σr ur vrT . Mirsky (1960) showed that this result in fact
212
      holds for any matrix norm that depends only on the singular values.
213   There are several generalizations of this result, for instance to bounded-rank approximation with
214   some fixed entries (Golub et al., 1987), weighted least squares (Ruben & Zamir, 1979; Dutta & Li,
215   2017), and approximation of symmetric matrices by rank-bounded symmetric positive semidefinite
      matrices (Dax, 2014). However, for general norms or general matrix constraints, the problem is

217   of approximating a given matrix with a rank-bounded matrix that is constrained to within the set of

503   5                 C ONCLUSION
504
505   This work explores learning with invariances from the perspective of the associated optimization
506   problems. We investigate the loss landscape of linear invariant neural networks across the settings
507   of data augmentation, constrained models, and explicit regularization, for which we characterized
508
      the form of the global optima (Proposition 3, Theorem 1, Proposition 2). We find that data augmen-
      tation and constrained models share the same global optima (Theorem 3), which also correspond
509
      to the limit of the global optima in the regularized problem (Theorem 2). Additionally, the critical
510
      points in both data augmentation and constrained models are identical, while regularization gener-
511   ally introduces more critical points (Proposition 4). Though our theoretical results are for linear
512   networks, since we consider a non-convex function space, it is natural to conjecture that some of the
513   conclusions might carry over to other models with non-convex function space. Empirical results in
514   Appendix A.11 indicate that data augmentation and a constrained model indeed achieve a similar
515   loss in the late phase of training for two-layer neural networks with different activation functions. At
516   the same time, we observe that for models with a higher expressive power it is more difficult to learn
517   invariance from the data. Based on our theoretical results, we suggest that data augmentation may
518   have similar performance to constrained models, but will incur higher data and computing costs.
519
      The regularized model does not require more data and should have a performance close to the con-
      strained model, but it may induce more critical points. The constrained model should have the best
520
      performance though one might need to design the invariant architecture carefully before feeding the
521
      data to the model.
522
523   Limitations and future work We are focusing on deep linear networks, which are a simplified
524   model of neural networks. Nonetheless, we considered the interesting case of rank-bounded end-
525   to-end maps, which is a non-convex function space. Owing to the geometry of this model and the
526   mean squared error loss (MSE), the global optima in all three optimization problems are the same.
527   However, this is generally not true when the function class is more complicated or the loss is not
528   the MSE. Moskalev et al. (2023) empirically suggest that data-driven methods fail to learn genuine
529   invariance in weight-tying shallow ReLU networks for classification tasks with the cross-entropy
530   loss. Experiments suggest that nonlinear networks may still learn invariance via data augmentation
531
      when trained with enough data. It would be interesting to investigate this phenomenon theoretically.
      Furthermore, as mentioned in Section 4.3, the training dynamics of our setup is also worth studying.
