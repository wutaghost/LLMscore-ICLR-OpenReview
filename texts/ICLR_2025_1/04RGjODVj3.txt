                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               Existing EEG-based brain-computer interface (BCI) systems require long calibra-
               tion sessions from the intended users to train the models, limiting their use in real-
016
               world applications. Additionally, despite containing user-specific information and
017
               features correlating with BCI performance of a user, resting-state EEG data is un-
018            derutilized, especially in motor imagery decoding tasks. To address the challenge
019            of within and across-user generalisation, we propose a novel architecture, Hyper-
020            EEGNet, which integrates HyperNetworks (HNs) with the EEGNet architecture
021            to adaptively generate weights for motor imagery classification based on resting-
022            state data. Our approach performs similarly in a Leave-Subject-Out scenario using
023            a dataset with 9 participants, compared to the baseline EEGNet. When the dataset
024            size is scaled, with 33 participants’ datasets, the model demonstrates its generali-
025            sation capabilities using the information from resting state EEG data, particularly
026
               when faced with unseen subjects. Our model can learn robust representations in
               both cross-session and cross-user scenarios, opening a novel premise to leverage
027
               the resting state data for downstream tasks like motor imagery classification. The
028
               findings also demonstrate that such models with smaller footprints reduce mem-
029            ory and storage requirements for edge computing. The approach opens up avenues
030            for faster user calibration and better feasibility of edge computing, a favourable
031            combination to push forward the efforts to bring BCIs to real-world applications.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The growing use of electroencephalograms (EEGs) in brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) has gained
037   attention due to their non-invasive nature and high temporal resolution, making them ideal for de-
038   coding brain activity patterns in real-time (Schalk et al., 2024). BCIs, providing an interface between
039   the brain and external devices, have applications in neurorehabilitation, assistive technologies, and
040   neuroprosthetics. Among the various paradigms within BCIs, motor imagery (MI) decoding, which
041   involves classifying imagined movements by the users from EEG signals, is of greater interest for
042   decoding motor control. However, despite advances in hardware and software pipelines, MI-based
043   BCIs have substantial challenges to bring them to real-world applications for generalised usage. The
044
      challenges with non-invasive BCIs are particularly in achieving robust and consistent performance
      across users and sessions (Saha & Baumert, 2020).
045
046   An outstanding challenge in MI-BCI systems is the variability in brain signals across users and
047   sessions. This variability arises from differences in individual neural patterns, low signal-to-noise
048   ratio, and varying conditions of the users, like fatigue or attention (Pan et al., 2022; Kobler et al.,
049   2022). These differences cause inconsistencies in decoding MI patterns, limiting the generalizability
050
      of BCIs in real-world applications. The ability to generalize across users and sessions is necessary
      for practical and accessible applications of BCIs, especially in scenarios where collecting large
051
      amounts of personalized data is unfeasible.
052
053   In addition to cross-user variability, BCI performance is also hindered by BCI illiteracy (Allison
      & Neuper, 2010), where certain individuals cannot generate the neural signals necessary for BCI

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      control. Though the concept of BCI illiteracy has been debated and its cause is a subject of research
055   (Becker et al., 2022; Thompson, 2019; Alonso-Valerdi & Mercado-Garcı́a, 2021), the result is ev-
056   ident across datasets. This makes designing universally effective BCIs challenging. Research has
057   also focused on the predictors of BCI performance, helping to pre-identify individuals who may
058   face difficulties with BCIs, which may guide personalized interventions or optimizations. Impor-
059   tantly, much of the research has used resting-state EEG data to develop these predictors (Tzdaka
060   et al., 2020; Trocellier et al., 2024; Blankertz et al., 2010). Resting-state data, collected while the
061   user is relaxed, offers insights into baseline brain activity without task-specific requirements, mak-
062   ing it an attractive candidate for predicting BCI proficiency. Moreover, resting state EEG data is
063
      also a marker for user identification (Ma, 2015; Choi et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019), depicting
      user-specific information.
064
065   Data-driven deep-learning models have effectively improved BCI performance on different tasks
066   (Hossain et al., 2023; Tibrewal et al., 2022). Transfer learning, where models trained on data from
067   one individual or group can be adapted to another, holds promise for creating systems that work
068   across different users, including able-bodied and SCI patients (Nagarajan et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
069
      2021). Furthermore, a study by Camille Benaroch & Lotte (2022) has used user-specific frequencies
      to optimize decoding algorithms, applying data-driven approaches. However, to our knowledge, this
070
      is the first work to use resting state EEG data to train the model for motor-imagery classification.
071
      The major contributions of this work are as follows:
072
073          • Propose a novel HyperEEGNet architecture using HyperNetworks to learn unique user-
074            specific representations as adaptive weights for the underlying task in EEG decoding.
075          • Demonstrate the significance of resting state EEG data to solve downstream tasks like mo-
076            tor imagery classification using data-driven learning.

080   2.1   DATASETS
081
082   The dataset used in this study consists of electroencephalogram (EEG) recordings from 87 individ-
083   uals who participated in motor imagery (MI) tasks and resting-state conditions Dreyer et al. (2023).
084   The dataset is unique, given the large number of participants and the available recordings for each
085   user. The EEG data were collected using 27 electrodes placed with a 10-20 configuration system,
086   each sampling at a rate of 512 Hz. The dataset consisted of 70 hours of recordings of 8-second long
087
      runs when participants performed motor imagery, i.e. imagining left and right-hand movements
      following a visual cue on the screen.
088
089   This work used the sub-dataset ”A” with 60 participants. The dataset mentions that 18 participants
090   reported having noisy channel data or distractions from the environment during the sessions. These
091   participants are ignored in the study. The dataset has two runs for each participant, which were
092   used for training the model, while the rest of the four runs are termed ”online” runs. Following the
093
      benchmark set by Dreyer et al. Dreyer et al. (2023), each participant’s two ”acquisition” runs are
      used for training, and the four online runs are used as test sets for the within-user across-session
094
      scenario. For the across-user scenario, data from the last 9 participants ( 20%) was considered a
095
      test set, while the rest of the data from the 33 participants was used for training. A band-pass filter
096   with a frequency range of 0.5-40 Hz was used to prepare the raw EEG data for analysis. Epochs, or
097   time segments of EEG data, were created by segmenting the 3 seconds of data following the event
098   marker at the onset of the visual cue for movement imagination. The resting state data was extracted
099   from the first two seconds of the trial, where the participants focused on a fixation cue and were not
100   explicitly instructed to rest.
101
      To understand the effectiveness of the proposed approach on a comparatively smaller dataset with 9
102   participants, BNCI 2014 IIa Competition Dataset Brunner et al. (2008) is used. The dataset consists
103   of electroencephalogram (EEG) signals from 9 individuals who participated in motor imagery (MI)
104   tasks and resting state conditions. The EEG data were collected using 22 electrodes, each sampling
105   at a frequency of 250 Hz. The analysis involved two classes: right-hand and left-hand movement
106   imagery, while feet and tongue movements were ignored. Each epoch consisted of 4 second-long
107   motor imagery activity. The resting state data was extracted from the first two seconds of the trial,
      where the participants focused on a fixation cue and were not explicitly instructed to rest.

      2.2     R ESTING S TATE C ONNECTIVITY A NALYSIS
109
110   The resting state analysis for both datasets was common. The preprocessing phase for the analysis
111   involved using MNE-Python Gramfort et al. (2013) to process resting-state EEG data from each
112   epoch spanning a time window from 0 to 2 seconds relative to the trial start onset. A continuous
113   wavelet transform (CWT) using Morlet wavelets Tallon-Baudry et al. (1997) was then applied to
114   decompose the EEG signals into these desired frequency bands: theta (4–8 Hz), alpha (8–13 Hz),
115   and beta (13–30 Hz). To analyze steady-state connectivity patterns from resting-state EEG data,
116
      we employed spectral connectivity measures, including coherence (COH) and phase-locking value
      (PLV) Lachaux et al. (1999).
117
118   Spectral connectivity was estimated using Coherence (COH) and phase-locking value (PLV) as con-
119   nectivity metrics to evaluate both amplitude and phase coupling between different brain regions.
120   Coherence measures the linear relationship between two signals in the frequency domain, capturing
121   both the amplitude and phase coupling across frequency bands.

140   2.3     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING
141
142   This study proposes a novel architecture that combines the feature extraction capabilities of EEGNet
143   (Lawhern et al., 2018) with the adaptability of HyperNetworks (Ha et al., 2017) for motor imagery
144
      classification. This method uses a hypernetwork to generate adaptive weights for EEGNet, lever-
      aging user-specific information from the resting state EEG data for cross-session and cross-user
145
      generalisation. Figure 1 depicts the model architecture and the learning mechanism.
146
147
      2.3.1    EEGN ET
148
149   EEGNet is a specialized neural network architecture designed to handle the unique characteristics
150   of EEG signals. The model includes temporal and spatial convolutional layers optimized to cap-
151   ture relevant patterns from the multi-channel EEG data. Temporal convolutional layers focus on
152   identifying patterns within the time domain of the signals, while spatial convolutional layers extract
153   information based on the relationships between different EEG channels (Tshukahara, 2021). The
154
      EEGNet model was implemented using the Torcheeg framework (Zhang et al., 2024).
155
      2.3.2    H YPER N ETWORK
156
157   Hypernetworks are neural networks that generate the weights for another network (the main net-
158   work: EEGNet) instead of learning them directly. For this study, the designed hypernetwork gener-
159   ated the weights of the core layers (conv2d and linear layers) of EEGNet. The hypernetwork (Hy-
160   perNet) is a fully connected neural network with hidden layers of sizes 256 and 512, followed by a
161   dropout with a probability of 0.3 to improve generalization. The resting state EEG data extracted
      from the two-second long time window are the inputs to this hypernetwork.

      2.3.3    H YPER EEGN ET T RAINING
181
182   The combined architecture, including the HyperNet and EEGNet, is trained as follows:
183
184           • HyperNet is used as a feedforward network to generate weights for EEGNet using resting-
185             state connectivity data with dropout.
186           • Motor imagery activity data is extracted from a predefined time window (based on the
187             experimental paradigm) in the raw data to perform the binary class classification with a
188             forward pass on EEGNet with the generated weights from HyperNet.
189           • Cross entropy loss is accumulated for a batch of 50 epochs, and backpropagation is per-
190             formed only on HyperNet parameters. Adam optimiser with learning rate 1e-4 is used.
191
192   2.4     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION
193
194   The experiment is set to evaluate two conditions: cross-session and cross-user for each dataset:
195   BCI IV IIa and Dreyer et al. (2023) with a baseline comparison with EEGNet. The experiments
196   were performed using the dataset from MOABB library (Aristimunha et al., 2023), and models were
197   trained and evaluated using Torch and sci-kit-learn libraries.

      For the dataset from Dreyer et al. (2023), the ”acquisition runs” from 33 participants are used for
201
      training and stratified 5-fold cross-validation is used to select the best model. Performance evalua-
202
      tion with accuracy metrics is performed for the ”online” runs to evaluate HyperEEGNet compared
203   to EEGNet.
204
205   For the BCI IV IIa dataset, the data from all nine participants is divided into five folds with stratified

      cross-validation; each fold in the iteration is considered as a test set while the other set is split with
      an 80-20 ratio to choose the best-performing model on the validation set. Accuracy metrics on the
207
      test set are evaluated for HyperEEGNet and compared with EEGNet.

277

      4.1   L EARNING R EPRESENTATIONS FROM R ESTING S TATE
279
280   Experimental results indicate the unique possibility of leveraging resting state EEG data for learning
281   downstream tasks like motor imagery classification. The comparison across two different sizes of
282   datasets also confirms the positive outcomes of the efforts in the field to build large, robust datasets
283   for training foundational models (Ferrante et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). During the training phase
284   for HyperEEGNet architecture on the Dreyer et al. 2023 dataset, we also observed a steep learning
285   curve, indicating a rapid convergence in 5̃0 epochs. The HyperNet was also prone to overfitting with
286   a larger epoch size (500+) for training, especially in the case of cross-user conditions, where it was
287   more evident. Notably, the smaller standard deviation for HyperEEGNet across the performance
288
      benchmarks indicates more stable performance across subjects than EEGNet.
289   Though the proposed approach focused on learning the adaptive weights for two class motor imagery
290   classification, it opens up a future direction to generalise the learning mechanism across different
291   downstream tasks or a larger number of classes for motor imagery.
292
293
      4.2   I NTERPRETING H YPER EEGN ET
294
295
      Apart from the performance metrics, some takeaways indicate potential as well as raise interesting
296
      questions in learning via the proposed approach. There are participants from both datasets known to
297   have lower BCI performance across studies using different classifiers, and the contrary is true where
298   few participants have consistently higher accuracies when trained specifically on the participant’s
299   data. For example, Participant ID 4 in the BCI Competition dataset has low BCI performance. How-
300   ever, Participant ID 3 has consistently high accuracies when using different classifiers. Surprisingly,
301   the proposed approach performs well on Participant IDs 4, 5, and 7 but doesn’t do well enough for
302   Participant ID 3 in cross-user scenarios. These questions are open for exploration since they need
303   to interpret the weights generated by the HyperNet; how do they compare with an EEGNet trained
304   directly on activity data? Moreover, the resting state data can be represented in many ways; the
305
      proposed work did not explore optimising the representations for resting-state brain connectivity.
      There could be other important features useful for downstream tasks that are not captured in the
306
      connectivity measures.
307
308
309   4.3   T RANSFER L EARNING AND F EW- SHOT L EARNING
310
311   While the current approach can be considered an approach towards meta-learning by learning to
312   learn weights of the downstream task, the work has not explored the paradigm of few-shot learning
313   for faster adaptation compared to other existing approaches or the efficacy of this architecture com-
314   pared with other transfer learning approaches. A benchmark against approaches for transfer learning
315
      and few-shot learning successful on EEG datasets is necessary to justify the approach holistically.
316
317   4.4   H YPER N ETS FOR SMALLER FOOTPRINTS
318
319   Current work focused on successfully learning representations from resting state EEG data for mo-
320   tor imagery without optimising the size of the HyperNet. However, hypernetwork architectures
321   are helpful for model compression. Efforts towards model compression without an impact on per-
322   formance can be fruitful for real-world deployment of the BCI models. Task-specific information
323   like restricting the input frequency bands and identifying efficient connectivity metrics can be an
      interesting future direction.

      5   C ONCLUSION
325
326
      This work propose a novel HyperEEGNet architecture and introduces a promising new direction in
327   EEG-based brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) by leveraging HyperNetworks to adaptively generate
328   weights for EEGNet, utilizing resting-state EEG data for downstream motor imagery classification.
329   This approach underscores the untapped potential of resting-state EEG, not only as a passive baseline
330   to evaluate or correlate the BCI performance or illiteracy but as a source of user-specific features
331   that enhance generalization across subjects and sessions. The positive results across cross-user and
332   cross-session conditions indicate that resting-state data can be effectively harnessed for learning
333   personalized representations in BCIs.
334   With focused efforts, instead of relying solely on task-related data, using resting-state data for model
335   training can reduce the need for large amounts of labelled task data, which is often a bottleneck in
336   real-world BCI applications. Furthermore, the architecture’s rapid convergence and susceptibility
337   to overfitting emphasize the need for further research into regularization techniques and adaptive
338   training strategies specific to hypernetwork-based models. Looking forward, the findings suggest
339   several key avenues for future exploration. This work sets the stage for more scalable, adaptive, and
340   personalized BCIs, bridging the gap between laboratory research and practical, everyday use.
