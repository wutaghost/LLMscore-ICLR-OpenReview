009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the continuous advancement of video generation, researchers have achieved
012            speech-driven body language synthesis, such as co-speech gestures. However,
013            due to the lack of paired data for visual speech (i.e., lip movements) and body
014            languages, existing methods typically rely solely on audio-only speech, which
015            struggles to correctly synthesize target results in noisy environments. To overcome
016            this limitation, we propose an Audio-Visual Speech-Driven Synthesis (AV-SDS)
017            method tailored for body language synthesis, aiming for robust synthesis even
018
               under noisy conditions. Given that each body language modality data has its
               corresponding audio speech, AV-SDS adopts a two-stage synthesis framework
019
               based on speech discrete units, consisting of the AV-S2UM and Unit2X modules.
020
               It uses speech discrete units as carriers to construct a direct mapping from audio-
021            visual speech to each body language. Considering the distinct characteristics of
022            different body languages, AV-SDS can be implemented based on semantic and
023            acoustic discrete units, respectively, to achieve high-semantic and high-rhythm body
024            language synthesis. Experimental results demonstrate that our AV-SDS achieves
025            superior performance in synthesizing multiple body language modalities in noisy
026            environments, delivering noise-robust body language synthesis. For samples and
027            further information, please visit demo page at https://av-sds.github.
028            io/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent years have witnessed great advancements in video generation (Tian et al., 2024; Richard et al.,
      2021), and researchers have successfully achieved various forms of speech-driven body language
034
      synthesis (Liu et al., 2023), including talking head generation (Prajwal et al., 2020; 2022), co-gesture
035
      generation (Liu et al., 2022b;a; Yang et al., 2023b), 3D facial animation (Richard et al., 2021; Fan
036   et al., 2022; Xing et al., 2023), etc. Despite significant progress in these fields, existing methods
037   are limited to employing audio-only speech for synthesis, as shown in Figure 1. However, in some
038   complex environments such as construction sites and plants (Ahmed & Gadelmoula, 2022), it is
039   difficult to extract clear audio signal, especially where headsets are unavailable due to regulations of
040   production safety.
041
      To understand speech in noisy scenes, researchers often use visual speech assistance (Afouras et al.,
042   2018b; Shi et al., 2022b). For example, (Wang & Zhu, 2021) proposed a vision-based human-
043   machine communication framework through gesture recognition, and (Ray & Teizer, 2012) also
044   confirmed the feasibility of real-time posture analysis used in workers’ ergonomics training. However,
045   in most speech-driven body language synthesis tasks, such as Cross-ID talking head synthesis (Wang
046   et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022), Cross-ID landmark generation (Hsu et al., 2022), 3D facial animation,
047   and speech gesture synthesis, there is a paucity of paired data between visual speech and body
048   languages (Daněček et al., 2022). This scarcity hinders the realization of audio-visual speech-driven
049   body language synthesis models.
050   Since the audio speech modality has a large amount of data supporting self-supervised learning (Kahn
051   et al., 2020; Zen et al., 2019), many researchers have successfully used speech discrete units to
052   represent speech information. Inspired by direct speech-to-speech translation (Lee et al., 2021),
053   and considering that each body language (including visual speech, i.e., lip movements) is aligned
      with audio and has ample training data, can we use discrete speech units extracted from audio

      In this paper, we introduce a two-stage audio-visual speech-driven body language synthesis model

074
      The AV-S2UM module consists of an audio-visual speech encoder (Shi et al., 2022a) and several
075
      transposed convolutional layers, which can map the audiovisual speech to the corresponding discrete
076   speech units. Subsequently, these discrete speech units are input into the Unit2X module to
077   synthesize the corresponding multimodal body language data. In particular, to meet the characteristics
078   of different body language modalities, the AV-SDS can be implemented based on different speech
079   discrete units: an acoustic-centered model for co-speech gestures which focus more on the emotion
080   and rhythm of speech (Loehr, 2007), implemented based on acoustic discrete units; and a semantic-
081   centered model for modalities that focus on the semantic information of speech (talking head, facial
082   landmark, and 3D face mesh), proposed based on semantic discrete units. Experiments show that the
083   Unit2X module can successfully resynthesize various body languages from discrete speech units.
084
      Furthermore, experiments on various speech-driven body language synthesis tasks under different
      noise conditions demonstrate that our AV-SDS is capable of noise-robust speech-driven synthesis.
085
      The main contributions are as follows:
086
087   • We propose a novel two-stage, noise-robust, audio-visual speech-driven body language synthesis
088     model (AV-SDS) based on discrete speech units.
089   • We propose AV-S2UM module, which excels in retaining speech information in noisy environments.
090

092
      • Our experiments confirm the robustness and effectiveness of AV-SDS across varying noise condi-
093
        tions, validating its potential for noise-robust speech-driven body language synthesis.

099
      Body language (Liu et al., 2023) plays a pivotal role in facilitating effective communication and
100
      enhancing social interactions. Over the years, numerous researchers (Ye et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
101
      2020) have dedicated their efforts to speech-driven body language synthesis, aiming to create digital
102   avatars that seamlessly synchronize with spoken content. Leveraging advancements in generative
103   technology, researchers have made notable progress, culminating in the development of both 2D
104   talking head avatars (Zhou et al., 2020; Prajwal et al., 2020) and 3D facial animation (Richard et al.,
105   2021; Xing et al., 2023), driven by audio and speech inputs. Moreover, recognizing the importance of
106   comprehensive avatar technology, researchers (Yang et al., 2023b;c) have expanded their exploration
107   into co-speech gesture synthesis, harnessing the rhythm and cadence of speech to imbue digital
      avatars with lifelike gestures.

      However, existing methods can only achieve multimodal body language synthesis driven solely by
109   audio speech, lacking robustness in noisy environments. To address this gap, we propose the first
110   direct audio-visual speech driven multi-modal body language synthesis model, enabling speech-driven
111   synthesis even in noisy conditions.

113   2.2   ROBUST AUDIO -V ISUAL S PEECH L EARNING
114
115   Audio speech understanding (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021) technology has advanced rapidly,
116   effectively conveying speech content information. However, in noisy environments like outdoors,
117   audio-only models often lack robustness and struggle to resist environmental noise interference.
118   To address this, researchers (Afouras et al., 2018b) have begun exploring the use of visual speech
119
      (lip movements) to enhance speech understanding capabilities under noisy conditions. Some re-
      searchers (Afouras et al., 2018a;b) initially collected paired audio-visual speech corpus from TED and
120
      BBC for research. Subsequently, AV-HuBERT (Shi et al., 2022a) achieves noise-robust audio-visual
121
      speech recognition as well as lip reading, successfully recognizing speech content in noisy condition.
122   Additionally, some (Gao & Grauman, 2021; Hsu et al., 2023) propose using visual speech to tackle
123   the cocktail party problem, effectively addressing the challenge of distinguishing the active speaker
124   track among multiple speakers.
125
      However, in speech-driven multimodal generation tasks, many body language modalities (e.g., meshes
126
      and gestures) lack paired data with visual speech (Daněček et al., 2022), hindering robust audio-visual
127
      speech-driven synthesis. In this paper, we introduce a two-stage AV-SDS framework that utilizes
128   discrete speech units as carriers to overcome this challenge of unpaired data.
129
130
      2.3   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING IN S PEECH
131
132   Self-supervised learning methods (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021; Zeghidour et al., 2021; Yang
133   et al., 2023a) leverage unlabeled audio speech data to significantly enhance speech representation and
134   improve the performance of various speech-related tasks. For instance, some researchers (Baevski
135   et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021) employ a continuous alternation between unsupervised clustering
136   and mask prediction to augment the contextual semantic representation. Later, some (Zeghidour
137   et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023a) integrate the RVQ (residual vector quantization) module to achieve
138   a fine-grained representation of speech acoustic information. Building upon these advancements,
139
      researchers have proposed utilizing Speech SSL model to discretize speech, thereby exploring new
      capabilities of speech models. Specifically, Lee et al. (2021) employs semantic discrete units as
140
      a bridge to achieve direct speech-to-speech translation between speeches of different languages,
141
      while Jiang et al. (2023) employs acoustic discrete units to represent acoustic information of speech,
142   enabling zero-shot TTS.
143
144   While speech discrete units hold promise in various speech-related tasks, their application in speech-
145
      driven multi-modal generation tasks remains largely unexplored. In this paper, we introduce a unit-
      based multi-modal generation module, Unit2X, which represents a pioneering effort in leveraging
146
      speech discrete unit information for multi-modal generation exploration.

154

160   U = {U1 , U2 , · · · , UT }, where T is the number of discrete speech units. By using discrete speech
161   units as carriers, we achieve direct audio-visual speech-driven multimodal synthesis. Specifically, as
      described in Section 3.2, we adopt the AV-S2UM module to construct the mapping from audio-visual

192   Speech Discrete Units. The information conveyed by speech can be broadly classified into two
193   categories: semantic information and acoustic information. HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) employs
194   multiple iterations of mask prediction and K-means clustering to continuously enhance its ability
195
      to understand speech context, effectively extracting semantic discrete units that encapsulate speech
      semantics, denoted as U s . Encodec (Défossez et al., 2022) and SoundStream (Yang et al., 2023a;
196

197
      acoustic discrete unit, denoted as U a . In this paper, we utilize the semantic discrete unit U s for
198   generating modalities closely associated with semantics, such as the talking head, facial landmarks,
199   and the mesh of a 3D avatar. Conversely, the acoustic discrete unit U a is employed for generating
200   co-speech gestures, which are more closely related to emotion and rhythm.

285
      To ensure consistency across different body language modalities, we resample the audio speech in all
286   datasets to 16kHz in this paper. This allows us to extract unified speech discrete units to represent
287   semantic or acoustic information. Specifically, we use the HuBERT BASE model (Hsu et al., 2021)
288   to extract semantic discrete units and the 16kHz version of the hificodec model (Yang et al., 2023a)

290
      To facilitate effective research and encourage broad adoption, we intentionally selected fundamental,
291
      widely applicable implementations for each modality. Specifically, we use Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al.,
292   2020) for talking heads, GeneFace (Ye et al., 2023) for 3D landmarks, CodeTalker (Xing et al.,
293   2023) for face meshes, and Tri-Modal (Yoon et al., 2020) for co-speech gestures to implement the
294   corresponding speech-driven body language synthesis tasks. We believe that experiments on basic
295   implementations of different body language modalities are sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness
296   of our approach, which can be integrated with any speech-driven body language synthesis model in
297   the future. For additional details and evaluation metrics, please refer to Appendix B.

299   4.3   U NIT 2X: U NIT-BASED B ODY L ANGUAGE S YNTHESIS
300
301   Polyak et al. (2021) demonstrated the feasibility of reconstructing corresponding speech from discrete
302   units. Building on this, we attempt to synthesize different body language data from corresponding
303
      speech discrete units. As shown in Table 1, we compare the performance of various synthesis methods
      across different body language modalities. This demonstrates that speech discrete units can effectively
304
      replace the original speech as input for synthesizing corresponding body language data. For additional
305
      qualitative comparisons, please refer to Appendix B.
306
307   Audio-Based vs. Unit-Based. The audio-based method (S2X) directly extracts the corresponding
308   embedding from the mel spectrum for body language modality synthesis, while the unit-based method
309
      (Unit2X) synthesizes from discrete units. As demonstrated in the experiments, across various body
      language modalities, the unit-based method achieves performance comparable to that of the audio-
310
      based method, affirming that speech discrete units can efficiently represent the speech information
311
      necessary to generate the corresponding body language data. Additionally, in scenarios with limited
312   training data (e.g., mesh) or where cross-modal mapping is challenging to build (e.g., co-speech
313   gesture), unit-based methods prove to be more effective than methods using raw speech as input. For
314   instance, the FGD of Unit2X is 3.976, while that of S2X is 4.133. This demonstrates that speech
315   discrete units can distinctly capture various semantic and acoustic information from speech, leading
316   to superior performance in these challenging contexts.
317
      U2S+S2X vs. Unit2X. To synthesize the body language data corresponding to the speech discrete
318   unit, the simplest approach is to first re-synthesize the audio speech corresponding to the discrete unit,
319   and then synthesize the corresponding body language data from the audio speech (i.e., U2S+S2X).
320   However, this cascade method tends to accumulate errors, resulting in a significant decline in the
321   correlation between the generated body language modality data and the audio speech compared
322   to direct synthesis from the unit (the LSE-C of Unit2X is 7.34, while the LSE-C of U2S+S2X is
323   only 5.74). This further underscores the importance of the unit-based body language synthesizer
      (Unit2X).

      Audio-Only vs. Audio-Visual. In a noisy environment, models that rely solely on audio-speech

422   demonstrating the importance of visual speech for audio understanding in noisy environments.
423
      Naive Audio-Visual Method vs. Unit-Based Audio-Visual Method. Traditional visually guided
424
      speech enhancement methods only rely on a limited amount of audio-visual speech pairing data
425
      for training, making it difficult to achieve high-fidelity audio reconstruction. However, methods
426   based on discrete units, such as ReVISE and our AV-S2UM (U a ), train the AV-S2UM module and

431   can retain more original audio information compared to the end-to-end model, which can only use
      extremely limited paired data.

470   5     C ONCLUSION
471
472   Speech-driven body language synthesis aims to create intelligent digital humans that align with audio
473   speech. However, due to the lack of paired data for visual speech and body language modalities,
474
      existing methods can rely on audio-only speech, which struggles to produce accurate results under
      noisy conditions. To address this issue, we propose the first direct audio-visual speech-driven
475
      multi-modal synthesis framework, AV-SDS. This framework employs speech discrete units as an
476
      intermediate carrier in a two-stage approach to bridge audio-visual speech and various body language
477   modalities. Firstly, AV-S2UM maps audio-visual speech to unified discrete units. Then, Unit2X
478   synthesizes various multi-modal body language data from these units. We introduce Unit2X, the
479   first multi-modal body language synthesis model based on speech discrete units, and demonstrate the
480   feasibility of using speech discrete units instead of raw audio speech for body language synthesis.
481   Additionally, we propose two different implementations based on semantic discrete units and acoustic
482   discrete units for semantically related and rhythm-related body language modalities, respectively. In
483   various speech-driven multi-modal body language synthesis tasks, our AV-SDS achieves state-of-the-
484   art performance under different noise conditions, confirming its effectiveness in noisy environments.
