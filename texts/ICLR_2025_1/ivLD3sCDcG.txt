009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Oriented object detection is crucial for complex scenes such as aerial images
012            and industrial inspection, providing precise delineation by minimizing back-
013            ground interference. Recently, the weakly-supervised oriented object detection
014            has gaining attention due to its cost-effectiveness. However, the majority of exist-
015            ing weakly-supervised methods are either point-supervised or HBox-supervised,
016            which presents a challenge in achieving an optimal balance between annotation
017            cost and detection performance. In response, we introduce a novel form of line
018
               annotation, which is intermediate between point-level and plane-level annotation.
               Based on this, we present L2RBox, an end-to-end anchor-free detector that is the
019
               first line-supervised method for oriented object detection. The fundamental objec-
020
               tive of the L2RBox is to utilise line labels for the completion of label assignment
021            and the calculation of loss. In particular, the line is mapped to the correspond-
022            ing circle domain, which is then used to select training samples and calculate the
023            center-ness target by the minimum circumscribed rectangle of the circle in the di-
024            rection of the line. The regression loss that we propose is designed to support the
025            line as an optimisation target. It comprises four components, namely scale loss Ls ,
026            height loss Lh , position loss Lp and angle loss La . Extensive experimentation on
027            DOTA-v1.0 and DIOR-R has demonstrated that our L2RBox significantly outper-
028            forms point-supervised methods, while requiring only a slight increase in labeling
029
               costs. It is also noteworthy that the proposed approach also demonstrates a slight
               performance advantage over the fully-supervised FCOS in certain categories.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, oriented object detection has progressed rapidly, leveraging advancements in hori-
035
      zontal object detection Liu et al. (2020). Its fine-grained rotated bounding box (RBox) has proven
036   highly effective in complex scenarios such as aerial imagery, scene text, and industrial inspection
037   Wen et al. (2023). Although detectors have made significant progress with extensive annotated data,
038   full supervision in oriented object detection faces several challenges: the RBox annotation format is
039   less prevalent in many existing datasets, and producing RBox annotations is more expensive.
040
      To mitigate the dependence on labor-intensive RBox labeling, weakly-supervised object detection
041
      represents a solution. As illustrated in Fig. 1, existing weakly-supervised methods employ coarser-
042   grained annotations as weakly-supervised signals to predict RBox, which are roughly divided into
043   point-supervised methods and HBox-supervised methods according to the annotation. For HBox-
044   supervised methods, H2RBox Yang et al. (2023) and H2RBox-v2 Yu et al. (2024b) have explored
045   the HBox-to-RBox setting that learns RBox detectors from horizontal bounding box (HBox) anno-
046   tation. However, Plane-level annotations are still inefficient and labor-intensive. Therefore, point-
047   supervised methods PointOBB Luo et al. (2024) and Point2RBox Yu et al. (2024a) have further
048   explored more cost-effective point-level annotation forms, but these methods suffer from lower de-
049   tection accuracy and often require additional knowledge or pseudo-label generation. In summary,
050
      these weakly-supervised methods have complex structures and cannot balance detection accuracy
      and annotation costs.
051
052   In response, we trade off the annotation cost with the detection accuracy for weakly-supervised
053   methods and first propose line annotation format for oriented object detection. Specifically, we label
      the object along its central axis, and the process is flexible, allowing for some margin of error in the

      The line-level annotation introduces a novel task setting: using line annotations to achieve signif-
      icantly better performance than point-supervised methods. In this paper, we propose a simple yet
090
      effective approach dubbed as L2RBox, the first line-supervised oriented object detector. As an end-
091
      to-end anchor-free detector, the core of our L2RBox is to use line labels to complete label assignment
092   and loss calculation, where label assignment includes training sample selection and center-ness tar-
093   get calculation. Specifically, we map the line to the corresponding circle domain and use this to
094   select training samples and calculate the center-ness target by the minimum circumscribed rectan-
095   gle of the circle in the direction of the line (see Fig. 2 top L-LA). In the regression branch (see
096   Fig. 2 bottom Branches), our proposed regression loss supports the line as an optimization target
097   comprising four components: scale loss Ls , height loss Lh , position loss Lp and angle loss La .
098   Extensive experiments demonstrate that our L2RBox achieves significantly better performance than
099   point-supervised methods with only a slight increase in labeling costs. Meanwhile, our L2RBox
100
      achieves optimal results on the trade-off metric, indicating that our method can effectively balance
      detection accuracy and cost. Our main contributions are as follows:
101
102   1) To our best knowledge, we first propose line annotation format for oriented object detection,
103   which trade-off the annotation cost with the detection accuracy in weakly-supervised methods.
104   2) We propose specialized end-to-end detectors for line supervision, including label assignment
105   and loss functions that support line annotation, where label assignment includes training sample
106   selection and center-ness target calculation.

117   2.1   F ULLY-S UPERVISED O RIENTED O BJECT D ETECTION
118
119   Oriented object detection algorithms primarily focus on aerial objects, multi-oriented scene texts,
120
      retail, etc. Notable approaches in this field include the anchor-based detector Rotated RetinaNet Lin
      et al. (2017c), the anchor-free detector Rotated FCOS Tian et al. (2019), and two-stage detectors
121
      such as Oriented R-CNNXie et al. (2021), RoI Transformer Ding et al. (2019), and ReDet Han et al.
122
      (2021). To address the boundary problem caused by the periodicity of angles, RSDet Qian et al.
123   (2021) proposes a modulation loss to alleviate loss jumps. CSL Yang & Yan (2020) and DCL Yang
124   et al. (2021a) convert the angle into boundary-free coded data. GWD Yang et al. (2021b), KLD
125   Yang et al. (2021c), and KFIoU Yang et al. (2022) propose Gaussian-based losses that convert RBox
126   into a Gaussian distribution. PSC Yu & Da (2023) proposes a Phase-Shifting Coder that encodes
127   the orientation angle into periodic phases. Additionally, RepPoint-based approaches Yang et al.
128   (2019); Hou et al. (2023); Li et al. (2022a) provide new alternatives for oriented object detection by
129   predicting a set of sample points that bounds the spatial extent of an object. In this study, in order to
130   reduce the reliance on labor-intensive RBox labeling, we concentrate on the more challenging task
131
      of weakly-supervised oriented object detection.
132
133
      2.2   W EAKLY- SUPERVISED O RIENTED O BJECT D ETECTION
134
135
      Existing mainstream weakly-supervised oriented object detection approaches can be divided into
136
      HBox-supervised (plane-level) and point-supervised (point-level) methods. Furthermore, we ex-
137
      plore the feasibility of line-supervised (line-level) methods.
138
139   HBox-supervised. HBox-supervised instance segmentation methods Tian et al. (2021); Li et al.
140   (2022b); Kirillov et al. (2023) employ the HBox-Mask-RBox pipeline to derive RBox from the
141
      segmentation mask, though this is less cost-effective. A pioneering approach, H2RBox Yang et al.
      (2023), bypasses the segmentation step and directly detects RBox from HBox annotations. As a
142
      new version, H2RBox-v2 Yu et al. (2024b) exploits the inherent symmetry of objects. EIE Wang
143
      et al. (2024) leverages various contrastive cues related to angle prediction, facilitating the learning
144   of equivariance between boxes. Nevertheless, these techniques still necessitate the acquisition of
145   a considerable number of bounding box annotations. Additionally, OAOD Iqbal et al. (2021) uses
146   extra object angle, whereas KCR Zhu et al. (2023) employs RBox-annotated source datasets with
147   HBox-annotated target datasets. However, these specialized annotation forms lack universality.
148
      Point-supervised. Point-based annotations have been widely used in horizontal object detection
149
      Chen et al. (2021); Ying et al. (2023). Due to its cost-effectiveness and efficiency, point-supervised
150   oriented object detection has garnered attention. P2BNet Chen et al. (2022) uses Multiple Instance
151   Learning (MIL) to select the box with the highest confidence from multiple boxes containing points.
152   Point-to-Mask Li et al. (2023) provides a potential method for point-supervised rotating target detec-
153   tion. PointOBB Luo et al. (2024) learns object scale and angle information through self-supervised
154   learning across different views, enabling the generation of oriented bounding boxes from points.
155   Point2RBox Yu et al. (2024a) transfers object features to synthetic patterns using a sampling strat-
156   egy and trains output RBox on transformed images, enhancing the network’s perception of size
157   and rotation for improved detection accuracy. However, these methods suffer from lower detection
158
      accuracy and often require additional knowledge or pseudo-label generation.
159   Line-supervised. In light of the aforementioned methods, we have sought to strike a balance be-
160   tween the annotation cost and the detection accuracy in weakly supervised methods. To this end,
161   we explore the potential of utilizing line as a means of labeling objects, which has a cost between
      point-level and plane-level. This paper aims to fill this blank and provide a valuable starting point.

184   3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
185
186   In this section, we introduce the first implementation of oriented object detection using line as super-
187   vised information. First, an overview of our L2RBox is provided in section 3.1. Next, we introduce
188   the line annotation-based label assignment (L-LA) containing training sample selection and center-
189   ness target computation in sections 3.2 and 3.3. Finally, we present the proposed loss functions,
190   which guide the optimization of RBox based on line annotations in section 3.4.
191
192   3.1   OVERVIEW
193
      For simplicity and efficiency, our L2RBox follows the FCOS-based Tian et al. (2019) detection
194
      paradigm, which features a one-stage, anchor-free architecture. The overview of the L2RBox is
195
      shown in Fig. 2. Our L2RBox initially leverages a backbone and feature pyramid network (FPN)
196   Lin et al. (2017a) to extract multiscale features from original images. Notably, our detector is adapt-
197   able to various backbone networks, including ResNet He et al. (2016), Swin Transformer Liu et al.
198   (2021), and ConvNeXt Liu et al. (2022). Unless otherwise specified, all methods in this paper use
199   ResNet50 by default for fair comparison.
200
      Following FPN, we detect objects of different sizes at various feature map levels. Each pyramid
201
      level, denoted by Pi , corresponds to a feature level. Specifically, P3 , P4 , and P5 are derived from
202   the backbone CNN’s feature maps C3 , C4 , and C5 , followed by a 1 × 1 convolutional layer with
203   top-down connections. P6 and P7 are generated by applying a convolutional layer with a stride of 2
204   on P5 and P6 , respectively. Each feature pyramid level {P3 , P4 , P5 , P6 , P7 } is then fed directly into
205   the L2RBox Head.
206
      The L2RBox Head consists of two branches. The classification branch predicts the confidence
207
      scores, while an additional single-layer convolution predicts the center-ness of the location in par-
208   allel. The other branch is responsible for RBox regression, enabling loss computation between the
209   predicted RBox and the ground truth lines. The total loss is calculated as the weighted sum of the
210   classification and regression losses. Furthermore, we propose a line annotation-based label assign-
211   ment strategy (L-LA) that contains training sample selection and center-ness target computation.

345   4.1   DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
346
      DOTA-v1.0: DOTA-v1.0 Xia et al. (2018) is a commonly used dataset in the field of oriented
347
      object detection, which contains 2806 large-scale images. The dataset encompasses the following
348   15 distinct object classes: Bridge (BR), Harbor (HA), Ship (SH), Plane (PL), Helicopter (HC), Small
349   vehicle (SV), Large vehicle (LV), Baseball diamond (BD), Ground track field (GTF), Tennis court
350   (TC), Basketball court (BC), Soccer-ball field (SBF), Roundabout (RA), Swimming pool (SP), and
351   Storage tank (ST). The proportion of the training set, validation set, and testing set are 1/2, 1/6, and
352   1/3, respectively. For training and testing, we adhere to a standard protocol by cropping images into
353   1,024 × 1,024 patches with a stride of 824. The detection accuracy is obtained by submitting testing
354   results to DOTA’s evaluation server.
355   DIOR-R: DIOR-R Cheng et al. (2022) is an aerial image dataset. Different imaging conditions,
356   weather, seasons, and image quality are the major challenges of DIOR-R. Besides, it has high inter-
357   class similarity and intra-class diversity. The dataset comprises 190,288 objects of interest across
358   20 categories, totaling 23,463 optical images collected from Google Earth. The categories are de-
359   fined as: Airplane (APL), Airport (APO), Baseball Field (BF), Basketball Court (BC), Bridge (BR),

534
535   This paper introduces a novel line annotation format that balances the annotation cost with the de-
536   tection accuracy in weakly-supervised oriented object detection methods. We also propose the first
537   line-supervised detector, L2RBox, which includes label assignment and loss functions that support
538   line annotation. The detector employs an anchor-free architecture, enabling end-to-end detection.
539   Extensive experiments demonstrate that our approach significantly outperforms point-supervised
      methods while requiring only a slight increase in labeling costs.
