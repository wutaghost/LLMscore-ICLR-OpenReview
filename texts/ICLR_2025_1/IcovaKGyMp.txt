009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The conventional paradigm of using large language models (LLMs) for evaluat-
012            ing natural language generation (NLG) systems typically relies on two key inputs:
013            (1) a clear definition of the NLG task to be evaluated and (2) a list of pre-defined
014            evaluation criteria. This process treats LLMs as “passive critics,” strictly following
015            human-defined criteria for evaluation. However, as new NLG tasks emerge, the
016            criteria for assessing text quality can vary greatly. Consequently, these rigid eval-
017            uation methods struggle to adapt to diverse NLG tasks without extensive prompt
018            engineering customized for each specific task. To address this limitation, we in-
019
               troduce ACTIVE -C RITIC, a novel LLM-based NLG evaluation protocol that en-
               ables LLMs to function as “active critics.” Specifically, our protocol comprises
020
               two key stages. In the first stage, the LLM is instructed to infer the target NLG
021
               task and establish relevant evaluation criteria from the data. Building on this self-
022            inferred information, the second stage dynamically optimizes the prompt to guide
023            the LLM toward more human-aligned scoring decisions, while also generating
024            detailed explanations to justify its evaluations. Experiments across four NLG
025            evaluation tasks show that our approach achieves stronger alignment with human
026            judgments than state-of-the-art evaluation methods. Our comprehensive analy-
027            sis further highlights the effectiveness and explainability of ACTIVE -C RITIC with
028            only a small amount of labeled data. We will share our code and data on GitHub.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recent advances in language technologies have catalyzed the development of natural language gen-
034   eration (NLG) systems, benefiting a variety of downstream applications such as text summariza-
035   tion (Fabbri et al., 2021), dialogue generation (Mehri & Eskenazi, 2020), and storytelling (Guan
036   et al., 2021). However, despite the rapid growth of NLG systems, reliable techniques for automatic
037   NLG evaluation still lay far behind, primarily due to the inherent challenges posed by the open-ended
038   nature of NLG and the diverse demands of different stakeholders. This gap, in return, undermines
039
      the dependability of machine-based generators in real-world deployment.
040   Traditional NLG evaluation methods typically center on a specific criterion and require human-
041   written references for comparison (Li et al., 2024). Commonly considered criteria include similarity-
042   based reference alignment, text fluency, human likeness, and information adequacy. Some NLG
043   tasks, such as text summarization, incorporate additional criteria like coherence and consistency (Lin
044   & Chen, 2023). Notably, the measurement of alignment between machine-generated candidates
045
      and human-written references has been widely designed into various metrics, ranging from n-gram
      matching like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) to embedding-based matching
046
      such as BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019) and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021). To save the efforts of
047
      picking multiple appropriate single-aspect evaluators for all criteria regarding a specific NLG task,
048   recent advent of large language models (LLMs) has introduced a new evaluation paradigm that is
049   capable of scoring machine-generated candidates across multiple criteria simultaneously for diverse
050   NLG tasks, either by fine-tuning the LLMs (Zhong et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023;
051   Ke et al., 2023) or by prompting the model for assessment (Chiang & Lee, 2023; Gong & Mao,
052   2023; Lin et al., 2023). To save the high cost of human annotation on references and avoid potential
053   biases caused by limited references, some latest works further emphasize designing reference-free
      evaluation methods (Fu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2023).

054
      Despite remarkable contributions made by prior work, existing NLG evaluation approaches com-
055   monly require two key inputs: (1) a clear definition of the target NLG task to be evaluated, and (2)
056   a set of predefined evaluation criteria (e.g., coherence, relevance) to guide the machine evaluator’s
057   judgments. Shaped by the developers’ understanding and expectations of the evaluation process,
058   these machine evaluators often function as “passive critics”, constrained by the fixed criteria estab-
059   lished by the developers. This limitation may hinder the machine from discovering valuable insights
060   within the data that could enhance its assessment capabilities. Such constraints become even more
061   pronounced when new NLG tasks are introduced, as the criteria for evaluating text quality can vary
062   widely. As a result, these rigid evaluation methods struggle to adapt to diverse NLG tasks without
063
      extensive prompt engineering tailored to each specific task.
064   In this study, we concentrate on the potential of LLMs to evolve into “active critics”, motivated
065   by the impressive performance of recent LLMs in in-context few-shot learning via prompting. We
066   introduce ACTIVE -C RITIC—a novel LLM-based NLG evaluation protocol that assesses NLG sys-
067   tems based solely on the model’s active engagement with data (i.e., machine-generated responses
068   and their human-annotated scores). By automatically inferring what and how to evaluate from a few
069
      labeled data, this protocol offers flexibility in assessing diverse NLG tasks, particularly when adapt-
      ing to new ones. Furthermore, it shows promise in capturing the diverse requirements of different
070
      stakeholders through their annotated data, facilitating more personalized evaluations.
071
072   To build ACTIVE -C RITIC, we develop a multi-stage evaluation pipeline that directs an LLM to (1)
073   infer the target NLG task and identify relevant evaluation criteria from the data, and (2) optimize
074   prompts to make its scoring better align with human judgments. To enhance the trustworthiness of
075
      ACTIVE -C RITIC, we also prompt the model to generate explanations alongside its scoring. Exper-
      iments on four NLG tasks using an open- and a closed-source LLM demonstrate that the ACTIVE -
076
      C RITIC outperforms various state-of-the-art baselines. Additionally, we show that ACTIVE -C RITIC
077
      is helpful in identifying more fine-grained evaluation aspects compared to developer-defined crite-
078   ria, and its scoring explanations at both the aspect and overall levels can provide valuable insights.
079   In summary, our contributions include:
080
081
      A novel NLG evaluation protocol: ACTIVE -C RITIC functions as an active critic by automatically
      inferring the target task, establishing necessary evaluation criteria, refining its scoring, and generat-
082
      ing fine-grained explanations to support its decisions.
083
084   Extensive experiments across multiple NLG tasks: We conduct extensive experiments to demon-
085   strate the effectiveness and trustworthiness of ACTIVE -C RITIC in diverse NLG tasks.
086   Comprehensive analysis and findings: We show that combining fine-grained, criteria-specific
087   scoring with explanation generation encourages the LLM to engage more deeply with the test cases,
088   resulting in improved overall quality assessments.

      NLG Evaluation. The current landscape of NLG evaluation methods can be viewed from two
      key perspectives. First, in terms of methodological design, these methods can be categorized into
094
      three groups, early human-centric evaluation (Mellish & Dale, 1998), followed by untrained ma-
095
      chine evaluation (Papineni et al., 2002; Lin, 2004; Lavie & Denkowski, 2009), and more recently,
096   machine-learned evaluation (Sennrich et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2019; Yuan et al., 2021; Kim et al.,
097   2023). Second, with respect to functionality, existing studies largely concentrate on single-criteria
098   metric design, targeting either general NLG tasks like reference alignment (Liu et al., 2023b) or a
099   specific NLG task like coherence for text summarization (Wang et al., 2023b). To enhance evalu-
100   ation efficiency, some latest works have advocated for unified evaluation frameworks built on top
101   of a pre-trained language model, aiming to transcend task-specific boundaries and assess multiple
102   criteria simultaneously (Chiang & Lee, 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Gong & Mao, 2023). Since our work
103   falls into this group, we discuss the details of these works below.
104   Overall, the study of unified evaluation frameworks encompasses two major strands. The first strand
105   emphasizes the generality of evaluation methods, specifically targeting the estimation of instance
106   quality scores based on defined tasks and criteria, w/wo human-written references (Xiao et al., 2023;
107   Gao et al., 2024). Within this strand, researchers concentrate on two major strategies: (1) developing
      criteria-centered prompts that guide LLMs for multi-faceted, train-free evaluations (Fu et al., 2024;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Liu et al., 2023a; Lin & Chen, 2023), and (2) curating a large-scale multi-scenario benchmark to
109   fine-tune an LLM as a generalized evaluator (Zhong et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a;
110   Ke et al., 2023). Comparatively, the first strategy offers a cost-effective approach to evaluation,
111   while the second enhances scoring consistency and reproducibility. Moving beyond generality, the
112   second strand of research focuses on evaluation interpretability, particularly in analyzing prediction
113   errors by prompts (Xu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023). In this study, we address both aspects of
114   evaluation by introducing a new paradigm that guides the model to self-infer the target task and
115   relevant evaluation criteria, ultimately making a final decision with free-text explanations drawn
116   from several rated data instances, enabling the machine to be more active and flexible in evaluation.
117
118   Dynamic Prompt Optimization. Dynamic prompt optimization focuses on iteratively adjusting
119
      prompts to improve the performance of static LLMs on specific tasks. Existing methods can be
      categorized by inference depth into two groups: single-layer and multi-layer prompt optimization.
120
      Single-layer methods, such as APE (Zhou et al., 2023), APO (Pryzant et al., 2023), OPRO (Yang
121
      et al., 2023), and IPC (Levi et al., 2024), focus on optimizing prompts within a single stage, which
122   limits their adaptability to complex tasks. In contrast, multi-layer methods like DSPy (Khattab et al.,
123   2023) and MIPRO (Opsahl-Ong et al., 2024) optimize prompts across multiple stages, enabling more
124   comprehensive reasoning but relying on scalar-based comparisons between data points, which fall
125   short for tasks requiring correlations across data vectors. Our approach introduces a correlation-
126   based comparison instead of a scalar-based comparison optimizing multi-stage NLG evaluation
127   tasks. Specifically, we redesigned DSPy’s prompt optimization algorithm using a two-stage process
128   of Task Inference and Self-Optimizing Scoring, incorporating correlation-based validation metrics.

132   Problem Definition. We target an LLM-based reference-free NLG evaluation task where ground-
133   true human-written references are not provided. We are given a training dataset of N examples
134   Dtrain = {(xi , yi , ri )}N
                                i=1 , where xi is the i-th input text from the original NLG task, yi is the
135   i-th output text of an NLG system, and ri ∈ R is a numerical score measuring the output qual-
136   ity by humans. We denote the process of sampling a response from an LLM given a prompt as
137   LLM([Prompt]) → [Response]. Our goal is to develop an explainable LLM critic that can auto-
138   matically estimate a quality score r̂i 1 and a corresponding text explanation ei for an input-output
139
      pair xi , yi . That is, LLM(xi , yi ) → r̂i , ei . We optimize the LLM critic such that their estimated
      scores strongly correlate with human judgments in Dtrain , and evaluate the LLM critic on a hold-out
140
      test set Dtest .
141
142
      Prior Work. Existing prompt-based methods using LLMs for evaluation often rely on manually
143
      designed rule-based criteria crule (e.g., coverage, conciseness) tailored to a particular NLG task (e.g.,
144   text summarization), and incorporate these criteria into the prompt as LLM(xi , yi , crule ) → r̂i with-
145   out explanations. These methods face two major limitations. First, their rule-based criteria may not
146   generalize well across different NLG evaluation tasks. Second, they require human effort to design
147   criteria that align with the considerations human critics use for quality ratings. Consequently, verify-
148   ing these rule-based criteria against the dataset Dtrain passively is often considered an afterthought.

185   4.1      TASK I NFERENCE
186
187   The task inference stage, depicted on the left side of Figure 1, focuses on identifying two key com-
188
      ponents for NLG evaluation: (1) task description and (2) criteria definition. This stage aims to allow
      the LLM to autonomously analyze the input dataset, infer the characteristics of the NLG task, and
189
      establish relevant evaluation criteria without human intervention.
190
191
      Task Description. This module focuses on prompting the LLM to formulate an accurate task
192
      description T by reviewing examples in Dtrain and identifying key information that characterizes the
193   target NLG task (e.g., summarization, storytelling) for evaluation. Considering that LLM’s context
194   length limit may not fit in all examples in Dtrain , we split these examples into N mini-batches,
195   and generate one task description Tn from each mini-batch Dtrain,n . That is, LLM(ft (Dtrain,n )) →
196   Tn , ∀n ∈ [1, N ], where ft is a prompt template shown in Table 9 in the appendix. The final task
197   description T is generated by the LLM through the ensemble of all task descriptions {Tn }N n=1 over
198   all mini-batches.
199
200   Criteria Definition. After establishing the task description for each mini-batch, the LLM is in-
201   structed to define task-specific evaluation criteria for assessing the quality of machine-generated
202
      texts. Unlike traditional evaluation frameworks that rely on predefined criteria (e.g., coherence, flu-
      ency), we instruct the LLM to automatically identify the most relevant evaluation dimensions for
203
      the target NLG task. Similar to the task description, the final criteria set C = {c1 , c2 , ..., cm }2 is
204
      composed of all relevant dimensions inferred from all the reviewed mini-batches.
205
206   To enhance efficiency, we instruct the LLM to decide whether to stop early based on the compre-
207   hensiveness of the generated task description and criteria set after processing each mini-batch.

286   Baselines and Metrics We compare ACTIVE -C RITIC with a variety of state-of-the-art publicly
287   accessible NLG evaluation methods. The baselines are grouped into two categories: (1) fine-tuned
288   models including Auto-J (Li et al., 2023) and UniEval (Zhong et al., 2022); and (2) prompting-
289   based, train-free methods, including GPTScore (Fu et al., 2024), ExplainEval (Mahmoudi, 2023),
290   and G-eval (Liu et al., 2023a). To ensure a fair comparison between the train-free baselines and
291
      ACTIVE -C RITIC, we use the same backbone LLM in comparisons. For GPTScore, we denote
      GPTScore-src to indicate the use of the src-hypo scoring type.
292
293   We focus on three correlation coefficients to assess the evaluation consistency between machine-
294   based evaluators and humans: Pearson (γ) (Mukaka, 2012), Spearman (ρ) (Zar, 2005) and Kendall-
295   Tau (τ ) (Kendall, 1938).
296
297   Meta-evaluation We establish ACTIVE -C RITIC using two widely adopted backbone models: one
298   open-source LLM—Orca2-13b, and the other close-source LLM—GPT-3.5 (gpt-3.5-turbo-1106).
299   In our preliminary study, we also tested ACTIVE -C RITIC on LLaMA2-13B. Since Orca2-13B out-
300
      performed LLaMA2-13B in achieving higher alignment with human judgments, we selected Orca2-
      13B for further analysis. Given the long-term accessibility and lower costs of open-source LLMs
301
      compared to closed-source models, we focus on Orca2-13B-based ACTIVE -C RITIC across four
302
      NLG tasks. For comparison, we built the GPT-3.5-based ACTIVE -C RITIC with a specific focus
303   on the two most commonly used NLG tasks (SummEval and TopicalChat) in the meta-evaluation.
304
305   We focus on three variants of ACTIVE -C RITIC (AC) in the meta-evaluation. First, AC-Vanilla is a
306
      protocol that directly prompts the base LLM to score the overall quality of the input test case using
      self-optimizing scoring. Without providing or guiding the LLM to generate task-specific informa-
307
      tion, this variant explores the base LLM’s ability to make judgments based solely on its intrinsic
308
      understanding of few-shot graded exemplars. The second variant AC-Coarse, performs a coarse-
309   grained, explainable evaluation by prompting the LLM to infer task-specific information (i.e., task
310   definition and necessary criteria) and produce an overall score along with an explanation for each
311   test case. This process considers all inferred criteria simultaneously during the self-optimizing scor-
312   ing. Finally, AC-Fine provides a fine-grained, explainable evaluation. Similar to AC-Coarse, it
313   begins with task inference, but during self-optimizing scoring, it assesses the input test case against
314   each criterion individually, offering detailed explanations for each score. The overall quality score
315   is then generated by combining the evaluations across all criteria. Details of the parameter settings
316   and implementation are provided in Appendix B.

469   7    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
470
471
      We proposed ACTIVE -C RITIC, a novel LLM-based NLG evaluation protocol that relies solely on
472
      lightweight human-scored data. Unlike existing machine-based evaluators that depend on human-
473
      predefined task descriptions and evaluation criteria, ACTIVE -C RITIC actively infers the necessary
474   details about the target evaluation task and provides explainable assessments by drawing insights
475   directly from the data. This paradigm shift will open the door to endow ACTIVE -C RITIC with the
476   adaptability to evaluate systems on new NLG tasks without extensive task-specific prompt engi-
477   neering. Experiments across four distinct NLG tasks demonstrate LLMs’ potential as active critics,
478   achieving a higher correlation with human judgments compared to state-of-the-art baseline evalua-
479   tors. Fine-level criteria-specific scoring, paired with the explanation generation setting, prompts the
480   LLM to engage more deeply with the test cases, leading to improved overall quality scoring.
481   Our work has several limitations. First, due to resource constraints, we primarily focused on four
482   existing NLG tasks and benchmarks for meta-evaluation in our experiments. It would be valuable
483   to deploy our protocol in a broader testing environment to assess its performance in more diverse
484   settings. Additionally, building ACTIVE -C RITIC on a wider range of backbone LLMs could provide
485   deeper insights. Overall, we hope this study will contribute to advancing generic NLG evaluation
      research and promote system development across diverse NLG scenarios.

       B     D ETAILS OF PARAMETER S ETTING AND I MPLEMENTATION
973
974
       We randomly sample 25% of the data for ACTIVE -C RITIC tuning and use the remaining 75% for
975    meta-evaluation across each NLG task. During task inference, we set the number of mini-batches
976    to 25, with a batch size of 5. The LLM is instructed to generate one task description and a set of
977    evaluation criteria per mini-batch. To enhance tuning efficiency, we allow the LLM to decide when
978    to stop early, capping the number of task descriptions and criteria sets at 5. For the scoring stage,
979    we run 11 epoches of prompt optimization. The number of in-context exemplars used per epoch is 3
980    for SummEval and TopicalChat, and 8 for SFRES and OpenMeVA (ROC), with the difference due
981    to varying input text lengths across tasks. All parameter settings are based on empirical testing of
982    sequential values to determine optimal configurations.
983    Our experiments were carried out using two NVIDIA V100 GPU cards. For prompt optimization in
984    the scoring stage, we utilized the “BootstrapFewShotWithRandomSearch” method in DSPy (Khat-
985    tab et al., 2023) as the optimizer, which leverages random search to generate examples.
