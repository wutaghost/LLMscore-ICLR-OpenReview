009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper considers the problem of annotating datapoints using an expert with
012            only a few annotation rounds in a label-scarce setting. We propose soliciting
013            reliable feedback on difficulty in annotating a datapoint from the expert in addition
014            to ground truth label. Existing literature in active learning or coreset selection
015            turns out to be less relevant to our setting since they presume the existence of a
016            reliable trained model, which is absent in the label-scarce regime. However, the
017            literature on coreset selection emphasizes the presence of difficult data points in
018
               the training set to perform supervised learning in downstream tasks (Mindermann
               et al., 2022). Therefore, for a given fixed annotation budget of T rounds, we model
019
               the sequential decision-making problem of which (difficult) datapoints to choose
020
               for annotation in a sparse linear bandits framework with the constraint that no arm
021            can be pulled more than once (blocking constraint). With mild assumptions on
022            the datapoints, our (computationally efficient) Explore-Then-Commit algorithm

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In niche industrial applications such as Named Entity Recognition (Nguyen et al., 2023) and learning
036   tasks on low-resource languages (Hedderich et al., 2021), obtaining high-quality labels is challeng-
037   ing due to the lack of expert annotators. However, high-quality labels are critical for effective model
038   training (Li et al., 2023), and thus, expert annotators must provide ground truth labels. (Sorscher
039
      et al., 2022) demonstrated that selecting high-quality data can reduce the power-law association of
      test error with dataset size to an exponential law. In annotation-expensive tasks with large volumes
040
      of unlabeled data, the challenge is to select a representative subset of datapoints for labeling. In
041
      label-scarce tasks, where the number of expert annotators is extremely low, often only one, it is im-
042   practical to query the same datapoint multiple times. While crowd-sourcing literature reduces noise
043   by aggregating labels from multiple annotators (Verroios & Garcia-Molina, 2015), the annotation
044   from a single or aggregated expert is considered the final ground truth label in our setting. We term
045   this restriction, where a datapoint cannot be re-queried after annotation, the blocking constraint. Ad-
046   ditionally, the annotation budget is typically much smaller than the datapoint embedding dimension.
047   An efficient annotation strategy should be sequential (instead of one-shot) in such cases, as each
048   annotation informs future decisions and helps identify more informative datapoints.
049   In addition, data-pruning techniques like coreset selection emphasize selecting hard examples (Ma-
050   harana et al., 2023), while (Sorscher et al., 2022) justifies this for perceptron learning. Curriculum
051   learning (Bengio et al., 2009) also uses increasingly difficult examples, but defining the ‘hardness’
052   of unlabeled data is ambiguous. Hard examples identified by heuristics are often noisy, mislabeled,
053   or outliers (Mindermann et al., 2022). To address this, we propose soliciting annotation difficulty
      feedback directly from expert annotators. This adds minimal annotation overhead and helps identify

054
      hard examples more reliably than heuristics. Additionally, leveraging expert feedback to learn and
055   predict hardness (via an auxillary model) is simpler than addressing complex downstream task itself.
056
057   Thus, the primary problem we aim to solve is: “Given a small annotation budget T, how should we
058
      sequentially identify unique and difficult unlabeled datapoints for annotation as we receive expert
      feedback on hardness of annotation?” Theoretically, we model this sequential decision-making
059
      process using sparse linear bandits with a blocking constraint. Specifically, suppose we have a
060
      large set A of M unlabeled datapoints, each associated with a d-dimensional embedding. With T
061   rounds available, at each round t ∈ [T], a datapoint at is selected for annotation, and the expert
062   provides noisy feedback rt on the difficulty of labeling that datapoint, along with the ground truth
063   label. To address the limited annotation budget and the relevance of a few important features in high-
064   dimensional spaces (Hao et al., 2020), we model the expected hardness as a sparse linear function of
065   the embedding. We ensure robustness to sparsity - our theoretical guarantees degrade gracefully as
066   the parameter vector becomes less sparse, i.e., as it deviates from sparsity due to an increasing tail.
067   Although our primary motivation comes from data selection for annotation in a label-scarce regime,
068   our theoretical framework is broadly applicable. For example, resource-constrained edge devices,
069   such as smartphones, consider the problem of recommendation of personalized products. In ap-
070   plications such as movie/book recommendations, typical users will hardly consume an item more
071   than once - so any feedback provided is not subject to change. Furthermore, the user will provide
072   feedback for every item (books/movies) in a large repository of items. Here, our theoretical frame-
073   work (sparse linear bandits with blocking constraint) is directly applicable as it involves learning
074   unknown user taste from sequential item recommendations based on user feedback. While existing
075
      literature addresses this by combining feedback from multiple users (Bresler et al., 2014; Pal et al.,
      2024), such algorithms compromise user privacy. Our framework will not have this limitation.
076

090   embedding vectors are contained in a ball of radius R that is ∥a∥2 ≤ R ∀a ∈ A.

092

093

      Our Contributions and Techniques: The sparse linear bandits framework in the data-sparse regime
      was first studied by Hao et al. (2020); Jang et al. (2022) - however, these works do not consider either
124
      the blocking constraint or robustness to sparsity - both of which pose novel technical challenges. We
125
      propose BSLB (Blocked Sparse Linear Bandits), an efficient algorithm in this setting which is pri-
126   marily an Explore-Then-Commit algorithm. In the exploration period, we carefully choose a subset
127   of arms from which we sample without replacement - the goal is to ensure that the expected covari-
128   ance matrix has a large minimum eigenvalue. In the exploitation period, we use a lasso estimator
129   to estimate the unknown model parameters while being robust to sparsity. The optimal exploration
130   period depends on the correct sparsity level of the unknown parameter vector, which is difficult
131   to set in practice. Therefore, we also present a meta-bandit algorithm based on corralling a set of
132   base bandits algorithms (Agarwal et al., 2017) - obtaining the same order-wise regret guarantees but
133   without needing the knowledge of hyperparameters. Below, we summarize our main contributions:
134   1. We theoretically model the problem of identifying hard (informative) unlabeled datapoints by
135
          high dimensional sparse linear bandits framework with blocking constraint - the number of
          rounds or annotation budget is very small, even smaller than the ambient dimension. The hard-
136
          ness is modeled as a sparse linear function of the datapoint embedding - we allow for robustness
137
          to sparsity in the parameters where the tail magnitude is non-zero and unknown.
138   2. We propose an efficient “explore then commit” (ETC) algorithm BSLB for regret minimization in
139                                                                                                 1/2
          our framework (Theorem 2) that achieves a regret guarantee of O(k 1/3 T2/3 + k −1/12 βk T5/6 )
140       for a fixed sparsity k and the corresponding tail magnitude βk - for the special case of hard
141       sparsity (tail magnitude βk = 0), BSLB achieves a regret guarantee of O(k 1/3 T2/3 ) (Corollary
142       2). The run-time of BSLB is polynomial in the number of datapoints and is therefore efficient.
143   3. A novel ingredient (function G ET G OOD S UBSET()) in the proposed algorithm BSLB is to select
144       a good set of representative datapoints from the large set of unlabelled datapoints. This objective
145       entails solving a discrete non-convex optimization problem in Equation 2. We propose a convex
146       relaxation of the objective and obtain a feasible solution via randomized rounding - Theorem 3
147       states that our computationally efficient algorithm has good approximation guarantees.
148   4. BSLB requires knowledge of sparsity k (which also controls the tail magnitude βk ) to set explo-
149
          ration period length. Since this is challenging to set in practice, we propose a meta-algorithm
          C-BSLB that combines base algorithms with different exploration periods. C-BSLB achieves
150
          same regret guarantees order-wise (Theorem 4) as BSTB but without knowledge of sparsity.
151
      5. To validate our hypothesis and theory, we present experiments on the public image classification
152       dataset PASCAL VOC 2012. Additional experiments on text classification on SST-2 and person-
153       alized recommendation with MovieLens, Jester, and Goodbooks datasets are in Appendix A.2.

      Technical Challenges: At a high level, our proof follows a similar structure as in the analysis of
155
      Algorithm ESTC proposed in (Hao et al., 2020). First of all, both in (Hao et al., 2020) and our
156   setting, there are no assumptions on the datapoint embeddings - unlike most existing literature on
157   sparsity where the Gram matrix is assumed to satisfy Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) (Boche
158   et al., 2015). A much weaker condition (namely Restricted Eigenvalue (RE)) in characterizing nice
159   statistical guarantees of the Lasso estimator for sparse linear regression was shown in (Bickel et al.,
160   2009; Rudelson & Zhou, 2013) - it was shown that if the covariance matrix of a distribution satisfies
161   RE, then a sufficient number of independent samples will also satisfy RE. However, in our setting,
      the additional blocking constraint and our desired robustness to sparsity present three significant


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      technical challenges. (A) Sampling: While the above techniques can be used directly in the analysis
163   of Algorithm ESTC, the blocking constraint in our setting entails that sampling of datapoints cannot
164   be independent in the exploration phase. (B) Statistical guarantees: To the best of our knowledge,
165   when the Gram matrix only satisfies RE, the statistical guarantees of the Lasso estimator hold only
166   for hard sparse vectors - for these vectors, all but k ≪ d entries are zero. Existing guarantees
167   for soft sparsity (non-zero tail) hold only for Gram matrices satisfying the stronger RIP condition
168   (Wainwright, 2019). (C) Knowledge of hyper-parameters: Our first proposed algorithm BSLB
169   (similar to ESTC) requires as input a fixed sparsity k (corresponding tail magnitude βk ). If the input
170   sparsity is too low or too high, then the regret guarantee will not be reasonable. However, it is
171
      challenging to set the sparsity parameter without having the knowledge of parameter vector itself.
      We resolve all technical challenges with novel techniques - in particular, the latter two might be of
172
      independent interest in other applications.
173
174   To resolve (A), we need to optimize for a probability distribution on datapoints (for sampling) with
175   a well-conditioned expected covariance matrix under the constraint that it is uniform on a subset
176   of datapoints and has zero mass on others. The related optimization problem is discrete and non-
177
      convex - we describe a convex relaxation of the objective and show a randomized rounding proce-
      dure to obtain a feasible solution with good approximation guarantees on the minimum eigenvalue
178
      of the expected covariance matrix. Subsequently, we show that subsampling from the recovered
179
      distribution in the exploration component ensures that the data covariance matrix satisfies RE with
180   high probability. To resolve (B), we follow two steps 1) We extend guarantees in (Rudelson & Zhou,
181   2013) (Theorem 23) that demonstrates a variant of restricted isometry - the idea is that the data ma-
182   trix acts as a near isometry on the image of all sparse vectors under a linear transformation. Such a
183   technique, in turn, allows us to extend RE guarantees for design matrices whose rows are sampled
184   without replacement 2) using the RE condition we derive guarantees for soft sparsity from the re-
185   sults on hard sparse vectors using an iterative approach to ensure robustness ((Boche et al., 2015)
186   Theorem 1.6). For (C), we use a corralling algorithm based on the techniques of (Agarwal et al.,
187   2017) that combines several base algorithms and provides guarantees with respect to the optimal
188
      one. However, a naive application leads to an additional corralling cost with a linear dependence on
      dimension d, making the regret vacuous (T ≪ d). We use a covering argument - we only choose a
189
      small representative subset (covering set) of base algorithms for input to corralling and show that for
190
      all remaining base algorithms, their regret is close enough to a base algorithm in the covering set.
191
192   Related Work: Conceptually our work is similar to active learning (Settles, 2009; Lesci & Vla-
193   chos, 2024) where unlabeled samples are annotated adaptively, based on the confidence of a trained
194
      model (Coleman et al., 2022). Active learning works well with good initialization and informative
      confidence intervals. However, in our label-scarce setting, active learning is particularly challenging
195
      with complex data due to the absence of a reliably trained model in the first place - this is more pro-
196
      nounced for difficult datapoints for which prediction is hard. Active Learning (AL) needs an initial
197   set of high-quality labeled samples to reasonably train a model - also known as the cold-start prob-
198   lem - when labels are scarce, uncertainty based sampling techniques (AL) are unsuitable (Li et al.,
199   2024). Our goal is to identify informative samples with the help of the expert annotator(s), whom
200   the final model aims to emulate. Coreset selection (Guo et al., 2022; Albalak et al., 2024; Sener
201   & Savarese, 2018) aims to select a subset of datapoints for training. However, coreset selection
202   assumes that a large amount of labeled data already exists, and the focus is on reducing compu-
203   tational costs. In contrast, our setting deals with the lack of labeled data, making existing coreset
204   selection approaches, which rely on the entire labeled dataset, inapplicable. Our work also aligns
205
      with curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009), where a model is trained on samples of increasing
      difficulty/complexity. Due to the ambiguity in hardness definition, often heuristics are used to infer
206
      the difficulty of samples (Soviany et al., 2022) and can turn out unreliable and not generalizable.
207
      For niche tasks where an expert annotator is available, the difficulty ratings from the annotator are
208   more informative since the goal is to train a model to mimic the expert. In computer vision, there
209   has been recent work regarding estimating the difficulty of a dataset for the model using implicit
210   difficulty ratings of annotation (Ionescu et al., 2016; Mayo et al., 2023). For NLP tasks, Ethayarajh
211   et al. (2022) constructs information theoretic metrics to estimate the difficulty of data points.

523   4     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      This work formulates the problem of adaptive annotation with expert-provided difficulty feedback
526
      as regret minimization in blocked sparse linear bandits. The goal is to annotate difficult samples to
527
      improve generalization on hard datapoints, assuming difficulty ratings are linked to sample features
528   by an unknown parameter. We consider the practical consideration when each sample can be anno-
529   tated once and only a few rounds of annotations are available. We propose an explore-then-commit
530   algorithm BSLB, a two-shot algorithm for unlabelled data-point selection (for annotation). We show
531   theoretical results establishing the sub-linear regret of the proposed BSLB algorithm - we also en-
532   sure that our guarantees work with minimum assumptions on datapoints and are robust to sparsity.
533   Finally, we present a meta-algorithm C-BSLB that corrals BSLB algorithms with different input
534   parameters - the result is a bandit algorithm that suffers the same regret orders as BSLB without the
535   knowledge of the optimal sparsity hyper-parameter. Numerical studies on real-life image and text
536
      datasets show the efficacy of our methods in the label-scarce regime. We demonstrate experiments in
      the appendix for recommendation and simulation setup using our theoretical framework and BSLB.
537
538   Future Work: Our experimental results are done on classifying state-of-the-art embeddings; how-
539   ever, more extensive experimentation can be considered in future work, especially for creating
      datasets used for generative tasks.

1284   For an environment E, we define the environment E ′ induced by importance weighting, which is the

1287

1291   ft′ (θ, x) = 0 and θt′ ∈ Θ to be arbitrary.
1292
       4. E ′ reveals the loss ft′ (θt , xt ) to the learner, and passes θt′ to E.
