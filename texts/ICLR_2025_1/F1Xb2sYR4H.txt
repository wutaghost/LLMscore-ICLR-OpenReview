011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            While problem solving is a standard evaluation task for foundation models, a crucial
014            component of problem solving—actively and strategically gathering information
015
               to test hypotheses—has not been closely investigated. To assess the information
               gathering abilities of foundation models in interactive environments, we introduce
016
               a framework in which a model must determine the factors influencing a hidden
017
               reward function by iteratively reasoning about its previously gathered information
018            and proposing its next exploratory action to maximize information gain at each
019            step. We implement this framework in both a text-based environment, which
020            offers a tightly controlled setting and enables high-throughput parameter sweeps,
021            and in an embodied 3D environment, which requires addressing complexities
022            of multi-modal interaction more relevant to real-world applications. We further
023            investigate whether approaches such as self-correction and increased inference time
024            improve information gathering efficiency. In a relatively simple task that requires
025            identifying a single rewarding feature, we find that Gemini’s information gathering
026
               capability is close to optimal. However, when the model must identify a conjunction
               of rewarding features, performance is suboptimal. The hit in performance is
027
               due partly to the model translating task description to a policy and partly to the
028
               model’s effectiveness in using its in-context memory. Performance is comparable
029            in both text and 3D embodied environments, although imperfect visual object
030            recognition reduces its accuracy in drawing conclusions from gathered information
031            in the 3D embodied case. For single-feature-based rewards, we find that smaller
032            models curiously perform better; for conjunction-based rewards, incorporating self
033            correction into the model improves performance.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Foundation models have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP) and multi-modal dialogue,
039
      achieving remarkable proficiency in understanding and generating human-like text (e.g., Achiam
      et al., 2023; Gemini Team et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Dai
040
      et al., 2024; Deitke et al., 2024). Their impact extends to applications like machine translation, text
041
      summarization, and, increasingly, interactive dialogue agents. As agents, LLMs must not only answer
042   questions but also ask questions and gather information to achieve goals. This type of information
043   gathering is distinct from classic exploration in reinforcement learning (RL), which often uses random
044   exploration policies or prioritizes visitation of novel states (e.g., Burda et al., 2018; Ecoffet et al.,
045   2019; Badia et al., 2020). Targeted information acquisition requires formulating hypotheses about the
046   task and strategically gathering information to test and refine these hypotheses. This hypothesis-driven
047   approach to information gathering remains largely unexamined in foundation models.
048   To systematically study information-gathering capabilities in foundation models, we need to carefully
049   control the environment (or hypothesis space) in which the model operates. To this end, we propose
050   a parametric class of environments varying in complexity. We realize these formal environments
051   in two distinct implementations: a text-based interaction and an embodied 3D simulation. In the
052   text-based environment, we run experiments at scale, and in embodied 3D environments, we evaluate
053   the effects of additional complications such as partial observability, credit assignment, and scene
      understanding—complications relevant to real-world applications such as robotics and video games.

054
      More specifically, this paper investigates the capacity and efficiency of foundation models to conduct
055   exploratory behavior within interactive environments in the zero-shot setting, using in-context
056   prompting alone and without requiring task-specific training or fine-tuning. We examine whether
057   foundation models can effectively navigate, adapt, and reason in text-based scenarios that require
058   sequential decision-making and strategic exploration. Furthermore, to assess the generalization of
059   our findings to more complex and multi-modal settings, we evaluate these foundational models in an
060   embodied, vision-based setting that is designed to closely match the text-based environment.
061   Our experiments with Gemini 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024) reveal significant exploratory capabilities,
062   effective navigation of complex abstract problem spaces, the discovery of novel solutions, and the
063   achievement of predefined objectives with minimal guidance. While performance tends to decrease
064   as environmental complexity increases, such as more complex reward functions or when moving to
065   3D environments that require visual understanding, exploration efficiency significantly outperforms
066   random baselines. These findings suggest that foundation models possess a latent ability to adaptively
067   gather information in interactive environments, both text-based and embodied, opening exciting
068   avenues for research and application. Overall, this work makes three key contributions:
069
070
             • Framework development: We propose a novel framework for evaluating the directed explo-
               ration capabilities of LLMs and VLMs in interactive environments, outlining methodologies
071
               for assessment in the zero-shot setting, without the need for fine-tuning or other post-training
072
               modifications.
073
074          • Empirical analysis: We conduct extensive experiments across various environments and
075            tasks, and across several model variants and prompting strategies, to analyze the exploration
076            performance and behaviors of LLMs and VLMs in interactive settings.
077
             • Insights and implications: We provide a detailed discussion on the implications of our
078            findings for future research in foundation models and the development of autonomous
079            intelligent agents.
080
081   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work, Section 3 details
082   our proposed framework and experimental setup, Section 4 presents the results and analysis of our
083   experiments, and Section 5 concludes the paper and discusses potential directions for future research.

086   2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Exploration in RL Information gathering is related to exploration in RL, which has been studied in
089   particular for tasks with sparse rewards such as Montezuma’s Revenge and Pitfall in Atari and the
090   DM-HARD-8 tasks (e.g., Burda et al., 2018; Ecoffet et al., 2019; Badia et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2022;
091   Saade et al., 2023) as well as in unsupervised settings (e.g., Pathak et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2022).
092   These methods commonly derive an “intrinsic” reward from the error of a predictive model (e.g.,
093
      Pathak et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2022) or by estimating the density of visited states
      (e.g., Saade et al., 2023). Badia et al. (2020) use a combination of both of these types of intrinsic
094
      rewards and Tam et al. (2022) additionally use pre-trained representations. In contrast, this work
095
      relies on the prior knowledge of foundation models from internet-scale pre-training for exploration
096   (e.g., Wang et al., 2023a; Feng et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024b) rather than using random exploratory
097   actions and intrinsic rewards.
098
099
      Foundation models for games Foundation models have also been used to build agents that play
      games (e.g., Wang et al., 2023a;b; Feng et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024), which often involves some
100
      form of exploration. Wang et al. (2023a) show that GPT-4 can reach impressive performance in
101
      Minecraft by incrementally building a skill library via an “automatic curriculum” stage where GPT-4
102   is prompted to propose novel tasks. Feng et al. (2023) prompt an LLM to explore an environment and
103   subsequently use the collected experiences for fine-tuning the model. Unlike Wang et al. (2023a) and
104   Feng et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2023b) and Tan et al. (2024) use image observations rather than relying
105   on access to environment internal states. All of these works, however, focus more on improving agent
106   performance rather than performing an explicit, systematic investigation of information gathering
107   for iterative hypothesis testing with foundation models in a controlled, zero-shot setting and in
      comparison to known optimal policies.

      Exploration with foundation models Several other works investigate exploration with foundation
109   models, e.g., for text-based environments (Lu et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024), reinforcement
110   learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Dwaracherla et al., 2024), and multi-armed bandit problems
111   (Coda-Forno et al., 2023; Krishnamurthy et al., 2024). Unlike Krishnamurthy et al. (2024) and
112   Dwaracherla et al. (2024) and similar to Lu et al. (2024b), this work considers stateful environments.
113   While Lu et al. (2024b) replace components of the exploration method introduced in Ecoffet et al.
114   (2019) with an LLM, this work studies the ability of foundation models to gather information and test
115   hypotheses in-context via zero-shot prompting rather than using LLMs in a more modular fashion.
116   Also adopting more modular approaches, Hu et al. (2024) use foundation models as components in a
117
      larger exploration framework and Huang et al. (2024) propose to use a smaller agent to explore the
      environment and a larger agent to leverage the gathered information.
118
119   Active learning The field of active learning (Settles, 2009) has long studied how to best acquire data
120   to improve a model’s predictions. Active learning methods commonly focus on highly structured
121   data (either i.i.d. or on a graph). In contrast, this work explores efficient knowledge acquisition in
122   more general interactive environments.
123   Active embodied question answering This work studies a similar setup to embodied question
124   answering (EQA) (e.g., Das et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2023; Majumdar et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024).
125   Similar to our work, agents in the EQA setting need to actively explore an environment to gather infor-
126   mation. Unlike our tasks, however, EQA typically does not involve performing iterative experiments
127   to infer unknown mechanisms in dynamic environments.
128   AI for science Hypothesis generation and testing is central to the scientific method and recent works
129   research the application of foundation models in this broader domain (e.g., Romera-Paredes et al.,
130   2023; Trinh et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a). Such works typically use foundation models in a highly
131   structured protocol designed for the fixed domain in question. In contrast, this work explores the
132   capabilities of base foundation models as the complexity of the domain varies.

134   3     E NVIRONMENTS FOR EVALUATING EXPLORATION
135
136   Existing RL environments (e.g., Todorov et al., 2012; Brockman et al., 2016; Tassa et al., 2018) often
137   conflate exploration with other aspects of agent performance, making it difficult to isolate and assess
138   a model’s inherent exploratory capabilities. Such aspects include sparse or deceptive rewards and
139   noisy, non-stationary, or multi-agent environments. We therefore designed a suite of environments
140   that allows us to systematically disentangle and control the factors influencing exploration.
141
      We begin with simplified, text-based environments where the world is abstractly represented. This
142   allows us to assess the model’s ability to explore and update its beliefs based solely on textual
143   information, without the complexities of visual perception and motor control. We then progress to a
144   more naturalistic setting, evaluating the model on video inputs from agents acting in an embodied 3D
145   environment. This transition allows us to assess the generalizability of exploratory behaviors to a
146   setting more reflective of real-world applications.
147
148   3.1   T EXT- BASED ENVIRONMENT
149
150   Drawing inspiration from well-studied cognitive tasks explored in Shepard et al. (1961), we adapt
151   a similar structure to investigate information gathering strategies in foundation models. Our tasks
152
      involve presenting the model with multiple objects, only a few of which lead to a reward. This mirrors
      the sparse reward setting common in RL. Each object possesses two or three properties (“features”),
153
      such as color, shape, and texture. A specific property or a combination of two properties determines
154
      whether an object yields a reward.
155
156   For instance, the model might be presented with numerous objects, but only red objects are rewarding.
157   In this case, the model is informed that a single property (either color or shape) determines reward.
158
      The optimal strategy then involves exploring unique combinations of color and shape with each
      attempt. If a “red book” yields no reward, the model should eliminate both “red” and “book” from
159
      further consideration.
160
161   To assess the capabilities of foundation models in this environment, we modulate task difficulty
      by adjusting two key aspects: the number of distinct colors (increasing the cognitive load) and the

194   3.2      C ONSTRUCTION L AB E NVIRONMENTS
195
196   To further evaluate the foundation models in a 3D embodied environment, we implement an analogous
197   task to the text-based environment in a factory-style simulation called Construction Lab. Construction
198
      Lab was introduced in [reference-anonymized] as a simulation environment that includes both
      game-like mechanics and simplified but non-trivial object manipulation and physical reasoning.
199
200   In this work, we focus on a task that requires the player to operate a simple machine called the
201   Exchanger. The Exchanger requires objects with specific properties to be placed on an input conveyor
202   belt (Figure 2). If an object matches the requirement, the input is consumed, a green light shows
203   for a few seconds, and an output object is produced on an output belt. If the object is invalid, the
204
      machine rejects it by reversing the input belt and a red error light is activated. No cues are provided
      regarding the correct input object required, and thus the task entails determining what the correct
205
      object properties are through trial and error, observing how the machine responds to input objects,
206
      and drawing appropriate inferences.
207
208   Through the use of this 3D, visually rich environment that mirrors the challenges of the text-based
209   environment, we are able to disentangle the effects of visual complexity and imperfect image
210
      understanding from the patterns of performance related to reasoning from language alone.

240   Baselines We compare to two baselines: Optimal Baseline: This baseline represents an upper bound
241   on exploration performance. It selects actions that maximize information gain at each step by reducing
242   uncertainty about the target property. Random Baseline: This baseline establishes a lower bound by
243   choosing actions randomly. We further differentiate the baseline into two variants: Random without
244   Replacement: This variant assumes perfect memory of past actions, making it a more challenging
245   baseline. It’s akin to an agent with infinite cognitive capacity, never repeating the same action.
246   Random with Replacement: This variant allows for repeated selection of the same actions, reflecting
247   a more realistic scenario with limited memory.
248   To illustrate the optimal strategy, consider a single-feature task where color is the only determinant
249   of reward, and the target property is “red.” An optimal strategy would systematically test unique
250   color-shape combinations. For instance, if the agent tries “blue toy” and receives no reward, it can
251   rule out both “blue” and “toy”. Subsequently, “yellow sphere” would be an optimal choice, as it
252   provides maximum information gain, unlike “blue sphere” which offers no new information about
253   the target property. Once “red box” is tried and yields a reward, the optimal strategy would then focus
254
      on isolating whether “red” or “box” is the target property by changing one factor at a time.
255   In contrast, the random baseline in a pick-up object task might simply select any previously unpicked
256   object to discover its reward. This highlights the contrast between strategic information gain (optimal
257   baseline) and undirected exploration (random baseline).
258   Foundation Models We evaluate on state-of-the-art (SOTA) foundation models of two different sizes,
259   Gemini 1.5 Pro and Gemini 1.5 Flash (Reid et al., 2024).

261
      Evaluation Effective exploration necessitates two key abilities. 1) Deciding what to explore: agents
      must strategically select actions to gather information about the environment. 2) Reasoning and
262
      updating beliefs: agents must interpret observations, update their understanding of the world, and
263
      make inferences based on new evidence. To assess these abilities, we evaluate the following: (a)
264   Exploration efficiency: We assess how quickly models gather sufficient information (in an information
265   theoretic sense) to identify the specific properties associated with rewards, assuming perfect reasoning.
266   This measures the model’s active exploration capabilities independent of its ability to draw conclusions
267   from its observations. (b) Property identification accuracy: We assess the accuracy with which the
268   model identifies the rewarding property or properties based on its observations collected during a
269   fixed budget of exploration steps. This measures both the model’s ability to collect observations
      efficiently and its ability to reason and draw conclusions from those observations.

289   4.1   E FFECT OF E NVIRONMENTAL C OMPLEXITY ON E XPLORATION
290
291   We first examine the effect of varying the amount and type of environmental complexity on strategic
292   exploration and performance. We evaluate the 2 Gemini models’ performance in both single-feature
293   and conjunction tasks. We benchmark against an optimal baseline (upper bound) and random
294   baselines with and without replacement (lower bounds assuming perfect memory and no memory,
295   respectively), and evaluated the optimal and random policies on 1000 episodes and the Gemini models
296
      on 200 episodes.
297   Impact of reward function complexity To investigate how reward function complexity affects
298   exploration efficiency, we designed tasks where reward is determined by either a single feature (like
299   “red” or “square”) or a conjunction of two features (e.g., “red” and “square”). The latter requires the
300   agent to reason about multiple properties to identify the reward-relevant combination.
301   Figure 3a compares performance in the single-feature task, while Figure 3b shows performance in the
302   conjunction task. We observed that Gemini’s performance relative to the optimal baseline declines
303   as reward function complexity increases (from single to conjunction of features). This degradation
304   suggests that in more complex tasks, increased reasoning complexity hinders efficient information
305   gathering.
306   Impact of in-context memory load In LLMs, in-context memory acts as a form of working memory,
307   and as the volume of information stored in this memory grows, so does the cognitive load on the
308   model, potentially affecting its processing and response generation capabilities. To investigate this
309   relationship, we vary the number of unique colors or shapes in the environment, forcing the agent to
310   utilize its in-context memory more efficiently. By evaluating exploration efficiency as the number
311   of unique colors increases in both single-feature and conjunction tasks, we can assess the impact of
312   cognitive load on performance. Figure 3 shows the average number of steps required for the model to
313   gather sufficient information in both single-feature and conjunction tasks. In both tasks, the model
314
      significantly outperforms the two random baselines, and is very close to the optimal baseline.
315   In the single-feature task, both Gemini 1.5 Pro and Gemini Flash perform comparably to the optimal
316   baseline, even as the number of unique colors increases. However, in the conjunction task, while still
317   significantly outperforming the random baselines, performance degrades slightly as the number of
318   unique colors increases. This indicates that increased cognitive load negatively impacts the model’s
319   exploration efficiency. In both single-feature and conjunction tasks, Gemini 1.5 Pro and Gemini 1.5
320
      Flash performed comparably for individual numbers of colors. Interestingly, however, Gemini 1.5
      Flash was found to outperform the Pro model in the single-factor condition when considering all
321
      numbers of colors taken together (see statistical analysis below).
322
323   We also find that Gemini effectively exploits information gathered over time, efficiently updating its
      understanding of the environment. See Section A.1 for details.

343   4.2   E FFECTS OF PROMPTING AND CONTEXT LENGTH
344
345   We also investigate whether existing methods for enhancing reasoning abilities in LLMs can improve
346   performance on our tasks. We evaluate the impact of two techniques: 1) Self-correction: allowing the
347   LLM to critique and revise its own reasoning, and 2) Increased inference time: providing the model
348   with its previous response and additional time to reason.
349   Self-correction Building upon previous work exploring the self-correction capabilities of LLMs
350   in mathematical problem-solving (e.g., Huang et al. (2023)), we investigate whether LLMs can
351   self-correct within our task framework. We adapt self-correction prompts similar to those used in
352   Zheng et al. (2024), prompting the model at each step to revise its output, including its reasoning
353   traces. This process allows the model to verify its chosen action and make corrections if necessary.
354   Figure 4 presents the results of this process for both single-feature task and conjunction task scenarios
355   using Gemini 1.5 Pro, and the results for the Flash model is found in Figure 7 in the appendix. In
356   single-feature tasks, it improves performance with up to 6 unique colors, but its benefits diminish
357
      with a larger number of colors. Notably, self-correction appears more effective in more complex
      conjunction tasks, either performing comparably, or slightly outperforming the base model.
358
359   Longer Inference Time We also explored whether encouraging the model to engage in more
360   deliberate reasoning, by providing it with additional context, could improve performance. Instead
361   of simply providing the model with its previous observations (actions and outcomes), we also
362   included its reasoning traces, explaining why it selected previous actions. This approach, while
363
      increasing inference time, allows the model to reflect on its own chain-of-thought, potentially leading
      to improved reasoning and better decision-making. Figure 4 presents the results of incorporating
364
      the model’s reasoning traces. In both single-feature and conjunction tasks, this approach, which
365
      encourages more deliberate reasoning, yields comparable performance to the baseline approach
366   without reasoning traces.
367
368   Statistical comparisons across models and approaches In order to quantitatively assess whether
369
      model size or prompting method affected exploration efficiency, controlling for the number of
      colors, we performed analyses of covariance for the single-feature and conjunction tasks separately.
370
      First we compared Gemini 1.5 Pro to Gemini Flash: in the single-feature tasks Gemini Flash was
371
      significantly better (F (1, 7649) = 6.1, p < 0.05), in the conjunction tasks there is no significant
372   difference. Second we compared the variants of each model to the base model: in the single-feature
373   task there were no significant differences, however for Gemini 1.5 Pro in the conjunction task we
374   found that the guided reasoning and self-correcting variants were significantly better than the base
375   model (F (3, 5514) = 5.3 and 3.0 respectively, p < 0.05 corrected for multiple comparisons). This
376   suggests that when the reward function is simple, it may help to have a smaller model and simple/short
377   reasoning process. Conversely, when the reward function is more complex, it may help to have a
      more iterative reasoning process.

      4.3     I MPACT OF R EASONING AND I N -C ONTEXT M EMORY
379
380   Efficient exploration requires agents to reason effectively about exploration strategies and maintain
381   a working memory of untried options. However, we observed suboptimal exploration performance
382   by our agent on the conjunction task (Figure 3). To investigate this performance gap, we sought to
383   disentangle the effects of reasoning and memory limitations. We provided Gemini with a guided
384   reasoning strategy, eliminating the need for the agent to independently derive the optimal approach,
385   refer to Table 4 in the appendix for detailed prompts).
386   Figure 4 (b) demonstrates a clear and consistent performance improvement with guided reasoning,
387   indicating that reasoning challenges contribute significantly to the performance gap. While imperfect
388   adherence to the guided strategy could be a factor, the gap between the guided reasoning model and
389   the optimal policy widens as the number of unique colors increases. This strongly suggests that
390   memory constraints also play a crucial role in limiting the performance of the standard Gemini policy.

392   4.4     E XPLORATION IN 3D EMBODIED ENVIRONMENTS
393
394   4.4.1    TASK SETUP
395
      A number of additional challenges must be addressed when performing this exploration task in a 3D
396   embodied environment. First, the agent must assess both the current state of the environment and
397   the consequences of any actions taken through vision. Second, the agent requires a motor control
398   module to execute exploratory actions. We use Gemini 1.5 Pro’s multi-modal functionality to ingest
399   video input from Construction Lab sub-sampled to 1.5 Hz and 320 x 240 resolution. To disentangle
400   vision and reasoning performance from translation of natural language instructions into a complex
401   keyboard-and-mouse action space, we adopt a setup in which instructions are provided to a human
402   actor who performs the exploratory actions online.
403   We assess Gemini 1.5 Pro’s ability to generate these exploratory instructions by comparing against
404   an optimal and random baseline, mirroring those in the text environment. The optimal strategy was
405   performed by a single human performing the task according to an optimal policy that maximally
406   reduced uncertainly about the correct property. The random strategy was performed according to a
407   policy that selects a random object from the room at each step, with replacement.
408   As running the 3D environment and using human actors in the loop reduces experimental throughput,
409   we limit ourselves to a single level of environment and reward function complexity. We choose the
410   condition with 3 colors and 1 causal factor, as conditions with more colors had significant visual
411   clutter. Each task is randomly generated as follows: at the beginning of the episode, 3 unique
412   colors and 3 unique shapes are randomly selected from 6 colors and 5 shapes, and objects with each
413   shape-color combination are placed in random locations in the environment, for a total of 9 objects.
414   One property, either a shape or a color, is randomly selected as the correct property, for a total of 3
415   correct objects. The player and the Exchanger machine with input and output conveyor are likewise
416
      placed randomly in the room. A gameplay episode ends when either all 3 correct objects are placed
      on the input conveyor or 2 minutes have elapsed.

      4.4.2    G EMINI - BASED AGENT
420   The Gemini agent is implemented as follows: every 10 seconds, the model is fed the most recent
421   100 video frames (or 67 seconds) of gameplay and queried in two stages, during which gameplay is
422   paused for the human actor. We implement a two-stage procedure with a vision stage and a reasoning
423   stage, which we found improves accuracy for each stage compared with running both together. In
424   the first stage, Gemini is asked to list, for every object placed on the input conveyor, the timestamp
425   at which it was placed, its color and shape, and whether it was correct or not (as indicated by a red
426   or green light on the machine). In the second stage, Gemini is provided the output of the first stage
427   (subsequent video frames and list of objects placed with their reward values) and prompted to select a
428
      next exploratory action to maximize information gain, similar to the text environment. The human
      actor is provided only with the command generated by the second stage, such as “place the red cube
429
      on the conveyor.”

466   4.4.4    R ESULTS
467
      In the exploration efficiency metric, we see the same trends in the results for the 3D embodied
468   environment as for the text environment, with Gemini’s exploration efficiency significantly out-
469   performing the random baseline and approaching the optimal baseline (Figure 5a). The absolute
470   performance matches that seen in the text experiments, within the margin of error: a mean of 2 steps
471   for Gemini and the optimal baseline, and 4 steps for the random baseline. These results suggest
472   that the additional complexity of an imperfect vision system and partially observed environment
473   state are not significant limitations in generalizing directed exploration capabilities to embodied 3D
474   environments. In the accuracy metric (Figure 5c), the picture is more nuanced. For relevant property
475   accuracy, the difference between performance with the Gemini agent and the random agent was not
476
      statistically significant (p > 0.05, paired sample t-test).
477   This result is interesting because VLM vision is also necessary for the exploration phase, where
478   there was no discrepancy in performance. A likely reason for this is that the iterative nature of the
479   exploration task makes it robust to occasional errors. Because the model must re-list all objects
480   placed at each step, chance errors made during one step do not propagate to later steps.
481   To probe the reason for the gap in accuracy performance, we also computed results where we filtered
482   out trajectories in which the vision step made an error (Figure 5b,d). In these results, accuracies
483   for the Gemini and optimal agents are nearly identical and their differences with the random agent
484   are statistically significant (p < 0.05, two sample t-test). These results suggest that errors in the
485   vision step, rather than reasoning or exploration, are responsible for the relatively reduced accuracy
      in the Gemini agent condition. Investigating relative numbers of errors, there appear to be more

486
      vision errors in the Gemini agent condition than in the optimal or random agent conditions (Figure 8),
487   although these differences aren’t statistically significant.
488
489   Taken together, results in the Construction Lab show that the directed exploration capabilities of
490
      foundation models robustly generalize from text-based environments to embodied 3D environments,
      though overall accuracy of the system is somewhat reduced by imperfect performance of the VLM’s
491
      object and action recognition in videos. This indicates that the challenges of multi-modal reasoning
492
      from realistic simulated video could be addressed by focusing on the vision and action recognition
493   capabilities of foundation models separately from their reasoning capabilities.

496   5   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
497
498   We introduce a framework for evaluating how effectively foundation models can explore new interac-
499
      tive environments and predict hidden reward functions. Our analysis identifies distinct challenges in
      generating and executing strategic exploratory actions, offering potential solutions.
500
501   In a text-based implementation, we evaluate leading foundation models across varying environment
502   and reward complexities. We find that exploration efficiency remains relatively constant compared
503   to an optimal baseline, even as complexity increases. However, performance declines with reward
504   functions based on multiple features, partly due to limitations in policy translation and in-context
505
      memory use. (See Section 4.3 for details).
506   Statistical analysis reveals that Gemini Flash excels with simpler reward functions, while Gemini
507   Pro with self-correction performs better on those with multiple factors. This suggests a potential
508   trade-off between model size/reasoning complexity and reward function complexity. Further research
509   is needed to understand how iterative reasoning influences effective exploration strategies.
510   In 3D environments, Gemini 1.5 Pro achieves near-optimal exploration efficiency, mirroring its
511   performance in text-based settings. However, accurate interpretation of visual observations remains
512   a challenge due to limitations in the vision system. Improving visual accuracy, potentially through
513   fine-tuning, is important for achieving comparable performance in 3D embodied environments.
514
      The strong performance of foundation models in our exploration tasks motivates further research
515   with more complex environments and methods for improving visual perception. Future directions
516   include replacing the human actor in our 3D setup with language-conditioned agents (Abi Raad et al.,
517   2024; Wang et al., 2023a;b; Feng et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024) or utilizing real-world footage from
518   head-mounted cameras to further enhance exploration capabilities.
519
      We are excited by the potential of foundation models to autonomously explore and test hypotheses in
520
      interactive environments, a crucial aspect of human learning and scientific progress. We anticipate
521   further research in this promising area.
