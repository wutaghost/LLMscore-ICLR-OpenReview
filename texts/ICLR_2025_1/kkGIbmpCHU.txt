009                                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011           Autoregressive models are predominant in natural language generation, while their
012           application in tabular data remains underexplored. We posit that this can be at-
013           tributed to two factors: 1) tabular data contains heterogeneous data type, while the
014           autoregressive model is primarily designed to model discrete-valued data; 2) tabular
015
              data is column permutation-invariant, requiring a generation model to generate
              columns in arbitrary order. This paper proposes a Diffusion-nested Autoregressive
016
              model (TAB DAR) to address these issues. To enable autoregressive methods for
017
              continuous columns,TAB DAR employs a diffusion model to parameterize the con-
018           ditional distribution of continuous features. To ensure arbitrary generation order,
019           TAB DAR resorts to masked transformers with bi-directional attention, which simu-
020           late various permutations of column order, hence enabling it to learn the conditional
021           distribution of a target column given an arbitrary combination of other columns.
022           These designs enable TAB DAR to not only freely handle heterogeneous tabular
023           data but also support convenient and flexible unconditional/conditional sampling.
024           We conduct extensive experiments on ten datasets with distinct properties, and the
025           proposed TAB DAR outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods by 18% to 45%
026
              on eight metrics across three distinct aspects.

      (GANs)(Xu et al., 2019), Diffusion Models (Zhang et al., 2024b), and even Large Language Models
047

048
      models, a crucial category of generative models, have been largely overlooked in this process. In
049   language modeling, autoregressive models have become the de facto solution (e.g., GPTs (Mann et al.,
050   2020; Achiam et al., 2023)). Tabular data shares similarities with natural language in its discrete
051   structure, making an autoregressive decomposition of its distribution a natural approach, i.e.,

      where each xi represents the value at the i-th column, x<i = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xi−1 }. However, research
055   on autoregressive models for tabular data generation has not received adequate attention.
056
057   We posit that this is primarily due to two key challenges (see Fig. 1): 1) Modeling continuous
058
      distribution: Autoregressive models aim to learn the per-token (conditional) probability distribution,
      which is convenient for discrete tokens (such as words in Natural Languages) because their probability
059
      can be represented as a categorical distribution. However, the same approach is challenging to apply to
060
      continuous tokens unless there are strong prior assumptions about their distribution, such as assuming
061   they follow Gaussian distributions. 2) No fixed order: Unlike natural language, which possesses
062   an inherent causal order from left to right, tabular data exhibits column permutation invariance .
063   To reflect this property in the autoregressive model, we need to ensure a sequence of tokens can
064   be generated in arbitrary order. Existing autoregressive models for tabular data generation adopt
065   simplistic approaches to address these challenges. For instance, they discretize continuous columns,
066   allowing them to learn corresponding categorical distributions (Castellon et al., 2023; Gulati &
067   Roysdon, 2023). However, this method inevitably leads to information loss. Regarding the generation
068   order, these models typically default to a left-to-right column sequence (Castellon et al., 2023), failing
069
      to reflect the column permutation invariant property.
070   To address these challenges, this paper proposes Diffusion-nested AutoRegressive Tabular Data
071   Generation (TAB DAR in short). TAB DAR addresses the aforementioned issues through two design
072   features: 1) Nested diffusion models for modeling the conditional probability distributions of
073   the next continuous-valued tokens. TAB DAR nests a small diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020;
074   Karras et al., 2022) into the autoregressive framework for learning the conditional distribution of
075
      a continuous-valued column. Specifically, we employ two distinct loss functions for learning the
      distribution of the next continuous/discrete-valued tokens, respectively. For discrete columns, their
076
      conditional distribution is learned by directly minimizing the KL divergence between the prediction
077
      vector and the ground-truth one-hot category embedding. For continuous columns, we learn their
078   distribution through a diffusion model conditioned on the output of the current location. In this
079   way, TAB DAR can flexibly learn the distribution of tabular data containing arbitrary data types. 2)
080   Masked Transformers with bi-directional attention. TAB DAR simulates arbitrary sequence orders
081   via a transformer-architectured model with bi-directional attention mechanisms and masked inputs.
082   The masked/unmasked locations indicate the columns that are missing/observed, ensuring that when
083   predicting a new token, the model has only access to the known tokens. During training, TAB DAR
084   follows the form of masked language modeling, predicting the distribution of masked tokens based
085   on unmasked tokens. During testing, TAB DAR generates entire rows of data in an autoregressive
086
      manner according to a given order.
087   TAB DAR offers several advantages. 1) TAB DAR employs the most appropriate generative models
088   for different data types, i.e., diffusion models for continuous columns and categorical prediction
089   for discrete columns, seamlessly integrating them into a unified framework. This avoids contrived
090   processing methods such as discrete diffusion models (Lee et al., 2023; Kotelnikov et al., 2023),
091   continuous tokenization of categorical columns (Zhang et al., 2024b), and ineffective discretiza-
092
      tion (Castellon et al., 2023). 2) Through an autoregressive approach, TAB DAR models the conditional
      distribution between different columns, thereby better capturing the dependency relationships among
093
      various columns and achieving superior density estimation. 3) Combining autoregression and masked
094
      Transformers enables a trained TAB DAR to compute and sample from the conditional distribution of
095   target columns given an arbitrary set of observable columns, enabling generation in arbitrary order.
096   This capability facilitates precise and convenient posterior inference (e.g., conditional sampling and
097   missing data imputation).
098
      We conduct comprehensive experiments on ten tabular datasets of various data types and scales to
099
      verify the efficacy of the proposed TAB DAR. Experimental results comprehensively demonstrate
100
      TAB DAR’s superior performance in: 1) Statistical Fidelity: The ability of synthetic data to faithfully
101   recover the ground-truth data distribution; 2) Data Utility: The performance of synthetic data
102   in downstream Machine Learning tasks, such as Machine Learning Efficiency; and 3) Privacy
103   Protection: Whether the synthetic data is sampled from the underlying distribution of the training
104   data rather than being a simple copy. In missing value imputation tasks, TAB DAR exhibits remarkable
105   performance, even surpassing state-of-the-art methods that are specially designed for missing data
106   imputation tasks. For a genuine and fair comparison, we have released the code to reproduce
107   our method and all baseline results. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
      science/r/ICLR-TabDAR.

110
      Synthetic Tabular Data Generation Generative models for tabular data have become increasingly
111
      important and have widespread applications Assefa et al. (2021); Zheng & Charoenphakdee (2022);
112   Hernandez et al. (2022). For example, CTGAN and TAVE (Xu et al., 2019) deal with mixed-type
113   tabular data generation using the basic GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and VAE (Kingma & Welling,
114   2013) framework. GOGGLE (Liu et al., 2023) incorporates Graph Attention Networks in a VAE
115   framework such that the correlation between different data columns can be explicitly learned. DP-
116   TBART (Castellon et al., 2023) and TabMT (Gulati & Roysdon, 2023) use discretization techniques
117   to numerical columns and then apply autoregressive transformers for a generation. Recently, inspired
118   by the success of Diffusion models in image generation, a lot of diffusion-based methods have been
119   proposed, such as TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023), STaSy (Kim et al., 2023), CoDi (Lee et al.,
120
      2023), and TabSyn (Zhang et al., 2024b), which have achieved SOTA synthesis quality.
121
122   Autoregressive Models for Continuous Space Data In text generation, autoregressive next-token
123
      generation is undoubtedly the dominant approach (Mann et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023). However,
      in image generation, although autoregressive models (Van den Oord et al., 2016; Salimans et al.,
124
      2017) were proposed early on, their pixel-level characteristics limited their further development.
125
      Subsequently, diffusion models, which are naturally suited to modeling continuous distributions, have
126   become the most popular method in the field of image generation. In recent years, some studies have
127   attempted to use discrete-value image tokens (van den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019) and
128   employ autoregressive transformers for image-generation tasks (Kolesnikov et al., 2022). However,
129   discrete tokenizers are both difficult to train and inevitably cause information loss. To this end, recent
130   work has attempted to combine continuous space diffusion models with autoregressive methods.
131   For example, Li et al. (2024b) employs an autoregressive diffusion loss in a causal Transformer
132   for learning image representations; Li et al. (2024a) proposes using a diffusion model to model the
133   conditional distribution of the next continuous image and employs a masked bidirectional attention
134
      mechanism to enable the generation of any number of tokens in arbitrary order.

138   3.1       D EFINITIONS AND N OTATIONS
139
140   In this paper, we always use uppercase boldface (e.g., X) letters to represent matrices, lowercase
141   boldface letters (e.g., x) to represent vectors, and regular italics (e.g., x) to denote scalar entries
142   in matrices or vectors. Tabular data refers to data organized in a tabular format consisting of rows
143   and columns. Each row represents an instance or observation, while each column represents a
144
      feature or variable. In this work, we consider heterogeneous tabular data that may contain both
      numerical and categorical columns or only one of these types. Let D = {xi }N       i=1 denote a tabular
145
      dataset comprising N instances, where each instance x = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xD ) is a D-dimensional
146
      vector representing the values of D features or variables. We further categorize the features into
147
      two types: 1) Numerical/continuous features: N = {i | xi ∈ R} is the set of indices corresponding
148   to numerical features. 2) Categorical/discrete features: C = {i | xi ∈ Ci } is the set of indices
149   corresponding to categorical features, where Ci is the set of possible categories for the i-th feature.
150   Note that N ∪ C = 1, 2, . . . , D and N ∩ C = ∅.

176   Modeling tabular data using autoregression. TAB DAR follows the autoregressive criteria for
177   modeling the distribution of tabular data. Autoregressive modeling decomposes the joint data
178
      distribution into the product of a series of conditional distributions in raster order, and the optimization
      is achieved by minimizing the standard negative log-likelihood:

      conditional distribution, i.e., p(xi |x<i ), separately, which is intuitively much simpler.

186   Autoregression is natural for language modeling, as language inherently possesses a left-to-right
187   (L2R in short) order, and we can expect that the generation of a new word depends entirely on the
188   observed words preceding it. Therefore, Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) with causal attention
189   (where only the previous tokens are observable to predict the following tokens) are widely used for
190
      text generation. Unlike text data, tabular data has long been considered column permutation invariant.

245   4.2         D ETAILED A RCHITECTURE OF TAB DAR
246
247   In this section, we introduce the detailed architecture and implementations of TAB DAR. The overall
248   framework of TAB DAR is presented in Fig. 3.
249   Tokenization layer. Given a row of tabular data x = (x1 , x2 , · · · , xD ), the tokenization layer
250   embeds each feature to a d-dimensional vector using column-specific learnable linear transformation.
251   For a continuous column, the linear transformation is Wi ∈ R1×d , while for a discrete column,
252   the transformation matrix is Wi ∈ R|Ci |×d . The output token of column i is denoted by hi , and
253   we obtain an embedding matrix of D tokens H ∈ RD×d . We also add column-specific learnable
254   positional encoding so that the token embeddings at different locations can be discriminated.
255   Input masking. As illustrated in Section 4.1, we can use masked bidirectional attention to simulate
256   arbitrary causal attention. Therefore, we first uniformly sample the number of tokens toP
                                                                                              mask/predict
257   M ∼ U(1, D). Given M , we randomly sample a masking vector m ∈ {0, 1}D , s.t., i mi = M .
258   mi = 1 indicates that column i is masked and vice versa. Then, the input embeddings of masked
259   columns are set as zero, making them unobservable when predicting the target columns.

      Loss function and model training. After obtaining the latent vector zi for a target/masked column i,
271   we use it as the condition for predicting the distribution of xi , i,e., p(xi |zi ).
272
273   Discrete columns. As presented in Eq. 6, we use a column-specific prediction head fi (·) to obtain the
274
      |Ci |-dimensional prediction then compute the cross-entropy loss.
275   Continuous columns. For predicting the distribution of the continuous column xi , we use the
276   conditional diffusion loss described in Eq. 7. The training of the conditional diffusion model follows
277   the standard process of diffusion models (Karras et al., 2022). To be specific, we first randomly
278   sample a timestep t ∼ p(t), and standard Gaussian noise ε ∼ N (0, 1), obtaining xit = xi + σ(t) · ε.
279   Then, a denoising neural network ϵθ (xit , t, zi ) takes xit , t, and zi as input to predict the added noise ε
280
      (via the MSE loss in Eq. 7). The denoising neural network is implemented as a shallow MLP, and its
      detailed architecture is presented in Appendix C.
281
282   Model Training. The training of TAB DAR is end-to-end: all the model parameters, including the
283   Embedding layer, transformers, prediction heads for discrete columns, and diffusion models for the
284   continuous columns, are jointly trained via gradient descent on the summation of the losses of the
285   target/masked columns in the current batch.
286   Model inference. After TAB DAR is trained, we can easily perform a variety of inference tasks, e.g.,
287   unconditional and conditional generation. An illustration of TAB DAR’s auto-regressive sampling
288   process is presented in Fig. 4.
289
      Unconditional Sampling. To generate unconditional data examples x ∼ p(x), we can first randomly
290   sample a generation order. Then, starting with all columns masked (except the [pad] token), we can
291   generate the value of each column one by one according to the sampled order.
292
293
      Conditional Sampling. One important advantage of TAB DAR’s autoregressive generation manner
      is that one can perform flexible conditional sampling, such as simple class-conditional generation.
294
      This can be achieved by directly setting the corresponding column values to the desired ones and
295
      then randomly sampling the generation order of other columns. Missing value imputation can be
296   regarded as a special case of conditional sampling by resorting to the conditional distribution of
297   missing columns given observed values. More implementation details can be found in Appendix D.4.

303
304   Datasets. We select ten real-world tabular datasets of varying data types and sizes: 1) two contain
305   only continuous features – California and Letter; 2) two contain only categorical features – Car
306   and Nursery; 3) six datasets of mixed continuous and discrete features – Adult, Default, Shoppers,
307   Magic, News, and Beijing. The detailed introduction of these datasets can be found Appendix D.2.
308   Baselines. We compare the proposed TAB DAR with ten powerful synthetic tabular data generation
309   methods belonging to six categories. 1) The non-parametric interpolation method SMOTE (Chawla
310   et al., 2002). 2) VAE-based methods TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) and GOGGLE (Liu et al., 2023). 3) GAN-
311   based method CTGAN (Xu et al., 2019). 4) LLM-based method GReaT (Borisov et al., 2023). 5)
312   Diffusion-based methods: STaSy (Kim et al., 2023), CoDi (Lee et al., 2023), TabDDPM (Kotelnikov
313   et al., 2023), and TabSyn (Zhang et al., 2024b). 6) Autoregressive methods DP-TBART (Castellon
314
      et al., 2023) and Tab-MT (Gulati & Roysdon, 2023). The proposed TAB DAR belongs to the
      autoregressive family, yet it utilizes a diffusion model for modeling continuous columns.
315
316   Evaluation Methods. We evaluate the quality of synthetic tabular data from three distinct dimensions:
317   1) Fidelity - if the synthetic data faithfully recovers the ground-truth data distribution. 2) Utility -
318   the performance when applied to downstream tasks, and we focus on Machine Learning Efficiency
319   (MLE). 3) Privacy - if the synthetic data is not copied from the real records. More introduction of
320
      these metrics is in Appendix D.6.
321   Implementations. Since TAB DAR’s diffusion loss is applied to each single column, the denoising
322   network εθ can be light-weighted, and we implement it as a three-layer MLP with 256 hidden
323   dimension. For the diffusion model, we follow Zhang et al. (2024b) and set σ(t) = t, which enables
      a small number of diffusion step, i.e., NFE = 50.

347   Statistical Fidelity We first investigate whether synthetic data can faithfully reproduce the distribu-
348   tion of the original data. We use various statistical metrics to reflect the degree to which synthetic
349
      data estimates the distribution density of the original data. These metrics include univariate density
      estimation (i.e., the marginal distribution of a single column), the correlation between any two vari-
350
      ables (which reflects the joint probability distribution), α-Precision (reflecting whether a synthetic
351
      data example is close to the true distribution), β-Recall (reflecting the coverage of synthetic data
352   over the original data), Classifier Two Sample Test (C2ST, reflecting the difficulty in distinguishing
353   between synthetic data and real data), and Jensen-Shannon divergence (JSD, estimating the distance
354   between the distributions of real data and synthetic data). Due to space limitations, in this section, we
355   only present the average performance with standard deviation on each metric across all ten datasets.
356   The detailed performance on each individual dataset is in Appendix E.
357
      In Table 1, we present the performance comparison on these fidelity metrics. As demonstrated, our
358   model achieved performance far surpassing the second-best method in five out of six fidelity metrics,
359   with advantages ranging from 18.18% to 45.65%. Considering that these metrics have already been
360   elevated to a considerably high level due to the explosive development of recent deep generative
361   models for tabular data, our improvement is very significant. The only exception is β-Recall, which
362   measures the degree of coverage of the synthetic data over the entire data distribution. On this metric,
363   the simple classical interpolation method SMOTE achieved the best performance, far surpassing
364   other generative models. On the other hand, SMOTE also achieves very good performance on other
365   metrics, surpassing many generative methods. These phenomena indicate that simple interpolation
366
      methods can indeed obtain synthetic data with a distribution close to that of real data. However, the
      limitation of interpolation methods lies in the fact that the synthetic data is too close to the real data,
367
      making it resemble a copy from the training set rather than a sample from the underlying distribution,
368
      which may cause privacy issues. Detailed experiments are in the Privacy Protection section.

416   Privacy Protection Finally, a good synthetic dataset should not only faithfully reproduce the
417
      original data distribution but also ensure that it is sampled from the underlying distribution of the real
      data rather than being a copy. To this end, we use the Distance to Closest Record (DCR) score for
418
      measurement. Specifically, we divide the real data equally into two parts of the same size, namely the
419
      training set and the holdout set. We train the model based on the training set and obtain the sampled
420   synthetic dataset. Afterward, we calculate the distribution of distances between each synthetic data
421   example and its closest sample in both the training set and the holdout set. Intuitively, if the training
422   set and holdout set are both drawn uniformly from the same distribution and if the synthetic data has
423   learned the true distribution, then on average, the proportion of synthetic data samples that are closer
424   to the training set should be the same as those closer to the holdout set (both 50%). Conversely, if
425   the synthetic data is copied from the training set, the probability of it being closer to samples in the
426   training set would far exceed 50%.
427   In Table 3 and Figure 5, we present the probability comparison and the distributions of the DCR
428   scores of SMOTE, TabDDPM, TabSyn, and the proposed TAB DAR. As demonstrated, SMOTE is
429   poor at privacy protection, as its synthetic sample tends to be closer to the training set rather than the
430   holdout set, as its synthetic examples are obtained via interpolation between training examples. By
431   contrast, the remaining deep generative methods are all good at preserving the privacy of training
      data, leading to almost completely overlapped DCR distributions.

      Since TAB DAR directly models the factorized conditional probability of the target column(s) given
454
      the observation of other columns, it has a strong potential for missing data imputation. In this section,
455
      we compare the proposed TAB DAR with the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods for missing
456   data imputation, including KNN (Pujianto et al., 2019), GRAPE (You et al., 2020), MOT (Muzellec
457   et al., 2020), and Remakser (Du et al., 2024). We consider the task of training the model on the
458   complete training data and then imputing the missing values of testing data. The missing mechanism
459   is Missing Completely at Random (MCAR), and the missing ratio of testing data is set at 30%.
460   In Figure 6, we present the performance comparison on four datasets: Letter, Adult, Default, and
461   Shoppers (as well as the average imputation performance). The proposed TAB DAR demonstrates
462   superior performance on these four datasets, significantly outperforming the current best methods on
463   three out of four datasets. On the Adult dataset, it was slightly inferior. These results confirm that
464
      TAB DAR is not only suitable for unconditional generation but also applicable to other conditional
      generation tasks, demonstrating a wide range of application values.

468
469   Effects of diffusion loss and random ordering. We first study if the two key ingredients of
470   TAB DAR– 1) the diffusion loss and 2) random ordering – are indeed beneficial. We consider three
471
      variants of TAB DAR: ‘w/o. Diff. Loss’, which indicates that we discretize the continuous columns
      into 100 uniform bins, treating them as discrete ones, such that we can apply the cross-entropy loss;
472
      ‘w/o rand. order,’ which indicates that we follow the default left-to-right order in both the training
473
      and generation phases using a causal attention Transformer model; ‘w/o. both’, which indicates the
474   version that combines the two variants. In Table 4, we compare TAB DAR with the three variants on
475   the average synthetic data’s fidelity across all the datasets. We can observe that both the diffusion loss
476   and random training/generation order are important to TAB DAR’s success. Specifically, the diffusion
477   loss targeting the modeling of the conditional distribution of the continuous columns contributes the
478   most, and the random ordering further improves TAB DAR’s performance. Furthermore, random
479   ordering enables TAB DAR to perform flexible conditional inference tasks like imputation.
480    Sensitivity to hyperparameters. We then study TAB DAR’s sensitivity to its hyperparameters
481    that specify its transformer architecture: the model depth (number of Transformer layers) and the
482    embedding dimension d. From Table 5 and Table 6 (grey cells indicate the default setting), we
483    can observe that although there exists an optimal hyperparameter setting (i.e., depth = 6, d = 32),
484    the change of the two hyperparameters has little impact on the model performance. These results
485    demonstrate that our model is relatively robust to these hyperparameters. Therefore, good results can
       be obtained for different datasets without the need for specific hyperparameter tuning.

531
532
        This paper has presented TAB DAR, a generative model that embeds a diffusion model within an
533
        autoregressive transformer framework used for multi-modal tabular data synthesis. TAB DAR uses
534     a novel diffusion loss and traditional cross-entropy loss to learn the conditional distributions of
535     continuous columns and discrete columns, respectively, enabling a single autoregressive model to
536     generate both continuous and discrete features simultaneously. Furthermore, TAB DAR employs
537     masked bidirectional attention to simulate arbitrary autoregressive orders, allowing the model to
538     generate in any direction. This not only enhances the accuracy of joint probability modeling but
539     also enables more flexible conditional generation. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the
        effectiveness of the proposed method.

919   embedding, and then we apply a]the zero mask to the predefined masked/target tokens. Furthermore,

1046   Following (Zhang et al., 2024a), we use the Expected A Posteriori (EAP) estimator to impute the
1047   missing values. Specifically, denote a sample x with missing values as x = (xobs , xmis ) where xobs
1048   and xmis are the observed and missing values, respectively. Our goal is to compute the expectation of
1049
       the missing values conditioned on the observed values:
1050                                           Exmis ∼p(xmis |xobs ) [xmis ]
1051
1052   To estimate the expectation, we sample k times from the posterior distribution p(xmis |xobs ) (with
1053
       Algorithm 5) and obtain a set of samples {x̂imis }ki=1 .
1054   For numerical features, the imputation x̂mis is set to the mean of the samples:
1055
                                                                k
1056                                                       1X i

1057                                                       k i=1 mis
1058

1060   Suppose the column has categories C = {c1 , c2 , · · · , cm }, the imputation x̂mis is set to the most
1061   frequent category:
1062                                                          k

1066   vote can be understood as a mean estimator that is more robust to outliers.

1074   Tab-MT and DP-TBART. Tab-MT (Gulati & Roysdon, 2023) and DP-TBART (Castellon et al.,
1075   2023) are two recently proposed tabular data generation models based on MAE. To handle numerical
1076   features (with continuous distribution), Tab-MT quantizes the numerical features into 100 uniform
1077   bins, and DP-TBART quantizes the numerical features into 100 bins where each bin has the same
1078   nearest center determined by K-means. Additionally, DP-TBART employs DP-SGD (Abadi et al.,
1079   2016) to enhance the differential privacy performance. Since the focus of this paper is not on
       differential privacy, in our implementation, we use Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer.

1160   D.6.3    α-P RECISION AND β-R ECALL
1161
       α-Precision and β-Recall are two sample-level metrics quantifying how faithful the synthetic data
1162   is proposed in Alaa et al. (2022). In general, α-Precision evaluates the fidelity of synthetic data
1163   – whether each synthetic example comes from the real-data distribution, β-Recall evaluates the
1164   coverage of the synthetic data, e.g., whether the synthetic data can cover the entire distribution of the
1165   real data (In other words, whether a real data sample is close to the synthetic data).
1166

1168
1169   C2ST studies how difficult it is to distinguish real data from synthetic data, therefore evaluating
1170   whether synthetic data can recover real data distribution. The C2ST metric used in this paper is
1171
       implemented by the SDMetrics19 package.

1173   D.6.5    M ACHINE L EARNING E FFICIENCY (MLE)
1174
       In MLE, each dataset is first split into the real training and testing set. The generative models are
1175
       learned on the real training set. After the models are learned, a synthetic set of equivalent size is
1176   sampled.
1177
1178   The performance of synthetic data on MLE tasks is evaluated based on the divergence of test scores
1179
       using the real and synthetic training data. Therefore, we first train the machine learning model on the
       real training set, split into training and validation sets with a 8 : 1 ratio. The classifier/regressor is
1180
       trained on the training set, and the optimal hyperparameter setting is selected according to the perfor-
1181
       mance on the validation set. After the optimal hyperparameter setting is obtained, the corresponding
1182   classifier/regressor is retrained on the training set and evaluated on the real testing set. We create
1183   20 random splits for training and validation sets, and the performance reported is the mean of the
1184   AUC/RMSE score over the 20 random trails. The performance of synthetic data is obtained in the
1185   same way.

       D.6.6      D ISTANCE TO C LOSEST R ECORD
1189
1190   We follow the ‘synthetic vs. holdout’ setting 20 . We initially divide the dataset into two equal parts:
1191   the first part served as the training set for training our generative model, while the second part was
1192   designated as the holdout set, which is not used for training. After completing model training, we
1193   sample a synthetic set of the same size as the training set (and the holdout set).
1194   We then calculate the DCR scores for each sample in the synthetic set concerning both the training
1195   set and the holdout set. We further calculate the probability that a synthetic sample is closer to the
1196   training set (rather than the holdout set). When this probability is close to 50% (i.e., 0.5), it indicates
1197   that the distribution of distances between synthetic and training instances is very similar (or at least
1198   not systematically smaller) than the distribution of distances between synthetic and holdout instances,
1199   which is a positive indicator in terms of privacy risk.
