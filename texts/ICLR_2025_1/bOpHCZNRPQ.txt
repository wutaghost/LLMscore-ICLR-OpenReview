                                                      A BSTRACT
008
009
                Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities
010
                across various applications, but their performance on long-context tasks is of-
011
                ten limited by the computational complexity of attention mechanisms. This paper
012             introduces a novel approach to accelerate attention computation in LLMs, par-
013             ticularly for long-context scenarios. We leverage the inherent sparsity within at-
014             tention mechanisms, both in conventional Softmax attention and ReLU attention
015             (with ReLUα activation, α ∈ N+ ), to significantly reduce the running time com-
016             plexity. Our method employs a Half-Space Reporting (HSR) data structure to
017             rapidly identify non-zero or “massively activated” entries in the attention matrix.
018             We present theoretical analyses for two key scenarios: attention generation and
019             full attention computation with long input context. Our approach achieves a run-
020             ning time of O(mn4/5 ) significantly faster than the naive approach O(mn) for
021
                attention generation, where n is the context length, m is the query length, and d
                is the hidden dimension. We can also reduce the running time of full attention
022
                computation from O(mn) to O(mn1−1/⌊d/2⌋ + mn4/5 ). Importantly, our method
023
                introduces no error for ReLU attention and only provably negligible error for Soft-
024
                max attention, where the latter is supported by our empirical validation. This work
025             represents a significant step towards enabling efficient long-context processing in
026             LLMs, potentially broadening their applicability across various domains.

030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have showcased remarkable capabilities across various applica-
032   tions, including context-aware question answering, content generation, summarization, and dialogue
033   systems, among others (Thoppilan et al., 2022; Coenen et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
034   2024b). Long-context tasks of LLMs have gained more and more attention. Several LLMs ex-
035   tend their context length to 128K tokens, such as Yarn (Peng et al., 2023), GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023),
036   Claude 3.5 (Anthropic, 2024), Llama 3.1 (Meta, 2024), Phi-3.5 (Abdin et al., 2024), Mistral Nemo
037
      (MistralAI, 2024), etc. A bottleneck for long-context tasks is the computational cost of the atten-
      tion mechanism in LLMs. The key to LLM success is the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al.,
038
      2017), wildly used in various practical scenarios (Radford et al., 2019; Kenton & Toutanova, 2019;
039
      Wang et al., 2023b;a; 2024), whose critical component is the attention mechanism. Let n be the data

061   m = Θ(1) and m = Θ(n). Our approach is inspired by the inherent sparsity found within attention
062   mechanisms. Numerous prior studies have highlighted the significant sparsity in the attention matrix
063   (Child et al., 2019; Anagnostidis et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).
064   This manifestation of sparsity in Softmax attention is that a large number of attention scores, i.e.,
065   QK ⊤ , concentrate on a small number of entries, which is known as “massive activation”. Due to
066   this nature, Softmax attention can be accelerated by only calculating the entries that contain large
067   attention scores, introducing negligible approximation errors (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).

118

143
144   Attention acceleration for long context input. Long context window is essential for transformer
145   based LLMs in many downstream tasks. However, due to the quadratic time complexity associated
146   with self-attention mechanisms, transformers are usually hard to inference efficiently. Numerous
147
      methods have been proposed to enhance the inference efficiency. One approach involves using
      alternative architectures as proxies for attention to support faster inference, such as Mamba (Gu &
148
      Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), PolySketchFormer (Kacham et al., 2023), and Linearizing Transform-
149
      ers (Zhang et al., 2024a; Mercat et al., 2024). However, the broad applicability of these methods
150   across different applications and modalities remains to be fully validated. Another line of research
151   focuses on approximating attention matrix computation (Alman & Song, 2023; 2024a;b; Han et al.,
152   2024; Zandieh et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024d; Poli et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024; Liang et al.,
153   2024c;a; Gao et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024b). Nevertheless, these methods
154   often rely on assumptions that may not be practical. For instance, some approaches use polyno-
155   mial methods to approximate the exponential function, which requires all entries to be bounded by
156   a small constant. However, our HSR-enhanced attention framework is designed based on practical
157   observation and validated by empirical support.
158   ReLU attention. ReLU attention is an innovative mechanism that employs the ReLU activation
159   function in place of the traditional Softmax function for attention computation. Previous studies
160   have highlighted the promise potential of ReLU attention in various domains. From empirical side,
161   Wortsman et al. (2023) has demonstrated that incorporating ReLU as the activation function in
      vision transformers enhances performance on downstream tasks. Shen et al. (2023) has shown that


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      transformers equipped with ReLU attention outperform those with Softmax attention, particularly
163   when dealing with large key-value memory in machine translation tasks. From theoretical side, the
164   scale-invariant property of ReLU attention (Li et al., 2022) facilitates the scalability of transformer
165   networks. Furthermore, Bai et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2023) have shown that the inherent properties
166   of ReLU attention contribute positively to the learning process of transformer models. Another
167   key advantage of ReLU attention is that the ReLU function effectively sets all negative values to
168   zero, allowing us to bypass these non-contributory elements during attention computation, thereby
169   reducing the running time of attention computation. Importantly, omitting these zero and negative
170   entries does not introduce any error into the final output of the ReLU attention mechanism.
171   Half-space reporting (HSR) data structure. The Half-Space Reporting (HSR) data structure, ini-
172   tially proposed by Agarwal et al. (1992), was developed to address the half-space range reporting
173   problem. The expedited range query capability inherent to HSR has been demonstrated to sig-
174   nificantly enhance computational efficiency across a variety of tasks, as evidenced by numerous
175   previous works in the literature. Studies such as Jiang et al. (2021) and Bhattacharya et al. (2023)
176   have applied HSR to facilitate solving general linear programming (LP) problems. Another line of
177
      research has highlighted HSR’s potential in expediting the training process of contemporary neural
      networks (Qin et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2022). There is also a collection of research that concentrates
178
      on leveraging HSR for the advancement of solutions to geometric and graphical challenges (Chen
179
      et al., 2005; Ju et al., 2013; Eppstein et al., 2017).

515    8           D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
516
517    The sparsity within neural networks arises primarily from the incorporation of non-linear activation
518    functions. These non-linear functions determine the mechanism or circuit of the neural networks,
519    e.g., the induction head in transformers (Olsson et al., 2022). Gaining insight into these non-linear
520    layers not only enhances our understanding of how neural networks work but also paves the way for
521    optimizing training and inference. We hope our analysis may inspire efficient neural network archi-
522    tecture design. This work represents the initial point of this envisioned blueprint. We concentrate on
523
       analyzing the combinations of LLMs and fundamental non-linear activation functions—ReLU and
       Softmax, which are most relevant to contemporary applications. By analyzing these functions, we
524
       aim to demonstrate to the research community that a thorough examination of a model’s non-linear
525
       characteristics can significantly enhance the running time complexity of neural networks.
526
527    In real-world scenarios, a multitude of non-linear activation functions exist beyond ReLU and Soft-
528    max, such as those designated as SELU(x) = scale · (max(0, x) + min(0, α · (exp(x) − 1)))
529
       (Klambauer et al., 2017), CELU(x) = max(0, x) + min(0, α · (exp(x/α) − 1)) (Barron, 2017), and
       PRELU(x) = max(0, x)+weight·min(0, x) (He et al., 2015). However, analyzing these alternative
530
       functions poses multiple challenges. Hence, we will explore these additional functions in the future.

533    9           C ONCLUSION
534
535    This work investigates the exploitation of the intrinsic sparsity present in both ReLU and Softmax
536    attention mechanisms to decrease the computational complexity of full attention computation and
537    attention generation scenarios. Specifically, we employ the Half-Space Reporting (HSR) data struc-
538    ture to accelerate the process of identifying non-zero or “massive activated” entries within ReLU
539    and Softmax attentions, respectively. Importantly, our approach does not import any errors to ReLU
       attention, and it results in only a negligible approximation error for Softmax attention.
