                                               Abstract
014
              The safety of Connected and Autonomous Vehicles (CAVs), Micro-mobility
015
              devices (e-scooter, e-bikes) and smartphone users rely on trusting the trajec-
016
              tory data they generate for navigation around each other. There is a need for
017           real-time verification of mobility data from these devices without compromis-
018           ing privacy as malicious data used for navigation could be deadly, specially
019           for vulnerable road users. In this paper, we propose Secure-FLOATING, a
020           scalable framework leveraging federated learning and blockchain for nearby
021           nodes to coordinate and learn to trust mobility data from nearby devices
022           and store this information via consensus on a tamper-proof distributed
023           ledger. We employ lightweight Secure Multi-party computation (SMPC)
024           with reduced messages exchanges to preserve privacy of the users and ensure
025
              data validation in real-time. Secure-FLOATING is evaluated using realistic
              trajectories for up to 8, 000 nodes (vehicles, micro-mobility devices and
026
              pedestrians) in New York City, and it shows to achieve lower delays and
027
              overhead, thereby accurately validating each others’ mobility data in a
028           scalable manner, with up to 75% successful endorsement for as high as 50%
029           attacker penetration.

032   1   Introduction
033
034   Connected and Autonomous Vehicles (CAVs) and micro-mobility devices (e-scooters, e-bikes,
035   etc.) are poised to transform transportation, promising increased safety, efficiency, and
036   accessibility. We envision a scenario where CAVs and other road users specially Vulnerable
037   Road Users (VRUs) such as pedestrians with smartphones and e-bike/ e-scooters riders share
038   their intended trajectories for the next few seconds or minutes with nearby road users and
039
      coordinate navigation around each other in real time. Imagine a signal-less intersection with
      CAVs and other road users safely and seamlessly navigate around each other by sharing
040
      sensory and future trajectory data with each other prior to crossing the intersection, thereby
041
      communicating intention along the exact speed, location and other information needed for
042   intersection crossing. However, nodes will hesitate, unable to ascertain the intentions of
043   their counterparts, or worse, where malicious actors could exploit system vulnerabilities. In
044   addition to faulty sensors, false data generation could mislead the users relying on it for their
045   real-time navigation decisions around CAVs, and therefore could lead to deadly consequences.
046   It is therefore vital need for a robust, reliable, and trustworthy decision-making framework
047   within the CAV ecosystem.
048   Current trust models, often centralized in nature are inadequately designed for such networks.
049   Centralizing vast amounts of sensitive mobility data creates a prime target for attacks
050   and privacy breaches, while relying on a single point of authority introduces a dangerous
051   single point of failure. Additionally, the opaque nature of centralized models leaves room
052   for manipulation, potentially compromising the integrity of the entire network. Stronger
053   anonymization and encryption techniques can achieve strong privacy but reduce the usefulness
      of the data in time and safety-critical scenarios due to their complexity and time consuming

054
      computation involved resulting in high computation and communication overhead. Moreover,
055   such approaches focuses solely on privacy preservation and ignore the data trustworthiness
056   challenge with no method to detect or mitigate impact of faulty or compromised sensors
057   generating misleading information resulting in dissemination of incorrect or manipulated
058   information.
059
      We introduce Secure-FLOATING: Federated Learning for Optimized Automated Trust
060
      ING, a blockchain-based platform as a paradigm shift in trust establishment for real-time
061   interactions regarding navigation between CAVs and other road users to collaboratively learn
062   representation of each others mobility locally without revealing data. It is a novel synergistic
063   combination of Verifiable Federated Learning (VFL), Secure Multi-Party Computation
064   (SMPC) as a scalable and privacy-aware technique targeted at real-time validation of mobility
065   data from CAVs and other devices through a blockchain based consensus, ensuring safety of
066   road users. However, doing so in real-time is challenging due to the increasing computation
067   and communication overhead of SMPC, the consensus process along the frequency of model
068   aggregation rounds in federated learning, in addition to the complexity of underlying
069
      prediction models. Secure-FLOATING is a lightweight solution to minimize this overhead
      from multiple aspects. First, the choice of a simple addition-based function for SMPC,
070
      although Secure-FLOATING is not limited to addition-based function and a complex function
071
      could be used based on the application requirements. Second, we suggest lightweight models
072   for the predictions of trajectories and show that such models performs comparable with
073   relatively complex ones, without the added computation overhead measured in FLOPs.
074   Third, our theoretical analysis show that Secure-FLOATING is scalable with linear increase
075   in overhead with the increase in network size (number of nodes), while ensuring privacy
076   guarantees. and finally, we are the first ones to set the basis for reducing message exchange
077   rounds for the SMPC, consensus process, and model updates in federated learning to further
078   reduce communication overhead.
079   At the heart of Secure-FLOATING lies VFL, a novel approach that enables collaborative
080   learning of a trust model across vehicles without the need for sharing raw data. This preserves
081   privacy while ensuring the integrity of each node’s contribution through cryptographic
082   verification mechanisms. To securely aggregate model updates from multiple nodes without
083   revealing sensitive information, we employ a lightweight MPC protocol to guarantee the
084   confidentiality of individual contributions while maintaining the accuracy of the global model.
085   Secure-FLOATING employs a consensus mechanism, allowing nodes to collaboratively
086
      validate each other’s trajectory data for real-time navigation decisions. We leverage zero
      knowledge proof as "trust-but-verify" mechanism to filter out noisy or malicious data, thereby
087
      significantly enhancing the reliability of the trust model. We provide rigorous theoretical
088
      guarantees, proving that the communication overhead of this mechanism scales linearly
089   with the number of vehicles, making it eminently suitable for large-scale deployments. It
090   also leverages the decentralized nature of blockchain or the InterPlanetary File System
091   (IPFS) (2) to store the global trust model and validation results. This ensures transparency,
092   immutability, and auditability of the system, further bolstering its trustworthiness.
093
      The paper organization is such that the following section discusses the related work and
094
      positions the novelty of our contribution. Section 3 describes the proposed Secure-FLOATING
095   platform, followed by a comprehensive theoretical analysis in Section 4. Section 5 evaluates
096   the platform, Section 6 discusses ethical considerations and limitations with future insight.
097   Section 7 concludes the paper.

      2   Related Work
100
101
      Trust establishment in CAV networks is a critical area of research, with various models
102   proposed to address the challenges of safety, cooperation, and reliability. Centralized trust
103   models, such as those based on reputation scores (e.g., Dhurandher et al. (8), Liu et al. (15))
104   or data-driven approaches (e.g., using deep learning by Yao et al. (26) and game-theoretic
105   approaches by Asvadi et al. (1)), have been explored. However, they suffer from inherent
106   limitations. Centralized data aggregation raises privacy concerns, as sensitive vehicle data is
107   transmitted to and stored in a central location (19). Additionally, these models are vulnerable
      to single points of failure and can be susceptible to manipulation by malicious actors (18).


                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Decentralized trust models have emerged as a promising alternative, empowering individual
109   vehicles to collaboratively build trust without relying on a central authority. Blockchain-based
110   solutions have been proposed to leverage the decentralized and immutable nature of the
111   technology for trust management in CAVs (20; 25). However, these approaches often focus
112   on transaction verification and data integrity, without adequately addressing the privacy
113   concerns associated with sharing raw data on the blockchain (3).
114
      Federated learning (FL) has also been explored for trust model building in CAVs (16; 23).
115   FL enables collaborative learning while keeping data local to each vehicle, enhancing privacy.
116   However, existing FL-based trust models often lack robustness against malicious participants
117   and do not provide formal guarantees on the security of the aggregation process (6). VFL
118   extends traditional FL by incorporating mechanisms to verify the correctness of model
119   updates contributed by individual clients (14). Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) have been
120   widely used in VFL to enable verification without revealing the actual model updates (5; 24).
121   However, existing VFL approaches primarily focus on general machine learning tasks and
122   may not be optimized for the specific challenges of trust model building in CAVs, such as
123
      handling heterogeneous data distributions, ensuring robustness against specific attacks (e.g.,
      Sybil attacks), and incorporating real-time validation of data like inter-vehicle distances.
124
125   SMPC has been utilized in various domains for privacy-preserving data aggregation and
126   analysis (7). In the context of CAVs, SMPC has been proposed for secure intersection
127   management (17) and cooperative perception (13), while (9) proposed secret sharing in
128   federated learning, the threat model is different where the authors simplify assumption of
129
      considering an "honest but curious" node instead of a malicious one. We target real-time
      validation of malicious data shared among vehicles leveraging blockchain or distributed ledger
130
      for tasks that are time sensitive, such as navigating safely around each other. Nevertheless,
131
      traditional SMPC based approaches suffer from their complexity and overhead making them
132   unsuitable for real-time safety-critical applications where split-second decision- making is
133   paramount on resource constrained CAVs, thus, facing a trade-off between achieving privacy
134   vs. utility at scale.
135
      We propose a novel framework with lightweight addition-based SMPC integrated with
136
      VFL as a secure consensus-based trajectory validation mechanism to achieve decentralized,
137
      privacy-preserving, and robust trust establishment. This is critical for the safety of road
138   users, where wrong trajectory estimation could have fatal consequences, specially on VRUs.
139   One of the biggest challenge currently to achieve real-time requirements is to reduce the
140   time in computing the functions used in SMPC. The use of addition-based SMPC is to
141   leverage and show a simple (linear) function for nodes to compute in order to comply
142   to real-time requirements of the application towards a lightweight validation. However,
143   Secure-FLOATING is not limited to an additive function and a more complicated function
144   could be used with different time requirements depending on the complexity of function,
145   where the time it takes to run the function is proportional to its complexity. There exist no
146
      paper in the literature to address real-time CAVs data validation, critical for time-sensitive
      safety applications for CAV. Secure-FLOATING is the first trust model that goes beyond
147
      traditional offline cyber attack detection solutions in CAVs with simple assumptions and
148
      threat models and instead targets real-time malicious data validation in a scalable manner,
149   evaluated on realistic trajectory data involving 8, 000 connected vehicles in New York City.

      3.1   System Overview
155
156   The system model considers a set V = {v1 , v2 , ...vn } representing nodes (i.e. CAVs, micro-
157   mobility sources, pedestrians with smartphones, etc. ) in the wireless communication range
158   of each other. The communication range can differ depending on the underlying wireless
159   communication technology. For ease of implementation, the communication model between
160   nodes we assume considers but not limited to wireless short range communication technologies
161   such as the 2.4/5GHz IEEE 802.11 standard or 5.9 GHz Cellular Vehicle-to-Everything
      (C-V2X) recently adapted by US DOT for V2X communication.

170   Threat Model
171
172
      The threat model we consider is compromised node v’s trajectory data Cv (l, t), resulting
      in either the node generating fake or malicious trajectories that when shared with nearby
173
      vehicles for real-time navigation decisions could lead to deadly consequences. To address
174
      this, we define a blockchain based zero trust protocol for nearby nodes to verify trajectory
175   data shared by nearby nodes and endorse each others trajectories at regular time intervals
176   on a distributed ledger, where the frequency at which they are endorsed depending on
177   the application and resource consumption requirements, however, we consider it to be less
178   frequent in order to conserve the computing and communication resources on the nodes.
179
      The blockchain model we consider is a permissioned blockchain where nodes are authenticated
180
      prior joining the network using state of the art public/private key based authentication or a
181   similar encryption mechanism, however, developing such system is out of the scope of the
182   paper. We employ the Inter Planetary File System (IPFS) (2), a distributed storage system
183   for the nodes to store the endorsement information on an immutable distributed ledger.
184   Interested readers can refer to (10) (11) for Reinforcement Learning (RL) based optimized
185   frequency of endorsements and updates on the distributed ledger or blockchain.

      We consider all nodes participating in the SMPC based VFL approach employ the same
217   model to ensure consistency in representing model updates, however the exact prediction
218   model the nodes agree to use could vary since an application could require different methods
219   of predicting trajectories of nearby nodes. For instance, a vehicle can use GPS and/or other
220   sensors data (LiDARs, cameras, etc.) to observe neighboring node and predict its next
221   coordinates. Similarly, a node could use part of the currently shared trajectories or the
222   history of the previously shared trajectories to predict what the future trajectories would be
223   for a given node in its surroundings. We leave the choice of the prediction algorithm open,
224   as the success of Secure-FLOATING is independent of the underlying prediction algorithm,
225
      though we prefer simple models compared to resource intensive ones.

      Addition-based SMPC
227
228   To better understand the above SMPC protocol for model updates sharing, we describe a toy
229   problem for a simple addition based secure multi-party computation function below. Consider
230   three nodes, v1 , v2 , v3 , with reference distances between v1 d(v1 , v1 ) = 0, d(v1 , v2 ) = 5.5 and
231   d(v1 , v3 ) = 4.5, where v1 is the consensus initialization node. The addition-based encryption
232   in node v1 splits 0 randomly into −1,1 and 0. It keeps −1 and sends 1 to v2 and 0 to v3
233   respectively. Similarly v2 splits its distance 5.5 randomly into 2,3 and 0.5. It sends 2 to v1
234   keeps 3 at v2 and sends 0.5 to v3 . Node v3 randomly generates 5, −1 and 0.5, sends 5 to v1 ,
235
      −1 to v2 and keeps 0.5. The node v1 computes the sum of −1, 2, and 5 as 6, v2 computes
      the sum of 1, 3, and −1 as 2, and v3 computes the sum of 0, 0.5, 0.5 as 1. The three nodes
236
      exchange each other’s computed sum resulting in a total sum of (6 + 2 + 1 = 10), with a
237
      function to compute mean value of 3.33. The nodes then exchange the mean value among
238   themselves, where a match in values at each node would serve as validation of the transaction.
239   In case any of these value differ at the nodes, the consensus process is considered void and
240   the corresponding nodes are untrustworthy.
241
      The above toy problem uses an addition-based function, however Secure-FLOATING will
242
      work with any function computed and matched among peers, and therefore we leave the
243
      choice of the function open to the specific application/ use case where simple functions are
244   preferred to reduce complexity.

246   3.3   Endorsement on Distributed Ledger
247
248   The workflow for the endorsement process via consensus is summarized as follows:
249
      Step 1. Each node exchanges future trajectory Cv with neighboring nodes in its wireless
250   communication range.
251
252   Step 2. It employs a lightweight federated learning model to predict future trajectories for
253
      each of its neighboring nodes by levering either the on-board sensors, or the trajectories shared
      by the neighbor using the global model gradients (θglobal ) retrieved from the distributed
254
      ledger.
255
256   Step 3. It compares the predicted trajectories with the ones shared by the neighbor vj
257   between two time instants or the interval ∆t = [t0 , tl ], and if the difference is within an
258   acceptable threshold ω, it endorses the truthfulness of the corresponding neighboring node.
259

260                                             Cvj (l, t)[shared] − Cvj (l, t)[predicted] ≤ ω
                          |∆t|
261                              ∆t∈[t0 ,tl ]

      Step 4. It splits the model updates (θvi ) using SMPC and exchanges shares of it with
263   neighbors running the same federated learning model.
264
265   Step 5. It combines the shares of the model updates received from neighboring nodes and
266
      updates the distributed ledger (IPFS) with the global model along the node IDs of the nodes
      for which it endorsed the respective trajectories.
267
268   Step 6. To reach consensus, the aggregated model updates from nodes for whom the majority
269   of neighbors (51%) endorsed the trajectories is updated as the global model on the distributed
      ledger along storing the respective node IDs.

293   5   Experimental Evaluation

295
      Dataset
296
297   To evaluate the effectiveness and practicality of the proposed Secure-FLOATING platform,
298   we used realistic trajectories generated from real nodes in the New York City connected
299   vehicles pilot project (21) comprising of around 8, 000 vehicles as our training data. An
300   open-source microscopic traffic simulation software, SUMO (12), and its internal extension
301   API, Traffic Control Interface (TraCI) (22) are used to generate trajectories. SUMO can
302   simulate intricate and realistic vehicular driving behavior as well as traffic performance,
303
      while TraCI enables online interaction with the primary simulation by connecting external
      applications to SUMO via sockets. The studied area encompasses a 1.6-mile stretch on
304
      Flatbush Avenue between Tillary Street and Army Plaza, recognized as one of the US DOT
305
      Connected Vehicle Pilot Program test sites located in Brooklyn, New York. The simulation
306   parameters, such as acceleration rate, minimum gap, reaction time, distribution of different
307   types of vehicles, and spatial and temporal features of local traffic flow, are regarded as
308   random variables. They are calibrated as probability distributions founded on real trajectory
309   data extracted from drone and CCTV camera videos, as well as actual local traffic data.
310
      We considered the area between Fulton Street and DeKalb Avenue to analyze high traffic
311
      volume and capture significant urban driving behavior. The simulation time is divided
312   into 72 segments, each consisting of 100-second intervals for a total of two hours duration.
313   Mobility data for the heterogeneous set of nodes (comprising passenger cars, trucks, buses,
314   e-bikes) are generated for the selected road section and time duration.
315
      The trajectory data from SUMO is fed into an open source network simulator (NS3) to
316
      implement the wireless network among nodes, where different network configurations are
317
      defined to account for the number of neighbors in each other’s wireless communication range
318   based on their coordinates. We consider the communication range of 100 meters to define the
319   neighbors for a node, i.e. nodes with coordinates that are within 100 meters are considered
320   neighbors, however we varied the range from 50-300 meters to understand potential impacts
321   and uncertainty from the wireless communication medium. The temporal neighbors are
322   defined as the nodes that are within this communication range for at least 5 seconds. The
323   rationale for this is to ensure nodes stay in each others communication range for a sufficient
      amount of time to complete the endorsement process.

      Baseline
325
326   Secure-FLOATING is not limited to a particular model and different machine learning
327   models can be used for trajectory prediction. Although we recommend using lightweight
328   models for their low computational complexity and lower delays, we compared an array of
329   models, considering simple models such as vanilla RNN, LSTM, GRU, as well as complex
330   models such as the Transformer (Informer (27)) and image based Object Detection with
331   Assistance (ODA) models such as YOLO (4) for nodes to predict trajectories of nearby nodes
332
      to evaluate a variety of techniques for trajectory prediction, where nodes run each model
      with and without SMPC based VFL to highlight its benefits.
333
334   For the network overhead of message exchanges between node, we configured message sizes
335   of 1kb and 100kb to be exchanged between nodes at different frequencies, notably, every 1
336   second, 10 seconds and 1 minutes respectively. Secure-FLOATING can also adapt RL-based
337   adaptive approach (11) to the message exchanges for consensus, however, to demonstrate
338
      the low complexity, we are using fixed message sizes and frequency of exchanges between
      nodes endorsing each other’s trajectories. For a similar reason, we considered four network
339
      configuration, neighborhoods of 5, 10, 30 and 50 nodes endorsing each others trajectories
340
      as we believe increasing the number of nodes endorsing each other will further increase the
341   complexity.
342

      Metrics
344
345   The key metrics we consider to evaluate all models in Secure-FLOATING are Mean Absolute
346   Error, training time, accuracy in correct neighbors trajectory prediction, Floating Operations
347   Per Second (FLOPs) and CPU utilization. The metric used for scalability analysis is
348   the overhead computed with the increase in the network size, with varying message size
349   (1kb,100kb) and exchange frequency (1s,10s,1min) as described above.
350   All data preparation and model runs are conducted on an Intel(R) Core(TM) i7-7700HQ
351   CPU @ 2.80GHz Processor with 16 GB of installed RAM. The training and test data as well
352   as models are optimized to train on a single NVIDIA 1670 GPU with 8 GB of VRAM made
353   possible with PyTorch’s CUDA toolkit version 11.8. All model runs are conducted with a
354   Windows 10 operating system utilizing Python version 3.6. The machine learning framework
355   used is PyTorch 1.13.1 with CUDA version 11.6.

      Ethical Considerations
467
468   While Secure-FLOATING aims to enhance privacy and security, it is essential to consider
469   the broader societal implications and potential risks associated with this technology. De-
470   centralized trust models, like any machine learning system, can inadvertently perpetuate or
471   amplify existing biases in the data. This could lead to discriminatory outcomes, where certain
472   vehicles or groups of vehicles are unfairly penalized or favored based on factors like location,
473   manufacturer, or driving patterns. To mitigate this, it is crucial to carefully design the trust
474
      model’s learning algorithms, incorporate fairness constraints, and regularly audit the model
      for biases. In the event of an accident or malfunction, determining liability and responsibility
475
      in a decentralized trust system can be complex. It is crucial to establish clear guidelines and
476
      legal frameworks that address these issues, balancing the autonomy of individual nodes with
477   the overall safety and accountability of the network. While our framework aims to enhance
478   security through VFL and SMPC, it is essential to acknowledge that no system is completely
479   immune to attacks. Continuous monitoring, vulnerability assessment, and the development
480   of robust defense mechanisms are necessary to maintain the security and safety of the trust
481   model.
482
483   Limitations and Future Work
484
485   While Secure-FLOATING show promising results, several limitations and potential avenues
      for future research warrant consideration. While we have demonstrated linear scalability, the

486
      communication overhead of the federated learning process itself could still be a bottleneck
487   in very large-scale networks. Further research is needed to explore more communication-
488   efficient VFL algorithms or techniques for reducing the frequency of model updates. The
489   heterogeneity of data across different vehicles (e.g., varying sensor quality, driving patterns,
490   and environmental conditions) can pose challenges for the convergence and generalization
491   of the federated trust model. Techniques like personalized federated learning or model
492   adaptation will be investigated to address this issue. While our framework incorporates
493   several defense mechanisms against Byzantine faults and data poisoning attacks, it is essential
494   to continuously evaluate its robustness against new and evolving attack strategies. Research
495
      into more sophisticated anomaly detection techniques and robust aggregation methods could
      further enhance the system’s security.
496
497   Secure-FLOATING is not limited to a specific model or hardware or use-case but is designed
498   for broader applications. CAVs research is gaining momentum and the need for such
499   integrated solutions is important to accommodate real-time trust establishment on CAVs
500   at scale, validate the fidelity of new multi-modal sensors (LiDARs, high-res cameras), V2X
501
      communication technologies (C-V2X, mmWave, THz), models (beyond transformers), and
      privacy-preserving solutions towards ensuring safety of road users. Multiple hospitals can
502
      collaborate and train models in a federated learning manner for disease diagnosis without
503
      sharing patient data directly. VFL could ensure the integrity of model updates with
504   privacy-awareness, while SMPC enabling secure aggregation towards a consensus among
505   them to validate diagnoses in real-time, constantly improving accuracy on different diseases
506   diagnosis. Similarly, pharmaceutical companies can perform clinical trials across multiple
507   institutions for real-time data validation while preserving patient privacy and data integrity.
508   Financial institutions can collaborate and train models for fraud detection without sharing
509   sensitive customer transaction data. VFL and SMPC could ensure privacy and security,
510   while consensus could be used to validate transactions in real-time, towards faster and more
511   accurate fraud detection.
512   The current trust model is primarily focused on accuracy and robustness. However, it is
513   crucial to develop SMPC-based trust models that are also interpretable and explainable.
514   This would allow human operators or regulators to understand the reasoning behind the
515   trust scores and intervene if necessary. The design and implementation of effective incentives
516   that encourage honest participation and deter malicious behavior remain an open research
517   problem. We are actively exploring solutions that leverage reinforcement Q-learning within
518
      a federated learning framework to design effective incentive schemes and personalize the
      system’s behavior based on individual node constraints and data characteristics. Deploying
519
      Secure-FLOATING in a real-world network would involve additional challenges, such as
520
      dealing with intermittent connectivity, varying network topologies, and legal and regulatory
521   considerations. These aspects would need to be carefully addressed in future research.
522
523   In addition to the above-mentioned research directions, we will explore more efficient VFL
524
      algorithms and investigate incentive mechanisms to encourage wider participation in the
      decentralized trust network. Ultimately, our goal is to pave the way for the widespread
525
      adoption of CAVs, ensuring that they operate in a secure, private, and cooperative manner.

531
      We proposed Secure-FLOATING, a novel framework for decentralized trust establishment
      in real-time for CAVs and other nodes navigating around each other. We propose (1) a
532
      tailored VFL algorithm that preserves privacy while ensuring the integrity of model updates
533
      through zero-knowledge proofs, (2) SMPC protocol for secure model aggregation with rigorous
534   privacy guarantees, and (3) a scalable and secure blockchain based consensus mechanism for
535   trajectory validation with provable linear complexity making it not only theoretically sound,
536   as demonstrated by formal proofs for privacy, security, and scalability, but also practically
537   effective, as evidenced by our extensive evaluation based on real-world data from New York
538   City. Results show that Secure-FLOATING is an efficient trust model in terms of accuracy,
539   privacy preservation, robustness, and scalability and represents a significant step towards
      the development of trustworthy and reliable autonomous transportation systems.
