011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Neural networks (NNs) have shown promise in solving constrained optimization
               problems in real-time. However, ensuring that NN-generated solutions strictly ad-
014
               here to constraints is challenging due to NN prediction errors. Recent methods have
015
               achieved feasibility guarantees over ball-homeomorphic sets with low complexity
016            and bounded optimality loss, yet extending these guarantees to more general sets
017            remains largely open. In this paper, we develop Bisection Projection, an efficient
018            approach to ensure NN solution feasibility for optimization over general compact
019            sets with non-empty interiors, irrespective of their ball-homeomorphic properties.
020            Our method begins by identifying multiple interior points (IPs) within the constraint
021            set, chosen based on their eccentricity modulated by the NN infeasibility region.
022            We utilize another unsupervised-trained NN (called IPNN) to map inputs to these
023            interior points, thereby reducing the complexity of computing these IPs in run-time.
024
               For NN solutions initially deemed infeasible, we apply a bisection procedure that
               adjusts these solutions towards the identified interior points, ensuring feasibility
025
               with minor projection-induced optimality loss. We prove the feasibility guarantee
026
               and bound the optimality loss of our approach under mild conditions. Extensive
027            simulations, including non-convex optimal power flow problems in large-scale
028            networks, demonstrate that bisection projection outperforms existing methods in
029            solution feasibility and computational efficiency with comparable optimality losses.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033   Constrained Optimization (CO) plays an essential role in various engineering fields, such as supply
034   chain management, transportation, and power systems. To solve CO problems, iterative algorithms,
035   such as interior point methods, have been developed and embedded within commercial solvers like
036   Gurobi. These tools are designed to tackle CO with high precision, providing exact or approximated
037   solutions. However, they can be slow for real-time applications with tight time constraints.
038   Recent advancements in machine learning (ML) have introduced innovative strategies for solving
039   real-time CO problems, including the end-to-end (E2E) solution mapping (Amo22), the learning-to-
040   optimize (L2O) iterative scheme (CCC+ 21), and hybrid approaches (KFVHW21). One powerful
041   idea is to leverage the universal approximation ability of neural networks (NNs) (HSW89; LLPS93)
042   to predict high-quality solutions given input parameters, significantly reducing computation time
043   compared to traditional iterative solvers. For instance, by employing this idea, NNs have been trained
044
      to solve critical optimal power flow problems in modern grid operations, achieving a 2-4 orders of
      magnitude speedup over iterative solvers (PZC19; GWWM19; PZCZ20; FMVH20; ZB20; DRK20).
045
046   Despite the minor optimality loss and significant speedup of NN-based methods, guaranteeing the
047   feasibility of NN solutions with respect to problem constraints remains a challenge due to inherent NN
048   prediction errors. While recent advances have managed to ensure NN solution feasibility within ball-
049   homeomorphic sets with low complexity and bounded optimality loss (LCL23; LCL24), establishing
050
      similar guarantees for general sets remains an open problem. A discussion on related works is
      provided in Section 2, and a summary is presented in Table 1.
051
052   In this paper, we develop Bisection Projection (BP) as an efficient scheme to recover infeasible
053   NN solutions with bounded optimality loss, with respect to general compact sets beyond ball-
      homeomorphic ones under mild conditions. Our main contributions are as follows:

065   ▷ In Sec. 6, we carry out extensive experiments over convex and non-covnex problems to evaluate the

068   To our knowledge, this is the first work to guarantee NN solution feasibility over general compact
069   sets with bounded optimality loss and low run-time complexity under mild conditions.

072   2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074
      Recently, ML-driven optimization has been an active research field (PZC19; KFVHW21; CCC+ 21;
075
      Amo22). A core challenge is ensuring the NN prediction feasibility over input-dependent constraints.
076
      Researchers have developed various methods to enhance solution feasibility, summarized in Table 1.
077
078   Equality constraints. Linear equations and certain non-linear equations with constant ranks, can be
079   embedded as neural network layers by predicting a subset of variables and solving for the remaining
080
      variables to satisfy the equality constraints (Aba69; Lee13; PZC19; DRK20; LCL23; DWDS23).
081   Projection approach. To enforce solution feasibility, orthogonal/L2 projection is often employed.
082   However, solving the projection problem either by iterative solver or equivalent optimization lay-
083
      ers (AK17; AAB+ 19; CDB+ 21; WZG+ 23) is computationally intensive in real-time. Alternative
      strategies include gradient-based methods (e.g., DC3 (DRK20)) and L2O models that mimic iter-
084
      ative projection procedures to adjust infeasible solutions (HWFGY21; HFL+ 22). However, those
085
      projection-analogous approaches do not guarantee feasibility for general input-adaptive constraints.
086
      Warm-start approach. The NN predictions can serve as warm-start points for iterative solvers,
087
      potentially reducing the number of iterations required to reach the optimal solution (Die19; Bak19;
088
      SHAS23; SHAS24). However, it may still be computationally intensive for real-time operations.
089
      Penalty approach. To reduce constraint violations in predicted solutions, researchers have incorpo-
090
      rated various penalty functions, such as quadratic penalties, into the NN loss function (COMB19;
091
      PZCZ20; ZB20; FMVH20). Additionally, integration of the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) condi-
092   tions as equality constraints has been explored to refine NN performance (NC21b; NC21a; ZCZ21).
093   However, these methods do not consistently ensure solution feasibility due to NN prediction errors.
094
      Sampling approach. To guarantee feasibility, an inner approximation of the original constraint
095   set can be constructed. For linear constraints, a convex combination of sampled vertexes and rays
096   is adopted to ensure feasibility (FNC20; ZSRZ21). For general compact but fixed constraint sets,
097   sampling-based methods are theoretically studied in (KZLD21). However, scalability remains a
098   challenge due to the exponential growth in required samples with increasing problem dimensionality.
099   Preventive learning. a Preventive Learning framework is proposed for ensuring linear constraint
100   feasibility in (ZPC+ 20; ZPCL23). It first adjusts inequality constraints to account for NN prediction
101   errors. Subsequently, it trains the NN using mixed-integer programming techniques to limit the worst-
102   case prediction error. However, it lacks an optimality guarantee. Additionally, other NN verification
103   techniques can also be applied to assess the worst-case performance (VQLC20; uAYKJ22; LAL+ 21).
104   Gauge function. These works utilize gauge functions (BM08) to constrain the NN. A closed-form
105   bijection, known as gauge mapping, between a hypercube and a polytope is used to bound the
106   NN output within the polytope (TZ22a; TZ22b; LKM23). For fixed convex constraints, RAYEN
107   and several works apply analytic expressions for gauge functions to find feasible boundary solu-
      tions (THH23; KU23; LM23). However, these approaches only work for specific convex sets.

      Homeomorphic Projection has been proposed to ensure the NN solution feasibility over ball-
      homeomorphic constraint (LCL23; LCL24). It applies invertible NN to construct a homeomorphism
122
      between the constraint set and a unit ball, such that the projection operation can be efficiently
123
      conducted over the ball through simple bisection. Nevertheless, the ball-homeomorphism assumption
124   limits its application for optimization problems over general constraint sets.
125
126   In summary, existing works either incur high run-time complexity or have limited applicable con-
127
      straints. In this work, we propose Bisection Projection as an efficient scheme to guarantee NN solution
      feasibility over (fairly) general compact sets with bounded optimality loss under mild conditions.

169

173   denoted as IPNN, to obtain IPs fast in run-time.

391   5.3   D ISCUSSIONS
392
393   IPNN training complexity. As discussed in Sec. 4.3, IPNN training requires sampling over
394   (local) boundary Γθ to evaluate the eccentricity. Although accurately approximating the eccentricity
395   over general compact sets in the worst case requires a covering dataset (Zho02), we use stochastic
396   training by sampling a small batch of boundary points at each iteration and optimizing the smoothed
397
      eccentricity loss. Our numerical studies show that this approach guides IPNN in finding IPs with low
      eccentricity, leading to minor bisection-induced optimality loss.
398
399   IPNN sampling complexity. To obtain boundary samples, we apply the bisection projection in
400   Alg. 1 with runtime complexity O(KG), where K is the number of bisection steps and G is the
401   complexity of checking constraint satisfaction. This algorithm provides linear convergence to find
402   boundary samples and can be applied to a batch of infeasible predictions, enabling low-complexity
403
      IPNN training, as demonstrated in Sec. 6.2. The detailed pseudocode for training and sampling is
      provided in Appendix G.3.
404
405   IPNN convergence. Despite the empirical success of training the IPNN to be valid over the training
406   dataset, a premise on the generalization condition in Theorem 1, we remark that the theoretical
407   convergence guarantee on IPNN training is non-trivial, similar to regular NN training, which largely
408   depends on the optimizer, data coverage, and NN initialization, and requires future exploration.
409   Impact of number of IPs. As we used multiple IPs to reduce the bisection-induced projection
410   distance, we analyzed the trade-off between the projection distance and the number of used IPs.

      open and warrants future exploration.

      7       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
529   We introduce the Bisection Projection framework, an efficient scheme to recover feasible solutions
530   from infeasible NN predictions over a general compact constraint set through simple bisection
531   operation and with minor optimality loss. Extensive experiments in this paper demonstrate the
532   general applicability and efficiency of the approach beyond existing methods.
533
      Meanwhile, there are several limitations of the framework that can be viewed as important future
534   directions, as discussed in Sec. 5.3. (i) In addition to the covering-based upper bound in Prop.
535   5.1, the lower bound of the number of required IPs for specific sets is useful and subject to further
536   investigation. (ii) The convergence guarantee for training IPNN to learn feasible solutions over
537   the training dataset under the overparameterized NN framework will also be an interesting future
538   direction. (iii) The two sufficient conditions in Theorem 1 do not universally work for arbitrary
539   constraint sets; more general and computationally efficient conditions should be explored in the
      future.

1000
1001
       To train the IPNN, we minimize the loss function defined in (6), which incorporates an adversarial
       penalty term and a smoothed eccentricity term, as delineated in Alg. 2. The IPNN is initialized
1002
       randomly with m IP predictors. During the training process, Gaussian noise is introduced to perturb
1003
       the IP predictions, and the penalty term is computed to ensure the identification of valid IPs. For
1004   IPs deemed valid, the smoothed eccentricity is calculated using boundary points sampled from a
1005   specified region of interest. We consider the following training scenarios with different boundary
1006   sampling approaches.
1007
1008   D.1    O BJECTIVE - AWARE TRAINING
1009
1010   In this scenario, we are provided with a trained NN predictor F (·) or a dataset containing optimal
1011   solutions x∗θ under various inputs. Our objective is to identify IPs with minimized eccentricity in a
1012   local region of the boundary. As illustrated in Alg. 3, we initially sample an infeasible solution by
1013   introducing noise to the NN prediction or the optimal solution; subsequently, we apply the bisection
1014
       projection described in Alg. 1 to determine the projected feasible solution in the local boundary.
1015   This training scheme emphasizes the local region surrounding the optimal solution, taking into
1016   account the objective function. Consequently, it has the potential to yield a smaller eccentricity and
1017   overall optimality gap.
1018
