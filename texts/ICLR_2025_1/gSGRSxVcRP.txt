009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks often learn similar internal representations, both across
012            different models and within their own layers. While inter-network similarities have
013            enabled techniques such as model stitching and merging, intra-network similarities
014            present new opportunities for designing more efficient architectures. In this paper,
015            we investigate the emergence of these internal similarities across different layers in
016            diverse neural architectures, showing that similarity patterns emerge independently
017            of the datataset used. We introduce a simple metric, Block Redundancy, to detect
018
               redundant blocks, providing a foundation for future architectural optimization
               methods. Building on this, we propose Redundant Blocks Approximation (RBA),
019
               a general framework that identifies and approximates one or more redundant com-
020
               putational blocks using simpler transformations. We show that the transformation
021            T between two representations can be efficiently computed in closed-form, and
022            it is enough to replace the redundant blocks from the network. RBA reduces
023            model parameters and time complexity while maintaining good performance. We
024            validate our method on classification tasks in the vision domain, using a variety of
025            pretrained foundational models and datasets.

039
      As Neural Networks (NNs) grow in size and complexity, their demand for computational resources
040
      has become a significant bottleneck. Despite the impressive performance of large models, they often
041
      come with substantial trade-offs, such as slower inference times and increased memory and power
042   consumption. This has led to a growing interest in methods that can reduce model complexity without
043   sacrificing performance. However, most approaches to mitigating these challenges either require
044   additional training or complex fine-tuning, or they result in a non-trivial loss in performance. However,
045   recent research showed that there exists internal representation similarities within and between NNs.
046   Thus, many layers or components within these networks may perform similar functions or yield
047   highly correlated outputs, suggesting the potential for simplifying these networks. Understanding
048   and leveraging these internal similarities can open up new opportunities for reducing model size,
049   enhancing inference speed, and improving computational efficiency.
050   In this paper, we address two key research questions: (i) how to identify redundant blocks, and (ii) how
051   to effectively approximate these blocks while preserving the final representations and the network’s
052   overall functionality. To address the first question, we introduce a straightforward metric, the Block
053   Redundancy (BR) score, which helps identifying components that do not contribute significantly to
      the network’s final representation. By carefully selecting which blocks to approximate, we can ensure


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      minimal impact on the network’s final output. For the second question, we propose the Redundant
055   Blocks Approximation (RBA), a novel method that leverages internal representation similarities
056   to approximate redundant computational blocks using lightweight transformations, such as linear
057   mappings. Once the blocks that have minimal impact on model functionality are identified, instead of
058   using these redundant blocks in each forward pass (e.g., transformer blocks containing attention and
059   normalization operations), RBA completely replaces them with a simpler transformation. Thanks to
060   this approximation, RBA reduces model parameters and accelerates inference while maintaining the
061   integrity of the final representation produced by the original model.
062   Our main contributions are as follows:
063
064          • We provide a comprehensive analysis of internal representation similarities across various
065            pretrained foundation models, revealing consistent patterns between blocks within each
066            architecture, independent of the dataset (Figures 2 and 8 to 9).
067          • We show that a simple metric such as the MSE is enough for assessing the redundancy of
068            individual blocks within a NN (Figure 3).
069          • We introduce RBA, a general framework for identifying and approximating redundant
070            computational blocks in NNs using simpler transformations (e.g., linear), reducing model
071            parameters and complexity with minimal to no impact on the produced representations
072            (Figure 1).
073          • We validate our method on vision-based classification tasks using diverse pretrained models
074            and datasets, demonstrating its applicability and effectiveness across different architectures
075            and datasets (Tables 1, 7 and 8).

077   2    R ELATED WORK
078
079   Measuring Similarities. A range of metrics have been introduced to assess the similarity between
080   latent spaces generated by different NNs Klabunde et al. (2023); Ballester et al. (2023). One
081   established approach is Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) (Hotelling, 1992), known for its
082   invariance to linear transformations. Variants of CCA, such Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) and
083   Singular Value CCA (SVCCA) (Raghu et al., 2017), aim to enhance robustness, while techniques like
084   Projection Weighted CCA (PWCCA) (Morcos et al., 2018) mitigate sensitivity to small perturbations.
085   Another widely used metric, Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) (Kornblith et al., 2019), captures
086   the similarity between latent spaces while ignoring orthogonal transformations. However, recent
087
      work (Davari et al., 2022) highlights that this metric can be sensitive to shifts in the latent space.
      Additionally, Barannikov et al. (2021) proposes a method to compare two data representations
088
      by measuring the multi-scale topological dissimilarity, while Fumero et al. (2024) leverages the
089
      principles of spectral geometry to model and analyze the relationships between distinct latent spaces.
090
091   Leveraging Similarities. Analyzing the similarities between internal representations, both within
092   and across NNs, has received significant attention in recent research. Valeriani et al. (2024) examines
093
      the intrinsic dimensions and neighbor compositions of representations in various transformer models.
      Similarly, Kvinge et al. (2022) explores how models process different variations of data points across
094
      layers, while Nguyen et al. (2020) investigates how changes in network depth and width impact hidden
095
      representations, revealing characteristic block structures. Finally, Crisostomi et al. (2023) investigates
096   under what assumptions two latent spaces be merged into one. All these insights have been applied
097   across various contexts. Moschella et al. (2023) constructs a unified space shared by different NNs,
098   enabling zero-shot stitching of independently trained models across different modalities Norelli
099   et al. (2023), even without explicit assumptions on the transformation class that connects the latent
100   manifold embeddings Cannistraci et al. (2024) or with partial correspondence within the latent spaces
101   Cannistraci et al. (2023). While Ricciardi et al. (2023) proves the feasibility of zero-shot stitching
102   between encoders and policies trained on different environmental variations. Other works Lähner &
103   Moeller (2024); Maiorca et al. (2024) demonstrate that representations learned by distinct NNs can
104
      be aligned using simple transformations. Finally, Tang et al. (2023) leverages similarities in unified
      visual-language models to dynamically skip layers in both encoders and decoders.
105
106   Architectural Efficiency. While large-scale models with billions or even trillions of parameters
107   continue to achieve state-of-the-art performance, their growth comes with trade-offs, including
      slower inference times and significantly higher computational costs. To address these issues, various


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      techniques have been developed, such as early exiting and model pruning. Early exit strategies, which
109   introduce intermediate output layers at different stages of the network, have been shown to improve
110   efficiency and reduce inference time (Xin et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022). However,
111   this approach requires the additional training of intermediate classifiers to enable exits at predefined
112   layers. On the other hand, model pruning reduces the computational load of Deep Neural Network
113   (DNN) by either removing individual weights based on certain criteria (Ma et al., 2023; Liao et al.,
114   2023) or eliminating or compressing larger structural components such as channels or attention heads
115   (Zhang & He, 2020; Sajjad et al., 2023; Venkataramanan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Bai et al.,
116   2023). Although effective, this approach usually requires first training the full model in its dense
117
      form, followed by multiple iterations of pruning and retraining or training the pruned model from
      scratch.
118
119   Instead of removing layers or components, we focus on identifying redundant computational blocks
120   within the network and replacing them with lightweight transformations. Unlike other approaches,
121   RBA is an architecture-agnostic method to reduce model complexity and computational overhead
122   without the need for additional training or fine-tuning while still maintaining competitive perfor-
123
      mance.

125   3    R EDUNDANT B LOCKS A PPROXIMATION
126
127   The core principle of our approach, RBA, is to detect similar representations within NNs, identifying
128   redundant blocks, and approximate them with simpler transformations instead of executing the entire
129   DNN. A visual overview is provided in Figure 1.
130   In this section, we first show how to identify redundant blocks, and how to effectively approximate
131   their representations while preserving the network’s overall functionality.
132
      Identifying Redundant Representations. We hypothesize that certain foundation model archi-
133
      tectures, such as Vision Transformers (ViTs), may contain redundant blocks that produce similar
134   representations. This redundancy may stem from overparameterization or task-specific characteristics.
135   In this context, a ”block” refers to a self-contained unit in the model that typically contains several
136   layers, such as self-attention, normalization, or feed-forward layers, but functions as a cohesive unit.
137
      To quantify redundancy, we introduce a simple metric called Block Redundancy (BR), which measures
138
      the degree of change in internal representations between blocks. This helps to identify essential
139
      blocks versus those that contribute minimally to the overall model.
140
141   Let B represent the total number of blocks in the model, and let h(b) denote the internal representation
142   (i.e., the output) of block b, where b ∈ 1, 2, . . . , B. For a given subset of the training data Dsub , we
143   compute the representations h(b) (x) for each input x ∈ Dsub . The BR for block b is defined as the
144   negative Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the output representations of blocks b and b − 1:
                                               1      X                              2
145
                                 BR(b) = −                     h(b) (x) − h(b−1) (x)                           (1)
146                                          |Dsub |                                 2
                                                     x∈Dsub
147
      A higher BR(b) indicates a minimal change between the outputs of block b and the preceding block
148
      b − 1, suggesting a potential redundancy in block b. Conversely, a lower BR(b) implies that block b
149   plays a significant role in transforming the internal representations.
150
151   By systematically evaluating the BR for each block, we can identify redundant components that can
152
      be simplified, enabling a reduction in the NN’s complexity without compromising the original final
      representation or its performance.
153
154   Approximating Redundant Blocks. After identifying redundant representations using BR, the next
155   step is to approximate their outputs through more computationally efficient transformations, rather
156   than directly removing the blocks. While this approach applies to consecutive blocks such as bi and
157   bi+1 , it generalizes naturally to non-consecutive blocks as well. Specifically, for any block bi and
158
      block bi+n (where n ≥ 1), our method enables the approximation of the output of block bi+n from
      the output of block bi , provided they exhibit low BR scores. This allows us to skip the computation
159
      of blocks bi+1 , bi+2 , . . . , bi+n , effectively reducing the overall computation.
160
161   Let X ∈ Rn×d1 represent the output of block bi for a subset of n data points from the training set,
      where d1 is the dimensionality of the latent space. Similarly, let Y ∈ Rn×d2 represent the output of


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      block bi+n for the same subset of data points, with d2 being the dimensionality of the latent space at
163   block bi+n . Our objective is to find a function T : Rd1 → Rd2 such that:
164
165                                                 Y ≈ T (X)
166   In this work, we consider T to be a linear transformation (T) that can be estimated by minimizing
167   the squared error between the transformed output of block bi and the actual output of block bi+n ,
168   which can be solved using least squares:
169
                                           T = arg min∥Y − T (X)∥22
170                                                 T
171
      This optimization problem allows for a closed-form solution that efficiently computes the optimal
172
      transformation T. The solution bypasses the computation of any redundant blocks between bi and
173
      bi+n , replacing them with T. This approximation results in a significant reduction in computational
174   complexity, as one or more full transformer block consisting of multi-head self-attention and feed-
175   forward layers can be replaced by a low-cost linear transformation.
176
177
      To sum up, the overall pipeline of our approach comprises two main stages:
178
            1. Redundancy Identification: We apply the BR metric to identify redundant blocks across
179            the model based on their contribution to the transformation of internal representations.
180
            2. Block Approximation: For blocks deemed redundant, we compute an efficient linear
181
               approximation, using the transformation matrix T to bypass these blocks.
182
183   This process reduces model parameters and computational complexity with minimal impact on the
184   resulting representations, as shown in Figures 3, 4 and 10 to 13. Additionally, it is possible train
185   any downstream linear classifier on top of the simplified model for the desired task, retaining the
186   original architecture’s overall structure while significantly decreasing the number of parameters and
187   computation costs, as shown in Tables 1, 2 and 7 to 9.

      Experimental Setting. In Section 4.1, we empirically demonstrate that different blocks in pretrained
271   models exhibit similarities. To further investigate this, introduce the Block Redundancy metric. As
272   illustrated in Equation (1), this metric measures the level of redundancy of a block: a high score
273   indicates minimal change between two blocks output, suggesting that the second block may be
274   redundant. Conversely, a low score implies that the second block contributes significantly to the final
275   prediction. After identifying redundant blocks, we restructure the models accordingly to reduce their
276   complexity and parameter count. These redundant blocks are approximated using a shared linear
277   transformation applied across all tokens, based on a subset of 3,000 training samples. We compute BR
278   scores for each block across different datasets and pretrained encoders: ViT-S, DiNO-S, DEiT-S,
279
      utilizing MNIST, F-MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. Additionally, we compute the MSE
      between the representations of the last layer in the original model and the RBA model when skipping
280
      the ith block. We also visualize the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) projections of these
281
      representations when specific blocks are approximated to assess the impact on representation fidelity.
282
283   Quantitative Analysis. As illustrated in Figure 3, in most cases, the BR decreases as the block depth
284   increases. This suggests that approximating the final blocks would lead to significant changes in
285
      the final representations, indicating their critical role in maintaining similar final representations.
      However, in the case of DEiT-S, the trend is reversed. Here, the BR is higher in the central blocks and
286
      lower in the initial ones. This is confirmed by the dissimilarity between the last-layer representations,
287
      which increases when the earlier blocks are removed in DEiT-S, whereas the opposite is observed in
288   other models. These findings reinforce the intuition behind the BR metric, demonstrating a correlation
289   between BR and the final representation similarity when approximating blocks. In some instances,
290   such as with the MNIST dataset, the BR scores remain relatively consistent across blocks, indicating
291   that the representations are largely similar one to another. However, for more complex datasets like
292   CIFAR-100, the representations in the final or in the first blocks become increasingly dissimilar,
293   making it advantageous to approximate intermediate blocks. This suggests that the BR metric is
294   influenced not only by the architecture but also by the complexity of the dataset, allowing for targeted
295   approximations that reduce model parameters and complexity without significantly compromising
296
      performance.

429   the results highlighted in violet in the table, demonstrates that approximating four redundant blocks
430   yields better results than approximating three non-redundant blocks. Overall, performance remains
431   similar or improved, demonstrating that a simple linear transformation is sufficient to approximate
      different blocks of a NN, significantly reducing the number of parameters and model complexity.

523   5   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we introduced a novel framework for approximating redundant representations in
526
      transformer-based foundation models and proposed a simple yet effective metric to identify such
527
      redundancies. By leveraging a simple linear transformation, shared across all tokens, between
528   consecutive and non-consecutive blocks output, we demonstrated that it is possible to significantly
529   reduce model parameters and complexity without sacrificing performance, and in some cases even
530   improving it. Our approach provides an efficient way to optimize model architecture, maintaining
531   essential representation fidelity while streamlining the network for downstream tasks.
532
      Limitations and Future Works. While our framework shows promising results, it has been primarily
533
      tested on transformer-based architectures. We leave to future work to explore the application of our
534   framework across different modalities (e.g., text), architectures (e.g., ResNets and AutoEncoders), and
535   downstream tasks (e.g., reconstruction). Additionally, we plan to enhance the representation analysis
536   by incorporating topological metrics, which could provide a different perspective on structural
537   similarities between representations. This alternative viewpoint may uncover new insights into
538   redundancy patterns and further refine our approach. By expanding the framework’s scope, we aim to
539   validate its versatility and continue optimizing model efficiency across a broader set of architectures
      and tasks, advancing its practical applicability in diverse settings.

737   A.2.1     T RANSLATORS
738
739   The first implementation, referred to as the Res-MLP, is composed of two normalization layers and
740   a feedforward submodule. The first layer normalization processes the input tensor, followed by a
741   feedforward submodule comprising a linear transformation, a SiLU activation, a dropout layer, and
742
      a final linear transformation. The output of the feedforward submodule is added to the normalized
      input via a residual connection. This sum is then passed through the second layer normalization to
743
      produce the final output. While the second implementation, referred to as the MLP, is a simplified
744
      MLP that employs a sequential architecture with a first linear transformation that reduces the input
745   dimensionality to half of the target dimension, followed by a GELU activation function for smooth
746   non-linearity, and a final linear transformation that restores the reduced features to match the target
747   dimensionality. Refer to Listings 1 and 2 for the code snipped of the two translators.
748

753                self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(num_features)
754                self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(num_features)
755
                   self.ff = nn.Sequential(
