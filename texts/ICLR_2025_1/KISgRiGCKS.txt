011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
               Federated learning is a subfield of machine learning that avoids sharing local data
014
               with a central server, which can enhance privacy and scalability. The inability
015            to consolidate data in a central server leads to a unique problem called dataset
016            imbalance, which is where agents in a network do not have equal representation
017            of the labels one is trying to learn to predict. In FL, fusing locally-trained mod-
018            els with unbalanced datasets may deteriorate the performance of global model
019            aggregation; this further reduces the quality of updated local models and the ac-
020            curacy of the distributed agents’ decisions. In this work, we introduce an Optimal
021            Transport-based preprocessing algorithm that aligns the datasets by minimizing
022            the distributional discrepancy of data along the edge devices without breaking pri-
023
               vacy concerns. We accomplish this by leveraging Wasserstein barycenters when
               computing channel-wise averages. These barycenters are collected in a trusted
024
               central server where they collectively generate a target RGB space. By projecting
025
               our dataset towards this target space, we minimize the distributional discrepancy
026            on a global level, which facilitates the learning process due to a minimization of
027            variance across the samples in the analyzed network. We demonstrate the capa-
028            bilities of the proposed approach over the CIFAR-10 dataset, where we show its
029            capability of reaching higher degrees of generalization in fewer communication
030            rounds.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Federated learning (FL) is a subfield of machine learning (ML) that tackles decentralized, or dis-
038   tributed, learning. It lends itself as a solution to machine learning problems where datasets cannot
039
      be shared due to privacy concerns (McMahan et al., 2017) and, in turn, enhances scalability. Its
      privacy-centered approach led to its adoption in various fields, such as healthcare (Xu et al., 2021;
040
      Rieke et al., 2020). Another interesting paradigm tackled by FL is the personalization of models
041
      where the expected behavior varies from user to user in the network. In Tan et al. (2022), the authors
042   perform a comprehensive dive over the challenges and use cases of personalized federated learning.
043
044   While FL has a myriad of applications, especially with the increasing regulations on personal data,
045
      its constraints must be carefully handled to ensure the stable training of models. These constraints
      include transmission rates, computational power at the edge, privacy, and dataset imbalance. The
046
      problem of imbalanced datasets, as can be seen in Li et al. (2020), arises when edge devices do not
047
      all have the same number of samples of each predicted class and/or have different training dataset
048   sizes; this can equivalently mean they have different generating distributions. Similarly, the quality
049   of the local data provides another obstacle in this setting (Wu et al., 2023). Both of these issues create
050   imbalanced representations that worsen the learning process. Our goal is to tackle this imbalance
051   problem by looking at it as an alignment of the different local generating distributions. To this end,
052   we design a preprocessing mechanism that is model- and learning algorithm-agnostic and allows for
053   a transformation of the local data to a distribution space comprised of information from all agents in
      the network.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
055
056
057   The issue of dataset imbalance, also known as the domain-distribution discrepancy problem or the
058   multiple-source domain problem, has been a core challenge in federated learning. In this section,
059
      we will review various approaches to address this challenge. To our knowledge, our approach is the
      first of its kind in terms of performing a zero-shot alignment and the second to introduce optimal
060
      transport (OT) to perform a distribution alignment in FL. While it may be interesting to think of
061
      various preprocessing techniques to compare against, to our knowledge, none exist. In our research,
062   we have only seen local alignments and processing techniques such as feature normalization (e.g.,
063   min-max scaling) or standardization (e.g., Z-score scaling). While these techniques are useful to
064   help train models by scaling feature values to desirable ranges, they do not align samples across
065   agents. The lacking literature further supports the importance of our idea.
066
      The most relatable work we have found is that of Farnia et al. Farnia et al. (2022) where the au-
067
      thors introduce an OT-based alignment step during the process of training the model. Their work
068   is comprised of an iterative approach to both computing the projection map and the space they are
069   projecting to. The authors begin by extending the standard OT task between two distributions to
070   a multi-marginal OT problem. They then use these results to create a min-max optimization prob-
071   lem which leads to their algorithm called FedOT. While FedOT introduces a dynamic approach to
072   learning the necessary maps to transform the data, we instead bypass this step. Rather than itera-
073   tively learning a transport map and the target space, we instead compute the target space using a two
074   step approach (described later as an RGB-wise Wasserstein barycenter) and then leverage Ferradans
075   et al. (2014) which demonstrates how to compute the alignment map. Our approach introduces two
076
      benefits. First, by computing the target space in one shot rather than dynamically, we align images
      to a unified representation. Secondly, by simplifying the alignment process, we lower computation
077
      cost by removing the iterative process that is needed to learn the map which is then used to perform
078
      the alignment.
079
080   In Wang et al. (2022), Wang et al. comprehensively surveyed the field of domain adaptation. They
081   describe three primary techniques used to solve this problem: data manipulation, representation
082
      learning, and learning strategy. Out of the diverse list of papers surveyed, few are directly solving
      the problem. First, Chen et al. (2020) focuses on personalization through transfer learning. The
083
      pseudo domain generalization occurs in the transfer step, as the FedHealth algorithm aligns the
084
      lower levels of the convolutional neural network aggregated in the cloud with those locally stored,
085   and personalized, in each agent’s device. On the other hand, in Wu & Gong (2021), Wu and Gong
086   tackle the learning strategy approach of domain generalization (DG) and unsupervised domain adap-
087   tation (UDA). In their algorithm, called Collaborative OPtimization and Aggregation (COPA), each
088   agent in the network contains a local dataset, a feature extractor, and a classifier. The local feature
089   extractors are collected to create a global feature extractor that is domain-invariant. The local clas-
090   sifiers are ensembled to create the global classifier. These global models are used to update the local
091   models. After various iterative updates, COPA was able to converge to a point where it was com-
092   parable with state-of-the-art algorithms that focused on DG and UDA. Lastly, Zhang et al. (2021)
093
      subscribe to the “learning strategy” approach to solving domain generalization. They accomplish
      this through their algorithm FedADG, which uses an adversarial component to measure and then
094
      align different source distributions to a globally known, shared distribution. Similarly to COPA,
095
      their algorithm also focuses on learning a domain-invariant feature. While FedADG improves on
096   some test cases, the authors mention their approach to generating invariant features yields results
097   that are typically worse than an OT-based approach called L2A-OT (Zhou et al., 2020).
098
099
      Thus far, we have covered papers that are directly related to our work. We now focus on other re-
      search that indirectly tackles, or at least provides, a direction to work with imbalanced datasets. We
100
      first turn to clustering in federated learning. In Sattler et al. (2020), Sattler et al. propose their main
101
      algorithm, clustered federated learning (CFL), which relies on a generalization of the FL problem:
102   “There exists a partitioning of the clients such that all clients in each partition satisfy the conven-
103   tional FL problem.” The “conventional problem” implies there exists one model that can fit all data
104   distributions across the network. In an imbalanced dataset paradigm, the agents’ data-generating
105   distributions are different. To overcome the inability of FL to handle this problem, CFL clusters
106   the incoming gradients of the agents through a recursive cosine similarity-based bipartitioning and
107   then updates the model’s parameters once for each cluster by averaging the gradients of the clients
      in each cluster respectively.

      3.2   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
163
164   Optimal Transport (OT), also known as the Monge-Kantorovich problem, is a mathematical frame-
165   work that addresses the problem of finding the most cost-efficient way to transport mass from a
166   source distribution to a target distribution (Torres et al., 2021). OT was originally formulated by
167   Gaspard Monge in the 18th century (Monge, 1781). In the 20th century, the problem was refined
168   by soviet mathematician Kantorovich and has since found widespread applications in various fields,
169   including economics, computer vision, machine learning, and statistics. At its core, OT seeks to
170
      quantify the discrepancy between two probability distributions by defining a distance metric that
      considers both the magnitudes of the masses being transported and the distances over which they
171
      are moved. Unlike traditional distance metrics, such as Euclidean distance, which focus solely on
172
      point-to-point comparisons, OT provides a more geometrically nuanced measure that captures the
173   structural similarities between distributions.
174

176

239   4   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT- BASED P REPROCESSING
240
241   In this section, we introduce the preprocessing step that minimizes the distributional discrepancy in
242   our network. We achieve this distribution-alignment goal by generating a target space to which we
243   project all local data. The target space is generated from all data without losing privacy because
244   WBs obfuscate the data in an irreversible fashion (more clarifications on the prevention of privacy
245   leakage can be seen in A.1. Our proposed method has two main steps: the creation of the target
246   space, and the projection step to perform alignment.
247   To create a relevant target space to align images to, it needs to contain information from all the
248   agents in the network. We accomplish this in a two-step fashion. First, we compute representations
249   of the local data through a channel-wise Wasserstein barycenter of the local images. The approach
250   requires separating each local image its three color channels, grouping them by the respective color
251   channels, and lastly computing the WB for each channel. Figure 2 demonstrates this workflow.
252   In this work, we are using colored images, therefore, channel-wise implies red, green, and blue
253   channels. The local computations produce an RGB-triplet called the local WBs. Next, the second
254   step is to generate the target space by aggregating all local WBs in a central server, and repeating the
255
      same process of computing channel-wise barycenters; this yields the RGB-tripled called the global
      WB, or our target space. Readers will notice the steps just explained cover steps one and two of
256
      Figure 3.
257
258   The final steps of our preprocessing algorithm are steps three and four in Figure 3. First, we broad-
259   cast the global barycenters to the agents in the network. Then, we align the local images to tar-
260   get space by projecting them to the global WB. The projection process is composed of computing
261
      transportation plans to the target space that allows the color channels of the original images to be
      transferred and aligned, similarly to computing color transfer maps or domain adaptation in Fer-
262
      radans et al. (2014); Courty et al. (2016) respectively. The entire preprocessing steps just described
263
      is explicitly laid out in algorithm 1. For more in-depth information on the implementation details of
264   the steps just described, we refer readers to A.2.
265

      5       E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
351
352
      The goal of our paper is to demonstrate the functionality and utility of our preprocessing step in
353
      federated learning. Our framework is built independently of the learning algorithm, which allows
354
      for flexible integration into any FL pipeline that may have different goals (e.g., increase privacy
355   through differential privacy solutions to FL). Nonetheless, to demonstrate the advantages of using
356   our preprocessing step, we require a learning algorithm. To this end, we chose to work with federated
357   averaging, FedAvg, which trains local algorithms and performs parameter averaging to aggregate the
358   local models into a global model. To show the improvements our method provides to the learning
359   process, we designed a few experiments. Each experiment begins with the following common setup
360   steps. First, we design the network by choosing the number of agents that make up our network.
361   Next, we initialize their models identically (we refer readers to A.2 for implementation details such
362   as hyperparameters). Starting with equivalent initial conditions is currently a requirement for FL,
363
      otherwise averaging these models can yield “arbitrarily bad models” (McMahan et al., 2017; Good-
      fellow et al., 2015). The base model architecture is a convolutional neural network (CNN) with
364
      roughly one million parameters and can be seen in Figure 5. Next, we distribute the data randomly
365
      by uniformly sampling, without replacement, images. By sampling uniformly without replacement,
366   we ensure that each agent has varying amounts of data and are not completely homogenous (mean-
367   ing each agent has equal number of images per class), which introduces additional difficulties during
368   training. With the data distributed to the edge devices, we use our preprocessing algorithm (1) to
369   align them. Once the data is aligned, we use FedAvg to train a global model.
370
      There are various ways of deciding when to perform the synchronization step, which is where pa-
371
      rameter averaging happens. Similar to other works, we run more than one epoch per synchronization
372   step. On one hand, choosing one epoch per accumulation step did not induce sufficient local training.
373   Conversely, too many local epochs resulted in local overfitting and yielded worse global outcomes.
374   To demonstrate complete results, we ran simulations where all agents contributed to learning the
375   global model and also ran simulations where a random selection of agents contributed (the agents
376   were sampled according to a uniform distribution). As per convention, this is labeled N/P where
377   N is the total number of agents in the network and P is the number of agents that participate in
      training the global model. In experiments where N = P , we used two local training epochs per

470   6    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
471
472   An important tradeoff to consider with our algorithm is the additional time that must be paid to
473   convert the original dataset into an aligned dataset. As our method does not affect the learning
474   algorithm, we only analyze the complexity of computing the barycenters and projecting the local
475   data to the global barycenter, which is done prior to training. Therefore, this is an additional cost paid
476   in addition to training. First, Kroshnin et al. (2019) gives us a complexity analysis for the iterative
477
      Bregman projection method of computing regularized barycenters, as introduced by Benamou et al.
      (2015). Given n samples of dimension d and regularization parameter ϵ, we have a complexity
478
      of O(nd2 /ϵ2 ). Moreover, the projection of the local images onto the global barycenters requires
479
      solving the OT problem. Cuturi (2013) demonstrates an empirical complexity of O(d2 ) with respect
480   to the input dimension d. The complexity of our preprocessing is a combination of these two,
481   contingent on the number of agents. Assume we have N agents in our network, each with M
482   images. We must compute N barycenters. Since these are computed in parallel, the time complexity
483   is equivalent to computing one, with the addition of computing the global barycenter. Therefore, we
484   have O(M d2 /ϵ2 ) for local barycenters and O(N d2 /ϵ2 ) for the global barycenter. The projection
485   time complexity is scaled in accordance with M , not N , as each agent projects in parallel. Therefore,
      the overall time complexity for our preprocessing algorithm is O(M d2 /ϵ2 )+O(N d2 /ϵ2 )+O(M d2 ).

      7   C ONCLUSION
513
514
      In this work, we demonstrate the ability of our preprocessing algorithm to improve the convergence
515
      speed and generalization of traditional federated learning. We accomplish this by projecting local
516
      data to a space that encodes all local data. The projection to the same local space minimizes the
517   distributional discrepancy between agents given a heterogeneous distribution of data. After aligning
518   the local datasets, we train a model using FedAvg; this is an arbitrary choice, allowing any learning
519   algorithm to be paired with our method. Our simulations show superior results than FedAvg and
520   other comparable work, all of which do not contain preprocessed datasets. In comparison with the
521   original work of McMahan et al. (2017), not only do we reach higher accuracy scores, but we do
522   so with a model of fewer parameters. Our algorithm can be leveraged in any FL paradigm and
523   paired with any learning algorithm as its functionality is in preprocessing the data to minimize the
524   distribution discrepancy among different agents. Our work opens the door for future work where our
525
      preprocessing algorithm is fused with already existing learning-based algorithms. These potential
      fusions can lead to faster convergence and better generalization of already existing methods.

636   A.1   P RIVACY C ONCERNS
637
638   The concept of privacy is at the heart of federated learning. When introducing a novel approach that
639
      shares information between different devices, this could open an attack vector for the recreation of
      the original data. For example, when training a model using Federated Averaging over the gradients
640
      of local models, Geiping et al. (2020) show that the local images can be reconstructed. With this
641
      in mind, we asked ourselves about the vulnerability of computing the Wasserstein barycenters in
642   the fashion described. To compute a Wasserstein barycenter, we must solve several optimal trans-
643   port problems. Once solved, this mapping from probability distributions to the barycenter is not
644   invertible; reasons for non-invertibility include information loss when solving OT problems with
645   entropy-regularized formats and non-uniqueness of the solution of WBs. Furthermore, the RGB-
646   wise barycenters do not share information across the color channels, making it further impossible to
647   regenerate any source images. Therefore, distributing Wasserstein barycenters that are products of
      non-invertible optimization problems ensures the privacy of the local agents’ data is not broken.

659   A.2.2    O PTIMAL T RANSPORT D ETAILS
660
661   It is important to clarify the details regarding the algorithms used to solve the OT problem and to
662
      compute Wasserstein barycenters. In this section of the appendix we will cover the details needed
      to implement the parts of our preprocessing framework that use OT. First and foremost, the core of
663
      our algorithm relies on computing the Wasserstein barycenter efficiently Benamou et al. (2015). We
664
      leverage the python optimal transport package Flamary et al. (2021) which contains a solver for the
665   Bregman projection for regularized optimal transport. These methods require a cost function and a
666   regularization term. We use a quadratic cost function and a regularization term equal to 1e − 2. On
667   another note, a technique that can be used to reduce the computational requirement is to sample a
668   subset of pixel from each image and computer barycenters on this subset. In our work, we uniformly
669   sampled 250 pixels from the input images, which greatly reduced computational time (the difference
670   can be measured analytically using the time complexity shown in section 6).
671   Next, we will cover the details related to the projection step. To accomplish this, we leverage the
672   work of Ferradans et al. (2014); Courty et al. (2016) and the python optimal transport package. With
673   the global Wasserstein barycenter computed, we solve the entropic-regularized transport problem
674   using the Sinkhorn algorithm; this can be accomplished using the SinkhornTransport method of
675   python package. Similar to computing the barycenters, it is possible once again to select a subset
676   of the pixels to compute the transportation map. For this step we used a regularization parameter
677   of 1e − 1 and once again selected a subset of 250 pixels. With the transport model in hand, we
678   can transform the full image, yielding our transformed sample which will be used for training. The
679
      mathematical details of these algorithms are covered in detail in the aforementioned papers.
