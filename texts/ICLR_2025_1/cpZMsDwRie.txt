009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Open-source Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated re-
012               markable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, leading
013               to widespread adoption across various domains. However, their increasing model
014               sizes render local deployment impractical for individual users, pushing many to
015               rely on decentralized computing service providers for inference through a black-
016               box API. This reliance introduces a new risk: a computing provider may stealthily
017
                  substitute the requested LLM with a smaller, less capable model without consent
                  from users, thereby delivering inferior outputs while benefiting from cost savings.
018
                  In this paper, we formalize the problem of verifiable inference for LLMs. Ex-
019
                  isting verifiable computing solutions based on cryptographic or game-theoretic
020               techniques are either computationally uneconomical or rest on strong assump-
021               tions. We introduce SVIP, a secret-based verifiable LLM inference protocol that
022               leverages intermediate outputs from LLMs as unique model identifiers. By train-
023               ing a proxy task on these outputs and requiring the computing provider to return
024               both the generated text and the processed intermediate outputs, users can reliably
025               verify whether the computing provider is acting honestly. In addition, the inte-
026               gration of a secret mechanism further enhances the security of our protocol. We
027               thoroughly analyze our protocol under multiple strong and adaptive adversarial
028
                  scenarios. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that SVIP is accurate, general-
                  izable, computationally efficient, and resistant to various attacks. Notably, SVIP
029
                  achieves false negative rates below 5% and false positive rates below 3%, while
030
                  requiring less than 0.01 seconds per prompt query for verification.

124   2   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
125
126   We begin by formalizing the verifiable inference problem in the context of LLMs. We consider three
127   parties: the user, the computing provider, and a trusted third party. The user intends to use a specified
128
      LLM, Mspec , to perform inference on a prompt x ∈ V ∗ , where V ∗ represents the set of all possible
      string sequences for a vocabulary set V. Lacking sufficient computational resources to run Mspec
129
      locally, the user relies on the computing provider to execute the model and accordingly pays for the
130
      service. Ideally, the computing provider would run Mspec as requested and return the completion.
131   However, a dishonest provider might stealthily substitute an alternative LLM, Malt , which could
132   be significantly smaller than Mspec in terms of model size, and return an inferior result.
133
134
      The goal of a trusted third party (e.g., a decentralized computing platform that profits by connecting
      users and computing providers), is to design and implement a verification protocol that verifies
135
      whether the computing provider uses Mspec for inference. Based on this protocol, the user can
136
      determine with high confidence whether the computing provider used Mspec (True) or not (False)
137   for any prompt query x. A satisfactory protocol should meet the following criteria:
138
139   1. Low False Negative Rate (FNR): The protocol should minimize instances where the computing
140   provider did use Mspec but is wrongly flagged as not using it.
141   2. Low False Positive Rate (FPR): The protocol should minimize cases where it incorrectly con-
142   firms that the computing provider used Mspec when, in fact, it used an alternative model, Malt .
143   3. Efficiency: The verification protocol should be computationally efficient and introduce minimal
144   overhead for both the computing provider and the user.
145   4. Preservation of Completion Quality: The protocol should not compromise the quality of the
146   prompt completion returned by the computing provider.

148   3   R ELATED WORK
149
150   Verifiable Computing (VC) allows users to verify that an untrusted computing provider has executed
151   computations correctly, without having to perform the computation themselves (Walfish & Blum-
152   berg, 2015; Yu et al., 2017; Costello et al., 2015; Kosba et al., 2018). VC approaches can be broadly
153   categorized into cryptographic methods and game-theoretic methods.
154
      Cryptographic VC techniques either require the provider to return a mathematical proof that con-
155
      firms the correctness of the results (Ghodsi et al., 2017; Setty et al., 2012; Parno et al., 2016), or
156   rely on secure computation techniques (Gennaro et al., 2010; Madi et al., 2020; Laud & Pankova,
157   2014). These techniques cryptographically guarantee correctness and have been applied to machine
158   learning models and shallow neural networks (Niu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023a;
159   Lee et al., 2024; Ghodsi et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2022). However, they typically require the compu-
160   tation task to be expressed as arithmetic circuits. Representing open-source LLMs in circuit form
161   is particularly challenging due to their complex architectures and intricate operations. Moreover,
      the sheer size of these models, with billions of parameters, introduces substantial computational

195   4     M ETHODOLOGY
196
197   Motivation Despite the fact that larger language models typically offer superior text generation
198   quality (Kaplan et al., 2020), it is often challenging to verify whether a computing provider is using
199   Mspec for inference based solely on the returned completion text. Our framework addresses this
200   by requiring the computing provider to return not only the generated text but also the processed
201   intermediate outputs (hidden state representations) from the LLM inference process.
202   We design and train a proxy task specifically to perform well only on the hidden representations
203   generated by Mspec during the protocol’s training stage. The intuition behind is that the proxy task
204   transforms the hidden representations into a unique identifier for the model. During deployment, the
205   user can evaluate the performance of the returned intermediate outputs on the proxy task. Strong
206   performance on the proxy task indicates that the correct model was used for inference, while poor
207   performance suggests otherwise.
208   Our approach does not depend on expensive cryptographic proofs or protocols, and is highly effi-
209   cient. Furthermore, it does not involve retraining or fine-tuning the LLMs, operates independently
210   of the model publisher, and can be applied to any LLM with publicly available weight parameters,
211   making it widely applicable. Next, we formalize and illustrate our proposed framework in detail.

215   Protocol Overview We denote the prompt input as x ∈ V ∗ . For any LLM M, let hM (x) ∈
      RL×dM represent the last-layer hidden representations of x produced by M, where L is the length

      of the tokenized input x, and dM denotes the hidden dimension of M. The computing provider
217   receives x from the user, runs M, and returns hM (x) to user for subsequent verification. However,
218   to reduce the size of the intermediate outputs returned, we additionally apply a proxy task feature
219   extractor network gθ (·) : RL×dM → Rdg parameterized by θ, where dg represents the proxy task
220   feature dimension. The computing provider now also runs gθ (·) and returns a compressed vector
221   z(x) := gθ (hM (x)) of dimension dg to the user, significantly reducing the communication over-
222   head. Specifically, for each prompt query, the compressed vector only takes approximately 4 KB
223   when dg is set to 1024.
224   The user is required to perform two tasks locally: obtaining the predicted proxy task output and
225   the label. First, the user runs fϕ (·), using the returned proxy task feature z(x) as input to compute
226   fϕ (z(x)). Here, fϕ (·) : Rdg → Y is the proxy task head parameterized by ϕ, where Y denotes the
227   label space. Second, the user applies a labeling function for the proxy task. We adopt a self-labeling
228   function y(x) : V ∗ → Y, which derives the label directly from the input, eliminating the need for
229   external labels or specialized annotators3 . Note that the label can either be a scalar or a vector.
230   Finally, the user checks whether fϕ (z(x)) matches y(x). Our training process below ensures that,
231   with high probability, fϕ (z(x)) = y(x) when Mspec is used for inference, and that this does not hold
232   for other models, as the proxy task is exclusively trained on the hidden representation distribution
233   induced by Mspec . This completes our protocol. Refer to Figure 2a for a detailed illustration.

      From Simple Protocol to Secret-based Protocol The simple protocol, despite its strong potential
255
      in discriminating whether the specified model is actually used, is vulnerable to malicious attacks
256   from the computing provider. A dishonest provider may attempt to bypass the verification process
257   without running Mspec . Since all the provider needs to return is a vector of dimension dg , an
258   attacker could adversarially optimize a vector z̃ ∈ Rdg directly, without actually running gθ∗ (·)
259   and using any LLM. We refer to this as a direct vector optimization attack. Specifically, if the
260   self-labeling function is public, the adversary can run the labeling function y(x) themselves for each
261   input x and then directly find z̃ so that
262                                        z̃ ∗ = arg min ℓ (fϕ∗ (z̃), y(x)) .                                  (3)
263                                                     z̃

264   Ultimately, z̃ ∗ is returned to the user to deceive the verification protocol. As shown in our case study
265   in Appendix D.7, this attack achieved an attack success rate (ASR) of 99.90%, indicating that the
266   security of the protocol requires further enforcement.

      4.3   S ECURITY A NALYSIS
315
316   As previously discussed, the direct vector optimization attack described in Eq. (3) is no longer
317   feasible due to the introduction of the secret mechanism. In this section, we discuss other potential
318   attacks as a comprehensive security analysis towards our secret-based protocol. A more detailed
319   discussion of other possible attacks is provided in Appendix C.
320
321   Adapter Attack Under Single Secret A malicious attacker could attempt an adapter attack if they
322   collect enough prompt samples D′ = {xi }M   i=1 under a single secret s. The returned vector from an
323   honest computing provider should be z(x) = gθ∗ (tψ∗ (s) ⊕ hMspec (x)). The attacker’s goal is to
      train an adapter that mimics the returned vector, but by using an alternative LLM, Malt .


                                                           6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      To this end, we define the adapter aλ (·) : RdMalt → RdMspec , parameterized by λ, which transforms
325   the hidden states of Malt to approximate those of Mspec . The returned vector is then gθ∗ (tψ∗ (s) ⊕
326   aλ (hMalt (x))). The attacker’s objective is to minimize the L2 distance between the returned vector
327   generated by Mspec and the vector produced by Malt with the adapter. This can be expressed as:
328            λ∗ = arg min Ex∼D′ ∥gθ∗ (tψ∗ (s) ⊕ hMspec (x)) − gθ∗ (tψ∗ (s) ⊕ aλ (hMalt (x)))∥2 .     (9)
329                        λ

330   By minimizing this objective, the attacker seeks to make the output of Malt with the adapter in-
331
      distinguishable from that of Mspec , effectively bypassing the protocol. Once the adapter is well-
      trained, as long as the secret s remains unchanged, the attacker can rely solely on Malt in future
332
      verification queries without being detected.
333
334
      Secret Recovery Attack Under Multiple Secrets The secret mechanism is enforced by distribut-
335   ing the secret s to the user, while only providing the secret embedding tψ∗ (s) to the computing
336   provider. However, a sophisticated computing provider may attempt to recover the original secret
337   by posing as a user and collecting multiple secrets and corresponding embeddings. A straightfor-
338   ward approach would involve recovering s from tψ∗ (s), thereby undermining the secret mechanism.
339
      Suppose the attacker has curated a dataset of secret-embedding pairs, Dsecret = {sj , tψ∗ (sj )}N
                                                                                                      j=1 .
340
341   The attacker could then train an inverse model iρ : RdM → S, parameterized by ρ, to map the secret
342
      embedding back to the secret space. If S is continuous, the training objective can be formalized as:
343                                ρ∗ = arg min Es∼Dsecret ∥iρ (tψ∗ (s)) − s∥2 .                      (10)
                                                  ρ
344   Once the inverse model is optimized, the true label y(x, s) again becomes accessible to the malicious
345   computing provider. Consequently, the secret-based protocol effectively collapses to the simple
346   protocol without secret protection, leaving it vulnerable to the direct vector optimization attack.
347
348   Defense: The Update Mechanism To defend against the attacks discussed above, we propose
349   an update mechanism for our secret-based protocol: (1) In defense of the adapter attack, once the
350   prompt queries for a given secret reach a pre-defined threshold M ∗ , the next secret is activated.
351   Meanwhile, we enforce a limit on how often the next secret can be activated, preventing attackers
352   from acquiring too many secrets within a short period. (2) When a total of N ∗ secrets have been
353
      used, the entire protocol should be retrained by the trusted third party4 . In practice, the values of
      M ∗ and N ∗ can be determined empirically, as discussed in Section 5.4.

471   and verifying the proof takes 3.95 seconds for the same LLM. The proxy task feature extractor gθ (·),

473   minimal overhead. On the user side, the proxy task head fϕ (·) and labeling network yγ (·) require
474   a total of 1428 MB, making it feasible for users to run on local machines without high-end GPUs.

      Additionally, we record the required proxy task retraining time in Table 3b. Overall, retraining the
      proxy task takes less than 1.5 hours on a single GPU, allowing for efficient protocol update.

      6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we formalize the problem of verifiable inference in the context of LLMs. We introduce
534   a novel framework SVIP, which transforms the intermediate outputs from LLMs into unique model
535   identifiers through a carefully designed proxy task. To bolster the security of our protocol, we further
536   incorporate a secret mechanism. We also provide a thorough analysis of potential attack scenarios.
537   Our protocol demonstrates high accuracy, strong generalization, low computational overhead, and
538   resilience against strong adaptive attacks. We hope that our work will spark further exploration into
539   verifiable inference techniques for LLMs, fostering trust and encouraging wider adoption, with the
      ultimate goal of accelerating the development of more advanced open-source LLMs.

      In this work, we address the challenge of verifiable LLM inference, aiming to foster trust between

      A     D ISCUSSIONS
757
758
      A.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
759
760   In our SVIP protocol, although the labeling network yγ (·) can be applied to multiple specified mod-
761   els once trained, the proxy task head fϕ (·), proxy task feature extractor gθ (·), and secret embedding
762   network tψ (·) need to be optimized for each specified model. Future work could explore the possi-
763   bility of designing a more generalizable architecture that allows these networks to be shared across
764   different specified models, reducing the need for model-specific optimization.
765   Additionally, due to the secret mechanism, our protocol currently relies on a trusted third party to
766   distribute secrets to the user and secret embeddings to the computing provider. Developing a proto-
767   col that operates independently of a trusted third party, involving only the user and the computing
768   provider, would be an interesting direction. However, ensuring security in this setting, particularly
769   preventing malicious attacks by dishonest providers, remains a significant challenge.
770
      Moreover, unlike cryptographic verifiable computation techniques, our approach does not offer a
771   strict security guarantee. However, such strict guarantees are inevitably associated with prohibitively
772   high computational overheads. In contrast, our method strikes a practical balance between compu-
773   tational efficiency and security, making it more suitable for real-world applications.
774
775   A.2   S IMPLE A PPROACHES TO V ERIFIABLE LLM I NFERENCE C AN B E V ULNERABLE
776
777   One straightforward solution to verifiable LLM inference, as briefly mentioned in Section 1, involves
778   the user curating a small set of prompt examples from established benchmarks and sending them
779   to the computing provider. If the provider’s performance significantly deviates from the reported
780   benchmark metrics for the specified model, the user may question the provider’s honesty. However,
781   a malicious provider can easily bypass this method by detecting known benchmark prompts and
782   selectively applying the correct model only for those cases, while using an alternative model for
783
      all other queries. Additionally, testing such benchmark prompts also increases the user’s inference
      costs.
784
785   Another seemingly promising approach is to directly train a binary (or one-class) classifier on the
786   returned intermediate outputs to verify if the hidden representations come from the specified model.
787   However, a simple attack involves the provider caching hidden representations from the correct
788   model that are unrelated to the user’s input. The dishonest provider could then use a smaller LLM
789
      for inference and return these cached irrelevant representations to deceive the classifier while saving
      costs.
790

915   Open-source LLMs Open-source LLMs are freely available models that offer flexibility for use
916   and modification. Popular examples include GPT-Neo (Black et al., 2022), BLOOM (Le Scao et al.,
917   2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and Falcon
      (Almazrouei et al., 2023). These models, ranging from millions to over 100 billion parameters,


                                                                                17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      have gained attention for their accessibility and growing capacity. However, larger models like
919   Falcon-40B (Almazrouei et al., 2023), and Llama-3.1-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) come with steep
920   computational costs, making even inference impractical on local machines due to the significant
921   GPU memory required. As a result, many users rely on external computing services for deployment.
922
923   Additional Background on Cryptographic VC Techniques Among cryptographic VC tech-
924   niques, proof-based methods involve the generation of mathematical proofs that certify the correct-
925   ness of outsourced computations. Representative techniques in this class include interactive proofs,
926   Succinct Non-Interactive Arguments of Knowledge (SNARK), and Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP).
927   Interactive proofs involve multiple rounds of interaction between a verifier (the user) and a prover
928   (the computing provider) to ensure the computation’s integrity (Cormode et al., 2011; Goldwasser
929   et al., 2015; Thaler, 2013). SNARK allows a verifier to validate a computation with a single, short
930   proof that requires minimal computational effort (Fiore et al., 2020; Bontekoe et al., 2023). ZKP
931   further enhances privacy by enabling the prover to convince the verifier of a statement’s truth without
932   revealing any additional information beyond the validity of the claim (Fiege et al., 1987; De Santis &
933   Persiano, 1992). Due to their rigorous guarantees of correctness and privacy, these techniques have
934   been widely applied in blockchain and related areas (Yang & Li, 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Šimunić
935   et al., 2021).
936
937   LLM Watermarking and Fingerprinting LLM watermarking involves embedding algorithmi-
938
      cally detectable signals into the text generated by LLMs, with the goal of identifying AI-generated
      texts (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023b; Christ et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023). Meanwhile,
939
      LLM fingerprinting implants specific backdoor triggers into LLMs, causing the model to generate
940
      particular text whenever a confidential private key is used (Xu et al., 2024). Consequently, model
941   publishers are able to verify ownership even after extensive custom fine-tuning.
942
943   However, such techniques are not suitable for the verifiable inference setting. First, these methods
944
      are typically designed and implemented by the model publisher, who is not directly involved in the
      verification process between the user and the computing provider. Second, even if these techniques
945
      have been implemented, a malicious computing provider, with full control over how the open-source
946
      LLM is deployed or modified, could easily replicate or manipulate the implanted patterns. There-
947   fore, these techniques cannot offer sufficient protection for verifiable inference in most cases.
