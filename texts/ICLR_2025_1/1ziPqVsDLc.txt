009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Diffusion Models (DMs) have evolved into advanced image generation tools, es-
012              pecially for few-shot fine-tuning where a pretrained DM is fine-tuned on a small
013              set of images to capture specific styles or objects. Many people upload these per-
014              sonalized checkpoints online, fostering communities such as Civitai and Hugging-
015              Face. However, model owners may overlook the potential risks of data leakage by
016              releasing their fine-tuned checkpoints. Moreover, concerns regarding copyright
017              violations arise when unauthorized data is used during fine-tuning. In this paper,
018
                 we ask: “Can training data be extracted from these fine-tuned DMs shared on-
                 line?” A successful extraction would present not only data leakage threats but
019
                 also offer tangible evidence of copyright infringement. To answer this, we pro-
020
                 pose FineXtract, a framework for extracting fine-tuning data. Our method approx-
021              imates fine-tuning as a gradual shift in the model’s learned distribution—from the
022              original pretrained DM toward the fine-tuning data. By extrapolating the models
023              before and after fine-tuning, we guide the generation toward high-probability re-
024              gions within the fine-tuned data distribution. We then apply a clustering algorithm
025              to extract the most probable images from those generated using this extrapolated
026              guidance. Experiments on DMs fine-tuned with datasets such as WikiArt, Dream-
027              Booth, and real-world checkpoints posted online validate the effectiveness of our
028              method, extracting approximately 20% of fine-tuning data in most cases, signif-
029
                 icantly surpassing baseline performance. The code is available at an anonymous
                 link1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
      Recent years have witnessed the advancement of Diffusion Models (DMs) in computer vision. These
035
      models demonstrate exceptional capabilities across various tasks, including image editing (Kawar
036
      et al., 2022), and video editing (Yang et al., 2022), among others. Particularly noteworthy is the
037   advent of few-shot fine-tuning methods (Hu et al., 2021; Ruiz et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023), in which
038   a pretrained model is fine-tuned to personalize generation based on a small set of training images.
039   These approaches have significantly reduced both memory and time costs in training. Moreover,
040   these techniques offer powerful tools for adaptively generating images based on specific subjects or
041   objects, embodying personalized AI and making AI accessible to everyone.
042
      Building on these innovations, several communities, such as Civitai (civ) and HuggingFace (hug),
043   have emerged, hosting tens of thousands of fine-tuned checkpoints and attracting millions of down-
044   loads. Although many users willingly share their fine-tuned models, they may be unaware of the risk
045   of data leakage inherent in this process. This is particularly concerning when fine-tuning involves
046   sensitive data, such as medical images, human faces, or copyrighted material. Moreover, many of
047   these checkpoints are fine-tuned using unauthorized data, including artists’ work. This unauthorized
048   fine-tuning process raises significant concerns regarding “reputational damage, economic loss, pla-
049   giarism and copyright infringement” as mentioned in Jiang et al. (2023), and has prompted numerous
050   objections from data owners (Liang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Shan et al., 2023).
051   In this paper, we pose a critical question: “Is it possible to extract fine-tuning data from these fine-
052   tuned DM checkpoints released online?” Successfully doing so would confirm that fine-tuning

077   fine-tuned data distribution. With this approximation, we demonstrate that extrapolating the score

079   the high-density regions of the fine-tuned data distribution, a process we refer to as model guidance.

085   distribution. Combined with model guidance, this leads to an extrapolation from the noise predicted

134   2.2   M EMORIZATION AND DATA E XTRACTION IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
135
136   Recent studies on DMs have highlighted their tendencies toward data memorization and methods
137   have been proposed to extract training data based on it. Carlini et al. (2023) used a graph algorithm
138   to identify the generated data most likely to have been included in the training set, thereby retriev-
139   ing DM’s memorized training data. Further investigations (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b) explore the
140
      underlying causes of this memorization, revealing that conditioning plays a significant role, and the
      nature of training prompts notably influences the likelihood of reproducing training samples. How-
141
      ever, these studies are primarily empirical with no parametric formulation on learned distribution
142
      of DMs, and they do not address personalization scenarios. In contrast, our approach introduces a
143   parametric approximation of the learned distribution of fine-tuned DMs, enabling the design of a
144   pipeline that efficiently extracts training samples from fine-tuned checkpoints upload online.

160   more discussed in Appendix Sec. A, where we show that even without direct access, captions can be
161   partially extracted using inversion on linear projection layers.) Attackers have no prior knowledge
      about the image dataset X0 . Their goal is to extract as much information about X0 as possible. A

308   lini et al., 2023), sampling N images and applying a clustering algorithm to identify the images with
309   the highest probability, where N ≫ N0 and N0 is the number of training images.
310   Specifically, inspired by Carlini et al. (2023), we compute the similarity between each pair of gen-
311   erated images and construct a graph where each image is represented as a vertex. We connect

345   5       E XPERIMENTS
346
347   In this section, we apply our proposed method, FineXtract, to extract training data under various
348   few-shot fine-tuning techniques across different types of DMs. We conduct experiments on two
349   common scenarios for few-shot fine-tuning: style-driven and object-driven generation. For style-
350   driven generation, which focuses on capturing the key style of a set of images, we randomly select
351   20 artists, each with 10 images, from the WikiArt dataset (Nichol, 2016). For object-driven gen-
352   eration, which emphasizes the details of a given object, we experiment on 30 objects from the
353   Dreambooth dataset (Ruiz et al., 2023), each consisting of 4-6 images. This setup aligns with the
354
      recommended number of training samples in the aforementioned fine-tuning methods (Ruiz et al.,
      2023; Hu et al., 2021). We experiment with two most widely-used few-shot fine-tuning techniques:
355
      DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). More details for the fine-tuning setting
356
      are available in Appendix Sec. E.
357
358   The default model used for training is Stable Diffusion (SD) V1.42 . Additionally, we demonstrate
359   the adaptability of our method to various types and versions of DMs, larger training datasets, and
360
      different numbers of generated images (refer to Sec. 5.2 for more details).
361   By default, we set the generation count N to 50 × N0 , where N0 represents the number of training
362   images. The number of extracted images is set equal to N0 to best evaluate our method’s ability to
363   extract the exact training dataset. For DreamBooth, the guidance scale w′ for both FineXtract and
364   CFG set to 3.0 by default, with the correction term scale k set to -0.02 in Equations 8 and 13. For
