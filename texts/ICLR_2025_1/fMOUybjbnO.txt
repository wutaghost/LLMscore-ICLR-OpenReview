029                                                   A BSTRACT
030
031            Computer vision (CV) datasets often exhibit biases in the form of spurious cor-
032            relations between certain attributes and target variables that are perpetuated by
033
               Deep Learning (DL) models. While recent efforts aim to mitigate such biases and
               foster bias-neutral representations, they fail in complex real-world scenarios. In
034
               particular, existing methods excel in controlled experiments on benchmarks with
035
               single-attribute injected biases, but struggle with complex multi-attribute biases
036            that naturally occur in established CV datasets. Here, we introduce BAdd a sim-
037            ple yet effective method that allows for learning bias-neutral representations in-
038            variant to bias-inducing attributes. It achieves this by injecting features encoding
039            these attributes into the training process. BAdd is evaluated on seven benchmarks
040            and exhibits competitive performance, surpassing state-of-the-art methods on both
041            single- and multi-attribute bias settings. Notably, it achieves +27.5% and +5.5%
042            absolute accuracy improvements on the challenging multi-attribute benchmarks,
043            FB-Biased-MNIST and CelebA, respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
046
047
      Deep Learning (DL) models have demonstrated impressive capabilities, as evidenced by ground-
048   breaking performance across various Computer Vision (CV) tasks (Deng et al., 2019; Feichtenhofer
049   et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2020). However, a concerning issue has emerged alongside these advance-
050   ments: the potential for bias in AI systems, disproportionately impacting specific groups (Barocas
051   et al., 2019; Fabbrizzi et al., 2022; Sarridis et al., 2023b). Specifically, when AI systems base their
052   decisions - often indirectly - on attributes like age, gender, or race, they become discriminatory.
053   Considering the profound impact AI decisions can have on individuals’ lives, such biases should
      be mitigated prior to deployment in high-stakes applications (Bobadilla et al., 2013; Taigman


                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2014; Creswell et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2020). Moreover, even when such biases are not
055   demographic-related but stem from “shortcuts” that prioritize irrelevant features, addressing them
056   is crucial for building more robust and reliable CV systems (Sagawa et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023).
057
058
      For CV systems, bias often originates from the composition of the datasets used for training (Fab-
      brizzi et al., 2022). One of the main ways in which bias arises in training sets is through a data
059
      selection process where specific groups of people or objects are largely associated with certain vi-
060
      sual attributes (e.g., women are illustrated wearing earrings in the majority of images). When such
061   data is used to train DL models, such common attribute associations can act as “shortcuts”, lead-
062   ing the model to prioritize irrelevant attributes in its decision-making process (Zhang et al., 2018).
063   Motivated by this issue, several approaches have been proposed to enable learning bias-neutral repre-
064   sentations that are robust to the so-called bias attributes (Sarridis et al., 2023a; Hong & Yang, 2021;
065   Barbano et al., 2022), i.e. attributes that exhibit spurious correlations with the target classes. Such
066   methods often leverage labels associated with protected attributes to guide model training towards
067   learning bias-neutral representations (Bahng et al., 2020; Cadene et al., 2019; Clark et al., 2019;
068   Hong & Yang, 2021; Barbano et al., 2022; Sarridis et al., 2023a) through techniques like adversarial
069
      training (Kim et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019) and regularization approaches (Tartaglione et al., 2021;
      Hong & Yang, 2021; Barbano et al., 2022; Sarridis et al., 2023a). A fundamental limitation of ex-
070
      isting bias mitigation methods lies in their loss-based nature. Typically, such approaches introduce
071
      additional loss terms to penalize the biased model’s behavior, which retroactively corrects bias that
072   is already introduced in the model’s learning process. While methods adopting this strategy may
073   appear sound in theory and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on simplistic datasets, they
074   struggle with more complex forms of bias, especially when dealing with multiple biased attributes,
075   and demonstrate sub-optimal performance. To overcome these challenges, there is a need for more
076   proactive bias mitigation approaches that intervene earlier in the training process, addressing the
077   root cause of bias propagation within the model itself. By disrupting the process through which bias
078   is introduced to the model, we can build models that are more effective for a wide range of complex
079   biases present in standard CV datasets.
080   In this paper, we recognize the need to enhance the applicability of bias-aware CV models in
081   complex application settings by proposing BAdd, a simple yet effective method to mitigate bias
082   at its core. The proposed method relies on the principle that injecting bias-capturing features
083   into the penultimate layer’s output enables learning representations invariant to these features (see
084   Fig. 1). Deriving bias-capturing features is straightforward since it can be formulated as the task
085   of predicting the values of biased attributes. BAdd intervenes in the mechanism by which bias is
086
      introduced to the DL models during training via the minimization of the loss function. In particular,
      a vanilla model optimizes its parameters by taking advantage of biases present in the data, as doing
087
      so reduces the overall loss. Such a model learns to prioritize features associated with the biased
088
      attributes, reinforcing and perpetuating the bias within its representations. To alleviate this issue,
089   BAdd suggests that the intentional inclusion of bias-capturing features within the training process
090   ensures that the attributes introducing the bias do not exert undue influence on the loss function
091   optimization, and thus the trainable parameters of the model are not affected by them. In essence,
092   BAdd decouples the learning of biased features from the optimization process and thus allows
093   for learning bias-neutral representations. BAdd outperforms or is on par with state-of-the-art bias
094   mitigation methods on a wide range of experiments involving four datasets with single attribute
095   biases (i.e., Biased-MNIST, Biased-UTKFace, Waterbirds, and Corrupted-CIFAR10) and three
096   datasets with multi-attribute biases (i.e., FB-Biased-MNIST, UrbanCars, and CelebA). Where BAdd
097
      shines is on datasets with multi-attribute biases, where it outperforms the state of the art by +27.5%,
      and +5.5% absolute accuracy improvements on FB-Biased-MNIST, and CelebA, respectively. In
098
      summary, the paper makes the following contributions: (i) we introduce BAdd, an effective method-
099
      ology for learning bias-neutral representations concerning one or more protected attributes by
100   incorporating bias-capturing features into the model’s representations (ii) we provide an extensive
101   evaluation involving seven benchmarks, demonstrating the superiority of BAdd on both single- and
102   multi-attribute bias scenarios. The data and code are provided in supplementary material.

105   2   R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Bias-aware image classification benchmarks. Most standard benchmarks for evaluating bias
      mitigation techniques in CV involve artificially generated single-attribute biases. Biased-MNIST


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Bahng et al., 2020), a MNIST derivative dataset, associates each digit with a specific colored back-
109   ground. Similarly, Corrupted-CIFAR10 (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2018) introduces biased textures
110   across the classes of CIFAR10. The Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2019) dataset is constructed by crop-
111   ping birds from the CUB-200 (Wah et al., 2011) dataset and transferring them onto backgrounds
112   from the Places dataset (Zhou et al., 2017), introducing correlations between bird species and cer-
113   tain backgrounds (i.e., habitat types). On the other hand, datasets like Biased-UTKFace (Hong &
114   Yang, 2021) and Biased-CelebA (Hong & Yang, 2021) are carefully selected subsets of UTKFace
115   (Zhifei et al., 2017) and CelebA (Liu et al., 2015), respectively, designed to exhibit an association
116   of 90% between specific attributes, such as gender and race. Despite their value in research, all
117
      these benchmarks share a crucial limitation: they are far from capturing the complexities of realis-
      tic scenarios, as they typically exhibit uniformly distributed single attribute biases. To approximate
118
      more realistic cases, recent works introduced benchmarks that involve multi-attribute biases, such
119
      as Biased-MNIST variations (Ahn et al., 2022; Shrestha et al., 2022a;b) and UrbanCars (Li et al.,
120   2023). The latter introduces a multi-attribute bias setting by incorporating biases related to both
121   background and co-occurring objects and the task is to classify the car body type into urban or
122   country car. In addition to the above benchmarks, in this paper, we create a variation of Biased-
123   MNIST, termed FB-Biased-MNIST, which builds on the background color bias in Biased-MNIST by
124   injecting an additional foreground color bias. Furthermore, we consider a benchmark that utilizes
125   the original, unmodified CelebA dataset but focuses on evaluating performance against the most
126   prominent bias-inducing attributes in the dataset. This allows for evaluating bias-aware methods on
127   multiple biases in a more realistic setting - without artificially enforced biases.
128
129   Bias-aware approaches. Efforts on learning bias-neutral representations using biased data en-
130   compass techniques like ensemble learning (Clark et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020), contrastive learn-
131
      ing (Hong & Yang, 2021; Barbano et al., 2022), adversarial frameworks (Xie et al., 2017; Alvi et al.,
      2018; Kim et al., 2019; Song et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019; Adel et al., 2019), and regularization
132
      approaches (Cadene et al., 2019; Tartaglione et al., 2021; Hong & Yang, 2021; Sarridis et al., 2023a).
133
      For instance, the Learning Not to Learn (LNL) approach (Kim et al., 2019) penalizes models if they
134   predict protected attributes, while the Domain-Independent (DI) approach (Wang et al., 2020) intro-
135   duces the usage of domain-specific classifiers to mitigate bias. Entangling and Disentangling deep
136   representations (EnD) (Tartaglione et al., 2021) suggests a regularization term that entangles or dis-
137   entangles feature vectors w.r.t. their target and protected attribute labels. FairKL (Barbano et al.,
138   2022) and BiasContrastive-BiasBalance (BC-BB) (Hong & Yang, 2021) are contrastive learning-
139   based approaches that try to mitigate bias by utilizing the pairwise similarities of the samples in the
140   feature space. Finally, there are several works that can be employed without utilizing the protected
141   attribute labels, such as Learned-Mixin (LM) (Clark et al., 2019), Rubi (Cadene et al., 2019), Re-
142
      Bias (Bahng et al., 2020), Learning from Failure (LfF) (Nam et al., 2020), and FLAC (Sarridis et al.,
      2023a). The latter achieves state-of-the-art performance by utilizing a bias-capturing classifier and
143
      a sampling strategy that effectively focuses on the underrepresented groups. It is worth noting that
144
      methodologies for distributionally robust optimization (Sagawa et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021a; Wu
145   et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; 2023) are relevant to the field of bias mitigation, as
146   they aim at mitigating biases arising from spurious correlations in the training data. Similarly to the
147   aforementioned methods, Sagawa et al. (2019) and Li et al. (2022) suggest regularization terms to
148   mitigate such correlations, while Liu et al. (2021a) and Wu et al. (2023) introduced methods that
149   try to balance the datasets w.r.t. the spurious correlations by increasing or decreasing the weights
150   of certain training samples. Based on the same idea, Qiu et al. (2023) focuses on reweighting the
151   features rather than the samples. Finally, the Last Layer Ensemble (LLE) (Li et al., 2023) employs
152   multiple augmentations to eliminate different biases (i.e., one type of augmentation for each type of
153
      bias). However, LLE requires extensive pre-processing (e.g., object segmentation), which makes it
      challenging or even infeasible to apply to new CV datasets. On the other hand, BAdd is a simple yet
154
      effective approach that can be easily applied to any network architecture and to any CV dataset.

159   3.1   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
160
161   Consider a dataset D comprising training samples (x(i) , y (i) ), where x(i) represents the input sample
      and y (i) belongs to the set of target labels Y. Let h(·) denote a model trained on D and h the model


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      feature representation (e.g., output of penultimate model layer). Let also T be the domain of tuples
163   of protected attributes, e.g., t = (male, 25, black) ∈ T for protected attributes gender, age and
164   race. The objective is to train h such that the protected attributes are not used to predict the targets
165   in Y. In addition, we also assume that a bias-capturing model b(·), with feature representation b,
166   has been trained to predict the value of the protected attribute(s) t ∈ T from x.
167
      We define D as biased with respect to the protected attributes in T if there is high correlation of
168
      certain values in Y with a value or a combination of values of protected attributes in T . Within a
169   batch B, samples exhibiting the dataset bias are termed bias-aligned (BA ), while those that deviate
170   from it are referred to as bias-conflicting (BC ). The set D is assumed to include at least some bias-
171   conflicting examples. Note that bias-aligned and bias-conflicting samples correspond to the over-
172   represented and under-represented groups within D, respectively. Using such a biased dataset for
173   training often introduces model bias, by leading h to encode information related to t. Our objective
174   is to mitigate these dependencies between representations h and b, leading to a bias-neutral feature
175   representation.
176
177   3.2   T HE V ICIOUS C IRCLE OF B IAS
178
179   Training a classification model h(·) on a biased dataset D very often prioritizes learning features
180
      related to the protected attributes instead of features directly characterizing the target class. This
      phenomenon arises in cases of high correlation between protected attributes and targets, provided
181
      that the protected attribute’s visual characteristics are easier to capture than the visual characteris-
182
      tics of the target (Zhang et al., 2018). Below, we delve into the details behind a vanilla model’s
183   inherent inclination towards this kind of bias and explain how the proposed approach addresses this
184   limitation.
185

250   3.3   BA DD
251
252
      BAdd proposes incorporating the features b that capture the protected attributes of the dataset in
      the model’s feature representation h. Feature representation b encapsulates all the desired protected
253
      attributes and can be considered as b = b1 + b2 + · · · + bM where M = |T | is the number of
254
      protected attributes in the dataset. These features can be obtained either by training a bias-capturing
255   classifier or, in case the protected attribute labels are known, by projecting them into the dimension
256   of h through one-hot encoding. In the first case, a typical DL model is trained to predict the attribute
257   of interest, e.g., race, gender, hair color, or background, which the main model should avoid “using”
258   in its prediction. Note that training a classifier to predict the protected attributes encourages the
259   learning of richer, more diverse latent features associated with them. This approach helps capture
260   subtle, underlying patterns in the data that may otherwise be lost when relying solely on labeled
261   attributes. On the other hand, directly projecting attribute labels through one-hot encoding is easier
262   to implement and computationally less intensive. However, it may not capture the complexity of
263
      visual features as effectively as a dedicated bias-capturing classifier.
264   The combined representation h + b is then fed to the final classification layer. Thus, during training,
                                             (j)
265   model predictions are computed as ŷk = σk (W(h(x(j) ; θh ) + b(x(j) )) + ρ), where W and ρ
266   are the parameters of the last linear layer of h(·). By incorporating the biased features b into the
267   training, we equip the model with the necessary information to consistently account for the bias-
268   aligned samples. This means that the LBA values are consistently close to 0, preventing the loss
269   spikes, and thus enabling features h to encode information about the target classes rather than the
      protected attributes, without having a negative impact on the loss of the bias-aligned samples. In

277   While BAdd is found to be very effective in mitigating bias in cases of highly biased datasets, we
278   observe that it does not adversely affect model performance in cases of datasets where bias is much
279   less prevalent (cf. experimental results in the Appendix). This is an expected behavior because, in

289
      bias through the association of each digit with a specific color. The degree of bias, represented by the
290   probability q of samples belonging to class y and at the same time possessing the attributes t, thus
291   determining the strength of this spurious correlation. We consider four variations of Biased-MNIST
292   with q values of 0.99, 0.995, 0.997, and 0.999, as commonly used in previous works. Biased-
293   CelebA (Hong & Yang, 2021) is a subset of the CelebA facial image dataset, which is annotated with

297   race, and age labels. Gender is the target label, with race or age considered as protected
298
      attributes. In both Biased-CelebA and Biased-UTKFace, the enforced correlation between the target
      and protected attributes is 0.9. The Corrupted-CIFAR10 dataset (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2018)
299
      consists of 10 classes with texture-related biases uniformly distributed in the training data using
300
      four different values of q: 0.95, 0.98, 0.99, and 0.995. Finally, the Waterbirds (Sagawa et al.,
301   2019) dataset demonstrates a co-occurrence of 0.95 between waterbirds (or landbirds) and aquatic
302   environments (or terrestrial environments) as background.

      4.2   E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
352
353   Different evaluation setups are used for each dataset, following the conventions of the literature to
354   be comparable with previous works. In particular, following the Hong & Yang (2021); Barbano
355   et al. (2022); Sarridis et al. (2023a), the test sets used for Biased-MNIST and FB-Biased-MNIST
356   are composed using q = 0.1 that ensures each digit-color group is equally represented. For Biased-
357   UTKFace and CelebA datasets, we utilize bias-conflicting and unbiased accuracy as in (Sarridis
358   et al., 2023a; Hong & Yang, 2021). In particular, bias-conflicting accuracy refers to the accuracy
359   of the under-represented samples (e.g., males wearing lipstick), and unbiased accuracy refers to the
360
      average accuracy across all the subgroups defined by the target (i.e., gender) and the protected
      attributes (i.e.,WearingLipstick and HeavyMakeup). The original test set, as shared by the
361
      dataset creators, is used in the case of Corrupted-CIFAR10. Regarding the Waterbirds dataset, we
362
      employ the average accuracy between different groups and the Worst-Group (WG) accuracy. Finally,
363   for the UrbanCars dataset, we measure the In Distribution Accuracy (I.D. Acc) which is the weighted
364   average accuracy w.r.t. the different groups, where the correlation ratios are the weights. The I.D.
365   Acc is used as a baseline to measure the accuracy drop with respect to the background (BG Gap),
366   co-occurring objects (CoObj Gap), and both the background and co-occurring objects (BG+CoObj
367   Gap). Note that the implementation details are provided in the Appendix.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we propose a method for bias mitigation in CV deep-learning models, termed BAdd.
535   The proposed method injects bias-capturing features in the features of a model in order to force
536   the model parameter updates to rely only on unbiased samples, thus leading to bias-neutral repre-
537   sentations. The main requirement for BAdd is to either have access to the labels of the attributes
538   introducing bias in the data or to be able to train attribute label predictors on another dataset where
539   these labels are available. Through a comprehensive experimental evaluation, we show that the
      proposed approach surpasses the state-of-the-art in single- as well as multi-attribute bias scenarios.

      A.1   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
705
706   In experiments involving the MNIST-based datasets, namely Biased-MNIST and FB-Biased-
707   MNIST, we utilize a simple Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture outlined in (Bahng
708   et al., 2020), which comprises four convolutional layers with 7×7 kernels and a classification head.
709   For the experiments involving the Biased-UTKFace, Corrupted-CIFAR10, and CelebA datasets, we
710   adopt the ResNet-18 architecture (He et al., 2016). For Waterbirds and UrbanCars datasets, we use
711   ResNet-50 networks.
712
713   A.2   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
714
715   We employ the Adam optimizer with a 0.001 initial learning rate, which is divided by 10 every
716   1/3 of the training epochs. Batch size is fixed at 128 and weight decay is set to 10−4 . Following
717
      previous works (Hong & Yang, 2021; Sagawa et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023), we train the models on
      Biased-MNIST and FB-Biased-MNIST datasets for 80 epochs. For Biased-UTKFace and CelebA,
718
      training duration is set to 20 and 40 epochs, respectively. As for the Corrupted-CIFAR10 dataset,
719
      models are trained for 100 epochs using a cosine annealing scheduler. For the Waterbirds and
720   UrbanCars datasets, we do not use a learning rate scheduler, and the models are trained for 300
721   and 100 epochs, respectively. Following the initial training phase, the classification head of all
722   models is fine-tuned for an additional 20 epochs. Regarding the bias-capturing models, for Biased-
723   MNIST, FB-Biased-MNIST, Waterbirds, UrbanCars, and CelebA datasets, they are trained on the
724   same dataset as the main model using the attributes introducing bias as target attributes. For Biased-
725   UTKFace we employ the pretrained bias-capturing classifiers provided by Sarridis et al. (2023a).
726   Finally, for Corrupted-Cifar10 we just project the one-hot vectors representing the texture labels
727   (i.e., the attribute introducing the bias) to the feature space of the main model without using a
728
      trainable bias-capturing model. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX-3090 Ti
      GPU and repeated for 5 different random seeds.

      A.6    C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
900
901   In this subsection, we discuss the computational complexity of BAdd compared to a baseline
902   (vanilla) model, focusing on both training and inference phases. The conducted analysis assumes
903   a typical setup with a ResNet-18 backbone, input images of size 3 × 224 × 224, and two classes
904   for the biased attribute, similar to datasets such as Biased-CelebA. Figure 7 illustrates the BAdd’s
905   training and test phases. It should be stressed that the bias-capturing model is a pretrained model
906   and remains fixed throughout the training of the main model.
907   The baseline model has a computational cost of 1.818 GFLOPs. When bias-capturing features are
908   added to the penultimate layer of the main model in the BAdd approach, the computational complex-
909   ity increases slightly by 512 FLOPs, which represents an increase of approximately 2.82 × 10−7 %
910   of the total computational cost. During the fine-tuning phase, where only the final classification
911   layer is updated, the additional computational cost is 1.024 FLOPs, corresponding to an increase of
912   about 5.63 × 10−7 %. For the non-trainable components, if label projection is used to extract the
913   bias-capturing features, the additional computational cost is 1024 FLOPs. On the other hand, the
914
      computational cost of a bias-capturing model depends on its architecture (which in our case matches
      the main model). However, it is important to note that this model acts as a feature extractor, so its
915
      corresponding features need to be computed only once.
916
917   In terms of inference complexity, BAdd introduces no additional computational cost compared to
      the baseline model, as the bias-capturing component is not utilized during inference. Similarly, the

936   A.7    BA DD R EQUIREMENTS
937
938
      As discussed in Section 6, BAdd requires access to labels (or predicted labels) for the attributes
      introducing the bias. However, it is important to emphasize that BAdd is more flexible than typical
939
      bias-label aware methods. It allows for the use of a bias-capturing model that can be trained on
940
      different datasets, making it more adaptable and practical for real-world applications. For example,
941   models processing facial images are often required to avoid biases related to predefined attributes
942   such as race, gender, or age. In these cases, it is straightforward to extract the bias features from
943   existing pretrained models. Additionally, when the specific bias types are unknown, existing bias
944   identification methods can be utilized to infer them (Kim et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). Once the
945   biases are identified, BAdd can be applied to mitigate them. However, exploring bias identification
946   techniques is beyond the scope of this work.
