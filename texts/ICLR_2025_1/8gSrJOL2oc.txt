009                                                 Abstract
010
011              Compositional zero-shot learning (CZSL) aims to recognize novel compositions
012              of attributes and objects learned from seen compositions. Previous works disen-
013              tangle attribute and object by extracting shared and exclusive parts between image
014              pairs sharing the same attribute (object), as well as aligning them with pretrained
015              word embeddings to improve unseen attribute-object recognition. Despite the sig-
016
                 nificant achievements of existing efforts, they are hampered by three limitations:
                 (1) the efficacy of disentanglement is compromised due to the influence of the
017
                 background and the intricate entanglement of attribute with object in the same
018
                 parts. (2) existing word embeddings fail to capture complex multimodal semantic
019              information. (3) overconfidence exhibited by existing models in seen composi-
020              tions hinders their generalization to novel compositions. Being aware of these, we
021              propose a novel framework named Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM)
022              embeddings and atTRibute smoothIng guiDEd diseNTanglement (TRIDENT)
023              for CZSL. First, we leverage feature adaptive aggregation (FAA) modules to mit-
024              igate the impact of background, and utilize learnable condition masks to capture
025              multi-granularity features for subsequent disentanglement. Then, the last hidden
026              states of MLLM are employed as word embeddings for their superior represen-
027
                 tation capabilities. Moreover, we propose attribute smoothing through leveraging
                 auxiliary attributes generated by Large Language Model (LLM) for each seen
028
                 composition, addressing the issue of overconfidence by encouraging the model
029
                 to learn more attributes in one given composition instead of just fitting a fixed
030              attribute-object combination. Extensive experiments demonstrate that TRIDENT
031              achieves state-of-the-art performance on three challenging datasets: MIT-States,
032              C-GQA, and VAW-CZSL, respectively.

035   1     Introduction
036
037   As for the study of compositional generalization ability inherent to humans, compositional zero-
038   shot learning (CZSL) (Misra et al., 2017; Nagarajan & Grauman, 2018; Purushwalkam et al., 2019)
039
      is proposed to enable machines to recognize unseen attribute-object compositions by leveraging
      knowledge of attributes and objects (i.e., primitives) learned from seen compositions. Specifically,
040
      in the training phase, models are provided with images and compositional labels (e.g., ripe orange
041
      and peeled apple). During the testing phase, given an image depicting a novel composition (e.g.,
042   peeled orange), models are assigned to classify the image into the corresponding category (Zhang
043   et al., 2022b).
044
045
      Prior works (Misra et al., 2017; Nan et al., 2019) focus on mapping the visual features and the word
      embeddings of compositions into a joint space. These methods have poor generalization capability
046
      to unseen compositions, as they fail to learn primitives. Therefore, recent studies (Saini et al., 2022;
047
      Hao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) consider visual disentanglement. Among them, some prominent
048   works deploy a triplet of images to disentangle: a given image (noted as the main image), and two
049   supplementary images, each sharing either the same attribute or the same object as the main image.
050   The triplet of images is treated as two image pairs for subsequent analysis. These approaches aim
051   to disentangle attribute and object by analyzing the shared and exclusive features of the image pair,
052   as well as aligning them with word embeddings (e.g., GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014)), as shown in
053   Figure 1. Although these pioneer research studies have achieved great progress, they exhibit three
      limitations:

062
063   L1: Disentanglement is impeded due to the influence of the background and the intricate entangle-
064   ment of attribute with object in the same parts of image. On the one hand, models tend to extract
065   the background feature unique to one image in the pair as the disentangled exclusive features. On
066   the other hand, some existing methods (Ruis et al., 2021; Saini et al., 2022) compute the similarity
067   of image pairs for disentanglement at the spatial level. However, disentangling attribute and object
068
      at the spatial level presents significant challenges because they entangle in the same spatial features.
      Taking an image of ripe apple as an example, the spatial regions corresponding to the attribute
069
      "ripe" and the object "apple" are fully co-located.
070
071   L2: Existing word embeddings lack the depth needed to capture complex multimodal semantic
072   information. To begin with, word embeddings, such as Word2Vec (Mikolov, 2013) and GloVe
073   (Pennington et al., 2014), are grounded in word frequency and contextual co-occurrence, rather than
074
      capturing high-level semantic nuances(Sarzynska-Wawer et al., 2021). Moreover, the process of
      aligning visual features with word embeddings can be viewed as a form of cross-modal matching;
075
      however, these word embeddings are trained only in a single text modal, preventing them from
076
      capturing cross-modal information between images and texts.
077
078   L3: Existing methods display excessive confidence in seen compositions, impairing their ability
079   to generalize toward novel compositions. Due to the one-hot label used during training, these ap-
080
      proaches are limited by learning only one attribute and object, neglecting the fact that objects nat-
      urally exhibit multiple attributes (Xu et al., 2024). Consequently, models exhibit overconfidence
081
      in the disentangled ground-truth attribute, treating other attributes that can describe the object as
082
      negative attributes, which results in the diminished performance on unseen compositions.
083
084   To address the aforementioned limitations, we propose a novel framework named Multimodal
085   Large Language Model (MLLM) embeddings and atTRibute smoothIng guiDEd diseNTanglement
086
      (TRIDENT), which consists of three major modules: visual feature extraction, attribute-object dis-
      entanglement, and feature alignment. The first module leverages feature adaptive aggregation (FAA)
087
      modules to mitigate the impact of background noise, and exploits learnable condition masks to learn
088
      multi-granularity features to improve subsequent disentanglement. The second module aims at lever-
089   aging shared and exclusive weights of image pairs to disentangle attribute and object under the the
090   paradigm that apart from the shared features of the image pair, each image has its own exclusive
091   features. The third module is intended to align the visual features of compositions and disentangled
092   primitives with the last hidden states of an MLLM, LLaVA v1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a), i.e., MLLM em-
093   beddings. This is inspired by some works (Wang & Kuo, 2020; Muennighoff, 2022; Muennighoff
094   et al., 2024; Koh et al., 2023), which find that the last hidden states of (M)LLM exhibit power-
095   ful representational capabilities in embedding tasks, such as retrieval and classification. Moreover,
096   to tackle the issue that the ineffective overconfidence of the models regarding ground-truth attribute
097
      hinders them from generalizing to unseen compositions, we exploit a Large Language Model (LLM),
      GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2023) to generate auxiliary attributes based on attribute-object compositions and
098
      perform label smoothing for attributes, i.e., attribute smoothing.
099
100   In summary, the main contributions of our work are three-fold:
101   1. We propose novel feature adaptive aggregation modules to reduce the impact of background, and
102   utilize learnable condition masks to capture multi-granularity features for disentanglement in CZSL.
103
104
      2. We employ both LLM and MLLM to guide attribute-object disentanglement by generating aux-
      iliary attributes and representing word embeddings, respectively. To the best of our knowledge, we
105
      are the first to leverage both LLM and MLLM to advance disentanglement in CZSL task.
106
107   3. We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate our method on three CZSL benchmarks, showing
      that TRIDENT has achieved state-of-the-art performance.

      2     Related Work
109
110
      Compositional zero-shot learning (CZSL). Prior works in CZSL can be broadly divided into two
111   main streams. One main stream is to learn representations of compositions in a joint space. SymNet
112   (Li et al., 2020) proposes to learn symmetry property in compositions. Co-CGE (Mancini et al.,
113   2022) leverages a Graph Convolutional Neural Network to learn compositional representations. The
114   other main stream aims at disentangling visual representations of primitives to reduce composition
115   learning into primitive learning. SCEN (Li et al., 2022) leverages contrastive loss to excavate dis-
116   criminative prototypes of primitives. INV (Zhang et al., 2022b) learns domain-invariant primitives.
117   OADis (Saini et al., 2022) and ADE (Hao et al., 2023) disentangle primitives by affinity modules
118   and the multi-head attention mechanism, respectively. CANet (Wang et al., 2023) learns conditional
119
      attribute conditioned on the recognized object and the input image.
120   More recent works (Nayak et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024) focus on leverag-
121   ing the encyclopedic knowledge of pretrained vision-language models (VLM), such as Contrastive
122   Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) and Context Optimization (CoOp) (Zhou
123   et al., 2022), to encode and align images and texts.
124   Large language model (LLM). LLMs have realized significant advancements thanks to the scal-
125   ing up of training data and the increase in the number of parameters. Early models, such as BERT
126   (Devlin et al., 2019) and GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019), initially exhibit strong capabilities in un-
127   derstanding and generating human-like language. Subsequently, GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) with
128   about 175 billion parameters demonstrates great breakthroughs across numerous language bench-
129   marks. This development has facilitated the emergence of many LLMs, including OPT (Zhang
130   et al., 2022a) and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023). Moreover, by performing instruction fine-tuning
131
      on LLM, ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023) are
      competent at comprehending and following human instructions better.
132
133   Expanding on LLM, Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) incorporates a pretrained visual
134   encoder for vision-language tasks. Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) first integrates Vision Trans-
135   former (ViT) (Dosovitskiy, 2020) and LLM by gated cross-attention. BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) puts
136   forward a Q-Former module to bridge the modality gap. Recently, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) and
137
      LLaVA v1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) introduce visual instruction tuning to enhance instruction following
      capability. The visual understanding part of LLaVA v1.5 consists of a ViT and a multilayer percep-
138
      tron (MLP) cross-modal connector (CMC). CMC processes visual features before the last layer of
139
      ViT, aligning the visual space of ViT with the linguistic space of LLM. We choose LLaVA v1.5 as
140   our foundational MLLM as it has demonstrated state-of-the-art performance across various tasks.
141
142   Recently, exploring the powerful language capabilities of (M)LLM to handle representation tasks
143
      (e.g., retrieval) has emerged as a prominent research domain. SGPT (Muennighoff, 2022) exploits
      the last hidden states of LLM for the input token sequence or a special learnable token to derive rep-
144
      resentational embeddings. Subsequently, GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2024) applies mean pooling
145
      over the last hidden states of LLM to yield the textual embeddings. FROMAGe (Koh et al., 2023)
146   leverages a learnable [RET] token to represent the content fed into MLLM for image retrieval.
147

149   3     Approach
150
151   3.1   Task Formulation
152
153
      Compositional zero-shot learning (CZSL) aims at learning a model that can recognize unseen com-
      positions of attributes and objects that are learned from seen compositions. Given an attribute set A
154
      and an object set O, the attributes and objects are composed to form a composition set C = A × O.
155
      The composition set C is divided into two disjoint sets: the seen composition set C s and the unseen
156   composition set Cu , where C s ∩ Cu = ∅ and C s ∪ Cu = C. The model is trained with a seen training
157   set Dtr = {(x s , c s )}, where x s ∈ X s is an image from the seen image set X s corresponding to the seen
158   composition set C s , and c s ∈ C s is the label of x s . Following the Generalized CZSL (Purushwalkam
159   et al., 2019), the model is evaluated on a predefined test set Dte = {(xte , cte )}, where xte ∈ Xte is an
160   image from the unseen image set Xte corresponding to the composition subset Cte of C, i.e., Cte ⊆ C,
161   and cte ∈ Cte is the label of xte . The aim of CZSL task is to learn a model M : Xte → Cte that predicts
      labels cte from Cte for the input images xte ∈ Xte .

      3.2       TRIDENT
183
184
      As the major novelty, we propose a novel framework named MLLM embeddings and attribute
185   smoothing guided disentanglement framework (TRIDENT) for CZSL, as shown in Figure 2. It
186   consists of three major modules: (1) visual feature extraction, (2) attribute-object disentanglement,
187   and (3) feature alignment. We now detail each module of TRIDENT in this section.
188
189
      3.2.1        Visual Feature Extraction
190
191   As shown in Figure 2, we denote a given image with the attribute-object composition label (e.g.
192   ripe apple) as the main image xm , and randomly sample an image with the same attribute xa (i.e.,
193   ripe orange), as well as an image sharing the same object xo (i.e., peeled apple) to comprise a
194   triplet image set. For the convenience of expression, we simply use ximg (where img ∈ {m, a, o}) to
195   collectively denote the images as they are processed using the same module.
196   Visual feature extraction backbone. As mentioned before, since LLaVA v1.5 is used as our fun-
197   damental MLLM, we directly leverage the visual encoder, ViT, and cross-modal connector (CMC)
198   from the model to extract visual features. Specifically, the image ximg is partitioned into n patch
199   tokens, which are subsequently put into ViT along with the [CLS] token. Afterward, the output of
200   patch tokens before the last layer of ViT is fed into the CMC module, as implemented in LLaVA
201   v1.5. To align the dimension of patch tokens output by CMC with that of [CLS] token produced
202   by ViT, the patch tokens output by CMC are input into a linear layer. Consequently, we obtain one

      3.2.3   Feature Alignment
289
290   Inspired by Muennighoff et al. (2024) that leverages the last hidden states as the representation
291   embeddings, we consider the last hidden states of LLaVA v1.5 (Liu et al., 2024a) as our MLLM
292   embeddings for words. Moreover, to tackle the problem that the ineffective overconfidence exhib-
293   ited by the models in terms of the ground-truth attribute hinders them from generalizing to unseen
294   compositions, GPT 3.5 is employed to generate several auxiliary attributes that describe an object
295   with only one ground-truth attribute and perform label smoothing during attribute alignment. Now
296   we detail each part of feature alignment.
297   Generating auxiliary attribute words by LLM. Since only attribute text needs to be generated, we
298   leverage a LLM, GPT-3.5, instead of MLLM, to generate several auxiliary attributes for each com-
299   position. Specifically, the following prompt is input to LLM: ’Please give me t adjectives that can
300   describe the visual feature of a photo of a/an ... well.’, where t is the number of auxiliary attributes
301   and attribute-object composition (e.g., peeled apple) is filled in ’...’. Please refer to Appendix A for
302   more details about the generation of auxiliary attributes by GPT-3.5. Subsequently, the generated
303
      auxiliary attribute words form a set Aa . Therefore, the set of all words Y is obtained, including
      attributes, objects and auxiliary attributes as follows:
304
305                                                 Y = A ∪ O ∪ Aa                                                (8)

      the obtainment of the last hidden states of LLaVA v1.5 for an input word. Subsequently, they are

317   Word expanding. Prior works compute cosine similarities of disentangled features and word em-
318   beddings only within the respective domains of attributes or objects, which results in the disentan-
319   gled attributes and objects still retaining the information of each other. To address the problem, we

507   Inspired by Hao et al. (2023), we use TRIDENT to conduct both image-to-text retrieval and text-

512   extract its visual features by image embedder and retrieve the top-5 closest composed pair embed-
513   dings of compositions. For successful cases, such as the image labeled burnt house, we notice that
514   the top and other predictions can both describe the image. In this image, there are logs burning on
515   fire, so the top-4 predictions of the image can also describe it. In terms of the image labeled green
516
      leaf, another successful case, the predicted attributes can also describe leaf, which is thanks to
      attribute smoothing learning more attributes for an object. For the failure cases, such as the image
517
      labeled multicolored teddy-bear, the model pays more attention to the main puffy and orange
518
      bear and neglects the background of multicolored bears.
519
520   We then consider text-to-image retrieval. Successful cases are shown in Figure 3b, while failure
521   cases are shown in Figure 3c. Given a text composition, we embed it and retrieve the top-5 closest
522
      images. We can observe that the retrieved images of peeled orange are definitely correct. How-
      ever, the retrieved images of green grapes are all wrong. This is due to the fact that the training
523
      images of green grapes in C-GQA dataset are almost filled with a single grape, making it difficult
524
      for the model to capture the contour features of a bunch of green grapes. The image-to-text and
525   text-to-image retrieval experiments confirm that our model effectively projects visual features and
526   word embeddings into a unified space.

      5            Conclusion
530
531
      In this work, we propose a novel framework termed MLLM embeddings and attribute smoothing
      guided disentanglement (TRIDENT) to address the challenging CZSL task. First, we leverage fea-
532
      ture adaptive aggregation modules to mitigate the impact of background, and utilize learnable con-
533
      dition masks to capture multi-granularity features for attribute-object disentanglement. In addition,
534   we exploit the last hidden states of MLLM to replace ordinary word embeddings, as they can cap-
535   ture complex multimodal semantic information. Moreover, we leverage LLM to generate auxiliary
536   attributes and perform attribute smoothing to diminish overconfidence of models in seen compo-
537   sitions, which enables models to generalize to unseen compositions better. Extensive experiments
538   have been conducted on three challenging datasets, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness
539   of TRIDENT. In the future, we plan to extend our method to harness the powerful capabilities of
      LLMs, MLLMs, and CLIP to more effectively address the CZSL task.

      A    Auxiliary Attributes Generation by LLM and Post-process
757
758
      As mentioned before, we leverage GPT-3.5 to generate some auxiliary attributes for attribute
759   smoothing. The auxiliary attributes are generated based on the contextual composition of the object
760   and its attribute, such as ripe apple or sliced apple. The model takes into account the unique
761   characteristics that arise from the combination of attribute and the object’s context. This ensures that
762   the auxiliary attributes accurately capture the nuances of the specific composition, rather than gen-
763   eral object-level attributes. Since the generation ability of LLM is affected by many factors, in this
764   section, we first explore the impact of different prompts on LLM-generated content. Then we study
765   on the influence of the number of auxiliary attributes t. In addition, we introduce post-processing of
766   generated text to eliminate potential noise.

856   B    Obtainment of The Last Hidden States of MLLM
857
858   We input the attribute (object) word into LLaVA v1.5, which first tokenizes the word into z tokens.
859   These tokens pass through all attention blocks in the MLLM, ultimately generating z embeddings
860   of dimension dm after the last block, named the last hidden states. Subsequently, we apply average
861   pooling to these z embeddings of dimension dm to obtain a dm -dimensional embedding that repre-
862   sents the attribute. Since the last hidden states are designed to generate the next token rather than for
863   representation, Muennighoff et al. (2024) leverages instruction to fine-tune the model. Therefore,
      we fine-tune the last hidden states with a low learning rate during the training phase of TRIDENT.
