011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated exceptional capabil-
014            ities in a variety of vision-language tasks, but suffer from “vision hallucinations”
015            - a tendency generating text inconsistent with the image. This issue hampers their
016            practical use in real-world applications. To effectively evaluate and detect these
017            hallucinations, we introduce VISCON (VISual Concept cONsistency), a bench-
018            mark framework comprising a benchmark image dataset and quantitative evalu-
019
               ation pipelines to assess vision hallucinations in LVLMs. VISCON extends be-
               yond previous hallucination metrics by offering: a) diverse image styles across
020
               multiple visual domains, b) evaluation of a broader range of visual concepts, in-
021
               cluding objects, attributes, and relationships, and c) high annotation density from
022            detailed scene-graph annotations to reduce false negatives. These improvements
023            enable comprehensive analysis of hallucinations related to both domain shifts
024            and concept types and offer more accurate hallucination evaluation. To detect
025            vision hallucinations, we propose two innovative evaluation pipelines within VIS-
026            CON: an Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD)-based pipeline and an “Evaluate-By-
027            Edit” pipeline. The EMD-based pipeline measures the distributional similarity
028            between the reference visual concepts and those mentioned by LVLMs, robust
029            against vocabulary shifts between annotations and natural language responses.
030
               The “Evaluate-By-Edit” focuses on the edit distance between the original LVLM
               response and a hallucination-reduced version revised according to the rich visual
031
               concept annotations, providing an interpretable analysis of hallucinated content.
032
               Importantly, our method directly evaluates captioning responses, unlike previous
033            metrics that query the existence of individual visual concepts. This approach is
034            more challenging, as it requires models to handle multiple concepts simultane-
035            ously, providing better discrimination of LVLM performance. Through extensive
036            experiments on six leading LVLMs, VISCON reveals crucial insights into the na-
037            ture of vision hallucinations. Our findings indicate that factors such as image do-
038            main shifts, complexity of visual concepts and model response length significantly
039            influence the occurrence of hallucinations in LVLM responses. Additionally, hu-
040            man evaluations confirm that VISCON aligns with human preferences better than
041
               established hallucination metrics.

      Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs), which integrate Large Language Models (LLMs) with
047
      visual perception capabilities, have made significant strides towards developing generalist AI sys-
048   tems. Models like GPT-4V(OpenAI, 2023), LLaVA(Liu et al., 2023c), BLIP-2(Li et al., 2023a),
049   InstructBLIP(Dai et al., 2023), and Fuyu(Bavishi et al., 2023) have showcased exceptional perfor-

051   the phenomenon of “vision hallucinations” (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023a), where they generate
052   responses inconsistent with the image. As shown in Figure 1, even the leading LVLMs like GPT-4V
053   and LLaVA-1.5 produce errors misrecognizing visual concepts (colored text), serving as a proof of
      the importance to evaluate and investigate the vision hallucinations.

073   To effectively address vision hallucinations and improve LVLMs practicality in real-world applica-
074   tions, robust metrics are essential. Existing methods like POPE (Li et al., 2023b) and CIEM (Hu
075   et al., 2023) assess hallucinations by querying models about visual concepts, providing useful in-
076   sights but with notable limitations. POPE focuses solely on object categories, overlooking attributes
077   and relationships, which our empirical evidence (Figure 1, Table 4) suggests are also prone to hal-
078   lucination. CIEM relies on image captions, which suffer from human reporting bias. Both methods
079   suffer from incomplete visual concept coverage, leading to potential false negatives. Additionally,
080   querying individual concepts one by one is too simplistic for large-scale pretrained LVLMs, limiting
081
      their ability to fully assess the model’s performance on more complex, multi-concept tasks. Hallu-
      sionBench (Liu et al., 2023a) offers qualitative analysis but lacks a quantitative metric. Additionally,
082
      none of these methods evaluate hallucinations across a broad range of image domains.
083
084   To address these deficiencies, we present VISCON (VISual Concept cONsistency), a comprehensive
085   vision hallucination benchmark framework comprising a benchmark image dataset and quantitative
086   evaluation pipelines. VISCON advances prior metrics by: a) incorporating diverse image domains,
087
      b) evaluating a broad range of visual concepts (objects, attributes, and relationships), thus extending
      to a comprehensive vision spectrum in evaluation and c) leveraging very dense scene-graph anno-
088
      tations to reduce false negatives and improve accuracy. Additionally, our method directly evaluates
089
      captioning responses rather than querying each individual visual concepts, requiring LVLMs to han-
090   dle multiple visual concepts together, offering a more rigorous and discriminating assessment.
091
092   Our benchmark image dataset is meticulously curated, incorporating real-world images from the
093
      Visual Genome dataset (Krishna et al., 2017), known for its rich scene-graph annotations of diverse
      objects, attributes, and relationships. VISCON also includes 3D-rendered images from the PROC-
094
      THOR dataset (Deitke et al., 2022), selected for its extensive and easily accessible scene-graph
095
      annotations, offering a more scalable and complete perspective on visual concepts. Furthermore,
096   VISCON broadens the scope of evaluation to include various image domains, such as different
097   styles (cartoon, sketch, etc.) and sources (real-world and 3D-rendered), facilitating the analysis of
098   impact of vision domain shifts on hallucination. Upon acquiring a diverse set of probe image, we
099   construct reference visual concept set for each image from their scene-graph annotations, serving as
100   a gold standard to compare LVLM’s output with. While we recognize that annotations, particularly
101   for attributes and relationships, may not be entirely exhaustive, our dataset provides significantly
102   more comprehensive coverage compared to previous hallucination benchmarks or captioning met-
103   rics (Table 1).
104   For quantitative hallucination evaluation, we propose two pipelines: an Earth Mover’s Distance
105   (EMD)-based pipeline and a novel “Evaluate-By-Edit” pipeline. The EMD-based pipeline mea-
106   sures the distributional similarity between referential visual concepts present in image and the
107   LVLM’s mentioned concepts, providing robust assessment against vocabulary shifts between
      scene-graph annotations and natural LVLM responses. This objective approach is complemented

108
      by the “Evaluate-By-Edit” pipeline, which enhances interpretability on detailed hallucinated
109   content. It assesses hallucinations by calculating the edit distance between the LVLM’s original
110   response and a revised version with reduced hallucinations, refined through a query-and-revise pro-
111   cess. This revision process uses an LLM to generate queries based on objects, attributes, and rela-
112   tionships mentioned in the original text, and refines the response using query results based on the
113   reference visual concept set. The combination of these pipelines ensures a comprehensive, robust
114   and interpretable evaluation of vision hallucinations in LVLMs.
115   We apply VISCON to six leading models including LLaVA-1.5(Liu et al., 2023b), InstructBLIP(Dai
116   et al., 2023), Fuyu(Bavishi et al., 2023), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), Phi-3V (Abdin et al., 2024)
117   and GPT-4V(OpenAI, 2023), conducting comprehensive evaluations. Our quantitative experiments
118   evaluate vision hallucinations of model responses from LVLMs, and analyze the relationship be-
119   tween vision hallucination and multiple factors, including image domains and their domain shifts,
120   visual concept type, and model response length. With empirical evidences, VISCON aligns more
121   closely with human preferences than existing hallucination metrics. These findings provide signif-
122   icant insights into the nature of vision hallucinations in LVLMs and offer methodologies for their
123
      effective assessment.
124   In our research, we present the following key contributions: 1) we introduce VISCON (VISual Con-
125   cept cONsistency), a unique metric for assessing vision hallucinations in large vision-and-language
126   models (LVLMs). VISCON stands out by its wide range annotations of objects, attributes, and re-
127   lationships from easily-accessed image scene-graphs, comprehensive scope of tested images with
128   diverse sources and styles, vocabulary-shift robust evaluation with proposed EMD-based pipeline
129
      and interpretable hallucination evaluation with “Evaluate-By-Edit” pipeline. 2) we conduct exten-
      sive quantitative and qualitative experiments to explore the phenomenon of vision hallucinations
130
      in a wide range of popular LVLMs, including LLaVA-1.5, InstructBLIP, Fuyu, Qwen-VL, Phi-3V
131
      and GPT-4V. Through these experiments conducted using VISCON, we not only highlight the preva-
132   lence of hallucinations in current models but also uncover and analyze various factors that contribute
133   to hallucinations in LVLM responses. 3) we validate VISCON through human evaluation, demon-
134   strating its enhanced alignment with human judgments compared to existing metrics, which is key
135   to evaluate free-form LVLM responses.

139
140   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) and Vision Hallucination: In the quest for versatile
141   artificial intelligence, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) aim to equip powerful Large Lan-
142   guage Models (LLMs) with visual comprehension. These models have demonstrated state-of-the-
143   art performances in zero-shot and fine-tuned scenarios, such as GPT-4(OpenAI, 2023), BLIP-2(Li
144   et al., 2023a), InstructBLIP(Dai et al., 2023), LLaVA(Liu et al., 2023c) and Fuyu(Bavishi et al.,
145   2023), etc. Despite advancements, vision hallucination remains a critical issue, with models often
146   producing image descriptions that misalign with the actual contents. This undermines LVLM re-
147
      liability, especially in tasks like image captioning, questioning both their accuracy and real-world
      applicability. Our metric contributes to addressing this challenge, by detecting and evaluating such
148
      hallucinations. We construct a comprehensive visual concept set derived from image, including
149
      objects, attributes and relations present in image, serving as a gold standard against which model
150   outputs can be evaluated.
151
152   Vision Hallucination Metrics: Assessing vision hallucinations in LVLMs is pivotal for their prac-
153
      tical deployment. While metrics for linguistic hallucinations in LLMs are well-established (Chen
      et al., 2023)(Yang et al., 2023)(Dong et al., 2023), evaluating vision hallucinations presents unique
154
      challenges. Existing LVLM metrics, such as POPE (Li et al., 2023b) and CIEM (Hu et al., 2023),
155
      are limited in scope. POPE focuses only on object presence, ignoring attributes and relationships,
156   while CIEM relies on incomplete visual concepts from captions due to human reporting biases. AM-
157   BER(Wang et al., 2024) extends hallucination evaluations towards attributes and relations, but only
158   annotate scarse visual concepts from image, leading to potential false negatives. These methods
159   adopts a VQA format, which simplifies the task by querying individual concepts rather than assess-
160   ing the model’s ability to handle multiple concepts in free-form responses. HallusionBench (Liu
161   et al., 2023a) provides qualitative insights but lacks quantitative metrics. VISCON overcomes these
      limitations by leveraging dense scene-graph annotations for a comprehensive evaluation of halluci-

      nations. It assesses free-form descriptions rather than individual existence query of visual concepts,
163   reflecting real-world LVLM use, and covers diverse image domains. Human studies confirm VIS-
164   CON’s alignment with human preferences, underscoring its efficacy and applicability.

      diverse visual domains.

      3.2     H ALLUCINATION M ETRICS
217
218   We propose two innovative metrics, Evaluate-By-EMD and Evaluate-By-Edit, to assess vision hal-
219   lucinations in LVLMs with aforementioned diverse probe image set and constructed reference set of
220   visual concepts. In principle, EMD and Evaluate-By-Edit are complementary metrics with distinct
221   focus and functionality: a) EMD-based pipeline is designed to provide a quantitative assessment
222   that is robust to vocabulary shifts. In operation aspect, it compares two visual concept sets: one from
223   annotations and another from model responses. By embedding all visual concepts into a language
224
      embedding space, EMD calculates the minimum transport cost from the model’s mentioned set to
      the annotated reference set. This approach is robust to vocabulary shifts, as synonyms typically
225
      have similar embeddings. b) Evaluate-By-Edit pipeline is designed to offer hallucination evalua-
226
      tion interpretability by enabling visualization of text edits made during the hallucination cleaning
227   process. In operation aspect, It calculates the edit distance between two captions: the original model
228   response and a refined, hallucination-reduced version. The revision is done by querying the exis-
229   tence of mentioned visual concepts and revising them according to query results. This allows for
230   visualization and interpretation of hallucinations by showing the edited parts.
231
232   3.2.1    E VALUATE - BY-EMD
233
234
      As depicted by Figure 2, our “Evaluate-by-EMD” pipeline evaluates LVLMs by comparing the
      visual concepts they mention with a pre-established reference set from image scene-graphs. This
235
      pipeline consists of two stages:
236
237   Extraction of Visual Concepts from LVLM Output: As illustrated in Figure 2b, we first prompt
238   the LVLM to describe the image in detail. Using GPT-4 with few-shot prompts, we extract visual
239   concepts (objects, attributes, relationships) from the model’s response. These concepts form a set
240
      representing the model’s interpretation of the image, in the same format as the constructed reference
      set (i.e., a set of object names, attribute pairs and relationship triplets). Some LVLMs, like GPT-4V,
241
      often produce vague responses (e.g., “a remote control or smartphone”) to avoid inaccuracies. Our
242
      visual concept extraction process, however, extracts both options from such expressions, penalizing
243   this “smart” strategy and providing a more accurate measure of the model’s real hallucination level.
244
245   Vocabulary Shift-Robust Hallucination Evaluation with EMD: In comparing two set of vi-
246
      sual concepts, either from reference or model output, we confront the challenge of vocabulary
      shift — the variance between the scene-graph annotation and the LVLM’s open-ended text gen-
247
      eration (e.g., “dog” vs. “puppy”). To reconcile this ambiguity, we compute the Earth Mover’s

524   5    C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this study, we introduce VISCON, a novel vision hallucination benchmark framework designed
527   for LVLMs. VISCON comes with a diverse set of probe image set to better investigate the factors
528   of vision hallucinations, and comprehensive referential visual concept set as evaluation standard to
529   detect vision hallucinations about objects, attributes and relations in model’s reponse. VISCON em-
530   ploys both EMD-based and “Evaluate-By-Edit” based pipelines, providing a comprehensive analysis
531
      that blends quantitative assessment with interpretive clarity to understand hallucination in LVLMs.
      VISCON evaluates LVLMs through their captioning responses, offering better model discrimination
532
      by requiring them to handle multiple concepts simultaneously. Our empirical findings reveal key in-
533
      sights into the nature of vision hallucinations in LVLMs. We discovered that image domain shifts
534   consistently lead to increased hallucinations, and more complex visual concepts such as relation-
535   ships and attributes are particularly prone to hallucination. Additionally, we observed that longer
536   model responses do not necessarily equate to more informative content and can, in fact, exacerbate
537   the issue of hallucinations. In conclusion, VISCON represents a step forward in the evaluation and
538   understanding of LVLMs, assessing the prevalent issue of vision hallucinations. As LVLMs ad-
539   vance and their applications grow in complexity and diversity, the methodologies and insights from
      our research could be beneficial in enhancing their applicability.

      EMD Stablity Against Prompts We investigated different prompts with varying “length-control”
689
      words to control output detailedness (Table 8). Prompts like “in great detail” likely result in longer
690   responses, while “concisely” may lead to shorter ones. Interestingly, GPT-4V can be controlled
691   with exact word count prompts like “in 80 words,” achieving an average response length close to 80.
692   Comparing EMD scores and average response lengths, we found that when response length is within
693   a mild range of 100±50 words, EMD scores only vary slightly (within ±0.5) across different length-
694   control words. However, excessively long or short responses (>200 or <20 words) can increase
695   hallucinations. Thus, EMD are quite robust against the variation of prompts leading to moderate
696   lengths (e.g., 100±50 words).
697   Stability Against Visual Concept Extraction Model we conducted an ablation study using alter-
698   native LLMs for visual concept extraction from model response, using claude-3.5-sonnet

1084
       our Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD)-based and Evaluate-By-Edit-based evaluation pipelines. In all
1085   experiments unless specifically mentioned, the model response is generated with prompt “Describe
1086   the image in detail”. For GPT-4V, “Describe this image in 80 words” is used to control its response

       D.1    D ETAILED I NSIGHTS INTO P ROBE I MAGE S ELECTION AND S TYLIZATION T ECHNIQUES
1090
1091
       Probe Image Set: To evaluate LVLMs under various scenearios, we meticulously select a range of
1092   images with detailed visual concept annotations (Figure 10). We opt for the VisualGenome dataset
1093   (Krishna et al., 2017) for its rich scene-graph annotations, encompassing a wide array of visual con-
1094   cepts. Additionally, we generated views of 3D indoor scenes from the PROCTHOR dataset (Deitke
1095   et al., 2022), chosen specifically for its detailed metadata on visible objects, including names, at-
1096   tributes, colors, spatial positions, and bounding boxes.
1097
       In processing 3D-rendered images, we employ a careful selection criterion, discarding images with
1098   minimal object presence. Specifically, images capturing fewer than two objects were excluded,
1099   and we manually verify the images to be visually correct. For these images, we establish object
1100   relationships using a set of pre-defined rules. For instance, two objects were considered ’close to’
1101   each other if their distance fell within a certain threshold. We utilize 24 types of relation predicates
1102   inspired by the 3D-SSG dataset.
1103
       In the curation of our probe image set for VISCON, we aim for a balance between diversity and the
1104   practicality of resource utilization. While it is feasible to curate a large-scale image dataset auto-
1105   matically, the linear increase in LLM inference resources with the dataset’s size posed a significant
1106   constraint. Therefore, we opt for a more moderate dataset size. Our selection process results in
1107   a diverse and representative set of images, comprising 46 real-world and 62 3D-rendered images
1108   without image stylizations, and 540 images if counting their stylizations as discussed below.
1109
       Image Stylization Process: To explore the influence of visual domain shifts on vision hallucina-
1110   tions, we apply stylization techniques to each image in our set. Four distinct styles are generated:
1111   sketch, line painting, oil painting, and cartoon. For the sketch and oil painting styles, we utilize the
1112   CMD method (Kalischek et al., 2021) for art style transfer, drawing inspiration from Claude Monet’s
1113   ”Autumn on the Seine, Argenteuil” for oil painting and Vincent van Gogh’s ”Village Street, Sketch,
1114   1890” for sketch, to stylize the images accordingly. The cartoonization of images is achieved using
1115   the InstructPix2Pix model (Brooks et al., 2023)(Paul, 2023), while line painting effects were created
1116   via an edge detection model (Soria et al., 2020). In general, the image domain shift increase from
1117   cartoon and oil painting styles to sketch and line painting styles. Note that for 3D rendered views,
1118
       cartoonized images are quite similar with the original image, due to the already flat color configu-
       ration in 3D rendered views. A visual representation of these stylizations is presented in Figure 10,
1119
       and after the stylization process, 540 images are acquired for VISCON evaluation.

1148   D.3   M ORE D ETAILS IN E VALUATE -B Y-E DIT- BASED E VALUATION
1149
1150   In our Evaluate-By-Edit approach, detailed in Section 3.4 of the main paper, we utilize GPT-4 for a
1151   two-step process: generating queries based on the LVLM’s response and then revising this response
1152   accordingly. Figure 12 illustrates the prompts used for this query-and-revise procedure.
1153   Query Execution and Response Revision: Upon executing a query, we encounter three potential
1154   outcomes: 1) Non-existence Confirmation: If the query confirms the non-existence of a visual
1155   concept (such as an object or relationship not present in the scene-graph), it returns ”NONE.” This
1156   result prompts GPT-4 to remove the corresponding descriptions from the LVLM’s response. 2)
1157   Uncertainty: In cases where the existence of a concept (like an attribute or relation) is uncertain,
1158   possibly due to incomplete annotations, the query returns ”KEEP.” This instructs GPT-4 to leave
1159
       the related text unchanged in the revised response. 3) Existence Confirmation: When the query
       confirms the existence of a visual concept, it returns the exact visual concept information found in
1160
       the visual concept database. This result prompts GPT-4 to modify the original description in the
1161
       model response to align with the more accurate information found in the visual concept database.
1162
1163   After receiving the outputs of each query, we further prompt GPT-4 to revise the original model
1164   response based on the query execution results. This involves either removing incorrect information,
1165
       keeping the text unchanged, or modifying parts of the text to be more accurate. This process ensures
       that the revised responses are as accurate and reliable as possible.
1166
1167   Evaluating Hallucinations through Edit Distance: After acquiring the revised model response,
1168   our focus shifts to evaluating hallucinations by analyzing the word-level edit distance between the
1169   original and revised LVLM responses. This process begins by tokenizing the sentences into indi-
1170   vidual words, facilitating a detailed, word-level evaluation. Through this method, we can precisely
1171
       quantify the extent of revisions needed, aligning the LVLMs’ outputs with a more accurate visual
       representation of the images. This approach offers a granular insight into the nature and extent of
1172
       hallucinations present in the LVLM’s original responses.
1173
1174
1175
       Limitations against Image Domain Variations: Empirically, we observe that edit distance met-
       rics are significantly influenced by the stylistic variations in model outputs across visual domains,
1176
       which may include additional image’s style descriptions (e.g., ”this image appears to be a digitally
1177
       altered representation of ...”). Such stylistic texts are tend to be remained by the revise procedure,
1178   which impact intercept metrics, and introduce confounders into the comparison that are hard to mit-
1179   igate due to the free-form nature of model responses. EMD-based pipeline ignores these texts, thus
1180   are robust to model response styles. Therefore, our analysis primarily focuses on examining the
1181   impact of domain gaps on EMD evaluations.
1182
1183   D.4   M ORE D ETAILES IN Q UESTION -A NSWERING (QA) BASED E VALUATION
1184
1185   In this section, we broaden our evaluation pipelines of VISCON with a question-answering (QA)
1186   based metric inspired by POPE. POPE asks LVLMs about the existence of all objects in an image,
1187   as well as a negative set of non-existent objects, to evaluate misrecognitions of image contents.
       We propose extending this to ask about the existence of objects, attributes, and relations, using


                                                         22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       a comprehensive set of annotated visual concepts from scene-graphs. This QA-based evaluation
1189   provides a fuller assessment of visual hallucinations compared to POPE, which only queries objects.
1190
1191   Specifically, in our QA-based evaluation pipeline, we ask the LVLMs about each annotated object,
1192

       Limitations Despite the comprehensive visual concepts and meticulously designed evaluation
1203
       pipelines in VISCON, our method has some limitations: 1) Influence of Stylistic Variations on
1204   Edit Distance Metric: Edit distance metrics are significantly affected by stylistic variations in
1205   model outputs across visual domains. These variations often include additional style descriptions
1206   of the image (e.g., ”this image appears to be a digitally altered representation of ...”). Such stylis-
1207   tic texts remain after the revision process, impacting intercept metrics and introducing confounders
1208   that are difficult to mitigate due to the free-form nature of model responses. Therefore, our analysis
1209   primarily focuses on examining the impact of domain gaps on EMD-based evaluations. 2) Cover-
1210   age of Probe Image Set: Our probe image set does not encompass all image types that are truly
1211   ”unseen” by current LVLMs. Certain types of hallucinations might not be evaluated and showcased
1212
       by our metrics. For instance, highly abstract or conceptually complex images, such as avant-garde
       art or specialized medical imaging, may not be adequately represented in our current dataset. This
1213
       limitation suggests that there could be other hallucination patterns that remain unexplored.
