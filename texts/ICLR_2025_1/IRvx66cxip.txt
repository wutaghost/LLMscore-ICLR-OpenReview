011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding the decision-making processes of large language models (LLMs) is
014            critical for ensuring transparency and trustworthiness. While Integrated Gradients
015            (IG) is a popular method for model explainability, it faces limitations when
016
               applied to autoregressive models due to issues like exploding gradients and the
               neglect of the attention mechanisms. In this paper, we propose an enhanced
017
               explainability framework that augments IG with emphasis factors and attention
018
               mechanisms. By incorporating attention, we capture contextual dependencies
019            between words, and the introduction of emphasis factors mitigates gradient issues
020            encountered during attribution calculations. Our method provides more precise
021            and interpretable explanations for autoregressive LLMs, effectively highlighting
022            word-level contributions in text generation tasks. Experimental results demonstrate
023            that our approach outperforms standard IG and baseline models in explaining
024            word-level attributions, advancing the interpretability of LLMs.
025

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As large language models (LLMs) become increasingly prominent in natural language processing
030   tasks (Kenton & Toutanova (2019); Jha et al. (2020)), understanding their decision-making processes
031   is critical for ensuring transparency and trustworthiness Lipton (2018). Autoregressive models, in
032
      particular, generate text by predicting one word at a time based on the preceding context, making
      it essential to interpret how individual words influence subsequent predictions. Traditional model
033
      explainability techniques, such as Integrated Gradients (IG), have been widely used to quantify the
034
      contribution of input features to model outputsShrikumar et al. (2017); Lundberg (2017); Murdoch
035   et al. (2018). However, when applied to autoregressive models, IG faces inherent challenges due to
036   their sequential nature, often leading to inaccurate or incomplete explanations Enguehard (2023).
037   Further related works has been discussed in Appendix A.1. In autoregressive text generation,
038   capturing the contextual dependencies between words is crucial for reliable interpretability Vaswani
039   (2017). Moreover, common challenges, such as exploding gradients during the gradient calculation
040   for long texts using the IG method, further complicate the task of identifying meaningful token-level
041   contributions. To address these challenges, we propose an enhanced explainability framework
042   integrating attention mechanisms and emphasis factors with IG. Attention allows us to account for the
043
      relationships between words in the context window while scaling factors mitigate the gradient-related
      issues that can obscure proper explanations. We make the following key contributions in this paper:
044
045
            1. We identify the limitations of the exploding gradient problem when applying the Integrated
046
               Gradients (IG) method for attribution analysis for long texts using generative LLMs.
047
048         2. We propose a novel solution to address the exploding gradient problem encountered during
049
               attribution calculations in the Integrated Gradients method.
050         3. We integrate the Attention mechanism into the attribution calculation, as it plays a critical
051            role in predicting the next token in large language models (LLMs).
052         4. We conduct a comprehensive comparative study, evaluating our proposed method against
053            several baseline models across multiple datasets and architectures.

101
      to neural machine translation models utilizing LSTM architectures. They compute the contribution
102   of each input token to the probability of every output token, which is represented in the form

106   contribution of each input word to the network’s output. Consider an input x ∈ Rn and a baseline
107   input x′ ∈ Rn (typically a zero vector). Integrated Gradients compute the attributions of the input
      relative to this baseline.

      3.1   L IMITATIONS IN I NTEGRATED G RADIENT M ETHOD
148
      While the method outlined in this paper has proven effective in numerous applications, it encounters
149
      specific challenges when applied to long text generation with auto-regressive models such as GPTs.
150   Firstly, the method struggles with problems similar to exploding and vanishing gradient issues
151   that arise when computing the gradients of the output relative to input tokens, as shown in figure
152   1(c). Secondly, it neglects the impact of attention—a critical component in large language models
153   (LLMs)—in the attribution calculations of input tokens concerning the output tokens. Thirdly,
154   this method assigns equal importance to all gradients, even in regions where the model’s decision
155   remains unchanged, leading to the accumulation of low-quality gradients. The issue of exploding and

      We aim to identify the positive contributions of individual input tokens towards the generation of the
      output token. In this work, we address the limitations of Integrated Gradients (IG) discussed in the
202
      previous section. Our approach aims to mitigate the risk of gradient explosion by scaling down the
203
      value of dFdx and focusing only on high-quality gradients, i.e., those where the logits exhibit rapid
204   changes (Walker et al. (2024)). By selectively considering these gradients, we effectively reduce
205   the likelihood of gradient explosion in dF dx . Also, given the critical role that attention mechanisms
206   play in Large Language Models (LLMs) for next-token generation, it is essential to incorporate
207   attention weights when calculating attributions. The attention mechanism, introduced by Vaswani
208   (2017), allows models to focus on relevant parts of an input sequence, emphasizing key information
209   when processing long sequences, shown in figure 1(b), and improving performance in tasks like
210   translation, summarization, and question-answering. Consequently, attention is crucial for LLMs’
211   decision-making in the next token generation and should be considered alongside integrated gradients
212
      in attribution calculations. Additionally, large language models like GPTs (Radford et al., 2019) use
      a technique known as masked self-attention, which plays a pivotal role in sequence generation tasks.

      A.1   R ELATED W ORKS
651
652
      Explainability in machine learning, particularly for large language models (LLMs), has become a
653   crucial area of research as these models grow in complexity. The ”black-box” nature of models
654   like GPT and LLaMA-2 Touvron et al. (2023) poses significant challenges in understanding how
655   these models make predictions, leading to a demand for more transparent methods to interpret their
656   behaviour.
657
      Local explainability techniques such as SHAP Lundberg (2017) and LIME Ribeiro et al. (2016) have
658
      been widely adopted to provide insight into the contributions of individual input features. These
659   methods rely on perturbations and attribution techniques to assess the influence of tokens on model
660   outputs. However, they are often computationally intensive and assume feature independence, which
661   may not hold in real-world datasets Feng et al. (2018). Also, these methods may not be able to capture
662   the decision of why the model generated the token under consideration. Gradient-based methods such
663   as Integrated Gradients Sundararajan et al. (2017) accumulate the gradients along the input feature
664   path, providing a smoother attribution but at the cost of higher computational demand and reduced
665   faithfulness ( Sikdar et al. (2021); Shrikumar et al. (2017)).
666   Global explainability focuses on extracting and interpreting broader patterns within models.
667   Probing-based methods have been essential in identifying the syntactic and semantic representations
668   encoded within LLMs (Hewitt & Manning (2019); Peng et al. (2022)). Studies by Geva et al.
669   (2022) and Kobayashi et al. (2023) delve into the internal mechanisms of models, showing that
670   feed-forward networks and attention heads capture complex linguistic knowledge. Mechanistic
671   interpretability has also become an essential field, aiming to reverse-engineer neural networks into
672   comprehensible circuits Wang et al. (2022), allowing for a deeper understanding of tasks like indirect
673
      object identification.
674   Model editing has recently garnered attention as a way to directly alter specific knowledge within
675   LLMs without extensive retraining. Techniques such as hypernetwork-based editing Mitchell et al.
676   (2022) and causal tracing Meng et al. (2022)) allow for targeted interventions in model behavior,
677   improving its responses to particular inputs. These techniques have shown potential in enabling
678   models to adapt without disrupting overall performance Yao et al. (2023).
679   Explainability has also been used to enhance task-specific capabilities. In-context learning (ICL),
680   for instance, has benefited from studies showing that specific attention heads play a pivotal role in
681   transferring knowledge from prompt examples to downstream tasks (Hendel et al. (2023); Todd et al.
682   (2023)). Moreover, explainability methods like inference-time intervention (ITI) have been leveraged
683   to address issues of hallucination in text generation, where models generate outputs that deviate from
684   factual content. Li et al. (2024) demonstrated that truthful interventions in attention layers could
685
      significantly enhance the factuality of model outputs, mitigating the impact of hallucinations.
686   Beyond improving factuality, explainability has also been used to tackle biases within models.
687   Techniques like integrated gradients (Sundararajan et al. (2017)) and its variations have been applied
688   to identify neurons responsible for social biases (Liu et al. (2024)), offering a pathway to fairer and
689   more ethically aligned language models.
690   Overall, the body of research highlights the importance of developing both local and global
691   explainability methods to improve trust and transparency in LLMs. These methods not only facilitate
692   understanding but also open new avenues for enhancing the performance and ethical alignment of
693   models in diverse NLP applications.
