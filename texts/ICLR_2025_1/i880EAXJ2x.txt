009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The discontinuous operations inherent in quantization and sparsification introduce
012            obstacles to backpropagation. This is particularly challenging when training deep
013            neural networks in ultra-low precision and sparse regimes. We propose a novel,
014            robust, and universal solution: a denoising affine transform that stabilizes training
015            under these challenging conditions. By formulating quantization and sparsifica-
016            tion as perturbations during training, we derive a perturbation-resilient approach
017            based on ridge regression. Our solution employs a piecewise constant backbone
018
               model to ensure a performance lower bound and features an inherent noise re-
               duction mechanism to mitigate perturbation-induced corruption. This formulation
019
               allows existing models to be trained at arbitrarily low precision and sparsity levels
020
               with off-the-shelf recipes. Furthermore, our method provides a novel perspective
021            on training temporal binary neural networks, contributing to ongoing efforts to
022            narrow the gap between artificial and biological neural networks.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The recent surge in the size and complexity of generative AI models has elevated computational
028   efficiency to the forefront of AI research (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023). Among the
029   diverse approaches to achieving efficiency, quantization and sparsification techniques stand out as
030   two classic and widely explored methods. Quantization and sparsity techniques can effectively
031   reduce the computational requirements of large language models (LLMs). Quantization reduces the
032   precision of model weights and activations, thereby decreasing storage requirements. Sparsification,
033
      on the other hand, prunes redundant weights, leading to a more compact model. These techniques
      enable LLMs to be deployed on resource-constrained devices, such as mobile phones and embedded
034
      systems, while also improving their speed and memory efficiency. This facilitates the widespread
035
      adoption of LLMs by a broader range of individuals and businesses.
036
037   Despite their promise, quantization and sparsification introduce non-differentiable operations, such
038   as rounding and hard thresholding, which are incompatible with the differentiable design of back-
039
      propagation, the cornerstone of neural network training. This incompatibility has plagued training
      algorithms for decades, hindering progress in the field of efficient neural networks (Bengio et al.,
040
      2013; Li et al., 2017; Yin et al., 2019).
041
042   To tackle the discontinuity challenges inherent in training efficient neural networks, algorithms have
043   primarily focused on adapting gradient descent algorithms to work with non-differentiable opera-
044   tions. Empirical techniques such as the straight-through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013) have
045
      been employed to define gradients for non-differentiable operations. However, even with the STE,
      the perturbation introduced by quantization has been observed to disrupt existing training recipes
046
      (Fig. 1(a)). Consequently, clipping is commonly applied to limit the signals within a small range
047
      to prevent divergence. Despite these techniques, training quantized networks remains restricted to
048   specific precisions (Rastegari et al., 2016; Courbariaux et al., 2016; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
049   2021a) or models (Zhang et al., 2022; 2023). When moving to low precisions, these approaches
050   usually also make changes to model architectures and recipes, such as inserting extra normaliza-
051   tions(Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), changing learning rates(Wang et al., 2023), replacing
052   optimizers (Liu et al., 2021a), keeping several layers unquantized (Abdolrashidi et al., 2021; Liu
053   et al., 2021a) or through fine-tuning (McKinstry et al., 2018). It is unclear if these techniques are
      effective and flexible enough in the large generative AI models.

070   In contrast to the intricate and often empirically tuned techniques of prior methods, we adopt an-
071
      other approach that formulates discontinuous operations as the introduction of perturbations (Golub
      & Van Loan, 2013). We address this challenge directly by suppressing the effects of these perturba-
072
      tions, effectively denoising the signal. Our approach comprises three fundamental steps:
073
074   1. Affine Transform for Quantization (Sec. 3.1): An initial affine transform f scales the input signal
075   without introducing additional operations (e.g., clipping).
076   2. Perturbation Injection (Sec. 3.2): A controlled perturbation δ is injected into the signal, precisely
077   modeling the effect of quantization.
078
      3. A Denoising Affine Transform for Reconstruction (Sec. 3.3): A key innovation of our approach is
079
      the introduction of another affine transform g that effectively reconstructs the original signal while
080
      mitigating quantization noise.
081
082   Our method offers several key advantages: 1. Continuous control over quantization noise: This en-
083   sures stable model training and prevents divergence. 2. Graceful degradation: Under extreme noise,
084
      our approach seamlessly transitions to a lower-resolution (through averaging) model, guaranteeing
      a performance lower bound. 3. Compatibility: Our method works with existing architectures and
085
      training recipes, eliminating the need for extensive modifications or hyperparameter tuning.
086
087   These innovations enable the development of diverse, efficient neural networks for various appli-
088   cations. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach by training sparse models and temporal
089   binary neural networks. Our key contributions include:
090
                          • A simple, robust, and universal solution for training quantized neural networks.
091
092                       • A novel theoretical perspective and practical solution for training networks with discontin-
093
                            uous operations, based on decomposition and denoising.
094                       • State-of-the-art results with ultra-low precision models, using standard architectures and
095                         training recipes.
096                       • Easy adaptation to train sparse neural networks.
097                       • A novel shortcut formula for computing quantized matrix multiplication.
098
                          • Successful training of temporal binary neural networks, demonstrating its potential for
099
                            bridging the gap between artificial and biological neural networks.

117   2.2   A B IOPHYSICAL BASIS FOR S PARSE Q UANTIZATION
118
119   One of the most significant achievements in 20th-century neural physiology was the development of
120   the Hodgkin–Huxley model for modeling animal neural networks (Hodgkin & Huxley, 1952; Dayan
121
      & Abbott, 2005). This model unveiled the intriguing fact that animal neural networks primarily
      consist of brief electrical impulses, commonly known as spikes in their activity patterns. Despite
122
      this critical insight, the question of how animals efficiently learn and process information through
123
      these spike trains continues to pose a profound and unresolved challenge. Since the rise of regular
124   neural networks, researchers have strived to find a more biophysically plausible approach to artificial
125   intelligence through spiking neural networks (Yamazaki et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2016; Tavanaei et al.,
126   2019; Gallego et al., 2020). However, the lack of differentiability in spiking neural networks prevents
127   the straightforward application of gradient descent algorithms, the foundation of modern neural
128   networks. Consequently, these networks have limited application and popularity. The development
129   of universal training algorithms for quantized and sparse neural networks could open up new avenues
130   in the field of spiking neural networks.

149   3.2   P ERTURBATION I NJECTION
150
151   Quantization reduces the number of bits used to represent f (x) by rounding to the nearest inte-
152   ger. This rounding operation introduces discontinuities at half-integer values, rendering it non-
153   differentiable. These discontinuities can lead to training difficulties, as neural networks rely on
154   gradients for learning. Most existing methods for training neural networks with non-smooth op-
155   erations employ the straight-through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013). The STE provides a
156
      technique for defining gradients for non-differentiable operations. However, even with the STE,
      training algorithms may diverge when moving to lower bits (Fig. 1(a)).
157
158   Building upon prior research (Golub & Van Loan, 2013; Bengio et al., 2013; Lee et al., 2016), we
159   model the impact of these operations as perturbations within the network, recognizing their role in
160   causing training instability. Specifically, quantization of f (x) can be modeled as injecting a bounded
161   perturbation:
                                                   q = f (x) + δ,                                          (2)

      3.4     E XTENSION TO S PARSIFICATION
258
259   Similar to quantization, sparsification introduces discontinuities through the hard thresholding oper-
260   ation H. Consequently, the sparsification process can also be modeled as introducing perturbations,
261   where δ = H(x)−x. In our experiment section we show this leads to significant gain in sparsifying
262   the model. Since the perturbation from sparsification rarely leads to divergence, we may set λ = 0
263   to pass the entire perturbed signal when only sparsification is applied.
264   When dealing with extreme sparsity or quantization, it is noteworthy that 100% sparsity, under the
265   traditional definition, results in an all-zero vector. To address this limitation, we revisit Equation 7,
266   which defines the reconstructed signal. Equation 7 involves adding back significant deviations to a
267   mean vector of x, ensuring that the mean x remains the foundation of the signal even at extreme
268   sparsity levels. Therefore, sparsification towards the mean presents itself as the most intuitive ap-
269   proach. In this context, significance is measured by the deviation from the mean xi − x, not from
      zero. Consequently, extreme sparsity results in the mean x being preserved, rather than zero.

303
      To ensure consistency, we maintained a fixed block size of 128 across all experiments unless oth-
304
      erwise specified. Our experiments revealed that the regularization parameter λ = 0.01 consistently
305
      yielded satisfactory results. Quantization was applied to all linear transforms within the model,
306   including convolutional layers. All models were trained from scratch, with architectures and pa-
307   rameters unchanged to enable a fair comparison between full-precision and quantized models. We
308   utilize the notation “AxWy” to represent the quantization configuration of a neural network, where

311

312   can mimic data augmentation and enhance the training process, they can also introduce unwanted
313   variability into the training signal, especially when dealing with ultra-low bit and extremely sparse
314   representations. This unwanted variability can prevent the model from learning effectively and slow
315   down the model’s convergence. To further assess the performance of the low-precision models, we
316   extended our main experiments by four times the training duration. With extended training our 1-bit
317   models remain highly competitive without any architectural change or recipe tweaking (Tables 1, 3).
318   This demonstrates the robustness of our method and its ability to maintain model performance over
319   extended training periods.

401   In biological neural networks, information is transmitted via electrical impulses called action po-
402   tentials, or spikes. The complex processes governing spike transmission within the nervous system
403   have been extensively investigated (Dayan & Abbott, 2005). While binary signals can effectively
404
      represent these spike trains, the learning rule for spikes remains an open question (Yamazaki et al.,
      2022; Tavanaei et al., 2019).
405
406   Inspired by the temporal nature of the transformer model, we reduced the activation precision to
407   1-bit for all linear layers, transforming it into a temporal binary network, akin to a quasi-spiking
408   neural network. However, unlike traditional spiking neural networks, our model doesn’t simulate
409   spike generation or consider spiking frequency.
410   To evaluate our approach, we assigned 1, 2, and 4 bits to the weights (Table 5). While introduc-
411   ing perturbations into spiking neural networks during training using backpropagation is not entirely
412   new, our approach differs from previous attempts in several key aspects. Earlier studies have pre-
413   dominantly focused on introducing perturbations only at the spikes (Lee et al., 2016). In contrast,
414   our formulation introduces perturbations during signal quantization, irrespective of the spikes. This
415   mirrors the inherent noisiness of the learning process and aligns with the biological reality of neu-
416
      ral networks, where noise is an intrinsic part of neural signaling. Our results showcase that these
      converted binary transformers remain highly competitive with full-precision counterparts.

450   4.2     Q UANTIZATION AND S PARSIFICATION
451
452   In this section, we assess the effectiveness of our sparsification through perturbation proposal
453   by comparing it to a baseline approach that employs a multiplicative mask, wsparse = w
454   1|w|>threshold , obtaining significant improvements. Additionally, we evaluate the performance of
455
      combining the proposed sparsity technique with quantization. Our findings indicate that moder-
      ate levels of sparsification perturbations introduce beneficial regularization effects during training,
456
      leading to improved BLEU scores. These findings are summarized in Table 6. Our supplementary
457
      section A.2 further explores the integration of structured sparsity, demonstrating how its combina-
458   tion with binarization techniques yields sub-1 bit models that achieve competitive performance.
459
460   Quantization and sparsification are both valuable approaches for compressing neural networks, but
461
      both can also introduce performance degradation. Therefore, carefully balancing these techniques is
      crucial to achieve the desired trade-off between model size and accuracy. Sparsification is performed
462
      within each block (of size 128) before quantization. This choice of order is made because quantized
463
      values have limited sparsity levels, making the reverse process less well-defined and potentially less
464   effective. Striking a balance between compression and accuracy is paramount when applying quan-
465   tization and sparsification techniques. Excessive application can lead to substantial performance
466   degradation. Our experiments demonstrate that low-level sparsification perturbations are beneficial.
467   However, as quantization levels increase, the tolerable level of sparsity decreases (Table 6).
468

      5   R ELATED W ORK
496
497   Neural network quantization has become a widely adopted technique for reducing memory foot-
498
      print and accelerating inference time, enabling efficient deployment on resource-constrained de-
      vices (Gholami et al., 2022). While full-precision models typically store weights in floating-point
499
      format, quantized weights are represented as integers, typically using 8 bits (Dai et al., 2021; Worts-
500
      man et al., 2023; Jacob et al., 2018), 3-4 bits (Dettmers et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021b; Abdolrashidi
501   et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2021), or even 1 bit (Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021a; Wang et al.,
502   2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Courbariaux et al., 2016; Rastegari et al., 2016). In addition to quantizing
503   weights, model activations can also be quantized to further enhance computational efficiency (Dai
504   et al., 2021; Jacob et al., 2018; Esser et al., 2019).
505
      Although 8-bit quantization is commonly used as a standard practice in industry (Jacob et al., 2018),
506
      achieving lower-bit quantization remains challenging and requires specialized techniques to ensure
507   robust training. Several common techniques include: 1. Mixed precision quantization: This ap-
508   proach selectively assigns different bit levels to different weights, aiming to optimize the trade-off
509   between model size and accuracy (Wang et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2023; Han et al., 2015; Défossez
510   et al., 2021). 2. Training recipes: These techniques compensate for the discontinuities introduced
511   by quantization by employing strategies such as sharpness-aware minimization (Liu et al., 2021b;
512   Foret et al., 2020), state-aware optimization (Liu et al., 2021a), knowledge distillation (Kim et al.,
513   2019), and multi-phase training (Liu et al., 2021c). 3. Quantization-friendly network architectures:
514   This approach involves replacing original network layers with alternatives that are more amenable
515
      to quantization (Zhang et al., 2022).
516   In contrast to prior work, our method explicitly models quantization discontinuities as perturbations.
517   We decompose the perturbed signal into clean and noisy components, then apply denoising to sup-
518   press the noise. This approach leads to a closed-form solution that guarantees training convergence
519   even at extremely low bitwidths. While previous methods have also modeled quantization noise
520   using continuous distributions (e.g., Uniform or Gaussian) for gradient estimation (Défossez et al.,
521
      2021; Ballé et al., 2016), they do not optimize the reconstruction process itself to enhance training
      stability.
522
523   To further reduce model footprint, researchers have been combining sparsity/pruning and quanti-
524   zation in a unified formulation to further compress neural networks (Park et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
525   2020). In this paper, we extend our noise injection and denoising reconstruction theory to sparsity
526   and argue that instead of pruning small values to zero, moving near-mean values to mean better
527
      preserves signal fidelity.
528

      6   S UMMARY
530
531
      Discontinuous operations such as quantization and sparsification pose a significant challenge to
532
      backpropagation training, as they introduce non-differentiability into the learning process. This
533
      non-differentiability has long been considered the Achilles heel of backpropagation, hindering the
534   development of efficient and accurate neural network training algorithms. We address this challenge
535   by re-framing these discontinuities as perturbations. This allows us to introduce a novel, continuous
536   control mechanism that seamlessly handles non-differentiability during training. Our approach is
537   expected to facilitate the deployment of large-scale neural network models on resource-constrained
538   devices, enabling the widespread adoption of deep learning for mobile applications. Additionally,
539   our technique holds promise for the development of biophysically plausible neural networks, which
      have the potential to revolutionize artificial intelligence and machine learning.

702   def quantize(x, bits, axis, eps=1e-8):
703       # quantize to [0, 2^bits-1]
704       max_value = jnp.max(x, axis=axis, keepdims=True)
          min_value = jnp.min(x, axis=axis, keepdims=True)
705       scaled_x = (
706           (x - min_value) / (max_value - min_value + eps)
707           * (2**bits - 1)
          ) # scale to [0, 2^bits-1]
708       delta_x = jax.lax.stop_gradient(jnp.round(scaled_x) - scaled_x)
709       q = scaled_x + delta_x # perturb to integers
710       return q
