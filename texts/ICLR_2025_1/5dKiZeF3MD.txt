009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in multivariate time series forecasting (MTSF) have increas-
012            ingly focused on the core challenge of learning dependencies within sequences,
013            specifically intra-series (temporal), inter-series (spatial), and cross-series dependen-
014            cies. While extracting multiple types of dependencies can theoretically enhance
015            the richness of learned correlations, it also increases computational complexity and
016            may introduce additional noise. The trade-off between the variety of dependen-
017
               cies extracted and the potential interference has not yet been fully explored. To
               address this challenge, we propose G RAPH STAGE, a purely graph neural network
018
               (GNN)-based model that decouples the learning of intra-series and inter-series
019
               dependencies. G RAPH STAGE features a minimal architecture with a specially
020            designed embedding and patching layer, along with the STAGE (Spatial-Temporal
021            Aggregation Graph Encoder) blocks. Unlike channel-mixing approaches, G RAPH -
022            STAGE is a channel-preserving method that maintains the shape of the input data
023            throughout training, thereby avoiding the interference and noise typically caused
024            by channel blending. Extensive experiments conducted on 13 real-world datasets
025            demonstrate that our model achieves performance comparable to or surpassing
026            state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, comparative experiments between our channel-
027            preserving framework and channel-mixing designs show that excessive dependency
028
               extraction and channel blending can introduce noise and interference. As a purely
               GNN-based model, G RAPH STAGE generates learnable graphs in both temporal
029
               and spatial dimensions, enabling the visualization of data periodicity and node
030
               correlations to enhance model interpretability.

071     Recent models such as UniTST (Liu et al., 2024a) and FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024) attempt to capture
072     multiple types of dependencies, including cross-series dependencies, by blending the temporal and
073     spatial dimensions. They reshape the input data Xin from RN ×T into a RN T ×1 structure. While this

075     two significant challenges: (1) increased computational complexity and (2) a heightened risk of

077     First, mixing the channels may increases computational complexity. The complexity of weight
078     multiplication operations escalates from O(N 2 ) to O((N T )2 ) (Liu et al., 2024a; Yi et al., 2024),
079     leading to exponentially higher computational costs. Consequently, these models often implement
080     some compression mechanisms, such as router mechanism (Zhang & Yan, 2023), to mitigate the
081     computational burden. Despite these efforts, a trade-off between model size and performance
082
        persists. Achieving better performance frequently requires larger models, indicating that compression
        techniques may not fully address the efficiency concerns. To further illustrate this point, we conducted
083
        model variants experiments in Section 4.3. As shown in Table 4, our model outperforms VarC — a
084
        channel-mixing model similar to UniTST (Liu et al., 2024a) and FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024), as
085     depicted in Figure 7, while also reducing memory usage by 83%.
086
087     Second, while blending channels allows these models to account for cross-series dependencies, it
088
        may introduce additional noise into the modeling process. Existing studies have often emphasized the
        benefits of capturing cross-series dependencies without fully considering the potential downsides of
089
        added noise. As shown in Figure 3, aggregating all dependencies may enhance predictive accuracy to
090
        some extent (as demonstrated by the improvement of performance on the ETTm1 dataset). However,
091     it can also lead to overly complex models that struggle to compensate for the interference caused by
092     the introduced noise, resulting in a sharp reduction in performance on the ECL dataset. This raises a
093     crucial question: Is it truly necessary to model all these dependencies?
094
        We argue that modeling either a single type of dependency or multiple dependencies in a coupled
095
        manner is inefficient. Recently, channel-preserving approaches have demonstrated efficiency and
096
        effectiveness (Liu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024). To address the challenges of computational inef-
097     ficiency and noise introduced by channel-mixing, we propose G RAPH STAGE, a purely GNN-based
098     model that decouples the learning of inter-series and intra-series dependencies while preserving the
099     original channel structures. Unlike existing channel-mixing approaches, G RAPH STAGE maintains
100     the shape of the input data throughout the training process, thereby avoiding the interference caused
101     by channel blending. To our knowledge, G RAPH STAGE is the first purely graph-based, channel-
102     preserving model. This design not only enhances computational efficiency but also reduces the noise
103     associated with channel blending. Our contributions are threefold:
104
105               • We reflect on the extraction of dependencies in current time series models and emphasize
106                 that existing methods tend to overlook certain dependencies. Furthermore, we highlight that
107                 channel blending and excessive correlation extraction can introduce noise, and propose a
                    channel-preserving framework to enable more accurate and robust dependencies modeling.

121   2   R ELATED W ORKS
122
123   Single Dependency Modeling. Traditional multivariate time series forecasting methods often focus
124   on capturing a single type of dependency—either temporal (intra-series) or spatial (inter-series).
125   Deep learning models such as CNNs, RNNs, GRUs and Formers (Hochreiter, 1997; Chung et al.,
126   2014; Rangapuram et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021;
127   Zhang et al., 2024) excel at modeling sequential data by capturing temporal dynamics within each
128
      series. However, these models typically treat each spatial node independently, failing to account for
      inter-series dependency. On the other hand, models that focus solely on inter-series dependency, such
129
      as GNNs (Bai et al., 2020) and Formers (Kitaev et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024c; Cai et al., 2024), while
130
      effective at capturing spatial correlations, may not adequately model the temporal correlations within
131   each series. Consequently, methods that concentrate on one type of dependency may fail to fully
132   capture the complex correlations inherent in multivariate time series data.
133
134   Modeling Combined Dependencies. To address the limitations of single-dependency extracting
135   models, several GNNs (Kipf et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; 2020; Shang et al., 2021; Xu et al.,
136   2023) have attempted to extract dependencies in both the temporal and spatial domains. However,
137   these models often ignore global information extraction in either the spatial or temporal domain,
138   focusing instead on local neighborhood information. Recent approaches have explored to capture
139   multiple types of dependencies simultaneously by blending the temporal and spatial dimensions.
140
      FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024) and UniTST (Liu et al., 2024a) construct hypervariate graph as input
      embeddings to represent time series with a unified view of spatial and temporal dynamics but overlook
141
      the potential interference caused by channel-mixing. Recognizing this issue, DGCformer (Liu et al.,
142
      2024b) identifies irrelevant nodes in channel-mixing and adopts a grouping mechanism to focus
143   attention on relevant nodes. Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023) and CARD (Wang et al., 2024) propose
144   a two-stage framework to extract inter-series and intra-series dependencies, applying attention across
145   both dimensions and then fuses the results. Building on these insights, we propose G RAPH STAGE, a
146   purely GNN-based model that decouples the learning of inter-series and intra-series dependencies
147   while preserving the original input channels to avoid the interference introduced by channel blending.

194      3.1        T OKENIZATION VIA E MBEDDING AND PATCHING
195
196
         Channel-preserving Embedding Strategy. Most signal intra-series dependency modeling models
         regard multiple nodes of the same time as the (temporal) token. As a result, they project the input
197
         data shaped as Xin ∈ RN ×T into RT ×D , where D is the hidden dimension, and the original spatial
198
         dimension N is not preserved. Inspired by inter-series oriented models (Liu et al., 2024c) in MTSF, we
199      preserve the nodes dimension throughout the model, which proven competent by previous works (Cai
200      et al., 2024). Given a time series with N nodes, X ∈ RN ×T , we divide each univariate time series xi
201      into patches xip ∈ RP ×s , with stride s and number of patches P (Nie et al., 2023). A projection layer
202      is then applied to map all the series into Xp ∈ RN ×P ×D , where D is the embedding dimension.
203
204      Refined Time Embedding to Enhance Relative Positioning. The effectiveness of static covariates
205      that are available in advance has been validated in several MTSF models (Lim et al., 2021; Jiang
206      et al., 2023; Huang & Xiao, 2024). However, for datasets with a fixed sampling frequency below
207      one hour (e.g., five minutes or fifteen minutes), previous models only embedded the ‘Hour of Day’
208      and ‘Day of Week’ information (Cai et al., 2024), which is insufficient to reflect the relative position
209      within an hour. To address this limitation, we modify existing embedding methods by replacing the
210      ‘Hour of Day’ embedding with a ‘Timestamp of Day’ embedding. This allows the embedding layer
211
         to adapt to the sample frequency, providing a more fine-grained time embedding that fully utilize the
         relative positions of data points within an hour as prior knowledge. Additionally, we introduce an
212
         learnable embedding to adaptively capture underlying dependencies. The process is presented below:

      3.2   S PATIAL -T EMPORAL AGGREGATION G RAPH E NCODER
221
222   Our proposed STAGE block is illustrated in Figure 4. STAGE employs a decoupled yet unified archi-
223   tecture to aggregate information learned by Temporal Learnable Graph (AT ) and Spatial Learnable
224   Graph (AS ). The Intra-series Pruned-Graph AGgregation module (Intra-GrAG) is responsible for
225   extracting intra-series (temporal) dependencies and generating the AT . Similarly, the Inter-series
226   Pruned-Graph AGgregation module (Inter-GrAG) extracts inter-series (spatial) dependencies and
227
      generates the AS .
228
229
      Decoupled Spatial-Temporal Extraction with Unified Aggregation. STAGE is capable of learn-
      ing intra-series and inter-series dependencies separately within a single block by utilizing a decoupled
230
      architecture composed of Intra-GrAG and Inter-GrAG modules. In STAGE block, the input tensor
231
      has dimensions H ∈ RN ×P ×D , where N is the number of nodes, P is the number of patches, and
232   D is the embedding dimension. To learn intra-series dependencies, we first transpose the input
233   tensor to shape RP ×N ×D , swapping the spatial and temporal dimensions. This restructure allows
234   the model to focus on temporal relationships within each node across different time steps. After
235   learning the intra-dependencies, we transpose the tensor back to its original shape RN ×P ×D to learn
236   inter-series dependencies, concentrating on the relationships between different nodes at each time
237   step. By adopting this approach, we can employ a unified architecture for both intra-dependency and
238   inter-dependency learning, simply by changing the order of the input dimensions.
239   Furthermore, since STAGE is a purely GNN-based method, the correlations among nodes or patches
240   (time steps) learned by the model can be directly visualized, enhancing interpretability and providing
241   insights into the data periodicity and node correlations.
242
243   Learnable Graph Generator for Temporal and Spatial Dimensions. Learnable Graphs are
244   essential for characterizing both temporal and spatial similarities. STAGE adaptively learns the graph
245   structures by generating separate adjacency matrices: AT for patches (temporal dimension) and AS
246   for nodes (spatial dimension).
247   Since STAGE employs a unified aggregation mechanism, the principles of the Inter-GrAG and
248   Intra-GrAG modules are analogous. Therefore, to avoid redundancy, the subsequent discussion will
249   focus only on the components of the Inter-GrAG module. First, a Pooling layer downsamples the
250   extracted temporal information. We can choose any pooling mechanisms in the temporal dimension
251   as the Pool operation, such as max-pooling and mean-pooling. To capture directed similarities among
252   nodes, we apply two Linear mappings to each node:

268   Pruned-Graph Aggregation Mechanism. In the Intra-GrAG module, this mechanism performs
269   graph convolutions on the learned graph AT . In the Inter-GrAG module, it performs graph convolu-
      tions on the learned graph AS , aggregating information from global nodes while pruning irrelevant or

      weak connections. The pruning operation reduces noise and enhances the model’s ability to focus on
271   the most significant correlations. To avoid redundancy and for simplicity, the subsequent discussion
272   will focus only on the components of the Inter-GrAG module.
273
274
      Graph attention network (GAT) (Velickovic et al., 2017) is a powerful model for extracting spatial
      dependencies, allocating different weights to neighbor nodes. Pruned-Graph Aggregation (PGA)
275
      can be regarded as a Special GAT with three specific improvements: 1) input embeddings are the
276
      extracted temporal embeddings rather than the original features; 2) the input nodes learnable graph
277   will be pruned to make the model concentrate on the most significant connections; 3) the spatial
278   dependencies among nodes is global rather than localized in neighborhoods. In this way, PGA
279   incorporates spatial information effectively and aggregates global information without any prior
280   knowledge, such as pre-defined static graph. The whole process can be formulated as below:
281
282                     Hag = Hin W1 + Prune(AS )Hin W2 + Prune(AS )T Hin W3 ,                         (5)
283
      where W1 , W2 , W3 ∈ RD×D are trainable matrices and Hag ∈ RN ×P ×D . The Prune operation
284   retains the top-k values to focus on the most significant connections, where k = N ×α for Inter-GrAG
285   module and k = P × α for Intra-GrAG module, with a coefficient α between 0 and 1 (e.g., 0.7). After
286   that, a Feed-Forward Network (FFN) and Gate is employed to obtain the output of Encoders HE .
287   The FFN processes the aggregated features to capture nonlinear transformations, while the gating
288   mechanism controls the flow of information. This gating enhances the model’s capacity to capture
289   complex dependencies by adaptively weighing the importance of different features. The detailed
290   implement about the FFN and Gate layer can be found in Appendix A.
291   In summary, STAGE decouples intra-series and inter-series dependencies within a unified pruned-
292   graph aggregation mechanism, avoiding computational overhead and potential noise introduced by
293   channel blending. Its fully graph-based mechanism enhances interpretability. Further discussion
294   about the variants of STAGE will be delivered in the Section 4.3.

446   Figure 7: Model Variants. Orig (G RAPH STAGE) follows an input→T→S structure, sequentially
447   extracting temporal and then spatial dependencies. VarA uses input→S→T, reversing the order but
448   remaining sequential. VarB employs input→S+T, a parallel structure that decouples temporal and
449   spatial extraction before fusion. VarC utilizes input→S+T+C (C represents cross-series dependency
450   as shown in Figure 2), incorporating channel-mixing with a unified architecture similar to Fouri-
451   erGNN (Yi et al., 2024), extracting all three types of dependencies within a unified framework.
452
453   In Variant VarA, we swapped the positions of the Inter-GrAG and Intra-GrAG modules. The Inter-
454   GrAG module now processes the original features, rather than the temporal embeddings extracted
455   by the Intra-GrAG module. The swap aims to validate the rationale of the proposed sequential
456
      architecture. VarA’s performance in Table 4, shows that the original sequence—inputting the extracted
      temporal embeddings into the Inter-GrAG—contributes positively to the model’s effectiveness.
457
458   In Variant VarB, the Inter-GrAG and Intra-GrAG modules are connected in parallel rather than
459   sequentially. This configuration investigates whether simultaneous processing of inter-series and
460   intra-series dependencies impacts model performance compared to the original sequential architecture.
461   VarB’s performance in Table 4 confirms the sequential structure is more effective than the parallel.
462   In Variant VarC, we adopt the same channel-mixing architecture as UniTST (Liu et al., 2024a)
463   and FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2024), which reshapes the input data Xin from RN ×T to a RN T ×1
464   structure. This reshaping enables the coupled learning of three types of dependencies within a unified
465   structure. By comparing Orig with VarC, we are able to evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed
466   channel-preserving framework. From the results in Table 4, we observe that although channel-mixing
467   demonstrates stronger results in some cases—e.g., on the ETTm1 dataset with an input length of
468
      96 and forecast length of 720, it outperforms Orig by 5.8%—this improvement comes at the cost
      of increased memory usage. Moreover, on larger datasets like ECL, channel blending leads to an
469
      exponential increase in parameters and a sharp decrease in prediction accuracy. By treating the
470
      original multivariate time series as a univariate time series of length N × T , the coupled dependencies
471   learning introduces more interference and noise compared to the proposed decoupled framework.
472   This highlights the advantages of our channel-preserving strategy, which maintains computational
473   efficiency and reduces noise while effectively capturing the essential dependencies.
474
      The comparisons among these variants validate the design of G RAPH STAGE. The sequential struc-
475
      ture in Orig (G RAPH STAGE) proves to be more effective than altering the module order (VarA)
476
      or processing dependencies in parallel (VarB). Additionally, our channel-preserving framework
477   demonstrates superior scalability and efficiency compared to the channel-mixing strategy in VarC,
478   especially on larger datasets. This underscores the importance of preserving the original data structure
479   and decoupling the learning of inter-series and intra-series dependencies in MTSF models.

530
531
      Current models primarily focus on the advantages of channel-mixing methods for extracting multiple
      dependencies, often neglecting the noise these approaches can introduce. G RAPH STAGE is the first
532
      model to directly address this issue. Through the model variants experiments in Section 4.3, we
533
      validated the presence of such interference, underscoring the limitations of excessive dependency
534   extraction. To mitigate these challenges, G RAPH STAGE utilizes a decoupled architecture that
535   independently extracts inter-series and intra-series dependencies. As a fully graph-based, channel-
536   preserving framework, G RAPH STAGE maintains the integrity of the original channel structures,
537   effectively avoiding the interference and noise associated with channel blending. Extensive experi-
538   ments conducted on 13 real-world datasets demonstrate that G RAPH STAGE achieves performance on
539   par with, or surpassing, state-of-the-art methods. Future research could explore decoupled extraction
      of cross-series dependencies and develop inductive models that maintain channel preservation.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
703
704
      The detailed implementation for the Feed-Forward Network (FFN) and Gate layers are presented
705   below. Since the Spatial-Temporal Aggregation Graph Encoder (STAGE) block employs a unified
706   aggregation mechanism, the principles of the Inter-GrAG and Intra-GrAG modules are analogous.
707   Therefore, to avoid redundancy, we focus on the components of the Inter-GrAG module. The
708   transposed input to the Inter-GrAG module is Hin ∈ RN ×P ×D , and the output of the Pruned-Graph
709   Aggregation (PGA) is Hag ∈ RN ×P ×D . The module employs a FFN and a Gate layer to generate
710   the encoder output HE .
711
712   Feed-Forward Network (FFN). The FFN is responsible for processing the aggregated features to
713   capture nonlinear transformations. It introduces nonlinearity and enhances the model’s capacity to
714   learn complex representations. As formulated in Equation 6, the FFN consists of two linear layers
715
      with ReLU activation functions. To facilitate better gradient flow and mitigate the vanishing gradient
      problem, residual connections are employed. Specifically, after the FFN processes the features,
716
      a residual connection adds the dropped HFFN back to the original input Hin , followed by layer
717
      normalization.
718
719
720                            Hres = LayerNorm (Dropout(HFFN ) + Hin ) ,                              (6a)
721
                              HFFN = ReLU (Linear (ReLU (Linear(Hag )))) .                             (6b)
722
723
      Gate Layer. We use the same Gate layer as UniTS (Gao et al., 2024). The Gate layer is placed at the
724
      output of each Inter-GrAG and Intra-GrAG module within the STAGE blocks to regulate the flow of
725   information. Specifically, given an input Hres ∈ RN ×P ×D , a linear layer maps the input to a scaling
726   factor Hl ∈ RN ×P ×1 along the embedding dimension. This is followed by a Sigmoid function to
727   ensure the scaling factor lies between 0 and 1. The final gating operation involves element-wise
728   multiplication of the input with the Sigmoid-activated scaling factor, as formulated in Equation 7.
729
730
                             HE = Sigmoid(Hl ) ⊙ Hres ,       Hl = Linear(Hres ).                        (7)
731
732   This gating mechanism enhances the model’s ability to capture complex dependencies by adaptively
733   weighing the importance of different features.
734
      Additionally, the pseudocode of G RAPH STAGE, which outlines the key steps and components, is
735
      provided in Algorithm 1. This serves as a comprehensive guide to understanding the implementation
736   details of our proposed model.
