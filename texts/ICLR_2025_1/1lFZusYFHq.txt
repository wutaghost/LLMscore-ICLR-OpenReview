009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Transformers have demonstrated exceptional in-context learning capabilities, yet
012            the theoretical understanding of the underlying mechanisms remains limited. A
013            recent work (Elhage et al., 2021) identified a вҖңrichвҖқ in-context mechanism known
014            as induction head, contrasting with вҖңlazyвҖқ n-gram models that overlook long-
015            range dependencies. In this work, we provide both approximation and dynamics
016            analyses of how transformers implement induction heads. In the approximation
017            analysis, we formalize both standard and generalized induction head mechanisms,
018            and examine how transformers can efficiently implement them, with an emphasis
019            on the distinct role of each transformer submodule. For the dynamics analysis, we
020
               study the training dynamics on a synthetic mixed target, composed of a 4-gram
               and an in-context 2-gram component. This controlled setting allows us to precisely
021
               characterize the entire training process and uncover an abrupt transition from lazy
022
               (4-gram) to rich (induction head) mechanisms as training progresses.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Transformer, introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017), have achieved remarkable success across various
028
      domains, including natural language processing, computer vision, and scientific computing. An
029
      emergent observation is that transformers, trained on trillions of tokens, can perform (few-shot)
030   in-context learning (ICL), which makes prediction based on the contextual information without
031   needing model retraining (Brown et al., 2020). This ICL ability is widely regarded as crucial for
032   enabling large language models (LLMs) to solve reasoning tasks, representing a key step toward
033   more advanced artificial intelligence.
034
      To understand how transformers implement ICL, Elhage et al. (2021) and Olsson et al. (2022)
035
      identified a simple yet powerful mechanism known as induction head. Specifically, given an input
036   sequence [В· В· В· abВ· В· В· a], an induction head predicts b as the next token by leveraging the prior
037   occurrence of the pattern ab in the context, effectively modeling an in-context bi-gram. In contrast,
038   traditional n-gram model (Shannon, 1948) (with a small n) utilizes only a limited number of recent
039   tokens to predict the next token, which is context-independent and inevitably overlooks long-range
040   dependence. Based on the extent of context utilization, we categorize n-gram model as a вҖңlazyвҖқ
041   mechanism, whereas the induction head represents a more вҖңrichвҖқ mechanism.
042   Practically, induction heads have been demonstrated to play a critical role in enabling LLMsвҖҷ ICL
043   capabilities (Song et al., 2024; Crosbie and Shutova, 2024), and even used to test new LLM archi-
044   tectures (Gu and Dao, 2023). Theoretically, induction heads also serve as a controllable tool for
045   understanding various aspects of LLMs, such as multi-step reasoning (Sanford et al., 2024b) and
046   inductive biases of different architectures (Jelassi et al., 2024).
047
      In this paper, we aim to provide a theoretical analysis of how transformers can efficiently implement
048   induction heads. The first key problem is to rigorously formalize induction heads and evaluate the
049   efficiency of transformers in representing them. According to Elhage et al. (2021), the original
050   induction head can be implemented using a two-layer, twelve-head transformer without feed-forward
051   networks (FFNs). However, practical scenarios demand more powerful induction heads. Thus, it is
052   crucial to generalize the mechanism behind and explore how different transformer submodules, such
053   as varying the number of attention heads or incorporating FFNs, impact the transformerвҖҷs ability to
      implement them. This forms our first research objective:

      (Approximation). Investigate how two-layer transformers express the induction head mechanism and
055                                         its potential variants.
056
057   The next problem is to investigate the dynamics of transformers in learning induction heads. The
058   pioneering works by Elhage et al. (2021) and Olsson et al. (2022) demonstrated that transformers
059   undergo an abrupt phase transition to learning induction heads. A recent empirical study on synthetic
060   datasets replicate this behavior, further showing that 2-gram is always learned prior to induction
061   heads (Bietti et al., 2024). However, a rigorous theoretical analysis of this learning progression is still
062
      lacking. Closing this gap forms our second research objective:
063
          (Optimization). Understand how transformers transition from relying on n-gram patterns to
064                    employing the induction head mechanism as training progresses.
065
066   Focusing on these two key problems, in this paper, we make the following contributions:
067
068       вҖў Approximation analysis: how transformers express induction heads. We consider three
069         types of induction heads with varying complexities. First, we show that two-layer, single-head
070         transformers without FFNs can efficiently approximate the vanilla induction head (Elhage et al.,
071
            2021). We then introduce two generalized induction heads, which leverage richer in-context
            n-gram information and incorporate a general similarity function. Our analysis clarifies the
072
            distinct roles of multihead attention, positional encoding, dot-product structure, and FFNs in
073
            implementing these generalized induction heads.
074
075
          вҖў Optimization analysis: how learning undergoes a sharp transition from n-gram to induc-
            tion head. We study the learning dynamics of a two-layer transformer without FFNs for a
076
            mixed target, composed of a 4-gram and an in-context 2-gram component. This toy setting
077
            allows us to capture the entire training process precisely. Specifically, we show that learning
078         progresses through four phases: partial learning of the 4-gram, plateau of induction head learn-
079         ing, emergence of the induction head, and final convergence, showcasing a sharp transition from
080         4-gram to induction head. Our analysis identifies two key drivers of the transition: 1) time-scale
081         separation due to low- and high-order parameter dependencies in self-attention, and 2) speed
082         differences caused by the relative proportions of the two components in the mixed target.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
085
086   Empirical observations of induction head. The induction head mechanism was first identified
087   by Elhage et al. (2021) in studying how two-layer transformers perform language modeling. Subse-
088   quently, Olsson et al. (2022) conducted a more systematic investigation, revealing two key findings: 1)
089   induction head emerges abruptly during training, and 2) induction head plays a critical role in the de-
090   velopment of in-context learning capabilities. To obtain a fine-grained understanding of how induction
091   head emerges during training, recent studies have developed several synthetic settings (Reddy, 2024;
092
      Edelman et al., 2024; Bietti et al., 2024). Particularly, Bietti et al. (2024) successfully reproduced
      the fast learning of (global) bigrams and the slower development of induction head. Despite these
093
      efforts, a comprehensive theoretical understanding of how the induction head operates in two-layer
094
      transformers and how it is learned during training remains elusive.
095
096   Expressiveness of transformers. Theoretically, Dehghani et al. (2019); PГ©rez et al. (2021); Wei et al.
097   (2022) explored the Turing-completeness of transformers; Yun et al. (2019) established the universal
098
      approximation property of transformers. Subsequent studies examined the efficiency of transformers
      in representing specific functions or tasks, such as sparse functions (Edelman et al., 2022), targets with
099
      nonlinear temporal kernels (Jiang and Li, 2023), practical computer programs (Giannou et al., 2023),
100
      long but sparse memories (Wang et al., 2024), induction head (Sanford et al., 2024a;b; Rajaraman
101   et al., 2024), and memorization and reasoning (Chen and Zou, 2024). Besides, many studies
102   suggest that transformers achieve in-context learning by approximating gradient-based iterations
103   across various layers (Garg et al., 2022; AkyГјrek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Mahankali
104   et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023). Besides, several studies explored the limitation of
105   transformerвҖҷs expressivity, particularly in modeling formal languages or simulating circuits (Hahn,
106   2020; Weiss et al., 2021; Bhattamishra et al., 2020; Merrill et al., 2022; Merrill and Sabharwal,
107   2023). Among all these works, the most closely related to ours are Rajaraman et al. (2024), which
      examined a generalized induction head similar to our Eq. (6). Specifically, they showed that multi-

111   Training dynamics of transformers. To gain insights into the dynamics of training transformers,
112
      several studies have analyzed simplified transformers on toy tasks. These tasks include learning
      distinct/common tokens (Tian et al., 2023), leaning balance/inblanced features (Huang et al., 2023),

117   by Ataee Tarzanagh et al. (2023), Tarzanagh et al. (2023) and Vasudeva et al. (2024) have analyzed
118   scenarios where transformers converge to max-margin solutions. Furthermore, Thrampoulidis (2024)
119   has examined the implicit bias of next-token prediction. Among these works, the most closely related
120   to ours are Nichani et al. (2024) and Chen et al. (2024b), which proved that two-layer transformers
121   can converge to induction head solutions. In this work, we explore a setting where the target is a

      to this mixed target and provide a precise description of the learning process associated with each
      component. Importantly, we are able to capture the abrupt transition from learning 4-gram patterns
124
      to mastering the induction head mechanismвҖ”a critical phase in the learning of induction heads, as
125
      highlighted in the seminal works (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022).

534   In this work, we present a comprehensive theoretical analysis of how transformers implement
535   induction heads, examining both the approximation and optimization aspects. From the approximation
536   standpoint, we identify the distinct roles of each transformer component in implementing induction
537   heads of varying complexity. On the optimization side, we analyze a toy setting, where we clearly

539   direction for future research is to investigate the dynamics of learning general induction heads, which

978    The well-known universal approximation result for two-layer FNNs asserts that two-layer FNNs
979    can approximate any continuous function (Barron, 1992; 1993; 1994). Nonetheless, this result lacks
980    a characterization of the approximation efficiency, i.e., how many neurons are needed to achieve
981    a certain approximation accuracy? Extensive pre-existing studies aimed to address this gap by
982    establishing approximation rates for two-layer FFNs. A representative result is the Barron theory (E
983
       et al., 2019; 2021; Ma et al., 2020): any function f in Barron space B can be approximatedвҲҡby a
984
       two-layer FFN with M hidden neurons can approximate f efficiently, at a rate of O(вҲҘf вҲҘB / M ).
       This rate is remarkably independent of the input dimension, thus avoiding the Curse of Dimensionality.
