                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Inspired by the success of transformers on natural language processing (NLP) and
               computer vision (CV) tasks, graph transformers (GTs) have recently been pro-
011
               posed to boost the performance of graph learning. However, the attention mecha-
012
               nisms used in existing GTs face certain limitations in capturing crucial topological
013            information or scaling to large graphs, due to their quadratic complexity. To ad-
014            dress these limitations, in this paper, we propose a high-order information prop-
015            agation strategy within the transformer architecture to simultaneously learn the
016            local, long-range, and higher-order relationships of the graph. We first propose
017            a flexible sampling method to extract communities from the graph, and create
018            new community nodes and in particular a learnable community sampling method
019            with reinforcement learning. We then propose a three-step message-passing strat-
020            egy dubbed HOGT to capture the local and higher-order information in the com-
021
               munities and propagate long-range dependency information between the commu-
               nity nodes to finally obtain comprehensive node representations. Note that as
022
               structural information has been flexibly integrated into our designed community-
023
               based message-passing scheme, HOGT discards the positional encoding which
024            was thought to be important for GT. We theoretically demonstrate that GTs with
025            effective substructures can achieve an approximate global attention. HOGT can
026            be viewed as a unified framework, taking many existing graph models as its spe-
027            cial cases. We empirically show that HOGT achieves highly competitive results
028            consistently across node and graph classification tasks.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Learning from graph-structured data, such as social networks, biological networks, and brain net-
034   works, is critical for many real-world applications. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling
035   (2017); Veličković et al. (2018); Gasteiger et al. (2019); Hamilton et al. (2017)) are one type of
036   mainstream architecture that adopts a local Message-Passing (MP) scheme where the information is
037   propagated and aggregated between the connected nodes. However, traditional GNNs suffer from
038   the over-smoothing (NT & Maehara (2019)), over-squashing (Topping et al. (2022)), and limited ex-
039
      pressiveness (Xu et al. (2019b)) problems because of this neighbourhood-dependent message pass-
      ing strategy.
040
041   The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al. (2017)) has recently attracted great attention for graph
042   learning as its global attention mechanism provides a potential solution to the above problems. In
043   contrast to traditional GNNs, Graph Transformers (GTs) (Kreuzer et al. (2021); Mialon et al. (2021);
044   Ying et al. (2021)) enable information to pass between any two nodes, regardless of the original
045
      graph connections. When applying transformers on graphs, the key is to properly incorporate graph
      structural information. This motivates several studies (Kreuzer et al. (2021); Dwivedi & Bresson
046
      (2021); Ying et al. (2021)) to focus on constituting good positional encoding or attention bias to
047
      integrate graph structure. However, Müller et al. (2023) showed that current graph transformers
048   still suffer from limited expressivity, and no clear expressivity hierarchy exists for commonly used
049   positional or structural encodings. Moreover, when developing GTs on real graph tasks, especially
050   for node classification, existing models (Chen et al. (2021a); Kreuzer et al. (2021); Park et al. (2022))
051   suffer from high computational complexity due to dense connections. In conclusion, the current GT
052   models not only fail to fully capture useful topological information (e.g., intrinsic local structure,
053   implicit higher-order correlations) of the graph but also cannot effectively propagate long-range
      information.

      Inspired by the successful use of patches in the vision domain, some recent works (Gao et al.
055   (2022a); Zhao et al. (2023)) have incorporated patch/substructure representations into GTs. While
056   these introduced substructures can benefit graph representation in some cases, existing works
057   (Kuang et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2023; 2024)) still face challenges in achieving flexible and suit-
058   able substructures for different graphs and theoretically demonstration for success of GTs with sub-
059   structures. Therefore, it is important to develop a new scheme to effectively capture the complex
060   structural relationships in the graph for different graphs and data types, while also providing theo-
061   retical support.
062   In this work, we develop a powerful architecture that can effectively propagate comprehensive in-
063   formation with a flexible sampling method and term it as HOGT. To better capture the intricate
064   relationships within a graph, we group graph nodes into multiple communities where all nodes
065   within the same community share similar properties (semantic or information). Notably, we design
066   a learnable community sampling method based on reinforcement learning (RL). When encoding
067   closer graph nodes into the same community, the challenge is how to capture the local high-order
068   information in the community and propagate it globally for effective and comprehensive representa-
069
      tion learning. To tackle this challenge, we introduce a new node to represent each community which
      serves as the bridge to allow the graph node information to propagate and aggregate along these
070
      introduced nodes to establish global connections among all nodes. The generated communities can
071
      encode more complex structural information as a substitute for positional encoding.
072
073   Based on community-structured data, we adopt a three-step message-passing strategy: 1) Graph
074   Node-to-Community Node (G2C-MP); 2) Community Node-to-Community Node (C2C-ATTN);
075
      and 3) Community Node-to-Graph node (C2G-MP). In the first step, within each community, the
      information of each node is propagated and aggregated to its corresponding community node to
076
      capture local high-order information. Then, based on the community-level representations of the
077
      community nodes, we apply a self-attention mechanism between them to allow each community
078   node to capture long-range information from other communities. Finally, we update the representa-
079   tions of the graph nodes by aggregating information from their respective communities. We can see
080   that the community nodes effectively connect to almost all nodes in the graph.
081
      Our proposed HOGT is a general framework and several other existing graph models can be viewed
082
      as special cases. At the level of message-passing strategy: if removing Community Node-to-
083
      Community Node (C2C-ATTN), the framework simplifies to a Message-Passing Architecture. At
084   the level of community generation: if we view the whole graph as a community, our model simpli-
085   fies to a GT model Wu et al. (2021), which takes a special token to connect with all other nodes
086   to achieve global information, representing the lower bound of HOGT; if we view each node as a
087   community, our model essentially becomes the vanilla transformer, representing the upper bound
088   of HOGT. In comparison to the existing graph models, the advantage of our proposed HOGT in
089   processing various graph information and graph types is shown in Table 1.
090   Our proposed framework demonstrates its versatility by accommodating various graph types (graph
091   and hypergraph), data types (homophily and heterophily), data scales (same-scale and large-scale),
092   and different graph tasks. We mainly evaluate HOGT on node classification tasks in which GT
093   models have a performance gap, and also extend HOGT for graph classification. We find improve-
094   ments in accuracy on almost all datasets, especially on heterophilic datasets. In summary, our main
095   contributions are as follows:
096
097         • We propose a flexible sampling method followed by a three-step message-passing frame-
098           work in GTs to capture comprehensive information achieving high expressiveness for graph
099           representation learning.
100
            • We unify message-passing and GTs by constructing communities and introducing new
101
              community nodes. We demonstrate that our model can approximate any other message-
102
              passing model and theoretically show that the three-step message-passing with newly in-
103           troduced community node can achieve global attention as general transformers do.
104
105         • We conduct extensive experiments on benchmark datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness
106           of the proposed method for node and graph classification. The experimental results also
107           verify the effectiveness of higher-order representations.

      General Graph Transformers. Recently, the transformer architecture has been successfully ap-
121
      plied to the graph domain, showing competitive or even superior performance on many tasks when
122
      compared to GNNs. The standard transformer was first extended to graphs (Dwivedi & Bresson
123   (2021)), with four special designs including the position encoding for nodes in a graph. Subse-
124   quently, many other GTs (Rong et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021b); Wu et al.
125   (2021); Hussain et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022a); Nguyen et al. (2022); Kreuzer et al. (2021)) and
126   applications of GTs (Xu et al. (2019a); Zhu et al. (2021; 2022); Cai et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023);
127   Deng et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2024)) have been developed — Rampášek et al. (2022) and Min et al.
128   (2022) provide a more detailed introduction and review of different GTs. However, the above meth-
129   ods are mostly designed for graph-level tasks, as they impose great time and memory constraints due
130   to the self-attention layer. Therefore, several works (Zhao et al. (2021); Choromanski et al. (2022);
131
      Guo et al. (2022); Park et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023)) have been proposed to make
      graph transformers more scalable and efficient, but they still suffer from various challenges such as
132
      missing long-range and higher-order information or noise aggregation.
133
134
135   Graph Transformer Utilizing Substructures. Due to the exponentially increasing scale of graph
136
      data, researchers have attempted to utilize substructures to scale up graph representation learning
      through methods such as subgraph learning (Kim & Oh), and graph condensation (Wang et al.
137
      (2024); Zheng et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2021); Fey et al.
138
      (2020)). In terms of GTs, substructures (Zhang et al. (2022b)) (such as hierarchical structure, clus-
139   ters, communities, and subgraphs) have been utilized for both graph and node classification. For
140   graph classification tasks, some methods (Gao et al. (2022a); Zhao et al. (2023)) segment the graph
141   into patches or subgraphs and use the substructural representations to learn topological high-level
142   information. For node classification, researchers have explored extracting substructures and utiliz-
143   ing these substructural representations to reduce the quadratic complexity of global self-attention,
144   while effectively capturing the global information of the graph. Specifically, Kuang et al. (2021) and
145   Zhu et al. (2023) employed coarsening techniques to obtain a coarser graph with fewer nodes to cap-
146   ture long-range information. With the obtained clusters, Xing et al. (2024) introduced inter-cluster
147
      and intra-cluster Transformers to extract local information and long-range dependent information
      from distant nodes. Zhu et al. (2024) and Jiang et al. (2023) selected several topologically important
148
      nodes as anchors, allowing information to propagate over a large receptive field, where the anchor
149
      nodes can be viewed as a substructure of the original graph. Furthermore, Fu et al. (2024) transferred
150   global and long-range information by establishing multiple virtual connections using personalized
151   PageRank.
152
153
      While these introduced substructures can benefit graph representation in some cases, there are limita-
      tions of existing works for achieving flexible and suitable substructures for different graphs. Specif-
154
      ically, the anchor nodes in Zhu et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023) are derived from the original graph
155
      nodes and therefore cannot introduce additional information, such as higher-order information. Sim-
156   ilarly, the supernodes in the coarse graphs in Kuang et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2023) which are used to
157   propagate high-level information are also constrained by the original graph structure. Moreover, it
158   is not trivial to provide theoretical support for GTs with substructures in capturing global attention,
159   while ensuring adaptability across various graph datasets. In this work, we demonstrate that the
160   proposed HOGT offers a general and theoretically grounded framework. It shows significant advan-
161   tages in capturing comprehensive information through the flexible community sampling method and
      demonstrates its versatility by its effectiveness across diverse graph datasets.

      3.1   C OMMUNITY S AMPLING
217
218   Effectively utilizing the structural information of the graph is the key challenge for graph repre-
219   sentation learning. We note that data correlations in practice can be complex and are often beyond
220   pairwise, for example, a community of friends shares their common interest in basketball in a social
221   network. To encode these higher-order correlations, we consider extracting meaningful communities
222   from the whole graph. Here, a community is introduced to collect multiple vertices sharing similar
223   properties (semantic or information), similar to how a hyperedge connects multiple objects.
224   We design the community sampling method tailored to different graph types: 1). For hypergraphs,
225   we intuitively view each hyperedge as a community; 2). For regular graphs, we explore a learnable
226   sampling method that employs reinforcement learning to determine the optimal communities.
227
228   Learnable sampling. For graph G = {A, X}, we learn a GNN-based encoder and obtain the

231   projection on p is yi = hi p/∥p∥.. Here, yi measures how much information of node vi can be

307   4   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
308
309   Here, we analyze several properties of HOGT including 1) the lower bound of HOGT, 2) the up-
310   per bound of HOGT, and 3) a general case of HOGT. We show that HOGT is a powerful model
311   that can approximate the GT model and achieve global attention, i.e., unifying MP and GT with
312   the community and newly introduced community nodes. We also analyze the role of community
313   nodes in capturing the high-order representation versus the function of hyperedges in hypergraph
314   convolutional networks in Appendix A.4.
315
316   Viewing the Whole Graph as a Community. In this case, GT can be simplified by Message-
317   Passing Neural Networks (MPNN) with an additional node that connects to all graph nodes. This
318   forms the lower bound of HOGT (number of communities m = 1). It has been demonstrated by Cai
319   et al. (2023) that MPNN with a virtual node can approximate a self-attention layer arbitrarily well.
320
321   Viewing Each Node as a Community. In this case, HOGT is the standard transformer. Specifi-
322   cally, the three-step MP in HOGT is reduced to one step: Community Node-to-Community Node.
323   Since a node is a community, HOGT is equivalent to propagating information between any two
      nodes.

      results for the HOGT model, including hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness evaluations, can

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduced a higher-order message-passing strategy within the Transformer archi-
530   tecture to learn long-range, higher-order relationships for graph representation. Initially, we extract
531
      communities from the entire graph and introduce a new node for each community. Subsequently,
      leveraging community-structured data, we adopt a three-step message-passing scheme to aggregate
532
      information from the graph node to the community node, propagate information between commu-
533
      nity nodes and send the community-level information back to the graph nodes. The introduced nodes
534   act like hyperedges in a hypergraph to effectively propagate information to other graph nodes. We
535   theoretically demonstrate the powerful expressiveness of HOGT and empirically show the effective-
536   ness of HOGT across diverse datasets on node classification. While HOGT is designed to capture
537   comprehensive information across various types of graphs, achieving an optimal balance between
538   different (local, global, and higher-order) information in complex graph structures remains a chal-
539   lenge. In the future, we will consider designing more flexible community sampling methods and
      message-passing framework for different data types.

      A.1   M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
832
833   Higher-Order Representation learning. In computer vision, it is a common approach to divide
834   the whole image into multiple local patches. Vision Transformers (ViTs) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)
835   then generate the image representation by aggregating high-level representations from these patches
836   rather than individual pixels. Following the transformer architecture, Han et al. Han et al. (2021)
837   further subdivide each local patch into smaller patches. This innovative approach enables the model
838   to capture more detailed representations, thus enhancing feature representations. The high-order,
839   or high-level representations derived from local patches, which often share similar content, play
840
      a critical role in learning visual representations. In the graph domain, several studies Feng et al.
      (2019); Wang et al. (2022) also consider encoding higher-order correlations for graph representation
841
      learning. Typically, the hypergraph structure with a series of hyperedges is introduced to model
842
      the complex higher-order relationship. Within the context of GTs, some recent studies Gao et al.
843   (2022a); Zhao et al. (2023) have attempted to extract substructures, treat them as patches, and utilize
844   the substructural representations for graph classification tasks. As graphs continue to grow rapidly
845   in size, the relationships among nodes become increasingly complex. Therefore, exploring and
846   exploiting higher-order representations is essential for graph representation learning.
847
848   Virtual Node in Message-Passing. The introduction of a virtual node expands the graph by
849   adding an extra node that facilitates information exchange among all pairs of nodes. Its effec-
850   tiveness in improving performance has been observed in various tasks Hu et al. (2021b). Recently,
851   there has been a significant focus on studying its theoretical properties. Hwang et al. Hwang et al.
852   (2022) analyzed the virtual node’s role in the context of link prediction. They found that virtual
853
      nodes can help to add expressiveness of the learned link representation and decrease under-reaching
      and over-smoothing. Cai et al. Cai et al. (2023) demonstrated the power of message-passing with
854
      a virtual node, showing that it can approximate an arbitrary self-attention layer within GTs. While
855
      the function of virtual node as READOUT has been explored in existing GNNs, the community
856   nodes in our HOGT have a slightly different function. In addition to aggregate information like
857   the READOUT, they act as bridges connecting the entire graph to propagate long-range dependent
858   information, while also saving computational costs, as the number of communities is significantly
859   smaller than the number of graph nodes.
