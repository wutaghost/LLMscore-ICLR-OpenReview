009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Associating unstructured data with structured information is crucial for real-world
012            tasks that require relevance search. However, existing graph learning benchmarks
013            often overlook the rich semantic information associated with each node, ignor-
014            ing other available modalities such as the corresponding images. To bridge this
015            gap, we introduce the Multimodal Graph Benchmark (MM-G RAPH), the first
016            comprehensive multi-modal graph benchmark that incorporates both textual and
017            visual information, going beyond the prior focus on just text-attributed graphs.
018            MM-G RAPH consists of seven graph learning datasets of various scales that are
019            appropriate for different learning tasks, and enable a comprehensive evaluation of
020
               graph learning algorithms in real-world scenarios thanks to their multimodal node
               features. To facilitate research on multimodal graph learning, we further provide
021
               an extensive study on the performance of various graph learning frameworks in the
022
               presence of features from various modalities. MM-G RAPH aims to foster research
023            on multimodal attributed graphs and drive the development of more advanced and
024            robust multimodal attributed graph learning algorithms. By providing a diverse set
025            of datasets and benchmarks, MM-G RAPH enables researchers to evaluate and com-
026            pare their models in realistic settings, ultimately leading to improved performance
027            on real-world applications that rely on multimodal attributed graphs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Graphs are ubiquitous data structures that can effectively represent complex relationships and in-
      teractions between entities in various domains, such as social networks, biological systems, and
034
      recommendation systems. In real-world scenarios, these entities often possess rich semantic infor-
035
      mation in the form of unstructured data, such as images and text descriptions. Associating these
036   unstructured data with the structured graph information is essential for tasks that require relevance
037   search and information retrieval, such as recommendation systems Su et al. (2021); Crandall et al.
038   (2009); Guo et al. (2011).
039
      Despite the importance of multimodal graph learning, existing graph learning benchmarks typically
040
      focus on investigating graphs with various connectivity patterns, such as long-range links, out-of-
041
      distribution scenarios, etc Dwivedi et al. (2022); Gui et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024b); Morris et al.
042   (2020); Hu et al. (2020). These benchmarks aim to evaluate the performance of graph neural networks
043   (GNNs) and other graph learning algorithms on different graph structures and topologies. However,
044   these benchmarks under-utilize the rich semantic information naturally present in each node, limiting
045   their ability to evaluate graph learning algorithms in realistic settings.
046
      With the prevalence of language modeling, there has been an outbreak in the effective integration
047
      of text and graph topology on text-attributed graphs, leading to a comprehensive benchmark on
048   text-attributed graphs Yan et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2024a); Jin et al. (2023a); Peng et al. (2024).
049   While textual information undoubtedly plays a crucial role in understanding the data and making
050   predictions for user-centric tasks, it is essential to recognize that other modalities, particularly visual
051   information, can be helpful and provide complementary information that text alone cannot capture.
052   For example, as shown in Figure 1(a), visually similar products may have distinct text features
053   and ignoring such semantic gap between modalities may prevent GNNs from fully exploiting the
      relationships between multimodal semantics and structures.

071   To address this limitation, we introduce the Multimodal Graph Benchmark (MM-G RAPH), the first
072   comprehensive graph benchmark that incorporates both textual and visual information. MM-G RAPH
073   consists of seven graph learning datasets of various scales, designed for diverse downstream tasks
074   such as node classification, link prediction and knowledge graph completion. These datasets contain
075
      multimodal node features, including text and images, alongside the traditional graph structure. By
      providing a diverse set of datasets and benchmarks, MM-G RAPH enables researchers to rigoriously
076
      evaluate and compare their models in realistic settings, fostering research on multimodal attributed
077
      graph learning. MM-G RAPH also standardizes GNN architectures, feature encoders, knowledge
078   graph embedding methods, dataloaders and evaluators in order to enable a comprehensive evaluation
079   on multimodal attributed graphs.
080
081
      To show the importance of these datasets in for advancing multimodal graph learning , we adapt two
      state-of-the-art multimodal graph neural networks from the recommendation systems to the graph
082
      learning domain. These models have demonstrated strong performance in leveraging multimodal
083
      information for recommendation tasks. By applying them to the datasets in MM-G RAPH, we aim to
084   provide a comprehensive evaluation of their effectiveness in various graph learning scenarios. We
085   conduct an extensive study on the performance of MMGCN Wei et al. (2019), MGAT Tao et al. (2020),
086   VISTA Lee et al. (2023) and other popular graph learning methods, such as GraphSAGE Hamilton
087   et al. (2017), in the presence of multimodal node features.
088
      Surprisingly, we find that GNNs specifically designed for multimodal scenarios, such as MMGCN
089
      and MGAT, do not consistently outperform general-purpose GNN models like GraphSAGE across all
090   datasets in MM-G RAPH. This finding highlights the need for a standard multimodal graph benchmark
091   to rigorously evaluate the performance of different models and rethink where we are in terms of
092   multimodal attributed graph learning. Moreover, while multimodal knowledge graphs (MMKG) exist
093   in the community, there is only a limited amount of datasets available, most of which face issues
094   such as outdated or inaccessible image URLs and poor dataset quality Liu et al. (2019); Safavi &
095   Koutra (2020); Liang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024). As a result, higher-quality MMKG datasets
096   are strongly needed.
097   MM-G RAPH fills this gap by providing a diverse set of datasets with varying scales, tasks, and
098   modality combinations. By establishing a common ground for evaluation, MM-G RAPH enables
099   researchers to reflect the strengths and limitations of existing multimodal graph learning algorithms
100   and identify areas for improvement. Moreover, MM-G RAPH opens up new research opportunities
101   in multimodal graph learning. The availability of datasets with rich textual and visual information
102   allows researchers to explore novel architectures and techniques that can effectively capture and
103   integrate the semantic information from multiple modalities. This can also lead to the development of
104
      more advanced and robust graph learning algorithms that can handle the complexities of real-world
      multimodal graph data.

133   2    R ELATED W ORK
134
135   Multimodal Feature Learning. Multimodal feature learning aims to learn joint representations
136   from multiple modalities, such as text, images, and audio. Transformer-based models have shown
137   remarkable success in multimodal feature learning Xu et al. (2023); Radford et al. (2021). These
138   models learn typically transferable visual representations by leveraging corresponding natural lan-
139   guage supervision. Models like FLAVA Singh et al. (2022) and Perceiver Jaegle et al. (2021) have
140   demonstrated the effectiveness of jointly pre-training transformers on unpaired images and text
141
      while CLIP has shown that contrastive objectives can effectively align representations from different
      modalities Singh et al. (2022); Jaegle et al. (2021); Radford et al. (2021). We refer to the survey for
142
      more details Xu et al. (2023).
143
144   Multimodal Graph Learning. While most existing multimodal learning approaches focus on
145   1-to-1 mappings between modalities, real-world data often exhibits more complex many-to-many
146   relationships that can be represented as graphs. Multimodal graph learning (MMGL) aims to address
147
      this challenge by leveraging graph structures to learn from multimodal data with complicated relations
      among multiple multimodal neighbors Ektefaie et al. (2023); Yoon et al. (2023). Recent efforts in
148
      MMGL have focused on integrating GNNs/Knowledge Graph Embeddings(KGEs) with pretrained
149
      language models (LMs) Yan et al. (2023). Our work builds upon the recent advances in multimodal
150   feature learning and MMGL by introducing a comprehensive benchmark that incorporates both
151   structured and unstructured modalities. We adapt up-to-date multimodal feature learning frameworks
152   and state-of-the-art multimodal graph learning and provide an extensive study on their performance
153   in the presence of features from various modalities.
154
      Benchmarks for graph representation learning. Several established graph benchmarks have been
155
      developed and widely adopted Morris et al. (2020); Hu et al. (2020); Freitas & Dong (2021). However,
156   when it comes to learning on graphs with rich features, these benchmarks exhibit notable deficiencies.
157   Firstly, these datasets suffer from the absence of raw features, limiting the investigation of attribute
158   modeling. Secondly, these datasets often neglect to explore the impact of feature modeling. Thus,
159   there is a compelling necessity to construct a comprehensive graph benchmark with rich natural
160   features. Yan et al. proposed the first standardized graph benchmark with rich textual information,
161   fostering the development of encompassing GNNs with prevalent language models Yan et al. (2023).
      However, we argue that it is critical to investigate modalities other than text, especially images in

178   the sports category and the link captures whether two products are co-purchased together. The text

      images. We follow the same data curation procedure.

188   et al. (2019). Here we construct the graph in the way where each node corresponds to a book on
189   Goodreads and the link captures if a user who likes this book will like the other book, following Zhu
190   et al. (2024a). The text features are the descriptions of the books and the visual features are the cover
191   of the book. Nodes without images are removed.
192
      G OODREADS -NC is a node classification dataset, based on the Goodreads dataset Wan & McAuley
193   (2018); Wan et al. (2019), where each node corresponds to a book on Goodreads and the link captures

196

198
      Hou et al. (2024), where each node corresponds to a product and the link captures if a user who buy

199
      visual features are the images of the products.
200
201   MM-C O DE X - S is a knowledge graph completion dataset, based on the CoDEx-s dataset Safavi &

203   crawl the images of the person/place/item from the Wikipedia using Beautiful Soup.
204   MM-C O DE X - M Similar to MM-CoDEx-s, MM-CoDEx-m is based on CoDEx-M dataset Safavi &
205   Koutra (2020). And text features are the description of the wikipedia article. For visual features, we
206   crawl the images from the Wikipedia using Beautiful Soup.

234   3.2   TASKS
235
236   Link Prediction. The task is to predict new association edges given the training edges. The evaluation
237   is based on how well a model ranks positive test edges over negative test edges. Specifically, for
238   Amazon-Sports and Amazon-Cloth, we generate hard valid/test negatives using HeaRT Li et al.
239   (2024a). HeaRT is recognized as a better way of generating negatives for link prediction in the
240
      community. For Goodreads-LP, since it takes more than 120 hours to generate hard negatives using
      HeaRT, we perform random sampling. Each positive edge in the validation/test set is ranked against
241
      150 hard negative edges. For evaluation metrics, we report MRR, Hits@10, and Hits@1 - the three
242
      most commonly-used evaluation metrics for link prediction Hu et al. (2020); Li et al. (2024a). For
243   Amazon-Sports and Amazon-Cloth, edges are randomly split into train/valid/test splits according to
244   8/1/1 ratio. For Goodreads-LP, edges are randomly split according to 6/1/3 ratio. Validation and test
245   edges are explicitly removed from the graphs to avoid any potential leakage Zhu et al. (2024b).
246
247
      Node Classification. The task is to predict the book category from N available categories. We report
      accuracy following Hu et al. (2020); Yan et al. (2023). For Goodreads-NC, there are 10 categories
248
      such as History, Children and comics. For Ele-Fashion, there are 12 categories such as shoes, jewelry
249
      and dresses. For evaluation metrics, we report accuracy, as it’s the most common metric for node
250   classificationHu et al. (2020). Nodes are randomly split into train/valid/test following 6/1/3 ratio.
251
252   Knowledge Graph Completion. The task is to predict missing links in a knowledge graph given
253   entities and relations. Similar to link prediction, the evaluation is based on how well a model ranks
254
      positive test edges over negative test edges. Specifically, we use the splits and negatives designed
      in Safavi & Koutra (2020) to ensure the scope and level of difficulty of the task. Entities without
255
      multimodal information (do not have descriptions or images) are filtered out. For evaluation metrics,
256
      we report MRR, Hits@10, Hits@3 and Hits@1 following Safavi & Koutra (2020).

286   4     E VALUATION F RAMEWORK
287
288   To enable a comprehensive evaluation on multimodal graph data, MM-G RAPH standardizes the GNN
289   architectures, KGEs, feature encoders, and dataloaders and evaluators used across all datasets.
290
291   4.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
292
293   To enable a comprehensive evaluation of GNN architectures on multimodal graph data, MM-G RAPH
294   includes five representative GNN models: GCN Kipf & Welling (2016), SAGE Hamilton et al.
295   (2017), MMGCN Wei et al. (2019), MGAT Tao et al. (2020), and BUDDY Chamberlain et al. (2022)
296   . Additionally, following Hu et al. (2020), we report the performance of an MLP as a baseline to
297
      evaluation the usefulness of graph structure.
298   Conventional GNNs. We standardizes GCN, SAGE, MLP for both link prediction and node
299   classification. Since BUDDY is specifically designed for link prediction, so we do not report its
300   performance on node classification.
301
      Multimodal GNNs. While GNNs have shown strong performance on tasks involving graphs with
302
      unimodal features, there has been limited work on developing GNNs that can effectively handle
303
      multimodal graph data. In order to evaluate the effectiveness of GNNs on multimodal graphs,
304   we adapt two recent architectures from the recommendation systems domain to our standardized
305   multimodal graph benchmarks:
306
307
      MMGCN constructs separate user-item graphs for each modality. The information interchange of
      users and items in each modality is encoded using GNNs. Modality representations are fused in the
308
      prediction layer to predict possible recommendations Wei et al. (2019). For LP, we use the most
309
      common dot product decoder to decode possible links. For NC, we stack a 3-layer MLP as transform
310   representations to a number of dimensions equal to the number of node classes.
311
312   MGAT extends the popular GAT architecture and learns modality-specific node representations which
313
      are then combined using a cross-modal attention layer Tao et al. (2020). This allows MGAT to
      weigh different modalities based on their importance. Similar to MMGCN, we decode links using
314
      dot product for LP and perform NC through a 3-layer MLP.
315
316
      4.2   K NOWLEDGE G RAPH E MBEDDINGS
317
318   MM-G RAPH includes two most up-to-date multimodal KGEs: MoSE Zhao et al. (2022) and VISTA
319   Lee et al. (2023). MoSE performs modality split relation embeddings for each modality instead of
320   a single modality-shared one, which alleviates the modality interference. VISTA incorporates the
321   visual and textual representations of entities and relations using entity encoding, relation encoding,
322   and triplet decoding transformers. For VISTA, since the designed architecture scores entities instead
323   of triples, we use the standard 1vsAll setting to report performance instead of hard negatives Lee
      et al. (2023); Ruffinelli et al. (2020).

350   4.3     F EATURE E NCODERS
351
352   Text encoders. For text encoders, we select CLIP Radford et al. (2021), T5 Raffel et al. (2020) and
353   ImageBind Girdhar et al. (2023). T5 is selected as the STOA text embedding models, while CLIP
354   and ImageBind are selected so that the output text representations aligns with visual representations.
355
356
      Visual encoders. For visual encoders, we select CLIP Radford et al. (2021), ViT Dosovitskiy et al.
      (2020), ImageBind Girdhar et al. (2023) and Dinov2 Oquab et al. (2023). ViT and Dinov2 are
357
      selected as two different types of visual encoders, while ViT are explicitly trained with supervision,
358
      Dinov2 learns robust visual features without supervision. CLIP and ImageBind are selected so
359   that the output text representations aligns with visual representations. Specifically, Imagebind has
360   potentials to extend multimodal graph learning to other modalities, such as audio and video thanks
361   to its ability to bind all modalities into one embedding space. Studies on various feature encoders
362   give insights on design choices of multimodal graph learning, such as (1) how important it is to align
363   multiple modalities into a unified embedding space, (2) if supervised feature encoders work better
364   than unsupervised feature encoders. Note that feature encoders are only used to extract features and
365   are never trained upon.
366
367
      4.4     DATALOADER AND E VALUATORS
368
369
      MM-G RAPH provides a standardized dataloader and evaluators implementation for all five datasets.
370
      The dataloader is built upon PyTorch’s DataLoader class and the Deep Graph Library (DGL) . It
371   efficiently handles the creation and transmission of graph samples, allowing for mini-batch training of
372   GNNs. The evaluator is based on OGB’s evaluator and performs standardized and reliable evaluation.
373
374
      The dataloader supports various sampling strategies, such as neighbor sampling and layer-wise
      sampling, to scale the training process to large graphs. It also enables distributed training using
375
      PyTorch’s distributed data parallel (DDP) for faster training on multiple GPUs.
376
377   By standardizing the GNNs, KGEs, feature encoders, dataloader and evaluators, MM-G RAPH ensures
      a fair and comprehensive evaluation of graph learning algorithms on multimodal graph data.

464   datasets and tasks. And this justifies the necessity of MM-G RAPH.

466   5.2   C HALLENGES AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS .
467
468    First, existing multimodal GNNs are all based on full-batch training schemes and it would go
469   out of GPU memory when the graph is large, e.g. Goodreads-NC and Goodreads-LP. Mini-batch
470   multimodal GNNs are worth exploring in the future. Second, we find that it is hard to find a graph
471   dataset with audio/video features with open-source access, even though music/video recommendation
472   is an task of great importance in real-world deployed systems. We believe the study is similar to
473   what is conducted here and the ImageBind features we use can be easily extended to these scenarios.
474
      However, integrating multimodal graphs with audio/video features still require future exploration.

524   Ethics Statement. MM-G RAPH has the potential to significantly advance research on multimodal
525
      graph representation learning, which can enable novel applications that positively transform various
      aspects of society. Many real-world domains, such as healthcare, social networks, and urban
526
      computing, involve multimodal graph data. By providing a comprehensive evaluation framework,
527
      MM-G RAPH can accelerate the development of more powerful graph learning algorithms for these
528   domains. MM-G RAPH also promotes reproducible research by offering standardized benchmarks,
529   fostering collaboration within the research community. Ultimately, MM-G RAPH aims to unlock the
530   value of multimodal graph data to enable impactful real-world applications. All of the data that MM-
531   G RAPH uses do not involve interaction with humans or private data. However, as with all technologies
532   of this nature, it is important to consider how it can be used responsibly and with responsible
533   development in mind. There are risks of these algorithms perpetuating or amplifying societal biases
534   if the underlying graph data contains biased patterns. Additionally, misuse of multimodal graph
535   can cause disinformation and targeted manipulation. It is therefore crucial that researchers using
536
      MM-GRAPH prioritize the responsible development of multimodal graph algorithms in line with
      ethical principles.
