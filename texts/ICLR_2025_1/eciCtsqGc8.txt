009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Interpretability of artificial intelligence models is vital in healthcare, as a poorly
012            informed decision can directly impact the health and well-being of patients. This
013            means that, owing to their black box nature, deep-learning solutions that may even
014            yield high accuracy often fail to be adopted in real-world healthcare settings. To
015            this end, we employ the generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) framework to
016
               clinical heart time-series data, to create two pre-trained general purpose cardiac
               models, termed PPG-PT and ECG-PT. We place a special emphasis on making
017
               both such pre-trained models fully interpretable. This is achieved firstly through
018
               aggregate attention maps which show that, in order to make predictions, the model
019            focuses on similar points in previous cardiac cycles and gradually broadens its at-
020            tention in deeper layers. Next, we show that tokens with the same value, which oc-
021            cur at different distinct points in the electrocardiography (ECG) and photoplethys-
022            mography (PPG) cycle, form separate clusters in a high dimensional space. Such
023            clusters are formed according to the phase of the cardiac cycle, as the tokens
024            propagate through the transformer blocks. Finally, we highlight that individual at-
025            tention heads correspond to specific physiologically relevant features, such as the
026            dicrotic notch in PPG and the P-wave in ECG. Importantly, it is also demonstrated
027
               that these pre-trained models are straightforward to fine-tune for tasks such as the
               classification of atrial fibrillation (AF), and beat detection in photoplethysmog-
028
               raphy. The so introduced PPG-PT and ECG-PT models achieve accuracy com-
029
               parable to the state-of-the-art for both tasks, whilst crucially retaining their inter-
030            pretability and explainability. This is demonstrated in the AF-screening fine-tuned
031            model, with attention clearly shifting to regions in the context that are strongly in-
032            dicative of atrial fibrillation.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The generative pretrained transformer (GPT) (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018) (Brown et al., 2020)
038   is a large language model (LLM) which forms the basis of the chat-GPT models (OpenAI, 2024).
039
      It consists of layers of decoder-only transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) that are trained to predict
      the next token (words, sub-words, or characters), using all previous tokens provided to the model as
040
      context. The attention mechanism is masked, such that tokens inputted to the model can only com-
041
      municate with past tokens and thus a context window of length N effectively provides N different
042   training examples, whereby the model tries to predict the same context window shifted by one token
043   into the future. The whole premise of the GPT model is that in order to accurately predict the next
044   token, across a large and diverse array of texts, a model must gain an efficient and general compre-
045   hension of these texts. Given that language is a tool that we use to summarise and communicate
046   the world around us, this efficient and general understanding allows the pre-trained model to be fine
047   tuned for a myriad of complex tasks (OpenAI, 2024).
048   The assertion of this work is that we can leverage the GPT mechanism, i.e. stacks of decoder-only
049   transformers trained to predict the next token, and develop it for physiological time series. Our
050   hypothesis is that if our models gain an efficient and general understanding of a physiological time
051   series, such as photoplethysmography (PPG) or electrocardiography (ECG), then these models will
052   be simple to fine-tune for downstream tasks such as the classification of heart conditions. The ECG
053   signal refers to the monitoring of the electrical activity of the heart through external non-invasive
      electrodes that measure the potential difference across the heart (Arora & Mishra, 2021; Davies


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2024a). In this work, we use only single-lead ECG. The photoplethysmography (PPG) signal
055   refers to the non-invasive measurement of blood volume (Charlton et al., 2023). The PPG operates
056   by shining light through tissue with a light emitting diode, which is absorbed by the blood, and then
057   either measuring the transmitted light or reflected light with a corresponding photo-diode. The PPG
058   is thus able to measure changes in blood volume related to pulse and even breathing (Davies et al.,
059   2022). In PPG, for example, in order to accurately predict the next time point the model must gain
060   knowledge on several underlying phenomena, such as the subtle changes in heart rate and pulse
061   amplitude due to breathing and blood pressure. This leads us to postulate that a large model that
062   is good at predicting the next token of PPG will be straightforward to fine-tune for specific PPG
063
      health-related tasks.
064   As GPT and other large language models are rapidly gaining in capability, and thus decision making
065   power, it is of crucial importance that LLMs do not remain black boxes. Indeed, the black box
066   nature of deep learning models is an immediate problem in healthcare, where decisions made by
067   artificial intelligence (AI) have the potential to directly impact our health and even our lives. It is
068   therefore paramount that models are developed which give clear insights and explanations into why
069
      decisions are made. A natural way to interpret the operation of transformer-based LLMs is to take
      a close examination of the attention weights of the individual transformer blocks. This attention
070
      mechanism provides information on which tokens a model looks at in order to make a decision,
071
      and thus it is common to use heat maps that visualise this attention as an avenue for interpretability
072   (Zhao et al., 2023). This has been applied to great effect in large vision language models (LVLMs)
073   (Stan et al., 2024) where it is possible to visualise the focus points on an image based on the context
074   of a language prompt. Language is complex, and there are often several pathways to achieve the
075   same result. In many instances this makes it difficult to decipher attention, which means that in the
076   context of LLMs attention cannot always be relied upon for explainability (Jain & Wallace, 2019).
077   However, like images, both the PPG and ECG signals are far less complicated than language, and
078   downstream tasks, such as screening for heart conditions, offer well understood constraints on the
079   possible pathways to achieve a correct diagnosis. This claim is evaluated in Appendix A.1, where it
080
      is demonstrated that the possibilities for the next token of PPG become highly constrained as context
      length increases. It is therefore a core focus of this paper not just to provide generalised transformer
081
      models for PPG and ECG related tasks, but to provide models that are fully interpretable so that they
082
      may be safely used in a healthcare setting.
083
084   To this end, we firstly develop two pre-trained decoder-only time series transformer models for use
085   with heart signals, namely PPG-PT and ECG-PT. Furthermore, we demonstrate that both the PPG-
086
      PT and ECG-PT models behave exactly as expected for the task of predicting the next time series
      point in PPG and ECG through:
087
088          • Aggregate attention of different transformer layers.
089
             • Changes in cosine similarity between core PPG and ECG features in the embedding space
090
               upon propagation through transformer layers.
091
092
             • The analysis of the attention weights of individual attention heads in the final transformer
               block.
093
094   Finally, we demonstrate how these models can be fine-tuned to detect atrial fibrillation (AF), a
095   common type of abnormal heart rhythm. The changes in attention from the task of next token
096   prediction to the task of classification of AF can be used to further explain the reason why a model
097   has made a decision. This makes it possible to provide clinicians with both accurate classification
098   and the reasoning behind it, thus allowing for a conjoint operation between a clinician and AI in
099   diagnostics and treatment through informed decisions.
100
101   2   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   There are several valuable prior works which leverage self supervised learning (SSL) to train large
104   models for application to ECG and PPG (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2020; Spathis
105   et al., 2021). Pre-trained transformer architectures have previously been shown to be effective for
106   PPG (Chen et al., 2024), but tokenise based on patches rather than individual time points, thus
107   limiting model interpretability. Another such pre-training method is contrastive learning (Kiyasseh
      et al., 2021), shown to be effective for pre-training a generalisable representation of ECG signals.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Whilst these models provide impressive accuracies on a wide range of downstream tasks, none of
109   these works were designed for end user interpretability. Where attempts at interpretability exist, it
110   is through class activation maps (CAM) to visualise regions in the input time-series that are of high
111   importance to classification (Lai et al., 2023; Torres-Soto & Ashley, 2020). However, the regions
112   of high intensity in the generated interpretability heat-maps are either too broad or have peaks at
113   regions that are not of interest (Lai et al., 2023). Moreover, the aforementioned works either do not
114   use publicly available datasets, or provide model files or model interpretability code. This highlights
115   a clear need for models pre-trained on bio-signals that are transparent and interpretable. With this
116   in mind, the models presented in this work are designed with interpretability and explainability as
117
      the primary goal. For generality and reproducibility, we use publicly available datasets, provide all
      code and trained model files and even graphical user interfaces explicitly created for interpretability.

122   3.1   T OKENISATION AND E MBEDDING
123
124   Both the ECG and PPG signals are largely locally periodic, and in most applications we can ignore
125   any mean offset. This allows us to divide each PPG and ECG signal into a range of finite tokens,
126
      forming a vocabulary from which each signal can be constructed. To tokenise the PPG signals, they
      were resampled to 50Hz and each 10-second context window (500 samples) was scaled to between
127
      100 and 0. The resulting signal was then rounded to integer values, yielding a total vocabulary size of
128
      101 tokens. Importantly, in our models each sample corresponds to a token. The 10-second context
129   window was chosen so as to be long enough to preserve low frequency variations in the PPG (such
130   as respiratory variations), but not too long so as to run into memory issues when training. For the
131   ECG, the process was similar, apart from resampling to 100Hz and thus using a context window that
132   corresponded to 5-seconds instead of 10-seconds of data. A higher sampling frequency was required
133   in the ECG tokenisation in order to preserve the high frequency components of the QRS complex.
134   In GPT models, common sequences of token values can be combined during tokenisation, in order
135   to allow for a longer context length for the same number of tokens. This method of tokenisation was
136   not implemented in the PPG-PT and ECG-PT models, as further extending the length of the context
137
      window was not necessary.
138   Upon tokenisation, each token was embedded with a token embedding table and a position embed-
139   ding table. The dimensions of the token embedding were the vocabulary size times the embedding
140   dimension (dmodel ), giving a dmodel -dimensional vector for every possible token in the vocabulary.
141   The dimensions of the position embedding table were the maximum context length times dmodel ,
142   giving a dmodel -dimensional vector for every possible position in the context window. In the original
143
      transformer paper, the positional embeddings utilised sine and cosine functions of different frequen-
      cies (Vaswani et al., 2017); whereas in our models, both of these embeddings are learnt as the model
144
      trains. The token and positional embeddings were added together, which means that the attention
145
      mechanism in the subsequent transformer blocks had information on both the specific token and the
146   position of the token in the context.
147
148
      3.2   A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING
149
150   Our models were developed in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and adapted from a tutorial by Andrej
151   Karpathy (Karpathy, 2023) titled “Let’s build GPT: from scratch, in code, spelled out”. For both
152   the photoplethysmography pre-trained transformer (PPG-PT) and electrocardiography pre-trained
153   transformer (ECG-PT) we used an embedding dimension (dmodel ) of 64. The original GPT paper
154   used an embedding dimension of 768 (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018), but given that PPG and ECG
155   are far less complex than language, dmodel = 64 was found to be sufficient in our case. For our
156   decoder-only transformer we used 8 transformer blocks, each with 8 attention heads for the multi-
157   head attention as described in the original transformer paper (Vaswani et al., 2017) and in Appendix
158
      A.2. In the areas where dropout was applied (the feedforward subsection of the transformer and the
      attention weight matrix), the dropout was set to 0.2. Our models use a context length of N = 500
159
      samples, corresponding to 5-seconds of ECG, resulting in 443,493 trainable parameters.
160
161   For pre-training, multiple publicly available datasets from different regions of the world were com-
      bined for both the ECG and PPG models, resulting in 128 million tokens for PPG-PT training and 42


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      million tokens for ECG-PT training and corresponding to a similar ratio between the number of pa-
163   rameters and data-size as state-of-the-art LLMs (Dubey et al., 2024). The full pre-training details are
164   given in Appendix A.3. Importantly, unlike conventional time-series transformer models (Das et al.,
165   2024), we opted to train our models purely with cross-entropy loss and next token prediction, rather
166   than mean squared error (MSE) and windowed prediction (see Appendix A.4). This, combined with
167   the locally periodic and deterministic nature of PPG and ECG, resulted in the interpretable attention
168   mechanism that we demonstrate in this work.
169   For fine-tuning, for both tasks of screening for atrial fibrillation and beat detection in PPG, only the
170   last layer of the pre-trained model was fine-tuned. In addition, the final linear layer which transforms
171   the model dimension into the vocabulary size was replaced my a linear layer which transforms
172   the model dimension into 1-dimension. The full fine-tuning methodology, including details of the
173   publicly available datasets used, are provided in Appendix A.5.

198   In training, for all points in the context window the model outputs logits which are transformed into
199   a next token probability distribution. For the generation of future tokens, the probability distribution
200   corresponding to the last point in the context is taken. The multinomial function in PyTorch
201
      is then used to sample this distribution and generate a predicted next token. The single generated
      token can then be appended to the previous context, which is then used to generate another token.
202
      This process can be repeated, generating one token at a time, until a maximum number of generated
203
      tokens has been reached. Another way to examine the predictive capabilities of each model would
204   be to predict the next token as the one with the highest probability, instead of sampling from a
205   distribution. The problem with this second approach is that sometimes in low frequency periods,
206   such as a trough in PPG or a period between P-QRS-T regions in the ECG, taking just the token
207   with maximum likelihood could allow the model to become stuck in predicting the same token
208   value over and over again. Because of this issue, the first approach of sampling from a probability
209   distribution was chosen to evaluate the models.
210   The full dataset and pre-processing details for the evaluation of our pre-trained generative models are
211   provided in Appendix A.3.1. For each predicted token, we calculated the absolute distance between
212   the predicted token and the true token. We were then able to calculate the median and inter-quartile
213   range of the absolute prediction error against the prediction horizon for both the PPG-PT and ECG-
214   PT models. In Figure 1, example generation waveforms and maximum absolute errors are shown
215   for both the PPG-PT and ECG-PT models. Observe that the errors stem not from a failure to predict
      the morphology of the PPG or ECG, but from slight temporal misalignment between the prediction

      5.1   I NTERPRETABILITY OF AGGREGATE MODEL ATTENTION
240
      As is the case with GPT models (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018), our pre-trained transformer models
241   (PPG-PT and ECG-PT) are trained to predict the next token at each point, by using the attention
242   mechanism to collate knowledge from previous tokens. Given the task of predicting the next token
243   in a periodic signal, it is natural to think that a model or a human would look at tokens which are
244   at the same point in the previous cardiac cycles in order to make that prediction. For example, if
245   the current token to predict was a peak, it would be logical to look at all previous peaks, with an
246   emphasis on peaks of similar height and width, in order to make an accurate prediction. However,
247   the information we start with is just the position of a token in the context window and the value of a
248   token. Given that the fundamental frequency changes between contexts and that different points in
249
      the local cycle can have the same value, we cannot rely on the initial position and value information
      alone to find similar points in the same cycle. In order to understand the point at which a token lies
250
      in a cardiac cycle, it is therefore necessary to first look at the context of surrounding tokens. Once
251
      this relationship between a token and its local cycle is understood, attention can broaden to look at
252   all cardiac cycles.
253
254   This mechanism of broadening attention is indeed found in both of our PPG-PT and ECG-PT mod-
255
      els. The last row in the attention weight matrix corresponds to the final token in the context. To
      examine the attention span of the model, we calculated the central point of attention for this final
256
      row of attention in each attention head in each transformer block, across all context windows in
257
      the generative test set. Table 1 (Appendix A.7) shows that for PPG-PT, the mean central point of
258   attention is 0.43 seconds in the first transformer block, and thus the focus is within the immediate
259   cycle. By the last transformer block, this central point of attention has broadened to 2.31 seconds,
260   indicating that the PPG-PT model was updating the current token based on the tokens in previous
261   cycles of PPG. The same effect is seen in ECG-PT, with a broadening of attention from 0.33 seconds
262   in the first block to 1.88 seconds in the last block (the typical duration of a cardiac cycle is around
263   0.8-1 seconds).
264   The next step to interpreting the aggregate attention of the model is to examine the attention weights
265   of different transformer blocks for specific context examples. In Figure 2 (a) we examine the
266   summed attention weights in PPG-PT from the row associated with predicting the peak point high-
267   lighted with a blue circle. As expected, in order to predict the peak point the first transformer block
268   focuses on the immediate points before the peak point in order to learn how the point integrates into
269   the local PPG cycle. This also is true for the attention matrix rows in the first transformer block
      that are associated with all previous tokens in the context. Importantly, observe that in the final

      Remark 2. Through pre-training we have obtained models which focus on the important features
480
      of PPG and ECG, and generalise well to unseen morphologies. However, rather like in LLMs,
481   predicting the next token of PPG and ECG lacks utility. To make full use of these pre-trained models,
482   we fine-tune the models to classify atrial fibrillation (AF), as outlined in Appendix A.5.
483
484   Atrial fibrillation is the most common arrhythmia, characterised by an irregular heart rate which
485   manifests itself in rapid increases in heart rate and periods where it slows down dramatically (Wi-
      jesurendra & Casadei, 2019). The fine-tuned AF-PPG-PT model achieved a leave-one-subject-out


                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      area under the curve (AUC) of 0.93 when cross-validated across all test subjects, and 0.96 when two
487   subjects with poor signal to noise ratio were excluded. The fine-tuned AF-ECG-PT model achieved
488   an AUC of 0.99 when cross-validated across all test subjects. A comparison between our results and
489   the state-of-the-art is provided in Appendix A.8. Further fine-tuning experiments, for beat detection
490   (Appendix A.9), for ventricular premature contractions along with corresponding interpretability
491   analysis (Appendix A.10) and for four class arrhythmia classification (summarised in Appendix
492   A.11). It is worth reiterating that these results were achieved with 11 minutes of fine-tuning com-
493   puter time, corresponding to roughly 0.2% of the total time it took to train the base models.
494   The key result of fine-tuning is not just the accuracy, but the interpretability of the results. To
495   ascertain why a model made a classification in the way it did, we simply aggregated the final row
496   of SoftMaxed attention weights across all heads in the final transformer layer for the base model
497   and compared with the fine-tuned model. Any increases in attention weights therefore indicate that
498   the model deems the corresponding tokens valuable for classification of AF. In Figure 5(a), two
499   representative attention maps are displayed for the classification of a PPG context as AF. Observe in
500   both cases that the model shifts attention to periods where beats occur later than expected and earlier
501
      than expected, which is the obvious characteristic of AF. This is even clearer in the fine-tuned ECG
      attention, given that peaks in ECG are more precisely localised in time. In Figure 5(b), an example is
502
      highlighted where a beat is expected to occur based on the previous beat timing, but it does not and
503
      thus the model attention spikes. A second example is also highlighted, where based on the previous
504   beat timing a beat occurs much earlier than expected, and model attention therefore spikes exactly
505   at this point. These interpretable maps of shifts in attention, which demonstrate the reason why the
506   model has made the classification, can easily be provided along with the probability of AF.

      7   C ONCLUSION
510
      This work has demonstrated that GPT-like models, when trained to predict the next token locally
511
      in periodic physiological time series such as PPG and ECG, can be fully interpretable in their
512   operation. This has been illustrated through aggregate attention maps, which are natural for the
513   task of predicting the next token, and through the clustering of different PPG and ECG features in
514   high-dimensional space. This has been further demonstrated through individual attention heads that
515   correspond strongly to specific features, such as the dicrotic notch in PPG or the P-wave in ECG.
516   Moreover, we have shown that the so introduced interpretability is carried forward when fine-tuning
517   for abnormal heart rhythms. Indeed, in the classification of atrial fibrillation, attention shifts to re-
518   gions in the input context that most indicate the presence of the arrhythmia. This work represents a
519   step forward in the interpretability and explainability of large transformer networks when applied to
520
      healthcare settings.

740   Once tokens have been embedded with a token embedding and positional embedding which are
741
      added together, this allows the model to use information on both the position of the token in the
      context window (length N ) and the value of the token; these are fed to transformer blocks in their
742
      high-dimensional vector form. In multi-head attention, the embedding space (of size dmodel ) is
743
      divided into lower-dimensional spaces of equal size (dk ), before the results of attention are con-
744   catenated after the attention mechanism to reconstruct the original embedding space size (dmodel )
745   (Vaswani et al., 2017).
746
747
      The attention mechanism operates by allowing tokens to communicate with one another and thus
      update each other’s information. Each attention head transforms tokens into keys (K), using a linear
748
      layer which compresses the tokens from dmodel dimensions to dk dimensions. It also separately
749
      transforms tokens into queries (Q), which again use another linear layer to compress the tokens from
750   dmodel dimensions to dk dimensions. Queries (Q) can be thought of as each token broadcasting what
751   it is looking for, and keys (K) as each token broadcasting what it contains. The dot product is then
752   taken between both the queries and the keys, such that if there is a match between the information
753   a token is looking for in Q and the information a token emits in K, there√will be a spike in the
754   corresponding attention weights QK T . This dot product is next scaled by dk , and the SoftMax
755   of each row is taken (resulting in attention rows that sum to 1). This scaled dot product results in
      an N × N matrix of attention weights, which conveys how tokens communicate with each other,


                                                                                     14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      for all tokens in the context window. In masked self attention, the mechanism used to train GPT
757   models, the upper triangle of this weight matrix is set to zero. This means that tokens can only
758   communicate with themselves or with past tokens in the sequence, and allows for efficient training
759   by allowing for N separate training examples with an input block of N token length. During training
760   of the model, dropout (the random zeroing of weights) is also applied to the weight matrix. The
761   final step in the masked self-attention process is to multiply the weight matrix with a value matrix.
762   The value matrix, V , is formed from another linear layer that compresses the tokens from dmodel
763   dimensions to dk dimensions. A combination of all of these steps produces the following attention
764   function (Vaswani et al., 2017)

769   As previously mentioned, the results of each head, which are of dimension dk = dmodel /(# Heads),
770
      are next concatenated to give the original dimension dmodel . This output from multi-head attention
      is then added to the input to the multi-head attention, forming a residual connection that allows the
771
      model to bypass a transformer block if needed. Finally, this new combined output is normalised
772
      and passed through a feed-forward network which firstly expands the embedding dimension to 4 ×
773   dmodel , applies a ReLU activation function, and then compresses the dimension back to dmodel .
774   During training, dropout is also applied to this feed-forward network. The feed-forward network
775   after multi-head attention can be thought of as allowing the model to process the results of self-
776   attention, before these results are fed into another multi-head attention block. The combination of
777   the multi-head attention and this feed-forward network constitute one transformer block. After the
778   final transformer block, the output is passed through a final linear layer, which converts the output
779   from a dimension of dmodel to the size of the vocabulary. When passed through a SoftMax activation
780   function, this provides the probabilities for all tokens in the vocabulary, at all N points.

847   A.4     C ROSS E NTROPY L OSS AND N EXT T OKEN P REDICTION VS M EAN S QUARED E RROR
848
849
      Conventionally, time series transformer models produce a continuous output values for each token
      prediction and are thus trained with a mean squared error (MSE) loss function (Das et al., 2024) or a
850
      combination of likelihood and MSE (Liu et al., 2022). However, unlike many time series prediction
851
      tasks, we were able to leverage the highly periodic nature of the PPG and ECG signals to create
852   models with a well-defined small vocabulary. This allowed us to train the model exclusively with a
853   cross-entropy loss function, in a similar fashion to a conventional large language model. It should
854   be noted that we did also experiment with continuous output values and mean squared error as a
855   loss function, but these results were less able to capture long range trends and importantly lacked
856   interpretability in the attention weights. A full investigation into this finding is beyond the scope of
857   this work.
858   Through training for next token prediction in combination with cross entropy loss, for each part of
859   the context window, we force tokens to update in a way which is still relevant to that specific token.
860   For example, tokens around peaks update to become more “peak-like” as they propagate through
861   the transformer blocks, and the same principle is the case for all other positions in each cardiac
862   cycle. In that sense, the final layer of attention operates on a ECG or PPG signal where each token is
863   rich in period-specific information, and attention can thus be fine-tuned in a way that is more easily
      interpretable to a clinician.

      A.5.1    ATRIAL F IBRILLATION D ETECTION IN ECG AND PPG
871
872   During the fine-tuning, the final linear layer, which converts the output from dmodel dimensions to
873   the dimensions of the vocabulary size, was replaced with a linear layer which instead converts the
874   output from dmodel dimensions to 1 dimension. This new output was then passed through a sigmoid
875   activation function to scale the values between 0 and 1; the final value was then used to classify the
876   signal as either healthy (0) or atrial fibrillation (1). A binary cross-entropy loss criterion was then
877   used to map the output value to the true class. This same conversion of the final linear layer of the
878   model could be used for any suitable classification or regression task, by simply scaling the number
879   of output dimensions to be in line with the specific task. During training, just the new linear layer
880
      and the final transformer block were trained, whilst all other layers were frozen. In this example, the
      learning rate was maintained at 3 × 10−4 within the AdamW optimiser.
881
882   To train and evaluate the fine-tuning of the model for classification of atrial fibrillation (AF), the
883   “MIMIC PERform AF” dataset (Charlton et al., 2022) was used, which is a subset of MIMIC III
884   dataset (Johnson et al., 2016). This dataset contains 20 minutes of continuous PPG and ECG record-
885   ings from 35 subjects, of whom 19 had AF and 16 did not have AF. These signals, originally recorded
886
      at a sample frequency of 125Hz, were band-pass filtered to between 1 and 15Hz and then downsam-
      pled to 50Hz in the case of the PPG, and band-pass filtered to between 1 and 45Hz and downsampled
887
      to 100Hz in the case of the ECG. A window length of 500 samples was maintained, corresponding
888
      to 10 seconds of PPG (50Hz) and 5 seconds of ECG (100Hz), and sliding windows were extracted
889   from the data and tokenised with a shift of 50 samples each time, in order to artificially increase
890   the volume of data for the model training. In both the cases of ECG and PPG, the models were
891   fine-tuned in a leave-one-subject-out fashion by training on 34 subjects and testing on 1, over 1,000
892   iterations and with a batch size of 128. Each model took 11 minutes to fine tune on an RTX A2000
893   GPU, which is a fraction of the over 5 days that it took to train each of the base models.

895   A.5.2    B EAT D ETECTION IN PPG
896
897   As was the case with fine-tuning to detect atrial fibrillation, the final linear layer was again replaced
898   with a linear layer which converted the output from dmodel dimensions to 1 dimension. However,
899   instead of using just the final value in the context as was the case with AF, all values from the context
900   were used. This 1-dimensional array was passed through a Sigmoid activation function, to map to
901   values of 0 (no peak) or 1 (peak) for all tokens in the context. Only the new linear layer and the
902   final transformer layer were trained. The learning rate was again maintained at 3 × 10−4 within the
903
      AdamW optimiser, with a binary cross entropy loss function. The model was fine-tuned over 5000
      iterations, with a batch size of 128.
904
905   To train and evaluate the fine-tuning of the model for beat detection, the “MIMIC PERform” training
906   and test datasets were used (Charlton et al., 2022). Both datasets consist of real-world clinical PPG
907   and ECG recordings from 200 subjects at a sample frequency of 125Hz, for 10 minutes per subject.
908   The training dataset was used to train and validate, and the unseen test dataset was then used to
909
      evaluate performance of the trained model. The PPG signals were band-pass filtered between 1
      and 30 Hz to remove low frequency variations, and ECG between 4 and 60Hz to isolate the higher
910
      frequency QRS complex and remove P and T waves. Labels for peaks were determined by using the
911
      inbuilt MATLAB findpeaks function on the filtered reference ECG data. Peaks, and the points
912   immediately neighbouring the peaks, were labelled as 1. All other points were labeled as 0. This
913   signal was then aligned with the filtered PPG signal to the point of maximal cross correlation, to
914   ensure peak labels that mapped to the PPG peaks. Both the reference signal and the PPG signal
915   were resampled to 50Hz, and the PPG signal was then tokenised as per previous examples. In the
916   reference signal, the difference in the time between each peak should be minimal. When the standard
917   deviation of this difference was greater than 0.3 seconds, a sign that the reference signal was of poor
      quality, the subject was excluded from the training data.

      When testing, the criterion outlined by Charlton et al. (Charlton et al., 2022) was followed to
919   allow for comparability of results. Poor quality labels were excluded from the test set by removing
920   reference segments which had no beats for 2 seconds or more. Input segments with constant values
921   for more than 0.2 seconds, an indication of clipping and thus data loss, were also excluded from
922   analysis. Output peaks were determined by applying findpeaks to the output of the model,
923   with a minimum height of 0.015, a maximum peak width of 0.4 seconds, and a minimum distance
924   between peaks of 0.28 seconds (corresponding to a heart rate of 214 beats per minute). If a predicted
925   beat was within 0.15 seconds of a reference beat, it was deemed to be correct. Results were then
926   analysed in terms of the median F1 score (%) across test subjects.
927   It should be noted that the fine-tuned model produced continuous values between 0 and 1, for each
928   position in the context, which are a measure of its confidence that a beat is present. In our analysis,
929   we also investigated the utility of using these beat confidence values as a measure of signal quality.

       A.10    F INE - TUNING TO D ETECT P REMATURE V ENTRICULAR C ONTRACTIONS IN ECG
1108   In order to further demonstrate the capabilities of our model for interpretable classification, we fine-
1109   tuned our model our model to detect premature ventricular contractions (PVCs) in ECG. Unlike AF,
1110   the premature ventricular contraction is also characterised by a clear morphological change in the
1111   ECG (Yamada, 2019). Given this, classification of PVCs were selected for further interpretability
1112   analysis in addition to AF, given that the presence of a PVC is clear for clinicians and non-clinicians
1113   alike. A subset of over 200 subjects with PVCs was created from the Chapman dataset (Zheng et al.,
1114   2020), and matched with over 5000 subjects without PVCs. Shifted windows were extracted from
1115   subjects with PVCs in order to balance the proportion of PVC to non-PVC dataset. To evaluate our
1116
       model, we performed 4-fold cross validation, ensuring no subject leakage between train and test, and
       repeated with 5 random seeds. The resulting AUC of 0.99 is reported in Table 2 along with further
1117
       performance metrics. The interpretability analysis, which looks at the shift in final layer attention
1118
       weights from the pretrained model to the fine-tuned model, is shown in two examples in Figure 10.
1119   It can be seen that attention shifts to regions of PVCs, in order to inform the classification of a PVC.

1146   A.12                                T HE EFFECTS OF NOISE ON CLASSIFICATION
1147
1148   The presence of noise is a principal issue in the analysis of physiological time-series data (Charlton
1149   et al., 2023). Previous work on classification of in PPG AF has demonstrated that classification
1150   performance can increase dramatically when signal quality is classified simultaneously with AF
1151
       (Torres-Soto & Ashley, 2020). To this end, we investigated the effects of noise on our classification
       of AF, by employing our fine-tuned beat detector (Appendix A.9) for signal quality estimation in our
1152
       AF dataset. It is noted in Figure 11(a) that the majority of falsely classified test segments have low
1153
       signal quality. By removing the bottom 15% of signal qualities from the test set, as determined by
1154   average beat confidence from our beat detector, the false positive rate is reduced from 26% to 12%,
1155   and the AUC is increased from 0.93 to 0.98. In Figure 11(b), an example workflow is shown, in
1156   which only signals that have high signal quality and are predicted as AF are flagged for a clinician.

       A.13                                F UTURE WORK
1181
1182   Many tasks require inputs of longer context lengths, such as sleep staging in which 30 seconds is
1183   the normal interval but for measurements of the autonomic nervous system contexts of 5 minutes
1184   may be required (Li et al., 2021). Future work should aim to scale up the model to larger context
1185   lengths, whilst trying to maintain efficiency, by employing methods such as Sparse Attention (Roy
1186   et al., 2021). Moreover, the models should be scaled up to account for applications that require novel
1187   sensor locations, such as in-ear PPG (Davies et al., 2022) and ear-ECG (von Rosenberg et al., 2017).
