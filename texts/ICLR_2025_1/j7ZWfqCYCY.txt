011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have demonstrated significant
014            advancements in artificial intelligence, transforming tasks across various domains.
015            Despite their capabilities, these models are susceptible to jailbreak attacks, which
016            can compromise their safety and reliability. This paper explores the trade-off be-
017            tween jailbreakability and stealthiness in VLMs, presenting a novel algorithm to
018            detect non-stealthy jailbreak attacks and enhance model robustness. We intro-
019
               duce a stealthiness-aware jailbreak attack using diffusion models, highlighting the
               challenge of detecting AI-generated content. Our approach leverages Fano’s in-
020
               equality to elucidate the relationship between attack success rates and stealthiness
021
               scores, providing an explainable framework for evaluating these threats. Our con-
022            tributions aim to fortify AI systems against sophisticated attacks, ensuring their
023            outputs remain aligned with ethical standards and user expectations.

               Content Warning: This paper contains harmful information which intend to aid
025
               the robustness of generative models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023;
032   Duan et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022) and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) (Devlin et al., 2019;
033
      Lu et al., 2019; Alayrac et al., 2022) have become transformative tools in artificial intelligence,
      demonstrating exceptional capabilities across diverse domains. LLMs, such as GPT-4 (Achiam
034
      et al., 2023), excel in generating coherent, human-like text, facilitating applications from content
035
      creation to programming. In parallel, VLMs synthesize visual and textual inputs, powering advanced
036   tasks like image captioning (Radford et al., 2021; Su et al., 2020), visual question answering (Li
037   et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2022), and multimodal reasoning (Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021).
038   These models harness extensive datasets and sophisticated architectures, predominantly rooted in
039   transformer networks (Vaswani et al., 2017), contributing to their indispensability in both academia
040   and industry.
041
      Nevertheless, LLMs and VLMs are susceptible to jailbreak attacks (Wallace et al., 2019; Zhang
042   et al., 2020), including black-box and white-box strategies. Black-box attacks modify inputs (text,
043   image, or both) to subtly alter outputs without accessing the model’s internal structures, often as-
044   suming encoders similar to CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or BLIP (Li et al., 2022; 2023) or using
045   inventive heuristics. Conversely, white-box attacks exploit knowledge of the model’s architecture
046   and parameters, enabling attackers to craft inputs that circumvent safety measures.
047
      Text-based attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2024; Wei
048   et al., 2023; Yong et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023) are frequently detected using blacklisted sensitive
049   words or perplexity-based filters (Jain et al., 2023) that evaluate text coherence and complexity.
050   Similarly, image-based attacks (Liu et al., 2024b; Ying et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Shayegani et al.,
051   2024) can be identified using entropy-based detectors analyzing image complexity. In Figure 1,
052   we illustrate how high-perplexity text prompts and high-entropy image prompts can be effectively
053   discerned. This observation drives our investigation into highly covert jailbreak attacks and the
      enhancement of model robustness using covert detection criteria.

054
      Our research investigates the harmlessness alignment in state-of-the-art VLMs, including Chat-
055   GPT (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023), and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) (based on
056   Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), an LLM open-sourced by Meta). We test our attack on these models,
057   focusing on this alignment similar to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Chris-
058   tiano et al., 2017), crucial for ensuring outputs are non-malicious. Conversely, significant research
059   has focused on heuristic jailbreaking methods, yet the relationship between attack success rates and
060   stealthiness remains unclear. We are the first to reveal an information-theoretical tradeoff between
061   jailbreakability and stealthiness in VLMs.
062   Our contributions are threefold: (1) We propose an algorithm that detects non-stealthy jailbreak
063   attacks, improving VLM defense robustness. (2) To evade this detection algorithm, we introduce
064   a stealthiness-aware jailbreak attack using diffusion models, highlighting the link between detect-
065   ing these attacks and the challenge of identifying AI-generated content (AIGC), given their similar
066   detection difficulty. (3) Most importantly, through Fano’s inequality (Cover & Thomas, 2006), we
067   characterize the relationship between jailbreak attack success rates and a specified stealthiness score,
068   offering an explainable tool for existing methods.

131   3.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODEL
132
133   A Vision-Language Model (VLM) is a multimodal system processing both textual and visual inputs.
134   Formally, we define the text domain as T and the image domain as I. Let tprompt ∈ T be a text prompt
135   and iprompt ∈ I an image prompt. The VLM is modeled as a probabilistic function M : Q → T ,
136
      where the query domain Q = (I ∪ ∅) × (T ∪ ∅).
137
      3.2   S AFE Q UERIES AND R ESPONSES
138
139   To ensure a VLM generates safe responses, we define the prohibited query oracle: Op : Q → {0, 1},
140   which returns 1 if a query q ∈ Q is prohibited by the safety policy and 0 otherwise.
141
142   Prohibited Query Oracle in Practice. Typically, three main methods are used to detect prohibited
143   queries in language models. The first, substring lookup, searches for predefined phrases like “I am
144   sorry” or “I cannot assist with that” in the model’s response to flag refusals. While efficient, it
145   may miss subtler refusals. The second method, LLM-based review, employs an advanced language
146   model to contextually assess responses for harmful or restricted content, even without explicit refusal
147   phrases. Lastly, manual review involves human evaluators inspecting responses for compliance with
148   safety guidelines, ensuring thorough detection, especially for sensitive content, though it is time-
149   consuming.

153   In this section, we introduce the Intra-Entropy Gap Algorithm for detecting jailbreak attacks in
154
      VLMs. We then present a novel jailbreak attack crafted to evade this algorithm. Finally, we pro-
      pose a trade-off analysis between jailbreakability and stealthiness to elucidate the limitations and
155
      performance of prior methods.

157   4.1   D ETECTING NON - STEALTHY JAILBREAK ATTACKS
158
159   To detect non-stealthy jailbreak attacks, we propose two algorithms, Algorithms 1 and 2 (in Ap-
160   pendix D), utilizing entropy and perplexity-based gap analysis, one for image data and another for
161   text data. Both algorithms identify inconsistencies or anomalies indicating an attack by analyzing
      differences in randomness or complexity across data segments.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Algorithm 1 divides an image into two non-overlapping regions R1 and R2 such that R1 ∪ R2 = I,
163   computing the entropy for each, which measures the randomness or information density of pixel
164   intensities. An attack altering part of the image, such as texture changes or artificial elements, likely
165   causes an entropy imbalance between R1 and R2 . By measuring the entropy gap—the difference
166   in entropy between R1 and R2 —this algorithm detects visual anomalies indicative of non-stealthy
167   manipulations, like noise patterns or detectable alterations.
168
      Despite the simplicity, we demonstrate the effectiveness of Algorithm 1 in Section 5 by evaluating
169   them on non-stealthy jailbreak attacks. It is important to note that Algorithm 1 is a procedure
170   designed to generate a useful feature for classification. Therefore, to evaluate its performance, we
171   require a classifier, such as Logistic Regression, to compute metrics like AUROC and F1 scores.

      Line 6 of Algorithm 1 can be implemented in various ways. In image processing, random parti-
      tioning into two non-overlapping regions can be achieved through several methods. Pixel-based
192
      partitioning (Gonzalez, 2009) assigns each pixel randomly to a region, while block-based parti-
193
      tioning (Jain, 1989) divides the image into blocks for random assignment. Line-based partitioning
194   (Haralick & Shapiro, 1985) splits the image along a random line, and Voronoi partitioning (Tessel-
195   lations, 1992) assigns pixels based on proximity to random seed points. Despite their variety, these
196   methods can be computationally intensive for large images. Therefore, we utilize rotation partition-
197   ing (Algorithm 3 in the Appendix D) for practical efficiency. Furthermore, we provide a formal
198   analysis showing that K trials achieve probabilistic guarantees of detection with confidence 1 − δ,
199   as demonstrated in Appendix B.

201   While there are still many circumvention techniques that can bypass our detection system, we are
202   pioneering efforts to address this challenge. In this work, we focus on scenarios involving clean
203   images without common benign noise patterns (such as Gaussian, Laplacian, or salt-and-pepper
204   noise). A detailed discussion of these limitations can be found in Appendix D.

269   the underlying reasons for the effectiveness of typographic attacks, primarily through experimental
      analysis. In contrast, adopting an information-theoretic approach, we employ Fano’s inequality to

273   Before presenting Theorem 1, we outline the setting. Let X be a finite set of jailbreak responses, with
274   X ∈ X as a chosen response. We define two Markov chains: X → Y1 → X̂ and X → Y2 → X̂.
275   Here, X is a selected response from X . The variables Y1 and Y2 are data derived from X, with Y1 as
276   text data and Y2 as image data. X̂ is the prediction of X, based on both Y1 and Y2 . Here, the Markov
277   chain structures X → Y1 → X̂ and X → Y2 → X̂ imply that: In the first chain, X̂ depends on X
278   only through the text data Y1 . In the second chain, X̂ depends on X only through the image data Y2 .
279
      Thus, X̂ is an estimation of X, which relies on both the text and image data Y1 and Y2 .

338
      quantifying the trade-off between False Positive Rate for natural samples and True Positive Rate for

340   classification accuracy. Together, these metrics offer a comprehensive assessment of the classifier’s
341   ability to distinguish between natural and jailbreak samples.

      7   C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In conclusion, our research enhances the robustness of defense mechanisms against jailbreak at-
531
      tacks on vision language models (VLMs) by introducing an algorithm that detects non-stealthy
532   attacks. We advance this field by developing a stealthiness-aware jailbreak attack using diffusion
533   models, bridging the gap between detecting such attacks and the broader challenge of identify-
534   ing AI-generated content (AIGC). Additionally, through Fano’s inequality, we provide a theoretical
535   framework explaining the relationship between jailbreak attack success rates and their stealthiness
536   scores. This work contributes to AI security and offers an explainable tool for evaluating the stealth-
537   iness of jailbreak attacks. Future research will focus on refining these detection algorithms and
538   exploring their applicability to a broader range of AI systems to enhance defenses against increas-
539   ingly sophisticated threats.

       In this section, we present Algorithm 2, a text-based detection method and Algorithm 3, the Maxi-
1104
       mum Entropy Gap via Rotation Partitioning algorithm for jailbreak detection.
1105
1106   Algorithm 2 is designed for text-based data, where jailbreak attacks may involve inserting or mod-
1107   ifying sections of a conversation. The text is split into two halves, and for each half, we calculate
1108   the perplexity, which measures the uncertainty in predicting token sequences based on a language
1109
       model. A non-stealthy attack, such as unnatural language injections or abrupt changes in style, will
       manifest as a significant difference in perplexity between the two halves of the text. The perplexity
1110
       gap between the prefix and suffix serves as an indicator of unusual language patterns, thus detecting
1111
       such jailbreak attempts.
1112
1113
       Algorithm 2 Compute Perplexity Gap Between Prefix and Suffix Tokens
1114
1115    1: Input: Token set T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn } from a text document
1116    2: Output: Perplexity gap ∆P
1117    3: Partition T into Tprefix and Tsuffix : T = Tprefix ∪ Tsuffix , Tprefix ∩ Tsuffix = ∅
        4: Define probability distribution P (T )(t) for token t ∈ T basedP       on the language model
1118
        5: Compute the entropy H(X) for a subset X ⊆ T : H(X) = − t∈X P (X)(t) log P (X)(t)
1119
1120
        6: Calculate perplexity P(X) for a subset X ⊆ T : P(X) = 2H(X)
        7: Compute the perplexity for Tprefix and Tsuffix : Pprefix = P(Tprefix ), Psuffix = P(Tsuffix )
1121

       Adversarial Example Detection Adversarial example detection for VLMs presents unique chal-
1175
       lenges that differentiate it from traditional classifier detection. While classifier attacks typically aim
1176   to change a single predicted class label, VLM attacks target a broader space of possible outputs in
1177   the form of natural language descriptions. This fundamental difference makes traditional detection
1178   methods that rely on label consistency or classification boundaries less applicable. VLM attacks can
1179   subtly alter the semantic meaning of outputs while maintaining grammatical correctness and natu-
1180   ral language structure, making detection more challenging. Additionally, the multi-modal nature of
1181   VLMs means attacks can exploit either visual or textual components or their interactions, requiring
1182   detection methods that can operate effectively across both modalities. This expanded attack sur-
1183   face and output space requires rethinking detection strategies beyond the binary correct/incorrect
1184
       classification paradigm used in traditional adversarial example detection.
1185   Although theoretical results (Tramer, 2022) imply that finding a strong detector should be as hard
1186   as finding a robust model, there are some early approaches focused on statistical detection meth-
1187   ods, (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017) detects adversarial images by performing PCA whitening on
       input images and checking if their low-ranked principal component coefficients have abnormally

1189   man et al., 2017), though many were later shown vulnerable to adaptive attacks (Carlini & Wagner,
1190   2017). Researchers have also explored feature-space analysis methods, including local intrinsic di-
1191   mensionality (Ma & Liu, 2019), feature squeezing (Xu, 2018), and unified frameworks for detecting
1192   both out-of-distribution samples and adversarial attacks (Lee et al., 2018). A work has leveraged
1193   generative models for detection (Yin et al., 2020) and developed certified detection approaches with

1204   Early work by (Zhang et al., 2022) established the foundation for multimodal adversarial attacks
1205
       with Co-Attack, which demonstrated that collectively attacking both image and text modalities
       while considering their interactions is more effective than single-modal approaches. (Lu et al., 2023)
1206
       advanced this concept with Set-level Guidance Attack (SGA), introducing data augmentation and
1207
       enhanced cross-modal guidance through multiple image scales and text pairs to improve transfer-
1208   ability. Subsequently, several approaches emerged to address different aspects of adversarial attacks
1209   on VLMs. (Han et al., 2023) approached the problem from an optimal transport perspective with
1210   OT-Attack, formulating image and text features as distinct distributions to determine optimal map-
1211   pings between them, thereby mitigating overfitting issues. (He et al., 2023) focused on data diversity
1212   with SA-Attack, employing self-augmentation techniques for both image and text modalities to en-
1213   hance transferability. Recent work has introduced more sophisticated generation-based approaches.
1214   (Xu et al., 2024) developed MDA, leveraging Stable Diffusion’s cross-attention modules to gener-
1215   ate adversarial examples in the latent space, using adversarial text both as guidance for diffusion
1216
       and in loss calculations. (Pan et al., 2024) proposed SCA, utilizing Multimodal Large Language
       Models for semantic guidance and employing edit-friendly noise maps to minimize semantic distor-
1217
       tion. (Zhang et al., 2024) introduced AnyAttack, a self-supervised framework that enables targeted
1218
       attacks without label supervision through contrastive learning. This progression shows the field’s
1219   evolution from basic multimodal attacks to increasingly sophisticated approaches that leverage ad-
1220   vanced techniques in cross-modal interactions, data augmentation, and generative models to achieve
1221   better transferability across VLMs.
