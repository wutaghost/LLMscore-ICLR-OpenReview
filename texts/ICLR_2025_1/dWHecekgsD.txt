009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding molecular structure and related knowledge is crucial for scientific
012            research. Recent studies integrate molecular graphs with their textual descrip-
013            tions to enhance molecular representation learning. However, they focus on the
014            whole molecular graph and neglect frequently occurring subgraphs, known as
015            motifs,which are essential for determining molecular properties. Without such fine-
016
               grained knowledge, these models struggle to generalize to unseen molecules and
               tasks that require motif-level insights. To bridge this gap, we propose FineMolTex,
017
               a novel Fine-grained Molecular graph-Text pre-training framework to jointly learn
018
               coarse-grained molecule-level knowledge and fine-grained motif-level knowledge.
019            Specifically, FineMolTex consists of two pre-training tasks: a contrastive alignment
020            task for coarse-grained matching and a masked multi-modal modeling task for
021            fine-grained matching. In particular, the latter predicts the labels of masked motifs
022            and words, leveraging insights from each other, thereby enabling FineMolTex to
023            understand the fine-grained matching between motifs and words. Finally, we con-
024            duct extensive experiments across three downstream tasks, achieving up to 230%
025            improvement in the text-based molecule editing task. Additionally, our case studies
026            reveal that FineMolTex successfully captures fine-grained knowledge, potentially
027
               offering valuable insights for drug discovery and catalyst design.
028
029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Comprehending molecular structure and related knowledge is pivotal in scientific investigations
032   spanning diverse fields, including chemistry, drug discovery, and materials science (Gilmer et al.,
033
      2017). Recent advancements in artificial intelligence and machine learning have yielded promising
      outcomes for molecule-based tasks such as retrosynthesis (Yan et al., 2020) and drug discovery
034
      (Gilmer et al., 2017). The majority of these studies (Krenn et al., 2020; Duvenaud et al., 2015; Liu
035
      et al., 2019a; Toshev et al., 2023; Atz et al., 2021) concentrate solely on the molecular structure, such
036   as SMILES strings, molecular graphs, and geometric structures. They learn molecular representations
037   under supervised signals such as toxicity level and drug activity. However, this supervised learning
038   requires extensive and costly labeling of pre-defined categories, limiting the application of previous
039   methods to unseen categories and tasks.
040
      Fortunately, compared to task-specific labeled data, textual descriptions of molecules are fairly
041
      abundant. These descriptions can be found in chemical database annotations, research papers in
042   chemistry and biology, and drug instruction sheets (Liu et al., 2023a), providing general information
043   on molecular usage, efficacy, chemical properties, and even detailed insights into specific functional
044   groups and chemical moieties (Kim et al., 2021). Hence, several studies explore molecular structures
045   along with their corresponding descriptions. MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a) and MoMu (Su et al.,
046   2022) align the whole molecular graphs with their textual descriptions employing a contrastive
047   learning approach as shown in Figure 1(a). MolCA (Liu et al., 2023b) further utilizes a cross-modal
048   projector to map the graph embedding space to the input space of the language model. In this way,
049   these studies reduce the reliance on task-specific labels.
050   However, these approaches primarily focus on the overall structure of the molecule level, failing to
051   capture fine-grained knowledge of the sub-molecule level, such as functional groups. A natural tool to
052   model sub-molecular structures is the motif (Zhang et al., 2021), which refers to frequently recurring,
053   significant subgraphs within molecular graphs. Motifs often play a key role in determining the
      properties of the whole molecular graph (Zhang et al., 2021), and motif-level knowledge is frequently

071   of various motifs that have been seen before. For example, consider the zero-shot graph-text retrieval
072   task shown in Figure 1(c), which aims to find the molecule most relevant to the given text. Even if
073   the model has not been trained on the candidate molecules, it has seen many of the motifs within the

075   and “carboxylic”, respectively. Thus, the model can easily recognize the relevant molecule. Second,
076

      molecule editing task illustrated in Figure 1(c), the model aims to modify part of the molecular
077

078
      of the motifs like “chloride”.
079
080   Despite the significance of this fine-grained knowledge, it is challenging to jointly learn both molecule-
081   and motif-level knowledge, and also non-trivial to capture fine-grained matching without supervised
082
      signals. To overcome these issues, in this work, we propose a novel Fine-grained Molecular graph-
      Text framework (FineMolTex) to learn fine-grained motif-level knowledge, as well as coarse-grained
083
      molecule-level knowledge. First, we extract each motif or word token with an individual embedding
084
      for fine-grained knowledge and utilize two global tokens to holistically represent a molecular graph
085   and its corresponding text description as coarse-grained knowledge. Second, to align coarse- and
086   fine-grained matching, we propose two pre-training tasks respectively: the contrastive alignment
087   task based on the global tokens, and the masked multi-modal learning task based on the motif and
088   word tokens. Specifically, in the masked multi-modal modeling task illustrated in Figure 1(b), we
089   randomly mask some motif and word tokens, and further incorporate a cross-attention transformer
090   layer to integrate the embeddings of motifs and words. By predicting the labels of masked motifs and
091   words based on information from each other, the learning of fine-grained alignment knowledge is
092   enhanced. In summary, we outline our contributions as follows:
093
      • We are the first to reveal that learning fine-grained motif-level knowledge provides key insight for
094     bridging molecular graphs and text descriptions, further empowering the ability to generalize to
095     unseen molecules and tasks.
096
      • We introduce a novel framework named FineMolTex, consisting of two self-supervised pre-training
097
        tasks, to simultaneously learn coarse- and fine-grained knowledge. In particular, the masked
098     multi-modal learning task enhances the prediction for masked tokens leveraging information from
099     the other modality, promoting the learning of fine-grained alignment information.
100
      • Experimental results across three downstream tasks underscore the effectiveness of FineMolTex,
101
        with a notable improvement of up to 230% in the text-based molecule editing task. Furthermore,
102     case studies demonstrate that FineMolTex effectively aligns motifs and words, further facilitating
103     applications such as drug discovery and catalyst design.

107   We provide a brief review on molecular multi-modal learning. Prior works predominantly concentrate
      on modeling the chemical structures such as 1D SMILES (Krenn et al., 2020), 2D molecular graphs

108
      (Duvenaud et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2019a; Zhang et al., 2021), and 3D geometric structures (Toshev
109   et al., 2023; Atz et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). They utilize supervised signals on a predetermined
110   set, and thus cannot generalize to unseen categories without labeled examples. Recently, KV-PLM
111   (Zeng et al., 2022) bridges this gap by linking SMILES with biomedical texts through a unified
112   language modeling framework. Nonetheless, 1D SMILES may omit certain structural details and
113   fail to identify structural similarities among molecules due to its non-uniqueness. To address these
114   limitations, MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a) and MoMu (Su et al., 2022) employ a contrastive
115   learning approach to align the molecular graph with its corresponding text, thus performing well on
116   unseen molecules and texts. However, these models are less effective on molecule-to-text generation
117
      tasks because language models are not yet well-versed in interpreting graphs as generative conditions.
      Therefore, MolCA (Liu et al., 2023b) introduces a cross-modal projector to align the embedding
118
      space of the molecular graph with the language model’s input space, enabling the comprehension of
119
      2D graphs as generative conditions. This approach has also been extended to 3D graph structures,
120   where 3D-MoLM (Li et al., 2024) uses a cross-modal projector to synchronize the embedding space
121   of the 3D geometric structure with that of the language model. Additionally, various efforts have been
122   devoted to tackling specific molecular tasks based on textual data, including zero-shot instruction
123   molecular learning (Zhao et al., 2023), molecular reaction prediction (Shi et al., 2023), and molecular
124   relational modeling (Fang et al., 2024).
125
      More related works on graph-based molecular learning, as well as more general multi-modal learning,
126   can be found in Appendix A.

130
131   We propose FineMolTex, a novel fine-grained molecular graph-text framework, learning both
132   molecule- and motif-level knowledge. The model architecture is outlined in Figure 2. This section
133
      first introduces the key components in the architecture and then describes the two pre-training tasks.
134
135   3.1   K EY C OMPONENTS OF F INE M OLT EX
136
      To capture coarse- and fine-grained knowledge, we propose FineMolTex, consisting of five key
137
      components: 1) the tokenization component to decompose molecular graphs and texts into motif and
138
      word tokens; 2) a graph encoder to capture the structure of molecules and motifs; 3) a text encoder
139   to extract the knowledge from texts and words, 4) a cross-attention layer to integrate information
140   from different modalities; 5) a Transformer layer to generate embeddings for each token based on its
141   contextual tokens from the same modality.
142
      Tokenization. As shown in Figure 2, for fine-grained modeling, we fragment the molecular graphs
143
      and texts into motif tokens and word tokens. We employ the BRICS (Degen et al., 2008) algorithm
144
      to transform the molecular graph into a motif tree, and then generate a motif sequence following
145   a breadth-first search order. Then we utilize the post-processing procedure (Zhang et al., 2021)
146   to consolidate the motif vocabulary. We break the textual description into word tokens using the
147   word tokenizer of SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019). For coarse-grained modeling, the global tokens
148   of molecule and text, <MOL> and <CLS>, are inserted at the beginning of the motif and word
149   sequences, respectively, resulting in the sequences m0 , m1 , . . . , mJ and t0 , t1 , . . . , tD , where J and
150   D are the lengths of the sequences.
151   Graph Encoder. Let G = (V, E, X) represent a molecular graph with N atoms, where V =
152   {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN } is the set of atoms, E ⊆ V × V denotes the bonds, and X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ] ∈
153   RN ×ζ is the atom feature matrix. Here, xi is the feature vector of atom vi , and ζ is the dimension of
154   atom features. We utilize GraphMVP (Liu et al., 2019b), a pre-trained Graph Isomorphism Network
155   (GIN), to encode each motif token. GraphMVP employs multi-view pre-training to connect 2D
156   topologies and 3D geometries, leveraging the GEOM dataset (Axelrod & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2022),
157   which contains 250K molecular conformations. Denoting the GraphMVP encoder as fGraphMVP , we
158   encode each atom v into an embedding as follows:

236

238

240   iterated for ℓ times.

295   4.1   G ENERALIZATION TO U NSEEN M OLECULES (RQ1)
296
297   To answer RQ1, we conduct a zero-shot graph-text retrieval task to examine the generalizability
298   of FineMolTex on unseen molecules and texts. Given a molecular graph and T candidate textual
299
      descriptions, the goal is to identify the textual description that best aligns with the molecular graph.
      Conversely, given a textual description and T candidate molecular graphs, identify the molecular
300
      graph that best matches the text. This task can be addressed by calculating the similarity of the
301
      molecular graphs and texts in the joint embedding space, thus allowing zero-shot inference.
302
303   Datasets and Baselines. We utilize DrugBank-Pharmacodynamics, molecule-ATC, and DrugBank-
304   Description (Liu et al., 2023a) extracted from the DrugBank database (Wishart et al., 2018) for
305
      evaluation. These datasets include molecular graphs and their chemical descriptions. Details of the
      datasets can be found in Appendix C.1.2. We compare with five multimodal molecular models:
306
      KV-PLM (Zeng et al., 2022), MolCA (Liu et al., 2023b), MoMu-S (Su et al., 2022), MoMu-K (Su
307
      et al., 2022), and MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a). Specifically, KV-PLM uses SMILES to represent
308   the structure of the molecule, while others use graph structures.
309
310   Results. We report the results on the first two datasets in Tables 1 and 2, and defer those on
311
      DrugBank-Description to Appendix D.1 due to space limit. We make the following observations. 1)
      Across different values of T , FineMolTex consistently outperforms the baselines that neglect motif-
312
      level knowledge. The superior performance demonstrates that fine-grained motif-level knowledge
313
      facilitates generalization to unseen molecules, which likely contain seen motifs. 2) FineMolTex
314   maintains strong performance in both directions (given graph, and given text). The symmetry further
315   indicates that the embedding spaces of both modalities are well-aligned and similarly well-learned.
316   3) We observe that KV-PLM, which utilizes SMILES to capture molecular structures, is less effective
317   than other models employing graphs, consistent with previous findings (Liu et al., 2023a) that 2D
318   graph structure is more expressive than 1D SMILES.

      4.3    A PPLICATION TO S INGLE -M ODALITY TASK (RQ3)
393
394
      While FineMolTex can simultaneously utilize pre-trained knowledge from both graphs and texts, we
395   also verify its effectiveness on single-modality tasks, namely, molecular property prediction tasks.
396   We use MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018) as the dataset, which only provides molecular graphs as input
397   without texts. More specifically, there are eight binary classification tasks, and we report ROC-AUC
398   for evaluation. More detailed dataset descriptions are provided in Appendix C.1.3.
399
      Baselines. We compare FineMolTex against nine baselines, including 1) five pre-trained GNN
400
      models: AttrMasking (Hu et al., 2019), ContextPred (Hu et al., 2019), InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019),
401   MolCLR (Wang et al., 2021b), and GraphMVP (Liu et al., 2019b); 2) three graph-text multimodal
402   models: MoMu-S (Su et al., 2022), MoMu-K (Su et al., 2022), and MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a),
403   and 3) one SMILES-text multimodal model: KV-PLM (Zeng et al., 2022).
404
      Results. As shown in Table 3, FineMolTex consistently outperforms all baselines, achieving relative
405
      gains of 3.2%, 2.4%, and 4.7% on SIDER, MUV, and BACE, respectively, compared to the best
406
      baseline. The promising performance of FineMolTex indicates that it implicitly utilizes pre-trained
407   knowledge from the text modality even when the input consists solely of graphs. Additionally,
408   KV-PLM exhibits a notable performance gap from other models, due to its use of 1D SMILES strings
409   for molecular structure and a smaller pre-training dataset.

494   5   C ONCLUSIONS
495
496   In this paper, we reveal that fine-grained motif-level knowledge is crucial for molecular representation
497   learning. We propose FineMolTex to jointly learn both coarse- and fine-grained knowledge through
498   a contrastive alignment task and a masked multimodal learning task, respectively. By masking the
499
      fine-grained tokens and predicting their labels using tokens from the other modality, we can effectively
      learn fine-grained alignment between motifs and words. Experimental results on three downstream
500
      tasks and two case studies demonstrate the effectiveness of FineMolTex.
