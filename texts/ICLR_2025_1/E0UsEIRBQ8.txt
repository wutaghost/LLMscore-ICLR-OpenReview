011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Semi-supervised underwater object detection aims to improve the performance
014            of detectors on unlabeled underwater images by leveraging knowledge from la-
015
               beled ones. However, existing methods often overlook the distribution differences
               between labeled and unlabeled underwater images. In this paper, we propose
016
               a novel underwater image enhancement method guided by attribute-based data
017
               distribution (UIEG+), which focuses on reducing the discrepancies between en-
018            hanced and original unlabeled images across different attributes, thereby effec-
019            tively addressing the challenges in semi-supervised underwater object detection.
020            Specifically, we explore an underwater image enhancement strategy based on two
021            attributes: color and scale distributions. For the color attribute, we construct
022            a 3-dimensional grid memory, where each grid cell represents a color subspace
023            and records the number of samples in that subspace. Similarly, for the scale at-
024            tribute, we design a 1-dimensional vector memory that dynamically stores the
025            number of samples in each scale subspace. Subsequently, we propose an effective
026
               sampling method to derive parameters for color and scale transformations based
               on the aforementioned distribution analysis, increasing the likelihood of trans-
027
               formations in low-distribution regions. To evaluate its effetiveness and superior-
028
               ity, massive semi-superivised underwater object deteciton experiments in multiple
029            datasets have been conduted by integrating UIEG+ into existing semi-supervised
030            object detection frameworks. The code will be released.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   With the development of deep learning, object detection models have also experienced unprece-
036   dented growth, and many outstanding detection models have been proposed (Ren et al., 2017; Tian
037   et al., 2019; Carion et al., 2020). However, when dealing with underwater scenes and objects, these
038   detectors still face challenges related to complexity and diversity. To address these issues, a simple
039
      solution is to gather a more extensive dataset that encompasses a wide range of scenes and object
      styles. Nevertheless, this is an extremely challenging task that requires significant resources and
040
      makes it difficult to cover all possible scenarios.
041
042   In recent years, semi-supervised object detection based on image enhancement has also emerged
043   as a pivotal solution, which primarily emphasizes the generation of high-quality enhanced images
044   accompanied by labels or pseudo-labels. Existing image enhancement methodologies for semi-
045
      supervised underwater object detection (SSUOD) can be broadly classified into two categories. The
      former encompasses traditional image enhancement techniques (Zhang et al., 2022; Peng et al.,
046
      2018; Liu et al., 2023b), which employ physics-based operations to manipulate various attributes
047
      such as color, scale, and contrast, often through random transformations, as illustrated in Fig. 1
048   (a). Despite their widespread application in semi-supervised object detection frameworks, such as
049   the teacher-student model with weak and strong augmentations (Zhang et al., 2023b), these meth-
050   ods exhibit certain limitations: firstly, they fail to account for the distribution of unlabeled images
051   across different attribute spaces, potentially resulting in the generation of unrealistic enhanced im-
052   ages that could detrimentally affect model training; secondly, they overlook the adverse effects of
053   the imbalanced distribution of unlabeled images within these attribute spaces. The latter involves
      the construction of learnable networks (Jiang et al., 2022; Cong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) to

      Contribution Different from the aforementioned methodologies, this paper considers the actual
077   distribution of unlabeled underwater images, which aims to mitigate the distributional discrepancies
078   between enhanced and unlabeled underwater images. To be specific, we address the distribution
079   of unlabeled underwater images in terms of color and scale attributes. For the color attribute, we
080   develop a three-dimensional color memory, wherein each cell represents a three-dimensional color
081   subspace within a defined range and records the number of unlabeled images in that subspace. Anal-
082   ogously, for the scale attribute, we construct a one-dimensional memory that records the number of
083   unlabeled images in each cell. The color and scale subspaces of the images are determined using
084   the mean values of each color channel and the average scale of all objects within the image. Sub-
085
      sequently, based on these distributional insights, we propose a sampling strategy to derive color

086

087

107   existing detection models are generally classified into two categories, ı.e one- and two-stage de-
      tectors. One-stage detectors (e.g.FCOS (Tian et al., 2019), Yolo (Redmon & Farhadi, 2018), and

      DETR (Carion et al., 2020)) directly predict the classes and rectangular boxes of objects on fea-
109   ture maps without medium proposals. Different from one-stage detectors, two-stage detectors (Ren
110   et al., 2017; Cai & Vasconcelos, 2018) design the region proposal network (RPN) to obtain more
111   accurate proposals and further conduct the classification and regression of these proposals. In this
112   work, we explain the principles of our method based on the PseCo framework (Li et al., 2022) with

116   2.2   S EMI -S UPERVISED D ETECTION
117
118   Semi-supervised detection seeks to improve the performance of detectors on unlabeled images by
119   exploring the training strategies on labeled and unlabeled images. One branch (Zhou et al., 2022;
120   Liu et al., 2023a; Shehzadi et al., 2024) performs self-training on labeled and unlabeled images. The
121
      core idea is to acquire high-quality pseudo-labels or samples. Some works (Wang et al., 2021; Li
      et al., 2023) pay attention to uncertain regions or class imbalance problems. Despite their effective-
122
      ness, these methods are limited by the noise in predicted pseudo-labels or samples, which primarily
123
      arises from differences between labeled and unlabeled images and the selection of hyperparameters.
124   Moreover, another popular trend (Jeong et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a) is to employ
125   consistency analysis of internal features or external predictions from the detection network to learn
126   more robust feature representations or outputs. However, achieving more effective consistency guid-
127   ance is challenging, particularly when it comes to the feature representation of foreground objects
128   affected by various disturbances. In this work, we utilize our UIEG+ method instead of the strong
129   augmentation found in previous teacher-student frameworks (Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Liu
130   et al., 2023a), resulting in enhanced images that match the data distribution of the unlabeled images.
131
132   2.3   S EMI -S UPERVISED U NDERWATER D ETECTION
133
134   Semi-supervised underwater detection aims at improving the adaptation of detectors to various un-
135
      derwater scenes by leveraging both labeled and unlabeled images in the training process. Exist-
      ing works (Sharma et al., 2024; Noman et al., 2021) mainly address this issue by using current
136
      semi-supervised detection strategies. Different from these works, we propose an underwater image
137
      enhancement guided by attribute-based data distribution to reduce the distribution discrepancies be-
138   tween enhanced images and the original unlabeled images in different attributes, effectively handling
139   semi-supervised underwater object detection.
140
141
      2.4   I MAGE E HANCEMENT
142
143   Image enhancement is an effective method to handle semi-supervised underwater detection. The
144   previous works can be grouped into traditional underwater image enhancement (Zhang et al., 2022;
145   Peng et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2023b) and learning-based underwater image enhancement (Jiang et al.,
146   2022; Cong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Traditional underwater image enhancement relies on
147   physics-based operations that are manually defined for different attributes, i.e.color (Ancuti et al.,
148   2012; Wang et al., 2018), contrast (Zhang et al., 2022), and scale. These works use random sampling
149
      to enhance images but do not account for the distribution of unlabeled images across different at-
      tributes in semi-supervised underwater detection. As a result, some unreasonable enhanced images
150
      can negatively impact the model’s training. In contrast, learning-based underwater image enhance-
151
      ment achieves the style transfer between different domain images by constructing the learnable
152   network. While this approach can reduce style differences between enhanced and unlabeled images,
153   it fails to address issues related to imbalanced style distribution in the unlabeled underwater images
154   and differences in the distribution of other attributes (e.g., scale).
155

347   Our framework is implemented based on the PseCo framework (Li et al., 2022) in PyTorch (Paszke
348   et al., 2019). Similarly, we use Faster-RCNN (Ren et al., 2017) with ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016)

350   trained with a learning rate of 1e-3, momentum of 0.9, and weight decay of 1e-4. γlie and γuie are
351   set to 1.0 and 0.1 in Eq. 1, respectively. drgb and dscale are set to 0.05 and 64, respectively. ηrgb
352
      is 0.3 in Eq. 11. To evaluate the effectiveness and generalizability of our UIEG+, we integrate it
      into two additional SSOD frameworks, i.e.ARSL (Liu et al., 2023a) and DenseTeacher (Zhou et al.,
353
      2022), utilizing both FCOS (Tian et al., 2019) as the base detectors. Specifically, in both ARSL
354
      and DenseTeacher, we substitute only the strong augmentation with our UIEG+, keeping all other
355   components unchanged.

           536   data distribution (UIEG+) from a novel perspective, which reduces the differences in color and
