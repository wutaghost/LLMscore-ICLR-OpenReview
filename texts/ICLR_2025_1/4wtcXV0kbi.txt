                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            A central challenge in sequence modeling is efficiently handling tasks with ex-
012            tended contexts. While recent state-space models (SSMs) have made significant
013            progress in this area, they often lack input-dependent filtering or require substan-
014            tial increases in model complexity to handle input variability. We address this gap
015            by introducing S7, a simplified yet powerful SSM that can handle input depen-
016            dence while incorporating stable reparameterization and specific design choices
017            to dynamically adjust state transitions based on input content, maintaining effi-
018
               ciency and performance. We prove that this reparameterization ensures stability
               in long-sequence modeling by keeping state transitions well-behaved over time.
019
               Additionally, it controls the gradient norm, enabling efficient training and pre-
020
               venting issues like exploding or vanishing gradients. S7 significantly outperforms
021            baselines across various sequence modeling tasks, including neuromorphic event-
022            based datasets, Long Range Arena benchmarks, and various physical and biolog-
023            ical time series. Overall, S7 offers a more straightforward approach to sequence
024            modeling without relying on complex, domain-specific inductive biases, achiev-
025            ing significant improvements across key benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
      Sequence modeling is a fundamental challenge in deep learning, with applications spanning natu-
030
      ral language processing, computer vision, audio processing, and genomics (Sutskever et al., 2014;
031   Graves et al., 2013). The core problem lies in effectively capturing and utilizing information from
032   long input sequences while maintaining computational efficiency. Traditional approaches, such as
033   recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), struggle with long-range
034   dependencies due to vanishing gradients (Bengio et al., 1994), while attention-based models like
035   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) face quadratic complexity in sequence length, limiting their
036   scalability. While efficient, convolutional models (Bai et al., 2018) cannot often capture global con-
037   text. The key challenge is to design a model that can (1) efficiently process very long sequences, (2)
038   adaptively filter and retain relevant information over extended time horizons, (3) perform content-
039
      based reasoning, and (4) maintain a compact state representation. Recent advances in Deep State
      Space Models (Deep SSMs) (Gu et al., 2020; Hasani et al., 2020) have shown promise, but existing
040
      approaches like S4 (Gu et al., 2022a) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) still face limitations in balanc-
041
      ing these requirements. S4 models, while efficient, lack input-dependent filtering capabilities, and
042   Mamba, though more flexible, introduces significant complexity. There is a clear need for a model
043   that combines the efficiency of recurrent architectures with the adaptive, content-aware processing
044   capabilities of more complex models without sacrificing simplicity or generalizability across diverse
045   sequence modeling tasks (Tay et al., 2022; Schlag et al., 2021).
046
      The importance of effective sequence modeling cannot be overstated in today’s AI landscape. It
047
      forms the backbone of large language models (Brown et al., 2020), which have revolutionized nat-
048   ural language processing and are increasingly applied across diverse domains. In computer vision,
049   sequence modeling enables video data processing and event-based vision (Zubić et al., 2024a; 2023),
050   critical for applications like autonomous driving and robotics. Genomics allows for analyzing long
051   DNA sequences, potentially unlocking breakthroughs in personalized medicine and drug discovery
052   (Avsec et al., 2021). However, the problem is inherently challenging due to several factors. First, the
053   sheer length of sequences in real-world applications (often millions of tokens) makes it computation-
      ally intensive to process and retain relevant information (Tay et al., 2021a). Second, the relevance

      of information can vary dramatically across the sequence, requiring adaptive filtering mechanisms
055   (Katharopoulos et al., 2020). Third, capturing long-range dependencies and performing content-
056   based reasoning demands sophisticated architectures to maintain and update a meaningful state over
057   time (Dai et al., 2019). Finally, there’s a fundamental tension between model expressivity and com-
058   putational efficiency – more powerful models often come at the cost of increased complexity and
059   resource requirements (Tay et al., 2021b). Balancing these competing demands while maintaining
060   generalizability across diverse tasks remains an open challenge in the field (Schlag et al., 2021),
061   driving the need for innovative approaches to push the boundaries of what’s possible in sequence
062   modeling.
063   Recent advancements in sequence modeling have made significant strides. Transformer architec-
064   tures (Vaswani et al., 2017) revolutionized the field with their attention mechanisms, enabling par-
065   allel processing and capturing long-range dependencies. However, their quadratic complexity in se-
066   quence length remains a limitation. Efficient transformer variants (Kitaev et al., 2020; Beltagy et al.,
067   2020) attempted to address this, but often at the cost of reduced model capacity. The emergence
068   of Deep State Space Models (SSMs) marked a new frontier, with S4 (Gu et al., 2022a) demon-
069
      strating impressive performance on long-range tasks while maintaining linear complexity. Mamba
      (Gu & Dao, 2023) further improved upon this by introducing input-dependent dynamics, enhancing
070
      the model’s ability to perform content-based filtering. Despite these advances, the field has yet to
071
      achieve an optimal balance between efficiency, adaptability, and simplicity. The primary stumbling
072   block lies in reconciling the need for input-dependent processing—crucial for adaptive filtering and
073   content-based reasoning—with the computational efficiency of recurrent architectures. S4 mod-
074   els, while efficient, lack input-dependent dynamics, limiting their ability to adapt to varying content.
075   Conversely, Mamba introduces input dependence at the cost of increased complexity and reliance on
076   specialized hardware implementations. The challenge now is to develop a model that combines the
077   strengths of these approaches—the efficiency and simplicity of recurrent models with the adaptive
078   capabilities of input-dependent systems—without compromising on performance or generalizability
079   across diverse tasks (Schlag et al., 2021; Tay et al., 2022; Zubić et al., 2024b). This balance is critical
080
      for pushing sequence modeling towards more general and scalable AI systems capable of handling
      the complexities of real-world data across various domains.
081
082   Our paper introduces S7, a simplified yet powerful State Space Model (SSM) that advances the fron-
083   tier of sequence modeling by making the purely recurrent, time-domain S5 model input-dependent.
084   This critical insight combines the efficiency of recurrent architectures with the adaptive processing
085   capabilities of more complex models. By dynamically adjusting state transitions based on input con-
086
      tent, S7 performs content-based reasoning and adaptive filtering while preserving recurrent models’
      simplicity and computational efficiency. Unlike S4, which lacks input-dependent dynamics, or S6
087
      (Mamba), which introduces hardware-specific complexity, S7 achieves a balanced design. We in-
088
      troduce stable reparameterization and additional design choices that ensure long-term stability and
089   performance across diverse tasks.

112   2     R ELATED WORK
113
114   Sequence modeling has evolved from traditional RNNs (Elman, 1990), including LSTMs (Hochre-
115   iter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and GRUs (Cho et al., 2014), which struggle with long-range depen-
116   dencies (Bengio et al., 1994), to CNNs adapted for sequential data (Bai et al., 2018; van den Oord
117   et al., 2016), and then to attention-based models like Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). While
118   Transformers excel at capturing long-range dependencies, their quadratic complexity led to the de-
119
      velopment of efficient variants using linear (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020) or sparse
      attention (Child et al., 2019; Beltagy et al., 2020). SSMs emerged as a promising approach, with
120
      S4 (Gu et al., 2022a) achieving state-of-the-art performance on long-range tasks while maintaining
121
      linear complexity. Subsequent work refined SSMs, leading to S4D (Gu et al., 2022b) and S5 (Smith
122   et al., 2023). The limitation of fixed dynamics in traditional SSMs motivated input-dependent mod-
123   els, notably the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023) with its selective state spaces and its recent
124   extension, Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024), which further improves performance and efficiency. These
125   advancements have impacted various domains, including event-based vision processing (Zubić et al.,
126   2024a) and have been evaluated on long-range sequence modeling benchmarks (Tay et al., 2021a).
127   Theoretical work has explored connections to control theory (Gu et al., 2021), approximation ca-
128   pabilities (Gu et al., 2020), and complexity analysis (Dao et al., 2022). Our work, S7, builds upon
129   these foundations, particularly SSMs and input-dependent models, aiming to combine the efficiency
130
      of recurrent architectures with adaptive capabilities to address limitations in existing approaches.
      Specifically, S7 applies to S5 the same principle of input-dependence that Mamba introduced to S4,
131
      but within the context of a purely recurrent, time-domain model.

136   3.1   BACKGROUND
137
138   State Space Models (SSMs) SSMs are a class of models widely used in control theory, neuro-
139   science, and machine learning for modeling sequential data. The core of SSMs lies in their rep-
140   resentation of a system’s evolution over time through a latent state. Mathematically, SSMs are
141   typically represented as:
142
                              ẋ(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t)         y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)                         (1)
143
144   where x(t) ∈ RH is the latent state vector, u(t) ∈ RN is the input signal, and y(t) ∈ RN is
145   the output. The system is governed by the matrices A ∈ RH×H , B ∈ RH×N , C ∈ RN ×H ,
146   and D ∈ RN ×N , which are the parameters to be learned. SSMs capture long-range dependencies
147   in sequential data by evolving the latent state over time in a continuous manner (Gu et al., 2020;
148   Smith et al., 2023). In deep learning, SSMs can be stacked in multiple layers, allowing them to
149
      process complex sequential data more effectively. By stacking SSM layers, these models can capture
      intricate temporal patterns while maintaining a compact state representation, efficiently handling
150
      long sequences (Gu et al., 2022a).
151
152
153
      Discretization of Continuous SSMs In practice, continuous SSMs must be discretized to apply
      them in computational models, particularly for deep learning tasks. The discretization process con-
154
      verts continuous-time dynamics into a form that can be computed at discrete time steps, typically
155
      using methods such as the zero-order hold (ZOH). The discrete equivalent of the continuous system
156   is given by:
157
                                 xk = Λ̄xk−1 + B̄uk        yk = C̄xk + D̄uk                           (2)
158
159   where Λ̄ = eA∆t and ∆t is the time step size (Smith et al., 2023). This formulation allows the
160   model to process input sequences at discrete intervals, making it suitable for training on modern
161   hardware. Efficient discretization techniques are essential to ensure that SSMs retain their ability to
      model long-range dependencies without becoming computationally expensive (Gu et al., 2022a).

      3.2   I NPUT D EPENDENCY IN S TATE -S PACE M ODELS
163
164   To improve the performance of SSMs, input dependence can be introduced by making the transition
165   matrices a function of the input. In S7, the system evolution at time step k can be described by the
166   following discretized equations:
167                              xk = Λ̄k xk−1 + B̄k uk          yk = C̄k xk + D̄k uk                       (3)
168
      Here, the transition matrix Λ̄k , along with the input matrices B̄k , C̄k , and D̄k , are functions of the
169
      input uk , allowing the model to adapt to the current input at each time step dynamically. This en-
170
      ables the model to filter information, selectively determining what to retain and forget. Doing so
171   enhances the model’s ability to capture essential long-term dependencies while filtering out irrel-
172   evant information, improving performance and generalization. The system output yk is processed
173   through normalization layers, followed by a GeLU activation and a gating mechanism. The gating
174   function, represented by a sigmoid activation σ(W · GeLU(yk )), helps regulate how much of the
175   processed information passes through, enabling the model to control the flow of information based
176   on the input signal and current state.
177   This dynamic gating allows the model to adjust the information flow based on the input signal and
178   the current state, providing a more robust and flexible state evolution. Introducing input-dependent
179   dynamics improves S7’s ability to handle diverse temporal dependencies, effectively filtering and
180   retaining relevant information over time. By making the state transition matrices depend on the
181   input uk , S7 improves on the limitations of static state transitions found in previous models, such as
182   S4 and S5 (Gu et al., 2022a; Smith et al., 2023), which lacked the flexibility to adapt state transitions
183   based on the input. This selective updating of internal states allows S7 to balance long-term and
184   short-term dependencies, leading to better performance and more effective memory management in
185
      sequence modeling tasks.

      Efficient Tokenization for Event-Based Data In S7, we introduce an event-based tokenization
      scheme that captures the neuromorphic data’s spatial and temporal nature. This method utilizes a
302
      sensor of size (sx , sy ), where sx is the number of horizontal pixels and sy is the number of vertical
303
      pixels. Each event, ε, is defined by the following quadruple: (x, y, t, p), where x and y represent
304   the spatial coordinates of the event on the sensor, t represents the timestamp of the event, and
305   p ∈ {−1, 1} is the polarity, indicating the nature of the event (positive or negative change). We then
306   define a unique token for each event ε using the following formula:
307
308
                                          TS7 (ε) = 2 · (x · sx + y) + p                                (10)
309   In this formula, TS7 (ε) denotes the token generated for the event ε using the S7-specific tokenization
310   scheme, as indicated by the subscript. This bijective mapping ensures each event produces a unique
311   token, preventing collisions where different events could share the same token, as seen in models
312   like EventSSM (Schöne et al., 2024). By encoding spatial and polarity information, the S7 scheme
313   enhances the model’s ability to efficiently process asynchronous, real-time data.

520   5     C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this work, we introduced S7, a novel state-space model that effectively balances efficiency, adapt-
523   ability, and stability in long-sequence modeling tasks—building upon the foundation of prior mod-
524   els like S4 (Gu et al., 2022a), S5 (Smith et al., 2023) and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). S7 leverages
525
      input-dependent dynamics and stable reparameterization to improve its ability to capture long-range
      dependencies while maintaining computational efficiency. The key contribution of S7 is its abil-
526
      ity to dynamically adjust state transitions based on input content, allowing for selective filtering
527
      and content-based reasoning without adding unnecessary complexity. Through extensive experi-
528   mentation on a diverse range of benchmarks, including event-based neuromorphic tasks, long-range
529   sequence modeling, dynamical system prediction, and real-world applications like human activity
530   recognition and genomics classification, S7 has demonstrated its superiority. It achieves state-of-
531   the-art results in multiple domains while preserving computational efficiency, even in challenging
532   settings that require processing sequences with irregular sampling and long-term dependencies.
533
      Moreover, incorporating stable reparameterization ensures the robustness and stability of S7 during
534   training and inference, making it highly scalable for real-world applications. The model’s ability
535   to handle asynchronous data streams and complex temporal patterns extends its utility to a wide
536   range of practical tasks, from neuromorphic vision to genomic analysis. In conclusion, S7 offers a
537   significant advancement in sequence modeling, pushing the boundaries of what is possible in terms
538   of scalability, generalization, and adaptability. By achieving an optimal balance between simplicity
539   and performance, S7 sets a new standard for state-space models, opening new avenues for research
      and application across numerous domains in artificial intelligence.
