009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we propose an efficient transformer architecture that uses reinforced
012            positional embedding to obtain superior performance with half the number of
013            encoder decoder layers. We demonstrate that concatenating positional encoding
014            with trainable token embeddings, normalizing across tokens in the token embed-
015            ding matrix, and using the normalized token embedding matrix as the value of
016
               the attention layer improve the training and validation loss and the training time
               in an encoder-decoder Transformer model for a Portuguese-English translation
017
               task with 10 epochs or 12 hours of training across 10 trials. Our method, with
018
               roughly a threefold parameter reduction compared to the baseline model, yields a
019            mean training loss of 1.21, a mean validation loss of 1.51, and an average train-
020            ing time of 1352.27 seconds per epoch, surpassing the baseline model with the
021            same embedding dimension that employs addition of positional encoding and to-
022            ken embeddings, which achieves a mean training loss of 1.96, a validation loss of
023            2.18, and an average training time of 4297.79 seconds per epoch. Additionally,
024            we evaluated our proposed architecture and the baseline across 14 diverse transla-
025            tion datasets from TensorFlow. The results indicate that our method consistently
026            achieves lower or comparable training and validation losses, suggesting enhanced
027
               learning efficiency.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In this study, we propose modifications to the encoder-decoder Transformer architecture from
032   Vaswani (2017) to enhance training performance on machine translation tasks using the dataset from
033   Ye et al. (2018). Previous work by Ke et al. Ke et al. (2021) demonstrated that employing separate
034   query and key projection matrices for token embeddings and absolute positional embeddings in the
035   self-attention layer accelerates convergence with respect to validation loss in BERT models Lee &
036   Toutanova (2018). They also indicated that the standard attention mechanism in Transformers might
037   be inefficient due to weak correlations between words and their absolute positions.
038   We present three modifications to enhance the efficiency and performance of the encoder-decoder
039   Transformer architecture. Firstly, we reason that the addition of the token and positional embedding
040   matrix may cause loss of information. To address this, we concatenate the token and positional
041   embedding matrices before the initial encoder and decoder blocks, as shown in Figure 1 (b). Second,
042   we normalize the token embedding matrix across tokens, as shown in Figure 1 (c). Third, we use
043   the normalized token embedding matrix as the value in the attention layer, as shown in Figure 1 (d).
044   Specifically, let m denote the token embedding dimension and n the sequence length. The input
045   to the first encoder or decoder block consists of the concatenated token and positional embedding
046   matrices, resulting in a matrix of dimension n × 2m. Each column of the token embedding matrix is
047   normalized to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. In the attention layer, the key and
048   query matrices are of dimension n × 2m, while the value matrix is the normalized token embedding
049   matrix, yielding an n × m dimension. The output of each attention layer is an n × 2m matrix, which
050
      allows for residual connections that maintain positional information across layers. The feedforward
      layer processes an input matrix of dimension n × 2m and outputs a matrix of the same dimension.
051
      The modified architecture is shown in Figure 2.
052

      but with double the number of layers and the same embedding dimension. Namely, in the baseline,

282   3.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
283
284   We first evaluated our proposed model and the baseline model using the Portuguese-English trans-
285   lation dataset from TensorFlow Abadi et al. (2015) for 10 epochs or 12 hours of training across 10
286   trials. The dataset contains 51,785 training, 1,193 validation, and 1,803 test pairs of Portuguese and
287
      English text. The encoder’s input is the Portuguese text, and the decoder’s input is the English text,
      with both sequences capped at 128 tokens. The target labels are the decoder inputs shifted right by
288
      one token. We applied wordpiece tokenization for the Portuguese-English translation dataset from
289
      TensorFlow Abadi et al. (2015). For the second experiment, we evaluated our proposed model and
290   the baseline model using the Portuguese-English translation dataset from TensorFlow Abadi et al.
291   (2015) for 10 epochs of training or 12 hours in one trial. We applied wordpiece tokenization for
292   each dataset from TensorFlow Abadi et al. (2015).
293
      The model was trained using the Adam optimizer from Vaswani (2017):
294
295                                lr(Ns ) = m−0.5 min(Ns−0.5 , Ns Nw−1.5 ),                           (25)
296
297   where m is the embedding dimension, Ns ∈ Z is the current training step, and Nw = 4000 is called
298   the warm-up step. The loss function is a masked cross-entropy loss. The training epoch is 10. The
299
      drop-out rate is 0.1. The batch size is 64.
300   For our proposed model (Section 2.2), we used 2 encoder and decoder blocks, the token embedding
301   dimension of m = 64, 256 hidden neurons in the feed-forward network, 4 attention heads, and
302   the projection dimension of 64 for query, key, and value. The proposed model contains 2,809,634
303   parameters.
304   For the baseline model, the number of encoder and decoder blocks is 4; the token embedding di-
305   mension is m = 128; the number of hidden neurons in the feed-forward network is 512; the number
306   of attention heads is p = 8; the projection dimension is 128 for query, key, and value. The baseline
307   model contains 10,184,162 parameters, which is roughly three times more than the proposed model.
308
309   3.2   E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
310
311   We trained both the baseline and proposed models for 10 epochs or 12 hours across 10 different
312   trials on two A100 40GB GPUs on the Portuguese to English translation dataset from Ye et al.
313   (2018). For the baseline model, the 10 epochs of training were completed for 4 trails; 9 epochs
314   of training is completed for 4 trails; and 8 epochs of training were completed for 2 trails. For the
315
      proposed model, 10 epochs of training were completed for all the trials. The comparison of the
      learning curves between the baseline and the proposed method are shown in Figure 3 (a). The mean
316
      training losses of the baseline are roughly 6.60, 4.55, 3.82, 3.29, 2.89, 2.57, 2.30, 2.11, 1.96, and
317
      1.84. The mean training losses of the model from the proposed method for each epoch are roughly
318   6.68, 4.55, 3.62, 2.89, 2.36, 1.97, 1.67, 1.46, 1.32, and 1.21, which are less than the mean training
319   losses of the baseline after 3 epochs. The mean validation losses of the baseline are roughly 5.04,
320   4.06, 3.46, 3.01, 2.77, 2.49, 2.35, 2.25, 2.18, and 2.14. The mean validation losses of the model
321   from the proposed method for each epoch are roughly 5.08, 3.93, 3.10, 2.51, 2.17, 1.88, 1.72, 1.63,
322   1.56, and 1.51, which are less than the mean validation losses of the baseline after 2 epochs. The
323   comparison from the learning curves shows that the improvement of the training loss and validation
      loss of our model compared to the baseline is consistent for 10 different trials.

418   mean and unit variance, concatenates token embeddings with positional embeddings before the first
419   encoder and decoder blocks, and uses the normalized token embedding matrix as the value in the
420   attention layer, demonstrates improvements in training loss, validation loss, and computational time
421   across thirteen translation datasets from Ye et al. (2018) compared to the baseline model with the
422   same embedding dimension but with double the number of encoder and decoder blocks. The pro-

426   4297.79 seconds per epoch for 10 epochs or 12 hours of training across 10 trials on the Portuguese
427   to English translation dataset from Ye et al. (2018). The proposed model shows a lower training
428   and validation loss compared to the baseline on thirteen out of fourteen different translation datasets
429   from Ye et al. (2018) and shows around two to four times reduction on the training time on all the
430   fourteen datasets from Ye et al. (2018). The proposed model shows promising improvements on
