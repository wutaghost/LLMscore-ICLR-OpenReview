011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The behaviour of natural and artificial agents is shaped by underlying reward sys-
014            tems, which signal rewards based on internal and external factors, driving reward-
015
               oriented actions. However, real-world scenarios often impose constraints that
               reward alone cannot capture. While existing inverse (constrained) reinforcement
016
               learning methods can recover either rewards or constraints from demonstrations,
017
               the simultaneous inference of both remains unexplored due to the complexity of
018            inference and the lack of knowledge of their relationship. To address this gap, we
019            propose a novel algorithm that simultaneously infers both rewards and constraints
020            within an adversarial learning framework, where both are updated through a policy
021            optimisation process guided by expert demonstrations. Crucial to this framework
022            is the introduction of the “reward-feasibility contrast prior,” a hypothesis that
023            correlates rewards and constraints. It is inspired by patterns observed in animal
024            behaviour (particularly meerkats), positing that states with high rewards nearby
025            are more likely to be associated with weaker feasibility (stronger constraints). Our
026
               experiments on virtual robot control tasks with safety constraints and real-world an-
               imal behaviour data with spatio-temporal causal constraints validate our proposed
027
               framework’s effectiveness and the reward-feasibility contrast prior hypothesis. The
028
               results show accurate recovery of rewards and constraints, reflected by strong
029            alignment with expert demonstrations and a low rate of constraint violations. Addi-
030            tionally, the performance improvement by embedding this prior into other inverse
031            constraint inference methods further confirms its general effectiveness.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Understanding the motivations behind the behaviour of agents, particularly natural beings, and
037   designing intelligent agents that behave as desired are key desiderata in artificial intelligence, robotics,
038   neuroscience (Maia & Frank, 2011) and ethology (Mori et al., 2022). Inverse reinforcement learning
039
      (IRL) (Ng & Russell, 2000) offers a framework for achieving this by recovering the underlying
      reward function – often considered the most succinct representation of the objective of the behaviour
040
      (Abbeel & Ng, 2004; Fu et al., 2018) – from demonstrations, making it a powerful tool for interpreting
041
      behaviour and generating synthetic actions in real-world scenarios, such as robot control (Bogert &
042   Doshi, 2014; Kretzschmar et al., 2016) and animal behaviour modelling (Inga et al., 2017; Ashwood
043   et al., 2022). However, real-world behaviour is often constrained by factors beyond rewards, such
044   as avoiding hazards in robot navigation (Bogert & Doshi, 2018) or environmental and cognitive
045   limitations in humans and animals (Niv, 2009). These constraints cannot be fully captured by rewards
046   alone and must be considered alongside rewards to fully capture behaviour dynamics.
047
      Inverse constrained reinforcement learning (ICRL) (Scobee & Sastry, 2019; Malik et al., 2021)
048   offers a promising approach for inferring constraints from demonstrations. However, most existing
049   ICRL methods rely on the assumption of known reward functions (Malik et al., 2021; Liu & Zhu,
050   2022), whereas real-world scenarios, such as animal behaviour modelling, often lack such reward
051   signals. While few approaches assume unknown rewards (Xu & Liu, 2024; Lindner et al., 2024), they
052   impose linear assumptions on the environment dynamics and reward functions, reducing the problem
053   to a linear programming-like formulation and involving convex optimisation, which significantly
      limits their expressive power and applicability in real-world settings. As IRL and ICRL remain

069
070   Unifying IRL and ICRL presents two primary challenges. First, an efficient framework is needed
071   to infer both complex rewards and constraints simultaneously. While IRL and ICRL have practical
072   implementations individually (Fu et al., 2018; Malik et al., 2021), their mechanisms are not inherently
073
      designed to handle unknown constraints or rewards, preventing their combined application. Second,
      without prior knowledge of the correlations between constraints and rewards, there is a significant risk
074
      of deriving counterfactual solutions that diverge from reality. For example, in behaviour modelling,
075
      multiple reward-constraint combinations might explain the observed demonstrations, but not all align
076   with the true underlying dynamics.
077
078   To address the first challenge, we extend the adversarial IRL framework (Fu et al., 2018) to infer both
079
      rewards and unknown constraints simultaneously. The adversary updates these components based on
      agent-generated samples, while the agent’s policy is optimised using these updates, creating a more
080
      integrated inference process. To tackle the second challenge, we investigate the correlations between
081
      rewards and constraints in natural settings, focusing on meerkats due to their complex social structures
082   (Drewe et al., 2009; Madden et al., 2009; 2011). We extract spatiotemporal transitions from footage
083   data of a meerkat mob (Rogers et al., 2023), and our analysis reveals that long-distance movements,
084   despite being less feasible, are likely driven by high rewards. This observation forms the basis of
085   our hypothesis, the “reward-feasibility contrast prior.” This prior, independent of our proposed
086   framework, reflects the common understanding that higher rewards are often tied to greater risks
087   (Lopes, 1987). We formalise this prior as a regularisation term and integrate it into our framework.
088   Our primary contribution is a novel algorithm that unifies IRL and ICRL, which we name Adversarial
089   Inverse Reward-Constraint Learning (AIRCL) as illustrated in Figure 1. Unlike prior methods in
090   IRL and ICRL, AIRCL allows for the efficient and simultaneous inference of complex unknown
091   rewards and constraints (Section 5), while capturing their correlations through the reward-feasibility
092   contrast prior (Section 6). We evaluate AIRCL on simulated benchmark robot-control tasks with
093   safety constraints and real-world animal behaviour modelling tasks with spatio-temporal causal
094   constraints (Section 7). Our method effectively recovers reward functions and reveals underlying
095   constraints, evidenced by reproducing behaviours that closely match the demonstrated behaviours
096
      while adhering to the inferred constraints. Experimental results confirm its effectiveness in modelling
      natural and robot behaviours and its broad effectiveness. In addition, the performance improvement
097
      of ICRL by embedding the proposed prior further confirms its versatility and general effectiveness.

100   2    R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Our work is grounded in inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) (Ng et al., 1999), which originates
103   from inverse optimal control (Kalman, 1964), where the goal is to identify the function optimised
104   by a given system governed by some known control law. In machine learning, IRL was formally
105   introduced by Ng & Russell (2000) and applied to imitation learning (Ho & Ermon, 2016), also
106   known as apprenticeship learning (Abbeel & Ng, 2004), to train agents that perform as well as human
107   experts. Compared to traditional imitation learning methods like behaviour cloning (Bain & Sammut,
      1995) and Dataset Aggregation (DAgger) (Ross et al., 2011), which directly mimic behaviour, IRL


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      offers better explainability and robustness, as the recovered reward function not only interprets agents’
109   motivations but also remains invariant to changes in environmental dynamics (Fu et al., 2018). Early
110   IRL approaches relied on margin optimisation and assumed linear reward functions (Syed & Schapire,
111   2007; Syed et al., 2008; Ratliff et al., 2006), though these were ill-defined due to the potential for
112   multiple reward functions explaining the same demonstrations. Maximum (causal) entropy (MaxEnt)
113   IRL (Ziebart et al., 2008; 2010) addressed this issue by selecting the reward function that maximises
114   the entropy of the distribution of demonstrations. Variants of MaxEnt IRL have been developed
115   for scenarios such as non-optimal demonstrations (Boularias et al., 2011), multi-objective settings
116   (Babes et al., 2011), and specific Markov decision processes (Dvijotham & Todorov, 2010), as well
117
      as Bayesian (Ramachandran & Amir, 2007) and Gaussian process-based methods (Jin et al., 2015).
      However, MaxEnt IRL is computationally challenging for large or continuous state spaces due to its
118
      dependence on forward RL subroutines. Deep learning advancements, such as guided cost learning
119
      (Finn et al., 2016b) and adversarial IRL (AIRL) (Finn et al., 2016a; Fu et al., 2018), resolved this
120   limitation through sampling-based approximations to MaxEnt IRL, which we also leverage in our
121   approach. Extensions to meta-learning (Yu et al., 2019b), multi-agent (Yu et al., 2019a), and many-
122   agent settings (Chen et al., 2023; 2024) have followed. However, these methods focus exclusively on
123   rewards, overlooking constraints. Our approach builds on this foundation, extending IRL to infer
124   both rewards and constraints, offering enhanced explainability and practical utility.
125
      This paper is closely related to inverse constrained reinforcement learning (ICRL) (Liu et al., 2024),
126   which focuses on recovering unknown constraints from demonstrations. ICRL is the inverse problem
127   of constrained reinforcement learning (CRL) (Tessler et al., 2019), where the goal is to optimise
128   rewards while satisfying constraints related to safety (Garcıa & Fernández, 2015), security (Lei
129   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), fairness (Jabbari et al., 2017), or other considerations (Qin et al.,
130   2021). The ICRL problem was studied by Scobee & Sastry (2019) in discrete settings, with the term
131   ICRL introduced by Malik et al. (2021) for continuous state-action spaces. These and other works
132   (McPherson et al., 2021; Baert et al., 2023) use the MaxEnt IRL framework to identify constrained
133   state-action pairs. In addition to MaxEnt IRL, alternative methods include linear programming
134
      (Lindner et al., 2024), robust optimisation (Xu & Liu, 2024), Bayesian methods (Papadimitriou et al.,
      2022), and generative models (Xu & Liu, 2023), with some ICRL extensions addressing multi-agent
135
      scenarios (Xu & Liu, 2024; Liu & Zhu, 2024). However, many ICRL approaches assume known or
136
      linear reward functions, limiting their practical use in real-world applications. Our work overcomes
137   this limitation by enabling simultaneous inference of both rewards and constraints without these
138   restrictive assumptions.
139
140
      Our research is also connected to the literature on human and animal behaviour modelling using
      reinforcement learning, both in physical (Ashwood et al., 2020; Mori et al., 2022) and psychological
141
      contexts (Niv, 2009; Maia & Frank, 2011; Gershman & Daw, 2017). Unlike previous studies focusing
142
      on directly building reinforcement learning methods to reproduce human or animal-like behaviour,
143   our approach seeks to uncover intuitive clues about the correlation between rewards and constraints
144   from observed meerkat behaviour in their habitat. Specifically, from the phenomenon of relatively
145   frequent long-distance movements, we formalise the reward-feasibility contrast prior, which serves
146   as a critical regularisation component in our proposed framework.

190   3.2   I NVERSE C ONSTRAINED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
191
192   Constrained RL (CRL) operates on a constrained MDP (CMDP) Mc = (S, A, P, r, γ, T, c, α)
193   that extends an MDP M with a cost function c : S × A → R and a budget α ≥ 0 (Altman,
194   2021). The goal is to find a policy that maximises rewards Jr (π) while ensuring the expected costs
                                                     PT −1
195   Jc (π) = Eτ ∼π [c(τ )] ≤ α, where c(τ ) = t=0 c(st , at ). When α = 0, hard constraints apply
196   and all state-action pairs with non-zero (positive) costs are strickly prohibited to visit. The cost
197   function thus reduces to a binary function: c(s, a) ∈ {0, 1}, where 1 indicates any positive costs.
198   Hard constraints simplify optimisation while capturing safety or other broad constraints imposed by
199
      physical laws. For α > 0, soft constraints allow limited violations within the budget α. Inverse CRL
      (ICRL) aims to recover the cost function from expert demonstrations such that the optimal policy
200
      under Mc \ c reproduces the demonstrations, typically assuming a known reward r. ICRL uses the
201
      MaxEnt principle and focuses on hard constraints, introducing a feasibility function δϕ (s, a) ∈ {0, 1}
202   (1 for feasible and 0 for not) and reducing to the following problem:

308   While the adversarial learning framework facilitates the simultaneous and efficient inference of
309
      rewards and constraints, it lacks the ability to incorporate prior knowledge about the correlation
      between the two. This limitation can lead to inferred rewards and constraints deviating from the
310
      ground truth. To address this, we seek insights from animal behaviour, particularly meerkats, due to
311
      their complex social structures (Drewe et al., 2009; Madden et al., 2009; 2011), which offer a natural
312   context for understanding the reward-constraint relationship. By analysing footage of a meerkat mob
313   at a zoo (Rogers et al., 2023), we extract spatio-temporal transitions between different behaviours.
314   The overview of our data analysis is shown in Figure 2, with detailed procedures in Appendix B.
315

316

317

528   8                     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we introduce AIRCL, a method for simultaneously inferring both unknown reward and
531   constraint functions from expert demonstrations. We demonstrate AIRCL’s effectiveness in both
532   simulated robotic tasks with continuous states and actions, as well as real-world animal behaviour
533   modelling, where it outperforms baseline methods in reward-constraint recovery and causal inference.
534   The proposed reward-feasibility contrast prior, inspired by animal behaviour, proves critical, as its
535   removal often leads to significant performance decline.
536   In a broader context, our results highlight the importance of integrating prior knowledge into inverse
537   reward-constraint inference, as it significantly improves constraint recovery, though a prior may not
538   be applicable in every setting. AIRCL’s success across diverse tasks suggests the potential for broader
539   applications, particularly in complex, high-dimensional environments. Future work could explore
      extending AIRCL to more diverse scenarios, enhancing its generalisation and scalability.

      B     M EERKAT DATA P ROCESSING
795
      To obtain the meerkat behaviour, two GoPro Max cameras are set on the back wall of the enclosure,
796
      one focusing on the replica termite mound in the centre of the enclosure and the other overlooking
797
      the foraging area and entrance to the enclosure, which are hubs of activity (Figure 5). For example,
798   the mound is a popular area for guarding behaviour, and the foraging area is popular when meerkats
799   are looking for food. The cameras are set to automatically record videos every 12 minutes, and the
800   contents recorded are filtered, which exclude the fragments that include visitors. Videos with many
801   individuals, social interactions, and other interesting behaviours were selected for the annotation
802   (Figure 6). During the annotation process, the computer vision annotation tool CVAT version 2.3
803   is utilised to sign the behaviour in the videos. Besides, masking techniques are used to protect the
804   privacy of visitors and maintain the vision information of human activities at the same time. The adult
805   and baby meerkat are annotated specifically in the dataset, with annotators using a small bounding
806
      box to note the baby meerkat’s positions relative to the adults. Through multiple checks as well as
      using scripts to automatically detect the error, the accuracy and the consistency of the annotations are
807
      ensured (Rogers et al., 2023).

904   We utilise the open-source library from Gleave et al. (2022), which provides high-quality, reliable,
905   and modular implementations of various reinforcement learning and imitation learning algorithms.
906   Built on Stable Baseline 3 (Raffin et al., 2021), the imitation library offers accurate experimental
907   baselines, allowing us to easily train and compare a range of algorithms. We extend the library

909
      algorithms to support the implementation of a trajectory-based discriminator as our design.
