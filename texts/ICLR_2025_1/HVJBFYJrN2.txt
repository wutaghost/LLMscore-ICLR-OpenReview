                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Self-supervised pretraining methods with masked prediction demonstrate remark-
013
               able within-dataset performance in skeleton-based action recognition. However,
               we show that, unlike contrastive learning approaches, they do not produce well-
014
               separated clusters. Additionally, these methods struggle with generalization in
015
               few-shot settings. To address these issues, we propose Self-supervised Tuning
016            for 3D Action Recognition in Skeleton sequences (STARS). Specifically, STARS
017            first uses a masked prediction stage using an encoder-decoder architecture. It
018            then employs nearest-neighbor contrastive learning to partially tune the weights
019            of the encoder, enhancing the formation of semantic clusters for different ac-
020            tions. By tuning the encoder for a few epochs, and without using hand-crafted data
021            augmentations, STARS achieves state-of-the-art self-supervised results in various
022            benchmarks, including NTU-60, NTU-120, and PKU-MMD. In addition, STARS
023            exhibits significantly better results than masked prediction models in few-shot
024
               settings, where the model has not seen the actions throughout pretraining. Our
               code and trained weights are available at: https://anonymous.4open.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Human action recognition is receiving growing attention in computer vision due to its wide applica-
032
      tions in the real world, such as security, human-machine interaction, medical assistance, and virtual
033   reality Kazakos et al. (2019); Yang et al. (2019); Nikam & Ambekar (2016); Wei et al. (2014).
034   While some previous works have focused on recognizing actions based on appearance informa-
035   tion, other approaches have highlighted the benefits of using pose information. In comparison to
036   RGB videos, Representing videos of human activities with 3D skeleton sequences offers advan-
037   tages in privacy preservation, data efficiency, and excluding extraneous details such as background,
038   lighting variations, or diverse clothing types. Recent models for 3D action recognition based on
039   skeleton sequences have demonstrated impressive results Lee et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2022a;b);
040   Chen et al. (2021). However, these models heavily depend on annotations, which are labor-intensive
041
      and time-consuming to acquire. Motivated by this, in this study, we investigate the self-supervised
      representation learning of 3D actions.
042
043   Prior studies in self-supervised learning have employed diverse pretext tasks, such as predicting
044   motion and recognizing jigsaw puzzles Lin et al. (2020); Zheng et al. (2018); Su et al. (2020). More
045   recently, current research has shifted its focus towards contrastive learning Lin et al. (2023); Mao
046   et al. (2022; 2023b) or Mask Autoencoders (MAE) Wu et al. (2023); Mao et al. (2023a).
047   Contrastive learning approaches, although effective in learning representations, rely heavily on data
048   augmentations to avoid focusing on spurious features. Without using data augmentations, they are
049   prone to the problem of shortcut learning Geirhos et al. (2020), leading to potential overfitting on
050   extraneous features, such as a person’s height or the camera angle, which do not provide a valid cue
051   to discriminate between different actions. As a result, some knowledge expert Tian et al. (2020)
052   is needed to design different augmentations of the same sequence; and methods that incorporate
053
      extreme augmentations in their pretraining pipeline Guo et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023) have shown
      significant improvements.

      2.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED S KELETON -BASED ACTION R ECOGNITION
095
      The objective of self-supervised action recognition is to train an encoder to discriminate sequences
096   with different actions without providing any labels throughout the training. Methods such as LongT-
097   GAN Zheng et al. (2018) pretrain their model with 3D skeleton reconstruction using an encoder-
098   decoder architecture; and P&C Su et al. (2020) improves the performance by employing a weak
099   decoder. Colorization Yang et al. (2021) represents the sequence as 3D point clouds and colorizes it
100   based on the temporal and spatial orders in the original sequence.
101
      Several studies explored various contrastive learning approaches, showing promising results Li
102   et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Mao et al. (2022; 2023b). CrosSCLR Li et al.
103   (2021) applies the MoCo He et al. (2020) framework and introduces cross-view contrastive learn-
104   ing. This approach aims to compel the model to maintain consistent decision-making across different
105   views. AimCLR Guo et al. (2022) improves the representation by proposing extreme augmentations.
106   CMD Mao et al. (2022) trains three encoders simultaneously and distills knowledge from one to an-
107   other by introducing a new loss function. I2 MD Mao et al. (2023b) extends the CMD framework
      by introducing intra-modal mutual distillation, aiming to elevate its performance through incorpo-

136   2.2    C OMBINING M ASKED AUTOENCODERS WITH I NSTANCE D ISCRIMINATION
137
138   Some recent works in the image domain investigated the effect of combining MAE and Instance
139   Discrimination (ID) methods Zhou et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022); Mishra et al. (2022); Tao
140
      et al. (2023); Lehner et al. (2023). iBOT Zhou et al. (2021) combines DINO Caron et al. (2021)
      and BEiT Bao et al. (2021) for the pretext task. RePre Wang et al. (2022) extends the contrastive
141
      learning framework by adding pixel-level reconstruction loss. CAN Mishra et al. (2022) adds gaus-
142
      sian noise to the unmasked patches and it reconstructs the noise and masked patches, and adds a
143   contrastive loss to the encoder output. MSN Assran et al. (2022) aligns an image view featuring
144   randomly masked patches with the corresponding unmasked image. SiameseIM Tao et al. (2023)
145   predicts dense representations from masked images in different views. MAE-CT Lehner et al. (2023)
146   proposes a sequential training by adding contrastive loss after MAE training.
147
      Our work is a sequential self-supervised approach for pretraining of skeleton sequences. It initially
148
      employs an MAE approach using an encoder-decoder architecture and further enhances the output
149   representation of the encoder by tuning its weights using contrastive learning.

      3.1    F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
155
      The overall framework of STARS is illustrated in Fig. 2. It is a sequential self-supervised approach
156   consisting of two main stages. The first stage relies on an MAE-like framework to pretrain the
157   weights of the encoder. We use MAMP Mao et al. (2023a) because it shows promising result in
158   3D action representation learning; however, any alternative MAE-based approach is also applicable.
159   The next stage is designed to tune the parameters of the encoder using an instance discrimination
160   method. Specifically, the second stage replaces the decoder with a projector and predictor Grill
161   et al. (2020). It trains them in addition to the encoder using Nearest-Neighbor Contrastive Learning
      (NNCLR) Dwibedi et al. (2021) to converge to a representation capable of discriminating different

      sequences. This approach helps the encoder learn to output distinct clusters for different actions,
163   improving its ability to discriminate between various sequences.

      3.2   MAMP P RE - TRAINING (S TAGE 1)
166
167   MAMP Mao et al. (2023a) uses a transformer encoder-decoder architecture to reconstruct motions
168   from the 3D skeleton sequence. It receives the input skeleton sequence S ∈ RTs ×V ×Cs , where Ts ,
169   V , and Cs are the temporal length, number of joints, and coordinate channels, respectively. Next,
170   the sequence is divided into non-overlapping segments S′ ∈ RTe ×V ×l·Cs , where Te = Ts /l and
171   l is the segment length. This division results in having Te × V tokens and reduces the temporal
172   resolution by a factor of l. Subsequently, the input joints are linearly projected into joint embedding
173   E ∈ RTe ×V ×Ce where Ce is the dimension of embedding features.
174   As for the pretraining objective and the masking strategy, MAMP leverages the motion informa-
175   tion. Given an original sequence S, the motion M ∈ RTs ×V ×Cs is derived by employing temporal
176   difference on joint coordinates:

247   4.1   DATASETS
248
      NTU-RGB+D 60 Shahroudy et al. (2016) is a large-scale dataset containing 56,880 3D skeleton
249
      sequences of 40 subjects performing 60 actions. In this study, we use the recommended cross-
250   subject (X-sub) and cross-view (X-view) evaluation protocols. In the cross-subject scenario, half of
251   the subjects are selected for the training set, and the remaining subjects are used for testing. For
252   the cross-view evaluation, sequences captured by cameras 2 and 3 are employed for training, while
253   camera 1 sequences are used for testing.
254
      NTU-RGB+D 120 Liu et al. (2019) is the extended version of NTU-60, in which 106 subjects
255
      perform 120 actions in 114,480 skeleton sequences. The authors also substitute the cross-view
256   evaluation protocol with cross-setup (X-set), where sequences are divided into 32 setups based on
257   camera distance and background. Samples from half of these setups are selected for training and the
258   rest for testing.
259
      PKU-MMD Liu et al. (2017) contains around 20,000 skeleton sequences of 52 actions. We follow
260
      the cross-subject protocol, where the training and testing sets are split based on subject ID. The
261
      dataset contains two phases: PKU-I and PKU-II. The latter is more challenging because of more
262   noise introduced by larger view variations, with 5,332 sequences for training and 1,613 for testing.
263

      Network Architecture: We adpoted the same network architecture as MAMP Mao et al. (2023a).
      It uses a vanilla vision transformer (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) as the backbone with Le =
310
      8 transformer blocks and temporal patch size of 4. In each block, the embedding dimension is
311
      256, number of multi-head attentions is 8, and hidden dimension of the feed-forward network is
312   1024. It also incorporates two spatial and temporal positional embeddings into the embedded inputs.
313   The decoder used in first stage is similar to the transformer encoder except that it has Ld = 5
314   layers. In the contrastive tuning modules used in the second stage, the projector module is solely a
315   Batch Normalization Ioffe & Szegedy (2015), given the relatively small size of the 256-dimensional
316   embedding space. The predictor module consists of a feed-forward network with a single hidden
317   layer sized at 4096.
318   Pre-training: The first stage follows the same setting as MAMP Mao et al. (2023a). For the second
319   stage, we use the AdamW optimizer with weight decay 0.01, betas (0.9, 0.95), and learning rate
320   0.001. In the second stage, we train the projection and prediction modules in addition to finetuning
321   the encoder for 20 epochs, and the best representation is chosen based on K-NN (K=10) on validation
322   data. We employ layer-wise learning rate decay with a decay rate of 0.20. All the pretraining
323   experiments are conducted using PyTorch on four NVIDIA A40 GPUs with a batch size of 32 per
      GPU.

347   4.3   E VALUATION AND C OMPARISON
348
349
350   In all evaluation protocols, we report on STARS, the method proposed in section 3, as well as
351
      STARS-3stage. STARS-3stage involves a three-stage pretraining process. The second stage is di-
      vided into two parts: the Head Initialization stage, where only the projector and predictors are
352
      trained, and the contrastive tuning stage, where the encoder is fine-tuned along with the head mod-
353
      ules. More details can be found in the supplementary materials.
354
355   Linear Evaluation Protocol: In this protocol, the weights of the pretrained backbone are frozen
356   and a linear classifier is trained with supervision to evaluate the linear-separability of the learned
357
      features. We train the linear classifier for 100 epochs with a batch size of 256 and a learning rate of
      0.1, which is decreased to 0 by a cosine decay schedule. We evaluate the performance on the NTU-
358
      60, NTU-120, and PKU-II datasets. As shown in Tab. 1, our proposed STARS outperforms other
359
      methods on both NTU benchmarks. On the PKU-II dataset, STARS achieves second-best result, and
360   SkeAttnCLR Hua et al. (2023) outperforms it using a three-stream input method.
361
362   KNN Evaluation Protocol: An alternative way to evaluate the pretrained encoder is by directly
363
      applying a K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) classifier to their output features. Following other works Su
      et al. (2020); Mao et al. (2022; 2023b), each test sequence is compared to all training sequences using
364
      cosine similarity and the test prediction is based on the label of the most similar neighbor (i.e. KNN
365
      with k=1). Tab. 2 compares different methods using KNN evaluation protocol. Notably, we find
366   that MAMP cannot achieve competitive results compared to contrastive learning models, despite
367   showing superior results on linear evaluation. We believe that this is because of the pretraining
368   objective of contrastive learning models, which, by pushing different samples into different areas
369   of the representation space, results in better-separated clusters. Our STARS approach leverages
370   contrastive tuning to enhance the feature representation of MAMP, outperforming all other methods.
371   This demonstrates the superiority of contrastive tuning over contrastive learning approaches.
372   Fine-tuned Evaluation Protocol: We follow MAMP and by adding MLP head on the pretrained
373   backbone, the whole network is fine-tuned for 100 epochs with batch size of 48. The learning rate
374   starts at 0 and is gradually raised to 3e-4 during the initial 5 warm-up epochs, after which it is reduced
375   to 1e-5 using a cosine decay schedule. As shown in Tab. 3, both MAMP and STARS notably enhance
376   the performance of their transformer encoder without pretraining. However, these results indicate
377   that contrastive tuning following MAMP pretraining does not impact the fine-tune evaluation, and
      MAMP and STARS achieve nearly identical results, both outperforming other approaches.

      Transfer Learning Protocol: In this protocol, the transferability of the learned representation is
      evaluated. Specifically, the encoder undergoes pretraining on a source dataset using a self-supervised
395
      approach, followed by fine-tuning on a target dataset through a supervised method. In this study,
396
      NTU-60 and NTU-120 are selected as the source datasets, with PKU-II chosen as the target dataset.
397   Tab. 4 shows that when fine-tuned on a new dataset, masked prediction techniques like Skeleton-
398   MAE and MAMP demonstrate superior transferability compared to contrastive learning methods.
399   Moreover, STARS enhances performance when pre-trained on NTU-60, but its effectiveness dimin-
400   ishes when pre-trained on NTU-120.

      Few-shot Evaluation Protocol: This protocol evaluates the scenario where only a small number
402
      of samples are labeled in the target dataset. This is crucial in practical applications like education,
403   sports, and healthcare, where actions may not be clearly defined in publicly available datasets. In
404   this protocol, we pretrain the model on NTU-60 (XSub) and evaluate it on the evaluation set of
405   60 novel actions on NTU-120 (XSub) using n labeled sequences for each class in n-shot setting.
406   For the evaluation, we follow MotionBERT Zhu et al. (2023) and calculate the cosine distance
407   between the test sequences and the exemplars, and use n-nearest neighbors to determine the action.
408   Tab. 5 compares different methods in the few-shot settings. Notably, MAMP demonstrates poor
409   generalization performance, in contrast to its robust performance in transfer learning and evaluations
410   within the dataset. By applying contrastive tuning, STARS surpasses contrastive learning approaches
411
      in all settings, demonstrating its strength in various evaluations.
412   Qualitative Comparison: Fig. 3 compares the t-SNE visualization of our proposed STARS method
413   with AimCLR Guo et al. (2022), CMD Mao et al. (2022), HiCo-Transformer Dong et al. (2023), and
414   MAMP Mao et al. (2023a). CMD adds cross-modal mutual distillation loss to contrastive learning
415   and by ensuring that various input modalities (joint, bone, and motion) exhibit the same neighbor-
416   hood, it scatters actions across different areas of space and mitigates the impact of applying con-
417
      trastive learning loss. On the other hand, AimCLR and HiCo-Transformer create distinct clusters
      through the use of extreme augmentations and by applying contrastive loss at different hierarchical
418
      levels, respectively. When compared to MAMP, these two contrastive learning methods exhibit a
419
      higher inter-cluster distance than MAMP. Interestingly, actions involving interactions between two
420   individuals, such as kicking, giving objects, and shaking hands, create a distinct higher-level clus-
421   ter compared to actions involving a single person across various methods. Specifically, MAMP
422   shows the highest distance between these two cluster groups, whereas within each group, the action
423   clusters are closely situated. By employing contrastive tuning, STARS effectively minimizes intra-
424   cluster distance (as seen in examples like sneeze/cough) while maximizing inter-cluster distance.
425   This leads to the formation of clearly separated clusters, each representative of different actions.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we proposed a sequential contrastive tuning method. We find that masked prediction
535   methods, despite showing promising results in various within-dataset evaluations, cannot outperform
536   contrastive learning based methods in few-shot settings. By using our STARS framework, we show
537   that we can further enhance the masked prediction baseline while achieving competitive results in
538   few-shot settings, outperforming other models in 5-shot setting. However, when the dataset size
539   is limited for pretraining and for evaluations when encoder is fine-tuned with supervision, STARS
      cannot add significant value to its baseline.

779   A    3-S TAGE D ESIGN
780
781
      An alternative pretraining method is to follow MAE-CT Lehner et al. (2023) and tune the encoder in
782   3 stages. Figure 5 shows the 3-stage design. Initially, when we initialize the projector and predictor
783   modules, we freeze the encoder weights. In the second stage, we exclusively train the projector
784   and predictor modules until they can effectively differentiate between different sequences using the
785   NNCLR approach. Finally, in the third stage, we fine-tune the encoder weights using layer-wise
786   learning rate decay. One motivation for this staged approach is the idea that the NNCLR head’s
787   random weights could interfere with the representation quality by mapping the features into a ran-
788   dom space, disrupting the learned structure. However, our findings challenge this assumption. The
789   t-SNE visualization in Fig. 6 demonstrates that even with random NNCLR head weights, the cluster
790
      structure in the encoder’s output space remains intact in the new representation space. Furthermore,
      we observed with STARS-3stage that replicating this three-stage process not only increases training
791
      time but also leads to a drop in final accuracy. As a result, we use a two-stage design in our proposed
792
      STARS method.

878   Algorithm 1 PyTorch-style pseudo-code of contrastive tuning in the second stage.
879    1    # f: MAMP Encoder. Only second-half is tuned.
880    2    # g: Projector. Batch normalization module
       3    # h: Predictor. 2 layer MLP, hidden size 4096, output 256
881    4    # Q: Queue with length of 32,768
       5
882    6    for x in loader:
883    7       y = f(x) # encoder forward pass
       8       z = g(y) # projection forward pass
884    9       p = h(z) # prediction forward pass
      10       z, p = normalize(z, dim=1), normalize(p, dim=1)
885   11       nn = top_nn(z, Q) # finding nearest-neighbor sample in Q
886   12       loss = L(nn, p)
      13       loss.backward()
887   14       optimizer.step()
      15       update_queue(Q, z)
888   16
889   17
      18    def top_nn(z, Q):
890   19       similarities = z @ Q.T
      20       idx = similarities.max(dim=1)
891   21       return Q[idx]
892   22
      23    def L(nn, p, temperature=0.07):
893   24       logits = nn @ p.T
894   25       logits /= temperature # sharpening
      26       labels = torch.arange(p.shape[0])
895   27       loss = cross_entropy(logits, labels)
      28       return loss
