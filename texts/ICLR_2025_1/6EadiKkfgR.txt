                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this work, we explore the definition of semantic equivalence to establish a con-
010
               nection between contrastive tasks and their downstream counterparts. Specifically,
011
               we investigate when a contrastive dataset can learn representations that encode for-
012            mal semantic equivalence relations for a specific downstream task. In our analysis,
013            we recover a surprising hypothesis resembling the distributional one—dubbed dis-
014            tributional alignment hypothesis. Under this assumption, we demonstrate that the
015            optimal model for simple contrastive learning procedure must generate represen-
016            tations that encode formal semantic equivalence relations for the downstream task.
017            Furthermore, we support the theory with a series of experiments designed to test
018            the presented intuitions.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Overview. Contrastive Learning has emerged as a prominent self-supervised training technique,
024   demonstrating success across diverse data modalities. These include, among others, images (Chen
025   et al. (2020); He et al. (2020)), audio (van den Oord et al. (2018); Saeed et al. (2021)), video (Dave
026
      et al. (2022); Pan et al. (2021)), text (Fang et al. (2020); Xiong et al. (2020)), graphs (Thakoor
      et al. (2021); Ling et al. (2023)), and time series (Zheng et al. (2024)). Notable contrastive learning
027
      techniques encompass SimCLR (Chen et al. (2020)), MoCo (He et al. (2020)), BYOL (Grill et al.
028
      (2020)), SwAV (Caron et al. (2020)), MSimCLR (Korman (2021)), and Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al.
029   (2021)).
030
031   Problem Statement. The primary goal of these techniques is to generate high-quality embedding
032
      representations that can be effectively utilized for downstream tasks (potentially with limited data
      availability). Empirical evidence suggests that semantically similar objects tend to be encoded into
033
      proximate embedding representations during contrastive training (Chen et al. (2020); Jaiswal et al.
034
      (2020)). While the emergence of semantic structure in these embeddings is well-documented (Le-
035   Khac et al. (2020); Kang et al. (2020); Poklukar et al. (2022)), to the best of our knowledge, a formal
036   treatment of this process remains lacking in the current literature.
037
038
      Proposed Solution. This work aims to address the aforementioned gap by formalizing the con-
      cept of semantic equivalence, as proposed by Bertolotti & Cazzola (2024) for sequence modeling
039
      tasks. Briefly, two symbols are defined as semantically equivalent if they can be substituted without
040
      altering the outcome distribution of a certain task (see Definition 2.1). This property is fundamen-
041   tally rooted in the notion of semantic equivalence in programming languages (Scott & Strachey
042   (1971)), where two code snippets are considered semantically equivalent if their substitution does
043   not affect the program’s output regardless of the context (borrowing the denotational semantics for
044   an instant, we would write ∀ρ : Jp1 K(ρ) = Jp2 K(ρ)).
045
      Research Questions.      To summarize, our research aims to address the following questions:
046
047          • RQ1 : Under what conditions is a contrastive task useful for a downstream task?
048          • RQ2 : How can we train embeddings that effectively encode semantical equivalence
049            relations?
050   Findings. Our findings demonstrate that the SimCLR contrastive learning procedure (Chen et al.
051   (2020)) inherently encodes semantically equivalent symbols in close proximity within the embed-
052   ding space. Furthermore, we find a fundamental requirement for a contrastive task to be useful
053   for the downstream counterpart. We dub this requirement distributional alignment hypothesis, for
      its similarity with the classical distributional hypothesis (Bertolotti & Cazzola (2024)). These re-

058   2     BACKGROUND & N OTATION
059
060
      Let us begin by introducing some recurring notations. From a classical supervised learning stand-
      point, one often uses a data distribution from which input-label pairs are sampled, x, y ∼ D. In this
061
      work, we split the input component into symbol and context components. Therefore, we will use a
062
      data distribution from which we can sample symbol-context-label triplets, σ, ρ, y ∼ D. In practice,
063   a symbol may correspond to a word, while the context may represent the rest of the sentence. Sim-
064   ilarly, with images, a symbol may represent an image patch, while the context may represent the
065   rest of the image. We will also use Σ, P, and Y to denote the symbol, context, and label domains
066   respectively. We will use the notation pD (or simply p when the underlying data distribution is evi-
067   dent) to denote the probability function for data sampled according to D. For example, pD (y|σ, ρ)
068   represents the probability of label y when conditioned on the symbol context pair (σ, ρ) when σ, ρ
069   and y are sampled according to D.
070
071   2.1   S EMANTIC E QUIVALENCE
072
      Intuitively, two symbols u and v are semantically equivalent if we can use them interchangeably
073
      without affecting the output distribution, or formally:
074
075
      Definition 2.1 (semantic equivalence (Bertolotti & Cazzola (2024))). Given u, v ∈ Σ, u ⊜ v (u is
      semantically equivalent to v) iff
076
077                                   ∀ρ ∈ P, y ∈ Y . p(y|u, ρ) = p(y|v, ρ)
078
      For example, consider a simple Masked Language Modeling (MLM) tasks (Devlin et al. (2019)),
079
      and, consider the sentence
080
081                         the <MASK> of water is half empty/full.
082
083   Here, we could use both empty and full without affecting the outcome distribution of the mask
084   token. Therefore, we can say that, in this context, empty and full are semantically equivalent.
085   If this relation were to hold true for all contexts, then, we would say that empty and full are
086
      semantically equivalent in general.
087   In a different scenario, consider a cat-vs-dog image classifier where each symbol represents an image
088   patch—similar to the setup used in Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)). In this context,
089   two image patches, such as one depicting a clouded sky and another showing a clear sky, would be
090   considered semantically equivalent since they do not influence the classification outcome.
091   A similar notion of semantics is also used by Chiang & Yogatama (2023).
092
093
      Throughout this work, we will often use the term semantic with its formal meaning in mind rather
      than it liguistic meaning. Therefore, unless stated otherwise, when speaking of semantic relations
094
      or semantic learning, we refer to the formal definition of semantic equivalence (Definition 2.1).

137   3       C ONTRASTIVE L EARNERS ( WITH LABELS ) ARE S EMANTIC L EARNERS
138
139
      Contrastive learning techniques typically assume that labels for downstream tasks are unavailable,
140
      as they are often costly to obtain. However, as we will show, if labels are provided to a simple
141
      variation of SimCLR, it is possible (under appropriate assumptions) to recover representations that
142   encode semantic equivalence relations (as in Definition 2.1). This means that if two symbols are
143   semantically equivalent, their representations will also be equivalent. In the next section, we will
144   explore how to eliminate the need for labels in this framework.
145
      The variant of SimCLR, depicted in Figure 1b, introduces two main differences. First, we employ
146
      two specific types of augmentation functions: ti : X → Σ and t′i : X → P. The former is
147
      responsible for extracting the i-th symbol from the input, while the latter returns the context of the
148   i-th symbol. Here, the i-th symbol may represent the i-th image patch, and the i-th context may
149   represent the remaining patches. Using SimCLR terminology, the first augmentation could be a
150   crop and resize, resulting in the symbol, while the second could be a Cutout specifically removing
151   the previous symbol to produce the context. Let us refer to the set of these augmentations with Tb .
152
153
      Secondly, we introduce an asymmetric architecture to SimCLR. Specifically, we denote the embed-
      ding function E : Σ → Rd , which maps symbols to a d-dimensional representation. Meanwhile,
154
      let E : P × Y → Rd represent the function that maps context-label pairs to a d-dimensional repre-
155
      sentation. The parameters between the embedding function and the encoder function can be shared,
156   partially shared, or separate.
157
158   Consider a batch D = {(σi , ρi , yi )}i=1...N consisting of symbol-context-label triplets, where σi =
159   t(x) and ρi = t′ (x) for x, y ∼ D, t ∼ Tb , and t′ ∼ Tb |t. Here, D represents the input-label data
160   distribution, Tb denotes the augmentation distribution for extracting the symbol from x, and Tb |t
161   represents the augmentation distribution for extracting the context of σ. Our goal is to minimize this
      variation of the InfoNCE loss between the encoder E and the embedding function E:

171   Further, it can be shown that, an optimal model, made of E ∗ and E ∗ , for LN CE needs to encode
172   semantically equivalent symbols with exactly the same vector. This is the subject of the following
173   theorem:
174   Theorem 3.1. Given the symbols u, v ∈ Σ such that 1. u ⊜ v, 2. ∀ρ : p(ρ|u) = p(ρ|v), and 3. if
175   there are (ρ1 , y1 ), . . . , (ρd , yd ) context-label pairs such that E ∗ (ρi , yi ) form a basis for Rd then
176   E ∗ (u) = E ∗ (v)
177   On one hand, semantic equivalence relations do not depend on the conditional distribution (p(ρ|u) =
178   p(ρ|v)); therefore, these hypotheses may seem unnecessary. On the other hand, the data must still
179   reflect these semantic equivalence relations to some extent. For instance, consider an edge case
180   where two symbols are semantically equivalent, but one of them never appears in the dataset. In this
181   scenario, it is natural that we would be unable to discover this semantic equivalence relation.
182
      The final hypothesis, concerning the basis, ensures that the only solution is E ∗ (u) = E ∗ (v). Given
183   that most realistic scenarios encompass thousands of possible context-label pairs while the embed-
184   ding size typically does not exceed d = 1024. Further, it should be noted that establishing a basis
185   for Rd only requires d linearly independent embeddings. The determinant forms a polynomial of
186   the entries E ∗ (ρ1 , y1 ), . . . , E ∗ (ρd , yd ) and has a Lebesgue measure of zero. Thus, slight perturba-
187   tion of the encoded vectors would establish a basis almost surely. This hypothesis is employed in
188   the context of invertible neural networks Finzi et al. (2019), where assuming a weight matrix to
189   be a basis implies non-singularity, allowing for inversion. Additionally, one can always reduce the
190   embedding size to ensure that this hypothesis holds. Ultimately, this hypothesis can be considered
191
      realistic for most use cases.
192   Please refer to Appendix A.2 for a formal proof of Theorem 3.1.

      4    C ONTRASTIVE L EARNERS ARE S EMANTIC L EARNERS
195
196
      As we have seen, the notion of semantic equivalence is strongly tied to a label distribution. However,
197   the rise of contrastive learning is partially due to the fact that it does not require labels (which are
198   usually expensive to obtain) to generate embeddings organized in a semantic structure (Gao et al.
199   (2021); Le-Khac et al. (2020); He et al. (2020)). This raises a natural question: how do contrastive
200   learning techniques learn formal semantics without access to labels?
201
      To answer this question, we need to formalize the concepts of downstream and contrastive tasks. Let
202
      us denote with D the data distribution for the downstream task. We also assume to be able to sample
203   symbol-context-label triplets, σ, ρ, y ∼ D. Further, we will denote with pD (y|σ, ρ) the probability
204   of label y given the symbol-context pair σ and ρ.
205
206
      Now that the downstream task has been defined, we proceed to the contrastive task. Here, we
      are given a data distribution C. From this distribution, we can only sample context-symbol pairs,
207
      σ, ρ ∼ C but we have no access to a label.
208
209   Of course, we need to realize that we cannot apply contrastive learning to any data distribution and
210   hope to learn useful representations for a downstream task of interest. For example, we cannot hope
211   to learn useful representations for an animal classifier from a white noise distribution. Therefore,
212
      we will need some kind of hypothesis between contrastive and downstream data to hold. We refer
      to this hypothesis as the distributional alignment hypothesis:
213
214
      Definition 4.1 (Distributional Alignment Hypothesis). A downstream distribution D and a con-
      trastive distribution C are aligned iff.
215
                  ∀y ∈ Y, ρ ∈ P : pD (y|u, ρ) = pD (y|v, ρ) ⇐⇒ ∀ρ ∈ P : pC (ρ|u) = pC (ρ|v)

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      On the left, we state that u and v are semantically equivalent according to the downstream task. On
217   the right, we state that u and v are conditionally equivalent for the contrastive task. Notice that, this
218   definition highly resembles the definition of distributional hypothesis as formalized by Bertolotti
219   & Cazzola (2024). The main and only difference between the distributional hypothesis and the
220   distributional alignment hypothesis is simply that the first is concerned with a single data distribution
221   while the latter is concerned with two distributions (one for the downstream data and one for the
222   contrastive data).

224   minimize the following:

230   One can now show that the following theorem holds:
231   Theorem 4.2. Given the symbols u, v ∈ Σ such that: 1. ∀ρ : p(ρ|u) = p(ρ|v). 2. If there are
232   ρ1 , . . . , ρd contexts such that E ∗ (ρi ) form a basis for Rd then E ∗ (u) = E ∗ (v)
233
234   This theorem shows that an optimal embedding function needs to encode such symbols u and v with
235   the same vector. The proof of this theorem is provided in Appendix A.3.
236   Similar argument discussed for Theorem 3.1 can be applied here. The hypothesis concerning the
237   basis is necessary to ensure that the only solution is E ∗ (u) = E ∗ (v), and it can be considered
238   realistic. The p(ρ|u) = p(ρ|v) hypothesis, despite being more strong, is necessary to ensure that the
239   data reflects the semantic equivalence relations.
240   By combining Definition 4.1 and Theorem 4.2, it is easy to derive the following corollary:
241
242
      Corollary 4.3. 1. Let C and D be a distributionally aligned contrastive and downstream distri-
      butions, respectively. 2. Let E ∗ and E ∗ be the optimal embedding and encoder functions for the
243
      contrastive task. 3. Let ρ1 , . . . , ρd contexts such that E ∗ (ρi ) form a basis for Rd . Then,
244
245
246                                       u ⊜D v ⇐⇒ E ∗ (u) = E ∗ (v)
247
248
      Here, u ⊜D v denotes the semantic equivalence relation according to the downstream task D. The
      proof of this corollary is immediate. Firstly, from the distributional alignment hypothesis (Defini-
249
      tion 4.1), we have that, two symbols are semantically equivalent for the downstream task if and only
250
      if they are conditionally equivalent for the contrastive task. Further, two conditionally equivalent
251   symbols are encoded with the same vector by the optimal embedding and encoder functions for the
252   contrastive task (Theorem 4.2). Therefore, two symbols are encoded within the same vector only if
253   they were semantically equivalent to begin with. The backward implication is derived in Proposition
254   A.2 of the Appendix Section A.4.
255
      This result highlight a fundamental connection between contrastive learning and semantic learning
256
      (we refer to semantic learning as the process to learn formal semantic relations). In particular, it
257   shows that contrastive learning can be seen as a form of semantic learning, where the semantic
258   equivalence relations (precisely those state in Definition 2.1) are learned from the data distribution
259   itself.

      Modular Addition Task.      We will consider an algorithmic task commonly referred to as mod-
      ular addition, or ModAdd for short (Power et al. (2022); Gromov (2023); Furuta et al. (2024)). In
276
      ModAdd, a model is tasked with solving simple modular addition equations of the form:
277
278                                           (a + b)   mod k = y
279   where y represents the unknown quantity, and a, b, k ∈ {0, . . . , N } with N ∈ N. Typically, k is fixed
280   at the start of training, and the neural network (NN) is presented with pairs of natural numbers and
281   tasked with predicting the correct modulo class. A few training examples are provided in Table 1.
282   Semantic Equivalence in ModAdd. It should be apparent that all symbols of the form a + ik
283   are semantically equivalent to a in ModAdd. For example, consider the problem instantiated with
284   k = 10 and N = 100. In this case, symbols like 0, 10, 20, . . . , 100 are semantically equivalent. In
285   other words, if we swap 10 with 80, we do not alter the outcome distribution of the problem (i.e.,
286   ∀y, a : p(y | a + 80 mod 10) = p(y | a + 10 mod 10)).
287
      Contrastive Modular Addition Task. The contrastive modular addition task, or CModAdd for
288   short, is a simple extension of the previous ModAdd task. In CModAdd, we generate sequences of
289   three symbols. The first symbol represents the first addend, a, which is sampled according to a ∼
290   U(0, N ). The second symbol represents the second addend, b, sampled according to b ∼ U(0, N ).
291   The third symbol represents the result of the modulus operation, c = (a + b) mod k. Additionally,
292   we randomly mask one of the symbols a or b, and refer to the masked symbol as y. The goal of this
293   task is to train a representation for the symbols that can be useful for the downstream task ModAdd.
294   Table 2 provides a few examples for clarity.
295   Conditional Equivalence in CModAdd. It should be apparent that all symbols of the form a+ik
296   are conditionally equivalent to a in CModAdd. For example, consider the problem instantiated with
297   k = 10 and N = 100. In this case, symbols like 0, 10, 20, . . . , 100 are conditionally equivalent. In
298   other words, the probability of seeing 10 under the mask token is always the same as the probability
299   of seeing 80 under the mask token (i.e., ∀y, a : p(a+? mod 10 = y | 10) = p(a+? mod 10 = y |
300   80)).
301   CModAdd and ModAdd are aligned. Let u and v be semantically equivalent symbols according
302   to ModAdd (i.e., ∀y, a : p(y | a + u mod k) = p(y | a + v mod k)). Then, it is easy to see that
303   u and v are conditionally equivalent for CModAdd (∀y, a : p(a+? mod k = y | u) = p(a+?
304   mod k = y | v)) and vice versa. This property aligns exactly with Definition 4.1. Therefore,
305   we can say that CModAdd and ModAdd are distributionally aligned. Furthermore, by applying
306   Theorem 4.2, we find that employing a simple contrastive procedure will result in conditionally
307   equivalent symbols for CModAdd being encoded into spatially close vectors. Since conditionally
308
      equivalent symbols in CModAdd are semantically equivalent in ModAdd, we can conclude that
      semantically equivalent symbols in ModAdd will be encoded into spatially close embeddings as
309
      stated from Corollary 4.3.

369   6              D ISCUSSION
370
371   Let us recall that the main goal of contrastive learning is to learn good representations of data for
372   downstream tasks, which often have limited availability. But what constitutes a good representation?
373   One might be tempted to define it as a representation that facilitates fast and efficient learning of
374
      the downstream task. However, this definition doesn’t specify any particular properties that the
      representation should possess. As a result, questions like When is a contrastive dataset good for
375
      a specific downstream task? remain unanswered.
376
377   We argue that good representations should, at a minimum, encode semantic equivalence relations
      (Definition 2.1). In other words, if two symbols u and v are semantically equivalent (as defined in


                                                                              7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Definition 2.1), their representations should be close to each other, meaning they should be encoded
379   as vectors in close proximity. Conversely, non-equivalent symbols should have representations that
380   are further apart. This belief is strongly supported by Figure 2b, where a model initialized with
381   semantic equivalence relations achieves perfect accuracy early in training compared to the randomly
382   initialized one.
383
      If we accept that good representations need to encode semantic equivalence relations, the next ques-
384
      tion is: how do we train embeddings that encode these equivalence relations? To address this,
385   we turn to a popular pre-training method—contrastive learning. Empirically, contrastive learning
386   has often been observed to generate semantically meaningful relations (Wu et al. (2018); Ge et al.
387   (2022)), although semantics is rarely discussed formally. If we accept the definition of semantic
388   as the one presented in Definitions 2.1, we notice that it heavily depends on the presence of la-
389   bels. Now, if we imagine feeding labels into a contrastive procedure, one could show that a model
390   minimizing Equation 2 would need to encode semantic equivalence relations. This is the subject of
391   Theorem 3.1. However, contrastive learners usually do not have access to labels. In such cases, it can
392   be shown that when the contrastive data are aligned with the downstream data (Definition 4.1), the
393
      trained representation must encode these equivalence relations for the downstream task. This is the
      subject of Corollary 4.3. This behavior is also empirically demonstrated in Figure 2a, where, during
394
      contrastive training, embeddings of semantically equivalent symbols converge, while embeddings
395
      of semantically different symbols do not.
396
397   To summarize the results of this work let us answer briefly the proposed research question:
398   Under what conditions is a contrastive task useful for a downstream task?
399
400         A contrastive dataset can be used for a downstream task when the distributional alignment
401                                              hypothesis holds.
402
403   How can we train embeddings that effectively encode semantical equivalence relations?
404
405       The optimal SimCLR variant (under the proper hypotheses), discussed in Section 3, is formally
406       guaranteed to encode semantic equivalence relations. Empirically, the research community has
407
                             observed this behavior in a variety of different settings.

409   7     T HREATS TO VALIDITY
410
411
      In this section, we aim to address the limitations and potential threats that could impact our conclu-
412
      sions.
413
414   External Validity We claimed that the distributional alignment hypothesis is crucial to the suc-
415   cess of contrastive learning. We supported this claim with both empirical and theoretical evidence.
416
      However, it should be noted that, in most realistic scenarios, verifying this hypothesis is impractical.
      Consequently, in real-world applications, the achieved performance on downstream tasks remains
417
      the only empirical measure of the quality of the representation.
418
419   Internal Validity While Theorems 3.1, and 4.2 formalize the intuitions of this work, they also
420   rely on somewhat strong hypotheses that limit their applicability to most real-world scenarios.
421   One common assumption in this manuscript is the conditional equivalence ∀ρ : p(ρ|u) = p(ρ|v).
422   This assumption is often too strong for practical applications. However, it can be justified by the fact
423   that if the symbols u and v never appear in the same context, discovering their semantic equivalence
424   would be impossible. Nevertheless, it is important to note that contrastive learners are often capa-
425   ble of encoding semantic similarities even when this assumption is violated to some extent. This
426   suggests that the results of this work could be generalized by relaxing the conditional equivalence
427   assumption.
428   The alignment hypothesis (Definition 4.1) used in the proof of Corollary 4.3 is quite strong. In
429   practice, the alignment between representations of semantically equivalent symbols is often only
430   partial, yet contrastive learning has shown success even in such cases. Nevertheless, the conditions
431   under which a contrastive dataset can effectively support a downstream task remain very stringent.
      For example, randomly generated data is unlikely to be useful for any downstream task, and genomic


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      contrastive data would provide little value to a natural language sentiment classifier. Ultimately, this
433   suggests that strong hypotheses are necessary even in practical applications.

440   Contrastive Learning in practice. Contrastive learning is one of the most popular and studied
441   pre-training techniques. While we present a fairly general framework, other works focus on specific
442   data modalities. For example, contrastive learning techniques for text sentences are discussed by
443
      Gao et al. (2021) and Aberdam et al. (2021), see Xu et al. (2023) for a review. On source code
      contrastive learning has been applied by Bertolotti & Cazzola (2023); Wang et al. (2022b) and Ding
444
      et al. (2023). You et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2020), and Qiu et al. (2020) focus on graph represen-
445
      tations, see Liu et al. (2022) for a review. In particular, the work of Xia et al. (2022) focuses on a
446   framework that does not require augmentation. Meanwhile, the work of Wang et al. (2020); Park
447   et al. (2020) and Wang et al. (2023) focuses on images. Tsai et al. (2021a) combine a mixture of
448   expert techniques with contrastive learning image representations. Several long-term forecasting
449   problems are addressed by Park et al. (2024). Other works focus on multiple data modalities (Daun-
450   hawer et al. (2023) and Yuan et al. (2021)), see Zong et al. (2024) for a recent survey. Lo et al. (2024)
451   applied contrastive learning to the agent communication problem. Refer to Rani et al. (2023) and
452   Gui et al. (2024) for general survey regarding self-supervised learning and contrastive learning.
453   Contrastive Learning Techniques. While we mainly focus on the InfoNCE loss with SimCLR.
454   However, there are several other contrastive objectives and technique that have been proven useful
455   in practice that we do not address in the previous sections. Cho (2005) propose one of the first con-
456   trastive objectives introduced in the literature that aims to maximize similarity from pairs of the same
457   class and minimize otherwise. A similar objective in principle is the triple loss (Schroff et al. (2015)).
458   An extension to the triple loss, known as Lifted Structured Loss is proposed by Oh Song et al. (2016).
459   NCE and InfoNCE losses developed by Gutmann & Hyvärinen (2010) and van den Oord et al. (2018)
460
      exploit the concept of matching a data point with the correct one among a set of noise data points.
      More recently, Shidani et al. (2024) proposes a multi-view objective. Lu et al. (2022) tackles the
461
      out-of-distribution problem proposing invariant Causal Representation Learning—iCaRL. Zero-CL
462
      (Zhang et al. (2022)) is a contrastive learning technique that does not rely on negative pairs. Wang
463   et al. (2022a) develops PiCO, a contrastive learning framework for partial label learning. Zhang et al.
464   (2023) proposes a contrastive loss based distributionally robust optimization. The work of Fort et al.
465   (2021); Ge et al. (2021); Wen & Li (2021) focuses on studying and designing the effect multiple and
466   different augmentations.
467
      Understanding Contrastive Learning.           In their works, Arora et al. (2019); Lee et al. (2021)
468
      present some of the first theoretical analyses of contrastive learning, establishing a connection be-
469   tween the contrastive task and the downstream task. Similarly, HaoChen et al. (2021) and Wang
470   et al. (2022c) relax some of the strong assumptions made in earlier analyses. Their work is based on
471   the existence of a latent variable from which positive and negative contrastive data pairs can be sam-
472   pled. The work of Saunshi et al. (2022) challenge prior theoretical result in scenarios with different
473   inductive biases. Wang & Isola (2020) prove that cross-entropy contrastive loss optimizes closeness
474   and uniformity. An information-theoretical perspective is provided by Tian et al. (2020); Tsai et al.
475   (2021b); Tosh et al. (2021), focusing on learning sufficient statistics for the downstream task. John-
476   son et al. (2022) unveils a connection between contrastive learning and kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al.
477
      (1997)). Alon et al. (2023) focus on providing a bound on the sample complexity for several con-
      trasting learning scenarios. Simon et al. (2023) study the stepwise learning phenomenon observed
478
      in contrastive learners through the lenses of linearized model. The work of Shen et al. (2022) and
479
      Garg et al. (2024) compares these techniques when distributional shifts occur. Cui et al. (2023) fo-
480   cuses on contrastive learning when joined with weak supervision. The works of Tschannen et al.
481   (2019); Poole et al. (2019); Lee et al. (2024); Gálvez et al. (2023) study the relation between con-
482   trastive learning and mutual information. In this line of work, Levy & Goldberg (2014) shows that
483   skip-grams with negative sampling is equivalent to perform matrix factorization. Wu et al. (2023)
484   offers a perspective from distributionally robust optimization theory. Compared to these works, our
485   analysis stems from the notion of semantic equivalence and explores under what conditions this
      relationship can be learned using contrastive data.

488   In this work, we analyzed what constitutes a good contrastive task from the theoretical perspective
489   of semantics. In Sections 3 and 4 we formalized the conditions under which contrastive learning can
490   effectively encode semantics equivalence relations in both scenarios—with and without labels avail-
491   able. In the latter scenario, we found that the distributional alignment hypothesis (Definition 4.1)
492   plays a fundamental role. Furthermore, we empirically verified the intuitions formalized earlier
493   in Section 5 through small, controlled experiments where all hypotheses could be easily tested.
494   To further validate the presented intuitions, we conducted additional experiments discussed in Ap-
495   pendix A.6. The code to reproduce the experiments is freely available at the following URL:

499   10    F UTURE W ORK
500
501   In this work, we have shown that an optimal contrastive model must learn to encode semantic equiva-
502
      lence relations under the distributional alignment hypothesis (Definition 4.1) and ideal assumptions.
      However, in practical scenarios, the alignment between contrastive data and downstream tasks is of-
503
      ten partial, and the conditional equivalence assumption is frequently violated. A potential direction
504
      for future research is to explore the robustness of the proposed theory when these assumptions are
505   only partially met or entirely invalid.
506
507   Multitask learning is a paradigm focused on training models to perform multiple tasks simultane-
508
      ously. In the work of Maurer et al. (2016), the learning model is structured with a shared repre-
      sentation function across tasks and a task-specific classification (or regression) head for each task.
509
      We hypothesize that the shared representation function should encode semantic equivalence rela-
510
      tions across all tasks concurrently. However, an intriguing question arises: what happens when the
511   semantic structure is not well-aligned across tasks? Addressing this issue presents a compelling
512   avenue for future work.

1324

1325

1328   with naturally occurring synonyms.
1329
       To generate symbol-context pairs, we will sample a sentence and randomly mask one word. The
1330
       masked word will serve as the symbol, while the remainder of the sentence will act as the context.

1335
