                                                                                                            A BSTRACT
031
                         The intelligent driving cockpit, an important part of intelligent driving, needs to
032                      match different users’ comfort, interaction, and safety needs. This paper aims to
033                      build a super-aligned and generalist driving agent, sage deer. Sage Deer achieves
034                      two highlights: (1) Super alignment: It achieves different reactions according to
035                      different people’s preferences and biases. (2) Generalist: It can understand the
036                      user’s physiological indicators, facial emotions, hand movements, body movements,
037                      driving scenarios, and behavioral decisions. (3) Multimodal: He can understand
038                      RGB, NIR, and depth video to build more robust perception, understanding, and
039                      reasoning. To achieve the above requirements, we design retrieval-enhanced
040
                         multimodal frameworks. We collected multiple data sets and built a large-scale
                         benchmark. This benchmark measures the deer’s perceptual decision-making
041
                         ability and the super alignment’s accuracy.

044   1       I NTRODUCTION
045
046   Owing to advancements in artificial intelligence and high performance computing hardware, intelli-
047   gent connected vehicles have garnered significant attention from both academia and governmental
048   bodies due to their profound potential to revolutionize future transportation paradigms Li et al.
049   (2023b); Teng et al. (2023). By integrating advanced sensors, artificial intelligence algorithms, and
050
      technologies, s possess the capability to perceive their environment, make informed decisions, and
      execute control, which will significantly enhance vehicle safety, optimize traffic flow, and deliver a
051
      comfortable and convenient driving experience.
052
053   The intelligent driving cockpit serves as the interface for human interaction with s, offering a seamless
      and user-focused experience through the provision of real-time data, personalized settings, and

      intuitive controls Li et al. (2023b); Yang et al. (2022). This advanced system enhances communication
055   between the driver, passengers, and the vehicle, increases safety via comprehensive monitoring
056   systems, and improves comfort by adapting to individual preferences and varying driving conditions.
057
058
      However, the pursuit of a universal intelligent driving cockpit that encapsulates both general principles
      and individual-specific requirements is inherently complex. Individuals possess distinct driving habits
059
      and needs, necessitating the development of an agent that aligns with their preferences. However, it
060
      is impractical to train a unique model for each individual user. Existing research Cui et al. (2023b;a);
061   Li et al. (2023c; 2022); Liu & Zhang (2020); Tao et al. (2024) on intelligent driving cockpits
062   predominantly emphasizes rigid decision-making and control by integrating external environmental
063   perception data and in-vehicle occupant status, which usually overlooks the individual preferences
064   and limits the adaptability of cockpit system.
065
      Recent advancements in multi-modal large models have demonstrated their capabilities in understand-
066
      ing and reasoning with multi-modal inputs, including videos and natural language. Sima et al. Sima
067   et al. (2023) proposed the utilization of large language models to perform end-to-end autonomous
068   driving through a visual question answering manner. While Wang et al. Wang et al. (2023) proposed
069   DriveMLM, which can perform close-loop autonomous driving in realistic simulators by mitigate
070   the gap between language decisions and vehicle control commands. As for in-vehicle situations,
071   multi-modal large models are also applied to driver health monitoring Hecht et al. (2018), driving
072   decision individuation with simple strategy Cui et al. (2023b). A robust and personalized intelligent
073   driving cockpit can incorporate driving scenario understanding, language interaction, user behavior
074   and sentiment analysis, processing of personalized driver needs, and intelligent decision-making.
075
      However, the above mentioned research has yet to achieve comprehensive functionality and full-
      modal information processing and reasoning. Thus, there remains a significant gap between current
076
      capabilities and the envisioned intelligent driving cockpit.
077
078   To bridge the gap between current approaches in providing a personalized driving experience and com-
079   prehensive omni-modal information processing, we propose a super-aligned and generalist driving
080   agent, sage deer. We present a retrieval-augmented framework designed to develop a super-aligned
081
      and generalist agent for smart cockpits. This framework is characterized by three core strengths:
      comprehensive tokenization of multi-mode and multi-view videos, robust generalist understanding,
082
      and super-aligned responses. To effectively integrate diverse visual and physiological data, we
083
      tokenize inputs from multiple sensing modalities—including RGB, near-infrared (NIR), and depth
084   cameras—using a pre-trained ResNet18 for feature extraction, followed by mapping to a language
085   space with a two-layer linear layer. Multi-view perspectives are similarly tokenized with distinct
086   perspective markers to ensure comprehensive scene interpretation. Additionally, physiological infor-
087   mation is processed through spatio-temporal representations and encoded into tokens, enabling the
088   model to monitor driver states accurately. The framework employs a retrieval-augmented generation
089   mechanism that leverages an updatable document containing both fundamental and personalized
090   information, enhancing the large language model’s (LLM) ability to generate contextually relevant
091   responses without extensive fine-tuning. Expert knowledge fusion further integrates domain-specific
092
      insights, allowing the system to dynamically adapt based on real-time physiological and environ-
      mental data. This holistic approach ensures high-quality scene understanding, precise physiological
093
      monitoring, and personalized, context-aware interactions, significantly improving the alignment and
094
      generalization capabilities of agents within complex cockpit environments.

      Multi-modality Large Model in Driving Multi-modality large models are widely used in au-
130
      tonomous driving, such as automatic planning and control Mao et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2023a),
131   perception Wang et al. (2020), and driver health monitoring Hecht et al. (2018) among others. By
132   aggregating multi-modal information (e.g., vision, speech, point cloud, etc.) Yang et al. (2023b),
133   multi-modal large models can more effectively help autonomous driving systems perceive the scene
134   inside and outside the vehicle, and make more intelligent decisions.
135
      In the vehicle’s user-facing interface, the driver’s emotions, health, posture, and actions can help the
136
      autonomous driving system interact dynamically and adapt to various driving styles. Cui et al. Cui
137   et al. (2023b;a) first proposed to precisely enhance the decision-making process of autonomous
138   driving by perceiving multi-modal information inside and outside the vehicle. Although these
139   methods can perceive multi-modal information, they adopt a single strategy for different users and
140   cannot make adaptive decisions for different users. Subsequent improvements Cui et al. (2023c);
141   Yang et al. (2024) attempt to promote fine-grained understanding of user language and enhance the
142   control performance of the vehicle system, but these methods overlook the impact of hidden emotions,
143   health, and action information on vehicle safety and efficiency.
144   In this paper, we super-align various modal information of users, which not only includes the
145   user’s own interaction, but also perceives the user’s health, emotions, posture, behavior, and other
146   information, thereby enhancing the intelligence of the vehicle system.
147
148
149
150   Retrieval-Augmented Generation Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances the accuracy
151   of knowledge-intensive tasks by retrieving relevant information from an external knowledge base,
152   which can be continuously updated Gao et al. (2023). Specifically, RAG first collects external
153   information and saves old user queries Ma et al. (2023), organizes the knowledge, aligns it, and
154   stores it in a repository provided by the user. Then, an efficient index is built to retrieve image data.
155   Afterward, RAG retrieves internal information based on the prompts provided by the user, which
156   mainly relies on priority ranking and retrieval. Finally, RAG merges the proposed query and selected
157   information inputs it into a large model and returns a more accurate response. Based on this, RAG
158
      can help answer questions that vary according to specific needs while also aggregating the dialogue
      history scenario, enabling the model to remember customized information effectively.
159
160   In this paper, we use RAG to store customized information of users, including their driving habits and
161   behaviors, which helps build a more human-centered vehicle driving agent, and can use customized
      requirements of specific users.

258   we incorporate multiple sensing modalities, including RGB, near-infrared (NIR), and depth cameras.

      4.2   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
285
      The training of large language models is very expensive, and it is easy to lose the ability to generalize
286   in a small training data. User needs for a smart cockpit are often very different, but we can’t fine-tune
287   the LLM to each user’s specific preferences. To do this, we have adopted a retrieval-augmented
288   generation framework ash shown in Fig. 3.
289
      Specifically, we built an updatable document. This document includes some basics and personal
290
      requirements. Then we chunk the document, here we use a very concise way to divide the document
291
      by sentences. Each sentence is then tokenized, and then the sentence is the same length by filling
292   in the tokens. A four-layer convolutional encoder is then used to compress the literal token into a
293   1/8-length feature. Then, we calculate the similarity between visual features and text features, and
294   only retain the first N features with high similarity. Splicing into visual features. Then, we calculate
295   the similarity between visual features and text features, and only retain the first N features with high
296   similarity. These retrieved temporal features are spliced onto visual features.

300   Reasonable knowledge and carefully crafted prompts can significantly enhance the potential of Large
301   Language Models (LLMs), enabling them to generate more nuanced and context-aware outputs. In
302   the context of the smart cockpit, there exists a strong and intricate relationship between various
303
      factors such as physiological indicators, emotions, behaviors, and the vehicle itself. Understanding
      these correlations is crucial for optimizing driver assistance systems, improving safety, and providing
304
      a more personalized driving experience. To this end, we conducted a systematic investigation of
305
      task correlations in collaboration with vehicle human factors experts, enabling us to explore these
306   dependencies in greater detail.
307
308   The Relationship Between Physiological State and Emotion: Classical studies in psychophysiology
309
      (e.g., James-Lange, Schachter-Singer) have long established that physiological states, such as heart
      rate variability, skin conductance, and muscle tension, are closely linked to emotional responses. In
310
      the context of smart cockpits, real-time monitoring of physiological signals can provide valuable
311
      insights into the emotional state of the driver. For instance, elevated heart rate and increased skin
312   conductance may be indicative of stress or anxiety, particularly in challenging driving scenarios.
313   Recognizing this connection allows the system to adapt accordingly, offering calming feedback or
314   assistance in real-time.
315
      In addition, we investigated prior knowledge of other tasks, including: The Relationship Between
316
      Physiological State and Behavior,The Relationship Between Physiological State and Behavior, The
317
      Relationship Between Physiological State and Behavior. These relationships are not isolated but
318   rather dynamically interact in real-world driving scenarios. By leveraging real-time data on cabin
319   conditions — including environmental factors, driver behavior, and physiological/emotional states —
320   the smart cockpit can generate a wider range of more accurate and contextually appropriate responses.
321   This dynamic adaptation not only enhances the comfort and safety of the driver but also improves the
322   overall driving experience. For example, in high-stress situations, the system may offer enhanced
323   alerts or autonomous driving support, while in calm conditions, it might prioritize fuel efficiency or
      offer entertainment options.
