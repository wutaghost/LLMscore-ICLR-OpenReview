009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The excessive energy and memory consumption of neural networks has inspired
012            a recent interest in quantized neural networks. Due to the discontinuity, training
013            binary neural networks (BNNs) requires modifications or alternatives to standard
014
               backpropagation, typically in the form of surrogate gradient descent. Multiple
               surrogate methods exist for feedforward BNNs; however, their success has been
015
               limited when applied to recurrent BNNs, but successful when used in binary-
016
               like spiking neural networks (SNNs), which contain intrinsic temporal dynamics.
017            We show that standard binary activation approaches fail to train when applied to
018            layer with explicit recurrent weights, and present a theoretical argument for the
019            necessity of temporal continuity in network behavior. By systematically incorpo-
020            rating mechanisms from SNN models, we find that integrative state enables recur-
021            rent binary activation networks to reach similar performance as floating-point ap-
022            proaches, while explicit reset and leakage terms do not affect performance. These
023            results show how spiking units enable the training of binary recurrent neural net-
024            works and identify the minimally complex units required to make recurrent binary
025
               activations trainable with current surrogate methods.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028   As large neural network models continue to permeate both data center and edge application spaces,
029   counteracting the increasing energy and memory demands of these networks is critical. Quantized
030   neural networks are one such method, which attempt to balance the increased size of the networks
031   with decreased precision in activation functions and weights (Hubara et al., 2017). Today, sev-
032   eral software ecosystems exist to perform quantization-aware training and post-training fine-tuning,
033   enabling the reduction of weights and activations to 4-bit or lower resolution for deployment to spe-
034   cialized hardware for inference (Pappalardo, 2023). The limit of decreased precision is binary neural
035   networks (BNN), which may use binary activation functions (BANN) or binary weights (BWNN)
036
      (Hubara et al., 2016), and enable extremely energy efficient inference (Zhu et al., 2024). Binary
      weights and binary activations provide different advantages over their floating-point equivalents. In
037
      digital devices, replacing inter-unit activity transmission from floating-point to binary can drastically
038
      reduce the large energy overhead of data transfers to and from memory (Orchard et al., 2021). Both
039   activity and weight quantization reduce the memory requirements of networks. In machine learning
040   accelerators based on analog in-memory computing (Aguirre et al., 2024), quantized weights enable
041   tolerance to intrinsic variability in the stored analog memory states, while BANNs can eliminate the
042   complexity and overhead of multi-bit analog-to-digital converters (Xiao et al., 2023).
043
044   Recurrent Binary Activations While heavily quantized activations have been demonstrated in
045
      a wide range of neural network topologies and BWNNs have been trained in recurrent networks
      (Alom et al., 2018), standard BANNs have only been reported for feedforward topologies. How-
046
      ever, in many edge computing tasks, where the low power consumption of BNNs is particularly
047
      desirable, recurrent layers are critical (recurrent neural networks; RNNs). Examples include audio
048   and video processing applications, such as keyword spotting or object tracking. Other use-cases for
049   RNNs may rely on large networks; in video processing, for example, the network size must grow
050   proportionally with the size of the input videos, and therefore require minimized memory consump-
051   tion per unit. These varied use cases highlight the need for binary recurrent layers under a variety
052   of constraints. Though binary activations are typically absent from the RNN literature, a notable
053   exception is spiking neural networks (SNNs), which utilize temporal dynamics along with binary-
      valued activations, and are often used as recurrent layers in neuromorphic computing studies (Bittar

054
      & Garner, 2022; Bellec et al., 2018). These networks incorporate loosely biologically inspired tem-
055   poral dynamics to keep a pre-activation state within each neuronal unit. The state accumulates over
056   time, and the units communicate via binary ‘spikes’ only when this state reaches a critical threshold.

058   Previous Work Training BANNs Prior methods of training BANNs utilizing backpropagation
059
      fall into three broad categories: surrogate gradient descent, probabilistic surrogates, and progressive
      sharpening. Surrogate gradient descent, which uses an approximation of binary activations during
060
      the backward pass, is the most reported and a variant of this method is used to train SNNs (Neftci
061
      et al., 2019; Eshraghian et al., 2023). Probabilistic approaches convert the input to an activation layer
062   to a probability function during activity propagation, and perform gradient descent on the underlying
063   probabilities rather than the stochastic activities themselves (Chung et al., 2017). The final method
064   uses a tuneable activation function which progresses from smooth to discrete over the course of
065   training (Severa et al., 2019). Additional methods for training binary activation networks exist,
066   but these use training methods which are more removed from standard backpropagation, such as
067   random perturbations (Bengio et al., 2013; Ma et al., 2023) or more complex biologically-grounded
068   local learning rules that rely on memory-intensive traces of recent activity (Nicola & Clopath, 2017).

      Explicit Versus intrinsic Recurrence In order to process temporal relationships among the data,
096
      RNNs must have a mechanism for retaining information over time. This mechanism can come in the
097
      form of explicit recurrence or intrinsic recurrence, as shown in Fig. 1. Explicit recurrence, where
098   the layer’s output is used as part of the same layer’s input on the next timestep, is more common in
099   standard machine learning frameworks. In the simplest case of a dense recurrent (Elman) layer this
100   follows the form of:
101                                   y(t + 1) = Θ(Wf f x(t) + Wrec y(t))                               (1)
102   Where y is the output of of the layer, Θ is a nonlinear activation function that operates on the sum
103   of weighted inputs, Wf f is the feedforward weight matrix that operates on the feedforward activity
104   x, and Wrec is the recurrent weight matrix. The recurrent matrix takes the output of a neural layer
105   at one timestep and re-presents them on the next timestep, to be summed with input from earlier
106   layer of a network. This ability to mix partially-processed information from previous timesteps with
107   current information, often in a trained manner, allows high-dimensional temporal relationships to
      be learned. If the recurrent weights of an explicit-RNN were set to zero, the network would operate


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      as a feedforward network. Intrinsic recurrence, in contrast, stores a set of units with time-varying
109   state, just as the spiking neural networks outlined above do. An intrinsic recurrent layer may be
110   expressed as the dynamical system:
111
112                                v(t + 1) = F (v(t)) + G(y(t)) + Wf f x(t)
                                                                                                          (2)
113                                y(t + 1) = Θ(v(t + 1))
114
      Here the term v(t) represents a local state which accumulates information over time without an
115   explicit recurrent connection. F and G are terms which change the state in response to the pre- (F )
116   or post-(G) activation state in the previous timestep. Finally, the nature of this equation prevents the
117   local state (v(t)) from instantaneously changing in response to Wf f x(t).
118
      Explicit without intrinsic recurrence is typical of standard machine learning approaches such as the
119
      gated recurrent unit, where the recurrent weights directly determine the interaction of the previous
120
      activity and current inputs. intrinsic without explicit recurrence can act on temporal locality in
121   the processed data, and may occur in applications which emphasize speed of processing, but operate
122   primarily on spatial features with simple temporal patterns (Pedersen et al., 2023; Subramoney et al.,
123   2023; Bing et al., 2020). However, many SNN models utilize both explicit and intrinsic recurrence,
124   which can be written in the general form:
125
                       v(t + 1) = F (v(t)) + G(y(t)) + H(Wrec , y(t)) + K(Wf f , x(t))
126                                                                                                       (3)
127                    y(t + 1) = Θ(v(t + 1))
128   This equation includes terms from both equations 1 and 2. In the case of all-to-all layers, the func-
129   tions H and K are standard matrix multiplication, but may also take other forms, such as convolu-
130   tion. Utilizing this unified equation allows us to drop various terms to more exhaustively investigate
131   the sub-dynamics which are necessary for training.
132
      Related Work As noted above, several lines of research have investigated binary activation func-
133
      tions in feedforward topologies. Additional work has investigated recurrent layers that received
134   binarized inputs, but which still use real-valued activation in recurrent transmission (Edel & Köppe,
135   2016). Recent work has also combined the explicit recurrence of more advanced networks such
136   as GRUs with intrinsic recurrence, including spike-based transmission, but still use a real-valued
137   pre-activation state (Dampfhoffer et al., 2022). While spiking neural networks have utilized ex-
138   plicit recurrence, the multiple differences between these studies have so far led to an incomplete
139   understanding of the required dynamics for training more general BARNNs.
140   Contributions Observing that SNNs constitute a subset of BARNNs, specifically combining mul-
141   tiple intrinsic dynamics with explicit recurrent weights, we sought to determine the essential com-
142   ponents of SNN-based training which would allow backpropagation-like training. Our main contri-
143   butions are to:
144
             • Illustrate temporal discontinuities for binary activation explicit recurrent layers, leading to
145
               unsuccessful backpropagation through time (section 4.1).
146          • Demonstrate that surrogate gradient methods fail to converge when employed with a binary
147            activation in a recurrently connected layer (section 4.2).
148          • Demonstrate, across multiple surrogate approaches, that incorporating pre-activation inte-
149            grative state allows training of recurrent binary activation networks (section 4.2).
150          • Show robustness of performance when including additional state dynamics such as explicit
151            reset and proportional leakage of sub-threshold state (section 4.3).

342   4.1 R ECURRENT B INARY ACTIVATIONS ARE D ISCONTINUOUS T HROUGH T IME

469   can benefit BARNNs. In the context of the binarization methods introduced here, the reset and leak
470   can be integrated into the state dynamics of Equation 13 to become:

476   be the STE or probabilistic mechanisms from section 2. The terms ‘I’,‘R’,and ’L’ allow enabling or

478   across all possible combinations of dynamics-sets. We find no systematic differences in overall
479   performance in any of the tasks.

      lows the output activity of a unit to more strongly affect the pre-activation state on the next timestep.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
521
522   We investigated current methods for training binary activation neural networks, and their generaliza-
523   tion to recurrent networks. We found that without additional modifications, surrogate-based, prob-
524   abilistic, and progressive sharpening approaches to train BANNs could not be adapted for recurrent
525
      layers due to the temporal discontinuities induced by binary activations. However, incorporating
      an integrative state smooths these temporal dynamics, and results in successful training in recurrent
526
      layers. Additional SNN-based dynamics, such as slow leak and explicit reset, did not substantially
527
      alter the performance of such stateful networks. This demonstration, along with the theoretical ar-
528   gument of section 4.1 lead us to conclude that pre-activation state is necessary and sufficient for
529   training of binary recurrent networks.
530
531
      The ability to train binary activation RNNs has the ability to resolve several constraints currently
      facing their widespread use. The reduced activation precision can reduce the memory usage of
532
      existing CPU/GPU and FPGA accelerators, allowing larger RNN networks to be utilized in both
533
      edge and HPC scenarios. These benefits could be further increased by informed quantization of the
534   state (Venkatesh et al., 2024; Apolinario et al., 2024), allowing it to be implemented with a simple
535   register shift. Probabilistic binary activation functions open the possibility of deployment to highly
536   efficient but noisy emerging devices (Ma et al., 2023). When designing a network for inference
537   on a hardware accelerator, the inherent properties of the device will determine the best choice of
538   dynamics which show otherwise show similar performance. These additional findings illustrate
539   how non-standard neural network models can open additional use-cases and influence the design of
      emerging accelerators.
