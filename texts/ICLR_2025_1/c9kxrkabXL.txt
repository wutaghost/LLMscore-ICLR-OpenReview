011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Goal-conditioned sequence-based supervised learning with transformers has
014            shown promise in offline reinforcement learning (RL) for single-agent settings.
015            However, extending these methods to offline multi-agent RL (MARL) remains
016            challenging. Existing transformer-based MARL approaches either train agents in-
017            dependently, neglecting multi-agent system dynamics, or rely on centralized trans-
018            former models, which face scalability issues. Moreover, transformers inherently
019            struggle with long-term dependencies and computational efficiency. Building on
020
               the recent success of Structured State Space Sequence (S4) models, known for
               their parameter efficiency, faster inference, and superior handling of long con-
021
               text lengths, we propose a novel application of S4-based models to offline MARL
022
               tasks. Our method utilizes S4вҖҷs efficient convolutional view for offline training
023            and its recurrent dynamics for fast on-policy fine-tuning. To foster scalable co-
024            operation between agents, we sequentially expand the decision-making process,
025            allowing agents to act one after another at each time step. This design promotes
026            bi-directional cooperation, enabling agents to share information via their S4 latent
027            states or memory with minimal communication. Gradients also flow backward
028            through this shared information, linking the current agentвҖҷs learning to its prede-
029            cessor. Experiments on challenging MARL benchmarks, including Multi-Robot
030            Warehouse (RWARE) and StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC), demon-
031
               strate that our approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art offline RL and
               transformer-based MARL baselines across most tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has demonstrated significant success in learning com-
039
      plex policies that require coordination among multiple agents to maximize a shared objective (Cao
      et al., 2012; Berner et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2015). However, this success often relies on a substantial
040
      number of interactions with the environment, which can be computationally expensive in high-
041
      fidelity simulations or prohibitively risky in real-world applications. To enhance sample efficiency,
042   offline reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms (Lee et al., 2021; Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al.,
043   2019; 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023) have been de-
044   veloped, enabling learning from pre-collected offline datasets, thus reducing the need for extensive
045   online interactions.
046
      Offline RL is plagued by the well-known issue of distribution shift, which leads to extrapolation
047
      errors when encountering out-of-distribution (OOD) samples during policy training. This occurs
048   when the learned policy deviates from the unknown behavior policy used to collect the training data.
049   To mitigate this, various forms of regularization (Kumar et al., 2019) are introduced to ensure that
050   the learned policy remains close to the behavior policy (Kumar et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023; 2022a).
051   In multi-agent settings, the joint state-action space expands exponentially as the number of agents
052   increases. This makes it challenging to apply these regularization techniques globally on the joint
053   state-action space, leading to sparse and less effective regularization constraints, especially when
      working with a limited and less diverse offline dataset.

054
      Sequence-based supervised learning has been concurrently applied to address offline MARL, lever-
055   aging the significant success of supervised learning in capturing complex patterns from large offline
056   datasets. This approach, first introduced by the Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021), has
057   demonstrated its ability to learn policies in an autoregressive fashion by predicting the next action
058   based on the current state, previous action and the desired return-to-go. While efforts have been
059   made to extend DT-based architectures to offline MARL (Meng et al., 2021; Tseng et al., 2022),
060   certain limitations persist. MADT (Meng et al., 2021) adapts DT independently for each agent
061   in the multi-agent settings, failing to explicitly model cooperation between agents. (Tseng et al.,
062   2022) adopts a similar approach and trains a centralized teacher policy to capture agent interactions,
063
      with individual agents learning through policy distillation. However, centralized transformers face
      scalability issues, requiring training on information from all agents. Additionally, these approaches
064
      inherit the inherent limitations of transformers, such as large model sizes, inefficient runtime infer-
065
      ence, and restricted ability to capture long-range dependencies due to fixed window size constraints.
066
067   Structured State Space Sequence (S4) models (Gu et al., 2021a) have recently been shown to outper-
068   form transformer-based models in single-agent offline RL tasks (Bar-David et al., 2023). Building
069
      on this success, we propose a sequence-learning-based offline MARL algorithm leveraging S4 vari-
      ants. These models provide superior parameter efficiency compared to transformers, effectively
070
      capture longer temporal contexts, and enable constant-time inference over the quadratic time com-
071
      plexity of transformers with respect to the sequence length. Unlike previous works, such as MADT,
072   which trains agents independently, our method explicitly models cooperation through a Sequentially
073   Expanded MDP (SE-MDP) paradigm. In this framework, recently used in online MARL settings
074   (Li et al., 2023), each decision step is divided into mini-steps, with agents acting sequentially based
075   on their predecessorsвҖҷ actions. Unlike (Li et al., 2023), we enable limited communication, requiring
076   each agent to access only its immediate predecessorвҖҷs information, shared through the latent state
077   representation of the S4 model. Utilizing this hidden state of the S4 module of the current agent,
078   information on all its prior agents is efficiently passed down to the next agent, and gradients flow
079   backward from the current agent through this shared memory to the previous agents during training.
080
      This design enables scalable training with constant memory communication overhead, unlike tra-
      ditional communication-based MARL algorithms, where memory overhead increases quadratically
081
      with the number of agents. Additionally, this streamlined information-sharing mechanism helps
082
      mitigate non-stationarity issues during online fine-tuning. This form of training also shares similar-
083   ities with the way information is passed between segments of long sequences in Recurrent Memory
084   Transformer (RMT) (Bulatov et al., 2022).
085
086
      The S4-based agents are trained directly on sequences or trajectories from the offline dataset in
      an efficient convolutional manner. The offline pre-trained models can be further used for sample-
087
      efficient online fine-tuning based on individual tuples instead of sequences leveraging the recurrent
088
      view of S4. We evaluate the performance of our developed algorithm, called Multi-Agent Decision
089   S4 (MADS4), on the challenging offline MARL benchmarks of Multi-Robot Warehouse (RWARE)
090   (Papoudakis et al., 2020) and StarCraft2 Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) (Samvelyan et al., 2019),
091   where MADS4 achieves superior performance across many tasks over state-of-the-art offline RL-
092   based and transformer-based baselines.

098   Offline Reinforcement Learning Offline RL allows for policy learning based on pre-collected
099
      datasets without having access to active interactions with the environment (Levine et al., 2020),
      which is then directly used as the final policy or is used as a starting point for further improvement
100
      (Uchendu et al., 2023). This learning paradigm however results in severe distribution shift and
101
      extrapolation errors during policy evaluation on OOD samples not present in the offline dataset
102   (Kumar et al., 2019; Fujimoto et al., 2019). Several approaches have been developed to mitigate this
103   issue which typically involves various types of regularizations to be near the offline data distribution.
104   Policy-based regularizations implicitly or explicitly constrain the policy to be close to the behavior
105   policy of the dataset (Wu et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022). Value-
106   based regularizations aim to learn conservative value functions on OOD samples (Kumar et al.,
107   2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022b). Other approaches involve including uncertainty (Wu
      et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022) or penalizing OOD rewards (Yu et al., 2020).

139
140   Offline MARL Extending single-agent RL methods to multi-agent settings presents significant
141   challenges due to the exponential growth of the joint state-action space. Most MARL algorithms
142
      adopt the Centralized Training with Decentralized Execution (CTDE) paradigm. In CTDE, global
      information is shared during training, and local, decoupled policies are used for execution (Oliehoek
143
      et al., 2008; Sunehag et al., 2017; Rashid et al., 2020; Son et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Foerster
144
      et al., 2017; Lowe et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2021). Recently, offline RL-based MARL algorithms
145   have emerged, typically applying regularizations on local policies or value functions (Yang et al.,
146   2021; Jiang & Lu, 2023; Pan et al., 2022). On the other hand, (Meng et al., 2021) extends the
147   Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021) to a multi-agent setting, where agents are trained
148   independently by sharing weights within a goal-conditioned supervised learning framework.
149
      However, these algorithms do not provide guarantees of global-level regularizations and fail to ex-
150
      plicitly or implicitly learn cooperative behavior. Only a few recent works have tried to tackle these
151   limitations. For example, (Wang et al., 2024) uses an implicit global to-local regularization, and
152   (Tseng et al., 2022) uses knowledge distillation to distill cooperation in the local policies.
153
154
155
      S4 S4(Gu et al., 2021a;b) and their variants (Gupta et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2022), which are
156   developed on time-invariant linear state space layers, have outperformed transformers in capturing
157   long-range contexts. These models require far fewer parameters and have constant time inference;
158   hence, they have been suitably utilized in reinforcement learning domains in single-agent learning
159   (Bar-David et al., 2023) and in-context learning (Lu et al., 2024). Commonly, the model uses the
160   convolutional mode for efficient parallelizable training (where the whole input sequence is seen
161   ahead of time) and switched into a recurrent mode for efficient autoregressive inference (where the
      inputs are seen one timestep at a time).

      Sequentially Expanded MDP In this section, we present our approach based on sequence learn-
165   ing with state-space layers. In this work, the Multi-agent Markov Decision Process (MMDP)
166   is transformed into a Sequentially Expanded Markov Decision Process (SE-MDP), where each
167   timestep is divided into n mini timesteps and a multi-agent decision by n agents is expanded into a
168   sequence of n individual decisions, with only one agent acting during each mini-timestep. Thus, a
169   single-step transition in the original MMDP (st , at , st+1 ) resulting in a shared reward r(st , at ) is
170   composed of a sequence of n intermediate transitions, which in turn result in the same shared reward
171   r(st , at ), as shown in Figure 1.
172

175   Within this framework, at each timestep, an agentвҖҷs action is based on information passed by the
176   immediately preceding agent in the sequence. This creates a bidirectional dependency between the
177   agents as shown in Figure 1: in the forward direction, an agentвҖҷs action is influenced by the actions
178   of its predecessors, while in the backward direction, gradients can propagate from the current agent
179   back to the previous agents.

      Network Architecture and Training The MADS4 architecture consists of three key components,
287
      as illustrated in Figure 2: (i) Input, state, and output projection layers: each of these consists of a
288
      fully connected layer followed by ReLU activations; (ii) Input encoder layers: These layers han-
289   dle states, actions, and rewards/returns, each implemented as fully connected linear layers; (iii)
290   Sequence modeling component: This component consists of stacked S4 blocks, where each block
291   consists of Batch Normalization layer followed by S4 layer, linear mixing layer with GELU activa-
292   tion, and a dropout layer. We employed various kernels for S4, with the вҖқNormal Plus Low RankвҖқ
293   kernel initialized using HIPPO, achieving the best performance. In all experiments, we set the input
294   channel size to H = 96 and the S4 state size to N = 96. An ablation study detailing the effects of
295   varying state and input sizes is provided in Appendix C.1.
296   In the offline setting, the S4 model is trained efficiently using the convolutional view on entire tra-
297   jectories sampled randomly from the offline dataset. The trajectories are zero-padded to a constant
298   context length. Unlike transformers, which face limitations on context length due to the expensive
299   quadratic time and space complexity of self-attention, S4-based models can be trained on complete
300   trajectories that are often much longer than those typically used for transformers in most environ-
301   ments. The impact of truncating trajectory lengths has significant implications for model perfor-
302   mance, as detailed in Appendix C.2. Actions are predicted based on the action logit outputs of the
303
      model, and the model is trained based on loss computed using cross-entropy between the true action
      labels and the predicted actions.

307
      For online fine-tuning, the offline pre-trained agent is used to interact with the online environment
308
      and is further updated based on an on-policy training scheme. The agent interacts with the envi-
309   ronment while creating the buffer, which stores the local observations and actions of the individual
310   agents, shared global states of the environment, rewards, returns-to-go, and also the latent states of
311   the S4 modules of the agents. Within the well-known MAPPO-based (Yu et al., 2021) actor-critic
312   framework, the offline-pretrained S4-based model is used as actor networks of the agents which pre-
313   dict actions via the recurrent view based on latent states and other inputs as used in the pretraining
314   stage. The critic network is conditioned on both the global states of the environment as well as the
315   encoded latent S4 states to evaluate the state value function.
316

358   Datasets We evaluate the performance of MADS4 on challenging cooperative MARL benchmarks
359   of Multi-Robot Warehouse (RWARE) (Papoudakis et al., 2020) and StarCraft2 Multi-Agent Chal-
360   lenge (SMAC) (Samvelyan et al., 2019). The offline datasets in the RWARE domain are obtained

523   MADS4 in decentralized setting To adapt MADS4 for a decentralized setting, where agents act
524   in parallel, we leverage the hidden state information of each agent from the previous timestep as a
525   proxy for the current timestep. This approach removes the sequential dependency in updating agent
526
      memory, as all agentsвҖҷ memory information from the previous timestep is available when making
      decisions at the current timestep. Since memory accumulates over multiple timesteps, relying on
527
      the previous timestepвҖҷs information does not compromise performance, as demonstrated in Figure
528
      5(b). This modification enables our algorithm to function effectively in decentralized policy settings
529   without performance degradation.

532   5   C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In this work, we showcase the effectiveness of S4-based models in surpassing transformer-based ar-
535   chitectures for sequence-to-sequence offline multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) tasks. By
536   restricting communication to the exchange of information between unique, arbitrarily selected pairs
537   of agents, MADS4 fosters superior cooperation compared to state-of-the-art offline RL and central-
538   ized transformer-based baselines, which require complete access to all agentsвҖҷ information during
539   training. MADS4 offers a low-latency and lightweight model that can be trained more efficiently
      than transformers and fine-tuned online using recurrent computations.

707   S4Gu et al. (2021a) layer is a variant of linear and time-invariant (LTI) state-space model (SSM)Gu
708   et al. (2021b) which adopts the HIPPO Gu et al. (2020)-based initializations in order to better capture
709   longer contexts, and proposes efficient ways for kernel computations and parallel training.

711   A.1.1     R ECURRENT V IEW
712
713   Given an input scalar function u(t) : R вҶ’ R, the continuous LTI SSM is defined by the following
714
      first-order differential equation:
715
716                             xМҮ(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t),        y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)                            (10)
717
718   The model maps the input stream u(t) to y(t). It was shown that initializing A by the HIPPO matrix
719
      Gu et al. (2020) grants the state-space model (SSM) the ability to capture long-range dependencies.
      Similar to previous works Gu et al. (2021a); Gupta et al. (2022), D is replaced by parameter-based
720
      skip-connection and is omitted from the SSM by assuming D = 0.
721
722   This SSM operates on continuous sequences, and it is discretized by a step size вҲҶ to operate on
723   discrete sequences. Let the discretization matrices be AМ„, BМ„, CМ„:

725                 AМ„ = (I вҲ’ вҲҶA/2)вҲ’1 (I + вҲҶA/2),          BМ„ = (I вҲ’ вҲҶA/2)вҲ’1 вҲҶB,            CМ„ = C            (11)

728
729                                      xk = AМ„xkвҲ’1 + BМ„uk ,     yk = CМ„xk                                   (12)
730
731   Using the recurrent Eq.12, SSM asymptotically allows for constant O(1) time and memory inference
732   for each token/ timestep, as compared to O(L2 ) inference for transformers. SSM can be interpreted
733   as a linear RNN in which AМ„ is the state-transition matrix, and BМ„, CМ„ are the input and output matrices.
734   Thus, it essentially requires O(L) training, L being the sequence length, as compared to O(L2 )
735   (parallelizable) training complexity for transformers.

792   A.2   S HARING H IDDEN S TATE R EPRESENTATIONS
793
794   The raw outputs from the S4 layer consist of yk = CМ„xk , where yk вҲҲ RH and the latent states
795
      xk вҲҲ RN Г—H for H input channels. Since the outputs are linear projections and offer a compact
      representation of the latent states (or, memory of the agent), this has been used as the message that
796
      is transmitted from one agent to the next in the SE-MDP. This offers several advantages: i) results in
797
      better team performance; ii) offers scalable cooperation between agents, which eliminates the need
798   for a centralized transformer or a critic, which requires access to information from all agents; one
799   agent needs access to only its immediate neighbor in the sequence; (iii) allows parallel training via
800   convolution.
801
      We also experimented with passing the raw hidden states xk вҲҲ RN Г—H from one agent to another.
802
      The hidden states can be complex, depending on the parameterization of the S4 kernel. Therefore,
803
      before passing the latent states directly, we first linearly mix the hidden states across the H channels
804   to obtain xk вҲҲ CN . Then, we linearly project the real and imaginary parts of xk after concatenation.
805   This mode of information transfer, however, has notable drawbacks: i) it requires computing the
806   S4 hidden state at every timestep, which requires recurrent rollouts of the S4 kernel, and ii) it fails
807   to outperform the method of passing the S4 outputs; possibly due to errors accumulated during
808   recurrent training. A comparison of performance using S4 output representation versus S4 latent
809   state representation is shown in Figure6, where passing S4 outputs resulted in better performance
      across all tasks.

814   A.3   P RELIMINARY STUDY USING M AMBA
815
816   We also explored Mamba as an alternative to LTI S4-based models. Mamba allows time-variant pa-
817   rameters to be considered in the SSM equations. Though convolution cannot be applied here since
818   the kernel cannot be computed apriori since the parameters B, C are input-dependent, efficient par-
819   allel scan operation allows for parallelizable O(log L) complexity. However, preliminary analysis
820   utilizing Mamba resulted in suboptimal performance, and it requires more extensive analysis.

822   A.4   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND T RAINING
823
      In all experiments, we set the input channel size to H = 96 and the S4 state size to N = 96. Offline
824
      training is conducted on batches of 64 trajectories, with the maximum trajectory length in the offline
825
      dataset used as the length for each batch. The shorter trajectories are zero-padded to a constant
826   length. The training was performed using Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10вҲ’4 .
827
828   The offline trained model is fine-tuned online using on-policy MAPPO. During the initial stage
829
      of fine-tuning, the actor network is kept frozen, and the critic is first trained for the first 50,000
      iterations. After this, both the actor and critic are trained simultaneously, with a slower learning
830
      rate for the actor network (10вҲ’5 ) compared to the critic (10вҲ’4 ). During on-policy fine-tuning, the
831
      returns-to-go is set at 10% higher than the highest returns encountered during training. On-policy
832   training is conducted in batches of 64. To mitigate the issue of deteriorating performance with
833   prolonged on-policy training, the S4 kernel A can be kept frozen. All experiments were run on a
834   single NVIDIA RTX 2080Ti GPU. Experiments on the RWARE domain take less than 2 hrs to reach
835   optimal performance, and experiments on the SMAC domain take less than 6hrs, 12 hrs, 12 hrs, and
836   30 hrs for maps 2c vs. 64zg, 5m vs. 6m, 6h vs. 8z and Corridor, respectively.

999    C.6   MADS4 VS . D ECENTRALIZED MADS4
1000
1001   When decisions are made at the current timestep, all decisions from the previous timestep will al-
1002   ready be finalized. As a result, the memory information of all agents is readily available for use.
1003
       This eliminates the need for any agent to wait for its peer to decide the current timestep. By utiliz-
       ing the memory information from the previous timestep, agents can make decisions without relying
1004
       on sequential dependencies during the current timestep. Since memory accumulates over multi-
1005
       ple timesteps, relying on the previous timestepвҖҷs information does not compromise performance,
1006   as demonstrated in Figure 11. This modification enables our algorithm to function effectively in
1007   decentralized policy settings without performance degradation.
