011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large multi-modal models inevitably decay over time as facts change and pre-
014            viously learned information becomes outdated. Traditional approaches such as
015            fine-tuning are often impractical for updating these models due to their size and
016            complexity. Instead, direct knowledge editing within the models presents a more
017            viable solution. Current model editing techniques, however, typically overlook
018
               the unique influence ranges of different facts, leading to compromised model
               performance in terms of both generality and locality. To address this issue, we
019
               introduce the concept of the generality-locality trade-off in multi-modal model
020
               editing. We develop a new model editing dataset named OKEDIT, specifically
021            designed to effectively evaluate this trade-off. Building on this foundation, we
022            propose BalancEdit, a novel method for balanced model editing that dynamically
023            achieves an optimal balance between generality and locality. BalancEdit utilizes a
024            unique mechanism that generates both positive and negative samples for each fact
025            to accurately determine its influence scope and incorporates these insights into the
026            model’s latent space using a discrete, localized codebook of edits, without modi-
027            fying the underlying model weights. To our knowledge, this is the first approach
028            explicitly addressing the generality-locality trade-off in multi-modal model editing.
029
               Our comprehensive results confirm the effectiveness of BalancEdit, demonstrating
               minimal trade-offs while maintaining robust editing capabilities. Our code and
030
               dataset will be available.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large multi-modal models (Zhu et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a;
037   Rombach et al., 2022) have recently brought about significant advancements in artificial intelligence,
038   demonstrating impressive results in tasks such as Visual Question Answering (VQA) (Antol et al.,
039
      2015). However, these models are susceptible to issues like hallucination (Rawte et al., 2023) and
      fact alteration (De Cao et al., 2021). After deployment, these models may generate numerous errors,
040
      leading to potential problems like the propagation of hate speech or the dissemination of outdated
041
      factual information. Given these challenges, it is critical to continually update and maintain these
042   large multi-modal models to ensure their accuracy and relevance.
043
044   While retraining or fine-tuning can update a model’s knowledge, it is often infeasible to frequently
045
      edit individual facts due to the high computational costs involved. Fortunately, model editing
      techniques (Hartvigsen et al., 2024; Mitchell et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023) provide a promising
046
      approach to implementing cost-effective, targeted updates to large, pre-trained models. These
047
      techniques typically involve injecting new layers or modifying weights to alter the knowledge
048   embedded in language models. A successful edit generally exhibits three characteristics (Mitchell
049   et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023): reliability, which ensures the output changes to the target answer
050   for the same question; locality, which leaves unrelated knowledge and outputs unchanged; and
051   generality, which produces the correct answer for all questions within the influence scope. As
052   illustrated in Fig. 1, each fact has its own influence scope. For instance, if we wish to edit the name
053   of a specific cat, the influence scope would be confined to that particular cat. If we aim to edit the
      name of a cat breed, the influence scope would extend to all cats within that breed. However, if we

066
      However, current model editing techniques often overlook the dynamic nature of the influence scope.
      Some methods treat all influence scopes as if they are large and uniform, while others focus solely on
067
      a specific edit. For instance, IKE (Zheng et al., 2023) employs in-context learning to edit knowledge,
068
      using the closest piece of knowledge as a prompt to guide the language model. This approach causes
069   the language model to rephrase the nearest fact, resulting in an oversized influence scope. Conversely,
070   GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2024), a lifelong model editing method, assumes each edit has a small
071   and similar influence range, leading to limited generality. Consider an example where we aim to edit
072   a “fact” that HP computers have been renamed Lenovo, as shown in Table 1. Ideally, model editing
073   should update the answer from HP to Lenovo whenever it encounters an image of a HP computer,
074   while leaving the answer unchanged for other brands. However, existing model editing techniques,
075   such as IKE (Zheng et al., 2023) and MEND (Mitchell et al., 2021), may achieve the target edit
076   but neglect the influence scope, inadvertently editing other brands as well. Even when presented
077
      with a black image, these models may still output the new answer, leading to hallucination. On the
      other hand, while GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2024) maintains the backbone model’s answer for
078
      unrelated images, it fails to edit the knowledge to the desired scope. These observations suggest that
079
      existing multi-modal model editing methods struggle to dynamically adjust the influence scope of a
080   knowledge edit, and to balance generality and locality effectively
081
082   To address this issue, we first create a dataset designed to evaluate the trade-off between generality
083
      and locality in model editing techniques. We then introduce an efficient multi-modal model editing
      method named BalancEdit, which dynamically balances this trade-off with minimal computational
084
      costs. Specifically, we incorporate an adapter into a chosen layer of a vision language model without
085
      altering its weights. This adaptor modifies layer-to-layer transformations for select inputs. By caching
086   embeddings for input errors and the updated knowledge transformation layer that decodes into the
087   desired model outputs, BalancEdit functions as a codebook where edits are stored. To strike a balance
088   between generality and locality, we generate the corresponding positive and negative samples for each
089   edit. The model’s semantic similarity in its latent space can be visualized as dynamic spheres around
090   cached edits, with the radius determined by the distance between positive and negative samples. By
091   adjusting the radius over time, BalancEdit allows for immediate edits, retains previous edits, and
092   preserves correct model behaviors, making it parameter-efficient. Furthermore, since BalancEdit’s
093   codebooks do not alter model weights and are fully model-agnostic, they also pave the way for
094
      plug-and-play, cost-effective model editing. This is particularly useful for making critical spot-fixes
      between larger retraining efforts.
095
096   Our contributions are as follows: 1) We first formulate the generality-locality trade-off in multi-modal
097   model editing and build a dataset named OKEDIT to empirically demonstrate it. 2) We introduce
098   BalancEdit, an efficient method for multi-modal model editing that dynamically and effectively
099   balances generality and locality without requiring training data beyond individual edits. 3) Our
100
      experiments reveal that BalancEdit outperforms baseline models and consistently achieves SOTA
      performance across a range of metrics.

148   up-to-date. However, updating the model’s behavior through retraining or fine-tuning is impractical
149   due to exorbitant training costs. In this context, multi-modal model editing techniques, which allow
150   for targeted edits, provide a feasible solution to this challenge.

191
192   As illustrated in Fig. 2, to satisfy the aforementioned properties, we propose BalancEdit, an efficient
193   model editing method for multi-modal models that dynamically determines the equilibrium between
194   generality and locality without compromising the original model. BalancEdit operates by wrapping a
195   selected layer of the pre-trained model with a BalancEdit module. This module consists of a codebook
196   and a mechanism that dynamically determines the radius of the influence scope.

198   design a discrete codebook at layer l which contains three components.

201             a specific question answer pair. Mathematically, it can be expressed as K = {k =

244   overall architecture of the model. The parameters that are tuned include all the weights within the

246   Influence Radius Determination. As shown in Fig. 1, each fact has its unique influence scope.
247   However, existing methods do not consider the dynamic influence scope during the editing process,
248   which results in an imbalanced generality-locality trade-off, as illustrated in Table 1. To address this
249   issue, BalancEdit incorporates a dynamic influence radius determination mechanism. As depicted in
250   Fig. 3, the knowledge of the fact is at the center of the influence scope. Ideally, the radius should
251   encompass the majority of generality samples, while excluding locality samples. Since similar
252   semantic sentences will result in close embeddings (Liu et al., 2023b; Menon & Vondrick, 2023), we
253   can use it to find an efficient way to approximate this process. Specifically, we construct positive
254
      and negative samples to dynamically estimate the influence scope without model training or external
      knowledge.
255
256   To construct a positive sample, we need to design a general rephrasing method that is highly similar
257   to the fact itself. We find that rephrasing the text will not affect the semantic information of the edited
258   knowledge. Therefore, we rephrase the text prompt t while keeping the image input i unchanged.
259   The positive sample can be formulated as (i, R(t)), where R(t) denotes the rephrased text prompts.
260
      The generation of a rephrased prompt is efficient, requires no additional data or training process, and
      can be generated directly by the backbone model.
261
262   On the other hand, the negative sample should be close to the border of locality samples to accurately
263   estimate the radius. Additionally, the generation process should be efficient and fact-agnostic. In
264   this case, we use a pure black image as the image input, which contains no semantic information on
265   the image side. The choice of black images as a proxy for out-of-scope knowledge is based on their
266
      characteristic as minimal or null visual signals. This makes them universally applicable negative
      samples across various visual recognition tasks. Furthermore, the generation of a negative sample is
267
      highly efficient, and can be applied to almost all knowledge editing tasks.
268

292
293   Datasets. Since there are few published vision language model editing datasets, we perform extensive
294   experiments on two such datasets in the vision question answering task (Antol et al., 2015): 1)
295   MMEDIT(Cheng et al., 2023), the first multi-modal model editing dataset based on the VQA-
296
      v2(Goyal et al., 2017) dataset, which includes 2093 testing samples; However, this dataset has its

297
      deviate from the original images, leading to inconsistencies and potentially less accurate evaluations.
298
      2) We introduce a new dataset, OKEDIT, based on the OKVQA dataset (Marino et al., 2019), which
299   includes 5046 testing samples, encompassing over 20 unique categories such as vehicles, people,
300   plants, animals, geography, history, language, brands, science and technology. Unlike MMEDIT,
301   OKEDIT enhances the quality of the rephrased images and adjusts the difficulty of the locality
302   samples to evaluate the trade-off between generality and locality. Detailed information about the
303   datasets is provided in Appendix A.
304
      Backbone Models. Following previous work (Zheng et al., 2023), we adopt two vision language
305   models as the base models. MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) is a powerful vision language model,
306   leveraging Vicunna (Chiang et al., 2023) as the language model and a Vit-G/14 from EVA-CLIP (Sun
307   et al., 2023) and a Q-former as the image encoder. BLIP-2 OPT (Li et al., 2023) utilizes a lightweight
308   Q-former to bridge the gap between vision modality and text modality, where the ViT-L is adopted in
309   the vision block, and the unsupervised-trained OPT model (Zhang et al., 2022) is used for decoder-
310   based LLM. Metrics Following previous work (Zheng et al., 2023), we adopt the Editing Success
311   Accuracy (Acc); Text Generality (T-Gen); Image Generality (I-Gen); and Locality (Loc) as the main
312   metrics. To quantify the trade-off between generality and locality, we introduce the harmonic mean
313
      (HM) of the T-Gen, I-Gen and Loc. The detailed informations are in Appendix C.

344   4.3   C OMPARISONS TO E XISTING M ETHODS
345
346   Table 3 presents the main results of our BalancEdit and other baseline methods on the VQA task.
347   We observe that our BalancEdit significantly outperforms the existing editing methods without
348
      requiring additional training data. Specifically, we examine both the accuracy and the trade-off
      between generality and locality. First, in terms of editing success accuracy, BalancEdit achieves
349
      the highest performance, resulting in 100% editing success across all datasets and backbones. In
350
      contrast, baseline models do not consistently reach this level of performance. This demonstrates that
351   our BalancEdit satisfies the Reliability Property.
352
353   For the Generality metric, BalancEdit achieves the best text generality performance compared to
354
      other methods. For instance, BalancEdit shows a 70% improvement in text generality accuracy over
      the GRACE method. Additionally, it reaches comparable performance in image generality. Since
355
      the MMEDIT dataset is relatively simple, the performance is very similar across all model editing
356
      methods, converging around 99%. However, in the challenging OKEDIT dataset, where we focus
357   on balancing trade-off performance, we must compromise on the more difficult aspects of image
358   generality. The high generality performance underscores the Generality Property of our method.
359
360
      For locality performance, BalancEdit consistently achieves the best results, with the exception of the
      GRACE method, which primarily focuses on specific local edits. Specifically, BalancEdit shows
361
      an improvement in locality 20% to 80% compared to other baseline methods. For example, on the

516   5    L IMITATIONS
517
518   Multi-modal model editing is a novel and challenging field, with the balance between generality
519   and locality remaining largely underexplored. It is evident that employing similarity search across
520   a model’s layers inevitably slows down inference times (Hartvigsen et al., 2024), despite reducing
521
      the need for extensive training. Thus, accelerating inference time represents a crucial area for future
      improvements.
522
523   Another significant limitation is the memory-augmented approach’s handling of multi-hop model
524   editing. The key-based similarity search struggles to capture multi-hop queries that depend on newly
525   introduced knowledge, often due to the ambiguity of real-world facts. For example, if the CEO of X
526   (formerly Twitter) were to change to Elon Musk, it would be difficult to update the response to the
527
      question, ‘Which social app is headed by the leader of SpaceX?’ A potential solution to this problem
      could involve dynamically defining the fine-grained influence scope, which would allow for more
528
      precise adjustments to changes in real-world facts and their implications for multi-hop questions.

531   6    C ONCLUSION
532
533   In conclusion, we identified the limitation of existing imbanlanced generality and locality in model
534   editing. Specifically, we formulated the generality-locality trade-off, and developed a specialized
535   dataset, OKEDIT, to empirically explore this phenomenon. In addition, we introduced BalancEdit, an
536
      innovative approach for multi-modal model editing that efficiently balances the generality and locality
      of edits. Our method reduces the need for extensive retraining or fine-tuning, relying solely on the
537
      data provided by individual edits. The experimental results demonstrate that BalancEdit significantly
538
      outperforms existing baseline models, consistently achieving state-of-the-art performance across
539   various metrics.

803   Lemma: Embeddings of semantically similar concepts are close in the embedding space.
804
805   Proof. 1. Definition of Embeddings: Embeddings are vector representations of concepts in a high-
806
      dimensional space. Formally, let f : C → Rd be an embedding function that maps a concept c ∈ C
      to a vector f (c) ∈ Rd .
807
808   2. Semantic Similarity: Semantic similarity between two concepts c1 and c2 can be quantified using a
809   similarity measure S(c1 , c2 ). Common choices include cosine similarity, Euclidean distance, or dot
      product.

826
827   Finetune In this method, we carry out a fine-tuning process on a selected layer of the pretrained
828
      model using Adam optimization for a fair comparison, while keeping all other layers fixed. For the
      training loss, the Cross Entropy loss is used for fine-tuning.
829
830
      IKE (Zheng et al., 2023) IKE (In-Context Knowledge Editing introduces a system that utilises an
831
      unsupervised retriever. This retriever uses cosine similarity to pinpoint pertinent demonstrations from
832
      the training set. This method is grounded in the principles set forth by (Liu et al., 2022) and aims to
833   insert new factual knowledge into language models in a non-disruptive fashion, eliminating the need
834   for direct parameter updates. IKE’s approach ranks demonstrations according to their resemblance
835   to the editing target and organizes them in sequence to form a supplementary knowledge base
836   that steers the model’s generation process. This technique not only conserves the model’s existing
837   knowledge base but also presents a scalable and efficient method to refresh factual information. It
838   shows considerable promise in mitigating unintended side effects, such as over-editing and knowledge
839   forgetting, typically linked with gradient-based editing methods. However, it is also designed for pure
840   text models for retrievel, to make it adapt to vision language models, we used composed embedding
841
      as the augmented database, such that it can retrieve the image information as well.
842
843
      MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a) MEND employs a hypernetwork to predict new weights for a
      selected layer of a pre-trained model by estimating the low-rank decomposition of the weight matrix
844
      of the layer. The hypernetwork is trained on a set of training edits, which comprises a new edit, a
845
      set of inputs that are semantically equivalent to the edit, and samples from the model’s pre-training
846   data. However, MEND is designed for the language model, to fit it to the vision language model, we
847   keep the vision encoder fixed and only choose the language model layer for finetuning. In addition,
848   in our situation, we only have single edits that are streaming in, we train the hypernetwork to predict
849   updated weights as edits stream in using continuous fine-tuning.
850
851   GRACE (Meng et al., 2022a) GRACE is a lifelong model editing method for large language
852   models. It handles sequential edits with a discrete key-value codebook. GRACE replace one layer
853   to a GRACE adaptor which stores the key-value pair of the target edits, where the key is the last
854   embedding of the key for the text prompt and value is trained by backpropagation with the target
855   results. Keep handling the key conflicts could make it successfully deal with the multiple sequential
856
      editing in language models. However, to adapt it to the vision language model, we select the language
      part as the edited layer and prepend the image embedding before the text prompt so that it can be
857
      regarded as long text questions.

862   Training Specifications We use the Adam optimizer (?) for all methods. Given that edits in
863   our setup are single and sequential, the batch size is consistently 1. We trained all methods using
      a variety of GPUs, including 24GB NVIDIA RTX A5000s, 40GB NVIDIA A100s, and 80GB

      NVIDIA A100s. Timing experiments are reported from experiments performed on an NVIDIA RTX
865   A100 GPU. The scale of BalancEdit is not dependent on the model’s scale, but the model’s scale is
866   dependent on the available computational resources. To avoid sharding, we utilize models that can be
867   accommodated on a single GPU, although the principles of BalancEdit are applicable beyond this
868   setup. For Adaptor-based editors, such as GRACE, we employ 100 iterations of gradient descent per
869   input.

871   Hyperparameters In our comparisons of Finetuning, MEND and GRACE, we explore learning
872   rates of 1.0, 1e−1 , 1e−2 , 1e−3 , 1e−4 , and 1e−5 . We observe that Finetuning, Memory, and MEND
