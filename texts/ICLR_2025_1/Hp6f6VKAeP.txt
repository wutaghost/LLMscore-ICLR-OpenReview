009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                This work aims to generate long-duration audio while preserving acoustic co-
012                herence, utilizing existing text-conditional audio generation models through
013                diffusion-based approaches. Current diffusion models, however, encounter signif-
014                icant challenges in generating long audio sequences due to memory constraints,
015                as output size scales with input length. While one possible solution is to con-
016                catenate short clips, this often leads to inconsistencies due to a lack of shared
017                temporal information across segments. To address these challenges, we propose
018
                   InfiniteAudio, a novel inference technique designed to generate long audio with
                   consistent acoustic attributes. Our method is based on three key components.
019
                   First, we implement a curved denoising approach with a fixed-size input, enabling
020
                   theoretically infinite audio generation while maintaining a constant memory foot-
021                print. Second, we introduce conditional guidance alternation, a mechanism that
022                enhances intelligibility in long speech generation. Finally, initial self-attention
023                features are shared across future frames to maintain temporal coherence. The ef-
024                fectiveness of InfiniteAudio is demonstrated through comprehensive comparisons
025                with existing text-to-audio generation baselines. Generated audio samples are
026                available on our anonymous project page1 .

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) have received considerable attention across
032   various domains due to their ability to generate high-quality, diverse outputs. They have demon-
033   strated impressive results in tasks including image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rombach
034
      et al., 2022), video generation (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), and text-to-
      audio (TTA) generation (Liu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). TTA
035
      models generate audio from text description prompts and typically utilize generative frameworks
036
      such as latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022) as illustrated in Fig. 1(a) or flow matching
037   models (Vyas et al., 2023). Recently, VoiceLDM (Lee et al., 2024) has advanced this capability by
038   generating both speech and background audio simultaneously, as shown in Fig. 1(b). The generated
039   speech not only reflects the background description prompt but also adapts to the content prompt.
040   For example, when prompted with ”Hello” in a cathedral setting, the speech will naturally include
041   reverberation to match the environment.
042
      Despite these advancements, existing TTA generation models based on diffusion approaches face
043   significant challenges when generating longer audio sequences. To extend the output size during
044   inference, the input size must also be increased, given that diffusion models require the input and
045   output dimensions to remain unchanged. Moreover, these models struggle to manage long text
046   conditions when producing extended speech. While long audio can be generated by concatenating
047   short clips created by existing TTA models, ensuring a smooth and continuous audio stream remains
048   challenging due to the lack of temporal consistency between inter-clip segments.
049   To address these challenges, we introduce InfiniteAudio, a novel inference method for generating
050   long and consistent audio. InfiniteAudio generates extended audio by utilizing a fixed input size
051   with progressively increasing noise levels over time. As shown in Fig. 2, at each inference step, the
052   fully denoised audio segment at the beginning of the input is removed, while a new random noise

      Additionally, InfiniteAudio addresses the challenge of generating long audio sequences from ex-
084
      tended text inputs by segmenting the text and applying a guidance alternation technique. By dividing
085
      long text prompts into smaller segments, we reduce memory overhead. However, when processing
086   consecutive prompts, the generated audio can be affected by preceding segments, leading to reduced
087   intelligibility. To resolve this, we propose a guidance alternation strategy that switches between con-
088   ditional and unconditional guidance when processing following text inputs. This approach preserves
089   the clarity of long speech while minimizing interference between segments.
090

091
      prompts within a single clip can disrupt coherence, as the prompts often lack the specificity required
092   to retain consistent speaker characteristics. To mitigate this issue, we share a query, key, and value
093   (QKV) features within the self-attention layers of the diffusion model. Propagating the initial QKV
094   features across successive segments ensures uniform speaker attributes and continuity, preserving
095   vocal consistency and maintaining intelligibility of both background audio and speech, even with
096   varying text inputs. Our experiments demonstrate that InfiniteAudio can generate extremely long
097   and coherent audio without any degradation in quality over time. Further details are provided in
098   Sec 4.

105

132   2.1       T EXT TO AUDIO AND S PEECH G ENERATION
133
134   TTA generation (Liu et al., 2023; Kreuk et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023) has attracted considerable
135   attention in recent years, driven by advancements in generative modeling techniques (Ho et al.,
136
      2020; Song et al., 2020b). Several works (Liu et al., 2023; Ghosal et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023)
      use the latent diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) to generate audio, mitigating the
137
      large computational costs of the original diffusion process. In the diffusion-based TTA models,
138
      contrastive language audio pretraining (CLAP) (Wu et al., 2023) is utilized in many models (Liu
139   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024), in order to align language and audio embeddings.
140   Additionally, large language models (LLMs) are exploited due to their strong text understanding
141   capabilities (Ghosal et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024).
142
      Besides TTA, text-to-speech (TTS) generation is also an active area of research, with early models
143
      using autoregressive (AR) models (Wang et al., 2017; Oord et al., 2016). To address the issue of
144
      slow inference speed that arises in AR models, researchers have introduced non-AR models (Ren
145   et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2020) that offer improved performance compared to AR models. Using the
146   diffusion model, Grad-TTS (Popov et al., 2021) produces high-quality speech with a score-based
147   decoder. Furthermore, several works have addressed environment-related speech generation. For
148   example, VoiceLDM (Lee et al., 2024) introduces an efficient model that generates audio closely
149   aligned with both descriptive and content prompts. Audiobox (Vyas et al., 2023), a unified model
150   based on flow matching, can produce audio that contains various audio conditions, such as non-
151   verbal sounds (e.g., coughing, screaming) or acoustic environments (e.g., rural, stadium, indoor).
152   While these environment-related speech generation models produce high-quality results, they strug-
153
      gle to generate long outputs that containing multiple sentences simultaneously.
154
155   2.2       L ONG G ENERATION U SING D IFFUSION M ODELS
156
157   Producing large-scale output with diffusion models is challenging due to the high computational
158   costs and memory footprints. For image generation, Multi-Diffusion (Bar-Tal et al., 2023) and
159   SyncDiffusion (Lee et al., 2023) use several windows to generate images with arbitrary aspect ra-
160   tios but focus on smoothing the overlap regions of windows, falling short of solving the repetition
161   problem. Scalecrafter (He et al., 2023) dynamically increases the receptive field and succeeds in
      generating ultra-high-resolution images up to 4096 × 4096.

162
      In addition to image generation, research into long video generation has become increasingly active.
163   Many AR models (He et al., 2022; Voleti et al., 2022; Harvey et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) can
164   generate long videos, but due to error accumulation and a lack of temporal consistency between the
165   frames, there are quality issues. FreeNoise (Qiu et al., 2023) addresses this issue with a window-
166   based function but cannot generate infinitely long videos as it requires memory proportional to the
167   output length. FIFO-Diffusion (Kim et al., 2024) can produce infinitely long videos with a fixed
168   amount of memory by conducting diagonal diffusion across different timesteps.
169   In the audio domain, residual vector quantization (RVQ) is widely used to generate audio faster and
170   more efficiently (Défossez et al., 2022; Zeghidour et al., 2021). SoundStorm (Borsos et al., 2023b),
171   which combines RVQ with AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023a), efficiently generates audio sequences
172   up to 30 seconds long, a relatively extended length. To generate longer audio, (Evans et al., 2024a)
173   tackles this issue by leveraging LDMs, resulting in output up to 95 seconds long. Moreover, (Evans
174   et al., 2024b) leverage the diffusion transformer(DiT) to generate even longer audio, stretching up
175   to 4m 45s. However, these approaches (Evans et al., 2024a;b) require additional training on datasets
176   that match the desired output length. Our method does not require any additional training to generate
177
      audio of theoretically infinite length.

      4.1   C URVED D ENOISING WITH R EDUCED S AMPLING S TEPS
221
222
      Because diffusion models are trained to estimate the noise in the input, both the input and output
223
      must have the same size. This inherent property increases memory demands, as generating longer
224   audio requires longer inputs. For instance, on a single GPU with 12GB of memory, AudioLDM is
225   limited to generating audio no longer than 22.5 seconds.
226
227
      To overcome memory limitations, we propose InfiniteAudio, which operates with a fixed input
      size but can theoretically generate infinite-length audio. Inspired by FIFO-Diffusion (Kim et al.,
228
      2024), which addresses text-to-video generation, we initiate the diffusion inference process with a
229
      fixed-length audio segment using a small portion of the output predicted by existing TTA models.
230   Although mel-spectrograms, which consist of time and frequency axes, can be treated as images,
231   audio generation tasks must handle temporal information, similar to video generation. Therefore,
                                          T   F
232   we treat the input latent zτ ∈ RC× r × r as containing Tr audio frames, analogous to video frames.
233                                                                       F
      Each encoded mel-spectrogram frame corresponds to zi1 ∈ RC×1× r , where i ∈ [1, Tr ].
234
235   For infinite audio generation, noise is progressively added to the input audio frames over time, ex-
236   cept for the initial frames, which act as a ’buffer zone’ with no added noise. Since no further training
237   occurs in InfiniteAudio, using different diffusion timesteps during inference can produce a perfor-
238
      mance gap. The buffer zone mitigates this by applying the same timesteps as during training, helping
      to reduce the performance gap. Beyond the buffer zone, the latent frames gradually transition: the
239
      earlier frames are almost fully predicted, whereas the Tr -th frame is treated as Gaussian noise. The
240
      input to the inference stage consists of the initial buffer frames and Tr frames with varying noise
241
      levels. As represented in Fig. 2 (b), after each inference step, the first frame following the buffer
242
      zone reaches diffusion timestep τ = 1 and is then removed. To maintain a total of Tr frames, we
243
      insert a new noisy frame at the Tr -th position. By repeating this process iteratively, we can generate
244
      N frames in N inference steps. To effectively tackle memory limitation issues, InfiniteAudio keeps
245
      the input size constant during inference, regardless of the desired output length. However, employ-
246   ing the full set of diffusion timesteps still necessitates long input sequences. To mitigate this issue,
247   InfiniteAudio reduces input size by selecting only the most critical diffusion steps. By leveraging
248   deterministic denoising (Song et al., 2020a), existing models perform inference without requiring all
249   timesteps. Additionally, we found that we can further reduce the number of timesteps by skipping
250   unimportant steps while still preserving sample quality.

290   content prompt ccont into smaller, manageable sentence segments: ccont1 , ccont2 , ..., ccontk . Each

352   Datasets. To evaluate our method on TTA generation, we randomly selected 500 audio-text pairs
353   from the 975 test files in the Audiocaps dataset (Kim et al., 2019), which is commonly used for eval-
354   uating existing TTA models. For text-to-long-speech (TTLS) generation, we constructed a test set
355   from the English subset of the CommonVoice 13.0 corpus (Ardila et al., 2019), randomly selecting
356   60 text samples, each consisting of more than five sentences, to assess long-form speech generation.
357   For TTAS, which involves generating both audio and speech, we used 60 randomly selected text
358
      sets from the CommonVoice 13.0 corpus (Ardila et al., 2019). The test set for audio description was
      sourced from the Audiocaps test set (Kim et al., 2019), specifically focusing on samples from the
359
      ”speech” category, such as those involving ”talking” or ”speaking.”

      Baselines. For comparison, we evaluate the performance of InfiniteAudio against two publicly
      available TTA models: AudioLDM2 and VoiceLDM3 . Notably, VoiceLDM is currently the only
363
      available model capable of generating both audio and speech simultaneously, making it the sole
364

367
      Evaluation Metrics. We employ several quantitative metrics to evaluate the audio quality and the
      alignment between the input text prompt and the generated audio. These metrics include Frechet
368
      Distance (FD), Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, and the CLAP score, which are standard in text-
369
      to-audio generation evaluations (Liu et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024; Vyas et al., 2023). FD and KL
370   divergence quantify how closely the generated audio matches the ground truth, with lower values
371   indicating better performance. The CLAP score, in contrast, assesses the relevance between the text
372   prompts and the generated audio, where higher values are preferable. For subjective evaluation of
373   the audio produced by TTA models, we use two metrics: (i) overall quality (OVL) and (ii) relevance
374   to the input text description (REL). Both were rated on a scale of 1 to 5 by 20 domain experts, based
375   on 30 speech samples. Further details on the human evaluation process are available in App. C.3.

530   6       C ONCLUSION
531
532   We introduce InfiniteAudio, a novel inference method designed to generate infinitely long, consistent
533   audio using pretrained text-to-audio models. InfiniteAudio effectively maintains a fixed memory
534   footprint, addressing the memory limitations of existing models. Additionally, we propose a new
535   guidance alternation method that can produce long speech with high intelligibility. By sharing QKV
536   pairs in the self-attention layers, InfiniteAudio ensures consistent speech generation and mitigates
537
      issues such as voice variations. These contributions open up possibilities for long text-to-audio
      generation and pave the way for continuous, coherent long audio content.

       while VoiceLDM is tested exclusively on the AudioCaps dataset.

983
       InfiniteAudio: We utilize the Audiocaps test set for text-to-audio generation, which comprises
984
       audio files paired with corresponding caption texts. Each audio file is accompanied by several text
985
       captions, from which we randomly select 860 audio-text pairs for evaluation.
986
987    For text-to-speech (TTS) evaluation, we employ the CommonVoice 13.0K test set. Unlike traditional
988    TTS evaluations, our focus is on generating longer speech segments. Therefore, we specifically
989
       target sentences exceeding 90 characters in length. For each text input, we utilize more than four
       selected sentences, resulting in a total of 60 text pairs and approximately 300 sentences for testing.
990
991    Both audio and speech generation evaluations leverage the aforementioned datasets. We include
992    captions categorized as ”speech” from the Audiocaps test set as prompts for audio descriptions and
993    randomly select 60 long speech pairs from the CommonVoice 13.0K test set for content prompts.

995    C.2   C ONFIGURATION
996
997
       We conducted experiments using InfiniteAudio alongside existing text-to-audio generation models,
       AudioLDM and VoiceLDM. Both models are based on Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) utilizing a
998
       U-Net architecture. We increased the number of inference steps to optimize performance, deviating
999
       from the default settings of the original models, and employed DDIM sampling.
1000
1001   For AudioLDM, we set the inference steps to 300, aligning with the original model but omitting the
1002   initial and middle regions, except for multiples of 4, while retaining the final steps, which introduce
1003

       from the original 50 to 200, skipping the middle and final regions except for multiples of 5, while
1004
       including the initial timesteps to introduce Gaussian-like noise.
1005
1006   In text-to-long speech generation (TTLS), we segment long content prompts at the sentence level.
1007   For QKV sharing, we apply the sharing mechanism every 200 audio frames. However, at the start
1008   of each new sentence, the sharing process is reset, beginning again with the first 200 frames.

1136   We briefly review key papers on long-form generation using pretrained diffusion models, focusing
1137   specifically on methods that do not involve additional training in other generative domains.
1138
1139   D.4.1      M ULTI D IFFUSION FOR TTI
1140
1141
       MultiDiffusion6 is a robust framework for text-to-image generation that leverages a pre-trained dif-
       fusion model without requiring additional training or fine-tuning. At its core, MultiDiffusion merges
1142
       multiple independent diffusion processes through an optimization algorithm, reconciling them into a
1143
       coherent and high-quality image. This approach enables user-controllable image generation, making
1144   it highly adaptable for a wide range of tasks.
1145
1146   The primary innovation of MultiDiffusion lies in its ability to simultaneously process multiple image
1147
       regions, adhering to user-defined constraints such as text prompts, aspect ratios, and spatial layout
       signals (e.g., segmentation masks or bounding boxes). However, a key limitation of the framework
1148
       is its inability to incorporate temporal information, restricting its utility to image generation. This
1149
       makes it unsuitable for video generation, where maintaining temporal coherence across frames is
1150   essential.
1151
1152   Despite this limitation, the underlying optimization process ensures that all image regions conform
1153
       closely to the reference diffusion model, preserving both high image quality and visual consistency.
       While not applicable to temporal tasks, MultiDiffusion remains a flexible and efficient solution for
1154
       generating complex images that meet diverse spatial constraints.
1155
1156
       D.4.2      F REE N OISE FOR TTV
1157
1158   FreeNoise7 is a framework designed to extend the capabilities of text-to-video diffusion models
1159   for generating longer, temporally coherent videos. Traditional text-to-video models are typically
1160   trained on a limited number of frames, restricting their ability to generate high-fidelity long videos
1161   during inference. FreeNoise addresses this limitation by introducing a tuning-free paradigm that
1162   dynamically reschedules noise over time to maintain consistency across frames.
1163
1164
       Key Innovations
1165
                • Noise Rescheduling: Unlike traditional methods that initialize noise uniformly for all
1166              frames, FreeNoise dynamically adjusts the noise distribution during video generation. This
1167              method captures long-range temporal correlations, ensuring that visual consistency is pre-
1168              served across extended video sequences.
1169
                • Temporal Attention Mechanism: FreeNoise incorporates a window-based temporal at-
1170              tention mechanism, which helps maintain coherence over longer time frames. By focusing
1171              attention over localized windows, the model can efficiently capture and retain relevant tem-
1172              poral dependencies.
1173
                • Motion Injection for Multi-Prompt Videos: The framework supports multi-prompt video
1174              generation by enabling dynamic changes in video content based on evolving text prompts.
1175              This allows FreeNoise to generate videos where different segments adhere to different
1176              prompts, accommodating more complex narrative transitions over time.
1177
1178   Limitations Despite its strengths, FreeNoise has certain limitations. Since the framework does
1179   not involve any fine-tuning of the pre-trained models, it might not be optimally adapted to domain-
1180   specific datasets, potentially leading to suboptimal performance in specialized contexts. Moreover,
1181   while it addresses temporal consistency, the model’s reliance on pre-trained diffusion models can
1182   limit its ability to handle diverse or complex motion dynamics inherent in specific generative tasks.
