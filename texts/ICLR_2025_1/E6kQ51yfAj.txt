009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Alignment of large language models is a critical process designed to ensure that
012            the modelвҖҷs responses to user prompts accurately reflect human intentions and
013            adhere to societal values. The standard Reinforcement Learning from Human
014            Feedback (RLHF) framework primarily focuses on optimizing the performance of
015            large language models using pre-collected prompts. However, collecting prompts
016            that provide comprehensive coverage is both tedious and challenging, and often
017            fails to include scenarios that LLMs need to improve on the most. In this paper,
018            we investigate alignment through the lens of two-agent games, involving iterative
019
               interactions between an adversarial and a defensive agent. The adversarial agentвҖҷs
               task at each step is to generate prompts that expose the weakness of the defen-
020
               sive agent. In return, the defensive agent seeks to improve its responses to these
021
               newly identified prompts it вҖңstruggledвҖқ with, based on feedback from the reward
022            model. We theoretically demonstrate that this iterative reinforcement learning op-
023            timization converges to a Nash Equilibrium for the game induced by the agents.
024            Experimental results in safety scenarios demonstrate that learning in such a com-
025            petitive environment not only fully trains agents but also leads to policies with
026            enhanced generalization capabilities for both adversarial and defensive agents.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT Ouyang et al. (2022), Claude Anthropic (2024),
032   and others, have achieved great success due to their remarkable generalization and versatility. One
033   crucial component of LLM development is alignment Ouyang et al. (2022); Bender et al. (2021);
034   Bommasani et al. (2021), which ensures LLMs can follow instructions, understand human inten-
035   tion, and align with social values. Performing the alignment of LLMs requires the preparation of a
036   set of prompts. The traditional alignment method optimizes the modelвҖҷs response on pre-collected
037   prompts, which are mostly contributed by human labelers and could fail to cover all task types.
038   Later, several methods have been proposed to expand the scope of prompts used, including based on
039
      difficulty Xu et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023), paraphrase Yu et al. (2023b), and self-instruct Wang
      et al. (2022). Nonetheless, these methods are often rule-based and do not customize or adapt their
040
      design to the capabilities of aligning LLMs, i.e., identify prompts that the aligning LLM struggles
041
      at responding to. Furthermore, using a static prompt dataset may lead to saturation of LLM perfor-
042   mance due to the loss of discernment by the reward model. Therefore, a more dynamic and adaptive
043   approach is necessary for LLM alignment to improve its generalization.
044
045
      To address these limitations, we introduce a novel framework inspired by the tutor-student model
      of human learning, conceptualizing the alignment process as a two-player game. In this framework,
046
      an adversarial agent (adversarial LLM) and a defensive agent (defensive LLM) engage in iterative
047
      interactions to enhance both their performances. Specifically, the adversarial LLM, acting as the
048   tutor, learns to automatically generate prompts that challenge and reveal the weaknesses of the de-
049   fensive LLM. Meanwhile, the defensive LLM, functioning as the student, is tasked with adapting
050   and improving its responses to these adversarially generated prompts. Our framework is grounded
051   in research on learning in competitive multi-agent environments Bansal et al. (2017); Lowe et al.
052   (2017). This approach fosters a natural curriculum of increasing complexity, allowing both agents
053   to develop progressive behaviors that surpass the inherent complexity of their training environment.
      Figure 1 illustrates our proposed framework using two players.

075

076
      equilibrium between the adversarial and defensive agents. This equilibrium signifies a state where
077
      neither agent can unilaterally improve their strategy, implying a more comprehensive training pro-
078   cess that leads to better coverage of prompts for alignment. Our experiments, conducted in scenarios
079   involving harmful inputs and jailbreak settings, validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
080   The results show that our approach not only enhances the generalization capabilities of the agents
081   but also ensures that both parties in the interaction are thoroughly trained. As a by-product, in ad-
082   dition to creating a generalizable and well-aligned defensive LLM, our adversarial agent also serves
083   as an adaptive red teaming partner, continuously generating challenging prompts to enhance the
084   alignment of the defensive LLM.

132   3     G AME - THEORETICAL P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION (GPO)
133
134   Inspired by the tutor-student model of human learning, we aim to create a dynamic learning environ-
135   ment for LLMs, featuring iterative interactions between an adversarial and a defensive agent. The
136   adversarial agent, serving as the tutor, evaluates past errors and current performance of the defen-
137   sive agent to dynamically identify and exploit potential weaknesses. In turn, the defensive agent,
138   functioning as the student, continuously adapts and strengthens these identified vulnerabilities. This
139   iterative cycle is repeated to consistently improve performance.

234

478
479
      LLM Alignment. Despite the impressive capabilities of Large Language LLMs), they are suscep-
480   tible to unintended behaviors like fabricating facts and generating biased or harmful content. RLHF
481   presents a straightforward method to address these issues. In RLHF, an agent utilizes reinforcement
482   learning to maximize guidance signals from a reward model acting as a human proxy. Subsequent
483   works have also been proposed to further enhance LLM performance by addressing reward hacking
484   issues Coste et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024a) and enabling self-correcting abilities Kumar et al.
485   (2024), etc. In addition to employing RL, recently introduced preference learning techniques oper-
      ate independently of RL, such as RSO Liu et al. (2024a), RRHF Yuan et al. (2023), and RAFT Dong


                                                                                                                                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      et al. (2023), DPO Rafailov et al. (2023), SLiC-HF Zhao et al. (2023), and IPO Azar et al. (2023)
487   etc. However, all of these methods concentrate on enhancing the performance of LLMs on the pre-
488   collected prompts, without inspecting the construction of the prompt sets. Collecting prompts that
489   offer comprehensive coverage is a laborious and challenging task that frequently overlooks crucial
490   scenarios where LLMs require the most improvement.
491
      Self-play in RLHF. In recent research, there has been an emergence of studies exploring two-player
492
      adversarial setups to align LLMs. To tackle the issue of human preference variation, recent studies
493   Wu et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b) suggest maximizing the likelihood of the generated response
494   being preferred over its opponent, instead of relying on a fixed preference dictated by a reward
495   model. In essence, this approach involves both players optimizing towards pre-selected prompts
496   while competing with each other by generating superior responses. Studies have also explored a two-
497   player game involving an aligned LLM and a reward model Liu et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2024a);
498   Cheng et al. (2024b) to tackle reward hacking issues. In this setup, the aligned model strategically
499   selects the most conservative reward from the reward model. Additionally, Kirchner et al. (2024)
500   have examined the Prover-Verifier Game to produce accurate yet easily understandable solutions
501
      for mathematical problems. However, all these studies concentrate on enhancing response quality
      based on pre-collected prompts. Recognizing the pivotal role of high-quality and diverse prompts
502
      in optimizing robust and versatile LLM performance, particularly within out-of-distribution (OOD)
503
      scenarios, our research delves into the interplay between prompt generation and aligned LLM. As far
504   as we know, our work is the first to investigate two player game from this perspective. Furthermore,
505   the game we investigate faces specific challenges. Notably, we found that maintaining an effective
506   yet diverse distribution of the adversary, as explained in Sections 3 and 4, is key to success.
507
      Cheng et al. (2024a) have also explored the self-play setting, primarily investigating whether en-
508
      gaging in an adversarial language games (e.g., Adversarial Taboo) can enhance general reasoning
509
      abilities. This is fundamentally distinct from the alignment algorithm that is the focus of our paper.
510
511   Safety Alignment. Ensuring the safety and alignment with ethical norms of language models is a
512   crucial part of the language model alignment Hendrycks et al. (2020); Schramowski et al. (2022). A
513
      commonly adopted safety alignment framework involves iterative red teaming and model hardening
      Dinan et al. (2019); Bai et al. (2022b). Automated red teaming methods typically require human
514
      involvement or learn how to automatically generate adversarial prompts through techniques such as
515
      prompting, SFT, and RL Perez et al. (2022); Ganguli et al. (2022); Hong et al. (2024); Samvelyan
516   et al. (2024). With the assistance of red team LMs, model safety can be enhanced using methods
517   such as SFT and RLHF Ouyang et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2022a). However, previous red team LMs
518   were primarily designed to attack static models, and MART iteratively conducts red teaming and
519   safety enhancements but relies on supervised fine-tuning, which makes it difficult to balance the
520   capabilities of attackers and defendersGe et al. (2023). Our work incorporates red team attacks and
521   safety alignment into a framework of two-player gaming, ensuring that the optimizations of both
522   agents ultimately reach a Nash equilibrium.

525   6   C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527
528   In this work, we introduced a novel framework for aligning LLMs by conceptualizing the process
529   as a two-player game between an adversarial agent and a defensive agent. Through iterative in-
530   teractions, the adversarial agent learns to generate diverse and challenging prompts to uncover the
531
      weaknesses of the defensive LLM, while the defensive LLM adapts and improves its responses. By
      incorporating diversity constraints and demonstrating convergence to a Nash equilibrium, our ap-
532
      proach enhances the generalization capabilities of both agents and ensures thorough training. Our
533
      experiments validate the effectiveness of the proposed method in scenarios involving harmful inputs
534   and jailbreak settings. Our solution does require training two separate LLM agents, and this work
535   primarily focused on prototyping our idea using safety-related tasks. In the future, we aim to ex-
536   tend the scope of our alignment framework to address the challenges that arise in other domains.
537   Specifically, we hope to investigate the application of our approach in helpfulness and mathemat-
538   ical reasoning related tasks, where LLMs are required to provide accurate and useful responses.
539   Additionally, we intend to explore synergies between our two-player game framework and other
      established alignment methods, e.g. DPO.
