009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Real-world vision-language applications demand varying levels of perceptual
012            granularity. However, most existing visual large language models (VLLMs), such
013            as LLaVA, pre-assume a fixed resolution for downstream tasks, which leads to
014            subpar performance. To address this problem, we first conduct a comprehensive
015            and pioneering investigation into the resolution preferences of different vision-
016
               language tasks, revealing a correlation between resolution preferences with ❶ im-
               age complexity, and ❷ uncertainty variance of the VLLM at different image input
017
               resolutions. Building on this insight, we propose an empirical formula to deter-
018
               mine the optimal resolution for a given vision-language task, accounting for these
019            two factors as the zeroth-order and first-order terms in the Taylor expansion on a
020            given image input. Second, based on rigorous experiments, we propose a novel
021            parameter-efficient fine-tuning technique to extend the visual input resolution of
022            pre-trained VLLMs to the identified optimal resolution. Extensive experiments on
023            various vision-language tasks validate the effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Visual Large Language Models (VLLMs) represent a powerful class of models capable of handling
028
      vision-language tasks (Yin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024; Alayrac et al., 2022). There is
029
      a growing body of research focused on the application of VLLMs in real-world scenarios, where
030   different tasks necessitate varying levels of perceptual granularity. For instance, autonomous driving
031   systems require high resolution to capture multiple objects and intricate details (Zhou et al., 2023;
032   Ding et al., 2023), whereas image classification tasks involving singular, simple objects can be
033   effectively performed at lower resolutions (Li et al., 2024a; 2023d; Zhang et al., 2024). Despite this,
034   most existing VLLMs, e.g., LLaVA, pre-assume a fixed resolution for downstream tasks, which leads
035   to sub-optimal performance (Liu et al., 2023b;a; Li et al., 2023b). A direct “exhaustive training”
036   strategy to adapt current VLLMs for diverse vision-language applications by training the models at
037   different resolutions during the pre-training phase to create a series of checkpoints corresponding
038
      to various image input resolutions, followed by the selection of the most effective checkpoint for
      downstream tasks. While this method is viable, it incurs significant training costs. Consequently,
039
      we pose the first research question (RQ1):
040
041       RQ1: For a given vision-language task, how to accurately determine the optimal resolution
042   without such exhaustive training for VLLMs?

093   Vision Large Language Models. Vision Large Language Models (VLLM), as one the most capa-
094   ble and popular solutions to multimodal tasks, extends the reasoning and generating ability of Large
095   Language Model (LLM) beyond language modalities such as image, video, and audio (Alayrac et al.,
096   2022; Liu et al., 2023a; McKinzie et al., 2024a; Tong et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024). VLLMs can
097
      be divided into encoder-decoder and decoder-only VLLM according to their architecture (Liu et al.,
      2023b; Driess et al., 2023; fuy; Team, 2024). The encoder-decoder VLLM introduces additional
098
      multimodal encoders and a modality connector to project multimodal features into the spaces of lan-
099
      guage models. The implementations of modality connector include: the projector that directly maps
100   features into language model (Liu et al., 2024; 2023a;b); the resampler that compresses the visual
101   feature and inserts cross-gated attention layers into the LLM decoder (Alayrac et al., 2022; Awadalla
102   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a). This study mainly focuses on the LLaVA-style VLLM, which adopts
103   encoder-decoder architecture with a projector connector.
104

145   3.1     M ETHOD F RAMEWORK
146
      Figure 2 illustrates our approach, which consists of two key components.
147
148   The first component focuses on task-specific resolution selection, where we introduce two heuristic
149   approaches to determine the optimal resolution for a given vision-language task, detailed in Se-
150   cion 3.2.1 and 3.2.2. We explore the theoretical connection between these heuristics and the Taylor
151   expansion on image input in Secion 3.2.3, leading to an empirical formula that facilitates task-wise
152
      resolution selection in Section 3.2.4.
153   After identifying the optimal resolution, the second component adapts the VLLM to this specific
154   resolution using a PEFT approach. This involves post-training a existing VLLM checkpoint without
155   retraining the model from scratch. The PEFT adaptation process is discussed in detail in Section 3.3.
156
157   3.2     TASK - WISE O PTIMAL R ESOLUTION S ELECTION
158
159   As highlighted in Section 1, different vision-language tasks have varying requirements for the per-
160   ceptual capacity of VLLMs. Therefore, it is critical to do task-wise resolution selection. While
161   tuning VLLMs at different image input resolutions and obtaining the best-performing one is feasi-
      ble, it imposes heavy training costs, which leads to RQ1. In this section, we propose a training-free

162
      method for determining the optimal resolution for a specific vision-language task, utilizing two
163   heuristic approaches.
164
165   The first heuristic estimates the complexity of the images for each task, while the second evaluates
166
      the variance in model uncertainty at different input resolutions. We then derive an empirical formula
      to guide the resolution selection process.
167
168
      3.2.1   M EASURING I MAGE C OMPLEXITY
169
170   The initial step in VLLM processing is the perception of visual input. Intuitively, images with vary-
171   ing complexity levels demand different degrees of perceptual capacity, with more complex images
172   requiring finer granularity in perception. Thus, for any given vision-language task, image complex-
173   ity can serve as an indicator of resolution preference.
174   We use an existing tool (Mahon & Lukasiewicz, 2023) to measure image complexity, which applies
175   hierarchical clustering on image pixels and leverages the minimum description length principle to
176   determine the number of clusters. The average image complexity across samples of the specific task
177   serves as an indicator for determining the appropriate resolution.
178
179   3.2.2   M EASURING U NCERTAINTY VARIANCE ACROSS RESOLUTIONS
180
      In addition to the image complexity, which addresses only the visual aspects of a task, it is crucial to
181
      account for the model uncertainty of VLLMs, as it provides insights into the interaction between the
182
      visual and linguistic components of vision-language tasks. Furthermore, the method in Section 3.2.1
183   only captures static complexity, neglecting the effects of varying image resolutions. To complement
184   this, we introduce the second heuristic approach based on model uncertainty.
185
186
      Specifically, for a VLLM pre-trained at a fixed resolution (e.g., 3362 for LLaVA), we extend the
      visual encoder’s resolution using position embedding interpolation, following methods employed
187
      in previous studies (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). We denote the original model as M 1 and
188
      the extended-resolution model as M 2. We first apply random augmentation to the images from
189   the task, following the existing RandAugment algorithm (Cubuk et al., 2020). After augmentation,
190   inference is conducted on the task samples using models M 1 and M 2, from which we extract the
191   softmax probabilities corresponding to each generated token. To quantifyPthe
                                                                                 n
                                                                                    uncertainty associated
192   with each token, we calculate the information entropy using H(p) = − i=1 pi log pi . Here, H(x)
193   represents the entropy for token x, where p(xi ) is the softmax probability of the ith token and n is
194   the number of possible tokens in the vocabulary. We denote the entropy values derived from M 1 and
195   M 2 as U 1 and U 2, respectively, which provide a measure of uncertainty in the model’s predictions.
196
      The uncertainty variance is computed as the ratio of the difference between U 1 and U 2 to U 1,
197
      as shown in r = U2U−U  1
                               1
                                 . Here, V (T ) represents the uncertainty variance for task T . This ratio
198
      quantifies how much the uncertainty changes between the two VLLMs, with higher values indicating
199   a greater impact of resolution on the model’s uncertainty. This ratio is averaged across all generated
200   tokens for a given sample, and the final uncertainty variance is computed by averaging this ratio
201   across all samples in the task.
202
      This heuristic approach serves two functions: (1) it computes entropy based on the tokens generated
203
      by VLLM, thus accounting for both visual and linguistic features during inference; and (2) it quan-
204
      tifies the variance caused by resolution changes, thereby capturing the dynamic effects of resolution
205   shifts. Unlike the static image complexity heuristic, this method emphasizes the impact of resolution
206   modifications, making these two heuristics complementary.
207
208
      Notably, we extend the image input resolution of VLLM without tuning the model parameters,
      allowing us to avoid additional training costs.

      3.2.4    E MPIRICAL F ORMULA
234
235   Intuitively, tasks characterized by high image complexity often necessitate higher input resolutions.
236   Similarly, tasks with high uncertainty variance indicate that increasing resolution heightens model
237   uncertainty, which suggests a need for greater perceptual capacity. Conversely, low uncertainty vari-
238   ance suggests that resolution changes exert minimal impact, making higher resolutions unnecessary.
239   Based on these observations, we hypothesize that image complexity and uncertainty variance are
240   positively correlated with the preferred resolution. Consequently, we propose Equation 3 to deter-
241
      mine the optimal resolution for a specific vision-language task T :
242
243                               Reso(T ) = Reso0 (1 + k × C(T ) × V (T ))                              (3)
244
245
      In this empirical formula, C(T ) represents the averaged normalized image complexity for task T ,
246   V (T ) denotes the averaged uncertainty variance across different image input resolutions on task T ,
247   k is a user-specified hyperparameter, and Reso0 is the baseline image input resolution of the original
248   VLLM. The expression 1 + k × C(T ) × V (T ) quantifies the scaling factor between the baseline and
249   the preferred resolution. In practice, the value of k can be adjusted based on prior experience.

      3.3     PARAMETER - EFFICIENT R ESOLUTION A DAPTATION
252
253   After determining the optimal resolution for a given task, the next step is adapting the VLLM to the
254   selected resolution. To answer RQ2, We propose a parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) approach
255   that post-train an existing VLLM checkpoint, thus avoiding retraining from scratch.
256
      As depicted in Figure 2, existing VLLMs (e.g., LLaVA) consist of three main components: a visual
257
      encoder that processes visual inputs, a projector that maps visual features to the word embedding
258   space, and an LLM backbone that autoregressively generates language tokens based on the combined
259   visual and linguistic inputs.
260
261
      Increasing the input resolution results in more image patches, which introduces incompatibility with
      the original position embeddings. To address this, we interpolate the position embeddings from
262
      the initial number of patches (e.g., 242 ) to the extended number (e.g., 322 ), following previous
263
      research (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). Although this allows the VLLM to process extended res-
264   olutions, performance degrades without further adaptation (as discussed in Secion 3.2). To counter
265   this performance decline, we employ a PEFT method that fine-tunes three key components: (1) po-
266   sition embeddings within the visual encoder, crucial for resolution adaptation due to the change in
267   image patch count; (2) the lightweight projector parameters; and (3) the parameters of the LoRA
268   adapters integrated into the LLM backbone. By keeping all other parameters frozen, the PEFT ap-
269   proach offers an efficient method for adaptation. Figure 2 provides a visual representation of the
      components that are fine-tuned versus those that remain frozen.

442   4.3.2    OVERALL RESULTS OF TASK - WISE A DAPTIVE M ODEL AND BASELINES
443
444   Table 3 presents the performance of baseline methods and LLaVA variants across six tasks that
445   demand high visual perception capacity from VLLMs.
446   Among the LLaVA variants, the training-free method to extend the input resolution through PE
447   interpolation shows performance degradation at varying levels. This confirms that the position em-
448   beddings in the visual encoder and LLM backbone in LLaVA cannot fully adapt to the increased
449   number of image tokens without post-training. On the other hand, the task-wise adaptive LLaVA
450   variant, which optimally selects the input resolution for each task, achieves the best overall perfor-
451   mance compared to fixed-resolution LLaVA variants, regardless of whether the resolution is 3362 ,
452
      4482 , or 5602 . Notably, the task-wise adaptive LLaVA variant with a 7B backbone performs com-
      parably to the 13B variant, underscoring the importance of adaptive perception capacity in VLLMs.
453
454   When comparing the task-wise adaptive LLaVA variant with other state-of-the-art baselines, it out-
455   performs all but the TextVQA task. In the case of TextVQA, the Qwen-VL and Qwen-VL-Chat
456   methods have observed training images or annotations of the dataset during their training. Impor-
457   tantly, as previous studies (McKinzie et al., 2024b) have highlighted, resolution plays a crucial role
458
      during pretraining. The Qwen-VL series are pretrained at an image resolution of 4482 , while the
      LLaVA variants were fine-tuned at extended image resolutions in a post-training phase with far fewer
459
      data (665K) compared to Qwen’s 1.4B pretraining and 50M fine-tuning samples. Nevertheless, the
460
      task-wise adaptive LLaVA variant achieves better overall results than the Qwen-VL series.
461
462   The superior performance of the task-wise adaptive LLaVA variant across multiple vision-language
463   tasks demonstrates that, compared to fixed-resolution approaches, adaptive resolution selection is
464
      more suitable for real-world applications. So far, we have verified the effectiveness of our proposed
      task-wise resolution selection strategy through the generalization of the empirical formula and the
465
      overall experimental results, answering RQ1.

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we take a step towards adapting VLLMs to real-world applications by providing an
531   in-depth investigation of resolution preferences in different vision-language tasks. Based on the
532   findings, we introduce an empirical formula that combines image complexity and uncertainty vari-
533   ance to enable task-specific resolution selection without the need for retraining. Additionally, we
534   propose a parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach, enabling extension of the image input resolution
535   for existing VLLM checkpoints. We expect that our research will offer valuable insights for the
536
      VLLM research community.
537   Future Work While this study focuses on LLaVA as a representative VLLM architecture, future
538   work will explore other VLLM architectures. Moreover, our current work centers on task-wise
539   resolution selection; future research will investigate more granular resolution selection strategies,
      such as sample-level resolution adaptation.

      A.3      M ETHOD DETAILS
770
771   Image Complexity Heuristic Approach Image complexity for vision-language tasks is calculated
772   using an open-source tool2 . We utilize the author-recommended hyperparameters: the number of
773   clusters is set to 8, and the subsample rate is 0.8. To reduce computational overhead, the input
774   image resolution is set to 112 × 112, and two cluster levels are used, with their combined scores
775   yielding the final complexity value. The complexity scores are normalized via min-max scaling,
776   where the minimum and maximum values are computed from 100 sampled images from the Ima-
777   geNet dataset Deng et al. (2009).
778   RandAugment Perturbation on Image Input When assessing model variance across different
779   resolutions, we apply random perturbations to each input image using the RandAugment algorithm,
780   implemented via an existing tool3 . For each image, we perform three random augmentations. To
781   mitigate the effects of randomness and enhance result stability, we repeat the variance measurement
782   process three times, each using a different random seed. The final uncertainty variance is obtained
783   by averaging the results from these three iterations.

872
873   The sampling proportions vary from 10% to 50%, with each experiment repeated 10 times using
874
      different random seeds. The success rate was defined as the percentage of instances where the
      empirical formula accurately predicted the optimal resolution for all tasks. The results, presented in
875

877   At a sampling ratio of 10%, the success rate drops to 50%, indicating that a smaller subset size
878   introduces variability that adversely affects prediction accuracy.
