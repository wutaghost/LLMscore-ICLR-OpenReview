009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Locally differentially private (LDP) graph analysis allows private analysis on a
012            graph distributed across multiple users. However, such computations are vulnerable
013            to poisoning attacks, where an adversary can skew results by submitting malformed
014            data. This paper studies the impact of poisoning attacks on graph degree estimation
015            protocols under LDP. We make two key contributions. First, we observe that
016
               LDP makes protocols more vulnerable to poisoning – the impact is worse when
               adversaries poison their (noisy) responses rather than their input. Second, we note
017
               that graph data is naturally redundant, as every edge is shared between two users.
018
               Leveraging this redundancy, we design robust degree estimation protocols under
019            LDP that reduce the impact of poisoning and compute accurate degree estimates.
020            We evaluate our protocols on real-world datasets to demonstrate their effectiveness.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   A distributed graph is defined over a set of users, where each user only knows the edges involving
026   them—in other words, each user has access to their own adjacency list. This means each user
027
      has a local view of the graph, and no single entity has knowledge of the entire graph. A real-
      world example of this can be found in decentralized social media platforms, such as Mastodon,
028
      Diaspora, PeerTube, where each user (account holder) represents a node, and an edge between
029
      two users indicates they are "friends" (i.e., they follow each other). In this scenario, an un-
030   trusted aggregator, such as Mastodon itself, may attempt to compute statistics for the entire
031   graph. However, since the edges represent sensitive information (e.g., edges reveal users’ per-
032   sonal social connections), users cannot submit their data to the aggregator directly. Instead, they
033   add noise to their data to achieve a local differential privacy (LDP) guarantee before sharing
034   it with Mastodon. LDP has already been deployed by major commercial organizations such
035   as Google Erlingsson et al. (2014), Apple Greenberg (2016), and Microsoft Ding et al. (2017).
036

047   Prior work, which focuses on tabular data Cheu et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022), finds
048   that poisoning attacks can be carried out against LDP protocols. However, the impact of poisoning
049   under LDP for graph analysis is largely unexplored. In this paper, we initiate a formal study on the
050
      impact of poisoning on LDP protocols for graph statistics. We focus on the task of degree vector
      estimation, one of the most fundamental tasks in graph analysis Raskhodnikova & Smith (2016).
051
052   A real-world use-case for a poisoning attack is as follows – suppose a company is interested in hiring
053   the most influential nodes (users) of a graph for marketing its product on Mastodon and uses a node’s
      degree as its measure of influence. An adversary might want to promote a specific malicious node to


                                                         1
054
      be selected as an influencer or prevent an honest node from being selected as an influencer; concretely,
055   suppose a single malicious user wants to be selected as influential. If the LDP protocol used is the
056   Laplace mechanism, where each user directly submits their (noisy) degree to the analyst, then the
057   malicious user can lie flagrantly and report their degree to be n − 1, the maximum degree possible!
058
      We address this challenge and design degree estimation protocols that are robust to poisoning
059
      attacks. Our algorithms are based on the key observation that graph data is naturally redundant –
060
      the information about an edge eij is shared by both users Ui and Uj . Importantly, the users do not
061   explicitly share this information; rather, it is implicitly shared by the structure of the graph itself. For
062   example, in a social media graph, both users are aware of their mutual "friend" connection (i.e., the
063   edge between them). Leveraging this observation, we propose robust protocols based on two new
064   ideas. First, we use distributed information – we collect the information about each edge from both
065   users. The second idea is to verify that the collected information is consistent. Specifically, as long as
066   at least one of Ui or Uj is honest, the analyst can check for consistency between the two edge reports
067   and detect malicious behavior.
068   A key challenge to our mechanism design is that LDP forces all consistency checks to be probabilistic—
069   edge inconsistencies may arise from both malicious behavior and random noise. Consequently, our
070   protocols will flag malicious users using confidence intervals based on the expected number of
071   inconsistent edges. We must set these intervals precisely, so that malicious users who fabricate many
072   edges are caught, while honest users whose users are never falsely flagged as malicious. This requires
073   us to carefully define what it means for a protcol to behave robustly. In summary, we are the first to
074   study the impact of poisoning on LDP degree estimation for graphs. Our main contributions are:
075    • Novel Formal Framework. We propose a formal framework for analyzing the robustness of a
076      protocol. Specifically, we measure the robustness along two dimensions, accuracy (for honest
077      users) and soundness (for malicious users). Intuitively, good accuracy means that the protocol has
078      high utility for honest users, and good soundness means that it can detect/restrict malicious users.
079    • New Results on Poisoning Attacks. Based on the proposed framework, we study the impact
080      of poisoning on private degree estimation in the LDP setting. The attacks can be classified into
081      two types: (1) input poisoning where the adversary does not have access to implementation of
082
         the LDP protocol and can only falsify their input (Fig. 2a), and (2) response poisoning where
         the adversary can tamper with the LDP implementation and directly manipulate the (noisy)
083
         responses of the LDP protocol (Fig. 2b). The former is independent of LDP while the latter
084
         utilizes the characteristics of LDP. We observe that LDP makes a degree estimation protocol
085      more vulnerable to poisoning – the impact of response poisoning is worse than that of input
086      poisoning. Additionally, we provide a lower bound for input poisoning.
087
       • Novel Robust Degree Estimation Protocols. Leveraging the natural redundancy in graph data,
088      we design robust degree estimation protocols under LDP that significantly reduce the impact of
089      adversarial poisoning and compute degree estimates with high utility. Our robustness guarantees
090      are attack-agnostic – they work for all attacks on all graphs. Additionally, our results matches
091      with the lower bound (upto constants) for input poisoning.
092    • Comprehensive Attack Evaluation. We conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation to
093      validate our theoretical results. First, we assess the threat of poisoning attacks through 16 real-
094      world motivated scenarios. Our findings reveal that even a small number of malicious users
095      (m = 1%) can inflict significant damage. Next, we demonstrate the robustness of our degree
096      estimation protocols against these attacks. Our results show that our protocols effectively mitigate
097      poisoning attacks even with a larger number of malicious users (m = 33%) in real-world datasets.

135   3.1   P ROTOCOL S ETUP
136
      Problem Statement. We consider single round, non-interactive protocols in which each user
137
      Ui , i ∈ [n] runs the local randomizer Ri : {0, 1}n → X on their adjacency lists li . The aggregator
138
      collects the noisy responses and applies a function D : X n → (N ∪ {⊥})d to produce final degree
139
      estimates d̂ = ⟨dˆ1 , . . . , dˆn ⟩. Here, dˆi is the aggregator’s estimate for di for user Ui . Note that the
140
      aggregator is allowed to output a special symbol ⊥ for a user Ui if they believe the estimate dˆi to be
141
      invalid (i.e., Ui is malicious). Degree vector estimation is one of the most fundamental tasks in graph
142
      statistics and is very commonly used for measuring the influence of a node (also known as degree
143   centrality Borgatti & Everett (2006); Kempe et al. (2005)). As ours is the first study to examine the
144   impact of data poisoning on graphs under LDP, we begin by addressing this fundamental problem.
145
146
      Threat Model. In executing protocol P, a subset of users M ∈ [n] may be malicious, with m = |M|
      representing their count. The malicious users may return arbitrary output to perform a poisoning
147
      attack on P. We refer to H = [n] \ M as the set of honest users. We do not make any assumptions
148
      on how the malicious users are instantiated in practice – they could be fake accounts created by
149   an adversary, or compromised real accounts, or a combination of both. Based on the practical
150   implementation of the LDP protocol, there is an important distinction between the way in which the
151   malicious users may carry out their poisoning attacks:
152
       • Input Poisoning. Here the users do not have access to the implementation of the LDP randomizer.
153
         For instance, mobile applications might run proprietary code which the users do not have
154      permission to tamper with. The only thing a malicious user can do is falsify their underlying
155      input, i.e., change their input from li to an arbitrary li′ , and then report qi = Ri (li′ ) (Fig. 2a).
156
       • Response Poisoning. This is a stronger threat model where a malicious user has direct control
157      over the implementation of the LDP randomizer. For instance, the user could hack into the mobile
158      application collecting their data. Consequently, the user can completely bypass the randomizer
159      and submit an arbitrary response qi (Fig. 2b) to the aggregator.
160
161   Note that input poisoning applies to any protocol, private or not, because a user is free to change their
      input anytime. However, response poisoning attacks are unique to LDP – the distinction between

      attack, a target malicious user Ut ∈ M wants to inflate his degree estimate to affect his influence.

186   In this section, we present our formal framework for analyzing the robustness of a protocol for degree
187   estimation. Specifically, we measure the robustness along two dimensions, honest error and malicious
188   error. Intuitively, low honest error means that the protocol results in accurate degree estimates for
189   honest users. On the other hand, low malicious error prevents a malicious user from manipulating
190
      their degree estimate too much without detection.
191   Honest Error. The honest error of a protocol quantifies the manipulation of an honest user’s estimator.
192   Specifically, malicious users can adversely affect an honest user Ui ∈ H by (1) tampering with the
193   value of Ui ’s degree estimate dˆi (by introducing additional loss in accuracy), or (2) attempting to
194   mislabel Ui as malicious (by influencing the aggregator to report dˆi = ⊥. The honest error of a
195   protocol accounts for these scenarios and is formally defined as follows:

198   H be the set of honest users. Then, the protocol has (α1 , δ1 )-honest error w.r.t. an attack from M if
199   for all input graphs G ∈ G we have:

336   colluding and report consistently, while the 1ϵ factor corresponds to the scaling factor required for

340   privacy, the collected responses are more noisy thereby making it harder to distinguish honest users
341   from malicious ones. In particular, a protocol should not return ⊥ for honest users (i.e., mislabel
342   them) to ensure good robustness. Here, more malicious error is tolerated before a ⊥ is returned for
343   a malicious user. This is evident in Eq. 4–threshold τ grows with smaller ϵ. See App. B for more
344   details on this price of privacy.

533   8     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we have investigated the impact of poisoning attacks on degree vector estimation for
536   graphs under LDP. We have presented a formal framework for analyzing the robustness of a protocol
537   against poisoning. Our framework can quantify the impact of a poisoning attack on both honest
538   and malicious users. We have shown a lower bound on such poisoning attacks. Additionally, we
539   have proposed novel robust degree estimation protocols under LDP by leveraging the natural data
      redundancy in graphs that can match the lower bound for a specific class of attacks.

      Complementary to Defn. 2 in Sec. 4, we introduce the notion of α1 -tight honest error. A protocol
      has α1 -tight honest error w.r.t an attack, if there exists a graph such that the attack is guaranteed to
713
      either skew the the degree estimate of at least one honest user by exactly α1 . We use this definition to
714
      show the existence of strong attacks that are guaranteed to be very successful in manipulating the
715   data of an honest user, which motivates the need for robust solutions.
716
717   Similarly for malicious error we introduce the notion of α1 -malicious error. A protocol has α1 -tight
718
      malicious error w.r.t an attack if there exists a graph G ∈ G such that at least one malicious user
      is guaranteed to have their degree estimate mis-estimated by exactly α1 without getting detected.
719
      In other words, an attack with (n − 1)-tight honest error/malicious error represents the strongest
720
      possible attack– an user’s estimate can always be skewed by the worst-case amount. We use this
721   definition to motivate the need for more robust protocols.

724   I MPACT OF P OISONING ON BASELINE M ECHANISMS C NTD .
725
726   Within our robustness framework, we analyze the two naive private mechanisms – the Laplace
727   mechanism and randomized response. The shortcomings of these mechanisms motivate the design of
728
      our robust protocols as discussed in the main paper.
729
730   A.1        L APLACE M ECHANISM
731
      The simplest mechanism for estimating an user’s degree is the Laplace mechanism, RLap , where each
732
      user directly reports their noisy estimates. Consequently, the degree estimate of an honest user cannot
733
      be tampered with at all – the Õ( 1ϵ )1 term is due to the error of the added Laplace noise. This error is
734   in fact optimal (matches that of central DP) for degree estimation. On the flip side, a malicious user
735   can flagrantly lie about their estimate without detection resulting in the the worst-case malicious error.
736   Specifically, there exists a graph and an attack against RLap in which a malicious user is guaranteed
737   to manipulate their true degree by n − 1 – this holds for the case where the malicious user is an
738   isolated node but lies that their degree is n − 1. The robustness of RLap against response poisoning
739   attacks is formalized as follows:
740   Theorem 6. Let M be a set of malicious users with |M| = m. The RLap protocol has ( 1ϵ log nδ ), δ)-
741   honest error w.r.t any response poisoning from M. However, there is a response poisoning attack A
742   such that RLap has (n − 1)-tight malicious error w.r.t A.
743
744   The proof is in App. G.3. Thus according to our robustness framework, RLap has low honest error but
745   high malicious error. Intuitively, RLap fails to provide low malicious error because there is no way to
746
      verify the malicious users’ reports. It is important to note that RLap has low honest error even with
      n − 1 malicious users while the worst-case malicious error is inevitable even with a single malicious
747
      user.

      Here, we present our results for input poisoning for the Laplace mechanism and the RRCheck

      mechanisms.
845
846   Recall in the Laplace mechanism, each user simply reports a private estimate of their degree. Under
847   input poisoning attacks, Laplace noise is added to the poisoned input before it is reported to the
848   data aggregator. Consequently, the response poisoning attack in which a malicious user could
849
      deterministically report their degree as n − 1 (Thm. 6) is no longer possible – in order to manipulate
      their degree by n − 1, the malicious user needs to get lucky with the sampled Laplace noise, resulting
850
      in the following theorem:
851
852   Theorem 9. Let M be a set of malicious users with |M| = m. The RLap protocol has ( 1ϵ ln nδ , δ)
853   honest error and (n − 1, 21 )-malicious error with respect to any input poisoning attack from M.
854
      The proof of the above theorem is in App. G.8. Unsurprisingly, compared to Thm. 6 for response
855
      poisoning, the honest error is unchanged because no attack is possible for honest users. However, the
856
      malicious error is different. Thm. 6 delineates an (n − 1)-tight malicious error, demonstrating the
857   feasibility of the worst-case attack in which a malicious user can always manipulate their degree by
858   n − 1. In contrast, RLap has (n − 1, 12 )-malicious error with respect to any input poisoning attack.
859   This is because the sampled Laplace noise is negative with probability 12 . Hence, even a worst-case
860   malicious user who sends the maximum degree of n − 1 will only get assigned a final estimate this
861   high if the sampled noise is non-negative. Thus, the noise in the Laplace mechanism prevents the
862   adversary from carrying out the deterministic worst-case attack.

1057   the amount by which Ut can skew its degree estimate (i.e., improves malicious error α2 ) However,
1058   the cost is an increase in the chance of erroneously flagging an honest user (false positive rate, FPR).
1059   In order to obtain a rigorous formal guarantee on both true positive rate (honest error) and false
1060   positive rate (malicious error), the theoretical thresholds presented in our theorems are quite stringent.
1061
       However, we observe that in practice we can use lower values of threshold. We show this in Fig. 8a
       and 8b on the strongest inflation attack evaluated (A11 in Table 2). There is no notion of threshold
1062
       for the naive protocol SimpleRR, hence it has TPR=FPR=0. For our robust protocols, Hybrid and
1063
       RRCheck, we test 10 different values of lower thresholds q · τ where q ∈ (0, 1). We observe that
1064   lowering the threshold delineates a trade-off between TPR and FPR. For all our experiments, we
1065   select a threshold that maximizes TPR at FPR=0 through empirical testing.

1260   A recent line of work Cheu et al. (2021); Cao et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022) has
1261   explored the impact of poisoning in LDP. However, these works focused either on tabular data or
1262   key-value data. Additionally, prior work mostly focuses on the task of frequency estimation which
1263   is different from our problem of degree estimation. For the former, each user has some item from
1264   an input domain and the data aggregator wants to compute the histogram over all the users’ items.
1265   Whereas, we compute the degree vector ⟨dˆ1 , . . . , dˆn ⟩ – each user directly reports their degree di
1266   (a count or via an adjacency list). More specifically, Cao et al. Cao et al. (2021) proposed attacks
1267   where an adversary could increase the estimated frequencies for adversary-chosen target items or
1268
       promote them to be identified as heavy hitters. Wu et al. Wu et al. (2021) extended the attacks for
       key-value data where an adversaryaims to simultaneously promote the estimated frequencies and
1269
       mean values for some adversary-chosen target keys. Cheu et al. Cheu et al. (2021) formally analyzed
1270
       the poisoning attacks on categorical data and showed that local algorithms are highly vulnerable to
1271   adversarial manipulation – when the privacy level is high or the input domain is large, an adversary
1272   who controls a small fraction of the users in the protocol can completely obscure the distribution of
1273   the users’ inputs. This is essentially an impossibility result for robust estimation of categorical data
1274   via non-interactive LDP protocols. Additionally, they showed that poisoning the noisy messages can
1275   be far more damaging than poisoning the data itself. A recent work Li et al. (2022) studies the impact
1276   of data poisoning for mean and variance estimation for tabular data. In the shuffle DP model, Cheu et
1277   al.Cheu & Zhilyaev (2022) have studied the impact of poisoning on histogram estimation. In terms
1278   of general purpose defenses, prior work has explored strategies strategy based on cryptographically
1279
       verifying implementations of LDP randomizers Kato et al. (2021); Ambainis et al. (2003); Moran &
       Naor (2006) – this would restrict the attacks to input poisoning only.
