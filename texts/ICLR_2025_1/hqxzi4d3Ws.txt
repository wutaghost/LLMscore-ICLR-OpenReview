                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Advancements in quantum computing have spurred significant interest in harness-
012            ing its potential for speedups over classical systems. However, noise remains a
013            major obstacle to achieving reliable quantum algorithms. In this work, we present
014            a provably noise-resilient training theory and algorithm to enhance the robust-
015            ness of parameterized quantum circuits. Our method, with a natural connection to
016            Evolutionary Strategies, guarantees resilience to parameter noise with minimal ad-
017            justments to commonly used optimization algorithms. Our approach is function-
018            agnostic and adaptable to various quantum circuits, successfully demonstrated in
019
               quantum phase classification and quantum state preparation tasks. By developing
               provably guaranteed learning theory with quantum circuits, our work opens new
020
               avenues for practical, robust applications of near-term quantum computers.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   In the past few decades, the field of quantum computing has grown dramatically (Farhi et al., 2014;
026   Harrigan et al., 2021; Moll et al., 2017; Klco et al., 2018; Peruzzo et al., 2014). Enticed by the
027   potential of speed-ups over classical computers, great efforts have been devoted to not only building
028   quantum computers (Bluvstein et al., 2024), but also exploring how to achieve these speed-ups when
029   the proper devices exists (Biamonte et al., 2017) with a wide variety of approaches, from carefully-
030   crafted algorithms (Dalzell et al., 2023) to data-learned optimized approaches (Cerezo et al., 2021).
031   However, all existing quantum algorithms suffer from the existence of noise on the current quantum
032   hardware. Until the theory and practice of error-corrected quantum computing makes significant
033   progress, all quantum algorithms need to find ways to be robust to the noise that exists in noisy
034   intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) computers (Preskill, 2018). Furthermore, there are many types
035   of noise, from noise inherent to quantum systems that can’t be fully isolated from their environment,
036   to simple instrumentation noise that occurs whenever preparing a continuously-parameterized op-
037
      eration (Saki et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2023). As a result, a number of works have explored different
      ways to fight noise in the NISQ era (West et al., 2023; Weber et al., 2021; 2022).
038
039   In this work, we offer another tool to fight against noise for algorithms deployed on NISQ devices.
040   Specifically we tackle parameter gate noise, in which the prepared parameters of a continuously-
041   parameterized quantum gate may be different than it is intended to be. Our method is a certified
042   robustness method, in which we can guarantee a certain level of noise will not affect the end result
043
      of the quantum process. We highlight our contributions as follows:
044        1. Our work provides a provably guaranteed framework and theory for training parameterized
045           quantum circuit classifier under parameter noise. While most other methods consider ad-
046           versarial attacks on inputs or mid-circuit noise inherent to quantum systems, we explicitly
047           tackle the use case that accounts for instrumentation error in devices. As long as the desired
048           noise-model and algorithm can be expressed as a parameterized circuit, our method can be
049           used to find an optimal robustness certificate for changes in those parameters.
050        2. Our method is simple, easy to deploy, and naturally connected to Evolutionary Strategies
051           (ES), an optimization algorithm already commonly used by the variational quantum algo-
052           rithm (VQA) community (Gil-Fuster et al., 2023; Anand et al., 2021). Due to its ability to
053           avoid computing costly quantum gradients (Abbas et al., 2023), a practitioner can achieve
              robustness certificates using our method with minimal effort. Furthermore, our method


                                                       1

054
               can be applied on top of any error-mitigation method, allowing to be easily combined with
055            other methods to further boost the robustness they provide.
056
057         3. Our approach is successfully demonstrated on quantum phase classification and quantum
058
               state preparation tasks. Our results present clear robustness-variance trade-off and different
               robustness-variance correlation in different tasks, which provide insights and understanding
059
               on the sensitivity of the parameters in quantum parameterized circuits.
060
061   In summary, we develop a provably noise-resilient training theory and algorithm for parameterized
062   quantum circuits. With its flexibility and ease of adaptation, our approach achieves noise robustness,
063   paving the way for practical applications on near-term quantum computers.

066   2   P RIOR W ORK
067
068   Certified Robustness : In classical machine learning, there is a large body of work that aims to
069   make models more robust to attacks (Madry et al., 2017; Silva & Najafirad, 2020; Zhang et al.,
070
      2019; Pitropakis et al., 2019). A sub-section of this work is certified robustness, where bounds and
      theorems are derived to allow one to prove their model is robust to attacks. One large portion of
071
      this work focuses on exactly computing the possible output space of a model given a defined input
072
      space, which is usually defined to be the desired input plus the possible space of attack-perturbations.
073   Often this requires incorporating relaxations that make the computed space not strictly tight, but
074   allow the bound to be computed in non-exponential time. Initially these relaxations were devised to
075   accommodate for common non-linear activations in neural networks (Wong & Kolter, 2017; Zhang
076   et al., 2018), but these works have since been extended to allow for more general forms of relaxations
077   and can be formulated into semi-definite programming (SDP) problems (Raghunathan et al., 2018;
078   Salman et al., 2020c).
079   Another method used to certify robustness is randomized smoothing. In essence, by instead certify-
080   ing a ”smoothed” classifier, which predicts the most likely class your underlying model will output
081   if your input has noise added to it, one can achieve robustness guarantees to perturbations on said
082   input. This was first applied to achieve robustness guarantees for L2 norm perturbations on neural
083   networks, and was even shown to be provable optimal for any given smoothed classifier (Cohen
084   et al., 2019). There has since been numerous extensions. Some extensions allow for extra flexibility
085   in the method, allowing for the use of different noise models, different optimal-robustness guaran-
086   tees (Yang et al., 2020), different certified regions (Levine & Feizi, 2019; Dvijotham et al., 2019),
087
      or more flexibility in customizing the amount of noise per input parameter (Tecot & Hsieh, 2021).
      Other extensions focus on using various new training techniques to produce better smoothed classi-
088
      fiers (Salman et al., 2020b; Zhang et al.), or devise ways to achieve good smoothed models using a
089
      pre-trained model as a base (Salman et al., 2020a).
090
091   Quantum Certified Robustness : Although a newer area, there has been a sizable amount of in-
092   terest in studying adversarial robusness in the quantum setting (West et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2020).
093
      Just like with classical robustness, these works typically either use SDP to calculate possible output
      spaces, or use randomized smoothing as a tool to get robustness bounds. On the SDP side, tools
094
      and methods have been developed to apply similar tactics in the classical setting but for analyzing
095
      quantum models. They have been tested in on a variety tasks for a variety of noise models (We-
096   ber et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2024). Similarly, a variety of works have developed
097   quantum-versions of randomized smoothing, usually either by adding noise to the input and applying
098   randomized smoothing theorems (Du et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023) or by developing quantum-
099   versions of the smoothing process (Gong et al., 2024; Sahdev & Kumar, 2022). However, Weber
100   et al. (2021) takes arguably a more fundamental approach by realizing that because measuring quan-
101   tum states is already a probabilistic distribution, one can treat repeatedly measuring a quantum state
102   itself as a randomized smoothed model. From this you can derive bounds relating to the measured
103   state, and extrapolate this to different bounds on noise that may occur.
104   Evolutionary Strategies : Evolutionary Strategies (ES) are a group of algorithms that take a pop-
105   ulation, sample it for a desired property, and then change said population to on average improve
106   on said property. At least in the context of using this method for continuous optimization, often
107   the population is modelled as a probability distribution, and to optimize it gradient descent is per-
      formed on the parameters of the distribution. There are numerous different variations of ES (Li


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2020; Maheswaranathan et al., 2019), but among the most common are Covariance Matrix
109   Adaptation Evolution Strategies (CMA-ES) (Hansen, 2006) and Natural Evolution Strategies (NES)
110   (Wierstra et al., 2011). At their core, both of these methods simply take a multivariate Gaussian,
111   sample it, evaluate the loss function at each sample, and perform gradient descent on the parameters
112   of the Gaussian from said samples. They then repeat this process until convergence. They each have
113   their own ways to improve both the practicality and theoretical guarantees of this process, however
114   it has been show that these two methods are just re-formulations of the same process and are ex-
115   tremely similar (Akimoto et al., 2010). Furthermore, both of these methods are commonly used for
116   optimizing parameterized quantum circuits (Gil-Fuster et al., 2023; Anand et al., 2021).

      3.3   VARIANCE R EGULARIZATION
236
237   After we have identified how to optimize to improve the right side of the bound in Theorem 3.1, we
238   now turn to improve the left side in the bound. Notice that there is an implicit trade-off occurring
239   in this bound; while the left side always benefits from a larger σ, making it larger introduces the
240   risk of decreasing the accuracy of the smoothed classifier and in turn decreasing the right side of
241   the bound. As such, to address this trade-off we add regularization into our optimization procedure.
242   This allows us to define an optimization trade-off between improving the left-side of the bound via
243   a large σ and the right-hand side by encouraging high accuracy. We control this trade-off with a
244
      hyperparameter, and in practice we often sweep over many values of this coefficient to understand
      the nature of accuracy-robustness trade-off per experiment. To control the magnitude of σ, we utilize
245
      two types of regularization methods, both of which have similar performances in our experiments.
246
      (See Section B.2 for more details.)

      As randomized smoothing only works in classification tasks, we primarily consider phase classi-

      5.4   L IMITATIONS
487
488   While we provide a provably noise-resilient approach for training PQC classier, there are limitations
489   of the work. Since other types of noise observed in a quantum system might be difficult to translate
490   back into the parameter-perturbation setting, it is an open question on how to generalize our results
491   with provably guarantee to certify arbitrary types of quantum noise. Furthermore, due to resource
492   constraint, we only provide numerical simulations and it will be valuable to perform experiments on
493   real quantum devices in the future.
494
495   6     C ONCLUSION
496
497   In this work we have developed a provably noise-resilient approach for training parameterized quan-
498   tum circuit classifiers. Our method is flexible for any quantum circuit and easy to deploy on NISQ
499   quantum device with a natural connection to a Evolutionary Strategies. This makes it extremely
500   simple for practitioners to use our method on any of their existing experiments, both to enhance ro-
501   bustness and to gain insights into the sensitivity of the quantum devices. We have demonstrated our
502
      method’s capabilities through experiments that not only certify the robustness of PQC classifiers, but
      also input parameterized quantum state-preparation. Future work could explore using the shape of
503
      the σ vector to understand the sensitivity and importance of parameters in PQC classifiers, especially
504
      for different quantum ansatz. Additionally, further optimization and regularization of the PQC clas-
505   sifier could be customized based on specific performance metrics. Expanding the method to include
506   a full-covariance matrix in randomized smoothing could also provide more flexibility in smoothing
507   techniques. Our work integrates the frontier classical machine learning algorithm with quantum
508   computation, opening up opportunities for robust applications of near-term quantum computers.

852   B.3   P ROBABILITY E STIMATION
853
854   When in practice using our method outlined in Section 3 and Theorem 3.1, it is impossible to exactly
855
      evaluate pA and pB . This is because doing so would require you to compute the expectation over
      a Gaussian on the PQC. As such, when practically using this method, one must estimate the values
856
      of pA and pB . To do so in such a way that still guarantees the conditions of theorem 3.1 hold, we
857
      can use some form of concentration inequality or confidence interval method. These mathematical
858   methods will give us values pA , pB such that pA ≥ pA and pB ≤ pB with probability 1 − δ ′ . Note
859   that theorem 3.1 still holds if we replace pA with a lower bound on the true value, and likewise if
860   we replace pB with an upper bound on the true value. As such, the whole theorem can still be used
861   if these probabilities are estimated via sampling. And the more sampling a practitioner does, the
862   better their estimate will become, and as a result also their robust certificate. In practice one can use
863   whichever concentration inequality or confidence interval method they prefer, but in most cases the
      Clopper-Pearson confidence interval is used (Cohen et al., 2019; Tecot & Hsieh, 2021).
