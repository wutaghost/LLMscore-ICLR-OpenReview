009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have significantly en-
012            hanced the ability of LLM-based systems to perform complex tasks through natural
013            language processing and tool interaction. However, optimizing these LLM-based
014            systems for specific tasks remains challenging, often requiring manual interven-
015            tions like prompt engineering and hyperparameter tuning. Existing automatic
016            optimization methods, such as textual feedback-based techniques (e.g., TextGrad),
017            tend to focus on immediate feedback, analogous to using first-order derivatives
018
               in traditional numerical gradient descent. However, relying solely on first-order
               derivatives can be limited when the gradient is either very small or fluctuates irreg-
019
               ularly, which may slow down or stall optimization. To address these limitations,
020
               better adaptation in regions with small or fluctuating gradients is necessary. Second-
021            order gradient methods, which incorporate the Hessian matrix, offer a promising
022            solution by enabling more precise adjustments. Inspired by this, in this paper, we
023            introduce HessianGrad, a novel optimization method that leverages textual feed-
024            back and tracks the iterative evolution of LLM systems responses across iterations,
025            leading to more dynamic and adaptive optimization. We evaluate the effectiveness
026            of HessianGrad on three tasks: prompt optimization, solution optimization, and
027            code optimization. Experimental results demonstrate that HessianGrad consistently
028            improves performance across all three tasks, achieving a 7.8% improvement in
029
               prompt optimization, a 20.72% gain in solution refinement, and a 29.17% in-
               crease in code optimization compared to baselines, highlighting its adaptability
030
               and effectiveness in optimizing LLM-based systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION

      In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have dramatically advanced AI’s ability to handle
036   complex tasks through natural language processing, enabling LLM-based systems, often referred to
037   as language agents, to interact with external tools and solve problems previously considered out of
038   reach (Brown, 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023;
039   Zheng et al., 2024). However, the development of these language agents still requires significant
040   manual effort to break down tasks and fine-tune prompts, tools, and APIs, limiting scalability and
041   adaptability (Wei et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). This raises the need for automated,
042   scalable optimization techniques that can enhance language agents efficiently.
043   To this end, a number of recent efforts has been made on automatic optimization of language agents.
044   For instance, DSpy (Khattab et al., 2024) uses bootstrapping and random search to optimize LLM
045   prompts by exploring a combinatorial space of prompt components. GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al., 2024)
046   builds on this by introducing an iterative process to manage DSpy’s complexity. Other methods like
047   Agent-Pro (Zhang et al., 2024) and AgentOptimizer (Zhang et al.) target specific modules, refining
048   prompts, and agent policies. However, these approaches often suffer from local optimization, where
049   improvements in isolated components do not lead to overall system performance gains—similar to
050
      early neural network practices (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006).
051   Building on these efforts, more advanced research has introduced gradient descent-inspired techniques
052   for automatic prompt optimization. ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023) pioneered the use of textual
053   gradients, where natural language feedback refines prompts. Agent Symbolic Learning (ASL) (Zhou
      et al., 2024) extended this to optimize the entire agent system by treating prompts and tools as

105
106   Our approach draws inspiration from several key areas of research, particularly automated prompt
107   engineering, agent optimization, and gradient-based learning. Below, we highlight foundational
      works in these areas and situate our method within this broader context.

      From Prompt Engineering to Agent Optimization. Prompt engineering has become a key focus
109   in both academia and industry, leading to several methods aimed at automating the process. Early
110   works (Pryzant et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024) explored the use of structured prompts that enable
111   LLMs to optimize their own inputs. Other approaches (Prasad et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023)
112   use search algorithms, like genetic algorithms, to automatically refine prompts. Building on the
113   success of automated prompt engineering, researchers have extended these concepts to broader agent
114   optimization. Techniques like Agent-Pro (Zhang et al., 2024) and AgentOptimizer (Zhang et al.) focus
115   on optimizing individual components, such as prompts or tools. However, these methods often treat
116   components in isolation, which can result in local improvements without significantly enhancing the
117
      overall system. Search-based approaches, such as DSpy (Khattab et al., 2024) and GPTSwarm (Zhuge
      et al., 2024), take a more comprehensive view by optimizing across the combinatorial space of agent
118
      components. Despite their scope, these methods rely heavily on numerical metrics that are often
119
      inadequate for real-world tasks like software development or creative writing. Additionally, they
120   struggle to optimize multiple components simultaneously or adapt dynamically to changes in the
121   agent pipeline.
122
123
      Gradient-Based Approaches for Agent Optimization. Recent advancements have introduced
      gradient descent-inspired techniques to optimize prompts more effectively. ProTeGi (Pryzant et al.,
124
      2023) is among the first to use natural language feedback—referred to as textual gradients—to
125
      iteratively refine prompts. However, as a first-order optimization method, ProTeGi adjusts based only
126   on immediate feedback from a single iteration, limiting its capacity to handle more complex, multi-
127   step tasks. Agent Symbolic Learning (ASL) (Zhou et al., 2024) extended this concept by treating
128   the entire agent system—including prompts, tools, and configurations—as learnable components,
129   much like backpropagation in neural networks. This allows for a more comprehensive optimization
130   but remains dependent on immediate feedback from each iteration. Textgrad (Yuksekgonul et al.,
131   2024) further advanced this first-order gradient approach by optimizing LLM responses using natural
132   language feedback. By treating feedback as a gradient, Textgrad refines responses without directly
133   altering the model’s parameters. While effective for simpler tasks, Textgrad struggles with deeper,
134
      multi-step optimizations, frequently getting stuck in suboptimal states.
135   To address these limitations, momentum-based methods (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) have been
136   introduced. These techniques track feedback trends across iterations, adjusting step sizes when
137   feedback becomes repetitive. This approach helps break stagnation but can sometimes lead to
138   overshooting, where adjustments are too drastic, destabilizing the optimization process. While
139   momentum-based methods provide more variation, they may still lack the fine-tuned control needed
140
      for long-term improvement.
141   HessianGrad: Optimization Through Response Evolution. HessianGrad enhances traditional
142   optimization methods by focusing on how responses evolve over multiple iterations, enabling more
143   refined adjustments throughout the process. Instead of relying solely on immediate feedback, our
144   approach tracks the evolving relationship between consecutive prompts and their corresponding
145   responses. This parallels second-order methods in traditional optimization, where the the Hessian
146
      matrix is used to capture changes in the gradient to guide more precise adjustments. However, rather
      than directly computing numerical second derivatives, we model these iterative shifts in responses to
147
      inform our adjustments, giving the system a broader understanding of response dynamics over time.
148
      The key advantages of our approach include:
149
150   • Response Evolution Awareness: HessianGrad monitors changes across iterations, allowing for
151     more refined and adaptive optimization, unlike first-order methods that rely only on immediate
152
        feedback.
      • Avoiding Local Optima: By tracking iterative changes, HessianGrad prevents models from getting
153
        stuck in suboptimal solutions, effectively overcoming a common limitation of first-order methods.
154
      • Stabilized Optimization: Unlike momentum methods, which risk overshooting due to large
155     adjustments, HessianGrad applies carefully measured adjustments that ensure smoother and more
156     consistent progress throughout the optimization process.
157
158
      Key Differences Between momentum-based methods and HessianGrad. The primary distinction
      between momentum-based methods and our HessianGrad approach lies in how they address feedback
159
      and response adaptation. Momentum-based methods focus on feedback similarity across iterations,
160
      making larger adjustments when feedback becomes repetitive. However, they do not account for
161

      deeper changes in how the responses themselves evolve, overlooking key aspects like shifts in the
163   underlying response patterns.
164
165   In contrast, HessianGrad directly tracks how responses change over time, focusing on the long-term
166
      evolution of the model’s outputs. This allows our method to adapt more effectively by considering
      both the immediate feedback and the broader dynamics of the responses. By doing so, HessianGrad
167
      avoids the issues caused by overshooting in momentum methods and ensures more stable, guided
168
      improvements throughout the optimization process.

178   3.2    OVERVIEW OF O PTIMIZATION P IPELINE
179
      Our method builds upon the general optimization pipeline used in LLM-based systems (Zhou et al.,
180
      2024; Yuksekgonul et al., 2024), introducing natural language feedback (textual gradients) to refine
181
      system responses over multiple iterations, instead of relying on numerical gradients.
182
183   Forward Pass. In the forward pass, the AI system is modeled as a computation graph where each
184   node represents a specific task. Inputs are processed sequentially through the nodes, with each node
185
      generating outputs based on prior results. These intermediate outputs are stored in a trajectory, which
      is later used in the backward pass.
186
187   Language Loss Computation. After the forward pass, an evaluator LLM assesses the system’s
188   performance by generating textual feedback, which serves as the loss. This feedback reflects how well
189   the system’s outputs align with the task objectives and drives the subsequent optimization process.
190   Backward Pass. In the backward pass, similar to numerical gradients in conventional deep learning,
191   textual gradients are backpropagated through the nodes of the system. These gradients, in the form
192   of natural language instructions, indicate how the system’s variables—such as prompts, tools, and
193   decisions—should be adjusted to improve the objective function. Starting from the final node, the
194   system computes the necessary updates for these variables as it moves backward. This process mirrors
195   backpropagation in neural networks, but the adjustments are determined by language feedback rather
196   than numerical values.
197   While the above process sets the stage for optimizing the system, the effectiveness of the optimization
198   depends on how well this feedback is utilized. In this context, different gradient-based optimization
199   methods come into play.
200

      We proceed to evaluate HessianGrad on the solution optimization task. This task aims to refine
      and improve the solution to complex scientific or technical problems, such as questions in quantum
352
      mechanics or organic chemistry. The solution will evolve dynamically through self-evaluation and
353
      critique, challenging the LLM to continually refine its responses. This process aligns with test-time
354   training (Sun et al., 2020; 2024), where models are refined during testing, as well as with recent
355   progress in self-refinement for reasoning tasks (Yao et al., 2022; Madaan et al., 2024; Shinn et al.,

357
      Task Setup: We evaluate solution optimization on two challenging benchmarks: Google-proof
358
      Question Answering (GPQA)(Rein et al., 2023), which consists of expert-level multiple-choice
359
      questions in physics, biology, and chemistry, and two subsets of the MMLU benchmark(Hendrycks
360   et al., 2020), specifically focused on Machine Learning and College Physics. GPQA is a highly
361   difficult benchmark, with experts achieving 81% accuracy and skilled non-experts reaching only
362   22%, highlighting the challenge of the questions. Performance of LLMs on these benchmarks has not
363   yet saturated, making them ideal for benchmarking solution refinement. We use three iterations of
364   optimization for each question when using the iterative optimization methods. The final answer is
365   determined through majority voting across all iterations for all the iterative optimization methods.
366   Consistent with (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024), we use the string-based exact match metric. Detailed
367   task setup is in Appendix C.
368   Baselines and LLM Backends: We compare HessianGrad against three primary baselines for
369   solution optimization: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022), TextGrad
370   (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) and Momentum-Enhanced TextGrad. All methods use the Llama 3.1 8B

      second-order optimization approach makes it highly effective across diverse tasks, ensuring robust

533   In this paper, we introduced HessianGrad, an optimization framework that extends traditional methods
534   by considering the evolution of responses over multiple iterations. Instead of focusing only on
535   immediate feedback, HessianGrad incorporates insights from the similarity between consecutive
536   responses, akin to how second-order information is used in classic optimization. This approach
537   allows for more informed adjustments, addressing issues like stagnation and instability seen in earlier
538   methods. By capturing these iterative changes, HessianGrad achieves more stable and consistent
539
      improvements across tasks, particularly in complex scenarios where simpler methods fall short. This
      makes HessianGrad a valuable step forward in effective optimization for AI systems.
