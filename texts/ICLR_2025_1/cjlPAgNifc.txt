009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving complex chart Q&A tasks requires advanced visual reasoning abilities in
012            multimodal large language models (MLLMs). Recent studies highlight that these
013            abilities consist of two main parts: recognizing key information from visual in-
014            puts and conducting reasoning over it. Thus, a promising approach to enhance
015            MLLMs is to construct relevant training data focusing on the two aspects. How-
016
               ever, collecting and annotating complex charts and questions is costly and time-
               consuming, and ensuring the quality of annotated answers remains a challenge. In
017
               this paper, we propose Code-as-Intermediary Translation (CIT), a cost-effective,
018
               efficient and easily scalable data synthesis method for distilling visual reason-
019            ing abilities from LLMs to MLLMs. The code serves as an intermediary that
020            translates visual chart representations into textual representations, enabling LLMs
021            to understand cross-modal information. Specifically, we employ text-based syn-
022            thesizing techniques to construct chart-plotting code and produce R EACH QA, a
023            dataset containing 3k reasoning-intensive charts and 20k Q&A pairs to enhance
024            both recognition and reasoning abilities. Experiments show that when fine-tuned
025            with our data, models not only perform well on chart-related benchmarks, but
026            also demonstrate improved multimodal reasoning abilities on general mathemati-
027
               cal benchmarks such as MathVista.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made significant achievements, particularly in
032   visual recognition tasks (OpenAI, 2024a; Anthropic, 2024). While they can efficiently handle sim-
033   ple visual inputs, there has been a growing emphasis on complex chart understanding, driven by the
034   widespread use of charts in real-world contexts (Masry et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024). However,
035   addressing reasoning-intensive questions involving charts remains challenging for these models. Ex-
036   isting benchmarks underscore the need for more advanced and generalized visual reasoning abilities,
037   which remain underdeveloped in current MLLMs (Wang et al., 2024c; Lu et al., 2024). In contrast,
038   humans excel in such tasks since they can purposefully identify query-relevant information from
039
      images and engage in step-by-step reasoning (Wang et al., 2024c;a). These behaviors offer insights
      into how advanced visual reasoning could be modeled. Our analysis of the error distribution on
040
      ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022) in Figure 1 also highlights two main types of model failure. Specif-
041
      ically, 62% of errors occur during the initial recognition phase, while 36% arise from reasoning
042   mistakes following correct recognition. It shows that even advanced MLLMs struggle with basic
043   recognition and make superficial errors during reasoning. In light of these findings, learning to solve
044   problems in a human-like manner becomes essential for current models.
045
      One promising strategy is to distill expert trajectories, such as those annotated by human experts or
046
      stronger models (Han et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024; Masry et al., 2024a;b). However, constructing
047
      such high-quality trajectories for complex chart tasks is a costly and time-consuming process. The
048   challenges arise from both the chart and the instruction data. Collecting charts from specific online
049   sources typically involves manual crawling and filtering, followed by extensive human annotation
050   to create relevant questions (Masry et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024c). While some approaches use
051   LLMs to automate Q&A synthesis, they rely on the data table of charts (Han et al., 2023; Masry
052   et al., 2024a), which neglects features like color, layout, and structure critical for visual recognition.
053   Even when MLLMs are used for Q&A generation (Masry et al., 2024b), their performance remains
      suboptimal. Our preliminary analysis (§ 2.2) further indicates that proprietary MLLMs like GPT-

082   contrast, LLMs tend to perform better when processing charts in textual format, demonstrating lower
083   costs and higher accuracy, as also observed by Fu et al. (2024).
084
      Given the limitations of MLLMs in this task, we propose leveraging the strengths of LLMs through
085   an intermediary representation: code. Inspired by the concept of intermediary translation (Zarech-
086   nak, 1986; Léon, 2007), which refers to using a bridge language to improve translation quality across
087   diverse languages in literary studies, we introduce Code-as-Intermediary Translation (CIT). The
088   code acts as an intermediary, translating chart images into textual representations while preserv-
089   ing visual features faithfully. This process enables LLMs to interpret cross-modal information and
090   generate more accurate, visually complex Q&A pairs. Furthermore, it facilitates the adoption of
091   text-based instruction augmentation strategies, such as Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2023) and Evol-
092   Instruct (Xu et al., 2024), to enhance the diversity and complexity of the generated charts. Starting
093
      with 33 seed codes collected from the Matplotlib gallery, we synthesize more chart-plotting codes
      covering diverse types and topics, and then complicate them to create richer ones. Finally, using
094
      the synthetic codes as a bridge, we generate charts (via Python) and instructions (via LLMs) in a
095
      bi-directional process, ensuring the alignment between the two modalities.
096
097   With the CIT approach, we construct R EACH QA, a multimodal instruction dataset containing 3, 249
098   reasoning-intensive charts and 19, 963 Q&A pairs, all at a remarkably low cost of just $300. The
099
      dataset comprises 8k questions focused on visual recognition and 12k on reasoning, designed to
      address the dual challenges of extracting visual information and performing advanced reasoning.
100
      Additionally, we create 500 charts and 2, 000 manually verified Q&A pairs to independently assess
101
      models’ recognition and reasoning abilities. When fine-tuning with R EACH QA, all models demon-
102   strate substantial performance gains across benchmarks, with LLaVA-Next-Llama3-8B (Li et al.,
103   2024) improving by over 30% on average. More importantly, we observe that these gains gener-
104   alized beyond chart-specific tasks to broader multimodal reasoning tasks, such as MathVista and
105   MATH-Vision—an outcome previously unattainable with existing chart-focused datasets. Further-
106   more, we conduct experiments to investigate deeper insights, including (1) the real impact of expert
107   trajectories on reasoning abilities, (2) the effects of recognition- and reasoning-oriented training data
      ratios, and (3) the benefits of mixing general-purpose multimodal instruction data.

129   2     BACKGROUND
130
131   2.1    D EFICIENCIES IN E XISTING C HART-R ELATED DATASETS
132
133   Existing chart-related datasets fall into two categories: benchmarks for evaluation and training data
134   to improve model performance. These datasets are either collected from online data sources or gen-
135   erated by LLMs, sometimes requiring manual annotation or automated question generation. Most
136
      of them focus on basic visual recognition tasks. Though some recent works target more advanced
      reasoning, they often lack scalability. Table 1 summarizes these datasets, with further details below.
137
138
      Chart Properties. The visual diversity is shaped by the variety of chart types and topics (Wang
139
      et al., 2024c). Early datasets like ChartQA and OpenCQA (Kantharaj et al., 2022), sourced from
140
      limited websites, featured uniform styles with minimal diversity. To address this, recent works like
141   ChartAst (Meng et al., 2024) synthesize charts with randomized attributes (e.g., color, fonts) using
142   LLMs. However, beyond the superficial variations in chart appearance, many of them overlook
143   the visual complexity (Zeng et al., 2024). As models evolve, simple style changes no longer pose
144   challenges. Datasets like CharXiv and MMC (Liu et al., 2024a), which include complex scientific
145   charts from arXiv papers, naturally exhibit greater complexity in recognition. Additionally, the
146   textual format of charts is critical, as it can be used to expand the datasets via LLMs.
147
148   Q&A Properties. Some benchmarks like PlotQA and ChartBench (Xu et al., 2023) use predefined
149   templates to generate Q&A pairs, resulting in monotonous and simplistic questions. Other datasets,
150   such as ChartQA and CharXiv, required manual annotation, which improved quality but increased
151
      costs and hindered scalability. With the advent of LLMs, works like ChartLlama (Han et al., 2023)
      and ChartInstruct (Masry et al., 2024a) use them to generate diverse questions from data tables while
152
      also providing rationale annotations for training. However, these methods fail to capture fine-grained
153
      visual elements like color, layout, and structure because they rely on only the data. To address this,
154   ChartGemma (Masry et al., 2024b) uses MLLMs to generate Q&A pairs directly from charts.
155
156   Dataset Properties. While manually annotated datasets like MathVista (Lu et al., 2024) and
157   CharXiv provide high-quality data, their development is resource-intensive, typically resulting in
158   datasets of only a few thousand samples. In the era of LLMs, such methods are impractical for scal-
159   ing to the size needed for training large models. Recent efforts, such as ChartAst, ChartInstruct, and
160   ChartGemma, have explored Q&A generation for dataset expansion, but they remain limited by the
161   difficulty of collecting a large set of charts. A more scalable approach is to leverage the generative
      capabilities of LLMs to synthesize charts like ChartBench and ChartX (Xia et al., 2024).

186   3       R EACH QA: S YNTHESIZING C HART Q&A WITH CIT
187
188   R EACH QA is a multimodal Chart Question-Answering (CQA) dataset fully generated by LLMs,
189   consisting of synthetic charts paired with corresponding Q&A. It is constructed with Code-as-
190   Intermediary Translation (CIT), a data synthesis method for distilling visual reasoning abilities from
191   LLMs to MLLMs, as illustrated in Figure 2. In the following sections, we describe how we synthe-
192   size the codes (§ 3.1), generate charts and instructions (§ 3.2), and ensure data quality (§ 3.3).
193
194   3.1       I NTERMEDIARY C ODE S YNTHESIS
195
196
      This section will describe how the intermediary code is synthesized. The prompt templates are
      detailed in Appendix H.1.
197
198
199
      Seed Code Collection. We start by collecting a small set of 33 seed code samples, which we
      refer to as Cseed . These samples are sourced directly from the official Matplotlib gallery1 to ensure
200
      quality and minimize manual effort. Collectively, the code samples, each averaging around 40 lines
201
      in length, cover a diverse range of chart types, including common types like bar, line, and scatter
202   charts, as well as more specialized charts such as bubble, contour, and donut charts. All samples are
203   verified for executability to guarantee the reliability of the subsequent code synthesis process.
204
205   Self-Instruct for Diverse Code Generation. To expand the diversity and coverage of the synthetic
206   chart set, we apply the Self-Instruct method (Wang et al., 2023), which has been used to generate
207   new instructions by presenting LLMs with existing ones. Plenty of works have demonstrated its
208   effectiveness (Liu et al., 2024b). In our approach, instead of instructions, we provide code samples
209   as context, guiding the model to generate novel chart-plotting code. At each step, 3 randomly
210   selected code snippets from the code pool serve as few-shot examples (Brown et al., 2020).
211   To diversify chart generation, a chart type is randomly chosen from 10 major and 32 minor categories
212   for the model to synthesize. For chart content, we provide two topic options, allowing the model
213   to freely combine or expand on these themes based on its knowledge, leading to varied topics and
214   data. A chain-of-thought (CoT) process (Wei et al., 2022) is used for code generation, starting with

264   3.2   B I - DIRECTIONAL T RANSLATION
265
266   This section outlines the construction process of charts and instruction sets. The prompt templates
267   are detailed in Appendix H.2.
268
269   Chart Generation through Code Execution and Self-Repair. We generate charts by executing
      the Python plotting code. However, during the generation and evolution process, program errors


                                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      are inevitable. To ensure correctness, we will validate the code before adding it to the pool. When
271   errors occur, the code is not immediately discarded; instead, we apply a Self-Repair method (Chen
272   et al., 2024a), feeding the code and execution results into the LLMs for correction. This process
273   repeats until the code is fixed or reaches an iteration limit, after which the code is discarded if it
274   remains faulty. On average, this approach fixes about 15% of the code generated by GPT-4o, with
275   5% remaining unrepairable and filtered out.
276
277   Instruction Generation through Guided Prompting. After verifying the executability of codes,
278   we use them to create instruction sets in the form of question-answer pairs. Building on prior work
279   of in-context Q&A generation (Chen et al., 2023; He et al., 2024), we guide the model through a
280   two-step process: first synthesizing a batch of questions and then generating corresponding answers
281   separately. To ensure high-quality answers, we also employ a step-by-step approach where the
282
      model first generates detailed calculations and analyses, which are subsequently refined into concise,
      educational answers optimized for learning (Gunasekar et al., 2023).
283
284   The model will generate two types of instructions: recognition-oriented, focusing on visual infor-
285   mation retrieval, and reasoning-oriented, requiring both comprehensive recognition and multi-step
286   reasoning. With minimal constraints on question content, the model is encouraged to explore cre-
287   ative and diverse instructions. Each chart can be used to generate multiple questions, and we filter
288
      redundant ones using ROUGE-L overlap, following the Self-Instruct method (Wang et al., 2023).

333   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
334
335   Benchmarks. We evaluate the models on three categories of tasks. (1) Traditional chart-related
336
      benchmarks focused on recognition tasks, including ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022), ChartBench (Xu
      et al., 2023), and ChartX (Xia et al., 2024). For ChartQA, we use its 2.5k test set. For multi-task
337
      datasets like ChartBench and ChartX, we only use QA tasks. Specifically, from ChartBench, we
338
      select 2k binary QA samples (Yes/No answers) and 2.1k numerical QA samples. From ChartX, we
339   use its 6k QA samples. (2) Novel chart-related benchmarks that assess both recognition and rea-
340   soning abilities, including CharXiv (Wang et al., 2024c) and our R EACH QA test set. For CharXiv,
341   we use the validation set, which contains 4k descriptive questions and 1k reasoning questions. Our
342   R EACH QA test set includes 1k recognition-oriented and 1k reasoning-oriented questions. (3) Gen-
343   eral multimodal reasoning benchmarks, including MathVista (Lu et al., 2024) and MATH-Vision
344   (Wang et al., 2024a). For MathVista, we use the testmini set with 540 math-targeted questions and
345   460 general VQA questions. For MATH-Vision, we use its 3,040 math competition problems.
346
347   Models and baselines. We evaluate a range of multimodal large language models (MLLMs)
348   across three categories. (1) Powerful proprietary models, including GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), GPT-
349
      4o mini (OpenAI, 2024b), and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024). (2) Chart-augmented open-
      source models, such as ChartInstruct-7B (Masry et al., 2024a), ChartAssistant-13B (Meng et al.,
350
      2024), and ChartGemma-3B (Masry et al., 2024b), which are specifically enhanced for chart-related
351
      tasks. (3) Latest general open-source models, including LLaVA-Next-Llama3-8B (Li et al., 2024),
352   MiniCPM-V2.5-Llama3-8B (Yao et al., 2024), and InternVL2-8B (Chen et al., 2024b). Besides, we
353   provide a text-only baseline, denoted as Random (GPT-4o), where we prompt GPT-4o to reasonably
354   guess the answer without seeing the image following Wang et al. (2024c).
355
356   Experiment Details. For each general open-source model, we conduct supervised fine-tuning
357   (SFT) using our R EACH QA training set. We fine-tuned three versions of each model: (1) using
358   8k recognition-oriented Q&A samples (denoted as Reco.), (2) using 12k reasoning-oriented sam-
359   ples (denoted as Reas.), and (3) a combined version with 20k samples (denoted as All). We apply
360   Low-rank Adapters (LoRA, Hu et al., 2022) to all linear layers of the language model and projector,
361   with a LoRA rank of 16, a LoRA alpha of 8 and a learning rate of 2e-5.
362
363   Evaluation Metric. To fully leverage the capabilities, we evaluate all models using a zero-shot
364
      CoT prompt, “Let’s think step by step” (Kojima et al., 2022), following OpenAI (2024a) and An-
      thropic (2024). Thus, to extract answers from the model responses and assess their correctness, we
365
      employ the LLM-as-a-judge method to calculate relaxed accuracy (Zheng et al., 2023). The judge
366
      model used is GPT-4o, and the prompt template for evaluation can be found in Appendix H.4.

475      5.2                              I NTERACTION BETWEEN R ECOGNITION AND R EASONING A BILITIES
476
477      As mentioned before, the recognition and reasoning abilities are likely interdependent. Wang et al.
478      (2024c) also suggest that recognition skills serve as prerequisites for effective reasoning. To explore
479      this further, we conduct an experiment by fixing the total training data at 8k and varying the ratio of
480      reasoning to recognition data from 8 : 0 to 0 : 8. We train the LLaVA-Next-Llama3-8B model and
481      evaluate them on both recognition-focused benchmarks (i.e., ChartQA, ChartBench, ChartX) and
482
         reasoning-focused ones (i.e., R EACH QA-Reas., CharXiv-Reas., MathVista).
483      As shown in Figure 3, increasing the proportion of recognition or reasoning data results in improve-
484      ments in corresponding task performance. Notably, models trained with a higher ratio of recognition
485      data outperform those trained on 20k total data in recognition tasks. In contrast, as the ratio of rea-
         soning data increases, performance gains plateau and even decline when the ratio reaches 100%.


                                                                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      This suggests that simply increasing the proportion or scale of reasoning-oriented data may lead to
487   diminishing returns. We hypothesize that this is because reasoning abilities are partially dependent
488   on recognition skills. When a model fails to accurately interpret an image, its reasoning process is
489   likely compromised (Wang et al., 2024c). Due to resource limitations, we are unable to scale the
490   total data size further in this study. However, we expect that with larger datasets, the interaction
491   between recognition and reasoning data would become even more significant.

493   5.3       BALANCING G ENERAL V ISUAL U NDERSTANDING AND R EASONING A BILITIES
494
495   We are also interested in how models trained on specialized data perform in non-reasoning general-
496
      purpose multimodal tasks. To investigate this, we conduct an experiment using 7 popular gen-
      eral multimodal benchmarks—MME-Reasoning, MME-Perception (Fu et al., 2023), SeedBench
497
      (Li et al., 2023a), CCBench (Liu et al., 2023), POPE (Li et al., 2023b), HallusionBench (Guan
498
      et al., 2024), and OCRBench (Liu et al., 2024c)—along with 5 reasoning-focused benchmarks:
499   R EACH QA, CharXiv, MathVista, MATH-Vision, and We-Math (Qiao et al., 2024). And we test
500   three models: the vanilla LLaVA-Next-Llama3-8B, one fine-tuned with 20k R EACH QA samples,
501   and another trained on 20k R EACH QA samples combined with 20k general-purpose multimodal
502   data randomly selected from the 779k LLaVA-NeXT-Data2 . This dataset is chosen because the
503   LLaVA-NeXT family of models was officially fine-tuned on it, allowing us to approximate its orig-
504   inal data distribution (Li et al., 2024).
505   The results are shown in Figure 4. We can observe that the vanilla model (green area) struggles with
506   reasoning-intensive tasks, while the model trained on 20k R EACH QA data (orange area) improves
507   in reasoning but loses general visual performance. Surprisingly, by incorporating just 20k of general
508   instruction data (blue area), the model not only recovers its general multimodal performance but
509   also retains the enhanced reasoning ability. This results in a well-balanced model with a significant
510   boost in reasoning improvements and minimal drops in general domains.
511
512   6       C ONCLUSION
513
514   This paper investigates the challenges complex chart question-answering (CQA) tasks pose for mul-
515   timodal large language models (MLLMs), focusing on deficiencies in two core abilities: recognition
516   and reasoning. Existing datasets for training these skills often face high annotation costs and an
517   over-reliance on chart data tables, which compromises data quality. To address this, we develop a
518   cost-effective, scalable data synthesis method that distills LLMs’ abilities to boost MLLMs’ perfor-
519   mance. Specifically, we introduce Code-as-Intermediary Translation (CIT), a novel technique that
520   aligns visual and textual modalities through code. Using CIT and LLMs, we create R EACH QA,
521
      a high-quality multimodal instruction dataset to train and evaluate models. Extensive experiments
      validated the effectiveness of our method and dataset, demonstrating significant improvements in
522
      the performance of the fine-tuned models.
