009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal inference seeks to estimate the causal effect given a treatment such as a
012            kind of medicine or the dosage of a medication. To address the issue of con-
013            founding bias caused by the non-randomized treatment assignment on samples,
014            most existing methods reduce the covariate shift between subpopulations receiv-
015            ing different values of treatment. However, these methods split training samples
016            into smaller groups, which cuts down the number of samples in each group, while
017            precise distribution estimation and alignment highly rely on a sufficient number
018            of training data. In this paper, we propose a distribution alignment paradigm
019
               that involves all the training samples without data splitting, which can be natu-
               rally applied in the settings of binary and continuous treatments. To this end, we
020
               characterize the distribution shift by considering different probability measures of
021
               the same set including all the training samples, and reduce the shift between the
022            marginal covariate distribution and the conditional covariate distribution given a
023            treatment value. By doing this, data reduction caused by splitting is avoided, and
024            the outcome prediction model trained on samples receiving one treatment value
025            can be generalized to the entire population. In specific, we exploit the optimal
026            transport theory built on probability measures to analyze the confounding bias
027            and the outcome estimation error, which motivates us to propose a balanced rep-
028            resentation learning method for causal inference of binary and continuous treat-
029            ments. The experimental results on both binary and continuous treatment settings
030
               demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Causal inference aims to estimate the causal effects of treatments for supporting decision-making,
036   where the treatments are usually binary (Shalit et al., 2017) or continuous (Schwab et al., 2020). The
037   gold standard for estimating causal effects is to conduct randomized control trials (RCTs) (Fisher,
038   1936), in which the assignment of treatment for samples is completely random without relying on
039
      the covariates of samples. However, it is usually infeasible to conduct RCTs, and the effects are
      estimated from observational data involving confounding bias, which means that the data distribu-
040
      tion of a subpopulation receiving one value of treatment differs from the distribution of the entire
041
      population (Hammerton & Munafò, 2021), i.e., p(x|t) ̸= p(x), where x is the covariates and t is the
042   treatment value.
043
044   To address the confounding bias, most existing methods adopt a data-splitting strategy to partition
045
      samples into smaller subpopulations according to the treatment values, and then reduce the dis-
      tribution shift between different subpopulations. For binary treatments, one usually splits training
046
      samples to the treated group receiving treatment and the control group without receiving treatment,
047
      and then reduces the distribution shift between the two groups (Kuang et al., 2017; Shalit et al.,
048   2017). For continuous treatments, the natural and widely used strategy is to split samples into mul-
049   tiple groups based on their received treatments. After that, the distribution shift reduction approach
050   for binary treatments can be applied by considering the shift between each pair of groups (Wang
051   et al., 2022). However, data splitting cuts down the number of samples in each subpopulation, and
052   only a part of the samples are leveraged in distribution estimation and alignment. This decreases
053   the performance of distribution estimation and confounding bias reduction, which highly relies on a
      sufficient number of training samples.

054
      In this paper, we propose a distribution alignment paradigm involving all the training samples with-
055   out data splitting, which can be naturally applied to effect estimation of binary and continuous
056   treatments. Rather than reducing the distribution shift between subpopulations receiving different
057   treatment values in existing methods, we characterize the distribution shift by different probability
058   measures of the same set including all the samples. In other words, we model the conditional distri-
059   bution p(x|t) by all the samples, instead of only a subpopulation receiving t which is widely used in
060   existing works (Shalit et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022). By doing this, data splitting is avoided and
061   all the samples can be leveraged to improve the performance of distribution alignment.
062   In specific, we establish the connection between the treatment effect estimation and optimal trans-
063   port built on probability measures involving all the samples (Villani, 2008; Peyré & Cuturi, 2017).
064   We show that for the marginal covariate distribution and the conditional covariate distribution given
065   a treatment value, both the bias of covariates and the bias of outcome estimation errors can be upper
066   bounded by the Wasserstein distances between these two distributions. Motivated by our theoreti-
067   cal results, we propose a method named Optimal transport for Reducing bIas in Causal inference
068   (ORIC), which learns balanced representations to reduce the confounding bias and outcome esti-
069
      mation error jointly. As a result, the outcome prediction model trained on samples receiving one
      treatment value can be generalized to the entire population. Our theoretical results and algorithm
070
      can be naturally applied to both binary and continuous treatments. We conduct experiments on syn-
071
      thetic and semi-synthetic datasets under the binary and continuous treatment settings, and the results
072   demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method compared with existing methods.
073
074   The principal contributions are summarized as follows:
075
             • To address the confounding bias in causal inference, we propose to characterize the dis-
076            tribution shift by considering different probability measures of all the training samples
077            without data splitting.
078
             • We construct the theoretical connection between the estimation error of treatment outcomes
079
               and optimal transport, which measures the distribution shift between the marginal covariate
080            distribution and the conditional covariate distribution given a treatment value.
081
             • Motivated by our theoretical results, we propose a balanced representation learning algo-
082
               rithm to reduce confounding bias and outcome estimation error jointly, and conduct exper-
083            iments under different settings to demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

      2.1   C AUSAL E FFECT E STIMATION
089
      Causal inference has been widely used in real-world applications, such as economics (Davis &
090   Heller, 2020; Kreif et al., 2021; Cockx et al., 2023), healthcare (Sanchez et al., 2022; Karboub &
091   Tabaa, 2022; Van Goethem et al., 2021), and advertising (Chen et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Wei
092   et al., 2021). Due to the confounding bias, the data distribution of a subpopulation receiving one
093   value of treatment differs from the distribution of the entire population (Hammerton & Munafò,
094   2021). For example, in the treatment of a disease, the group receiving surgery usually has more
095   severe conditions compared with the group receiving medication, the patients receiving higher doses
096   of drugs usually have more severe conditions compared with the patients receiving lower doses,
097   resulting in a distribution discrepancy between a subpopulation and the entire population.
098   Most existing works consider the binary and continuous treatment settings. The binary setting only
099   considers whether the treatment is conducted or not (Shalit et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Zhang et al.,
100   2020), and the continuous treatment setting considers the outcome of the dosage of the treatment to
101   estimate the dose-response function (Schwab et al., 2020; Nie et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022).
102   Binary Treatment. Causal effect estimation of binary treatments considers only two groups, i.e.,
103   the one receiving the treatment the one not receiving the treatment (Chipman et al., 2010; Dis-
104   muke & Lindrooth, 2006; Yoon et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020). To address the confounding bias
105   between the two groups, one class of methods is to create a pseudo-balanced group by learning
106   weights for samples. Kuang et al. (2017) proposed to reweight samples by reducing the distribu-
107   tion discrepancy between the two groups, where the discrepancy is measured by the difference of
      the moments. The other class of methods is to learn balanced representations for the two groups


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Johansson et al., 2016). Shalit et al. (2017); Johansson et al. (2022) proposed to learn represen-
109   tations with the minimized distribution discrepancy between two groups, where the discrepancy is
110   measured by the integral probability metric and a theoretical analysis regarding the effect estimation
111   error is provided.
112
      Our proposed learning model can be naturally applied in the binary treatment setting. Actually,
113
      distribution alignment between two groups split training samples into two subsets, also cutting down
114
      the number of samples in each group. By modeling a distribution as a probability measure of all the
115   samples, we avoid data splitting and obtain more samples for learning.
116
117   Continuous Treatment. Causal effect estimation of continuous treatments considers that the treat-
118
      ment lies in an interval, e.g., the dosage of a medication (Imbens, 2000). The natural strategy is to
      partition training samples into multiple groups, each of which receives a similar dose of the treat-
119
      ment. By doing this, the existing methods for binary treatments can be applied. Schwab et al. (2020)
120
      adopted a multi-head architecture to deal with multiple intervals of treatment separately. Wang et al.
121   (2022) calculated the discrepancy between each pair of two groups and reduced the largest discrep-
122   ancy to learn balanced representations. The strategy of data splitting cuts down the training samples
123   in each group, highly affecting the performance of distribution estimation and alignment. Different
124   from them, we characterize the distribution discrepancy by different probability measures of all the
125   samples, avoiding data reduction in splitting.
126
      There are also a few works of continuous treatments without data splitting. Nie et al. (2021) pro-
127   posed a varying coefficient model to estimate the effects of continuous treatment and apply a targeted
128   regularization paradigm for estimation. Different with it, we explicitly reduce the confounding bias
129   and theoretically reveal the connection between the confounding bias and the generalization error of
130   the outcome estimation, which are missing in (Nie et al., 2021). Kazemi & Ester (2024) measured
131   the distribution discrepancy based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and employed an ad-
132   versarial learning paradigm to learn the representations. However, the KL divergence suffers from
133   the issue of gradient vanish when the distribution discrepancy is too large (Arjovsky et al., 2017),
134   and the adversarial architecture is usually difficult to train (Gulrajani et al., 2017). Different from it,
135
      we measure the discrepancy by the Wasserstein distance to avoid the issue of gradient vanish, which
      can be easily estimated by the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi, 2013).

138   2.2   O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
139
      Optimal transport studies how to move mass from one distribution to another with a minimal trans-
140   port cost (Monge, 1781; Kantorovitch, 1958; Villani, 2008). Beneficial from the powerful ability
141   to model probability distributions and exploit geometry, optimal transport has been widely applied
142   in many applications (Peyré & Cuturi, 2017), such as computer vision (Rubner et al., 2000), do-
143   main adaptation (Courty et al., 2014; 2017), data generation (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Tolstikhin et al.,
144   2018), graph data analysis (Peyré et al., 2016; Titouan et al., 2019), etc.
145
      Optimal transport has also been introduced into causal effect estimation of binary treatments re-
146   cently (Yan et al., 2024a;b; Wang et al., 2024). Li et al. (2021) proposed to transport the factual dis-
147   tribution to the counterfactual distribution for estimating counterfactual outcomes. Dunipace (2021)
148   employed optimal transport to learn an intermediate distribution by reweighting samples. Different
149   from the above studies that only consider binary treatments, we address the confounding bias in the
150   setting of continuous treatments. Besides, in the above methods, a distribution usually considers
151   only a subpopulation, while our model represents a distribution by involving all the training samples
152   and a probability measure, improving the number of training samples for distribution estimation and
153   alignment.
154
155   3     P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
156
157   We assume a dataset of the form {(xi , ti , yi )}ni=1 , where (x, t, y) is a realization of random vector
158   (X, T, Y ). Here xi ∈ X denotes the covariates of the i-th sample, ti ∈ T is the treatment value that
159   the sample i received which can be binary or continuous, and yi ∈ Y denotes the outcome of interest
160   for the sample i after receiving treatment ti . Under Neyman-Rubin potential outcome framework
161   (Rubin, 1974; Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983), the observed outcome Y is the potential outcome Y (t)
      corresponding to the actually received treatment T = t.

      Given input covariates X = x and the treatment T = t, our goal is to derive an estimator h(x, t) for
163   the ground-truth individual response function µ(x, t) as follow:
164
165                                        µ(x, t) = E[Y (t)|X = x].                                    (1)
166
167   For simplicity, we will use the shorthand µt (x) = µ(x, t) and ht (x) = h(x, t). The following
168
      assumptions have been made to ensure that µt (x) is identifiable from observational data.
169
      Assumption 1 (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption) The potential outcomes for any sam-
170   ple do not vary with the treatments assigned to other samples, and for each sample, there are no
171   different forms or versions of each treatment value which leads to different potential outcomes.
172
173   Assumption 2 (Ignorability) Conditional on covariates, the treatment assignment is independent
174   of potential outcomes: T ⊥
                               ⊥ Y (t)|X.
175
176   Assumption 3 (Positivity) Conditional on covariates, the treatment assignment is not determinis-
177   tic: 0 < p(T = t|X = x) < 1.
178
      With these assumptions, µt (x) can be rewritten as follows, and we can estimate it as :
179
180                            µt (x) = E[Y (t)|X = x] = E[Y |X = x, T = t].                            (2)

182   Without ambiguity, we omit the random variables to write p(X = x) as p(x) for simplicity.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we estimate the effect of binary and continuous treatments by reducing the confounding
533
      bias from non-RCTs. We characterize the confounding bias by different probability measures of the
534   same set of all the samples, and analyze the confounding bias and outcome prediction error based
535   on optimal transport built on probability measures. Motivated by this, we propose to learn balanced
536   representations to reduce the outcome estimation error and the confounding bias simultaneously.
537   By doing this, we avoid data reduction from splitting which is commonly used in existing methods,
538   and enhance the generalization ability of the model. We conduct experiments on both binary and
539   continuous settings and synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets are adopted. The experimental results
      demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
