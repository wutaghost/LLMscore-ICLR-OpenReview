008                                                     A BSTRACT
009
010              Given large data sets and sufficient compute, is it beneficial to design neural archi-
011              tectures for the structure and symmetries of each problem? Or is it more efficient to
012              learn them from data? We study empirically how equivariant and non-equivariant
013
                 networks scale with compute and training samples. Focusing on a benchmark prob-
                 lem of rigid-body interactions and on general-purpose transformer architectures, we
014
                 perform a series of experiments, varying the model size, training steps, and dataset
015
                 size. We find evidence for three conclusions. First, equivariance improves data
016              efficiency, but training non-equivariant models with data augmentation can close
017              this gap given sufficient epochs. Second, scaling with compute follows a power
018              law, with equivariant models outperforming non-equivariant ones at each tested
019              compute budget. Finally, the optimal allocation of a compute budget onto model
020              size and training duration differs between equivariant and non-equivariant models.

      A common intuition is that strong inductive biases bring the biggest benefits when little training
055   data is available, and that symmetry properties can just as well be learned from data given sufficient
056   samples and compute. Recently, high-profile models of protein folding (Abramson et al., 2024) and
057   conformer generation (Wang et al., 2023) have received considerable attention for their choice of
058   non-equivariant architectures for geometric problems.
059
      At the same time, there is reason to expect that equivariance is still beneficial in the large-data limit.
060
      Learning means successively narrowing down a hypothesis class based on evidence. From this
061   perspective one can explain (Bahri et al., 2021) the empirical observation that test losses often scale
062   as a power law with the training compute (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Whereas non-
063   equivariant methods start from the space of virtually all functions, equivariant models start from the
064   subspace of all functions that abide by the symmetries of the problem. The learning process may
065   benefit from that by focusing solely on further refining this smaller hypothesis class, narrowing down
066   to the correct solution with fewer training steps.
067   Until the theory of scaling laws is fully understood, the effects of equivariance on scaling is an
068   empirical question, and in this work we study it empirically. We focus on a benchmark problem of
069   modelling the physical interactions between rigid three-dimensional objects described by meshes.
070   This task is known to be challenging (Allen et al., 2022). It is manifestly equivariant under E(3),
071   the symmetry group of rotations, translations, and reflections. We compare a standard transformer
072   architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) to an E(3)-equivariant transformer (Brehmer et al., 2023).
073   In this setup we ask three questions:
074
075         1. How do equivariant and non-equivariant models scale as a function of the available data?
076
               Does data augmentation affect this?
            2. How do equivariant and non-equivariant models scale as a function of training compute?
077
               Does this scaling follow power laws? Are their coefficients affected by equivariance?
078
            3. Given a compute budget, how should one allocate it to the model size and the number of
079            training iterations? Is this trade-off different for equivariant and non-equivariant models?
080
081   In our attempt to answer these questions, we train equivariant and non-equivariant models for different
082
      training compute budgets, trade-offs between model size and training steps, and dataset sizes. We
      then analyze these results both qualitatively as well as quantitatively by fitting empirical scaling laws.
083
084   Our experiments provide evidence for three conclusions. As expected, equivariance improves data
085   efficiency. However, data augmentation largely closes this gap. Second, equivariant transformers are
086   also more compute-efficient, and this advantage persists across all compute budgets studied. Both
087   model classes exhibit power-law scaling behaviour. Finally, the optimal allocation of a training
088
      compute budget to model size and training steps differs between equivariant and non-equivariant
      models. Overall, our findings hint that strong inductive biases may not only yield benefits in the
089
      low-data regime, but can also be beneficial with large datasets and large compute budgets.

092   2    BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
093
094   Neural scaling laws The scaling of neural network performance as a function of model size
095   or training steps has been studied extensively (Ahmad & Tesauro, 1988; Hestness et al., 2017;
096   Rosenfeld et al., 2019; Henighan et al., 2020). Kaplan et al. (2020) first observed that the test loss of
097   autoregressive language models follows a power law over many orders of magnitude. Hoffmann et al.
098   (2022) improved the methodology further and found the “Chinchilla” scaling laws, which still serve
099   as a reference point for many language models. In our quantitative analysis of compute scaling, we
100
      largely follow their approach.
101   Several works have extended scaling laws from model size and training steps to other dimensions:
102   Muennighoff et al. (2023) studied the effect of the training dataset size, which we also discuss,
103   Alabdulmohsin et al. (2023) analyzed scaling of different architecture hyperparameters separately,
104   and Jones (2021) investigated the scaling with problem complexity.
105
106   Scaling laws and inductive biases There has been comparatively little research into the relation be-
107   tween inductive biases and scaling behaviour, perhaps because the transformer architecture (Vaswani
      et al., 2017) is so established in language modelling. Tay et al. (2022) compared the scaling behaviour


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of different architectures. Recently, Qiu et al. (2024) investigated how structured linear transforma-
109   tions in transformers affect scaling laws. The authors conclude that imposing structure in them can
110   improve the scaling behaviour. Our work differs from both of these studies through its focus on sym-
111   metric problems and equivariant architectures.
112
113   Geometric deep learning Geometric deep learning (Bronstein et al., 2021) is a paradigm for
114   machine learning in which network architectures are designed to reflect geometric properties of the
115   problem. One of its core ideas is that of equivariance to symmetry groups (Amari, 1978; Wood &
116   Shawe-Taylor, 1996; Makadia et al., 2007; Cohen & Welling, 2016): roughly, a network f is said to
117   be equivariant to a symmetry group G if f (g · x) = g · f (x) for all elements g ∈ G and all inputs
118   x, where · is the group action. This means that when you transform the inputs into an equivariant
119   network, its outputs transform consistently. An equivariant network thus does not have to learn the
120   symmetry structure from data, like a non-equivariant network does.
121   Equivariance has been found to improve performance, data efficiency, and robustness to out-of-
122   domain generalization in fields as diverse as quantum mechanics and quantum field theory (Pfau et al.,
123   2020; Hermann et al., 2020; Boyda et al., 2021; Gerdes et al., 2023), molecular force fields (Batatia
124   et al., 2022; Batzner et al., 2022; Liao & Smidt, 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023; Batatia et al., 2023),
125   generative models of molecules (Zeni et al., 2023; Igashov et al., 2024), particle physics (Bogatskiy
126   et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2022; Spinner et al., 2024), biological and medical imaging (Veeling
127
      et al., 2018; Bekkers et al., 2018; Winkels & Cohen, 2018; Winkens et al., 2018; Mohamed et al.,
      2020; de Ruijter & Cesa, 2024; Suk et al., 2024), wireless communication (Hehn et al., 2024), and
128
      robotics (Wang et al., 2022a;b;c; Brehmer et al., 2024). The potential of equivariance to improve
129
      generalization has also been shown theoretically (Sokolic et al., 2017; Lyle et al., 2020; Elesedy &
130   Zaidi, 2021; Sannai et al., 2021; Behboodi et al., 2022; Petrache & Trivedi, 2024).
131
132   At the same time, equivariant architectures are often more complex than non-equivariant architectures.
133
      Some researchers believe that equivariant architectures are more difficult to scale up, but to the best
      of our knowledge there has been little systematic study into this. However, recent impactful works
134
      on protein folding (Abramson et al., 2024) and conformer generation (Wang et al., 2023) found that
135
      equivariant architectures did not offer any benefits and opted for non-equivariant models and data
136   augmentation instead.
137
138
      E(3) equivariance One symmetry that is important in many scientific and industrial applications is
139   the group E(3) of isometries of Euclidean space. It consists of translations, rotations, and reflections.
140   This group is the focus of our investigation.
141
142
      As an E(3)-equivariant architecture, we use the Geometric Algebra Transformer (GATr) (Brehmer
      et al., 2023). It has two defining features. First, GATr uses multivectors from projective geometric
143
      algebra as representations, in addition to the usual unstructured representations. These multivectors
144
      are 16-dimensional objects that can represent various geometric primitives, including absolute
145   positions in space, directions, as well as translations and rotations. Geometric algebra representations
146   power a number of recent architectures (Brandstetter et al., 2022; Ruhe et al., 2023b;a; Brehmer et al.,
147   2023; de Haan et al., 2024; Spinner et al., 2024; Zhdanov et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b). Second,
148   GATr is a transformer. It processes inputs in the form of a set of tokens. Pairwise interactions are
149   not computed through local message passing, as in many other equivariant architectures, but through
150   an equivariant dot-product attention mechanism that is compatible with efficient implementations
151   like FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022). We choose GATr as the equivariant model for our scaling
152   investigation because of this similarity to the standard transformer.

154   3     P ROBLEM SETUP
155
156   3.1   B ENCHMARK PROBLEM
157
158   Desiderata A benchmark task for this empirical scaling study should be characterized by a low
159   floor and a high ceiling: a small model trained on few samples should perform poorly, while a large
160   model trained on many samples should score orders of magnitude better. To study data scaling, we
161   need a large number of training samples. To study equivariance, we look for a geometric problem in
      which the symmetries and representations are known and exact.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Rigid-body modelling problem We choose a rigid-body modelling problem as our benchmark.
163   Three-dimensional meshes are initialized at some position, orientation, and velocity; they then interact
164   with each other under gravity and collision forces. Concretely, the inputs to the network consist of
165   a set of triangular meshes for two time points t = t0 , t0 + ∆t, and the task is to predict all mesh
166   vertices at time t = t0 + 2∆t. As a loss function and evaluation metric, we use the mean squared
167   error of the predicted mesh vertex positions.
168
      This problem satisfies all desiderata for our study. Rigid-body interactions are known to be challenging
169   to model: collisions are difficult to detect, since they do not usually occur at or near vertices; the
170   forces acting during a collision are nearly discontinuous (Bauza & Rodriguez, 2017; Pfrommer et al.,
171   2021; Allen et al., 2022). Synthetic data can be generated cheaply with physics simulators. Finally,
172   the physics of the process is clearly equivariant under E(3), provided that the direction of gravity is
173   treated as a feature and rotated along with the scene.
174
175   Dataset We construct a dataset of rigid-body interactions following a proposal by Allen et al.
176   (2022). We use the Kubric simulator (Greff et al., 2022), which is based on the PyBullet physics
177   engine (Coumans & Bai, 2016–2024). We recreate the MOVi-B dataset used by Allen et al. (2022) as
178   best as we can, using parameters from their paper and private communication; see Appendix A for
179
      details. Our dataset consists of 4 · 105 trajectories, each consisting of 96 time steps. Each trajectory
      includes between 3 and 10 objects, each consisting of between 98 and 2160 mesh faces. The average
180
      number of total mesh faces in a scene is 5470.

      3.2   M ODELS
184
      In selecting architectures, our main objective is not to achieve state-of-the-art results on the particular
185   rigid-body benchmark problem we chose. That would lead us to highly problem-specific architec-
186   tures (Allen et al., 2022; Rubanova et al., 2024). Instead, we aim for general-purpose architectures
187   that are applicable to broad classes of problems.
188
189   Baseline architecture The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) has become the de-facto
190   standard across a wide range of machine learning tasks. It is versatile with respect to the input data,
191   propagates gradients effectively, and scales well to large model sizes and input tokens. Most scaling
192   studies have focused on transformers as well. We therefore use a standard pre-LN (Baevski & Auli,
193   2018) transformer with multi-query attention (Shazeer, 2019) as our non-equivariant architecture.
194   We represent each mesh face as a token and the positions and velocities of vertices with random
195   Fourier features (Tancik et al., 2020), which improved performance in initial tests.
196
      Even this baseline architecture is hardly “free from inductive biases”. Because the tokens form
197
      not a sequence, but an unordered set, we do not use positional encoding. Therefore, the model is
198
      equivariant with respect to one of the symmetries of our problem: that of permutations of the input
199   tokens. In this respect, there is no difference between the two architectures, and we do not compare
200   to any models that are not permutation-equivariant.
201
202   Equivariant architecture For the E(3)-equivariant architecture, we again look for broad applicabil-
203   ity (at least within the class of E(3)-symmetric problems). In addition, we would like the architecture
204   to be as structurally similar to the transformer, to isolate the effects of equivariance on scaling as well
205   as possible. We therefore opt for the (to the best of our knowledge) only E(3)-equivariant architecture
206   that is based on dot-product attention with unlimited receptive fields, and which also otherwise follows
207   the transformer blueprint closely: the Geometric Algebra Transformer (GATr) (Brehmer et al., 2023).
208   Again, we represent each mesh face as a token. GATr uses geometric algebra representations in
209   addition to the usual scalar channels, and we can represent the geometric properties of a mesh face in
210   these geometric representations. We describe this embedding in more detail in Appendix B.
211
212   Hierarchical attention While we focus on general-purpose architectures, we find that both models
213   benefit from two minor modifications to the transformer blueprint. First, we use a novel hierarchical
214   attention mechanism, in which multiple attention heads use different attention masks: half of the
215   heads are restricted to attend only to mesh faces in the same object, while the other half attends to all
      tokens (mesh faces). This allows us to embed awareness of the mesh structure into the transformer

216
      architecture, while preserving the efficiency of dot-product attention.
217
218
      Enforcing object rigidity Second, we enforce object coherence and rigidity when computing the
219
      outputs. Either transformer model first outputs a translation vector and a rotation quaternion for
220   each mesh face. These are averaged over each object, resulting in a translation vector and a rotation
221   for each rigid object. These E(3) operations are then applied to the input meshes. In this way, the
222   networks by design translate and rotate rigid objects consistently. We describe this procedure in more
223   detail in Appendix B. In preliminary experiments, enforcing object rigidity in this way improved
224   performance substantially compared to directly predicting the positions or velocities of mesh vertices.
225   We also experimented with outputting and exponentiating elements of the Lie algebra for each object,
226   but found that that worked marginally worse.

250   3.3   S CALING - LAW ANALYSIS
251
252   Experiments We perform two series of experiments. First, we study the scaling with compute,
253   in the (practically) infinite-data setting. We vary a training compute budget over three orders of
254   magnitude, between 1016 and 1019 FLOPs. For each FLOP budget, for both the baseline and the
255   equivariant transformer, we perform multiple experiments: each with a different trade-off between
256   model size N and training length D. This requires understanding the relation between N , D, and the
257
      total training FLOPs; we discuss that later in this section.
258   Second, we study the scaling with training data, fixing the training compute budget, the model size,
259   and the number of training tokens. For both models we choose settings that performed compute-
260   optimally in the first series of experiments for a compute budget of 1018 nominal FLOPs. The number
261   of unique samples in the dataset is varied over five orders of magnitude, from 2 · 106 tokens to 2 · 1011 .
262   The lower end of this scan corresponds to training for 6 · 105 epochs, while every sample is seen
263
      only once on the upper end of this scan. For each of these settings, we train a baseline transformer,
      an equivariant transformer, and a baseline transformer trained with data augmentation, in which
264
      symmetry transformations are applied to the samples, independently for each epoch.
265
266
      Counting FLOPs Setting up our experiments (see above) and analyzing the scaling with compute
267
      both require knowing the relation of the total number of training FLOPs C(N, D) and the model size
268
      N as well as training tokens D. This relation is different for the baseline and equivariant transformer.
269
      Following Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022), we perform this FLOP counting in the

270
      limit where the number of model parameters is much larger than the sequence length, which in turn is
271   much larger than 1. The training compute is then dominated by the linear layers. For both of our
272   models, we find
273                                          C(N, D) ≈ ξN D ,                                          (1)
274   where ξ is an architecture-dependent constant.
275
276   For the baseline transformer, famously ξ = 6 (Kaplan et al., 2020). For the equivariant transformer,
277
      the value of ξ depends on the ratio of scalar and multivector channels: a model with only scalar
      channels would also have ξ = 6, while a pure-multivector model would have more weight sharing
278
      and thus a higher FLOPs-per-parameter ratio ξ = 6 · 162 /9 ≈ 171. For the hyperparameters we use

280
281   Note that these nominal FLOPs do not necessarily correspond to the actual compute required to train
282   the model. For one, the assumed hierarchy between the model parameters and the sequence length is
283
      not always satisfied. Second, our implementations of the models may not be able to fully utilize the
      GPUs. We observe this in particular for small models and for the implementation of the equivariant
284
      transformer, which involves many smaller operations and faces CPU bottlenecks. Additional overhead
285   comes from inter-GPU communication, data loading, logging, checkpoint saving, validating, and so
286   on. In our experiments, two models with the same nominal FLOP count would differ by as much as
287   an order of magnitude in real training duration.
288
      So why do we still analyze models in terms of the nominal FLOPs? While they are an imperfect
289
      measure, they do not depend on the implementation and hardware environment, and we believe they
290
      are still the best predictor of the theoretically achievable compute cost after sufficient optimization
291   and at scale.

338   Uncertainties No realistic scaling study directly measures the optimal model performance as a
339   function of some parameters. Reasons for sub-optimality include the choice of hyperparameters,
340   stochasticity in initialization and training, choosing a scaling-law ansatz that does not include the

467   5     D ISCUSSION
468
469   Our empirical results provide evidence for the following three conclusions.
470
471   1. Equivariant transformers are more data-efficient, but data augmentation largely closes
472   this gap. The first (and expected) benefit for the equivariant architecture is that it performs better
473   than a non-equivariant architecture when only little training data is available, as we show in Fig. 2.
474   However, we find a non-equivariant model trained with data augmentation performs just as well as the
475   equivariant architecture, at least when the number of epochs (i. e. repeated uses of the same training
476   sample) is sufficiently large.
477
478   2. The scaling with compute follows power laws, and equivariant models outperform non-
479   equivariant ones at each tested compute budget. Both for non-equivariant and equivariant models,
480   the test loss is well described by the power-law ansatz of Eq. (1), with parameters given in Tbl. 2.
481   The best achievable model performance for a given training compute budget therefore also scales as a
482
      power law, as given in Eq. (8). We find consistent exponents for the two models, but a substantially
      smaller prefactor for the equivariant architecture.
483
484   This shows a second (and perhaps less expected) benefit for the equivariant architecture: for any fixed
485   compute budget, even in the infinite-data limit, it clearly outperforms the baseline method. As we
      show in Fig. 1, this benefit is approximately constant over the range of compute budgets we study.


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Under the assumption that the implementations of equivariant and baseline architectures are similarly
487   efficient and one can achieve the same FLOP throughput, this implies that equivariant models can
488   outperform the non-equivariant counterparts even in the large-data, large-compute regime. In practice,
489   non-equivariant architectures may be easier to optimize for high FLOP throughput, in which case it
490   remains to be seen which architecture is more efficient.
491
492   3. Equivariant and non-equivariant models require different trade-offs between model size and
493   training duration. Our power laws indicate that the optimal allocation of a given compute budget
494   onto the model size and training steps is different for equivariant and non-equivariant transformers, as
495   shown in Fig. 5. For small compute budget, a compute-optimal equivariant transformer is significantly
496   smaller than a compute-optimal baseline transformer. This gap becomes smaller for larger compute
497
      budgets.
498   We hypothesize three possible explanations for this observation. First, the baseline transformer, the
499   more mature architecture, may have a better initialization scheme and thus require less training steps
500   to reach a good performance. Second, the different trade-offs may be related due to the different
501   choice of width and depth between the architectures. A third possible explanation is linked to the
502   internals of the equivariant transformer architecture, which can express certain primitives particularly
503
      efficiently: the free movement and gravitational acceleration of rigid bodies can be represented with
      few multivector channels, thanks to the geometric product operation integrated into the architecture.
504
      This explains why the architecture can achieve a good performance with very few parameters.
505
      However, lowering the loss further requires precise collision detection and modelling. These need
506   substantially more computational operations and a substantial amount of scalar channels, similar
507   to the non-equivariant transformer. This offers a possible explanation for why at a larger compute
508   budget, a model size closer to that of the baseline transformer is compute-optimal.
509
510   Limitations and open questions As much as we would like to, we cannot conclusively settle the
511   question raised in the title of this paper. Our work is limited in several ways. First, we only analyzed
512   a single benchmark problem and two model families. We chose a task with a common symmetry
513   group and general-purpose architectures that are frequently applied to a wide range of problems. We
514   believe it is important to study to what extent our findings generalize to other problems or to other
515   architectures, for instance those based on message-passing over graphs. Moreover, on the problem
516
      we studied, we did not set a new state of the art: we deliberately focused on general-purpose models,
      which do not achieve the same level of performance as highly problem-specific architectures (Allen
517
      et al., 2022).
518
519   Another limitation of our work is that our analysis measures compute with an idealized FLOP counting
520   procedure, as is common practice (Hoffmann et al., 2022). As we discussed in Sec. 3.3, this does not
521   map one-to-one to real-world run time, at least not before further optimization of the implementation.
522
      In Appendix C we show the relation between wall time and nominal FLOPs in our experiments.
523   Finally, we are only able to study training compute budgets of up to 1019 FLOPs per model—this
524   does not come close to the approximately 1025 FLOPs that the currently largest language models
525   are trained for (Dubey et al., 2024). We did not see power-law scaling break down in the range we
526   studied, but we cannot make claims about the extrapolation beyond it.
527   Keeping these limitations in mind, we believe that our findings provide some evidence that symmetry-
528   aware modelling can be a sensible choice even for large compute and data budgets. The benefits and
529   disadvantages of strong inductive biases at scale are important for problems spanning several fields
530   of science and engineering. We hope that our study can encourage further investigations into this
531   question.

824   B    M ODELS
825
826   Input representations For both the baseline and equivariant transformer, we tokenize the problem
827   by assigning one token to each mesh face. In addition to the vertex positions, we compute the central
828   position of each mesh face, the relative vector from the center to each vertex, the surface normal on
829   the mesh face, and the linearly interpolated velocity between t0 and t1 for each vertex and the center
830   of each mesh face. Together these form the input features.
831   For the baseline transformer, these features are embedded using random Fourier features (Tancik
832   et al., 2020) with 128 frequencies sampled from a Gaussian with standard deviation 0.1.
833
      For the equivariant transformer, these features are embedded in the projective geometric algebra
834
      (PGA) described in Dorst (2020); Ruhe et al. (2023b); Brehmer et al. (2023). Specifically, vertex
835   and center positions are represented as PGA trivectors, the relative vector from the center to each
836   vertex as PGA vectors, the mesh face surfaces with the associated normals as PGA vectors, and all
837   velocities as PGA bivectors.
838
839
      Enforcing object rigidity The transformer networks output eight features h for each token (mesh
840   face) that represent transformations like translations and rotations. The final predictions for future
841   vertex positions x̂(t2 ) are then computed by applying these transformations to the current vertex
842   positions x(t1 ).

879   C    S CALING - LAW ANALYSIS
880
881   In Sec. 3.3 we argue that the nominal FLOPs we count are not fully indicative of real-world run
882   time. We illustrate this in Fig. 6, where we show relation between these FLOPs and wall time in our
883   experiments. The throughput of nominal FLOPs varies by two orders of magnitude. Larger models
884   as well as non-equivariant transformers lead to a better GPU utilization and thus increase the FLOP
885   throughput. We expect that the FLOP throughput could be improved by optimizing the batch size or
886   (especially in the case of the equivariant transformer) the model implementation.
