009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited impressive zero-shot performance
012            on inference tasks. However, LLMs may suffer from spurious correlations be-
013            tween input texts and output labels, which limits LLMs’ ability to reason based
014            purely on general language understanding. In other words, LLMs may make pre-
015            dictions primarily based on premise or hypothesis, rather than both components.
016            To address this problem that may lead to unexpected performance degradation, we
017            propose task calibration (TC), a zero-shot and inference-only calibration method
018            inspired by mutual information which recovers LLM performance through task
019            reformulation. TC encourages LLMs to reason based on both premise and hy-
020
               pothesis, while mitigating the models’ over-reliance on individual premise or hy-
               pothesis for inference. Experimental results show that TC achieves a substantial
021
               improvement on 13 inference tasks in the zero-shot setup. We further validate the
022
               effectiveness of TC in few-shot setups and various natural language understanding
023            tasks. Further analysis indicates that TC is also robust to prompt templates and
024            has the potential to be integrated with other calibration methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2024; Abdin et al., 2024)
031   have demonstrated strong generalization ability to excel in a wide range of downstream tasks. In
032   particular, prompt-based learning has been an effective paradigm for LLMs, enabling zero-shot or
033
      few-shot learning (Brown et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023). Ideally, an LLM with advanced language
      understanding capabilities could perform natural language inference (NLI) in a zero-shot setting
034
      without relying on annotated examples. However, research has shown that zero-shot capabilities of
035
      models on inference tasks are currently constrained by the presence of spurious correlations that
036   often lead to biased prediction (McKenna et al., 2023).
037
038   To mitigate spurious correlations, previous work (Zhao et al., 2021; Holtzman et al., 2021; Fei et al.,
039
      2023; Han et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) has explored model calibration, which reweighs output
      probabilities based on various bias estimators. However, existing calibration methods fall short of
040
      addressing the bias that stems from LLMs’ reliance on either the premise or hypothesis for prediction
041
      (McKenna et al., 2023), which we call preference bias. This limits their capacity to generalize in
042   inference tasks. Figure 1 shows an example from QNLI dataset (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), where the
043   task is to determine whether a given context sentence contains the answer to a given question. We
044   observe that the model prediction is incorrect because it relies excessively on the question itself
045   when making the prediction in this example.
046
      Motivated by this observation, we propose task calibration (TC), a zero-shot and inference-only
047
      calibration method. Our work is inspired by mutual information (Tishby et al., 1999; Peng et al.,
048   2005), which measures how much one random variable tells us about another. Intuitively, for a
049   specific task, proper use of mutual information can reveal how much more informative the com-
050   bined presence of premise and hypothesis is concerning the label, compared to their individual
051   presences. Based on this insight, we reformulate LLM inference by factoring out the probabilities
052   of premise-only and hypothesis-only inputs. TC requires no annotated data and is easy to imple-
053   ment, involving only two extra inference stages using premise-only and hypothesis-only inputs for
      each sample. As shown in Figure 1, although the model’s initial answer is incorrect, it finally makes

      2    R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Spurious Correlations in Inference Tasks. The issue of spurious correlations between labels and
098   some input signals has attracted considerable attention in the NLP field. It has been shown that a
099
      model that only has access to the hypothesis can perform surprisingly well on NLI tasks, suggesting
      the existence of hypothesis-only bias within the datasets (Poliak et al., 2018; Gururangan et al., 2018;
100
      Tsuchiya, 2018; Glockner et al., 2018). Similar bias can be observed in QA (Kaushik & Lipton,
101
      2018; Patel et al., 2021), fact verification (Schuster et al., 2019) and stance detection (Kaushal et al.,
102   2021) tasks, where models can achieve remarkable performance without considering any question,
103   evidence and target, respectively. Recently, McKenna et al. (2023) identify the attestation bias,
104   where LLMs falsely label NLI samples as entailment when the hypothesis is attested in training
105   data. In Section 4, we observe that, when provided with premise-only or hypothesis-only inputs,
106   LLMs often struggle to predict not entailment, and frequently make identical predictions with those
107   using both components. This indicates the potential existence of preference bias that enables LLMs
      to perform inference without relying on both premise and hypothesis.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Calibration of Language Models. Previous attempts to mitigate spurious correlations include
109   training a debiased model with residual fitting (He et al., 2019) or a debiased training set (Wu et al.,
110   2022). However, these methods necessitate fine-tuning, and thus pose challenges for pursuing effi-
111   cient LLMs. Zhao et al. (2021) propose contextual calibration (CC), which first estimates the bias
112   of language models with a content-free test input, and then counteracts the bias by calibrating the
113   output distribution. Holtzman et al. (2021) find that different surface forms compete for probability
114   mass. Such competition can be greatly compensated by a scoring choice using domain conditional
115   pointwise mutual information (DCPMI) that reweighs the model predictions. Fei et al. (2023) further
116   identify the domain-label bias and propose a domain-context calibration method (DC) that estimates
117
      the label bias using random in-domain words from the task corpus. Han et al. (2023) propose pro-
      totypical calibration to learn a decision boundary with Gaussian mixture models for zero-shot and
118
      few-shot classification. Zhou et al. (2024) propose batch calibration (BC) to estimate the contextual
119
      bias for each class from a batch and obtain the calibrated probability by dividing the output prob-
120   ability over the contextual prior. In contrast, we tackle the problem from a different perspective of
121   task reformulation, which mitigates bias while recovering model performance across challenging
122   inference tasks.

      Without loss of generality, we use NLI as the main target for discussion in this section and Section 5,
      despite that our method can be used in other tasks. NLI requires distinct types of reasoning (Condo-
166
      ravdi et al., 2003), with the ideal inference depending on both premise and hypothesis (Poliak et al.,
167
      2018). Here, we empirically demonstrate LLMs’ preference bias, which refers to a model’s ten-
168   dency to perform inference tasks without relying on both the premise and the hypothesis. This bias
169   may potentially lead to performance degradation on out-of-distribution inference tasks. McKenna
170   et al. (2023) identify the attestation bias, which can be seen as a special case of preference bias
171   where LLMs falsely associate the hypothesis with entailment.
172
      We explore the preference bias from a novel viewpoint, i.e., we examine whether LLMs can accu-
173
      rately predict not entailment when the premise or hypothesis is absent from the input. Specifically,
174   we evaluate Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 on binary NLI tasks RTE (Dagan et al., 2005), SciTail (Khot
175   et al., 2018) and QNLI (Rajpurkar et al., 2016) datasets where outputs include not entailment or
176   entailment. Ideally, LLMs should be able to discern the absence of premise or hypothesis and make
177   predictions on not entailment. As shown in Figure 2, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 exhibits a tendency to
178   associate premise-only or hypothesis-only inputs with labels other than not entailment, as evidenced
179   by the gap between the bars and the ideal value (i.e., 100%). It suggests the existence of spurious
180   correlations (which we call preference bias) that can distract LLMs from relying on both premise and
181   hypothesis when making predictions. In addition, the performance of LLMs on premise-only and
182
      hypothesis-only inputs varies across datasets. For example, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 exhibits supe-
      rior performance in the premise-only setting for SciTail and performs better in the hypothesis-only
183
      setting for RTE.
184
185   Building upon the observation, we further investigate the correlation between incorrect LLM predic-
186   tions (using both premise and hypothesis) and the labels derived from premise-only or hypothesis-
187   only inputs. Results are shown in Figure 3. We observe that LLM predictions based solely on the
188
      premise or the hypothesis frequently align with incorrect predictions of using both components. For
      example, in the SciTail dataset, over 90% of incorrect LLM predictions align with the labels ob-
189
      tained from hypothesis-only inputs. It reveals that the LLM excessively relies on the premise or
190
      hypothesis alone when making predictions.

511
512   To get a better impression of how TC works, we perform an in-depth analysis on QNLI and present
513   three examples in Table 5. Correct answers are highlighted in bold. Results show that TC accu-
514   rately predicts 61% of the instances that were initially misclassified by the original LLM using both
515   the sentence and the question as input on QNLI (Ex. 1-2). In the second example, despite the
516   incorrect predictions of ‘Original’, ‘S’ and ‘Q’, TC successfully identifies the correct label false,
517   which demonstrates the effectiveness of reducing LLMs’ reliance on individual component (i.e., the
518   sentence or the question) at inference time. However, we also observe that TC encounters failure
519   in some rare cases (Ex. 3), accounting for approximately 5% of the erroneous predictions by the
520
      original LLM. As shown in the third example, TC fails to correct the LLM prediction when both
      ‘S’ and ‘Q’ provide the accurate predictions. Overall, we see that TC can effectively calibrate LLM
521
      predictions by utilizing the predictions of the premise (sentence) and the hypothesis (question).

525   7       C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
526
527
      We proposed task calibration (TC), a zero-shot and inference-only calibration method that reformu-
528   lates inference tasks to mitigate the effects of spurious correlations. Experimental results show that
529   TC achieves state-of-the-art performance on 13 inference datasets under zero-shot setting. Further-
530   more, our method demonstrates its effectiveness in few-shot settings and other NLU tasks such as
531   hate speech detection. TC is also robust to various prompt templates and has the potential to be inte-
532   grated with other calibration methods. To our knowledge, we are the first to consider the synergistic
533   effect of premise and hypothesis over their individual effects in model calibration.
534   A limitation of our proposed method is that it requires extra computational cost owing to the use
535   of premise-only and hypothesis-only predictions at inference time, which could be alleviated with
536   model acceleration techniques such as pruning and quantization. In addition, our method may not
537   be fully compatible with closed-source LLMs such as GPT-4 and Claude-3 due to the potential lack
538   of access to prediction logits, which is also prevalent among most previous calibration methods. We
539   acknowledge that this is not an exhaustive study on all existing tasks, where further exploration of
      extending our method to more diverse NLP tasks should be done in future work.
