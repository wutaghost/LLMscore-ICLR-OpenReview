011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Cropping high-resolution document images into multiple sub-images is the most
014            widely used approach for current Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
015            to do document understanding. Most of current document understanding meth-
016
               ods preserve all tokens within sub-images and treat them equally. This neglects
               their different informativeness and leads to a significant increase in the number
017
               of image tokens. To perform a more adaptive and efficient document understand-
018
               ing, we propose Token-level Correlation-guided Compression, a parameter-free
019            and plug-and-play methodology to optimize token processing. Firstly, we pro-
020            pose an innovative approach for assessing the pattern repetitiveness based on the
021            correlation between each patch tokens. This method identifies redundant tokens,
022            allowing for the determination of the sub-image’s information density. Secondly,
023            we present a token-level sampling method that efficiently captures the most infor-
024            mative tokens by delving into the correlation between the [CLS] token and patch
025            tokens. By integrating these strategies, we develop a plug-and-play Token-level
026            Correlation-guided Compressor module that can be seamlessly incorporated into
027
               MLLMs utilizing cropping techniques. This module not only enhances the pro-
               cessing speed during training and inference but also maintains comparable perfor-
028
               mance. We conduct experiments with the representative document understanding
029
               model mPLUG-DocOwl1.5 and the effectiveness is demonstrated through exten-
030            sive comparisons with other compression methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Document understanding is a vital and complex task that combines computer vision with natural
036   language processing. The challenge arises from dealing with high-resolution document images with
037   diverse aspect ratios, and parsing sparse or dense text with varied formats such as graphic or table.
038   Recently, the rapid development of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) has demon-
039   strated significant capabilities in image comprehension and instruction following (Bai et al., 2023;
040   Dai et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b; Ye et al., 2023c; 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). Some work further
041   enhanced these models by incorporating high-resolution image processing and document parsing ca-
042   pabilities, leading to the development of sophisticated document understanding models (Feng et al.,
043   2023; Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Ye et al., 2023a;b).
044   Despite their impressive achievements, current MLLMs still struggle with efficient document un-
045   derstanding. As shown in Figure 1 (a), these models crop the original high-resolution image into
046   multiple non-overlapping low-resolution sub-images (Dong et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
047   2024c; Xu et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2023b). A large number of visual tokens are encoded by a vision
048   encoder from all sub-images, and then collectively fed into a Large Language Model (LLM). This
049   paradigm makes it hard for MLLMs to scale up to higher resolution documents as a dramatically
050
      growing number of visual tokens have to be dealt with. This significantly hinders the scalability and
      deteriorates the efficiency of current document understanding MLLMs.
051
052   To efficiently process high-resolution images, it is commonly believed that tokens within sub-images
053   have different degrees of informativeness (Kong et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2024b),
      allowing for the compression of sub-images. Therefore, instead of simply feeding all tokens into

068
      To address these challenges, it is essential to measure the informativeness of each token. In this
069
      paper, we try to leverage the correlation between tokens to reflect the relative degree of informative-
070   ness. Specifically, we propose a Token-level Correlation-guided Compression method and explore
071   the token-level correlations from two aspects, patch-patch and CLS-patch. 1) Using patch-patch
072   correlation to determine the compression ratio. We observe that some tokens within a sub-image
073   exhibit repeated patterns and can be regarded as relatively less informative. To identify such less
074   informative tokens, we investigate the patch-patch correlation to quantify the degree of the pattern
075   repetitiveness regarding each token, and define the information density of a sub-image by the pro-
076   portion of highly repetitive tokens. This information density could be subsequently utilized as the
077   cues to determine compression ratio for each sub-image. 2) Exploiting CLS-patch correlation to
078
      sample tokens. The [CLS] token can aggregate and summarize descriptive global information of
      an image when it exhibits a higher correlation with the informative patch tokens. Consequently, we
079
      might detect and sample the most informative patch tokens based on the correlation between [CLS]
080
      and patch tokens. Based on this idea, to effectively sample the most informative tokens, we analyze
081   the CLS-patch correlation and form a probability distribution to guide the sampling process.
082
083   Through the guidance of token-level correlation, we construct a plug-and-play high-resolution
084
      Token-level Correlation-guided Compressor module. It can be applied as plug-and-play to high-
      resolution MLLMs that use cropping methods, improving training and inference speed with slight
085
      or even no performance loss. We conduct experiments with mPLUG-DocOwl1.5 (Hu et al., 2024),
086
      which is a representative cropping-based model in document understanding. Experimental results
087   indicate that the proposed method can maintain comparable performance with DocOwl1.5 while
088   achieving a maximum compression ratio of 11.5%. Further extensive ablation experiments also
089   verify the effectiveness of the method. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
090
091   • We conduct an in-depth study of patch-patch correlation to quantify the pattern repetitiveness of
092     tokens and introduce the concept of information density of sub-image. Furthermore, we reveal
093     the distribution of informative tokens with the guidance of CLS-patch correlation and propose a
094     strategy to effectively sample informative tokens based on the distribution.
095   • We propose the Token-level Correlation-guided Adaptive Compression method. It aims to adap-
096     tively compress visual token according to the information density. This adaptation allows for more
097     efficient understanding of document with diverse types by fitting them into different lengths.
098   • Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach can significantly compress
099     image tokens while maintaining comparable performance. The proposed method achieve an av-
100     erage compression ratio of 66% across different datasets, with a maximum ratio of up to 11.5%,
101     significantly enhancing the model’s efficiency.

      2.2      T OKEN C OMPRESSION
144
145   Introducing high-resolution images into MLLM will significantly increase the number of visual to-
146   kens, necessitating methods to reduce the length of visual token sequences for efficient training and
147   inference. Most existing works choose to use vision-to-text modules with compression capabilities,
148   including using learnable queries along with cross attention mechanism (Bai et al., 2023; Dai et al.,
149   2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Ye et al., 2023b;c; 2024), convolutional layer with strides (Hu et al., 2024;
150   Lu et al., 2024), or simply concatenate the adjacent tokens into a single new token via the channel
151   dimension (Dong et al., 2024). Although these methods demonstrate promising token compression
152   ability, when it comes to cropping-based high-resolution MLLMs, they are still not efficient due
153
      to the different informativeness of different tokens. Previous studies have explored the token com-
      pression for efficient transformer processing within the fields of natural language processing (Goyal
154
      et al., 2020; Kim & Cho, 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Lassance et al., 2021) and computer vision (Meng
155
      et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2021; Song et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2022; Yu & Wu, 2023) independently.
156   However, to our knowledge, investigations into token compression in the multimodal domain remain
157   relatively limited. Concurrently, Shang et al. (2024) propose a PruMerge algorithm to adaptively
158   select unpruned visual tokens based on their similarity to class tokens and spatial tokens. TextMon-
159   key (Liu et al., 2024c) has proposed a Token Filter module that retains the most unique visual tokens
160   for compression. However, they do not thoroughly consider token-level correlations from different
161   perspectives, which limits their ability to adaptively compress visual tokens for diverse data types
      and their capacity to maintain performance.

      The overall architecture of the proposed method is presented in Figure 2. Following previous
175   works (Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Ye et al., 2023b), the model begins by cropping high-
176   resolution input image into multiple non-overlapping sub-images that fit the pre-training size of
177   vision encoder. All sub-images along with the resized global image are fed into the vision encoder
178   to get visual tokens. In previous methods, the visual token sequences are aligned with text inputs via
179   a vision-to-text module. Then all of them would be concatenated with text tokens and collectively
180   fed into LLM for processing, which is extremely inefficient in the face of high-resolution docu-
181   ment images. In this work, we introduce Token-level Correlation-guided Compressor module to
182   adaptively compress visual tokens. The Token-level Correlation-guided Compressor first uses an in-
183
      formation density calculation module to adaptively decide the compression ratio of each sub-image,
      which will be discussed in section 3.1. After that, this module utilizes a correlation-guided token
184
      sampling method to sample the most informative tokens, which will be discussed in section 3.2. The
185
      workflow of Token-level Correlation-guided Compressor module will be detailed in section 3.3.

      3.1   PATCH - PATCH C ORRELATION - GUIDED I NFORMATION D ENSITY C ALCULATION
188
189   Document images often contain large areas of whitespace and color blocks, which visually present
190   repetitive patterns and can be considered relatively less informative and redundant for understanding
191   the image. To determine an appropriate compression ratio for a sub-image, it is necessary to identify
192   the redundant areas and reflect the proportion of unique regions within the image.
193   Since patch tokens typically contain local information within the image, we believe that the patch
194   tokens corresponding to visually repetitive patches are highly correlated. This inspires us to explore
195   patch-patch correlation for identifying redundant tokens. Specifically, we utilize the keys vectors
196   from attention layers in CLIP to represent each token, denoted as K ∈ RN ×Dk , where N represents
197   the number of visual tokens in a image and Dk represents the dimension. We then calculate the
198   cosine similarity between pairs of tokens (Bolya et al., 2022) by the following formula:

203   enables the differentiation between redundant patch tokens from others.

      Based on this finding, we design a method to adaptively calculate the proportion of non-redundant
      tokens in a sub-image, termed information density. Specifically, we calculate the cosine similarity
206
      between pairwise tokens S ∈ RN ×N using Equation 1. For a given token, if the number of tokens
207

208   dant. We calculate the proportion of redundant tokens in the sub-image to the total number of tokens
209   as the information redundancy, noted as r, and d = 1 − r represents the information density. Fi-
210   nally, we treat the information density as the compression ratio of each sub-image. The patch-patch
211   correlation-guided information density calculation is detailed in Algorithm 1.

        To effectively sample informative tokens from sub-images, we design methods that respectively
271     sample tokens with the guidance of CLS-patch correlation from both deep and low layers. Lever-
272     aging the CLS-patch correlation from deep layers, we adopt the Interquartile Range (IQR) method
273     to distinguish outliers (Boukerche et al., 2020) for preserving informative tokens with global infor-
274     mation. Specifically, IQR calculates the first quartile (Q1) and the third quartile (Q3) in a set of
275     numbers. It considers values that are 1.5 times the IQR above Q3 as upper outliers and values that
276     are 1.5 times the IQR below Q1 as lower outliers. As a typical outlier recognition method, IQR can
277     adapt well to different data distributions and determine an adaptive number of outliers. We preserve
278     tokens identified as upper outliers, thereby retaining informative tokens with global information.
279     To further preserve informative tokens with local information, we quantify the relative informative-
280     ness of different tokens through the attention map from low layer. Since the attention map only
281     displays relative relationships, directly setting a threshold is not feasible. Instead, we treat the at-
282     tention score as the probability of sampling each token and perform sampling without replacement.
283     This method ensures that more informative tokens are more likely to be selected.

        We propose a plug-and-play Token-level Correlation-guided Compressor module by integrating the
288
        information density calculation and informative tokens sampling. As shown in Figure 2, the module
289     consists of two parallel branches, the global token mining branch and the local token mining branch.
290     The global token mining branch focuses on selecting and preserving tokens that contain global
291     information. It utilizes the CLS-patch correlation in the deep layers of CLIP-ViT to adaptively retain
292     an appropriate number of tokens through IQR. Meanwhile, the local token mining branch aims to
293     sample tokens that hold local information. To determine the sampling ratio adaptively, we calculate
294     the information density of the sub-image with the guidance of patch-patch correlation and use it as
295     the sampling ratio. To effectively sample the most informative tokens, the CLS-patch correlation
296     in the low layers of CLIP-ViT is leveraged to form the sampling distribution. Consequently, tokens
297
        are sampled without replacement based on the token-level correlation-guided sampling ratio and
        distribution. All sampled tokens are then concatenated as the preserved tokens.

352   ent lengths adaptively. The method maximally retains information while concurrently minimizing
353
      the number of visual tokens for all sub-images, thus significantly enhancing model efficiency. We
      would like to emphasize the distinction between the proposed method and the Token Filter intro-
354
      duced by TextMonkey Liu et al. (2024c). While the Token Filter relies on similarity to identify
355
      tokens for pruning, requiring manual specification of how many tokens to remove, the proposed
356   method uses similarity to assess information density, allowing for an adaptive determination of the
357   compression ratio. Additionally, the Token Filter assumes that tokens with similar counterparts carry
358   less information and prunes them directly. In contrast, the proposed approach focuses on mining in-
359   formative tokens based on the CLS-patch correlation. Experimental results in Section 4.1 highlight
360   the superiority of the proposed method.

      applications. We hope to address these issues in future work.
