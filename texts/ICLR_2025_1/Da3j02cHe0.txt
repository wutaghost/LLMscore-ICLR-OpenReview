009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving inverse problems in scientific and engineering domains often involves
012            complex, nonlinear forward physics and ill-posed conditions. Recent advance-
013            ments in diffusion model have shown promise for general inverse problems, yet
014            their application to scientific domains remains less explored and is hindered by the
015            complexity and high non-linearity of physics constraints. We present a physics-
016            constrained diffusion model (PCDM) designed to solve inverse problems in scien-
017            tific and engineering domains by efficiently integrating pre-trained diffusion mod-
018
               els and physics-constrained objectives. We leverage accelerated diffusion sam-
               pling to enable a practical generation process while strictly adhering to physics
019
               constraints by solving optimization problems at each timestep. By decoupling
020
               the likelihood optimization from the reverse diffusion steps, we ensure that the
021            solutions remain physically consistent, even when employing fewer sampling
022            steps. We validate our method on a wide range of challenging physics-constrained
023            inverse problems, including data assimilation, topology optimization, and full-
024            waveform inversion. Experimental results show that our approach significantly
025            outperforms existing methods in efficiency and precision, making it practical for
026            real-world applications.

032

098
099          • We present a physics-constrained diffusion model (PCDM) designed to address inverse
100            problems in the scientific and engineering domains by efficiently integrating physical
101            knowledge with diffusion models.
102          • Our method offers a feasible generation process using accelerated diffusion sampling,
103            while strictly adhering to the physics constraints by solving optimization problems at fewer
104            timesteps.
105          • From pre-training diffusion models from scratch for each physics-constrained problem
106            benchmark, we demonstrated that our model outperforms existing approaches in terms of
107            accuracy within a feasible time and is readily applicable to a variety of physical problems.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      In recent years, machine learning has been emerging in solving inverse problems in scientific and en-
111   gineering domains. Roughly speaking, its applications can be categorized into two main approaches.
112   The first category is supervised end-to-end methods (Li et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021), which directly
113   learn the inverse mapping from paired inputs and outputs. These methods enable fast evaluations
114   and rely solely on observational data without accessing the physical solvers. For these practical
115   advantages, it has been widely used for physics-related inversion problems (Wu & Lin, 2020; Zhang
116   & Lin, 2020; Nie et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Molinaro et al., 2023). However, due to operating
117   in a supervised manner, they typically require thousands of paired data generated by physics-based
118   solvers in advance and are often limited in the zero-shot scenarios where the observation process
119
      differs from the training conditions. Additionally, because these methods do not explicitly enforce
      physical constraints during inference, they can produce unrealistic outputs that violate the underly-
120
      ing physics, leading to significant issues.
121
122   The second category involves unsupervised (or semi-supervised) plug-and-play methods which typ-
123   ically leverage deep generative models as learned priors (Chung et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Mar-
124   dani et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). These methods do not rely on large amounts
125
      of paired data and instead focus on capturing the underlying distribution of plausible solutions in an
      unsupervised manner. Then, the pre-trained generative prior serves as a regularizer to generate plau-
126
      sible solutions when solving physics-constrained optimization problems. By doing so, they allow
127
      for more flexible and data-efficient solutions to inverse problems, particularly in cases where col-
128   lecting paired data is expensive. For these advantages, it has been emerging for inversion problems
129   in scientific and engineering domains (Raissi et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Mazé & Ahmed,
130   2023; Shu et al., 2023; Jacobsen et al., 2023; Rozet & Louppe, 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Wang
131   et al., 2023; 2024; Taufik et al., 2024). While the methods can provide flexibility and robustness
132   in dealing with physics-constrained inverse problems, the reliance on iterative solvers can result in
133   slower inference times compared to supervised end-to-end methods. Nevertheless, the combinations
134   of learned priors and physics-based constraints hold great promise for addressing the limitations of
135   pure data-driven approaches. We focus on utilizing promising generative models, such as diffusion
136
      models, and improving the efficiency of the optimization process to make this method practical.

454
      Topology optimization (TO) is another example of the physics-constrained inverse design that aims
455
      to identify an optimal physical structure (x) that satisfies elastic equilibrium (A = {C, KU = F }),
456
      with given loads and boundary conditions (y = {F, V0 }). The problem can be represented as
457
458           min C(x) = F T U (x),        s.t.   K(x)U (x) = F,      V (x) ≤ V0 ,   0 ≤ xij ≤ 1.      (20)
459             x

460
      where C is compliance as the objective, F is applied loads, U (x) is the node displacement, K(x)
461
      is the stiffness matrix, V (x) and V0 are the volume fraction and volume constraint, and the de-
462
      sign variables xij are continuous value between 0 and 1. This problem is traditionally solved using
463   the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalization (SIMP) method, which is based on the finite element
464   method (FEM) (Bendsoe & Sigmund, 2013). We utilize a dataset composed of given constraints and
465   their corresponding optimal topologies solved by the SIMP as our ground truth described in (Mazé
466   & Ahmed, 2023). The dataset includes 30,000 optimal topologies with various boundary condi-
467   tions and two levels of test sets with in-distribution and out-of-distribution boundary conditions. We
468   compare PCDM with state-of-the-art approaches that employ diffusion models for topology opti-
469   mization. TopoDiff-G (Mazé & Ahmed, 2023) introduces a diffusion model guided by a gradient
470   update from auxiliary surrogate models in every, which helps to reduce compliance and enforces
471
      boundary conditions at each step of the reverse sampling process. DOM (Giannone et al., 2023)
      aligns the denoising trajectory with the optimization trajectory of the traditional iterative solver.
472
      PIDM (Bastek et al., 2024) proposes a novel physics-informed framework during the training phase,
473
      but not applying correction during inference. We follow the same train/test split and evaluation
474   metrics as described in (Mazé & Ahmed, 2023; Giannone et al., 2023; Bastek et al., 2024) to en-
475   sure fair comparisons. The evaluation metrics include the median residual error of the predicted
476   solution (Res), which quantifies the extent of violating of elastic equilibrium equation, the median
477   compliance error (MDN % CE) which is relative to the ground truth, CE = (C(x) − C(x∗ ))/C(x∗ ),
478   and the mean volume fraction error (% VFE), which is relative to the input volume fraction, VFE
479   = |V (x) − V (x∗ )|/V (x∗ ).
480   As shown in Table 3, PCDM significantly outperforms existing methods in residual and compliance
481   errors, demonstrating that our approach ensures elastic equilibrium and produces more stable struc-
482   tures. Notably, our method achieves a negative compliance error across in-distribution test sets and
483   examples illustrated in Figure 4. This indicates that the generated topology exhibits lower compli-
484   ance, i.e. a more stable structure under the given boundary conditions compared to the topology
485   produced by SIMP. By strictly enforcing physics constraints and utilizing efficient diffusion sam-
      pling, our method consistently provides superior structures compared to existing methods.

      solutions; otherwise, the performance decreases. Performing more than 10 likelihood iterations

521   slight improvements at the cost of increased computational time. Additionally, starting likelihood
522   steps after ts /T = 25% of the reverse process achieves stable convergence. This demonstrates that
523   conducting likelihood steps during only the final 25% of reverse steps is sufficient, significantly re-
524   ducing computational times. This reduction is due to the marginal impact of likelihood steps during
525   the early stages of diffusion reverse steps, as discussed in (Yu et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024).

527   5         C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we presented the physics-constrained diffusion model (PCDM), a framework designed
530   to solve the inverse problems in scientific and engineering domains effectively. By integrating pre-
531   trained diffusion models with physics-constrained objectives, PCDM provides plausible and physi-
532   cally consistent solutions in a feasible inference time. Our approach leverages accelerated diffusion
533   sampling, enabling effective reverse steps in fewer timesteps, while strictly adhering to physics con-
534   strained by multi-step optimizing the likelihood objective at each reverse timestep. Extensive exper-
535   iments on a variety of challenging physics-constrained inverse problems, including full-waveform
536   inversion, data assimilation, and topology optimization, demonstrate that our method consistently
537
      outperforms existing approaches. The results highlight PCDM’s ability to provide high-quality so-
      lutions without compromising computational complexity, making it a practical tool for real-world
538
      applications in fields where complex and nonlinear physics are prevalent.

759
760
      Full waveform inversion. We used CurveFault B and Style B datasets from the OpenFWI bench-
761
      marks (Deng et al., 2022). The original velocity field represents a 700 m×700 m, discretized on a
762   70×70 grid with 10 m grid spacings. We cropped 3 pixels from each boundary, reducing the grid
763   size to 64×64 and the corresponding velocity field 640 m×640 m which represent v(r) in equa-
764   tion 17. We set the 5 sources located from x = 0 m to x = 640 m with evenly spacing dx = 160
765   m at a depth of z = 10 m, and the 64 receivers located from x = 0 to x = 640 m with spacing dx
766   = 10 m at the same depth of z = 10 m. The Ricker wavelet with a central frequency of 15 Hz is
767   used as the source function s(r, t) in equation 17. Simulations were conducted for 1 s with a time
768   step of 0.001 s, resulting in measurements y with the size of 5×64×1000. In this case, we make 5
769   batches of gradient updates for 1 likelihood iterations. We utilize the open-sourced Deepwave pack-
770
      age (Richardson, 2023), which implements the discretized wave equation using PyTorch (Paszke
      et al., 2019) via the finite difference method. A 4th-order finite difference scheme was employed,
771
      along with a perfectly matched layer (PML) of 120 grid points to prevent reflections from the edges
772
      of the model. Due to the significant discrepancy in value ranges - where the true velocity fields
773   v(r) typically span from 1500 m/s to 4500 m/s, while random initialization of the diffusion models
774   or optimization solvers usually follows a standard normal distribution N (0, 1) - we preprocess the
775   training set of velocity fields with min-max normalization before pre-training the diffusion model.
776   During the inference stage, at each likelihood step, the output of the previous diffusion reverse step
777   is denormalized to compute the measurement-consistency term. Subsequently, the updated state of
778   the likelihood step is normalized back into the appropriate range of values for the diffusion models.
779   For the measurement-consistency term, we employed a combination of l1 and l2 loss function (Deng
780   et al., 2022). Specifically, the term is expressed as c1 · ∥y − A(x)∥1 + c2 · ∥y − A(x)∥2 , with c1
781
      and c2 both set to 0.5.
782   Data assimilation. We used the Lorenz 1963 and Kolmogorov flow datasets as benchmarks, follow-
783   ing (Rozet & Louppe, 2023). For Lorenz 1963, the system parameters were set to σ = 10, ρ = 28,
784   and β = 83 . The partial observations y are given by 8x coarsening the original data with noise added
785   with σn = 0.25. The time derivative of the state for residual computations was computed by the
786   2nd-order central finite difference. For the Kolmogorov flow, the state consists of 2-dimensional
787
      velocity fields of size 64×64×2 within the domain [0, 2π]2 with periodic boundary conditions. The
      Reynolds number was set to Re = 1000, the fluid density ρ = 1, and external forcing f followed
788
      Kolmogorov forcing with linear damping (Chandler & Kerswell, 2013). The partial observations y
789
      are obtained by 8x spatially coarsening and 4x temporal coarsening to the original data with noise
790   added with σn = 0.1. To compute the residuals, the time derivative is obtained using three consec-
791   utive frames. The convection and diffusion terms in equation 19 are calculated by approximating
792   the Laplacian and gradient of vorticity in Fourier space, followed by transforming them back to the
793   physical space, as described in (Shu et al., 2023). Data generation and implementation for both
794   datasets were based on (Rozet & Louppe, 2023).
795
      Topology optimization. We used topology optimization benchmarks (Mazé & Ahmed, 2023)
796   which include 30,000 topologies for training, about 1,800 new topologies for the in-distribution
797   test set, and 1,000 new topologies for the out-of-distribution test set. In this problem, it includes
