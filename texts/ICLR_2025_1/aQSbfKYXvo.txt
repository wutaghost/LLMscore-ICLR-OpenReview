011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            FedProx is a simple yet effective federated learning method that enables model
014            personalization via regularization. Despite remarkable success in practice, a rig-
015
               orous analysis of how such a regularization provably improves the statistical ac-
               curacy of each client‚Äôs local model hasn‚Äôt been fully established. Setting the reg-
016
               ularization strength heuristically presents a risk, as an inappropriate choice may
017
               even degrade accuracy. This work fills in the gap by analyzing the effect of reg-
018            ularization on statistical accuracy, thereby providing a theoretical guideline for
019            setting the regularization strength for achieving personalization. We prove that
020            by adaptively choosing the regularization strength under different statistical het-
021            erogeneity, FedProx can consistently outperform pure local training and achieve a
022            nearly minimax-optimal statistical rate. In addition, to shed light on resource al-
023            location, we design an algorithm, provably showing that stronger personalization
024            reduces communication complexity without increasing the computation cost over-
025            head. Finally, our theory is validated on both synthetic and real-world datasets and
026
               its generalizability is verified in a non-convex setting.

      Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as an attractive framework for aggregating distributed data,
031   enabling clients to collaboratively train a shared global model while preserving data privacy. In the
032   currently prevalent paradigm (McMahan et al., 2017), FL is formulated as a finite sum minimization
033   problem focusing on a single shared model. Nevertheless, it has been well recognized that one of the
034   key challenges in FL is the statistical heterogeneity of the client datasets. As the participants collect
035   their own local data, it often reflects client-specific characteristics and is not identically distributed.
036   With high statistical heterogeneity, training a single model for all clients by minimizing their average
037   in-sample loss becomes questionable.
038   To address this challenge, one solution is to relax the common model constraint and solve alterna-
039   tively the following objective in FedProx (Li et al., 2020):

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   In recent years, federated learning has become an attractive solution for training models locally on
091   distributed clients, rather than transferring data to centralized data centers (Mammen, 2021; Wen
092   et al., 2023; BeltraŐĀn et al., 2023). As each client generates its local data, statistical heterogeneity is
093
      common with data being non-identically distributed between clients (Li et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2023).
      Given the variability of data in network, model personalization is a natural strategy used to improve
094
      learning accuracy for each client. Formulations enabling model personalization have been studied
095
      independently from multiple fields. For example, meta-learning (Chen et al., 2018; Jiang et al.,
096   2019; Khodak et al., 2019; Fallah et al., 2020) assumes all models follow a common distribution.
097   By minimizing the average loss, these methods aim to learn a meta-model that generalizes well
098   to unseen new tasks. Multi-task learning methods (Agarwal et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2020) and
099   transfer learning methods (Li et al., 2022; He et al., 2024a;b) study formulation similar to (1), but the
100   statistical rate is established for either the average of all models or only the target model. Therefore,
101   it is not clear if all clients can benefit from the procedure and learn a more accurate model than purely
102   local training. Representation learning (Zhou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024), on the other hand,
103   focuses on a different model structure where the local models can be represented as the composition
104
      of two parts, with one common to all clients and the other specific to each client.
105   In the FL community, personalized FL methods can be broadly divided into two main strands. Dif-
106   ferent from the works mentioned previously, studies here focus primarily on the properties of the
107   iterates computed by the algorithms. One line of works (Arivazhagan et al., 2019; Liang et al., 2020;
      Singhal et al., 2021; Collins et al., 2021) is based on the representation learning formulation, where


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      both the algorithmic convergence and statistical accuracy of the iterates have been investigated. An-
109   other line of works (Smith et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020; Hanzely & RichtaŐĀrik, 2020; Hanzely et al.,
110   2020; 2023), including FedProx, achieves personalization by relaxing the requirement of learning a
111   common global model through regularization techniques. In particular, algorithms and complexity
112   lower bounds specific to the FedProx objective were studied in works (Li et al., 2020; Hanzely et al.,
113   2020; T Dinh et al., 2020; Hanzely & RichtaŐĀrik, 2020; Li et al., 2021), see Table 5 for a detailed
114   comparison. These works study the problem from a pure optimization perspective and cannot show
115   personalization improves learning accuracy.
116   There are only a few recent works we are aware of studying the statistical accuracy of regularization-
117   based PFL in the form of (1). Specifically, Cheng et al. (2023) investigates the asymptotic behav-
118   ior of the FedProx estimator under an over-parameterized linear regression model; neither a finite-

120   strong assumptions like bounded parameter space. By defining a new notion of algorithmic stabil-
121   ity, they establish the non-asymptotic sample complexity for FedProx and induce minimax bound.

       C.3   C OMPARISON OF F ED P ROX WITH A D ICHOTOMOUS S TRATEGY
1823
1824   A simple baseline in personalized federated learning is the dichotomous strategy, where the dataset
1825   on each client is split into two subsets: one is used for global training (GlobalTrain) with pooled
1826   client data, while the other is used for purely local training (LocalTrain). After training, the two
1827   models are compared on the test set, and the best-performing model is selected for each client. Such
1828   a strategy is known to achieve minimax-optimal statistical accuracy under certain conditions; see
1829   Chen et al. (2023c) for a detailed discussion.
1830   Even though the convergence rate coincides with such a dichotomous strategy, we may reiterate that
1831   the key contribution of our work lies in the fine-grained analysis of the FedProx method‚ÄĒa method
1832   that has demonstrated strong empirical performance in federated learning [2]. Through our analysis,
1833   we have, for the first time, established the minimax optimality of FedProx, showing that its statistical
