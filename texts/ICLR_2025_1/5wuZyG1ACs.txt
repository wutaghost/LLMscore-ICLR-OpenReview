                                                        ABSTRACT
010
011
                Inference-time techniques are emerging as highly effective tools to enhance large language
012             model (LLM) capabilities. However, best practices for developing systems that combine
013             these techniques remain underdeveloped due to our limited understanding of the utility
014             of individual inference-time techniques and the interactions between them. Additionally,
015             efficiently and automatically searching the space of model choices, inference-time
016             techniques, and their compositions is challenging due to the large design space. To address
017             these challenges, we introduce ARCHON, a modular framework for selecting, combining,
018             and stacking layers of inference-time techniques to construct optimized LLM systems
019             for target benchmarks. Rather than relying on a single LLM called once, we leverage
020
                a diverse set of LLMs and inference-time techniques, creating LLM systems greater
                than the sum of their parts. ARCHON defines an extensible design space, encompassing
021
                techniques such as generation ensembling, repeated sampling, ranking, fusion, critiquing,
022
                verification, and unit testing. It transforms the problem of building LLM systems into
023             a hyperparameter optimization objective. Given the available LLMs, inference-time
024             techniques, and compute budget, ARCHON utilizes hyperparameter search techniques
025             to discover optimized architectures for target benchmark(s). We evaluate ARCHON
026             architectures across a range of instruction-following, reasoning, and coding benchmarks,
027             including MT-Bench, Arena-Hard-Auto, AlpacaEval 2.0, MixEval, MixEval Hard,
028             MATH, and CodeContests. ARCHON architectures outperform frontier models, such
029             as GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, on these benchmarks, achieving an average accuracy
030             increase of 15.1 percentage points by using all available LLMs.

      1    INTRODUCTION
033
034
      Inference-time techniques are gaining traction as effective methods for improving model capabilities.
035
      Examples include generation ensembling, ranking, and fusion, where models in the ensemble are queried in
036   parallel, their responses are ranked, and the best ones are fused into a single, higher quality output, respectively
037   (Jiang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024). Other types of inference-time techniques are based on querying
038   a single LLM successively (via repeated sampling) and using a voting strategy or unit tests to select the top
039   generation (Brown et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). We divide these existing inference-time
040   techniques into three categories: generative, meaning that new candidate responses are drawn from the
041   models (e.g. generation ensembling and repeated sampling), reductive, meaning that the existing responses
042   are aggregated or filtered to keep the top responses (e.g. fusion and ranking), or comparative, meaning they
043   provide analysis of candidate responses (e.g. critiquing and unit testing), as shown in Table 2.
044      Recent work has made progress towards building robust inference-time architectures, which are systems
045   composed of one or more large language models (LLMs) and inference-time techniques. Examples include
046   Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) (Wang et al., 2024) and LLM-Blender (Jiang et al., 2023b), as well as single-model
047   systems like LeanStar (Lin et al., 2024) and rStar (Deng et al., 2024). However, our experiments show that
048   existing architectures, such as MoA, still suffer from lack of generalization and become significantly less
049   effective beyond the task(s) they were developed on (see Section 4.2). We argue that designing effective
050
      and generalizable inference-time architectures requires:

052     delegate their additional inference budget towards more model sampling calls (Chen et al., 2024; Brown
053     et al., 2024), which can be effective for math and coding tasks. Other tasks such as instruction-following
        and reasoning are shown to benefit from additional techniques, including ranking and fusion (Wang

         In our work, we address each of these challenges. Firstly, we evaluate the utilities of a comprehensive
084   set of existing and proposed inference-time techniques across instruction-following, reasoning, and
085   coding tasks. Using both open-source and closed-source models, we examine a range of techniques such
086   as ensembling, fusion, ranking, critiquing, and verification and introduce new methods such as model-based
087   unit test generation and evaluation (Sections 3.1 and 3.2).
088
         Secondly, we analyze the interactions between inference-time techniques, and explore the benefits
089
      of adding new models and new techniques individually. We find that candidate fusion substantially improves
090   the quality of the final response generation, and when combined with additional techniques like critiquing,
091   verifying, and ranking, can improve generation quality beyond the oracle best candidate from individual
092   (non-fused) responses (Figure 3; Figure 7). Additionally, we find that candidate verification, unit test
093   generation, and unit test evaluation are most effective for reasoning tasks, whereas critiquing and ranking
094   are effective across instruction-following and reasoning tasks (Section 3.1; Table 12).
095
         Thirdly, drawing upon our analysis of inference-time techniques, we present ARCHON, a framework
096   for building inference-time architectures. ARCHON utilizes automatic inference-time architecture search
097   (ITAS) algorithms to maximize generation quality for a wide range of tasks, including instruction-following,
098   reasoning, and coding. Our ARCHON framework and ITAS algorithms draw inspiration from neural
099   architectures and neural architecture search (NAS) (Zoph & Le, 2017; Ren et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2018; 2021),
100   respectively. ARCHON is constructed of layers of LLMs, in which LLMs within the same layer run in parallel,
101   but each layer runs sequentially. The layers perform different inference-time techniques, either transforming
102   the number of candidate responses through generation and fusion (analogous to linear transformations) or
103   reducing the number of candidate responses to improve quality (akin to non-linearities) (Section 3.1). The
104
      number of generators, samples per model, fusion layers, fusion models per layer, and more, are all treated
      as hyperparameters for optimization in our ITAS algorithms (Section 3.3).
105
106      Overall, our work makes the following contributions: (1) We develop ARCHON, an open-source modular
107   framework for designing LLM systems that combine inference-time techniques (Section 3.1). We utilize ITAS

122   2    RELATED WORK
123
124   Scaling Laws of Language Models: Language models (Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023a; Team
125   et al., 2024a; OpenAI et al., 2024) have transformed the field of artificial intelligence across a vast number
126   of domains and tasks. LLMs are pretrained on substantial amounts of textual data before being further aligned
127   with human preferences through instruction fine-tuning (Wei et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022), DPO (Rafailov
128   et al., 2023), KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024), RLAIF (Bai et al., 2022b), and other techniques. As language
129
      models continue to gain improved abilities with further scaling of data, parameters, and compute (Kaplan
      et al., 2020; Gadre et al., 2024), the cost of developing new LLMs is ever increasing, requiring the curation
130
      of trillions of new tokens as well as substantial GPU-hours for pretraining. Furthermore, as the current
131
      state-of-the-art in LLMs are primarily closed-source APIs, such as OpenAI’s GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024),
132   Google’s Gemini (Team et al., 2024b) and Anthropic’s Claude (Anthropic, 2024), it is difficult to effectively
133   explore and push the frontier of existing LLMs without being able to manipulate the parameters of these
134   closed-source models and employing techniques such as continual pretraining (Jin et al., 2021), instruction
135   fine-tuning (Wei et al., 2022), data mixing (Ye et al., 2024), chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2023), among others.
136
      Inference-Time Techniques: Inference-time architectures combine multiple frozen LLMs and inference-time
137
      techniques (e.g., generation ensembling, sampling, ranking, and fusion), achieving superior performance
138   compared to individual models. Notable works include Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) (Wang et al., 2024), LLM
139   Blender (Jiang et al., 2023b), RouteLM (Ong et al., 2024), Smoothie (Guha et al.), and various approaches
140   around compound AI, which are AI systems that use multiple components (e.g. LLMs, retrievers, tool use,
141   APIs, etc.) (Chen et al., 2024; Davis et al., 2024; Lewis et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2024; Kapoor et al., 2024).
142   LM frameworks like DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023) and TextGrad (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) have emerged
143   for orchestrating LMs and other components. Even with a single LLM, various techniques can improve
144   performance by building better reasoning strategies, such as OpenAI’s o1 (OpenAI, 2024b), Chain of Thought
145   (Wei et al., 2023), and Branch-Solve-Merge (Saha et al., 2024), as well as inference-time frameworks, such
146
      as ADAS (Hu et al., 2024) and AFlow (Zhang et al., 2024).
147      Despite these advancements, challenges remain in developing inference-time architectures. Many archi-
148   tectures focus on additional generations (Jiang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024; Davis et al., 2024), which
149   is effective for reasoning tasks (Brown et al., 2024). However, for tasks like chat and instruction-following,
150   techniques such as fusion and ranking are useful (Wang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023b). For tasks without
151   built-in verification, additional compute for reasoning and verification can improve accuracy (Davis et al.,
152
      2024). We still lack understanding of trade-offs between different inference-time techniques. Prior studies have
      explored limited aspects of configurations, often focusing on specific benchmarks (Jiang et al., 2023b; Wang
153
      et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a). It’s crucial to efficiently develop inference-time architectures,
154
      as optimal configurations vary based on benchmarks, available models, and inference call limits (Section 4.2).
155   To address these challenges, we analyzed multiple inference-time techniques (Section 3.1) and developed the
156   ARCHON framework for automating the development of inference-time architectures with ITAS (Section 3.3).

170   3.1   LLM COMPONENTS OF ARCHON
171
      In this section, we discuss the LLM components of ARCHON, which are LLMs that perform a specific
172
      inference-time technique. We test an array of different components inspired by recent work, incorporating
173
      approaches for generating, ranking, and fusing candidates (Wang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023b) as well as
174   approaches for improving candidate response quality through critiquing, verifying, and unit testing (Bai et al.,
175   2022a; Zheng et al., 2023). The components and their prompts are summarized in Table 2 and Section A.1.
176
177
      Generator is an LLM that takes in the instruction prompt and outputs candidate responses. Generators
      can be called in parallel to perform generation ensembling (i.e. calling multiple LLMs in parallel) (Wang
178
      et al., 2024), or sampled multiple times (Brown et al., 2024). When calling the Generators in parallel, you
179
      can sample one or more LLMs one or more times. The number of models, samples, and temperature for
180   generation can be varied based on model configuration.
181
182   Fuser is an LLM that, given an instruction prompt and a set of proposed responses as input, combines these
183
      responses to generate one or more higher-quality fused responses that better address the instruction prompt.
184   Ranker is a LLM that, given an instruction prompt and a set of proposed responses as input, ranks the
185   candidate generations based on their quality, producing a ranked list of responses as output.
186
      Critic is an LLM that, given an instruction prompt and a set of proposed responses as input, produces a
187   list of strengths and weaknesses for each response, which is then used to improve the quality of the final
188   response (Section 3.2; Figure 3).
189
190
      Verifier is a LLM that verifies whether a provided candidate response has appropriate reasoning for a given
      instruction prompt. It proceeds in two stages: Stage #1 takes in the instruction prompt and a candidate
191
      response as input and outputs reasoning for why the candidate response is correct; Stage #2 takes in the
192
      instruction prompt, candidate response, and produced reasoning before outputting reasoning and a verdict
193   (i.e. binary [Correct] or [Incorrect]) for whether or not the candidate response is correct according to the
194   provided instruction prompt and reasoning.
195
196
      Unit Test Generator is a LLM that takes only the instruction prompt as input and outputs a list of unit tests
      that assess the accuracy and relevance of candidate responses. These unit tests are verified by the Unit Test
197
      Evaluator to rank different responses. Each test is a concise statement that can be passed or failed. We make the
198
      number of unit tests generated a configurable choice for the unit test generator but we find 5-10 generated unit
199   tests to be most effective with our set of LM prompts (Section 4.2; Figure 5). For examples, please see Table 10.
200
201   Unit Test Evaluator is a LLM that takes in the instruction prompt, candidate response(s), and set of unit tests
202
      before outputting the candidate response(s), ranked in descending order by how many unit tests they pass. We
      use model-based unit test evaluation by prompting the LLM to provide reasoning and verdicts for each unit test
203
      across each of the candidate responses. By aggregating the unit test verdicts for each candidate response, the
204
      unit test evaluator ranks the candidate responses. For reasoning tasks, particularly coding tasks, it can be useful
205   to compare different candidate responses by the number of unit tests they pass to gauge for quality (Figure 5).
206

      3.2   COMBINING THE LLM COMPONENTS
208
209
      Overview: Inspired by the structure of neural networks (Hinton et al., 1992), ARCHON is constructed of layers
210   of LLM components (Figure 1; Section 3.1). Each layer is composed of sets of these LLM components that
211   are called in parallel, performing a text-to-text operation to the instruction prompt and the candidate responses
212   from the previous layer. Furthermore, like a neural network, some layers perform transformations of the
213   provided list of strings (e.g. the Generator and Fuser components), converting a list of strings into a different
214   list of strings (the numbers of candidates can vary from the original number of candidates). Other components
215   introduce non-linearities into the ARCHON structure, performing filtering of the list of strings (e.g. Ranker
      and Verifier). Ultimately, the inputs and outputs for each layer is always a list of strings, whether that is the


                                                              4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      instruction prompt (e.g. a list with a single string) or a list of candidate responses (e.g. a list of many strings).
217   If a list of strings are outputted at the last layer of the ARCHON structure, the first string in the list is returned.
218
219       Unlike a classical neural network, no weights are learned between the LLM components and the layers;
220
      in turn the ARCHON architecture can be deployed off-the-shelf without any tuning. Additionally, a single state
      is transformed sequentially from the input layer to the final output; this single state is the initial instruction
221
      prompt and the current candidate responses. In Figure 2, we provide an example ARCHON architecture
222
      composed of six layers.

239   Rules for Construction: The LLM components in Section 3.1 can only be placed in specific orders:
240   1. Only one type of module can be present in any given layer.
241   2. Generator components must and can only be placed in the first layer of ARCHON; you can put multiple
242      Generators or a single Generator in the layer.
243   3. The Critic component must come before a Ranker or a Fuser, otherwise the generated strengths and
244      weaknesses cannot be incorporated into generation ranking or fusion, respectively.
245   4. Ranker, Critic, Verifier, and Unit Test Generator/Evaluator layers can go anywhere in the ARCHON structure
246
         (besides the first layer); for each of these components, it must be the one and only module in its layer.
      5. Fuser components can also be placed anywhere in the ARCHON structure (besides the first layer); you
247
         can put multiple Fusers or a single Fuser in the layer.
248
      6. Unit Test Generators and Evaluators are placed in layers next to each other: generator first, then evaluator.

      sequentially (Section A.2).
254   Performance Gains from Scaling Inference-Time Techniques: By scaling both the layers of inference-time
255   techniques and the diversity of inference-time techniques included, we were able to significantly improve AR-
256   CHON performance across instruction-following, reasoning, and coding tasks (Figure 3). In particular, repeated
257   model sampling and additional ensemble models led to substantial gains (Figure 4), leading to 9.3 and 18.5 per-
258   centage point increases, respectively. On coding tasks, additional samples provided the largest marginal benefits,
259
      leading to a 56% boost in Pass@1 for CodeContests when repeated sampling was combined with model-based
      unit test generation/evaluation (Figure 1). Multiple layers of Fusers were found to be particularly effective for
260
      instruction-following tasks, delivering notable performance improvements as the number of layers increased
261
      (Figure 3). In reasoning tasks, incorporating the Verifier and Unit Test Generator/Evaluator modules alongside
262   the Fuser improved performance by filtering out flawed responses, contributing to significant performance
263   gains in tasks like MixEval and CodeContests. For detailed analysis of interactions between LLM components,
264   please see Section A.2, where we perform a series of ablation experiments in which we vary ARCHON compo-
265   nent combinations (Table 12) and the models used in the combinations (Table 13; Table 14; Table 15; Table 16).

321
322   1. Top-K Generators for Ensemble: The top-K models to be used for the initial Generator ensemble,
323      ranges from 1 to 10. The top-K models are the best-K LLMs for the given task, based on their individual

      2. Top-K Generator Samples: The number of samples gathered from each Generator in the ensemble
325      (it is the same for all the models), ranges from 1 to 5. For Code-Contests, we explore high sample settings:
326      [1, 10, 100, 500, 1000].
327   3. Number of Fusion Layers: Ranges from 1 to 4. The last fusion layer will always have a single Fuser.
328   4. Top-K Fusers: Number of models used for each fusion layer, ranges from 2 to 10 and increases by

331      Fuser layer.
332      By combining all the hyperparameters, we create a search space of 18,750 configurations by multiplying
333   each of the configuration option counts together (10 ∗ 5 ∗ 5(4−1) ∗ 3 = 18,750). However, we remove
334   configurations that are not viable: configurations in which the number of initial generations exceeds the
335   context window of the fusers (i.e. 24 candidate generations) and configurations with only one fuser layer
336   but multiple fusers declared. This reduces our search space to 9,576 configurations. For these configurations,
337   we add critic and ranker layers before each fuser layer since they’ve been shown to have added benefits
338   across the benchmarks explored (Figure 7; Figure 3). The ranker selects the top-5 candidate generations
339   to send to the next layer. The unit test generator uses a default setting of 5 unit tests generated.
340   Search Methodology: Within ITAS, we use Bayesian Optimization to select the most promising hyperparam-
341   eter configurations (Snoek et al., 2012; Nardi et al., 2019). For generator ensemble, we add the models to the
342   pool in a greedy manner, starting from the best performing model (on average) on the target benchmarks. For
343   each fuser ensemble layer, we use the same approach, adding the best fuser models in a greedy manner. To
344   rank them, we evaluate their fusion performance on the samples from an ensemble of top 10 generator models.
345   We found that the best generator and fusion models could vary widely across datasets, making it beneficial
346
      to perform these rankings for new datasets (Table 18). For search, we use a 20% sample of each dataset
      for guiding architecture search to improve the evaluation speed while getting meaningful development signal.
347
348      Overall, Bayesian Optimization was the most effective search algorithm for constructing ARCHON
349   systems, outperforming other methods like random and greedy search by more efficiently finding optimal
350   configurations (Section A.6). It found the best architectures in 96.0% of iterations and required 88.5%
351   fewer evaluations than greedy search and 90.4% fewer than random search (Figure 13). The effectiveness
352
      of Bayesian optimization increases with the number of initial testing points, up to around 230-240 samples,
      after which further testing is better focused on configuration search (Table 26). However, for limited inference
353
      call budgets (<20 calls), Bayesian optimization is less effective, and traditional methods like greedy search
354
      may perform comparably (Table 27).
355
356       For our implementation, we use a Python package of Bayes global optimization with Gaussian processes.
357   As inputs, our Bayes implementation takes in the integer lists of configuration choices for the generators
358
      (i.e. number of models and samples), layers of fusers, numbers of fusers per layer, and final verifier / unit
      tester. Bayes algorithm then proceeds to select different combinations of integers from these lists in its
359
      search process, iteratively evaluating each generated ARCHON architecture on the development set to find
360
      the optimal ARCHON configuration. For more information, please see Section A.6.

      4.3   ARCHON BY TASK
456
457   We analyze ARCHON performance by task style: instruction-following tasks that use pairwise ranking for
458   scoring, reasoning tasks that use accuracy-based metrics for scoring, and coding tasks that use Pass@1. On
459   instruction-following tasks like MT Bench, AlpacaEval 2.0, and Arena-Hard-Auto, open-source ARCHON
460   architectures outperform current open-source baselines by 10.0 percentage points, on average, while
461   closed-source ARCHON outperforms current closed-source baselines by 20.1 percentage points (Table 1).
462   On reasoning tasks like MixEval, MixEval-Hard, and MATH, open-source ARCHON architectures outperform
463   existing open-source baselines by 2.9 percentage points while closed-source ARCHON architectures
464   outperform current closed-baselines by 4.2 percentage points (Table 1). On coding tasks (i.e. CodeContests),
465
      open-source ARCHON architectures match existing open-source baselines (0.2 percentage points difference)
      and all-source ARCHON architectures outperform all-source baselines by 2.5 percentage points (Figure 5).
466
      All-source architectures of ARCHON outperform existing all-source baselines by 16.1 and 3.8 percentage
467
      points, on average, for instruction-following tasks and for reasoning tasks, respectively (Table 1).
468
469   Instruction-Following and Reasoning: With ARCHON, multiple models used for Generators and the depth
470   of fusion layers lead to performance boosts on instruction-following tasks, increasing the richness of responses
471
      and allowing multiple iterations for step-by-step instruction-following (Table 19). For reasoning, while
      the performance boost from ARCHON is smaller when we consider the aggregate scores for MixEval and
472
      MixEval-Hard, we do see meaningful increases in performance when we create inference-time architectures
473
      for each individual task under MixEval and MixEval-Hard (Table 31; Table 32). When we create individual
474   ARCHON architectures for each subtask, we see 3.7 and 8.9 percentage point increases in accuracy, on
475   average, for MixEval and MixEval-Hard, respectively. This finding suggests that reasoning tasks (e.g. math,
476   sciences, logic) require more individualized inference-time architectures for their particular queries.

      Coding: We have observed that ensembling, fusion, and ranking techniques have limited impact on
478
      CodeContests (Figure 3). For example, when we apply the general all-source architecture from Table 29

      to CodeContests problems, we achieve small gains from ARCHON (see Figure 5). One contributing factor

482   unit test generation/evaluation, and scale the number of samples, ARCHON’s performance on CodeContests

      4.4   TASK-SPECIFIC AND GENERAL-PURPOSE ARCHON ARCHITECTURES
487
488   Task-Specific vs. General-Purpose: We also compare custom ARCHON architectures, specifically
489   configured to a single evaluation dataset ("Task-specific ARCHON Architectures"), and a generalized ARCHON
490   architecture configured to handle all the evaluation datasets ("General-purpose ARCHON Architectures")
491   (Table 1). For our three model selection settings for ARCHON (i.e. open-source, closed-source, and all-source),
492   we utilize ITAS to find targeted ARCHON architectures for each task (7 architectures total) and find a single
493   generalized ARCHON architecture for maximizing performance over all the tasks (Table 1). The benchmarks
494   are concatenated together and shuffled for generalized Archon architecture search. For examples of targeted
495   and generalized ARCHON architectures, please see Figure 2 and Section A.4.
496      We utilize ITAS to find the generalized ARCHON architectures in Table 1 (Section 3.3), maximizing
497   performance over all of the benchmarks explored except CodeContests. While we use ITAS to find a targeted
498   ARCHON architecture for CodeContests, we exclude the dataset from the generalized ARCHON architecture
499   search since we found that ARCHON architectures for coding tasks are most effective with a different set
500   of inference-time techniques compared to instruction-following and reasoning tasks (i.e. increased model
501   sampling combined with model-based unit test generation/evaluation) (Section 3.2; Figure 3). For open-source
502
      models, we find that our generalized ARCHON architecture only lags behind the specialized ARCHON
      architectures by 3.4 percentage points, on average, across all the benchmarks, demonstrating the robustness
503
      of the ARCHON architecture found by the ITAS algorithms (Table 1). We see similar gaps between the
504
      generalized and specialized ARCHON architectures for closed-source models (4.0 percentage points) as
505   well as the all-source models (3.3 percentage points) (Table 1).
506
507   Insights from Architecture Construction: We include examples of our learned effective generalized
508
      ARCHON architectures constructed by ITAS in Section A.4. For instruction-following and reasoning tasks,
      we found a generalizable ARCHON architecture to be most effective with multiple layers of critic-ranker-fuser,
509
      chained sequentially to improve candidate generation (Figure 9). However, the specific models chosen
510
      for these LLM components could change task by task, with some tasks benefiting from using a single
511   SOTA closed-source LLM for all the components (e.g. Arena-Hard-Auto and MixEval) (Figure 11) whereas
512   others benefited from a diversity of LLMs in their ensemble (e.g. MT Bench and MixEval-Hard) (Figure 9;
513   Figure 10). Regardless of models used, we found that scaling inference layers including critics, rankers,
514   and fusers improved performance on instruction-following and reasoning tasks (Figure 3; Section A.4).
515   For instruction-following and reasoning tasks, the verifier module is more effective than the unit test
516   generation/evaluation module for task-specific ARCHON architectures (Section 3.2; Table 12). For coding
517   tasks, we found a high-sample setting to be the most effective, with added layers of unit test generation
518   and evaluation to boost the quality of the final candidate generation (Figure 12; Figure 5).

      4.5   LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK OF ARCHON
521
522   Parameter Count: The ARCHON framework is most effective with LLM with about 70B parameters
523   or more. When we utilize the ARCHON architecture with only 7B open-source models, we get a notable
524   decrease in performance (Table 21). The best 7B ARCHON configurations lag behind single SOTA (and
525   much larger) models by 15.7% on across all the benchmarks, on average; 7B models work well for ranking
526   but are less effective for critic and fusion.
527
      Latency and Costs: Since ARCHON architectures make multiple LLM API calls successively for different
528   operations (e.g. ensembling, critiquing, ranking, etc.), it can often take 5x more time than a single LLM
529   API call (Section A.4). Furthermore, it can require calling multiple API endpoints for a single query, leading
530   to increased expenditures (Table 22; Table 23). Note that these increases in compute costs and latency
531   translate to higher quality responses, and can be justified in many application domains, such as science,
532   math, programming, and complex customer service issues. For tasks in which speed is most preferred, future
533   work should explore how distillation strategies (Sreenivas et al., 2024) could be used to pack the aggregate
534   knowledge of ARCHON architectures into a smaller LM.
535   ARCHON Components: While ARCHON is a modular framework, allowing the easy incorporation of new
536   LLMs, new inference-time techniques, and even tool use, we only explore seven LLM inference time tech-
537   niques in our work (Section 3.1). The addition of new techniques is a promising avenue for future research. Fur-
538   thermore, while different queries can be best suited by different ARCHON architectures (Table 31; Table 32), the
539   ITAS algorithm selects the best single architecture for the evaluation set queries combined. Future architecture
      search could focus on dynamic selection of ARCHON components, LLMs, and tools on a query-by-query basis.

839   A.2.2    FUSER
840
841   Utility: For every benchmark explored, we found that the Fuser module substantially improved performance
842   (Figure 6; Figure 7; Figure 3). For the single-generation 10-model ensemble of 70B+ models, the Fuser
843
      module improved downstream accuracy by 5.2 points, on average, compared to the single-generation best
      model (Figure 7). When combined with the Ranker module for ranking the top-5 candidate responses, the
844
      Fuser improved downstream accuracy by 7.3 points and 3.6 points, on average, compared to the single-sample
845
      best model and the oracle best candidate response, respectively (Figure 7). Overall, we found that Fuser
846   efficacy increased as more candidate responses were provided, demonstrating that additional candidate
847   generations can continue to bolster inference-time architecture performance when combined with a Fuser.
848
849
         In previous work like Mixture-of-Agents (MoA) (Wang et al., 2024), multiple layers of Fusers was found
      to boost performance on some instruction-following tasks (i.e. MT Bench and Alpaca Eval 2.0). Across all the
850
      benchmarks explored, we observed similar benefits in the ARCHON framework when adding multiple layers
851
      of Fusers (Figure 3). However, based on our results in Figure 8, the number of Fuser layers needed to improve
852   performance varied by task, with some tasks receiving limited benefits from added layers (1-2 point increase
853   in accuracy for MixEval) while others experienced significant benefits with 3-4 fusion layers and more (2 to
854   5 point increase in win rate for MT Bench and Alpaca Eval 2.0). We attribute this distinction to the difference
855   in task requirements, with chat and instruction following tasks benefiting more from multiple iterations
856   of revisions through the multiple Fuser layers, leading to greater diversity in the final generation (Table 19).
857
      Component Interactions: To better understand how the Fuser module works with the other LLM
858   components, we took the single-sample 10-model ensemble of Generators with a Fuser and tried adding each
859   of these components individually: a Critic, a Ranker, a Verifier, and a Unit Test Generator/Evaluator. Across
860   all of the benchmarks, the added candidate response analyses from the Critic improved the Fuser’s ability
861   to effectively merge the different candidate responses, increasing performance by an average of 3.1 percentage
862   points (Figure 3). With the added Ranker, the ARCHON architecture improved the combined Ensemble
863   + Critic + Fuser performance across all the benchmarks by 4.8 percentage points, on average (Figure 3).
      The Ranker proved most effective for style-oriented tasks (e.g. MT Bench and AlpacaEval 2.0) since

875   A.2.3    CRITIC
876   Utility: The Critic module proved effective for every task we explored in Figure 3 and Table 12. With
877   our 10-model 70B+ Generator ensemble and Fuser configuration of ARCHON, the added Critic improved
878   performance on average by 3.1 percentage points across the benchmarks explored.
879
      Component Interactions: While useful for most ARCHON architectures, the added strengths and weaknesses
880
      from the Critic module are particularly useful when combined with the Fuser module, helping guide
881
      generation fusion for a single layer and even useful when placed between multiple fusion layers (on average
882   3.2 percentage point boost across benchmarks in Figure 3). The Critic module was also effective with the
883   Ranker module, providing additional information for comparing candidate responses (Figure 6) and leading
884   to a 5.9 percentage point increase, on average (Table 12).
885
886   A.2.4    RANKER
887
888   Utility: From our results in Table 12, Figure 6, and Figure 7, we found the Ranker to be most effective
889   for instruction-following tasks, where pair-wise comparisons of answers focus on style and adherence to
890
      the prompt. To examine the candidate selection improvement provided by candidate ranking, we compare
      three approaches to the Ranker: (1) random selection of candidate generation, (2) oracle selection of candidate
891
      generation, and (3) the top-ranked candidate selected by our Ranker. For MT Bench and Arena-Hard-Auto,
892
      we find that the ranker improves generation output quality by 3.8% compared to random candidate selection
893   and performs within 2.7% of oracle selection (Figure 6).
894
895   Component Interactions: Based on our benchmark results in Table 12, the Ranker pairs well with the Critic
896
      module; the provided strengths and weaknesses helps guide ranking, particularly for instruction-following
      tasks, improving performance by 5.9 percentage points, on average. Furthermore, the Ranker was also
897
      effective when paired with the Fuser; the filtered list of candidate responses helped improve the final
898
      condensed response produced by the Fuser by 3.8 percentage points, on average (Figure 7). When paired
899   with the Verifier and Unit Test Generator, the Ranker had neutral effects; performances changed marginally,
900   either positively or negatively by 1-2 percentage points (Table 12).
901
902
         Overall, our findings demonstrate the value of added Rankers for instruction-following and reasoning tasks
      when paired with Fusers. We find that when Rankers are used alone with an ensemble of Generators, their
903
      performance lags behind the 10-sample best single model configuration by 3.0 percentage points, on average
904
      (Table 12). Additionally, our findings show the importance of building better rankers for more complex
905   reasoning tasks, such as math and coding, which is a challenge also raised by Brown et al. (2024).
906
907
      A.2.5    VERIFIER
908
909   Utility: The Verifier was most effective for the reasoning benchmarks explored in Table 12. When just using
910   a 70B+ Generator ensemble with Verifier module after generation, the ARCHON configuration lagged behind
911   the ARCHON ensemble and fuser configuration by 1.5 percentage points, on average, across all benchmarks
912   explored. This suggests that the Verifier is most effective when combined with other inference-time techniques.
913   Component Interactions: As noted in Section A.2.2, the Verifier augmented the performance of the Critic
914   and Fuser on reasoning tasks (e.g. Arena-Hard-Auto, MixEval, MixEval-Hard), boosting performance by
915   3.7 percentage points, on average, when combined together with these modules. Overall, the Verifier is
916   most powerful when augmenting additional components for tasks requiring verification of intermediate
917   steps and the final response (Table 12). Therefore, the Verifier was less helpful for instruction-following tasks
      (e.g. MT Bench and AlpacaEval) but more effective for reasoning tasks (e.g. Arena-Hard-Auto and MixEval).

       A.6     BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
1232
1233   Bayesian Optimization is a sequential design strategy for global optimization of black-box functions that
1234   are expensive to evaluate Snoek et al. (2012). It is particularly useful when dealing with functions that have
1235   unknown forms and are costly to evaluate, such as hyperparameter tuning in machine learning.
1236
1237   A.6.1    OVERVIEW OF BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
1238
       The core idea behind Bayesian Optimization is to build a probabilistic model of the objective function and
1239
       use it to select the most promising points to evaluate next. This process involves two main components:
1240
1241   1. Surrogate Model: A probabilistic model (often a Gaussian Process) that approximates the unknown
          objective function.


                                                                        23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       2. Acquisition Function: A function that guides the search for the optimum by suggesting the next point
1243      to evaluate, based on the surrogate model.
1244
1245
1246   A.6.2    STEPS IN BAYESIAN OPTIMIZATION
1247   1. Initialization: Begin with a set of initial points D ={(x1,y1),(x2,y2),...,(xn,yn)}, where xi is the input,
1248      and yi =f(xi) is the objective function value at xi.
1249   2. Model Building: Fit a surrogate model (e.g., Gaussian Process) to the observed data D.
1250   3. Acquisition: Use the acquisition function to select the next point xn+1 to evaluate:
1251
1252
                                                  xn+1 =argmaxa(x|D)
1253                                                             x
1254
1255         where a(x|D) is the acquisition function.
1256   4. Evaluation: Evaluate the objective function at xn+1 to get yn+1 =f(xn+1).
1257   5. Update: Add the new data point (xn+1,yn+1) to the dataset D.
1258
       6. Repeat: Repeat steps 2-5 until convergence or a stopping criterion is met (e.g., budget exhausted, no
          significant improvement).

1261   A.6.3    GAUSSIAN PROCESS AS A SURROGATE MODEL
1262
1263   A Gaussian Process (GP) is commonly used as a surrogate model in Bayesian Optimization. It is defined
1264   by a mean function µ(x) and a covariance function (kernel) k(x,x′):
1265
1266
1267
                                               f(x)∼GP(µ(x),k(x,x′))
1268
1269
         Given a set of observations D, the GP provides a predictive distribution for the objective function at a
1270
       new point x:
1271
1272
       • Predictive Mean: The expected value of the function at x:
1273
1274
1275                                             µ(x|D)=kn(x)T K−1
                                                                n y
1276
1277       where kn(x) is the covariance vector between x and the training points, and Kn is the covariance
1278     matrix of the training points.
1279   • Predictive Variance: The uncertainty in the function value at x:
1280
1281
                                         σ2(x|D)=k(x,x)−kn(x)T K−1
                                                                n kn (x)

1286
1287   Acquisition functions guide the search for the optimum by balancing exploration (trying out areas with high
1288   uncertainty) and exploitation (focusing on areas with high predicted values). Common acquisition functions
1289   include:
1290
1291   1. Expected Improvement (EI):
1292
1293                                         EI(x)=E[max(0,f(x)−f(x+))]
1294
1295
             where f(x+) is the best observed value so far.

1297

1302   3. Upper Confidence Bound (UCB):
1303
1304                                           UCB(x)=µ(x|D)+κσ(x|D)

             where κ controls the trade-off between exploration and exploitation.

1334   A.7     BAYES OPTIMIZATION VS. ALTERNATIVE APPROACHES
1335
1336   Search Techniques: Within the hyperparameter space, we explored three search algorithms for automating
1337
       the development of inference-time architectures:
1338   1. Random Search: Randomly selects a combination of hyperparameters for our ARCHON architecture.
1339   2. Greedy Search: Starting with a base ARCHON configuration, marginally changes each hyperparameter
1340      and test if it improves performance or not. If it does, incorporate the change. If not, move on to the next
1341      hyperparameter.
1342   3. Bayesian Optimization: Efficiently selects the most promising hyperparameter configurations for
1343      ARCHON by building a probabilistic surrogate model and leveraging an acquisition function for
1344
          hyperparameter selection (Snoek et al., 2012; Nardi et al., 2019) (Section A.6).
1345      To get our model ranking for the benchmark, we calculate the model ranking by testing each model
1346   individually on a 20% sample of each dataset benchmark in the first stage of the search. To get our fusion
1347   model ranking for the benchmark, we use the same approach, testing each model’s fusion performance
1348   with an ensemble of 10 randomly selected models from the available set. From our experiments, we found
1349   that the best generator and fusion models could vary widely dataset to dataset, making it beneficial to perform
       these rankings for new datasets (Table 18). For search, we use the same 20% sample of each dataset that was


                                                             25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       used for evaluating generation and fusion, allowing us to guide architecture search with improved evaluation
1351   speed while getting meaningful development signal.
1352
1353   Comparing Search Algorithms: In Figure 13, we compare the effectiveness of each search algorithm
1354
       on our explored benchmarks. While random search guarantees the optimal ARCHON configuration, we found
       Bayesian optimization to be most effective in terms of tradeoff between finding the optimal configurations
1355
       and minimizing the number of configurations tested. For 96.0% percent of the search iterations tested in
1356
       Figure 13, we found that Bayesian optimization had the optimal configuration amongst the four explored
1357   search algorithms. We use 230 initial samples for our Bayes Optimization architecture search (Section A.6).
1358   Bayesian optimization also found the best architecture configuration in 88.5% less evaluations than greedy
1359   search and 90.4% less evaluations than random search.

       Bayesian Optimization Analysis: In Table 26, we explore how the number of initial testing points, the
1361

1362
       optimization. Additional initial testing points continue improving search efficacy up until 230-240 samples,

1364   ARCHON (e.g. <20 inference calls), Bayesian optimization proved less effective, performing more similarly to
