011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities through
014            wide-scale pre-training on a wide range of domains. However, they often suf-
015            fer from catastrophic forgetting when learning sequential tasks. In this paper,
016
               we propose a novel parameter-efficient approach for continual learning in LLMs,
               which empirically explores the role of different effective layerwise ranks, lever-
017
               aging lower ranks to mitigate catastrophic forgetting of previous tasks and higher
018
               ranks to enhance generalization on new tasks. By employing a subspace similar-
019            ity metric that evaluates the orthogonality of low-rank subspaces between tasks,
020            we gradually increase the rank of layerwise matrices for each new task, mini-
021            mizing interference with previously learned tasks while enhancing generalization.
022            Experimental results on standard continual learning benchmarks and challenging
023            math benchmarks demonstrate that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-
024            art approaches, effectively mitigating forgetting, improving task performance, and
025            maintaining strong generalization to unseen tasks in a memory-efficient manner.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As Large Language Models (LLMs) (Raffel et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Achiam et al.,
031   2023; Touvron et al., 2023) continue to scale, adapting pre-trained foundation models to numerous
032   downstream tasks become common practice, but fully fine-tuning these models is impractical given
033
      the large model sizes. Consequently, low-rank adaptation methods like LoRA Hu et al. (2021)
      and its multiple variants (Zhang et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024) have emerged to enable parameter-
034
      efficient fine-tuning for LLMs.
035
036   While pre-trained LLMs have achieved great success on fine-tuning on static tasks, continual learn-
037   ing (CL), the process of learning multiple sequential tasks, remains a significant challenge Wu et al.
038   (2021; 2024). Two key obstacles are (i) catastrophic forgetting, where a model’s performance on
039
      earlier tasks degrades when trained on new tasks (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Ratcliff, 1990), and
      (ii) generalization ability, where the previously learned model improves new tasks. Within the realm
040
      of LLMs, CL goes beyond enhancing linguistic and reasoning abilities, involving complex pro-
041
      cesses such as continual pretraining Jin et al. (2021), continual instruction Zhang et al. (2023c), and
042   continual alignment Zhang et al. (2023a).
043
044   Although existing LoRA-based parameter-efficient tuning (PET) methods for CL have mitigated
045
      the forgetting issue, such as O-LoRA Wang et al. (2023) that incrementally learns new tasks in or-
      thogonal subspaces, most approaches apply the same rank across all layers in the model. However,
046
      the effectiveness of heterogeneous nature of different layers in overparameterized models has been
047
      extensively studied as highlighted in Zhang et al. (2022). Moreover, in the context of pre-training
048   and adaptation for LLMs, AdaRank Dong (2024) introduces a simple model disagreement-based
049   technique for determining layerwise ranks for low-rank adaptation induced by random module per-
050   turbations. Additionally, both AdaLoRA Zhang et al. (2023b) and SoRA Ding et al. (2023) exploit
051   the relationship between the rank and the singular value decomposition of the weight update ma-
052   trices to dynamically adjust layerwise ranks during adaptation. Specifically, AdaLoRA achieves
053   this by pruning the singular values associated with less significant updates, while SoRA employs a
      learnable gating mechanism that gradually reduces the rank as training progresses. These findings

085   approach in CL for LLMs within the PET framework, one that utilizes the role of the layerwise tanks
086
      to balance catastrophic forgetting and generalization across a continual stream of tasks. Inspired by
      this, we aim to address the following fundamental question:
087
088           How can we design an adaptive parameter-efficient CL algorithm that leverages the
089        forgetting-mitigation nature of low ranks and the generalization strengths of high ranks to
090                                          optimize the trade-off?
091   To answer this question, we propose a novel adaptive algorithm dubbed as CP-Rank (Cautious
092   Passive Low-Rank), that gradually increases the rank of layerwise weight matrices during train-
093   ing among layers. This is accomplished by empirically examining how layerwise ranks affect both
094   forgetting and generalization, with rank adjustments guided by a between-task low-rank subspace
095   similarity metric. Specifically, CP-Rank focuses on the subsequent tasks after the first task. For
096   each task after the first task, CP-Rank starts by setting the incremental LoRA rank to 1, aiming to
097   minimize interference with previously learned tasks from the beginning of new task training. It then
098   applies SVD decomposition to compute the left singular layerwise matrices of LoRAs from both the
099
      current and previous tasks, thus calculating the subspace similarity between their low-rank matrices.
      With this dynamic similarity during training, CP-Rank evaluates the orthogonality of the subspaces
100
      and decides whether to cautiously increase the rank for the current task by allocating additional
101
      low-rank parameters, or whether to passively maintain the current rank, balancing learning of new
102   information with retention of previously acquired knowledge. It is important to note that CP-Rank
103   freezes all previously learned incremental LoRAs during the training of each new task. Our exper-
104   imental results demonstrate that CP-Rank outperforms state-of-the-art methods on standard contin-
105   ual learning benchmarks and excels in more challenging math tasks, such as GSM8K Cobbe et al.
106   (2021) and MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021). Furthermore, our analysis explores the impact of various
107   hyperparameters and evaluates different rank update rules, highlighting CP-Rank’s effectiveness in
      robustness to task orders, mitigating forgetting, and enhancing generalization.

      ■ Summary of Contributions. This paper makes three key contributions: (1) A novel parameter-
109   efficient continual learning method for LLMs that effectively balances forgetting and generalization
110   through cautious passive low-rank updates; (2) Through comprehensive evaluations, our method
111   demonstrates superior performance over existing state-of-the-art approaches both on standard con-
112   tinual learning benchmarks and math datasets; and (3) We provide an in-depth analysis that deepens
113   our understanding of the dynamics of gradually increasing rank within continual learning for LLMs,
114   pinpointing critical factors that drive its effectiveness.

292   3.1.3    BASELINES
293
294
      We compare our method against various baseline approaches:
295   • SeqFT de Masson D’Autume et al. (2019): train all model parameters on a sequence of tasks
296     (without adding any regularization or replaying samples from the previous tasks).
297   • SeqLoRA: fixed-size LoRA parameters are trained on a sequence of tasks (without adding any
298     regularization or replaying samples from the previous tasks).
299   • IncLoRA: incremental learning of new LoRA parameters on a sequence of tasks (without adding
300
        any regularization or replaying samples from the previous tasks).
      • Replay: fine-tune the whole model with a memory buffer, and replay samples from old tasks
301
        when learning new tasks to avoid forgetting.
302
      • EWC Kirkpatrick et al. (2017): fine-tune the whole model with a regularization loss that prevents
303     updating parameters that could interfere with previously learned tasks.
304   • LwF Li & Hoiem (2017): constrains the shared representation layer to be similar to its original
305     state before learning the new task.
306   • L2P Wang et al. (2022): uses the input to dynamically select and update prompts from the prompt
307     pool in an instance-wise fashion.
308   • LPT5 Qin & Joty (2021): continuously train a soft prompt that simultaneously learns to solve the
309     tasks and generate training samples, which are subsequently used in experience replay.
310   • ProgPrompt Razdaibiedina et al. (2023): adopts task-specific soft prompts for each task, training
311
        distinct models per task and using task IDs during inference.
      • O-LoRA Wang et al. (2023): incrementally train new tasks in an orthogonal subspace while fixing
312
        the LoRA matrices of previous tasks.
313
      • PerTaskFT: train a separate model for each task individually.
314   • MTL: train a multi-task learning model on all tasks simultaneously, serving as the performance
315     upper bound for the benchmark.

      4   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose a parameter-efficient continual learning method, CP-Rank, which gradually increases
534   the layerwise rank of incremental LoRAs for new tasks based on the between-task low-rank sub-
535   space similarity metric. CP-Rank not only accounts for the low-rank relationships between tasks’
536   incremental LoRAs but also adapts to the unique low-rank dynamics across different model lay-
537   ers. This approach effectively mitigates forgetting of previous tasks while enhancing generalization
538   on new tasks in a memory-efficient way. We perform extensive experiments on both natural lan-
539   guage processing and challenging math reasoning tasks, demonstrating that CP-Rank captures rank
      patterns effectively in CL and consistently outperforms existing methods.

      A.1     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
759
760
      Continual Learning. Continual learning aims to develop algorithms that can continuously accumu-
761
      late and refine knowledge, especially when handling dynamic data streams. The key challenge is
762   overcoming catastrophic forgetting, where a model’s performance on previously learned tasks sig-
763   nificantly declines after being trained on new tasks. To tackle this issue, existing approaches are
764   generally classified into three main categories: (i) Rehearsal-based methods, which use a memory
765   buffer to retain data samples from previous tasks, incorporating techniques such as experience re-
766   play Rolnick et al. (2019), or constrained optimization to allow the model to learn from current and
767   previous tasks simultaneously (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Han et al., 2020). (ii) Regularization-
768   based methods, which add extra terms to the loss function to penalize changes in important model pa-
769   rameters, limiting interference with previously learned tasks (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem,
770
      2017; Farajtabar et al., 2020; Smith et al., 2023). For example, EWC Kirkpatrick et al. (2017)
      preserves knowledge of old tasks by slowing down learning on weights deemed important for those
771
      tasks, while OGD Farajtabar et al. (2020) ensures that parameters move within the orthogonal space
772
      defined by previous task gradients. (iii) Architecture-based methods, which aim to reduce task in-
773   terference by dynamically expanding the model’s capacity or creating separate components for each
774   task (Rusu et al., 2016; Yoon et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019; Rao et al., 2019; Razdaibiedina et al.,
775   2023). For instance, Progressive Prompts Razdaibiedina et al. (2023) improves forward transfer
776   and mitigates forgetting by learning a distinct prompt for each new task and sequentially appending
777   these task-specific prompts to previously learned ones.
778
      Parameter-efficient Tuning. Recent works on parameter efficient tuning (PET) He et al. (2021)
779   have demonstrated that training only a subset of model parameters can achieve performance com-
780   parable to full model fine-tuning, while significantly reducing computational and annotation costs
781   (Zaken et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b).
782   For instance, BitFit Zaken et al. (2021) finds that shows that updating only the bias terms during
783   fine-tuning is highly effective. Prompt tuning Lester et al. (2021) leverages learnable ’soft prompts’
784   via back-propagation to condition frozen language models for specific tasks. LoRA Hu et al. (2021)
785   employs low-rank adapters to adapt models to new tasks with minimal additional parameters, and
786   AdaLoRA Zhang et al. (2023b) builds on LoRA by dynamically allocating the parameter budget
787
      based on the importance of the weight matrices. While most PET methods focus on learning a single
      task, some efforts have extended PET to continual learning. AdapterCLMadotto et al. (2020)intro-
788
      duces a dedicated adapter block for each task, and LFPT5 Qin & Joty (2021) continuously trains a
789
      large soft prompt across multiple tasks. ConPET Song et al. (2023) adapts existing continual learn-
790   ing strategies—originally developed for smaller models—to LLMs by integrating PET with a dy-
791   namic replay mechanism. O-LoRA Wang et al. (2023) incrementally learns new tasks in orthogonal
792   subspaces, keeping LoRA parameters from previous tasks fixed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
793   However, O-LoRA uses the fixed same rank for all incremental LoRAs without investigating the
794   rank patterns in CL.
795
