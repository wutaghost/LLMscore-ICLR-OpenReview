011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Link prediction is of key importance in many real-world applications like social
014            network analysis and recommender systems. To leverage the expressive power for
015            achieving SOTA performance, many recent works adapt the attention mechanism
016            to the structured data for link prediction, in which dense or relational attention is
017            often unaffordable on large-scale structured data. Moreover, in a realistic setting,
018
               the time-evolving topological and feature information can raise more challenging
               questions about the efficiency and effectiveness of attention mechanisms. In spite
019
               of the expressive power, we discern that the attention mechanism may not always
020
               be as irreplaceable as expected for temporal graph representation learning, at
021            least not for temporal link prediction tasks. Formally, we discover that some
022            deliberately-designed simple positional encoding can enable MLPs to exploit
023            attributed graph information to achieve SOTA performance than complex graph
024            transformers. Hence, we propose a simple temporal link prediction model, named
025            SimpleTLP. In detail, for SimpleTLP, we first propose to adapt Fourier Transform
026            on temporal graphs for learning informative positional encoding, then we (1)
027            prove this learning scheme can make positional encoding preserve the temporal
028            graph topology from the spatial-temporal spectral viewpoint, (2) verify MLPs can
029
               fully exploit the expressiveness and reach and even surpass Transformers on that
               encoding, (3) change different initial positional encoding inputs to show robustness,
030
               (4) analyze the theoretical complexity and obtain less empirical running time than
031
               SOTA baselines, and (5) demonstrate its temporal link prediction out-performance
032            in a comprehensive way on 13 classic datasets and with 10 algorithms in both
033            transductive and inductive settings using 3 different sampling strategies. Also,
034            SimpleTLP obtains the leading performance in the large-scale TGB benchmark
035            (the newest TGB 2.0).

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Link prediction is an important research topic in the graph learning community (Kumar et al., 2020),
041
      especially in complex temporal networks (Lu & Zhou, 2010; Martínez et al., 2017). Learning from
042   the time-evolving topological structures and time-evolving node and edge features, and determining
043   whether the link (i.e., edge or interaction between two nodes) exists at a certain timestamp are
044   challenging but have attracted much research interest like dynamic protein-protein interactions (Tay-
045   lor et al., 2009; Gupta et al., 2015), recommender systems (Huang et al., 2005), social network
046   analysis (Daud et al., 2020), and many more.
047
      Recently, with the success of the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), graph transformers
048   have been proposed for the structured data to obtain the out-performance in many graph applications
049   for their powerful expressiveness, like GraphGPS (Rampásek et al., 2022) for graph classification,
050   TokenGT (Kim et al., 2022) for node and graph classification, and DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023)
051   for link prediction. However, to preserve the long-distance relation into the representation vectors,
052   graph transformers usually need high time complexity due to the dense attention or relational
053   attention (Müller et al., 2023; Min et al., 2022), i.e., each pair of nodes need to be attended with
      O(n2 ) time complexity, which can even approach O(n3 ) in a more complex relational setting (Diao


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      & Loynd, 2023). A detailed theoretical time complexity and empirical running time comparison with
055   SOTAs can be found in Appendix E.2 and Appendix E.3.
056
057   The above complexity analysis places the burden on the temporal link prediction task from at least
058
      two aspects (Kazemi et al., 2020). First, to make the current link prediction, the decision models
      need to consider the interaction history, and the corresponding spatial-temporal structure will force
059
      the attention mechanism to become more complex to attend to more interactions. Second, besides
060
      the spatial-temporal structure, the node and edge features also evolve over time, and the prolonged
061   features also increase the size of neural architectures. Promisingly, some nascent work (Cong et al.,
062   2023) proposed to only use Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) to accomplish the temporal link prediction.
063   Although (Cong et al., 2023) does not reach the superior performance of the graph transformers (Yu
064   et al., 2023), it paves the way for exploring the ability of MLPs in the temporal link prediction task.
065
      In light of the above discussion, an intriguing question emerges: instead of a pre-defined fixed
066
      positional encoding, can we design a learnable positional encoding along with time such that
067   a simple neural architecture can achieve a similar or even better performance than graph
068   transformers? Thus, in this paper, we design a simple temporal link prediction model, named
069   SimpleTLP. In SimpleTLP, we propose two novel techniques: Learnable Positional Encoding
070   Module (LPE) and Node-Link-Positional Encoder based on Discrete Fourier Transform.
071
      With those proposed techniques, our goal is that even using MLPs can exploit temporal graph
072
      information and achieve competitive performance compared with graph transformers in the temporal
073   link prediction task. To demonstrate this, we first prove that the proposed positional encoding method
074   in SimpleTLP can preserve the temporal graph topology from the spatial-temporal spectral viewpoint
075   and the corresponding theoretical time complexity, as shown in Section 4. Moreover, in Section 5,
076   we evaluate the empirical performance of SimpleTLP extensively, including (1) the comprehensive
077   effectiveness comparison with 13 classic datasets, 10 algorithms, 2 learning settings (transductive
078   and inductive), and 3 sampling strategies, where our SimpleTLP performs the best across the board;
079   (2) the verification of the role of MLPs and Transformers upon SimpleTLP; (3) the robustness of
080   SimpleTLP in terms of different initial positional encoding inputs; (4) various parameter analysis and
081
      ablation studies; and (5) the leading performance in the large-scale TGB open benchmark (Gastinger
      et al., 2024).

      2    P RELIMINARIES
085   Here, we introduce preliminaries to pave the way for deriving our SimpleTLP in the next section.
086
      Temporal Graph Snapshot. A snapshot of a temporal graph G is a collection of temporal interactions
087
      (i.e., edges) at time t ∈ {1, . . . , T }, which is defined as Gt = {(u, v, t) u ∈ V t ; v ∈ V t ; (u, v, t) ∈
088
      E t }. Each event (u, v, t) denotes an interaction between nodes u and v that occurs at time t, and the
089
      node and edge sets of Gt are denoted as V t and E t respectively. Additionally, for node u ∈ V t , we
090
      denote the node features as stu ∈ RdN , and the edge features associated with an event (u, v, t) as
091   etu,v ∈ RdE , where dN and dE are the dimensions of the node and edge features.
092
093   Temporal Neighbors. The temporal neighborhood of a node u ∈ V t at time t ∈ {1, . . . , T }, denoted
094   as Nut , is the set of all interactions that involve u at time t. Formally, Nut can be mathematically
095
      expressed as Nut = {(u, v, t) v ∈ V t ; (u, v, t) ∈ E t }.
096   Time Encoder. A time encoder is a function to obtain the vector representation of time t (Xu
097   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021b; Cong et al., 2023). Let dT be the dimension of the time encoding
098   vector. We define our time encoding function as fT : R+ → RdT . In SimpleTLP, for t ∈ R+ ,
099   fT (t) = cos(t · ω), where ω ∈ RdT with the ith entry computed as ωi = α−(i−1)/β , and α, β ∈ R
100   are hyper-parameters. In order to enhance the ability of dT in distinguishing all timestamps, given
101   that tmax is the maximum timestamp being considered, α and β should be selected in such a way
102   that tmax · α−(i−1)/β → 0 as i goes towards dT . Moreover, α, β, and ω are fixed during the training
103   process of SimpleTLP.
104   Positional Encoding for Graphs. Originated from the transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), positional
105   encoding is proposed to add node feature information to support the attention mechanism by indicating
106   the relative position of a node within the input graph (Dwivedi et al., 2023), and the common positional
107   encoding methods include computing the Laplacian eigenvector or Personalized PageRannk vector
      for each node (Rampásek et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Mathematically, given an n-node (static)


                                                            2

108
      graph G = (V, E) and its adjacency matrix A ∈ Rn×n , the normalized Laplacian of G is computed
109
      as L = In − D−1/2 AD−1/2 , where In is the n × n identity matrix, and D is the diagonal degree
110
      matrix of G. Let the eigen-decomposition of L be L = UΛU⊤ , where Λ = diag(λ1 , λ2 , . . . , λn ) is
111   a diagonal matrix consisting of L’s eigenvalues and U consists n eigenvectors of L as its columns.
112   Let ui ∈ Rn be the ith eigenvector of L and its j th entry denoted as ui,j . Then, the positional
113   encoding of node i ∈ V is defined as pi = [u1,i , . . . , un,i ]⊤ ∈ Rn . In practice, the dimension dP of
114   positional encoding is usually set to be less than n for computational efficiency (Dwivedi & Bresson,
115   2020), i.e., dP ≪ n and pi = [u1,i , . . . , udP ,i ]⊤ ∈ RdP .

      To begin with, we focus on a general research question, i.e., given two nodes u and v, how can
128   we predict whether the link (interaction or edge) exists between u and v at a certain timestamp t,
129   effectively and efficiently?
130
131
      To answer this question, we propose a simple temporal link prediction model named SimpleTLP.
      It retrieves the interaction history of two nodes and decides whether the current link exists or
132
      not. Without heavy neural architectures like dense attention (Rampásek et al., 2022) or relational
133
      attention (Diao & Loynd, 2023), SimpleTLP only depends on two of our proposed simple yet effective
134   techniques, i.e., Learnable Positional Encoding Module (LPE) and Node-Link-Positional Encoder,
135   such that SimpleTLP can preserve the spatial-temporal topology rigorously (as shown in Section 4)
136   and outperform attention-based state-of-the-art link prediction methods (as shown in Section 5).

159   3.1   L EARNABLE P OSITIONAL E NCODING (LPE) M ODULE
160
161   The main idea of Learnable Positional Encoding (LPE) is to leverage historical positional encoding to
      determine whether the link exists at the current timestamp. However, learning from the history data


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      in an effective and efficient way and only through positional encoding is challenging, because SOTA
163   methods usually need a complex attention mechanism (Poursafaei et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023).
164
165   To this end, we aim to only use positional encoding to carry informative and useful knowledge to
166
      record what had happened in the past (e.g., what interactions occurred and what the node and edge
      features looked like around the interaction). Existing simple hand-crafted positional encoding can be
167
      efficient but tend to induce suboptimal performance (Wang et al., 2021b; Cong et al., 2023).
168
169   Thus, we propose a “learnable” positional encoding module (LPE), enabling it to reflect complex and
170   non-linear knowledge. As shown in Figure 1, in our SimpleTLP, LPE estimates (or approximates) the
171   positional encoding at current time t by applying the Fourier Transform (with learnable parameters)
172
      on the past positional encoding. It is worth noting that obtaining the positional encoding through
      LPE: (1) records the complex spatial-temporal information (proved from the spectral viewpoint in
173
      Section 4), (2) and avoids computing the Laplacian eigendecomposition for each timestamp from
174
      scratch, which is computationally expensive (e.g., O(|V |3 ) (Chen et al., 2023) or O(|E|3/2 ) (Dwivedi
175
      et al., 2023))
176
177   Because of no peeking at the current graph topology structure and features, our LPE is an iterative
178   updating module. Next, we introduce LPE in two-folds: (1) given the past positional encoding, how
179
      LPE approximates the current positional encoding (that will be used to determine links by Node-Link-
      Positional Encoder in Section 3.2), and (2) how to store the approximate positional encoding and
180
      update/optimize it in the future round with current topology structure and features observed.
181

223   3.2   N ODE -L INK -P OSITIONAL E NCODER
224
225   For node u, the Node-Link-Positional Encoder is designed to aggregate its historical node and edge
226   features with the positional encoding of its recent 1-hop neighbors prior to time t, and, eventually,
227
      derive a compact representation associated with u that encodes all the information.
228   In general, Node-Link-Positional Encoder works as follows. We first use htu,N and htu,E to denote the
229   node and link encoding of recent 1-hop neighbors of node u. Then, we apply MLP transformations
230   on htu,N and htu,E to obtain the encoding, denoted by htu,N ∥E , which represents the combination
231   of node-link information. For adding positional information, we use htu,P to denote the aggregated
232   positional encoding of recent 1-hop neighbors, and further transform them by MLP layers. Eventually,
233   htu,N ∥E and htu,P are combined by a 1-layer MLP to derive a temporal representation at time t for u,
234   denoted by htu , which will be sent together with htv to the link predictor in Section 3.3 to determine
235   whether the link exists between nodes u and v at time t.

338   at the previous timestamp can well preserve the next close future timestamp’s positional encoding.
339   In other words, as Theorem 1 suggests, the positional encoding function of SimpleTLP can be a

342   Note that the above statements do not necessarily mean that SimpleTLP does not need to update the
343   positional encoding in every case. Actually, SimpleTLP indeed updates the positional encoding when
344   new interaction happens. The role of Theorem 1 is to demonstrate the effectiveness of our positional
345   encoding in the inductive setting, where the future scenario is usually not given or difficult to observe.
346   Complexity Analysis. Next, we provide the analysis on SimpleTLP’s computational complexity.
347   SimpleTLP consists of two main components: LPE Module and Node-Link-Positional Encoder.
348   In LPE Module, the approximated positional encoding is derived by applying Discrete Fourier
349   Transform on a L-length sequence of previous positional encoding, so the complexity is O(L log(L)),
350   achieved by utilizing Fast Fourier Transform. Then, we update the positional encoding by sampling
351   the K most recent interactions, adding O(K) time. Therefore, for n nodes, LPE Module costs
352   O(n(L log(L) + K)). For Node-Link-Positional Encoder, for each node we retrieve node-level
353
      information from its neighborhood in the interval [t−tgap ; t] and sample its K most recent interactions,
      so the complexity is O(n·(tgap +K)), which can be achieved by pre-computing all temporal neighbors
354
      in the range [t − tgap ; t] of each node. Thus, the total time complexity is O(n(tgap + K + L log(L)),
355
      which scales linearly with the number of nodes, as L, K, tgap are constants independent of n.
356
357   Compared to recent SOTAs, DyGFormer (Yu et al., 2023) and FreeDyG (Tian et al., 2024), Sim-
358   pleTLP is more efficient as both of these methods employ neighborhood co-occurrence for each node
359
      pair of interaction, making their worst-case complexity scales with the number of edges, |E|, while
      SimpleTLP’s complexity scales with n, the number of nodes, and its quite common that n << |E|
360
      , showing SimpleTLP’s advantage in efficiency. Detailed theoretical time complexity comparison
361
      can be found in Appendix E.2, and detailed empirical running time comparison can be found in

512   6        R ELATED W ORK
513
514   Most temporal graph learning methods are designed to learn node temporal representations at a certain
515
      timestamp, and then obtain the link prediction between a pair of nodes (u, v) at time t. They typically
      start by concatenating the temporal representations of u, v at time t, and then process the concatenation
516
      with MLP layers to estimate the link occurrence probability. Usually, the temporal representations of
517
      nodes are obtained by aggregating intrinsic node/edge features from recent temporal neighbors and
518   interactions, which are then processed using more complex architectures. For example, TGN (Rossi
519   et al., 2020) and JODIE (Kumar et al., 2019) employ the recurrent architecture, TGAT (Xu et al., 2020)
520   utilizes the self-attention mechanism, DyRep (Trivedi et al., 2019) uses Temporal Point Processes,
521   and more recently, GraphMixer (Cong et al., 2023) leverages Mixer-MLP layers, while DyGFormer
522   (Yu et al., 2023) harnesses the power of Transformer and FreeDyG (Tian et al., 2024) utilizes Fourier
523   Transform to encode neighborhood co-occurrence information. From another aspect, some methods
524   aggregate information from higher-order graph structures. For example, TGN (Rossi et al., 2020)
525   and TGAT (Xu et al., 2020) both aggregate information from k-hop neighborhoods, and CAWN
526
      (Wang et al., 2021b) samples random walks and encode them to derive the node representations.
      Our SimpleTLP achieves effectiveness with efficiency. First, our SimpleTLP only considers 1-
527
      hop neighborhood for information aggregation and processes representations with simple MLP
528
      transformations. More importantly, SimpleTLP integrates the domain of node and edge features by
529   integrating evolving positional encoding, which is informative to support task out-performance, and
530   the acquisition is easy and does not really rely on heavy attention mechanisms.

534
      In this paper, we propose a simple temporal link prediction model, SimpleTLP, which only relies on
535
      learnable positional encoding and MLPs to achieve out-performance over SOTA graph transformers
536
      in link prediction tasks. In addition to theoretical analysis of the effectiveness and efficiency, we
537   design comprehensive experiments to demonstrate the empirical performance of SimpleTLP.

      In this section, we provide comparative discussion and experimental comparison between our
      method SimpleTLP and PINT (Souza et al., 2022), a method for temporal link prediction that
915
      also leverage positional features.
916
917   To be more specific, PINT (Souza et al., 2022) proposes relative positional features that count
      the number of temporal walks of a given length between 2 nodes. As stated in PINT (Souza


                                                                          17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      et al., 2022), temporal walks are defined as follows. An (L − 1)-length temporal walk is W =
919   {(w1 , t1 ), (w2 , t2 ), . . . , (wL , tL )}, where t1 > t2 > . . . tL and (wi−1 , wi , ti ) is an interaction in the
920   given temporal graph. In summary, PINT proposes a relative positional encoding that represents
921   pair-wise node “distance” (here the “distance” is the number of L-length temporal walks, assuming
922   L is given). On the other hand, our SimpleTLP learnable positional encodings (LPE) fall into the
923   category of global positional encodings, which encode the position of the node relative to the global
924   structure of the graph.

       E.2   T HEORETICAL TIME COMPLEXITY COMPARISON WITH SOTA S
1081
1082   Now, we give an in-depth analysis of the complexity of DyGFormer and FreeDyG as follows.
1083
       Suppose we are given E query links: {(ui , vi , ti )}E
                                                            i=1 . As we move to new timestamps, new
1084
       connections and new nodes will emerge, resulting in the changes in some nodes’ neighborhoods;
1085
       then, both SimpleTLP and DyGFormer need to update historical neighbors for some nodes. (Note
1086   that for a node, both SimpleTLP and DyGFormer consider its historical neighbors, so both methods
1087   would need to perform an update if its neighborhood changes).
1088
1089
       For each link (ui , vi , ti ), DyGFormer and FreeDyG first need to obtain the neighbor co-occurrence
       information of the node pair (ui , vi ) as follows. Suppose ui has p historical neighbors, a1 , . . . , ap ,
1090
       and vi has q historical neighbors, b1 , . . . , bq , then the neighbor co-occurrence scheme of DyGFormer
1091
       and FreeDyG requires counting (1) the number of times a1 , . . . , ap are involved in interactions with
1092   vi before time ti and (2) the number of times b1 , . . . , bq are involved in interactions with ui before
1093   time ti . In other words, for an arbitrary link (ui , vi , ti ), the neighbor co-occurrence framework of
1094   DyGFormer and FreeDyG makes the computation of ui depend on vi ’s information, as they have to
1095   count how many times historical neighbors of ui are also vi ’s historical neighbors, and vice versa.
1096   Thus, in order to make predictions for the aforementioned query links, DyGFormer and FreeDyG
1097   require at least O(E) complexity.
1098   On the other hand, for SimpleTLP, we first consider the unique nodes among u1 , . . . , uE , v1 , . . . , vE .
1099   Suppose there are n unique nodes, and let us denote them as c1 , . . . , cn , then we could compute
1100   their temporal representation as stated in Eq. 9. Moreover, in the process of obtaining the necessary
1101   components for Eq. 9, for any node ci , we only use ci ’s related information (most recent temporal
1102   neighbors / links and its previous positional encoding). Thus, it takes SimpleTLP O(n) time to
1103   compute the representation for these n nodes. To predict an arbitrary link (ui , vi , ti ), we simply
1104   retrieve the representation for ui , vi that we just computed and apply Eq. 10
1105   In summary, DyGFormer and FreeDyG have O(E) since they process a node pair (u, v)-related
1106   information, while SimpleTLP’s complexity linearly scales with the number of nodes.

1368   F.3   RUNNING S IMPLE TLP ON C ONTINUOUS T IME DYNAMIC G RAPHS
1369
1370   The definition of discrete time and continuous time dynamic graphs can be referred to the survey
1371   paper (Kazemi et al., 2020), where
1372
1373          • Continuous Time Dynamic Graph (CTDG) is represented as ((v2 , v5 ), t1 ), ((v1 , v2 ), t2 ),
1374            . . . , as shown in its Example 2;
1375          • Discrete Time Dynamic Graph (DTDG) is represented as a set of G1 , G2 , . . . , GT , where
1376            Gt = (Vt , Et ) is the graph at snapshot t, Vt is the set of nodes in Gt , and Et is the set of
1377            edges in Gt .
1378
1379   In this viewpoint, our method is designed for discrete time. However, we can see a clear transformation
1380   between CTDG and DTDG: if we aggregate edges that happen at the same timestamp t into a set,
1381   then it is the graph snapshot Gt . This is not invented by us, a similar example can be seen in Example
1382   3 of the survey paper (Kazemi et al., 2020).
1383   Because the introduction and theoretical derivation on the snapshot level are much easier to understand
1384   by people, and using “a set of continuous-time edges” every time when we do theoretical derivation
1385   and illustration can be wordy and hurt the presentation. Since graph snapshot and relevant concepts
1386   are already existing in the community and cover the meaning, then we choose to follow them.
1387   Then, in this section, we elaborate how SimpleTLP can be evaluated on Continuous Time Dynamic
1388   Graphs. Firstly, we explain the data batching procedure for Continuous Time Dynamic Graphs as
1389   follows. Suppose we are given a CTDG with T interactions, G = {ui , vi , ti }Ti=1 , where t1 ≤ . . . tT
1390   and (ui , vi , ti ) denotes the link occurrence between ui , vi at time ti . Let the batch size be B, then a
1391   data batch would be B consecutive interactions from {ui , vi , ti }Ti=1 . Specifically, suppose T = B · K
1392   (for some K), then our 1st data batch would be the first B link occurrences of G: {ui , vi , ti }B i=1 . The
1393   2nd data batch would be the next B consecutive interactions, {ui , vi , ti }2B    i=B+1 , and so on. In this
1394   way, now we obtain K data batches. Moreover, as a batch is a stream of B events, so we can regard a
1395   data batch as a mini temporal graph with B link occurrences. This data batching technique is also
1396   employed by other baselines.
