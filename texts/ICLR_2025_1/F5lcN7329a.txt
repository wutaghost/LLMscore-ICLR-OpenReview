011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The primary objective of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) is to establish a di-
014            rect connection between neural activity and behavioral actions through neural de-
015
               coders. Consistent neural representation is crucial for achieving high-performance
               behavioral decoding over time. Due to the stochastic variability in neural record-
016
               ings, existing neural representation techniques yield embedding inconsistency,
017
               leading to the failure of behavioral decoders in few-trial scenarios. In this work,
018            we propose a novel Flow-Based Dynamical Alignment (FDA) framework that
019            leverages attractor-like ensemble dynamics on stable neural manifolds, which fa-
020            cilitate a new source-free alignment through likelihood maximization. The consis-
021            tency of latent embeddings obtained through FDA was theoretically verified based
022            on dynamical stability, allowing for rapid adaptation with few trials. Further ex-
023            periments on multiple motor cortex datasets validate the superior performance of
024            FDA. The FDA method establishes a novel framework for consistent neural la-
025            tent embeddings with few trials. Our work offers insights into neural dynamical
026
               stability, potentially enhancing the chronic reliability of real-world BCIs.

069   Despite the stochastic variability within neural recordings, regions like the motor cortex (Inagaki
070   et al., 2019; Finkelstein et al., 2021; Hira et al., 2013) exhibit a shared low-dimensional manifold
071   when similar tasks are performed. Within this manifold, latent states converge toward similar ones
072   over time, a property known as attractor-like ensemble dynamics (Gonzalez et al., 2019; Khona &
073   Fiete, 2022). This mechanism inspires us to leverage attractor-like ensemble dynamics, where the
074   final similar states serve as neural embeddings. As shown in Fig. 1, this dynamical property enables
075
      the rapid adaptation of raw neural signals with stochastic variability, thereby achieving consistent
      neural embeddings within the neural manifold.
076
077   In this work, based on the fact that attractor-like ensemble dynamics is a key property of dynami-
078   cally stable systems (Bhatia & Szegö, 2002), we propose a novel Flow-Based Dynamical Alignment
079   (FDA) framework to establish such systems with attractor-like dynamics and achieve consistent neu-
080   ral embeddings. Specifically, our FDA approach leverages recent advances in flow matching (Lip-
081
      man et al., 2023), with the explicit likelihood maximization formulation provided by flows further
      facilitating a new source-free unsupervised alignment. The consistency of FDA embeddings was
082
      theoretically verified through the dynamical stability of neural manifolds, allowing for rapid adap-
083
      tation with few target trials. Furthermore, extensive experiments on multiple motor cortex datasets
084   validate the superior performance of our FDA over existing approaches. The FDA approach intro-
085   duces an innovative framework for consistent neural latent embeddings and successfully achieves
086   unsupervised alignment in few-trial scenarios. Our FDA, based on attractor-like ensemble dynam-
087   ics, offers insights into neural dynamical stability, potentially improving the long-term reliability of
088   real-world BCIs (Dabagia et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Karpowicz et al., 2024). The main contribu-
089   tions of this paper are summarized as follows:
090          • Consistent Neural Embeddings: Flow matching was initially employed on stable neural
091            manifolds using attractor-like ensemble dynamics to achieve consistent neural embeddings.
092            The explicit formulation of likelihood maximization from flow matching provides a novel
093            source-free unsupervised alignment. We establish a new neural representation character-
094            ized by consistent embeddings using the mechanism of attractor-like ensemble dynamics.
095          • Flow-Based Dynamical Alignment (FDA): We propose an innovative framework for
096            Flow-Based Dynamical Alignment (FDA) grounded in consistent neural embeddings. The
097            dynamical stability of FDA is theoretically validated and effectively applied to unsuper-
098            vised alignment in few-trial scenarios. Our approach has the potential to enhance the
099            chronic reliability of real-world BCIs in the presence of non-stationary neural signals.
100          • Experimental Validation: We extensively validated FDA on several motor cortex
101            datasets (Ma et al., 2023). Results demonstrate that FDA significantly enhances cross-
102            session decoding performance using few target trials. Furthermore, we numerically demon-
103            strate the dynamical stability of neural manifolds based on the Lyapunov exponents.

107   Neural Representation for Behavioral Decoding Previous representation researches have explored
      various strategies for discovering shared latent neural embeddings over time. Linear dimensionality

      reduction methods, such as PCA (Degenhart et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2008; Gallego et al., 2018), were
109   leveraged for interpretable neural state spaces. Non-linear methods (Schneider et al., 2023; Cho
110   et al., 2023) were shown to be effective for representation across trials and sessions. For instance,
111   typical approaches included variational autoencoders (VAEs) with auxiliary variables (Zhou & Wei,
112   2020; Sani et al., 2021; Klindt et al., 2021) or self-supervised techniques (Liu et al., 2021). More-

116   based architectures (Liu et al., 2022; Le & Shlizerman, 2022) demonstrated effectiveness for the
117
      unified and scalable neural representation. Pre-trained foundation models based on transformer
      architectures (Azabou et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023) also achieved desirable latent features across
118
      various subjects and sessions. Nonetheless, existing neural representation approaches usually yield
119
      dynamical instability due to non-stationary neural signals, resulting in unreliable long-term behav-
120   ioral decoding. Here, we propose a novel framework that leverages attractor-like ensemble dynamics
121   on neural manifolds, ensuring dynamical stability.
122
123
      Alignment for Behavioral Decoding Unsupervised alignment of these neural representa-
      tions (Dabagia et al., 2023) is crucial for behavioral decoding. Some works focused on directly
124
      aligning raw neural signals. For instance, ADAN (Farshchian et al., 2018) and Cycle-GAN (Ma
125
      et al., 2023) achieved this through adversarial learning techniques. In addition, latent features such
126   as low-dimensional neural dynamics (Jude et al., 2022; Karpowicz et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023;
127   Vermani et al., 2024) were aligned across sessions. Few-trial supervised alignment can be accom-
128   plished via fine-tuning with certain pre-trained models (Ye et al., 2023; Azabou et al., 2023). How-
129   ever, existing unsupervised alignment approaches usually lead to unreliable behavioral decoding due
130   to non-stationary neural signals, particularly in few-trial scenarios. In this work, our FDA method
131   achieves source-free unsupervised alignment through likelihood maximization with few target trials,
132   a challenge that most existing unsupervised alignment approaches have not effectively addressed.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
136
137   We define the problem of long-term behavioral decoding based on the unsupervised domain adap-
138   tation (Long et al., 2013). First, we define the domain D = {(x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )}, where
139   xi (l)(l = 1, 2, . . . , m) represents the raw neural signal sample from the l-th channel in one or
140   more sessions. The signal window has a length of w time points, much smaller than the length of
141   trials, i.e., xi (l) ∈ Rw . The first signal window of each trial begins at the initial time point, while
142   the second window starts one step later. yi denotes the behavioral label corresponding to the w-th
143
      time step of xi , with yi ∈ Rd . The behavioral label is assigned at the w-th time step to meet real-
      time decoding requirements using short-time causal windows and to leverage previous time steps as
144
      contextual information effectively.
145
146   Based on D, we define the source domain DS , consisting of signals and labels from one or more
147   sessions: DS = {(xS1 , y1S ), . . . , (xSnS , ynSS )}. Similarly, the unlabeled target domain DT consists of
148   signals from a separate session: DT = {xT1 , . . . , xTnT }, where nT ≪ nS , and typically only contains
149   signals of few trials. For convenience, we define xS and yS as the random variables representing
150   neural signals xSi and their corresponding labels yiS in DS . Samples xTj from DT are represented as
151   random variables xT . We aim to obtain consistent latent embeddings from DS and DT , obtaining
152   high-performance decoders for behavioral labels yT associated with xT .
153

184   3.2.1     F LOW M ATCHING ON S TABLE AND F LEXIBLE N EURAL M ANIFOLDS
185
186   During the pre-training phase, we propose a novel framework based on the continuous normalizing
187   flow conditioned on latent dynamics. FDA offers several distinct benefits in obtaining neural latent
188   embeddings. First, flow matching imposes fewer assumptions on the underlying statistics of latent
189
      variables, allowing for more flexible modeling on the neural manifolds and improving adaptability
      to diverse decoding tasks. Second, the flow is governed by a dynamical system with external inputs.
190
      Our theoretical analysis demonstrates the dynamical stability of this system, and the empirical results
191
      further validate this stability.
192
193   Conditional Feature Extraction Based on Neural Dynamics We begin by extracting the latent
194   dynamics from xSi as conditional features cSi . For spike signals, a single channel usually records
195
      neuron-level activity (Buzsáki, 2004), where the short-term dynamics are relatively limited for sim-
      ilar tasks (Izhikevich et al., 2004). Moreover, inter-channel relationships in spike signals are gener-
196
      ally more stable compared to the temporal dynamics, which often exhibit warping (Williams et al.,
197
      2020). The above observations are validated, as demonstrated in Fig. 4(c).
198
199   Based on these observations and inspired by (Liu et al., 2024), we utilize short-term dynamics to
200   establish conditional feature spaces, and leverage the more stable inter-channel relationships for their
201
      coefficients. This approach can flexibly accommodate changes in the number of channels, which is
      quite common during neuron growth and apoptosis (Degenhart et al., 2020). Specifically, we feed
202
      the raw neural signal sequence xSi = [xSi (1), . . . , xSi (m)], containing tokens from m channels,
203
      into a transformer-based network fα (with parameters α) using the classical sinusoidal positional
204   encoding. After processing through multi-head self-attention modules and projection networks, we
205   obtain conditional latent dynamics: cSi = fα (xSi ), where cSi ∈ Rkc . The detailed architecture is
206   illustrated in Appendix A.1.
207
      Flow Matching Conditioned on Latent Dynamics After learning latent dynamics, we establish
208
      the continuous normalizing flow conditioned on these dynamics for long-term decoding. Traditional
209
      normalizing flows (Chen et al., 2019; Dinh et al., 2022) typically rely on invertible transformations,
210   but these often constrain the representational capacity of networks. Recent researches have utilized
211   continuous normalizing flows (Yang et al., 2019) to alleviate this. For instance, flow matching (Liu
212   et al., 2023a; Lipman et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) extends diffusion models, enabling more flexible
213   diffusion paths. Conditional flow matching (Liu et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2023; Dao et al., 2023;
214   Isobe et al., 2024; Atanackovic et al., 2024) further incorporates conditional features for modeling
215   conditional distributions. Inspired by these approaches, we adopt conditional flow matching to
      implement the continuous normalizing flow.

282   3.2.2   R APID A DAPTATION WITH F EW TARGET T RIALS
283
      While fine-tuning, existing adaptation methods (Gong et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2023a; Sagawa & Hino,
284
      2022) based on normalizing flows typically consider the source distribution as the starting point, and
285   the target distribution as the endpoint. However, this approach often requires a large number of target
286   samples. Based on flow matching conditioned on latent dynamics, we propose a more efficient
287   strategy with few trials. The pre-trained flow network vθ is fixed, while the conditional feature
288   extractor fα is fine-tuned, aligning the distribution of final decoding embeddings z(1). Furthermore,
289   the flow path is approximated as a straight line, allowing us to obtain final latent states in just one
290   step. This significantly simplifies the explicit computation of likelihood functions. Unlike ERDiff,
291   which maximizes the log-likelihood upper bound, we propose a direct log-likelihood maximization
292   approach that achieves source-free unsupervised alignment.
293   Maximum Mean Discrepancy Alignment with Few Target Trials (FDA-MMD) When target
294   sizes are small, the alignment based on individual sample probabilities, such as Kullback–Leibler
295   (KL) divergences in GANs, often leads to training instability. In contrast, Maximum Mean Discrep-
296   ancy (MMD) leverages higher-order moments as overall sample properties, effectively reducing the
297   influence of outliers in limited samples. This is empirically demonstrated in Fig. 4(a). Hence, we
298   adopt a strategy that minimizes MMD distances to align the distributions of latent neural embed-
299
      dings.

      Datasets We employed three distinct datasets of extracellular neural recordings from the primary
357
      motor cortex (M1) of non-human primates (Ma et al., 2023), as detailed below. Additional informa-
358
      tion about the datasets can be found in Appendix B.1.
359   Center-Out Reaching (CO-C&CO-M). Monkeys C and M engaged in a center-out reaching task,
360   where each trial required them to move to one of eight randomized targets, earning a reward for
361   successful reaching.
362   Random-Target (RT-M). Monkey M performed a random-target task, reaching for three sequen-
363   tially presented targets at random locations. Each trial started at the workspace center, with a 2.0-
364   second limit to reach each target.
365   Data Preprocess and Spilt We extracted trials from the ’go cue time’ to the ’trial end,’ followed
366   by digitizing, filtering, and spike detection of the neural signals. The data was then timestamped
367
      and smoothed for firing rates in 50 ms bins. Sessions containing approximately 200 trials, along
      with 2D cursor velocity labels, were used as DS for pre-training, while a separate session without
368
      labels was used as DT for fine-tuning. For few-trial alignment, we used the target ratio r to evaluate
369
      the number of target trials from all recorded ones, typically setting r to 0.02, 0.03, 0.04, and 0.06,
370   with 0.02 corresponding to no more than 4 trials. The decoded cursor velocity is assessed using R2
371   scores, with results averaged over five different random seeds. Additional experimental details and
372   hyper-parameter settings can be found in Appendix B.2.

      4.2.1     E MPIRICAL VALIDATION ON L ATENT S PACE S TABILITY
409
410   To validate the dynamical stability of latent spaces, we measured the maximum Lyapunov exponent
411   (MLE) λ of zS (t) after pre-training on DS . The value of λ was computed as described in (Wolf
412   et al., 1985), with a non-positive λ typically indicating dynamical stability. The detailed definition
413   and computation of λ are available in Appendix B.5. Since MLE is based on sequential variables, we
414   compared the λ values obtained by FDA with those of ERDiff and NoMAD, focusing on sequential
415   latent factors. The results are presented in Fig. 3(a) and Appendix C.1.1. Consistent with the
416
      findings discussed in Theorem 3.1, we found that FDA achieved negative λ across all selected
      datasets, indicating latent space stability. In contrast, both ERDiff and NoMAD frequently exhibited
417
      positive λ, with ERDiff showing greater instability than NoMAD.

527   5      C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   In this paper, we establish a new neural representation characterized by consistent neural embed-
530   dings based on the mechanism of attractor-like ensemble dynamics. An innovative framework for
531   FDA was proposed on the ground of consistent neural latent embeddings. We achieve the stable dy-
532   namics through flow matching on neural manifolds, which enables a novel source-free alignment via
533   likelihood maximization. The dynamical stability of FDA was theoretically verified, allowing for
534   few-trial unsupervised alignment. Extensive experiments on motor cortex datasets demonstrate that
535   FDA significantly enhances decoding performance, offering insights into neural dynamical stability.
536
      Our FDA method potentially improves the long-term reliability of real-world BCIs.
537   This work has several limitations that warrant further investigation. First, the effectiveness of FDA
538   in scenarios such as cross-task or cross-subject alignment needs to be further validated. Second,
539   future studies using clinical data from human subjects could further advance the clinical and chronic
      applications of BCIs.

889   CO-C&CO-M(Ma et al., 2023). Monkeys C and M conducted a center-out (CO) reaching task
890   while holding an upright handle. Monkey C utilized its right hand, whereas Monkey M used its left.
891   Each trial commenced with the monkey positioning its hand at the center of the workspace. After
892   a random delay, one of eight evenly spaced outer targets arranged in a circle was displayed. The
893   monkey then maintained its position through a variable pause until hearing an auditory go cue. To
894   earn a liquid reward, the monkey needed to reach the outer target within 1.0 second and sustain its
895
      hold for 0.5 seconds.
896   RT-M(Ma et al., 2023). Monkey M also participated in a random-target (RT) task, where it reached
897   for sequences of three targets shown in random locations on the screen. This task utilized the same
898   apparatus as the CO reaching task. Each trial started with the monkey placing its hand at the center
899   of the workspace, followed by the sequential presentation of three targets. The monkey had 2.0
900   seconds to move the cursor to each target after seeing it. Due to the random positioning of the
901
      targets, the cursor trajectory varied with each trial.
902   Preprocess Process. For all datasets, we extracted trials from the ’go cue time’ to the ’trial end.’
903   Next, we processed the neural signals by digitizing, applying a bandpass filter (250-5000 Hz), and
904   detecting spikes using thresholds based on root-mean square activity. The data was then times-
905   tamped and smoothed with a Gaussian kernel to compute firing rates over 50 ms bins.
