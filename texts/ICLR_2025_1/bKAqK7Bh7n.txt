009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Current generative models for drug discovery primarily use molecular docking as
012            an oracle to guide the generation of active compounds. However, such models are
013            often not useful in practice because even compounds with high docking scores do
014            not consistently show experimental activity. More accurate methods for activity
015            prediction exist, such as molecular dynamics based binding free energy calcula-
016            tions, but they are too computationally expensive to use in a generative model.
017            To address this challenge, we propose Multi-Fidelity Latent space Active Learn-
018            ing (MF-LAL), a generative modeling framework that integrates a set of oracles
019            with varying cost-accuracy tradeoffs. We train a surrogate model for each ora-
020
               cle and use these surrogates to generate compounds with high predicted activity.
               Unlike previous approaches that separately learn the surrogate model and genera-
021
               tive model, MF-LAL combines the generative and multi-fidelity surrogate models
022
               into a single framework, allowing for more accurate activity prediction and higher
023            quality samples. We train MF-LAL with a novel active learning algorithm to fur-
024            ther reduce computational cost. Our experiments on two disease-relevant proteins
025            show that MF-LAL produces compounds with significantly better binding free
026            energy scores than other single and multi-fidelity approaches.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Generative models for de novo drug design have gained significant interest in machine learning for
033
      their promised ability to quickly generate new compounds for specific applications. However, gener-
      ating compounds with real-world biological activity remains a fundamental challenge (Handa et al.,
034
      2023; Coley et al., 2020). One of the main difficulties is the computational evaluation of compound-
035
      protein binding affinities. The generated compounds are often highly novel, so an activity predictor
036   trained with existing experimental data is insufficient due to poor out-of-distribution generalization
037   (Chatterjee et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2022). Instead, physics-based methods that model 3D interactions
038   between compound and target are commonly used.
039
      Due to its speed, molecular docking is the prevalent physics-based method to evaluate novel com-
040
      pounds by generative models (Eckmann et al., 2022; Jeon & Kim, 2020; Lee et al., 2023; Noh et al.,
041
      2022; Fu et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022; Guan et al., 2023a;b). However, docking is a relatively poor
042   predictor of activity (Pinzi & Rastelli, 2019; Handa et al., 2023; Coley et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2022),
043   so it would be desirable to apply more accurate binding free energy calculation techniques (Pinzi &
044   Rastelli, 2019; Feng et al., 2022). These techniques, based on molecular dynamics simulations, are
045   currently considered the most reliable approach to predict affinity (Moore et al., 2023; Cournia et al.,
046   2021). However, they have not been used by generative models due to their high computational cost
047   (Thomas et al., 2023), with a single compound-protein pair taking hours to days to simulate on a
048   powerful computer (Wan et al., 2020). Thus, neither docking nor binding free energy techniques
049   alone can guide the real-world application of generative models.
050   Multi-fidelity surrogate models aim to fuse multiple data sources as oracles spanning a range of ac-
051   curacy and cost (Fernández-Godino et al., 2016).They are frequently learned using an active learning
052   approach, where a model selects or generates queries that it is most uncertain about to send to a cho-
053   sen oracle (Ren et al., 2021). The results from the oracle are then added to the training data of the
      model. We will focus on “query synthesis” approaches (Angluin, 1988), where the model gener-


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ates its own queries to send to the oracles, speeding up learning compared to approaches that query
055   oracles with samples from a fixed candidate set.
056
057   Combining docking (low fidelity) and binding free energy (high fidelity) using multi-fidelity surro-
058
      gate models holds promise to make generative models more practical. Yet, the use of multi-fidelity
      methods in drug discovery has been limited. Prior work from Hernandez-Garcia et al. (2023) uses a
059
      generative model to generate query compounds with high acquisition function values computed by
060
      a separate multi-fidelity surrogate model. However, since we want to generate query compounds to
061   send to oracles at multiple fidelity levels, the distribution of optimal query compounds may differ
062   across fidelities. A separate generative model is not aware of such differences across fidelity levels,
063   hence it cannot send queries to the multi-fidelity oracles efficiently.
064
      We aim to address the problem of multi-fidelity generation with Multi-Fidelity Latent space Active
065
      Learning (MF-LAL), an integrated framework for compound generation using multi-fidelity active
066
      learning. Instead of separating the generative model and surrogate model, we perform surrogate
067   modeling and generation together at each fidelity level using a sequence of hierarchical latent spaces.
068   This improves the quality of generated queries because there is a separate latent space and decoder
069   specialized for each fidelity, and improves surrogate modeling and inter-fidelity information passing
070   because each latent space can be organized for predicting at just that level. We use both docking
071   and binding free energy methods as oracles in our multi-fidelity environment to achieve a favorable
072   trade-off between cost and accuracy. In summary,
073
074          • we introduce a novel multi-fidelity surrogate and generative modeling framework, MF-
075            LAL, which integrates data from multiple fidelity levels to generate high-quality samples
076
               at the highest fidelity (binding free energy).
077          • we employ an active learning approach with a novel query generation technique that en-
078            sures compounds generated at higher fidelities also scored well at lower fidelities, improv-
079            ing the quality of generated samples.
080          • we evaluate MF-LAL and baseline methods on a real-world problem setting involving opti-
081            mizing the binding free energy of compounds against two disease-relevant human proteins,
082            and find that MF-LAL generates compounds with significantly better scores than baselines.

      2.1   M OLECULAR GENERATIVE MODELS
088
      Generative models in drug discovery have gained much interest for their ability to quickly gener-
089
      ate compounds with desired properties (Paul et al., 2021). Early works (Jin et al., 2018; Gómez-
090   Bombarelli et al., 2018; You et al., 2018) focus on properties such as the octanol-water partition
091   coefficient (logP) or quantitative estimate of drug-likeness (QED), which are of very limited prac-
092   tical utility (Coley et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021). More recently, there has been an understanding
093   that the binding affinity to a targeted protein is much more relevant for practical drug discovery (Xie
094   et al., 2021; Eckmann et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022).
095
      One approach to guide generative models in optimizing compound binding affinity is to use an
096   oracle for compound evaluation. This oracle can be applied to reinforcement learning (Jeon &
097   Kim, 2020; Fu et al., 2022; Mazuz et al., 2023), VAEs (Eckmann et al., 2022; Noh et al., 2022),
098   genetic algorithms (Spiegel & Durrant, 2020; Fu et al., 2022), diffusion models (Lee et al., 2023;
099   Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024), or other generative frameworks Zhu et al. (2024). All of
100   them use docking software, such as AutoDock (Morris et al., 2009), as the oracle, because it is the
101   only reasonably fast option. However, docking is known to be inaccurate (Pinzi & Rastelli, 2019),
102   and compounds with high docking scores do not consistently show experimental activity (Handa
103   et al., 2023; Coley et al., 2020; Feng et al., 2022).
104   Molecular dynamics-based binding free energy calculations are much more accurate than docking
105   (Moore et al., 2023; Cournia et al., 2021), but have not yet been applied to de novo generative drug
106   design due to their high computational cost (Thomas et al., 2023). While Ghanakota et al. (2020)
107   use binding free energy calculations in combination with a molecular generative model, they focus
      on the optimization of an existing known lead compound. This allows them to rely on much cheaper

      Structure-based generative models are trained on 3D structures of protein-ligand pairs, and aim to

      predict a 3D ligand that fits in a given protein pocket with high binding affinity. Techniques include

131   2.2    M ULTI - FIDELITY SURROGATE MODELING
132
133   Multi-fidelity modeling methods aim to fuse multiple data sources of variable accuracy and cost
134   (Fernández-Godino
        fi     fi           et al., 2016), and are widely used in scientific fields for surrogate modeling and
135   uncertainty quantification (Brevault et al., 2020). A popular choice of surrogate model is a Gaussian
136   process (GP), which performs well in low data settings and produces well-calibrated uncertainty
137
      estimates (Brevault et al., 2020). One such technique to apply GPs to multi-fidelity modeling is
      described by Wu et al. (2020), where a downsampling kernel is used to output predictions at each
138
      fidelity level. Other multi-fidelity surrogate modeling approaches utilize neural processes (Wang &
139
      Lin, 2020; Wu et al., 2022; 2023; Niu et al., 2024) and ordinary differential equations (Li et al.,
140   2022) as an alternative to GPs.
141
142   Multi-fidelity models are frequently trained in an active learning fashion, where one uses an estimate
143
      of a model’s uncertainty to most efficiently acquire more datapoints from an oracle (Ren et al., 2021).
      In the multi-fidelity setting, this means iteratively querying across both the sampling space and each
144
      different fidelity oracle (Li et al., 2020; Hernandez-Garcia et al., 2023). Traditional active learning
145
      involves selecting from a fixed candidate set with the highest acquisition function value to query
146   oracles with, which limits the training set to only existing samples. It also limits how much the
147   model can learn with each query, since the maximally informative sample may not be present in
148   the candidate set. Query synthesis approaches (Angluin, 1988) have been proposed to avoid this
149   problem by using a generative model to generate new queries. Hernandez-Garcia et al. (2023)
150   have applied these ideas to drug discovery problems by training a generative model to optimize the
151   acquisition function computed by a separate multi-fidelity surrogate model, which does not take into
152   account the different distributions of optimal query compounds at each fidelity.
153
154   3     MF-LAL
155
156   We introduce Multi-Fidelity Latent space Active Learning (MF-LAL), an integrated framework for
157   compound generation using multi-fidelity Bayesian active learning. An overview of our framework
158   is shown in Figure 1. We encode molecules into a hierarchy of latent spaces (left panel), one for
159   each fidelity, and learn surrogates that predict the oracle output based on the latent vectors (middle
160   panel). These oracles are used to reverse optimize in the latent spaces to generate new compounds
161   with high predicted scores. The generated molecules are fed to an oracle at a chosen fidelity in an
      active learning loop, the output of which is used to re-train the latent representation and surrogate


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      models (right panel). After training, we use the surrogates to reverse optimize in the highest fidelity
163   latent space and generate compounds with high property scores at the highest fidelity. See Appendix
164   A for details of our model and a diagram of the network architecture.

179
      Latent representation. To learn the generative models, we first learn an encoding of the input
180
      molecule to the lowest fidelity latent space. Specifically, we use a single probabilistic encoder qϕ
181   parameterized by ϕ that encodes a molecule x into mean and variance vectors µ1 and σ1 . The latent
182   vector z1 ∼ N (µ1 , σ1 ), corresponding to the first (lowest) fidelity, is sampled from the resulting
183   distribution. Since we want a separate latent space at each fidelity level, we define a set of prob-
184   abilistic networks hξ1 (z1 ), . . . , hξK−1 (zK−1 ) with parameters ξ1 , . . . , ξK−1 that pass information
185   between latent spaces. Specifically, hξk takes the vector zk as input and outputs a mean and variance
186   vector in the subsequent latent space, µk+1 , σk+1 . We sample from this distribution to obtain latent
187   vector zk+1 , i.e. zk+1 ∼ N (µk+1 , σk+1 ). We also define a set of probabilistic decoder networks
188   pθ1 (·|z1 ), . . . , pθK (·|zK ) to reconstruct the original molecule x from the latent vectors. The use of
189
      a specialized decoder for each fidelity level improves reconstruction quality compared to previous
      methods that only use one, thus making the generated samples more tailored for their fidelity level.
190
191   We represent molecules using SELFIES strings (Krenn et al., 2020). The encoder and decoder of
192   MF-LAL are fully-connected neural networks that use a flattened, one-hot encoded SELFIES string.
193   See Table 2 for comparison with other encoder and decoder designs.

      Surrogate modeling. In order to generate molecules with high property scores, we aim to learn
196
      differentiable surrogates fˆ1 , . . . , fˆK that approximate the oracles and use them for reverse optimiza-
197
      tion in the latent spaces. Each surrogate fˆk maps from its corresponding latent vector zk to an
198
      estimate of fk . We use gradient-based optimization to find a point in a given latent space that has
199
      a high property score predicted by the surrogate (“reverse optimization”), which can then be de-
200   coded to a molecule (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018). The hξk networks, which pass information
201   between latent spaces, allow us to re-use information learned about the molecule’s binding proper-
202   ties at lower fidelities to aid in prediction at the higher fidelities without having to re-learn it using
203   large amounts of high-fidelity data. This is because training the surrogate models organize each
204   latent space (Tevosyan et al., 2022) for property prediction at that level, and so the latent vectors
205   contain information about the binding properties useful for predicting the oracle output that can then
206   be passed to higher fidelities. Additionally, the use of separate latent spaces for each fidelity level, as
207   opposed to previous approaches that use only a single latent space shared across all levels, improves
208
      surrogate modeling performance because each latent space can be organized for prediction at just
      that level.

244   Training a multi-fidelity surrogate model requires significant computational resources, especially to
245   gather data at the highest fidelity level. Instead of passively collecting training data, we develop a
246   Bayesian active learning approach to efficiently query the oracles, allowing us to make fewer queries
247
      to the most expensive oracles. As show in Algorithm 1, our active learning cycle consists of first
      generating a molecule to query at a chosen fidelity, querying the oracle to obtain the property score,
248
      appending the result to the dataset, and then retraining the model. We repeat the process until some
249
      computational budget is reached.
250
251   Similar to Kandasamy et al. (2016), we start with only querying the oracle at the lowest fidelity level
252   k = 1 and increase to higher fidelities when the model’s uncertainty falls below certain thresholds.
253

254

255

257   putational budget is reached. We use this stepwise approach to ensure all surrogates have enough

297
      Our goal is to generate compounds at fidelity k that maximize some generation objective. To accom-
298
      plish this, we perform gradient-based optimization to find a point zk in the kth latent space that max-
299
      imizes the generation objective, and then decode zk into a molecule using pθk . For our generation
300
      objective, we do not want to simply maximize fˆk , but instead the upper confidence bound (Eq. 2)
301
      to ensure exploration during active learning. In addition, we also introduce a novel likelihood-based
302   term to the generation objective that encourages the model to only sample compounds at higher
303   fidelities that also scored well at the lower fidelities. Specifically, when generating a molecule at fi-
304   delity k, we maximize the likelihood that the molecule would also be generated at fidelity k − 1 with
305   a high property score. This additional term greatly restricts the area of the chemical space explored
306   by the high fidelity oracles, reducing the computational cost wasted on non-promising areas and
307   making the use of high-cost oracles feasible. It also means the higher fidelity latent spaces encode
308   a more limited distribution of compounds, improving the quality of samples generated from those
309   latent spaces. Indeed, we show that the likelihood term is critical for strong performance (Table 2).

335   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
336
      We define a multi-fidelity environment for binding affinity which uses four oracles, each of which
337
      takes a molecule as input and outputs an estimate of its binding affinity with increasing accuracy:
338
339         1. Linear regression (f1 ). Simple linear regression model trained on experimental data from
340            the BRD4(2)/c-MET target from BindingDB (Liu et al., 2007) to predict the Ki , a measure
341            of binding affinity. Morgan fingerprints are used to represent the molecule.
342         2. AutoDock4 (f2 ) (Morris et al., 2009). Uses 3D geometric and charge information from the
343            protein and compound to estimate the binding energy.
344         3. Ensembled AutoDock4 (f3 ) (Morris et al., 2009). Same as above, except we dock the
345            compound into the binding pockets of eight BRD4/five c-MET cocrystal structures that
346            were solved with different known ligands, and then take the minimum predicted energy.
347            This ensemble approach is generally more accurate than using a single protein structure
348            (Amaro et al., 2018).
349         4. Absolute binding free energy (ABFE) (f4 ) (Heinzelmann & Gilson, 2021). A binding
350            free energy method applicable to de novo drug discovery that uses molecular dynamics
351            simulations to accurately predict the binding energy.
352
      We target the BRD4(2) and c-MET proteins (PDB 5UF0 and 5EOB), both of which are implicated
353
      in human cancer development, although through different biological mechanisms. We chose these
354
      targets because ABFE is already well-validated on them and known to have good agreement with
355   experimental data (Heinzelmann & Gilson, 2021). See Appendix B for further experimental details
356   and analysis of the oracles, including experiments confirming that our higher fidelity oracles are
357   more costly yet more accurate at distinguishing experimental actives from inactives.
358
      Each model is provided with an initial dataset of random ZINC250k (Irwin et al., 2012) compounds
359
      queried at each fidelity (see Appendix B for further details). To compare models, we run each in
360
      an active learning loop using a fixed computational budget of 7 days, and then generate 15 unique
361   compounds at the highest fidelity predicted to have the best scores. We then run these compounds
362   through ABFE and compare their scores. Based on the real-world use case of our method, and
363   following other works (Lee et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2022), we focus particularly on
364   the properties of the top 3 generated compounds. This is because drug campaigns would take only
365   the top compounds generated and use them as starting points for further optimization, so the binding
366   affinities of the top compounds are the most relevant for measuring performance. See Appendix C
367   for additional results showing the oracle-predicted binding energy of the generated query compounds
368   over the active learning process, and the reconstruction accuracy for each fidelity decoder during
369
      training.

504   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
505
506   We present Multi-Fidelity Latent space Active Learning (MF-LAL), an integrated framework for
507   generative and multi-fidelity surrogate modeling. Our experiments show that MF-LAL generates
508
      compounds with significantly higher activity, as predicted by a gold-standard binding free energy
      oracle, than other single and multi-fidelity approaches. Thus, MF-LAL shows promise as a way to
509
      generate compounds with real-world binding while incurring a reasonable computational cost.
510
511   Limitations of our approach include a limited set of oracles and a potential lack of synthesizability of
512   the generated compounds, since SAscore is known to be imperfect (Skoraczyński et al., 2023). The
513   SVGP technique we use for our surrogate models is known to overestimate the posterior variance far
514
      away from the inducing points (Bauer et al., 2016), potentially biasing our method towards out-of-
      distribution molecules and increasing the unpredictability of the surrogate outputs. We also note that
515
      by design, MF-LAL may miss scaffolds at the highest fidelity that have strong binding if the lower
516
      fidelity oracles did not score that scaffold well. We consider this a necessary tradeoff to make the
517   search for good compounds at the highest fidelity computationally tractable. Finally, it should also
518   be noted that our experimental setup of evaluating each method after a fixed run time of 1 week may
519   lead to high variance in the results, because each method has not been trained to convergence. While
520   this most accurately reflects real-world usage, it may mean the ABFE values of the top generated
521   compounds are somewhat variable between runs. Future work could include using a reaction-aware
522   generative model that generates more synthesizable molecules (Horwood & Noutahi, 2020).

      A       M ODEL DETAILS
757
758
      The encoder, decoder, and h networks are all 3-layer feed-forward networks with ReLU activations
759   and a 512-dimensional hidden layer. Each latent space has 64 dimensions. Molecules are repre-
760   sented using SELFIES strings (Krenn et al., 2020), and fed to the network using a flattened one-hot
761   encoding representation. The loss on the latent encodings is the ELBO, which is the sum of the KL
762   divergence and the cross-entropy loss of the reconstruction.
763
      The surrogate models consist of a 4-layer deep kernel (Wilson et al., 2016) to encode the input and a
764
      Matern kernel for the covariance function. To accelerate training, we use an approximate GP trained
765   with the ELBO loss (Hensman et al., 2015).
766
767   At each active learning step, we train the whole model from scratch until convergence with the Adam
768
      optimizer using a learning rate of 0.0001. For the molecule generation procedure using gradient-
      based optimization, we use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.1 for 100 epochs.

800   All experiments were conducted on a server with 8 RTX 2080 Ti GPUs. For our model and each
801   baseline, we performed a random hyperparameter search with 20 trials using only the first 3 fi-
802   delities, and took the set of hyperparameters with the best generated samples at f3 following 3
803   hours of active learning. We excluded ABFE from the hyperparameter search due to computational
804   cost, and just used the same set of hyperparameters for all subsequent experiments using all 4 fi-
805   delities. We note that MF-LAL was somewhat sensitive to choice of hyperparameters, especially
806
      the KLD/reconstruction ratio for the decoders and the diversity coefficient used during generation.
      Thus, we advise users of MF-LAL to conduct a hyperparameter search similar to the method we
807
      used before applying MF-LAL to a new set of oracles.

      B.1   O RACLES
817
818   For all oracles, we estimated the cost (for the baselines that require it) using the average run time
819   over 10 samples with random input compounds. We also computed the ROC-AUC (shown below)
820   of each oracle for the BRD4(2) and c-MET targets to confirm that the higher cost oracles are more
821   accurate. To do this, we generated a dataset of 32 known active and presumed inactive compounds
822   for BRD4(2) and c-MET, and then ran each oracle on all of the compounds. The actives were
823   retrieved from the BRD4(2)/c-MET target from BindingDB with Ki < 10µM , and the inactives
824   were generated using DUD-E (Mysinger et al., 2012). As expected, for both targets, the ROC-AUC
825
      increases with higher fidelity, as well as the computational cost. This shows that the higher cost
      oracles are indeed more accurate, and so the hierarchy of fidelities we use is sensible and applicable
826
      to multi-fidelity learning.
827
828
829
      Linear regression (cost: 0.1s, ROC-AUC BRD4(2): 0.59, c-MET: 0.68) We used BRD4(2) and,
      separately, c-MET data from BindingDB (Liu et al., 2007) to train a simple linear regression model.
830
      The input to the model was 2048-bit Morgan fingerprints, and the output was the experimental
831
      binding energy in Ki , converted to kcal/mol.
832
833
834
      AutoDock4 (cost: 4s, ROC-AUC BRD4(2): 0.73, c-MET: 0.72) We prepared the AutoDock4
      grid files using AutoDockTools (Morris et al., 2009). Arbitrary ligands were prepared using obabel
835
      (O’Boyle et al., 2011) with pH 7.4 and gasteiger partial charges. We used AutoDock-GPU (Santos-
836
      Martins et al., 2021), a GPU-accelerated version of AutoDock4, for all computation. For each
837   ligand, we performed 20 random restarts and selected the minimum predicted energy.
838
839
      Ensembled AutoDock4 (cost: 44s, ROC-AUC BRD4(2): 0.80, c-MET: 0.80) Same as above,
840
      except we used the minimum energy from 8 separate AutoDock4 runs using the same ligand and
841   each of the following protein crystal structures (listed as PDB IDs): 5ues, 5uet, 5uev,
842   5uez, 5uf0, 5uvs, 5uvy, 5uvz for BRD4(2), and 2wd1, 4deg, 4dei, 4r1v,
843   5eob for c-MET

845   Absolute binding free energy (ABFE) (cost: 9:20hrs, ROC-AUC BRD4(2): 0.92, c-MET: 0.89)
846   We use the Binding Affinity Tool (BAT.py) implementation (Heinzelmann & Gilson, 2021) for ab-
847   solute binding free energy calculation, available at https://github.com/GHeinzelmann/
848   BAT.py. For BRD4(2), we use the short tevb calculations, which were introduced recently to re-
849   duce computational cost (Heinzelmann et al., 2024). For c-MET, we use the standard SDR method,
850   but we found we could reduce simulation times for all components to 30% of their original amounts
851   and still retain strong performance. All molecular dynamics simulators are run with AMBER with
852   GPU support. As BAT.py requires a starting pose for the ligand, we used the pose generated from
853
      AutoDock4. We additionally wrote custom scripts to parallelize molecular dynamics runs across all
      available GPUs.
