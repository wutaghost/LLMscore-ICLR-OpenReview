009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               3D Referring Expression Segmentation (3D-RES) aims to generate precise seg-
012            mentation masks for targets based on free-form text descriptions. Despite sig-
013            nificant advancements, current methods still rely on costly point-level mask-
014            description pair annotations. In this paper, we introduce the Multi-Expert Net-
015            work (MEN), a novel weakly supervised framework that utilizes the multimodal
016            alignment of vision-language models across various semantic cues to reveal the
017            relationships between descriptions and 3D instances. The primary challenges lie
018            in effectively extracting and matching visual and textual context, while eliminat-
019            ing potential distractions. To address this, we propose the Multi-Expert Mining
020
               (MEM) and Multi-Expert Aggregation (MEA) modules. The MEM module em-
               ploys multiple experts to extract semantic cues from full-context, attribute, and
021
               category dimensions. The MEA module mathematically consolidates the outputs
022
               of these experts, automatically assigning greater weight to more accurate ones,
023            thus improving target selection accuracy and robustness. Extensive experiments
024            on the ScanRefer and Multi3DRefer benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of
025            our method in addressing the challenges of weakly supervised 3D-RES.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   3D Referring Expression Segmentation (3D-RES) has gained significant attention in the multimodal
032   field, aiming to segment 3D objects by aligning referring expressions with point clouds (Qian et al.,
033
      2024; He & Ding, 2024; He et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b;a). Unlike 3D refer-
      ring expression comprehension (Luo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2022; He et al.,
034
      2021; Feng et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2022), which only requires
035
      locating the target and generating a simple bounding box, 3D-RES involves producing an accurate
036   segmentation mask for the target based on free-form text descriptions. This advancement is crucial
037   for applications in human-computer interaction and autonomous driving.
038
039
      Despite notable progress (Huang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b; Qian et al., 2024), current methods
      still rely on costly point-level mask annotations for each description. For instance, annotating a scene
040
      in the ScanNet-v2 (Dai et al., 2017) dataset takes an average of 22.3 minutes. The labor-intensive
041
      nature of these annotations hinders the collection of large-scale 3D datasets, limiting the task’s
042   scalability to data-scarce domains. To address this, we propose a weakly supervised 3D-RES setting,
043   where only 3D point clouds and referring expressions are provided without mask annotations, as
044   shown in Fig. 1. In this setting, both 3D scenes and referring expressions are easier to obtain,
045   eliminating the need for laborious point-level mask annotations.
046
      One straightforward approach to address this challenge is to first utilize a pre-trained 3D model to
047
      extract proposals and render them into 2D images, which are then matched directly with referring
048   expressions using well-established 2D image-text matching techniques such as CLIP (Radford et al.,
049   2021). However, this approach isolates instances from their 3D scene context, thus failing to leverage
050   the crucial contextual cues provided in the text for accurate target localization. Another approach
051   involves selecting images with the highest visibility of 3D proposals from the video used to construct
052   the 3D point cloud (Boudjoghra et al., 2024), and then employing CLIP for matching. While this
053   method more effectively utilizes the contextual information from the text, it introduces a significant
      challenge: if the image contains multiple proposals, it can lead to considerable matching ambiguity.

067   Thus, the main challenge of this task lies in how to efficiently mine and match both visual and textual
068   context while filtering out potential distractions.
069   To achieve this goal, we propose a Multi-Expert Network (MEN), a framework that leverages mul-
070   tiple cues to accomplish text-based referential reasoning. This framework breaks the problem into
071   two sub-tasks: first, how to effectively mine and match visual and textual context; second, how to
072   integrate the results of the first step to filter out distracting information while highlighting the target
073   instance. For the first sub-task, we introduce a Multi-Expert Mining (MEM) module, which designs
074   experts to extract information from multiple semantic cues, specifically focusing on full-context, at-
075
      tribute, and category dimensions to derive the target’s distribution. The full-context expert processes
      the complete visual and textual context, assessing the match between proposals and the descrip-
076
      tion from the perspective of spatial relationships and overall scene. The attribute expert focuses on
077
      fine-grained details such as shape, color, and texture of the instance, distinguishing proposals at the
078   attribute level. The category expert, meanwhile, concentrates on the semantic category match be-
079   tween the proposal and the text description, ensuring robust matching unaffected by noise. For the
080   second sub-task, directly integrating the probability distributions produced by the experts through
081   summation or pooling may seem intuitive, but it fails to effectively suppress noise and emphasize
082   the target. Therefore, we propose a Multi-Expert Aggregation (MEA) module that consolidates the
083   match distributions generated by the three experts through the framework of distribution conflation.
084   This module automatically assigns greater weight to input distributions derived from more accurate
085   experts, specifically those exhibiting smaller standard deviations. In contrast to straightforward in-
086
      tegration methods, the MEA module effectively filters out distractions and enhances the significance
      of the proposal corresponding to the target object.
087
088   To sum up, the contributions of this paper are three-fold:
089
090            • We propose a weakly supervised 3D-RES task, and introduce the Multi-Expert Network
091
                 (MEN), a framework that leverages multiple cues for referring expression reasoning, sig-
                 nificantly reducing the need for costly mask-description pair annotations.
092
093            • We design the Multi-Expert Mining (MEM) module, which effectively extracts and aligns
094              visual and textual context from three complementary cues. Additionally, we introduce the
095              Multi-Expert Aggregation (MEA) module, which integrates the knowledge from multiple
096
                 experts, filtering out interference while emphasizing the target instance.
097            • Extensive experiments on ScanRefer and Multi3DRefer datasets demonstrate the effective-
098              ness of our proposed method in addressing weakly-supervised 3D-RES.

      2.1     3D R EFERRING E XPRESSION C OMPREHENSION AND S EGMENTATION
103
104   The 3D-REC aims to locate a target object in a 3D scene based on a referring language description
105   and generate a bounding box (Luo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2022; He et al.,
106   2021; Feng et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2022), while 3D-RES
107   focuses on producing a corresponding segmentation mask (Qian et al., 2024; He & Ding, 2024; He
      et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b;a). The primary benchmark datasets for 3D-REC

108
      and RES include ScanRefer (Chen et al., 2020) and ReferIt3D (Achlioptas et al., 2020). Recent
109   approaches have started to explore weakly supervised settings for 3D-REC (Wang et al., 2023b;
110   Xu et al., 2023). However, 3D-RES remains in its infancy, with current advancements primarily
111   concentrated on fully supervised methods that necessitate point-level annotations. To reduce the
112   reliance on costly annotations, we pioneer a weakly-supervised framework tailored for 3D-RES.
113
114   2.2   W EAKLY-S UPERVISED R EFERRING I MAGE S EGMENTATION
115
116   Similar to 3D-RES, Referring Image Segmentation (RIS) aims to segment objects in images based
117   on linguistic descriptions. Recent approaches (Hu et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2019; Li
118
      et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2023d; Yang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2020;
      Liu et al., 2023a; Xia et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022) have achieved significant performance improve-
119
      ments in fully supervised settings. However, compared to fully supervised RIS, weakly supervised
120
      RIS (Lee et al., 2023; Dai & Yang, 2024; Kim et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c) is more challenging
121   due to the lack of pixel-level annotations. TSEG (Strudel et al., 2022) introduces a weakly su-
122   pervised setting to the RIS task for the first time. PPT (Dai & Yang, 2024) effectively leveraging
123   SAM’s (Kirillov et al., 2023) segmentation capabilities to obtain high-quality segmentation masks.
124
125   2.3   C ONTRASTIVE L ANGUAGE -I MAGE P RE - TRAINING
126
127
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is a multimodal model based on contrastive learning, trained on a large
      dataset of images and corresponding textual descriptions, which integrates visual and language
128
      modalities. It has been demonstrated to solve various tasks, such as open vocabulary segmenta-
129
      tion (Takmaz et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024). Although CLIP is widely used,
130   its restricted input text length renders it inadequate for processing detailed descriptions. To address
131   this issue, Zhang et al. (2024) proposed Long-CLIP as a plug-and-play alternative to CLIP, which
132   supports longer text inputs while aligning with CLIP’s latent space. In this paper, we introduce
133   Long-CLIP processing of detailed descriptions to achieve a more comprehensive understanding.
134
135   2.4   O PEN -VOCABULARY 3D S EGMENTATION
136
      Open-Vocabulary 3D Segmentation aims aims to extend the scope of 3D instance segmentation to
137
      any given short category name. OpenScene (Peng et al., 2023) leverages 2D open-vocabulary seman-
138   tic segmentation models to map pixel-wise 2D CLIP features into 3D space. OpenMask3D (Tak-
139   maz et al., 2023) leverages SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to gen-
140   erate 3D clip features for each class-agnostic 3D proposal using RGB-D images of the 3D scene.
141   Open3DIS (Nguyen et al., 2024) integrates 2D and 3D proposals using a novel fusion approach for
142   2D masks through hierarchical agglomerative clustering. Unlike Open-Vocabulary 3D Segmenta-
143   tion, which primarily emphasizes the extraction of visual features, 3D-RES often focuses on the
144   alignment between complex textual descriptions and visual information.

148   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
149
150   Given a point cloud scene containing Np points, represented as P ∈ RNp ×(3+F ) , where each point
151   has its 3D coordinates and F -dimensional auxiliary information, such as RGB. Correspondingly,
152
      given a text description containing Nw words, represented as T ∈ RNw , which describes the object
      of interest. The goal of the 3D-RES task is to locate the target object based on the text and output
153
      the corresponding segmentation mask M ∈ {0, 1}Np . In the weakly-supervised setting, the training
154
      phase does not provide description-mask pairs for supervision, but only the class annotations Ycls ∈
155   RNcls of the target objects, where Ncls is the number of classes. Thus, we model the selection of
156   the target object as solving for the discrete probability distribution Q of the instance containing the
157   target. By sampling from Q, we obtain the final predicted instance.

285   3.4    M ULTI -E XPERT AGGREGATION
286
      The MEM module utilizes various experts to extract diverse and complementary visual-language
287
      cues, resulting in distinct discrete probability distributions: Pcls , Pattr , and Pf ull . However, inte-
288
      grating these distributions while eliminating distractions and emphasizing the target instance is a
289   significant challenge. Thus, we first analyze the strengths and weaknesses of each expert.
290
291   Strengths and Weaknesses of the Experts. The full-context expert excels at capturing complete
292
      spatial context but can introduce distractions from complex 2D video frames, making it suitable
      for scenarios where spatial relationships are crucial for identifying targets. The attribute expert
293
      focuses on the visual attributes of the instance and the corresponding textual descriptions but may be
294
      influenced by rendering accuracy and parsing tools, making it ideal for cases with unique and distinct
295   target attributes. The category expert leverages the classification capabilities of the visual backbone,
296   demonstrating strong resistance to distractions by effectively eliminating irrelevant objects that do
297   not match the target category, but it often lacks intra-class differentiation.
298
      These experts exhibit different strengths, and their predicted distributions contain various distrac-
299
      tions and conflicts, as illustrated in Fig. 3. Therefore, simply integrating these distributions through
300
      summation or pooling cannot effectively eliminate mutual interference, potentially undermining the
301   enhancement of the final target distribution.
302
303   Aggregation Approach. To address this, we propose the Multi-Expert Aggregation (MEA) mod-
304
      ule, which mathematically assigns greater weight to the inputs from more accurate experts, specif-
      ically those with smaller standard deviations (Hill, 2011). The final distribution obtained through
305
      MEA is both the unique minimax likelihood ratio consolidation and the unique proportional likeli-
306
      hood ratio consolidation of the given input distributions (Hill, 2011), ensuring a robust integration
307   of expert predictions.
308

341   4.1   DATASETS
342
      ScanRefer (Chen et al., 2020) contains 51,583 natural language expressions of 11,046 objects from
343
      800 ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017) scenes. It can be split into “Unique” and “Multiple”, depending on
344
      whether there are multiple objects of the same category as the target in the scene.
345
346
      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRIC
347
348   We adopted mIoU (mean Intersection over Union) and “R@n, IoU@m” as evaluation metrics,
349   which represents the percentage of at least one of the top-n predicted proposals with the high-
350   est probability having an IoU > m compared to the ground truth mask, with n ∈ {1, 3} and
351   m ∈ {0.25, 0.5}. For the mIoU metric, we set n = 1.
352
353   4.3   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
354
355   In our study, we utilize the pre-trained SoftGroup (Vu et al., 2022) as our 3D instance segmentation
356
      backbone. We freeze a pre-trained CLIP with ViT-B/32(Radford et al., 2021) and a pre-trained Long-
      CLIP with LongCLIP-L(Zhang et al., 2024). The text classifier is an MLP trained from scratch,
357
      starting with an initial learning rate of 0.001. To optimize this rate, we implement the Cosine
358
      Annealing strategy. It takes 20 epochs to train our model with a batch size of 32. The proposal
359   number No , the number of rendering views Kv are set to 256 and 6, respectively. For top-K frames
360   selection we use K = 15. To ensure reproducibility, our sampling procedure is configured for
361   greedy sampling, wherein the target is selected from the top n proposals with the highest probability
362   mass, with n ∈ {1, 3}. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU.
363

534
      In this work, we introduce the Multi-Expert Network (MEN) for weakly supervised 3D Referring
535   Expression Segmentation, eliminating the need for costly annotations. MEN consists of two mod-
536   ules: MEM and MEA. The MEM module employs multiple experts to extract semantic cues from
537   various dimensions, while MEA robustly consolidates their outputs. Experiments demonstrate the
538   effectiveness of our method in addressing the challenges of weakly supervised 3D-RES.

771   into 5 types: horizontal proximity, vertical proximity, between, allocentric and support, and then
772
      generates descriptions using language templates.

970   weakly supervised setup. It uses only the category labels Yzt ∈ R2 , where Yzt = (0, 1) indicates
971   “zero-target” and Yzt = (1, 0) indicates “not zero-target,” without the need for costly point-level
