009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge distillation typically employs the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to
012            constrain the output of the student model to precisely match the soft labels provided
013            by the teacher model. However, the optimization process of KL divergence is
014            challenging for the student and prone to suboptimal points. Also, we demonstrate
015            that the gradients provided by KL divergence depend on channel scale and thus tend
016            to overlook low-probability channels. The mismatch in low-probability channels
017
               also results in the neglect of inter-class relationship information, making it difficult
               for the student to further enhance performance. To address this issue, we propose
018
               an auxiliary ranking loss based on Kendall’s τ Coefficient, which can be plug-
019
               and-play in any logit-based distillation method, providing inter-class relationship
020            information and balancing the attention to low-probability channels. We show that
021            the proposed ranking loss is less affected by channel scale, and its optimization
022            objective is consistent with that of KL divergence. Extensive experiments on
023            CIFAR-100, ImageNet, and COCO datasets, as well as various CNN and ViT
024            teacher-student architecture combinations, demonstrate that the proposed ranking
025            loss can be plug-and-play on various baselines and enhance their performance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The recent advancements in deep neural networks (DNN) have significantly enhanced performance
031
      in the field of computer vision. However, the heightened computational and storage costs associated
      with complex networks limite their applicability. To address this, knowledge distillation (KD) has
032
      been proposed to obtain performant lightweight models. Typically, knowledge distillation involves a
033
      well-trained heavy teacher model and an untrained lightweight student model. The same data is fed
034   to both the teacher and the student, with the teacher’s outputs serving as soft labels for training the
035   student. By constraining the student to produce predictions that match the soft labels, the knowledge
036   in the teacher model is transferred to the lightweight student model.
037
      Most logit-based KD methods adhere to the paradigm introduced by Hinton (Hinton et al., 2015),
038
      employing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence (Kullback & Leibler, 1951) to align the logits
039
      output by the student and teacher models:

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
130
131
      Knowledge Distillation. Knowledge distillation, initially proposed by Hinton (Hinton et al., 2015),
132
      serves as a method for model compression and acceleration, aiming to transfer knowledge from a
133   heavy teacher model to a lightweight student model. By feeding the same samples, the teacher can
134   produce soft labels, and training the student with these soft labels allows the transfer of knowledge
135   from the teacher model to the student. Knowledge distillation tasks can be mainly divided into two
136   categories: feature-based distillation (Chen et al., 2022; Zhang & Ma, 2021; Yang et al., 2022b; Guo
137   et al., 2023; Park et al., 2019) and logit-based distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Li et al., 2023; Zhao
138   et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Chi et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2021). Feature-based
139   distillation additionally utilizes the model’s intermediate features, providing more information to
140   the student and enabling the student to learn at the feature level from the teacher, often resulting
141
      in better performance. Considering safety and privacy, the intermediate outputs of the model are
      often not obtainable; hence, logit-based methods that only use the model outputs for distillation offer
142
      better versatility and robustness. Our proposed method can act as a plug-and-play module added to
143
      logit-based methods, offering higher flexibility and further enhancing the performance of logit-based
144   methods.
145
146   Recent knowledge distillation methods have found that overly strict constraints can sometimes
147
      hinder the student’s transfer of knowledge from the teacher’s soft labels. For instance, (Cao et al.,
      2022) discovered that differences in feature sizes in feature-based methods could limit the student’s
148
      learning; while (Sun et al., 2024) found that using the same temperature for both teacher and student
149
      in logit-based methods could affect further improvements in student performance. To reduce the
150   learning difficulty for the student, (Sun et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2022) provided methods to ease the
151   logit matching difficulty between student and teacher, yet we find that methods providing additional
152   guidance to the student have not been fully explored.
153
      Ranking Loss in Knowledge Distillation. For knowledge distillation, ranking loss is a relaxed
154
      constraint that can provide rich inter-class information. It was first applied in the distillation of
155
      recommendation systems (Reddi et al., 2021; Tang & Wang, 2018; Choi et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023;
156   Yang et al., 2022a), (Li et al., 2022) explored the application of ranking loss in object detection
157   tasks, and (Gao et al., 2020) discussed the role of ranking in the distillation of language model tasks.
158   However, the exploration of ranking loss in logit-based image classification task distillation is not yet
159   comprehensive. We find that the KL divergence used for distillation in classification tasks tends to
160   overlook information from smaller-valued channels and may lead to suboptimal results. Our proposed
161   method leverages ranking loss to balance the model’s attention to larger and smaller-valued channels,
      while also using inter-class relationships to help the model avoid suboptimal outcomes.

191   In this section, we introduce the plug-and-play ranking loss function designed to constrain the ranking
192   of channels in the logits output by the student model. With the aid of the ranking loss, the knowledge
193
      distillation task can balance the overemphasis on larger logit values and the neglect of smaller ones
      as measured by the KL divergence. Additionally, the ranking loss imposes a constraint on the leading
194
      channel value, which helps to correct the optimization direction and avoid suboptimal solutions.
195

196   compute the ordinal consistency between the teacher and student logits. In Section 4.2, we discuss
197   how ranking loss benefits optimizing logits distillation with KL divergence. Furthermore, we discuss
198   the differentiable form of Kendall’s τ coefficient and, based on this, design three distinct forms of
199   ranking loss functions in Appendix A.4.

253   The gradient scale of the KL divergence is influenced by the scales of the teacher and student models.
254   Consequently, for outputs from smaller channels, when the scales of the teacher and student are
255   similar, the KL divergence’s gradient for these components becomes negligible, leading to the neglect
256

      this issue, it uniformly amplifies the gradients of all logits, thereby continuing to overlook the outputs
257

258
      scale of the teacher’s logits. The gradient of a logit channel primarily depends on the difference
259   between its rank and the target rank, effectively harnessing the knowledge from smaller channels.
260   Therefore. In the early stage of training, ranking loss helps the model quickly learn the overall
261   ranking of the teacher model, which helps the model converge to a better initial solution faster.

288   the use of cross-entropy loss can mitigate this issue, it does not incorporate information from the

297   a tiger is more similar to a cat than to a fish. By learning such inter-class relationships, the student

301
302
303
      Datasets. In order to validate the efficacy and robustness of our proposed method, we conduct widely
      experiments on three datasets. 1) CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) is a significant dataset for
304
      image classification, comprising 100 categories, with 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images.
305
      2) ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) is a large-scale dataset utilized for image classification,
306   comprising 1,000 categories, with approximately 1.28 million training images and 50,000 test images.
307   3) MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014) is a mainstream dataset for object detection comprising 80 categories,
308   with 118,000 training images and 5,000 test images.
309
      Baselines. As a plug-and-play loss, we apply the proposed ranking loss to various logit-based
310
      methods, including KD (Hinton et al., 2015), CTKD (Li et al., 2023), DKD (Zhao et al., 2022), and
311
      MLKD (Jin et al., 2023), to verify whether it can bring performance gains to knowledge distillation
312   methods. We also compare it with various feature-based methods, including FitNet (Adriana et al.,
313   2015), CRD (Tian et al., 2019), and ReviewKD (Chen et al., 2021). Additionally, we compare it with
314   other auxiliary losses and modified KL divergence methods, including DIST (Huang et al., 2022) and
315   LSKD (Sun et al., 2024).
316
      Implementation Details. To ensure the robustness of the proposed plug-and-play loss without
317
      introducing excessive configurations, we maintain the same experimental settings as the baselines
318   used (KD+Ours and KD share the same experimental setups for example). We set the batch size to
319   64 for CIFAR-100, 512 for ImageNet and 8 for COCO. We employ SGD (Sutskever et al., 2013) as
320   the optimizer, with the number of epochs and learning rate settings consistent with the comparative
321   baselines. The hyper-parameters α, β in Eq. 7 are set to be the same as the compared baselines to
322   maintain fairness, and γ are set equal to α. We utilize 1 NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 to train models
323   on CIFAR-100 and 4 NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 for training on ImageNet. The algorithm of our
      method can be found in Appendix A.5.

522   6    C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, we investigate the optimization process of logit distillation and identify that the Kullback-
525   Leibler divergence tends to overlook the knowledge embedded in the smaller channels of the output.
526   Moreover, KL divergence does not guarantee alignment between the classification results of the
527   student and teacher models. To address this issue, we introduce a plug-and-play ranking loss based
528   on Kendall’s τ Coefficient that encourages the student model to pay more attention to the knowledge
529   contained within the low-probability channels, while also enforcing alignment with the teacher’s
530   predictive outcomes. We provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the gradients of the ranking
531   loss are less affected by channel scale and that its optimization objective is consistent with that of
532
      KL divergence, making it an effective auxiliary loss for distillation. Extensive experiments validate
      that our approach significantly enhances the distillation performance across various datasets and
533
      teacher-student architectures.

      Some methods address the issue of neglecting smaller channels by employing temperature scaling.
945
      We examined the probability distribution of LSKD (Sun et al., 2024), an approach using adaptive
946   temperature that has shown promising results. As shown in Fig.6, while LSKD somewhat alleviates
947   the problem of smaller channels, the transformed probabilities still predominantly occupy these
948   smaller channels. Thus, using temperature scaling does not effectively resolve the issue of neglecting
949   smaller channels.
