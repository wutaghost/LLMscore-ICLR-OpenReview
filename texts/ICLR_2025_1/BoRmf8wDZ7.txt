                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009            This paper explores Masked Autoencoders (MAE) with Gaussian Splatting. While
010            reconstructive self-supervised learning frameworks such as MAE operate on low-
011            level pixels, the image synthesis community has evolved to use latent, mid-level
012            representations for better generative visual data modeling. Our approach, named
013            Gaussian Masked Autoencoder, or GMAE, aims to reconcile these two and get the
014            benefits of both worlds. Like MAE, it reconstructs the image end-to-end in the pixel
015            space; but beyond MAE, it also introduces an intermediate, 3D Gaussian-based
016            representation and renders images via splatting. We show that GMAE can enable
017
               various zero-shot learning capabilities (e.g., figure-ground segmentation, image lay-
               ering, edge detection, etc.) while preserving the high self-supervised representation
018
               quality from MAE. To our knowledge, we are the first to employ Gaussian primi-
019
               tives in an image representation learning framework beyond optimization-based
020            single-scene reconstructions. We believe GMAE will inspire further research in
021            this direction and contribute to developing next-generation techniques for modeling
022            high-fidelity visual data.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Vision systems, by nature, process raw, low-level observations of the world, but visual reasoning
      frequently requires higher-level semantic abstractions of the data as well as spatial and geometric
028
      understanding. In this work we aim to effectively learn both semantic and geometric representations
029
      by learning masked auto encoding controlled by 3D Gaussians.
030
031   Learning high level semantics, can be achieved by supervised learning (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; He
032   et al., 2015; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) or by learning binding from large scale paired datasets (Radford
033
      et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). One promising approach in
      this direction is self-supervised learning Oquab et al. (2023); Grill et al. (2020); Bao et al. (2021); He
034
      et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2022). Initial efforts have tapped different tokens such as VAE (Ramesh et al.,
035
      2021; Kingma, 2013; Bao et al., 2021) or manually designed features Dalal & Triggs (2005); Wei et al.
036   (2022). Masked Autoencoders (MAE) (He et al., 2022) demonstrated that representation learning
037   could be just as effective by directly predicting the RGB values of masked patches. By directly
038   predicting pixels, MAE enjoys both simplicity – no complication in staged training or dependence on
039   external models, and end-to-end optimizability – a fundamental idea that underpins the success of
040   deep learning.
041
      On the other hand, visual reasoning also needs spatial understanding, and 3D awareness. To address
042   the spatial and 3D understanding required for visual reasoning, extensive research has explored
043   learning 3D representations from both single-view and multi-view setups (Kar et al., 2015; Tulsiani
044   et al., 2017; Yariv et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2018; Riegler & Koltun, 2020). Methods based on single-
045   view learning leverage prior knowledge to reconstruct 3D geometry from limited input, often relying
046   on implicit representations (Tatarchenko et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020). Multi-view
047   approaches, such as those utilizing photometric consistency, have demonstrated strong performance
048   in 3D reconstruction by fusing spatial cues from multiple perspectives (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Kerbl
049   et al., 2023). These advances form the foundation for integrating spatial understanding.
050   In this paper, we propose to learn high-level semantics and to have geometric understanding jointly
051   via self-supervised learning. Our idea is conceptually simple: we train a masked auto-encoder
052   pretraining, which has shown promising results on learning high level semantic tasks, along with
053   3D Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023) as intermediate representations to learn spatial and geometric
      understanding.

079
080   3D Gaussians were initially proposed for optimization-based 3D reconstruction. Different from
081   geometrically uniform representations like square pixel patches, their size, location, and information
082   distribution over the image are dynamically learned. Moreover, Gaussian-based representations
083
      could lend themselves to end-to-end learning thanks to splatting image rendering (Kerbl et al.,
      2023) that maps them back to the pixel space. We can, therefore, jointly learn such a mid-level
084
      representation within self-supervised frameworks such as MAE. We name our approach Gaussian
085
      Masked Autoencoders, or GMAE. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to explore such
086   Gaussian primitives in a visual representation learning framework, rather than an optimization-
087   based 3D reconstruction framework for single scenes. Our approach adds only a negligible overhead
088   compared to standard MAE training – the addition of splatting increases compute time by 1.5%.
089   Without compromising representation-learning performance, GMAE gains significant wins in zero-
090   shot capabilities.
091
092   Fig. 1 shows several built-in advantages of a Gaussian-based image representation. First, we note that
093   the non-uniformity of the representation leads to a spatial distribution of representational density that
094   correlates with the information density in the image. By allowing the 3D Gaussians to move along the
095   z-axis, our model learns underlying structure of the natural world by observing not only the single
096
      viewpoint of one image but millions of such single views. As a result, we can find figure-ground
      segmentation, simple layering, and edge detection as depth discontinuity without any tuning.
097
098
      Besides these advantages, we show that the representations learned with GMAE perform similarly to
099
      MAE on image classification and object detection tasks. The representation quality improves with the
100   number of Gaussians used. These results suggest GMAE augments MAE and can serve as a better
101   alternative in applications that can benefit from using mid-level representations. The advantage of
102   GMAE becomes apparent when considering that splatting-based rendering is highly efficient, and our
103   current training is almost as fast as vanilla MAE baselines.
104
105   We hope our exploration can inspire further research along this hybrid direction for representation
106   learning: the reconstruction target is grounded to pixels while jointly learning effective mid-level
107   representations for images. We believe it can contribute to the next generation of techniques for
      modeling high-fidelity visual data.

      2    R ELATED WORK
109
110
111   Self-supervised Learning: Over the years, self-supervised pre-training has proven effective in many
112   areas, including language, vision, and robotics. In computer vision, there are two primary schools of
113   thought: discriminative and reconstructive pre-training with visual data. Discriminative pre-training
114
      involves training an instance discrimination model to learn the similarity between different augmented
      image versions. Wu et al. (2018) and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) showed that instance discrimination
115
      training can be used to learn strong discriminative features via contrastive learning. Later, notable
116
      works such as MoCo (He et al., 2020) and DINO (Caron et al., 2021) have shown the effectiveness of
117   such trained visual representations for various downstream tasks.
118
119   Reconstructive pre-training learns to model the data distribution by trying to reconstruct an image
120
      or a video from its noisy version. One of the most successful methods for such pre-training in
      computer vision has been the BERT (Devlin et al., 2018)-style masked modeling of images proposed
121
      by BEiT (Bao et al., 2021), and MAE (He et al., 2022). Compared to BERT, MAE uses asymmetric
122
      encoder-decoders, allowing it to be very efficient at training with high masking ratios. This style
123   of reconstructive pre-training learns strong visual priors and shows impressive results on various
124   downstream tasks such as object detection (Li et al., 2022), pose estimation (Xu et al., 2022), and
125   robot tasks (Radosavovic et al., 2022).
126
127
128   Mid-level Representations: Image can be constructed by operating functions on some representations.
129   One line of approachs keep the representations in the latent spaces, and use a pretrained decoder
130
      network to re-construct the image. VAE (Kingma, 2013) with image synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022;
      Li et al., 2023) are good examples of this case, along with MAE He et al. (2022) and BEiT Bao
131
      et al. (2021). Other line of approaches follow a structured representations to represent an image.
132
      There are various such options: super-pixels, Gaussians, SVG code and multi-plane images etc. For
133   example Super-pixel sampling networks (Jampani et al., 2018) learns to predict super-pixels as the
134   representation to reconstruct and to predict segmentations and flow. Multi-plane images is another
135   way to represent an image (Tucker & Snavely, 2020), where an image is composed by multiple
136   layered planes, and can be learned end-to-end. There are hybrid approaches also exist. For example,
137   slot-attention (Locatello et al., 2020), learns an intermediate representation for objects, by adding a
138   bottleneck in the model architecture. Similarly Leopart (Ziegler & Asano, 2022) learns to cluster the
139   patches based on self-supervised clustering. In this paper, we take another approach which uses 3D
140   Gaussians as intermediate representations to reconstruct an image.
141
142
      Gaussian Splatting: Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) is a novel differentiable volume splatting
143
      technique using Gaussian primitives as the 3D representation, offering high optimization flexibility
144   and high fidelity in reconstruction. This idea follows a long list of differentiable rendering techniques
145   that recently gained significant attention as a method to bridge the gap between the 3D world and 2D
146   images. Differentiable rendering allows for reconstructing 3D representations (e.g., meshes, point
147   clouds) from 2D image signals by enabling gradient-based optimization. For example, (Liu et al.,
148   2019) introduces a differentiable rasterizer for meshes using probability aggregation. (Lassner &
149   Zollhöfer, 2021) proposes an efficient and differentiable formula to render large sets of point clouds.
150   (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and (Barron et al., 2022) apply differentiable volume rendering (Levoy,
151   1990) to reconstruct 3D radiance fields from a handful of multi-view images.
152   In this paper, we propose that 3D Gaussians are a useful learned mid-level image representation
153   due to their non-uniformity properties. We take advantage of the splatting operation from (Kerbl
154   et al., 2023) that enables end-to-end training of mid-level representations with image-based losses.
155   We then use Gaussian primitives in a representation learning framework rather than a single-scene
156   optimization-based 3D reconstruction as in (Kerbl et al., 2023), thus opening the door for using
157   learned 3D Gaussian representations in computer vision applications.
158   The key advantages of Gaussian splatting include its ability to adapt to scene complexity, efficient
159   rendering, and high-quality reconstructions. Unlike uniform voxel grids, Gaussian primitives can
160   vary in size and density, allowing for more compact and expressive representations of 3D scenes.
161   This adaptability makes them particularly suitable for a wide range of computer vision tasks, from
      3D reconstruction to novel view synthesis and beyond.

      3     M ETHOD
163
164   We propose a method that reconciles pixel-based learning, the mainstream in self-supervised learning,
165   and latent-based learning, which can impose extra properties on representations. Our key insight is
166   that end-to-end learnable 3D Gaussians are good candidates for mid-level image representations due
167   to their non-uniform properties. Given a large collection of images, we train a Masked Autoencoder
168   (MAE) (He et al., 2022) to reconstruct full images from their masked inputs. The MAE encoder is a
169   ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) that learns to encode the visible square patches of the masked images
170   into learned embeddings. However, rather than predicting patches of pixels directly as in MAE (He
171   et al., 2022), our ViT-based decoder predicts explicit 3D Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023) — their color,
172
      3D center location, scale, and orientation. We then render these Gaussians as images with a splatting
      differentiable renderer and train the entire model using an MSE loss in pixel space.
173
174   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
175
176   Self-supervised masking autoencoders. Masking autoencoders model the data distribution by
177   randomly masking parts of the data and predicting the masked parts. In language, BERT (Devlin
178
      et al., 2018) is trained by masking part of the text tokens and predicting them using a transformer
      model (Vaswani et al., 2017). In vision, MAE (He et al., 2022) and BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) mask image
179
      patches at the input and predict the masked regions. In MAE (He et al., 2022), a ViT (Dosovitskiy
180
      et al., 2020) encoder encodes the visible patches, and a smaller decoder ViT model is used with
181   masked tokens to reconstruct the masked patches.
182
183   3D Gaussian primitives and splatting. Our model learns a mid-level image representation using the
184
      3D Gaussian primitives originally proposed for optimization-based single-scene 3D reconstruction
      by (Kerbl et al., 2023). Each Gaussian is characterized by a 3D covariance matrix ! → R3→3 and
185
      a center location p → R3 . Additionally, each Gaussian is assigned a color r → R3 and an opacity
186
      o → R to encode the scene content. For image rendering, these Gaussian primitives are transformed
187   into camera coordinates and projected onto the image plane using volume splatting. Due to the
188   differentiable nature of this process, the attributes of the Gaussian primitives can receive gradients
189   from the rendered image. In our work, we adopt the standard approach of factorizing the covariance
190   matrix ! = RSS T RT into a scaling matrix S = diag(s) → R3→3 represented by a scale vector
191   s → R3 , and a rotation matrix R → R3→3 represented by a rotation quaternion ω → R4 . Consequently,
192   each Gaussian is parameterized by a 14-dimensional vector g = {p, s, ω, r, o} → R14 .
193
      3.2 O UR A PPROACH
194
195   Our model has a ViT-based encoder model, a lightweight decoder model, and a differentiable renderer.
196
      Fig. 2 shows a high-level overview of our method. For a given image, we first patchify it into N
      patches and randomly mask them with a masking ratio r, resulting in n visible patches. The ViT
197
      encoder model only sees the visible patches and encodes them from patches to latent embeddings,
198
      xi → Rdenc , i → {1, 2, 3, ...n}.
199
200   Assume the decoder has k learnable query tokens qj → Rddec , j → {0, 1, 2, ...k}. Note that k can be
201   any value irrespective of the number of masked tokens. We project the encoder latent to x̂i → Rddec
202   and concatenate it with the query tokens.
203                                        Xdec = {x̂i }ni=1 ↑ {qj }kj=1                                 (1)
204
205   The decoder sees the Xdec tokens and predicts k Gaussians, one for each query token (we discard
206
      the predictions for the latent tokens). Each Gaussian is parameterized by a 14-dimensional vector
      gj = {p, s, ω, r, o} → R14 .
207
208   Once we have k predicted Gaussians, we splat them on a plane with a fixed camera projection and
209   render the splatted Gaussians to generate an image. We limit the size of the Gaussians by using an
210   effective scale c · sigmoid(s). Here, c controls a Gaussian’s maximum size. After rendering, we use
211   a mean squared error loss to compare the reconstructed image with the input image on the originally
212
      masked pixels.
213   Note that since the Gaussians are the output of the decoder, they are effectively randomly initialized.
214   This is in contrast to the typical usages of Gaussian splatting for 3D reconstruction that rely on
215   point-cloud initialization. In this work, we do not use any prior knowledge. We directly learn all the
      Gaussian properties from reconstructing the image.

359   In this section, we study the properties of the decoder of our pre-trained models. Unlike MAE, we now
360   have access to an intermediate representation that we can edit and modify without re-training. Given
361   an input image, we fully encode the image and generate Gaussians that we splat to reconstruct the

      image. First, we evaluate the image reconstruction quality of our model. On the ImageNet validation
      set, our ViT-base model achieved 89.45 on reconstruction FID, while the MAE ViT-base model got
363
      98.12 (lower is better). This improvement in reconstruction quality is due to the non-uniformity of
364
      the learned distribution of Gaussians, which allows GMAE to model high-frequency information, as
365   shown in Fig. 5. As a result, our reconstructions can be used directly for other tasks without needing
366   to add a GAN loss or an upsampling layer on top.

368
      Another advantage of using a 3D Gaussian representation to represent 2D images is that it learns to
      separate objects in the z direction. This may be due to the fact that with random initialization, the
369
      points closer to the camera represent low-frequency information, while the points far from the camera
370
      model the high-frequency information (see Section 4.4). To segment an image along the z axis, we
371   simply sort the predicted Gaussians based on their depth value and group them into L = l0 , l1 , ...ld
372   groups. To split an image into two layers, we try to render it as two images using l0 , ..., ln and
373   l0 , ..., ln , ln+1 . If the difference at a pixel is larger than a specific threshold th, we assign that pixel to
374   the layer n + 1. Fig. 7 shows the layering effect of a Gaussian representation with 64 layers.

501   4.4   Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS
502
503   Distribution of Gaussians in xy: Fig. 11 shows how the Gaussians are arranged differently in space
504   for different images. In patch-based methods such as MAE, patches are arranged in a uniform tile.
505   Even if we decrease the patch size to increase the number of patches, they will still be uniformly
506   allocated across all regions. However, in our work, a Gaussian can go to any part of the image, which
507   allows them to dynamically position themselves based on the input image. This property allows
508   modeling high-frequency regions with high fidelity. As shown in Fig. 5, our reconstructions can
509
      capture high-frequency regions such as faces and intricate patterns.
510   Size vs Depth: Fig. 10 shows a clear trend: the Gaussians
511   with larger scale values lie closer to the camera, while the
512   ones with smaller scale values, lie away from the cam-
513   era, on average. This distribution validates our previous
514   hypothesis in Sec 4.3, that low-frequency blobs lie closer
515
      and cover larger regions, while high-frequency blobs lie
      away from the camera, on average. At the start, some
516
      Gaussians will be closer to the camera, influencing more
517
      pixels, therefore representing low-frequency regions. Our
518   layering results are the outcome of this property. In the real
519   world, backgrounds tend to have low-frequency regions
520   while objects usually have high-frequency details. This
521   correlation leads to our zero-shot results.                    Figure 10: Size vs. Depth: Distribution

524   5 D ISCUSSION
525
526   This paper presents GMAE, a self-supervised image representation learning approach that extends
527   MAE to include a learned intermediate Gaussian representation. We show that learning to represent
528   images with 3D Gaussians has several built-in advantages that stem from their non-uniform dynam-
529   ical allocation of scale, location, and distribution. Our method, therefore, lends itself to zero-shot
530
      capabilities such as foreground-background segmentation, image layering, and edge detection. Along
      with these advantages, we demonstrate that the representation learned by our method is on par with
531
      MAE on standard supervised image recognition tasks and that it transfers to downstream tasks such
532
      as detection and segmentation via fine-tuning.
533
534   GMAE still exhibits several empirical limitations. For example, setting larger scale values at the start
535   of training results in a more challenging optimization. Compared to the number of Gaussians typically
536
      used for 3D reconstructions (up to millions), the number of Gaussians we have used in GMAE is
      bottlenecked by compute, and increasing it to more than a thousand can cause major slow-downs for
537
      pre-training. An interesting future direction is to further accelerate our pipeline.
538
539   We hope our exploration can inspire more work in this direction and unlock the next generation of
      techniques that effectively model visual data.
