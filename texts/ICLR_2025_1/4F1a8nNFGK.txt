008                                               A BSTRACT
009
               Forecasting is a critical task in decision making across various domains. While
010
               numerical data provides a foundation, it often lacks crucial context necessary for
011            accurate predictions. Human forecasters frequently rely on additional information,
012            such as background knowledge or constraints, which can be efficiently commu-
013            nicated through natural language. However, the ability of existing forecasting
014            models to effectively integrate this textual information remains an open question.
015            To address this, we introduce “Context is Key” (CiK), a time series forecasting
016            benchmark that pairs numerical data with diverse types of carefully crafted textual
017            context, requiring models to integrate both modalities. We evaluate a range of
018            approaches, including statistical models, time series foundation models, and LLM-
019
               based forecasters, and propose a simple yet effective LLM prompting method that
               outperforms all other tested methods on our benchmark. Our experiments highlight
020
               the importance of incorporating contextual information, demonstrate surprising
021
               performance when using LLM-based forecasting models, and also reveal some
022            of their critical shortcomings. By presenting this benchmark, we aim to advance
023            multimodal forecasting, promoting models that are both accurate and accessible to
024            decision-makers with varied technical expertise. The benchmark can be visualized
025            at https://anon-forecast.github.io/benchmark report dev/.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The estimation of future conditions is the foundation of decision making (Hyndman & Athana-
030   sopoulos, 2018) and intelligence (Wang, 2019). Articulated as time-series forecasting, this problem
031   pervades many domains of science and commerce. Accurate forecasting relies on several crucial
032   decisions up to the practitioner (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, 2018), in particular on: 1. Model
033
      selection: Choosing the appropriate forecasting model for a given problem, and 2. Incorporating
      prior information: Determining what relevant information should be included in the model and how
034
      to effectively integrate it. This involves decisions about statistical priors, inductive biases in the
035
      model architecture, and other forms of domain knowledge integration. Traditionally, these decisions
036   have heavily relied on expert knowledge and manual intervention. However, recent advancements
037   in machine learning have shown particular promise in democratizing time-series forecasting by
038   automating both model selection and the integration of prior information.
039
      In the wake of the foundation model paradigm shift (Bommasani et al., 2021), several works (e.g.,
040
      Liang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023); Lim & Zohren (2021)) have addressed automatic model
041
      selection by learning flexible, adaptable models that can be applied across various problem scenarios.
042   Unfortunately, when compared to traditional statistical methods, current approaches provide debatable
043   performance improvements while requiring significantly more resources (Garza & Mergenthaler-
044   Canseco, 2024). Moreover, these models typically cast inputs and outputs as purely numerical time
045   series, which leaves no room for the context that human experts typically rely on to focus their
046   modelling efforts.
047
      An alternative class of recent approaches (Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Requeima et al., 2024)
048   adapt large language models (LLMs) for forecasting and leverage natural language as an intuitive
049   interface to integrate side information. These methods overcome a significant limitation of traditional
050   forecasting techniques by eliminating the need to manually encode priors or design specialized
051   models. They further hold the promise to capture a broader range of prior knowledge and context,
052   potentially leading to more comprehensive and accurate forecasts. Unfortunately, there are as of yet
053   no systematic evaluations of these models’ abilities to jointly leverage historical observations and
      natural language for forecasting. While several benchmarks for context-aided forecasting have been

078   Our contributions are:
079
      • We carefully design 71 forecasting tasks (Sec. 3) spanning 7 domains, which cover various kinds
080     of contextual information (Sec. 3.2), and in addition to basic natural language-processing and
081     time-series analysis, require various capabilities (Sec. 3.3).
082   • We introduce the Region of Interest CRPS metric (RCRPS) to evaluate context-aided forecasting
083     performance (Sec. 4), which prioritizes context-sensitive windows in the prediction and accounts

085   • We evaluate various methods on CiK (Sec. 5), including statistical models, time series foundation
086     models using only numerical data, and LLM-based forecasters capable of incorporating context.

088     analysis explores key factors such as the impact of context conditioning, prompting techniques,
089     model capabilities, and discusses failure modes of models.

093   Context-Aided Forecasting This work addresses the problem of context-aided forecasting, where
094   the goal is to produce statistical forecasts by incorporating relevant side information (i.e., context). Let
095   XH = [X1 , . . . , Xt ] represent a series of random variables corresponding to historical observations,
096   where each Xτ ∈ X ⊆ R, and let XF = [Xt+1 , . . . , XT ] represent future observations. In the
097   classical statistical forecasting problem, the objective is to estimate the joint distribution of the future
098
      observations given the historical data:
099                                                                                        P (XF | XH ).
100
101   We further assume access to context, denoted C ∈ C, which is additional data of arbitrary nature (C)
102   that contains information relevant for predicting XF and complementary to the history XH . The task
103   then becomes estimating the distribution:
104                                                                                       P (XF | XH , C).
105
106   Crucially, we restrict our focus to relevant context, which we define as context that does not degrade
107   the prediction of future time steps. Formally, for xF ∼ XF | XH , C, given some loss function L
      assessing a predictive distribution over XF against a realization xF , L : P (XF ) × xF → R, we are

131   3      C ONTEXT IS K EY: A NATURAL L ANGUAGE C ONTEXT-A IDED
132          F ORECASTING B ENCHMARK
133
134   We present the Context is Key (CiK) benchmark, a collection of probabilistic forecasting tasks that
135   cannot be solved without integrating natural language contextual information with numerical data.
136   CiK consists of 71 distinct tasks spanning seven application domains (Sec. 3.1) and that can be
137   instantiated in different ways, e.g., by changing target time series or by selecting different time
138
      windows. These tasks encompass diverse types of contextual information (e.g., past events and known
      causal relationships; Sec. 3.2), and are designed such that various capabilities (e.g., causal reasoning;
139
      Sec. 3.3) are required to fully leverage the context and unlock accurate forecasts (see Fig. 2 for an
140
      overview). One key particularity of CiK is that all tasks are carefully designed to ensure quality,
141   avoiding reliance on automation (e.g., via LLMs) or crowdsourcing (see Appendix A.2 for details). An
142   example task is illustrated in Fig. 1 and others are given in Appendix B. The complete set of tasks can
143   be explored at https://anon-forecast.github.io/benchmark report dev/ and the source code is
144   available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/context-is-key-forecasting-E391.

146   3.1      D OMAINS AND N UMERICAL DATA S OURCES
147
148   The vast majority (95%) of tasks in CiK draw numerical data from 2,644 real-world time series
149   acquired from public sources. These series cover a range of domains: Climatology (solar irradiance
150   and cloud coverage (Sengupta et al., 2018)); Economics (unemployment rates across states and
151
      counties (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2024)); Energy (electricity consumption and produc-
      tion (Godahewa et al., 2021)); Mechanics (experimental properties of physical systems (Gamella
152
      et al., 2024)); Public Safety (fire department intervention counts (Ville de Montréal, 2020)); Trans-
153
      portation (highway segment occupancy rates and average speeds (Chen et al., 2001)); and Retail (cash
154   withdrawals from various ATMs (Godahewa et al., 2021)). The remaining 5% of tasks use simulated
155   data from dynamical systems crafted specifically for the tasks. Overall, the time series in CiK exhibit
156   diverse sampling frequencies, with observations ranging from every 10 minutes to monthly intervals.
157   Additional details on data sources can be found in Appendix A.1.

195   3.2 NATURAL L ANGUAGE C ONTEXT
196   For each task in the benchmark, we jointly sample numerical data from one of the series described in
197   Sec. 3.1 and then manually craft the natural language context necessary to unlock accurate forecasts.
198   In some cases, this context is purely descriptive, providing information about the general nature of
199   the target variable and its historical behavior, as seen in the task illustrated in Fig. 1. In other cases,
200
      the raw numerical data is adjusted to reflect the influence of the context. For example, in one task
      based on data from Godahewa et al. (2021), an ATM is expected to be inaccessible during a specific
201
      period, leading to zero withdrawals (visualized in Appendix B.3). In another task, electricity demand
202
      is projected to surge due to an incoming weather event (visualized in Appendix B.2). In such cases,
203   we modify the series to incorporate patterns included in the context.
204
205   Overall, we include diverse forms of natural language context, capturing various aspects of the
206
      process underlying the time series and revealing complementary knowledge that could be provided
      by a human expert or an external information source. The types of context are described below and
207
      exemplified in the task illustrated in Fig. 3. Several additional examples are given in Appendix B.
208
209   Intemporal information (cI ) Information about the process that remains invariant in time. For
210   example, a description of the process and the nature of the target variable, as in Fig. 3 (point ⃝),2
211   patterns that cannot be inferred from the available numerical data (e.g., long-period seasonalities), or
212
      constraints on values (e.g., positivity).
213   Historical information (cH ) Information about the past behavior of the series that is not reflected in
214   the available numerical history. For example, statistics on past values of the series, as in Fig. 3 (point
215   ⃝),

231   3.3   M ODEL C APABILITIES
232
233   In addition to forecasting and natural language understanding, all tasks are designed such that fully
234
      utilizing the contextual information requires a range of capabilities, including instruction following,
      various forms of reasoning, and retrieval.
235
236   For example, to solve the task in Fig. 3 , the model could retrieve from memory that Montreal
237   experiences snowfall and cold weather during the winter months. It could then infer that trash fires
238   are less likely to occur during this period through deductive reasoning. This chain of thought reveals
239   a seasonal pattern that is not apparent in the short numerical history. Additionally, through causal
240
      reasoning, it is apparent that, despite a strong association between field fires and trash fires, the
      intervention described in ⃝ 5 is unlikely to reduce the frequency of the latter. Failure to recognize this
241
      distinction would lead to inaccurate forecasts.
242
243   A list of all capabilities with definitions is available in Appendix A.6 and the capabilities required
244   to solve each task are documented at https://anon-forecast.github.io/benchmark report dev.
245   The distributions of tasks per capability and context type are shown in Fig. 2, while the distribution
246
      of lengths of the numerical historical data, prediction horizons and natural language context are
      provided in Appendix A.7. Multiple example tasks from CiK are given in Appendix B, along with an
247
      explanation of their sources of natural language context and the capabilities required to solve them.

249   4 R EGION O F I NTEREST C ONTINUOUS R ANKED P ROBABILITY S CORE
250
      Alongside the tasks, we introduce the Region of Interest CRPS (RCRPS), a novel proper scoring
251
      rule designed specifically for context-aided probabilistic forecasting. This new scoring rule is an
252   extension of the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS; Gneiting & Raftery (2007)), a proper
253   scoring rule that provides a comprehensive assessment of forecast quality by evaluating the entire
254   predictive distribution rather than focusing solely on summary statistics. Importantly, since it is based
255   on the CRPS, the RCRPS can be calculated using only samples from the predictive distribution, and
256   so can be used even in cases where closed-form distributions are unavailable. The RCRPS extends
257   the CRPS via two key components: a region of interest and a measure of constraint satisfaction. This
258   allows assessing both forecast accuracy and the integration of contextual information.
259   Region of interest (RoI): The score reweighs a strict subset of time steps, denoted by I ⊆
260   [t + 1, . . . , T ], whose values are heavily informed by the context. For example, in the ATM task
261   described in Sec. 3.2 (visualized in Appendix B.3), this would correspond to the time intervals during
262   which the ATM is expected to be unavailable. In other tasks, such as those in Figs. 1 and 3, where
263   the context informs the value of all future time points, we set the RoI to an empty set, essentially
264   weighting all time steps equally (for readability, we report the definition of RCRPS for this special
265   case in Appendix E).
266   Constraint satisfaction: The score penalizes violations of constraints, whether explicitly or im-
267   plicitly included in the context, by measuring a task-specific function, denoted by vC , whose value
268   is zero for any trajectory that satisfies the constraints and > 0 for any trajectory that violates them.
269   Concrete examples are given in Appendix E.4. For tasks whose context does not imply constraints,
      we use vC (·) ≡ 0.

285
      on CiK (Sec. 5.2). We then present results on the benchmark (Sec. 5.3), along with an analysis

288   5.1 E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
289   Each task in CiK has many unique specifications, i.e. instances arising from the various time series
290   and windows in the associated numerical data, as well as minor variations in natural language context.
291   In order to make the evaluation reproducible and affordable, we deterministically select 5 instances
292   of each task for evaluation. For each instance, we generate 25 independent samples from each model
293   for evaluation. Since many of the tasks in the benchmark share similarities due to having been
294
      created from the same data sources or using variants of the same context, we identify these clusters
      of similar tasks, and design a weighting scheme such that each cluster has equal total weight in our
295
      aggregate score (see Appendix A.4 for more details). Finally, to prevent the aggregate scores from
296
      being dominated by rare instances where some models give forecasts which are orders of magnitudes
297   away from the ground truth, we introduce an upper bound of 5 to the RCPRS value for each instance,
298   which intuitively represents the value a forecast would get if the distance between the forecast and
299   the ground-truth was 5 times bigger than the range of the ground-truth of the instance.

301
302

304

      Comparing the LLMP and Direct Prompting Strategies
407
408   Clear patterns emerge when comparing these strategies. First, as shown in Fig. 5 (right), LLMP
409   baselines exhibit stronger numerical forecasting performance without context than Direct Prompt
410   baselines. This advantage likely stems from LLMP’s closer alignment with the forecasting task:
411   LLMP simply prompts the LLM to autoregressively predict the next value in the time series – a task
412
      well suited for non-instruction tuned LLMs. This contrasts with Direct Prompting which requires
      output forecasts to be structured, complicating the overall task.
413
414   This line of reasoning leads us to our second observation; as reflected in Tab. 1 and Fig. 5, instruction
415   tuning appears to generally degrade LLMP performance, with Llama-3 models showing a twofold
416   decrease in performance after tuning—a behavior previously observed by Gruver et al. (2024). Inter-
417   estingly, instruction tuning does not degrade Mixtral-8x7B performance. Finally, while instruction
418
      tuning generally harms LLMP, it is essential for models used with the Direct Prompt strategy. Again,
      Direct Prompt requires forecasts to be produced in a specific structure, a skill that base models
419
      typically hone during post-training (see Appendix D.1.1 for details).
420
421   No Baseline Excels Across All Capabilities
422   Based on the results in Tab. 1, it is evident that some models possess the necessary capabilities to
423   effectively utilize the contextual information provided. However, no single model is the best across all
424   capabilities. Llama-3.1-405B-Inst (Direct Prompt), our overall top-performing baseline, outperforms
425   its counterparts in only 4 out of 7 capabilities. This finding indicates that the benchmark remains
426   unsolved, leaving significant room for advancements from the research community.

460   5.5 I NFERENCE C OST
461
462   A key practical aspect for forecasting applications is the inference time of models and their associated
463   cost. Fig. 6 (left) shows that, while Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct has the best RCRPS, it comes at the
464   cost of a significantly higher parameter count than the quantitative forecasters. This emphasizes that,
465   while LLMs can be powerful context-aware forecasters, they come with a steep computational cost,
466   highlighting the need for efficient models that balance both accuracy and resource demands. Of note
467
      is also that many LLM baselines are Pareto dominated by quantitative forecasters such as Lag-Llama
      and Chronos. This suggests that the ability to ingest text is not enough and that a careful choice of
468
      LLM and prompting strategy is crucial for Pareto efficiency.
469
470   Fig. 6 (right) emphasizes the disparity in inference time between LLMs and quantitative models.
471   LLMs take significantly longer to make predictions, with the most accurate LLMs having inference
472   times that are orders of magnitude higher than their quantitative counterparts. Quantitative models,
473
      in contrast, maintain much lower inference times, making them far more efficient for practical
      use. The high computational demands of context-aware LLMs hinder their practical use in real-
474
      world forecasting, especially where speed and cost matter. The clear benefits of incorporating
475
      context warrants research into making them more efficient, aiming to match the cost-effectiveness of
476   traditional models and enabling their deployment in large-scale forecasting.

478   6                   R ELATED W ORK
479
480   We review two streams of related work: (i) work that introduce related benchmarks and datasets, and
481   (ii) work that repurpose LLMs to obtain foundation models for context-aided forecasting.
482   Benchmarks and Datasets Merrill et al. (2024) present a benchmark designed to evaluate LLMs’
483   ability to reason about time series, with context-aided forecasting as one assessed capability. Their
484   approach differs from ours in several important ways. First, they focus on purely synthetic time series,
485   which may not accurately reflect real-world dynamics, whereas our benchmark is based primarily on
      real-world data. Second, their evaluation is limited to point forecasting metrics, which do not measure


                                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the quality of the full forecast distribution. In contrast, we adopt probabilistic forecasting metrics, e.g.,
487   the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS; c.f. Gneiting & Raftery, 2007), to assess the quality
488   of entire forecast distributions. Other related datasets include Time-MMD (Liu et al., 2024a), which
489   integrates text extracted from reports and web searches, TGTSF (Xu et al., 2024), which incorporates
490   information such as weather reports and news articles, SysCaps (Emami et al., 2024), which includes
491   LLM-generated descriptions of building energy consumption systems, TS-Insights (Zhang et al.,
492   2023), which includes LLM-generated descriptions of trends and seasonalities, and the works of
493   Sawhney et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2024b) who propose automated filtering methods to construct
494   datasets of paired textual and numerical information. The key distinction between these works and
495
      ours lies in the role of textual information: while in these works, the text is not essential to generating
      high-quality forecasts, in our benchmark, all tasks are handcrafted to ensure that accurate forecasts
496
      cannot be achieved without using the provided textual information.
497
498   Repurposing LLMs for Forecasting A natural approach to this problem is to build forecasting
499   methods based on LLMs. Xue & Salim (2023) showed that forecasting could be framed as a question-
500   answering problem. Subsequently, Gruver et al. (2024) and Requeima et al. (2024) showed that
501
      zero-shot sequence completion could generate accurate forecasts and that textual side-information
      could be used to influence forecasts. However, their analysis is limited to illustrative examples rather
502
      than a comprehensive evaluation. Other approaches incorporate time series into pretrained LLMs (Jin
503
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2024) by introducing special tokens used to represent
504   patched time series patterns; or modifying their encoders to account for time series data (Jia et al.,
505   2024). While these methods show promising results, their evaluations primarily rely on datasets
506   where the contextual information is not guaranteed to improve forecasts over numerical data alone.
507   As a result, it remains unclear whether their success is driven by accurate numerical forecasting or by
508   effectively incorporating context; this shortcoming motivates our investigation into this question.
509
510   7    D ISCUSSION
511   In this work, we propose the Context is Key (CiK) benchmark: a collection of forecasting tasks
512   that require combining historical data with critical natural language context. We evaluate a range
513   of models on CiK, including our proposed LLM prompting method, Direct Prompt, which achieves
514   the best performance. We analyse and discuss the failure modes of these models, and our findings
515   underscore the critical role of contextual information in improving forecasts, while also revealing
516   both the unexpected strengths and notable limitations of the investigated LLM-based forecasters.
517   Limitations: While our benchmark provides valuable insights into the integration of contextual
518   information in time series forecasting, it is important to acknowledge its limitations. Our study
519   excludes modalities other than time series data and text, and excludes multivariate time series
520   scenarios. Although we carefully and deliberately designed the tasks to assess how well time series
521   forecasters can integrate contextual information, our focus was on relationships between context and
522   forecasts that are discernible to humans. Hence, our benchmark does not explicitly evaluate a models’
523   capacity to leverage latent relationships that might elude human observation. Moreover, while tasks
524
      are designed to require certain capabilities, we do not guarantee that alternative approaches to solving
      them do not exist. Our collection of capabilities and context types was not intended to be exhaustive
525
      but rather to serve as tools for analyzing forecasters’ performance on the benchmark. While we have
526
      taken steps to mitigate memorization concerns, as discussed in Sec. 3.1, achieving absolute certainty
527   in this regard is challenging without strictly held-out data.
528
529   Future work: There are several promising avenues for future work. All tasks in the proposed
530
      benchmark are univariate forecasting tasks with textual context. Enhancements to the benchmark
      could include tasks that require multivariate forecasting or incorporate additional modalities, such
531
      as images, structured databases, or spatiotemporal data. Tasks that deliberately challenge context
532
      length limitations or probe specific weaknesses of language models would also be valuable additions.
533   Methods to automate the generation of large, high-quality datasets for context-aided forecasting
534   are also a valuable direction of investigation. Furthermore, this benchmark strongly motivates
535   research into developing more accurate and efficient multimodal forecasting models, which it is
536   well-positioned to support. Lastly, as models become more robust, they could be integrated into
537   agentic systems with conversational interfaces, allowing forecasts to be augmented with human
538   expertise and automatically retrieved facts (e.g., via search engines). Such advancements would
539   represent a significant step toward automating and democratizing access to powerful forecasting
      tools.

      A.2    TASK C REATION P ROCESS
834
835   All tasks were manually designed, from scratch, by the authors of this work without resorting to
836   external annotators, crowdsourcing, or LLMs. We use the following procedure to create the tasks in
837   the benchmark.
838
      First, we identified high-quality sources of public time series data from various application domains
839
      (listed in Appendix A.1). Special care was taken to find data sources that are continuously updated to
840   facilitate future benchmark updates. Second, we established the categorization for sources of context
841   (Sec. 3.2) and capabilities (Sec. 3.3) as a framework to guide the creation of new tasks and ensure
842   their diversity. Third, team members created the tasks, each time
843
844         1. Selecting a data source
845         2. Implementing a time series window selection strategy (e.g., short or long history)
846         3. Brainstorming about context types and capabilities required to solve the forecasting problem
847         4. Writing a code to generate the context (e.g., calculating statistics of the series beyond the
848            observed numerical history), and
849         5. Finally, if required, writing code to modify the time series data to reflect the context (e.g.,
850            introducing some spikes in future values).
851
852   Then, the tasks were peer-reviewed by a committee composed of all other authors (each with time
853   series research experience). The creator of each task was not allowed to participate in the review. The
854
      review ensured that the text was of high quality, that it undoubtedly enabled a better forecast, and that
      the context source and capability tags were well-assigned. If a task was deemed of not high enough
855
      quality, it was either returned for revisions, or rejected.
856
857   The   code for all tasks is available here:           https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
858   context-is-key-forecasting-E391/.      An example task can be found here: https:
859   //anonymous.4open.science/r/context-is-key-forecasting-E391/cik benchmark/tasks/
      montreal fire/short history.py, where the time series window selection occurs from L94-112
860
      and the context generation occurs from L114-158.
861
862   After the benchmark was developed, we further assessed the quality of the context using an LLM-
863   based critique to validate that all the tasks are high-quality context aided forecasting tasks. This
      procedure is detailed in Appendix A.3.

962

963
      equals to the standard error. We then draw from this distribution and compute the weighted average

      ranks for each model. The results shown in Tab. 1 are the mean and standard deviation measured

       C.4   S IGNIFICANT FAILURES PER MODEL
1513
1514   We observe that in a few instances in the benchmark, some models tend to obtain significantly worse
1515   performance when evaluated with context. In our evaluation, we term all instances where the RCRPS
1516   value of a model is greater than 5, as significant failures of the model on those instances. We found 5
1517   as a suitable value for analyzing such failures, as it intuitively represents the value a forecast would
1518   get if the distance between the forecast and the ground-truth was 5 times bigger than the range of
1519   the ground-truth for the task. When we aggregate the RCRPS of instances in the benchmark (such
1520
       as in Tab. 1), we cap the RCRPS of such significant failures to 5, to avoid outliers with a much
       higher RCRPS affecting the aggregate score. In Tab. 5, we show the number of such instances in our
1521
       evaluation of the benchmark where we found models to have significant failures (out of a total of 355
1522
       evaluated instances). Interestingly, some models such as Direct Prompt with Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct
1523   and LLMP with Llama-3-70B and Llama-3-8B are more robust to such significant failures, and do
1524   not incur such failures. On the other hand, models such as Qwen family of models (that are notably
1525   significantly smaller than the rest) with LLMP achieve the most significant failures, followed by
1526   Llama-3-70B-Inst and Llama-3-8B-Inst with LLMP. We postulate that this is because of models
1527   misinterpreting context. It is still an open question as to how to increase the robustness of models to
1528   prevent or reduce such significant failures. We visualize such significant failures in Appendix C.6.

2380   D.1.1    M ETHOD
2381
2382   For Direct Prompt, we propose to use a simple prompt template that we describe below, where
2383   ((context)) is replaced with the context of the respective task, ((history)) is replaced with the
2384
       historical values in the given format, and ((pred time)) is replaced with the prediction timesteps.
       The prompted model is expected to output predictions in the given template style (i.e. within the
2385
       given forecast tags, in the given format) for all prediction timesteps in the prompt. Notably, unlike
2386
       LLMP which consists of predicting the single next digit in a loop, Direct Prompt expects models
2387   to forecast in a single pass in a highly structured format, which requires models to understand and
2388   adhere to the template.
2389
2390     ”
2391    I have a time series forecasting task for you.
2392
        Here is some context about the task. Make sure to factor in any background knowledge,
2393
        satisfy any constraints, and respect any scenarios.
2394    <context>
2395    ((context))
2396    </context>
2397
        Here is a historical time series in (timestamp, value) format:
2398
        <history>
2399    ((history))
2400    </history>
2401
2402    Now please predict the value at the following timestamps: ((pred time)).

2403
        Return the forecast in (timestamp, value) format in between <forecast> and </forecast> tags.
2404    Do not include any other information (e.g., comments) in the forecast.
2405
2406    Example:
2407    <history>
        (t1, v1)
2408
        (t2, v2)
2409    (t3, v3)
2410    </history>
2411    <forecast>
2412    (t4, v4)
        (t5, v5)
2413
        </forecast>
2414    ”
2415
2416
       We observe that models often produce samples that fail to adhere to the structure and are therefore
       rejected. When sampling 25 samples from the model, with Direct Prompt, we allow retrying for a
2417
       maximum of K times until we obtain 25 valid samples. If we do not have 25 valid samples from the
2418
       model at the end of K retries, we record a failure of the model and attribute the model the RCRPS
2419   upper bound of 5 for that task. In practice, we find that larger models (Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct,
2420   GPT-4o and GPT-4o) can produce 25 valid forecasts with 1 to 3 retries. However with the smaller
2421   models (such as Llama-3-70B-Instruct, Llama-3-8B-Instruct and Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct), up to 10
2422   retries can be required to obtain 25 valid forecasts. Further, we found that without an explicit “Do not
2423   include any other information (e.g., comments) in the forecast.”, models often included unwanted
2424   information along with the forecasts.
2425
2426   Instruction-tuning is necessary for models to work with Direct Prompt Direct Prompting
2427   requires forecasts to be produced in a specific structure. To generate structured outputs, models need
2428   to be steerable (Dubey et al., 2024), a capability that is typically elicited from base models with
2429   post-training methods such as instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2021). We observe this in our evaluations
       as we find that several base models, including Llama-3-8B, Llama-3-70B, Mixtral-8x7B, and even


                                                         45
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2430
       the biggest base model we tried, Llama-3.1-405B, are incapable of generating outputs adhering to the
2431   structure required for Direct Prompt, despite increasing the number of retries to as high as 50 retries.
2432   With Direct Prompt, these models often output irrelevant information, sometimes completing solely
2433   the context as a text completion task, and in other cases regurgitating forecasting datasets that they
2434   have memorized.
2435
2436   Extensions of Direct Prompt While very simple, such prompt templates can be powerful tools
2437   to understand how LLMs perform context-aided forecasting: as the prompt gives control over the
2438   structure and content of the output (particularly for instruction-tuned models), one may construct
2439   other, more involved template structures in the prompt. For instance, a prompt template could ask
2440   LLMs to explain the reasoning behind their (context-aided) forecasts, and more. We leave it to future
2441
       work to understand how such prompt-based techniques can lead to more detailed evaluations and
       give us better insights into what the models are capable of.

2532   In this section we outline LLM-processes (LLMP; Requeima et al. (2024)), one of the prompt-based
2533   baselines evaluated in Sec. 5.3. Prompts are constructed by first providing textual information
2534   followed by the numerical history. The context may include background knowledge, a scenario
2535   description and task constraints, replaced by ((background)), ((scenario)) and ((constraints)),
2536   respectively, in the prompt template below. The numerical history (((history))) is provided by
2537   converting the numerical data to text where values are separated by commas (,) and tuples by newline
       characters (\n). The LLM then outputs the continuation of the string prompt, forecasing the the value


                                                        47
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2538
       for the next time index (((next index))). This forecast and the next time index is appended to the
2539   prompt allowing the LLM to autoregressively complete the entire forecast. Numerical samples are
2540   rejected if they do not adhere to a decimal representation format. See Requeima et al. (2024)) for full
2541   details.

2736   E     D ETAILS OF THE PROPOSED METRIC
2737
2738   The CiK benchmark is designed to determine whether models can improve their probabilistic forecasts
2739   by leveraging associated textual information (see Sec. 2). To support this goal, the evaluation metric:
2740
2741         1. should be a proper scoring rule, such that a model who perfectly knows what the correct
2742            forecast is should have no reason to favor another prediction;
2743
             2. must be easy to compute using a finite sample from the forecast distribution, since many
2744
                models do not provide a functional form for their forecasts.
2745
2746
       To account for the importance of leveraging relevant context, the metric should also:
2747
2748         1. penalize obviously impossible forecasts, i.e. that can be inferred as implausible from the
2749            contextual information;
2750
             2. take a similar range of values across different tasks, to prevent some tasks to dominate
2751
                the score as we average the results across tasks;
2752
2753         3. prioritize forecast quality for timesteps with relevant context, even if these timesteps are
                a small portion of the forecast horizon.


                                                         51
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2754
       To satisfy the first two properties, we start with the Continuous Ranked Probability Score
2755   (CRPS) (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007), a reliable strictly proper scoring rule for univariate proba-
2756   bility distribution, and take its mean over all time steps. To compute the CRPS from a finite number
2757   of samples, we use the estimator based on its probability weighted moment form (Taillardat et al.,
2758   2016), since it is unbiased (Zamo & Naveau, 2018). See Appendix E.3 for more details about this
2759   estimator.
2760
       Many of our tasks are built to include information about a hard constraint on XF in their C, which
2761   can be written as vC (xF ) = 0. If we were only interested to measure by how much a forecast breaks
2762   the constraint, we could take inspiration from the threshold-weighted CRPS (Gneiting & Ranjan,
2763   2011) by using vC as its chaining function (Allen et al., 2023):
2764
2765                           twCRPSvC (X  e F , xF ) ≡ CRPS vC (X  e F ), vC (xF ) ,                 (1)
2766
2767   where X e F is the forecast of XF to be evaluated. Since, by construction, the ground-truth xF always
2768   satisfy the constraints, we have vC (xF ) = 0. But since we do not care only about whether forecasts
2769   break constraints, we sum both the original CRPS and this twCRPS, but we weight the later by a
2770   factor of β = 10, to denote the additional interest we show to these errors. See Appendix E.4 for the
2771   various vC used in the benchmark.
2772   One common approach to normalize the CRPS to get similar ranges for multiple problems is to divide
2773   it by the mean absolute value of the target ground-truth of the forecasted series (Alexandrov et al.,
2774   2020). This has two issues: the metric is no longer proper, and it leads to much larger values for
2775   series close to zero than those far from it. To solve the first issue, we take advantage that we can
2776   generate many more instances from each of our tasks, by computing a normalization factor α from
2777   25 instances not included in the benchmark. The details of this calculations are in Appendix E.1.
2778   Many tasks in our benchmark contains contextual information which is highly relevant for a small
2779   fraction of the time steps in the forecasting window, while being only marginally relevant for the
2780   majority of the time steps. If we were to weight these two categories equally, then the score for
