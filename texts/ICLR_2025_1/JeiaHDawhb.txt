009                                                               A BSTRACT
010
011                           Simplicity is a powerful inductive bias. In reinforcement learning, regularization
012                           is used for simpler policies, data augmentation for simpler representations, and
013                           sparse reward functions for simpler objectives, all that, with the underlying mo-
014                           tivation to increase generalizability and robustness by focusing on the essentials.
015                           Supplementary to these techniques, we investigate how to promote simple behavior
016                           throughout the duration of the episode. To that end, we introduce a modification
017
                              of the reinforcement learning problem, that additionally maximizes the total cor-
                              relation within the induced trajectories. We propose a practical algorithm that
018
                              optimizes all models, including policy and state representation, based on a lower
019
                              bound approximation. In simulated robot locomotion environments, our method
020                           naturally generates policies that induce periodic and compressible trajectories, and
021                           that exhibit superior robustness to noise and changes in dynamics compared to
022                           baseline methods, while also improving performance in the original tasks.

036     Reinforcement learning (RL) is currently the preferred approach for many challenging, practical
037     control problems, as it can learn complex neural network policies that effectively tackle the given
038     task. For example, in robotics, reinforcement learning is widely used to learn visuomotor policies
039     for quadrupedal and bipedal locomotion (Lee et al., 2020a; Radosavovic et al., 2024). However,
040     since RL is a learning-based method, it is prone to picking up spurious correlations between high-
041     dimensional sensory inputs and desired actions, which can lead to brittle policies, that fail under
042
        slight, natural variations in the state. An important countermeasure involves training policies using
        domain randomization, in particular in the sim-to-real setting, where the policy is learned in several
043
        varying simulation environments. Yet, even in such data-intense settings, it remains unclear whether
044
        we can obtain a sufficiently diverse training distribution to learn policies that transfer to more complex
045     real-world scenarios, such as those involving robot controllers that need to interact with humans.
046
047     Consequently, there is a growing interest in exploring additional techniques that add inductive biases
048
        to obtain simpler, less brittle policies, for example, by limiting the amount of state information used
        by the policy (Goyal et al., 2018; Igl et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2020), or the predictive information
049
        within learned representations (Lee et al., 2020b). Such information-theoretic biases have already
050
        been extended to sequences, to account for the sequential nature of reinforcement learning. Namely,
051     RPC (Eysenbach et al., 2021) aims to learn better representations by limiting the information
052     between state-sequences and embedding-sequences, and LZ-SAC (Saanum et al., 2023) improves
053

055   focus on specific aspects of the behavior—either state-consistency or action-consistency—without
056   considering the complete behavior in terms of state-action trajectory.
057
058
      In this work, we propose a novel inductive bias that operates on the level of trajectories. Specifically,
      we aim to learn policies that produce simple, consistent, and therefore compressible trajectories.
059
      Our hypothesis is that behavior that avoids unnecessary variations tends to be less brittle in general,
060
      thereby increasing its robustness to noise and changes in the dynamics. We introduce this inductive
061   bias by means of the additional objective of maximizing the total correlation within the trajectory
062   produced by the agent. This total correlation corresponds to the amount of information that we
063   can save by using a joint encoding of all (latent) states and actions within trajectories, compared to
064   compressing all time steps independently. By maximizing total correlation, the agent is encouraged
065   to produce compressible trajectories, such as periodic and symmetric gaits.
066
      The main contributions of our work are as follows. We introduce the maximum total correlation
067   reinforcement learning problem (MTC-RL), which extends the typical RL formulation with an addi-
068   tional objective of maximizing trajectory total correlation. We derive a lower-bound approximation
069   of the total correlation and use it to propose a practical algorithm for MTC-RL, based on soft-actor
070   critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018). Our algorithm features an adaptation scheme to automatically adapt
071   the coefficient of the total correlation objective by treating it as the Lagrangian multiplier of a
072   constrained optimization problem. We empirically evaluate our algorithm on eight tasks from the
073   DMC control suite (Tassa et al., 2018) and show that the learned policies induce more periodic and
074   better compressible trajectories than baseline methods (Eysenbach et al., 2021; Saanum et al., 2023),
075
      leading to an improve in performance, as well as robustness to observation noise, action noise, and
      changes in the system frictions.

078   2    R ELATED W ORK
079
080   Information theory provides effective tools to solve problems in RL (Memmel et al., 2022; Peters et al.,
081   2010; Ma et al., 2023; Chakraborty et al., 2023; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015), such as representation
082   learning (Oord et al., 2018), robustness (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and generalization (Goyal et al.,
083   2018; 2017). Motivated by the InfoMax principle (Bell & Sejnowski, 1995), some previous RL
084   methods preserve mutual information to extract useful representations from observations, and have
085   achieved improvement in terms of performance and robustness on downstream tasks (Kim et al., 2019;
086   Laskin et al., 2020; Mazoure et al., 2020; Rakelly et al., 2021; Dunion et al., 2024). These methods
087
      usually maximize mutual information in single transitions. In contrast, our approach maximizes
      the total interdependencies within the trajectories of an agent. Moreover, instead of using separated
088
      objectives to optimize policies and representations, we use a unified objective to optimize policy and
089
      representations with respect to the consistency within the resulting trajectories.
090
091   Total correlation is a fundamental concept in information theory to qualify the statistical dependency
092   among multiple random variables (Watanabe, 1960). Previous methods have shown that total
093
      correlation is an effective tool to enhance machine learning models in many tasks, such as disentangled
      representation learning (Steeg, 2017; Gao et al., 2019) or structure discovery (Ver Steeg & Galstyan,
094
      2014). Our work extends these results to the RL setting by observing that the agent can actively
095
      change its behavior to maximize consistency within state and action sequences. Our method is
096   also related to previous methods that endow RL agents with robust behavior (Tessler et al., 2019;
097   Tanabe et al., 2022; Reddi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020). While these methods have proposed
098   purpose-designed methods to achieve robustness benefits, we focus on demonstrating that maximizing
099   the total correlation is a simple and effective task-independent solution for improving robustness.
100
      The principle of simplicity has garnered substantial attention in constructing learning agents (Chater
101
      & Vitányi, 2003; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Grau-Moya et al., 2018; Igl et al., 2019; Goyal et al.,
102   2018; Tishby & Polani, 2010; Leibfried & Grau-Moya, 2020). Some previous works induce simple
103   policies by imposing temporal consistency in actions. For example, Saanum et al. (2023) propose to
104   capture the temporal consistency in action sequences and induce simple behaviors by incorporating the
105   preference for consistent actions into the reward function. Another class of methods enforces temporal
106   consistency in latent representations of states to obtain policies that produce simple behaviors. For
107   instance, RPC (Eysenbach et al., 2021) learns policies that visit states whose representations are
      temporally consistent in individual transitions, by minimizing the mutual information between a

      sequence of observations and a sequence of their representations. In contrast, our total correlation
109   objective maximizes the consistency among sequences of state representations and actions. This
110   difference, which corresponds to learning dynamic models that predict the future from a history of
111   actions and states, allows the agent to achieve consistent behavior throughout whole trajectories.

233

348   5.2                    Z ERO - SHOT ROBUSTNESS
349
350   Our main motivation for learning coherent behavior and representations is to improve robustness by
351   focusing on the essentials. Our policies are biased to produce trajectories that have fewer variations,
352   so we expect that they are more robust to unseen disturbances. Hence, we evaluated our method and
353   baselines in terms of zero-shot robustness to observation perturbations, action perturbations, and
354   perturbations to the dynamics.
355   Robustness to observation perturbations. We first investigate how observation perturbations
356   affect policy performance by injecting Gaussian noise into the observations, st ← st + ϵ, where
357   noise ϵ is sampled from a Gaussian distribution, ϵ ∼ N (0, diag(σ 2 )) with standard deviation σ.
358   Using the same tasks as before, we evaluate our method and baselines on a series of noise strength
359   σ ∈ [0.02, 0.04, 0.06, 0.08, 0.1]. To compare the robustness across all eight tasks, we normalized
360   the scores by the score achieved by the best method on each task. The aggregated robustness to
361   observation perturbations with different noise strengths is shown for different methods in Figure 2
362
      (left). MTC achieves the best aggregated performance when observations are perturbed with small
      Gaussian noise.
363
364   Robustness to action perturbations. As our total correlation objective encourages consistent

422   To better investigate the effect of our regularizer, we evaluated the effect of the hyperparameter Ip

429   objective by increasing Ip doesn’t hurt the final performance (rewards without perturbations) but
430   significantly decreases the encoding size of trajectories. This suggests that maximizing the lower
431   bound of the total correlation helps induce compressible or structured behaviors. We also find that

436   6    D ISCUSSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
437
438   Our regularizer in Eq. 4 is related to the regularizer of RPC (Eysenbach et al., 2021), but generalizes
439
      it by considering the previous trajectory instead of only using the information of the current step t,
      and by also including an action prediction model. These differences enable us to improve temporal
440
      consistency within trajectories, which significantly improves the consistency and robustness of the
441
      resulting behavior, as shown in our experiments. Furthermore, the regularizer in RPC was derived as
442   the negative of an upper bound on the mutual information between raw and latent state sequences,
443   I(s1:T ; z1:T ), whereas we prove in Appendix A.2, that our objective can not be derived from that
444   perspective. We can, however, derive RPC from our formulation, showing that maximizing total
445   correlation provides an important new perspective on regularization in reinforcement learning that
446   not only results in more coherent and robust behavior, but also deepens our theoretical understanding
447   of related works.
448   However, our lower bound of the total correlation corresponds to a sum of negated KL divergences,
449   and is therefore always negative. Hence, it is not useful for estimating the actual total correlation,
450   which we know to be positive. While a vacuous bound may not be useful for estimation, it can still be
451   valuable for optimization, as in the case of subtracting a constant offset from the true objective. As
452   demonstrated in our experiments, our lower bound is very effective for producing consistent behavior.
453
454
      7    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
455
456
      Auxiliary objectives that create inductive biases towards simpler solutions (regularizers) are com-
457   monly, and very successfully, used in machine learning to learn more generalizable and robust
458   solutions. We propose to use the total trajectory correlation as a novel regularizer for reinforcement
459   learning, which acts on the level of the behavior. By directly corresponding to the information-
460   theoretic compressibility of the induced trajectories, the total correlation is arguably the most princi-
461   pled choice to quantify the simplicity of a behavior. As directly maximizing the total correlation is
462   intractable, we derived a variational lower bound and used it to formulate a regularized reinforcement
463   learning problem that can be solved with standard techniques. Compared to similar sequence-based
464   regularizers, total correlation regularization achieved very promising results by producing more
465
      coherent behavior that is more robust to state-, action- and dynamics perturbations. Hence, we believe
      that total trajectory correlation may serve as an important goal post for future reinforcement learning
466
      methods. Developing alternate bounds or approximations that better capture the total correlation
467
      while maintaining tractability is a promising direction for future research.

      B.6   ROBUSTNESS TO OBSERVATION NOISE
833
834
      In all experiments of robustness and trajectory compression, for each agent, we evaluated the
835   performance of policies saved after finishing the training for 1M steps. Gaussian noise is regarded as
836   a strong state distractor for reinforcement learning algorithms in prior work (Bai et al., 2021). We
837   add the Gaussian noise to observations and the learned policies select the actions based on the noisy
838   observations.
839
840
841   B.7   ROBUSTNESS TO ACTION NOISE
842
843   Noise added to actions can be viewed as a type of environment perturbation. In this experiment, we
844
      first use the saved policies to select the action based on the current state, and add the Gaussian noise
      to the chosen action. We then clip the action into [−1, 1] before passing the action signal to the task.
845
846
847   B.8   ROBUSTNESS TO FRICTIONS
848
849   Modifying the friction of the robot body to test the robustness of learned policies has been inves-
850   tigated in previous work. In our experiment setup, we get the body friction of the robot via the
851   env.physics.model.geom_friction attribute provided by the environment. Since the body friction
852   varies across different tasks, we change the frictions by scaling it, rather than increasing or decreasing
853   a constant. We then evaluate the performance of the learned policies on the environment with the
854   changed frictions.
855
856   B.9   T RAJECTORY COMPRESSION
857
858   In our experiment, we measure the compressibility of trajectories using the bzip2 algorithm, which
859   is easily available by installing the common bz2 python package. For each seed, we collect 30
860   trajectories using learned policies. Since the collected trajectories have the same number of data
861   points and these data points have the same numerical precision, the uncompressed trajectories
862   collected by the different algorithms have the same file size. We compress individual trajectories by
863   calling the bz2.compress() method provided by the bz2 package. Smaller file sizes of compressed
      trajectories mean that trajectories can be better compressed.
