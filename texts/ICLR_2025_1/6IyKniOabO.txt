011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deciphering the pathways of protein folding and unfolding under tension is es-
014            sential for deepening our understanding of fundamental biological mechanisms.
015            Such insights offer the potential to develop treatments for a range of incurable and
016            fatal debilitating conditions, including muscular disorders like Duchenne Muscular
017
               Dystrophy and neurodegenerative diseases such as Parkinson’s disease. Single
               molecule force spectroscopy (SMFS) is a powerful technique for investigating
018
               forces when domains in proteins fold and unfold. Currently, manual visual in-
019
               spection remains the primary method for classifying force curves resulting from
020            single proteins; a time-consuming task demanding significant expertise. In this
021            work, we develop a classification strategy to detect measurements arising from
022            single molecules by augmenting deep learning models with the physics of the
023            protein being investigated. We develop a novel physics-based Monte Carlo engine
024            to generate simulated datasets comprising of force curves that originate from a
025            single molecule, multiple molecules, or failed experiments. We show that pre-
026            training deep learning models with the simulated dataset enables high throughput
027            classification of SMFS experimental data with average accuracies of 75.3 ± 5.3%
028
               and ROC-AUC of 0.87 ± 0.05. Our physics augmentation strategy does not need
               expensive expert adjudication of the experimental data where models trained using
029
               our strategy show up to 25.9% higher ROC-AUC over the models trained solely
030
               on the limited SMFS experimental data. Furthermore, we show that incorporating
031            a small subset of experimental data (∼ 100 examples) through transfer learning
032            improves accuracy by 6.8% and ROC-AUC by 0.06. We have validated our re-
033            sults on three new SMFS experimental datasets. To facilitate further research in
034            this area, we make our datasets available and provide a Python-based toolbox
035            (https://anonymous.4open.science/r/AFM_ML-2B8C).

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   Many biological processes depend on controlling mechanical forces achieved via the folding and
041   unfolding of domains in molecules like titin (Rief et al., 1997; 1998; Oberhauser et al., 2000),
042   dystrophin and its homologue utrophin (Rajaganapathy et al., 2019; Ramirez et al., 2023), neurotoxic
043   proteins (Hervás et al., 2012), and extracellular matrix protein tenascin (Oberhauser et al., 1998). For
044   example, dystrophin and utrophin work as molecular shock absorbers that limit myofiber membrane
045
      damage when undergoing reversible unfolding upon muscle stretching and contraction Ervasti (2007).
      Evidently, studying mechanical properties of single proteins can provide vital insights for unravelling
046
      mechanisms of diseases, that include neurodegenerative disorders (Hervás et al., 2012; Ruggeri
047
      et al., 2018) and muscle degeneration disease Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (Ramirez et al., 2023;
048   Hoffman et al., 1987). Instruments such as optical tweezers (Ashkin et al., 1986) and atomic force
049   microscopes (AFMs) (Binnig et al., 1986) have enabled single molecule force spectroscopy (SMFS),
050   where molecular forces in the femto to nano-Newton range, over sub-nanometer to micrometer
051   distances, can be measured and studied. In an SMFS experiment, measurements from a force probe
052   are recorded while it is made to interact with molecules of interest. Since the size of a typical force
053   probe is orders greater than a typical bio-molecule, the probe may come in contact with one or more
      molecules. Measurements obtained from interactions involving more than one molecule confound


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the interpretation of results and are undesirable in characterizing the behavior of single molecules.
055   Thus, identifying measurements that originate from single molecules is important in SMFS.
056
057   The use of chemical functionalization of probes, along with molecular fingerprints has emerged as an
058
      approach for identifying single molecule force curves (Yang et al., 2020). Chemical functionalization
      modifies the surface of the force probe and substrate to enable site-specific attachment of proteins,
059
      where fingerprints are well-characterized molecules that yield distinct unfolding patterns. However,
060
      surface chemical functionalization is time-consuming, often taking at least 6 hours (Zimmermann
061   et al., 2010), and demands careful handling and practice, as probes are delicate and easy to break.
062   Here it is possible for multiple molecules to attach to the probe; however, fingerprints in the force
063   curves can be leveraged to discern single molecule force curves from force curves that result from
064   multiple molecules.. Moreover, advanced filtering techniques, informed by an understanding of all
065   molecules involved in the complex, are essential for effectively identifying single molecule force
066   curves (Yang et al., 2020).
067   In contrast, conducting experiments without functionalizing probes and introducing fingerprints into
068   the native protein has significant advantages. Probes without functionalization are easier and less
069   expensive to manufacture, and bio-molecules without fingerprints engineered into their structure are
070   easier to synthesize. Moreover, there is greater confidence that the experimental data characterizes the
071   unaltered native bio-molecule without any confounding effects introduced by fingerprints. Despite
072   these advantages, when there are no fingerprints, distinguishing data that originate from single
073   molecules and multiple molecules is more challenging. Currently, the most widely accepted method
074   for distinguishing the data is based on visual inspection, which is a time-consuming process that
075
      demands a high level of expertise (Bornschlögl and Rief, 2011; Lyubchenko, 2018). Additionally,
      force curves need to be collected from a large number of experiments, not only to ensure statistical
076
      confidence but also because the protein concentration is generally lowered to minimize the possibility
077
      of multiple molecules (Ramirez et al., 2023; Oberhauser et al., 2000). These factors make it chal-
078   lenging to obtain precise statistics of single molecular force curves and to generate a large, annotated
079   dataset appropriate for training deep learning models.
080
081
      To address these challenges, we develop an effective classification strategy, where we augment deep
      learning models with the physics of protein unfolding, that can accurately classify force curves
082
      from real physical SMFS experiments into three classes: 1) no molecule, 2) single molecule, and 3)
083
      multiple molecules. We propose a physics-based Monte Carlo simulation algorithm to generate a
084   large, well-annotated, and balanced dataset for model training. We also test our model performances
085   on three new SMFS experimental datasets, obtained from non-specific pulling of multi-domain
086   molecules: titin, utrophin, and dystrophin (Hua et al., 2024). Our results show deep learning models
087   pre-trained with simulated datasets achieve average accuracies of 75.3 ± 5.3% and area under the
088   receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC-AUC) (Fawcett, 2006) of 0.87 ± 0.05 across three
089   SMFS experimental datasets. Our physics augmentation strategy not only lessens the need for
090   expensive annotations from experts but also outperforms the models trained from limited SMFS
091   experimental datasets directly, achieving up to 25.9% higher ROC-AUC. Additionally, the accuracy
092
      and ROC-AUC can be further improved by 6.8% and 0.06, respectively, when incorporating a small
      subset of experimental data (∼ 100 examples) via the transfer learning technique (Pan and Yang,
093
      2010). Furthermore, this work presents the first publicly accessible SMFS experimental datasets
094
      derived from non-specific pulling of three unique multi-domain proteins and associated codes.

157   3   SMFS PRELIMINARIES
158
159   We first describe an atomic force microscope (AFM) based SMFS setup that is employed to obtain
160   force curves of proteins with multiple domains. Here, a microcantilever with a sharp tip is pressed
161   against a substrate on which the proteins under study are deposited. Under applied force, parts of
      one or more protein molecules are non-specifically attached to the cantilever tip; characterized by


                                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a stochastic adhesion event (Leite et al., 2012). Upon retraction of the cantilever from the surface,
163   sections of the proteins between the tip of the cantilever and the substrate experience a tensile force.
164   The record of the force experienced by the cantilever (and therefore the protein) versus protein
165   extension q is known as a force curve, as depicted in Figure 1c-e. If only one protein molecule
166   is present between the cantilever tip and substrate, the force curve unveils important mechanical
167   properties of the protein molecule. We illustrate such a scenario in Figure 1a, where a single protein
168   molecule with four folded domains is attached between the substrate and the tip of the cantilever. As
169   the cantilever retracts, the protein experiences mechanical tension, causing the stochastic unfolding of
170   a folded domain. The applied force drops abruptly when a domain unfolds, as highlighted by the blue
171
      circles in Figure 1d. This process continues until either all domains are unfolded or the connection
      between the cantilever and the substrate is broken (which is a stochastic detachment event (Rief et al.,
172
      1997)), producing a saw-tooth pattern of force curves (Figure 1d).
173
174   In practice, the force curves can be categorized into one of three classes - 1) No molecule: where no
175   molecule is present between the tip and substrate, 2) Single molecule: when only a single molecule
176   or a section of a single molecule is present, or 3) Multiple molecules: where multiple molecules or
177
      sections of multiple molecules are present between the tip and substrate. Example experimental force
      curves corresponding to these three classes are depicted in Figure 1c, 1d, and 1e respectively. The
178
      single molecule class may include traces with a portion of multi-domain protein (Figure 1b (2,3));
179
      the multiple molecules class may involve a combination of different numbers of domains (Figure 1b
180   (4-6)). The no molecule traces do not contain useful information about the protein molecule. The
181   force curves originating from multiple molecules typically exhibit larger unfolding forces than those
182   with a single molecule (Figure 1e) and have a mixture of unfolding events that cannot be traced
183   back to a specific protein molecule, confounding useful interpretation. Therefore, excluding force
184   curves with no molecule and multiple molecules is necessary to obtain accurate and interpretable data
185   from SMFS. The identification of the single molecule force curves is challenging due to a number of
186   reasons: 1) a large number of force curves (1000-5000) need to be collected in a single experiment
187   since protein capture success rates are kept at 1-5% (Oberhauser et al., 2000; Ramirez et al., 2023),
188
      2) the study of a specific protein involves at least three replications for confidence on results, 3)
      the force curves are corrupted with substantial noise both from instrument measurement noise and
189
      intrinsic thermal noise of the molecules, and 4) often the proteins under investigation have no prior
190
      characterization which makes the adjudication time consuming and difficult even for domain experts.

286   4.2   AUGMENTING DEEP LEARNING MODELS WITH THE PHYSICS OF PROTEIN UNFOLDING
287
288
      Given the challenge of constructing a large, well-annotated dataset using SMFS experimental data, we
      pre-train the deep learning models with simulation data. By utilizing simulation data, we effectively
289
      incorporate the underlying physics of protein unfolding into our analysis. Our simulation (Sec 4.1) is
290
      carried out using the WLC model. The WLC model encapsulates the physics of the protein unfolding,
291   accurately describing the entropic spring like behavior of the protein between two unfolding events of
292   the protein, which is also corroborated by experimental data. Subsequently, we test the performance
293   of these pre-trained deep learning models using experimental data.
294
      Data augmentation via linear combinations of examples from different classes is shown to be effective
295
      in image classification (Summers and Dinneen, 2019; Tokozume et al., 2018a; Huang et al., 2020)
296
      and sound recognition (Tokozume et al., 2018b). Here, we incorporate M ∈ N reference curves
297   to augment the curve under classification. The reference curves are randomly sampled simulated
298   force curves from the training dataset. Each reference curve xi is augmented with the input curve
299   x via the difference between two curves (x − xi ) as additional channels, resulting in a total of
300   M + 1 channels. The input curve x can be either a simulated or experimental force curve undergoing
                                                                        T
301   classification. This augmented input [x, x − x1 , · · · , x − xM ] , comprising the input curve and the
302   reference curves, is then passed through deep learning models (Figure 2). The output layer is a
303   softmax layer with three neurons, corresponding to the three classes.
304
      We choose two baseline methods: multi layer perception (MLP) (Wang et al., 2016), which was
305   proposed as a baseline architecture for time series classification (TSC); and the triplet network
306   (Triplet), which was applied on the Discoidin domain receptors (DDRs) dataset (more details in
307   Section 5) (Waite et al., 2023). In addition to these baseline methods, we explore fully connected
308   neural networks (FCN), the residual networks (ResNet), which are two of the highest performing deep
309   neural networks on the UCR time series classification archive (Fawaz et al., 2019). We also investigate
310   the Inception network (InceptionNet) and InceptionTime, an ensemble of five InceptionNet models
311   initialized randomly, which is the current state-of-the-art deep learning model on the UCR archive
312   (Ismail Fawaz et al., 2020). More details of deep learning models can be found in Appendix B.1.

362
363
      For each of our three protein molecules, Titin I27O, UtrN-R3, and DysN-R3, unless otherwise
364
      specified, all deep learning models were pre-trained using 80% of corresponding simulated data for
365
      3-class classification, with the remaining 20% used for validation to monitor overfitting (A detailed
366   discussion of overfitting is provided in C.1). The pre-trained models were subsequently tested on
367   70% of the experimental datasets, while the remaining 30% was reserved for transfer learning or
368   direct training purposes. Each training was repeated five times, and the model performance was
369   evaluated based on overall accuracy, weighted F1-score, and weighted ROC-AUC. The data underwent
370   preprocessing, including trimming, resampling, and min-max normalization, with additional details
371   provided in Appendix B.3 and implementation details in Appendix B.4. Preprocessing and results for
372   DDRs are provided in Appendix C.2. Unless specified otherwise, one randomly selected reference
373   curve (M = 1) was used, as discussed in Section C.3.

376   experimental testing sets for all protein molecules. The average rank for each model across all

      7    C ONCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
487
488
      Single-molecule force spectroscopy (SMFS) data of protein molecules are time and resource intensive
489   to collect. Currently, manual visual inspection remains the primary method for classifying force
490   curves resulting from single proteins. These factors make it challenging to obtain precise statistics
491   of single molecular force curves and to generate a large, annotated dataset appropriate for training
492   deep learning models. Consequently, the study of deep learning methods for single-molecule force
493   spectroscopy data is very limited. To address these challenges, we developed an effective classification
494   strategy, where we augment deep learning models with the physics of protein unfolding, enabling
495   accurate detection and classification of SMFS experimental data as originating from no molecules,
496   single molecules, or multiple molecules.
497   We introduce a physics-based simulation algorithm to generate large and well-annotated data across
498   classes of no molecule, single molecule, and multiple molecules. We also provide experimental
499   datasets, obtained from non-specific single molecule force spectroscopy of three molecules: Titin
500   I27O, UtrN-R3, and DysN-R3. We demonstrate that deep learning models exhibit better performance
501   when pre-trained with our physics-based simulation data than when trained directly using the labeled
502   experimental data. Our physics augmented strategy does not require labeling of the experimental data,
503
      avoiding the need for expensive expert adjudication and human bias. In cases where the molecule
      under study does not have accurate model parameters, we show that the addition of a small amount
504
      (approximately 20%) supplemental experimental data to the training data and the use of transfer
505
      learning, improves average accuracies improved by 6.8%.
506
507   We identify the following limitations in our study. First, we use the adjudication of a single expert to
508   label the SMFS experimental data used for testing performances. Second, the simulation algorithm
509
      relies on energy landscape parameters, which are not straightforward to obtain and typically neces-
      sitate experiments involving multiple pulling speeds or constant forces. Transforming our physics
510
      augmented classification strategy to be agnostic to the energy landscape parameters is beyond the
511
      scope of this study. However, our method does not heavily depend on accurate simulation parameters,
512   as discussed in Section C.5. Third, in our physics-based protein unfolding model, we assume every
513   protein domain behaves identically. However, many proteins, including utrophin and dystrophin
514   have folded domains that are significantly different from each other. Developing methods that can
515   extract the individual energy landscapes of the different dissimilar domains in a protein is likely to
516   aid in identifying single molecule force curves arising from proteins with dissimilar domains. The
517   development of techniques to extract distinct energy landscapes from a small amount of experimental
518   data should be investigated in the future.

745   distance between the anchor and the positive sample while maximizing the distance between the
746   anchor and the negative sample in the embedding space. Subsequently, the resulting embeddings are
747   fed into the MLP described earlier to learn classification. The embedding network adopts the ResNet
748   structure, which will be elaborated on below.
749
750   FCN FCN (Wang et al., 2016) is composed of three convolutional blocks followed by a Global
751   Average Pooling (GAP) layer and a softmax layer, as illustrated in Figure 2. Each convolutional
752   block includes a 1D convolutional layer, batch normalization, and a Rectified Linear Unit (RELU)
753   activation layer. The output of the final convolutional block is passed through a GAP layer, followed
754   by a fully connected softmax layer. A stride of 1 with zero padding is used to preserve the length of
755   time series data. The filter sizes and kernel sizes of the three convolutional layers are {128, 256, 128}
      and {8, 5, 3}, respectively.


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      ResNet The ResNet (He et al., 2016) extends neural networks to very deep structures by incor-
757   porating shortcut connections. These connections enable the gradient flow directly through the
758   network, easing the training of deeper models. Our Residual Network (ResNet) comprises three
759   residual blocks followed by a GAP layer and a softmax layer. Each residual block contains three
760   convolutional blocks similar to those in the FCN architecture. The output of the last convolutional
761   block is added to the input of the residual block before proceeding to the next layer. The kernel size
762   for the three convolutional layers in each residual block remains consistent with FCN architecture,
763   set as {8, 5, 3}. The number of filters is the same within the same residual block, with filter sizes
764   specified as {64, 128, 128} for three residual blocks, respectively.
765
766   InceptionNet and InceptionTime InceptionTime ensembles the predictions of five InceptionNet
767   models, each with different initializations, to overcome the high variance across different runs
768
      Ismail Fawaz et al. (2020). The InceptionNet model consists of two residual blocks, followed by a
      global average pooling (GAP) layer and a softmax layer. Each residual block includes three Inception
769
      modules instead of fully convolutional layers. The default hyperparameter values are used.
