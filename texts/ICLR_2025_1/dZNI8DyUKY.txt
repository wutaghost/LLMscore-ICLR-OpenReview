009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The study of adversarial defense still struggles to combat with advanced adversarial
012            attacks. In contrast to most prior studies that rely on the diffusion model for
013            test-time defense to remarkably increase the inference time, we propose Masked
014            AutoEncoder Purifier (MAEP), which integrates Masked AutoEncoder (MAE)
015            into an adversarial purifier framework for test-time purification. While MAEP
016            achieves promising adversarial robustness, it particularly features model defense
017            transferability and attack generalization without relying on using additional data
018
               that is different from the training dataset. To our knowledge, MAEP is the first study
               of adversarial purifier based on MAE. Extensive experimental results demonstrate
019
               that our method can not only maintain clear accuracy with only a slight drop but also
020
               exhibit a close gap between the clean and robust accuracy. Notably, MAEP trained
021            on CIFAR10 achieves state-of-the-art performance even when tested directly on
022            ImageNet, outperforming existing diffusion-based models trained specifically on
023            ImageNet.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Proliferation of deep learning models across various domains has raised a pressing concern: Vulner-
029   ability of these models to adversarial attacks (Athalye et al., 2018; Croce & Hein, 2020a; Kurakin
030   et al., 2018; Madry et al., 2018) that aim to make the model behave abnormally by manipulating the
031   input data with imperceptible perturbations.
032   In response to these threats, researchers have actively investigated techniques to enhance the robust-
033   ness of machine learning models against adversarial attacks in two branches: (1) One promising
034   paradigm involves the integration of adversarial training (Gowal et al., 2021; Hsiung et al., 2023;
035   Huang et al., 2023a; Shafahi et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023; You et al., 2023; Rebuffi et al., 2021a;
036   Wang et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020; Suzuki et al., 2023) during model training, where both the clean
037   and adversarially perturbed data are used for training to improve robustness. While numerous studies
038   have explored adversarial training, a notable disparity (from RobustBench) persists between the
039
      natural accuracy and robust accuracy. (2) Another paradigm is adversarial purification (Alfarra
      et al., 2022; Ho & Vasconcelos, 2022), which aims to detect and remove adversarial perturbations
040
      from input data before being fed into the model. The benefit of adversarial purification is that the
041
      downstream task like classifier is not needed to be retrained and that can generalize to different
042   attacks at test time. The widely recognized adversarial purifiers (Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022;
043   Wu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) are based on diffusion models. They introduce noises to the input
044   images in the forward process and remove both the noises and adversarial perturbations in the reverse
045   process. Although diffusion models are generalized to different attacks, called “attack generalization,”
046   they should learn the data distribution at first and pay the price of losing “defense transferability”
047   (i.e., transferability to other datasets). In addition, they should tune the hyper-parameters, including
048   diffusion timestep (Nie et al., 2022) and guidance scale (Wang et al., 2022), carefully in different
049   datasets and tasks.
050   In contrast to most prior studies that rely on the diffusion model, we propose Masked AutoEncoder
051   Purifier (MAEP), which integrates Masked AutoEncoder (MAE) (He et al., 2022) into an adversarial
052   purifier framework for test-time purification, as illustrated in Fig. 1, and elucidate the feasibility of
053   this paradigm in Section 4.1. Specifically, in recent researches, MAE has leveraged the principles of
      self-supervised learning and masking image encoding to learn patch representations. While MAE

134   2.2   M ASKED AUTOENCODER (MAE)
135
136   MAE (He et al., 2022) implements a masking mechanism to enhance the performance of ViT
137
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). Inspired by the Masked Language Modeling (MLM) technique used in
      BERT (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019), MAE operates as a pre-training model, focusing on learning
138
      patch representations during the pre-training stage and fine-tuning for downstream tasks. The pre-
139
      training objective involves reconstructing images by masking partial patches, facilitating the learning
140   of meaningful patch representations. Hereafter, MAE and MLM will be interchangeably used.
141
142   Recently, some works (Huang et al., 2023b; Tsai et al., 2023; You et al., 2023) have employed MAE
143
      for the problems of demanding robustness. Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2023b) studied the robustness
      of ViT-based models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), including PeCo (Dong et al., 2023), BEiT (Bao
144
      et al., 2021), and MAE. However, the authors only focused on the adversarial perturbation with only
145
      small settings of attack budget, which is not enough in the challenging problem of the adversarial
146   robustness. DRAM (Tsai et al., 2023) proposes a test-time detection method to repair adversarial
147   samples in that the MAE reconstruction loss is directly used to detect the adversarial samples due to
148   the assumption of different distributions of clean and adversarial samples. DRAM achieves robustness
149   against adaptive attacks with the limitation that the adversarial sample should be close to the original
150   sample with the mean-square error that is far smaller than those in existing works so as to weaken the
151   adaptive attacks. NIM-MAE (You et al., 2023) uses the MAE structure to achieve adversarial training
152   by injecting the noise into the entire image instead of masking patches within an image.

      3.2   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
163
164   Given a classifier c parameterized by θ, and a clean data pair (x, y), an adversarial attack aims to find
165   an adversarial sample xa derived from x to deceive the classifier (i.e., c(xa ) ̸= y) by the optimization
166   as: maxxa L(xa , y; θ), s.t.||xa − x||p < ϵ, where L is the training loss function, || · ||p denotes
167   p-norm, and ϵ means the attack budget.
168
169   3.3   M ASKED AUTOENCODER
170
171   Given an image x ∈ RH×W and a binary mask M ∈ RH×W , the goal of MAE (He et al., 2022) is to
172   reconstruct the entire image x̃ = x ∈ RH×W from partial image M ⊙ x with the reconstruction loss
173
      defined as:
174                     LM AE (x, x̃) = ∥(1 − M ) ⊙ x̃ − (1 − M ) ⊙ g ◦ f (M ⊙ x)∥2 ,                       (1)

176   where 1 is a matrix equal to 1H×W , ⊙ is the element-wise product of two matrices of the same size,

178   masking ratio r as ||M ||1 = (1 − r) × (H × W ). The masking ratio r = 0 if there is no masking.

180   4     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
181
182   Masked Language Modeling (MLM) (Kenton & Toutanova, 2019) has demonstrated its effectiveness
183   across numerous vision and language tasks. The fundamental concept involves masking certain words
184   within sentences or patches within images, prompting the model to reconstruct the masked portions
185   based on the unmasked context. This process facilitates the learning of meaningful relationships
186   between words/patches and their representations. While MLM has consistently demonstrated state-of-
187   the-art performance in recent studies, its application in adversarial purifiers remains underexplored.
188   In this paper, inspired by our findings in Section 4.1 and Section 4.2, we propose a new adversarial
189
      defense paradigm of integrating MLM into a purifier in Section 4.3 and demonstrate its superiority
      over directly employing MLM in Section 4.4. Here, we will describe and verify the steps in MAEP.

192   4.1   M OTIVATION : D EFENSE T RANSFERABILITY VIA E MPIRICAL O BSERVATIONS
193
      The predominant purification-based approaches (Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022;
194
      Zhang et al., 2023) in adversarial defense involve the diffusion model, which primarily modify the
195   reverse diffusion process of DiffPure (Nie et al., 2022) using the same pre-trained model. However,
196   these approaches exhibit limited transferability of the purifier, as depicted in Tables 2 and 3. The
197   empirical evidence in Table 2 indicates that while DiffPure maintains a close gap between the clean
198   accuracy and robust accuracy on CIFAR10, it experiences a significant drop (from 89.45% to 69.00%)
199   in robust accuracy when transferred to CIFAR100. A similar phenomenon can also be observed in
200   Table 3. We exemplify DiffPure as the representative of diffusion-based adversarial defenses because
201   other methods share a similar concept by primarily modifying the reverse diffusion process.
202   Most importantly, in real-world applications, it is impractical to always have access to a well-trained
203   diffusion model, and training one from scratch is inefficient, especially when dealing with small
204   datasets or when substantial resources are required for multiple datasets. Moreover, diffusion model-
205   based defenses Nie et al. (2022)Zhang et al. (2023) suffer a significant drop in robust accuracy even
206   when the training and testing datasets are closely related like CIFAR10 and CIFAR100, indicating a
207   lack of defense generalization and vulnerability to images with minor differences.
208   The above observations motivate us to propose a new method that can achieve state-of-the-art robust
209   accuracy and satisfy model transferability at the same time. Note that, even for DISCO that has been
210   recognized to possess defense transferability, the clean accuracy of our MAEP is obviously higher
211   than that of DISCO no matter transferability is considered or not. Please see Sec. 5.3 for more results.

      4.3.2    T OTAL L OSS IN MAEP
325
326   Unlike the aforementioned designs, our method, MAEP, is investigated to integrate both the MLM
327   and purification loss of DISCO in Eq. (2) to boost adversarial robustness and model transferability.
328   We also show that solely utilizing MAE/MLM does not yield satisfactory performance.
329   The entire loss of MAEP can be separated into two parts. Part 1) The purify loss, adapted from
330   Eq. (2), addresses the purification task focusing solely on the unmasked region within an image.
331   Several reasons support this decision to exclusively handle the unmasked region. Firstly, training with
332   partial image information can generalize to the entire image via position embedding, as outlined in
333   MAE (He et al., 2022). In addition, the purifier is designed to operate on the entire image rather than
334   the masked region. Secondly, the incorporation of MLM discussed in Part 2) below can effectively
335   address the issue of dealing with the masked region. Lastly, a finetuning strategy, as will be discussed
336   in Section 4.4, is introduced to refine the gap between the results obtained from the unmasked and
337
      masked regions. Part 2) Compared to those using the unmasked region in Part 1) for purification, the
      MLM reconstructs the masked region of an image by the unmasked region. This masking mechanism
338
      will help the model learn the adversarial representation or recognize the adversarial perturbation so
339
      as to further boost the performance.

362   4.4     T RAIN -T EST D ISCREPANCY AND T RANSFER L EARNING
363
      Recall that representation learning, such as BERT and MAE, often adopts two-stage training, which
364
      learns the meaningful latent representation in the pre-training stage and boosts the performance of
365
      downstream tasks in the finetuning stage, as shown in Eqs. (9) and (10), respectively. So far, during
366   pre-training, MAEP has purified the adversarial image (Eq. (12)), eliminating the need of finetuning.
367   We will further consider an issue of train-test discrepancy within the masking mechanism and the
368   practical concern of transferring to different datasets, so that we can use finetuning in MAEP as well.
369
      Inspired by (Touvron et al., 2019), if the distribution of input images at inference time and that at
370
      training time are similar, the problem, called “train-test discrepancy,” due to the different conditions
371
      at the training and testing stages, can be properly alleviated to improve performance. Additionally,
372   the BERT-based methods (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; He et al., 2022; Kenton & Toutanova, 2019) have
373   good transferability by model finetuning. Actually, in MAEP, the masking ratio r = 0.5 in training
374   time is different from r = 0 in inference time that will cause train-test discrepancy. Although this
375   problem has been tackled by position embedding, as described in MAE (He et al., 2022), we find that
376   finetuning MAEP to alleviate the train-test discrepancy can further improve the performance. Based
377   on the above concerns, we design a simple finetuning strategy to solve the train-test discrepancy and
      maintain the goodness of transferability.


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      The finetuning strategy here is simple in that we train an MAEP with masked images and then use
379   LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) to only finetune the decoder with masking ratio r = 0. In practice, the
380   pre-training ViT-based model (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and diffusion-based model (Ho et al., 2020)
381   often require significant computing resources. In most usage in real life, we don’t want to spend
382   too much computing power to finetune a downstream task. That is why we adopt a lightweight
383   finetuning technique (i.e., LoRA) to finetune only a minimal number of trainable parameters. Here,
384   the lightweight finetuning technique can be replaced by any other similar approaches, such as Ladder
385   Side-Tuning(LST) (Sung et al., 2022), P-tuning(Liu et al., 2023), and Prefix-Tuning (Li & Liang,
386   2021). We can observe from Table 5 that the both the clean and robust accuracy can be increased
387
      via the use of finetuning. In addition, we further provide a straightforward comparison between the
      traditional finetuning methods and LoRA in Table 13 of Sec. 9 of Appendix. The results indicate that
388
      our MAEP can achieve both the dataset transfer and train-test discrepancy alleviation.

529   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
530
531   Different from diffusion model-based adversarial defenses, we are first to explore how to integrate
532   both the masking mechanism and purifier as a new defense paradigm. Our method is an MAE-
533   based adversarial purifier and possesses the characteristics of defense transferability and attack
534   generalization without needing additional data.
535   Compared to diffusion models, even without pre-trained model weights available for MAEP on well-
536   known datasets, MAEP still delivers competitive performance across various datasets. Additionally,
537   in the absence of pre-trained models, MAEP requires fewer training resources than diffusion-based
538   approaches. Moreover, recent advancements in NLP, driven by Transformer-based models, have
539   witnessed tremendous success. MAEP adopts the Vision Transformer (ViT) structure, indicating a
      promising direction for integrating large language models into adversarial purifiers.
