009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
                Various reversible deep neural networks (DNN) models have been proposed to
012             reduce memory consumption in the training process. However, almost all existing
013             reversible DNNs either require special non-standard architectures or are constructed
014             by modifying existing DNN architectures considerably to enable reversibility. In
015             this work we present the BDIA-transformer, which is an exact bit-level reversible
016             transformer1 that uses an unchanged standard architecture for inference. The basic
017             idea is to first treat each transformer block as the Euler integration approxima-
018             tion for solving an ordinary differential equation (ODE) and then incorporate the
019             technique of bidirectional integration approximation (BDIA) (originally designed
020
                for diffusion inversion) into the neural architecture, together with activation quan-
                tization to make it exactly bit-level reversible. In the training process, we let
021
                a hyper-parameter γ in BDIA-transformer randomly take one of the two values
022
                {0.5, −0.5} per training sample per transformer block for averaging every two
023             consecutive integration approximations. As a result, BDIA-transformer can be
024             viewed as training an ensemble of ODE solvers parameterized by a set of binary
025             random variables, which regularizes the model and results in improved validation
026             accuracy. Lightweight side information per transformer block is required to be
027             stored in the forward process to account for binary quantization loss to enable exact
028             bit-level reversibility. In the inference procedure, the expectation E(γ) = 0 is taken
029             to make the resulting architectures of BDIA-transformer identical to transformers
030             up to activation quantization. Our experiments in both image classification and
031
                language translation show that BDIA-transformers outperform their conventional
                counterparts significantly in terms of validation performance due to the regulariza-
032
                tion effect of the set of γ random variables while also requiring considerably less
033
                training memory.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038
      An active research trend in deep learning is to scale up the size of both the deep neural networks
      (DNNs) and the training data, with the aim of obtaining universal machine-learning models that are
039
      capable of accomplishing various tasks. Examples are large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4
040
      [1] and Llama 2 [26], which can, for example, have informative and friendly conversations with
041   humans, solve mathematical problems, and produce high-quality source codes for programming tasks.
042   A major bottleneck for training those large DNNs is that they often require large on-chip memory and
043   large inter-chip communication bandwidth across many chips to accommodate both the DNN model
044   and the intermediate activations of the input data-batch in back-propagation [8], which is referred to
045   as memory wall in the literature.
046
      One promising technique to alleviate the issue of memory wall is to design and train reversible DNNs
047   [5; 6; 2; 19]. By doing so, the intermediate activations in the forward pass do not have to be stored
048   in the memory to allow for back-propagation. Instead, they can be recomputed on-the-fly in the
049   backward pass by exploiting the reversibility of the DNN, thus saving memory consumption by a
050   large margin for deep DNNs. The procedure essentially reduces memory consumption at the cost of
051
052      1
           In fact, by following the same principle, we can also design exact bit-level reversible ResNets without
053   changing its architecture for inference. Here, we focus on the transformer because of its popularity in the field of
      generative artificial intelligence.

      a reasonable amount of additional computation. The reduction in memory also has the potential to
055   improve throughput by increasing the batch size.
056
057   Early reversible DNNs, such as NICE [5], the methods of [20], Real NVP [6], and Inverse Au-
058
      toregressive Flow [16] are differentiable transformations that were aimed at facilitating generative
      modeling. For an overview of such early normalizing flows see [17]. Inspired by these works, a range
059
      of reversible residual models were proposed subsequently, including RevNet [9], Glow [15], i-RevNet
060
      [13], i-ResNet [2], layerwise inversion [11], Fourier-transformation based CNN inversion [7], and
061   Mintnet [23]. Another line of research enforces reversibility in deep learning from the perspective of
062   ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which includes FFJORD [10], leapfrog networks [3], neural
063   ODEs [4], momentum residual networks [21], and neural ODE inversion [25].
064
      Recently, the research on reversibility has moved to other types of neural networks. The authors in
065
      [19; 34] proposed reversible vision transformers (referred to as RevViT) due to the popularity of
066
      LLMs. [27] utilized a reversible diffusion sampling method to optimize the noisy representation for
067   the task of diffusion based image editing. To our best knowledge, all the above existing reversible
068   DNNs either require non-standard architectures or are constructed by modifying the original DNN
069   architectures considerably to enable reversibility.
070
      In this paper, we propose BDIA-transformer, a new type of reversible transformer that uses an
071
      unchanged, standard architecture for the inference procedure. It is based on the bidirectional
072
      integration approximation (BDIA), which was recently proposed in [33] to enable diffusion inversion
073   for round-trip image editing. To be able to incorporate BDIA into transformers for online back-
074   propagation, we follow the common practice of treating each transformer block as an Euler integration
075   approximation for solving an ordinary differential equation (ODE).
076
      We make two main contributions in this work. Firstly, we propose BDIA-transformers by introducing
077
      a random hyper-parameter γ ∈ {−0.5, 0.5} per transformer block per training sample to regularize
078
      the DNN models for improvement of validation performance. Each γ parameter intends to average
079   every two consecutive integration approximations. The training procedure becomes training of
080   an ensemble of ODE solvers parameterized by a set of binary random variables. In the inference
081   procedure, the expectation E[γ] = 0 is utilized, which reduces BDIA-transformers to conventional
082   transformers. Therefore, our method for enforcing reversibility is more flexible than existing ones.
083
      Secondly, we perform activation quantization to allow for exact bit-level reversibility of BDIA-
084
      transformers. Note that the special setup of the γ values in the subset {−0.5, 0.5} when performing
085   activation quantization leads to a 1 bit information loss per activation value per transformer block.
086   Therefore, lightweight side information per transformer block needs to be stored during training to
087   recover this 1 bit information loss. Despite this, the overall memory use is significantly reduced.
088
      Experimental results on ViT for image classification and transformer for language translation show
089
      that the BDIA technique significantly improves the validation performance over that of the cor-
090
      responding baseline transformers and simultaneously reduces training memory significantly. The
091   improved performance results from the model regularization imposed by a set of γ random variables.
092   Experiments on text prediction demonstrate that BDIA-GPT2 significantly alleviates the over-fitting
093   issue of GPT2 when intentionally trained on a very small dataset. Our empirical study also indicates
094   that RevViT from [19] produces either inferior or comparable validation performance to that of its
095   original counterparts.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
098   In recent years, various quantization strategies [30; 29; 28] have been proposed in the training
099   and/or inference processes of DNN models on low-precision devices. For instance, the work [29]
100   successfully performed quantization on DNN weights, activations, gradients and errors in the training
101   and inference processes and obtained promising results. The recent work [18] demonstrated that
102   LLMs with a quantization of 1.58 bits per model parameter exhibit comparable performance to
103   non-quantized models. In summary, it was found that the validation performance of DNN models
104
      that incorporate those quantization operations is comparable with that of conventional DNN models.
      In our work, we only need to apply activation quantization to enable exact bit-level reversibility in
105
      training BDIA-transformers.
106
107   We note that in general, model quantization and design of reversible DNN models are two compli-
      mentary strategies for reducing memory consumption in the training process, where the first strategy

      operates on the model parameters and the second one operates on the activation values. With the
109   development of new model quantization methods such as [18], advancing the research frontier of
110   reversible DNN models has become of great interest.

      Neural networks as ODEs: [4] highlighted the interpretation that passing a hidden state across layers
      that add a correction to that state can be viewed as Euler integration. While that paper identified
116
      architectures including residual nets and normalizing flows as following this pattern, it is equally true
117
      of diffusion models and transformers. This interpretation emphasizes the importance of considering
118   more accurate integration schemes [14]. The need for improved integration is particularly apparent
119   in round-trip image editing in diffusion models, where significant integration error will result in
120   unintended visible alterations to the image.
121
      Diffusion sampling via solving ODE: Recently, the work [33] proposed the BDIA technique to
122
      enable diffusion inversion for effective round-trip image editing. From a high level point of view,
123

124   T , the diffusion-based sampling process for generating realisic images zϵ at time t = ϵ > 0 can be
125   realized by solving a probability ordinary differential equation (ODE)

354   Consequently, the computed gradient in the online back-propagation will not be drifted from the
355   ground truth, which is desirable in very deep transformer models.

      Remark 2. In principle, one can also make BDIA-transformer with {γk ∈ ±0.25}K−1            k=1 to be
357   exactly bit-level reversible. In this scenario, side information of 2 bits per activation value per
358   transformer block is required to be stored to account for the quantization loss when performing online
359   back-propagation. We omit the corresponding update expressions.
360
361   Limitations of BDIA-transformer: To our best knowledge, all existing reversible DNN models do
362
      not need to store any lightweight side information in the forward process. The primary objective of
      most existing works is to design reversible DNN models (e.g., RevViT) that produce comparable
363
      validation performance as the original counterparts. From the perspective of memory reduction,
364
      existing reversible DNN models are more efficient than BDIA-transformer.
365
366   On the other hand, BDIA-transformer is designed to not only save training memory but also improve
367   the generalization performance via model regularization. Our method is the first of its kind that
368
      attempts to maintain the transformer architecture in the inference procedure.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
525
      In this work, we have proposed the BDIA training technique to assist the training procedure of
526   transformers. Firstly, each transformer block is taken as the Euler integration approximation for
527   solving an ODE. The BDIA technique is then applied to average every two consecutive integration
528   approximations in a transformer as a regularizer via a set of random variables {γk }K−1
                                                                                          k=1 , one variable
529   per transformer block. Exact bit-level reversibility for lossless online back-propagation can be
530   achieved for BDIA-transformer by performing activation quantization and storing only lightweight
531   side information. The side information accounts for the binary quantization loss incurred by the
532   special setup of {γk = ±0.5}K−1k=1 . In the inference procedure, the expectation E[γk ] = 0 is taken to
533   replace γk in the update expression of BDIA-transformer, which reduces the update expression to
534   that of the conventional transformer up to activation quantization.
535   Experiments on image classification and language translation show that BDIA-transformer produces
536   significantly better validation performance than the corresponding baseline transformers while, at
537   the same time, reducing training memory by performing online back-propagation. Experiments on
538   text prediction indicate that BDIA-GPT2 alleviates the over-fitting issue of GPT2 over a very small
539   training dataset significantly. In comparison, the experiments with RevVit demonstrate that it yields
      either inferior or comparable validation performance to that of ViT.
