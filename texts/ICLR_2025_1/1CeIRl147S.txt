011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The reliable and objective evaluation of AI models is essential for measuring sci-
014            entific progress and translating methods into practice. However, in the nascent
015            field of multimodal foundation models, validation has proven to be even more
016
               complex and error-prone compared to the field of narrow, task-specific AI. One
               open question that has not received much attention is how to set up strong vi-
017
               sion language model (VLM) benchmarks while sparing human annotation costs.
018
               This holds specifically for domain-specific foundation models designed to serve a
019            predefined specific purpose (e.g. pathology, autonomous driving) for which per-
020            formance on test data should translate into real-life success. Given this gap in
021            the literature, our contribution is three-fold: (1) In analogy to the concept of data
022            augmentation in traditional ML, we propose the concept of task augmentation -
023            a resource-efficient method for creating multiple tasks from a single existing task
024            using metadata annotations. To this end, we use three sources to enhance existing
025            datasets with relevant metadata: human annotators (e.g. for annotating trunca-
026            tion), predefined rules (e.g. for converting instance segmentations to the number
027
               of objects), and existing models (e.g. depth models to compute which object is
               closer to the camera). (2) We apply our task augmentation concept to several
028
               domains represented by the well-known data sets COCO (e.g. kitchen, wildlife
029
               domain) and KITTI (autonomous driving domain) datasets to generate domain-
030            specific VLM benchmarks with highly reliable reference data. As a unique fea-
031            ture compared to existing benchmarks, we quantify the ambiguity of the human
032            answer for each task for each image by acquiring human answers from a total of
033            six raters, contributing a total of 162,946 human baseline answers to the 37,171
034            tasks generated on 1,704 images. (3) Finally, we use our framework to benchmark
035            a total of 21 open and frontier closed models. Our large-scale analysis suggests
036            that (I) model performance varies across domains, (II) open models have narrowed
037            the gap to closed models significantly, (III) the recently released Qwen2 72B is the
038
               strongest open model, (IV) human raters outperform all VLMs by a large margin,
               and (V) many open models (56%) perform worse than the random baseline. By
039
               analyzing performance variability and relations across domains and tasks, we fur-
040
               ther show that task augmentation is a viable strategy for transforming single tasks
041            into many and could serve as a blueprint for addressing dataset sparsity in various
042            domains.

045   1   I NTRODUCTION
046
047   The reliable and objective performance assessment, i.e., validation of AI models is crucial for both
048   the measurement of scientific progress and translation into practice. Benchmarking for traditional
049   narrow, task-specific AI already comes with numerous challenges (Myllyaho et al., 2021), but vali-
050   dation has proven to be even more complex and error-prone in the emerging field of generalist mul-
051
      timodal foundation models. For example, an award-winning Neurips paper (Schaeffer et al., 2024)
      on language foundation models recently showed a large discrepancy between progress claimed by
052
      researchers and fundamental changes in model behavior with scale. In the context of vision lan-
053
      guage models (VLMs), benchmarking-related issues include data leakage (Chen et al., 2024a), a


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      narrow task focus with inadequate coverage of vision-language capabilities, labor-intensive and
055   time-consuming human annotation, and frequent failure to report the number and qualifications of
056   human annotators, as well as potential disagreements in their annotations. One open question that
057   has not received much attention is how to set up strong domain-specific VLM benchmarks while
058   sparing human annotation costs. This paper therefore focuses on the specific task of VLM vali-
059   dation from the perspective of (annotation) resource investment. It is based on the following key
060   observations:
061   1. Cross-domain validation is not always desirable. While numerous datasets and benchmarks are
062   currently being released in the general computer vision field (e.g. 400 out of the 2700 CVPR 2024
063   publications propose a new or modified dataset, see Appendix C), these may not always be optimal
064   for validating domain-specific foundation models designed to serve a predefined specific purpose.
065   In such cases, performance on test data should translate into real-life success, thus requiring the
066   test set to represent the challenges and variability found in the specific domain the model was de-
067   signed for (e.g., medicine). Given this, arena-style evaluations—where users submit tasks and rate
068   models blindly—provide strong evidence for the need for domain-specific evaluation by ensuring
069
      task-relevant assessments, such as Chatbot Arena1 or WildVision Arena2 . Domain-specific evalua-
      tion, however, comes with challenges, as publicly available data may be sparse and human resources
070
      for labeling can be limited (see e.g. Maier-Hein et al. (2022)). An open question, therefore, is how
071
      to make the best out of available data and resources.
072
073   2. Picking the right tasks is challenging. Foundation models should be adaptable to a wide variety
074   of domain tasks. The challenge in traditional computer vision was to obtain a highly diverse set
075
      of representative images for reliable evaluation. Analogously, the rise of foundation models calls
      for a highly diverse number of tasks. An open challenge is to decide which tasks to include in a
076
      benchmark. Adding a task to an existing set of tasks may not necessarily provide additional insights,
077
      as performance across tasks can be highly correlated (Fu et al., 2024b). From a resource investment
078   perspective, picking the right tasks is thus highly desirable.
079
080   3. Balancing quantity and quality is non-trivial. Reliable reference data for robust evaluation has
081
      always been a priority. Generally speaking, increasing the number of annotators increases the qual-
      ity of the reference annotation (e.g. Guzene et al. (2023), and Dumitrache et al. (2015)). However,
082
      balancing the quantity of images with the number of annotators per image is challenging. In the field
083
      of image classification, for example, prior work suggests that a single human rater is not sufficient
084   to create reliable benchmarks (Schmarje et al., 2024). On the other hand, recent work suggests that
085   resources may be better invested when annotating more images rather than increasing the number
086   of annotators per item (Dorner & Hardt, 2024)]. In the context of VLM benchmarks, this issue has
087   not yet been well-explored. According to an analysis of 13 popular VLM benchmarks, information
088   on the number of annotators per image in VLM benchmarks is either not provided (38%), or only 3
089   or fewer raters (15%) contributed to creating the reference 4. Of note, MTurk has faced issues with
090   workers producing inconsistent or careless annotations, leading to a noticeable decline in annota-
091   tion quality in recent years (Kennedy et al., 2020; Rädsch et al., 2023; 2024). In general, there is
092
      insufficient evidence to determine the optimal number of annotators when considering the trade-off
      between annotation quality and cost.
093
094   Overall, our literature analysis clearly indicates that there is a lack of guidance on how to set up
095   strong VLM benchmarks while keeping annotation costs low. We address this gap in the literature
096   with the following three key contributions (see Fig. 1):
097
098

                 analogy to the concept of data augmentation in traditional ML, we propose the concept
099
                 of task augmentation—a resource-efficient method for generating multiple tasks from a

101              with fine-grained annotations (here: instance segmentations), metadata on each image is
102              acquired to convert the single task to a collection of tasks (Tab. 1.), enabling comprehensi-
103              ble in-domain validation. To enable the augmentation, we enrich the existing datasets with
104              metadata related to the image (e.g., number of objects, object relations, brightness) as well
105              as individual objects (e.g., object depth, occlusion).

      2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE B ENCHMARKS
153
      Recent studies have proposed a range of evaluation benchmarks for VLMs, varying in size and the
154   number and type of vision-language (VL) capabilities they assess (e.g. Fu et al. (2024b) and Bai
155   et al. (2023)). For instance, Blink (Fu et al., 2024b) and MMBench (Liu et al., 2024a) comprise
156   more than 3,000 multiple-choice questions and cover multiple VL tasks while MME (Fu et al.,
157   2024a) focuses on evaluating perception and cognition abilities using Yes/No questions.
158
      Among the largest benchmarks developed to date are MMT-Bench (Ying et al., 2024) which in-
159
      cludes more than 31,000 questions, MME-RealWorld (Zhang et al., 2024b) comprising more than
160   29,000 image-question pairs, and MMMU (Yue et al., 2024) featuring 11,500 questions. While
161   these large benchmarks cover multiple VL capabilities and domains, they require extensive labeling


                                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      efforts. For example, the authors of the MME-RealWorld benchmark involved 25 professional an-
163   notators and seven experts in VLMs, while the MMMU benchmark included contributions from 50
164   college students. The authors of MMT-Bench, however, do not provide the exact number of human
165   annotators.
166
      At the same time, MMStar (Chen et al., 2024b) and MM-Vet-v2 (Yu et al., 2024) allow the evaluation
167
      of core VL capabilities of VLMs using much smaller question sets. Additionally, several works in-
168
      tegrate multiple existing benchmarks into comprehensive evaluation frameworks (Jiang et al., 2024;
169   Al-Tahan et al., 2024). Platforms such as WildVision (Lu et al., 2024) and LVLM-eHub (Xu et al.,
170   2023) enable the collection of human preferences to further enhance VLM evaluation. Other studies
171   focus on specific aspects of VLM assessment such as accounting for uncertainty (Kostumov et al.,
172   2024), disentangling perception and reasoning (Qiao et al., 2024), evaluating complex vision tasks
173   (Liu et al., 2024b; Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024), or leveraging large language models (LLMs)
174   to assess visual storytelling abilities of VLMs (Bai et al., 2023). Very recently, (Tong et al., 2024)
175   presented a critical examination of multimodal LLM benchmarks.
176   Despite the variety of datasets and tasks, a resource-efficient and generalizable approach that enables
177   extensive evaluation of VLMs across multiple (domain-specific) tasks is still lacking. One potential
178   solution is task augmentation.
179
180   2.2   TASK AUGMENTATION AND METADATA
181
182   The challenge of generating multiple tasks from a single dataset for VLM benchmarking remains
183   relatively unexplored. A few examples include CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017), a benchmark dataset
184   of 3D objects used to assess various visual reasoning tasks by combining existing metadata attributes
185
      such as the size, color, or shape of objects. ”Task Me Anything” (Zhang et al., 2024a) generated
      an important contribution in showing the potential of automatic task generation, but focuses on
186
      computationally rendered images specifically created for the evaluation. Taskonomy (Zamir et al.,
187
      2018) proposed a fully computational approach for modeling the structure and connections within
188   the domain of visual tasks. Other related works are LVIS-INSTRUCT4V (Wang et al., 2023), a
189   visual instruction dataset generated with GPT-4V, and JourneyBench (Wang et al., 2024) which
190   comprises human-annotated, generated images and requires in-depth multimodal reasoning. In ad-
191   dition, GoogleAI’s Open Images Dataset (Kuznetsova et al., 2020) and Visual Genome (Krishna
192   et al., 2017) can serve as a valuable resource for VLMs benchmarking as they include compre-
193   hensive pre-generated metadata. However, these datasets do not support automatic generation of
194   metadata for other datasets, limiting their use to the specific data they contain. To the best of our
195   knowledge, our work is the first to develop an approach for automated task augmentation from real
196
      world instance segmentation datasets using metadata enrichment.
197
198   2.3   R ESOURCE - EFFICIENT VLM BENCHMARKING
199
      Most existing benchmarks often focus on performance metrics without considering the computa-
200
      tional resources required (see e.g. (Fu et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024a)). The work that has been done
201   on efficient benchmarking has been only in the realm of unimodal language models (Polo et al.,
202   2024; Perlitz et al., 2023). This is despite the fact that VLMs are becoming more prominent both in
203   research and industry (Li et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023) and multimodality has been described as a
204   key measure of intelligence (Bubeck et al., 2023).

      3.3   B ENCHMARKING STRATEGY
291
292   VLM benchmarking results can vary substantially with various factors, such as the images used, the
293   domain, and the prompts applied. This often renders comparison of results across papers infeasible.
294   For example, Accuracy is known to be a prevalence-dependent metric, meaning that results should
295   not be compared across datasets. To address this bottleneck, we fully homogenized our benchmark-
296   ing pipeline using the proposed task augmentation concept.
297   Model Selection: We selected 21 frontier and open VLMs of various sizes and from various
298   providers and sources, as illustrated in App. E. The oldest model was released in January 2024,
299   while the most recent one was released a few days before the manuscript submission.
300
      Benchmarking workflow: To ensure fair and consistent evaluation of all selected VLMs, we devel-
301
      oped a standardized benchmarking workflow applied uniformly across all models. We assessed them
302
      in a zero-shot setting without any additional fine-tuning or domain-specific training. We strictly fol-
303   lowed the configurations and setups recommended by each model’s authors, using the exact settings
304   provided in their official repositories (e.g., on Hugging Face) to ensure that each model was eval-
305   uated under conditions intended by its creators. For tasks requiring multiple images, they were
306   combined into one. Each model was provided with a carefully crafted text prompt alongside the
307   corresponding image(s). To eliminate potential ambiguities, we conducted iterative testing of these
308   prompts among human evaluators. Through multiple rounds of refinement, we adjusted the prompts
309   until all human testers consistently agreed on their interpretation.
310   VLM Tasks: We evaluated the models on a comprehensive set of 25 tasks derived from our task
311   augmentation framework (examples in App. A; details in App. I). Each task was associated with
312   specific evaluation criteria and standardized prompts. For instance, when dealing with multiple-
313   choice questions or tasks involving object selection, we established clear guidelines on how options
314   were presented and how objects were chosen within images. This attention to detail ensured that the
315   evaluation was both rigorous and reproducible.
316   Metrics and Rankings: Choosing an adequate strategy for performance assessment is far from trivial
317   and a research topic of its own (Maier-Hein et al., 2024; Reinke et al., 2024). In this work, we
318   were specifically interested in relative performance differences rather than in the specific ability
319   of models to serve a specific task. To obtain aggregated performance values across images, we
320   introduce the Accuracy% metric. This metric is configured with a percentage threshold t. Simply
321   put, the Accuracy%(t) metric outputs the percentage p of images, for which at least t% of questions
322   were answered correctly.

      5   D ISCUSSION
438
439
      This paper contributes to the advancement of VLM benchmarking in three ways.
440
           1. Framework for domain-specific benchmarking: We showed that task augmentation, using
441           instance segmentation as the root task, enables the generation of a diverse set of VLM
442           tasks and could thus evolve as a core method for resource-efficient domain-specific VLM
443           benchmarking. The insights gained on the varying difficulty of presented VLM tasks will
444           further guide the design of future benchmarks.
445        2. New data: Our two new datasets META-KITTI and META-COCO will help assess gener-
446           alist capabilities of future VLMs. Furthermore, we release the six human annotations per
447           task (totaling 162,946 annotations) to assist other researchers in their benchmarking efforts.
448
           3. New insights: The insights on current capabilities of closed and open VLMs highlight the
449           narrowing gap between closed and open models. Most importantly, we showcased the need
450           for domain-specific validation.
451
452   Core strengths of our contribution include the broad applicability of our concept, the open data
453   contribution, and the wide range of state-of-the-art closed and open models investigated here, with
454   the youngest model released only a few days before submission.
455   As an implicit contribution, we introduced the new metric Accuracy%, which offers several key
456   strengths. It measures the variety of tasks on the same image, enabling a comprehensive evaluation
457   across different capabilities, and provides a rigorous benchmark that challenges VLMs on a wide
458   range of tasks. The metric is extendable with additional tasks, allowing for gradually increasing
459   difficulty, and can be adapted to evaluate domain-specific tasks effectively. However, there are
460   limitations: some questions require specific conditions, such as the presence of multiple objects for
461   comparison, potentially resulting in variability in the number of questions per image. Additionally,
462
      tasks are treated equally without any weighting, which may overlook differences in task difficulty
      or importance.
463
464   A limitation of our work is model family dependence, as many models come from closely related
465   families, which may hinder statistical analysis. For closed-source models, specific information about
466   training and data is often unavailable, creating transparency issues. Model performance also shows
467   prompt dependence, with results potentially varying based on prompt phrasing. Additionally, our
468
      human annotations were performed by professional annotators, which may introduce ambiguity
      since annotators aim to complete tasks quickly.
469
470   Future work should focus on expanding the number of tasks generated, further enhancing the di-
471   versity and comprehensiveness of VLM benchmarks. Additionally, our method can be adapted to
472   different domains with domain-specific questions or scaled up to support continuous extension, pro-
473   viding a versatile approach for evaluating models across diverse applications.
