009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) excel in learning from relational datasets, pro-
012            cessing node and edge features in a way that preserves the symmetries of the
013            graph domain. However, many complex systems—such as biological or social
014            networks—involve multiway complex interactions that are more naturally repre-
015            sented by higher-order topological domains. The emerging field of Topological
016            Deep Learning (TDL) aims to accommodate and leverage these higher-order struc-
017            tures. Combinatorial Complex Neural Networks (CCNNs), fairly general TDL
018            models, have been shown to be more expressive and better performing than GNNs.
019            However, differently from the graph deep learning ecosystem, TDL lacks a princi-
020
               pled and standardized framework for easily defining new architectures, restricting
               its accessibility and applicability. To address this issue, we introduce Generalized
021
               CCNNs (GCCNs), a novel simple yet powerful family of TDL models that can be
022
               used to systematically transform any (graph) neural network into its TDL coun-
023            terpart. We prove that GCCNs generalize and subsume CCNNs, while extensive
024            experiments on a diverse class of GCCNs show that these architectures consistently
025            match or outperform CCNNs, often with less model complexity. In an effort to
026            accelerate and democratize TDL, we introduce TopoTune, a lightweight software
027            for defining, building, and training GCCNs with unprecedented flexibility and ease.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2008; Corso et al., 2024) have demonstrated
032   remarkable performance in several relational learning tasks by incorporating prior knowledge through
033   graph structures (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Zhang & Chen, 2018). However, constrained by the pairwise
034   nature of graphs, GNNs are limited in their ability to capture and model higher-order interactions—
035   crucial in complex systems like particle physics, social interactions, or biological networks (Lambiotte
036
      et al., 2019). Topological Deep Learning (TDL) (Bodnar, 2023) precisely emerged as a framework that
      naturally encompasses multi-way relationships, leveraging beyond-graph combinatorial topological
037
      domains such as simplicial and cell complexes, or hypergraphs (Papillon et al., 2023).1
038
039   In this context, Hajij et al. (2023; 2024a) have recently introduced combinatorial complexes, fairly
040   general objects that are able to model arbitrary higher-order interactions along with a hierarchical
041   organization among them–hence generalizing (for learning purposes) most of the combinatorial
042
      topological domains within TDL, including graphs. The elements of a combinatorial complex are
      cells, being nodes or groups of nodes, which are categorized by ranks. The simplest cell, a single node,
043
      has rank zero. Cells of higher ranks define relationships between nodes: rank one cells are edges,
044
      rank two cells are faces, and so on. Hajij et al. (2023) also proposes Combinatorial Complex Neural
045   Networks (CCNNs), machine learning architectures that leverage the versatility of combinatorial
046   complexes to naturally model higher-order interactions. For instance, consider the task of predicting
047   the solubility of a molecule from its structure. GNNs model molecules as graphs, thus considering
048   atoms (nodes) and bonds (edges) (Gilmer et al., 2017). By contrast, CCNNs model molecules as
049   combinatorial complexes, hence considering atoms (nodes, i.e., cells of rank zero), bonds (edges, i.e.,
050   cells of rank one), and also important higher-order structures such as rings or functional groups (i.e.,
051   cells of rank two) (Battiloro et al., 2024).

      TDL Research Trend. To date, research in TDL has largely progressed by taking existing GNNs
077
      architectures (convolutional, attentional, message-passing, etc.) and generalizing them one-by-one to
078
      a specific TDL counterpart, whether that be on hypergraphs (Feng et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020a;
079   Yadati, 2020), on simplicial complexes (Roddenberry et al., 2021; Yang & Isufi, 2023; Ebli et al.,
080   2020; Giusti et al., 2022a; Battiloro et al., 2023; Bodnar et al., 2021b; Maggs et al., 2024), on
081   cell complexes (Hajij et al., 2020; Giusti et al., 2022b; Bodnar et al., 2021a), or on combinatorial
082   complexes (Battiloro et al., 2024; Eitan et al., 2024). Although overall valuable and insightful, such a
083   fragmented research trend is slowing the development of standardized methodologies and software
084   for TDL, as well as limiting the analysis of its cost-benefits trade-offs (Papamarkou et al., 2024).
085   We argue that these two relevant aspects are considerably hindering the use and application of TDL
086   beyond the community of experts.
087
088   Current Efforts and Gaps for TDL Standardization. TopoX (Hajij et al., 2024b) and TopoBench-
089   mark (Telyatnikov et al., 2024) have become the reference Python libraries for developing and
090   benchmarking TDL models, respectively. However, despite their potential in defining and implement-
091   ing novel standardized methodologies in the field, the current focus of these packages is on replicating
092
      and analyzing existing message-passing CCNNs. Works like Jogl et al. (2022b;a) have instead
      focused on making TDL accessible and reproducible by porting models to the graph domain. They
093
      do so via principled transformations from combinatorial topological domains to graphs. However,
094
      although these architectures over the resulting graph-expanded representations are as expressive as
095   their TDL counterparts (using the Weisfeiler-Lehman criterion (Xu et al., 2019a)), they are neither
096   formally equivalent to nor a generalization of their TDL counterparts. Due to loss of topological
097   information during the graph expansion, the GNNs on the resulting graph do not preserve the same
098   topological symmetry as their TDL counterparts.
099

100
      standardization for outside practitioners. To that end, we introduce a novel joint methodological and

103   which of the field’s open problems (as defined in Papamarkou et al. (2024)) they help answer:

190   Augmented Hasse Graphs. In TDL, a Hasse graph is a graph expansion of a combinatorial
191   complex. Specifically, it represents the incidence structure NI,# by representing each cell (node,
192   edge, face) as a node and drawing edges between cells that are incident to each other. For example, if

195   collection of multiple neighborhood functions, every combinatorial complex C can be expanded into
196   a unique graph representation. We refer to this representation as an augmented Hasse graph (Hajij
197   et al., 2023). Formally, let NC be a collection of neighborhood functions on C: the augmented Hasse

229   Retaining expressivity, but not topological symmetry. Jogl et al. (2022a;b) demonstrate that
230   GNNs on augmented Hasse graphs GNC are as expressive as CCNNs on C (using the WL criterion),
231   suggesting that some CCNNs can be simulated with standard graph libraries. 2 . However, as
232   the authors state, such GNNs do not structurally distinguish between cells of different ranks or
233   neighborhoods, collapsing topological relationships into a single representation. For instance, in a
234   molecule (cellular complex), two bonds (edges) may simultaneously share multiple neighborhoods:
235
      lower-adjacent through a shared atom (node) and upper-adjacent through a shared ring (face). A
      GNN on GNC collapses these distinctions, applying the same weights to all connections and losing
236
      the structural symmetries encoded in the domain. While this may suffice for preserving expressivity,
237
      it is inherently a very different computation than that of TDL models.

      The Particular Case of Hypergraphs. Hypergraph neural networks have long relied on graph
240
      expansions (Telyatnikov et al., 2023), which has allowed the field to leverage advances in the graph
241
      domain and, by extension, a much wider breadth of models (Antelmi et al., 2023; Papillon et al., 2023).
242   Most hypergraph models are expanded into graphs using the star (Zhou et al., 2006; Solé et al., 1996),
243   the clique (Bolla, 1993; Rodríguez, 2002; Gibson et al., 2000), or the line expansion (Bandyopadhyay
244   et al., 2020). As noted by Agarwal et al. (2006), many hypergraph learning algorithms leverage graph
245   expansions.
246
      The success story of hypergraph neural networks motivates further research on new graph-based
247
      expansions that generalize and subsume current CCNNs. These expansions could, at the same time,
248   encompass current CCNNs and exploit progress in the GNN field. Therefore, returning to our core
249   goal of accelerating and democratizing TDL while preserving its theoretical properties, we propose a
250   two-part approach: a novel graph-based methodology able to generate general architectures (Section
251   4), and a lightweight software framework to easily and widely implement it (Section 5).

          Theoretical properties of GCCNs.
348
349       1. Generality. GCCNs formally generalize CCNNs.
350          Proposition 1. Let C be a combinatorial complex. Let NC be a collection of neighbor-
351          hoods on C. Then, there exists a GCCN that exactly reproduces the computation of a
352          CCNN over C using NC .
353
          2. Permutation Equivariance. Generalizing CCNNs, GCCNs layers are equivariant with
354
             respect to the relabeling of cells in the combinatorial complex.
355
356
             Proposition 2. A GCCN layer is cell permutation equivariant if the neighborhood mes-
             sage function is node permutation equivariant and the inter-neighborhood aggregator is
357
             cell permutation invariant.
358
359       3. Expressivity. The expressiveness of TDL models is tied to their ability to distinguish
360          non-isomorphic graphs. Variants of the Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) test, like the cellular
361          WL for cell complexes (Bodnar et al., 2021a), set upper bounds on their corresponding
362
             TDL models’ expressiveness, as the WL test does for GNNs (Xu et al., 2019a).
363          Proposition 3. GCCNs are strictly more expressive than CCNNs.
364
          The proofs are provided in Appendix B.1, B.2, and B.3, respectively.
365
366   Given Proposition 1, GCCNs allow us to define general TDL models using any neighborhood message
367   function !N , such as any GNN. Not only does this framework avoid having to approximate CCNN
368   computations, as is the case in previous works 4 (Jogl et al., 2022b;a; 2023), but it also enjoys the
369   same permutation equivariance as regular CCNNs (Proposition 2). We show in Appendix C that
370
      the resulting time complexity of a GCCN is a compromise between a typical GNN and a CCNN.
      Differently from the work in (Hajij et al., 2023), the fact that GCCNs can have arbitrary neighborhood
371
      message functions implies that non message-passing TDL models can be readily defined (e.g., by

      using non message-passing models as neighborhood message functions). Moreover, the fact that the

      5       T OPOT UNE
379
380   Our proposed methodology, together with its resulting GCCNs architectures, addresses the challenge
381   of systematically generating principled, general TDL models. Here, we introduce TopoTune, a
382
      software module for defining and benchmarking GCCN architectures on the fly—a vehicle for
      accelerating and democratizing TDL research. TopoTune is made available as part of TopoBenchmark
383
      Telyatnikov et al. (2024). This section details TopoTune’s main features.
384
385
      Change of Paradigm. TopoTune introduces a new perspective on TDL through the concept of
386
      “neighborhoods of interest,” enabling unprecedented flexibility in architectural design. Previously
387   fixed components of CCNNs become hyperparameters of our framework. Even the choice of topolog-
388   ical domain becomes a mere variable, representing a new paradigm in the design and implementation
389   of TDL architectures.
390
391   Accessible TDL. Using TopoTune, a practitioner can instantiate customized GCCNs simply by
392   modifying a few lines of a configuration file. In fact, it is sufficient to specify (i) a collection of
393   per-rank neighborhoods NC , (ii) a neighborhood message function !N , and optionally (iii) some
394   architectural parameters—e.g., the number l of GCCN layers.5 For the neighborhood message
395   function !N , the same configuration file enables direct import of models from standard PyTorch
396
      libraries, including PyTorch Geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019) and Deep Graph Library (Chen et al.,
      2020b). TopoTune’s simplicity provides both newcomers and TDL experts with an accessible tool for
397
      defining higher-order topological architectures.
398
399
      Accelerating TDL Research. TopoTune is fully integrated into TopoBenchmark (Telyatnikov
400   et al., 2024), a comprehensive package offering a wide range of standardized methods and tools for
401   TDL. Practitioners can access ready-to-use models, training pipelines, tasks, and evaluation metrics,
402   including leading open-source models from TopoX (Hajij et al., 2024b). In addition, TopoBenchmark
403   features the largest collection of topological liftings currently available—transformations that map
404   graph datasets into higher-order topological domains. Together, TopoBenchmark and TopoTune
405   organize the vast design space of TDL into an accessible framework, providing unparalleled versatility
406   and standardization for practitionners.

      6       E XPERIMENTS
408
409   We present experiments showcasing a broad class of GCCN’s constructed with TopoTune. These
410   models consistently match, outperform, or finetune existing CCNNs, often with smaller model sizes.
411   TopoTune’s integration into the TopoBenchmark experiment infrastructure ensures a fair comparison
412
      with CCNNs from the literature, as data processing, domain lifting, and training are homogeonized.
413
414   6.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
415
      We generate our class of GCCNs by considering ten possible choices of neighborhood structure NC
416
      (including both regular and per-rank, see Appendix E.1) and five possible choices of !N : GCN (Kipf
417   & Welling, 2017), GAT (Velickovic et al., 2017), GIN (Xu et al., 2019b), GraphSAGE (Hamilton
418   et al., 2017), and Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017). We import these models directly from PyTorch
419   Geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019) and PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019). TopoTune enables running
420   GCCNs on both an ensemble of strictly augmented Hasse graphs (eq. 5) and a single augmented
421   Hasse graph (eq. 4). While CCNN results reflect extensive hyperparameter tuning by Telyatnikov
422   et al. (2024), we fix GCCN training hyperparameters using the TopoBenchmark default configuration.
423
424   Datasets. We include a wide range of benchmark tasks (see Appendix E.2) commonly used in the
425   graph and topological domains. MUTAG, PROTEINS, NCI01, and NCI09 (Morris et al., 2020) are
426
      graph-level classification tasks about molecules or proteins. ZINC (Irwin et al., 2012) (subset) is a
      graph-level regression task related to molecular solubility. At the node level, the Cora, CiteSeer, and
427
      PubMed tasks (Yang et al., 2016) involve classifying publications (nodes) within citation networks.
428
      We consider two cases of combinatorial complexes, simplicial and cellular complexes. We leverage
429   TopoBenchmark’s data lifting processes to infer higher-order relationships in these datasets. We only
430   use node features to construct edge and face features.

459   GCCNs outperform CCNNs. Table 1 portrays a cross-comparison between top-performing CCNN
460   models and our class of GCCNs. GCCNs outperform CCNNs in the simplicial and cellular domains
461   across all datasets. Notably, GCCNs in these domains achieve comparable results to hypergraph
462   CCNNs, a feat unattainable by existing CCNNs in node-level tasks. Out of the 16 domain/dataset
463   combinations considered in our experiments, GCCNs outperform the best counterpart CCNN by > 1
464   in 11 cases. Evidence supports that GCCN’s architectural novelties contribute to this performance: (i)
465
      Representing complexes as ensembles of augmented Hasse graphs, rather than a single augmented
      Hasse graph, consistently improves results (Table 1). (ii) Some GCCNs with per-rank neighborhood
466
      structures outperform not only CCNNs but also other GCCNs with regular neighborhoods. For
467
      example, on MUTAG, a cellular GCCN with a lightweight, per-rank neighborhood structure makes it
468   19% the size of the best cellular CCNN on this task.
469
470
471
      GCCNs perform competitively to CCNNs with fewer parameters. GCCNs are generally more
      parameter efficient than existing CCNNs in simplicial and cellular domains, and in some instances
472
      (MUTAG, NCI1, NCI09), even in the hypergraph domain. Even as GCCNs become more resource-
473
      intensive for large graphs with high-dimensional embeddings—as seen in node-level tasks—they
474   maintain a competitive edge. For instance, on the Citeseer dataset, a GCCN (!N = GraphSAGE)
475   outperforms the best existing CCNN while being 28% smaller. We refer to Table 4. Training times
476   provided in Appendix G show that GCCNs train at comparable speeds on smaller datasets, and slow
477   down for larger datasets, most likely due to TopoTune’s on-the-fly graph expansion. In future work,
478   we expect that performing this expansion during preprocessing will address this lag.

      7      C ONCLUSION
532   This work introduces a simple yet powerful graph-based methodology for constructing Generalized
533   Combinatorial Complex Neural Networks (GCCNs), TDL architectures that generalize and subsume
534   standard CCNNs. Additionally, we introduce TopoTune, the first lightweight software module for
535   systematically and easily implementing new TDL architectures across many topological domains. In
536   doing so, we have addressed, either in part or in full, 7 of the 11 open problems of the field defined
537
      by some of its leaders in Papamarkou et al. (2024). Future work includes customizing GCCNs for
      application-specific and potentially sparse or multimodal datasets, and leveraging software from
538
      state-of-the-art GNNs. We hope TopoTune will also help bridge the gap with other fields such as
539
      attentional learning and k-hop higher-order GNNs (Morris et al., 2019; Maron et al., 2019).
