                                                   A BSTRACT
012
               Cognitive abilities, such as Theory of Mind (ToM), play a vital role in facilitating
013
               cooperation in human social interactions. However, Large Language Model (LLM)
014
               agents with higher ToM abilities do not necessarily exhibit better cooperative
015            trends compared to those with lower ToM abilities, highlighting the complexity
016            of translating human cognitive processes to artificial agents. To address this
017            challenge, we propose a novel matching coalition mechanism that leverages the
018            strengths of agents with different ToM levels by explicitly considering belief
019            alignment and specialized abilities when forming coalitions. Our proposed stable
020            coalition formation algorithm seeks to find the team that maximizes the potential
               for cooperative trends and ensures long-term viability. By incorporating cognitive
021
               insights into the design of multi-agent systems, our work demonstrates the potential
022
               of leveraging ToM to create more sophisticated and human-like coordination
023            strategies that foster cooperation and improve overall system performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Cooperation is a fundamental aspect of multi-agent systems, enabling agents to work together
028   effectively to achieve common goals and solve complex problems (Shenoy, 1979). In recent years, the
029   rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has opened up new opportunities for building
030   intelligent multi-agent systems. LLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language
031   understanding, generation, and reasoning, such as GPT-family (Eloundou et al., 2023). By leveraging
032   these powerful LLMs, multi-agent systems can enable agents to communicate and collaborate using
033   natural language, resulting in more flexible and human-like interactions in cooperation tasks.
034   To foster cooperation among LLM-based agents, most recent research focused on communication
035   and knowledge sharing among agents (Xu et al., 2023; Lan et al., 2023; Hua et al., 2023; Wu
036   et al., 2023b; Nascimento et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023). These studies demonstrate the potential
037
      of leveraging natural language capabilities to facilitate information exchange and coordination
      in multi-agent systems. Besides language understanding, some research has explored effective
038
      cooperation through agent cognitive abilities, such as reasoning and reflection, to coordinate actions
039   and make decisions (Qi & Vul, 2020; Li et al., 2023b; Liang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). These
040   studies highlight the importance of investigating how we can foster cooperation in LLM-based
041   multi-agent systems from a cognitive perspective.
042   One of the fundamental cognitive abilities is the Theory of Mind (ToM). By utilizing ToM in strategic
043   interactions, an agent can mentally simulate others’ thoughts and potential actions. Furthermore,
044   ToM can involve multiple levels of recursive belief attribution, known as higher-level ToM, where
045   players consider not only their opponent’s beliefs but also their beliefs about the other player’s beliefs,
046   and so on (Premack & Woodruff, 1978). In other words, ToM allows one to see things from others’
047
      perspective. In human social interactions, ToM plays a crucial role in facilitating cooperation by
      enabling individuals to understand and predict the behavior of others (Yoshida et al., 2008). For
048
      example, in a chess game, a player with higher-level ToM might think, “I believe my opponent thinks
049   that I will move my knight, so I will move my bishop instead, because I believe they will not expect
050   that move." Normally, in a specific scenario, agents with higher level ToM can better understand and
051   predict the actions of other agents leading to improved cooperation and coordination (Street, 2024).
052   Based on these insights, there is a growing interest in leveraging ToM in LLMs to enhance multi-
053   agent cooperation (Guo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), where ToM plays a vital role in facilitating
      the coordination of actions and the resolution of conflicts in cooperation. Recent research has

      highlighted the importance of accurate mutual understanding for effective cooperation in complex
055   environments (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Chan et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wu et al.,

060   To further identify the relationship between ToM ability and cooperative trend, we investigate the
061   cooperative trend of agents with high and low ToM abilities. However, our result reveals that agents

      conflicts, resulting in more cautious cooperation. This finding aligns with the psychological research
065
      by (Ridinger & McBride, 2017), which suggests that ToM capabilities alone are not sufficient to
066   guarantee good cooperation. Instead, agents may also need to be willing to positively reciprocate
067   and cooperate with others. Specifically, Ridinger & McBride (2017) highlights that when ToM
068   abilities are combined with a high proportion of individuals who are willing to engage in cooperation,
069   it can lead to improved cooperation within the group. This insight motivates our proposed approach
070   of incorporating belief alignment into the coalition formation process to promote cooperation
071   among agents with high cognitive abilities.
072   In this work, we propose a novel matching coalition mechanism to find coalitions that foster coopera-
073   tion and leverage the strengths of agents by explicitly considering belief alignment as captured by
074   their ToM capabilities. By forming coalitions with diverse ToM levels, we aim to create coalitions
075
      that can effectively reason about each other’s mental states while leveraging their unique strengths
      to solve complex problems. Moreover, our matching algorithm allows for stable matching, which
076
      ensures the long-term viability and effectiveness of the formed coalitions.

154   3      M OTIVATION
155
156   Theory of Mind (ToM) in multi-agent cooperation has been studied recently and agents with higher
157   ToM capabilities are generally expected to be more effective in understanding and predicting the
158   actions of others, which leads to improved cooperation and coordination. However, the relationship
159   between ToM ability and cooperative trend in multi-agent systems is not fully understood, and there
160   are still open questions regarding the impact of ToM ability on cooperation.
161   In this study, we employ a standard ToM model aligned with current research (Zhou et al., 2023;
      Street, 2024; Xu et al., 2024) to understand ToM in multi-agent cooperation. In practice, ToM


                                                             3
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
       typically does not exceed second-order reasoning due to cognitive limitations and diminishing
163    returns (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Frith & Frith, 2003). We consider two levels of ToM ability,
164    aligning with existing literature in both human cognitive research (De Weerd et al., 2015) and agent
165    cognitive modeling (Li et al., 2023c): Low ToM (Level 1), where agents can represent and reason
166    about others’ beliefs, desires, and intentions; and High ToM (Level 2), where agents can additionally
167    consider others’ ToM reasoning.
168    Higher-level ToM capabilities do not necessarily guarantee better cooperative trends. Accurate
169    mutual understanding is crucial for effective cooperation in complex multi-agent environments.
170    Recent research has explored communication for knowledge sharing (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al.,
171    2023a) and aligning agent beliefs with teammates (Chan et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wu
172
       et al., 2024), demonstrating that improved alignment can facilitate more informed decision-making
       and potentially lead to more cooperative outcomes. Motivated by these insights, we introduce the
173
      “Fraction of Trusted Members" (FTM) metric to quantify the cooperative trend (detailed in Section 6).
174    FTM measures the alignment between the Project Manager’s (PM’s) beliefs about the engineer agents’
175    actions and their actual actions, representing the proportion of engineer agents whose actions are
176    correctly anticipated by the PM. Additionally, we present comprehensive evaluations across multiple
177    performance metrics and tasks in Appendix F, which further support our findings.
178    To investigate the relationship between ToM and cooperative trend in multi-agent systems, we first
179    simulate the multi-agent system consisting of one project manager (PM) and four engineer agents
180    working together to solve a programming task, as shown in Figure 1 (Left). Specifically, PM is
181    enabled with ToM ability, which means he can recursively infer the actions of other agents (as beliefs)
182    and take his own actions accordingly, as illustrated Figure 1 (Right). Then, we investigate the multi-
183
       agent cooperation with iterative programming tasks (five rounds) on two benchmarks, H UMAN E VAL
       (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021). In Appendix F, we also investigate side effects
184
       of incorporating higher ToM in some state-of-art methods.

       may overthink and anticipate potential conflicts, resulting in more cautious cooperation. To leverage

334   6.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
335
      The experiment setup involves extending the MetaGPT framework (Hong et al., 2024) to incorporate
336
      the proposed multi-agent LLM cooperation mechanism. LLM agents will have varying levels of
337   ToM capabilities, including 1-level, and 2-level ToM. We evaluate our proposed coalition mechanism
338   on the following cooperative tasks:
339    • Iterative Programming: In this task, agents simulate a software development team, with each
340      agent assuming different roles. We use H UMAN E VAL (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP (Austin
341      et al., 2021) as benchmarks to evaluate the performance and cooperative trend of ToM agents in
342      this multi-agent environment.
343    • Debate: In a debate setting, agents are divided into two sides (affirmative and negative) and engage
344      in a multi-round debate on a given topic. The agents collaborate within their respective sides to

      7     L IMIATION , F UTURE W ORK , AND C ONCLUSION
527
528   Limitations and Future Work . In this paper, we propose a novel coalition matching for fostering
529   cooperation among LLM-based agents with ToM, demonstrating its effectiveness in enhancing co-
530   operative trends. However, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of our approach. Firstly,
531   LLMs may have limited reasoning ability despite their impressive language capabilities. Addi-
532   tionally, coalition formation is generally an NP-hard problem, and future research can explore
533   potential optimizations to reduce time complexity. Furthermore, future research should investigate
534
      the incorporation of additional cognitive architectures, e.g. more advanced ToM models.
535   Conclusion. In this work, we investigated the relationship between cognitive abilities (ToM) and
536   cooperative trends in LLM-based multi-agent systems. Our findings suggest that the relationship
537   between ToM and cooperation is not always straightforward. Furthermore, we proposed a novel
538   matching coalition mechanism incorporating cognitive insights into the design of multi-agent systems.
539   By incorporating cognitive insights into the design of coordination mechanisms, we pave the way for
      exploring the translation of cognitive abilities into cooperative actions in multi-agent settings.

835   Remarks. For LLM agents, our evaluation relies on semantic similarity analysis of natural language
836   outputs. While we acknowledge the inherent limitations of LLM-based measurements, our coalition
837   formation mechanism is a plug-and-play approach for improving multi-agent cooperation rather than
838   advancing LLM evaluation methods.
839   Besides, we provide an extension of our framework to non-LLM agents where belief-action alignment
840   can be more precisely quantified. For traditional reinforcement learning or robotic agents, we can
841   define the belief-action alignment using structured trajectories τ = {(st , at )}Tt=1 , where st and
842   at represent states and actions at time step t. The alignment score between agent i’s belief bki (τj )
843   about agent j’s trajectory and j’s actual trajectory τj can be computed using established trajectory
      similarity metrics. Specifically, we can embed the state-action pairs using domain-specific feature
844
      extractors ϕ(s, a) and measure alignment through cosine similarity: Aki,j = cos(ϕ(bki (τj )), ϕ(τj )).

      This provides a more rigorous quantitative foundation for evaluating belief-action alignment in non-
846   language-based multi-agent systems while maintaining the core principles of our coalition formation
