                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009            In order to sample from an unnormalized probability density function, we propose
010            to combine continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) with rejection-resampling steps
011            based on importance weights. We relate the iterative training of CNFs with regular-
012            ized velocity fields to a JKO scheme and prove convergence of the involved velocity
013            fields to the velocity field of the Wasserstein gradient flow (WGF). The alternation
014            of local flow steps and non-local rejection-resampling steps allows to overcome
015            local minima or slow convergence of the WGF for multimodal distributions. Since
016            the proposal of the rejection step is generated by the model itself, they do not suffer
017
               from common drawbacks of classical rejection schemes. The arising model can be
               trained iteratively, reduces the reverse Kullback-Leibler (KL) loss function in each
018
               step, allows to generate iid samples and moreover allows for evaluations of the
019
               generated underlying density. Numerical examples show that our method yields ac-
020            curate results on various test distributions including high-dimensional multimodal
021            targets and outperforms the state of the art in almost all cases significantly.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   We consider the problem of sampling from an unnormalized probability density function. That
027   is, we are given an integrable function g : Rd → R>0 and we aim to generate samples from the
028           R distribution ν given by the density q(x) = g(x)/Zg , where the normalizing constant
      probability
      Zg = Rd g(x)dx is unknown. Many classical sampling methods are based on Markov chain
029
      Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods like the overdamped Langevin sampling, see, e.g., Welling & Teh
030
      (2011). The generated probability path of the underlying stochastic differential equation follows
031   the Wasserstein-2 gradient flow of the reverse KL divergence F(µ) = KL(µ, ν). Over the last
032   years, generative models like normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015) or diffusion models
033   (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) became more popular for sampling, see, e.g., Phillips et al.
034   (2024); Vargas et al. (2023a). Also these methods are based on the reverse KL divergence as a
035   loss function. While generative models have successfully been applied in data-driven setups, their
036   application to the problem of sampling from arbitrary unnormalized densities is not straightforward.
037   This difficulty arises from the significantly harder nature of the problem, even in moderate dimensions,
038   particularly when dealing with target distributions that exhibit phenomena such as concentration
039
      effects, multimodalities, heavy tails, or other issues related to the curse of dimensionality.
040   In particular, the reverse KL is non-convex in the Wasserstein space as soon as the target density
041   ν is not log-concave which is for example the case when ν consists of multiple modes. In this
042   case generative models often collapse to one or a small number of modes. We observe that for
043   continuous normalizing flows (CNFs, Chen et al., 2018; Grathwohl et al., 2019) this can be prevented
044   by regularizing the L2 -norm of the velocity field as proposed under the name OT-flow by Onken
045
      et al. (2021). In particular, this regularization converts the objective functional into a convex one.
      However, the minimizer of the regularized loss function is no longer given by the target measure ν
046
      but by the Wasserstein proximal mapping of the objective function applied onto the latent distribution.
047
      Considering that the Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto (JKO) scheme (Jordan et al., 1998) iteratively applies
048   the Wasserstein proximal mapping and converges to the Wasserstein gradient flow, several papers
049   proposed to approximate the steps of the scheme by generative models, see Altekrüger et al. (2023);
050   Alvarez-Melis et al. (2022); Fan et al. (2022); Lambert et al. (2022); Mokrov et al. (2021); Vidal et al.
051   (2023); Xu et al. (2024). We will refer to this class of method by the name neural JKO. Even though
052   this approximates the same gradient flow of the Langevin dynamics these approaches have usually
053   the advantage of faster inference (once they are trained) and additional possibly allow for density
      evaluations.

      However, already the time-continuous Wasserstein gradient flow suffers from the non-convexity of
074   the reverse KL loss function by getting stuck in local minima or by very slow convergence times.
075   In particular, it is well-known that Langevin-based sampling methods often do not distribute the
076   mass correctly onto multimodal target distributions. As a remedy, Neal (2001) proposed importance
077   sampling, i.e., to reweight the sample based on quotients of the target distribution and its current
078   approximation which leads to an unbiased estimator of the Monte Carlo integral. However, this
079   estimator may lead to highly imbalanced weights between these samples and the resulting estimator
080   might have a large variance. Moreover, the density of the current approximation has to be known up
081   to a possible multiplicative constant, which is not the case for many MCMC methods like Langevin
082
      sampling or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo. In Del Moral et al. (2006) the authors propose a scheme for
      alternating importance sampling steps with local Monte Carlo steps. However, as a downside this
083
      strategy leads to samples which might not be iid.
084
085   Contributions In this paper, we propose a sampling method which combines neural JKO steps
086   based on CNFs with importance based rejection steps. While the CNFs adjust the position of the
087   generated samples locally, the rejection steps readjusts the inferred distribution non-locally based on
088
      the quotient of generated and target distribution. Then, in each rejection step we resample the rejected
      points based on the current constructed generative model. We illustrate this procedure in Figure 1.
089
090   Our methods generates independent samples and allows to evaluate the density of the generated
091   distribution. In our numerical examples, we apply our method to common test distributions up to the
092   dimension d = 1600 which are partially highly multimodal. We show that our importance corrected
093   neural JKO sampling (neural JKO IC) achieves significantly better results than the comparisons1 .
094   From a theoretical side, we prove that the velocity fields from a sequence of neural JKO steps strongly
095   converges to the velocity field of the corresponding Wasserstein gradient flow and that the reverse KL
096   loss function decreases throughout the importance-based rejection steps.
097
      Outline The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we recall the fundamental concepts
098   which will be required. Afterwards, we consider neural JKO schemes more detailed in Section 3.
099   We introduce our importance-based rejection steps in Section 4. Finally, we evaluate our model
100   numerically and compare it to existing methods in Section 5. Conclusions are drawn in Section 6.
101   Additionally, proofs, further numerical and technical details are presented in Appendix A- E.

110   2011; Rossky et al., 1978; Roberts & Tweedie, 1996) are heavily used in practice. The viewpoint

      samples requires to rerun the whole procedure which can be very costly.

120   Generative Models In the last years, generative models became very popular, including VAEs
121   (Kingma & Welling, 2014), normalizing flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015), diffusion models (Ho
122   et al., 2020) or flow-matching (Lipman et al., 2022) which is also known as rectified flow (Liu
123   et al., 2022). In contrast to our setting, they initially consider the modeling task, i.e., they assume
124
      that they are given samples from the target measure instead of an unnormalized density. However,
      there are several papers, which adapt these algorithms for the sampling task. For normalizing flows,
125
      this mostly amounts to changing the loss function (Hoffman et al., 2019; Marzouk et al., 2016;
126
      Qiu & Wang, 2024). Very recently, there appeared also a flow-matching variant for the sampling
127   task (Woo & Ahn, 2024). For diffusion (and stochastic control) models this was done based on
128   variational approaches (Blessing et al., 2024; Phillips et al., 2024; Vargas et al., 2023a;b; Zhang
129   & Chen, 2021) or by computing the score by solving a PDE (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2024;
130   Richter & Berner, 2024; Sommer et al., 2024). These methods usually provide much faster sampling
131   times than MCMC methods and are often used in combination with some conditioning parameter for
132   inverse problems, where a (generative) prior is combined with a known likelihood term (Ardizzone
133   et al., 2019; Altekrüger & Hertrich, 2023; Andrle et al., 2021; Denker et al., 2024). Combinations
134   of generative models with stochastic sampling steps were considered in the literature for generative
135
      modeling under the name stochastic normalizing flows (Hagemann et al., 2023; 2022; Noé et al.,
      2019; Wu et al., 2020) and for sampling under the name annealed flow transport Monte Carlo (Arbel
136
      et al., 2021; Matthews et al., 2022). Gabrié et al. (2022) use normalizing flows to learn proposal
137
      distributions in an Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. Additionally, flow-based generative sampling
138   algorithms can be combined with one final importance sampling step, which is done in many of these
139   references.
140
141
      Generative Models with Wasserstein Gradient Flows These generative models can be adapted to
      follow a Wasserstein gradient flow, by mimicking a JKO scheme with generative models or directly
142
      follow the velocity field of a kernel-based functional. Such approaches were proposed for generative
143
      modeling (Fan et al., 2022; Hagemann et al., 2024; Hertrich et al., 2024; Liutkus et al., 2019; Vidal
144   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), sampling (Fan et al., 2022; Lambert et al., 2022; Liu & Wang, 2016;
145   Mokrov et al., 2021) or other tasks (Altekrüger et al., 2023; Arbel et al., 2019; Alvarez-Melis et al.,
146   2022).

      2.2   C ONTINUOUS N ORMALIZING F LOWS AND OT-F LOWS

223   The concept of normalizing flows first appeared in Rezende & Mohamed (2015). It follows the
224   basic idea to approximate a probability distribution ν by considering a simple latent distribution
225   µ0 (usually a standard Gaussian) and to construct a diffeomorphism Tθ : Rd → Rd depending on
226   some parameters θ such that ν ≈ Tθ # µ0 . In practice, the diffeomorphism can be approximated by
227   coupling-based neural networks (Dinh et al., 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018), residual architectures
228   (Behrmann et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Hertrich, 2023) or autoregressive flows (De Cao et al.,
229   2020; Durkan et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2018; Papamakarios et al., 2017). In this paper, we mainly
230
      focus on continuous normalizing flows proposed by Chen et al. (2018); Grathwohl et al. (2019),
      see also Ruthotto & Haber (2021) for an overview. Here the diffeomorphism Tθ is parameterized
231
      as neural ODE. To this end let vθ : Rd × R → Rd be a neural network with parameters θ and let
232
      zθ : Rd × [0, τ ] → Rd for fixed τ > 0 be the solution of żθ = vθ , with initial condition zθ (x, 0) = x.
233   Then, we define Tθ via the solution zθ as Tθ (x) = zθ (x, τ ).

      We compare our method with classical Monte Carlo samplers like a Metropolis adjusted Langevin
463   sampling (MALA) and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), see e.g., Betancourt (2017); Roberts &
464   Tweedie (1996). Additionally, we compare with two recent deep-learning based sampling algorithms,
465   namely denoising diffusion samplers (DDS, Vargas et al., 2023a) and continual repeated annealed
466   flow transport Monte Carlo (CRAFT, Matthews et al., 2022). We evaluate all methods on a set of
467   common test distributions which is described in detail in Appendix E.1. Moreover, we report the
468   error measures for our importance corrected neural JKO sampler (neural JKO IC), see Appendix E.4
469   for implementation details. Additionally, we emphasize the importance of the rejection steps by
470   reporting values for the same a neural JKO scheme without rejection steps (neural JKO).
471   For evaluating the quality of our results, we use two different metrics. First, we evaluate the energy
472   distance (Székely, 2002). It is a kernel metrics which can be evaluated purely based on two sets
473   of samples from the model and the ground truth. Moreover it encodes the geometry of the space
474   such that a slight perturbation of the samples only leads to a slight change in the energy distance.
475   Second, we estimate the log-normalizing constant which is equivalent to approximating the reverse
476   KL loss of the model. A higher estimate of the log-normalizing constant corresponds to a smaller KL
477
      divergence between generated and target distribution and therefore to a higher similarity of the two
      measures. Since this requires the density of the model, this approach is not applicable for MALA
478
      and HMC. The results are given in Table 1 and 2. We can see, that importance corrected neural
479
      JKO sampling significantly outperforms the comparison for all test distributions. In particular, we
480   observe that for shifted 8 modes, shifted 8 peaky and GMM-d the energy distance between neural
481   JKO IC and ground truth samples is in the same order of magnitude as the energy distance between to
482   different sets of ground truth samples. This implies that the distribution generated by neural JKO IC
483   is indistinguishable from the target distribution in the energy distance. For these examples, the log(Z)
484   esitmate is sometimes slightly larger than the ground truth, which can be explained by numerical
485   effects, see Remark 16 for a detailed discussion. We point out to a precise description of the metrics,
      the implementation details and additional experiments and figures in Appendix E.

511   6        C ONCLUSIONS
512
513   Methodology We proposed a novel and expressive generative method that enables the efficient and
514   accurate sampling from a prescribed unnormalized target density which is empirically confirmed in
515   numerical examples. To this end, we combine local sampling steps, relying on piecewise geodesic
516
      interpolations of the JKO scheme realized by CNFs, and non-local rejection and resampling steps
      based on importance weights. Since the proposal of the rejection step is generated by the model
517
      itself, they do not suffer from the curse of dimensionality as opposed to classical variants of rejection
518
      sampling. The proposed approach provides the advantage that we can draw independent samples
519   while correcting imbalanced mode weights, iteratively refine the current approximation and evaluate
520   the density of the generated distribution. The latter property is a consequence of the density value
521   propagation through CNFs and Proposition 8 and enables possible further post-processing steps that
522   require density evaluations of the approximated sample process.
523
      Outlook Our method allows for the pointwise access to the approximated target density and
524
      the log normalization constant. These quantities can be used for the error monitoring during the
525   training and hence provide guidelines for the adaptive design of the emulator in terms of CNF -or
526   rejection/resampling steps and provide a straightforward stopping criterion.
527
528
      Limitations In the situation, when the emulator is realized through a stack of underlying rejec-
      tion/resampling steps, the sample generation process time is negatively affected, see Remark 11. In
529
      order to resolve the drawback we plan to utilize diffusion models for the sample generation. This
530
      is part of ongoing and future work by the authors. Finally, the use of continuous normalizing flows
531   comes with computational challenges, which we discuss in detail in Appendix F.2.

1215
       We evaluate our method on the following test distributions.
1216
1217          - Mustache: The two-dimensional log-density is given as log N (0, Σ) ◦ T with Σ =
1218            [1, σ; σ, 1] and T (x1 , x2 ) = (x1 , (x2 − (x21 − 1)2 )). Note that det(∇T (x)) = 1 for
1219            all x. In particular, we obtain directly by the transformation formula that the normalization
1220            constant is one. Depending on σ ∈ [0, 1) close to 1, this probability distribution has very
1221            long and narrow tails making it hard for classical MCMC methods to sample them. In our
1222            experiments we use σ = 0.9.
1223          - Shifted 8 Modes: A two-dimensional Gaussian mixture model with 8 equal weighted modes
1224            and covariance matrix 1 × 10−2 I. The modes are placed in a circle with radius 1 and center
1225            (−1, 0). Due to the shifted center classical MCMC methods have difficulties to distribute

1488   F.1     M ULTIMODALITIES , M ODE C OLLAPSE AND N ON -C ONVEX L OSS F UNCTIONS
1489
       For the sampling application, normalizing flows are usually trained with the reverse KL loss function
1490   F(µ) = KL(µ, ν), see, e.g., Marzouk et al. (2016). More precisely, a normalizing flow aims to learn
1491   the parameters θ of a family of diffeomorphisms Tθ such that Tθ # µ0 ≈ ν by minimizing F(Tθ # µ0 ).
1492   In the case that ν is multimodal it can be observed that this training mode collapses. That is, the
1493   approximation T# µ0 covers not all of the modes of ν but instead neglects some of them. Examples
1494   of this phenomenon can be seen in the numerical comparison in Appendix F.3. One reason for this
1495   effect is that the functional F is convex if and only if ν is log-concave and therefore unimodal, see
1496   (Ambrosio et al., 2005, Prop. 9.3.2). In particular, for multimodal ν, the functional F is non-convex.
1497   In the latter case of, then the mode collapses can correspond to the convergence to a local minimum,
1498
       as the following example shows.
1499   Example 17. We consider the case d = 1 and the target distribution
1500                                               ν = 21 N (− 12 , 0.052 ) + 12 N ( 12 , 0.052 ).
1501
       As latent distribution µ0 we choose a standard Gaussian. Then, we parametrize the normalizing flow
1502
       Tθ = (1 − θ)T0 + θT1 for θ ∈ [0, 1], where T0 is the optimal transport map between µ0 and ν and T1
1503   is the optimal transport map between µ0 and N ( 21 , 0.052 ). In particular, Tθ # µ0 perfectly recovers
1504   the target distribution for θ = 0 and produces a mode collapsed version of it for θ = 1. Now, we plot
1505   the reverse KL loss function L(θ) = KL(Tθ # µ0 , ν) and the densities of the generated distributions
1506   Tθ # µ0 in Figure 11. We observe that it has two local minima for θ = 0 and θ = 1 (for θ outside
1507   of [0, 1], T is no longer invertible), where θ = 0 is the perfectly learned parameter and θ = 1 is
1508   the mode collapsed version. At the same time, we note that the curve θ 7→ Tθ # µ0 is a geodesic in
1509   the Wasserstein space. In particular, the non-convexity of L is a direct consequence of the fact that
1510   F(µ) = KL(µ, ν) is geodesically non-convex.
1511   Remark 18 (Motivation of Wasserstein Regularization). This connection between the non-convexity
       of the loss function F and mode collapse also motivates the Wasserstein regularization from Section 3.

       F.2   C OMPUTATIONAL L IMITATIONS OF (C ONTINUOUS ) N ORMALIZING F LOWS
1523
1524   Similar to the literature on neural JKO schemes (Vidal et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), our implemen-
1525   tation of the neural JKO scheme relies on continuous normalizing flows (Chen et al., 2018). This
1526   comes with some limitations and challenges, which were extensively discussed in (Chen et al., 2018).
1527   Since they are relevant for our method, we give a synopsis below.
1528
1529   Derivatives of ODE Solutions For the training phase we need the derivative of the solution of
1530   an ODE. For this, we use the torchdiffeq package (Chen, 2018). In particular, this package
1531   does not rely backpropagation through the steps from the forward solver. Instead, it overwrites the
1532
       backward pass of the ODE by solving an adjoint ODE. This avoids expensive tracing in the automatic
       differentiation process within the forward pass and keeps the memory consumption low. Moreover,
1533
       the quadratic regularization of the velocity field leads to straight paths such that the adaptive solvers
1534
       only require a few steps for solving the ODE, see (Onken et al., 2021) for a detailed discussion.
1535   Indeed, we use in our numerical examples the dopri5 solver from torchdiffeq, which is an
1536   adaptive Runge-Kutta method. However, we still have to solve two ODEs during the training time.
1537   This still can be computational costly, in particular when the underlying model is large.
1538
1539   Trace Estimation for Density Computations In order to evaluate the (log-)density of our model,
1540   we have to compute the divergence div vθ = trace(∇vθ (zθ (x, t), t)) of the learned vector field vθ
1541   (and integrate it over time), see (6). Computing the trace of the Jacobian of vθ becomes computational
1542   costly in high dimensions. As a remedy, we consider the Hutchinson trace estimator (Hutchinson,
1543   1989), which states that for any matrix A and a random vector z with mean zero and identity
1544   covariance matrix it holds that E[z T Az] = trace(A). Applying this estimator to the divergence,
1545
       we obtain that the divergence coincides with E[z T ∇vθ (zθ (x, t), t)z]. The integrand is now again
       a Jacobian-vector product which can be computed efficiently. Finally, we estimate the trace by
1546
       empirically discretizing the expectation by finitely many realizations of z.
1547
1548   In our numerics, we choose z to be Rademacher random vectors, i.e. each entry is with probability
       1
1549   2 either −1 or 1. For the training of the continuous normalizing flow, an unbiased low-precision
1550   estimator is sufficient, such that we discretize the expectation with one realization of z. During
1551
       evaluation, we require a higher precision and use 5 realizations instead.
1552
1553
       Initialization In order to find a stable initialization of the model, we initialize the last layer of the
       velocity field vθ with zeros such that it holds vθ (x, t) = 0 for all x ∈ Rd , t ∈ [0, τ ]. In this case, the
1554
       solution zθ of the ODE żθ = vθ with initial condition zθ (x, 0) = x is given by zθ (x, t) = x for all
1555
       t. In particular, we have that the transport map Tθ = zθ (x, τ ) is the identity such that the generated
1556   distribution Tθ # µ0 coincides with the latent distribution for the initial parameters.
1557
       Remark 19 (Other Normalizing Flow Architectures). In general any architecture Tθ of normalizing
1558
       flows can be considered in the neural R JKO scheme. To this end, we can replace the velocity field
1559
       regularization in (4) by the penalizer Rd ∥Tθ (x)−x∥2 dµkτ . By Breniers’ theorem (Brenier, 1987) this
1560   is again equivalent to minimizing the Wasserstein distance. However, while discrete-time normalizing
1561   flows like coupling-based networks (Dinh et al., 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018) and autoregressive
1562   flows (De Cao et al., 2020; Durkan et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2018; Papamakarios et al., 2017) are
1563   often faster than CNFs, this is not generally true in our setting, since their evaluation time does not
1564   benefit from the OT-regularization. Additionally, we observed numerically that the expressiveness
1565   of discrete-time architectures scale much worse to high dimensions and are less stable to train.
       Nevertheless, the evaluation can be cheaper and the density evaluation since these architectures
