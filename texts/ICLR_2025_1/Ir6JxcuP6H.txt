009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present REM (Refer Everything Model), a framework for segmenting a wide
012            range of concepts in video that can be described through natural language. To
013            achieve this level of generalization, our method capitalizes on visual-language
014            representations learned by video diffusion models on Internet-scale datasets. A
015            key insight of our approach is preserving as much of the generative model’s orig-
016
               inal representation as possible, while fine-tuning it on narrow-domain Referring
               Object Segmentation datasets. As a result, despite being exclusively trained on
017
               object masks from a limited set of categories, our framework is able to accurately
018
               segment and track both rare, unseen objects and non-object, dynamic concepts,
019            such as waves crashing in the ocean. To better quantify the generalization capa-
020            bilities of our model, we introduce a new benchmark for Referring Video Process
021            Segmentation (RVPS), which captures dynamic phenomena that exist at the inter-
022            section of video and language. Our experiments show that REM performs com-
023            parably to state-of-the-art approaches on in-domain datasets while outperforming
024            them by up to 28% out-of-domain, leveraging the power of Internet-scale pre-
025            training. We include all of the video visualizations at this anonymous page.

      One of the most remarkable features of natural language is its ability to describe human visual
030   experience in all of its richness and complexity. Whether capturing fleeting moments, like raindrops
031   rolling down the window, or smoke dissipating from a cigarette (see row 2 in Figure 1), or describing
032   dynamic processes, such as a glass shattering or a whirlpool forming in the water (row 1 in Figure 1),
033   if we can utter them, we can also accurately localize them in space and time. This universal mapping
034   between the discrete, symbolic realm of language and the continuous, ever-changing visual world is
035   developed through a lifetime of visual-linguistic interaction (Barsalou, 1999; Popham et al., 2021).
036   The corresponding problem in computer vision - Referring Video Segmentation (RVS) (Gavrilyuk
037   et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2016), is defined as the task of segmenting a specific region in a video based
038   on a natural language description. However, virtually all existing benchmarks and methods focus on
039   a specific subset of RVS - Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS) (Seo et al., 2020; Wu et al.,
040   2022a), where the goal is to track and segment the object referenced by a given expression. Why
041   has the field concentrated so narrowly on this task? Although multiple factors contribute, we argue
042   that the primary reason lies in the data. Historically, RVOS datasets have been developed by adding
043   referring expression annotations to existing object tracking benchmarks (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017; Xu
044
      et al., 2018), which are inherently object-centric and limited in scale.
045   At the same time, recent advances in Internet-scale datasets with billions of paired image- and
046   video-language samples (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Bain et al., 2021) have opened new possibilities.
047   These datasets have been used to train powerful denoising diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022;
048   Wang et al., 2023), and provide excellent representations of the natural visual-language manifold.
049   In the image domain, numerous studies have shown that re-purposing diffusion models can yield
050
      highly generalizable representations of object shapes (Zhao et al., 2023; Ozguroglu et al., 2024).
      Very recently, Zhu et al. (2024) explored the application of video diffusion models for referring
051
      segmentation, but their approach exhibited limited generalization capabilities.
052
053   In this work, we introduce a novel approach to RVS that leverages large-scale video-language repre-
      sentations learned by diffusion models. Our method, described in Section 3 and shown in Figure 3,

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Referring Video Segmentation (RVS) involves segmenting specific regions in a video based on
135   a natural language description (Gavrilyuk et al., 2018; Khoreva et al., 2019; Seo et al., 2020).
136   Most benchmarks for this task were developed by adding referring expression annotations to ex-
137   isting Video Object Segmentation (VOS) datasets, such as DAVIS’17 (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017) or
138   YouTube-VOS (Xu et al., 2018). Consequently, the role of language in these benchmarks is limited
139
      to providing an interface for user-initialized object tracking (Wu et al., 2013; Perazzi et al., 2016).
      While this specific task — Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS) — is valuable, it addresses
140
      only a narrow subset of the possible interactions between language and the space-time continuum
141
      of videos. Equally important is the ability of RVS methods to segment video concepts beyond com-
142   mon object categories. To address this gap, we introduce a new benchmark focused on segmenting
143   dynamic processes, which we term Referring Video Process Segmentation (RVPS).
144
145
      Earlier RVOS approaches (Bellver et al., 2020; Ning et al., 2020; Hui et al., 2021) generally em-
      ployed a bottom-up strategy: first, image-level methods (Rother et al., 2004; Ye et al., 2019; Carion
146
      et al., 2020; Plummer et al., 2015) were applied to obtain frame-level masks, followed by spatio-
147
      temporal reasoning, such as mask propagation (Seo et al., 2020), to refine the segmentation across
148   frames. More recently, with the success of cross-attention-based methods (Vaswani, 2017; Mein-
149   hardt et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2022) in object segmentation and tracking, query-based architectures
150   have been introduced to RVOS, leading to significant improvements, with ReferFormer (Wu et al.,
151   2022a) and MUTR (Yan et al., 2024) being notable examples. The limited scale of paired video-
152   language data with segmentation annotations has always been a major limitation in RVOS, causing
153   most methods to train jointly on video and image samples (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014; Jhuang et al.,
154   2013). The latest approaches go even further and unify all object localization datasets and tasks in a
155   single framework to maximize the amount of training data (Yan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Cheng
156
      et al., 2023). However, while these models excel in object tracking, they struggle to generalize to
      more dynamic concepts. In contrast, we demonstrate that generative video-language pre-training on
157
      Internet-scale data (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Bain et al., 2021) results in a universal (i.e. not limited
158
      to one domain) mapping between the space of language and the ever-changing visual world.
159
160   Diffusion Models have become the de-facto standard for generative learning in computer vi-
161   sion (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) and beyond (Chi et al., 2023). Among them,
      the Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) leverages neural network

162
      components to model the denoising process and builds a weighted variational bound for optimiza-
163   tion. Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022) shifts the denoising process into the latent space
164   of a pre-trained autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013), allowing for model scaling. Expanding
165   from images to videos, diffusion models have seen success in text-to-video (T2V) generation (Wang
166   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Blattmann et al., 2023). In addition to
167   the capacity to generate high-fidelity images based on text prompts, the T2V diffusion models im-
168   plicitly learn the mapping from linguistic descriptions to video regions, providing an opportunity
169   to repurpose them for RVOS. Among current T2V methods, ModelScope (Wang et al., 2023) and
170   VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023; 2024) stand out for their open-source implementations, forming the
171
      backbone of our research.
172   Visual-language Pre-training for Perception: in addition to being highly effective in image and
173   video generation, diffusion models have been shown to learn a strong representation of the natural
174   image manifold. Several works have demonstrated that these representations can be re-purposed
175   for classical computer vision problems, including semantic segmentation (Xu et al., 2023; Zhao
176   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023) and pixel-level correspondence (Tang et al., 2023), achieving an
177   impressive degree of generalization. Others have shown that image diffusing models learn powerful
178   representations of objects, enabling open-world novel view synthesis (Liu et al., 2023) and amodal
179
      segmentation (Ozguroglu et al., 2024). Most recently, Zhu et al. (2024) also leverages pretrained
      T2V models for RVOS however, our analysis shows that their approach fails to fully capitalize on
180
      the universal visual-language mapping learned in generative pre-training. In this work, we explore
181
      the application of video diffusion models to RVS, demonstrating how to maintain a high-level gen-
182   eralizability during fine-tuning.
183
184   In a separate line of work, visual-language representations learned with contrastive objectives (Bao
185
      et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021) have been adapted for referring image (Lai et al., 2024; Rasheed
      et al., 2024; You et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) and video segmentation (Zhou et al., 2024). Although
186
      these models tend to be more light-weight, their performance remains limited, compared to both
187
      generative models, as well as classical referring segmentation approaches.

      We build our REM upon T2V diffusion models (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,

196   denoising in the latent space, following Rombach et al. (2022). Concretely, given a video sequence

      3.2   F ROM LANGUAGE - CONDITIONED DENOISING TO REFERRING VIDEO SEGMENTATION
241
242
      Referring Video Segmentation (RVS) is the task of segmenting an entity across space and time in a
243
      video based on a natural language expression. Formally, given a video sequence x ∈ RT ×3×H×W
244   and a text expression p, the goal is to produce a binary mask m ∈ RT ×H×W , where true values
245   indicate the presence of the referenced entity in each of the T frames. This task aligns naturally
246   with T2V diffusion models, as these models iteratively denoise latent representations during video
247   generation, establishing a strong mapping between the entities described in the text and their corre-
248   sponding regions in the video.
249
      Several prior works have applied diffusion models to segmentation tasks (Zhao et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
250   2023; Zhu et al., 2024), typically modifying the architecture to feed noisy latent representations and
251   text embeddings into the denoising UNet, extracting intermediate features for downstream tasks.
252   These approaches often employ task-specific decoders in a conventional discriminative learning
253   setup, effectively repurposing diffusion models as feature extractors. However, this extensive archi-
254   tectural modification results in divergence from the pretraining phase when fine-tuning on narrow-
255   domain datasets. This in turn causes the model to lose much of the general knowledge acquired
256   during pretraining, which is crucial for robust generalization.
257   In our approach, rather than using diffusion models solely as feature extractors, we preserve the
258   original architecture and specifically adapt it for the RVS task. This enables us to fully leverage
259   the extensive knowledge encoded in diffusion models while refining them to meet task-specific
260   requirements, without discarding critical information. As illustrated in Figure 3, REM re-purposes
261   the denoising network by shifting its objective from predicting noise to predicting mask latents. This
262   subtle yet powerful adaptation allows the model to retain its pretraining knowledge while enhancing
263   its ability to tackle video segmentation. The technical details of this adaptation are elaborated below.
264
265   3.3   R EFER E VERYTHING WITH D IFFUSION M ODELS
266
267   Instead of learning a mask decoder from scratch, we reuse the VAE from video diffusion models.
268   To adapt the target masks to the VAE, we broadcast the single-channel mask into three channels
269   by repeating the mask. For simplicity, we still denote this three-channel mask sequence as m. The
      pretrained VAE can then map the mask sequence into the latent space: E(m) = z m and D(z m ) ≈ m.

286   4     B ENCHMARK DESIGN AND COLLECTION
287
288   In this section, we discuss our approach to collecting a new benchmark that would expand the
289   focus of Referring Video Segmentation outside the domain of object tracking. As covering the
290   entire spectrum of concepts that can be spoken of in videos would be extremely costly, we seek to
291   identify a subset of the problem that requires joint modeling of language and temporal dynamics.
292   To this end, we choose to focus on dynamic processes, which we define as temporally evolving
293   events, where the subjects undergo continuous changes in state, shape, or appearance. Crucially, the
294   subjects in this context are not limited to objects, but include all concepts that are spatio-temporally
295
      localizable in videos, such as light or fire. The key steps for collecting this new benchmark, which
      we call Referring Video Process Segmentation (Ref-VPS), include selecting representative videos
296
      and annotating them with referring expressions and segmentation masks.
297
298
299   4.1   V IDEO S ELECTION
300
301   To source the videos for our benchmark we require a large, public and diverse database that is
302   queriable with natural language and allows re-distribution of content for research purposes. Based
303
      on these requirements, we choose the TikTok social media platform which has over 1 billion active
      users across the world and receives tens of millions of video uploads daily, capturing a wide range
304
      of dynamic visual content. TikTok’s policies generally allow for free redistribution of content, with
305
      individual users having the option to opt out.
306
307   To search for videos that capture dynamic processes, as defined above, we first identify a non-
308   exhaustive list of six broad and possibly overlapping concepts (e.g. ‘object transformations’, or
309
      ‘entities with dynamic boundaries’, full list together with definitions provided in Section A in the
      appendix). Then, for each concept we ask ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023) to provide a list of concrete
310
      examples together with multiple text queries for search on TikTok (e.g., ‘a wax candle melting’ for
311
      ‘object transformations’), resulting in 120 individual concepts. We retrieve over a 1000 samples
312   based on these queries; however a majority of the queries did not yield suitable videos because of
313   the physical nature of the event (e.g. events like ‘soil erosion’ are not typically captured on TikTok
314   due to their long temporal span), or ambiguity of search query not lending itself to being accurately
315   captured on TikTok. After removing irrelevant videos, the retrieved set is reduced to 342 samples.
316
      We then manually filter these videos based on the following criteria: (1) videos that do not feature
317
      significant dynamic changes of the subject (e.g., mostly stationary clouds in the sky); (2) dynamic
318   processes that occur too rapidly to allow for the labeling of a sufficient number of non-empty frames
319   (e.g., flashes of lightning); (3) video with frequent shot changes, which make it impossible to extract
320   an interrupted clip capturing the event of interest. Additionally, for videos that represent compila-
321   tions of similar events, we split them into individual clips and treat each one independently. The
322   resulting dataset contains 111 video clips representing 38 dynamic process concepts. The entire
323   dataset is intended for 0-shot evaluation, so we do not define any additional splits. A representative
      sample of the videos is shown in Figure 1.

      4.2   A NNOTATION COLLECTION AND EVALUATION
325
326   To label the videos selected above, we begin by adjusting the temporal boundaries of each clip to
327   focus on the event of interest and avoid shot changes. We also make sure that the event is captured
328   in its entirety whenever possible, including some context before and after it. The clips are then
329   exported at 24 FPS as image frames. If a video contains irrelevant frames, such as the TikTok logo
330   at the end, we crop the frames accordingly to remove the padding.
331   To collect referring expressions, we first manually identify the entity of interest in each clip. The se-
332   lected entity is then labeled with referring expressions by two independent annotators. Each annota-
333   tor provides two expressions for the target, resulting in a total of four expressions per clip, capturing
334   different ways to describe the same phenomenon. Following the standard protocol (Khoreva et al.,
335   2019; Seo et al., 2020), models are evaluated on all queries and the results are averaged.
336   Finally, we densely label the targets identified above with segmentation masks at 24 FPS. To this
337   end, we employ a semi-automatic pipeline, capitalizing on the recently introduced SAM2 (Ravi
338   et al., 2024) foundational model for interactive video segmentation. In particular, we provide posi-
339   tive and negative click annotation in the middle frame of a video first to ensure accurate boundary
340   segmentation. SAM2 then automatically segments the entity of interest in the frame, as well as prop-
341   agates the mask across the entire clip. We interactively improve segmentation quality by providing
342   additional clicks as needed. In the end, we manually refine the masks in frames where SAM2 fails
343   and label ambiguous regions as Ignore. A visualizations of Ref-VPS annotations together with addi-
344
      tional statistics of our benchmark are included in the appendix. For evaluation, we follow Tokmakov
      et al. (2023) and only report region similarity J as contour accuracy F is often not well defined for
345
      the entities like smoke or light which are frequent in Ref-VPS. Pixels inside the Ignore regions are
346
      not included in the metric calculation.

      2023) as well as on the ‘Stuff’ categories (Caesar et al., 2018) from VSPW (Miao et al., 2021) in

409   diversity that the standard RVOS benchmarks, whereas VSPW tests the ability to generalize to non-

411   sets of BURST and compare to the top performing methods from Table 1 that have public models.
412   All the evaluations reported in this section are zero-shot.
413

421   Table 3. Here the differences between the methods are a lot more pronounced on this benchmark
422   compared to other datasets, highlighting the value of our benchmark in assessing video-language
423   understanding capabilities of neural representations. Our approach outperforms all baselines by up
424   to 28% in Region Similarity, and notably surpasses the top RVOS method, UNINEXT, by 46%.

520   6    D ISCUSSION
521
522   In this paper, we proposed REM, a framework that capitalizes on Internet-scale video-language
523   representations learned by diffusion models to segment a wide range of concepts in video that can
524   be described through natural language. Our key insight is that changing as little as possible in
525   the representation is key to preserving its universal mapping between language and visual concepts
526   during fine-tuning. To illustrate the benefits of our approach, we have also collected Ref-VPS -
527   a new benchmark for referring segmentation of dynamic processes in videos, which significantly
528
      expands the scope of existing RVOS datasets. Our extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates
      that, despite only being trained on object masks, REM successfully generalizes to highly dynamic
529
      concepts in Ref-VPS, outperforming all prior work by up to 28% .
530
531   Despite REM’s impressive generalization abilities, the problem of RVS is far from being solved. In
532   Figure D in the Appendix we visualize a few failure cases of our method. REM still exhibits some
533   object-centric bias and struggles with extremely fast processes. Exploring ways to preserve even
534
      more of the representation learned during generative pre-training, e.g. via low-rank adaptation (Hu
      et al., 2022) of the visual backbone, is a very promising direction to address some of these issues.
535
      In addition, note that REM should be seen as a generic framework where the backbone of Wang
536
      et al. (2023) can be easily replaced with a more advanced representation, tracing the progress of
537   language-conditioned video generative models.

961   Accurately evaluating the temporal consistency of video segmentation methods is notoriously chal-
962   lenging, because it is hard to distinguish between predicted mask changes that are due to the
963   method’s inconsistency and the changes that are due to the true target deformations. Notably, the
964   temporal consistency metric proposed in the original DAVIS dataset (Pont-Tuset et al., 2017) was
965   only applied to videos with no significant object deformations and no occlusions and was eventually
966
      phased out by the dataset’s authors.

968   the average difference of IoU between the model’s prediction and the ground truth mask in consec-

       C.1    E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
1106
1107   Neither BURST (Athar et al., 2023) nor VSPW (Miao et al., 2021) contains referral text for the
1108   segmented entities. Since we want to strictly evaluate the entity recognition capacity of the models,
1109   we automatically generate referral expressions using only the category of the masked entity as ”the
1110   <class>” (e.g. ”the hat”). For VSPW we evaluate the validation set which has 66 different stuff
1111   categories. In the case of BURST, the validation and test sets contain object categories that the other
1112   split does not. So here we evaluate the combined validation and test set which contains 454 classes
1113   and a total of 2049 sequences.
1114
1115   C.2    T RAINING DETAILS
1116
       We train our model using 4 NVIDIA 80GB A100 GPUs. We use ModelScope T2V (Wang et al.,
1117
       2023) as our base video diffusion architecture and set the input noise level to 0. In the first stage,
1118
       we fine-tune only the spatial weights using image-text samples from Ref-COCO (Yu et al., 2016)
1119   for 1 epoch and then fine-tune all weights for 40 epoch using Ref-YTB (Seo et al., 2020) video-
1120   text samples and 12k samples from Ref-COCO jointly. In the second stage, the image samples
1121   from Ref-COCO are converted to pseudo videos through augmentations following Referformer (Wu
1122   et al., 2022b). We freeze the CLIP text encoder and the VAE encoder and decoder during training
1123   and only fine-tune the U-Net. We use a low learning rate of 1e-6 in both stages and the AdamW
1124   optimizer (Loshchilov et al., 2019). The number of frames T is set to 8 during training and 72 during
1125   evaluation.
