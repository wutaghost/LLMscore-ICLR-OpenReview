                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               This paper introduces the first online blending auction mechanism design for spon-
010
               sored items (ads) alongside organic items (organics), ensuring guaranteed Pareto
011            optimality for platform revenue, advertiser utilities, and user interest (measured
012            through clicks). We innovatively define an umbrella term, ”traffic item,” to en-
013            compass both organics and auctionable ad items, where an organic represents a
014            unit of traffic to be auctioned, valued positively by attracting user interest with
015            a fixed zero bid and payment. The online blending traffic distribution problem
016            is thus transformed into an auction problem with unified valuation metric for the
017            traffic item, which is subsequently formulated as an online multi-objective con-
018            strained optimization problem. We derive a Pareto equation for this optimization
019
               problem, characterizing the optimal auction mechanism set by its solution set.
               This solution is implemented through a novel two-stage Adaptive Modeled Mech-
020
               anism Design (AMMD), which (1) trains a hypernetwork to learn a family of pa-
021
               rameterized mechanisms, each corresponding to a specific solution of the Pareto
022            equation, and (2) employs feedback-based online control to adaptively adjust the
023            mechanism parameters, ensuring real-time optimality in a dynamic environment.
024            Extensive experiments demonstrate that AMMD outperforms existing methods
025            in both click-through rates and revenue across multiple auction scenarios, partic-
026            ularly highlighting its adaptability to online environments. The code has been
027            submitted and will be released publicly.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Online advertising has significantly contributed to the tech sector’s revenue, with PwC estimating
033
      that the online ads sector will reach $723.6 billion by 2026 (PwC, 2023). Google reported $224
      billion in advertising revenues in 2022 (Bianchi, 2023), while Meta earned $113 billion (Dixon,
034
      2023). The distribution of commercial advertisements (ads) is typically conducted through auc-
035
      tions, employing traditional techniques such as the Myerson auction (Myerson, 1981), GSP auction
036   (Edelman et al., 2007), and VCG auction (Varian & Harris, 2014). Deep learning has emerged as
037   a transformative force, enabling an end-to-end, modeled (i.e., parameterized) solution that learns
038   optimal auction mechanisms directly from online traffic (Sandholm & Likhodedov, 2015). In this
039   paradigm, an action mechanism—comprising an allocation rule and a pricing rule—is represented
040   by one or more deep neural networks, which are trained by optimizing objectives such as revenue
041   and regret concerning (dominant-strategy) incentive compatibility (Rahme et al., 2021). Due to their
042   efficiency, modeled auctions have been widely adopted in industry applications (Zhang et al., 2021;
043   Liu et al., 2021). However, in these works, organic traffic is treated as a static environment, with the
044
      auction mechanism focusing solely on ads.
045   This paper investigates the design of mechanisms for distributing both ads and organics. Organics
046   play a crucial role in attracting user interest, which can lead to increased ad clicks. While they do
047   not directly generate commercial income, they help foster a healthy ecosystem that contributes to
048   long-term engagement. Consequently, the objectives of distributing ads and organics involve two
049   resources: immediate commercial interest from ads and the goal of attracting user interest.
050   This scenario defines a new economic setting that extends beyond current theoretical understanding
051   (as illustrated in Figure 1). Existing works are closest in two respects: (1) some use independent
052   ranking modules to distribute personalized organics and ads (Zhao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b),
053   and (2) others unify the allocation and pricing of ads and organics, assuming a static environment
      (Giagkiozis & Fleming, 2015; Gunantara, 2018; Wang et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b).

070   However, these approaches do not adequately address our setting due to their failure to consider the
071   dynamics of traffic. In online auctions, ensuring the optimality of the mechanism necessitates that
072   the modeled auction be adapt to online traffic characteristics, which is absent in previous studies.
073
      To allocate and charge items with different attributes within a unified auction framework, we in-
074
      novatively define a ”traffic item” as an umbrella term encompassing all attribute variables. In this
075   context, an organic is a special case, where the positive valuation arises from attracting user interest,
076   accompanied by fixed zero bids and payments. The three objectives—platform revenue, advertiser
077   utilities, and user interest—are abstracted into two valuation metrics (Clicks and Costs) related to
078   the distribution and pricing of ads and organics, combined with multiple constraints. We model this
079   challenge as an online multi-objective constrained optimization problem.
080
      We derive a Pareto equation for this optimization problem, which characterizes the optimal auc-
081   tion mechanism set by its solution set. Our findings demonstrate that any independent or static
082   blending mechanism leads to suboptimal outcomes, highlighting the need for a unified adaptive auc-
083   tion mechanism. The solution is implemented through an Adaptive Modeled Mechanism Design
084   (AMMD) framework, which operates in a two-stage process.
085
      The first stage involves training a hypernetwork to learn a family of parameterized mechanisms, each
086
      corresponding to a specific solution of the Pareto equation. The model parameters include a virtual
087   value function for uniformly ranking both advertisements and organic items, along with incentive-
088   compatible pricing rules to derive revenue from ads. The hypernetwork dynamically controls these
089   mechanism parameters based on the evolving characteristics of online traffic.
090
      The second stage utilizes feedback-based online control to adaptively adjust the mechanism param-
091
      eters, ensuring real-time optimality in a dynamic environment. The optimal values of the weight pa-
092
      rameters are influenced by traffic characteristics and the distribution of advertiser values. Since these
093   distributions are often unknown in online auctions, we implement weight control using a feedback-
094   based method. By continuously adjusting the mechanism parameters through the hypernetwork, we
095   achieve an adaptive and optimal mechanism design for online scenarios.
096
      We conduct extensive experiments demonstrating that AMMD significantly outperforms state-of-
097
      the-art methods (SOTA) across various generalized online auction scenarios. In both static and
098
      dynamic auction contexts, AMMD consistently surpasses SOTA algorithms and achieves Pareto
099   optimality. To compare multiple objective results, such as clicks and costs, under a unified metric,
100   we define Utopia distance as a standard measure for different mechanisms. For generalized multi-
101   slot auctions with KPI constraints, AMMD improves the Utopia distance compared to SOTA by at
102   least 20%, successfully achieving Pareto optimality.

353
      this hypernetwork w ⃗ consists of multi-objective weights (α, β) and real-time bidding information
354   F, C, Z. The output is the parameters of the replaced layer, as shown in Figure 2.
355
356   To train the hypernetwork, we randomly sample a set of weight parameters (α, β) and value distri-
357
      butions as inputs during each iteration. By applying the same parameters to weight the loss function,
      we simultaneously train both the hypernetwork parameters θH and the fixed mechanism model pa-
358
      rameters θ−w through backpropagation (as detailed in Supplementary Metarial Algorithm 1). Since
359
      the modeled mechanism involves multiple sorting operations, we utilize the differentiable sorting
360   operator proposed in Grover et al. (2019) to support the training process.
361
362   By training the hypernetwork, we develop a family of mechanism parameters with strong general-
363
      ization capabilities. As the weight parameters and bidding environment evolve, the modeled auction
      can seamlessly adapt to the instantaneously optimal configuration.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we propose Adaptive Modeled Mechanism Design (AMMD) for multi-slot auctions
530
      that incorporate both ads and organics. We define the ranking and pricing rules based on a virtual
      value function for multi-objective auctions, demonstrating their effectiveness in maximizing rev-
531
      enue while maintaining incentive compatibility. We establish the Pareto equation that links multi-
532
      objective weights and constraints, enabling the decomposition of the constrained multi-objective
533   optimization problem in online auctions into offline learning of a static mechanism family and on-
534   line weight control. In AMMD, a hypernetwork is employed to learn a family of optimal static
535   mechanisms, each tailored to specific traffic characteristics. Additionally, PID controllers are used
536   to update the weight parameters online, ensuring the mechanism’s optimality as traffic distribution
537   evolve. We validate the effectiveness of AMMD through a series of multi-slot auction experiments.
538   The results indicate that AMMD achieves Pareto optimal mechanisms across all tested environ-
539   ments. We anticipate that this work will encourage further research on multi-objective auctions that
      blend ads and organic content on e-commerce platforms.

       Remark 3: The application of VCG pricing rule guarantees the incentive compatibility of AMMD.
973    For a complete proof, please refer to the work of (Varian & Harris, 2014). Here we give a simple
974    proof sketch.

981              bidding.

1008
       properties and wide application. Adjusting model parameters to control the percentage of advertise-
1009   ment exposed in online traffic scenarios can be viewed as a time-varying signal tracking problem in
1010   a nonlinear stochastic system. Theoretical analysis on the ability of the classical PID controller can
1011   be referred to in the work of Zhao & Yuan (2024).
1012
       Briefly speaking, such control systems can be stabilized in the mean square sense, provided that the
1013

1014
       an upper bound proportional to the sum of the varying rates of the reference signals, the varying rates
1015   of the disturbances and random noises. In our experiments, we found that the AMMD framework
1016   with this online controller met the constraints such as the PAE and CVR.

       In this section we present the experimental setup and results for generalized multi-slot auctions in
1243   detail. In all scenarios, four ad items and four organic items compete for four advertising slots. The
1244   click-through rates of the four advertising slots are set to (1.0,0.8,0.6,0.5), and the click-through
1245   rates of advertising items are sampled from uniform distribution U [0, 1], and the click-through rates
1246   of organic items are sampled from uniform distribution U [0, 2.5]. When there are CVR constraints,
1247   the CVR of each item is sampled from uniform distribution U [0, 0.175] with the constraint z̄ ≥ 0.1.
1248
       In static scenarios, the value distribution of ad items are sampled from static uniform distribution
1249   U [0, 1.5]. For VCG and GSP mechanisms, all items are sorted according to the metric V alue ×
1250   CT R + α × CT R, where α takes (0.01, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0). In the uniformly sorted sequence, the
1251   two highest ranked ad and organic items are each selected for impression. In the pricing process for
1252   ad items using VCG and GSP rules, items added in suboptimal sequences are restricted to ads to
1253   maintain the PAE constraint.
1254
       For SW-VCG, it maps the value and CTR of items to virtual values through neural networks and
1255   uses VCG rules for pricing. The neural network is trained using the clicks and revenue after sorting
1256   and pricing as the loss function Costs + αClicks, where α takes (0.01, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0).
1257
1258
       In Figure 9 we give the auction results for the different mechanisms under two sets of random sam-
       pling. Notice that the GSP mechanism does not satisfy incentive compatibility, and its actual results
1259
       should be approximately equivalent to the VCG mechanism. From the comparison of the SW-VCG
1260
       and VCG mechanisms we can see that SW-VCG strictly Pareto dominates the VCG mechanism at
1261   different parameter settings. This illustrates that the virtual values obtained using multi-objective
1262   training achieve a more optimal allocation compared to the real values.
1263
1264
       In the comparison between AMMD and SW-VCG, we can see that AMMD significantly outperforms
       the Pareto curve formed by the SW-VCG mechanism. Theorem 4 shows that charging the ad items
1265
       within the unified sequence strictly leads to an increase in total costs. However, for SW-VCG trained
1266
       with static multi-objective weights, it cannot naturally satisfy the PAE constraint. The process of
1267   ensuring that sequences satisfy the PAE constraint (limiting the proportion of ad items in a uniform
1268   sequence) makes it infeasible to charge for ads in a uniform sequence. This is due to the fact that
1269   suboptimal sequences in VCG pricing are also required to satisfy the PAE constraint. However,
1270   for AMMD, the satisfaction of PAE is achieved by adjusting the weights of multiple objectives.
1271   This allows ad items to be charged in a uniform sequence, which significantly improves the multi-
1272   objective revenue.
