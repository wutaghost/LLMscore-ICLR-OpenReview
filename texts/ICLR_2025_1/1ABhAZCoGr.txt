011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning from expert demonstrations has long remained a challeng-
014            ing research problem, and existing methods resorting to behavioral cloning plus
015            further RL training often suffer from poor generalization, low sample efficiency,
016
               and poor model interpretability. Inspired by the strong reasoning abilities of large
               language models (LLMs), we propose a novel strategy-based neuro-symbolic rein-
017
               forcement learning framework integrated with LLMs called DY NAMIC ST RATEGY
018
               I NDUCTION WITH L LMS FOR REINFORCEMENT LEARNING (DYSTIL) to over-
019            come these limitations. DYSTIL dynamically queries a strategy-generating LLM to
020            induce textual strategies based on advantage estimations and expert demonstrations,
021            and gradually internalizes induced strategies into the RL agent through policy
022            optimization to improve its performance through boosting policy generalization
023            and enhancing sample efficiency. It also provides a direct textual channel to observe
024            and interpret the evolution of the policy’s underlying strategies during training. We
025            test DYSTIL over challenging RL environments from Minigrid and BabyAI, and
026            empirically demonstrate that DYSTIL significantly outperforms state-of-the-art
027
               baseline methods by 17.75% success rate on average while also enjoying higher
               sample efficiency during the learning process.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Many important, yet challenging, reinforcement learning tasks (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023; 2019;
      Mnih et al., 2013) are highly hierarchical and structural, have sparse and delayed rewards, and require
034
      complex reasoning procedures based on understanding of higher-level abstractions. In practice,
035
      classical reinforcement learning algorithms often fail to learn these difficult RL tasks well from
036   scratch, because of the difficulty in collecting meaningful reward signals during exploration and the
037   lack of support for higher-level abstraction and reasoning. Therefore, it is often necessary to collect a
038   set of expert demonstration trajectories to aid reinforcement learning over these tasks (Ramírez et al.,
039   2022). Most of the best existing methods for reinforcement learning from expert demonstrations
040   typically first employ behavioral cloning (Pomerleau, 1988) to train the RL agent’s policy generator
041   to imitate the behavior and action decisions of the expert through supervised learning. They then
042   feed the agent into a more advanced RL algorithm (such as Proximal Policy Optimization (Schulman
043   et al., 2017)) to further improve its performance.
044   This approach of behavioral cloning plus further RL training suffers from several severe limitations:
045   (1) expert demonstrations are often expensive or hard to collect, so typically the amount of expert
046   demonstration trajectories is quite limited; (2) these limited expert demonstrations usually can only
047   cover a small region of the state space, and thus behavioral cloning over them often tends to cause
048   overfitting and results in poor generalization of the learned policy; (3) this approach can not enable
049   the RL agent to acquire higher-level abstractions and understanding of the RL tasks, thus limiting the
050
      efficiency with which it utilizes training samples as well as the level of performance it can achieve;
      (4) this approach treats the policy network of the RL agent as a black box and thus suffers from low
051
      model transparency and interpretability.
052
053   To overcome the aforementioned limitations, in this paper we present DYSTIL, a novel strategy-
      based neuro-symbolic reinforcement learning framework integrated with LLMs called DY NAMIC

      Language Grounding In DYSTIL we take a language-grounded approach to reinforcement
129
      learning. Previous work (Carta et al., 2023) has demonstrated that running reinforcement learning
130   using an LLM policy generator over textual descriptions of agent observations instead of the original
131   raw observations can largely boost learning performance and sample efficiency. A crucial prerequisite
132   for language-grounded RL is having access to a good observation-to-text converter that can convert
133   the agent’s raw observation information (such as images or state tensors) about the environment into
134   rich and accurate textual descriptions in natural language. In general, such an observation-to-text
135   converter can be either rule-based (such as BabyAI-text proposed in (Carta et al., 2023)) or trained
136   with neural network architectures. Without loss of generality, in this work we assume that our RL
137   agent has access to an accurate and well-functioning observation-to-text converter Co→t , which is
138
      a safe assumption given the recent rapid advances in pre-trained multimodal foundation models
      (Li et al., 2024). Please see Figure 7 in Appendix C for a concrete example of observation-to-text
139
      transformation using BabyAI-text.

143   2.2   S TRATEGY I NDUCTION WITH LLM S FROM E XPERT D EMONSTRATIONS
144
145   Recent research works have demonstrated the ability of LLMs to automatically extract generalizable
146   rules, knowledge and insight from examples (Zhu et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). Inspired by these
147   works, here we focus on using LLMs to automatically induce useful and generalizable strategies for
148   completing tasks in reinforcement learning environments from trajectories of expert demonstrations.
149
      We adapt and extend the prompting method in (Zhao et al., 2024) to design our prompt for automatic
150   RL strategy induction. Our prompt has three components: (1) Description of the RL Environment;
151   (2) Expert Demonstration Trajectories: this component includes a full textual description for each
152   of the expert demonstration trajectories in D including its goal and a concatenation of the textual
153   descriptions of all {observation, action} pairs in sequential order; and (3) Strategy Query Prompt:
154   this paragragh describes our expectations for the kind of strategies that the LLM should induce from
155   expert demonstrations and generate for us. Figure 2 demonstrates a concrete example of this strategy
156   induction process from expert demonstrations in GPT-4o for an RL environment called Dynamic
157   Obstacles from the Minigrid library (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). As we can see in Figure 2, the
158
      list of strategies induced by GPT-4o is indeed very relevant to successfully completing tasks in this
      Dynamic Obstacles RL environment, and also coincides with human intuition.
159
160   In the DYSTIL framework, we call the LLM used for inducing strategies the strategy-generating
161   LLM, which is typically a SOTA large-scale LLM (e.g. GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024)) that has strong
      reasoning abilities.

207   The induction method introduced in Section 2.2 is often able to generate a useful list of strategies
208   that can help RL agents make better decisions in RL tasks, but it also has one prominent limitation:
209   it is a one-time query and the induced list of strategies will remain static over time. As a result, if
210   the initial one-time induced list of strategies from the strategy-generating LLM is not accurate or
211   not comprehensive enough, there will be no opportunity for self-correction afterwards. Therefore, it
212
      would be much more desirable to upgrade this static approach into an iterative and dynamic algorithm
      that can allow the RL agent to continuously improve its induced list of strategies and its policy model
213
      based on interactions with the environment.
214

      Initialization of the RL Agent Model To begin with, we first construct and initialize a new
238   DYSTIL RL agent model (as introduced in Section 2.3) as our RL agent L. In particular, we create a
239   new empty memory ML in L to save its most recently updated list of strategies in real time. The
240   parameters of the core reasoning LLM and the language modeling head of L are initialized from
241   the pre-trained checkpoint of the corresponding LLM, and the parameters of the value network are
242   randomly initialized from scratch.
243
      Initial Strategy Induction from Expert Demonstrations Now in this step, we use the method
244   described in Section 2.2 to query a strategy-generating LLM Q (e.g. GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024)) to
245   induce an initial list of strategies S0 from all the expert demonstration trajectories in D, and store S0
246   in the agent L’s memory ML . We denote the prompt template used in this step as Pinitial .
247
      Behavioral Cloning with Induced Strategies Next, we run behavioral cloning (Pomerleau, 1988)
248
      through supervised learning to train our RL agent model L to imitate the action policy in the set
249
      of expert demonstration trajectories D. More specifically, we run optimization procedures (such as
250   Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015)) to gradually minimize the mean cross-entropy loss between the action
251   distributions πL (o | g, S0 ) generated by our agent model L and the action choices made by the expert
252   across all the observations contained in D, subject to a small entropy regularization (Williams & Peng,
253   1991; Mnih et al., 2016; Ahmed et al., 2019). Note that during behavioral cloning training we only
254   update the parameters of the core reasoning LLM and its corresponding language modeling head, and
255   keep the value network frozen. Intuitively, this behavioral cloning training process is very important
256   in that it helps the agent model L to gradually internalize the list of induced text strategies through
257   parameter tuning. This helps the agent model better understand how to reason with the strategies to
258
      make good action decisions under realistic scenarios in the RL environment.
259   Experience Collection and Advantage Estimation After the RL agent model L has been properly
260   trained through behavioral cloning with its initial list of strategies S0 over expert demonstrations D,
261   we follow the practice of the proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017)
262   to run agent L to execute its current policy πL (o | g, S) in the RL environment E for T time steps to
263   collect an experience buffer B containing T (observation, action, reward) triples. Then, we follow
264   the standard PPO procedures in (Schulman et al., 2017) to compute the estimated values Â of the
265   advantage function A for all the T (observation, action) pairs in the current experience buffer B.

      limiting their reasoning ability to analyze how to best adjust its induced rules to correct unfavorable

326
      We evaluate the performance of DYSTIL in four challenging RL environments: the Dynamic
327   Obstacles environment from the Minigrid library (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023), and the Unlock
328   Pickup environment, the Key Corridor environment and the the Put Next environment from the
329   BabyAI library (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019). Both Minigrid (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023) and
330   BabyAI (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019) are popularly used libraries of grid-world reinforcement
331   learning environments that are designed to have good support for language grounding. All the RL
332   environments in Minigrid and BabyAI are partially observable in that an agent can only see a field
333   of view of 7 × 7 grid cells in front of it (subject to object occlusion) at every time step. These four
334   RL environments we use all have sparse and delayed rewards, and require complex reasoning over
335
      higher-level abstractions.

337   3.1   RL E NVIRONMENTS FOR E VALUATION
338
      Dynamic Obstacles Dynamic Obstacles is a challenging dynamic RL environment from the
339
      Minigrid library (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). In this environment, the agent’s goal is to navigate
340   through a room with moving obstacles to get to a green goal square without hitting any of them along
341   the way (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). If the agent succeeds, it will be given a single reward of
342   value r = 1 − 0.9 × (total_steps/max_steps) at the final step; if it failed within maximum allowed
343   number of steps, it will receive a reward of 0; if it hits an obstacle along the way, it will receive a −1
344   penalty reward and the episode also terminates (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). This environment is
345   one of the most challenging ones in Minigrid because it is a dynamic and stochastic RL environment,
346   and thus requires the agent to have strong abilities to reason about the high-level mechanisms and
347   principles of this environment in order to make good action decisions in a safely manner. In our
348
      experiment we use the MiniGrid-Dynamic-Obstacles-6x6-v0 configuration.
349   Unlock Pickup Unlock Pickup is a challenging static RL environment from the BabyAI library
350   (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019). In each run of this environment, a target box is locked behind a
351   door, and your goal as an agent is to obtain the key to unlock that door and then pick up the box
352   using as few time steps as possible. And similar to the Dynamic Obstacles environment, the agent
353   will receive either a single reward of value r = 1 − 0.9 × (total_steps/max_steps) upon successful
354
      completion of the assigned task, or 0 reward if it failed within maximum allowed number of steps.
      Unlock Pickup is mainly difficult for its high requirement on the agent’s abilities of maze exploration
355
      and navigation, avoidance of obstructions, optimal path finding, and long-horizon task planning. In
356
      our experiment we use the BabyAI-UnlockPickupDist-v0 configuration and set max_steps = 60.
357
358   Key Corridor Key Corridor is another challenging static RL environment from the BabyAI library
359   (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019). In each run of this environment, the agent needs to explore a
360
      complex maze constituted of multiple rooms to find a key and then use that key to open a locked door
      in order to pick up a designated object locked behind that door, using as few time steps as possible.
361
      And again, the agent will receive either a single reward of value r = 1−0.9×(total_steps/max_steps)
362
      upon successful completion of the assigned task, or 0 reward if it failed within maximum allowed
363   number of steps. In our experiment we use the BabyAI-KeyCorridorS3R2-v0 configuration and set
364   max_steps = 60.
365
366
      Put Next Put Next is another challenging static RL environment from the BabyAI library (Chevalier-
      Boisvert et al., 2019). In each run of this environment, the agent will be assigned a randomly generated
367
      task in the form of moving a designated object to a position next to another designated object using as
368
      few time steps as possible. And similar to the Dynamic Obstacles environment, the agent will receive
369   either a single reward of value r = 1 − 0.9 × (total_steps/max_steps) upon successful completion
370   of the assigned task, or 0 reward if it failed within maximum allowed number of steps. Put Next is
371   mainly difficult for its high requirement on the agent’s abilities of maze exploration and navigation,
372   avoidance of obstructions, optimal path finding, and long-horizon task planning. In our experiment
373   we use the BabyAI-PutNextS5N2-v0 configuration and set max_steps = 60.

488   LLMs for Reinforcement Learning and Language-Grounded RL Traditionally, most policy
489   models of deep RL algorithms have been directly operating over low-level raw features of environment
490   observations (Mnih et al., 2013). This design choice has inevitably restricted these RL methods’
491   abilities to learn higher-level abstractions and concepts about the RL tasks. Recently more research
492   efforts has been made on grounding reinforcement learning into natural language (Chevalier-Boisvert
493   et al., 2019; 2023; Carta et al., 2023; Poudel et al., 2023) and use pre-trained LLMs as the policy
494   generator of RL agents (Carta et al., 2023). DYSTIL differs from these existing methods by enabling
495   LLM-based RL agents to efficiently learn higher-level strategies and abstractions of the RL tasks
496
      through strategy induction from large-scale LLMs.
497   LLMs for Sequential Decision Making Recently there has been a series of works that explore
498   different approaches for applying LLMs to sequential decision making tasks (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn
499   et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024). All these existing methods have two major limitations:
500   (1) they all require querying the API of a large-scale closed-source LLM for the agent’s decision
501   making at every single time step, which make them highly infeasible for many important real-world
502
      tasks and applications that require fast inference speed to make timely decisions or require offline
      and lightweight deployment to integrate with operating hardware (such as robots); (2) they all rely
503
      on prompting to make inference of action decisions with frozen closed-source LLMs at every single
504
      time step, and thus do not support parametrized policy learning. In contrast, for DYSTIL the decision
505   making inference at all time steps is run on a lightweight open-source LLM that supports full model
506   parameter tuning. As a result, DYSTIL has the advantage of fast real-time inference during decision
507   making, easy deployment over different application scenarios, and compatibility with on-policy
508   reinforcement learning algorithms, while still being able to learn high-level strategies through strategy
509   distillation from large-scale closed-source LLMs.
510
511
      5    C ONCLUSION AND B ROADER I MPACT
512
513   In this paper we presented DYSTIL, a novel strategy-based neuro-symbolic reinforcement learning
514   framework integrated with large language models. We carried out empirical experiments over
515   challenging RL environments to evaluate DYSTIL on the task of reinforcement learning from expert
516   demonstrations, and the results show that DYSTIL significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baseline
517   methods while exhibiting higher sample efficiency and superior model interpretability.
518
      Broader Impact Our work opens up new possibilities in leveraging powerful large language models
519   to generate textual strategies to help reinforcement learning algorithms improve their learning
520   performance, expedite their learning processes, and making their policy evolution more transparent.
521   In addition, our work also demonstrates the advantages of neuro-symbolic RL methods over traditional
522   neural-only RL methods. Last but not the least, in future works on LLM evaluation, it could also be
523   of research interest to include new evaluation metrics and benchmarks to quantitatively measure how
524   much performance gain can the textual strategies induced by different LLMs bring to reinforcement
525   learning algorithms. This could serve as an interesting new aspect to gauge the knowledge reasoning
526   and induction abilities of LLMs under cross-modal scenarios.

      B     D ESIGN D ETAILS OF THE A RCHITECTURE OF THE RL AGENT ’ S ACTOR

704
            M ODULE IN DYSTIL
705
      In coordination with the rise of research interests in language-grounded RL, recent works have also
706
      been exploring the direction of using language models as the core policy generators of the agents in
707
      reinforcement learning. For example, (Carta et al., 2023) proposes an architecture for a policy LLM
708   that directly takes a textual prompt comprised of an environment introduction, a task description, a
709   historical trajectory of observation descriptions and action names, and an action prompting phrase as
710   input, and then feed this prompt into an encoder-decoder language model to output the conditional
711   probability of each token in each action name given the prompt and the generated action tokens
712   through its language modeling head plus softmax. It then multiplies such condition probabilities for
713   all the tokens in each action name together, and then normalize to obtain a probability distribution
714   over the set of all possible actions to serve as its policy (Carta et al., 2023). This architecture suffers
715   a lot from the issue of slow inference, because for generating each single action decision we need
716
      to run this policy LLM for NA × MA times (Carta et al., 2023), where NA is the total number of
      possible actions and MA is the average number of tokens in all the action names. In this work, in
717
      order to improve inference speed and training efficiency, we design an upgraded architecture for
718
      the output side of the LLM policy generator (i.e. the actor module of our DYSTIL agent). First, if
719   necessary, we make some small tweaks on the names of the actions such that no two actions would
720   share the same first token in their names (e.g. we could change the two action names ‘turn left’ and
721   ‘turn right’ into ‘left turn’ and ‘right turn’ to avoid first token conflict). Next, when generating an
722   action decision we only need to run the policy LLM once and then we can directly take the logits
723   corresponding to the first token of each action name outputed by the language modeling head, group
724   them into a vector, and then run softmax to obtain a proability distribtion over the set of all possible
725   actions as our policy.

      The initial list of strategies learned by the DYSTIL RL agent in the Minigrid Dynamic Obstacles
867   environment:
868
869   1. Advance when clear and safe:
870   - The agent should move forward when there is no immediate obstacle (e.g., no ball directly in front
871   or very close on either side) and when the green goal is within a reasonable distance (1-3 steps away).
872   This minimizes time steps without unnecessary rotations.
873
      2. Turn towards the goal when necessary:
874
875   - The agent should make a left or right turn to align with the direction of the green goal when moving
876    forward directly is blocked by obstacles or walls. Turning should be based on the goal’s relative
877
       position to the agent.
878   3. Avoid obstacles by turning instead of moving forward:
879
      - The agent should prioritize turning when it detects obstacles (e.g., blue balls) directly in front.
880
      Moving forward into an obstacle should always be avoided as it ends the episode with a penalty.
881
882   4. Maintain goal orientation after turning:
883   - After turning, the agent should quickly readjust and move towards the green goal if the path is clear,
884    ensuring minimal time is spent making unnecessary turns.
885
      5. Turn early when a wall is close:
886
887   - The agent should turn left or right early if a wall is detected within 1-2 steps ahead. Moving forward
888    into a wall wastes a time step, and adjusting earlier is more efficient.
889   6. Favor forward movement when obstacles are distant:
890
891   - When obstacles are visible but farther than 1-2 steps away, the agent should continue moving forward
892
       as long as the path is clear. Turning preemptively when obstacles are distant can delay the agent
       unnecessarily.
893
894   7. Turn towards the side of the goal if obstacles are scattered ahead:
895
      - If multiple obstacles (blue balls) are scattered ahead and there’s no clear direct path to the green
896   goal, the agent should turn towards the side where the green goal is closer and avoid the cluster of
897   obstacles.
898
899
      8. Optimize by following the closest safe route to the goal:
900   - When the green goal is identified within 2-3 steps but obstacles obstruct direct movement, the agent
901    should prioritize a minimal deviation (either left or right turn) to navigate around the obstacles and
902    then move towards the goal.

904
905   - The agent should avoid making consecutive left-right turns in short succession unless absolutely
906
      necessary for obstacle avoidance. The strategy should be to realign with the goal and proceed forward
      as much as possible.
907
908   10. Move forward when the goal is directly ahead, even if other obstacles are nearby:
909
      - If the green goal is directly ahead and no obstacles block the forward path, the agent should prioritize
910    moving forward towards the goal, regardless of surrounding obstacles, as the task can be completed
911    in fewer steps.
