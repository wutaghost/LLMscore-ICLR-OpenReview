009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Automatic adversarial prompt generation provides remarkable success in jailbreak-
012            ing safely-aligned large language models (LLMs). Existing gradient-based attacks,
013            while demonstrating outstanding performance in jailbreaking white-box LLMs,
014            often generate garbled adversarial prompts with chaotic appearance. These adver-
015            sarial prompts are difficult to transfer to other LLMs, hindering their performance
016
               in attacking unknown victim models. In this paper, for the first time, we delve
               into the semantic meaning embedded in garbled adversarial prompts and propose
017
               a novel method that “translates” them into coherent and human-readable natural
018
               language adversarial prompts. In this way, we can effectively uncover the semantic
019            information that triggers vulnerabilities of the model and unambiguously transfer
020            it to the victim model, without overlooking the adversarial information hidden
021            in the garbled text, to enhance jailbreak attacks. It also offers a new approach to
022            discovering effective designs for jailbreak prompts, advancing the understanding of
023            jailbreak attacks. Experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly
024            improves the success rate of jailbreak attacks against various safety-aligned LLMs
025            and outperforms state-of-the-arts by large margins. With at most 10 queries, our
026            method achieves an average attack success rate of 81.8% in attacking 7 commercial
027
               closed-source LLMs, including GPT and Claude-3 series, on HarmBench. Our
               method also achieves over 90% attack success rates against Llama-2-Chat models
028
               on AdvBench, despite their outstanding resistance to jailbreak attacks. Our code
029
               will be made publicly available.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive abilities in understanding and generating
035   human-like text. To mitigate the risk of producing illegal or unethical content, many fine-tuning
036   methods have been proposed to obtain safety-aligned LLMs which encourage the LLMs to refuse
037   response to potentially harmful requests (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Korbak et al., 2023;
038   Glaese et al., 2022). Nevertheless, some work (Shen et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Perez et al., 2022;
039
      Chao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024) indicates that these models have not yet achieved
      perfect safety alignment. Instead, safety-aligned LLMs can be induced to respond to harmful requests
040
      through carefully designed prompts, referred to as “jailbreaking” (Wei et al., 2024).
041
042   Many automatic adversarial prompt generation methods have been proposed to improve the perfor-
043   mance of jailbreak attacks. Among them, methods appending adversarial suffix obtained by gradient-
044   based optimization to original harmful requests, e.g., Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) (Zou et al.,
045
      2023) and its variants (Sitawarin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), have demonstrated remarkable success
      in jailbreaking white-box LLMs (Mazeika et al., 2024). However, these methods often lead to garbled
046
      adversarial prompts with chaotic appearance, that can be composed of incoherent words and symbols.
047
      Due to differences in the architecture and parameters between the victim model and the substitute
048   model used to generate adversarial prompts, the victim model tends to overlook the adversarial
049   information hidden in the garbled text, thereby limiting the performance of transfer attacks. In
050   addition, the garbled adversarial prompts are susceptible to perplexity-based defense strategies (Jain
051   et al., 2023). Alternatively, some methods (Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Zeng et al.,
052   2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Paulus et al., 2024) turn to generate natural language adversarial prompts
053   that are coherent, human-readable, and semantically meaningful, to clearly convey the adversarial
      information to the victim model and bypass the perplexity-filter, thus enhance the jailbreak attacks.

054
      However, these methods still exhibit limited jailbreaking performance. They are either impaired by
055   the less informative feedback from the victim model to optimize the adversarial prompt (Chao et al.,
056   2023; Mehrotra et al., 2023), require careful hyper-parameter tuning (Zhu et al., 2023), or necessitate
057   training an additional model (Paulus et al., 2024).
058
      In this paper, we show that gradient-based attacks can be made highly effective and transferrable
059
      simply by making their generated adversarial prompts interpretable. Specifically, given a garbled
060
      adversarial prompt generated by gradient-based methods, we propose to interpret the semantic
061   meaning embedded in it and “translate” it into a coherent, human-readable natural language ad-
062   versarial prompt. Our method can discover the semantic information that can be used to elicit the
063   vulnerabilities of safety-aligned LLMs, enabling clear transfer of the adversarial information to
064   the victim model, thereby effectively improving the performance of jailbreak attacks. In addition,
065   our method is free of the manual design for adversarial prompts, careful hyper-parameter tuning,
066   additional computational costs for model training, and the need for informative feedback from the
067   victim model. Our method also offers an approach to developing new designs for jailbreak prompts.
068   The experimental results demonstrate that the coherent and human-readable adversarial prompts
069
      generated by our method can effectively transfer to other LLMs with at most 10 queries. In particular,
      the adversarial prompts generated on HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) achieve 81.8% average
070
      attack success rates in attacking GPT and Claude-3 series. When attacking the Llama-2-Chat (7B and
071
      13B), which are resilient to attacks even in white-box settings, our method can also achieve 93.3%
072   and 90.4% attack success rates on AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023).

075   2    R ELATED W ORK
076
077   Recent work demonstrates that the safety-aligned LLMs, which are trained to output harmless and
078   non-objectionable responses, can still be induced to produce harmful content by some carefully
079   designed prompts, known as jailbreak attacks (Shen et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2024). Broadly
080   speaking, jailbreak attacks or prompts can be designed manually (Shen et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024)
081   or more commonly, generated automatically by using some algorithms.
082   One way to automatically generate jailbreak attacks is by directly optimize the text input through
083   gradient-based optimization (Wallace et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2021; Shin et al., 2020; Jones et al.,
084   2023; Zou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024; Sitawarin et al., 2024). As a pioneer in
085   gradient-based adversarial prompt generation methods, GCG (Zou et al., 2023) achieves significant
086   success in jailbreaking white-box LLMs (Mazeika et al., 2024). To induce a safety-aligned LLM
087   to generate harmful content according to the user query, GCG appends an adversarial suffix to the
088
      original harmful request and iteratively modifies the adversarial suffix to encourage the model to
      output an affirmative target phrase, e.g., “Sure, here’s ...”. Optimization can be performed on multiple
089
      harmful requests and various models simultaneously to generate a universal adversarial suffix. This
090
      suffix can then be used to execute jailbreaks on different harmful requests and transfer to various
091   safety-aligned LLMs. Building on GCG, Li et al. (2024) further improves the optimization by
092   incorporating techiques from transfer-based attacks (Huang et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020). However,
093   these methods often produce garbled adversarial suffixes composed of incoherent words and symbols,
094   making it difficult to transfer them to other victim models with different architectures, parameters,
095   tokenizers, training data, and training methods. Some methods attempt to solve this problem by
096   training an adversarial suffix generator and posing a large number of queries (Liao & Sun, 2024),
097   or by accessing the probability of victim model’s output (Sitawarin et al., 2024; Andriushchenko
098   et al., 2024). Compared with them, our method requires a limited number of queries and no access
099
      to the output probabilities of victim model, which allows it to be applied to perform attacks in a
      more practical threat model. Another issue with the garbled adversarial suffixes is that they can be
100
      easily detected by perplexity-based defense strategies (Jain et al., 2023). To generate low-perplexity
101
      adversarial suffixes, AutoDAN (Zhu et al., 2023) regularizes the perplexity of adversarial suffixes
102   by an additional readability objective during gradient-based optimization. While it requires careful
103   hyper-parameter tuning to balance the jailbreaking and readability objectives. Our method does not
104   alter the generation of adversarial suffixes, thereby free of hyper-parameter tuning. An independent
105   work, AdvPrompter (Paulus et al., 2024) trains an additional LLM to generate human-readable
106   adversarial suffixes. In contrast, our method directly translates the garbled adversarial prompts
107   into human-readable adversarial prompts by utilizing an off-the-shelf LLM, thus eliminating extra
      computational costs for model training.

129   Another line of work uses LLMs as optimizers to jailbreak the victim LLM (Chao et al., 2023;
130   Mehrotra et al., 2023). These methods require high quality responses from victim model to help
131   the attacker LLM improves the adversarial prompt. While the victim model often generates refusal
132   content with limited information, such as “Sorry, but I can’t assist with that.”, leads to a challenge
133   for optimizing the adversarial prompts. Unlike them, our method conducts transfer attacks against
134   victim models without requiring feedback from the victim. There are also methods that design special
135
      jailbreaking templates or pipelines (Liu et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023; Deng
      et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024; Andriushchenko et al., 2024). Our method is fully
136
      automatic and does not require any manual work for the design of adversarial prompts, e.g., adding
137
      additional empirical rules like “for educational purposes”. Moreover, the interpretation of garbled
138   adversarial prompts offers a new approach to discovering effective designs of adversarial prompts.

      Preliminary explorations show that the garbled adversarial prompts generated by gradient-based
145   optimization are “relatively interpretable” (Zou et al., 2023). For instance, Table 1 shows two
146   adversarial suffixes generated on Llama-2-13B-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023) and Llama-3.1-8B-
147   Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), respectively, along with some examples of successful jailbreak attacks.
148   From the content of adversarial suffixes and the responses, it can be seen that adversarial suffixes
149   indeed contain some semantic meaning. Specifically, the adversarial suffix generated on Llama-
150   2-13B-Chat seems to instruct the model to provide information or insights about “Scotland” after
151   responding to the harmful request. Consequently, the suffix might divert model’s attention away from
152   the harmful request and to a more innocuous topic, thus misleading the model into responding to the
153
      harmful request as well. For the adversarial suffix generated on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, it perhaps
      requires the victim model to answer with a specific style (“straightforward and indifferent manner”
154
      in Example 3, and “a bit sarcastic” in Example 4) and sets a rule for the format of the response
155
      (“formatted as a single, uninterrupted sentence” in Example 4). In addition, a roleplay-like instruction
156   is also included in the adversarial suffix (“by Todd Ott” in Example 3), which is also found to be
157   useful in PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) and TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2023). Although garbled adversarial
158   prompts contain some semantic meaning to successfully exploit the vulnerabilities of the model
159   that is used to optimize them, their chaotic appearance makes it difficult to convey this adversarial
160   information to other victim LLMs trained on natural language data. We aim to address this issue by
161   generating coherent and human-readable natural language adversarial prompts that incorporate the
      adversarial information discovered by gradient-based methods.

195   We propose a novel method to enhance jailbreak attacks by automatically “translating” garbled
196
      adversarial prompts into interpretable and coherent natural language adversarial prompts using a
      translator LLM. Our method consists of two main steps: interpretation and translation. Given a
197
      garbled adversarial prompt, which consists of a harmful request (e.g., ‘How to build a bomb’) followed
198
      by a garbled adversarial suffix, along with the target string used to generate the garbled adversarial
199   suffix (e.g., ‘Sure, here’s ...’), we first state the effect of the adversarial suffix to the translator model,
200   i.e., the adversarial suffix causes the model to switch from refusing to answer the harmful request
201   to agreeing to respond, generating a response that begins with the target string. We then require
202   the translator model to interpret the semantic meaning contained in the garbled adversarial suffix.
203   Next, by combining these interpretations with the effect of the garbled adversarial suffix, we instruct
204   the translator model to translate the garbled adversarial prompt into a natural language adversarial
205   prompt. To achieve this, we directly utilize an off-the-shelf LLM, such as Llama (Dubey et al., 2024)
206   and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), as the translator LLMs. We develop prompt templates for both the
207
      interpretation and translation steps, as illustrated in Table 2. To induce better generation from the
      translator model, we prefill its response with the beginning strings: “Sure, here are interpretations
208
      of the suffix:” (for interpretation) and “Sure, here are {N} possible new prompts:” (for translation).
209
      Note that our prompt templates only describe the interpretation and translation tasks without any
210   additional tricks, such as role-playing or emotional manipulation used in PAIR and TAP. In this
211   way, we construct a fully automatic natural language adversarial prompt generation framework,
212   without any manual work for the design of adversarial prompts, careful hyper-parameter tuning,
213   additional model training, or the need for informative feedback of the black-box victim model to
214   refine the adversarial prompts. It can generate coherent and interpretable natural language adversarial
215   prompts that are understandable by the victim model, and remain stealthy against perplexity-based
      defenses (Jain et al., 2023).

      5       C ONCLUSIONS
477
478
      In this paper, we aim to make the garbled adversarial prompts generated by gradient-based methods
      more interpretable, thereby enhancing jailbreak attacks against various safety-aligned LLMs. To
479
      achieve this, we propose a novel method that automatically translates garbled adversarial prompts
480
      into coherent, human-readable, and interpretable natural language adversarial prompts using an
481   off-the-shelf LLM as the translator. Our method can uncover the semantic information that triggers
482   vulnerabilities of safety-aligned LLMs, offering a new approach to designing jailbreak prompts, and
483   aiding in the understanding of jailbreak attacks. Experimental results demonstrate that our method
484   significantly outperforms current state-of-the-arts in jailbreaking various safety-aligned LLMs.

1032
       advanced models affects attack performance. We conducted 7 × 7 × 5 × 3 experiments (7 choices
1033
       each for substitute and translator models, 5 independent runs per model pair, and attacks against
1034   3 victim models) on the HarmBench dataset to evaluate the effect of model selection. Following
1035   the suggestion, we selected the Llama-Instruct models (3.2-1B, 3.2-3B, and 3.1-8B) and Qwen2.5-
1036   Instruct models (0.5B, 3B, 7B, and 14B) as substitute and translator models. The victim models
1037   included GPT-4o-mini, Phi3.5-Mini-Instruct Abdin et al. (2024), and Gemma-2B-IT Team et al.
1038   (2024). The average attack success rate (AASR) across five runs for each pair of substitute and
1039   translator models is shown in Figures 6, 7 and 8 for attacking three victim models, respectively. To
1040   evaluate the effectiveness of each substitute (translator) model, we show their average AASR across
1041   different choices of translator (substitute) models in both the rightmost column and bottom row.
1042   Firstly, using larger models for both the substitute and translator models within the same architecture
1043   enhances attack performance. When attacking Phi-3.5-Mini-Instruct, for the choice of translator
1044   model, using Llama-3.x-Instruct, the 1B, 3B, and 8B models achieve average AASR of 36.5%, 42.7%,
1045   and 53.0%, respectively. When using Qwen-2.5-Instruct, the 0.5B, 3B, 7B, and 14B models achieve
1046   average AASR of 16.2%, 37.3%, 39.9%, and 51.5%, respectively. For the choice of substitute model,
1047   using Llama-3.x-Instruct, the 1B, 3B, and 8B models achieve average AASR of 25.0%, 39.5%, and
1048
       48.5%, respectively. When using Qwen-2.5-Instruct, the 0.5B, 3B, 7B, and 14B models achieve
       average AASR of 29.3%, 37.8%, 39.7%, and 57.3%, respectively. Similar observations can be found
1049
       in the results of attacking GPT-4o-mini and Gemma-2B-IT. The improved performance results from
1050
       the larger model capacity to understand the garbled adversarial prompts.
1051
1052   Secondly, with the same model size, using Llama-3.x provides better attack performance when
1053   serving as both substitute and translator models. Specifically, when attacking Phi-3.5-Mini-Instruct,
1054
       for the selection of the translator model, using Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct performs better than Qwen2.5-
       3B-Instruct (42.7% vs. 37.3% in average AASR). For the choice of substitute model, Llama-3.2-
1055
       3B-Instruct achieves a 39.5% average AASR, while Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct achieves 37.8%. When
1056
       attacking GPT-4o-mini, using Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct produces significantly better results compared
1057   with Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, i.e., 40.8% vs. 26.5% in average AASR when serving as the translator
1058   model, and 40.9% vs. 27.6% when serving as the substitute model. The same observation can be
1059   found when attacking Gemma-2B-IT. Comparing Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct,
1060   it achieves 37.9% vs. 21.6% and 32.0% vs. 21.7% in average AASR for serving as the translator

1062   better than Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct for serving as the translator model (53.0% vs. 51.5% for attacking

1064   attacking Gemma-2B-IT, in average AASR). The Llama-3.x-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Instruct models
1065
       differ in many aspects. For instance, they vary in training data (e.g., size, quality, etc.), training

1066
       the number of layers, number of attention heads, dimensions of hidden states, etc.). It is challenging
1067
       to investigate which specific factors contribute to this phenomenon, since it would require an immense
1068   amount of work and resources to train a large number of LLMs with different configurations.

1071
       Specifically, when attacking Phi-3.5-Mini-Instruct, using Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct as the substitute

1074   of transfer-based attacks depends on similar vulnerabilities between substitute and victim models.

1078   substitute model on the output of the victim model to improve jailbreak performance. Regarding the
1079   selection of the translator model, it can be seen that using Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct is always better
