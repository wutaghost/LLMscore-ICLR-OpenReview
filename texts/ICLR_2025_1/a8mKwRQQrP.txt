009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We tackle online inventory problems where at each time period the manager makes
012            a replenishment decision based on partial historical information in order to meet
013            demands and minimize costs. To solve such problems, we build upon recent works
014            in online learning and control, use insights from inventory theory and propose
015            a new algorithm called GAPSI. This algorithm follows a new feature-enhanced
016            base-stock policy and deals with the troublesome question of non-differentiability
017            which occurs in inventory problems. Our method is illustrated in the context of a
018            complex and novel inventory system involving multiple products, lost sales, per-
019
               ishability, warehouse-capacity constraints and lead times. Extensive numerical
               simulations are conducted to demonstrate the good performances of our algorithm
020
               on real-world data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025
      Inventory control is a standard problem in operations research and operations management where
026
      a manager needs to make replenishment decisions in order to minimize costs and meet demands
027   (Nahmias, 2011; Roldán et al., 2017). Classical inventory theories focus on the optimization side
028   of the problem, that is, determining what is the optimal replenishment strategy assuming all the
029   parameters of the inventory model are known. For instance, the manager knows future demands or
030   the distribution they are drawn from. However, in real-world scenarios an inventory manager rather
031   faces optimization and learning problems presented in a sequential fashion, that is, at each time
032   period the manager makes its replenishment decisions based on past observations. For these reasons,
033   machine learning frameworks such as online learning or reinforcement learning seem particularly
034   well-suited to solve these realistic inventory problems. For instance, there is now an important body
035
      of literature that builds upon techniques from online convex optimization like online gradient descent
      (Zinkevich, 2003) to solve inventory problems (Hihat et al., 2024). However, they are extremely
036
      specialized, designed for specific inventory models involving specific dynamics, cost structures and
037
      demand processes (see, for example, Huh & Rusmevichientong, 2009; Shi et al., 2016; Zhang et al.,
038   2018). On the other hand, other generic approaches for control problems such as Model Predictive
039   Control (MPC) (Mattingley et al., 2011) suffer from a prohibitive computational cost and a lack of
040   scalability.
041
042   Contributions. In this paper, we first show how realistic, general inventory problems fit within the
043   recent Online Policy Selection (OPS) framework of Lin et al. (2024) which is at the crossroads of
044   control and online learning. We detail how various constraints very common in the industry, such as
045   perishability, lead times, or warehouse capacity constraints, can be mathematically modeled in this
046   setting. We obtain a general online inventory problem, that can be simplified and applied to several
047   more classical problems such as usual perishable inventory systems (Nahmias, 2011).
048   We then present a new algorithm for solving this online optimization problem, called GAPSI. This
049   algorithm is adapted from GAPS (Lin et al., 2024) to take into account specific aspects of inventory
050   problems, in particular the fact that the functions we are dealing with are not differentiable. We find
051   that this non-differentiability problem cannot be ignored, since it leads to undesirable behaviors, and
052   we show how carefully selected derivatives for the policies and Adagrad-style learning rates solves
053   it. We also propose a new policy which draws on classical base-stock policies (Snyder & Shen,
      2019, Section 4.3.1) while dealing with uncertainty in future demand. The idea is to learn a target


                                                        1
054
      level which writes as a linear function of either past demand features or forecasts. In this way, we
055   obtain a general online method for solving real-world inventory problems that can take into account
056   many different constraints and domain-specific knowledge about demands. For example, if we know
057   that demand has a weekly seasonality, then this knowledge can be incorporated into the algorithm
058   by using indicators of the day of the week as features.
059
      Finally, we provide extensive experiments which demonstrate the good performance of GAPSI com-
060
      pared to classical approaches. We observe that GAPSI performs particularly well when demands are
061   not stationary, have some changes of regime, and when the features are well-chosen. We emphasise
062   that in this work we do not only propose an efficient new algorithm, but also want to show that online
063   learning is a promising approach for realistic inventory problems, and therefore draw the attention
064   of this community to these problems.
065
066   Related works. Many online learning frameworks have been applied or adapted to inventory prob-
067   lems. For instance, in the absence of inventory dynamics, Levina et al. (2010) see their problem as
068   prediction with expert advice and Lugosi et al. (2024) use partial monitoring. In the presence of
069   dynamics, the situation is much more complex, and this is what interests us. The research most
070   closely related to this paper typically considers Online Convex Optimization (OCO) (Zinkevich,
071   2003) as their main learning framework. This is the case of Huh & Rusmevichientong (2009), Shi
072
      et al. (2016), Zhang et al. (2018), Zhang et al. (2020), Guo et al. (2024). The common approach in
      this literature is to adapt OCO techniques, such as Online Gradient Descent (OGD), in various ways
073
      to handle inventory dynamics. However, this is mostly done on a case-by-case basis: specialized
074
      algorithms are designed for specific dynamics. So the manager can only implement these solutions
075   if they are facing a very similar problem.
076
077   The reason why most inventory problems are not instances of classical online learning problems is
078
      that they lack a notion central in control problems: that of a dynamical system influenced by the
      manager’s decisions and impacting the losses. Incorporating this notion in online learning leads us
079
      to the literature of online control. The latter has mostly focused on linear dynamics, as in Agarwal
080
      et al. (2019), but most inventory dynamics are not linear. Among the recent developments in online
081   control, the OPS framework of Lin et al. (2024), presented in Subsection 2.1, provides much more
082   flexibility by allowing for general dynamics. Nevertheless, some challenges remain when consid-
083   ering inventory problems through OPS among which the non-differentiability of the losses, policies
084   and dynamics.
085
      On the other hand, there exist general-purpose control techniques which are not specific to inventory
086
      problems, in particular, Model Predictive Control (MPC) (Mattingley et al., 2011), which became
087   popular in the 1980s. The main idea is to solve an optimization problem at each time step based on
088   a predictive model up to a receding planning horizon. These approaches are not the main subject of
089   this paper, but we consider MPC as a competitor in the experimental section.
090
091   Overview. We start in Section 2 by introducing the OPS framework and show through various
092   examples that it is well-adapted to model realistic inventory problems. Then, we present in Section
093   3 our algorithm, GAPSI, that is based on GAPS (Lin et al., 2024) while taking into account aspects
094   specific to inventory management, via the use of a feature-enhanced base-stock policy, AdaGrad
095   learning rates and carefully chosen partial derivatives. We conclude in Section 4 with an extensive
096   numerical simulation study. We refer to the appendix for precise mathematical details and additional
097   experiments.

      3.3   T HE TROUBLESOME COMPUTATION OF JACOBIANS FOR NONSMOOTH FUNCTIONS
274
275   As described in Section 3.2, at each iteration we need to compute the jacobians1 of the functions
276   πt , ft , and ℓt . A striking feature of these functions is that none of them is differentiable, due to the
277   presence of positive parts in their definition (see Sections 2.2 and 3.1). In standard machine learn-
278   ing, non-differentiability may cause theoretical difficulties (Bolte & Pauwels, 2021), but in practice
279   it is usually not a problem: most neural network architectures include ReLU (the positive part func-
280   tion) but still perform perfectly well. This apparent contradiction can be ignored by observing that,
281
      in general, points of non-differentiability are never reached during training (Bertoin et al., 2021,
      Theorem 2).
282
283   However, this story appears to be surprisingly different for online inventory problems. First, different
284   choices of subgradients can lead to drastically different trajectories for GAPSI, and some of them
285   can lead to disastrous performance. Second, in some real-world scenarios most jacobians simply
286   cannot be accessed. Therefore, the main message of this section is that one cannot blindly rely
287
      on automatic differentiation for such nonsmooth online problems. We explain below where those
      problems come from, and how to avoid them.
288
289
      Differentiating the policy πt . Imagine a scenario in which the demand for a product is zero on
290
      a given interval of time, pushing the manager to reduce the corresponding stock to zero. Then
291
      arises the question of what happens when the demand becomes positive again. One would expect
292   that the manager starts to order again the said product. But it appears that this depends heavily on
293   how the partial derivatives of πt are computed. To see this, look at Figure 1 where we simulate a
294   simple problem where the demand is 0 for 100 days, and then switches to 1 for the next 100 days.
295   When running GAPSI with standard autodifferentiation rules for derivating πt , one can see that after
296   the 100th day the base-stock level remains stationary: the manager keeps the inventory level to 0,
297   missing numerous sales. A simple analysis (see Appendix B.2 for the details) shows that because
298   autodiff computes the left-partial derivative of πt , as soon as θt = 0 the parameter will remain
299   this way even if the demand restarts. Therefore, we advocate for always taking the right-partial
300
      derivatives of πt . Our custom differentiation rule can be seen in action in Figure 1. Whenever the
      level reaches zero, our differentiation rule implies that a negative gradient is computed, therefore
301
      increasing the level. This leads to oscillations which decay thanks to the Adagrad learning rate (2).
302
      On the other hand, as soon as the level reaches zero, auto-differentiation leads to a zero gradient,
303   independently of the demand which leads to this undesirable stationary behavior. We highlight that
304   zero demand for a long time interval is not specific to the above toy problem, but can often be
305   observed in real-world problems (see Figure 4 for example).

      In this section we evaluate empirically the performances of GAPSI. We refer to Appendix C for

341
      Datasets. In this section, we use two real-world datasets, the M5 dataset provided by the company
342
      Walmart (Makridakis et al., 2022), and a proprietary dataset from the company Califrais, a food
343
      supply chain start-up. Both datasets include multiple sales time series over horizons of T = 1969
344   and T = 860 days respectively. The time series are organized hierarchically: the total demand is
345   split into categories which are split into subcategories, which are finally split into products. These
346   datasets are therefore very rich depending on where we place ourselves in the hierarchy, the total
347   demand (root of the hierarchy) having much less variability than the demand of one specific product
348   (leaves of the hierarchy). We will use different levels in this hierarchy, treating the time series as
349   demand for a single product, even if it is actually aggregated over different products (of a category
350   or all of them in the case of the total demand).
351
352   Metrics. To compare algorithms, our metric throughout the section is the ratio of cumulative losses
353   between the considered algorithm and the best stationary base-stock policy ST∗ . This policy picks
354   the single best base-stock level independently of time, given the demand realizations over a horizon
355
      T . It is therefore an oracle in the sense that it sees future demands and cannot be implemented in
      practice, and makes an assumption of stationarity. This metric follows the standard approach in
356
      online learning, which consists in comparing an algorithm to a constant strategy. A ratio below one
357
      therefore means that the algorithm considered has a better performance than ST∗ , and is equivalent to
358   having a negative regret in online learning. In the appendix, we complete the results with two other
359   metrics adapted to inventory problems, the lost sales and outdating percentage.

      5     C ONCLUSION
505
506
      In this paper, we used techniques from online learning and insights from inventory control theory
507
      to address realistic inventory problems. We showed that the recent framework of Online Policy
508
      Selection (OPS) (Lin et al., 2024), at the crossroads of online learning and control, is well-adapted
509   to model complex inventory problems involving, for instance, multiple products, perishability, order
510   lead times and warehouse-capacity constraints. To address such problems, we designed GAPSI, a
511   new online algorithm, and showed its efficiency through extensive numerical simulations.
512
      However, the question of theoretical guarantees remains open. The standard assumptions of OPS
513
      are not satisfied for the inventory problems we consider. Indeed, policies, losses and transitions are
514
      not differentiable so that both classical chain rules and smoothness do not hold. Furthermore, the
515   contraction property used in Lin et al. (2024) does not seem to hold. Proving regret bounds without
516   these assumptions is very challenging.
517
518
      The work of Bolte & Pauwels (2021) could be a promising way of handling the non-differentiability
      issue. They develop a flexible calculus theory for a new notion of generalized derivatives, allowing
519
      to consider chain rules for functions that are not differentiable in the classical sense.

1441   In this series of simulations, we evaluate GAPSI as an algorithm for learning stationary base-stock
1442   policies in the context of classical perishable inventory systems. More precisely, we consider the
1443   experimental setup of Bu et al. (2023, Subsection 7.1). In this setup, there is a single product K = 1,
1444   which is perishable with a lifetime of m = 3 periods and has no order lead time, L = 0, and no
1445   warehouse-capacity constraint Vt = +∞. Its demand is drawn independently across time periods
1446   from a Poisson distribution with mean 5. The losses include purchase costs, holding costs, penalty
1447
       costs and outdating costs with time-invariant unit costs.
1448   According to the simulations of Bu et al. (2023), in this setup, the best stationary base-stock policy
