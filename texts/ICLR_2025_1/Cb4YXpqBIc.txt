009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Most existing studies on few-shot learning focus on unimodal settings, where
012            models are trained to generalize on unseen data using only a small number of
013            labeled examples from the same modality. However, real-world data are inherently
014            multi-modal, and unimodal approaches limit the practical applications of few-
015            shot learning. To address this gap, this paper introduces the Cross-modal Few-
016
               Shot Learning (CFSL) task, which aims to recognize instances from multiple
               modalities when only a few labeled examples are available. This task presents
017
               additional challenges compared to classical few-shot learning due to the distinct
018
               visual characteristics and structural properties unique to each modality.
019
               To tackle these challenges, we propose a Generative Transfer Learning (GTL)
020            framework consisting of two stages: the first stage involves training on abun-
021            dant unimodal data, and the second stage focuses on transfer learning to adapt
022            to novel data. Our GTL framework jointly estimates the latent shared concept
023            across modalities and in-modality disturbance in both stages, while freezing the
024            generative module during the transfer phase to maintain the stability of the learned
025            representations and prevent overfitting to the limited multi-modal samples. Our
026            finds demonstrate that GTL has superior performance compared to state-of-the-
027            art methods across four distinct multi-modal datasets: S KETCHY, TU-B ERLIN,
028
               M ASK 1K, and SKSF-A. Additionally, the results suggest that the model can
               estimate latent concepts from vast unimodal data and generalize these concepts to
029
               unseen modalities using only a limited number of available samples, much like
030
               human cognitive processes.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Collecting large amounts of labeled data in real-world applications is often prohibitively expensive,
036   time-consuming, or simply impractical (Tharwat & Schenck, 2023; Sheng et al., 2024). Few-shot
037   learning (FSL) has emerged as a viable solution, enabling models to generalize effectively using
038   only a handful of labeled examples (Song et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023; Ke et al.,
039
      2024). However, existing few-shot methods face significant challenges in handling the increasing
      prevalence of multi-modal data, such as multi-spectral images or multimedia content, which differs
040
      significantly from the extensive RGB data typically used in research. Multi-modal data, collected
041
      from multiple different types of sources and modalities such as different sensors or imaging protocols,
042   is becoming increasingly essential in applications like surveillance (Wu et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2022)
043   and medical image analysis (Jiang et al., 2023b; Mok et al., 2024). These challenges highlight the
044   need for more advanced FSL frameworks capable of leveraging the complementary information
045   inherent in multi-modal data (Luo et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023a).
046
      Recent efforts have leveraged large pre-trained foundation models to extend their capabilities to novel
047
      multi-modal tasks, including tabular data (Ye et al., 2024), audio (Lin et al., 2023b; Duan et al., 2024),
048   and video classification (Qing et al., 2023), moving beyond traditional unimodal tasks like image
049   classification (Conti et al., 2023). Despite these advancements, the transfer of visual knowledge
050   across different visual modalities remains relatively underexplored. Visual data, such as images and
051   videos, constitute the most commonly studied data types; however, other visual modalities, such
052   as infrared, depth, and sketches, exhibit both shared characteristics and distinct differences. These
053   modalities share structural and contextual similarities with RGB data but possess unique attributes
      that make data collection and model adaptation more challenging.

076   To address these challenges, this paper introduces a new Cross-modal Few-Shot Learning (CFSL)
077   task, which aims to classify instances with multiple visual modalities using only a few labeled
078   samples per class. In this task, the multi-modal data are organized into a support set and a query set.
079   The support set contains a small number of labeled multi-modal examples per class, serving as the
080   basis for learning. The query set includes unlabeled instances from the same classes and modalities
081
      that need to be classified. The ultimate goal is to train a model solely on the support set that can
      accurately classify the query set, regardless of modality. As illustrated in Figures 1(a-c), unlike
082
      classical supervised learning and FSL confined to a single modality, CFSL incorporates data from
083
      multiple visual modalities. The primary challenge of CFSL arises from the inherent variability and
084   domain gaps between different visual modalities, which complicates feature extraction and alignment.
085   Additionally, the task requires models to identify and leverage shared underlying semantics across
086   modalities while adapting to the unique characteristics of each.
087
      Previous studies have shown that compact visual representations are closely linked to real-world
088
      images, emphasizing the importance of underlying concepts in enabling humans to identify target
089
      objects (Vinker et al., 2022; 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2024). As illustrated in Figure 1d, these
090   fundamental concepts can be readily learned with only a few examples (Lake & Piantadosi, 2020),
091   thanks to the humans’ ability to generalize from limited data, regardless of visual modality. This
092   capability, referred to as “evocation”, is grounded in the vast reservoir of previously encountered
093   visual experiences that support recognition and generalization. Inspired by this observation, we
094   hypothesize that models can achieve similar evocative capabilities by learning latent concepts from
095   abundant unimodal data.
096   We propose a Generative Transfer Learning (GTL) framework to facilitate knowledge transfer between
097   unimodal and multi-modal data. In the GTL framework, we posit that the latent concepts underlying
098   target objects consist of two components: (1) an intrinsic concept that captures the core characteristic
099   shared across modalities, and (2) an in-modality disturbance that accounts for variations unique to
100   each modality. Our approach aims to estimate these latent components and encode the relationships
101   between the intrinsic concepts and visual content. By disentangling the intrinsic concepts from the
102   in-modality disturbances, the GTL framework enables adapting to multi-modal data while preserving
103   the transferable relationships learned from unimodal data, thereby improving both adaptability and
104
      accuracy across various modalities.
105   Within the GTL framework, our methodology consists of a two-stage training process. In the first
106   stage, the generative learning stage, the model learns latent concepts label-free, relying solely on
107   pre-trained visual representations. This stage focuses on capturing the intrinsic concept and the
      variations in visual content across modalities, ensuring that the learned relationship between the latent

      concept and visual content is robust and transferable. In the second stage, the recognition stage, the
109   backbone network is frozen, and a separate classifier is trained on top of the learned latent intrinsic
110   concepts to perform label prediction. This two-stage framework enables the model to disentangle
111   intrinsic visual concepts from in-modality disturbances, facilitating effective recognition across visual
112   modalities.
113
      Our contributions are summarised as follows: (i) We introduce a new cross-modal few-shot learning
114
      task, which focuses on the connection and distinction between visual modalities, requiring models
115   to perform recognition on multi-modal data with minimal labeled samples. This task better reflects
116   real-world scenarios, where multi-modal data is scarce and diverse. (ii) We propose the generative
117   transfer learning framework, designed to disentangle intrinsic concepts from in-modality disturbances,
118   enabling efficient knowledge adaption across modalities. (iii) We demonstrate the effectiveness of our
119   method through extensive experiments on multiple cross-modal datasets, including S KETCHY (Sangk-
120   loy et al., 2016), TU-B ERLIN (Eitz et al., 2012), the fine-grained biometric dataset M ASK 1K (Lin
121   et al., 2023a) and SKSF-A (Yun et al., 2024).

      2    TASK S ETTINGS
124
125
      In this section, we formally define the proposed CFSL task and highlight its differences from
126
      the previous FSL task. Additionally, we provide an overview of the CFSL task, emphasizing the
127
      challenges posed by handling multiple visual modalities with limited labeled data.
128
129
130
      Dataset Setup For the proposed CFSL task, the dataset D = {(xim , y i ), y i ∈ Y } comprises a
      base unimodal dataset Dbase = {(xim , y i ), y i ∈ Ybase , m = 1} and a novel multimodal dataset
131
      Dnovel = {(xim , y i ), m ∈ {m1 , m2 , . . . , md }, y i ∈ Ynovel }. Here, xim denotes the feature vector of
132   the i-th sample in modality m, y i is the corresponding class label. Ybase and Ynovel represent the set of
133   class labels for the base and novel datasets, respectively. Notably, the class labels between the base
134   and novel datasets are disjoint, aka, Ybase ∩ Ynovel = ∅, such that Ybase ∪ Ynovel = Y .
135
136
      In classical FSL tasks, the goal is to train a model on Dbase with labels Ybase and transfer this
      knowledge to improve the recognition performance on novel classes Ynovel with the same modality,
137
      using only a few labeled examples for each class. In contrast, our CFSL task introduces the added
138   challenge of handling multi-modal data in the novel dataset Dnovel while the base dataset Dbase
139   contains only unimodal data (e.g., RGB images). As shown in Figure 2a, there is a clear boundary
140   among the multi-modal data. This setting reflects real-world scenarios where models are typically
141   trained on unimodal data but are expected to generalize effectively to multi-modal data.
142
      The novel dataset Dnovel is further divided into:
143
144
             • A support set Dsupport = {(xim , y i )|m ∈ {m1 , m2 , . . . , md }, y i ∈ Ynovel }, containing a
145            limited number of labeled samples per class from multiple modalities. Typically, this
146            consists of K labeled samples for each of N classes.
147
148
             • A query set Dquery = {xjm |m ∈ {m1 , m2 , . . . , md }}, containing a larger number of unla-
               beled samples from the same classes as Dsupport , but potentially from the different modalities.
149
150
      The fundamental challenge in this task is the limited labeled data in Dsupport , combined with the
151   multi-modal nature of the data in Dnovel . The scarcity of labeled data per class, along with the need to
152   generalize across different modalities, makes this task significantly more challenging.
153
154
      Task Overview To classify the samples in Dquery , the proposed CFSL involves a feature extraction
155
      function eΦ , which is initially pre-trained on the large and fully labeled base dataset Dbase . The goal
156   is to adapt eΦ to eΦ′ , allowing it to extract discriminative features from all modalities in Dnovel for
157   classification. The adapted feature extractor eΦ′ should ensure that samples from the same class y i
158   are mapped close to each other in the feature space, enabling accurate classification regardless of the
159   modality m. This adaptation process should help the classifier cϕ , parameterized by ϕ, in predicting
160   the correct class labels for samples in Dquery using features produced by eΦ′ . The adaptation allows
161   the model to generalize across the novel modalities and classes, even with very few labeled examples
      in the support set.

      3.4   T RAINING AND I NFERENCE
314
315   The operational framework of GTL is depicted in Figure 3, details the training and adaptation process
316   for CFSL scenarios. This section outlines the workflow, from initial training on the base dataset to
317   subsequent adaptation for novel data, clarifying the methodologies employed in each phase.
318
319   Phase 1: Training on Base Data The initial training phase is fundamental as it establishes the
320   distinction between transferable and non-transferable components within the model. During this
321   phase, all modules are trained using the base dataset Dbase . Specifically, the posterior estimator (α),
322   the disturbance estimator and aggregator (η and δ), and the generator (θ) are jointly trained using
323   Eq. 12. Afterward, the classifier (ϕ) is trained using Eq. 13. The primary goal is to robustly encode
      domain-specific variations and content-specific features into separate latent spaces.


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Phase 2: Transfer Learning for Adapting to Novel Data In this phase, the model is exposed
325   to a novel data support set Dsupport . Given our assumption that the relationship between latent
326   representations and visual content remains consistent across both base and novel datasets, we freeze
327   the generator (θ) in its trained state. This decision ensures that the foundational decoding process,
328   which reconstructs visual content from latent representations, remains stable and unaffected by
329   new data variability. We first fine-tune the posterior estimator (α), the disturbance estimator, and
330   aggregator (η and δ) using Eq. 12, and then update the classifier (ϕ) with a few labeled examples
331   using Eq. 13.
332
333   Inference with Novel Data Query Set In the inference phase, samples from the novel data query
334
      set Dquery are first processed by our posterior estimator (α), which generates latent intrinsic concepts
      based on the learned model. These representations are then used by the trained classifier (ϕ) to make
335
      predictions. We select the prediction ŷ with the maximum value, which corresponds to the highest
336
      predicted probability across all possible classes, to determine the most likely class label.

345
      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
346   Datasets We conduct experiments on multiple cross-modal datasets, including two object multi-
347   modal datasets and two biometric multi-modal datasets. Given that sketch is the most common and ac-
348   cessible visual modality beyond RGB, we use multi-modal datasets consisting of both RGB and sketch
349   data to verify the proposed method. For the object multi-modal datasets, S KETCHY (Sangkloy et al.,
350   2016) contains 75,471 sketches and 73,002 images across 125 categories, while TU-B ERLIN (Eitz
351   et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2016) includes 20,000 sketches and 204,489 images spanning 250 categories.
352   For the more challenging biometric multi-modal datasets which exhibit less inter-class variance,
353   M ARKET-S KETCH -1K (M ASK 1K) (Lin et al., 2023a) contains over 4,700 sketches representing 996
354
      identities in 6 styles, paired with 20,480 matching photographs from M ARKET 1501 (Zheng et al.,
      2015). Additionally, we use SKSF-A (Yun et al., 2024), consists of 938 face-sketch pairs of 134
355
      identities across 7 distinct styles.
356
357
      Dataset Split For experiments on S KETCHY, we follow the dataset splitting scheme used by Bhunia
358   et al. (2022), which contains 64 base classes, while the remaining 61 classes are used as the novel set
359   to make the task more challenging. For TU-B ERLIN, we split into 125 base and 125 novel classes
360   following Bandyopadhyay et al. (2024). For the smaller biometric multi-modal dataset, all data in
361   M ASK 1K and SKSF-A are considered novel; we use commonly adopted person and face datasets
362   MSMT17 (Wei et al., 2018) and CelebA-HQ (Karras et al., 2018) as base sets for person and face
363   recognition, respectively. Each base set is further divided into training, validation, and testing subsets
364   (60%:20%:20%) following Bhunia et al. (2022).
365
366   Evaluation Protocol We report the results under two settings: all-way-k-shot (Ju et al., 2022; Li
367   et al., 2024b) and standard 5-way-k-shot (Luo et al., 2023). In the all-way-k-shot setting, all classes
368
      are presented to the model, with k examples randomly sampled per class to form the support set S,
      while the remaining examples constitute the query set Q. In the 5-way-k-shot setting, 5 classes are
369
      randomly selected per episode, and for each of these 5 classes, k examples are sampled to form the
370
      support set S, with the remaining examples used for the query set Q.
371
372   In both settings, k denotes the number of labeled samples per class in the support set S. After splitting
373   the data into a base set and a novel set comprising S ∪ Q, k determines how many samples per class
374
      are included in S, with the remaining samples forming Q.
375   Evaluation metric We compare model performance using Top-1 Accuracy over the novel query set,
376   referred to as Acc@avg, which measures the proportion of instances where the model’s top predicted
377   label matches the true label across mixed modalities. Additionally, we report the average Top-1
      accuracy within each modality.

480   distributions. We trained models on base unimodal data, novel multimodal mixed data, RGB data,
481   and sketch data. As shown in Figure 5, pair plots of the generator’s outputs depict the relationship

483   novel data distributions supports our assumption. Notably, the overlap with sketch data is greater
484   than with RGB data from the novel set, likely due to differences in underlying data distributions and
485   content diversity. Sketches capture fundamental structures common to the base data, leading to closer
      alignment in the latent space.

520
      5   C ONCLUSIONS
521
522
      In this work, we tackled the limitations of unimodal few-shot learning by introducing the cross-modal
523
      few-shot learning task, which addresses real-world scenarios involving multiple visual modalities
524   with only a few labeled examples. Unlike classical few-shot learning, our task presents additional
525   challenges due to the inherent variability in visual characteristics, structural properties, and domain
526   gaps between modalities.
527
      To overcome these challenges, we proposed the generative transfer learning framework, designed
528
      to enable efficient knowledge transfer from abundant unimodal data to data-scarce multi-modal
529
      scenarios. By estimating shared latent concepts from unimodal data and generalizing them to
530   unseen modalities, the GTL framework effectively disentangles modality-independent representations
531   from in-modality disturbances. Our experimental results demonstrated that our GTL framework
532   significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on four multi-modal datasets: Sketchy, TU-Berlin,
533   Mask1K, and SKSF-A.
534
535   Limitations: While our approach demonstrates strong performance, the lack of diverse visual
536   multi-modal datasets for visual recognition remains a significant challenge. Most existing datasets
537   focus on a limited number of visual modalities (e.g., RGB and sketch), restricting the evaluation
538   of models designed to handle more complex visual data. Future work will require more extensive
539   and varied multi-modal datasets to fully explore and validate the potential of cross-modal few-shot
      learning in a broader range of visual contexts.

      A.1    R ELATION TO U NIMODAL F EW- SHOT L EARNING
813
814   In general, unimodal learning tasks can be broadly categorized into three types based on the availabil-
815   ity and quantity of labeled data: (i) Supervised Learning, where a large amount of labeled data is
816   available for training, allowing models to learn features and perform accurate recognition (Yan et al.,
817   2023a; Han et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a); (ii) Few-shot Learning, where only a limited number of
818   labeled samples are provided for each class, challenging the model to generalize effectively from
819   minimal data (Chen et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023); and (iii) Zero-shot Learning, where no labeled
820   examples are available for certain classes (Wei et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Mirza et al., 2024).
821   Specifically, Few-shot learning (FSL) encompasses a training phase where a model is trained on
822   a relatively large dataset and an adaptation phase in which the trained model is adjusted to previ-
823   ously unseen tasks with limited labeled samples. Most existing FSL tasks utilize unimodal datasets
824   for training and testing, including popular benchmarks such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), CI-
825   FAR (Oreshkin et al., 2018), CUB-200-2021 (Wah et al., 2011), and Stanford Dogs (Khosla et al.,
826   2011). FSL typically involves three main approaches: (i) Meta learning (Sun et al., 2019; Ma et al.,
827
      2024), or learning to learn, which optimizes model parameters across diverse learning tasks to enable
      rapid adaptation to new challenges; (ii) Data-centric learning (Li et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2023;
828
      Ma et al., 2024), which focuses on metric learning to compare distances between samples or expand
829
      synthetic data facing with data-scarce scenarios; (iii) Transfer learning (Tian et al., 2020; Luo et al.,
830   2023; Zhang et al., 2024) where models pre-trained on large-scale datasets are fine-tuned on few-shot
831   tasks to improve performance to improve performance by leveraging learned representations for more
832   efficient adaptation.
833
      The adaption-based methods (Sun et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2024) are densely connected to the model
834
      design, which attempts to directly establish a mapping function between input and prediction. By
835
      rapidly updating parameters on new tasks with a small number of samples, these methods facilitate
836   the transfer of knowledge from previously learned tasks, making them highly effective in few-shot
837   learning scenarios.
838
839
      The data-centric methods utilize synthetic data (Meng et al., 2023) or metric learning (Ma et al.,
      2024) to adapt data-insufficient scenarios. The former involves generation methods like Generative
840
      Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Li et al., 2020) and auto-encoders (Yan et al., 2023b). These approaches
841
      generate additional training data to enhance the model’s performance, mitigating the scarcity of
842   labeled examples and improving generalization to new tasks. The latter, metric learning, tries to
843   build data connections with the thoughts of nearby neighbors, focusing on learning a distance metric
844   that clusters similar examples together and separates dissimilar ones. These data-centric methods
845   emphasize the properties of the data to enhance the model’s ability to generalize from a few examples.
846
      The fine-tuning-based methods (Tian et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) involve using
847
      pre-trained models on large datasets and fine-tuning them on the target task with a small number of
848   examples. This approach leverages the knowledge gained from the pre-training phase and transfers it
849   to the specific requirements of the new task. However, with the increasing amount of raw data on
850   the Internet, it’s challenging for pre-trained models, including Vision-Language Pretraining Models
851   (VLM) (Zhu et al., 2024), to generalize to specific novel data, especially when the data is in different
852   modalities.
853
      Recent works on cross-domain learning (Wang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023) also focus
854   exclusively on learning from unimodal data. However, their limitations become apparent in complex
855   real-world applications that often require understanding multiple modalities simultaneously. In this
856   work, we extend its applicability of few-shot learning to real-world scenarios where data come from
857   diverse visual modalities.
858

      et al., 2024), or tabular data Liu et al. (2022); Ye et al. (2024); Han et al. (2024). These approaches

898   In contrast, our work specifically targets the problem of Cross-modal Few-Shot Learning (CFSL)

900

901
      the luxury of abundant multi-modal annotations. Unlike existing methods that leverage auxiliary
902   modalities to aid a primary modality, we aim to develop a model capable of understanding and
903   generalizing across diverse visual modalities in a few-shot setting.

905
      In summary, while existing multi-modal FSL methods aim to enhance performance by integrating
      different data types, our work addresses the unique challenges of cross-modal recognition within
906
      visual data. We emphasize the importance of transferring knowledge from abundant unimodal data to
907
      novel visual modalities in a few-shot context, without relying on auxiliary modalities for support.
908   Our approach better reflects real-world challenges and contributes a novel perspective to FSL.
