011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have emerged as a promising tech-
014            nique solving partial differential equations (PDEs). However, PINNs face chal-
015
               lenges in resource efficiency (e.g., repeatedly sampling of collocation points) and
               achieving fast convergence to accurate solutions. To address these issues, adaptive
016
               sampling methods that focus on collocation points with high residual values have
017
               been proposed, enhancing both resource efficiency and solution accuracy. While
018            these high residual-based sampling methods have demonstrated exceptional per-
019            formance in solving certain stiff PDEs, their potential drawbacks, particularly the
020            relative neglect of points with medium and low residuals, remain under-explored.
021            In this paper, we investigate the limitations of high residual-based methods con-
022            cerning learning stability as model complexity increases. We provide a theoreti-
023            cal analysis demonstrating that high residual-based methods require tighter upper
024            bound on the learning rate to maintain stability. To overcome this limitation, we
025            present a novel Langevin dynamics-based PINN (L-PINN) framework for adap-
026
               tive sampling of collocation points, which is designed to improve learning sta-
               bility and convergence speed. To validate the effectiveness, we evaluated the L-
027
               PINN framework against existing adaptive sampling approaches for PINNs. Our
028
               results indicate that the L-PINN framework achieves superior relative L2 error
029            performance in solutions while demonstrating faster or comparable convergence
030            stability. Furthermore, we demonstrated that our framework exhibits robust per-
031            formance across a range of model complexities, indicating its potential for com-
032            patibility with larger neural network size in addressing challenging PDEs.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Partial differential equations (PDEs) are crucial for describing various physical phenomenon such as
038   heat transfer (Haghighat et al., 2021, Cai et al., 2021b), flow dynamics (Shi et al., 2021, Jagtap et al.,
039
      2022, Nazari et al., 2022), propagation dynamics (Pettit & Wilson, 2020, bin Waheed et al., 2021),
      optics and epidemiology (Lin & Chen, 2022, Rodrı́guez et al., 2023). Getting accurate and efficient
040
      solutions to PDEs is essential across numerous industries reliant on these descriptions. With ad-
041
      vancements in deep learning, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have emerged as a promis-
042   ing method for solving PDEs. The training process of collocation-based PINNs involves minimizing
043   total errors, including initial condition (IC), boundary condition (BC), and PDE errors measured at
044   collocation points (Nabian et al., 2021, Zeng et al., 2022, Gao & Wang, 2023, Toloubidokhti et al.,
045   2024, Lau et al., 2024). In particular, IC, BC, and PDE errors are incorporated as soft constraints on
046   experimental data, ensuring that the predicted solutions satisfy these essential requirements.
047
      This collocation-based learning method enhances the capability of PINNs by reducing the need for
048   extensive experimental data collection across spatio-temporal ranges, demonstrating success in var-
049   ious industries as a promising alternative to traditional numerical methods like the finite difference
050   method and finite element method (Zhu et al., 2019, Bar-Sinai et al., 2019, Li et al., 2020). How-
051   ever, collocation-based PINN (hereafter referred to as PINNs) encounter challenges in efficiently
052   setting collocation points within the constraints of a limited sampling budget, and in achieving fast
053   convergence to accurate solutions. A key challenge arises from the presence of small regions with
      abrupt changes, in contrast to the larger, smoother regions. This issue is particularly evident in stiff

054
      PDEs, which are often characterized by discontinuities, such as sudden transitions or jumps across
055   the spatio-temporal domain.
056
057   To address these challenges, two main adaptive sampling approaches have been proposed: residual
058
      distribution-based sampling and high residual-based sampling. Residual distribution-based sam-
      pling resamples collocation points according to the residual distribution at each iteration, ensuring a
059
      proportional balance of points based on their residuals. In contrast, high residual-based approaches
060
      directly target collocation points with high residuals, replacing those with lower values and often
061   neglecting low-residual regions. This focus can make it difficult to discern the analytical form of the
062   residual distribution. Nonetheless, high residual-based methods have recently demonstrated superior
063   performance. This raises an important question: Should we then focus exclusively on high residual
064   points in an extreme manner? Addressing this unresolved issue requires a thorough analysis of the
065   trade-offs and risks involved in different adaptive sampling strategies.
066
      In this paper, we respond to this question by presenting a theoretical analysis that highlights the
067   importance of balanced adaptive sampling. We propose a novel Langevin dynamics-based PINN
068   (L-PINN) framework for balanced adaptive sampling of collocation points, ensuring an continu-
069   ous sampling process. We evaluated our L-PINN framework against existing adaptive sampling
070   approaches for PINNs, demonstrating consistently reliable relative L2 error rates and robust con-
071   vergence stability. Furthermore, our framework adapts well to varying learning rates, highlighting
072   its robustness across different training configurations. Notably, our proposed framework performs
073   effectively across in diverse PDEs, distinguishing itself with enhanced learning stability.

116   Adaptive sampling focused on high residuals. Alongside the residual distribution-based approach,
117   another prominent line of research focuses on sampling methods without directly approximating the
118
      underlying distribution. The methods introduced below could be interpreted as special cases of RAD

119
      mating the sampling distribution. One such approach is the high residual-based adaptive refinement
120
      (RAR) scheme Lu et al., 2021a, where the top-M high residual collocation points are added to the
121   training batches for PINN models. This process continues until the mean PDE residual satisfies a
122   predefined error tolerance. Although RAR showed remarkable improvement, the number of collo-
123   cation points could continuously grow without any replacement, leading increased computational
124   complexity. To address this, a retain-resample-release (R3) sampling method (Daw et al., 2023)

126   some points to improve diversity, and releasing collocation points with low residuals. While these
127   method focus on improving sampling strategies, a different question has arisen regarding whether
128   the L2 physics-informed loss is appropriate for Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equations (Wang
129
      et al., 2022a). In response, an adversarial training method has been proposed as an alternative, aim-
      ing to optimize the L∞ norm for solving high-dimensional PDEs. Later, we will describe this in
130
      more detail, during the process of conducting adversarial training to leverage L∞ , partial gradient
131
      information is utilized. As a result, it can be observed that this leads to a sampling technique that
132   tends to focus on high residual samples.
133
134   Unresolved questions in adaptive sampling methods. While adaptive sampling methods show
135
      promising results, several theoretical aspects remain unclear. In particular, there is a lack of theoret-
      ical analysis on the balancing effect. Although many studies report success with adaptive sampling,
136
      limited analysis exists on the impact of focusing on high residuals. Specifically, it remains unex-
137
      plained why algorithms that excessively emphasize high residuals exhibit instability during training.
138
139   To address these issues, we first investigate the relationship between learning stability and the degree
140   of emphasis on high residuals with respect to model complexity, and examine how an exclusive focus
141
      on high residuals may lead to performance degradation during PINN model training.

145   The effect of balancing method. Weighting high residual regions during PINN training signifi-
146   cantly enhances model accuracy and efficiency by minimizing errors and accelerating convergence

      2023). These studies, motivated by the goal of improving model accuracy and efficiency, have

150
      oretical analysis regarding the concentration of sampling in high residual regions. In this section,

      we aim to investigate the impact of sampling concentration through the resulting analysis.

341   4.2   P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
342
343   In general, achieving successful Langevin sampling requires careful selection of hyperparameters
344   (step size τ , number of Langevin iterations lL , etc.). While running Langevin dynamics for many
345   iterations with a small step size might allow sampling to be proportional to the actual residual land-
346
      scape, it can significantly slow down the training speed of the PINN model in practical applications.
      Thus, we considered the following concepts when setting the hyperparameters, which are crucial for
347
      effectively utilizing Langevin dynamics.
348
349   Adjusting step size and Langevin iteration. To increase the computational efficiency, we adopted
350   a strategy of increasing the step size τ and reducing the number of Langevin iterations lL , even at the
351   cost of some loss in sample quality. We anticipated that the temporal variation of the PINN model,
352
      fθ , would exhibit smooth behavior when utilizing adaptive sampling strategies. Consequently, even
      with fewer Langevin iterations, minimal changes in the loss landscape suggest that the sample tra-
353
      jectory would resemble that of a fixed landscape.
354
355   Normalizing the gradient size. Since L-PINN leverages gradient information from the residual
356   landscape, the step size τ needs to be set even smaller for stiff PDEs. Additionally, empirical
357   observations indicate that the gradient of the residual landscape exhibits substantial variations at the
358
      beginning of training. In contrast, towards the end of training, the residual landscape is characterized
      by relatively small gradients. This discrepancy restricts the movement of sample points, thereby
359
      making it challenging to secure reliable quality. To address these challenges, we normalized the
360
      magnitude of all residual gradients at each iteration relative to the largest residual gradient. This
361   method effectively mitigated the hyperparameter sensitivity inherent to Langevin dynamics.

      6       C ONCLUSIONS
464
      In this paper, we analyzed the impact of adaptive sampling methods on learning stability when
465
      training PINN models, particularly in relation to model complexity. Our theoretical analysis revealed
466
      that sampling methods overly focused on high residuals could compromise learning stability. To
467   mitigate this issue, we proposed the Langevin dynamics-based PINN (L-PINN) framework, which
468   updates collocation points based on Langevin dynamics proportional to PDE residuals. Through
469   extensive experiments and ablation studies, we demonstrated that high residual-based methods often
470   failed to converge to correct solutions when increasing hidden layers and learning rate rates, whereas
471   L-PINN maintained stable convergence.

651   A     E MPIRICAL VALIDATION OF A SSUMPTIONS
652
653   We begin by discussing the challenges of validation associated with our assumption, which states
654
      that proving a linear combination of the residual function to its feature vector is non-trivial. By
      definition, the residual Rθ involves transforming the neural network output fθ through an operator
655
      within a certain function space. This implies that the features of the function, resulting from the neu-
656
      ral network output combined with an additional operator, are not explicitly defined. Consequently,
657   we extract the feature vector of the resulting function through local feature vector estimation based
658   on the linearization of the residual function. Before delving into the main discussion, we first explain
659   the logic behind how the feature vector ϕ is inferred.
660
661   A.1    L OCAL A PPROXIMATION OF THE F EATURE V ECTOR ϕ

666   expansion for [g(fθ )](x). It is important to note that since g(fθ ) represents the behavior in the
667

      employs the Fréchet derivative.

       To evaluate computational complexities, we measured the computational costs for training deep
1569   neural networks using each algorithm. Specifically, to validate the scalability of the algorithms,
1570   we conducted experiments to analyze their computational requirements in terms of the number of
1571   collocation points Npde and the dimensionality of the PDE.
1572
       Based on our observations, the runtime of the sampling algorithms was independent of the specific
1573
       PDE. Thus, we utilized equations that allowed for a straightforward extension from 1D to 2D in
1574
       dimensionality. More specifically, we experimented with different sizes of collocation points (100,
1575   1,000, 10,000, 50,000, and 100,000) for both 1D and 2D Burgers′ equations.
1576
1577   As part of the detailed experimental process, we calculated the elapsed time over 1,000 epochs. The
1578
       measurement was repeated 10 times using 10 different random seeds, and the mean and standard
       deviation were computed. For additional clarity, the elapsed time was measured excluding auxiliary
1579
       operations such as saving the model or storing data, focusing solely on the computations required to
1580
       run the algorithms.
1581
1582   Hardware specification. NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with 24GB of memory.
1583   Changes with NPDE . As NPDE increases, the computational cost grows for all methods. However,
1584   the growth rate varies significantly between methods. Gradient-based algorithms such as L-PINN
1585   and L∞ show a particularly sharp increase in computational cost as NPDE grows. This is due to the
1586   iteration-intensive nature of their sampling processes. For example, with NPDE = 50, 000 in 2D,
1587   the computational cost of L-PINN (lL = 20) reaches 613.96 seconds, whereas simpler methods like
1588   Fixed or Random-R remain below 35 seconds. For NPDE = 100, 000, L-PINN and L∞ run out of
1589   memory in the 2D case, highlighting their scalability limitations for very large PDE sample sizes.
1590   Changes with dimensionality (1D vs. 2D). Extending from 1D to 2D consistently increases the
1591   computational cost for all methods. While simple methods like Fixed or Random-R exhibit a rela-
1592   tively modest increase in cost when transitioning from 1D to 2D, gradient-based methods such as
1593   L-PINN and L∞ show disproportionately higher computational times in the 2D case. For example,
1594   in the 2D case with NPDE = 1, 000, L-PINN (lL = 10) takes 45.74 seconds, compared to only 16.97
1595   seconds in 1D. At NPDE = 10, 000, L-PINN (lL = 10) takes 59.32 seconds in 2D versus 20.43
1596
       seconds in 1D.
1597   However, despite the overall computational expense of L-PINN for higher lL values, the case of
1598   lL = 1 demonstrates significantly lower computational costs, making it relatively practical and
1599   scalable. For example, at NPDE = 50, 000, L-PINN (lL = 1) takes 63.98 seconds in 2D, which
1600   is manageable compared to the prohibitive 324.49 seconds for lL = 10. Similarly, for smaller
1601   NPDE , such as 1,000, L-PINN (lL = 1) shows competitive runtimes (e.g., 26.62 seconds in 2D).
1602
       An additional advantage of using lL = 1 is that it avoids the out of memory issues observed for
       higher values of lL , even in large-scale scenarios such as NPDE = 100, 000 in 2D. Furthermore, the
1603
       relative L2 error reported in our paper uses lL = 1 as the baseline, demonstrating its effectiveness in
1604
       balancing computational efficiency with accuracy.
1605
1606   Finally, while gradient-based methods like L-PINN exhibit high computational costs due to the
1607   overhead of gradient computation, future work optimizing the gradient operations could signifi-
1608
       cantly enhance the scalability and practicality of these methods. Thus, with improved optimization
       techniques, L-PINN (lL = 1) remains a promising candidate for solving PDEs efficiently at scale.

       I    C OMPATIBILITY OF L-PINN WITH H IGH -D IMENSIONAL P ROBLEMS
1654
1655
       To analyze the compatibility of L-PINN with high-dimensional problems, we first highlight its dis-
1656   tinctions from existing adaptive sampling techniques, such as RAD. While both methods aim to
1657   achieve a balanced residual distribution for sampling, their approaches differ significantly. RAD uti-
1658   lizes Monte Carlo integration (MCI) to estimate E|Rθ (x)|k , whereas L-PINN bypasses this step by
1659   asymptotically converging to the desired distribution. However, this comes at the cost of increased
1660   hyperparameter complexity.
1661
       Effect of high dimensionality on PDEs. The difference between L-PINN and MCI-based methods
1662   becomes evident in high-dimensional PDEs with limited collocation points Npde . For MCI, the
1663   accuracy depends on evenly distributed residual points, which becomes challenging as the spatial
1664   dimension increases. In contrast, L-PINN achieves the desired asymptotic distribution with fewer
1665   collocation points, provided that parameters τ and lL are properly tuned. For instance, in a 1D
1666   domain divided into P uniform partitions, the probability of a sample falling into a specific partition
1667   is 1/P . However, in higher dimensions, this value decreases exponentially, further complicating
1668   MCI-based approaches.
1669   Experimental results. The limitations of adaptive sampling methods in high-dimensional settings
1670   are evident in the Heat equation experiments. Table 5 and Figure 11 summarize the results. For
1671   reproducibility, all experiments were conducted using default settings for both L-PINN and baseline
1672   methods. Detailed configurations are described in the main text section 5 and Appendix F.2. A
1673   notable observation is that Random-R performed better than other adaptive sampling techniques
1674   in the restricted 2D PDE cases we proposed. Nonetheless, it was observed that the proposed L-
1675   PINN consistently maintained the second-best performance, following Random-R, under default
1676
       hyperparameter settings. Additionally, based on the visual results, it can be inferred that gradient-
       based algorithms such as L∞ and L-PINN captured high-frequency components at t = 0 more
1677
       effectively than RAD or R3.
