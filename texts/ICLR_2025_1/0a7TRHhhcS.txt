009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional spatial-temporal models often overlook the complex decision-making
012            processes and social factors that shape spatial-temporal event data generated by
013            humans. This paper introduces a novel framework that integrates choice the-
014            ory with social intelligence to model and analyze counting processes, such as
015            crime occurrences or bike-sharing activity, where the observed discrete events re-
016            sult from individual decisions influenced by social dynamics. Our approach aims
017            to uncover latent human preference patterns, represented by utility functions, to
018            capture the diverse decision-making factors within a population that result in the
019
               observed event counts. These latent factors help explain how choices—such as
               where and when to commit a crime—are shaped by personal preferences, environ-
020
               mental conditions, and social influences. By modeling the aggregate outcomes of
021
               these individual choices, we can better understand and predict patterns in counting
022            processes. The proposed model adopts a preference-driven approach to counting
023            data, providing interpretable insights at a detailed level. It also enables in-depth
024            analysis of how external interventions, like law enforcement actions or policy
025            changes, influence individual decisions and how these effects spread through the
026            system. Empirical evaluation of crime and bike-sharing datasets demonstrates our
027            model’s ability to offer clear insights and achieve high predictive accuracy.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Many real-world counting processes, such as criminal or bike-sharing activities, are generated by
032   human behavior and result from a series of decision-making processes driven by individual prefer-
033   ences (Zhao & Tang, 2017; He et al., 2021). These spatial-temporal discrete events are not merely the
034   outcome of spatial-temporal interactions but are significantly influenced by human factors, includ-
035   ing diverse preferences and complex social dynamics. Traditional spatial-temporal models, such as
036   those using Gaussian processes in the Log-Cox Gaussian Process model (Møller et al., 1998; Diggle
037   et al., 2013) or incorporating triggering kernel functions in spatial-temporal point processes (Rein-
038
      hart, 2018), focus primarily on capturing spatial-temporal dependencies. However, these models
      often fall short of addressing the underlying human decision-making processes and social influences
039
      that shape these events.
040
041   To address this gap, we propose an alternative modeling perspective that aims to uncover the latent
042   utility functions driving the mechanisms behind human-generated discrete events. By incorporating
043   these latent preferences and decision-making factors, our approach seeks to provide a more compre-
044
      hensive and insightful framework for analyzing and predicting counting processes.
045   Our approach extends traditional models by integrating choice theory (Levin & Milgrom, 2004;
046   Bentz & Merunka, 2000) and social intelligence into a framework that automatically discovers and
047   explains patterns in counting processes. Specifically, we focus on modeling how individuals make
048   choices—such as where and when to commit a crime—based on underlying factors that may include
049   social influences, environmental cues, and personal preferences. Even though our data may be
050
      counts, such as the number of crimes in each location per hour, these counts reflect the aggregate
      outcomes of individual choices influenced by various factors.
051
052   At the core of our model is random utility function modeling (Azari et al., 2012; Aouad & Désir,
053   2022), which enables us to understand and quantify the preferences driving individual decisions.
      These preferences are influenced by a range of factors, including social norms, environmental cues,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and personal tastes. Our approach leverages a preference learning framework that utilizes a mixture-
055   of-expert models (Jacobs et al., 1991; Shazeer et al., 2017), each representing a distinct preference
056   pattern. This allows us to capture the diverse ways in which individuals decide where and when to
057   generate an event.
058
      Each preference function in our model is inspired by a two-stage decision-making process. In the
059
      first stage, we develop a ranking function to evaluate and prioritize all possible time-location pairs.
060
      This ranking function incorporates considerations of social norms and mutual influences—key as-
061   pects of social intelligence that reflect how people’s choices are shaped by their interactions and
062   the behavior of others. The ranking function is followed by a soft ranking-based sparse selection
063   method, which uses a learned sparse gating function to adaptively select a subset of options (Peters
064   et al., 2019; Correia et al., 2019). This approach allows the model to either spread out or focus on
065   specific time-location pairs based on the spatial-temporal dynamics of the data.
066
      In the second stage, we compare the selected options within the chosen subset using an additional set
067   of learnable utility parameters. This step introduces further flexibility and detail into the decision-
068   making process, enabling us to refine the final choice based on the previously ranked options.
069
070
      Overall, our model captures the intricate patterns in spatial-temporal counting processes, revealing
      how human choices, driven by varying preferences and social influences, shape the distribution of
071
      events over time and space. The use of sparse selection and ranking functions, combined with a
072
      mixture-of-expert models, enhances the interpretability and adaptability of our approach, providing
073   a detailed understanding of how different factors contribute to the decision-making process.
074
075   Our contributions are threefold. First, we present a novel human preference-driven model for spatial-
076
      temporal counting processes, capturing how individual choices influence the distribution of events
      over time and space. Second, our preference model is informed by social intelligence, incorporating
077
      flexible, adaptive techniques and offering interpretability through integrating social norms and mu-
078
      tual influences. Finally, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our model through empirical evaluation
079   of real-world crime and bike-sharing datasets, validating its ability to uncover insightful patterns
080   and improve predictive performance.

      2   R ELATED W ORK

084   Spatial-Temporal Modeling Spatial-temporal modeling involves analyzing and predicting the
085   evolution of state or value across both spatial and temporal dimensions. Recent advances in deep
086   learning methods enhance the capability of spatial-temporal modeling Luo et al. (2020); Zhang et al.
087   (2021). Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Yu et al., 2017b; Wang et al., 2017) and Temporal Con-
088   volutional Networks (TCMs) Wu et al. (2019) demonstrating their effectiveness in handling temporal
089   dynamics. To capture spatial dependencies, onvolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Li et al., 2022)
090
      and graph neural networks (GNNs) (Yu et al., 2017a) have been adopted to model spatial dependen-
      cies.
091
092   Spatial-Temporal Point Processes models also offer a robust methodology for handling the genera-
093   tive process of discrete events in continuous time and space through intensity functions, eliminating
094   the necessity to partition space and time into cells (Moller & Waagepetersen, 2003; Diggle, 2006;
095   Reinhart, 2018). The occurrence intensity of events is a function of space, time, and history, and
096
      explicitly characterizes how the events are allocated over time and space. The Log-Gaussian Cox
      process (LGCP), where the log intensity function is a random realization drawn from a Gaussian
097
      process (Møller et al., 1998; Diggle et al., 2013), although flexible, requires a pre-specified mean
098
      and covariance function to incorporate an accurate prior belief on the spacetime interleaved corre-
099   lation. (Miller et al., 2014) utilized non-negative matrix factorization for mining low-rank spatial
100   decompositions. Recent methods have improved the efficiency of fitting intensities through the use
101   of neural networks. (Yuan et al., 2023) leveragsd diffusion models to learn complex spatio-temporal
102   joint distributions. (Chen et al., 2020) applies Neural ODEs as the backbone, which parameterized
103   the temporal intensity with Neural Jump SDEs and the spatial PDF with continuous-time normaliz-
104   ing flows. However, the inherent incapacity to capture underlying patterns when constructing the
105   intensity function results in a lack of interpretability.
106
107   Choice Model Choice models are used to explain or predict the choice behaviour of an individ-
      ual or segment among a set of alternatives. The prevalent choice models include multinomial logit

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      model (MNL) (McFadden, 1972), Markov chain choice model (MCCM) (Blanchet et al., 2016),
109   non-parametric choice model (NP) (Farias et al., 2009), mixture choice model (McFadden & Train,
110   2000), etc. The work of (Bentz & Merunka, 2000) add model flexibility by using neural network
111   to capture the nonlinear effects of features on the latent utility. Recent studies such as (Wang et al.,
112   2020; Han et al., 2020; Sifringer et al., 2020; Gabel & Timoshenko, 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Ark-
113   oudi et al., 2023; Ko & Li, 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) have achieved high predictive performance by
114   employing various deep neural network architectures to model complexs feature-to-utilty mappings.
115   We utilize the choice model as the building block of our proposed model.
116
117   Mixture-of-Experts The concept of Mixture-of-Expert (MoE) was initially introduced by (Jacobs
118   et al., 1991), which is based on a simple yet powerful idea: different parts of a model, known
119
      as experts, specialize in different tasks or aspects of the data. This approach achieved large-scale
      success when Shazeer et al. (2017) refined the gating mechanism which enhanced the capacity of
120
      MoE remarkably. Subsequent developments in the field have included novel advancements in gating
121
      design (Fedus et al., 2022; Lepikhin et al., 2020), optimization algorithms (Zoph et al., 2022), and
122   distributed training frameworks (Rajbhandari et al., 2022). Recently, several studies adopt MoE
123   for spatial-temporal prediction. For instance, Rahman et al. (2020) introduces a spatial-temporal
124   MoE network with CNNs and RNNs as experts, applied to handle the source heterogeneity posed
125   by multi-city ride-hailing demand prediction. Liu et al. (2023) constructs embeddings encoded with
126   spatial-temporal knowledge and adopts MLPs as experts for prediction. Jiang et al. (2024) employs
127   multiple tailored adaptive graph learners as experts to capture traffic congestion spatial-temporal
128   patterns from various aspects and further introduces specialized experts to identify stable trends
129   and periodic patterns from traffic data. We aim to combine mixture-of-expert with choice model to
130
      capture more preference-guided patterns to explain event occurrence.

      Embedding of Time-Locations Pairs The inclusion of a time-location pair in the consideration
217   set depends not only on its own features but also on its interactions with other time-location pairs.
218   Consider, for example, a crime event scenario. A criminal’s consideration of a specific time-location
219   pair may be influenced by factors such as the time of day, police presence in the area, and the social
220   environment. Moreover, the consideration of one time-location pair can affect others: if a thief
221   considers a particular location for a potential crime, nearby locations may also be impacted.
222

      Baselines To evaluate the capability of our proposed models, we compare against commonly-
      used baselines and state-of-the-art models. i) ARMA (Araghinejad & Araghinejad, 2014): Auto-
358
      Regression-Moving-Average is well-known for predicting time series data. ARMA predicts the
359
      event number of a region solely based on the historical event records of the region, considering the
360   recent time slots for a moving average. ii) CSI (de, 1978): Cubic Spline Interpolation trains piece-
361   wise third-order polynomials which pass through event points of recent time slots, and then predicts
362   the event number in the near future by the trained polynomials. iii) LGCP (Diggle et al., 2013; Miller
363   et al., 2014): Log-Gaussian Cox Process is a kind of Poisson process with varying intensity, where
364   the log-intensity is assumed to be drawn from a Gaussian process. iv) NSTPP (Chen et al., 2020): It
365   applies Neural ODEs as the backbone, which parameterized the temporal intensity with Neural Jump
366   SDEs and the spatial PDF with continuous-time normalizing flows. v) DSTPP (Yuan et al., 2023):
367   it leverages diffusion models to learn complex spatio-temporal joint distributions. vi) ST-HSL (Li
368
      et al., 2022): It proposes a Spatial-Temporal Self-Supervised Hypergraph Learning framework for
      crime prediction.

      7       C ONCLUSION
532
      We propose a new approach to model spatial-temporal counting processes that effectively captures
533
      the complexity of human decision-making. By using a mixture model, our method learns diverse
534   sparse patterns representing different underlying preferences and decision strategies, accounting for
535   heterogeneous behaviors in the data. The gating function automatically identifies these sparse pat-
536   terns, while an additional utility function adds flexibility, ensuring adaptability across various sce-
537   narios. The asymmetry in the matrix H highlights the non-reciprocal nature of mutual influences,
538   crucial for modeling directional relationships between time-location pairs. Our approach not only
539   addresses the curse of dimensionality but also enhances interpretability, offering a clear understand-
      ing of individual behaviors in time and location contexts.

      A.1   S PATIAL - TEMPORAL EMBEDDING
720
721   We adopt a spatial-temporal embedding method akin to that described in (Alexey, 2020; Aksan et al.,
722   2021). Further details can be found in Fig.1. Initially, the region is segmented into distinct blocks.
723   To encode block order, we introduce sinusoidal positional encoding for area position embedding.
724   Subsequently, linear embedding is utilized for spatial information, typically latitude and longitude.
725   Temporal information is encoded using sinusoidal positional encoding as described in (Zuo et al.,
726
      2020). In the context of decision-making, relevant static features can be encoded using one-hot
      semantic encoding, while dynamic features are encoded linearly. The embeddings for spatial, tem-
727
      poral, and relevant feature information are then combined via element-wise addition to generate the
728
      comprehensive embedding.
729
730   First, we divide the area into disjoint blocks. To inject a notation of block ordering we add sinusoidal
731   positional encoding for these area position embedding. Then considering the spatial information
732
      which are usually latitude and longitude, we apply linear embedding. For encoding temporal infor-
      mation, we also adopt sinusoidal positional encoding a (Zuo et al., 2020). Considering other relevant
733
      features related to the decision-making process, we can apply one-hot semantic encoding for static
734
      feature and linear encoding for dynamic features. Finally, the embedding for spatial information,
735   temporal information, and relevant feature information then directly element-wise addition together
736   to obtain the overall embedding.
737
738
      This approach is similar to the use of positional embeddings and feature embeddings in attention
      mechanisms, where initial embeddings are transformed through linear projections to capture more
739
      nuanced information. By combining the base embeddings A and B with these flexible projections,
740
      our model can more accurately represent and adapt to diverse preference patterns and social influ-
741   ences, enriching the overall decision-making framework.

1111   substantial impact of increasing dataset size on model effectiveness. Hence, the results presented in

1113   level of detail in capturing time-location pair patterns. In the revised version of our paper, we will

1119
       analysis involving these features, with the coefficients reflecting the magnitude of impact on the
1120
       utility score. For each expert, the utility score patterns are different, reflecting different preference
1121   patterns. We take NYC Crime dataset with original 732 samples (temporal and spatial resolutions
1122   are 4 time slots and 10 × 10 area blocks) as an example and the results are shown in Fig.12, Fig.13,
1123   and Fig.14.
1124
       To illustrate the “human decision process” in a more clear way, we take an example for the top-
1125
       5 crime event time location-pair for different races of expert-1 with largest utility score. Distinct
1126
       patterns emerge based on the time and location of crime events across various racial groups. Black
1127   suspects tend to engage in criminal activities during the early morning or late night hours, while
1128   White Hispanic suspects are less active in criminal activities during the early morning hours. The
1129   timing of criminal activities among suspects of other races is more varied. At the regional level,
1130   the concentration areas for criminal activities among suspects of different races vary significantly.
1131   By incorporating social norms or individual information of suspects into the utility function, our
1132   approach better captures the role of individual differences and human decision-making processes in
1133   engaging in criminal activities.
