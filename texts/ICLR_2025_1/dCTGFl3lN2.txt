011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Selecting appropriate training data is crucial for successful instruction fine-tuning,
014
               which aims to (1) elicit strong capabilities from pretrained large language models
               (LLMs), and (2) achieve balanced performance across a diverse range of tasks.
015
               Algorithms based on influence estimation have shown promise in achieving (1)
016
               through estimating the contribution of each training example to model’s prediction
017            on a downstream task, but they often struggle with (2). Through systematic
018            experiments, we attribute their underperformance to an inherent bias—certain tasks
019            intrinsically have greater influence than others. Directly comparing influence scores
020            across different tasks would thus bias the selected data towards these tasks, hurting
021            the LM’s performance not only on other capabilities, but also, surprisingly, on the
022            tasks for which the selected data has high influence.
023            To address this issue, we propose BIDS, a Balanced and Influential Data Selection
024            algorithm. BIDS first normalizes influence scores of the training data with respect
025            to each downstream task at an instance level. It then applies an iterative process to
026            further balance the selection of influential training data. At each step, BIDS selects
027
               the training example that bears the highest influence on the most underrepresented
               capability by the currently selected data. We perform comprehensive experiments
028
               using both Llama-3 and Mistral-v0.3 on seven evaluation benchmarks spanning five
029
               diverse capabilities. Results demonstrate that BIDS consistently outperforms both
030            state-of-the-art influence-based data selection algorithms and other non-influence-
031            based selection frameworks under various budgets. Surprisingly, training on a 15%
032            subset selected by BIDS can even outperform full-dataset training with a much
033            more balanced performance across different tasks. Our analysis further highlights
034            the importance of both instance-level normalization and iterative optimization of
035            selected data for balanced learning of diverse capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
      Supervised instruction finetuing (SFT) plays a crucial role in eliciting strong capabilities from large
040
      language models (LLMs) and adapting them for various downstream tasks. Typically, a pretrained
041
      LLM is finetuned on a mixture of different datasets to achieve strong and balanced performance
042   across a vareity of tasks (Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023). Recent efforts
043   have identified the importance of instruction data quality for SFT (Zhou et al., 2024), spawning many
044   works on instruction tuning data selection (Cao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
045   Among them, influence-based algorithms have shown promise. They estimate each individual training
046   example’s influence on model’s prediction on a downstream task (Koh & Liang, 2017; Pruthi et al.,
047   2020). Recent advances in influence estimation methods have enabled their scaling to LLM scales,
048   where they have demonstrated success. These works generally select high-quality data from a large
049   and diverse collection of instruction datasets in order to boost a specific capability of the model (Xia
050
      et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024).
051   Most real-world applications call for general-purpose LLMs that have both strong and balanced
052   capabilities on a wide range of tasks (e.g., a math tutor that excels in both math problem solving and
053   user instruction following). However, existing influence-based data selection algorithms actually fall
      short in this aspect (Section 3; Xia et al., 2024). As our analysis reveals, the core of this issue lies in


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the fact that when influence-based methods are applied across various tasks, the influence on some
055   of them is inherently higher than others. As a result, directly comparing influence across tasks and
056   selecting examples with highest influence values often result in a selected dataset biased towards the
057   tasks with inherently higher influence. This leads to a couple of pitfalls. First, biasing the selection
058   towards some tasks inevitably comes at the cost of the model’s performance on others, making it
059   more challenging for the LLM to achieve balanced capabilities. Second, perhaps unexpectedly, it
060   may even hurt the model’s performance on the very task that the data selection is biased towards.
061   These issues call for an influence-based data selection algorithm designed for helping LLMs achieve
062   balanced capabilities across a wide range of diverse tasks through instruction finetuning.
063   BIDS, our proposed method, addresses these challenges with two key designs. It first normalizes
064   influence values with respect to each validation instance, enabling influence for different instances to
065   be distributed on the same scale. Then BIDS applies an iterative selection algorithm which, at each
066   iteration, selects the training example that provides largest influence improvement for the current
067   selected data. This ensures that each selected example contributes most to the underrepresented tasks
068   in the selected subset.
069   Our experimental results on two base models of different model families, Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al.,
070   2024) and Mistral-7B-v0.31 , show the consistent and exceptional effectiveness of BIDS. In our
071   evaluation suite composed of seven tasks that span five diverse capabilities including coding, math,
072   logical inference, world knowledge and general instruction following, BIDS consistently outperforms
073   both influence- and non-influence-based selection algorithms, not only in terms of macro-average
074   performance across diverse tasks, but also in most of the task-specific performance, demonstrating its
075
      effectiveness in achieving both balanced and influential data selection. Remarkably, a 15% subset by
      BIDS even outperforms full-dataset training, emphasizing the huge potential of selective training
076
      in fostering multi-capability learning of LLMs. Further analysis reveals the positive contributions
077
      from both the instance-level normalization and iterative selection. Investigation of the influence
078   distribution of BIDS-selected data also provides valuable insight on how BIDS reduces the influence
079   disparity across tasks and what might be the good properties of a balanced set of influential data.
080
081
      We summarize the contribution of this work in the following three aspects:
082
               1. We identify the problem of influence-based data selection methods in instruction tuning
083               LLMs for learning diverse tasks, and attribute this problem to an inherent bias in cross-task
084               influence through systematic analysis.
085
               2. We propose BIDS, a simple and effective influence-based data selection algorithm that
086
                  selects influential data for balanced capability learning.
087
               3. Through extensive experiments, we confirm the consistent and significant improvement by
088
                  BIDS, and provide valuable insights on what makes a balanced set of influential data.

091   2       BACKGROUND AND P RELIMINARIES
092
093   Influence-based instruction tuning data selection. Estimating the influence of individual training
094   examples on model predictions is critical for understanding model behaviors and selecting influential
095   training data to improve model predictions. Traditional methods, including retraining-based and
096
      gradient-based approaches (Ilyas et al., 2022; Koh & Liang, 2017), have proven effective but are
      computationally prohibitive when scaling to LLMs. Several recent advances have sought to address
097
      these challenges by extending gradient-based approaches to scale more effectively. Given a large
098
      training dataset to select from and a validation set representing some targeted capabilities, LESS (Xia
099   et al., 2024) models the influence between each pair of training and validation example through LoRA-
100   based low-dimensional gradient similarity, and then selects training points with highest influence on
101   the validation set. LOGRA (Choe et al., 2024) leverages a low-rank gradient projection algorithm
102   to further improve the efficiency. MATES (Yu et al., 2024) formulates the pointwise data influence
103   between each training point and the whole validation set, and uses a small data influence model to
104   learn this pointwise influence.
105   Upon closer investigation, these three LLM-scale influence-based data selection methods all use
106   similar problem formulations. They all need a validation set to represent a targeted data distribution
107

              https://huggingface.co/mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.3

      that the selected data are optimized for, and require the computation of pointwise data influence
109   between each training instance and the validation data. In this work, we aim to extend these influence-
110   based methods to a multi-capability instruction tuning setup. Concretely, since only LESS directly
111   targets instruction tuning among the three LLM-scale approaches, we ground our study on the specific
112   formulation of LESS. But we emphasize that due to the highly similar influence modeling patterns
113   shared among these methods, the results of our work should also provide useful insight for other
114   influence-based selection methods.
115   Setup. Below we introduce the problem setup and lay the groundwork for further discussion.
116
117   Definition 1 (Attribution Matrix (AM)). Assume an instruction tuning dataset D, a validation
118
      dataset V, which spans m diverse tasks that we want to optimize the LLM’s performance for:
      V = V1 ∪ · · · ∪ Vm , and an influence estimation method that can compute the influence of each
119
      training example on each validation example. We first compute influences between all training and
120
      validation instance pairs, yielding a |D| × |V| matrix A. Each row of A corresponds to an individual
121   training example, and each column a validation example. Element Aij indicates the influence of the
122   i-th example from D on the j-th example from V. We dub A an Attribution Matrix as it reveals
123   the overall attribution pattern from the training set to all target tasks, and each row Ai the Influence
124   Distribution of the i-th training example.
125
      Our goal is to design a data selection algorithm that can effectively select a subset T from D with
126
      size under a pre-defined budget. based on the influence information in A. Finetuning the LLM
127   on T is supposed to help the model achieve strong and balanced performance on all targeted tasks.
128   Specifically, the tasks are chosen to ensure minimal overlap in terms of the capabilities they evaluate,
129   so that we can maximize the scope of abilities that are evaluated for an LLM. The validation set size
130   for each task is also kept equal to avoid bias in the selection process.

      Models, datasets, and tasks. We use Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) as the base model for both
      influence estimation and evaluation of selected datasets in the exploratory analysis. For the instruction
136
      dataset to select from, we use UltraInteract (Yuan et al., 2024), a state-of-the-art, large-scale, high-
137
      quality dataset designed to enhance diverse reasoning capabilities, including mathematical reasoning,
138   coding, and general logical inference. We also follow the evaluation setup of Yuan et al. (2024),
139   with seven datasets spanning five diverse capabilities. We use HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) and
140   MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) for coding, GSM-Plus (Li et al., 2024) and MATH (Hendrycks et al.,
141   2021) for math, and BigBench-Hard (BBH) (Suzgun et al., 2022) for general logical inference. We
142   also use MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) to assess the model’s ability to understand and reason over
143   world knowledge, and IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023) for the fine-grained instruction following ability.
144   For more details about the training and evaluation setups, please refer to Appendix A.2.
145   For the influence estimation method, we follow the original pipeline introduced by LESS throughout
146   this work, with an equal number of validation instances sampled uniformly from each of the seven
147   evaluation tasks. In this exploratory analysis, we also adopt the task-wise max selection algorithm
148   (Appendix A.3) by LESS, which, for each training example, first computes its mean influence over
149   validation examples within the same task, followed by selecting training examples with the highest
150   maximum influence across different tasks. We compare this algorithm against a random selection
151   baseline, which represents the average performance of models trained on two sets of randomly
152
      selected data.
153   LESS fails to balance among multiple capabilities. Table 1 compares LESS against a random
154   selection baseline. Although LESS achieves better task-specific performance in some cases, its overall
155   performance is unbalanced across different tasks, consistently underperforming the random baseline

157   significant variability across different tasks. For all budget levels, it significantly lags behind in BBH,
158
      while consistently outperforms the random baseline in IFEval.

160   designed for learning multiple diverse capabilities, thus less suitable for general-purpose instruction

207   We first define the following two data analysis techniques to examine the influence distribution of
208   LESS-selected data: for any training subset T = {ti }Ni=1 :
209
             • Average Influence Distribution (AID). The AID of T is defined as the average of influence

               distributions of all the training points inside, i.e., AID ≜ N1 i=1 Ai
211
             • Task frequency with Highest Influence (THI). For each training point ti ∈ TP     , if its average
212

      discrepancies. Notably, MMLU exhibits the highest average influence which is nearly 50% less

      negative than BBH, despite neither of them being in-distribution for the training data. Moreover,
217   discrepancies of average influence inside the same task can exceed the largest instance-wise average
218   influence by 2.5 times. These results answer our question 1) by confirming that the scales of influence
219   values indeed differ significantly across various tasks.
220
      Further, the THI analysis of LESS-selected data (Figure 2) validates that the scale differences indeed
221
      make the selection algorithm of LESS disproportionately favor certain tasks over others. Specifically,
222
      MMLU has the highest frequency of being the most influential task, which is consistent with the
223   observations in Figure 1 that MMLU has the highest task-level average influence. However, the
224   oversampling of MMLU training data does not translate into proportionally better performance—
225   LESS often underperforms the random baseline on MMLU, even though it targets this task with its
226   selected data. This crucial observation underscores that a higher influence score does not necessarily
227   imply a larger performance improvement, and, paradoxically, it may hinder the learning of other
228   necessary capabilities. Thus, we answer the question 2) by concluding that the inherent difference
229   in the influence value scales across tasks is indeed a harmful bias that can severely undermine the
230   performance of the data selection algorithm employed by LESS.

233   4    BIDS: S ELECTING I NFLUENTIAL DATA FOR BALANCED C APABILITY
234        L EARNING
235
236
237   To address the imbalanced learning issue caused by the inherent bias in multi-task influence, we
238   propose BIDS, a simple and effective Data Selection algorithm aiming to select Influential training
239
      data in a Balanced way. Given the Attribution Matrix (AM) A, BIDS applies two crucial operations
      to A sequentially: instance-level normalization, and iterative selection favoring underrepresented
240
      tasks.
241
242   Instance-level normalization. The analyses presented in the above section reveal that validation
243   instances exhibit substantial variability in the scales of their influence values. If left unaddressed, such
244   variability introduces bias into influence-based data selection processes, thus hindering the balanced
245
      optimization of model performance across tasks. To mitigate this, we implement an instance-level
      normalization technique. From the perspective of Attribution Matrix, this technique applies a column-
246
      wise normalization in order to align the influence scores on a consistent scale. Specifically, for each
247
      validation instance vj , the influence of each training example ti is normalized as below:
248

                                                 Anorm

253   where µj and σj are the sample mean and standard deviation of all values in column j of the AM.
254   After such normalization, the influence scores of different columns should be distributed on the same
255   scale, with scores of similar rankings in different columns having similar values.
256
      One potential issue with such a normal standardization technique is that it may not work sufficiently
257
      well when the distribution of unnormalized data differs much from an approximate normal distribution.
258   But we show that after normalization, most of the columns in the AM indeed approximate a standard
259   normal distribution, justifying the use of this technique for instance-level normalization of influence
260   scores. Please refer to Appendix A.4 for more details.
261
      Iterative selection favoring underrepresented tasks. To further ensure a balanced distribution of
262
      influence across the selected data, we propose an iterative greedy selection algorithm (detailed in
263
      Algorithm 1). The algorithm begins with an empty set of selected instances and, at each iteration,
264   selects the training instance that provides the largest improvement in influence on any validation
265   instance, ensuring that each newly added instance contributes maximally to underrepresented tasks or
266   capabilities. The iterative process continues until the selection budget is fully utilized. This approach
267   essentially differs from LESS, which only scores each training instance independently and then
268   selects the top-ranked instances, by considering the interactions of influence distributions among
269   different selected instances and promoting the balance of overall influence distribution of the selected
      dataset.

      Basic setup. We follow the experimental setup outlined in Section 3, including the same set of LLMs,

      More Baselines. For a more thorough comparison, we evaluate BIDS against several other algorithms
      tailored for influence-based selection, extending beyond the original task-wise max algorithm in
291
      the exploratory analysis. Please refer to Appendix A.3 for the mathematical definition of all the
292
      influence-based selection algorithms compared in this work. To further show the strength of BIDS
293   among different classes of data selection frameworks, we also compare with a frequently used
294   non-influence-based data selection method. All the additional baselines included in this section are
295   listed below:
296
             • Instance-wise max. For each training example, this algorithm first computes the maximum
297
               of its influence values over all the validation instances and assigns it as the overall score.
298
               Then it selects training examples with highest overall scores.
299          • Sum. Similar to the instance-wise max algorithm, this algorithm also assigns an overall
300            score to each training example and then selects the highest, but by computing the summation
301            of its influence instead of maximum.
302
303          • RDS (Representation-based Data Selection) (Zhang et al., 2018; Hanawa et al., 2020). We
304            follow the same experimental setup in Xia et al. (2024) and opt for RDS as the non-influence-
305            based baseline to compare with. RDS uses the language model’s hidden representation for
306            data selection. More concretely, it extracts the final layer representation of the last token
307
               of each example sequence, and computes the cosine similarity scores between training
               and validation examples. Training examples with the highest similarity to any one of the
308
               validation examples are selected. In order to ensure fair comparison, we use the same model
309
               that computes gradient features in BIDS to extract the final layer representations for RDS.

      7    RELATED WORK
487
488
      Data Selection for LLM Instruction Finetuning. Since the pioneering work LIMA (Zhou et al.,
489   2024) showed that a mere 1000 carefully curated high-quality instruction data can already lead to
490   significant performance improvement, many works have been exploring automatic data selection
491   pipelines guided by different metrics. Quality-guided selection mostly defines the quality for each
492   data point based on natural language indicators (Cao et al., 2023), quality scores from strong
493   evaluators such as GPT-4 (Chen et al., 2023; Parkar et al., 2024), or principled metrics derived from
494   various learning dynamics (Kang et al., 2024; Mekala et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024; Choe et al.,
495   2024). Diversity-guided methods usually perform clustering over certain informative representation
496   of each data point (Yang et al., 2024), and also take inspiration from traditional core-set selection
497
      approaches (Das & Khetan, 2023). Both of these dimensions have been proved effective for instruction
      tuning LLMs (Bukharin & Zhao, 2023; Liu et al., 2023), and we remark that our method BIDS
498
      considers both quality and diversity metrics through its iterative selection algorithm based on influence
499
      distributions.
500
501   Influence Estimation. Influence estimation has long been an important type of data attribution
502   method, which can be classified into gradient-based and retraining-based approaches (Hammoudeh
503
      & Lowd, 2024; Ko et al., 2024). Gradient-based influence estimation focuses on the gradient
      trace of each training point, and assesses the gradient alignment between training and validation
504
      examples (Koh & Liang, 2017; Pruthi et al., 2020). Retraining-based estimation usually starts by
505
      training models on various subsets, and then inspects how the performance of these models changes
506   when a training example is added to these subsets (Ghorbani & Zou, 2019; Ilyas et al., 2022; Park
507   et al., 2023). Recently both lines of influence estimation works have been extended to LLM-scale
508   applications, covering various aspects including pretraining (Engstrom et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024;
509   Choe et al., 2024) and instruction tuning (Xia et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
510

      8    CONCLUSION
512
513
      In this work, we introduce BIDS, an influence-based instruction tuning data selection algorithm
514   specifically designed for balanced learning of multiple diverse capabilities. Motivated by the ob-
515   servation of an inherent bias in influence across various tasks, BIDS first applies instance-level
516   normalization to a given Attribution Matrix. Together with an iterative selection algorithm favoring
517   underrepresented tasks, BIDS consistently outperforms various selection algorithms as well as
518   full-dataset training with much more balanced performance. Our analysis further provides insight on
519   the good properties of an influential dataset with balanced capabilities.

680   A.2    D ETAILS OF TRAINING AND EVALUATION SETUPS
681
      Based on the LESS pipeline described above, we further introduce the implementation details of the
682
      training and evaluation setups in this work. All the experiments are carried out on 2 80GB H100
683   GPUs.
684
685   Training Details. We basically follow the same set of hyperparameters as LESS when training both
686
      Ms and Mt . Specifically, a batch size of 128 is used throughout all the training processes in this work,
      along with a learning rate scheduler with linear warm-up, cosine decay, and a peak learning rate of
687
      2 × 10−5 . For the influence estimation pipeline, we consistently conduct the warmup training of Ms
688
      using four epochs and the full training set. For gradient computation and projection, we uniformly
689   sample 50 validation examples from either the validation or the test split (when there is not a separate

843   A.6        D ISCUSSION ON THE COMPUTATIONAL COST OF BIDS
844
845   In this section we aim to discuss and show that BIDS does not incur much memory or latency
846
      overhead, and can thus serve as an efficient plug-and-play module. In our training and evaluation
      setup, the |D| dimension for the Attribution Matrix (AM) is about 288K, and the |V | dimension is 350.
847
      Therefore, the memory cost for storing the AM using FP64 precision is less than 800M. The latency
848
      cost for running the whole BIDS algorithm is less than 1 minute with GPU CUDA acceleration. More
849   generally, since most of the popular mixtures of instruction finetuning data are maintained on the
850   scale of hundreds of thousands (Wang et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Yue et al.,
851   2023), the memory and latency cost of BIDS should be light for most practical training setups.
