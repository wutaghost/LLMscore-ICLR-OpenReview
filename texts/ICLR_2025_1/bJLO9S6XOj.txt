009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph generative models often face a critical trade-off between learning com-
012            plex distributions and achieving fast generation speed. We introduce Autoregres-
013            sive Filtration Modeling (AFM), a novel approach that addresses both challenges.
014            AFM leverages filtration, a concept from topological data analysis, to transform
015            graphs into short sequences of monotonically increasing subgraphs. This enables
016            a structured autoregressive generation process, contrasting with the stochastic tra-
017            jectories of diffusion models. We propose a novel autoregressive graph mixer
018
               model to learn this filtration process, coupled with a noise augmentation strat-
               egy to mitigate exposure bias and a reinforcement learning approach to refine
019
               the generative model. Extensive experiments on diverse synthetic and real-world
020
               datasets demonstrate AFM’s superior performance compared to existing autore-
021            gressive models. Additionally, AFM achieves a 100-fold speedup in generation
022            time compared to state-of-the-art diffusion models while maintaining the qual-
023            ity of generated graphs. This work represents a significant advancement towards
024            high-throughput graph generation.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Graphs are fundamental structures that model relationships in various domains, from social networks
030   and molecular structures to transportation systems and neural networks. The ability to generate re-
031   alistic and diverse graphs is crucial in many applications, such as drug discovery (Liu et al., 2018;
032   Vignac et al., 2023), network simulation (Yu & Gu, 2019), and protein design (Ingraham et al.,
033
      2019). The space of drug-like molecules and protein conformations is, for practical purposes, in-
      finite, limiting the effectiveness of in-silico screening of existing libraries (Polishchuk et al., 2013;
034
      Levinthal, 1969). Consequently, high-throughput graph generation—the task of efficiently creating
035
      new graphs that faithfully emulate properties similar to those observed in a given domain—has thus
036   emerged as a critical challenge in machine learning and generative artificial intelligence (Gangwal
037   et al., 2024; Grisoni et al., 2021).
038
039
      While significant progress has been made in graph generation, existing approaches often face ex-
      pressive or computational challenges. Classical methods, such as Erdős-Rényi models (Erdos et al.,
040
      1960) or stochastic block models (Holland et al., 1983), are typically tailored to model specific graph
041
      families, thus struggling to capture the heterogeneous structural properties of real-world graphs (You
042   et al., 2018b). More recent deep learning-based approaches, particularly autoregressive (You et al.,
043   2018b; Liao et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2023) and diffusion models (Vignac et al., 2023; Bergmeister
044   et al., 2024), have shown promise in generating increasingly realistic graphs. However, these meth-
045   ods often suffer from scalability issues when handling large graphs, primarily due to their quadratic
046   or higher computational complexity with respect to the number of nodes. Furthermore, many current
047   diffusion-based approaches rely on iterative refinement processes involving a large number of steps.
048   This computational burden not only limits the applicability of these models to large-scale graphs but
049   also hinders their potential for high-throughput applications (Gentile et al., 2022; Gómez-Bombarelli
050
      et al., 2016; Polishchuk et al., 2013).
051   Recent work has explored the use of topological data analysis, particularly persistent homology and
052   filtration (Edelsbrunner et al., 2002; Zomorodian & Carlsson, 2005), for graph representation. A
053   filtration provides a multi-scale view of a given graph structure by constructing a nested sequence
      of subgraphs. This approach has shown promise in various graph analysis tasks, including classifi-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      cation and similarity measurement (O’Bray et al., 2021; Schulz et al., 2022). In the context of gen-
055   erative modeling, filtration-based representations have been used to develop more expressive tools
056   for generative model evaluation (Southern et al., 2023). However, the application of filtration-based
057   methods for graph generation remains unexplored.
058
      In this paper, we introduce Autoregressive Filtration Modeling (AFM), a novel approach to fast
059
      graph generation that models filtration sequences autoregressively. To generate a graph, our method
060
      produces a short sequence of increasingly dense and detailed subgraphs. Compared to diffusion
061   models (Vignac et al., 2023; Bergmeister et al., 2024), AFM requires fewer iterations during sam-
062   pling, resulting in significantly faster inference speed. Moreover, AFM incorporates advanced tech-
063   niques to mitigate exposure bias (Bengio et al., 2015), a common challenge in existing autoregres-
064   sive models, thereby improving sample quality. Our method offers a promising balance between
065   efficiency and accuracy in graph generation, addressing key limitations of current approaches.
066
      In summary, our contributions are as follows:
067
068          • We propose a novel autoregressive graph generation framework that leverages graph filtra-
069            tion to enable fast, high-throughput sampling.
070
             • We introduce a specialized autoregressive model architecture designed to learn the unique
               properties of filtration sequences, incorporating both structural and temporal information
071
               about filtration.
072
             • We identify exposure bias as a potential challenge in autoregressive graph generation and
073            propose noise augmentation and adversarial fine-tuning as effective strategies to mitigate
074            this issue.
075          • We conduct comprehensive ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different components
076            within our framework, demonstrating that noise augmentation and adversarial fine-tuning
077            substantially improve performance.
078          • Our empirical results highlight the strong performance and efficiency of our model com-
079            pared to recent baselines. Notably, our model achieves inference speed 100 times faster
080            than existing diffusion-based models.

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084
      AFM builds on the concept of graph filtration and is fine-tuned via reinforcement learning. In the
085   following, we provide a brief overview of related graph generative models, training schemes, and
086   applications of graph filtrations.
087
088
      Graph Generation. Historically, simple statistical models (Erdos et al., 1960; Holland et al.,
      1983) have been used to model distributions of graphs. While these approaches lend themselves
089
      to theoretical investigation, they are typically insufficient to model datasets seen in real-world ap-
090
      plications. GraphRNN (You et al., 2018b) made advances towards deep generative graph models
091   by autoregressively generating nodes and their incident edges to build up an adjacency matrix. In
092   a similar fashion, DeepGMG (Li et al., 2018) iteratively builds a graph node-by-node. Liao et al.
093   (2019) proposed a more efficient autoregressive model by generating multiple nodes at a time in
094   a block-wise fashion, leveraging mixtures of multivariate Bernoulli distributions in a similar way
095   to us. These models share the fact that they build graphs via node-addition and therefore require
096   some choice of node ordering, analogous to the choice of edge ordering that we make in our work
097   via a filtration function. To avoid an arbitrary choice, OM (Chen et al., 2021) and GraphArm (Kong
098   et al., 2023) learn node orderings, where the latter uses reinforcement learning to jointly learn the or-
099
      dering policy alongside the generative model. In contrast to autoregressive node-addition methods,
      approaches by Goyal et al. (2020) and Bacciu et al. (2020) generate graphs through edge-addition
100
      following a pre-defined edge ordering. While this strategy bears similarities to our proposed filtra-
101
      tion method, our approach distinctly differs by modeling sequences of graphs and allowing for edge
102   deletion as well as addition. Graph variational autoencoders (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Simonovsky
103   & Komodakis, 2018) generate all edges at one time, thereby reducing computational costs during
104   inference. However, these methods struggle to model complicated distributions and may fail in the
105   presence of isomorphic nodes (Zhang et al., 2021). Generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Bo-
106   jchevski et al., 2018; Cao & Kipf, 2018; Martinkus et al., 2022) are likelihood-free and avoid the
107   node-orderings and graph matching algorithms required in autoregressive models and VAEs. Graph
      diffusion models such as EDP-GNN (Niu et al., 2020) and GDSS (Jo et al., 2022), based on score


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      matching, or DiGress (Vignac et al., 2023), based on discrete denoising diffusion (Austin et al.,
109   2021), have emerged as powerful generators. Unfortunately, they require many iterative denois-
110   ing steps, making them slow during sampling. Some efforts have been made to increase inference
111   speed. Improvements were achieved in autoregressive models by generating multiple graph features
112   in a single step (Liao et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2023) while hierarchical approaches allowed scal-
113   ing diffusion models (Bergmeister et al., 2024) and autoregressive models (Karami, 2024) to larger
114   graphs. Additional efficiency gains have been achieved in diffusion models by using absorbing state
115   processes (Chen et al., 2023).
116   Reinforcement Learning Finetuning. Ranzato et al. (2016) demonstrated that reinforcement
117   learning (RL) allows optimizing non-differentiable sequence-level metrics in autoregressive mod-
118   eling and argued that training in free-running mode mitigates exposure bias (Bengio et al., 2015).
119   SeqGAN (Yu et al., 2017), which is most relevant to our work, avoids any extrinsic metrics by using
120   a discriminator to provide feedback to the generative model. In the context of graph learning, You
121
      et al. (2018a) train a generative model for molecules via reinforcement learning, combining adver-
      sarial and domain-specific rewards. In contrast to our work, they do not consider general graphs and
122
      do not use any autoregressive pre-training. Taiga (Mazuz et al., 2023) uses reinforcement learning
123
      to optimize chemical properties of molecules obtained from a language model that is pre-trained on
124   SMILES strings. Even graph diffusion models have been shown to be amenable to RL finetuning,
125   allowing extrinsic non-differentiable metrics to be optimized (Liu et al., 2024).
126
      Graph Filtration. Filtration is commonly used in the field of persistent homology (Edelsbrun-
127
      ner et al., 2002) to extract features of geometric data structures at different resolutions. Previously,
128   graph filtration has mostly been used to construct graph kernels (Zhao & Wang, 2019; Schulz et al.,
129   2022) or extract graph representations that can be leveraged in downstream tasks such as classifi-
130   cation (O’Bray et al., 2021). While filtration has also been used for evaluating graph generative
131   models (Southern et al., 2023), to the best of our knowledge, our work presents the first model that
132   directly leverages filtration for generation.

137   tion. We begin with the notion of filtration and present various filtration strategies in Sec 3.1. Then,

      In the following, we consider unlabeled and undirected graphs, denoted by G = (V, E), where V
141
      is the set of vertices and E ⊆ V × V is the set of edges. Without loss of generality, we assume
142   V = {1, 2, . . . , n} and denote by eij the edge between nodes i, j ∈ V . We assume that only
143   connected graphs are presented to our model during training and filter training sets if necessary. Our
144   approach is fundamentally based on the concept of graph filtration.

185   Filtration Function. In principle, any real-valued function defined on the set of node pairs can be
186   used as the filtration function. For graph generation, an effective filtration function should satisfy
187   two key criteria: diversity and structural consistency. Specifically, diversity means that the func-
188   tion should assign distinguishable values to different edges, facilitating a more granular filtration
189   sequence. Structural consistency describes that edges with similar structural properties or proximity
190   in the graph should receive similar weights from the filtration function. Based on these criteria, we
191   propose the following filtration functions:
192          • Line Fiedler function fFiedler : This function is derived from the second smallest eigenvec-
193            tor (Fiedler vector) of the symmetrically normalized Laplacian of the line graph L(G).
194            In L(G), nodes represent edges in the original graph G, and two nodes are connected if
195            their corresponding edges in G share a common vertex. This vector is not unique due to
196            multiplicities of eigenvalues and sign flip preservation (i.e., its negative is also an eigen-
197            vector with the same eigenvalue). We thus choose it arbitrarily with a random sign choice
198            per graph. This function captures global structural information and tends to assign similar
199
               values to neighboring edges.
             • Centrality functions fbetween and fremote : Following (Anthonisse, 1971; Brandes, 2008), we
200
               utilize the concept of betweenness-centrality to quantify the importance of an edge in fa-
201
               cilitating communication between different parts of the graph. We refer to Appendix A.16
202            for details.
203
204   Our empirical comparison (detailed in Appendix A.16) demonstrates that the line Fiedler function
205
      generally outperforms the other two options. Consequently, we primarily utilize this function in
      our experiments. By employing our proposed filtration function, we can effectively guide the graph
206
      generation process. While we focus on pre-determined filtration functions, it is worth noting that
207
      learning edge weights dynamically, in a similar fashion as in Kong et al. (2023), presents an intrigu-
208   ing avenue for future research.
209
210   Filtration Schedule Sequence. Analogous to noise schedules in diffusion models (Nichol &
211   Dhariwal, 2021), our filtration schedule governs the rate at which edges are added during sam-
212   pling or removed during filtration. We model this process using a continuous scheduling function
213   γ : [0, 1] → [0, 1] where γ(0) = 0 and γ(1) = 1. Given a graph, the discretized schedule sequence
214   (at )Tt=0 can then be defined as follows:
215
                   at := inf a ∈ R : |f −1 ((−∞, at ])| ≥ |E| · γ(t/T )

                                                                                             ∀ 0 ≤ t ≤ T,              (3)

      where f is the filtration function and T is the total number of steps. We note that the discretized
217   schedule (at )Tt=0 is defined per graph, as it depends on f and the cardinality |E|.
218
219   Drawing inspiration from noise scheduling in diffusion models, we propose three scheduling func-
220
      tions:
221          • Linear schedule γ(t) := t: This schedule intends to add approximately the same of number
222            of edges in each step. Assuming mostly distinct edge weights, this results in a roughly
223            linear increase in the density of graphs Gt over time.
224          • Convex schedule γ(t) := 1 − cos(πt/2): This schedule adds more edges in later steps,
225            potentially offering finer control over the graph generation process in its earlier stages.
226
             • Concave schedule γ(t) := sin(πt/2): This schedule adds more edges during the initial
               steps, which may help establish the overall graph structure more quickly while leaving
227
               room for finer adjustments over structural details in the later stages.
228
229   We conducted an empirical comparison of these schedules on a planar graph dataset in Ap-
230   pendix A.16. Our findings indicate that no single variant consistently outperforms the others across
231   all evaluation metrics. However, the concave schedule achieves the highest validity score. In the
232
      experiments we present in the main paper, we use the linear schedule for simplicity.

284   In contrast to existing autoregressive graph generators (You et al., 2018b; Liao et al., 2019; Goyal
285   et al., 2020; Bacciu et al., 2020; Kong et al., 2023), our model introduces a key innovation: the
286   ability to generate non-monotonic graph sequences. This means it can both add and delete edges.
287   We argue that this capability is crucial for mitigating error accumulation during sampling. Consider,
288   for instance, the task of generating tree structures. If a cycle is inadvertently introduced into an
289   intermediate graph Gt (where t < T ), traditional autoregressive approaches would be unable to
290
      rectify this error. Our model, however, can potentially delete the appropriate edges in subsequent
      timesteps, thus recovering from such mistakes. In Sec. 3.3.1, we introduce a data augmentation
291

      We employ the teacher-forcing approach (Williams & Zipser, 1989) to train our generative model
      pθ in a first training stage. We illustrate this training scheme in Figure 1b. Teacher-forcing allows
309
      the model to learn from complete sequences of graph evolution, providing a good initialization for
310
      subsequent reinforcement learning-based fine-tuning (second training stage). Given a dataset of
311
      graphs D, we convert it into a dataset of filtration sequences, denoted as D̃. Our objective is to
312
      maximize the log-likelihood of these sequences under our model:
313
314                                 L(θ) := E(G0 ,...,GT )∼D̃ [log pθ (G0 , . . . , GT )] .               (7)
315
      In practice, this objective is implemented as a cross-entropy loss. While the teacher-forcing approach
316
      is efficient, it can lead to exposure bias (Bengio et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2017; Ranzato et al., 2016),
317
      where the model’s performance during inference degrades due to a distribution shift caused by its
318   reliance on its own predictions. We propose two strategies to address this issue, namely noise
319   augmentation and adversarial fine-tuning with reinforcement learning.
320

      Adversarial Fine-tuning with Reinforcement Learning. While the above noise augmentation
      technique substantially improves the overall quality of generated graphs, it still falls short in gener-
338
      ating graphs with high structural fidelity. To address this, we propose a reinforcement learning based
339
      fine-tuning stage to refine the model trained with teacher-forcing. Adapting the SeqGAN frame-
340   work (Yu et al., 2017), we implement a generator-discriminator architecture where the generator (our
341   mixer model) operates in inference mode as a stochastic policy and is thereby exposed to its own
342   predictions during training. The discriminator is a graph transformer, namely GraphGPS (Rampásek
343   et al., 2022). During training, the generator produces graph samples, which the discriminator evalu-
344   ates for plausibility. The generator is updated using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman
345   et al., 2017) based on the discriminator’s feedback, while the discriminator is trained adversarially
346   to distinguish between generated and training set graphs. This training scheme is illustrated in Fig-
347   ure 1c. It is worth noting that only the final generated graph GT is presented to the discriminator,
348
      instead of the full sequence of graphs. Therefore, the generator is trained to maximize a terminal
      reward without constraints on intermediate graphs. We provide the pseudo-code in Appendix A.18.

351   4   E XPERIMENTS
352
353   We empirically evaluate our method on synthetic and real-world datasets. In Sec. 4.1, we first
354   present results on the commonly used small benchmark datasets (Martinkus et al., 2022), comparing
355   our method to a variety of baselines. We then demonstrate in Sec. 4.2 that we can improve upon
356   these results by using a more realistic setting with more training examples. Additionally, we present
357   results for inference efficiency. Finally, in Sec. 4.3, we demonstrate that our model is applicable
358   to real-world data, namely larger protein graphs (Dobson & Doig, 2003). In Sec. 4.4, we present
359
      ablation studies demonstrating the efficacy of noise augmentation and adversarial fine-tuning.
360
361   Evaluation. We follow established practices from previous works (You et al., 2018b; Martinkus
362   et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023) in our evaluation. We compare a set of model-generated samples
363
      to a test set via maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2012), based on various graph
      descriptors. These descriptors include histograms of node degrees (Deg.), clustering coefficients
364
      (Clus.), orbit count statistics (Orbit), and eigenvalues (Spec.). While we employ these metrics to
365
      facilitate comparison with previous methods, we acknowledge the criticisms raised by O’Bray et al.
366   (2022) regarding the use of indefinite kernels and arbitrary selection of kernel hyperparameters in
367   these evaluation techniques.

369
      In previous works (Martinkus et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023), very few samples are generated
      for the evaluation of graph generative models. In Appendix A.17, we show both theoretically and
370
      empirically that this leads to high bias and variance in the reported metrics. In Sec. 4.2 and 4.3, we
371
      generate 1024 samples for evaluation to mitigate this issue, while we generate 40 samples in Sec. 4.1
372   to fairly compare to previous methods. We sample the number of nodes to be generated from the
373   empirical training distribution, which is consistent with Vignac et al. (2023) but deviates from the
374   approach by Bergmeister et al. (2024), where the authors determine the number of nodes by using
375   the ground truth number of nodes from the test set. For synthetic datasets, we follow previous works
376   by reporting the ratio of generated samples that are valid, unique, and novel (VUN). In Sec. 4.2
377   and 4.3, we report inference speed, measured as the time needed to generate 1024 graphs on an
      H100 GPU, normalized to a per-graph cost.

528   5   C ONCLUSION
529
530   We proposed AFM, an efficient autoregressive graph generative model that relies on graph filtration.
531   AFM generates high-quality graphs, outperforming existing autoregressive models and rivaling dis-
532   crete diffusion approaches in terms of quality while being substantially faster at inference. Various
533   ablations demonstrated the configurability of AFM and indicate that exposure bias is an important
534   challenge for autoregressive graph modeling.
535   One limitation lies in the focus of our methodology solely on generating non-attributed graphs.
536   While extending this approach to include categorical edge labels seems feasible (see Appendix A.7),
537   the incorporation of node labels presents a bigger challenge. Although post-processing techniques
538   for node labeling may be applicable, the direct modeling of attributes remains a crucial area for fu-
539   ture investigation. Furthermore, exploring the possibility of learning to reverse a node label filtration
      process jointly with the edge filtration could be a promising direction for future research.

      A.1   E XTENDED R ELATED W ORK
759
760   In this section, we extend Sec. 2 and provide additional comparative analyses to previous works.
761
762   Other Graph Generative Models. In concurrent work, Zhao et al. (2024) introduce a hybrid
763   graph generative model, termed Pard, combining autoregressive and discrete diffusion components.
764   Similar to AFM, Pard generates graphs by building a sequence of increasingly large sub-graphs. In
765   contrast to the method we present here, Pard is limited to the generation of induced sub-graphs and
766   uses a shared diffusion model to sample them. While the authors state efficiency as one motivation
767
      for their approach, they do not present runtime measurements during inference.
768
      Graph Diffusion. Similar to graph diffusion models (Vignac et al., 2023), we propose a corrupting
769
      process to transform graph samples GT into graphs G0 from some convergent distribution (in our
770
      case the point-mass at the empty graph). However, in contrast to denoising diffusion models, the
771   process we are proposing is not Markov.
772
773   Absorbing State Diffusion. Absorbing state graph diffusion (Chen et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023)
774   resembles our approach in that it also generates a sequence of increasingly dense graphs. We aim
775   to increase efficiency by generating substantially shorter sequences than previous works. In prac-
776   tice, we choose to generate graphs within 15 or 30 steps. EDGE (Chen et al., 2023) requires be-
777   tween 64 and 512 denoising steps, depending on the dataset. To generate a graph on N nodes,
778   GraphARM (Kong et al., 2023) requires N denoising steps, as exactly one node decays to the ab-
779   sorbing state at a time. The larger number of denoising steps in these methods may increase infer-
780   ence time and necessitates a first-order autoregressive structure. Additionally, as detailed above, our
781
      proposed method does not readily fit into the framework of denoising diffusion models.

      A.5   P RACICAL A DVICE ON H YPERPARAMETER C HOICE
912
913   In the following, we provide some pracitcal advice on choosing some of the most important hyper-
914   parameters in AFM. Generally, we tuned few hyper-parameters in our experiments. We found the
915   number of generation steps T to be one of the most impactful hyper-parameters.
916
917   Filtration Function. The filtration function f : E → R is the main component determining the
      structure of the graph sequence during stage I training. As discussed in Sec. 3.1, we recommend


                                                                17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      that f should convey meaningful information about the structure (i.e., be structurally consistent) and
919   assign (mostly) distinct values to distinct edges (i.e., be diverse). We note that if f fails to be diverse,
920   many edges may be added in a single generation step, regardless of the choice of T . We found the
921   edge Fiedler function to perform well in many settings, and used this filtration function throughout
922   our experiments. We recommend that practioners utilize this filtration function and perform experi-
923   ments with further filtration functions that incorporate domain-specific inductive biases. In the case
924   of generating protein graphs, for instance, one may consider a filtration function that quantifies the
925   distance of residues in the sequence (this filtration would first generate a backbone path, followed
926   by increasingly long-range interactions of residues).
927
928   Filtration Granularity. As we demonstrate in Sec. 4.4, the choice of the number of generation
929
      steps T has a substantial influence on sampling efficiency and generation. Generally, T can be
      chosen substantially smaller than in other autoregressive models. In our experiments, we chose
930
      T = 15 or T = 30. We recommend that practitioners experiment with different values in this order
931
      of magnitude. We further caution that increasing T does not necessarily improve sample quality,
932   and may actually harm it.
933
934   Scheduling Function. The scheduling function γ : [0, 1] → [0, 1] governs the rate at which
935   edges are added at different timesteps and should be monotonically increasing with γ(0) = 0 and
936   γ(1) = 1. We found the heuristic choice of γ(t) := t to work well in many settings. However,
937   as we demonstrate in Appendix A.16, the concave schedule may be a promising alternative. We
938   recommend that practitioners validate stage I training with a convex, linear, and concave schedul-
939   ing function. We note that the scheduling function is no longer used during stage II training, as
940   the model is left free to generate arbitrary intermediate graphs. Hence, performance after stage I
941   training may be a suitable metric for selecting a scheduling function.
942
943
      Noise Augmentation. We use noise augmentation during training stage I to counteract exposure
      bias, i.e. the accumulation of errors in the sampling trajectory. Manual inspection of the graph se-
944
      quence G0 , . . . , GT may be difficult. However, we found that inspecting the development of the edge
945
      density over this graph sequence can provide a simple tool for diagnosing exposure bias. Namely,
946   we expect the density to be mostly governed by the scheduling function γ(t). E.g., for the linear
947   schedule, the density should increase roughly linearly with t. In models that do not utilize noise
948   augmentation, we can observe that, after some generation steps, the edge density can oftemtimes
949   deviate from this expected behavior (e.g. by suddenly increasing or becoming non-monotonic). In
950   this case we expect that noise augmentation can rectify exposure bias. In our experiments, we find
951   that we do not need to tune the noise schedule. Instead, we fix a single schedule that is shared across
952   all models. For details on this schedule, we refer to Appendix A.8.
953
954   Perturbation of Node Orderings. During training stage I, AFM may overfit on small datasets.
955   This manifests as an increase in validation loss, while the validation MMD metrics continue to
956
      improve. We observe this behavior only on the small datasets in Sec. 4.1 and find that it can be
      mostly attributed to the node ordering used to derive initial node representations (c.f. Appendix A.6).
957
      We recommend to monitor validation losses during stage I training. If the validation loss starts to
958
      slowly increase while the training loss continues to decrease, we recommend to randomly perturb
959   the node ordering, as described in Appendix A.6. Increase the noise scale σ until no over-fitting can
960   be observed.

       A.18     A DVERSARIAL F INETUNING D ETAILS
1491
1492   We provide pseudocode for the adversarial fine-tuning stage in Algorithm 1. We note that we do not
1493   make all procedures explicit and that many hyper-parameters must be chosen (including the number
1494   of steps and epochs in T RAIN G ENERATOR A NDVALUE M ODEL).
1495
1496   Generator. The generator operates in inference mode, meaning that all dropout layers are disabled
1497   and batch normalization modules utilize the (now frozen) moving averages from training stage I.
1498   Hence, the behavior of the generative model becomes reproducible. It acts as a stochastic policy in
1499   a higher-order MDP, where the graphs G0 , . . . , GT are the states. It receives a terminal reward for
1500   the plausibility of the final sample GT .
1501
1502   Discriminator. The discriminator is implemented as a GraphGPS (Rampásek et al., 2022) model
1503   which performs binary classification on graph samples GT , distinguishing real samples from gen-
1504   erated samples. It is trained via binary cross-entropy on batches consisting in equal proportions of
1505   generated graphs and graphs from the dataset D. For a given graph GT , the discriminator produces
1506   a probability of ”realness” by applying the sigmoid function to its logit. Following SeqGAN (Yu
1507   et al., 2017), the log-sigmoid of the logit then acts as a terminal reward for the generative model.
1508   We emphasize that only the final graph GT is presented to the discriminator.
1509
1510   Value Model. The value model uses the same backbone architecture as our generative model and
1511   regresses scalars from pooled node representations. It is trained via least squares regression. The
       value model is used to compute baselined reward-to-go values.


                                                           28
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1512
       Training Outline. While Algorithm 1 provides a technical description of the training algorithm,
1513   we also provide a rougher outline here. At the start of training stage II, the generator is initialized
1514   with the weights learned in training stage I, while the discriminator and value model are initialized
1515   randomly. Before entering the main training loop, we pre-train discriminator and value model to
1516   match the generator. Namely, we first pre-train the discriminator to classify graphs as either ”real”
1517   or ”generated”. The log-likelihood of ”realness” acts as a terminal reward of the generative model.
1518   The discriminator is then pre-trained to regress the reward-to-go. After pre-training is finished, we
1519   proceed to the training loop, which consists of alternating training of (i) the generator and value
1520   model and (ii) the discriminator. As described above, the generator is trained via PPO to maximize
1521
       the terminal reward provided by the discriminator. The value model is used to baseline the reward
       and is continuously trained to regress the reward-to-go. The discriminator, on the other hand, con-
1522
       tinues to be trained on generated and real graph samples via binary cross-entropy.
