009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual learning with language models (LMs) is a promising and challenging
012            research that greatly impacts many applications. Existing solutions treat previous
013            tasks equally, making them vulnerable to task interference, lacking scalability
014            with a large number of tasks, and oblivious to the intrinsic relationships among
015            tasks. This work presents selective knowledge transfer (SKT), a novel framework
016            towards continual learning with LMs. SKT aims to maximize positive knowledge
017            transfer while systematically minimizing the effects of irrelevant information
018
               from dissimilar tasks. To this end, SKT first assesses the degree of interference
               between the current and previous tasks and then selectively aggregates the tasks that
019
               maximize knowledge transfer for continual training. In addition, we integrate SKT
020
               into the current state-of-the-art continual language learning algorithm, Progressive
021            Prompts, to introduce Log-evidence Progressive Prompts (LePP), which facilitates
022            knowledge transfer between tasks. Comprehensive evaluations on challenging few-
023            shot continual learning benchmarks demonstrate that LePP can surpass existing
024            baselines for continual learning with LMs with minimal overhead. Our extensive
025            ablation studies reveal that SKT can discover useful task correlations without any
026            prior knowledge, many of which align with human evaluations. Code will be
027            published upon acceptance.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Modern language models are required to efficiently adapt to dynamic environments with only a few
033
      labeled samples, to enable widespread adoption in the real world. This adaptability allows users to
      effectively interact with language models, e.g. a chatbot, by providing a couple of input-output pairs
034
      to achieve their desired outcomes. This goal can be achieved through Few-shot Continual Learning
035
      (FSCL) (Zhang et al., 2024b; Pasunuru et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022), where the language models can
036   adapt to non-stationary distributions in a few-shot manner. However, two main obstacles arise in
037   learning a task stream: preventing catastrophic forgetting (CF), where the model’s performance on
038   previous tasks significantly deteriorates after learning a new one, and encouraging forward transfer
039   (FT), which leverages learned knowledge to facilitate the learning process of new tasks.
040
      Existing approaches (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Rusu et al., 2016; Nguyen et al., 2017; Yoon et al.,
041
      2018) address forgetting by isolating model parameters related to past knowledge. For example,
042   in regularization-based approaches (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2017; Huang et al.,
043   2021), the model parameters of future tasks are constrained to remain close to those of previous
044   ones by adding auxiliary regularizers to the final loss function. Besides, parameter isolation-based
045   approaches (Rusu et al., 2016; Yoon et al., 2018) allocate new parameters for each new task and freeze
046   the neurons associated with prior tasks to preserve knowledge. This method mitigates forgetting and
047   leverages prior knowledge for FT through parameter sharing.
048   Research on continual learning (CL) for NLP tasks has extended these ideas to enable language
049   models to continually learn new tasks without forgetting. In particular, the NLP community has
050   focused heavily on the parameter isolation-based approach (Ke et al., 2020; 2021; Razdaibiedina et al.,
051   2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Peng et al., 2024) because learning with pre-trained LMs significantly
052   improves the performance of CL systems and LMs are flexible to adapt to new tasks with parameter-
053   efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang,
      2021). We argue that existing PEFT-based approaches are vulnerable to task interference due to

054
      their naive aggregation mechanisms (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023), and they lack scalability because
055   of inefficient similar task selection procedures (Peng et al., 2024; Ke et al., 2020; 2021). Therefore,
056   these methods hinder the possibility of positive forward transfer, which plays a crucial role in FSL
057   where learning from similar tasks leads to better performance (Zhou et al., 2021), and the system
058   scalability with the number of tasks.
059
      In this study, we propose selective knowledge transfer (SKT), a novel and generalized framework
060
      to maximize the positive forward transfer of LMs in FSCL settings. In particular, our framework
061   consists of two stages: (1) Selection where relevant memories are chosen; (2) Aggregation where
062   the correlated past memories are effectively aggregated to facilitate the adaptation of the current task.
063   Following these principles, we devise Log-evidence Progressive Prompts (LePP), a CL algorithm
064   utilizing transferability measures (TMs) to select similar tasks. Unlike previous works that require
065   learning each task’s representation as a task key or a probe soft prompt, LePP only necessitates a
066   single forward pass over the few-shot dataset for each trained prompt, making it more computationally
067   efficient than its counterparts.
068   To validate the effectiveness of LePP, we conduct experiments on several challenging continual NLP
069   benchmarks. Experimental results demonstrate that LePP outperforms existing CL approaches with
070   prompt tuning. By integrating selective knowledge transfer (SKT) into its mechanism, LePP effec-
071   tively leverages relevant past knowledge while discarding irrelevant information, thereby accelerating
072   the learning of the current task and showcasing its scalability with an increasing number of tasks
073   in a sequence. Additionally, our ablation studies reveal that the task correlations identified by our
074   proposed framework align with human evaluations.
075   In summary, our paper presents three significant contributions:
076
077          • We introduce selective knowledge transfer (SKT), a novel CL framework for LMs. Our
078            framework leverages TMs, a recent advancement in model selection, to enable systems to
079
               autonomously identify memories relevant to the current task. To our best knowledge, this is
               the first exploration of applying TMs in CL with LMs.
080
081          • We integrate our proposed framework into current prompt-based CL algorithms that can
082            maximize positive knowledge transfer to improve system performance.
083          • We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the superiority of our proposed framework
084            over previous SoTAs on popular NLP benchmarks. Ablation studies reveal that SKT can
085            uncover task correlations within a stream, which helps to understand the effect of task
086
               relatedness on the performance of the CL system. In addition, we show that SKT can work
               with different data modalities including images.

140   Transferability measures In (Tran et al., 2019), transferability Tr(Ds , Dt ) of source task Ds to
141
      target task Dt is defined as the expected log-likelihood on the training set of trained weights ws
      with Ds on target dataset Dt i.e., Tr(Ds , DT ) = E[log p(ws , Dt )]. However, an exact calculation of
142
      Tr(Ds , Dt ) is not required to compare models in practice since we only need a measure having a
143
      positive correlation with Tr(Ds , Dt ). Therefore, transferability measures (TM) are developed as an
144   computationally efficient alternative for tasks or model comparison. In particular, a transferability
145   measure M is a real-valued metric taking (Ds , Dt ) as inputs and returns a real value M(Ds , Dt ) ∈ R.
146   In cases where the source task Ds is unavailable, we can approximate M(Ds , Dt ) ≈ M(ws , Dt ).
147   Given two pre-trained weights w1 and w2 learned from different sources, a target dataset Dt , trans-
148   ferability scores M(w1 , Dt ) and M(w2 , Dt ), and the actual performance scores Perf(w1 , Dt ) and
149   Perf(w2 , Dt ) of w1 and w2 on the target dataset Dt , M should satisfy the following equation:
150
151                        Perf(w1 , Dt ) ≤ Perf(w2 , Dt ) ⇐⇒ M(w1 , Dt ) ≤ M(w2 , Dt ).                                           (1)
152
153   As indicated in Eq.(1), the pre-trained weight w2 has a higher transferability score than w1 , and w2 is
154   expected to achieve higher performance on Dt than w1 .

158   dataset features extracted by a source model F ∈ Rn×d with its corresponding labels y ∈ Rn .

179   Selection We introduce the selection process of past memories with the help of transferability
180   measures. In parameter isolation-based CL approaches, a set of incremental parameters P =
181   {θ1 , . . . , θt−1 } associated with previous tasks {T1 , . . . , Tt−1 } are used to learn a subsequent task

      trained on the most prior similar tasks to the current task Tt , where K is a hyper-parameter. To

197   Discussions Our proposed framework is general, and can be applied to any parameter-efficient
198   tuning approaches such as Prompt Tuning (PT) (Lester et al., 2021), Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019)
199   and LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). In Sec. 3.2, we devise Log-evidence Progressive Prompts, a representative

201
      framework is its efficiency, as it does not require training per task’s representation (Zhang et al.,

202
      gradient descent calculation. Finally, using TMs offers a useful interpretation of task similarity, i.e.,
203
      identifying which tasks should be jointly trained with the current task to maximize accuracy. The
204   experiment in Sec. 4.4 demonstrates this capability of our proposed method.

289   4.2       I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
290
      Backbones SKT is a model-agnostic method for CL that can be integrated into any backbone.
291
      For NLP, we employ three main backbones with different scales and architectures including
292   BERTbase (Devlin et al., 2018) with 110M parameters, and T5 backbones (T5small and T5large
293   with 60M and 770M parameters, respectively). We use the model implementations and pre-trained
294   weights from Hugging Face (Wolf et al., 2020) to ensure consistent results.
295
296   Training configurations We conducted experiments with T5small and BERTbase on 4 NVIDIA
297   A5000 GPUs, while experiments with T5large were conducted on 2 NVIDIA A40 GPUs. We used
298   hyperparameters as indicated in (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) for a fair comparison. In particular, we
299   employ Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.3 and a batch size of 8. We set the prefix length of the
300   soft prompt to 10 tokens per task. Since T5 models are encoder-decoder models, we utilize the same
301   text-to-text format as in Progressive Prompts for training T5 models. For example, the label “0/1” is
302
      converted to “positive/negative” for text generation. For BERT-based models, the prefix length is 50
      tokens per task and the learning rate is chosen at 0.1. In addition, we applied the reparameterization
303
      trick via a two-layer MLP with 800 hidden neurons in each layer, which can be discarded during
304
      inference. Since the BERT-based model is encoder-only, we keep the original task labels and train an
305   additional linear layer on top of the encoder to classify sentences. In our experiments, we selected
306   five prompts with the highest transferability scores for knowledge aggregation.

467   5    R ELATED WORKS
468
469   Continual learning In continual learning (CL), neural networks are prone to catastrophic forgetting
470   when trained sequentially on a sequence of tasks with non-stationary data. To address this, constraints
471   can be placed on the parameter space to maintain stability, such as Elastic Weight Consolidation
472   (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) adding regularizers to keep task parameters close, Progressive Net-
473   works (Rusu et al., 2016) freezing trained parameters, and Dynamically Expandable Network (Yoon
474   et al., 2018) identifying key parameters for new tasks via retraining. In language models, these ideas
475
      enhance robustness to forgetting and knowledge sharing between tasks. For instance, IDBR (Huang
      et al., 2021) disentangles task-specific and task-generic information, while CTR (Ke et al., 2021) and
476
      CAT (Ke et al., 2021) introduce CL plug-in modules between BERT layers for knowledge sharing,
477
      but these methods face inefficiencies with large models due to whole network training, task masking,
478   and memory constraints. Especially, those methods are only tested on either NLP tasks (Ke et al.,
479   2020; Huang et al., 2021; Ke et al., 2021; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) or CV tasks (Nguyen et al.,
480   2017; Yoon et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022b;c; 2024a).
481
482   Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT) (Houlsby et al., 2019;
483   Liu et al., 2022; Li & Liang, 2021; Hu et al., 2021) has demonstrated significant success in fine-tuning
484   foundational language models with a remarkable reduction in parameters. Building on this success
485   in single-task adaptation, several approaches (Madotto et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
      2024b) have been devised to extend its application to continual learning (CL) settings. For example,


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      AdapterCL (Madotto et al., 2021) trains a separate adapter for each task to facilitate the continual
487   training of task-oriented dialog systems. LFPT5 (Qin & Joty, 2022) addresses the few-shot language
488   learning problem by learning a large soft prompt shared among tasks to enhance knowledge sharing.
489   PTCC (Zhang et al., 2024b) recalibrates the weights of each trained soft prompt for initializing
490   new tasks based on the similarity between tasks in both context and label spaces. However, these
491   approaches suffer from several limitations, including limited knowledge sharing (Madotto et al.,
492   2021), catastrophic forgetting (Qin & Joty, 2022), task interference (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023), and
493   limited scalability with the number of tasks (Peng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b).
494
495   Transferability measures Selecting the pre-trained model that can achieve the highest performance
496   after fine-tuning poses a significant challenge, given the vast number of off-the-shelf pre-trained
497
      models available on model hubs (Wolf et al., 2020; maintainers & contributors, 2016), and the
      impracticality of brute-force fine-tuning. Transferability measures (Nguyen et al., 2020; 2023; Huang
498
      et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2019; You et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024a), which produce a real-value
499
      score correlated with the actual model performance for model ranking, have emerged as a promising
500   solution to this problem. Although transferability measures have shown success in computer vision
501   tasks (Agostinelli et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b), their application in NLP tasks is still limited
502   to single-task transfer learning (Bassignana et al., 2022), while their potential in continual learning
503   (CL) remains largely unexplored. In a recent work (Bassignana et al., 2022), authors highlight that
504   LogME provides a more robust method for ranking model performance compared to the intuitions of
505   NLP practitioners. Motivated by this observation, we take the first step in applying transferability
506   measures to facilitate the forward transfer of CL systems.
507
508   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
509
510   Conclusions We present selective knowledge transfer (SKT), a novel framework for few-shot
511   continual learning with LMs, to advance the positive forward transfer for learning future tasks.
512   Our proposed framework is computationally efficient and can autonomously reveal similar and
513   dissimilar tasks, therefore its scalability is guaranteed with the length of the task stream. We develop
514   Log-evidence Progressive Prompts LePP, an enhanced version of Progressive Prompts following
515   our proposed principle. Extensive experiments confirm our proposed framework can significantly
516
      leverage existing SoTAs for continual learning with NLP and CV.
517
      Limitations This study has several limitations. First, it focuses solely on classification tasks,
518
      excluding other NLP tasks like text generation. This exclusion arises because existing transferability
519
      measures mainly cater to classification and regression tasks. Although text generation could be
520   treated as a regression task, this approach neglects the intricate relationships between words in
521   sentences, leaving the challenge of developing an efficient transferability measure for generative
522   models unresolved. Second, our framework relies on a hyper-parameter K to select the optimal
523   amount of relevant knowledge, which is a sub-optimal ideal. A potential solution is to use a greedy
524   algorithm for a near-optimal selection or develop a measure to identify tasks that might cause negative
525   transfer. These aspects remain open for future exploration.

776   A.4    A LGORITHM
777
      This section outlines the procedure for extracting features on a target dataset D using a trained prompt
778
      P . Firstly, for each sample, we feed the tokenized input xi through the embedding layer to obtain the
779   embedding vector Xi . Then, the trained prompt P is prepended to embedding Xi , resulting in the
780   prompted embedding[P ; X], which is then forwarded to h, the rest of the encoder model, to output
781   the feature fi . This process is repeated for every sample in the dataset D to get the feature matrix F .
