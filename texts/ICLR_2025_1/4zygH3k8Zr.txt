009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional end-to-end deep learning models often enhance feature representation
012            and overall performance by increasing the depth and complexity of the network
013            during training. However, this approach inevitably introduces issues of parameter
014            redundancy and resource inefficiency, especially in deeper networks. While ex-
015            isting works attempt to skip certain redundant layers to alleviate these problems,
016
               challenges related to poor performance, computational complexity, and inefficient
               memory usage remain. To address these issues, we propose an innovative training
017
               approach called Replacement Learning, which mitigates these limitations by com-
018
               pletely replacing all the parameters of the frozen layers with only two learnable
019            parameters. Specifically, Replacement Learning selectively freezes the parame-
020            ters of certain layers, and the frozen layers utilize parameters from adjacent lay-
021            ers, updating them through a parameter integration mechanism controlled by two
022            learnable parameters. This method leverages information from surrounding struc-
023            tures, reduces computation, conserves GPU memory, and maintains a balance be-
024            tween historical context and new inputs, ultimately enhancing overall model per-
025            formance. We conducted experiments across four benchmark datasets, including
026            CIFAR-10, STL-10, SVHN, and ImageNet, utilizing various architectures such as
027
               CNNs and ViTs to validate the effectiveness of Replacement Learning. Experi-
               mental results demonstrate that our approach reduces the number of parameters,
028
               training time, and memory consumption while completely surpassing the perfor-
029
               mance of end-to-end training.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Updating learnable parameters is a core component of training deep learning models Yang et al.
035   (2019). Currently, the primary mechanism for updating parameters in these frameworks is global
036   backpropagation Mostafa et al. (2018), a technique widely applied in various fields, including com-
037   puter vision Yoo (2015); Voulodimos et al. (2018), natural language processing Goldberg (2016;
038   2017), and speech processing Ahmad et al. (2004); Chauvin & Rumelhart (2013). However, the
039
      increase in network depth and model complexity during training leads to a rapid expansion in the
      computation time and parameter demands required by global backpropagation Nawi et al. (2008).
040
      This rise in computational and memory costs inevitably poses significant challenges to GPU pro-
041
      cessing capabilities and memory capacity Bragagnolo et al. (2022). Furthermore, the high similarity
042   in learning patterns between adjacent layers in deep learning models Kleinman et al. (2021) results
043   in parameter redundancy and inefficient resource usage throughout the computation process. With
044   the growing popularity of large models, there is an urgent need to find training methods that can
045   shorten computation time, reduce GPU memory usage, and still ensure model performance.
046
      To address these challenges, researchers have explored alternatives to traditional backpropagation,
047
      such as feedback alignment Lillicrap et al. (2014); Nøkland (2016), forward gradient learning Del-
048   laferrera & Kreiman (2022); Ren et al. (2022), and local learning Su et al. (2024a;b). These methods
049   aim to reduce computational overhead by updating weights without relying entirely on backpropa-
050   gation. However, these improvements do not completely address the inherent short-sightedness: the
051   separation into blocks can make each part of the network only focus on its local objectives, possibly
052   ignoring the overall objectives of the network. This can lead to the discarding of globally benefi-
053   cial information due to the lack of inter-block communication. Additionally, self-attention layers
      in Vision Transformers (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) exhibit high correlations between adjacent

      In this paper, we introduce a novel learning approach called Replacement Learning, designed to ad-
079   dress the challenge of maintaining model performance while reducing computational overhead and
080   resource consumption. Replacement Learning freezes specific layers, and during backpropagation,
081   these frozen layers utilize parameters from adjacent layers, updating them through a parameter inte-
082   gration mechanism controlled by two learnable parameters, further optimizing efficiency. Consider-
083   ing that parameters from adjacent layers, if solely derived from either shallow or deep layers, often
084   fail to simultaneously enable frozen layers to excel in learning both local and global features, the
085   frozen layers are designed to leverage parameters from both preceding and succeeding layers, which
086   facilitates a more effective fusion of low-level and high-level information. Moreover, to prevent
087
      the continuity of feature extraction from being disrupted, we introduce optimized interval settings
      for frozen layers in Replacement Learning, striking an effective balance between computational ef-
088
      ficiency and performance. Replacement Learning significantly reduces the number of parameters
089
      while allowing frozen layers to incorporate information from adjacent layers. Balancing historical
090   context with new inputs through the two learnable parameters, Replacement Learning enhances the
091   model’s overall performance. The effectiveness of Replacement Learning has been rigorously val-
092   idated on multiple benchmark image classification datasets, including CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al.
093   (2009), STL-10 Coates et al. (2011), SVHN Netzer et al. (2011), and ImageNet Deng et al. (2009),
094   across various architectures such as CNNs and ViTs. Experimental results demonstrate that Re-
095   placement Learning not only reduces the number of parameters, training time, and memory usage
096   but also outperforms end-to-end Rumelhart et al. (1985) training approaches.
097   We summarize our contributions as follows:
098
099         • We propose a novel learning approach, Replacement Learning, which reduces the number
100           of parameters, training time, and memory consumption while obtaining better performance
101           than end-to-end training Rumelhart et al. (1985).
102         • Replacement Learning has strong versatility, and as a universal training method, it can be
103           seamlessly applied across architectures of varying depths and performs robustly on diverse
104           datasets.
105         • The effectiveness of Replacement Learning has been validated on commonly used datasets
106           such as CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), STL-10 Coates et al. (2011), SVHN Netzer
107           et al. (2011), and ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) on both CNNs and ViTs structures, and its
              performance has fully surpassed that of end-to-end training Rumelhart et al. (1985).

136
137   2.1   A LTERNATIVES TO BACKPROPAGATION
138
139   To address the limitations of backpropagation, such as high computational cost, various alternative
140   methods have been proposed, including target propagation Lee et al. (2015); Bartunov et al. (2018),
141   feedback alignment Lillicrap et al. (2014); Nøkland (2016), and decoupled neural interfaces (DNI)
142   Jaderberg et al. (2017). These approaches bypass traditional global backpropagation by directly
143   propagating errors to individual layers, reducing memory usage and enhancing efficiency. Forward
144
      gradient learning Dellaferrera & Kreiman (2022); Ren et al. (2022) offers a new paradigm for train-
      ing deep networks more effectively. Local learning Zhang et al. (2024); Zhu et al. (2024) segments
145
      the network into smaller, independently trained modules, optimizing local objectives to lower com-
146
      putational demands while preserving some global features Su et al. (2024a;b). However, excessive
147   segmentation can lead to coordination issues, harming overall performance, especially on complex
148   datasets like ImageNet.
149
150
151
      2.2   U TILIZING S URROUNDING L AYERS
152
153
      Leveraging the high similarity in learning conditions of surrounding layers, researchers have solved
154
      many problems in deep learning. Some studies have applied Residual Networks (ResNets) He et al.
155
      (2016), by adding a shortcut connection to the activation function of the next layer, this identity
156   mapping enables ResNet to address the issues of degradation Philipp et al. (2018); Borawar & Kaur
157   (2023), enhancing both the convergence speed and accuracy of the network Zhang et al. (2019);
158   Allen-Zhu & Li (2019). Additionally, some researchers have proposed skipping attention, reusing
159   the self-attention calculations from one layer in the approximations for attention in subsequent lay-
160   ers, achieving higher throughput Venkataramanan et al. (2023). However, due to the repeated use
161   of prior layers, this method carries the risk of error propagation and could potentially cause losses
      during the learning process, impacting the model’s generalization ability.

      3.2   R EPLACEMENT L EARNING
192
193   Traditional end-to-end training Rumelhart et al. (1985) is the mainstream method for training mod-
194   els. However, as the network depth and model complexity increase during training, all layers are
195   involved in the optimization process. Combined with the high computational complexity of chain
196   rule-based gradient calculations in both forward and backward propagation, this results in a large
197   number of parameters and high demands on computation time and resources. Furthermore, given
198   the high similarity of features between adjacent layers, it becomes unnecessary for every layer to
199   participate in parameter updates during backpropagation Rumelhart et al. (1985), which results in
200
      parameter redundancy and inefficient training.
201   To address these issues, we propose Replacement Learning. The innovation of Replacement Learn-
202   ing lies in the mechanism of periodically freezing a layer’s parameters, denoted as θi , utilizing pa-
203   rameters from adjacent layers, and updating them through a parameter integration mechanism con-
204   trolled by two learnable parameters. This idea is inspired by Exponential Moving Average (EMA)
205   He et al. (2020), where two learnable parameters are introduced to balance the historical context
206
      with new inputs.
207   When the adjacent layers used come from the preceding layers of θi , they tend to perform well
208   in learning local features due to capturing preceding contextual information. Still, they are less
209   effective in acquiring global high-level semantic information. Conversely, when the adjacent layers
210   are from the succeeding layers, the deeper layer parameters can ensure the learning of higher-level
211   semantic and global features but perform poorly in extracting fine-grained features and capturing
212
      low-level details. Therefore, we opt to simultaneously utilize both preceding and succeeding layers,
      θi−1 and θi+1 , and integrate their parameters, which facilitates a better fusion of low-level and
213
      high-level information, thereby enhancing the overall performance of the model.
214
215   We incorporate learnable parameters for θi−1 and θi+1 , ai and bi . During the forward propagation,
      the parameters of θi are replaced by parameter integration results based on the parameters of θi−1

524   5   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      This paper introduces a novel learning approach called Replacement Learning to address the prob-
527
      lem of maintaining model performance while reducing computational overhead and resource con-
528   sumption. Replacement Learning effectively reduces the parameter count while enabling frozen lay-
529   ers to integrate information from neighboring layers. Utilizing two learnable parameters to balance
530   historical context and new inputs boosts the model’s overall performance. We apply Replacement
531   Learning to various model structures and evaluate its performance on four widely used datasets
532   across different deep network structures. The results demonstrate that our proposed Replacement
533   Learning not only reduces training time and GPU usage but also consistently outperforms end-to-end
534   training in terms of overall performance.
535   Limitations and future work: Although our proposed Replacement Learning can reduce the num-
536   ber of parameters to be computed, save memory, and decrease training time while outperforming
537   end-to-end training, it has only been applied to image-based tasks and has not yet been extended to
538   other large models, such as those in natural language processing or multimodal settings. In future
539   work, we plan to explore the impact of Replacement Learning on other tasks to achieve a more
      comprehensive evaluation of the model’s effectiveness.
