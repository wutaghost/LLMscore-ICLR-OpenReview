009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Cryptic crossword clues are challenging language tasks for which new test sets are
012               released daily by major newspapers on a global basis. Each cryptic clue contains
013               both the definition of the answer to be placed in the crossword grid (in common
014               with regular crosswords), and ‘wordplay’ that proves that the answer is correct (i.e.
015               a human solver can be confident that an answer is correct without needing cross-
016
                  ing words as confirmation). This work describes an LLM-based reasoning system
                  built from open-licensed components that solves cryptic clues by (i) hypothesis-
017
                  ing answers; (ii) proposing wordplay explanations; and (iii) using a verifier system
018
                  that operates on codified reasoning steps. Overall, this system establishes a new
019               state-of-the-art performance on the challenging Cryptonite dataset of clues from
020               The Times and The Telegraph newspapers in the UK. Because each proved solu-
021               tion is expressed in Python, interpretable wordplay reasoning for proven answers
022               is available for inspection.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
026
      Recent advances in computational models have significantly improved their ability to handle di-
027
      verse natural language tasks involving complex syntactic and semantic interpretations. Despite these
028
      strides, machines continue to fall short of human performance in several areas, most notable being
029   those involving reasoning. This work tackles the relatively under-studied reasoning task of cryptic
030   crossword solving, which is a popular activity across the world, with multiple papers in the UK,
031   Australia, India and elsewhere featuring daily puzzles for readers to solve.
032
      The following is an example of a cryptic clue1 of moderate complexity:
033
034   clue (4D):           "Cut up over politician on the French case (7)"
035
036   Solvers must interpret the clue to understand the supporting wordplay to arrive at the answer
037
      from two directions (see also Figure 2a for a visual depiction of the reasoning involved):
038   definition: "Cut up over politician on the French {case}"
039   wordplay:   "(AXE)< (cut, <up), MP, LE (the, in French)"
040   answer:     "EXAMPLE"
041
042   In the above, the reasoning steps are: (i) identifying which part of the clue is the definition
043
      (highlighted with curly braces) that acts as a regular crossword clue; (ii) parsing the remainder of
      the clue for the wordplay - here, for instance, (a) the indicator word ‘up’ tells us to reverse the
044
      word resulting from ‘Cut’ (since this is a down-oriented clue), (b) this is ‘over’ the abbreviation
045
      of Member of Parliament ‘MP’ (a politician in the UK), (c) and ‘on’ a French translation of ‘the’
046   (common knowledge); and (iii) finally assembling the parts to ‘prove’ that the correct answer is
047   ‘EXAMPLE’ (this agrees with the definition span, with the correct number of letters).
048
049
      In this work, we take our cue from the effectiveness of provers coupled with verifiers for math-
      ematical reasoning tasks (Jiang et al., 2023). We tackle the cryptic crossword clue solving task
050
      using an LLM to (i) suggest answer candidates; (ii) create informal proofs (i.e. coming up with
051
      wordplay); and (iii) perform a formalisation process (which rewrites the wordplay logic in
052   Python). The proposed solutions are then checked with a verifier for validity.

078   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
079
080   The following are the main contributions of this work:
081   • An open-license system for reasoning over Cryptic clues - our pipeline (illustrated in Figure 1)
082     enables 9B-scale local models to achieve state-of-the-art results on the Cryptonite dataset.
083
      • Local models for cryptic clue tasks - We show how local LLMs can be fine-tuned to produce
084     answer candidates, and wordplay suggestions, and then prompted to perform Wordplay for-
085     malisation. Following an approach akin to mathematical statement formalisation, but where there
086     are less than 10 examples of ‘good proofs’ available, our novel pipeline was specifically engi-
087     neered to avoid ‘reasoning steps’ becoming stuck in dead ends.
088
      • Python domain-specific verifier - Using the output of the formaliser, the verifier presented here
089
        deconstructs the Python AST, so that it can evaluate each assert statement on a line-by-line
090     basis. We believe that this is somewhat novel, since it enables the verifier to not only indicate
091     whether the proof is valid overall, but also point to specific failures (used to regenerate failed
092     formalisations) on all proof lines simultaneously.
093   To promote further study in this area, all code for the formaliser and domain-specific verifier is made
094   publicly available.

099   2.1   R EGULAR C ROSSWORDS
100
101   Non-cryptic (“regular”) crosswords are known throughout the world, and are the predominant type
102   found in newspapers in the U.S.A. One key difference from cryptic crosswords is that individual
103   regular crossword clues are generally not ‘standalone’ - there may be a number of different answers
104
      that fit the given clue. The key to solving regular crosswords is thus the interaction between answers
      (i.e. the crossing-words), which allows for planning/backtracking to enable solving rates in the high
105
      90% range (Wallace et al., 2022).
106
107   This work, in contrast, focuses on the solving of clues on a standalone basis, which requires elements
      of reasoning through the wordplay present in cryptic clues.

125   2.2     C RYPTIC C ROSSWORDS
126
127   In an 800 participant research study into the backgrounds of cryptic crossword solvers (Friedlander
128
      & Fine, 2016), the following observation was made about the skills required to solve these linguis-
      tic/reasoning puzzles:
129
130
                “... cryptic crossword skill therefore appears to be bound up with code-cracking
131             and problem-solving skills of a quasi-algebraic nature. Conversely, lexical ability,
132             although no doubt valuable, does not appear to be a critical discriminator of high
133             expertise among elite solvers.”
134
135   Cryptic crosswords have received surprisingly little attention from the machine learning community,
136   despite being a notable language-oriented reasoning puzzle with global appeal. One possible reason
137   is that cryptic crosswords are much less common in the United States than ‘regular crosswords’. See
138   Webb (2024) for an inspiring demonstration of expert solving a cryptic crossword in real-time.
139   The benchmark dataset used by this work is Cryptonite (Efrat et al., 2021) - a large-scale dataset of
140   Cryptic Crossword clues from The Times and The Telegraph (major UK newspapers). The dataset
141   contains 523,000 naturally sourced clues (published between 2001 and 2020), with the train, valida-
142   tion and testing splits being chosen so that a given answer can only appear in one of the splits.
143   While the dataset made available in Rozner et al. (2021) is also of interest, its clues are limited
144   to those from the Guardian newspaper, and Connor (2024) notes in the Guardian’s own blog “The
145   Times hosts an annual crossword-solving competition and it remains, until such time as the Guardian
146   has its own version, the gold standard.” Moreover, the smaller number (142,000) of clues the dataset
147   contains have no orientation markings (‘across/down’), which are required to make sense of some
148   wordplay.
149   For a more in-depth discussion of the decision to focus on the Cryptonite dataset (and not per-
150   form testing on the Guardian dataset), please see Appendix A.3. In summary, while the ’Init’ split
151   presented in Rozner et al. (2021) has attractive properties (explored there, and in other works), this
152   work specifically targets the wordplay reasoning side of cryptic clues, and since that involves fine-
153   tuning models on Cryptonite (including Wordplay examples with carefully matched train/val/test
154   splits), this precludes us from doing the same kind of multi-dataset comparisons found elsewhere.

180   2.2.2    LLM- BASED SOLVERS
181
182   Cryptonite is a challenging task for LLMs : Efrat et al. (2021) reported that fine-tuning T5-Large
183   (a 770M encoder-decoder model) on Cryptonite’s 470k cryptic clue training set achieved only 7.6%
184   test set accuracy, slightly below the 8.6% accuracy of the rule-based clue solver of Deits (2022).
185   Interestingly, prior to 2024, even large-scale Language Models scored very poorly on cryptic clues,
186
      likely due to (i) the misleading surface reading of the clues; (ii) the obliqueness of the definitions;
      and (iii) the reasoning steps required to prove the answer correct based on the wordplay.
187
188   Recent works, such as Sadallah et al. (2024) and Saha et al. (2024), tackle cryptic crosswords with
189   more up-to-date local models, and also commercial LLMs. Saha et al. (2024) reports results with
190   5- and 10-Shot prompting (without fine-tuning models), but also includes a wide-ranging study of
191   the capabilities of models for crosswords in general. We include experiments that bring the relevant
192
      baselines up-to-date, and also touch on their illuminating Partial Correctness Metrics (which are
      relevant when attempting full grids, but not the main focus here).
193
194   In this work, we use a pipeline of 9B-scale LMs to produce answer candidates and wordplay
195   suggestions, followed by a third LM to formalise each proposed solution using Python code and
196   then rewrite/update the solutions based on feedback from a purpose-built verifier. In our results, we
197   focus on the ‘pure’ Cryptonite benchmark: Accuracy is judged based on a Top-1 basis (with the
198
      model’s single answer being marked wholly correct or not), with no crossing letters being given.
      Framed as a reasoning task, if the model ‘understands’ the cryptic solution properly, the answer
199
      will be wholly correct - there should be no partial marks.
200
201
      2.3     C ODE & REASONING
202
203   To compensate for LLM approximate generation of logical reasoning, techniques like PAL (Gao
204   et al., 2023) exploit LLMs’ facility for writing code to create verifiable reasoning chains. An im-
205   portant influence on this work was also the Draft, Sketch, and Prove framework (Jiang et al., 2023)
206   which uses an LLM to draft and create proofs that are then verified formally.
207
      Informed by the evolution from AlphaCode (Li et al., 2022), in which huge numbers of programs
208   are generated and filtered in order to generate a valid solution, to AlphaCodium (Ridnik et al., 2024),
209   in which solutions are iterated upon and involving much less computation, this work uses a verifier
210   that can feed back ‘hints’ to the formalising LLM, so that the task of re-writing nearly-valid proofs
211   is made easier.

      3     M ETHODS
224
225
      The overall system described in the work is illustrated in Figure 1. The order of operations for the
226
      pipeline was chosen based on watching human solvers - who report going through the following
227   steps: (a) attempting to parse the clue in a number of ways, trying to isolate the definition from
228   the wordplay; (b) seeing which parts of the wordplay they are most confident about; (c) ‘having a
229   hunch’ of the final answer; and (d) gaining a full understanding of how a clue’s wordplay works
230   (such that the function of every element can be explained) as proof of the overall process.
231
      Observations of the behaviour of GPT-4 using Chain-of-Thought prompts Wei et al. (2023) (or more
232
      recently ‘o1’), suggest that even very capable models tend to fixate early on during the reasoning
233   process, and are only rarely able of completely re-hypothesising. Theses LLMs also frequently
234   becomes caught up with the literal (’surface’) meaning of the clue, which is often misleading. The
235   combination of these elements caused us to re-consider how to approach this kind of problem.
236
      By organising our system’s pipeline to hypothesis candidate answers as the first step (so that the
237
      models must try to fit the reasoning to the answer, with varying degrees of success) bakes re-
238
      hypothesisation into the process. Another aspect that required care was the looseness of the language
239   used in cryptic clues, which may stop even valid ‘reasoning’ from being provable.
240
241
      3.1   C ANDIDATE answer GENERATION
242
243   Our first step to solving a given cryptic clue is to generate multiple answer candidates from the
244   original clue, pattern and ad (across/down) fields. For this task, we fine-tuned a Gemma2 9B
245   base model (Gemma Team & Google DeepMind, 2024) using the LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) implemen-
246   tation provided by the unsloth package (unsloth.ai, 2024). The model was trained for 1 epoch on
247   the Cryptonite training set of approximately 470,000 examples.
248
      For each clue being evaluated, we generate 20 valid answer candidates, where candidates that
249   did not match the pattern were immediately rejected and regenerated, and those not contained in
250   the crossword words list (Beresford, 2000) were filtered out2 . The number of candidates was chosen
251   to balance generation cost with likelihood of the correct answer appearing in the candidate list - see
252   Figure 7 for a cumulative frequency analysis. The list of candidates was then grouped so that the
253   frequency of each answer could be found - enabling statistics to be collected.
254
255   3.2   G ENERATION OF definition AND wordplay SUGGESTIONS
256
257   To train the wordplay suggestion model, which translates each answer candidate into multiple
258   definition and wordplay suggestions, we make use of the Wordplay dataset of Andrews
259   (2024). For this task, we fine-tuned another Gemma2 9B base model using LoRA. The model was
260
      trained on 4 epochs on a set of approximately 16,800 examples (consisting of solution explanations
      of puzzles from The Times and The Financial Times from selected authors in the Wordplay dataset).

      3.5    P ROOF V ERIFICATION WITH H INTING
352
353   The verifier implemented here must decide whether a given formalisation is valid, and report any
354   errors found to iteratively improve the Python code as feedback to the LLM formaliser in a cycle,
355   as seen in Self-Debug (Chen et al., 2023), and AlphaCodium (Ridnik et al., 2024). Examples of
356   assertion failures, with constructive hinting, are shown in Figure 6.
357   This cycle is repeated until a formalisation is validated (zero assertion failures, considered a ‘SUC-
358   CESS’ with the answer having been proved), or max rewrites=2 is reached. If no Python
359   formalisation can be validated, then the fallback answer is used (defined as being the most fre-
360   quent answer amongst the original candidates produced in the first stage of solving the clue).
361
362   3.6    PARTIAL C ORRECTNESS M ETRICS
363
364   One interesting direction explored in Saha et al. (2024) was the performance of LLMs on cryptic
365   clues if some of the letters were known (as would be the case if an entire grid were being solved).
366
      The conditions examined were with 25%, 50% and 70% of letters ‘known’. Based on observa-
      tions working with the proposed system for single clues (and more general experience of crossword
367
      solving), we approached this problem in two ways.
368
369   For the 25% level of letters ‘known’, it was a simple matter to use our existing system with candidate
370   answers which didn’t match the known letters filtered out. For the higher levels of known letters, we
371   instead used the FastText embedding method of Mikolov et al. (2018) to find the nearest neighbour
372
      answer within The UK Advanced Cryptics Dictionary, Beresford (2000), by comparing against
      the embedding of the raw clue phrase itself.
373
374   The ‘Partial Correctness’ results, while not being a core thrust of our reasoning approach but inter-
375   esting in their own right, are given in Appendix A.5.7.

399   by observing that instruction fine-tuning may (to some extent) penalise off-the-wall answers, which
400   may be essential for our task. In addition, we also observed that while the Top-1 candidate from a
401   model generating with a temperature t = 0.5 had high accuracy, it was beneficial to run candidate
402   generation with t = 1.0 (even though the Top-1 accuracy was lower in this case) - since having a
403   wider spread of answer candidates was useful for our pipeline overall.
404   Figure 7a shows that the probability of the gold answer being among the candidates produced is
405   (unsurprisingly) monotonically increasing in the number of independent samples. It also shows that
406   this process is not yet asymptotically limited, although slowing down with increasing n.
407
      Figure 7b shows that choosing the highest-frequency answer candidate can be a very effective
408   strategy. However, there is a clear limit to this idea: There is a significant probability that cryptic
409   crossword answers are in the long tail of potential answers. Indeed, intentionally creating misleading
410   clue ‘surfaces readings’ is a hallmark of good cryptic clue setting.
411
412   4.2     G EMMA 2 9B wordplay CANDIDATE GENERATION
413
414   Since wordplay is so flexible, it is difficult to evaluate it for accuracy against other examples
415   (without, say, a large LLM to evaluate the differences). However, good wordplay should result in
416   good formalisations, so evaluation is available on an end-to-end basis.
417   One key assumption in the system proposed here is that a correct answer should lead to in-
418   terpretable wordplay, whereas an incorrect answer candidate should give rise to unformalis-
419   able/unverifiable wordplay. The following typical example illustrates how the correct answer
420   leads readily to correct wordplay (the workings of this clue are illustrated in Figure 2d), whereas
421   trials with an incorrect answer candidate (which was, in fact, the most frequent candidate for this
422   clue) give clearly unverifiable wordplay:

503   4.5   O BSERVED L IMITATIONS OF THE S YSTEM
504
      The verifier implemented for this work does not detect a number of potential errors in the Python
505   function it analyses:
506
507   • The entire Python function might consist of comments : Nothing triggers assert
508   • The Python function contains conditional execution, routing around assert statements
509
      • Occasionally, the hint assert XYZ failed results in the re-write : assert XYZ==False
510
511   • The proof may be logically disconnected, with left-hand-side terms not being supported / justified
512     by right-hand-side terms in other lines of the code
513
      These issues do not appear insurmountable, given time and effort. It should be noted that since the
514
      formalising LLM is only being used In-Context there is little chance that the above issues are being
515   systematically abused (which would almost certainly happen if there was learning-in-the-loop in a
516   Reinforcement Learning setting).

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
520
521
      It is hypothesised that the next-token-prediction task may be insufficient to get machines to reason
      and plan (Kambhampati, 2024). Our choice of platform for reasoning experiments was that of
522
      cryptic crossword clue solving - and we have demonstrated early success with a system that include
523
      both LLMs, verifiers and coding aids.
524
525   We believe that our results validate our overall approach to codification of the cryptic crossword
526   problem domain : Generating answer candidates and wordplay suggestions followed by pro-
527
      duction of code via an LLM-based formalisation process, verification using Python code analysis
      tools, and iterative prompting of the LLM formaliser proved quite effective.
528
529   We were happy to discover that our development work using the Gemini-Flash LLM as a formaliser
530   was directly transferable to a non-fine-tuned version of the open-licensed Gemma2-it model for the
531   same role, with little loss of performance, enabling the whole pipeline to be run locally. The weakest
532   link in the chain was, predictably, getting the ‘Aha’ of wordplay creation to work - humans can
533
      still generate wordplay that is beyond the capabilities of current models.
534   The authors sincerely hope that this work sparks interest in the cryptic crossword domain, which
535   presents an array of interesting and challenging reasoning problems on which fruitful research can
536   take place, even for those with limited computation budgets.

704
      A.1     C RYPTIC C ROSSWORD BACKGROUND
705
706   The following borrows extensively from the description on Wikipedia (2024) (kudos to the
707   authors there), to which we have added wordplay annotations in a notation typical of the
708   FifteenSquare.com website (and in the Wordplay dataset use in this work).
709
710   A.1.1    BASICS
711
712
      A cryptic clue leads to its answer only if it is read in the right way. What the clue appears to say when
      read normally (the surface reading) is usually a distraction with nothing to do with the solution. The
713
      challenge is to find the way of reading the clue that leads to the solution.
714
715   A typical clue consists of two parts:
716
717
      • The straight or definition. This is in essence the same as any non-cryptic crossword clue: a
        synonym for the answer. It usually exactly matches the part of speech, tense, and number of
718
        the answer, and usually appears at the start or end of a clue. For our annotations, the span that
719
        encompasses the definition is highlighted using curly braces.
720
721
      • The cryptic, subsidiary indication or wordplay. This gives the solver some instructions on how
        to get to the answer in another (less literal) way. The wordplay parts of clues can be obscure,
722
        especially to a newcomer, but they tend to utilise standard rules and conventions which become
723
        more familiar with practice.
724
725   Sometimes the two parts of the clue are joined with a link word or phrase such as ‘from’, ‘gives’
726   or ‘could be’. One of the tasks of the solver is to find the boundary between the definition and the
727   wordplay, and insert a mental pause there when reading the clue cryptically.
728
      We list below several of the important styles of wordplay that are commonly used, each with
729   an annotated example. For a more comprehensive list, along with an outline of the ‘Ximenean
730   principles’, please see Wikipedia (2024).
731
732   A.1.2    A NAGRAMS
733
734   An anagram is a rearrangement of a certain section of the clue to form the answer. This is usually in-
735   dicated by a codeword which indicates change, movement, breakage or something otherwise amiss.
736
      For example:
737
      clue:       Chaperone shredded corset (6)
738   definition: {Chaperone} shredded corset
739   answer:     ESCORT
740   wordplay:   (corset)* (*shredded)
741
742   A.1.3    C HARADE
743
      In a charade, the answer is formed by joining individually clued words to make a larger word
744
      (namely, the answer). For example:
745
746   clue:       Outlaw leader managing money (7)
747   definition: Outlaw leader {managing money}
748   answer:     BANKING
749   wordplay:   BAN (outlaw) + KING (leader)
750
751   A.1.4    C ONTAINERS
752   A container or insertion clue puts one set of letters inside another. For example (also starting to add
753   a little more indirection):
754
755   clue:       Utter nothing when there's wickedness about (5)
      definition: {utter} nothing when there's wickedness about


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      answer:         VOICE
757   wordplay:       O (nothing) with VICE (wickedness) around it (about)
758
759   A.1.5    D ELETIONS
760
761   Deletion is a wordplay mechanism which removes some letters of a word to create a shorter word.
      For example:
762
763   clue:       Bird is cowardly, about to fly away (5)
764   definition: {Bird} is cowardly, about to fly away
765   answer:     RAVEN
766   wordplay:   [c]RAVEN (cowardly) - 'C' (i.e. circa, about) (-fly away)
767
768   A.1.6    D OUBLE DEFINITION
769   A clue may, rather than having a definition part and a wordplay part, have two definition parts. For
770   example:
771
772   clue:       Not seeing window covering (5)
773   definition: {Not seeing} {window covering}
774
      answer:     BLIND
      wordplay:   Double Definition (DD)
775
776
      A.1.7    H IDDEN WORDS
777
778   With hidden word clues, the solution itself is written within the clue – either as part of a longer word
779   or across more than one word. For example:
780
781   clue:       Found ermine, deer hides damaged (10)
      definition: Found ermine, deer hides {damaged}
782
      answer:     UNDERMINED
783   wordplay:   [fo]UND ERMINE D[eer] (hides)
784
785   A.1.8    H OMOPHONES
786
787   Homophones are words that sound the same but have different meanings, such as ‘night’ and
788   ‘knight’. Homophone clues always have an indicator word or phrase that has to do with being
789
      spoken or heard. For example:
790
      clue:       We hear twins shave (4)
791   definition: We hear twins {shave}
792   answer:     PARE
793   wordplay:   "pair" (twins, "we hear")
794
795   A.1.9    R EVERSALS
796
      A word that gets turned around to make another is a reversal. For example:
797
798   clue:       Returned beer fit for a king (5)
799   definition: Returned beer {fit for a king}
800   answer:     REGAL
801   wordplay:   (LAGER)< (beer, <returned)

839   • pattern is the number of characters in the answer;
840
      • ad (across/down) is potentially significant, because some clues include directional hints such as
841
        ‘before’ or ‘upwards’ which are only meaningful if the orientation of the answer within the grid
842
        is known;
843
844   • answer is the clue’s final answer (not known to the solvers before solving); and
845   • wordplay is an informally annotated explanation of how the clue words act together to logically
846     build the letters in the answer (the resulting grid letters typically being in upper case) - here the *
847     symbol signifies that ALSO is to be anagrammed due to the anagram indicator (broadcast) in
848     the clue.
849
      The Wordplay dataset is publicly available as Andrews (2024). Note that care was taken to ensure
850
      that the training/validation/test splits follow those of the Cryptonite dataset (and the test set answers
851   are deliberately scrubbed from the retrieved data by the provided scripts, to reduce the chance that
852   they become training data for an over-eager crawling system).

      A.3   C HOICE OF C RYPTONITE VS ROZNER
855
856   At the start of this paper’s research program, the Cryptonite dataset of Efrat et al. (2021) was cho-
857   sen as being the focus, over the approximately contemporaneous dataset from Rozner et al. (2021)
858   (denoted Rozner here), for the following reasons:
859
860   • Cryptonite was larger (523k clues, compared to 142k in Rozner)
861   • Cryptonite consists of clues from The Times and The Telegraph (whereas Rozner is the UK’s
862     Guardian). While these are all fine newspapers, it is clear that in the cryptic crossword community
863     (found online via websites for wordplay discussions, or YouTube channels) that The Times is
        considered the Gold Standard of cryptic crosswords.


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
        – Indeed, Connor (2024) - one of the Guardian’s own cryptic blog posts - directly states: “The
865       Times hosts an annual crossword-solving competition and it remains, until such time as the
866       Guardian has its own version, the gold standard.”
867     – In the authors’ view, The Times deserves its role as Gold Standard due to (a) adhering to /
868       upholding the Ximenean standard Macnutt (1966) for what is allowed in clues; (b) doing so for
869       decades; and (c) maintaining high consistency of clue difficulty within puzzles (where solvers
870       frequently complain that the Guardian clues can often be rather haphazard)
871   • The Cryptonite dataset was made available for direct download - even though the licensing is
872     (politely) ‘fuzzy’, it remains a useable research dataset (and seems unlikely to be challenged
873     by The Times, since it is not possible to reconstruct their full puzzles from the clues given as
874     individual line-items, due to deduplication, for example)
875     – The Rozner dataset required researchers to ‘scrape their own data’, likely because while the
876       data was being retrieved from a public website, the data itself could reasonably be assumed to
877       be copyrighted. This slight inconvenience had a useful impact (please see below)
878
      • Unlike the Cryptonite dataset, the Rozner dataset does not include Across/Down markers for the
879     clues - which makes some of the clues difficult to resolve (for instance EXAMPLE on the paper’s
880     first page can only be read correctly if one sees that it is a Down clue - which converts ‘up’ into a
881     reversal indicator)
882
      • The Cryptonite dataset also includes ‘is quick’ annotations that show whether a clue was taken
883     from a ‘Quick Cryptic’ crossword (these clues are typically easier, which enables a further degree
884     of performance analysis).
885
      • The Cryptonite dataset splits were set in stone. Rozner, though, had a series of splits (random,
886
        disjoint, and ‘init’):
887
888
        – The ‘random’ split was clearly shown to be a poor way of separating train/test due to close
          overlaps
889
890
        – The ‘disjoint’ split is similar in spirit to the Cryptonite methodology
891     – The ‘Init’ split had the additional twist that common prefixes would only be found in their own
892
          splits. This had a catchy intuition, although it’s not clear from a cryptic cluing perspective
          whether this has much genuine basis. While there are some prefixes that are common (eg: EX-
893
          is easily clued by referring to divorce, etc), the impact seems overall marginal (particularly
894
          given the accuracy rate differences reported)
895
896   Our paper describes a system trained on Cryptonite clue/answer training data, and also (as a com-
897   ponent) the Wordplay dataset (which abides by the Cryptonite splits too).
898
      It would be possible to test our existing (Cryptonite trained) system on the Rozner ’Init’ test set.
899
      However, while Saha et al. (2024) could have the flexibility to run tests on either dataset (since no
900   training was performed), running our current model on the Rozner ‘Init’ test set would be clearly
901   mis-aligned vis-a-vis the data split.
902
903
      But there is also a structural reason against re-training the paper’s system on the Rozner ‘Init’ split
      for (specifically) Wordplay. The Wordplay dataset generation process was guided by the principle of
904
      maintaining the Cryptonite splits, it would be a disaster if Rosner ‘Init’ Wordplay splits were to be
905
      made public. The reason: It is very likely that the Cryptonite test set has a large intersection with the
906   Rozner ‘Init’ training set (and conversely). As seems evident from the baseline improvements shown
907   above, OpenAI likely trains on the Cryptonite training set (as they are welcome to do). However,
908   since (as of November 2024) Saha et al. (2024) appears to have released (or re-released) the ‘Init’
909   training set under an MIT license, a commercial vendor such as OpenAI would be quite within their
910   rights to also train on that. Thus, commercial systems (against which reviewers are forcing academic
911   papers to benchmark) will have been trained on the test sets (without commercial vendors explicitly
912   ‘cheating’ - they will just be training on all the available training data).
913   In the authors’ judgement, the reasoning paths that are being tested here through the cryptic cross-
914   word task are a prize cultural asset, generated over decades of human effort, and this should not be
915   squandered. Hopefully, this explains the authors’ reluctance to dataset-hop : We don’t want to make
916   it common to gather and distribute cross-contaminating datasets, specifically Wordplay datasets.
