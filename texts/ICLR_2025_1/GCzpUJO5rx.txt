009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The importance of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) in
012            aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values cannot be overstated.
013            RLHF is a three-stage process that includes supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward
014            learning, and policy learning. Although there are several offline and online ap-
015            proaches to aligning LLMs, they often suffer from distribution shift issues. These
016            issues arise from the inability to accurately capture the distributional interdepen-
017            dence between the reward learning and policy learning stages. Consequently, this
018            has led to various approximated approaches, but the theoretical insights and mo-
019
               tivations remain largely limited to tabular settings, which do not hold in practice.
               This gap between theoretical insights and practical implementations is critical.
020
               It is challenging to address this gap as it requires analyzing the performance of
021
               AI alignment algorithms in neural network-parameterized settings. Although bi-
022            level formulations have shown promise in addressing distribution shift issues, they
023            suffer from the hyper-gradient problem, and current approaches lack efficient al-
024            gorithms to solve this. In this work, we tackle these challenges employing the bi-
025            level formulation laid out in Kwon et al. (2024) along with the assumption Weak
026            Gradient Domination to demonstrate convergence in an RLHF setup, obtaining a
                                          7
027            sample complexity of ѕµвИТ 2 . Our key contributions are twofold: (i) We propose
028            a bi-level formulation for AI alignment in parameterized settings and introduce a
029            first-order approach to solve this problem. (ii) We analyze the theoretical conver-
030            gence rates of the proposed algorithm and derive state-of-the-art bounds. To the
031            best of our knowledge, this is the first work to establish convergence rate bounds
032
               and global optimality for the RLHF framework in neural network-parameterized
               settings. Our contributions are primarily theoretical, providing crucial insights for
033
               future practical implementations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      As state-of-the-art artificial intelligence (AI) systems begin to exceed human performance in nu-
      merous tasks, aligning these systems with human values and ethics becomes critically important.
040
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as an effective strategy for
041
      achieving AI alignment. Models such as GoogleвАЩs Gemini, AnthropicвАЩs Claude, and OpenAIвАЩs GPT-
042   4 have shown safe and aligned behaviors using this approach (Dai et al., 2023). However, most
043   current RLHF-based alignment approaches are offline (Ouyang et al., 2022a; Rafailov et al., 2023).
044   By offline it means that the preference datasets are collected offline to fine-tune the supervised fine-
045   tuned (SFT) model. This method introduces a fundamental bottleneck in improving performance
046   through RLHF because it depends on the data distribution coverage of possible query-response pairs,
047   leading to suboptimal alignment.
048   The existing literature has proposed online RLHF methods (Yuan et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024a;
049   Sharma et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2023) that address the issue of static preference datasets inherent
050   in offline settings. However, online RLHF introduces its own set of fundamental challenges, such
051   as determining the optimal way to generate new responses and collect preference feedback. These
052   challenges are generally addressed in current research (Sharma et al., 2024a; Lee et al., 2023) by
053   utilizing the LLM being trained and employing preference oracles for feedback. Interestingly, this
      approach leads to a well-known distribution shift due to the interdependence between the reward

054
      learning stage and response generation (Guo et al., 2024b; Chakraborty et al., 2024a; Shen et al.,
055   2024). This issue has been effectively captured by recently proposed bi-level optimization frame-
056   works for alignment (Chakraborty et al., 2024a; Ding et al., 2024a).
057
058
      Interestingly, all of the previously discussed approaches, to deal with the theoretical aspects of the
      alignment problem formulation, make a fundamental assumption that the underlying language model
059
      is tabular. This assumption renders the alignment problem strongly convex, enabling a closed-form
060
      solution. Such solutions are commonly utilized in existing methods to derive the final alignment
061   algorithms, for example, DPO, IPO, SLiC, SPIN, and SAIL. However, in real-world applications,
062   this is not the case; language models are invariably parameterized using neural networks. This neural
063   parameterization removes the strong convexity of the problem, making existing theoretical solutions
064   inapplicable, even though they may work empirically. This discrepancy reveals a significant gap
065   between theory and practice in RLHF alignment approaches.
066
      Existing theoretical analyses of RLHF have several significant limitations. For example, Wang et al.
067   (2023) establishes the optimality of a proposed algorithm within the RLHF framework but does not
068   include a parameterized reward model, which is a standard component in modern RLHF methods
069   (Hu et al., 2024). Similarly, Das et al. (2024) achieves convergence but only for linear reward
070   functions вАФa condition not met in practical RLHF implementations. In another instance, Du et al.
071   (2024) allows neural networks to represent the value function but still restricts the reward function
072   to be linear. Additionally, the sample complexity they achieve is on the order of ѕµвИТ18 , rendering
073   it impractical for real-world applications. Li et al. (2024) prove convergence within the RLHF
074   setup under the assumption that the reward function resides in a latent low-dimensional subspace.
075
      However, their convergence results pertain to the parameters of the neural networks representing
      the reward and value functions, rather than to the number of algorithm iterations, and thus do not
076
      provide a sample complexity analysis. Furthermore, Xie et al. (2024) demonstrate the convergence
077
      of the DPO algorithm (which also utilizes the tabular policy assumption to derive the DPO loss), and
078   requires access to minimizers of non-convex loss functions at each iterationвАФassumptions that are
079   difficult to satisfy in practice. Aside from these issues, none of the existing studies have theoretically
080   investigated the online RLHF problem formulated using bilevel optimization.
081
      We tackle this challenge by developing the first global convergence analysis for the online RLHF
082
      method. Our work employs neural network parameterizations and provides a sample complexity
083
      analysis. We summarize our contributions as follows.
084
085   вАҐ First order algorithm for parameterized bi-level formulation of online RLHF problem.
086     We consider the parameterized form of the bilevel formulation of the online RLHF problem
087     (Chakraborty et al., 2024a; Ding et al., 2024a) and develop a first-order approach to solve the
088     problem. True to our best knowledge, this algorithm is novel and is derived without the tabular
089     policy assumption which is common in the existing literature.
090   вАҐ Sample complexity analysis under neural parameterized settings. We derive the first sample
091     complexity bounds for online RLHF in parameterized settings. In order to obtain the sample com-
092     plexity of the above algorithm, we use the weak gradient domination assumption on the reward
093     loss function. Note that this is a weaker assumption than the one used in Chen et al. (2024) (which
094     relies upon the strong gradient domination assumption). This allows us to move beyond the linear
095
        reward assumption required in existing results. We are thus able to obtain a sample complexity of
           3
096     ѕµвИТ 2 , which is state of the art in online RLHF domain.

      8   C ONCLUSION
492
493
      Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values through Reinforcement Learning from
494
      Human Feedback (RLHF) is essential but faces lots of challenges, particularly due to distribution
495   shift issues between the reward learning and policy learning stages. Existing methods often lack the
496   theoretical foundation necessary for practical neural network-parameterized settings, limiting their
497   effectiveness. Our work bridges this critical gap by employing a bi-level optimization framework
498   based on the formulation in Kwon et al. (2024) and introducing the assumption of Weak Gradient
499   Domination. This approach allows us to analyze and demonstrate convergence in an RLHF setup,
500   achieving a sample complexity of ѕµвИТ7/2 . We propose a first-order method to solve the bi-level
501   problem and provide theoretical convergence rates with state-of-the-art bounds.
