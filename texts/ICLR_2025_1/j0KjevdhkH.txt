009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            While graph neural networks have demonstrated potential across various appli-
012            cations, explaining their predictions on dynamic graphs remains largely under-
013            explored. This paper introduces a new research task: self-interpretable GNNs
014            for continuous-time dynamic graphs (CTDGs). We aim to predict future links
015            within dynamic graphs while simultaneously providing causal explanations for
016            these predictions. There are two key challenges: (1) capturing the underlying
017            structural and temporal information that remains consistent across both indepen-
018
               dent and identically distributed (IID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) data, and (2)
               efficiently generating high-quality link prediction results and explanations. To
019
               tackle these challenges, we propose a novel causal inference model, namely the
020
               Independent and Confounded Causal Model (ICCM). ICCM is then integrated
021            into a deep learning architecture that considers both effectiveness and efficiency.
022            Extensive experiments demonstrate that our proposed model significantly out-
023            performs existing methods across link prediction accuracy, explanation quality,
024            and robustness to OOD data. Our code and datasets are anonymously released at
025            https://github.com/2024SIG/SIG.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in representing graph-
031   structured data. However, the inherent opacity of GNNs poses challenges in comprehending and
032   trusting their predictions, particularly in high-stakes domains such as fraud detection in financial
033
      systems (Kumar et al., 2019) or disease progression prediction in healthcare (Li et al., 2021), where
      interpretability is important.
034
035   Recent advancements in explainable Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have aimed to unravel the
036   underlying rationale guiding GNN predictions (Yuan et al., 2022). These models are broadly clas-
037   sified into two categories: post-hoc interpretable models (Ying et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2022; Luo
038   et al., 2020; Lv & Chen, 2023) and self-interpretable models (Zhang et al., 2022a; Wu et al., 2022b;
039
      Liu et al., 2022a). Post-hoc interpretable models focus on elucidating the behaviors of the pri-
      mary predictive GNN model after its construction without altering its structural or training aspects.
040
      Conversely, self-interpretable models are inherently transparent in their decision-making processes,
041
      obviating the requirement for additional post-hoc techniques. Existing self-interpretable models
042   include decision trees (Kotsiantis, 2013), subgraph extraction based models (Liu et al., 2024; Yin
043   et al., 2024; Deng & Shen, 2024), attention-based mechanisms (Shu et al., 2019), rule-based models
044   (Setnes et al., 1998; Geng et al., 2023), and causal inference models (Wu et al., 2022b; Zhang et al.,
045   2022b). These models inherently provide interpretability, offering explanations of their predictions
046   without relying on auxiliary interpretive methods.
047
      This work tackles a novel research task: the development of self-interpretable models for continuous-
048   time dynamic graphs (CTDGs). Unlike static graphs or discrete-time dynamic graphs (DTDGs), CT-
049   DGs continuously evolve with time, enabling more precise modeling of dynamic processes. How-
050   ever, achieving interpretability in CTDGs presents two challenges. The first challenge is suscep-
051   tibility to shortcut features, which is a prevalent issue in most existing self-interpretable models.
052   Shortcut features are patterns that provide good performance on test data but fail to generalize to
053   out-of-distribution (OOD) data (Geirhos et al., 2020). Recent causal inference methods (Wu et al.,
      2022b; Fan et al., 2022; Sui et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a) have been developed to address this


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      challenge by performing interventions on graphs. However, they are based on static graphs or DT-
055   DGs, and cannot effectively handle the CTDGs. The second challenge is the efficiency of the
056   self-interpretable model. This challenge is amplified in CTDGs due to their constantly evolv-
057   ing structures. Different from static or discrete-time graphs, CTDGs undergo continuous node and
058   edge additions/deletions, resulting in a much larger number of possible topologies. This signifi-
059   cantly increases the computational burden of performing interventions in causal inference models
060   for CTDGs. Exhaustive sampling of topologies becomes computationally expensive, while limited
061   sampling might hinder model effectiveness.
062   Designing self-interpretable models for CTDGs is intricate, as the model must meet three critical
063   requirements: (1) Handle both independent and identically distributed (IID) and out-of-distribution
064   (OOD) data; (2) Capture invariant subgraphs in both structural and temporal aspects; (3) Perform
065   interventions efficiently. To fulfill these requirements, we propose a novel self-interpretable GNN
066   (SIG) method. SIG initiates with a theoretical analysis of the problem from a causal effect perspec-
067   tive and proposes a novel causal inference model, namely the Independent and Confounded Causal
068   Model (ICCM). ICCM incorporates two key components: the Independent Causal Model (ICM) and
069
      the Confounded Causal Model (CCM). The ICM is designed for IID data, where the causal subgraph
      is the unique exogenous variable influencing the predictive label. In contrast, the CCM is tailored for
070
      OOD data, where shortcut features act as confounding factors, creating backdoor paths that result
071
      in spurious correlations between causal subgraphs and prediction labels. SIG employs interventions
072   to disrupt these “backdoor paths” and mitigate the influence of confounding factors in CCM. To
073   achieve efficient intervention optimization, SIG utilizes the Normalized Weighted Geometric Mean
074   (NWGM) (Xu et al., 2015) instead of directly pairing causal subgraphs or their representations with
075   each element in the confounders set. During implementation, SIG leverages a deep learning cluster-
076   ing technique to approximate the actual confounders within CTDGs, thereby reducing computational
077   costs. SIG generates final predictions based on both temporal and structural representations from
078   the CTDG, along with these confounders.
079   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
080
081   • We investigate a new research task on CTDGs, which outputs not only the prediction label but
082     also a concise causal subgraph for the prediction. To the best of our knowledge, the proposed SIG
083     is the first self-interpretable GNN for CTDGs that is capable of handling both IID and OOD data.
084   • We present a thorough causal analysis of SIG, elucidating the causal effects and underlying mech-
085     anisms. This theoretical analysis serves as the foundation for our innovative model design and
086     optimization strategies.
087
      • We develop a novel deep learning framework that implements the theoretically established causal
088
        models, effectively and efficiently addressing challenges of self-interpretability on CTDGs.
089
090   • Extensive experiments on five real-world datasets demonstrate that SIG significantly outperforms
091     state-of-the-art methods in link prediction, graph explanation, and handling OOD datasets.

      Dynamic Graph Neural Networks. Dynamic graph neural networks encompass two primary
096   classifications: Discrete-Time Dynamic Graphs (DTDGs) (Ma et al., 2019; Qin et al., 2023) and
097   Continuous-Time Dynamic Graphs (CTDGs) (Li et al., 2017; De Winter et al., 2018; Li et al.,
098   2023). DTDGs comprise a sequence of static graph snapshots captured at regular time intervals
099   (Sankar et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2018; Mahdavi et al., 2018). CTDGs capture the evolution of graphs
100   by considering modifications on the graph that occur continuously rather than discretely at prede-
101   fined time steps (Xu et al., 2020; Trivedi et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2023). However, these GNNs focus
102   on modeling graph dynamics and fail to offer sufficient interpretability for the underlying processes.
103   Explainability of Graph Neural Networks. The majority of existing explainable GNNs fall into
104   the category of post-hoc interpretable GNNs. These frameworks are formulated as an optimization
105   task that maximizes the mutual information between a GNN’s prediction and distribution of pos-
106   sible subgraph structures (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2022; Rossi et al., 2022).
107   However, post-explanation methodologies may encounter inaccuracies or incompleteness in eluci-
      dating the genuine reasoning process of the underlying model and require iterative executions of


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the prediction model to delve into the intricate relationships between inputs and outputs, conse-
109   quently incurring a notable computational overhead. Few efforts are devoted to self-interpretable
110   GNNs. Prototype-based methods (Zhang et al., 2022a) learn prototype vectors as explanations.
111   These methods either fail to produce an explainable subgraph or depend on computationally expen-
112   sive subgraph exploration techniques. Neighborhood-based methods (Dai & Wang, 2021), while
113   effective in capturing local node and structure similarity, might include many extraneous details or
114   miss out on key structural patterns that are critical for interpretation. They often fail to distill the
115   graph into a form that clearly communicates the rationale behind a model’s prediction. Subgraph
116   extraction-based methods (Liu et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; Deng & Shen, 2024;
117
      Feng et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b) identify the most influential subgraph for decision-making. They
      may neglect the influence of confounding factors, potentially leading to inaccurate explanations.
118
119   Causal Inference on Graph Neural Networks. Causal inference seeks to unveil and comprehend
120   the causal variables responsible for observed phenomena. On real-world graphs, uncovering these
121   causal variables becomes an act of explanation, revealing the “why” behind intricate relationships.
122   Most existing methods focus on static graphs. These approaches either manipulate non-causal el-
123
      ements within a graph to create counterfactual graph data, as demonstrated in (Wu et al., 2022b),
      or utilize implicit interventions at the representation level, as shown in (Sui et al., 2022; Fan et al.,
124
      2022; Miao et al., 2022). The method most closely associated with this context is DIDA (Zhang
125
      et al., 2022b), an invariant rational discovery approach specifically designed for DTDGs. DIDA re-
126   quires the construction of an intervention set for each node and snapshot. When the graph is divided
127   into too many snapshots, applying DIDA becomes time-consuming. Conversely, dividing the graph
128   into too few snapshots leads to a loss of significant time-related information. The correlations and
129   differences between related studies and this work can be found in App. D.2

138   (V, E, T ) comprises a set of vertices V, a set of edges E, and a time domain T . This graph evolves
139   continuously over time t ∈ T , where at each time instance t, edges might undergo additions, re-
140   movals, or changes in their characteristics. Formally, the graph G can be denoted as a sequence of
141
      edges G = ⟨eij (tk )⟩. Each edge eij (tk ) signifies an interaction occurring between the source node
      vi and the target node vj at time tk . Additionally, we introduce xeij (tk ) to denote the feature vector

144   Definition 2 (Self-interpretable GNN for CTDG). Given a CTDG G and two distinct nodes, vi ∈ V

146   predict whether an edge will form between nodes vi and vj ; and secondly, to discern a causal

      4.2   C ONFOUNDED C AUSAL M ODEL (CCM)
177
178   To handle the confounding variables that may introduce bias in OOD data predictions, we introduce
179   CCM. As shown in Figure 1 (b), CCM considers confounders consisting of non-causal subgraph Gb
180   and unobserved variables U , where Gb is the residual part of the graph once the causal subgraphs
181   are excluded. These confounders contain information about possible shortcut features, which could
182   lead to spurious correlations between the causal subgraph and the prediction labels.
183
      Let G∗ denote a causal subgraph which is either Gs or Gt . To block the backdoor paths G∗ ← Gb →

186   Cs and Ct are constant subgraphs. Through the replacement of Gs with Cs and Gt with Ct ,
187   these interventions effectively block the backdoor paths, thereby eliminating the previously existing
188   spurious correlation between G∗ and Y . The foundational assumptions guiding these models are
189   summarized as follows:

251   5.1   OVERVIEW
252
253   ICCM relies on structural and temporal representations derived from constant causal subgraphs Cs
254
      and Ct . However, in real-world scenarios, these causal subgraphs are typically unobserved. To
      address this issue, SIG employs two causal subgraph extractors to extract structural and temporal
255
      subgraphs Ĉs and Ĉt from the input data. These extracted subgraphs are then used to approximate
256
      Cs and Ct . Figure 2 illustrates the overall structure of the SIG framework. First, the causal subgraph
257
      extraction aims to identify structural and temporal subgraphs Ĉs and Ĉt . These subgraphs are then
258
      encoded into hidden representations H S and H T . Subsequently, the confounder generation com-
259
      ponent produces a confounder dictionary D̂. Finally, both H S and H T are passed to the classifier
260
      foI to generate y I . Simultaneously, along with the produced confounder dictionary, H S and H T are
261
      also fed into classifiers foS and foT to output y S and y T , respectively. We will delve deeper into the
262   details of each module in the following sections.

      The confounder dictionary is expected to contain representations of all confounders, including non-
      causal subgraphs and unobserved factors. In this paper, we collect the representation of each link’s
305
      temporal and structural subgraph, which includes both the causal subgraph and the confounder for
306
      that link. It is important to note that the causal subgraph for one link may serve as the non-causal sub-
307   graph for another. Therefore, it is reasonable to use these representations to approximate potential
308   confounders. As the dynamic graph evolves, the number of distinct confounders becomes extremely
309   large, making it computationally expensive to account for all of them. To address this, we cluster
310   the representations and use each cluster centroid to approximate a group of similar confounders.
311
      Specifically, given the dynamic graph G = (V, E, T ), we adopt a dynamic GNN encoder (Cong
312
      et al., 2023) to extract the representations for each link in E based on its temporal and structural
313
      subgraph, resulting in the matrix X ∈ R|E|×l , where l denotes the embedding dimension. By
314   utilizing the deep learning clustering method VaDE (Jiang et al., 2016), we group X into k clus-
315   ters, i.e., {C1 , . . . , Ck } = VaDE(X ). The centroids within each cluster serve as indicators of the
316   central tendencies, effectively summarizing the overall features or characteristics among subgraph
317   information within the same cluster. Consequently, computing the cluster-wise average yields a rep-
318   resentation for each cluster, resulting in a confounder dictionary with the shape D̂ ∈ Rk×l , where
319   D̂[i] = Mean(Ci ). Finally, the expectation of confounders is computed by:

479
      sults and explanatory subgraphs for continuous-time dynamic graphs. By analyzing this problem
480   from a causal effect perspective, we introduce the ICCM, a novel causal inference model meticu-
481   lously designed to address both IID and OOD scenarios for CTDGs. Building upon the theoretical
482   foundations of ICCM, we propose a novel deep learning architecture, which translates theoretically

672   Causal inference models. The causal relationships between variables can be reflected by causal
673   models. Figure 3 shows three instances of causal inference models. Chain (Fig. 3 (a)) represents se-
674   quential relationships where one variable influences another, which subsequently influences a third,
675   and so on. Fig. 3 (b) illustrates the instance of confound. A variable U is a confounder of the effect
676   of X on Y if U meets 3 conditions: U is associated with X; U is associated with Y conditional on
677   X; U is not on a causal pathway from X to Y . The confounder U and backdoor path X ← U → Y
678   make Y and X spuriously correlated. For instance, low blood pressure is seemingly linked to a
679   higher risk of mortality. However, this association may be misleading, as it would be influenced by
680
      the confounding effect of heart disease. In this scenario, blood pressure (X) might appear as a direct
      cause of mortality (Y ). Yet, the confounder heart disease (U ) is associated with both low blood
681
      pressure and mortality.
682
683   Do-operation. The do(X = x) operator is a mathematical tool used to simulate interventions within
684   a model. As shown in Fig. 3 (c), it works by altering specific functions associated with X in the
685   model, replacing them with a constant X = x, while keeping the remaining model unchanged.
686
      In the case of blood pressure (X) and mortality (Y ), employing the do(X = ‘low’) or do(X =
687   ‘normal’) operator entails fixing the blood pressure variable to a low or normal state for individu-
688   als. This intentional manipulation facilitates the analysis of mortality, particularly concerning the
689   alteration in blood pressure, while holding other influential factors constant. Since it’s impractical
690   to collect data directly using the do-operation, adjustment formulas are proposed to compute the
691   probability P (Y = y | do(X = x)). The adjustment formula is shown as follows:
692
693

743
      It takes O(|P1 | · N ) time to extract the temporal causal graph and generate the temporal represen-
744   tation, where |P1 | is the number of learnable parameters in the causal graph extractor fext , and N
745   is a hyperparameter denoting the number of recent edges for representation generation. It takes
746   O(|Vn | · |x|) time to extract the structural causal graph and generate the structural representation,
747   where |Vn | is the number of nodes in the n-hop neighborhoods of two nodes for prediction and |x| is
748   the number of node features. The prediction takes O(|P2 |) time, where |P2 | is the number of param-
749   eters in the prediction model. Therefore, the total complexity of SIG is O(|P1 |·N +|Vn |·|x|+|P2 |).

      D.2   R ELATIONSHIPS WITH R ELATED M ODELS
782
783   This section delves into the connections between the proposed SIG framework and other relevant
784   models in the research field.
785
786   DIR (Wu et al., 2022b) is an invariant rational discovery method specifically designed for static
787   graphs. Similar to SIG, DIR mitigates spurious correlations between Gc and Y through the adoption
788
      of a do-operation. However, DIR’s approach to performing the do-operation differs from that of SIG.
      DIR modifies non-causal elements in the graph to generate counterfactual graph data, while SIG
789
      employs the Normalized Weighted Geometric Mean (NWGM) approximation to efficiently estimate
790
      the causal effect without directly modifying the graph structure.
791
792   DIR’s learning strategy is formulated as follows:
793

                       min Ec [R(Ŷ , Y )|do(Gs = s)] + λV ars R(Ŷ , Y )|do(Gs = s) ,                    (21)
794
795   where R represents the risk function, Ŷ denotes the predicted label, and λ controls the trade-off be-
796   tween minimizing interventional risks and their variance. DIR aims to minimize both interventional
797
      risks and their variance, ensuring that the model is not overly sensitive to specific interventions.
      However, these interventions can pose computational challenges, especially as graph sizes increase.
798
799   Without classifiers foI and foT , and using the same do-operation implementation method as DIR, the
800   proposed SIG methodology becomes equivalent to DIR. This demonstrates that SIG encompasses
801   DIR as a special case, while capturing the temporal information and offering improved computa-
802   tional efficiency through the NWGM approximation.
803
      DIDA (Zhang et al., 2022b) is an invariant rational discovery method specifically designed for DT-
804   DGs. Its learning strategy aligns with that of DIR, aiming to minimize interventional risks and their
805   variance. However, DIDA proposes an approximation to the intervention process by sampling and
806   replacing the variant pattern representation instead of directly modifying the original graph structure.
807   This approach aims to reduce the computational burden of interventions in DTDGs.
808
