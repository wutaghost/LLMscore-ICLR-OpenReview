011                                                      A BSTRACT
012
013                The human visual system (HVS) employs foveated sampling and eye movements to
014                achieve efficient perception, conserving both metabolic energy and computational
015                resources. Drawing inspiration from this efficiency, we introduce the Foveated
016
                   Dynamic Vision Transformer (FDT), a novel architecture that integrates these
                   mechanisms into a vision transformer framework. Unlike existing models, the FDT
017
                   uses a single-pass strategy, utilizing fixation and foveation modules to enhance
018
                   computational efficiency and accuracy. The fixation module identifies fixation
019                points to filter out irrelevant information, while the foveation module generates
020                foveated embeddings with multi-scale information. Our findings show that the FDT
021                achieves superior accuracy and computational efficiency, with a 34% reduction in
022                multiply-accumulate operations. Additionally, the FDT exhibits robustness against
023                various types of noise and adversarial attacks without specific training for these
024                challenges. These attributes make the FDT a significant step forward in creating
025                artificial neural networks that mirror the efficiency, robustness, and adaptability of
026                the HVS. 1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent studies indicate that deep neural networks and the human brain interpret the environment
032   differently, with the human visual system (HVS) dynamically filtering task-irrelevant information to
033
      focus on potential objects of interest, a process that leads to greater robustness against imperceptible
      perturbations that can mislead neural networks (Dodge & Karam, 2017; Azulay & Weiss, 2019;
034
      Szegedy et al., 2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2017). The retina contains photoreceptors, with the fovea—a
035
      high spatial resolution area—playing a key role in color perception and visual detail recognition
036   (Curcio et al., 1990). The highest photoreceptor density at the fovea decreases with eccentricity,
037   resulting in a variable-resolution image transmitted to the brain, a phenomenon known as foveation,
038   highlighting HVS’s multi-resolution perception. Studying HVS to enhance deep neural network
039   design is therefore a promising research avenue for developing intelligent agents.
040
      Approximately 107 to 108 bits of information enter the visual nerve every second in the HVS (Itti &
041
      Koch, 2001). To manage this data efficiently, the HVS uses saccadic eye movements to direct the
042   fovea to selected targets, creating a detailed scene map from varied resolutions, known as fixation
043   points, and saving computational resources (Itti & Koch, 2001; Bruce & Tsotsos, 2009). Inspired by
044   the HVS, several studies have incorporated foveation and fixation mechanisms into neural networks
045   (Mnih et al., 2014; Akbas & Eckstein, 2017; Thavamani et al., 2021). Existing methods sequentially
046   process the input image, first locating the fixation points and then processing the features around the
047   fixation points. However, these approaches are not optimal for two reasons. First, several inferences
048   are required for each fixation point. Second, they require a fusion mechanism to exploit the collective
049   information acquired from various fixation points.
050   We therefore propose a biologically inspired transformer architecture dubbed Foveated Dynamic
051   Transformers (FDT) comprised of foveation and fixation modules that dynamically select multiscale
052   tokens based on the input image. To simulate foveation in HVS, we process input tokens with

081   specific challenges. Moreover, FDT achieves a 34% reduction in computational demand, measured in
082   Multiply-Accumulate operations (MACs), demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness in processing

085   2     F OVEATED DYNAMIC T RANSFORMER (FDT)
086
087   We introduce the FDT, a bio-inspired transformer with two additional modules: Foveation and
088   Fixation. The Foveation module mimics the human visual system’s multi-resolution perception by
089   embedding input features at various resolutions. The Fixation module selectively processes tokens
090   through gaze sampling, producing binary decisions for each foveated query. The MHSA module then
091
      processes a subset of the foveated queries, keys, and values based on the Fixation module’s decisions,
      while the remaining tokens are passed to the next block. The FDT architecture is shown in Figure 2.

      A.1    R ELATED W ORK
651
652   Several approaches inspired by the functioning of the human visual system have been devised to
653   replicate foveation and eye movement within the domain of computer vision. Mnih et al. (2014)
654   introduced a method that employs a sequence of image movements to aggregate information before
655   classification. This method utilizes a hard-attention mechanism implemented through reinforcement
656   learning to predict fixation points. In a similar vein, Akbas & Eckstein (2017) developed a foveated
657   object detection system that harnesses varying resolutions to comprehensively analyze the entire
658
      image, aligning its fovea with regions of interest in the input data. This approach amalgamates data
      from multiple fixations and leverages peripheral information, similar to the way the human visual
659
      system employs contextual cues to guide gaze.
660
661   Furthermore, Deza & Konkle (2020) explored the impact of foveation on machine vision employing
662   two-stage models, encompassing an image transformation stage and a deep convolutional neural
663   network. Their investigation primarily focused on evaluating the effects of texture-based coding
664
      in the visual periphery on subsequent visual representations. Thavamani et al. (2021) proposed a
      foveated object detector that utilizes an image-magnification approach, preserving high resolution for
665
      points of interest while maintaining a compact canvas size.
666
667   Lukanov et al. (2021) developed an efficient model that incorporates space-variant sampling, mim-
668   icking the human retina, and the mechanisms to generate sequences of fixations. They proposed a
669   CNN-based method that uses Foveal Cartesian Geometry (FCG) sampling, as outlined by Martínez
670
      & Robles (2006), to compress visual signals. An attention mechanism is employed for “eye move-
      ments" to progressively gather detailed information from a scene. Activation within the feature
671
      maps of the final convolutional layer is harnessed to guide the generation of fixation sequences.
672
      Additionally, Wang et al. (2021) investigate the role of foveation and saccadic eye movements as
673   biologically inspired proxies for data augmentation in the context of self-supervised learning (SSL).
674   They suggest that foveation through cortical magnification and saccade-like sampling of images can
675   replace conventional SSL augmentations, offering insights into potential biological implementations
676   of self-supervision and challenging spatially uniform processing assumptions in both human and
677   machine vision.
678
      These approaches have demonstrated promising results in emulating the foveation and eye movement
679   processes of the human visual system, with potential applications across a variety of computer vision
680   tasks. However, it is imperative to emphasize the critical necessity for further research, not only
681   aimed at optimizing and refining the functionality of the human visual system, particularly in the
682   context of vision transformers, but also unlocking its transformative potential for artificial intelligence,
683   ultimately advancing the frontiers of machine perception and understanding. To this end, we aim to
684   design a system that effectively mimics the human visual system’s process of selectively focusing on
685   certain regions of an image and using surrounding context to guide the gaze.
