009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Source-Free Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (SFUDA) has gained popularity
012            for its ability to adapt pretrained models to target domains without accessing
013            source domains, ensuring source data privacy. While SFUDA is well-developed in
014            visual tasks, its application to Time-Series SFUDA (TS-SFUDA) remains limited
015            due to the challenge of transferring crucial temporal dependencies across domains.
016            Although a few researchers attempt to address this challenge, they rely on specific
017            source pretraining designs, which are impractical as source data owners cannot be
018
               expected to follow particular pretraining protocols. To solve this, we propose
               Temporal Source Recovery (TemSR), a framework that recovers and transfers
019
               temporal dependencies for effective TS-SFUDA without requiring source-specific
020
               designs. TemSR features a recovery process that employs masking, recovery, and
021            optimization to generate a source-like distribution with recovered source tempo-
022            ral dependencies. To ensure effective recovery, we further design segment-based
023            regularization to restore local dependencies and anchor-based recovery diversity
024            maximization to enhance the diversity of the source-like distribution. With the
025            source-like distribution, the temporal dependencies can be effectively transferred
026            across domains using traditional UDA techniques. Extensive experiments across
027            multiple TS tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of TemSR, even surpassing exist-
028            ing TS-SFUDA method that requires source domain designs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the rapid growth of the Internet of Things, Time-Series (TS) data has been increasingly critical
      in various domains, such as healthcare (Klepl et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Ott et al., 2022) and
034
      industrial maintenance (Wang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2020). While deep learning models yield
035
      promising results in these areas, they heavily depend on extensive labeled data, which is hard to
036   obtain due to high labeling costs. To address this, Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) methods
037   (Wilson & Cook, 2020; Wang et al., 2024a), which transfer knowledge from a labeled source domain
038   to an unlabeled target domain, have gained attention to reduce label reliance in TS tasks.
039
      Although UDA techniques have proven effective, they typically require access to both source and
040
      target domains to bridge domain gaps. However, in many real-world applications, data privacy con-
041
      cerns prevent access to source domain data (Li et al., 2024), leaving only a pretrained model avail-
042   able for adaptation. This challenge significantly limits the applicability of existing UDA methods, as
043   they are not designed for such restricted settings. To address this issue, researchers have recently fo-
044   cused on a more practical scenario, Source-Free Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (SFUDA), which
045   adapts the pretrained model to the target domain without relying on source data, demonstrating
046   promising results. Despite these advancements, most existing techniques were developed for visual
047   tasks and overlook the temporal dependencies inherent in TS data (Ragab et al., 2023b), limiting
048   their generalizability to Time-Series Source-Free Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (TS-SFUDA).
049   In TS data, temporal dependencies refer to the temporal correlations among time points within a
050   sequence. For effective adaptation, transferring these dependencies from the source to the target
051   domain is essential to learn effective domain-invariant features for TS data (Ragab et al., 2023a;
052   Purushotham et al., 2017). However, without access to source data, directly transferring these de-
053   pendencies becomes challenging. To address this, recent research (Ragab et al., 2023b) has explored
      methods to preserve temporal dependencies during source pretraining and restore them during target

054
      adaptation. Although effective, these approaches require specific pretraining designs in the source
055   domain, which are impractical for real-world applications. Thus, a robust TS-SFUDA approach
056   must meet two key criteria: 1. Even without source data, the temporal dependencies can still be
057   transferred across domains; 2. Additional designs during source pretraining should be avoided.
058
      Following the criteria, we introduce Temporal Source Recovery (TemSR), a novel framework to
059
      recover and transfer source temporal dependencies for improved TS-SFUDA. TemSR contains two
060
      steps: recovery and enhancement, jointly restoring source temporal dependencies to facilitate trans-
061   fer using traditional UDA techniques. In the recovery step, we apply masking, recovery, and
062   optimization to generate a source-like distribution with recovered source temporal dependencies.
063   Masked target TS samples are recovered by a recovery model, then optimized to follow a source-
064   like distribution by minimizing their entropy computed using a fixed pretrained source model. With
065   the minimized entropy on source data, the source model can produce deterministic outputs for dis-
066   tributions with source characteristics. By minimizing the entropy of recovered samples, this output
067   constraint can inversely regularize these samples, forcing them to align with the source-like distribu-
068   tion. Meanwhile, this process forces the recovery model to recover the source temporal dependencies
069
      required to effectively fill in the masked parts using unmasked time points. However, focusing only
      on sample-level recovery for long-term patterns may overlook local temporal dependencies, which
070
      capture critical short-term trends and are essential for recovering source temporal dependencies.
071
      To address this, we improve the optimization as segment-based regularization, enforcing minimal
072   entropy across segments in recovered samples to ensure effective recovery of local dependencies.
073
074   A crucial aspect of the recovery process is the masking, which introduces the diversity necessary
075
      to effectively recover a source-like distribution. However, this presents challenges: a high masking
      ratio may lead the recovery model to collapse into constant values for entropy minimization, like ze-
076
      ros, while a low masking ratio may result in insufficient diversity, hindering effective recovery of the
077
      source-like distribution. To enhance the recovery, we introduce an anchor-based recovery diversity
078   maximization module, where recovery diversity maximization enhances diversity in recovered sam-
079   ples and anchors ensure this diversity aligns with the source distribution. By effectively enhancing
080   diversity, this module facilitates the recovery of an optimal source-like distribution.
081
      Our contributions are threefold. 1. We design a recovery process involving masking, recovery, and
082
      optimization to generate a source-like distribution with recovered source temporal dependencies,
083
      which is further refined by segment-based regularization to improve temporal dependency recovery.
084   2. We design an enhancement module to improve diversity in the source-like distribution through
085   anchor-based recovery diversity maximization, with anchors ensuring this diversity aligns with the
086   source distribution. By effectively enhancing diversity, this module facilitates the recovery of an op-
087   timal source-like distribution. 3. Extensive experiments across various TS tasks indicate the effec-
088   tiveness of TemSR, which even surpasses existing TS-SFUDA method that requires source pretrain-
089   ing designs. Additional analysis on distribution discrepancy changes between source, source-like,
090   and target domains further verify TemSR’s ability to recover an effective source-like domain and
091   thus reduce gaps between the source and target domains even without access to the source data.

135   Time-Series Unsupervised Domain Adaptation To reduce label reliance in TS tasks, UDA meth-
136   ods have been widely applied. The main challenge in TS UDA is transferring temporal dependencies
137   across domains to learn domain-invariant features (Ragab et al., 2023a), typically achieved through
138
      metric- and adversarial-based methods. Metric-based methods extract temporal features and align
      them using statistical measures such as Deep CORAL (Liu & Xue, 2021; He et al., 2023; Cai et al.,
139
      2021), while adversarial-based methods leverage discriminators to learn domain-invariant temporal
140
      features (Wilson et al., 2020; 2023; Purushotham et al., 2017). To enhance robustness, contrastive
141   learning has been explored to learn discriminative features (Eldele et al., 2023; Ozyurt et al., 2022),
142   and spatial dependencies have also been investigated (Wang et al., 2023; 2024a). Besides TS-related
143   works, video UDA has been explored Sahoo et al. (2021); Wei et al. (2023), which shares similar
144   sequential properties with TS data. However, video UDA methods cannot effectively leverage the
145   unique temporal properties of TS data, limiting their applicability in this area Ozyurt et al. (2022).
146
      Despite their potential, TS UDA methods rely on access to source data, which may not always
147   be feasible due to privacy concerns. This highlights the need for TS-SFUDA, where adaptation is
148   performed without source data. While a few researchers (Ragab et al., 2023b) have explored this,
149   demonstrating the effectiveness of transferring temporal dependencies in TS-SFUDA, they required
150   additional designs in source pretraining to preserve the dependencies. This is impractical, as source
151   data holders cannot be expected to follow specific pretraining steps. To overcome this, we propose
152   TemSR, which effectively transfers temporal dependencies across domains without extra operations
153   during source pretraining, ensuring both practicality and strong performance for TS-SFUDA.

      3.2   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
168
169   Fig. 1 presents the overall TemSR, where an encoder is pretrained on the source domain and then
170   adapted to the target domain without source data, using both the source-like and target branches. In

172   we derive entropy for the recovered samples through segment-based regularization, computing the

      ated distribution, we introduce an anchor-based recovery diversity maximization loss for better re-

      transferring temporal dependencies across domains for TS-SFUDA. Further details are provided in

      Optimization To align the recovered samples with the source domain, we propose leveraging the
217   pretrained source model with entropy minimization as guidance. Entropy minimization is widely
218   used in model adaptation, as models with minimized entropy can produce deterministic outputs,
219   and this ideal output constraint can be inversely employed to guide adaptation (Li et al., 2024; Liang
220   et al., 2020). Inspired by this, we introduce entropy minimization to optimize the recovered samples.
221   With the minimized entropy on source data, the source model can produce deterministic outputs for
222   distributions with source characteristics. By minimizing the entropy computed by the fixed source
223   model for recovered samples, this constraint can inversely regularize the samples, forcing them to
224   align with the source distribution. Here, the recovery model is forced to capture source temporal
225
      dependencies, as only by understanding these dependencies can the model effectively reconstruct
      masked parts, minimize entropy, and ensure recovered samples align with the source distribution.
226
227   While the recovery process can generate source-like distributions with recovered temporal depen-
228   dencies, it primarily focuses on sample-level recovery for long-term patterns, overlooking local tem-
229   poral dependencies. These local dependencies offer short-term context, enabling the model to infer
230   with local information that may not be apparent in broader trends. This highlights the importance
231
      of recovering local dependencies to restore natural temporal patterns and enhance overall temporal
      recovery. Thus, we improve the optimization as segment-based regularization, further optimizing
232
      segments that capture local dependencies to have minimized entropy, aligning them with source dis-
233
      tributions. Three types of segments are extracted from the recovered sample XiSl with an extraction
234   proportion ps , capturing local information from different regions (see examples in Appendix A.7):
235
236   1. Early Segment XiSl,E : Extracts the first ps proportion of the sequence, capturing local information
237   at the early stage of the recovered sample XiSl .
238
      2. Late Segment XiSl,L : Extracts the last ps proportion capturing local information at the later stage.
239
240   3. Segment with Recovered Parts XiSl,R : Extracts all recovered portions to ensure they have mini-
241   mized entropy and align with the source-like distribution.

      3.4   E NHANCEMENT
263
264   To optimize the initial distribution as a source-like distribution, masking introduces the essential di-
265   versity required for effective recovery. However, masking presents challenges. A large masking ratio
266   can introduce sufficient diversity, increasing the chances of finding an optimal solution. However,
267   it risks model collapse, where the recovery model shortcuts the learning process by filling masked
268   parts with constant values, minimizing entropy without capturing the true underlying structure, as
269   proof in Appendix A.1. On the other hand, using a small masking ratio avoids this collapse but fails
      to provide enough diversity for the model to learn an optimal source-like distribution.

      Anchor-based Recovery Diversity Maximization To effectively enhance diversity for optimal
271   recovery, we introduce the anchor-based recovery diversity maximization module. This module
272   encourages recovery diversity by maximizing the distance between recovered samples and their
273   original samples. By pushing the recovered samples to diverge from their original forms, the samples
274   are forced to enhance diversity (see proof in Appendix A.2), allowing to explore a broader range of
275   features that are crucial for capturing the complexity of the source distribution. However, without
276   proper constraints, this recovery diversity maximization may cause the recovered samples to deviate
277   in unintended directions, as shown in Fig. 3 (a), leading to distributions that are not aligned with the
278   source domain and hurting performance. To prevent this, we further introduce anchors to guide the
279
      process and ensure that the diversity remain consistent with the source distribution. Anchors act as
      reference points as shown in Fig. 3 (b), balancing diversity with fidelity to the source domain.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   To transfer temporal dependencies across domains for effective TS-SFUDA without relying on spe-
530   cific source pretraining designs, we propose the Temporal Source Recovery (TemSR) framework.
531
      TemSR aims to recover and transfer source temporal dependencies by generating a source-like time-
      series distribution. The framework features a recovery process that employs masking, recovery, and
532
      optimization to create the source-like distribution with recovered temporal dependencies. For effec-
533
      tive recovery, we further improve the optimization as segment-based regularization to restore local
534   temporal dependencies and design an anchor-based recovery diversity maximization loss to enhance
535   diversity in the source-like distribution. The recovered source-like distribution is then adapted to
536   the target domain using traditional UDA techniques. Additional analysis of distribution discrepancy
537   changes between source, source-like, and target domains confirms TemSR’s ability to recover and
538   align the source-like domain, ultimately reducing gaps between the source and target domains. Ex-
539   tensive experiments further demonstrate the effectiveness of TemSR, achieving SOTA performance
      and even surpassing the existing TS-SFUDA method that relies on source-specific designs.

      Effect of Masking Ratio The masking ratio, which introduces diversity to the initial distribution
945   for optimization as a source-like distribution, has been tested with values of [1/8, 2/8, 3/8, 4/8, 5/8,
946   6/8] following Ragab et al. (2023b), with larger values indicating more information removed in the
947   sample. Fig. 8 shows the impact of various masking ratios, suggesting that smaller masking ratios
948   lead to better performance. As discussed in Sec 3.4, while higher masking ratios introduce more
949   diversity to the source-like distribution, they can cause the model to collapse by exploiting shortcuts,
950   e.g., recovering the masked samples as a constant value. Although smaller masking ratios may limit
951   diversity, our proposed recovery diversity maximization loss compensates for this by balancing the
952   need for diversity with fidelity to the source domain. Thus, smaller masking ratios, e.g., 1/8 or 2/8,
953
      are recommended for achieving optimal results. (Sec 3.3 Masking and Recovery.)
