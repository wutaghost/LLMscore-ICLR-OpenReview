011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Automatically generating symbolic music scores tailored to specific user needs
014            offers significant benefits for musicians and enthusiasts alike. Pretrained symbolic
015            music autoregressive models have demonstrated promising results, thanks to large
016
               datasets and advanced transformer architectures. However, in practice, the control
               provided by such models is often limited, particularly when fine-grained controls
017
               are needed at the level of individual bars. While fine-tuning the model with
018
               newly introduced control tokens may seem like a straightforward solution, our
019            research reveals challenges in this approach, as the model frequently struggles
020            to respond effectively to these precise bar-level control signals. To overcome
021            this issue, we propose two novel strategies. First, we introduce a pre-training
022            task that explicitly links control signals with their corresponding musical tokens,
023            enabling a more effective initialization for fine-tuning. Second, we develop a
024            unique counterfactual loss function that enhances alignment between the generated
025            music and the specified control prompts. These combined methods substantially
026            improve bar-level control, yielding a 13.06% improvement over the fine-tuning
027
               baseline. Importantly, subjective evaluations confirm that this increased control
               does not compromise the musical quality produced by the original pretrained
028
               model.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Symbolic music generation, which focuses on the automatic creation of music scores, has gained
034   significant attention in recent years due to its intuitive readability and excellent editability (Zhang
035   et al., 2020; Wu & Sun, 2022). Autoregressive models using transformer architectures, such as Music
036   Transformer (Anna et al., 2018), Museformer (Yu et al., 2022), and MuseCoco (Lu et al., 2023),
037   have demonstrated promising results due to its impressive generation quality and scalability with
038   large-scale datasets. Conditional music generation models, which allow for music to be generated
039   based on user-defined attributes like style or rhythm, can also be integrated into autoregressive
040   transformer frameworks (Wu & Sun, 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Lu et al., 2023). However, due to the
041   many ways music generation can be controlled, it is challenging to account for all potential conditions
042
      during the training phase for large models. This raises the question of how a model can be quickly
      updated with new control conditions after it has already been trained on a large dataset.
043
044   In particular, granular control over music generation is highly desirable but remains lacking in
045   existing pretrained autoregressive music models, such as (Lu et al., 2023). These models have
046   predominantly focused on generating music based on broad, high-level descriptions, offering limited
047   control over elements like tempo and style within individual sections of a composition. This lack
048
      of fine-grained control at the bar level 1 restricts users from making detailed adjustments to specific
      musical elements. Introducing bar-level control would provide users with greater creative flexibility
049
      and enhance applications in automatic composition. For example, attributes from a particular bar
050
      could be extracted and applied to generate new pieces, enabling style imitation. This level of control
051   would also improve the alignment between lyrics and melody, ensuring the music conveys the desired
052

      emotional cues. Moreover, identifying and utilizing attributes from favored music pieces to generate
055   new compositions through bar-level manipulation would enable more customized and personalized
056   music creation.
057
058
      When a pretrained music generation model is available, a straightforward approach to incorporating
      bar-level control is to fine-tune the model with newly introduced control signals. Ideally, this fine-
059
      tuning should be performed using a relatively small dataset compared to the volume of the original
060
      training data for the foundation model, thereby avoiding the need for complete retraining. Specifically,
061   we can utilize bar-level music attributes extracted from the training set’s music scores as prefix control
062   prompts to train an autoregressive model, with the objective of optimizing the likelihood of the
063   training samples. However, we’ve found that models trained in this manner often fail to adhere to the
064   guidance of the bar-level attributes. Our analysis suggests that the model struggles with interpreting
065   the meanings of these new control prompts, leading to music that does not accurately align with these
066   prompts. Furthermore, when the training data is limited, the model is prone to overfitting, focusing
067   more on minimizing loss rather than effectively using the control prompt to steer music generation.
068   To address this limitation, we propose two strategies to improve bar-level controllability. The first
069   strategy involves pretraining the control prompt and fine-tuning the model on an auxiliary task
070   designed to promote accurate alignment between the control prompts and the generated music tokens.
071   The second strategy introduces a counterfactual loss that penalizes the model for neglecting the
072   bar-level guidance. By implementing these two techniques together, we significantly enhance the
073   accuracy of bar-level control without compromising the quality of the music produced.
074   In sum, the key contributions of this work are outlined as follows:
075
076   • We conducted the first study in achieving fine-grained control of symbolic music generation based
077     on the existing foundation model.
078   • We propose two innovative strategies, auxiliary task pre-training and counterfactual loss, to improve
079     bar-level control in the foundation model.

084   2.1   T EXT-D RIVEN M USIC G ENERATION
085
      Text-driven music generation, aimed at creating high-quality music from textual descriptions, has
086
      attracted considerable attention from researchers due to its user-friendly editing capabilities. However,
087   the scarcity of paired text-music data presents a major challenge. To address this, some studies
088   have employed diffusion-based methods (Zhu et al., 2023; Schneider et al., 2023) with self-collected
089   text-audio datasets to facilitate text-audio music generation. MuLan (Huang et al., 2022) tackles
090   the issue of data scarcity. They use techniques similar to those in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to
091   contrastively embed two modalities: music pieces and their textual annotations. Building on this,
092   MusicLM (Agostinelli et al., 2023) generates audio from MuLan’s embeddings (Huang et al., 2022),
093   enabling text-to-music conversion without the need for paired data. However, MusicLM’s process
094   of sampling to acquire fine-grained acoustic tokens is computationally intensive. Other efforts, like
095
      MeLoDy (Lam et al., 2024), seek to simplify music generation by efficiently translating conditioning
      tokens into sound waves. Furthermore, MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024) introduces a single-stage
096
      transformer LM framework that models multiple streams of acoustic tokens in parallel. Despite
097
      significant advancements in text-driven music generation, the methods are still relatively crude,
098   limiting users’ ability to edit musical elements within the generated audio. The controllability and
099   editability of the outputs remain constrained.
100
101
      2.2   S YMBOLIC M USIC G ENERATION
102
103   Compared to text-driven music generation, symbolic music provides easier editing capabilities, al-
104   lowing users to manipulate specific musical elements more effectively. The development of solutions
105   for this task has evolved significantly, from grammar rules-based methods (Collins, 2009; Gar-
106   cía Salas et al., 2011; Fernández & Vico, 2013) to probabilistic models and evolutionary computation
107   (Yanchenko & Mukherjee, 2017; Liu & Ting, 2016), and more recently to neural networks and deep
      learning (Ycart et al., 2017; Briot et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2018). The advent of transformer-based


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      models, known for their successes in text generation (Li et al., 2022; Ren & Liu, 2023), has also
109   influenced music generation. The Music Transformer (Anna et al., 2018) utilizes transformers with
110   relative attention, proving highly effective for generating symbolic music. Museformer (Yu et al.,
111   2022) addresses challenges in long-sequence and music structure modelling by capturing music
112   structure-related correlations, thus enhancing music generation efficiency. The recently introduced
113   MuseCoco (Lu et al., 2023) offers precise control over symbolic music generation through specific
114   attributes, using these attributes as a bridge to transition from text-to-music to attribute-to-music
115   generation. MuseCoco enables the adjustment of various musical attributes, offering a level of control
116   akin to the compositional process. However, this control is limited to entire compositions, diverging
117
      from a composer’s typical approach, which often involves more granular control, such as bar-level
      manipulation. Recent studies have explored the application of diffusion models to symbolic music
118
      generation, inspired by their success in the image and audio domains. Lv et al. (2023) introduced
119
      a framework called GETMusic, which includes a novel music representation, “GETScore,” and a
120   diffusion model, “GETDiff.” The diffusion model is trained to predict masked target tokens condi-
121   tioned on the source tracks. Similarly, Min et al. (2023) proposed a diffusion model, Polyffusion,
122   for generating polyphonic music scores by treating music as an image-like piano roll representation.
123   Their method enables controllable music generation, allowing users to pre-define parts of the music
124   and apply external controls, such as chords or other musical features. Wang et al. (2024) explored
125   the training of a cascaded diffusion model to capture hierarchical language structures, enabling
126   whole-song hierarchical generation. While diffusion models offer a promising direction for symbolic
127   music generation, the currently available pretrained models remain autoregressive. This paper will
128
      thus focus on updating autoregressive models with newly introduced control signals.

      Fine-grained control is particularly valuable to both musicians and amateurs as it enables users to
163   define specific properties for smaller segments of music. For instance, users can specify the chords2
164   in each bar and provide a chord progression, and then explore various musical outputs based on that

168   regenerate another piece, facilitating style mimicry. Additionally, this level of control can help
169   achieve a closer alignment between the emotional content of lyrics and the corresponding melody,
170   such as matching an intensification of emotion in the lyrics with an ascending melody line.

172   4     M ETHOD
173
174   To attain precise control, we present ControlMuse. This model overcomes the constraints of existing
175   music score generation models that largely produce music based on broad and vague descriptions.
176   Our method consists of three components: (1) we refine the control prompt in MuseCoco (Lu
177   et al., 2023) to facilitate bar-level control instead of global control. (2) we incorporate an auxiliary
178   task to pre-condition the model and the newly implemented control prompts. (3) we introduce a
179   counterfactual loss to enhance the adherence of the generated music to the bar-level control prompts.
180
181   4.1   C ONTROL P ROMPT AUGMENTATION
182
      MuseCoco (Lu et al., 2023) only incorporates global musical attributes that define the overall
183
      character of the music, which cannot achieve fine-grained bar-level control of music generation. To
184
      facilitate the latter, we first introduce a scheme to specify the bar-level music attribute. Specifically,
185   ControlMuse processes a sequence of music attribute tokens X = Xg , X1 , X2 , ...Xb as input, where
186   Xg = xg,1 , xg,2 , ...xg,|Xg | comprises |Xg | tokens representing global attributes, and b denotes the
187   number of music bars. Each ith bar Xi = xi,1 , xi,2 , ..., xi,|Xi | contains |Xi | tokens representing the
188   attributes at the bar level. For instance, token 24 in the chord attribute may indicate that the current
189   bar’s chord is “Eb: ”, while token 0 in the rhythm intensity attribute could denote a “serene” rhythm
190   (see Table 6 in Appendix). Those attributes can be extracted from the training music scores, i.e., from
191   y1 , y2 , ..., yn . For example, there are existing algorithms 3 to extract the chord implies in a given bar
192   based on the distribution of the note pitches in the bar, whereas rhythm intensity is determined by
193   the note density within the bar. Position embeddings are added to the bar-level tokens within this
194
      sequence X1 , X2 , ...Xb , distinguishing each bar’s tokens shown in Figure 2. Tokens corresponding
      to the same bar are assigned identical position embeddings. Conversely, position embeddings are not
195
      used for the global tokens Xg . As a result, the input sequence in our method is encoded as follows:
196
197                                   Xg , X1 , X2 , ..., Xb , [SEP ], y1 , y2 , ..., yn .                        (2)

      4.2         C ONTROL P ROMPTS P RE -A DAPTATION VIA AN AUXILIARY TASK (PA)
235
236   The first strategy is to utilize an auxiliary task to pre-adapt the bar-level control mechanism of the
237   MuseCoco model. In this context, "pre-adapt" refers to modifying the embeddings of the control
238   prompts and the LoRA parameters. The auxiliary task is framed to meet two key criteria: firstly, it
239   must be closely related to the task of bar-level controlled generation, ensuring that the parameters
240   refined during the auxiliary task can be effectively transferred to the main generation task. Secondly,
241   the model can only accomplish the auxiliary task by genuinely utilizing the interactions between the
242   control prompts and the corresponding music tokens. Specifically, to achieve bar-level controlled
243   generation, it is essential for the model to comprehend which music tokens and specific attributes of
244
      those tokens are governed by a bar-level prompt. Accordingly, we propose a recognition task: the
      model is presented with a sequence of bar-level control prompts alongside a sequence of music tokens,
245
      and it must determine whether the music sequence conforms to the guidelines set by the bar-level
246
      control prompts. For this purpose, we develop new linear projection heads that are trained on the
247   output embeddings of the bar-level control prompts. These projections perform a binary classification
248   task to assess whether the specified bar-level control, as indicated by the control prompt, has been
249   adhered to in the music sequence. This setup is depicted in Figure 3. Successfully completing this
250   task hinges on the accurate correlation between the control prompts and the music tokens, a skill that
251   is directly transferable to controlled music generation.
252
      In our implementation, we randomly select the number of t bars, denoted as M and |M| = t, and
253   modify the corresponding bar-level tokens in the input to reflect that the attributes of these bars do
254   not match the expected values. Then we create a sequence of ground-truth prediction labels {si },
255   with si = 1 for unmodified bar and si = 0 for modified bars (“1” indicates “match” and “0” indicates
256   “unmatch”). The training loss for this step is expressed as follows:

290   4.4     I NFERENCE
291
292   During the inference stage, music sequences are generated on a bar-by-bar basis. Within each
293   bar, up to K sampling attempts are allowed. If a sample accurately reflects the intended bar-level
294   attributes, the generation continues from the subsequent bar. Conversely, if K samples all fail to
295   exhibit the correct attributes, the token with the highest probability is chosen to continue the sequence
296
      prediction. When K = 1, this process simplifies to a typical auto-regressive model sampling
      procedure. Generally, a larger K enhances controllability at the expense of reduced sampling
297
      efficiency. It is noteworthy that in many cases, the model may produce valid music on the first attempt,
298
      implying that the actual number of sampling operations may increase sublinearly with K.

302
303   In this section, we assess our method through two case studies focused on bar-level chord control
304   and rhythm intensity control. In other words, the bar-level attribute corresponds to the chord and
305
      rhythm intensity for each bar, respectively. Bar-level chord control is useful as in music composition,
      particularly in pop music, it is customary to first establish a chord progression pattern before
306
      composing the music. Similarly, bar-level rhythm intensity control is beneficial for piano learners,
307
      particularly beginners, as it allows for the adjustment of note density to modify the difficulty of
308   the generated music. It’s important to note that the objective of our experiment is to evaluate the
309   effectiveness of the proposed methods in enhancing control based on a pre-trained music model.
310   We are not aiming to optimize performance specifically for chord-controlled and rhythm-intensity-
311   controlled generation.

315   5.1.1    DATASETS
316
      In our study, we use the POP909 dataset (Wang et al., 2020) to train and evaluate the proposed method.
317
      This dataset comprises multiple renditions of the piano arrangements for 909 popular songs, totalling
318   approximately 60 hours of music. These arrangements are meticulously crafted by professional
319   musicians and are provided in MIDI format. On average, each song consists of 270 bars. It is
320   important to note that, in comparison to the training data used for MuseCoco, POP909 represents
321   only a small subset. We chose this dataset for two reasons: (1) Pop music typically features distinct
322   chord progression patterns, making it ideal for this study. (2) Pop piano music is more accessible for
323   general audiences to evaluate its quality. Following (Lu et al., 2023), we randomly selected three
      16-bar clips from each MIDI file. From these clips, global attributes are extracted from the entire clip,

324
      while bar-level attributes—chords and rhythm intensity are extracted from each individual bar within
325   the 16-bar length clip. The specific predefined musical attribute values, including global attributes
326   sourced from the MuseCoco project (Lu et al., 2023) and chord and rhythm intensity attributes derived
327   from MIDI files, are displayed in Appendix A.1. Details regarding the distribution of chords within
328   this dataset are provided in Appendix A.3. To convert the MIDI files into token sequences, we employ
329   a REMI-like representation method (Huang & Yang, 2020). For training, validation, and testing
330   purposes, we partition the songs into three sets, with a split ratio of 8:1:1 for training, validation, and
331   testing, respectively.
332
333   5.1.2      I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
334   We employ the Linear Transformer architecture as our backbone model (Katharopoulos et al., 2020),
335   configured with a causal attention mechanism spanning 24 layers and utilizing 24 attention heads.
336   The hidden size is set to 2048, while the feedforward network (FFN) hidden size is 8192. During
337   the training phase, we began by initializing the MuseCoco-base weights (Lu et al., 2023) with
338   fp16 precision and subsequently applied a fine-tuning approach. Within the attention layers, LoRA
339   Adapters (Hu et al., 2021) was incorporated, with a rank size of r = 8. The maximum sequence
340   length was set to 5120. We use validation performance to set the margin η to 0.05. To execute the
341   fine-tuning process, we utilized 4 40GB-A100 GPUs, conducting 50 epochs for the first strategy (PA),
342
      and 40 epochs for the second strategy (BFT and CF). We utilize a batch size of 4 and employ the
      Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with a learning rate of 2 × 10−4 , incorporating a warm-up step
343
      of 16,000 and an invert-square-root decay schedule. For inference, we consider the top 15 highest
344
      probabilities as potential prediction hypotheses and perform sampling K = 15 times.

347
348   In this investigation, we conduct a comparative analysis between our proposed method and MuseC-
349   oco (Lu et al., 2023) for symbolic music generation. Since MuseCoco is only fed with global musical
350   attributes, we examine MuseCoco with Bar-level Fine-Tuning, represented as BFT which builds
351   upon the MuseCoco model by incorporating bar-level training techniques. This approach involves
352
      aligning the music attributes with each bar chord or rhythm intensity in the input.
353   We also conduct a comparative analysis between our proposed method and other Transformer-based
354   conditional models, specifically FIGARO (von Rütte et al., 2023), as well as the diffusion-based
355   model Polyffusion (Min et al., 2023). FIGARO employs a description-to-sequence learning approach,

357   information. Polyffusion, on the other hand, treats music as image-like piano roll representations and
358
      utilizes external control signals, such as chords, to achieve controllable music generation.

525   7     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we introduce ControlMuse, a method that allows for finer detail control at the level
528   of individual bars, significantly advancing the field of automated music score composition and
529   alignment. This innovative approach offers substantial value and potential for both musicians and
530   amateurs, enhancing creative efficiency and providing greater control over the composition process.
531   Our solution contains two innovative strategies that enhance bar-level control without compromising
532   the quality of the music produced. These designs enable the model to accurately adapt to the adjusted
533   bar-level attributes as new control prompts, thereby achieving impressive bar-level controllability.
534   Our research demonstrates the feasibility of bar-level editing in AI technologies. Successful results
535   in chord control and generation, as shown in Appendix A.2, suggest that more bar-level attributes,
536   such as melody trends, can be explored in the future. We hope that further inspiration for bar-level
537   attributes will enhance the ability to edit and control bar generation, thereby improving music creation.
538   It is possible to adopt ControlMuse for more bar-level attribute edits like melody trends. We hope
539   ControlMuse will be even more useful with additional bar-level attributes, utilizing the proposed two
      training strategies.
