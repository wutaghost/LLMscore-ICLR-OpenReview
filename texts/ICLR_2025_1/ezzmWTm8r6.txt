009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the role of pseudo-labels in the test-time adaptation (TTA) prob-
012            lem. When working with unlabeled samples in TTA, pseudo-labels have become
013            a natural approach to updating the target model. However, pseudo-label learning
014            also presents some challenges: it suffers from a memorization effect (the model
015            learns from clean labels first, then memorizes the noisy ones) and confirmation
016
               bias (errors from noisy labels increase over time and disrupt model performance
               when they become significant). Our work first identifies two underlying mech-
017
               anisms leading to these obstacles. On the one hand, existing methods follow a
018
               "slow" adaptation to the target domain, allowing sufficient time for the model to
019            memorize noisy labels (memorization effect) and accumulate errors (confirma-
020            tion bias). Furthermore, training with noisy labels blurs the decision boundary
021            with nearby classes. To address the first issue, we propose a novel loss function,
022            namely sparse cross logit (sparse-CL), that operates in the logit space and allows
023            the model to take larger learning steps in a stable training manner. This helps
024            the target model reach a better solution faster under the same number of updating
025            steps. To address the second issue, we introduce a regularization that penalizes
026            negative pseudo-labels while encouraging positive ones, which can increase the
027
               boundary between nearby classes. We demonstrate that our methods outperform
               state-of-the-art methods in a diverse set of TTA experiments.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Distribution shift – when the data distribution during inference differs from the training data, is a
033   common real-world scenario. Under distribution shift, it has been shown that a pre-trained model
034   can degrade its performance significantly (Quinonero-Candela et al., 2008; Koh et al., 2021; Fang
035   et al., 2020). Domain adaptation is one of the most dominant settings for distribution shift, wherein
036   the learner aims to perform well in a target domain given access to both labeled training data from
037   source domains and (unlabeled) data from the target domain (Ben-David et al., 2010). However, the
038   learner cannot access the pre-training data during inference in many privacy-sensitive applications,
039
      rendering domain adaptation approaches obsolete. Test-time adaption (TTA) is a more practical
      setting of distribution shift wherein the learner aims to adapt to any new unlabeled input data from
040
      a target domain to make a prediction using only a pre-trained model, without access to the training
041
      data during inference.
042
043   Due to the wide range of practical demands, many different methods have been proposed and have
044   achieved impressive results in TTA. TENT (Wang et al., 2020), inspired by the assumption that the
045
      batch normalization (BN) layer is responsible for scaling and shifting neural network features (Li
      et al., 2016), merely trains the BN layers of the target model based on minimizing self-entropy.
046
      This approach is then extended to EATA (Niu et al., 2022) and SAR (Niu et al., 2023), where
047
      SAR addresses limitations in TENT by introducing new techniques called reliable sample selection
048   and sharpness-aware loss to adapt to real-world scenarios. Additionally, works by (Zhao et al.,
049   2023; Lim et al., 2023) indicate that the current estimation of BN is inaccurate, and they suggest
050   a new one. On the other hand, the lack of labels in the target data has encouraged the use of self-
051   learning (Gandelsman et al., 2022; Jang & Chung, 2022), pseudo-label learning (Goyal et al., 2022;
052   Liang et al., 2020), and self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b) as effective
053   approaches. Among these approaches, pseudo-labeling is the most preferable because it naturally
      follows the training paradigm (training on supervised cross-entropy with true labels). Additionally,

074   the cost of generating pseudo-labels is low, making it more computationally efficient. Finally, in a
075   pseudo-learning setting, techniques like label smoothing (Liang et al., 2020) and label refinement
076   (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) have shown promising results in improving label quality,
077   therefore enhancing model adaptation performance.
078   Despite recent achievements in TTA utilizing pseudo-labels, working under this setting still presents
079   four obstacles:
080
081            • Memorization effect (Arpit et al., 2017): During the pseudo-label generation step (where
082              the target model predicts sample labels), some labels will inevitably be noisy. During
083
                 tuning, the target model learns from clean samples first, then memorizes the noisy labels,
                 which deteriorates model performance (Yi et al., 2023; Arpit et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2020).
084
085            • Confirmation bias (Arazo et al., 2020): Errors in noisy labels accumulate over time, leading
086              to the failure of the learned model.
087            • Unbounded noise ratio: As indicated in (Liu et al., 2020), the noise in pseudo-labels is
088              unbounded, whereas recent methods that work on robust noise are constrained by a bounded
089              noise ratio (Song et al., 2022; Natarajan et al., 2013). Therefore, there is a lack of theoretical
090              guarantees for applying common robust noise methods to pseudo-label learning.
091            • Adaptation cost: TTA is expected to work well in an online manner, which means we
092              need to trade off between the computation cost and the accuracy of the target model when
093              adapting to new samples. An acceptable model should achieve high performance with just
094
                 one or a few adaptation steps.
095
      Our work hypothesizes that these challenges can be solved efficiently if the adaptation process is
096   conducted in a "quick" learning manner. Quick learning here means that, under the same number of
097   training steps (including forward and backward steps), the algorithm will guide a model to converge
098   faster to an optimal point compared with the original training. This strategy accelerates adaptation,
099   enabling the model to partially avoid memorizing noisy samples through the early learning phe-
100   nomenon (Liu et al., 2020). Furthermore, larger learning steps facilitate rapid convergence to the
101   target domain, reducing the adaptation gap and cost. From this perspective, the generated pseudo-
102   label procedure becomes more accurate with a lower noise ratio compared to standard training, as
103   labels are produced at each inference step, benefiting from improved convergence speed. Lastly,
104
      updating the model with larger steps requires fewer iterations to achieve a satisfactory target model,
      mitigating the impact of confirmation bias, where errors accumulate proportionally to the number of
105
      updating iterations.
106
107   Naturally, we can achieve this "quick" adaptation goal by increasing the magnitude of the learning
      rate. However, a larger learning rate can make the network unstable during training. For example,

137   small gradient variance plays an important role in the stability of the learning process (Johnson &
138   Zhang, 2013; Agarwal et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021), where small gradient variance means that at
139   each updating step, the gradient does not vary too much, making the learning process more stable.
140   In this work, we follow the second approach to define a stable learning strategy by introducing a
141   new loss function named sparse-CL, which operates in the logit (pre-softmax) space and can yield
142   stable gradient norms between batches (or samples) during adaptation. We then investigate a neg-
143
      ative learning method called k-NL, which can be jointly trained with sparse-CL to improve model
      performance by enlarging the distance between each sample and its negative classes. (Figure 1).
144
145   Our works are organized as follows:
146
147          • We investigate factors that can affect the stability during adaptation, then introduce the
148
               sparse cross-logit learning method named sparse-CL, which works on logit space and helps
               stabilize the learning process.
149
150          • Then, we devise a new negative learning method named k-NL that can further boost the
151            performance of the sparse-CL loss.
152          • Finally, we empirically show that our method can work stable with a large learning rate,
153            leading to an efficient solution with memorization effect and accumulation error, and en-
154            hance the target model to achieve new SOTA results with a large improvement (improving
155
               over 6% on average in various settings compared to SAR).

      Setting Our work decomposes the source model Fs (·) into two components: the feature extractor
173
      fs (·) and the classifier hs (·), which is used to make a final prediction. The target model Ft (·)
174
      is initialized from the source model Fs (·), which means ft (·) = fs (·) and ht (·) = hs (·). For
175   each input sample x, we extract its representation vector fx = f (x). This vector is then input
176   into the classifier h(·) to obtain the logit vector hx = h(fx ). The pseudo label of the sample x
177   is then constructed from this logit vector by applying the one-hot operation on the softmax output:
178   ŷ = O(px ), where px = S(hx ) is the softmax vector, and O(·) and S(·) are the one-hot and softmax
179   functions, respectively. The pseudo label ŷ for each sample x is the one-hot vector created from
180   the output of the classifier hs (·). Additionally, we view the classifier h(·) as a list of C prototypes
181   P = {f1 , f2 , · · · , fC }, where fi , i ∈ {1, 2, · · · , C}, is the prototype of class i.

      3.2   S TABLE LEARNING MECHANIC : MOVING FROM SOFTMAX SPACE TO LOGIT SPACE
217
218   First of all, we discuss the limitation of the original cross-entropy loss from the gradient perspective.
219   Equation 5 reveals that cross-entropy loss pays more attention to hard negative classes (classes with
220   high probability but not the pseudo-class) and does not focus on the pseudo-class when it already
221   achieves high probability. Specifically, the magnitude of the derivative corresponding to class k
222   (where k is not the pseudo-class) is |pk − yk | = pk , which indicates that negative classes with high
223   probability will be updated faster. On the other hand, for the pseudo-class (class k), its derivative
224
      magnitude is 1−pk . Consequently, when pk approaches 1, the derivative update converges to 0. This
      updating mechanism, however, is not efficient under pseudo-label learning. When the probability of
225
      a pseudo label is close to 1, it means there is a higher chance this predicted label will be the true
226
      one. Therefore, a good loss function should still pay attention to these pseudo-classes during the
227   updating, instead of discarding them.
228
229   In order to overcome the above limitation and yield a stable training effect, we would like to propose
230
      a surrogate loss, which is motivated by cross-entropy. Back to Equation 4, the probability of class i:

407   Generally, we can summarize these insights as follows: 1. In the first row, adapting sparse-CL shifts
408   the true labels toward the left side, while forcing the noisy labels to the right side, which shows
409   that the target model generates clearer decision boundaries between positive and negative classes.
410   2. Noisy labels, when applied with sparse-CL, tend to lie in the right-most region (the high-density
411   region), and this phenomenon is increased when adding k-NL. 3. In the second row, the number of
412
      absolute true samples reaches approximately 9000, 12000, and 14000 samples when using entropy
      minimization, sparse-CL, and sparse-CL + k-NL loss, respectively. This indicates that we could use
413
      the difference between negative and positive logits as a criterion to decide whether the pseudo labels
414
      of samples are true labels or noisy ones (similar to how previous work (Niu et al., 2022) used the
415   magnitude of entropy to detect highly reliable samples).

      5   C ONCLUSION
497
      In this work, we propose a stable learning strategy to mitigate the noise problems of pseudo labels
498
      under TTA learning. Our study first analyzes the main reasons for the failure of current learning
499   methods using pseudo labels. We then highlight that gradient variance is a key factor in stable
500   learning and introduce a new loss function named Sparse-CL, inspired by cross-entropy loss, which
501   ensures stable updates. Our training strategy updates the model with a high learning rate, hypothe-
502   sizing that this rapid updating helps the model adapt well to the target domain and effectively combat
503   both memorization and confirmation bias problems. Additionally, we enhance cross-logit learning
504   by incorporating a negative loss named k-NL, which leverages complementary labels and helps the
505   model focus on k-hardness negative samples, thereby improving overall model performance.
