011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Counterfactual explanations (CE) identify data points that closely resemble the
014            observed data but produce different machine learning (ML) model outputs, offering
015            critical insights into model decisions. Despite the diverse scenarios, goals and tasks
016
               to which they are tailored, existing CE methods often lack actionable efficiency
               because of unnecessary feature changes included within the explanations that are
017
               presented to users and stakeholders. We address this problem by proposing a
018
               method that minimizes the required feature changes while maintaining the validity
019            of CE, without imposing restrictions on models or CE algorithms, whether instance-
020            or group-based. The key innovation lies in computing a joint distribution between
021            observed and counterfactual data and leveraging it to inform Shapley values for
022            feature attributions (FA). We demonstrate that optimal transport (OT) effectively
023            derives this distribution, especially when the alignment between observed and
024            counterfactual data is unclear in used CE methods. Additionally, a counterintuitive
025            finding is uncovered: it may be misleading to rely on a counterfactual distribution
026            defined by the CE generation mechanism in conducting FA. Our proposed method
027
               is validated on extensive experiments across multiple datasets, showcasing its
               effectiveness in refining CE towards greater actionable efficiency.

      1    BACKGROUND
031
032
      Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) is essential for making artificial intelligence systems trans-
033
      parent and trustworthy (Arrieta et al., 2020; Das & Rad, 2020). Within this area, feature attributions
034   (FA) methods, such as Shapley values (Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020; Lundberg & Lee, 2017), deter-
035   mine how much each input feature contributes to a machine learning (ML) model’s output. This helps
036   simplify complex models by highlighting the most influential features. For example, in a healthcare
037   model, Shapley values can identify key factors like age and medical history, assisting clinicians
038   in understanding the model’s decisions (Ter-Minassian et al., 2023; Nohara et al., 2022). Another
039   technique counterfactual explanations (CE) (Wachter et al., 2017; Guidotti, 2022) show how small
040   changes in input features can lead to different outcomes. While hundreds of CE algorithms have
041   been proposed (Guidotti, 2022; Verma et al., 2020) to date, it is hardly practical to find one single
042
      CE algorithm that suits for all user cases, due to each of them is tailored particularly for their own
      different scenarios, goals, and tasks. For instance, the objective in one CE algorithm can be defined
043
      as finding a single counterfactual instance for each factual instance sometimes, while at othertimes,
044
      it could be treating multiple instances as a group and seeking one or more/multiple counterfactual
045   instances for each factual observation. In some cases, the focus of a CE algorithm could be on the
046   entire dataset, aiming to identify global CE that indicate the direction to move the factual instances to
047   achieve the desired model output (Rawal & Lakkaraju, 2020; Ley et al., 2022; 2023; Carrizosa et al.,
048   2024). Yet in other scenarios, the group of factual instances is viewed as a distribution, aiming to
049   find a counterfactual distribution that remains similar in shape to the factual distribution (You et al.,
050   2024), and ensuring comparable costs. Besides, some CE algorithms assume differentiable models,
051   whereas others are designed specifically for tree-based or ensemble models.
052
053   Problem Description and Challenges Although both FA and CE are vital for making AI models
      more interpretable and accountable, relying only on one of them will cause drawbacks. That is, FA

079
      Three major challenges remain in addressing this problem. First, it is unrealistic to expect a single CE
080
      algorithm to meet all the needs universally, as the problem is often task-specific. Second, the approach
081
      should not rely on strong assumptions about the model (for example, requiring differentiability or
082   special structures) to ensure its applicability across a wide range of models. Third, FA like feature
083   importance can be misleading due to the lack of coherence between the FA scores and the changes
084   for counterfactual effect. In other words, it is not effective to perform FA independently of CE to
085   select the most important features to change. We will demonstrate later that this decoupling can result
086   in counterproductive feature modifications (also referred to as actions from users/stakeholders) in
087   Result II of Section 6, as the features deemed important generally may not align with the specific
088   pathways to achieve the desired counterfactual outcomes.
089
090   Main Contributions Our main contributions are listed as follows.
091
092   • A versatile algorithmic framework “COunterfactuals with Limited Actions (COLA)” is proposed,
093     which adapts to various CE methods and ML models. Extensive simulations show that the
094     framework produces action plans that require significantly fewer feature changes to achieve
095
        outcomes similar (or sometimes equal) to those generated by various CE algorithms. Especially,
        COLA is shown to have near-optimal performance under certain circumstances.
096
097   • A new Shapley method “joint-probability-informed Shapley (p-SHAP)” is proposed, utilizing joint
098     distribution of factual and counterfactual and resulting in remarkably well-performed action plans.
099     We discover that other Shapley methods without incorporating counterfactual knowledge lead to
100     unproductive attribution results for the aforementioned problem.
101
      • A counter-intuitive finding is identified, showing that associating each factual data instance with its
102
        explicitly generated counterfactual (i.e. an exact alignment is known) may still underperform our
103
        p-SHAP solution. This finding emphasizes the importance of the joint distribution of factual and
104     counterfactual instances, as a proper alignment serves as crucial knowledge for accurate contrastive
105     FA.
106

      Figure 1 without specifying certain CE algorithms and models.

143   This section introduces commonly used Shapley value methods for FA, which, together with our later
144   proposed one, are integrated into our algorithmic framework COLA. We first introduce the concept
145   of Shapley value in game theory, followed by a discussion on various commonly used Shapley
146   methods for FA. Readers could find in (Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020) for a comprehensive overview
147   of different Shapley value methods.
148   In cooperative game theory, a coalitional game is characterized by a finite set of players (in our
149   context, features), denoted by F = {1, 2, . . . d}, and a characteristic function v. This function
150   v : 2F → R maps each subset S ⊆ F to a real number v(S), representing the total payoff that can
151   be achieved by the members of S through cooperation, with the condition v(∅) = 0. The Shapley
152   value, as defined based on v later, is a fundamental concept in this framework, providing an equitable
153   method to distribute the overall payoff, v(F), of the grand coalition among the individual players.
154   Prior to defining the Shapley value, denote ∆(k, S) the incremental value produced by a player
155   (feature) k to a coalition S, aligning with the concept of marginal contribution. This is defined as the
156   additional payoff realized by the coalition due to the inclusion of the player k, i.e.,
157                                         ∆(k, S) = v(S ∪ k) − v(S).                                     (2)

666   A    C OMPARISON TO E XISTING A PPROACHES
667
668   The authors in (Albini et al., 2022) proposed CF-SHAP, which uses counterfactual data points as the
669   background distribution for Shapley. Yet, it assumes the counterfactual data distribution is defined
670   conditionally on each single data instance, which implies that there is a known probabilistic alignment
671   between every factual and every counterfactual instance. Similar assumptions are made in (Merrick
672   & Taly, 2020; Kommiya Mothilal et al., 2021). In many scenarios where global explanations are
673   expected, this assumption fails. We note that the setup in (Albini et al., 2022) for counterfactual data
674   distribution is a special case of ours. The authors in (Kwon & Zou, 2022) proposed WeightedSHAP,
675
      adding weights to features rather than treating them as contributing equally. Our proposed method
      weights the contributions for data points and can be straightforwardly extended to consider weighting
676
      both rows and columns. Literature (Kommiya Mothilal et al., 2021) establishes a framework for
677

1050   The experiments are conducted on a high performance computing (HPC) cluster, running with four
1051
       nodes (for the four datasets) in parallel, with each node equipped with two Intel Xeon Processor
       2660v3 (10 core, 2.60GHz) and 128 GB memory. The experiment runs approximately 5-10 hours in
1052
       each node, dependent on the size of the dataset. It is also possible to reproduce the experiment on a
1053
       laptop, while it costs more computational time generally than using an HPC cluster.
1054
1055   For the four datasets, the numerical features are standardized, and the categorical features follow
1056   either label-encoding or one-hot encoding. Practically, we did not observe remarkable difference
1057
       between the two encoding methods in terms of COLA’s performance. The train-test split follows
       7 : 3.

1059   The optimality baseline as shown in Figure 4 is solved by Gurobi 11.0.2 (gur). In order to reproduce
1060   the optimality baseline, a license of Gurobi is required. Otherwise, we can resort to open-source
1061   operations research libraries such as Google-OR tools (goo). We remark that solving the MILP in
1062   Appendix G is computationally expensive, such that it may only apply to small scale datasets such as
1063
       German Credit. If one wants to compute the optimality baseline for other datasets, then the number
       of used features needs to be reduced.
