009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models are versatile tools but are not suitable for small inference
012            budgets. Small models have more efficient inference, but their lower capacity means
013            that their performance can be good only if one limits their scope to a specialized
014            domain. This paper explores how to get good specialized small language models
015            using a large, generic, pretraining set and a limited amount of specialized data.
016            We consider two scenarios, depending on whether (i) one can afford pretraining
017            a model for each specialization task, or (ii) one wants to cheaply adapt a single
018
               pretrained model for each task. In the first scenario, we propose an effective
               solution based on importance sampling: we resample the pretraining set to imitate
019
               the specialization data and train a small model on it. In the second scenario, we
020
               propose a novel architecture, projected networks (PN). PN is a large network whose
021            parameters can be linearly projected into a small network for specialization. For
022            both scenarios, we demonstrate the empirical effectiveness of our solutions across
023            various domains, training set sizes, and training budgets.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a generic tool to address a wide range of language
029   tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Bommasani et al., 2022). This generality requires a large, diverse, generic
030   training set. This rich set ensures that the model fits many subdomains close to the tasks it will
031   eventually address. Model generality is particularly impactful for tasks where the cost of collecting a
032   representative training set cannot be justified. However, LLMs inference is costly because of their
033
      large number of parameters, required to fit a large training set. This cost restricts LLMs to high-value
      applications. Efficient inference is an active research area that follows multiple routes like model
034
      distillation (Hsieh et al., 2023), quantization (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2023), pruning (Ma et al.,
035
      2023) or hardware optimization (Aminabadi et al., 2022). However, reducing model size is the most
036   direct solution for applications under tight inference constraints, and it can be combined with all the
037   aforementioned techniques to further reduce inference costs. With that inference constraint in mind,
038   we focus on training Small Language Models (SLMs).
039
      A SLM cannot fit a generic training set as well as an LLM (Vapnik, 1995; Bishop & Bishop, 2023).
040
      It is hence necessary to forgo the generality of the model to devote its limited capacity to a targeted
041
      specialization domain. While a large specialized training set for the application at hand would be
042   ideal for training, such a set is costly and usually justified only for high-value applications. Many
043   applications, therefore, have to face both a limited inference budget and a limited in-domain training
044   set size. For instance, some applications cannot afford to collect more than a few million tokens
045   worth of training data (1m tokens ≃ 10 books). Such applications, with low inference and low data
046   collection budgets, are facing a challenging problem.
047
      This work tackles precisely this problem. We aim to answer the following question: How can
048   we get a specialized small language model when little specialization data is available? To
049   answer this question, we distinguish two scenarios of practical importance, each leading to different
050   recommendations: (i) when one needs a single small model for one specialized domain, a large
051   training budget can be considered for that domain and one can pretrain a model specifically targeted
052   to this domain; on the other hand, (ii) when one needs one model per domain for many specialized
053   domains, pretraining one model per domain quickly becomes too expensive, and it is interesting to
      share the training cost across domains.

062   For both scenarios, there are natural baselines that come to mind. The first baseline consists of
063   pretraining a small model using the large generic pretraining set and then fine-tuning it with the
064
      little available specialization data. Another widely used method is distillation (Hinton et al., 2015):
      one pretrains a large teacher and a small student model on the pretraining set, fine-tunes the large
065
      model on the specialization set, and then fine-tunes the small model with the distillation loss from the
066
      fine-tuned large model.
067
068   While these two baselines are widely used in practice, for both scenarios, we propose improved
069   pretraining strategies and demonstrate their superiority. The cornerstone of our improved strategies
070
      is a clustering of the pretraining set, which allows us to sample data points from each cluster of the
      pretraining set and pretrain on a mixture of these clusters with the flexibility to choose the weight of
071
      each cluster. When one targets a single domain (i), we find that an effective method is pretraining a
072
      small model over generic data with importance sampling (Owen, 2013). We pretrain on a mixture of
073   the clusters where the weights are chosen to mimic the targeted domain. We show that, even though
074   this emphasis relies on information from the specialization data, such a pretrained model still benefits
075   from fine-tuning over the specialization data and outperforms the previous baselines. When one
076   targets multiple domains (ii), we propose a novel architecture for pretraining, the projected network
077   (PN). This model is a large model trained on generic data in which some parameters are tied to a
078   cluster. Crucially, its parameters can be linearly projected into different small models — one per
079   cluster — prior to fine-tuning. When one is given a new specialization domain, we show that it is
080   effective to select a single projection and fine-tune the corresponding small model. In this paradigm,
081
      training the generic high-capacity model is costly, but its specialization phase (projection onto an
      SLM and SLM fine tuning) is not. This is ideal when many specialized models are needed.
082
083   We evaluate each method on several domains, pretraining and specialization budgets as well as
084   specialized dataset sizes. Compared to the baselines, both importance sampling and projected
085   networks bring a strong improvement, on all considered domains, specialized dataset sizes and
086   training budgets. We show that importance sampling results in better specialized-perplexity than
087
      projected networks. However, importance sampling incurs a high pretraining cost when targeting
      multiple domains since pretraining is not shared across domains. As a result, projected networks are
088
      beneficial in the second scenario, for applications requiring many specialized models.
089
090   Contributions In Section 2, we present classical pretraining strategies as well as our novel methods
091   based on a clustering of the pretraining dataset. We present our cluster-based importance sampling
092   strategy. We introduce Projected Networks, a novel application for hard mixture models and hyper-
093
      networks, and explain how this provides multiple models that can be quickly instantiated during
      the specialization phase. In Section 3, we propose a methodology to evaluate different strategies
094
      to train a specialized SLM and identifies four important variables: the generic training budget (for
095
      training before the target domain is known), the specialization budget (for training after the target
096   domain is known), the inference budget, and the in-domain training set size. In Section 4, we provide
097   a comprehensive empirical study by reporting experiments on 9 domains, 3 specialized training set
098   sizes with various training budgets. We show that, as expected, fine-tuning is essential. Surprisingly,
099   we highlight that distillation from a large model, albeit popular, brings little improvement when
100   accounting for the overall pretraining budget. We show that our cluster-based importance sampling
101   method is very effective for training specialized models in the large specialization budget case.
102   Finally, we find that Projected Networks lead to the best models in the small specialization budget
103   case. Figure 1 summarizes our practical recommendations.

      2.1   G ENERIC AND S PECIALIZATION DATASETS
109
110   We have access to a large, generic pretraining set, which is typically obtained as the output of a web
111   crawl. This set is large: it has enough samples to train a model on it or on a subset of it without risks
112   of overfitting. This set is generic: it covers a wide variety of topics and concepts. We train models on
113   this set using the next-token prediction loss.
114   We also have access to a small specialization set, which can be, for instance, the internal documen-
115   tation of a company, some emails, or a set of scientific articles. This set is small: one cannot train
116   a good model on it without overfitting. This set is specialized: it only covers specific topics. Once
117   again, we measure the quality of models on this set with the next-token prediction loss and perplexity.
118
119   2.2   BASELINES : S MALL M ODELS , F INE -T UNING & D ISTILLATION
120
121   We target a model with a good specialized perplexity under inference constraints. We abstract
122   the inference constraints as a limit on the model size. We call Small Language Model (SLM) a
123   transformer of the maximal possible size allowed by the inference constraint.
124   We consider 3 variants of SLMs. We first consider an SLM model trained only on the generic
125   pretraining data. This model can be effective if the generic and specialization data are close. We
126   then consider an SLM trained only on the specialization data . This model can be effective if the
127   specialization training set is large enough but will quickly overfit when the specialization set is small.
128   Finally, we consider a model pretrained on the generic data and fine-tuned on the specialization data.
129   The amount of fine-tuning can be adjusted via early stopping. This adjusts a trade-off between the
130
      proximity to the generic distribution and overfitting to the specialization set. Training and fine-tuning
      are done by minimizing the next-token prediction loss on the corresponding datasets.
131
132   Since our capacity constraint is motivated by inference efficiency, we can consider larger models
133   at training time and rely on distillation (Hinton et al., 2015) to satisfy the inference requirements.
134   Specifically, we consider a large language model (LLM) that has a bigger size than the inference con-
135   straint allows. We pretrain it on the generic pretraining set, and then fine-tune it on the specialization
136
      set, achieving a lower perplexity on the specialization set than the pretrained-then-fine-tuned SLM
      thanks to its larger size. A pretrained SLM student is then fine-tuned to a mixture of the specialization
137
      data and next-word distributions from the teacher. We call this model SLM-d.
138
139   This type of distillation is often called Response-based Knowledge distillation in the literature (Gou
140   et al., 2021, Sec 2.1) and is arguably the most widely used type of distillation. We do not consider
141   using the LLM as a data augmentation tool (Feng et al., 2021), to produce synthetic data (Tang
142
      et al., 2019; West et al., 2021) resembling the specialization data, and then use them to train or
      fine-tune the small model. Indeed, this requires training an LLM, which is time-consuming, and in
143
      our setup, this leads to more variance than response-based knowledge distillation and overall poorer
144
      performances (Menon et al., 2021).

      2.3   C LUSTERING OF THE P RETRAINING DATA
147
148   The subsequent strategies rely on a clustering of the generic pretraining set. Clustering associates
149   each training example with a discrete latent variable that we use to efficiently estimate importance
150   sampling weights with a conditional independence assumption (Section 2.4). It also allows us to
151   condition the projection in projected networks (Section 2.5).
152
      To cluster the data, we embed each document in the generic set as a vector using Sentence
153   BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). The documents longer than the maximum context we consider
154   (1,024) are broken into nonoverlapping windows. Then, we use the k-means algorithm to cluster
155   the generic set into k clusters. We can then query samples from each cluster. This also defines a
156   cluster assignment function assign(x) for any sample x.
157

186   2.5   A SYMMETRIC M ODELS : P ROJECTED N ETWORKS AND H ARD M IXTURES
187
188   Our inference constraints require that the final specialized model is a low-capacity SLM. Prior to fine-
189   tuning on the specialization data, the capacity limit does not apply to generic pretraining. We devise
190   a pretraining strategy to take advantage of this asymmetry. At pretraining time, we train a network
191   with many parameters, but each example only interacts with a projection of the parameters onto an
192
      SLM. Like distillation, this strategy trains a model with many parameters, but unlike distillation, all
      model evaluations during training are already constrained to operate within the size limits.
193

195   models or experts, {SLM-pni }ki=1 ; there is one expert per cluster i. Each expert is instantiated via its

197   parameters during generic pretraining. Once the specialized training data are available, specialized
198
      fine-tuning starts from one of the experts. Different strategies for expert selection are discussed in our
      experiments.

232   Hard Mixture of Experts As a simple alternative to the previous architecture, we consider SLM-mix,
233   a hard mixture of expert (Gross et al., 2017). We pretrain one SLM, SLM-mixi , on each cluster i,
234   independently. The pretraining cost and overall number of parameters of this method are high as
235
      both scale linearly with the number of clusters. The hard mixture can be compared to a special case
236
      of a PN network in which h = k, Ei = δi ∈ IRk and M (l) = I. In that case, all weight matrices
      rely on independent slices of the parameters. Unlike PN, the hard mixture does not allow one to
237
      set the number of experts k and the capacity multiplier h independently. The expert parameters are
238
      not shared and learning cannot leverage synergies between similar clusters. On the other hand, the
239   learning is embarrassingly parallel since each expert pretraining is independent of the other experts.
240   Like for PN, specialization can be performed inexpensively by fine-tuning a single expert. Despite
241   these conceptual differences, our experiments reveal benefits in both methods.

246   3.1   M ETHODOLOGY
247
      With inference cost and specialization training data constraints, we study the alternative training
248
      methods at various training costs. We report training costs and which part of the cost can be shared
249
      across multiple domains. We consider 4 important metrics:
250
251   Generic training cost: the cost of the training phase that can be performed before the specialization
252   data are available, on a generic training set. This cost is often called pretraining. It is domain-
253
      independent and can be shared across multiple specializations, e.g., via later fine-tuning. Although
      not mandatory, the generic training data are essential when specialization data are limited.
254
255   Specialization training cost: the cost of the training performed once the specialization data are
256   available. This cost is not shared across different specializations.
257   Inference cost: the cost of running inference on a specialized model. Low inference cost allows
258   wider, cheaper model deployment.
259
260
      Size of the specialization training set: it varies across applications and influences pretraining and
      specialization choices.
261
262   Taking the inference cost and the specialization data size as hard constraints, we study the operating
263   curves resulting from varying the generic and specialization training costs. We measure training
264   cost (pretraining and specialization) in hours of graphic processor compute time (GPUh) on the
265   same hardware (Nvidia-A100). We consider pretraining costs ranging from 10 to 650 GPUh and
266
      specialization costs ranging from 0.3 to 120 GPUh.
267   We evaluate language modeling with perplexity, using 20k held-out documents per dataset. We focus
268   solely on language modeling; evaluating the models on downstream tasks (e.g. question answering,
269   sentiment analysis, translation, etc) is beyond the scope of the paper. However, our conclusions could
      extend to downstream tasks as perplexity and downstream performance are often correlated Gonen

      We first report our main results before comparing the different methods. We vary pretraining budgets
353
      and report perplexity on the generic pretraining set (c4) for each method in Figure 3. When we
354   consider SLM-pn and SLM-mix, we observe that even if the number of pretrained parameters is
355   larger than LLM, they do not enjoy as good perplexity. However, their perplexity is better than SLM
356   while they are as efficient when tested or fine-tuned on a single cluster.
357
      Generic perplexity (c4) is not our primary goal and we now examine specialized perplexities. Figure 4
358
      (a) reports the results before fine-tuning. Specialized perplexities are much higher than the c4
359
      perplexities, indicating that specialization is necessary. Figure 4 (b) reports the results after fine-
360   tuning several pretrained checkpoints for each method on the 1M token dataset of each domain.
361   Each domain-specific model is evaluated before macro-averaging. Since 1M tokens is a small set,
362   fine-tuning relies on a small learning rate and early stopping (base learning rate divided by 3, stopping
363   when validation loss stops improving, which is always less than 2k fine-tuning steps on one GPU
364   when validation loss stops improving). Fine-tuning is highly beneficial for all methods and results in
365   significantly improved perplexity. We also remark that pre-fine-tuning perplexity on the Pile is not
366   necessarily a good indicator of post-fine-tuning perplexity: e.g. the SLM checkpoints ordering is
367   very different on the two curves, the ordering between SLM-mix and SLM-pn also changes during
368
      fine-tuning.
369   We also consider fine-tuning on 8 and 64 million tokens for each domain, see Figure 4 (c) and (d).
370   More data allows us to train slightly longer and keep the base learning rate without overfitting. We
371   stop at most after 4k steps and 30k steps for the 8M and 64M cases respectively. We observe that the
372   benefit of a good starting point provided by SLM-pn and SLM-mix (compared to SLM) erodes as the
373   domain training set size increases.

      4.3                            A SYMMETRIC MODELS : H ARD M IXTURE OF E XPERTS AND P ROJECTED N ETWORKS
456
457   The projected-networks allow one to select the overall number of parameters of the network while
458   keeping the size of the inference model constant. Unlike the hard mixture of experts, varying capacity
459   does not require changing the number of clusters either. The PN capacity is therefore a trade-off
460   between generic pretraining cost and specialized accuracy. Figure 7 shows perplexity on the Pile
461   subsets after fine-tuning on 1M tokens as a function of pretraining cost. While more experts always
462   perform better per iteration, 32 experts achieve a better cost/perplexity trade-off.
463   The hard mixture of experts relies on the generic dataset split in clusters, see Section 2.3, and its
464   number of experts corresponds to the number of clusters. For specialization, we fine-tune a single
465   expert. The results presented above, e.g. Figure 4, use the expert corresponding to the most frequent
466   cluster in the specialization data. Alternately, we also consider selecting the expert which has the
467   lowest loss on the specialization set before fine-tuning, which involves evaluating each expert. As a
468   third more costly option, we fine-tune all experts and pick the best one a posteriori. Table 5 reports
469   this result when fine-tuning on 1M tokens with 64 experts. The results of the different strategies are
470   close, ±0.3 PPL, and the most costly option of fine-tuning all experts performs slightly better.
471   As a final observation on SLM-mix, the strategy of fine-tuning only the expert corresponding to the
472   most frequent cluster can be very efficient when one targets a single domain. In that case, one can
473   train only the expert for the single cluster of interest. This single-cluster training strategy is however
474   a poorer approximation of the specialization distribution than IS, as shown in Figure 8.

      5                          R ELATED W ORK
477
478
      Domain adaptation for language modeling has a long history, predating neural network language
479
      models Rosenfeld (2000). This research stemmed from the observation that models trained on large
480   amount of data, even far from the targeted domain were impactful on end applications Brants et al.
481   (2007). After neural language models were introduced Bengio et al. (2000), they were also scaled
482   up to benefit from increasing amount of training data Raffel et al. (2020); Brown et al. (2020);
483   Chowdhery et al. (2022); Touvron et al. (2023). This growth involves a trade-off between training
484   a model from a large dataset (i.e. reducing estimation errors) or a dataset representative of the end
485   application domain (i.e. having a training distribution representative of test condition), both essential
      to good generalization Vapnik (1995).


                                                                                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Model fine-tuning and multi-task learning have become essential tools in order to both benefit from
487   large generic training data and limited in-domain data Caruana (1993); Collobert et al. (2011);
488   Gururangan et al. (2020). Data curation and selection methods have also been proposed in order to
489   resample generic data with a given application domain in mind Moore & Lewis (2010); Wang et al.
490   (2018); Xie et al. (2023). Most of these methods can be tied to importance sampling Kahn & Harris
491   (1951); Grangier & Iter (2022).
492
      Simultaneously with the growth in large language model size, concerns about model inference cost
493   gave rise to research on efficient inference. Several routes are investigated with this goal, including
494   model distillation Hsieh et al. (2023); FitzGerald et al. (2022), weight quantization Xiao et al. (2023);
495   Dettmers & Zettlemoyer (2023) and pruning Ma et al. (2023); Xia et al. (2023). Mixtures of experts
496   have been investigated as a way to decouple overall model capacity and inference efficiency Shazeer
497   et al. (2017); Du et al. (2022); Clark et al. (2022).
498
      Our asymmetric projected network can be seen as a hyper-network, a type of neural network whose
499   parameters are themselves predicted by a secondary network Ha et al. (2017); Karimi Mahabadi et al.
500   (2021). In our case, the secondary network is a cluster-conditioned linear projection. Gururangan et al.
501   (2023) also propose learning one model per domain of interest and using an ensembling technique at
502   inference time, which requires instantiating multiple models.
503

      6    L IMITATIONS
505
506
      Our experimentation covers multiple domains, training budgets and training set sizes but, at this point,
507
      we did not explore multiple sizes for our SLMs. We want to verify in the future if the advantage of
508   PN over distillation extends to different pair of large/small model sizes. Similarly, we studied the
509   impact of the number of clusters on SLM-is, SLM-pn and SLM-mix but all our clustering experiments
510   represent documents with sentence BERT, while different representations might impact our results.

512
      7    C ONCLUSIONS
513
514
      This work considers a common double practical constraint for language modeling: the scarcity of
515
      in-domain training data and a limited inference budget. We propose to train small, efficient language
516   models and improve their accuracy by rethinking the pretraining process on abundant, generic training
517   data. This paper formalizes the problem and proposes two main contributions. (i) When one can
518   afford pretraining a model per specialization domain, we introduce an importance sampling method
519   based on data clustering. This allows pretraining to focus on data close to the targeted domain. (ii)
520   When one needs to share the cost of pretraining across multiple specialization domains, we propose
521   Projected Networks, a novel architecture that trains a collection of small models jointly. Each model
522   of the collection can be used on its own, for instance, for fine-tuning on a new domain. The empirical
523   benefit of our contributions is shown across multiple domains, training budgets and training set
524
      sizes. Our work yields simple recommendations summarized in Figure 1. Another benefit of the
      projected networks is that they can be specialized without access to pretraining data. For instance, if
525
      the specialization data is sensitive, the data owner can cheaply instantiate and fine-tune the model
526
      themselves. Our methodology is not specific to language modeling and we plan to extend it to other
527   modalities where inference constraints are also important (e.g. computer vision).
