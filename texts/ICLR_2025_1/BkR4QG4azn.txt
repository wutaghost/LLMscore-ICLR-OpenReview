009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are generative models capable of produc-
012            ing graphs. While GFlowNet theory guarantees that a fully trained model samples
013            from an unnormalized target distribution, computing state transition probabilities
014            remains challenging due to the presence of equivalent actions that lead to the same
015            state. In this paper, we analyze the properties of equivalent actions in the context
016            of graph generation tasks and propose efficient solutions to address this prob-
017            lem. Our theoretical analysis reveals that naive implementations, which ignore
018            equivalent actions, introduce systematic bias in the sampling distribution for both
019
               atom-based and fragment-based graph generation. This bias is directly related to
               the number of symmetries in a graph, a factor that is particularly critical in ap-
020
               plications such as drug discovery, where symmetry plays a key role in molecular
021
               structure and function. Experimental results demonstrate that a simple reward-
022            scaling technique not only enables the generation of graphs that closely match
023            the target distribution but also facilitates the sampling of diverse and high-reward
024            samples.

028
029   Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) have emerged as a powerful framework for learning gener-
030   ative models capable of sampling complex, compositional objects with probabilities proportional to

032   a sequence of actions that iteratively modify the structure of the object being built. This approach
033
      is particularly well-suited for generating compositional objects, such as graphs, where each step in
      the process adds components in a structured and interpretable manner. A prominent application of
034
      GFlowNets is molecule generation, where molecules are sequentially constructed as graphs (Bengio
035
      et al., 2021; Jain et al., 2023a).

      2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081
082   Autoregressive graph generation. There are two primary formulations of autoregressive mod-
083
      els: one based on adjacency matrices and the other based on graph sequences (Chen et al., 2021).
      Methods based on adjacency matrices (You et al., 2018b; Popova et al., 2019; Liao et al., 2019)
084
      are unlikely to suffer from the equivalent action problem because they preserve the node order in-
085
      formation generated so far, making each pair of (graph, node order) a unique state. In contrast,
086   equivalent actions arise in methods based on graph sequences (You et al., 2018a; Li et al., 2018;
087   Shi et al., 2020). This becomes problematic if a method requires state transition probabilities, as in
088   GFlowNets. Chen et al. (2021) suggest that, for graph sequence-based methods, the size of a node’s
089   orbit is equal to the number of equivalent transitions, which inspired our work.
090
091
092   GFlowNets. Several learning objectives have been proposed for GFlowNets, including flow
093
      matching (Bengio et al., 2021), detailed balance (Bengio et al., 2023), trajectory balance (Malkin
      et al., 2022), sub-trajectory balance (Madan et al., 2023), as well as their variants to improve training
094
      efficiency (Pan et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023). Recently, GFlowNets have been found to be equiva-
095
      lent to maximum entropy reinforcement learning (Tiapkin et al., 2024; Mohammadpour et al., 2024),
096   which was previously known to be inadequate for directed acyclic graph (DAG) environments (Ben-
097   gio et al., 2021). However, none of these objectives can avoid the equivalent action problem, as they
098   are formalized based on state transitions, where multiple isomorphic graphs can represent the next
099   state.
100
      Ma et al. (2024) had noticed that equivalent actions must be accounted for to compute exact transi-
101
      tion probabilities. They proposed an approximate test to detect equivalent actions at each transition
102   using positional encoding. However, the bias in their model was demonstrated only experimentally
103   on synthetic dataset, without theoretical guarantees. Our work differs in that we provide an exact
104   and efficient solution to this problem, requiring corrections only once at the end of trajectories, as
105   opposed to at each transition within a trajectory, which makes our method straightforward to im-
106   plement. Our analysis reveals that the bias is present in general setting, namely in both atom- and
107   fragment-based generation schemes, and can significantly impact learning, particularly for highly
      symmetric graphs. We provide further details in Appendix B and Table 1.

130   3.1    G RAPH T HEORY
131
132   Let G = (V, E) denote a graph, where V = {v1 , . . . , vn } is the set of n vertices, and E ⊆ V × V
133   is the set of edges. For heterogeneous graphs, we also define labeling functions ln , le , and lg ,
134   which map nodes, edges, and graphs to their respective attributes. We denote G as the set of all
135   such graphs under consideration. A permutation π is a bijective mapping defined on the vertex
136
      set. We extend the permutation to the vertex and edge sets as π(V ) = {π(v) : v ∈ V } and
      π(E) = {(π(vi ), π(vj )) : (vi , vj ) ∈ E}, as well as to the graph as π(G) = (π(V ), π(E)). Since
137
      any permutation simply relabels node indices, it maps to a structurally identical graph. This notion
138
      is formalized as graph isomorphism.
139
140   Definition 3.1 (Isomorphism). Two graphs G = (V, E) and G′ = (V ′ , E ′ ) are isomorphic, denoted
141   G∼  = G′ , if there exists a permutation π : V → V ′ such that π(E) = E ′ . For heterogeneous graphs,
142   the permutation must also preserve labels: for every v ∈ V , ln (v) = ln′ (π(v)), for every (u, v) ∈ E,
143
      le (u, v) = le′ (π(u), π(v)), and lg (G) = lg′ (G′ ).
144
145
      An automorphism is a special case of an isomorphism where the graph is mapped to itself.
146   Definition 3.2 (Automorphism). An automorphism of a graph G = (V, E) is a permutation π on
147   the vertex set V that preserves the edge set, meaning π(E) = E. If labels are present, they must
148   also be preserved under the permutation. The set of all automorphisms of a graph G is called the
149   automorphism group of G, denoted by Aut(G).
150
151   In Figure 1, the graph has two automorphisms: the identity mapping and one that permutes nodes 4
152   and 5. We denote the order (or size) of the automorphism group as |Aut(G)|, which represents the
153   number of symmetries in the graph.
154   Definition 3.3 (Orbit). The orbit of a node u ∈ V in graph G is defined as Orb(G, u) = {v ∈
155   V : ∃π ∈ Aut(G), π(u) = v}. Similarly, the orbit of an edge (u, v) ∈ E in graph G is defined as
156   Orb(G, u, v) = {(h, k) ∈ E : ∃π ∈ Aut(G), (π(u), π(v)) = (h, k)}. More generally, the orbit of
157   a node set S ⊆ V in graph G is defined as Orb(G, S) = {S ′ : ∃π ∈ Aut(G), π(S) = S ′ }.
158
159   An orbit is a set of nodes or edges that are structurally identical. In Figure 1, the orbit of node 4 is
160   {4, 5}, and the orbit of the edge (4, 3) is {(4, 3), (5, 3)}. Equivalent actions occur because they act
161   on nodes in the same orbit; since nodes 4 and 5 are in the same orbit, adding a new node to either
      one is equivalent. This point will be further discussed in Section 4.2.

      4.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
204
205   Consider a sequential graph generation process (G, E, TG ) that constructs graphs by editing the nodes
206   and edges of existing partial graphs, where E is the set of graph editing actions and TG : G × E → G
207   is an acyclic transition function. In previous work, the relationship between the two processes,
208   (S, A, TS ) and (G, E, TG ), was not explicitly addressed, and they were assumed to be identical.
209   Here we relate two processes in a formal way.
210   Since isomorphism is an equivalence relation, it partitions the space G into classes, where each
211   graph in a class is structurally identical to the others. Let [G] = {G′ ∈ G : G′ ∼ = G} denote
212   the equivalence class of G induced by graph isomorphism. The state space S is defined as the set
213   of equivalence classes of graphs, S = {[G] : G ∈ G}, rather than the graph space G itself. This
214   is because our goal in using GFlowNets is to sample any graph within the equivalence class s in
215   proportion to R(s). If we allow individual graphs to represent states, the equivalence class of a
      larger graph will be sampled exponentially more often.

459   Task description. We investigate whether accurately modeling a given target distribution helps
460   generate diverse and high-reward samples in practice. We examine the atom-based generation task
461   from Jain et al. (2023b) and the fragment-based generation task from Bengio et al. (2021). In the
462   atom-based task, the goal is to generate molecules by sequentially adding new atoms, edges, or
463   setting their attributes. Rewards are provided by a proxy model trained on the QM9 dataset, which
464
      predicts the HOMO-LUMO gap. In the fragment-based task, we use a predefined set of fragments,
      each with a predefined set of attachment points—nodes on the fragment where edges can connect.
465
      The task involves building a tree graph, where each node represents a fragment, and edges specify
466
      the attachment points on the two connected fragments. Rewards are determined by a proxy model
467   that predicts the binding energy of a molecule to the sEH target.
468
469   For the atom-based task, we simply scale the final rewards by the order of the automorphism group
470
      as described in Equation (1). For the fragment-based task, we additionally correct for fragment

471
      sampling on high-reward regions in the state space. The correction is applied after rewards are
472
      exponentiated: C(x)R(x)β , where C(x) is the correction term.

      7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
523
524
      GFlowNets were first proposed as an alternative to previous methods, such as MaxEnt RL (Haarnoja
525
      et al., 2017), which are biased toward states with multiple action sequences leading to them in non-
526   injective cases. However, incorrect modeling of state-action probabilities introduces another type of
527   bias in graph generation. While it is unclear how previous works addressed the equivalent action
528   problem, it is likely that they employed the approximation pS (a|s) ≈ pG (→     −
                                                                                      e |G). Although we
529   believe that the previous experimental results remain valid if interpreted carefully with the problem
530   in mind, we recommend being explicit about the correction method in all future work.
531
      In this paper, we analyzed the properties of equivalent actions and proposed a simple correction
532   method that allows for unbiased sampling from the target distribution. Our analysis shows that,
533   without correction, highly symmetric graphs are less likely to be sampled, while symmetric frag-
534   ments are more likely to be sampled, which is crucial for molecule discovery. We demonstrated that
535   the reward scaling technique works for both TB and DB objectives. Experimental results suggest
536   that reward scaling effectively removes bias, allowing for accurate modeling of the target distri-
537   bution, which is essential for sampling diverse, high-reward molecules. A potential limitation of
538   this paper is that the proposed correction method is demonstrated primarily on specific objectives
539   (TB and DB) and datasets relevant to molecule discovery. Future work could explore applying the
      method to tasks with different symmetry patterns and reward structures.

       Compared to sampling trajectories, which involves multiple forward passes through a neural net-
1081   work, the compute time for |Aut(G)| is negligible. For comparison, we report the speed of molec-
1082   ular parsing algorithms measured using ZINC250k dataset: 0.06 ms ± 0.70 (SMILES → molecule)
1083   and 0.04 ms ± 0.05 (molecule → SMILES). The combination of two parsing steps is often used to
1084   check the validity of a given molecule in various prior works. In words, computing |Aut(G)| is in
1085   an order of magnitude faster than validity checking algorithm.
1086
       We used the bliss algorithm for our experiment. It is easy to use as it is included in the igraph
1087   package and is fast enough for our purposes. For large molecules, we can still count automorphisms
1088   in few milliseconds using the nauty package (McKay & Piperno, 2013) as can be seen in the table.
1089   We observed that the pynauty package does not natively support distinguishing between different
1090   edge types, requiring us to transform the input graphs by attaching virtual nodes to handle this. The
1091   reported time in the table reflects these preprocessing steps.
1092
       While we believe the compute time is already minimal considering current applications, we provide
1093   two more recipes to even further improve the run time: 1) Data processing tasks can be easily
1094   parallelized across multiple CPUs. Since GFlowNet is an off-policy algorithm, |Aut(G)| can be
1095   computed concurrently with the policy’s learning process. 2) For large graphs, fragment-based
1096   generation is highly likely to be employed. In such cases, we can utilize an approximate correction
1097   formula, as outlined in Appendix D.

1229   H.4   A PPROXIMATE C ORRECTION M ETHOD
1230
1231   For the fragment-based method, we propose an approximate correction formula, which offers com-
1232   putational benefits. This also aids in understanding the principle behind the correction term for the
1233   fragment-based method. The approximation works as follows: we assign a number to each fragment
1234   based on how many equivalent actions it is likely to incur during generation. We adjust the final re-
1235   wards by dividing them by the product of the assigned numbers N for the constituent fragments Ci :
               Qk
1236   R(X)/ i=1 N (Ci ).
1237   We assigned the number N to each fragment based on how likely it is to incur forward equivalent
1238   actions. This is because fragments that incur multiple forward equivalent actions are more likely to
1239   be selected if no adjustment is applied. For example, cyclohexane (C1CCCCC1) has six attachment
1240   points, all in the same orbit, so it will always incur at least six forward equivalent actions in subse-
1241   quent steps. In contrast, even if a fragment is highly symmetric, if it has only one attachment point,
       it will incur no equivalent actions. We assigned N = 1 to such fragments.
