009                                                                             A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose Amortized Posterior Sampling (APS), a novel variational inference
012            approach for efficient posterior sampling in inverse problems. Our method trains
013            a conditional flow model to minimize the divergence between the variational dis-
014            tribution and the posterior distribution implicitly defined by the diffusion model.
015            This results in a powerful, amortized sampler capable of generating diverse poste-
016            rior samples with a single neural function evaluation, generalizing across various
017
               measurements. Unlike existing methods, our approach is unsupervised, requires
               no paired training data, and is applicable to both Euclidean and non-Euclidean
018
               domains. We demonstrate its effectiveness on a range of tasks, including image
019
               restoration, manifold signal reconstruction, and climate data imputation. APS
020            significantly outperforms existing approaches in computational efficiency while
021            maintaining competitive reconstruction quality, enabling real-time, high-quality
022            solutions to inverse problems across diverse domains.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   We consider the following inverse problem
028                   \label {eq:ip} \y = \Ac (\x ) + \n ,\quad \y \in \Rd ^m,\,\x \in \Rd ^n,\,\Ac :\Rd ^n \mapsto \Rd ^m,\,\nb \sim \Nc (0,\sigma _y^2\Ib ),   (1)
029
      where the goal is to infer an unknown signal x from the degraded measurement y obtained through
030   some forward operator A, leveraging the information contained in the measurement and the prior
031   p(x). A powerful modern way to define the prior is through diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020;
032   Song et al., 2021c), where we train a parametrized model sθ to estimate the gradient of the log prior
033   ∇x log p(x).
034
      Solving inverse problems with the diffusion model can be achieved through posterior sampling
035
      with Bayesian inference. Arguably the standard way to achieve this is through modifying the re-
036   verse diffusion process of diffusion models (Daras et al., 2024). This adjustment shifts the focus
037   from sampling from the trained prior distribution pθ (x0 ) to sampling from the posterior distribu-
038   tion pθ (x0 |y). This transition is facilitated by employing iterative projections to the measurement
039   subspace (Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2021; Song et al., 2021c; Chung et al., 2022b; Wang et al.,
040   2023), guiding the samples through gradients pointing towards measurement consistency (Chung
041   et al., 2023a; Song et al., 2023a). It should be noted that diffusion models learn the gradient of the
042   prior, and diffusion samplers (Song et al., 2021a; Lu et al., 2022; Song et al., 2021c) are methods that
043   numerically solve the probability-flow ODE (PF-ODE) that defines the reverse diffusion sampling
044
      trajectory. Consequently, regardless of the specifics of the methods, standard diffusion model-based
      inverse problem solvers (DIS), even those that are considered fast, take at least a few tens of neural
045
      function evaluation (NFE), making them less effective for time-critical applications such as medical
046
      imaging and computational photography.
047
048   Another class of methods (Feng et al., 2023; Feng & Bouman, 2023) introduces the use of variational
049   inference (VI) to train a new proposal distribution qϕy (x) to distill the prior learned through the pre-
050   trained diffusion model. The problem is defined as the following optimization problem
051                                                          \label {eq:vi} \min _\phi D_{KL}(q_\phi ^\y (\x _0) || p_\theta (\x _0|\y )),                       (2)
052
053   where the superscript y emphasizes that the proposal distribution is specific for a single measure-
      ment y. For tractable optimization, q is often taken to be a normalizing flow (Rezende & Mohamed,

      2.1   S CORE - BASED DIFFUSION MODELS
151
152
      We adopt the standard framework for constructing a continuous diffusion process x(t), where t ∈
153   [0, T ] and x(t) ∈ Rd , as outlined by Song et al. (2021c). Specifically, our goal is to initialize x(0)
154   from a distribution p0 (x) = pdata , and evolve x(t) towards a reference distribution pT at time T ,
155   which is easy to sample from.
156
      The evolution of x(t) is governed by the Itô stochastic differential equation (SDE):
157
158                                          \label {eq:forward-sde} d\x = \f (\x , t)dt + g(t)d\w ,      (3)
159
160   where f : Rd × R → Rd represents the drift function, and g : R → Rd denotes the diffusion
161   coefficient. These coefficients are designed to drive x(t) towards a spherical Gaussian distribution
      as t approaches T . When the drift function f (x, t) is affine, the perturbation kernel p0t (x(t)|x(0))

162
      is Gaussian, allowing for the parameters to be determined analytically. This facilitates data pertur-
163   bation via p0t (x(t)|x(0)) efficiently, without necessitating computations through a neural network.
164
165   Furthermore, corresponding to the forward SDE, there exists a reverse-time SDE:
166                                                \label {eq:reverse-sde} d\xb &= [\fb (\xb , t) - g(t)^2 \nabla _\xb \log p_t(\xb )]dt + g(t) d\bar {\wb },                                               (4)
167
      where dt represents an infinitesimal negative time step, and w̄ denotes the backward standard
168
      Brownian motion. While the trajectory of Eq. (4) is stochastic, there also exists a corresponding
169
      probability-flow ODE (PF-ODE) that recovers the same law pt (x) as the time progresses (Song
170   et al., 2021c;a)
171
172                                                              \label {eq:pf-ode} d\xb &= [\fb (\xb , t) - \frac {g(t)^2}{2} \nabla _\xb \log p_t(\xb )]dt.                                               (5)
173
174   This allows a deterministic mapping between the reference and the target distribution, and hence
175   diffusion models can also be seen as a neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018).
176
      A neural network can be trained to approximate the true score function ∇x log pt (x) through score
177   matching techniques, as demonstrated in previous works (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021c).
178   This approximation, denoted sθ (x, t) ≈ ∇x log pt (x), is then utilized to numerically integrate the
179   reverse-time SDE. To effectively train the score function, denoising score matching (DSM) is often
180   employed (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005)
181
                   \label {eq:dsm} \theta ^* = \argmin _\theta \Ed _{t \sim U(\varepsilon , 1), \x (t), \x (0)}\left [\|\s _\theta (\x (t), t) - \nabla _{\x _t}\log p_{0t}(\x (t)|\x (0))\|_2^2\right ],   (6)

184   formula (Efron, 2011). Specifically, for p(xt |x0 ) = N (xt ; αt x0 , βt2 I),

189   2.2   D IFFUSION MODELS FOR INVERSE PROBLEMS (DIS)
190
      Solving the reverse SDE in Eq. (4) or the PF-ODE in Eq. (5) results in sampling from the prior
191   distribution pθ (x0 ), with the subscript emphasizing the time variable in the diffusion model context
192   x0 ≡ x. When solving an inverse problem as posed in Eq. (1), our goal is to sample from the
193   posterior pθ (x0 |y) ∝ pθ (x)p(y|x0 ). Using Bayes rule for a general timestep t yields
194
                                            \label {eq:bayes} \nabla _{\x _t} \log p_\theta (\x _t|\y ) = \nabla _{\x _t} \log p_\theta (\x _t) + \nabla _{\x _t} \log p(\y |\x _t).                        (8)
195
196   While the former term can be replaced with a pre-trained diffusion model, the latter term is in-
197   tractable and needs some form of approximation. Existing DIS (Kawar et al., 2022; Chung et al.,
198   2023a; Wang et al., 2023) propose different approximations for ∇xt log p(y|xt ), which yields sam-
199   pling from slightly different posteriors ∇xt log pθ (xt |y).
200   Algorithmically, the posterior samplers are often implemented so that the original numerical solver
201   for sampling from the prior distribution remains intact, while modifying the Tweedie denoised esti-
202   mate at each time x̂θ0|t to satisfy the measurement condition given as Eq. (1). From Tweedie’s for-
203   mula, we can see that this corresponds to approximating the conditional posterior mean E[x0 |xt , y]
204   in the place of the unconditional counterpart E[x0 |xt ]. The algorithms are inherently itera-
205   tive, and the modern solvers (Chung et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) require
206   at least 50 NFE to yield a high-quality sample. Moreover, as existing methods can be inter-
207   preted as approximating the reverse distribution p(x0 |xt ) with a simplistic Gaussian distribution
208   q(x0 |xt ) = N (x0 ; x̂θ0|t , s2t I) (Peng et al., 2024), it typically yields a large approximation error,
209   especially in the earlier steps of the reverse diffusion.

      3.2   D ISTILLATION OF THE DIFFUSION PRIOR
252
253
254   Our method involves distillation of the diffusion prior into a student deep neural network, in our
255
      case an NF model. Particularly, it involves evaluating the output of the model by checking the
      denoising loss gradients from the pre-trained diffusion model. This idea is closely related to variants
256
      of score distillation sampling (SDS) (Poole et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), where the gradient from
257
      the denoising loss is used to distill the diffusion prior by discarding the score Jacobian. Possibly a
258   closely related work is Diff-instruct (Luo et al., 2024), where the authors propose to train a one-step
259   generative model similar to GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) by distillation of the diffusion prior with
260   VI. By proposing an integral KL divergence (IKL) by considering KL minimization across multiple
261   noise levels across the diffusion, it was shown that SDS-like gradients can be used to effectively
262   train a new generative model. While having similarities, our method directly minimizes the KL
263   divergence and does not require dropping the score Jacobian.
264   Orthogonal to the score distillation approaches, there have been recent efforts to train a student net-
265   work to emulate the PF-ODE trajectory itself (Song et al., 2023b; Gu et al., 2023) with a single
266   NFE, one of the most prominent directions being consistency distillation (CD) (Song et al., 2023b).
267   While promising, the performance of CM is upper-bounded by the teacher PF-ODE. Thus, in order
268   to leverage CD-type approaches for diffusion posterior sampling, one has to choose one of the ap-
269   proximations of DIS as its teacher model. In this regard, applying CD for diffusion inverse problem
      solving is inherently limited.

      4.2   A RCHITECTURE
325
326   It has been demonstrated in (Lugmayr et al., 2020) that Conditional NFs are capable of learning
327   distributions on the ambient space that are constrained on measurement. To achieve an architecture
328   with invertible transformations, we extend RealNVP (Dinh et al., 2016) architecture to the con-
329   ditional settings by borrowing insight from (Sun & Bouman, 2021). In its plain form, RealNVP
330   architecture mainly consists of Flow steps, each containing two Affine Coupling layers. In each
331   affine coupling layer, input signal x is split into two parts: xa which stays unchanged, and xb which
332
      is fed into the neural network. In order to invoke the condition, we simply concatenate the condition-
      ing input y to the xb as these layers serve as the main and basic building blocks of entire invertible
333
      architecture. This seemingly simple integration led to very promising results in both Euclidean and
334
      non-Euclidean geometries as will be depicted in Section 5.
335
336
      4.3   M ANIFOLD
337
338   Many real-world datasets, particularly in environmental science, naturally reside on non-Euclidean
339   geometries, making inverse problems challenging. Our work extends conditional normalizing flows
340   (CNFs) to distributions on non-Euclidean manifolds, enabling direct solving of inverse problems on
341   these surfaces without additional rendering steps. We represent manifold data as point clouds of size
342   V ×C, where V is the number of vertices in the mesh discretization and C is the dimension of signal
343   features. By leveraging the expressive power of CNFs, our approach captures the intrinsic geometry
344   and structure of manifold data while enabling efficient inference and sampling. Our framework can
345
      handle complex geometries and severe masking levels across different manifolds, as demonstrated
      in our experiments with noisy inpainting and imputation tasks (see Section 5).
346

      6   D ISCUSSION
514
515
      We show a first proof of concept that we can construct a one-step posterior sampler that generalizes
516
      across any measurements in an unsupervised fashion (only having access to the measurements y).
517   Notably, APS extends to wide use cases with minimal constraints: 1) the operator A can be arbitrar-
518   ily complex and non-linear, as in DPS (Chung et al., 2023a), unlike many recent DIS that requires
519   linearity of the operator (Wang et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023); 2) training of the
520   sampler can be done without any strict conditions on the measurement, unlike recent unsupervised
521   score training methods that require i.i.d. measurement conditions with the same randomized for-
522   ward operator (Daras et al., 2023; Kawar et al., 2023); 3) method can be generalized into different
523   geometries and datasets in a blind manner, unlike recent DIS methods require to know forward op-
524   erator during sampling (Chung et al., 2023b; Mardani et al., 2023). We opted for simplicity in the
525
      architecture design of Gϕ , and avoided introducing inductive bias of spatial information by taking a
      vectorized input, potentially explaining the slight background noise in the reconstructions. Further
526
      optimization in the choice of network architecture is left as a future direction of study.
527
528
529   7   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we propose to use a conditional NF for a VI-based optimization strategy to train a
532   one-step posterior sampler, which implicitly samples from the posterior distribution defined from
533   the pre-trained diffusion prior. We show that APS is highly generalizable, being able to reconstruct
534
      samples that are not seen during training, applicable to diverse forward measurements, and types of
      data, encompassing standard Euclidean geometry as well as data on general Riemannian manifolds.
535
      We believe that our work can act as a cornerstone for developing a fast, practical posterior sampler
536
      that distills the diffusion prior.
