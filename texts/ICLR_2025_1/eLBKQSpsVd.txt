009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Limited availability of multilingual text corpora for training language models often
012             leads to poor performance on downstream tasks due to undertrained representation
013             spaces for languages other than English. This ‘under-representation’ has motivated
014             recent cross-lingual transfer methods to leverage the English representation space
015             by e.g. mixing English and ‘non-English’ tokens at input or extending model
016             parameters to accommodate new languages, which in turn increases computational
017             complexity. To address this, we introduce Fusion for Language Representations
018
                (FLARE) in adapters, a method designed to improve both the representation quality
                and downstream performance for languages other than English. FLARE integrates
019
                source and target language representations within the bottlenecks of low-rank
020
                LoRA adapters using lightweight linear transformations. This maintains parameter
021             efficiency as the method does not require additional parameters, while improving
022             transfer performance, further narrowing the performance gap to English. Further-
023             more, the proposed latent representation fusion does not increase the number of
024             input tokens, this way maintaining computational efficiency. Moreover, FLARE
025             provides flexibility to integrate various types of representations, e.g., we show that
026             it is possible to fuse latent translations extracted from machine translation models.
027             A series of experiments across representative cross-lingual natural language un-
028             derstanding tasks, including natural language inference, question-answering and
029
                sentiment analysis, demonstrate FLARE’s effectiveness, reducing the performance
                gap to English to 8.39% for XLM-R Large and 12.41% for Llama 3 across our
030
                benchmarks, with performance differences averaged over task-specific metrics.1

033   1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Representation degradation for ‘non-English’ languages poses a challenge in the context of pretrained
036   multilingual language models (mPLMs)2 . Large-scale English text corpora are widely available
037   for self-supervised pretraining, resulting in superior representation quality and downstream task
038   performance when compared to low(er)-resource languages (Lauscher et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022).
039   Training mPLMs on massively multilingual text data creates a unified representation space that
040   enables cross-lingual information transfer. Despite the substantial improvements, the imbalance in
041   pretraining resources still substantially reduces downstream performance (Winata et al., 2022).
042   Cross-lingual transfer (termed XLT henceforth) aims to narrow this performance gap by transferring
043   task-specific knowledge acquired in high-resource languages to lower-resource languages (Ruder
044   et al., 2019). Given the dominance of English in pretraining corpora, machine translations (MT)
045   are frequently utilized to avoid processing non-English data (Shi et al., 2010; Artetxe et al., 2020;
046   2023; Ansell et al., 2023). Techniques utilizing source and target language representation spaces
047   include language mixup (Yang et al., 2022), and concatenating multilingual input sequences for
048   in-context XLT (Kim et al., 2024; Tanwar et al., 2023; Cueva et al., 2024). These approaches, while
049
      improving XLT, typically focus on representations in a specific mPLM layer or require extensive
      training and computational resources by extending the input length. Additionally, these typically

088

092

093

094

095   from 13.63% to 12.41%. Further experiments illustrate that computational efficiency can be further

097   versatility of the method, which is orthogonal to the choice of mPLMs and MT systems.

107   Cross-lingual Representation Transfer. Enhancing performance for languages underrepresented in
      the mPLMs’ pretraining data often involves aligning and combining representations from various

108
      languages to facilitate XLT (Oh et al., 2022). By concatenating multilingual input sequences, mPLMs
109   leverage a shared representation space across both source and target language inputs (Kim et al.,
110   2024; Tanwar et al., 2023; Cueva et al., 2024). Techniques such as mixtures of task and language
111   adapters have been implemented to merge language representation spaces effectively (Lee et al.,
112   2022b). In projection-based approaches, target language representations are projected onto a high-
113   resource language (e.g., English), to enhance feature extraction in the high-resource language, before
114   re-projecting back to the target language (Xu et al., 2023). Yang et al. (2022) introduced X-Mixup,
115   combining source and target representations in one specific layer of the mPLM using cross-attention.
116   Building on this concept, Cao et al. (2023) used cross-attention with semantic and token-level
117
      alignment loss terms, aiming to transfer knowledge from the source to the target language. In
      contrast, our fusion method modifies the architecture of bottleneck adapters to combine source and
118
      target language representations. This enables the efficient fusion of multilingual representations
119
      across all transformer layers without adding model parameters, thereby contributing to the stream of
120   parameter-efficient XLT.
121
122   Representation fusion is also applied to integrate information across different modalities (Fang et al.,
123
      2021; Ramnath et al., 2021). For instance, Qu et al. (2024) employed feature routing in cross-modal
      vision-language tasks, guiding language model representations through the LoRA bottleneck using
124
      the last hidden state of a vision model. Our work differs in its scope and fusion methodology:
125
      FLARE extracts significantly richer representations from the source and target languages by capturing
126   layer-wise representations for each transformer block in the mPLMs. Moreover, by ensuring dimen-
127   sional alignment, we perform token-wise representation fusion within adapter bottlenecks, thereby
128   transferring finer-grained information across languages.
129
      PEFT in Multilingual Language Models and Cross-Lingual Transfer. PEFT aims to incorporate
130
      task or language-specific knowledge into mPLMs without updating all model weights (Pfeiffer
131
      et al., 2020). Most prominent techniques include sparse fine-tuning by selectively updating model
132   parameters (Ansell et al., 2022), and inserting adapter modules that reduce trainable parameters
133   to a small fraction of total weights of the underlying mPLM (Houlsby et al., 2019). Furthermore,
134   PEFT modules are composable, and thus information combination from multiple modules is possible
135   (Wang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022b). Bottleneck adapters project model representations into a
136   lower-dimensional space and then back to their original dimensions, creating a bottleneck that
137   regulates information flow (Houlsby et al., 2019). During this adaptation process, the weights of
138   the (m)PLM remain frozen. Following the same assumption that task-specific knowledge can be
139   compressed in a low-dimensional space, low-rank adapters (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) and its more
140
      recent variants (Liu et al., 2024) are widely utilized for fine-tuning language models. They are
      inserted into the attention modules of transformer architectures, maximizing the capacity to adapt
141
      to new task-specific information, while preserving parameter efficiency. In our work, we extend
142
      the task and knowledge acquisition capabilities of these adapters by modifying their architecture
143   to process inputs from multiple languages without increasing the parameter count. This involves
144   sharing parameters, such as the adapter projection layers, across language inputs, enabling the fusion
145   of different language representations within the bottleneck (e.g., as implemented in LoRA).

152   tations enhances cross-lingual knowledge transfer and distills task-relevant

156   as we believe this approach is the most realistic in practice. We contend that transferring information
157   during task fine-tuning is more resource-efficient compared to extensive pretraining on large-scale
158
      self-supervised text corpora.

      A key advantage of representation fusion is the reduction in computational complexity, thereby
217   enhancing parameter efficiency for both task and language adaptation. By processing multilingual
218   inputs separately and only fusing highly compressed representations within adapter bottlenecks, our
219   method avoids the computational overhead associated with quadratic scaling in attention computations
220   for model dimensions d, thus enhancing resource efficiency. Furthermore, the memory requirements
221   are limited to the last hidden states obtained from the output of each transformer block.
222
      Moreover, our fusion approach is agnostic regarding the source language representation. This
223   flexibility allows directly leveraging representations extracted from the MT encoder M as ‘latent
224   translations’
                     for fusion. We propose to extract a single representation from the MT model v T =
225   M x , where v T ∈ Rm×dM , which serves as a latent translation. To ensure compatibility
             T
226   between the dimensionality of the MT encoder outputs and the mPLM, we utilize a single linear
227   projection layer W proj . This projection is trained during the adaptation to the downstream task in
228   the target language, thereby maintaining efficiency. Moreover, projections between different model
229   representation spaces can be enhanced using self-supervised data, as demonstrated in related studies
230   (Liu et al., 2023). Consequently, the up-projected representation v T W proj is fused with the target
231
      language representation within the adapter bottlenecks of each mPLM layer; see Figure 3. This
      FLARE MT method enhances resource efficiency by bypassing a forward pass in the mPLM, which
232
      is required when using discrete text in the source language, and preserves the inherent translation
233
      uncertainty within the embeddings by avoiding discretization in the MT decoder, thus mitigating
234   potential translation errors (Ponti et al., 2021; Unanue et al., 2023).

      To fuse cross-lingual representations in bottleneck adapters, we evaluate both linear and non-linear
240   transformations that do not require additional model parameters, alongside cross-attention. We extract
241   token-wise representations from source and target language sequences, capturing rich contextual
242   information at the token level. Extracting source language and target language representations
243   from the same underlying mPLM ensures matching hidden dimensions d in each transformer layer,
244   facilitating subsequent representation fusion in the low-rank bottleneck adapters.
245

      3.3   T RAINING
271
272   For task adaptation in the target language, we insert LoRA adapters into query and value weight
273   matrices of the mPLM previously fine-tuned on English task data (referred to as the base model). In
274   FLARE, these adapters implement fusion function ϕ that combines source and target language input
275   representations into a single fused representation, as illustrated in Figure 1. Consistent with standard
276   PEFT training, only the task head and LoRA parameters and output layer are trainable, while all other
277   parameters remain frozen.
278   During the forward pass, detailed in Figure 2, representations from both the source and target
279   languages are extracted at each transformer block. Layer-wise source language representations are
280   obtained from the base model and stacked in matrix V S ∈ Rl×m×d , where l represents the number
281   of layers in the mPLM. Target language representations are obtained during the forward pass through
282   the base model with LoRA adapters. In each layer, source and target language representations are
283   transformed and compressed to lower dimensions r ≪ h in the adapter’s down-projection W down .
284
      The shared down-projection layers, applied to both source and target language representations before
      subsequent fusion, reduce the model’s reliance on the English representation space. The final steps
285
      include the application of a fusion function and standard up-projection, as already described in
286
      Sections 3.1 and 3.2.

353   5     R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
354
355   Main Results displayed in Table 1 confirm our hypothesis that task-specific knowledge can be
356   efficiently transferred from English to other languages within adapter bottlenecks. FLARE con-
357   sistently surpasses the zero-shot, translate-test, and translate-train baselines across various tasks,
358
      demonstrating robust performance with machine-translated training data in the target language and
      machine-translated source language data during inference. Moreover, the results from the few-shot
359
      adaptation scenario on NusaX suggest that FLARE does not require extensive labeled task data to
360
      improve downstream performance on lower-resource languages. While input-level fusion shows
361   competitive results on NusaX, FLARE significantly outperforms input-level fusion on XNLI. On
362   average, FLARE outperforms input-level fusion by 1.93%, and 1.75% for XLM-R Large and Llama
363   3, respectively. The results show that input-level fusion replicates English performance, indicating
364   its inability to leverage information from the target language, which highlights a key limitation of
365   this approach (see Table 2). In contrast, FLARE mitigates this issue through parameter sharing in the
366   down-projection of the adapter, ensuring that representations from both source and target languages
367   contribute to the final output. Additionally, fusion functions like add ensure a balanced combination
368   of both source and target language representations. Beyond performance benefits, FLARE reduces
369
      the average training time on XNLI by more than 30% when compared to input-level fusion.
370   Furthermore, FLARE consistently outperforms the X-Mixup baselines by 3.10%, and 3.22% for
371   XLM-R Large and Llama 3, respectively. This indicates that FLARE mitigates the performance
372   variability observed in X-Mixup by fusing compressed source and target language representations
373   within adapters inserted in all transformer layers.

511     6                    C ONCLUSION
512
513    We introduced Fusion for Language Representations (FLARE), a parameter-efficient method for
514    cross-lingual transfer (XLT) that enhances representation quality and downstream performance for
515    languages other than English. Our experimental results demonstrate that FLARE outperforms strong
516    XLT baselines, such as target language fine-tuning with LoRA adapters and input-level fusion,
517    on natural language understanding tasks, effectively narrowing the performance gap with English.
518
       FLARE is robust to lower-quality machine translations, outperforming strong cross-lingual transfer
       baselines. A key takeaway is that FLARE is representation-agnostic, allowing for the direct integration
519
       of latent translations from an MT model in place of translated English text. This further improves
520
       resource-efficiency and enhances knowledge transfer for lower-quality translations.
521
522     Limitations. Our work demonstrates that highly compressed English language representations can be
523     effectively transferred to other languages within adapter bottlenecks. However, our experiments focus
524
        on bilingual transfer settings. Extending fusion adapters to integrate multiple target languages is
        non-trivial, as it requires adapters to extract language-agnostic information across multiple languages.
525
526     The proposed FLARE method by design relies data availability for both source and target languages.
527     Consequently, the performance of FLARE is dependent upon the quality of machine translations, as
528     we also investigated empirically in this work. This dependency poses some significant challenges,
529     particularly for tasks that require precise positional alignment, like extractive question-answering,
530
        where the quality of machine translations affects downstream performance and model applicability.
531     Furthermore, our evaluation exclusively employs English as the high-resource source language for
532     representation fusion. While English is predominantly used in mPLM pretraining corpora, exploring
533     other high-resource languages that share linguistic similarities with the target languages could
534     potentially yield similar or improved cross-lingual transfer performance.
535     Finally, our choice of base multilingual LMs has been motivated by the current state-of-the-art (SotA)
536     in the field of multilingual NLP and XLT to low-resource languages for NLU tasks. The main
537     models are SotA encoder-only (XLM-R) and encoder-decoder mPLMs (mT5), and decoder-only
538     LLM (Llama 3). However, we note that the LLM technology and its adaptation to XLT for NLU in
539     lower-resource languages has not been proven to be fully mature yet Lin et al. (2024); Razumovskaia
        et al. (2024).

834   C    P RACTICAL I MPLICATIONS
835
836   The practical implementation of bilingual cross-lingual transfer methods, such as FLARE, requires an
837   additional step of language identification to determine bilingual adapter for model inference. While
838   this introduces a preprocessing stage, language identification systems are widely accessible and highly
839   accurate. For example, NLLB achieves a 95% F1 score across 193 FLORES languages, including
840   many low-resource languages (Burchell et al., 2023), ensuring that this step can be seamlessly
841   integrated into real-world applications.
842
843   D    A NOTHER A BLATION : R EPRESENTATION F USION DURING T RAINING O NLY
844
845   To investigate the importance of utilizing source language representations during inference, we
846   modified FLARE to restrict representation fusion to the training phase only. Specifically, we limited
847   the fusion with source language representations to 50% of the training instances and excluded source
848   language data during inference. This evaluates cross-lingual transfer capabilities based on instance-
849   independent patterns learned from source language representations during training. Our findings
850   reveal that fusion adapters struggle to learn patterns that are independent of specific instances from
851   source language representations during training. As a result, when implemented in the XLM-R
852
      Large model on the NusaX test set, the performance of the train-only FLARE variant decreased by
      30%. Crucially, this significant drop underscores the importance of incorporating source language
853
      representations during inference to achieve effective cross-lingual adaptation.
