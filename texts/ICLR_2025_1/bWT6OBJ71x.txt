009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The field of mechanistic interpretability has made strides in unraveling models’
012            hidden representations but is often puzzled by why specific representations form.
013            This paper addresses a crucial question on this front: when a neural network can
014            learn multiple distinct representations to solve a task, how does it "choose" among
015            them during training? We suggest that, at initialization, instead of starting from
016            an empty scratchpad, the model’s embedding already contains partially formed
017            representations of varying “completeness.” Models tend to develop a representation
018            that is more “complete” at initialization, disregarding less complete alternatives.
019            We empirically examine this hypothesis on algorithmic toy models with clearly
020
               defined final representations from which we can elicit an interpretable signal to
               evaluate such “completeness” of possible representations in the initial embedding.
021
               We find that the representations with high initial signals are chosen by the model
022
               with high probability, a pattern consistent across models with a single learned
023            representation (remainder equivalence, multi-digit XOR) and with multiple, re-
024            dundant representations (modular addition). Finally, we investigate the role of
025            embedding dimensionality on model’s representation and their “completeness.”
026            Our results with toy models show that the seemingly chaotic initialization contains
027            many interpretable patterns to understand the training dynamics of representations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      The field of mechanistic interpretability attempts to reverse-engineer the algorithms that neural
      networks learn. This involves understanding the representations (features) networks learn (Bricken
034
      et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2024; Zou et al., 2023) and how these
035
      play a role in larger circuits (Elhage et al., 2021; Nanda et al., 2023; Marks et al., 2024). While
036   most such work studies networks as static objects, some have recently begun to study how network
037   representations and circuits form over training (Liu et al., 2022; Olsson et al., 2022; Hoogland et al.,
038   2024; Chen et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024). By studying training dynamics, we hope to understand
039   not just what neural networks learn, but how and ultimately why they learn the algorithms they do.
040   This understanding may eventually be useful for training models with the properties we desire, such
041   as improved efficiency and safety, through careful design of the training process.
042   One broad question in mechanistic interpretability regards universality (Olah et al., 2020): can models
043   consistently learn the same algorithms across different seeds and scales? While some works have
044   found evidence of universality (Olsson et al., 2022; Gould et al., 2024; Gurnee et al., 2024), in other
045   cases, there seems to be some variability in algorithms and representations that networks learn to
046   solve particular tasks (Zhong et al., 2023; McCoy et al., 2019; Lampinen et al., 2024). In this work,
047   we ask: when networks have a choice between different representations, how do they choose which
048   to learn?
049   Investigating toy models trained on algorithmic tasks, we can visualize the structure of the final
050   representation forming very early in training using simple tools like PCA, especially under large
051   weight decay. This structure also varies due to different training runs. To understand the reason behind
052   this diversity of representations, we wonder if such structure of the final representation already exists
053   in the seemingly chaotic initialization, albeit not obvious to observe through simple visualization. To
      formalize this intuition, we propose the following hypothesis:

054
      Representation “Completeness” Hypothesis The random embedding initialization already contains
055   many partially formed representations at different levels of "completeness," and the final representa-
056   tion(s) the model learns are those with high "completeness" at initialization. We define "completeness"
057   as an interpretable similarity metric that compares such a partially formed representation to its pro-
058   jected, idealized representation, as if it were to be learned by the model.
059
      We empirically evaluate this hypothesis on three algorithmic tasks: remainder equivalence, multi-digit
060
      XOR, and modular addition. We analyze these tasks because they have well-defined representations
061   in mathematics. We analyze the first two tasks in §3 as a proof of concept for our hypothesis because
062   the models trained on these tasks are simple and learn a single final representation. In §4, we delve
063   deeper into the more interesting case of modular addition, as it is a harder task, and the model
064   learns multiple, redundant representations with complicated mechanisms operating on the Fourier
065   space studied extensively by the interpretability community (Liu et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023;
066   Zhong et al., 2023; Morwani et al., 2024). We find, in all three cases, we can find an interpretable
067   and intuitive "completeness" metric that projects the structure of the initialization onto the final
068   representation, where high initial “completeness” is a good indicator of this representation being
069
      learned by the model.
070   Lastly, we investigate how embedding dimensionality affects representations in §5. We show that,
071   as the embedding dimension increases, there are fewer representations with low “completeness” in
072   the initialization. In this case, the model can achieve lower test loss when we freeze the embedding
073   at initialization and train only the MLP to fit all the partially complete representations. This helps
074   explain why representation learning could benefit from large embedding dimensions.
075   Random initialization has been a subject of continuous studies, such as their general downstream
076   capabilities (Amid et al., 2022; Jarrett et al., 2009; Zhong & Andreas, 2024) and the “Lottery Ticket
077   Hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019),” where sparse subnetworks pruned at initialization achieve
078   similar capabilities. However, our study takes a first step towards understanding how interpretable
079   patterns for representations are structured in initialization and how they can evolve to be significant
080   in the final embedding. This understanding is not only driven by scientific curiosity but can also lead
081
      to practical benefits, such as speeding up training in our toy algorithmic tasks. We acknowledge that
      the extension of this approach to non-algorithmic tasks is non-trivial and hence left for future work,
082
      but it is encouraging to see that an intuitive understanding of representation dynamics is feasible and
083
      such understanding may potentially lead to algorithmic advancement.

088   2.1   TASK S ELECTION
089
090   Understanding how a model chooses a certain representation is difficult since representations, in
091   general, are high-dimensional and difficult to disentangle, let alone understanding the dynamics
092
      between multiple representations. Therefore, we resort to studying toy models, which might provide
      us with insights into more general models. We specifically need to study models that meet the
093
      following criteria: (1) the model has clearly interpretable representation(s) in its embedding, and (2)
094
      the model can form non-trivially distinct representations when trained with different initializations.
095   We find three algorithmic tasks that meet such criteria:
096
097   Remainder equivalence 1[a = b mod m], where a, b = 0, 1, . . . , p − 1, a number much larger
098
      than m. The final representation clusters numbers with the same remainder together and forms a line
      in the embedding, but how the clusters are ordered on the line is up to p! permutations.
099
100   Multi-digit XOR a ⊕ b = c. the representation forms a hypercube that each pair of opposite faces
101   have the numbers differ by exactly one bit. However, the exact arrangement of tokens in the hypercube
102   may vary.
103   Modular addition a + b = c (mod p), where a, b, c = 0, 1, . . . , p − 1. Prior works have shown
104   circular representations in the embedding, akin to how numbers are arranged around a clock (Power
105   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; 2023; Nanda et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023), and that the model learns
106   a few such circles that nicely correspond to Fourier frequencies up to p (Nanda et al., 2023). Over
107   different training runs, different sets of 3-5 frequencies form the final representation, despite all
      frequencies having a chance to become the final representation.

      2.2     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
109
110   We use a fixed model architecture to study all three tasks. Specifically, our model consists of an
111   embedding matrix WE and a two-layer MLP. The embedding matrix has size (p, d), where p is the
112   vocabulary size and d is the embedding dimension. Every integer t ∈ {0, 1, . . . , p − 1} is treated as
113   a token and has an associated embedding vector Et ∈ Rd . For each task, the model tokenizes the
114   two inputs a and b, concatenates their embeddings, and feeds [Ea , Eb ] ∈ R2d to the MLP, which
115   produces a categorical output c. Note that we set weight decay to be high in these models to ensure
116
      the model quickly forms the final representation.
117   As our study focuses on the embedding initialization, there are many other factors that influence the
118   representation features, such as initialization of the MLP and dataset training split (Lampinen et al.,
119   2024). Therefore, for our analysis in this paper, we aggregate training results over different random
120   initializations of embeddings, MLPs, and datasets and report the mean and standard deviation.

353   4.4   R ELATION TO L OTTERY T ICKET H YPOTHESIS
354
355   Our analysis can be related to the Lottery Tickets Hypothesis (LTH) (Frankle & Carbin, 2019),
356   where some subnetworks are “winning tickets” that achieve comparable test accuracy to the original
357   network when trained in isolation. Our results suggest that partially formed, decomposable circular
358   representations already exist even at initialization, similar to “winning tickets.” Our analysis differs
359   from LTH in two key ways: (1) LTH requires training and pruning to identify “winning” tickets,
360
      whereas circles are mathematically defined without training (but specific to modular addition); (2)
      LTH states only the existence of “winning” tickets at initialization, while we manage to characterize
361
      the properties of “winning” circles, that, when trained in isolation, achieve comparable accuracy (see
362
      Appendix G). Our work provides representation-level insights into the studies of LTH.
363
364
      Faster Training Convergence As suggested by Zhang et al. (2021), the pruned “winning ticket”
365
      has faster learning convergence, we conduct an ablation experiment in which we keep all frequencies
366   with initial norms one standard deviation above the mean and remove the rest. We train them in
367   separation; given that circles with high initial norms are more likely to be learned, this initialization
368   method significantly accelerates training while still achieving perfect test accuracy. We train the
369   models using 100 random seeds and demonstrate in Figure 6(d) a significant decrease in convergence
370   time, measured by the first time step at which the test accuracy reaches 100%.

      Mechanistic Interpretability on Algorithmic Tasks Many studies have been done to reverse
480   engineer how neural networks implement algorithmic tasks (Gromov, 2023; Chughtai et al., 2023;
481   Stander et al., 2023; He et al., 2024) because these tasks are mathematically well-defined and simple.
482   However, even on these toy tasks, neural networks display some intriguing phenomena, including
483   phase changes during training (Nanda et al., 2023), different algorithms (Zhong et al., 2023; Liao
484   et al., 2023), or demonstrate the existence of multiple copies of algorithms (Nanda et al., 2023; Zhong
485   et al., 2023). These works on toy models have also extended to similar findings in language models,
      such as how they compute addition (Zhou et al., 2024; Quirke & Barez, 2024).

      Representation Learning and Variability Representation learning is crucial for networks to
487   generalize (Huh et al., 2024; Bengio et al., 2013; Le-Khac et al., 2020; Zou et al., 2023), as paradigms
488   for eliciting better representations get developed (Zhou, 2018; Jaiswal et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020).
489   Meanwhile, studies have found variability to the learned representations due to random initialization
490   seeds, dataset orders, and representation complexity, leading to downstream consequences (McCoy
491   et al., 2019; Lampinen et al., 2024). On the flipside, prior works have also shown that a single model
492   can contain redundant representations and algorithms (Doimo et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024). Most
493   recent studies have begun to form understandings of representations at a mechanistic level (Li et al.,
494   2023; Engels et al., 2024; Marks & Tegmark, 2023).
495
496   Extracting Patterns from Random Initializations Prior work has investigated the capabilities of
497   randomly initialized, untrained networks, particularly convolutional networks, showing that convolu-
498
      tional networks can extract features (Amid et al., 2022; Burda et al., 2018) and achieve comparable
      downstream performance when linearly combining these random features (Jarrett et al., 2009; Saxe
499
      et al., 2011; Cox & Pinto, 2011). More recently, Zhong & Andreas (2024) evaluated the algorithmic
500
      capabilities of random transformers, training only the embeddings and fixing everything else. The
501   study shows that random transformers operate in low-dimensional spaces, which corroborates our
502   findings that a “completeness” metric mapping random initialization to a low-dimensional score can
503   explain the model’s choice of learned representation.
504
505   Lottery Ticket Hypothesis The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) posits that
506   some subnetworks–“winning tickets”–identified at initialization and trained in isolation can match the
507   test accuracy of the original, dense network. It has inspired extensions, such as a stronger conjecture
508   on finding such subnetworks without training (Zhou et al., 2019; Ramanujan et al., 2020; Malach et al.,
509   2020; da Cunha et al., 2022; Orseau et al., 2020; Pensia et al., 2020; Diffenderfer & Kailkhura, 2021),
510   transferring winning tickets across setups (Morcos et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021), and improving
511
      methods of pruning to find the subnetworks (Frankle et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2019; Frankle et al., 2021;
      Wang et al., 2020; Tanaka et al., 2020). Our work follows a similar style of analysis as the LTH, but
512
      our methods of extracting the “winning tickets” are different. While the LTH community focuses on
513
      pruning, we propose interpretable (but representation-specific) properties for these partially formed
514   “winning tickets” that allow them to eventually become the learned representations.
515
516
517   7    C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we propose a Representation “Completeness” Hypothesis that final representations
520   learned by the model often have certain intuitive, interpretable properties at initialization, which
521
      make them more "complete" and closer to the ideal representation than other structures not chosen
      by the model. We verify this hypothesis on three tasks, multi-digit XOR, remainder equivalence,
522
      and modular addition, and define such “completeness” metrics on each of the tasks. Finally, we
523
      attribute the reason for larger embedding’s better ability to generalize to the decrease in ill-formed
524   representations that take training for the model to unlearn, shown by an increase to the lower bound
525   in the “completeness” metrics.
526
527   Limitations We have focused on studying algorithmic toy models, as our work is made possible
528   due to their mathematically well-defined representations that can be clearly disentangled. Significant
529   additional work is needed to scale these analyses to even more complex, general models, which
530   remain a challenge.
531
532   Broader Impact Understanding training dynamics allows us to better control neural networks
533   in ways we desire, such as making them more accurate and safe. Any dual-use technology, like
534   machine learning models, has accompanying risks, so one should exercise caution when deploying
535   these techniques.

1070   on the embedding through backpropagation and weight decay of the initial data. To understand
1071   the effect of the former on frequency signals, we use the same Fourier transform procedure to
