011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Metric learning is often applied in scenarios where labels are well-defined or
014            where there is a ground truth for semantic similarity between data points. How-
015
               ever, in expert domains such as medical data, where experts perceive features
               and similarities differently on an individual basis, modeling psychological em-
016
               beddings at the individual level can be beneficial. Such embeddings can pre-
017
               dict factors that influence behavior, such as individual uncertainty, and support
018            personalized learning strategies. Despite this potential, the amount of person-
019            specific behavioral data that can be collected through similarity behavior sam-
020            pling is insufficient in most scenarios, making modeling individual cognitive em-
021            beddings challenging and underexplored. In this study, we proposed integrat-
022            ing supervised learning on small-scale similarity sampling data with unsupervised
023            autoencoder-based manifold learning to approximate person-specific psychologi-
024            cal embeddings with significantly improved similarity inference performance. We
025            conducted a large-scale experiment with 121 clinical physicians, measured their
026
               cognitive similarities using medical image data, and implemented person-specific
               models. Our results demonstrate that even in complex expert domains, such as
027
               medical imaging, where cognitive similarity varies between individuals, person-
028
               specific psychological embeddings can be effectively approximated using limited
029            behavioral data.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Deep machine learning (ML) models can provide abstract-level information regarding the similarity
035   between data through embedding (Liu et al., 2020b; Bengio et al., 2013; Mikolov et al., 2013). For
036   instance, samples positioned close together in the embedding space can be interpreted as semanti-
037   cally similar, whereas those that are far apart are different. However, the actual similarity between
038   the samples may not always be reflected in the learned embedding of the model. Metric learning is a
039
      method in which a model learns a function of the actual similarities and differences between samples
      and fits this function to low-dimensional embedding. In this context, the ’actual similarity’ refers
040
      to a conventional metric that is defined externally. For example, in labeled datasets, a clear metric
041
      states that data points with the same label should be closer to each other than those with different
042   labels. Even in the absence of labels, many datasets have commonly accepted metrics, such as the
043   general perception that dogs are more similar to cats than snakes. Most metric-learning approaches
044   operate under such conditions; thus, sufficient training data are available.
045
      By contrast, we introduce a specialized metric learning problem that approximates individual-level
046
      psychological (cognitive) embeddings in scenarios where there are significant differences in similar-
047
      ity metrics depending on the individual (Schroff et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2017; Hosseini et al., 2018;
048   Luo et al., 2003). This issue is particularly relevant in expert-driven fields such as medical data,
049   where interpretations often differ. For example, while chest X-ray (CXR) images are structurally
050   simple, their interpretation is highly complex (Delrue et al., 2011; Pham et al., 2021). Even among
051   experienced physicians, the cognitive similarities or diagnoses of CXR images can vary widely (see
052   Section 3.3) (Krupinski, 2010). Moreover, because labels are defined using partial data features,
053   they may not align with the similarities assessed from a holistic perspective. For example, in CXR
      data, it may be necessary to differentiate between ‘Male’ and ‘Female,’ while also distinguishing be-

      This scenario is important because approximating individual-level cognitive metrics with psycho-

078   Personalized learning strategies can be developed by identifying areas with high uncertainty be-

      Therefore, it is important to attempt to approximate cognitive embedding at the individual level;
083
      however, such attempts are rare. The biggest challenge lies in the inherent noise and difficulty
084   of obtaining sufficient behavioral sampling for individual-level psychological embedding model-
085   ing (Molenaar & Campbell, 2009). In practical scenarios, modeling individual-level psychological
086   embeddings requires overcoming the issue of insufficient behavioral sampling. As an alternative,
087   we demonstrated that by integrating a loss function that learns from behavioral sampling data col-
088   lected from individuals into an autoencoder-based framework, it is possible to synergistically achieve
089   person-specific psychological embedding modeling that represents cognitive similarity, even with a
090   limited amount of training data.
091   Our objective is not to improve metric learning algorithms or optimize models but to propose and
092   experimentally validate a practical approach for applying metric learning to individual embedding
093   learning. Specifically, we conducted a first-ever behavioral sampling experiment to measure the cog-
094   nitive similarity of actual CXR images with 121 clinical physicians, focusing on realistic scenarios.
095   After confirming significant variations in the cognitive similarity patterns across individuals, we im-
096   plemented autoencoder-based models to represent each person’s similarity metric at the embedding
097   level (Fig. 1). The performance of the model was evaluated using individual behavioral data, and
098
      the robustness of our hypothesis was validated through ablation studies and simulations.
099   The key contributions of our study are as follows:
100
      1) This is the first expert-based experimental study to model individual-level psychological embed-
101
      dings, demonstrating the applicability of our approach to a realistic scenario using actual clinical
102   physicians and medical data. 2) We showed that autoencoders can synergistically complement the
103   limitations of cognitive similarity sampling in individual-level cognitive similarity approximations.
104   (Proposing a new application scenario for autoencoders) Additionally, we empirically demonstrated
105   the utility of the variable triplet loss, which was proposed to learn the psychological embeddings in
106   the bottleneck layer of the autoencoder. 3) The experiment, which involved 121 physicians and med-
107   ical imaging data, required considerable time and effort. The raw experimental data will contribute
      to research in fields such as human–AI collaboration.

110   2.1   M ETRIC LEARNING
111
112   This study is closely related to metric learning in ML, which involves training models to learn the
113   similarity between samples (Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Xing et al., 2002; Weinberger et al., 2005).
114   Early algorithms focused on discriminating similar and dissimilar samples or with pre-defined met-
115   rics (Aherne et al., 1998; Elgammal et al., 2003). Recent approaches aims to learn the distance
116   function in the embedding space of the model, representing similar and dissimilar samples as close
117
      and distant, respectively. For instance, selecting a reference image (anchor) among three data points
      helps determine which one is closer or farther from the reference, referred to as triplets (Hoffer
118
      & Ailon, 2015; Wang et al., 2017; Le-Khac et al., 2020; Ge, 2018; Hoffer & Ailon, 2015; Kim
119
      et al., 2020). Consequently, the triplet loss that reflects the conditions of these triplets in embedding
120   space can be defined. It increases the distance between negative pairs more than the positive pairs.
121   From the perspective of metric learning, the goal of our work is not to improve existing algorithms
122   but rather to apply metric learning to model person-specific psychological metrics, which are not
123   defined externally. This distinguishes our work from other studies.
124
125   2.2   P ERSON - SPECIFIC COGNITIVE SIMILARITY MODELING
126
127   Human inference operates through cognitive mechanisms that can be conceptualized as a hypo-
128   thetical representational space, analogous to embeddings in machine learning models. In cognitive
129
      science, this representational framework is referred to as a psychological embedding. According to
      the theory of similarity-representation duality, embedding models trained on an individual’s cogni-
130
      tive similarity metrics can concurrently encapsulate their cognitive features (Roads & Love, 2024).
131
      This framework suggests that similarity metrics specific to an individual can uncover the relative
132   weighting of features involved in their perceptual processing of objects. By harnessing these in-
133   sights, personalized embedding models have the potential to identify optimal learning domains and
134   highlight knowledge deficits in experts (Cha & Lee, 2021).
135
      Previous research in cognitive science has investigated tasks pertinent to the development of indi-
136
      vidualized embeddings. These efforts include systematic evaluations of human perceptual charac-
137
      teristics (Zhang et al., 2018), fine-tuning neural networks to enhance predictions of human similarity
138   judgments (Tarigopula et al., 2023), and integrating neurological signals with cognitive embeddings
139   (Palazzo et al., 2020). Although substantial progress has been achieved in collecting behavioral data
140   for psychological embeddings across diverse stimuli (Hebart et al., 2020; Nosofsky et al., 2018;
141   Wilber et al., 2014), relatively few studies have addressed the technical challenges associated with
142   insufficient sampling for individual-level modeling. To address this gap, our study investigates the
143   potential of leveraging unsupervised learning techniques, specifically autoencoders, to amplify lim-
144   ited person-specific similarity information.
145   In contrast to conventional metric learning frameworks that rely on predefined, consensus-based
146   similarity metrics, person-specific metric learning becomes particularly valuable in contexts such as
147   expert-driven domains, where cognitive similarity metrics exhibit significant inter-individual varia-
148   tion. Nevertheless, most prior study in this area has relied on benchmark datasets characterized by
149   relatively minor differences in individual similarity perceptions (Peterson et al., 2018). Our work
150   presents a novel contribution by validating the feasibility of individualized embedding modeling in
151   real-world, professional datasets such as medical imaging, thereby bridging theoretical cognitive
152
      science and practical expert applications.

      2.3   AUTOENCODER AND MANIFOLD LEARNING
155

156   encoder and decoder by minimizing the reconstruction loss of the decoder for the input data (Be-

159   ifold theory, training an autoencoder is equivalent to determining the parameters of a data manifold
160   (Lempitsky, 2019; Lu et al., 2019). However, manifold structures are not always singular and it is
161   common for data to belong to multiple manifolds (Hettiarachchi & Peters, 2015). Although the ro-
      bustness of autoencoders has been demonstrated in numerous studies, explaining the local structure

      3     A PPROACH
185
      3.1    C OLLECTING SIMILARITY JUDGEMENTS
186
187   Sampling behavioral data for person-specific modeling may entail several limitations. First, behav-
188   ioral data from a subject can typically only be used to train an individual model for that subject.
189   Considering the laborious nature of behavioral sampling, the collection of triplets can incur high
190   costs. Consequently, the amount of behavioral data that can be gathered from a single individual
191   may be limited. Second, although the dimensionality of the embedding space is lower compared
192   to the stimulus (i.e., raw images) space, it remains high-dimensional, posing challenges in case of
193   intuitive handling (Roads & Love, 2024). This dimensionality can introduce significant noise when
194
      measuring similarity between data points. We implemented a triangular measurement framework
      treating each data point composing a triplet as an independent anchor to collect efficient behavioral
195
      data with minimal sampling and low uncertainty. Considering three data points, the selection of
196
      one of them as the anchor forms a unmeasured triplet T = (xa , x1 , x2 ), where xa denotes the
197   anchor point, and x1 and x2 denote the other points, respectively. When the subject s selects a
198   data point that is either more similar or dissimilar to xa from x1 and x2 , the T is expressed as the
199   ‘measured triplet’ T s = (xa , xc , xd ), where xc and xd denote the close and distant points, respec-
200   tively, based on the behavioral measurements of the subject s. Therefore, each measured triplet has
201   two possible labels. Excluding the anchor, the remaining two images in a triplet are labeled as one
202   close (“close” sample) and one relatively distant (“distant” sample). In our measurement procedure,
203   three images were presented to the participant without specifying any anchors. Subsequently, the
204   subject arranged the positions of these images in a scalene triangle shape, reflecting the degree of
205
      closeness or distance between each pair of images. Upon fixing the anchor image, a triplet was
      automatically determined with the remaining two images. Therefore, with three images presented
206
      in one instance, three triplets were collected. Thus, considering three data points sampled from the
207
      entire dataset without duplication m times, m measurement experiment instances can be conducted,
208   thereby yielding 3m triplets. Although the triplets obtained from a single instance may be inter-
209   dependent, this approach could regularize subject response by reducing the degree of freedom in
210   representing cognitive distances between data points. Moreover it facilitates the collection of three
211   sets of behavioral data from a single experiment, thereby enhancing sampling efficiency.

218   we empirically designed the embedding model architecture based on convolutional autoencoder

       3.5                 Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS
 286
 287   The performance of the embedding model can be qualitatively evaluated by comparing the loca-
 288   tions of the top n pixels predicted by the model to influence similarity judgments with the n pixels
 289   identified by experienced clinicians. The annotation process of the model begins by selecting a ref-
 290   erence unit with the highest variance among the embedding outputs of a separate reference dataset.
 291   Once the reference unit is determined, uniform noise is iteratively added to each pixel of the test
 292
       image, which is then input into the model to compute the variance in the reference unit’s output.
       This process is conducted individually for each pixel (Sec. A.6). Subsequently, the top n pixels that
 293
       caused the greatest variance in the reference unit’s output due to the added noise are identified and
 294
       compared with the pixels annotated by expert clinicians.

384   The best model architecture designed herein comprised an encoder with four convolutional layers,

386

461
      We showed that the embeddings of the original model could be reconstructed in preserved similarity
462
      even in simulations wherein the subjects were replaced with ML models. We sampled 100 images

      to the number of triplet samples for model training (Fig. 6 (b)). Thus, this implies increasing human

504   5   L IMITATION
505
506   While pioneering person-specific similarity-based cognitive embedding, this study faces limitations
507   inherent to human behavioral experiments, such as uncertainty in similarity judgments and interde-
508   pendent measurements. Future studies should adopt systematic experimental designs to address this
509
      noise. Additionally, our experiments focused on a limited set of neural network architectures and
      did not explore optimal hyperparameter tuning or provide theoretical proof for the hypothesis that
510
      person-specific cognitive similarity can guide autoencoder manifold learning.

513   6   C ONCLUSION
514
515   This study proposed an autoencoder-based person-specific embedding modeling framework that ap-
516   proximated cognitive similarities between subjects in CXR data and conducted a large-scale behav-
517   ioral experiment with clinical physicians. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first such study
518   attempt. Specifically, we demonstrated that our approach can be applied in domains where signifi-
519
      cant inter-observer variability in similarity perception exists, such as in the complex interpretation
      of CXR images. As our experimental design did not include any domain-specific constraints or as-
520
      sumptions unique to the medical field, we believe that our method can potentially be generalized to
521
      other domains.
522
523   We hypothesized that individual psychological embeddings reflect features learned independently
524   of external metrics (such as labels). According to this hypothesis, information derived from high-
525
      dimensional data that is cognitively interpreted may lie on a lower-dimensional psychological mani-
      fold. Autoencoders probabilistically learn the data manifold independent of external metrics. There-
526
      fore, autoencoders may offer a useful framework for approximating human-metric-independent em-
527
      beddings. We further suggest that triplet loss, which captures individual similarity, may have acted
528   as a perturbation that guided the autoencoder toward learning a specific manifold. However, theoret-
529   ical proof is beyond the scope of this study and should be explored in future studies. Additionally,
530   through simulations and ablation studies using person surrogate models, we confirmed the robust-
531   ness of the proposed method and demonstrated the potential for proportional improvements in the
532   model inference performance as the scale of behavioral data sampling increases.
533
      Our study, which uses a multidisciplinary approach that integrates cognitive science, machine learn-
534   ing, and expert knowledge applications, demonstrates the potential of aligning deep neural networks
535   with human representational mechanisms as a tool for understanding human cognitive representa-
536   tions. This approach could also potentially contribute to the development of machine-learning algo-
537   rithms that support personalized learning for experts. Future research will aim to provide theoretical
538   proof for the hypothesis that variable triplet loss can guide manifold learning in autoencoders and
539   enhance the scalability of the proposed method by applying various learning algorithms suggested
      in the field of metric learning.
