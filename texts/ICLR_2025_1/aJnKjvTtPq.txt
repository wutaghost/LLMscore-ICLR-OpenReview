009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As the parameters of Large Language Models (LLMs) increase, quantization has
012            emerged as a potent strategy for model compression and acceleration. Concur-
013            rently, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) has been recognized as an effective method
014            for enhancing LLM performance. However, integrating LoRA with quantization
015            presents significant challenges, particularly in preserving the quantization format
016
               after model optimization. In this paper, we introduce Low rank Quantization
               Adaptation (LoQA) for LLM, a novel approach that effectively fine-tunes holis-
017
               tic quantization parameters. Specifically, we first propose Holistic Quantization
018
               Low-Rank Adaptation (HQ-LoRA), a new perspective on the quantization opera-
019            tor that is compatible with LoRA. This approach enables simultaneous fine-tuning
020            of all parameters (scale and zero point), yielding notable improvements in model
021            performance. Thanks to the expanded optimization landscape, LoQA is broadly
022            applicable to various Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) techniques, ensuring bet-
023            ter generalizability in practical deployments. To address the varying magnitudes
024            of integer weights under different bit-widths, we further propose Quantized Bit-
025            Aware Scaling (QBAS), a strategy that adjusts the LoRA scaling factor based on
026            the current bit-width. This approach normalizes the influence of integer weights
027
               across different quantization levels, enhancing the efficiency and stability of the
               fine-tuning process. Compared to existing methods, LoQA consistently achieves
028
               performance gains across a wide range of models, proving its effectiveness and
029
               adaptability. Code is available in the supplementary materials.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, large language models (Zhang et al., 2022; Le Scao et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
035   2023a;b; Bubeck et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable performance across various fields,
036   attracting significant attention. However, the increasing number of parameters in these models has
037   made training and fine-tuning progressively more challenging. This has led to a research focus on
038   efficiently enhancing model performance on diverse tasks using massive datasets, thereby facilitating
039
      the deployment and utilization of LLMs by researchers and the general public.
040   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (Xu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2021; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
041   2024a) and quantization (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2023; Ma
042   et al., 2024) have emerged as prominent methods for improving training efficiency and compress-
043   ing models. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques aim to minimize the number of fine-tuning
044   parameters and computational complexity. These techniques enhance model performance while re-
045
      ducing fine-tuning costs, time, and computational resource consumption. For example, QLORA
      efficiently fine-tunes a 65B parameter model on a 48GB GPU using Low Rank Adapters and inno-
046
      vative 4-bit quantization (Dettmers et al., 2023). The low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021)
047
      method reduces the number of fine-tuning parameters through low-rank matrix multiplications. This
048   approach decreases memory usage during gradient updates and accelerates training speed. Addition-
049   ally, freezing parameters in the backbone network during optimization allows for the integration of
050   quantization methods. Mapping backbone network parameters to low-bit representations further im-
051   proves training efficiency. A series of post-training quantization methods (Frantar et al., 2022; Xiao
052   et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024) can quickly
053   produce high-performance low-bit quantized models for the backbone network. The integration of
      quantization and parameter-efficient fine-tuning presents substantial challenges within neural net-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      work optimization. Notably, maintaining the quantized format of the backbone network proves
055   difficult following the integration of fine-tuned parameters. Initially, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024)
056   addresses this issue by employing post-training quantization to preserve the structure post-fusion.
057   However, this method partially compromises the precision of fine-tuned parameters, impacting the
058   overall accuracy of the model. To tackle this, QA-LoRA (Xu et al., 2023) constrains the dimensions
059   of low-rank matrices, allowing the fine-tuning parameters to be incorporated directly into the zero
060   points of the quantized backbone network. This ensures the stability of the quantization fixed points
061   during parameter fusion, although it restricts the optimization space for fine-tuned parameters, thus
062   capping potential performance gains for the language model.
063   In response, this paper introduces a novel approach named Low-Rank Quantization Adaptation
064   (LoQA). This method enhances all quantized parameters with an efficient fine-tuning module
065   through two key components: Holistic Quantization Low-Rank Adaptation (HQ-LoRA) and Quan-
066   tized Bit-Aware Scaling (QBAS). HQ-LoRA provides a new perspective on the quantization oper-
067   ator, making it compatible with LoRA while maintaining mathematical equivalence to the original
068   operator. Conceptually, if the quantization zero points in the backbone network are viewed as trans-
069
      lational operations on intra-group weight parameters, the scale parameters then serve as scaling
      transformations that adapt these parameters to the quantization range. HQ-LoRA enables the simul-
070
      taneous fine-tuning of all quantization parameters (scale and zero point), significantly expanding
071
      the optimization space. Concurrently, it preserves the quantized structure of the backbone network,
072   ensuring that the quantization fixed points remain stable. To address the varying magnitudes of inte-
073   ger weights under different bit-widths, QBAS adjusts the LoRA scaling factor based on the current
074   bit-width, normalizing the influence of integer weights across different quantization levels. This
075   approach enhances the efficiency and stability of the fine-tuning process. LoQA comprehensively
076   optimizes both sets of quantization parameters through gradient-based methods, thereby broadening
077   the optimization space. The fine-tuning of the two sets of quantization parameters under this low-
078   rank framework minimizes both time and computational expenses, yielding an optimized quantized
079   model efficiently.
080   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
081
082          • A novel perspective on quantization: We introduce Holistic Quantization Low-Rank
083            Adaptation (HQ-LoRA), which expands the optimization space for fine-tuning all quan-
084            tized parameters. Through a comprehensive analysis of the dequantization process, HQ-
085            LoRA efficiently fine-tunes all quantized parameters, significantly enhancing the model’s
086
               capacity.
087          • An innovative LoRA scaling strategy: We propose the Quantized Bit-Aware Scaling
088            (QBAS) technique, which dynamically adjusts the LoRA scaling factor based on the cur-
089            rent bit-width. This approach normalizes the influence of integer weights across differ-
090            ent quantization levels, thereby enhancing the efficiency and stability of the fine-tuning
091            process. QBAS is particularly effective when dealing with varying magnitudes of integer
092
               weights under different bit-widths, ensuring consistent performance across diverse quanti-
               zation settings.
093
094          • Empirical validation of significant performance improvements: Extensive experiments
095            demonstrate that LoQA consistently outperforms previous fine-tuning methods that main-
096            tain quantized formats, and in many cases, matches the performance of state-of-the-art
097            4+16 bit methods. Notably, in ultra-low bit-width scenarios, LoQA’s effectiveness is even
098
               more pronounced, with its 2-bit version surpassing the current 2+16-bit state-of-the-art
               method by 4.7% and even outperforming the original 16-bit model.

138
      tational overhead. Our research mainly focuses on uniform quantization, which is more suitable for

141   Fine-Tuning of Quantized Parameters. Techniques such as QLoRA(Dettmers et al., 2023), IR-
142
      QLoRA(Qin et al., 2024), LQ-LoRA(Guo et al., 2023), and LoftQ(Li et al., 2023) quantize model
      parameters into low-bit representations, followed by the addition of LoRA modules for fine-tuning.
143
      However, these approaches require the integration of floating-point LoRA modules with the quan-
144
      tized weights, leading to the restoration of model weights to floating-point format, preventing direct
145   use of the quantized weights. In contrast, PEQA (Kim et al., 2024) employs a simple round-to-
146   nearest (RTN) method for low-bit quantization and fine-tunes the quantized model’s step size to
147   adapt to downstream tasks, allowing the quantized model to be directly utilized post-fine-tuning.
148   EfficientQAT (Chen et al., 2024) improves upon PEQA by replacing the simple RTN method to
149   provide a better starting point for fine-tuning. The closest related method to ours is QA-LoRA (Xu
150   et al., 2023), which redesigns the LoRA module to seamlessly integrate with zero-points. However,
151   QA-LoRA requires zero-points to be in floating-point format, limiting its practical applicability. Ad-
152   ditionally, it can only merge with zero-points, which constrains its overall performance, especially
153
      when fine-tuning on large downstream datasets.

      second stage, significantly reducing memory requirements. In the second stage of fine-tuning for

      3.1   P RELIMINARY
205
206   Low-Rank Adaptation. We adopt the symbolic notation system to elucidate the Low-Rank Adap-
207   tation (LoRA) methodology (Hu et al., 2021). Let W ∈ RDout ×Din represent the pretrained weights
208   for a specific layer. Given an input feature vector x ∈ RDin , the output vector y ∈ RDout is com-
209   puted as y = W x. The LoRA approach introduces two low-rank matrices, A ∈ RDint ×Din and
210   B ∈ RDout ×Dint , where Dint ≪ min(Din , Dout ). This ensures that the product BA is a low-rank
211   matrix yet aligns dimensionally with W . During training, the computation is augmented with a
212   scaling coefficient s:
213                                           y = W x + s · BAx                                       (1)
214
215   We define W ′ as the final learned weights after fine-tuning, obtained by combining LoRA with the
      original weights in floating-point format:

217                                            W ′ = W + s · BA                                             (2)
218
219   This formulation allows W to remain static while A and B are updated, enabling efficient parameter
220   tuning. Post-training, we employ the reparametrized weight matrix W ′ for inference, computing the
221   output as y = W ′ x, thus facilitating accelerated computation.
222   Joint Low-Rank Adaptation and Quantization. The integration of quantization and LoRA can
223   further reduce the resource overhead during fine-tuning. First, we quantize the original model. For
224   simplicity, we will use the uniform quantization formula to illustrate this process. To ensure clarity
225   in the following expressions, we will denote the data type in the upper right corner of the different
226   values, using FP16 to represent floating-point numbers. In this paper, we primarily discuss group
227   quantization, so the step sizes S and zero points Z are represented as matrices. If group quantization
228   seems confusing, we provide a detailed explanation of this process using a simple min-max group

254   3.2   H OLISTIC Q UANTIZATION L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
255
256   LoQA retains the prior fine-tuning steps by first quantizing the model and then applying LoRA for
257   fine-tuning, significantly reducing memory consumption for model weights, gradients, and optimizer
258
      parameters. During the LoRA fine-tuning phase, we introduce a novel module called HQ-LoRA
      (Holistic Quantization Low-Rank Adaptation). This module employs two LoRA variants to fine-
259
      tune all floating-point quantized parameters within the quantized model (step sizes S and zero points
260
      Z). This approach allows natural merging of LoRA weights with floating-point parameters from the
261   quantized model post-fine-tuning, without precision loss, ensuring the quantized model maintains
262   its properties. The forward process of HQ-LoRA is expressed as:
263
264
                           y ′ = W̃ FP16 x + s · BAx′ + s · (W Int ⊙ f ((B ′ A′ ), g))x′ ,                  (6)
265
266   where g is the group size for group quantization, and x′ is obtained from x using a grouping operator
267   (one-dimensional average pooling). A and A′ are shaped as Dint × Dgin .

291   LoQA introduces Quantized Bit-Aware Scaling (QBAS), a novel approach to adjusting the LoRA
292   scaling factor based on quantization bit-width. In traditional LoRA, the scaling factor s in Equation 1
293   is defined as s = αr , where α is a hyperparameter and r represents the intermediate dimension size
294   (Dint in Equation 3.1).
295

297   (B ′ A′ ). Since the magnitude of Wint varies with quantization bit-width, it significantly impacts
298   the scale-related LoRA updates.
