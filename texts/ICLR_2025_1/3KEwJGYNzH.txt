011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Model decomposition in large language models has drawn much attention due
014            to its superiority and good interpretability, where activation-aware singular value
015            decomposition (SVD) can achieve competitive performance by mitigating recon-
016            struction errors brought by outliers in activation. However, the performance of the
017            state-of-the-art SVD-based LLM compression method is limited to the selection
018
               of truncation positions. No work meticulously examines the details of this prob-
               lem theoretically and empirically tests its correlation with model performance. To
019
               fill the research gap, we propose an efficient method that can automatically select
020
               truncation positions, namely AutoTrunc. In our work, we first analyze the corre-
021            lation between truncation positions and the model performance. Then, the model
022            layer importance is modeled based on the correlation, followed by mathematical
023            proof to illustrate how to reach and obtain the optimal truncation position config-
024            uration for different layer types. Extensive experiments are carried out to verify
025            our presumption and evaluate our proposed method. Our proposed AutoTrunc
026            outperforms the state-of-the-art SVD-based LLM compression method, with per-
027            plexity scores dropping by 24.65% and 38.63% at the compression ratio of 50% in
028            LLaMA-2-7B and LLaMA-2-13B, respectively. The code will be released upon
029
               acceptance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The large language model has been proven to perform exceptionally in natural language processing
035   and related areas (Zhao et al., 2023). Despite the remarkable performance brought by billions of
036   model parameters (Kaplan et al., 2020), modern large language models (LLMs) have presented con-
037   siderable challenges to inference and deployment. It is necessary to reduce memory footprint during
038   the inference to facilitate LLM deployment and democratization. To achieve this goal, researchers
039
      have proposed various model compression techniques (Miao et al., 2023), where model decomposi-
      tion has recently drawn much attention due to its good interpretability. Despite some methods can
040
      achieve competitive performance without post-training (Yu & Wu, 2023; Yuan et al., 2023), it is
041
      still challenging to determine the truncation position for each layer. Selecting the most appropriate
042   truncation position plays a crucial part in model performance, where a configuration of poor quality
043   can lead to drastic performance degradation.
044
045
      Many efforts have been devoted to this field and distinct methods are tried to address this prob-
      lem. The most naive one is to adopt uniform truncation positions for all layers (Wang et al., 2024),
046
      which ignores the discrepancy between distinct layers to pursue each component to get equally com-
047
      pressed. Some propose to search for the best configuration in an iterative or adaptive manner (Hsu
048   et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Chavan et al., 2024). Still, the search process is time-consuming
049   since it involves expensive actual evaluation on the real-world dataset. Other methods leverage prior
050   knowledge provided by artificial metrics designed by experts to guide the determination of trunca-
051   tion positions (Yin et al., 2024). The works discussed above have drawbacks to different extents,
052   making it hard to reach a graceful balance between overhead and model quality. To the best of our
053   knowledge, there is no work dedicated to studying how to determine truncation positions for each
      layer.

056   correlation between reconstruction error and inference quality through both theoretical and empiri-

058   it to be an NP-hard problem. In order to obtain solutions of good quality at an acceptable time

060   modeling and then we proposed a highly efficient method to search the truncation configuration that

141    Perplexity is a widely used metric to evaluate model performance, and it is closely related to the
142    cross-entropy loss of the language model. The more likely it is for the language model to generate
143    sentences that appear in the test set, the less the resulting perplexity is. The value of performance
144    estimation F (K) thus should strongly correlate with the perplexity. To end this, we test the correla-
145    tion between the perplexity on Wikitext-2 (Merity et al., 2016) and F (K), with data collected from
146    two different strategies, the first of which is to compress all the layers uniformly, and the second is
147
       adopting greedy search to search the optimal truncation positions according to Equation (4). Both
       strategies are respectively applied to the model with all layers, w/o the first and last, w/o the first
148
       two and last two layers. Additionally, the compression ratio is gradually increased from 10% to

       50%, making 6 × 40 = 240 data points available in total. The results are shown in Figure 1(a) and
150    Figure 1(b).
151
152    Notably, the perplexity tends to decrease with the value F (K) increasing overall. However, consid-
153
       ering samples from different sources, there is a conspicuous divergence in the perplexity as F (K)
       decreases, and it is therefore unreliable to estimate model performance based on the value of F (K).
154
       One intuitive explanation for this phenomenon is the lack of layer importance, where in LLMs some
155
       layers are more important than others and the unweighted sum ignores this essential factor (Yin
156    et al., 2024; Gromov et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024). We thus conjecture that for each layer, there
157    is a factor representing its importance, which can make the weighted sum (also denoted as F (K))
158    of the performance scores and their importance factors able to estimate the model’s quality. With
159    effective layer importance factors, our defined F (K) can be a powerful tool to efficiently estimate
160    model performance, as illustrated in Figure 1(c) and Figure 1(d), where the divergence is signif-
161    icantly mitigated. The problem and solution regarding layer importance will be particularized in
       §3.

      2.3   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
163
164   Our vision of estimating the model performance by theoretical calculation can be divided into two
165   smaller problems. The first problem is how to define and find a coefficient for each layer that
166   can represent its importance so that their weighted sum (i.e., the value of F (K)) can be used to
167   discriminate the model performance. The second problem is determining the truncation position for
168   each layer, where the value of F (K) can reach maximal.
169   For the first problem, we notice the calculation of perplexity and the way we define performance
170   scores have a strong relationship, intuitively. However, since the LLM is essentially a black-box
171   model, it is impracticable to find layer importance where there is a strictly monotonic mapping
172   between its F (K) and the model’s perplexity. Therefore, we try to establish a strong correlation
173   between the value of F (K) and the model performance. The first problem thus can be formalized as
174   follows.
175   Definition 1. Layer Importance Fitting problem (LIF problem). For a large language model, given
176   its layers l ∈ L, for each layer l, the layer importance fitting needs to find its coefficient αl , making
177   their weighted sum with performance score, i.e., F (K) = αl f (kl ; l), where kl ∈ K, has a strong
178   correlation with model’s performance.
179
      With the given presumption (i.e., it is solvable for LIF problem), where each layer has a coefficient
180
      that can represent its importance and their weighted sum can be leveraged to discriminate the model
181
      performance, we formalize the truncation selection problem in SVD-based LLM compression as
182   follows.
183
      Definition 2. Truncation Position Selection problem (TPS problem). For a large language model,
184
      given its layers l ∈ L and their importance αl , layers’ corresponding performance score function
185
      f (kl ; l) under truncation position kl , and memory consumption function g(kl ; l), the objective of
186   TPS is to determine the truncation position kl for each layer such that:

198   3     M ETHODOLOGY
199
200   For the LIF problem, even though there are many existing works whose proposed artificial metrics
201   are proven to be effective in measuring layers’ importance (Yin et al., 2024; Gromov et al., 2024;
202   Men et al., 2024), it is infeasible to employ these metrics straightforwardly. This is because the
203   correlation between F (K) and the model performance is not captured through the priori knowledge.
204   Adopting these artificial metrics will lead to the failure of performance estimation. Instead of em-
205
      ploying the prior knowledge, we propose fitting layer importance with a learning-based method,
      where the resulting layer importance scores can successfully establish a correlation between F (K)
206
      and the model performance.
207
208
      3.1   L EARNING - BASED L AYER I MPORTANCE M ODELING
209
210   One intuitive idea for solving the LIF problem is regression. However, predicting the perplexity
211   is not our goal, and the linear regression can not fit highly complicated non-linear data, which
212   significantly undermines its feasibility in solving the LIF problem. Moreover, simply employing
213   linear regression can lead to negative values of the layer importance scores, which conflict with
214   existing practices. Notably, our goal is to establish a correlation between F (K) and the perplex-
215   ity so that F (K) can be used to guide us in selecting the best truncation position configuration for
      model decomposition. Therefore, we do not need to seek high accuracy on the perplexity prediction,


                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      but rather, the accuracy of discriminating the model quality under different configurations. Conse-
217   quently, we choose to solve the LIF problem with a ranking model. The ranking model uses F (K)
218   as its scoring function, whose parameters are essentially the layer importance. Once the ranking
219   model is well trained, the F (K) can strongly correlate with the perplexity. To this end, we intro-
220   duce a listwise ranking method called LambdaRank (Burges et al., 2006) to learn to rank different
221   truncation position configurations.
222
      LambdaRank is a listwise learning-to-rank method that can capture information existing in the
223   change of scores on metrics such as NDCG (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002). Considering an or-
224   dered pair (i, j), where i has a higher relevant score, the loss function for the LambdaRank model
225   can be formulated as Equation (6).

509   5 R ELATED W ORKS
510
511   Large Language Model Compression LLM compression has drawn much attention for its cru-
512   cial part in LLM deployment. According to the granularity and methodology, they can be roughly
513   categorized into the following types: unstructured-pruning (Ma et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; Dong
514   et al., 2024); Quantization (Frantar et al.); structured-pruning (Ashkboos et al., 2024; Men et al.,
515   2024); knowledge distillation (Du et al., 2024); model decomposition (Yuan et al., 2023; Wang
516   et al., 2024; Yu & Wu, 2023). Different types of LLM compression techniques have their own
517   strength and drawbacks. They are orthogonal and can be applied at the same time. There is no par-
518   ticular technique that can significantly outperform others in terms of overhead, efficiency, generation
519
      quality, and performance on the downstream tasks at the same time (Miao et al., 2023).
520
521
      Model Decomposition for LLMs Vanilla SVD suffers from reconstruction errors brought by out-
      liers. To mitigate the error, researchers have proposed different methods to capture data distribution
522
      in the input and output. Yu & Wu (2023) noticed low-rank structure does not exist in the pre-
523
      trained weights but their features, proposing to approximate the features (WX) instead of the weight
524   (X) with Atomic Feature Mimicking (AFM). Activation-aware singular value decomposition (Yuan
525   et al., 2023) and SVD-LLM (Wang et al., 2024) employ the same idea, using improved SVD to
526   compress LLMs. To summarize, all advanced model decomposition methods realize the problem
527   incurred by outliers and try to solve it, i.e., AFM-based (Yu & Wu, 2023; Kaushal et al., 2023; Ji
528   et al., 2024; Chavan et al., 2024), improved SVD (Yuan et al., 2023; Chavan et al., 2024), mask-
529   ing (Li et al., 2023), and fine-grained decomposition (Liu et al., 2024).
530

      6        C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose AutoTrunc, an efficient method to address the truncation position selection
534   problem with only theoretical calculation. It facilitates model performance estimation with learning-
535   based layer importance modeling, followed by searching the truncation configurations that are most
536   likely to have the best model performance. Extensive experiments are carried out to evaluate our
537   proposed method AutoTrunc. We have demonstrated the superiority of AutoTrunc under different
538   compression ratios on 8 datasets and 3 models from the LLaMA-2 family. Compared with the
539   state-of-the-art method, the perplexity on WikiText-2 by 24.65% and 38.63% in LLaMA-2-7B and
      LLaMA-2-13B, under 50% compression ratio drops, respectively.

      This appendix aims to provide additional information and in-depth analysis to supplement the main
705   content, which mainly includes three parts: further discussion, hardness of truncation position se-
706   lection problem, and supplementary materials. In the first section, we discuss the advantages and
707   limitations of AutoTrunc, as well as our insight on the implications of AutoTrunc. Then, we provide

709   more experiment results in the last section.

      A     F URTHER D ISCUSSION
712
713   A.1   A DVANTAGES AND L IMITATIONS
714
715   The major advantage of AutoTrunc is it can select appropriate truncation positions for each layer au-
716   tomatically, where the whole process is highly efficient since it only requires theoretical calculation.
717   However, the superiority of AutoTrunc largely depends on the performance of the ranking model,
718   i.e., F (K) in the main content. A ranking model with high accuracy in discriminating truncation
719   configurations helps improve the quality of the derived configuration.
720
721   A.2   F EASIBILITY OF G ENERALIZATION
722
      Our proposed AutoTrunc can be generalized and integrated into other model decomposition-based
723
      LLM compression methods as long as they satisfy the following three conditions: (1) It can define
724   a function f (kl ; l) to estimate compressed layer l’s performance based on its truncation position kl ;
725   (2) It collects enough data pairs that include values of f (kl ; l) for each layer of the model and its
726   resulting perplexity score. With these two key component, we can successfully build a metric that
727   strongly correlated with the model performance.

      To show that TPV problem is NP-complete, we will reduce it from the 0-1 Knapsack Problem.
757   Consider an arbitrary instance of the 0-1 Knapsack Problem, which includes n binary variables
758   {x1 , x2 , ..., xn }, where w[j] and p[j] represent the weight and profit of item xj , respectively. The
759   backpack can hold items up to a total weight of W . The objective is to find whether or not there
760   exists a “solution” with profit no less than P , where P is the desired profit. Then, we construct a
761   TPV corresponding to the 0-1 Knapsack Problem instance as follows:
762

764
