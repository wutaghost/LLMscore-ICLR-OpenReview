009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning-based watermarking models play a crucial role in copyright pro-
012            tection across various applications. However, many high-performance models are
013            limited in practical deployment due to their large number of parameters. Mean-
014            while, the robustness and invisibility performance of existing lightweight models
015            are unsatisfactory. This presents a pressing need for a watermarking model that
016            combines lightweight capacity with satisfactory performance. Our research iden-
017            tifies a key reason that limits the performance of existing watermarking frame-
018            works: a mismatch between commonly used decoding losses (e.g., mean squared
019
               error and binary cross-entropy loss) and the actual decoding goal, leading to pa-
               rameter redundancy. We propose two innovative solutions: (1) Decoding-oriented
020
               surrogate loss (DO), which redesigns the loss function to mitigate the influence of
021
               decoding-irrelevant optimization directions; and (2) Detachable projection head
022            (PH), which incorporates a detachable redundant module during training to han-
023            dle these irrelevant directions and is discarded during inference. Additionally, we
024            propose a novel watermarking framework comprising five submodules, allowing
025            for independent parameter reduction in each component. Our proposed model
026            achieves better efficiency, invisibility, and robustness while utilizing only 2.2%
027            of the parameters compared to state-of-the-art frameworks. By improving effi-
028            ciency while maintaining robust copyright protection, our model is well-suited
029            for practical applications in resource-constrained environments. The DO and PH
030
               methods are designed to be plug-and-play, facilitating seamless integration into
               future lightweight models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Digital watermarking is crucial for protecting the copyright of various digital assets, including im-
037   ages, videos, and 3D content. Typically, digital watermarking involves two main tasks: information
038   hiding and extraction. In this process, digital media owners conceal secret information within the
039
      digital media, which can later be extracted to authenticate copyright ownership. In this process, two
      crucial properties need to be considered: 1) invisibility, which requires that the visual quality of the
040
      digital media does not significantly degraded after embedding secret information, and 2) robust-
041
      ness, which ensures that even when watermarked digital media encountered various distortions, the
042   embedded information can be accurately extracted (Wan et al., 2022; Singh, 2023).
043
044   In recent years, with the development of deep learning, many deep learning-based watermarking
045
      models have emerged, which utilize complex architectures to improve performance (Liu et al.,
      2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023). However, these
046
      high-performance models often come with a large number of parameters and huge computational
047
      demands, limiting their practical deployment. As a result, fields such as diffusion models (Fernan-
048   dez et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023b) and Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Luo et al., 2023; Jang
049   et al., 2024) have chosen to adopt lightweight watermarking models like HiDDeN (Zhu et al., 2018)
050   for copyright protection, as their backbone models are already large and computationally intensive.
051   Although lightweight models are easy to deploy, they often sacrifice robustness. This limitation
052   impedes effective and efficient copyright protection in numerous fields, especially where compu-
053   tational resources are limited, highlighting an urgent need for watermarking models that combine
      lightweight capacity with satisfactory performance.

054
      This paper aims to design and train a lightweight watermarking model with state-of-the-art perfor-
055   mance. To understand the potential reasons limiting the performance of lightweight watermarking
056   models, we investigate the decoding process. For the decoding task, accuracy serves as a prevalent
057   evaluation metric. However, the straightforward empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation
058   for accuracy includes minimizing the 0-1 loss, which is computationally intractable. Consequently,
059   researchers resort to differentiable losses (e.g., mean squared error or binary cross-entropy loss)
060   as tractable surrogate losses, transforming the decoding task into a reconstruction task (Zhu et al.,
061   2018; Liu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023).
062   The feasibility of optimization comes at the expense of model efficiency. Our observation reveals
063
      a mismatch between the actual decoding goal and the optimization objectives of the commonly
      used decoding losses. By dissecting the decoding losses into deflation, inflation, and regularization
064
      losses, we identify that decoding accuracy depends primarily on the deflation loss. However, in-
065
      flation and regularization losses, while stabilizing training, inevitably consume model parameters,
066   negatively affecting the efficiency of lightweight models. To address this issue, we propose two
067   methods. For the first method, we append an additional projection module to the lightweight model
068   during training. This module manages decoding-irrelevant optimization directions and is discarded
069   during inference. In the other approach, we propose a new surrogate loss to mitigate the negative
070   impact of decoding-irrelevant optimization directions while ensuring stable training.
071
      Moreover, previous works typically treat encoders and decoders as basic design units and often lack
072   detailed categorization of internal functions. This makes it challenging to conduct fine-grained ab-
073   lation studies. To address this problem, we propose to subdivide encoders and decoders into smaller
074   functional units and, for the first time, summarize a new deep watermarking framework consisting
075   of five modules. We construct a lightweight watermarking model within this new framework using
076   only transposed convolution and convolution layers. Experimental results reveal that certain mod-
077   ules are crucial when facing specific distortions while contributing minimally to other distortions.
078   This insight allows us to selectively remove non-essential modules, further compressing the model
079   with minimal performance degradation.
080   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows: 1) We are the first to identify the
081   mismatch between the optimization objectives of the commonly used decoding losses (e.g., mean
082   squared error and binary cross-entropy loss) and the actual decoding goal. Ablation studies confirm
083   the presence and impact of this mismatch. 2) We propose the detachable projection head (PH) and
084   decoding-oriented surrogate loss (DO) to mitigate the negative impact of irrelevant optimization di-
085   rections, enabling lightweight models to achieve state-of-the-art performance. 3) We introduce a
086
      fine-grained deep watermarking framework with five modules. Our experiments analyze the roles
      of different modules under various distortions, enabling further model compression with minimal
087
      performance loss. 4) Our lightweight model will see broader application in other fields, especially
088
      where computational resources are constrained. 5) Extensive experiments demonstrate the superior-
089   ity of our approach over existing models in terms of invisibility, robustness, and efficiency.

094   2.1   R ELATED W ORKS
095
096   Image Watermarking Digital watermarking (Van Schyndel et al., 1994) is widely used to protect
097   copyrights and trace the origins of unauthorized copies. Invisibility and robustness are crucial prop-
098   erties of digital watermarks. In pursuit of balancing invisibility and robustness, traditional digital
099
      watermarking algorithms often embed watermarks in mid-frequency domain coefficients. The com-
      monly used domains include the discrete cosine transform (DCT) domain (Ahmidi & Safabakhsh,
100
      2004), the discrete wavelet transform (DWT) domain (Daren et al., 2001), and the discrete Fourier
101
      transform (DFT) domain (Hamidi et al., 2018). With the advancement of deep learning techniques,
102   deep learning-based watermarking models have received increasing attention for achieving a better
103   trade-off between invisibility and robustness. The first deep learning-based watermarking model
104   was introduced by Kandi et al. (2017), demonstrating the feasibility of using autoencoder convo-
105   lutional neural networks (CNN) for watermarking tasks. Subsequently, Zhu et al. (2018) proposed
106   the Encoder-NoiseLayer-Decoder (END) framework, pioneering end-to-end training and incorpo-
107   rating a noise layer to enhance robustness against various distortions. Jia et al. (2021) introduced
      the Mini-Batch of Simulated and Real Jpeg compression (MBRS) training method and advanced


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) blocks (Hu et al., 2018) to improve robustness against JPEG compres-
109   sion. Ma et al. (2022) developed the Combining Invertible and Non-invertible Mechanisms (CIN),
110   leveraging invertible neural networks for embedding and extraction, significantly improving invisi-
111   bility and robustness. Fang et al. (2023) proposed the Flow-based Invertible Network (FIN), which
112   explored the use of invertible structures as differentiable simulators for both white-box and black-
113   box distortions, surpassing many state-of-the-art END-based watermarking models. However, in
114   pursuit of better invisibility and robustness, the watermarking models have become bigger, with
115   increased computational complexity.
116
117   Knowledge Distillation Recently, deep learning has achieved significant success in many fields.
118   These substantial advancements are mainly due to the massive number of model parameters. Al-
119
      though large-scale models exhibit better performance, the massive storage requirements and high
      computational complexity hinder their further deployment. Therefore, knowledge distillation (KD)
120
      has received significant attention, which aims to distill knowledge from a larger model (teacher
121
      model) into a smaller model (student model) to achieve model compression (Hinton et al., 2015;
122   Kim et al., 2018; Mirzadeh et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022). Although knowledge distillation has
123   been successfully applied in various applications such as visual recognition, speech recognition,
124   and natural language processing, the theoretical understanding of knowledge distillation is limited
125   (Cho & Hariharan, 2019; Cheng et al., 2020), and the reasons for its success are not fully clear.
126   Moreover, when there is a significant difference in model architecture or size between the student
127   model and the teacher model, this model capacity gap can degrade knowledge transfer (Mirzadeh
128   et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021). This implies that typically, the student model performs inferior to the
129   teacher model. Therefore, obtaining a lightweight model with state-of-the-art performance through
130
      knowledge distillation is challenging.

299   3.2   P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
300
301   To delve deeper into the encoder and decoder, we propose a novel deep learning-based watermark-
302   ing framework composed of five modules, as shown in Fig. 1. These modules provide a clearer
303   functional division of the encoder and decoder, which has helped us find an efficient parameter al-
304
      location method when facing different distortions. We divide the encoder into three parts: 1) The
      image preprocessing (IP) module, which aims to extract features from the original image compre-
305
      hensively, facilitating subsequent feature fusion, such as the SE block (Hu et al., 2018) in MBRS
306
      (Jia et al., 2021) and De-END (Fang et al., 2022), or it transforms image features into the frequency
307   domain to enhance robustness, as seen in CIN (Ma et al., 2022) using the Haar transform. 2) The
308   message preprocessing (MP) module’s primary task is to generate message features that align in
309   shape with the image features generated by the image preprocessing module. HiDDeN (Zhu et al.,
310   2018) employs a non-deep learning method by directly duplicating the messages. On the other
311   hand, MBRS (Jia et al., 2021) and CIN (Ma et al., 2022) utilize deep learning methods based on
312   transposed convolution layers. 3) The feature fusion (FF) module integrates the message features
313   and image features to produce the final watermarked images. StegaStamps (Tancik et al., 2020)
314   adopts UNet-like model for multi-scale feature fusion, while CIN (Ma et al., 2022) and FIN (Fang
315
      et al., 2023) employ multiple invertible neural blocks for deep feature coupling. We divide the de-
      coder into two parts: 4) The noised watermarked image preprocessing (NWIP) module is the
316
      first module directly facing the noise layer. Its role is to mitigate the impact of distortions on the wa-
317
      termarked image and perform the initial extraction of message features from the noised watermarked
318   images. 5) The message extraction (ME) module is used to further extract the messages from the
319   preprocessed features and reshape them to match the shape of the messages. In previous decoder
320   architectures, these two modules were often deeply coupled, requiring uniform dimensions for the
321   intermediate features. By decoupling, we can investigate the roles of these two modules separately
322   and selectively remove non-essential modules to further compress the model. More detailed ablation
323   experiments and analyses of the individual modules’ impact on model performance are provided in
      AppendixE.2.

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
506
      Impact of MSE components on model performance Table 5 presents the performance of the pro-
507   posed lightweight model when trained using different components of the MSE loss: Lregularization ,
508   Linf lation , and Ldef lation . The results provide insights into the impact of each component on the
509   model’s performance in terms of invisibility and robustness against combined noise. The results
510   demonstrate that using Lregularization or Linf lation in isolation does not effectively reduce the de-
511   coding error. Lregularization and Linf lation result in poor decoding accuracy, suggesting that these
512   components do not contribute positively to the information extraction task and can lead to issues such
513   as output collapse or explosion. In contrast, training the model with Ldef lation alone achieves better
514   performance than the original MSE loss, particularly in robustness metrics. This improvement stems
515
      from the removal of irrelevant optimization directions, which allows the model to utilize previously
      consumed parameters more effectively. Despite these gains, the training process remains unstable,
516
      often requiring multiple adjustments to the weight of Ldef lation to avoid model collapse. Addition-
517
      ally, while Ldef lation improves robustness, the DO method still outperforms it in both visual quality
518   and average accuracy, particularly for JPEG compression. In conclusion, while Ldef lation shows
519   potential in enhancing the lightweight model’s performance, the stability and overall effectiveness
520   of the DO method make it a superior choice for improving both invisibility and robustness in deep
521   watermarking models.

      5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This paper enhances the efficiency of deep learning-based watermarking models. We identify a
526
      mismatch between the commonly used decoding losses and the information extraction task. To ad-
527   dress this, we propose the detachable projection head (PH) and decoding-oriented surrogate loss
528   (DO), which reduce the impact of irrelevant components and improve model efficiency. We validate
529   our methods by designing a lightweight model that achieves state-of-the-art visual quality and ro-
530   bustness against various distortions. Additionally, we introduce a five-module deep learning-based
531   watermarking framework, providing a finer-grained division of the encoder and decoder. Ablation
532   studies reveal effective module selection strategies for different distortions. Extensive experimen-
533   tal results demonstrate the superiority of our proposed method over existing models in terms of
534   invisibility, robustness, and efficiency. However, there is room for improvement. For PH, while
535
      the lightweight model is retained for inference, the training stage requires additional storage space
      and computational resources to jointly train the detachable projection head, leading to inefficiencies
536
      during training. For DO, although it avoids additional modules during training and inference, it in-
537
      troduces a new hyperparameter, the safe distance ϵ. As shown in Appendix E.10, achieving optimal
538   performance requires manual tuning of ϵ, which is not straightforward.

765   B    P ROPOSED L IGHTWEIGHT M ODEL S TRUCTURE
766
767   Here, we provide a comprehensive overview of the proposed lightweight model depicted in Fig. 4,
768   which exclusively employs fundamental transposed convolution and convolution layers. The activa-
769   tion function utilized between each pair of layers is LeakyReLU.
770   In previous works, the partitioning of the encoder submodule has been well established, whereas
771   our contribution lies in the delineation of the decoder. The separation of the two modules within
772   the decoder is based on a specific criterion: layers with a stride of 1, which do not reduce the
773   shape of the input, are designated as data processing layers, while layers with a stride greater than
774   2, which do reduce the input shape, are designated as data extraction layers. Previous approaches,
775   such as those in MBRS and CIN, often mix or alternate these two kinds of layers. In our framework,
776
      however, these two kinds of layers are grouped into two distinct blocks, the noised watermarked
      image preprocessing (NWIP) module and the message extraction (ME) module. This division is
777
      primarily a structural separation. Since NWIP and ME are trained simultaneously and share the
778
      same objective function, a complete functional distinction between them is not feasible. However,
779   the advantage of this structural separation is that the two modules do not need to share the same
780   number of channels. This allows us to independently reduce the parameters of each part, enabling a
781   more focused study and design of the decoder.

1226   Our work primarily aims to explore the feasibility of lightweight deep learning-based watermarking
1227   models. To this end, we validate the effectiveness and applicability of the proposed training methods,
1228   DO and PH, using a model with an intentionally simple structure and minimal parameters. We
1229   further investigate the broader applicability of these methods to different scenarios.
1230   While our original lightweight model demonstrates strong robustness and visual quality against
1231   combined and multi-single distortions, achieving significant parameter reduction without compro-
1232   mising robustness across all distortion types remains inherently challenging. The distortions evalu-
1233   ated above are primarily digital channel-based distortions, which tend not to alter the geometric fea-
1234   tures of images significantly. Consequently, the substantial parameters and architectural components
1235   (e.g., the SE block in MBRS) designed for handling geometric distortions can be reduced without
1236   impacting performance in these cases. This characteristic explains why our original lightweight
1237   model, combined with the proposed DO and PH methods, performs effectively under such digital
1238
       distortions.
1239   However, experiments in Table 20 reveal that our lightweight model’s robustness declines when sub-
1240   jected to geometric distortions like RandomPerspective (RP), RandomAffine (RA), and RandomE-
1241   lasticTransform (RET). These distortions introduce greater complexity, requiring more sophisticated
       feature extraction to achieve optimal performance. While our original lightweight model does not


                                                        23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       achieve the best performance under geometric distortions, the DO method still ranks second, just
1243   behind MBRS.
1244
1245   To address this limitation, we extended the original lightweight model to enhance its robustness
1246
       against geometric distortions. Specifically, we modified the noised watermarked image preprocess-
       ing (NWIP) module, the first module interacting with the noise layer, by incorporating an SE block
1247
       and increasing the intermediate channel count from 12 to 32. The enhanced model, referred to as
1248
       Lightweight Model +, achieves significant improvements in robustness to geometric distortions. De-
1249   spite the enhancements, its total parameter count remains remarkably low at 56.01K, representing
1250   only 12.33% of HiDDeD, 7.49% of FIN, 0.27% of MBRS, and 0.16% of CIN.
1251
1252
       As shown in Table 20, the enhanced Lightweight Model + achieves substantial improvements across
       all geometric distortions. When coupled with the DO method, it attains the best performance in
1253
       visual quality and robustness against all three distortions. The PH method also performs strongly,
1254
       achieving comparable robustness, with only a slight gap in RandomElasticTransform, while outper-
1255   forming MBRS in both visual quality and average accuracy.
1256
1257   In summary, the proposed training methods, DO and PH, exhibit broad applicability across various
1258
       lightweight model architectures. We believe these methods will prove instrumental in advancing
       the development of lightweight watermarking models and will assist other researchers in achieving
1259
       superior performance in this domain.

1262   F    L IMITATIONS
1263
1264   We propose two effective training methods: the detachable projection head (PH) and the decoding-
1265   oriented surrogate loss (DO). For PH, although we retain and use only the lightweight model during
1266   the inference stage, the training stage still requires additional storage space and computational re-
1267
       sources to jointly train the detachable projection head, which is not efficient during training. For DO,
       while it does not require extra modules during training or inference, it introduces a new hyperpa-
1268
       rameter, the safe distance ϵ. To achieve optimal performance, manual tuning of this hyperparameter
1269
       is required, as demonstrated in Appendix E.10, which is not straightforward. Furthermore, as dis-
1270   cussed in Appendix E.11, there is still room for improvement regarding geometric distortions. How-
1271   ever, future researchers aiming to train lightweight models can readily adopt our training approaches
1272   to enhance model performance without modifying the underlying architecture. In conclusion, our
1273   work represents a significant advancement in balancing efficiency and robustness in watermarking
1274   models.
