011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We study the personalized federated fine-tuning task with heterogeneous client
014            data in the context of foundation models, where clients collaboratively fine-
015            tune a foundation model (e.g., BERT, GPT) without sharing their local data,
016
               achieving personalized models simultaneously. While recent efforts have ap-
               plied parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques like low-rank adaptation (LoRA)
017
               or training prompts in federated settings, they often overlook data heterogene-
018
               ity and model personalization. The primary challenge is that a single common
019            adapter or prompt learner may not suffice for the diverse data of all clients. To ad-
020            dress this issue, we propose PF2LoRA, a new personalized federated fine-tuning
021            algorithm based on a novel two-level low rank adaptation framework on top of
022            LoRA. Given the pretrained foundation model whose weight is frozen, our algo-
023            rithm aims to learn two levels of adaptation simultaneously: the first level aims
024            to learn a common adapter for all clients, while the second level fosters individ-
025            ual client personalization. This framework explicitly accommodates variations
026            in adapter matrix ranks across clients and introduces minimal additional memory
027
               overhead, as the second-level adaptation comprises a small number of parameters
               compared to the first level. Our experiments on natural language understanding
028
               and generation tasks demonstrate that PF2LoRA significantly outperforms exist-
029
               ing federated fine-tuning methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017a; Kairouz et al., 2021) has emerged as a crucial
035
      paradigm for enabling collaborative training of machine learning models across distributed clients
036   while preserving data privacy (McMahan et al., 2017b; Geyer et al., 2017). FL is particularly impor-
037   tant in some scenarios that involve sensitive data, such as healthcare (Brisimi et al., 2018; Sheller
038   et al., 2020), finance (Yang et al., 2019), and mobile devices (Bonawitz et al., 2019). However, in
039   the context of foundation models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and GPT (Radford et al., 2018),
040   traditional FL algorithms face significant challenges due to the complexity of these models. It re-
041   quires huge computing resources when directly fine-tuning model parameters on the heterogeneous
042   data distributed across different clients.
043   To address the issue of fine-tuning foundation models, many parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
044   methods such as prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) and low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
045   2021) have been explored, where LoRA freezes the original pre-trained parameters W ∈ Rm×n of
046   the foundation model while fine-tuning additional low rank matrices B ∈ Rm×r and A ∈ Rr×n ,
047   r ≪ min(m, n). This technique enables fine-tuning large models with a reduced number of trainable
048   parameters, making them more suitable for resource-constrained devices. This paper specifically
049   focuses on LoRA in the context of federated learning for heterogeneous data.
050   A natural method to perform low rank adaptation in federated learning is to adopt the same rank
051   r of matrices A and B across different clients. This method is referred to as homogeneous LoRA
052   (HOMLoRA), but it does not accommodate the personalized requirement of clients with hetero-
053   geneous data distributions. Recent work HETLoRA (Cho et al., 2024) highlights the importance
      of heterogeneous rank configurations to enable personalized federated learning, which proposed

      ranks rk for the heterogeneous data from clients. However, this approach suffers from two main
079
      drawbacks: (1) The initial rank for any client is fixed and in the range of predefined minimal and

      maximal ranks, which is independent of client data. However, it is possible that the clients learning

083   and maximal values of rank, the pruning parameter, and the sparsity parameter. It remains unclear
084   how to perform personalized federated fine-tuning such that the adapter is dependent on the data and
085   the procedure has a small number of tuning parameters.
086
      In this paper, we propose PF2LoRA, a novel personalized federated fine-tuning algorithm that ex-
087   plicitly incorporates heterogeneous ranks into the problem formulation. Our approach introduces
088   a two-level low-rank adaptation framework on top of LoRA. The first level learns a common
089   adapter for all clients x = {B ∈ Rm×r , A ∈ Rr×n }, while the second level facilitates individual
090   client personalization by learning client-specific and lightweight adapter yk for k-th client, where
091   yk = {Dk ∈ Rm×r̃ , Ck ∈ Rr̃×n , 0 < r̃ < r} and 1 ≤ k ≤ M . We formulate the proposed two-
092   level low-rank adaptation framework as a bilevel optimization problem: we aim to learn a common
093   adapter x to minimize the loss function given the fact the individual client adapters {yk }Mk=1 can
094   achieve the best performance when the common adapter x is given. The two-level LoRA frame-
095
      work explicitly accommodates variations in adapter matrix ranks across clients, which essentially
      circumvents the rank pruning, matrix truncation, and zero-padding in HETLoRA for the alignment
096
      of adapters. The introduced client-specific adapter actually enables the personalized adaptation for
097
      heterogeneous data. Besides, the whole framework increases negligible additional memory over-
098   head, as the second-level low rank adaptation comprises a small number of parameters compared to
099   the first level. Our main contribution is listed as follows:
100
101          • We propose a novel bilevel formulation for personalized fine-tuning on heterogeneous data,
102            and develop a two-level low rank adaptation framework to efficiently fine-tune foundation
103            model in the scenario of federated learning. The main workflow of our framework is illus-
104            trated in Figure 1.
105          • Through extensive experiments on various natural language understanding and genera-
106            tion tasks, we demonstrate that PF2LoRA significantly outperforms existing federated
107            fine-tuning baselines, providing a robust and efficient solution for personalized federated

      2   R ELATED W ORK
116
117   Parameter-efficient Fine-Tuning. There are various categories of parameter-efficient fine-tuning
118   (PEFT) techniques, where only a subset of parameters of the pretrained foundation model or a small
119
      number of additional parameters are updated to adapt to the target task. The first line of work
      includes bias update or head-tuning (Lee et al., 2019; Zaken et al., 2021; Lawton et al., 2023; Wei
120
      et al., 2021) and weight masking (Zhao et al., 2020; Sung et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021). The second
121
      line of work considers adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019; He et al., 2021a), prompt tuning (Lester
122   et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021) and low rank matrix adaptation (Hu et al., 2021). Different from
123   these works, our paper focuses on designing new federated learning algorithms based on low rank
124   adaptation with heterogeneous data, where the local client data is decentralized and not shared to
125   other clients.
126
      Federated Learning with Fine-tuning. The PEFT framework has been recently incorporated in
127
      the FL framework (Babakniya et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; 2023b; Cho et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
128   2023). However, most of them do not consider the data heterogeneity in the context of foundation
129   models. To the best of our knowledge, HETLoRA (Cho et al., 2024) is the only work which allows
130   data-independent heterogeneous ranks for each clients by a fixed rank initialization, zero-padding,
131   truncation, self-pruning and sparsity regularization. In contrast, our work promotes data-dependent
132   heterogeneous ranks of local clients by an explicit bilevel modeling and reduce the number of tuning
133   hyperparameters.

142   Low-rank adaptation (LoRA). LoRA is a technique designed to efficiently fine-tune large pre-
143

144

145

149   This decomposition allows the model to learn adaptations for down-stream tasks while keeping the
150   majority of the original weights frozen, thereby maintaining the pre-trained knowledge and signifi-
151   cantly reducing memory and computational overhead.

189

191   2 , which means the trainable parameters in the client-specific adapter are only 4 or 2 of those in

249   Detailed Implementation for Language Models. Next, we present the details about how to imple-
250   ment our framework in large language models like RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019), DeBERTa (He et al.,
251   2020), and GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019). All of these models are developed based on the transformer
252   (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture, which consists of multiple layers (e.g., L) of self-attention mod-
253   ules and feed-forward networks. The self-attention module contains query (Wq ∈ Rm×n ), key
254
      (Wk ∈ Rm×n ) and value (Wv ∈ Rm×n ) weight matrices, where LoRA is typically applied to Wq
      and Wv .
255
256   When initializing the language model for each client, we first load the pretrained weights into the
257   model and then locate all self-attention modules in every layer. For each W0i matrix in i-th self-
258   attention layer, we initialize two new matrices B i ∈ Rm×r , Ai ∈ Rr×n (r is typically set as 8 or
259   4), and then merge the multiplication of matrices B i , Ai and W0i to form new merged weight matrix
260
      W0i + B i Ai . After the model finishes initialization, we freeze all the pretrained parameters, i.e.,
261   W0 = {W00 , ..., W0L−1 } is frozen, and only matrices B = {B 0 , ..., B L−1 }, A = {A0 , ..., AL−1 }
262
      are allowed to be trained. Here {B, A} matrices form the common adapter x in equation 3. The
      above description outlines the standard model initialization procedure of HOMLoRA.
263
264   Now, let us discuss the differences in model initialization between our framework and HOMLoRA.
265   In addition to the initialization procedures mentioned above, our framework requires two matrices:
266   Dki ∈ Rm×r̃ and Cki ∈ Rr̃×n (r̃ is typically set as 4r or 2r ) to be initialized in i-th self-attention
267   layer on client k. Then the new matrix can be parameterized as W0i + B i Ai + Dki Cki . Note that
268   Dk = {Dk0 , ..., DkL−1 } and Ck = {Ck0 , ..., CkL−1 } along with {B, A} are trainable parameters of
269   the model. Here {Dk , Ck } matrices form the client-specific adapter yk in equation 3. Refer to
      PyTorch-style pseudocode 2 in Appendix B for implementation details.

501   7   C ONCLUSION
502
503   In this paper, we presented PF2LoRA, a novel personalized federated fine-tuning algorithm for het-
504   erogeneous data based on a two-level low-rank adaptation framework, where the first level aims to
505   learns a common adapter for all the clients and the second level fosters individual client personal-
506   ization. Our approach addresses the limitations of existing methods, such as data-independent rank
507
      initialization and excessive hyperparameter tuning. Through comprehensive experiments on NLU
      and NLG tasks, PF2LoRA demonstrated significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art
508
      baselines, with negligible additional memory overhead.

949   G    S TABILITY A NALYSIS
950
951   Despite that HETLoRA is a strong baseline which performs usually well on heterogeneous data.
952   However, we empirically observe that the training process of HETLoRA is not as stable as ours and
953   Centralized LoRA in Figure 3, where the training loss and perplexity (ppl) are averaged across all
954   the clients. A possible and reasonable explanation is that HETLoRA adopts dynamical rank pruning
955   and matrices truncation which directly change the intrinsic structure of local adapters, leading to
956
      unstable training. On the one hand, pruning removes some columns or rows from the original
      weights, which can degrade the model performance and require some steps of fine-tuning to recover
957
      the performance (Han et al., 2015). On the other hand, each client is required to truncate the common
958
      adapter matrices to align the matrices’ dimensions at each communication round, which inevitably
959   loses some potentially important information. In contrast, our method circumvents the alignment
960   issue of adapter matrices by assigning a uniform rank rk to the common adapter and uniform r̃k to
961   all the client-specific adapters.

1510                          r(E + F ) ≤ r[(E, F )] = r(E) + r(F ) − c ≤ r(E) + r(F ),
