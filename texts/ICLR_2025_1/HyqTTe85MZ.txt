011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
               Electrostatics solvers relate an imposed voltage to a corresponding charge den-
014            sity. Current classical methods require fine discretization and scale poorly due
015            to the construction of a large linear system of equations. We recast the problem
016            using neural networks and introduce neural electrostatics, a hybrid 3D boundary
017            element method (BEM). By using the boundary element form, we are able to
018            overcome many shortcomings of previous neural solvers, such as learning trivial
019            solutions and balancing loss terms between the domain and boundary, at the cost
020            of introducing a large integral containing a singular kernel. We handle this sin-
021            gularity by locally transforming the integral into polar coordinates and applying a
022
               numerical quadrature. We also show that previous neural solver sampling methods
               are unable to minimize the physics-informed residual, and propose a variational
023
               adaptive sampling method. This technique is able to reduce mean absolute error
024
               by 5 times, while keeping training time constant. Extensive scaling and ablation
025            studies are performed to justify our method. Results show that our method learns
026            a charge distribution within 1.2 pC/m2 of mean absolute error from a classical
027            BEM solver, while using 25 times fewer rectangular elements.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The computational modeling of electrostatics, relating electric fields to charge density, is critical in
034   the design and application of a wide range of modern technologies. Simulation of capacitor and
035   battery design (Zhan et al., 2017) relates the storage and release of electrical energy to material and
036   environmental properties, leading to improved storage solutions. Capacitive touch sensors, found
037   in smartphones and tablets, rely on simulations to adjust sensitivity and responsiveness reaching to
038   changes in electric fields (Zhou et al., 2020). As we shrink mechanical designs and electrical circuits,
039
      the electromagnetic forces dominate and their simulation is required for micro-electro-mechanical
      systems compatibility (Muhkopadhyay & Majumdar, 2006) and static electric discharge protection
040
      (Voldman & Gross, 1994). The advancement of computational tools for electrostatic field modeling
041
      is a requirement for future technologies, allowing for greater accuracy, efficiency, and innovation.
042
043   The last 80 years has seen the proliferation of computational electrostatics methods, from explicit
044   methods like the finite difference method (FDM) (Burden et al., 2016) to implicit methods like
045
      the finite element method (FEM) (Burden et al., 2016) and the boundary element method (BEM)
      (Brenner & Scott, 2003). BEM is typically referred to as the method of moments (MoM) (Harring-
046
      ton, 1993; Gibson, 2014) in the electrical engineering literature. Each method has its strengths and
047
      weaknesses, but they all fail to extend to large computational domains because of discretization and
048   memory requirements. Even BEM, which reduces the dimensionality of the problem, struggles to
049   scale to domains of practical interest, such as rooms or large vehicles. Techniques such as the multi-
050   level fast multipole method (Song, 1997) or the adaptive cross approximation Zhao et al. (2005)
051   can be applied to aid in scaling, but are still limited by the discretization and roots in classical tech-
052   niques. We seek to address this scaling problem instead by reformulating the method of moments
053   as a function regression problem using neural networks and stochastic gradient descent (Kiefer &
      Wolfowitz, 1952).

      The growing field of scientific machine learning combines the advances of modern machine learn-
055   ing with classical techniques for solving partial differential equations (PDE). The straightforward
056   application of these principles is the physics-informed neural network (PINN) (Raissi et al., 2017;
057   Cuomo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). PINNs leverage the automatic differentiation of neural net-
058   works to apply differential operators on the network and build a loss between the mapped network
059   and the forcing function at sampled points in the domain and boundary. The PINN method, how-
060   ever, often learns trivial solutions, is slow, and does not scale outside its original training domain.
061   Many variations of the original PINN method have been proposed (Lu et al., 2019; Ramabathiran &
062   Ramachandran, 2021), but meaningful extensions are difficult. A promising approach is variational
063
      PINNs (Kharazmi et al., 2021; 2020) and the combination of FEM theory with deep learning (Gao
      et al., 2022; Yu, 2017; Aylwin et al., 2023). By introducing a test function set, the PDE is recast
064
      into a weak form, trading additional network evaluations for reduced regularity requirements on the
065
      network. The variational form even reduces memory, as only lower order derivatives are necessary.
066   These applications, however, are normally evaluated in 1D or 2D domains, and do not immediately
067   remove the possibility of trivial solutions. By using a boundary element method and recasting the
068   problem with the method of moments, we are able to solve the electrostatics equation on arbitrary
069   3D geometry and reconstruct the field anywhere in space. By combining the classical methods with
070   deep learning, we present a flexible and easy to evaluate basis function in the form of a feed forward
071   neural network. Specifically, we make the following contributions:
072
073          • We present a novel 3D boundary element solver that uses a neural network basis function to
074            represent the charge density along a boundary. By formulating the problem in this manner,
075
               we avoid learning trivial zero solutions to the PDE and only need to minimize a single loss
               function, as there is no separation between domain and boundary. Once solved, the charge
076
               density can be used to compute the electric field anywhere in an infinite domain.
077
078          • We develop and empirical demonstrate a singularity removal technique. By locally trans-
079
               forming the integral into polar coordinates, we robustly handle the 1r term that arises in the
               boundary element form of the Poisson equation. Though this singularity is well explored in
080
               classical methods, we show that similar methods can be applied to neural solvers without
081
               hindering backpropagation.
082
083          • We introduce a variational adaptive sampling technique that decreases error without in-
084
               creasing training time. In our experiments, the mean absolute error, as compared to the
               finely discretized classical BEM solver, is decreased by 5 times as compared to existing
085
               loss functions, while training time is held constant.
086
087          • We validate our method on the standard problem of a flat metal plate held at a constant
088            voltage. We perform both scaling and ablation studies to both justify our decisions and
089
               show that the number of test functions is decoupled from the size of the network. Our
               baseline solution is able to represent the charge density within 1.2 pC/m2 of mean absolute
090
               error from a reference BEM solver with 25 times the rectangular elements. Training to a
091
               reasonable error on this problem takes approximately 1 hour, while inference of the charge
092            density is incredibly fast. This fast inference time is important as we must integrate the
093            network to compute fields at any point in space.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Since the original work of Harrington (1993), the method of moments has been the de facto standard
099
      for high fidelity solvers in electromagnetics. Assuming isotropic materials, the method is able to
      reduce PDEs from 3D volumes to 2D surface integrals. When the domain is large, or in many cases
100
      infinite, this method is invaluable. The surface integral equations can then be solved by standard
101
      BEM methods (Brenner & Scott, 2003), first by expanding the current or charge density by a set
102   of basis functions and then enforcing boundary conditions through a set of test functions. MoM, in
103   its standard form, produces a dense matrix which grows quadratically with the discretization of the
104   basis functions. Therefore, large objects require large matrices, often producing matrices so large
105   they cannot fit in system memory. Multiple techniques seek to address this scaling issue by discretiz-
106   ing space, recognizing that fields die off with increasing distance. The Multi-level Fast Multipole
107   Method (MLFMM) (Song, 1997) interacts these grids iteratively in a hierarchical structure. These
      interactions must be derived for the underlying equation. The Adaptive Cross Approximation (ACA)


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Zhao et al., 2005), in contrast, is a purely linear algebra technique that takes advantage of low rank
109   sub-blocks. ACA also requires no iteration and can be solved directly using block lower-upper (LU)
110   Decomposition (Burden et al., 2016). These scaling method are difficult to implement, and though
111   scaling is greatly improved, very large geometries (i.e., room scales) are computationally infeasible.
112   Instead of improving the linear solve, we seek to replace the basis function set with a neural network
113   that can easily adapt to arbitrary geometry.
114
      Reducing the 3D problem to a 2D problem comes at the cost of a newly introduced singularity
115   encountered during integration. If the singularity it not properly handled, training will not converge
116   and the solver will often produce NaNs. Singular kernels have been extensively addressed in the
117   classical literature but are relatively unexplored in the context of neural basis functions. Previous
118   work has simply ignored the singularity (Ruocco et al., 2023), which allows training to converge,
119   but introduces error. In contrast, classical singularity removal techniques separate the integral into
120   far and near terms and include the contribution of the singularity and the surrounding region. Monte
121   Carlo integration is theoretically robust to point singularities: the probability that the singular point
122   is sampled is zero. In practice, samples close to the singularity can produce NaNs, so at the cost add
123
      bias, a small exclusion region is introduced around the singularity. Instead of working around the
      singularity, other method seek to remove it from the integral. The near term can be specially treated
124
      (Taylor, 2003; Ylä-Oijala & Taskinen, 2003), while the far term integral can be easily integrated
125
      with a numerical quadrature. The simplest method casts the integral into polar coordinates, which
126   introduces a radial term canceling out the singularity. We investigate the Monte Carlo exclusion
127   and coordinate transform techniques applied to a neural network basis and derives bounds for their
128   approximation error.
129
      Engineers have begun tackling electrostatics problems with scientific machine learning, but none
130
      have yet to address the boundary element formulation or domain scaling. A straightforward appli-
131
      cation of machine learning techniques to electrostatics was through FDM, and its electrodynamics
132   parallel the finite difference time domain (FDTD) method (Tang et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2019; Yao
133   & Jiang, 2018). Adjacent work on BEM based PINNs (Lin et al., 2021; Nagy-Huber & Roth, 2024;
134   Sun et al., 2023) only considered 2D domains and a boundary integral derived from potential the-
135   ory not used in electrostatics problems. Just like their classical counterparts, the machine learning
136   based finite different methods required rigid grids and suffered from stability issues. Practitioners
137   then turned towards variational methods in the same vein as FEM, BEM, and MoM (Key & No-
138   taros, 2020; 2021). These methods mirror the classical methods and build off the more general
139   VPINN (Kharazmi et al., 2019), Deep Ritz (Yu, 2017), and Deep Galerkin methods (Sirignano &
140
      Spiliopoulos, 2018), by casting the PDE in a weak formulation. The weak form reduces the reg-
      ularity requirements on the neural network and makes the solution more stable at the expense of
141
      more network evaluations and more difficult convergence. We extend these variational methods to
142
      the electrostatics problem on an arbitrary boundary in 3D.

      4.4   VARIATIONAL A DAPTIVE S AMPLING
325
326   PINN methods can often struggle to minimize the PDE residual loss with mean squared error (MSE)
327   alone. MSE forces the network to obey the PDE on average; however, the PDE requires exactness
328   or else a nearby erroneous solution will be learned. L1 loss can help, but is a more difficult loss
329   function due to discontinuous derivatives at 0. Other works have used a hybrid loss function that
330   combines MSE and L∞ . The MSE term minimizes the residual overall, while the L∞ applies a soft
331   constraint to outliers. This adds additional complexity because the loss terms need to be balanced.
332
      Also, L∞ only penalizes the largest outlier, reducing efficiency of the approach. Instead, We propose
      an adaptive sampling scheme that uses MSE while additionally decreasing loss without increasing
333
      training time.
334
335   Taking inspiration from the residual adaptive resampling (Lu et al., 2019) algorithm, we improve
336   loss by sampling where the error is the highest; however, instead of gradually increasing the data
337   set (and, therefore, increasing the training time), we temporarily remove the test functions with the
338
      lowest error (below a hyperparameter δ) and train for a set number of epochs. After which, we
      reintroduce all the test functions back into the training set. This continual contraction and expansion
339
      of the training set allows the network to adapt to the regions with the largest error.
340
341   This error calculation requires little additional computation and no extra evaluations of the network,
342   as it leverages the output directly from the training step. We also only reduce the training set every
343   Nd epoch, so the small computation is amortized across the whole training run. By reducing the
344
      training dataset, the training time temporarily decreases as fewer evaluations are required until Nr
      when we add back the removed test functions. This is especially important as each evaluation
345
      requires integrating across the entire domain. The full algorithm is highlighted in Appendix G.

350   5.1   P ROBLEM AND M ETRICS
351
      For evaluation, we consider the problem of a 2-meter by 2-meter flat plate at constant voltage.
352
      The plate lies in the XY Plane centered at [0, 0, 0], with the normal facing along the positive z-
353   axis. Though simple, this problem poses a difficult challenge for other PINN formulations, as it
354   requires integration through a singular kernel. We validate our results using a textbook BEM solver
355   (Gibson, 2014) (Figure 2a), both at a fine “ground truth” discretization of 50 by 50 elements and a
356   discretization matching that used in the neural electrostatics algorithm. The comparison at coarser
357   discretization allows us to compare our method against the BEM solver at a set discretization. Both
358   evaluations are calculated as the average L1 distance between the solutions at the center of each
359   rectangular function. We estimate PDE residual error at each test function. We compute the absolute
360   error between the voltage inner product and the charge density inner product. To obey the physical
361
      relationship, we want the error between the left and right hand terms of Equation 5 to be as small
      and low variance as possible. For a good solution, we want to perform well on both metrics, as
362
      performing well on only one, could mean we found an incorrect, non-physical nearby solution to
363
      the PDE instead. We present additional evaluation in Appendix I.

366
367   In this section, we seek to understand how the size of the network and number of rectangular test
368   functions influences our results. 9 network sizes are considered with hidden layer depths of 1, 2,
369   or 3 and hidden layer widths of 32, 64, 128. The number of test functions are also varied from 25
370   to 100 to 225. We compare the performance of each evaluation against the BEM results shown in
371   Figure 2a. For every evaluation, the test function quadrature order is set to 3 along both x and y.
372   The grid integration scheme matches this sampling and aligns itself with the test functions. The near
373   term singularity then uses a 2 point quadrature along r and θ in each quadrant.
374   From Figure 2b, we find that the performance of the method in this problem is largely independent

      6   C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521
      We propose a novel scientific machine learning technique leveraging a boundary element formula-
522
      tion of the Poisson equation to solve electrostatics problems. The method includes robust handling
523   of the singularity introduced by the Green’s function and an integration scheme that can easily be
524   applied to quadrilateral meshes for solving arbitrary geometries. We study how the method scales
525   and demonstrate that, unlike classical methods, the neural network basis is decoupled from the prob-
526   lem discretization, allowing the method to potentially scale to larger domains. This is a key benefit
527   of PINN formulations, which leverage nonlinear mappings and gradient based optimization to cir-
528   cumvent the dense linear algebra solve that drive computation time. Ablation studies show that our
529   choice of test function, singularity removal, and novel variational adaptive training scheme produce
530   the strongest results. Neural electrostatics shows promise in solving general electrostatics problems,
531
      but it can still be optimized and improved to better rival classical methods. Training time of the
      technique is orders of magnitude longer than the BEM solver, as it uses highly optimized linear
532
      algebra solvers. There is also more to explore in test function design, such as using higher order
533
      polynomials. The collocation method could also be revisited with the residual-based adaptive dis-
534   tribution (RAD) (Wu et al., 2023) method, which improves upon RAR, but was out of scope for the
535   current evaluations. Though Monte Carlo integration performed poorly in our tests, advanced tech-
536   nique such as control variates or importance sampling could be used to improve performance. We
537   also emphasize that the proposed methods are insightful to a wide range of computational physics
538   problems by adopting similar singularity removals or variational forms. Our method improves on
539   previous learning approaches to 3D problems and develops a promising method for overcoming
      shortcomings of both classical and previous PINN methods.
