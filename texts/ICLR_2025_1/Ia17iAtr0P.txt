009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As data-driven scientific discovery increasingly demands explainable over ‘black-
012            box’ machine learning (ML) methods, Symbolic Regression (SR) that derives an-
013            alytical expressions can help identify key functional dependencies in complex
014            systems. However, traditional SR methods often suffer from (a) inefficient ex-
015            ploration due to their inability to compress the search space of equivalent expres-
016            sions, and (b) non-physical solutions that violate fundamental physics constraints.
017            We here introduce a symmetric invariant representation of candidate analytical
018            expressions using a Symbolic Graph (SG), on which the Symbolic Graph Neu-
019
               ral Network (SGNN) encodes operators, symmetries, constraints and constant
               fitting knowledge. We further develop reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms
020
               with Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) on our SGNN for SR. Such a physics-
021
               constrained graph symbolic regression (PCGSR) method effectively compresses
022            the search space for efficient SR. Experiments on synthetic and real-world scien-
023            tific datasets demonstrate the efficiency and accuracy of our PCGSR in discov-
024            ering underlying expressions and adhering to physical laws, yielding physically
025            meaningful solutions.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Symbolic regression (SR) (Angelis et al., 2023; Makke & Chawla, 2024) is an approach to unveil
031   the inherent dependencies governing the system under study in a symbolic form. Unlike traditional
032   regression techniques that adhere to predefined forms (e.g., linear, polynomial, exponential), SR
033   operates without assuming any specific model form. Instead, SR explores the space of closed-form
034   mathematical expressions, using variables and operations to find the most suitable analytical expres-
035   sions that capture the relationships of the underlying observed data. This approach balances accuracy
036   and interpretability, highlighting its potential in advancing AI for scientific discovery (Wang et al.,
037
      2019). Unlike SR, widely adopted black-box methods such as neural networks lack transparency,
      making it difficult to understand the underlying mechanisms and resulting in potentially perpetuating
038
      biases or inaccuracies in scientific research.
039
040   Symbolic regression (SR) has inspired extensive research due to its flexibility and expressive power.
041   Early methods focus on Genetic Programming (GP) (Koza, 1994; Schmidt & Lipson, 2009; Fortin
042   et al., 2012; Hernandez et al., 2019), which search the candidates by evolving expressions through
043
      selection, mutation, and crossover, avoiding brute-force methods like SISSO (Ouyang et al., 2018)
      or basis dependent methods like SINDy (Brunton et al., 2016). However, GP methods scale poorly,
044
      often yield overly complex solutions, and are sensitive to hyperparameters (Petersen et al., 2020).
045
      In contrast, modern SR methods leverage deep learning (DL) and reinforcement learning (RL) to
046   enhance heuristic search efficiency. DSR (Petersen et al., 2020) uses a recurrent neural network
047   (RNN) and risk-seeking policy gradients, excelling in simple tasks but struggling with complex ones
048   due to limited exploration. NGGP (Mundhenk et al., 2021) combines RL with genetic programming,
049   outperforming both GP and DSR on several benchmarks. MCTS-based methods (Świechowski
050   et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2022; Kamienny et al., 2023) achieve a superior exploration-exploitation
051   balance, delivering state-of-the-art performance. Deep generative models (Valipour et al., 2021;
052   Biggio et al., 2021) excel at inference but lack adaptability for out-of-distribution datasets due to
053   their static pretraining. This paper introduces a neural-guided MCTS framework, combining the pure
      Deep-RL’s exploitation capability and pure MCTS’s exploration capabilities, and further enhances

      efficiency and applicability through state space reduction by capturing invariances and constraints,
055   as detailed below.

057   Expression Representations and Symmetries
058   Despite extensive research addressing the challenge of exponentially increasing search space in SR
059   with growing complexity (number of operators and variables involved in an expression), less focus
060   has been given to the representation of expressions themselves. Most works (Petersen et al., 2020;
061   Sun et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2019) use expression trees (Makke & Chawla, 2024) to convert
062   expressions into input features for SR methods. However, such representations fail to capture the
063   symmetries and invariances within expressions, leading to redundant states for equivalent expres-
064
      sions and reduced exploration efficiency, especially for sequential encoding methods like RNNs. AI-
      Feynman (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Udrescu et al., 2020) attempted to address this by pre-training
065
      neural networks to capture modularities and symmetries to simplify SR problems into smaller sub-
066
      problems for brute-force search. However, this approach requires extra pre-training and the brute-
067   force search remains inefficient for complex systems. To address these limitations, we propose a
068   symbolic graph (SG) representation that not only inherently captures symmetries and invariances
069   without pre-training, but also uniquely identifies operator directions (e.g., −, ÷, ∧) through edge
070   features. This representation significantly compresses the search space, enhancing the exploration
071   efficiency and accelerating the convergence.
072
      Symbolic Regression with Constraints
073
074   Constraints exist in nearly all SR problems, especially for real-world problems involving constraints
075   governed by the fundamental physical laws in natural science and other principles/rules in different
076
      fields. SR methods that fail to account for these constraints can yield meaningless results. Existing
      approaches (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Tenachi et al., 2023; Keren et al., 2023) typically address
077
      this by either hand-crafted priors or penalty functions to incorporate domain-specific knowledge.
078
      Though domain-specific priors can prevent invalid expressions from generating, domain-specific
079   penalty functions as well as other hidden constraints can lead to sparse rewards, making it challeng-
080   ing for SR methods to converge. To overcome this issue, here we propose to employ a symbolic
081   graph neural network (SGNN) on SG, trained by MCTS to provide the encoding of prior knowledge
082   for domain-specific penalty functions and other hidden constraints, guiding MCTS simulations to
083   reduce reward sparsity. This approach ensures that the generated expressions adhere to the neces-
084   sary constraints while also mitigating overfitting, thereby enhancing the practical applicability and
085   robustness of SR in real-world scenarios.
086   In summary, we present Physics-Constrained Graph Symbolic Regression (PCGSR), a novel SR
087   methodology designed to address the challenges of inefficient exploration caused by redundant rep-
088   resentations of equivalent expressions and the limitations of random policy in MCTS simulation.
089   Our approach incorporates physics constraints directly into the search process to produce physically
090   meaningful results. We achieve these through 1) SG representations utilized in MCTS and SGNN
091   to effectively compress the search space by capturing symmetries and invariances within equivalent
092   expressions; 2) SGNN encoding that embeds inductive biases from MCTS and physics constraints
093
      into the SGNN representation; 3) SGNN-guided MCTS that replaces the random policy in MCTS
      simulation with an SGNN-based policy, enabling efficient exploration with encoded inductive biases
094
      and physics constraints. We validate the effectiveness of PCGSR through benchmarking on widely
095
      recognized synthetic datasets and a real-world application in materials science. The results highlight
096   the practical utility and robustness of our approach, demonstrating its capability to tackle complex
097   problems in real-world scientific discovery.

      3     M ETHODS
116
      The main challenge of symbolic regression (SR) originates from its infinite search space for mathe-
117
      matical expressions, as it encompasses extensive combinations of operators and features to compose
118
      expressions. To search for such a space efficiently, we propose our Physics-constrained Graph Sym-
119   bolic Regression (PCGSR) methodology, which strategically leverages symmetries, invariances, and
120   constraints to construct a condensed search space for accurate and physically meaningful governing
121   expressions. It is achieved through the following three key innovations :
122
123    • SG representations for expressions to capture symmetries and invariances within them;
124    • SGNN that encodes physics constraints, hidden constraints, and constraints fitting knowledge;
125
       • MCTS with SGNN-integrated simulation under constraints, that yields less sparse rewards, light
126
         cost for constants fitting, and physically meaningful solutions to real-world problems.

      3.1   S YMBOLIC G RAPH R EPRESENTATION
129
130   Sampling expressions in SR can be modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where a new op-
131   erator or feature is iteratively sampled based on the current expression state. The common approach
132   for representing this state is the expression tree (ET) (Makke & Chawla, 2024), as shown in Figure
133   1. In this structure, operators are modeled as nodes and edges represent relationships between op-
134   erators and their operands, with the tree growing from outer functions to inner ones. This approach
135   reflects the sequential construction of expressions through iterative sampling steps.
136   However, the ET representation, widely used in many existing SR methods (Petersen et al., 2020;
137   Mundhenk et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2019), has a significant limitation—
138   it fails to account for invariances regarding the operator generation sequence of expressions. For
139   example, as shown in Figure 1, two symmetric expressions (mathematically equivalent with the same
140   nodes) can have different operator-generating orderings, leading to distinct sibling relationships and
141   different tree structures. This lack of invariance modeling results in redundant representations for
142   equivalent expressions (common in polynomials or products), which reduces the learning efficiency
143   of SR models. This issue is particularly problematic for RL approaches, which may struggle to
144
      converge when faced with an unnecessarily large and diverse state space.
145   To overcome these challenges, we propose a novel expression representation called the Symbolic
146   Graph (SG), denoted as G, which converts the ET into an undirected graph-based representa-
147   tion (Figure 2). In this model, we retain the same node structure for unary operators, while binary
148   operators are represented through a combination of node and edge features, effectively unifying op-
149   erators like “+” and “−” or “×” and “÷”. Additionally, we differentiate the operands in directed
150
      binary operations such as “−”, “÷”, and “%”. This approach inherently captures symmetries and
      invariances, reducing both the search space for SR and the state space for RL. The benefits include:
151
      1) expanding commutative invariance for polynomial and product terms, 2) preserving permuta-
152
      tion invariance in the generation order by grouping consecutive operations at the same level, and 3)
153   enhancing the representation precision by uniquely distinguishing operands in directed operators.
154   Appendix B.2 provides a detailed description of our SG representation.

      3.4   C ONSTRAINTS I NCORPORATION
271
272   Constraints serve as crucial prior knowledge for SR. Their significance lies not only in confining
273   exploration to valid areas but also in guiding SR to uncover meaningful and robust solutions for
274   real-world applications. In our PCGSR model, we classify constraints into two categories:
275
       • Pre-constraints: Simple constraints testable during operation sampling, such as: basic math-
276
         ematical rules, void operations, maximum complexity (the number of nodes in SG), and hand-
277      crafted prior for a priori known physics constraints.
278
            – Strategy: Pre-constraints are incorporated by zeroing out probabilities of actions violating
279           constraints, preventing invalid expressions during operation sampling.
280
       • Post-constraints: Complex constraints requiring a complete expression for evaluation, such as
281
         hidden constraints from SR problems (e.g. log(A+?) will be invalid in the real-number realm if
282      “?” is further sampled to be “f (B)” and A + f (B) < 0), and hand-crafted penalty functions for
283      a priori known physics constraints (e.g., f (r → 0) = ∞ in Section 5.2).
284
            – Strategy: Post-constraints are incorporated by penalizing invalid outputs with zero rewards
285           after generating a complete expression.
286
287   PCGSR’s flexible constraint incorporation strategies allow users to define and categorize custom
288
      constraints for effective integration. Traditional SR methods cannot seamlessly incorporate post-
      constraints as pre-constraints to prevent invalid expressions during sampling, as these require com-
289
      plete expressions for evaluation. This limitation leads to sparse rewards in search spaces with com-
290
      plex constraints (e.g., Section 5), posing a significant challenge for sampling-based methods like
291   naive Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) or Genetic Programming (GP). These methods struggle
292   to adapt to constraint violations during certain training phases (e.g., random simulation in MCTS
293   or random crossover and mutation in GP). PCGSR overcomes these challenges by enabling self-
294   learning within MCTS through Equation 5, allowing physics and hidden post-constraint insights
295   to be effectively incorporated into pre-constraint strategies during the operation sampling phase of
296   MCTS. By leveraging the predicted prior P from Equation 2, PCGSR mitigates reward sparsity, en-
297   hances search efficiency, and proves particularly effective for real-world problems involving intricate
298   physics constraints, significantly boosting its practical applicability.
299
300   3.5   C OST E FFECTIVENESS
301
302   PCGSR excels in balancing exploration and exploitation through its neural-guided MCTS frame-
303
      work, outperforming the pure Deep-RL-based approach DSR (Petersen et al., 2020) and the pure
      sampling-based method SPL (Sun et al., 2022), as shown in Section 4. It is also significantly
304
      more cost-effective than DSR, SPL, and the neural-guided GP method NGGP (Mundhenk et al.,
305
      2021), primarily due to its efficient handling of the most computationally expensive aspect of
306   SR—constants fitting (detailed in Appendix B.1). Unlike SPL and NGGP, which rely on costly
307   constants fitting for reward evaluation at every step, PCGSR leverages SGNN’s lightweight forward
308   and backward passes to encode and predict rewards r in Equation 2. Additionally, SG represen-
309   tations reduce the search space by capturing symmetries and invariances, thereby minimizing the
310   number of constants fitting steps required. Finally, PCGSR’s computational cost is dynamically
311   adjustable via the controlling coefficient ϵ, as outlined in Appendix B.4, ensuring adaptability to
312   varying problem complexities.

380   SG reduces the search space by 59.56% (PCGSR vs. MCTS-GNN) and 57.29% (MCTS-SG vs.
381   MCTS), while SGNN further decreases the search size by 21.03% (PCGSR vs. MCTS-SG) and
382   16.60% (MCTS-GNN vs. MCTS). In terms of time efficiency (evaluations/second), the results are
383   as follows: PCGSR (52.08), MCTS-SG (49.02), MCTS-GNN (55.25), and MCTS (48.78). MCTS-
384   GNN achieves the highest efficiency due to its longer training phase and higher reliance on SGNN-
385   guided simulations, as detailed in Appendix B.4. These findings demonstrate that SGNN enhances
386   efficiency by reducing evaluation demands and avoiding costly coefficient fitting during simulations.

390
391   In this section, we employ PCGSR in a real-world application for materials science, to show its
392   efficacy in yielding physically meaningful solutions under constraints. Specifically, we focus on in-
393   teratomic potential energy prediction for atomistic simulations, which is an important area for novel
394   materials design and discovery. Though traditional methods for this area such as density functional
395   theory (DFT) (Hohenberg & Kohn, 1964; Kohn & Sham, 1965), one of the first-principles methods,
396   represent a critical and powerful solution, their ability is limited by the substantial computational
397
      cost and extensive memory requirements. As a consequence, surrogate ML models are under active
      development to expedite these simulations, among which SR has shown great promise for achieving
398
      both efficiency and interpretability. Appendix D.1 depicts the detailed problem backgrounds for this
399
      application, and Appendix D.2 depicts the practical usage of our PCGSR’s solution.

403

405   from Hernandez et al. (2019), which is detailed described in Appendix D.3. Our objective is to
406   discover the interatomic potential energy function f (·) that effectively maps the atoms’ pairwise
407   distances r to a total formation energy E. In this study, we consider four methods to learn the
408   interatomic potential energy function: 1) a GNN-based black-box method CGCNN from Xie &
409
      Grossman (2018); 2) a genetic programming-based SR method from Hernandez et al. (2019); 3) our
      PCGSR in this paper; 4) and the MCTS method which is adapted from PCGSR but uses random
410
      policy instead of SGNN to guide MCTS’s simulation, as an ablation study to assess the impact of
411
      SGNN in encoding physics constraints. Table 3 presents our comparison results for the formation
412   energy prediction, showcasing the performance of our PCGSR alongside other models. Specifi-
413   cally, GP1 and GP2 are two fitted expressions by genetic programming reported in Hernandez et al.
414   (2019). We summarize our experimental settings for baseline models in Appendix D.3.

417   5.2   P HYSICS C ONSTRAINTS
418
419   To ensure the resulting f (·) is physically meaningful, we consider the following additional physics
420   constraints besides the constraints mentioned in Section 3.4:
421    • (1) Scalar Output (pre-constraint): The output of f (r) has to be a single scalar  P to match E.
422      Because the input feature r is a vector of variable length to different samples, a  operator must
423      be included in the expression before any r.
424    • (2) Unit Consistency(pre-constraint): A scalar const should be multiplied before any polyno-
425      mial terms and in any power number in the exponential terms. It is defined to ensure that the unit
426      calculation aligns with physical meaning, as const can introduce an extra unit as a coefficient.
427
         An example is given in Appendix D.5.
       • (3) Electrostatic Repulsion(post-constraint): The energy approaches to infinite at an infinitesi-
428
         mal distance (i.e. f (r → 0) = ∞). It is due to the Coulomb’s law and a subtle consequence of
429
         the Pauli’s exclusion principle of quantum mechanics. An explanation is given in Appendix D.5.
430
431   Note that CGCNN only satisfies the scalar output (1) constraint; GP1 and GP2 satisfy both the scalar
      output (1) and unit consistency (2) constraints; and MCTS and PCGSR satisfy all the constraints.

520   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE WORK
521
522   In this study, we identified two critical challenges in current Symbolic Regression (SR) models: the
523   redundant representations for equivalent expressions due to an inability to capture inherent invari-
524   ances, and the lack of a mechanism to incorporate post constraints, which increases reward sparsity
525
      and hinders the practical applications to real-world problems. To address these issues, we introduced
      the Physics-Constrained Graph Symbolic Regression (PCGSR) method, built upon the Monte-Carlo
526
      Tree Search (MCTS) framework for its balanced exploration-exploitation trade-off, and enhanced
527
      by the Symbolic Graph (SG) representation and Symbolic Graph Neural Network (SGNN). The SG
528   representation effectively captures invariances, compressing the search space for SR, while SGNN
529   encodes post constraints and constants fitting knowledge to guide MCTS simulations, thereby reduc-
530   ing reward sparsity and improving efficiency. Benchmark results on synthetic datasets and ablation
531   studies demonstrate that the proposed SG representation significantly improves performance with
532   the compressed search space. A real-world application in materials science, involving domain-
533   specific physics constraints, further underscores the importance of SGNN in encoding these con-
534   straints to prevent overfitting and produce physically meaningful solutions.
535   PCGSR provides a comprehensive framework that enhances model accuracy, complexity manage-
536   ment, robustness, and applicability in real-world problems. Looking ahead, expanding the scope
537   of invariances to exploit deeper similarities between expressions could further simplify problem-
538   solving within the current framework. Additionally, exploring the use of pre-trained SGNN models
539   to encode general SR constraints for transfer learning could accelerate training and improve initial-
      ization, representing an intriguing avenue for future research.

719
720   B.1   T RANSPLANTATION AND C ONSTANT O PTIMIZATION
721
722   Transplantation: Inspired by the cross-over mechanism in Genetic Programming (GP), we intro-
723   duce the concept of function modularity to break down complex problems into smaller sub-problems
724
      (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Sun et al., 2022). Leveraging a divide-and-conquer heuristic search
      strategy, we store promising expressions with lower complexity than the current sampling average
725
      in a budget set. These stored expressions can then be directly inserted into newly generated expres-
726
      sions as a single operator, which we refer to as the “transplantation” operator, denoted by trans. The
727   trans operator functions as a feature that PCGSR can choose during exploration. When selected,
728   PCGSR randomly picks a subset of expressions from the budget and uses them to generate new
729   expressions, selecting the best one to calculate the action value of the trans operator. By default,
730   PCGSR enables the trans operator after half of the total epochs, with the budget set to store the top
731   n models that have a complexity lower than half of the maximum complexity.
732
      Constants Optimization: Constants play a crucial role in symbolic regression, not only for main-
733   taining unit consistency in real-world scientific problems but also for optimizing regression accuracy.
734   In our approach, constants are introduced into expressions via the const operator, functioning as a
735   feature operator. While enabling constant optimization enhances the accuracy of the regression,
736   it significantly increases the computational cost of evaluating an expression. To mitigate this, all
737   constants within an expression are optimized only once per evaluation using a non-linear regression
738   method, specifically the BFGS optimizer, as described in Petersen et al. (2020).
739
740   B.2   SG R EPRESENTATIONS FOR SGNN AND MCTS D ETAILS
741
742   To leverage the Symbolic Graph (SG) representation G as the input for SGNN encoding, we can di-
743   rectly feed G into SGNN due to the graph convolutional network (GCN)’s inherent aggregation and
744   message-passing mechanisms, which naturally preserve the symmetries encoded in SG. However, to
745
      use SG as a state in Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), we first convert G into a canonical form by
      sorting the nodes. Next, we modify the upper triangle of the sorted adjacency matrix by replacing its
746
      elements with the corresponding edge features, where ”0” denotes a disconnected edge. The diag-
747
      onal elements are replaced by the node features. Finally, we output this modified sorted adjacency
748   matrix in its Voigt form, ensuring the representation of states while preserving symmetries.
749

753   SGNN, which adopts a node updating function in the form:

      D.1   P ROBLEM BACKGROUNDS
882
883
      The physical world is composed of numerous ions and electrons, governed by quantum mechanics,
884
      often referred to as many-body problems. While density functional theory (DFT) can simulate
885   simple materials with few ions and electrons, extending DFT to larger systems is challenging. One
886   of the goal relevant to the physical properties of materials is to find an analytical approximation
887   that relates DFT-calculated potential energy to the interatomic distance (i.e. pairwise distances)
888   between atoms, adhering to Pauli’s exclusion principle. Using copper (Cu) crystal as an example,
889   we aim to identify analytical relationships between interatomic distances and total potential energy.
890   This functional form is crucial for materials science as it enables large-scale molecular dynamics
891   simulations to study materials’ mechanical, thermal, and kinetic properties and explain fundamental
892   physical mechanisms at the atomic level.
893   The following provides some explanations for domain-specific terms:
894
895          • Molecular dynamics (MD) simulates the structure and properties of materials under con-
896            stant or varying environments (e.g. temperature or mechanical strain). In MD simulations,
897            the atomic forces are computed from the derivatives of interatomic potential energy func-
898            tions with respect to the atomic distance placement, which then moves the atoms based
899            on Newton’s second law. The updated atomic positions lead to new interatomic potential
900            energy. The calculations are performed iteratively until a desired number of time steps.
901
               Statistics of total potential energy, kinetic energy, atomic forces, and stresses, etc. provide
               a systematic understanding and quantitative estimate of the physical properties of materi-
902
               als. MD simulations are often performed for systems with hundreds to millions of atoms,
903
               making them computationally expensive. This necessitates machine learning algorithms
904            like those employed in our study. Analytical expressions with low complexity and high
905            accuracy are particularly valuable for large-scale MD simulations.
906
907          • In MD simulations, the system state can be specified using ensembles like NVT (constant
908            number of atoms, volume, and temperature), allowing other properties such as pressure and
909            chemical potential to vary.
910
             • Atomistic simulations based on machine learning-derived potential energy functions use
911
               atomic species and interatomic forces to determine material properties. This method is
912
               efficient but requires accurate fitting from first-principles / quantum mechanics-based cal-
913            culations, such as density functional theory (DFT).
914
915          • Periodic boundary conditions (PBCs) enable the modeling of infinite or effectively infinite
916            systems by repeating a “unit cell” in all directions. This unit cell represents the smallest
917            section of the structure that can be repeated to create the correct crystal structure, allowing
               large systems to be modeled efficiently without losing structural or symmetrical integrity.

920          • Framework Generality: PCGSR is not limited by system size or atom types. The 32-atom
921            copper dataset is chosen to serve as a benchmark to compare PCGSR with prior work and
922            demonstrate its advantages. The derived potential energy function can be applied to larger

925
               consistent with the average system size (∼ 31 atoms/structure) of the widely used MPtraj
926            dataset (Deng et al., 2023).
927
928          • Scalability: While DFT methods are limited to simulating small systems (1–1000 atoms)
929            over very short timescales (a few picoseconds), our method enables large-scale simulations
930
               (millions of atoms) over extended timescales (microseconds)- which can then help deter-

               methods such as DFT. The analytical function from PCGSR can be applied to study real
