011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Person detection is widely used in safety-critical tasks but is known to be vulner-
014            able to physical adversarial attacks. Numerous pioneering attack methods have
015            been proposed, each claiming superior performance and exposing potential secu-
016
               rity risks. However, assessing actual progress in this field is challenging due to
               two common limitations in existing evaluations. First, inconsistent experimental
017
               setups and ambiguous evaluation metrics hinder fair comparisons. Second, the
018
               absence of a dedicated dataset for this task has led to evaluations on datasets orig-
019            inally designed for object detection, which, while informative, are inadequate. To
020            address these limitations, we present a comprehensive benchmark and introduce
021            S CALE P ERSON, the first dataset specifically designed for evaluating physical ad-
022            versarial attacks in person detection. This dataset incorporates critical factors for
023            this task, such as person scale, orientation, number of individuals, and capture
024            devices. Our benchmark includes standardized evaluation metrics and a modular
025            codebase to enhance reproducibility and transparency. Leveraging this bench-
026            mark, we conduct an extensive evaluation of 11 state-of-the-art attacks against 7
027
               mainstream detectors across 3 datasets, totaling 231 experiments. We present
               detailed analyses from multiple perspectives, examining the impact of various fac-
028
               tors on the efficacy of physical adversarial attacks in person detection. The source
029
               code and dataset will be made publicly available upon acceptance of this paper.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Person detection is a safety-critical task widely deployed in real-world applications such as au-
035
      tonomous driving (Chen et al., 2021) and surveillance systems (Thys et al., 2019). The reliability of
036   these systems is paramount, as failures can lead to severe consequences, including accidents and se-
037   curity breaches. Consequently, extensive research has proposed physical adversarial attacks against
038   these systems to uncover potential vulnerabilities (Huang et al., 2023). However, despite rapid de-
039   velopments and numerous pioneering attack methods, assessing actual progress in this field remains
040   challenging due to two primary limitations.
041
      Firstly, incongruent experimental setups and non-transparent implementation of evaluation metrics
042   impede fair comparisons. For instance, many methods employ fine-tuned detectors with varying
043   parameters. Moreover, ambiguity persists regarding whether the calculation of Average Precision
044   (AP) employs ground truth labels or benign predicted labels as the baseline, and whether the At-
045   tack Success Rate (ASR) calculation accounts for the detector’s inherent error (Huang et al., 2023).
046   These deviations from standardization hinder fair comparisons. Secondly, there is an absence of a
047   dedicated dataset. Existing methods are evaluated on traditional datasets like INRIAPerson (Dalal &
048   Triggs, 2005) or COCOPerson (Lin et al., 2014), which were originally proposed for object detection
049   tasks. These datasets only support coarse validation, precluding in-depth evaluation and analysis.
050   To address these limitations, we take action on two fronts: dataset and benchmark. First, we intro-
051   duce S CALE P ERSON, the first dataset specifically designed to evaluate physical adversarial attacks
052   in person detection. Our S CALE P ERSON dataset captures images of persons at various distances
053   across diverse real-world scenarios, including campuses, streets, forests, and indoor settings. It pro-
      vides annotations for factors critical to this task, including a person’s orientation, the number of

142   3     S CALE P ERSON DATASET
143
144   This section introduces the new S CALE P ERSON dataset, specifically designed to evaluate physi-
145   cal adversarial attacks against person detection. We first describe the construction process of the
146   S CALE P ERSON dataset and then analyze its statistical properties.

148   3.1       DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
149
150
      Dataset Acquisition. The primary principle of data collection was to provide a comprehensive set
      of person images with a uniformly distributed scale. To achieve this goal, we set up a real-world
151
      shooting setup, collecting over 5,000 person images at various scales by controlling the distance
152
      between the camera and the subject. The collected images feature diverse locations (e.g., campus,
153   streets, forests, and indoor scenes) and were captured by a variety of imaging devices, including
154   Sony, Canon, iPhone, Redmi, and Samsung. Additionally, we controlled the number of person in-
155   stances per image, ranging from 1 to 7, to support the study of whether applying multiple adversarial
156   perturbations to a single image results in additional attack gains. Some visual examples from our
157   dataset are shown in Figure 3.
158

201
      Comparison with Existing Datasets. Table 1 presents a comparative overview of popular person
      datasets and our proposed S CALE P ERSON dataset. As observed, our S CALE P ERSON dataset of-
202
      fers several significant advantages over existing datasets. Firstly, S CALE P ERSON considers a wider
203
      variety of perspectives and richer annotations. Commonly used datasets in this field, such as IN-
204   RIAPerson (Dalal & Triggs, 2005) and COCOPerson (Lin et al., 2014), do not account for factors
205   such as person scale, orientation, number of persons in an image, and indoor/outdoor settings. Note
206   that while these datasets may contain corresponding samples, they do not provide annotations for
207   these perspectives, which S CALE P ERSON does. The selected perspectives are all potential factors
208   influencing the performance of physical adversarial attacks. Compared to typical datasets focusing
209   only on instance-level annotations of persons, the diverse annotations in our S CALE P ERSON dataset
210   enable exploration of additional domains, such as multi-perspective attacks. This expanded research
211   capability can facilitate broader research opportunities and promote the development of more sophis-
212
      ticated algorithms. Additionally, the S CALE P ERSON dataset is larger than INRIAPerson but smaller
      than COCOPerson, balancing substantial performance with efficient computational resource usage.

254   Datasets. We evaluate attack effectiveness on three person datasets: (1) the INRIAPerson
255   dataset (Dalal & Triggs, 2005), a small-scale dataset containing 901 images, (2) the COCOPer-
256   son dataset (Lin et al., 2014), a subset of the MS COCO dataset that includes all images with person
257   instances, and (3) the S CALE P ERSON dataset, proposed in this paper specifically for evaluating
258   physical adversarial attacks against person detection. Table 1 provides a summary of these datasets.
259   Models. We experiment with seven widely-used person detection models: YOLOv3 (Farhadi &
260   Redmon, 2018), YOLOv5 (Ultralytics, 2020), YOLOv7 (Wang et al., 2023), YOLOv8 (Ultralytics,
261   2021), Faster R-CNN (Ren et al., 2015), Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), and DETR (Carion et al.,
262   2020), all trained on the MS COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014). For a fair evaluation, we use the
263   official pre-trained weights for all models without any fine-tuning. The Supplementary Material
264   provides download links for the corresponding weight files.
265   Attack Methods. To our knowledge, there are 11 classical methods for physical adversarial attacks
266   against person detection in the visible modality: InvisCloak (Yang et al., 2018), AdvPatch (Thys
267   et al., 2019), AdvT-shirt (Xu et al., 2020), UPC (Huang et al., 2020), AdvCloak (Wu et al., 2020),
268   NAP (Hu et al., 2021), LAP (Tan et al., 2021), TC-EGA (Hu et al., 2022), T-SEA (Huang et al.,
269   2023), AdvCaT (Hu et al., 2023), and DAP (Guesmi et al., 2024). We evaluate these methods in
      each setting.

303   Evaluation Metrics. To comprehensively evaluate attack effectiveness, we employ two primary
304   metrics: Average Precision (AP) and Attack Success Rate (ASR). AP is a widely used metric in
305   object detection tasks, summarizing the precision-recall curve into a single value. A larger drop in
306   AP indicates greater attack effectiveness. ASR quantifies the effectiveness of the adversarial attack
307   by measuring the proportion of successfully attacked instances. An attack is considered successful
308   if the adversarial perturbation causes the person detector to fail to detect the target person. ASR is
309   calculated using the following formula:
310                                                                 T PPred − T PAttack

313   where T PPred represents the number of true positives in benign sample predictions, and T PAttack
314
      denotes the number of true positives in attack sample predictions. A higher ASR indicates stronger
      attack effectiveness.

      Related Benchmarks. A benchmark in a given field can effectively evaluate progress and foster
453   development. In the adversarial attack domain, Hingun et al. (2023) introduced a large-scale real-
454   istic adversarial patch benchmark to evaluate attacks on traffic sign recognition tasks. Zheng et al.
455   (2023) proposed BlackboxBench, a comprehensive benchmark for black-box adversarial attacks in
456   the digital space. Li et al. (2023) presented the Physical Attack Naturalness (PAN) dataset for bench-
457   marking and assessing the visual naturalness of physical world adversarial attacks against vehicle
458   detection tasks. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) introduced TA-Bench for evaluating transfer-based at-
459   tacks. However, no benchmark has been established for physical adversarial attacks targeting person
460   detection. Thus, this study endeavors to address this deficiency.
461   Related Datasets. The majority of existing physical adversarial attacks targeting person detection
462   are trained and evaluated on the INRIAPerson dataset (Dalal & Triggs, 2005). This dataset, abundant
463   in full-body person instances, is conducive to optimizing adversarial perturbations. Additionally, the
464   MS COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014) is frequently employed, which exhibits substantial variability
465   in person representations, including instances where only partial body parts (e.g., a single hand) are
466   visible yet still annotated as person instances (see Figure 10). These severe occlusions introduce
467
      significant challenges for the effective application of adversarial perturbations. While these datasets
      were originally published for training and evaluating object detectors, a dedicated dataset tailored
468
      specifically for physical adversarial attacks in person detection remains absent. To address this gap,
469
      we propose ScalePerson, a novel dataset designed to meet the unique requirements of evaluating
470   physical adversarial attacks against person detection.

473   6     C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this study, we have tackled significant limitations in assessing physical adversarial attacks on
476   person detection systems. We introduce S CALE P ERSON, the first dataset tailored for this purpose.
477   Additionally, we establish a benchmark for evaluating attacks under diverse settings and devise a
478   unified evaluation protocol to enhance transparency and reproducibility in assessing attack effective-
479   ness. Leveraging these efforts, we offer quantitative analyses to provide novel insights for inspiring
480   future technological advancements.
481   Limitations. The dataset with uniformly distributed person scales may pose challenges to attack
482   algorithms. As discussed in Section 4.4, our vanilla PGD-based attack trained on the S CALE P ERSON
483   dataset produces adversarial patches that are less effective than those trained on the COCOPerson
484   dataset. The greater diversity and balance of person scales necessitate optimization processes that
485   can adapt to varying scale sizes. Future work will focus on designing attack methods that adapt to
      multiple scales and collaborating with experimentalists to augment the dataset.

       A                                    C OMPARISON OF DATASETS IN P ERSON S CALE D IMENSION
630
631
632   Figure 9 presents a comparison of the person scale distribution across three datasets, including the
633   training set, validation set, and the entire dataset. We proceed with a detailed analysis.
634
      Firstly, for the COCOPerson dataset, which is a subset of the COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014),
635
      consisting of 66,808 images and 273,468 person instances. Its strength lies in its large and diverse
636   data volume. However, this dataset is primarily designed to enhance the performance of object
637   detection tasks. Many samples within it are unsuitable for training and testing physical adversarial
638   attack methods. As shown in Figure 10, these samples contain only partial body parts of individuals,
639   sometimes just a single organ like a hand or foot. Yet, existing physical adversarial attack methods
640   typically apply adversarial perturbations to the central region of the body, assuming the presence of
641   the entire person in the image. These characteristics render the COCOPerson dataset less suitable
642   for physical adversarial attack tasks.
643   For the INRIAPerson dataset (Dalal & Triggs, 2005), which comprises 901 images and 3,874 person
644   instances, most samples contain upright and unoccluded person instances, making them suitable
645   for physical adversarial attack tasks. However, the dataset suffers from a small volume of data,
646   particularly noticeable in the validation set, where, as seen in the visualized person scale distribution
647   in Figure 9b, some scales have only a few samples or none at all. This limitation prevents the
      INRIAPerson dataset from effectively evaluating attacks at every scale.
