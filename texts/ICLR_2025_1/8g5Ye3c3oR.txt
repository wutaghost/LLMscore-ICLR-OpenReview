011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Increasing weakly supervised semantic segmentation methods concentrate on the
014            target segmentation by leveraging solely image-level labels. However, few works
015
               notice that a significant gap exists in addressing medical characteristics, which
               demands massive attention. In this paper, we note: (i) Lesion regions typically
016
               exhibit a sharp probability distribution pattern while healthy tissues adhere to an
017
               underlying homogeneous distribution, which deviates from typical natural images;
018            (ii) Boundaries of lesion foregrounds and structural backgrounds are blurred; (iii)
019            Similar structures frequently appear within specific organs or tissues, which poses
020            a challenge to concentrating models’ attention on regions of interest instead of
021            the entire image. Thus we propose a Commonality-specificity attention GAN
022            (CoinGAN) to overcome the above challenges, which leverages distribution dis-
023            crepancies to mine the knowledge underlying images. Specifically, we propose
024            a new form of convolution, contrastive convolution, to utilize the fine-grained
025            perceptual discrepancies of activation sub-maps to enhance the intra-image distri-
026
               bution, making lesion foregrounds (specificity) and structural backgrounds (com-
               monality) boundary-aware. Then a commonality-specificity attention mechanism
027
               and the GAN-based loss function are devised to jointly suppress similarity re-
028
               gions between different labels of images and accentuate discrepancy regions be-
029            tween different labels of images. This isolates lesion areas from the structural
030            background. Extensive experiments are conducted on three public benchmarks.
031            Our CoinGAN achieves state-of-the-art performance with the DSC of 71.69%,
032            84.73%, and 78.32% on QaTa-COV19, ISIC2018, and MoNuSeg datasets, mak-
033            ing a significant contribution to the detection of pneumonia, skin disease, and
034            cancer. Furthermore, the visualized results also corroborate the effectiveness of
035            CoinGAN in segmenting medical objects.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Semantic segmentation has shown substantial progress in a diverse array of computer vision tasks,
040
      e.g., autonomous driving, robotics and medical diagnosis Mo et al. (2022). However, these mod-
041
      els are heavily dependent on pixel-level annotations, which are notoriously laborious and time-
042   intensive. On the contrary, some weak supervision alternatives, e.g., image-level labels He et al.
043   (2024), points Gao et al. (2024), and bounding boxes Cheng et al. (2023), are easier to obtain.
044   Therefore, exploring the potential of weak annotations for semantic segmentation is appealing. In
045   this paper, we aim to advance weakly supervised semantic segmentation (WSSS) for medical im-
046   ages, utilizing solely the image-level annotations for supervision.
047
      Image-level WSSS is extremely challenging since these image-level labels solely indicate the pres-
048   ence or absence of the target object without specifying any location information. To counter this,
049   a pioneering approach, class activation maps (CAM) Zhou et al. (2016) endue convolutional neu-
050   ral networks with locating ability for recognizing the most discriminative regions. However, these
051   location maps inevitably suffer from sparsity (false negative) or inappropriately activate false back-
052   ground structures for the target objects (false positive) Chen et al. (2022a;b). Such incomplete cor-
053   respondence between location maps and actual object locations severely hampers the performance
      of CAM-like methods Lin et al. (2023b); Kim et al. (2024). Recent endeavors have spearheaded

      2   R ELATED W ORK
128
129   Weakly Supervised Semantic Segmentation. WSSS has attracted increasing attention via weak
130
      supervision alternatives, such as image-level annotations He et al. (2024), points Gao et al. (2024),
      and bounding boxes Cheng et al. (2023), which substantially alleviates the manual annotation bur-
131
      den. Among them, image-level annotations stand out for their minimal annotation costs, but the
132
      lack of location information presents a significant challenge. A mainstream solution, CAM Zhou
133   et al. (2016), adds a global average pooling layer to the convolutional neural networks for gener-
134   ating location maps. However, these location maps usually highlight the most discriminative areas
135   of the target or co-occurring objects, resulting in degraded segmentation performance. A plethora
136   of research efforts have been proposed to refine these location regions, e.g., semantic association
137   Zhang et al. (2020), boundary constraint Rong et al. (2023), threshold operation Lee et al. (2022b),
138   auxiliary information Xu et al. (2021); Lee et al. (2022a), new training strategies Lin et al. (2023b);
139   Zhao et al. (2024b) or network architectures Kweon & Yoon (2024). However, these methods suffer
140   from a severe performance gap when directly applied to medical images owing to more challenging
141
      medical characteristics Chen et al. (2022a).
142   Anatomical Priors. Prior knowledge is extremely crucial in semantic segmentation tasks, which
143   guides training models to converge in the correct direction Redondo-Cabrera et al. (2019). For in-
144   stance, SkelCon Tan et al. (2022) leverages skeletal priors for retinal vessel segmentation. PaNN
145   Zhou et al. (2019) incorporates abdominal anatomy priors into multi-organ segmentation. Min-
146   Max Belharbi et al. (2021) utilizes the intra-category histology variations to segment colon cancer.
147
      Swin-MIL Qian et al. (2022) reveals inter-instance correction for colon cancer segmentation. SAM-
      driven Zhao et al. (2024a) depends on the pre-trained priors for the nodule segmentation. However,
148
      these methods are mainly designed for some specific organs or scenarios, and sometimes need ad-
149
      ditional auxiliary information. This considerably restricts the model’s applicability. In contrast,
150   our proposed CoinGAN aims to explore prior knowledge directly from medical images themselves
151   without extra auxiliary information or tools.
152
153
      Attention Mechanisms. The attention mechanism is playing an increasingly important role in com-
      puter vision tasks Rahman et al. (2024). It can direct attention to the most salient regions, providing
154
      a discriminative insight that holds significant potential in WSSS tasks. Recently, a few works have
155
      attempted attention mechanisms to WSSS. For instance, Group Zhou et al. (2022) proposes a co-
156   attention mechanism to discover the semantic relations in images. MCTformer Xu et al. (2022)
157   employs the multi-head self-attention mechanism to capture global class-specific attention. Sparse-
158   VIT Hanna et al. (2023) inserts gating units to the multi-head attention mechanism for correlated
159   region sparsity control. However, to our best knowledge, there are few similar attempts in med-
160   ical WSSS. Inspired by it, our CoinGAN devises a commonality-specificity attention mechanism
161   for medical WSSS, which integrates the distribution discrepancies from the image themselves and
      attention mechanisms to investigate the knowledge underlying different labels of images.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
190

      3.1       M OTIVATION & OVERVIEW
192
      We analyze medical WSSS by two important questions. Question 1: Why do classification results
193   tend to be accurate but the segmentation performance is not satisfactory? Question 2: Why does the
194   segmentation shape severely deviate from ground truth contours? The first answer is that the clas-
195   sification model can establish associations between image-wise labels and discriminative regions.
196   These discriminative regions have a strong statistical correlation with the aligned category, that is,
197   the classification model discovers the category specificity, which is sufficient to make an accurate
198   classification prediction (a ‘shortcut’ convergence). However, previous works Zhao et al. (2024b)
199   have shown these specific regions also present a strong association with surrounding regions, this
200   is because the boundary ambiguity leads to an extended segmentation region, and further causes
201
      the oversegmentation of objects. For instance, when segmenting pneumonia lesions, we may get
      the entire thoracic cavity. The second answer is that the output results lack the constraints of back-
202
      ground structures, failing to prevent the model’s attention from deviating from object regions. This
203
      is mainly because pronounced distribution discrepancies between labels, as well as similar structures
204   within the same image label, further exacerbate the phenomenon of “shortcut” convergence, which
205   makes the model fail to effectively capture fine-grained local representations, resulting in inaccurate
206   segmentation shapes.
207
      Therefore, we propose the C-Conv module and CSA mechanism. Specifically, the C-Conv module
208
      is proposed to learn the intra-image distribution discrepancies for reducing the ambiguous bound-
209
      aries, which forms a clear distinction of different regional distributions within the image and prevents
210   oversegmentation. Subsequently, the CSA mechanism is designed to learn the inter-image distribu-
211   tion discrepancies for mitigating the inference of similar structures, which captures the similarity
212   regions and discrepancy regions between different labels of images to help enhance the shape con-
213   straints. Finally, for the pronounced distribution discrepancies across different labels, a GAN-based
214   adversarial loss function assists the model in performing the global distribution conversion and fur-
215   ther extracting valuable regions of interest, thereby forming the precise segmentation. The overview
      of CoinGAN is illustrated in Figure 2.

      tion conversion and further extract valuable regions of interest, thereby forming precise segmen-
338   tation. Specifically, dual-enhanced representations FCSA (C · FIFZ (Xp )) are fed into a Generative
339   Adversarial Network (GAN), comprising a generator G(·) and a discriminator D(·). The generator
340   G(·) transforms these dual-enhanced representations towards a converted healthy image to fool the
341   well-trained discriminator D(·). D(·) is trained to distinguish between different labels of medical
342   images. The difference between the original image and the converted healthy image serves as the
343   segmentation mask for the lesion region. This adversarial process is supervised by Ladv :

      4     E XPERIMENTS
350
351
      Datasets & Metrics. We conduct our extensive experiments on three public benchmarks: QaTa-
352
      COV19 Degerli et al. (2022) is a large-scale pneumonia benchmark dataset with 9,258 chest X-ray
353   images. Both ISIC2018 Codella et al. (2019) and MoNuSeg Kumar et al. (2019) are the challenge
354   datasets designed for skin lesions and kernel segmentation. ISIC2018 consists of 2,694 dermoscopy
355   images while MoNuSeg involves 21,623 annotations on histopathologic images. More details about
356   datasets are summarized in Appendix E. Six key metrics are utilized to assess the model perfor-
357   mance, namely Dice Coefficient (DSC), Jaccard Coefficient (JC), Average Surface Distance (ASD),
358   Accuracy (ACC), Specificity (SP), and Sensitivity (SE). Among them, the DSC, JC, and ASD met-
359   rics are specially utilized for measuring the precision of biomedical segmentation.
360   Implementation Details. We use CycleGAN Zhu et al. (2017) (a typical GAN) as the backbone
361   without pre-trained weights, which consists of three stride-2 convolutions, nine residual blocks,
362   and three 12 -strided convolutions. Our method is implemented in Python using the deep learning
363   framework PyTorch and tested on a Tesla P40 GPU with 22GB of memory. In medical WSSS tasks,
364   we use the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimizer with an initial learning rate of 2.5×1e-4 for
365   the generator. The momentum is set to 0.9 and the weight decay is 5×1e-4. For the discriminator,
366   we adopt the adaptive moment estimation (Adam) optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1e-4.
367
      Both learning rates are subjected to a polynomial decay scheduler with a decay power of 0.9. We
      train CoinGAN for 100 epochs for every dataset. In all experiments, we solely utilize image-level
368
      labels for supervision.

      4.4    H YPERPARAMETER S ENSITIVITY S TUDY
524
      To analyze the influence of the hyperparameter λ in the C-Conv module, we conduct experiments
525   with different λ values while keeping other parameters fixed. As depicted in Figure 4, overly large λ
526   values relax the limitations on boundary regions, introducing more noise to representation learning
527   and hence limiting the model performance. Conversely, overly small λ values render the convolution
528   oversensitive to changes within images. Even minor changes are mistaken for the appearance of a
529   boundary, causing the category activation maps Mc conv to filter out too many mistaken ambiguous
530   representations, which results in a loss of information. λ = 0.3 achieves the best performance,
531   striking a balance between sensitivity to boundaries and retaining medical information.

533   5     C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   In this paper, we propose CoinGAN, a novel model to address challenges posed by medical WSSS.
536   CoinGAN features two pivotal modules: the C-Conv Module and the CSA Mechanism, to un-
537   earth latent knowledge inherent in the images themselves. The C-Conv module leverages intra-
538   image representation perceptual discrepancies to eliminate the boundary uncertainty, while the CSA
539   mechanism exploits inter-image representation discrepancies for precise pathological segmentation.
      Comprehensive experiments substantiate the efficacy of CoinGAN across diverse datasets.

      F    I N - DEPTH A NALYSES OF C OMPARATIVE E XPERIMENTS
951
952   The comparative experimental results are presented in Tables 1 and 2 in the main paper. We analyze
953   the model performance from two key perspectives: metric analysis and scenario analysis.
954
      Metric Analysis. 1) Our CoinGAN achieves the best performance in all comprehensive evaluation
955   metrics on three datasets. This validates the effectiveness of CoinGAN in leveraging the distinc-
956   tive medical distribution divergence to mine the latent knowledge gap among different pathological
957   modalities. 2) We note that AffinityNet Ahn & Kwak (2018) achieves the highest SE but yields
958   inferior results in other metrics across all three datasets. This is because AffinityNet propagates lo-
959   cal responses to nearby areas based on semantic affinities, leading to representations that are biased
960   towards the positive category of positions and some false positive positions with high biological
961   affinities. This results in a high SE but low SP in the medical application scenarios. AuxSegNet
962   Xu et al. (2021) presents similar issues but its performance is slightly rectified by extra auxiliary
963
      information, e.g., saliency maps (S). On the other hand, some methods overly rely on image-level
      classification models, and the unique distribution patterns of medical images lead to rapid conver-
964
      gence through shortcuts, further resulting in incomplete exploration of discriminative regions (high
965
      SP, low SE), such as Seco. 3) Our CoinGAN demonstrates the closest performance to the Fully
966   supervised semantic segmentation (Fullsup), even keeps similar metric distributions. This suggests
967   that the knowledge discrepancy underlying medical modalities holds promising potential to com-
968   pete with manual annotations. Medical images inherently contain a wealth of information. 4) We
969   note that certain models rely on extra auxiliary information (S+I) to improve their performance, yet
970   are not effective across all application scenarios, e.g., MoNuSeg. Our PAIR effectively eliminates
971   the need for auxiliary information (S) and achieves superior performance solely using image-level
      annotations (I).

       Scenario Analysis. We discuss this part from two standpoints: the extracorporeal scenario and the
973    internal scenario. In the extracorporeal WSSS scenario, CoinGAN achieves a 2.83% boost in JC
974    and a 1.99% boost in DSC over the best-performing WSSS method on the ISIC2018 dataset. In
975    this context, extracorporeal lesions, e.g., skin cancer, are exposed to air and typically have well-
976    defined boundaries like natural scenarios, enabling them to work well in lesion segmentation, but
977    our CoinGAN still achieves a slight improvement. Simultaneously, in the internal WSSS scenarios,
978    CoinGAN demonstrates more significant performance improvements. Specifically, in pneumonia
979    segmentation on the QaTa-COV19 dataset, CoinGAN substantially achieves a 6.71% rise in JC and
980    a 6.33% rise in DSC. Additionally, in kernel segmentation on the MoNuSeg dataset, CoinGAN
981
       delivers remarkable improvements with a 17.88% increase in JC and a 17.11% increase in DSC.
       Such performance improvement can be attributed to the unique characteristics of medical images.
982
       Glass-frosted pneumonia lesions, for example, depend on nutrients provided by chest cavity tissues,
983
       having a tight relationship with the chest structure, which results in ambiguous boundaries. Sym-
984    metrical structures, besides, give rise to structural interference. Similarly, in histopathologic tissues,
985    tissues, and kernels are often intertwined, with different developmental cycles contributing to more
986    complex dependencies and elusive structural relationships. The capability of CoinGAN to address
987    these challenges makes it more effective in intra-body medical WSSS scenarios.

1095   L    E RROR ANALYSIS OF C OIN GAN
1096
1097   We perform the error analysis by elaborating on the core idea of CoinGAN - the utilization of
1098   discrepancy information. The discrepancies between different labels of medical images are pivotal
1099   for distinguishing discriminative regions (i.e., regions of interest), forming the foundation of our
1100   proposed CoinGAN. CoinGAN leverages this discrepancy information to enhance the segmentation
1101   of target objects in medical images.
1102   However, discrepancy information can be affected by potentially “inaccurate” image-level labels,
1103   which are not uncommon in clinical practice. For instance, some individuals labeled as healthy
1104   controls may exhibit subtle abnormalities in their medical images that resemble patient lesions but
1105   have not progressed to a diagnosable disease stage. These “healthy” images may introduce ambi-
1106   guity into the model’s learning process, impairing the effective use of discrepancy information and,
1107   consequently, the overall model performance.
1108   Moreover, such data quality issues stemming from the complexity of real-world clinical scenarios
1109   can also affect the performance of other weakly supervised semantic segmentation methods, as
1110   evidenced by the robustness study in Appendix M. Addressing these challenges remains a critical
1111   area for future exploration.

1147
       Discussion about the first challenge (distribution discrepancies): Most of the generative method-
1148
       based approaches can effectively address inherent distribution discrepancies. Specifically, we pro-
1149
       pose to use the generative adversarial network (GAN) within the CoinGAN model to exploit such
1150   discrepancies. Notably, some diffusion models, e.g., Hu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023a); Liang et al.
1151   (2023); Gonzalez-Jimenez et al. (2023) can present viable alternatives as backbones, which repre-
1152   sents a promising avenue for further exploration.
1153
       Discussion about the second challenge (ambiguous boundaries): We discuss three related studies,
1154
       including BoundaryCAM Prabakaran et al. (2023), CTO Lin et al. (2023a), and boundary-aware
1155
       CNNs Hatamizadeh et al. (2019). Below, we summarize the working principles of these studies
1156   with boundary-aware modules:
1157
1158
              • BoundaryCAM Prabakaran et al. (2023) employs an unsupervised clustering strategy to
1159
                extract clusters of pixels, which assist in defining an initial boundary of the target ob-
1160
                ject. Subsequently, BoundaryCAM combines Class Activation Mapping (CAM) with the
1161            Floodfill Fishkin & Barsky (1985) algorithm to refine this initial boundary and produce a
1162            fine-grained mask.
1163
1164          • CTO Lin et al. (2023a) integrates Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Vision Trans-
1165            former (ViT), and a boundary detection operator (e.g., Sobel Kanopoulos et al. (1988)).
1166            The CNNs and ViT form the encoder, capturing feature dependencies, while the decoder
1167            combines convolutional layers and the boundary detection operator to enhance boundary
1168
                segmentation. Specifically, a convolutional layer adaptively fuses the initial features from
                the boundary detection operator (Sobel operator) with the latent representations from the
1169
                encoder for boundary refinement. Ground truth boundary maps guide and supervise this
1170
                boundary learning process.
1171
1172          • Boundary-aware CNNs Hatamizadeh et al. (2019) utilize a standard encoder-decoder archi-
1173            tecture alongside a shape processing component to process feature maps at the boundary
1174            level. The shape processing component incorporates an attention layer and a dilated spatial
1175            pyramid pooling layer to jointly learn boundary information, supervised by ground truth
1176            boundary maps that distinguish boundary and non-boundary pixels.
1177
1178   Both CTO Lin et al. (2023a) and Boundary-aware CNNs Hatamizadeh et al. (2019) require ad-
1179   ditional boundary maps for supervision, making them unsuitable for weakly supervised semantic
1180   segmentation. BoundaryCAM Prabakaran et al. (2023) would require adaptation for such tasks.
1181
       Discussion about the third challenge (similar structures): The presence of consistent, similar
1182   structures within specific organs or adjacent tissues is crucial for effective segmentation. These
1183   structures require models to capture localized information rather than relying solely on global fea-
1184   tures. If not adequately exploited, such similarities can hinder models’ ability to distinguish regions
1185   accurately, especially in cases involving subtle or small lesions. For example, small lesions may be
1186   misclassified as healthy tissues, limiting models’ learning capacity. Conversely, leveraging struc-
1187   tural similarities can provide essential physiological and structural information for differentiating
       lesions from healthy tissues.

       O    D ISCUSSION ABOUT AVERAGE BUFFER AND SIMILAR TECHNIQUES
1189
1190
       To distinguish our devised Average Buffer from similar techniques (e.g., traditional prototypes and
1191   memory banks), we discuss their concepts and functionalities as follows:
1192
1193         • Average Buffer: This approach computes the average representation of a batch of data
1194           within a specific label during the model’s data update process. It focuses on capturing
1195           generalized representations across samples, which are then used as reference samples for
1196           subsequent processing.
1197         • Traditional prototypes: These typically represent the central or most representative sam-
1198           ples of a label, derived by aggregating features or samples within the label. Prototypes
1199           aim to encapsulate the core characteristics of a category, aiding the model in distinguishing
1200           between clustering centers of different categories and consolidating similar representations
1201           within the same category.
1202         • Memory banks: These are structures designed to store and manage large amounts of in-
1203           formation, such as features or historical data learned by the model during training. Mem-
1204           ory banks are dynamically updated or replaced throughout the training process, improving
1205           training efficiency and representational capacity.
1206
1207
       In summary, while our designed Average Buffer shares similarities with traditional prototypes and
       memory banks, it differs by emphasizing the computation of average representations and their dy-
1208
       namic updates, which are central to its functionality.
