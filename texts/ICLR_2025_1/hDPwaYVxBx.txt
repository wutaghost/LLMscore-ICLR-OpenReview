009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                The attention mechanism in vision transformers (ViTs) plays a key role in visual
012                processing by leveraging contextual information. In this work, we explore the
013                possibility of guiding the attention mechanism to focus more on a region of in-
014                terest by introducing visual cues to the input while avoiding intervention on the
015                internals of the ViT. This enables interaction with the ViT to prompt the model
016                to capture more information from that area during inference. A recent discovery
017                shows that placing a red circle on the input to the CLIP vision encoder causes the
018
                   model to extract more information from that marked region. However, we find
                   that this emergent property is unique to CLIP and does not apply to other vision
019
                   encoders such as DINOv2. Thus, we explore identifying visual patterns that in-
020
                   fluence the attention of different ViTs through optimisation, instead of resorting
021                to prior knowledge and guesswork. We learn visual cues that added to any input
022                image would redirect the attention of the pre-trained ViT to its location. These
023                learned prompts are optimised in a self-supervised manner, without requiring an-
024                notations, and fine-tuning of the vision transformer. 1

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The attention mechanism Vaswani et al. (2017) in vision transformers leverages contextually relevant
030   visual information from the entire input image to form embeddings for each token. This process
031   dynamically adjusts the representation of each token, allowing the model to focus selectively on
032   relevant areas, and integrates this context into the embeddings. Such context-aware embeddings
033   lead to enhanced recognition of complex visual data Dosovitskiy et al. (2021). In our study, we
034
      explore whether it is possible to direct the attention mechanism toward a specific input region by
      introducing and constructing a marker for pre-trained vision transformer models. We pursue this
035
      investigation to better understand what kind of visual information directs the attention mechanism
036
      of various pre-trained vision transformers and to explore its potential for visual prompting. Visual
037   prompting Gu et al. (2023a) refers to approaches that embed visual cues into input images with the
038   goal of adapting vision foundation models for new tasks.
039
040
      Adaptation to new tasks typically requires either pre-training the entire foundation model or fine-
      tuning it, both of which are computationally expensive compared to prompting. Recent works in-
041
      troduce manual prompting techniques, such as placing coloured circles Shtedritski et al. (2023) on
042
      images or blurring regions Yang et al. (2024) around the prompted location, demonstrating their
043   effectiveness in adapting the CLIP vision encoder for fine-grained recognition tasks. However, us-
044   ing these manually engineered prompts relies on prior knowledge of biases or emergent properties
045   formed during training. Interestingly, it has been reported that CLIP’s training data already contains
046   similar markers and blur effects Shtedritski et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024), with the marked po-
047   sitions often the focused content of the image, according to the corresponding caption, which may
048   explain the model’s responsiveness to these prompts.
049   In this work, we observe that this emergent property does not translate to other ViTs trained using
050   different learning paradigms. Notably, DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023), a purely visual self-supervised
051   model, does not respond to red circles. Identifying visual prompts for a self-supervised encoder
052   like DINOv2 is particularly important because it extracts significantly richer features from images

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
094   Visual Prompting Prompting has been extensively studied in the NLP community Liu et al. (2023).
095   Recently, researchers have begun exploring the benefits of prompting in image recognition as well.
096   This involves adding a learnable modification to images to guide models towards specific predic-
097   tions Zhou et al. (2022b); Jia et al. (2022); Bahng et al. (2022); Shen et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2023a).
098   These prompt-tuning methods Jia et al. (2022); Bahng et al. (2022); Shen et al. (2024); Zhou et al.
099
      (2022b) optimize a visual prompt that is appended and fixed to an input image. For instance, this
      could involve appending optimizable pixel regions around the image. By doing so, these methods
100
      enable the pre-trained model to adapt to new tasks without the need for retraining. This modification
101
      is typically applied universally across different images. Follow up approaches have been proposed
102   to improve these visual prompts Zhou et al. (2022a); Zang et al. (2022); Khattak et al. (2023). More
103   recently, researchers have shown that manually crafted prompts, like a red circle, can effectively
104   guide the attention of models like CLIP when trained on datasets containing similar markers Sht-
105   edritski et al. (2023). Moreover, other biases, such as blurring, can be used for prompting Yang
106   et al. (2024). However, such attention-guiding prompts only work effectively on certain models. In
107   this study, we propose learning prompts (without fine-tuning the model) to guide the attention of
      various models rather than manually designing them.

128   Adversarial Patch The seminal work Papernot et al. (2016) demonstrates that adversarial examples
129   can be generated by altering just a few pixels in the input. This white-box attack Karmon et al.
130   (2018); Liu et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2021) method can deceive models by
131   targeting a very small area. Additionally, certain studies Brown et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2020) have
132   successfully developed universal, transferable, and targeted adversarial patches. These patches are
133
      typically placed on the primary object within images. A related study Gu et al. (2022) found that
      model attention can be drawn towards adversarial patches designed to mislead classification results.
134
      Our visual prompt can similarly be viewed as an adversarial patch, aiming to divert model attention
135
      away from its original focus, but for adapting model behavior towards useful tasks.
136
137   Transferability and Universality of Adversarial Perturbation The transferability of adversarial
138   perturbation describes the phenomenon that perturbations crafted for one model can deceive another,
139   even with a different architecture Gu et al. (2023b). Different from the transferability, the univer-
140   sality of adversarial perturbation is a property that the perturbation is still effective when it is added
141
      to different input instances Chaubey et al. (2020). To improve transferability, we use generative
      adversarial perturbations Poursaeed et al. (2018). A simple way to increase the universality is to
142
      include various input images when optimizing an adversarial perturbation Moosavi-Dezfooli et al.
143
      (2017), which we also leverage in this work. The transferability and universality of adversarial per-
144   turbation have also been studied Brown et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2020); Xiao et al. (2021). These
145   intriguing properties increase the threats in real-world applications. However, how to leverage the
146   two properties for good has not been explored in the community.

151   Our goal is to learn a patch that, when applied to any part of an image, attracts more attention
152   than the initial underlying pixels of the image from the vision encoder, manipulating the image’s
153   final representation. We propose a self-supervised method to learn this patch using a frozen vision-
154   encoder and a collection of unlabeled images. We detail how the patch’s RGB color space is learned
155   via back-propagation of a self-supervised loss based on its location and attention values received.
156   Input with Prompt. We aim to discover a visual prompt (patch) that attracts the attention of a
157   transformer-based vision encoder toward a specific location. To achieve this, we work on the RGB
158   image input denoted as I ∈ Rn×n×3 where n is the number of pixels in one dimension of the image
159   and 3 indicates the three-dimensional color space. The prompt, denoted as P, is also in a similar
160   RGB color space and of size m×m (the impact of prompt size on performance is investigated in
161   section 4.1). The designated coordinates [i, j] within the image 0 ≤ i, j < n is then the exact pixel
      location where the visual prompt will be centrally inserted on. By selecting coordinates only with

      Neural Prior. To learn the optimized prompt, we aim to avoid the expensive need to fine-tune the
195
      large-scale vision encoder by employing it solely in a frozen state. In this way, the only parameters
196   being updated through the training process are the pixel values of the prompt. However, a potential
197   barrier we may encounter in efficiently achieving the optimal patch is the limited learnable variables
198   within the pixel space Ulyanov et al. (2018); Khakzar et al. (2022). Thus we parameterize the patch
199   input space with a neural network as in Ulyanov et al. (2018). Such parameterization is also shown
200   to improve the transferability of optimized perturbations Poursaeed et al. (2018). we use a neural
201   prior f starting from randomly initialized weights, that receives a random prior input η ∈ m × m
202   , and outputs an initial prompt Pprior = f (η) which is then masked by a predefined shape mask
203   P = Pprior ∗ Pmask and finally inserted centrally on the [i, j] pixel of the input image I to form
204
      the input to the vision encoder: V iT (IP ). Our neural prior f employs a CNN-based architecture, in
      particular a U-Net with three layers of downsampling and three upsampling with Sigmoid activation
205
      to ensure the values stay between [0, 1]. Leveraging shared spatial patterns among pixels in the patch
206
      enables effective optimization during training which facilitates efficient visual prompt learning Krull
207   et al. (2019).

235   4     E XPERIMENTS
236
237   In the following, we first present the learned prompts for CLIP-B/32 and CLIP-L/14, discussing
238   several design considerations and their impacts. We then evaluate the effectiveness of the prompts
239   in the context of Naming Keypoints task. We take it a step further by learning prompts for SigLIP
240   Zhai et al. (2023), DeiT Touvron et al. (2021), and DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023), each of which
241   either features a slightly different encoder architecture, or has a different training objective function,
242   or varies in pretraining datasets. Finally, we compare how the prompts impact their attention through
243   layers.
244   Implementation Details and Settings. All the vision encoders used are pretrained models available
245   from publicly accessible libraries such as Hugging Face Transformers Wolf et al. (2019) and PyTorch
246   Hub Paszke et al. (2017). Unless specified, we use a learning rate of 1e-3 and batch size of 32 and
247   train our framework for 10 epochs. All experiments were conducted on a machine equipped with a
248   single NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80GB of memory.
249   Datasets and Evaluation Metric. Training: Our framework is trained using a subset of ImageNet,
250   specifically 10 categories from Deng et al. (2009); Howard (2019), which provides over 13k di-
251   verse samples, enhancing the generalizability of our self-supervised method that does not rely on
252   labeled classes, avoiding the resource demands of using the full ImageNet dataset. Evaluation: We
253   evaluate our learned prompt on the CUB-200-2011 test set Wah et al. (2011), containing 5794 bird
254   images with 15 annotated body-part keypoints, by positioning the prompt on these keypoints and
255   measuring the accuracy based on the number of correctly identified body parts. To further evalu-
256   ate our learned prompt, we test its performance also on the RefCOCO Kazemzadeh et al. (2014),
257
      RefCOCO+Kazemzadeh et al. (2014), and RefCOCOgMao et al. (2016) datasets which consist of
      images, annotated with bounding boxes around objects in it, each of which is paired with expres-
258
      sions. More dataset details are in the appendix.
259
260   Models and Baselines. Specifically, CLIP-B/32 and CLIP-L/14 are employed for their robust zero-
261   shot learning capabilities, leveraging large-scale vision-language pretraining. SigLIP integrates im-
262   age and language understanding in a synergistic manner. DeiT and DINOv2 are utilized for their
263
      state-of-the-art performance in vision transformer architectures, with DeiT focusing on efficient
      training and DINOv2 providing self-supervised learning benefits. The baseline for comparison in-
264
      volves using the cropped region over the object bounding box in the RefCOCO dataset family. This
265
      approach isolates the relevant object from the surrounding context, allowing for a focused evaluation
266   of keypoint identification accuracy. Additionally, random location baselines are employed to assess
267   the robustness of our method against random visual prompts.

514   Limitations of our method: While our method is self-supervised and does not rely on annotated
515   training data, a key limitation is that its application and effectiveness still require annotated datasets,
516   such as CUB, for downstream tasks during inference. An interesting direction for future work would
517   be exploring methods to infer optimal locations for applying our learned prompts without rely-
518   ing on such annotations. Another limitation involves vision-only encoders like DINOv2 and DeiT,
519
      which lack zero-shot classification capabilities, preventing us from evaluating our approach on these
      models, unlike the CLIP family. Moreover, our work is constrained to open-source models, which
520
      presents a challenge for learning prompts on proprietary models due to the inaccessibility of their
521
      hidden states and attention values. Additionally, in our proposed framework, we only focused on
522   learning prompts within the RGB space and relied on predefined shape masks. Future work could
523   involve incorporating strategies for learning the shapes themselves, further enhancing the model’s
524   adaptability and precision.

529
530
531
      In this work, we proposed a self-supervised optimization-based visual prompting technique for
      guiding the attention of vision transformers, thereby avoiding the limitations of manually crafted
532
      prompts. Our method demonstrated the ability to guide the attention of various vision transformer
533
      models, such as the CLIP family, SigLIP, DeiT, and DINOv2, without requiring prior knowledge of
534   dataset biases and without fine-tuning the models. The transferability of the prompt across different
535   images was accomplished by leveraging a deep network prior. Our experiments confirmed that the
536   learned prompts successfully redirect the attention of not only the CLIP family of models which are
537   trained with language supervision, but also the purely self-supervised models such as DINOv2. As
538   new vision language foundation models are leveraging the self-supervised vision encoders for their
539   superior ability to extract visual features, the proposed optimization-based proves to be helpful for
      prompting upcoming models.
