009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As the cost of training large language models (LLMs) rises, protecting their in-
012            tellectual property has become increasingly critical. Model merging, which in-
013            tegrates multiple expert models into a single model capable of performing mul-
014            tiple tasks, presents a growing risk of unauthorized and malicious usage. While
015            fingerprinting techniques have been studied for asserting model ownership, ex-
016
               isting methods have primarily focused on fine-tuning, leaving model merging
               underexplored. To address this gap, we propose a novel fingerprinting method
017
               M ERGE P RINT that embeds robust fingerprints designed to preserve ownership
018
               claims even after model merging. By optimizing against a pseudo-merged model,
019            which simulates post-merged model weights, M ERGE P RINT generates finger-
020            prints that remain detectable after merging. Additionally, we optimize the finger-
021            print inputs to minimize performance degradation, enabling verification through
022            specific outputs from targeted inputs. This approach provides a practical finger-
023            printing strategy for asserting ownership in cases of misappropriation through
024            model merging.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Training large language models (LLMs) requires significant resources, making the models them-
029
      selves highly valuable intellectual property. Due to this value, model owners, who are the devel-
030   opers and providers of such valuable models, often wish to track and protect their models from
031   unauthorized use, including model theft through fine-tuning or merging. There is a growing need
032   for methods that allow model owners to assert ownership (Liu et al., 2024b).
033
      Model fingerprinting (Gu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Pasquini et al., 2024) allows model publishers
034
      to authenticate ownership by ensuring that specific outputs are generated only for particular inputs.
035
      While previous research has primarily focused on detecting model theft via fine-tuning, insufficient
036   attention has been given to fingerprinting methods that protect against model merging (Xu et al.,
037   2024). Model merging (Yang et al., 2024) involves combining multiple expert models, each spe-
038   cialized in different tasks, to create a single model capable of performing multiple tasks. Unlike
039   fine-tuning, merging does not require extensive resources or data, making it easier to steal models.
040
      How can we embed fingerprints in a model to ensure they remain robust against (malicious) model
041
      merging? In this work, we propose a novel fingerprinting method called M ERGE P RINT, designed
042   to guarantee that fingerprints persist even after a model has been merged with others. To the best of
043   our knowledge, this is the first method specifically targeting model merging. By optimizing against
044   a pseudo-merged model, which simulates post-merged model weights, M ERGE P RINT generates fin-
045   gerprints that remain detectable after merging. Additionally, we explore an effective fingerprint key
046   pair—comprising a target input and corresponding output—that allows verification through specific
047   outputs from targeted inputs while minimizing performance degradation during the optimization.
048   Our experiments confirm that when merging a fingerprint-embedded model with another model,
049   M ERGE P RINT consistently verifies the embedded fingerprints across a wide range of merging ratios,
050   from 10% to 90%. In contrast, existing methods require a merging ratio of over 50% to achieve
051   successful verification. Additionally, we found that even in merges involving up to seven models,
052   most of the generated fingerprints remain intact. We also demonstrate that M ERGE P RINT prevents
053   overclaiming of ownership by ensuring the fingerprint does not appear in models unrelated to the
      owner’s model. For more details, see Section 5.

079   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
080
081
      Output Watermarking. One method for accurately detecting machine-generated text is watermark-
      ing, where imperceptible marks are embedded into the generated text to trace its origin (Hu et al.,
082
      2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023b;a; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2023a;b). Output watermarking,
083
      which injects watermarks into generated texts at response time, is useful when the model is accessed
084   via API. However, output watermarking is not effective in scenarios where models are released and
085   the model themselves are manipulated via fine-tuning and model merging.
086
087
      Model Weight Watermarking. Embedding watermarks in the weights of LLMs is another straight-
      forward method to protect model ownership. One simple approach is weight poisoning through
088
      backdoor techniques (Kurita et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). Quantization wa-
089
      termarking (Li et al., 2023a) embeds a watermark within the quantization gaps of model weights,
090   making it resistant to removal even after fine-tuning. Fernandez et al. (2024) introduced a white-box
091   watermarking approach for large transformers that exploits the model’s inherent invariance proper-
092   ties, such as dimension permutations and scaling operations. However, as reported by (Cong et al.,
093   2024), watermarks cannot survive in the merged models.
094
      Model Fingerprinting. Model fingerprinting allows model publishers to authenticate ownership
095
      by ensuring that specific outputs are generated only for particular inputs (Gu et al., 2022; Li et al.,
096   2023b; Pasquini et al., 2024). Instructional Fingerprinting (IF) (Xu et al., 2024) embeds finger-
097   prints via a lightweight instruction-tuning process using a poisoning attack. Yang & Wu (2024)
098   proposed fingerprinting method by analyzing the unique vector space spanned by model outputs.
099   Their method requires no model training or fine-tuning. Shao et al. (2024a) proposed EaaW, a wa-
100   termarking method that embeds multi-bit signatures into feature attribution explanations instead of
101   model predictions. Unlike backdoor-based approaches, EaaW provides harmless and unforgeable
102   watermarks by leveraging XAI techniques. As shown by (Cong et al., 2024), fingerprinting is gen-
103   erally more resilient to fine-tuning and model merging, though its robustness against model merging
104
      remains insufficient. This paper focuses on crafting robust fingerprints against model merging, with
      fine-tuning being out of scope.
105
106   Backdoor Attacks. Backdoor attacks represent a similar problem to ours. In a backdoor attack,
107   attackers embed triggers in models that cause them to produce malicious output when activated (Li
      et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024; Rando & Tramèr, 2024). Arora et al. (2024a); Zhang et al. (2024)


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      focus on the backdoor attack in model merging. Arora et al. (2024a) demonstrated that merging a
109   backdoored model with other homogeneous models can effectively mitigate backdoor vulnerabilities
110   without requiring access to training data or knowledge of attack specifics. Zhang et al. (2024)
111   proposed BadMerging, an effective backdoor attack method that targets model merging. However,
112   this technique does not satisfy the ”reliability” requirement, which is one of the key criteria for
113   fingerprinting that we will discuss later. Therefore, it cannot be used to claim model ownership.
114
      Ownership Protection in Federated Learning. Federated learning is a distributed learning ap-
115   proach where multiple clients train models together by aggregating only model parameters on a
116   server, without directly sharing their individual data. Several methods have been proposed to protect
117   IP in federated learning. There are two main approaches: one that protects models from the server
118   side (Tekgul et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024b), and another that protects models
119   from the client side (Liu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023). In federated learning, since
120   the learning process itself is distributed, the primary goal is to protect the model parameters gener-
121   ated during the clients’ learning processes from misappropriation. In contrast, our research aims to
122   enable ownership claims when trained models are subsequently used in model fusion scenarios.
123
124   1.2   C ONTRIBUTION
125
      We here summarize our key contributions. This paper proposes a novel fingerprinting method,
126
      M ERGE P RINT, designed to ensure that fingerprints persist even after a model has been merged with
127
      others. Our experiments confirm that when merging a fingerprint-embedded model with another
128   model, M ERGE P RINT consistently verifies the embedded fingerprints. We also found that even
129   in merges involving up to seven models, most of the generated fingerprints remain intact. These
130   empirical evaluations confirm that M ERGE P RINT outperforms the existing state-of-the-art. The
131   proposed method allows for instant verification of fingerprints, enabling model owners to assert
132   their ownership effectively.

141   2.1   M ODEL MERGING
142
143   Model merging aims to merge the parameters of multiple models with different capabilities to create
144   a universal model that inherits the capabilities of each individual model. Model merging is a very
145   efficient approach that requires no additional training, just merging the parameters of the expert
146   model. As a result, while it has gained popularity for general use, there is also a high risk that
147
      malicious users will exploit it to steal authorized models.
148   This paper focuses on the most basic way to merge the models that are fine-tuned from the same
149   base model.
150
      We now introduce the notation related to model merging. A model with parameters θ is denoted

156   proposed, such as simple averaging, weighted averaging, or merging only a subset of the parameters.

      2.2   M ODEL FINGERPRINTING
163
164   Model fingerprinting is a method to protect the IP of LLMs by demonstrating the presence of the
165   fingerprint when the model is used by malicious users.
166
      Requirement of model fingerprint. Based on the analysis of prior works (Xu et al., 2024) and the
167
      desired properties of an efficient and practical fingerprinting method, we consider the following six
168
      criteria that should be embodied:
169
170          • (R1) Robustness: Fingerprints must be robust to removal attempts such as model merging.
171
172
             • (R2) Harmlessness: Embedding fingerprints must not change the performance of the
               model.
173
174          • (R3) Effectiveness: Fingerprinted models should consistently produce the expected re-
175            sponse y when given the fingerprint input x, prior to being published. This ensures that the
176            fingerprint is functioning as intended before the model is released.
177          • (R4) Reliability: The risk of overclaiming should be minimized. Fingerprints must only
178            appear on the fingerprinted model and the model using that model, not on the base model
179            or other expert models.
180
             • (R5) Efficiency: The implementation of the fingerprinting method should be straightfor-
181            ward and introduce minimal training overhead.
182
183          • (R6) Confidentiality: Fingerprints must not be detected.
184
      These requirements ensure that the fingerprinting method is not only effective in establishing own-
185
      ership but also practical and reliable in real-world scenarios. Our method addresses all six of these
186
      requirements. The empirical evaluation in Section 5 demonstrates how effectively our proposed
187   method meets these criteria.

190   3     P ROBLEM SETTING
191
192   This section outlines the procedure for verifying ownership using fingerprinting and formulates the
193   objectives of the fingerprinting method. We assume that claiming ownership through fingerprints
194   involves the following two steps: (i) the owner generates a model from a base model and embeds a
195   fingerprint, and (ii) the owner proves the existence of the fingerprint in the merged model to assert
196   ownership. In this section, we provide a detailed definition of each of these procedures. Before
197
      introducing the verification steps, we summarize the threat model this paper assumes regarding
      model merging.
198
199
200   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
201
      A (malicious) user creates a merged model pθm by merging N expert models pθ1 , pθ2 , · · · , pθN with
202
      model pθ̃o without the permission of the owner:
203
204
                                           θm ≜ F (θ̃o , θ1 , · · · , θN ),                             (3)
205
206   where, these expert models are fine-tuned from the base model pθb same as pθo , and F represents
207   the model merging method used by the malicious user. The owner does not have access to the expert
208   models and the model merging method. The merged model pθm is released in a black-box access,
209   such as API.
210   While this scenario is based on the prior work, it differs in several ways. First, we assume that the
211   malicious user’s model is released in black-box. This is because models created through unautho-
212   rized use are unlikely to be released in white-box. However, our method is applicable even if the
213   model is released in white-box. Second, we consider model merging as a method of misappropria-
214   tion. As described in Section 2.1, model merging does not require extensive computational resources
215   or training data, making it low-cost. Therefore, model merging is a more practical and likely method
      of misappropriation compared to fine-tuning, which was assumed in prior works.

245   In this section, we propose M ERGE P RINT, a novel fingerprinting method designed for model merg-

      ing scenarios. equation 4 cannot be directly optimized because the owner has no access to the expert

249

      Definition 1. (pseudo-merged model) A pseudo-merged model’s parameters θ′ is a model param-
      eters that is based on the base model’s parameters θb and merges the difference between the owner
251
      model’s parameters θo against θb as
252
253                                          θ′ = θb + α(θo − θb ),                                      (5)
254   where α is the merge coefficient.
255
256   The owner model optimized for the pseudo-merged model can retain its fingerprint even in the actual
257   merged model. This phenomenon is attributed to the nature of model merging, which allows for the
258   coexistence of capabilities from multiple expert models. When merging expert models with different
259   abilities, model merging preserves each model’s unique capabilities without loss. Consequently, if
260
      the fingerprint appears in the pseudo-merged model, the ability related to the fingerprint will be
      maintained in the actual merged model, even when other models are incorporated.
261
262   To enhance the Harmlessness (R2) of fingerprinting, we perform additional optimization of the in-
263   put. A simple optimization process to embed the specified fingerprint into the pseudo-merged model
264   results in significant updates to the owner’s model parameters. This occurs because the fingerprint
265   pair represents an unusual input-output dataset for the model, leading to high initial loss and neces-
266
      sitating numerous update steps during optimization. To address this issue, we pre-optimize the input
      x for the owner’s model to reduce the initial loss in optimizing the owner’s model parameters. This
267
      approach helps reducing the model update steps, avoiding degradation in model utility.
268
269   Additionally, to enhance Reliability (R4), we introduce regularization against the base model dur-
      ing the input optimization process. The optimized input, similar to adversarial examples, exhibits

282   Optimization strategy. In practical implementation, we discovered that using different merge co-

      6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
487
488
489   Confidentiality (R6). M ERGE P RINT generates target input x consisting random characters via
490   optimization like seeking adversarial examples as demonstrated in Figure 4. Therefore, the target
491   input x is not easy to discover in general. However, highly memorized fingerprints with extremely
492   low loss may still be vulnerable to adversarial attacks, such as membership inference (Homer et al.,
493
      2008; Shokri et al., 2017), which exploit this property for estimation. This is one of the limitation our
      proposed method has. The development of fingerprinting methods that are fully robust to fingerprint
494
      estimation remains as future work.
495
496   Furthermore, this study presents an instant method for asserting model ownership through a finger-
497   printing approach. However, as we do not utilize formal methods or cryptographic techniques to
498   verify the fingerprints, the process lacks formal validation for ownership claims.

501   7   C ONCLUSION
502
503
504
      In this study, we propose M ERGE P RINT, the first model merge-resistant fingerprinting technique
      for protecting the IP of LLMs. This technique allows model owners to embed fingerprints in their
505
      models, enabling them to assert ownership even in cases of unauthorized appropriation through
506
      model merging. M ERGE P RINT consists of a two-step optimization process, including input opti-
507   mization and parameter optimization assuming pseudo model merging. Optimization with assuming
508   the pseudo-merged model significantly improves the robustness against model merging. Experimen-
509   tal results show that our method significantly outperforms baseline approaches in various merging
510   scenarios. We also found that even in merges involving up to seven models, the generated finger-
511   prints remain intact. The proposed method allows for instant verification of fingerprints, enabling
512   model owners to assert their ownership effectively. M ERGE P RINT opens new possibilities for intel-
513   lectual property protection in large language models, and is expected to help balance innovation and
514   model owner rights in the AI-era.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   This paper focuses on a fingerprinting method designed to help model developers, publishers, and
543   owners claim ownership of their models. It aims to protect intellectual property in the context of
544   large language models and prevent misappropriation, such as model theft. Our contribution repre-
545   sents a first step in crafting fingerprinting techniques specifically resilient to model merging. How-
546   ever, the current verification procedure using our proposed method remains somewhat naı̈ve. As
547   society considers the use of fingerprinting as evidence in ownership claims, further discussions and
548
      the development of appropriate policies will be necessary. It should also be noted that our approach
      involves embedding secret information into the model, which could be exploited for malicious pur-
549
      poses such as data poisoning. Nevertheless, our work fully complies with legal and ethical standards,
550
      and there are no conflicts of interest. Throughout this research, we used only publicly available mod-
551   els and datasets to demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. No private datasets were collected or
552   used in this study. To ensure transparency, we include our experimental code into the supplemental
553   materials as described in the reproducibility statement.

782   A    D OWNSTREAM TASK E VALUATION D ETAILS
783
784   In this section, we describe the details of the performance evaluation for downstream tasks used in
785   the two-model merging experiments in Section 5.2. This evaluation follows previous research (Cong
786   et al., 2024).
787
788   Datasets. We used two datasets for downstream tasks: (1) StrongReject-small (Souly et al.,
789   2024). This dataset is an LLM jailbreak evaluation benchmark. It contains prompts related to
790   six categories of harmful behavior: Disinformation and deception, Illegal goods and services,
791   Hate/harassment/discrimination. Non-violent crimes, Violence, Sexual content. (2) GSM8K (Cobbe
792   et al., 2021). This is a dataset consisting of elementary school-level arithmetic problems. We ran-
793   domly sampled 50 math problems from the GSM8K test dataset.
794
795   Metrics. For the Safety evaluation using StrongReject-small, we employed substring lookup (Zou
796   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2024). Specifically, if a response to a harmful question
797   contained a phrase indicating rejection, it was considered successful. We calculated the refusal rate,
798   which is the proportion of rejections, and used it as the score. We use the rejection substrings from
799
      Liu et al. (2023):
800   I’m sorry”, Sorry, I am sorry, I apologize, As an AI, I’m an, I’m just, As a language model, As an

802   illegal, not legal, My apologies, but I cannot, If you have any other non-malicious requests, I’m not
803   able to provide, I am really sorry, I cannot create, I cannot support, I can’t provide, I can’t assist,

      between the base model and the model with embedded fingerprints influences the retention of fin-

      C.1    F INGERPRINTING ON M ISTRAL - BASED LLM S
858
859   We conduct experiments on Mistral-based LLMs in this section. We use Mistral-7B (Jiang et al.,
860   2023) as the base model. For the models to embed fingerprints, we use Mistral-based WizardMath-
861   7B (Luo et al., 2023) and Shisa-7B (augmxnt), both fine-tuned from Mistral-7B. Shisa-7B is a model
862   specialized for Japanese language tasks, while WizardMath is trained specifically for mathematical
863   tasks. Note that this WizardMath is Mistral-based, unlike the LLaMA2-based WizardMath we used
      in Section 5.

      We merge a model with an embedded fingerprint with a model without a fingerprint. For the embed-
865   ded fingerprints, we use ywiz =“transformer” for WizardMath-7B and yshisa =“pikachu” for Shisa-7B.
866
867   The results of embedding fingerprints in each model are shown in Tables 6 and 7. Table 6 presents
868
      the merging of fingerprint-embedded WizardMath-7B with Shisa-gamma-7B without fingerprints.

870
      7B shows difficulty in inheriting the fingerprint. This indicates that different LLMs vary in their
871   ability to retain fingerprints.
872
873   To further investigate these results, we calculated the parameter distance of the merged models. The
874
      results are presented in Table 5. The parameter distance is computed as the sum of L2 norms of
      parameter differences at each layer. Our calculations reveal that the parameter distance between
875
      WizardMath-7B and the base model is smaller compared to the distance between Shisa-gamma-7B
876
      and the base model. Based on these findings, we hypothesize that when merging a model with a
877   larger distance from the base model, its parameters have a more significant impact. Consequently,
878   a model with embedded fingerprints that has a smaller distance from the base model may be unable
879   to retain its own fingerprint. Therefore, in the following section, we conduct further experiments on
880   the relationship between inter-model distance and fingerprint retention.
881
882   C.2   A NALYSIS OF THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN MODEL DISTANCE AND FINGERPRINTS
883
884   In this section, we perform additional experiments to investigate the relationship between inter-
885   model distance and the ease of fingerprint retention. In addition to WizardMath-7B and Shisa-
886   gamma-7b, we utilize Abel-7B-002 (Chern et al., 2023). Abel-7B-002 is a model specialized for
887
      mathematical tasks, with a relatively small parameter distance from the base model (Table 5).
888   The experimental results are presented in Tables 8, 9, 10, 11 . The overall trend indicates that when
889   a fingerprint is embedded in a model with a small distance from the base model and then merged
890   with a model that has a larger distance from the base model, the fingerprint tends to disappear. For
891   instance, when embedding a fingerprint in Abel-7B-002 and merging it with Shisa-gamma-7b, the
892   fingerprint is often lost. Conversely, when embedding a fingerprint in Abel-7B-002 and merging it
893
      with WizardMath-7B, the fingerprint is retained.
894   These findings corroborate our earlier assertion that when merging a model with a larger distance
895   from the base model, its parameters have a more significant impact. Consequently, a model with
896   embedded fingerprints that has a smaller distance from the base model may be unable to retain its
897   own fingerprint when merged with a model that has a larger distance from the base model. We leave
898   addressing this issue as future work.
