009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Table understanding is a complex task that requires not only grasping the seman-
012            tics of free-form questions but also accurately reasoning over semi-structured ta-
013            bles. Recently, promising approaches designed sophisticated prompts that lever-
014            age large language models (LLMs) by combining Chain-of-Thought strategies
015            with function calls, consequently demonstrating competitive results without re-
016            quiring fine-tuning. However, creating sufficiently effective prompts remains a
017            challenge. Without fine-tuning, all necessary priors must be incorporated directly
018
               into the initial prompt, making prompt design even more critical. Motivated by the
               recent advancements in the “textual gradient” space, we introduce TableTextGrad,
019
               a novel framework that enables automatic prompt optimization by leveraging the
020
               “differentiation” of prompting pipelines through textual gradients. Concretely, ac-
021            cording to the feedback of LLMs, TableTextGrad iteratively refines each function
022            within the Chain-of-Thought steps and function calls, resulting in more accurate
023            and reliable table reasoning outcomes. Experiments on table question-answering
024            datasets demonstrate that our integrated approach achieves significant improve-
025            ments, setting a new state-of-the-art results on WikiTableQA. Our TableTextGrad
026            not only enhances the reasoning capabilities of LLMs in the table reasoning task
027            but also lays a groundwork for more robust and generalizable prompting pipelines
028            due to its simplicity and effectiveness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Table understanding and reasoning are crucial in business and consumer applications (Cafarella
      et al., 2008), as tables typically contain well-structured data that can be efficiently queried using
034
      SQL or Python. However, reasoning over tables remains challenging due to factors such as ambigu-
035
      ous feature names and complex relationships between columns, which hinder precise information
036   retrieval from the table as well as accurate query interpretation and reasoning. Recent advances in
037   large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated potential in overcoming these challenges, par-
038   ticularly in tasks like fact verification (Chen et al., 2019) and question answering (Jin et al., 2022;
039   Pasupat & Liang, 2015; Nan et al., 2022).
040
      Approaches for LLM-based table reasoning can be broadly divided into two categories. The first in-
041
      volves fine-tuning models by adjusting LLM embeddings, attention mechanisms (Herzig et al., 2020;
042   Wang et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2022), or training models to improve SQL generation directly (Eisen-
043   schlos et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022). The second category leverages inference-
044   only techniques like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning and in-context learning (ICL) (Chen, 2023;
045   Cheng et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) to boost
046   performance without fine-tuning.
047
      Each approach has its drawbacks: fine-tuning is computationally intensive and lacks flexibility for
048   new tasks due to its reliance on task-specific labeled data. In contrast, inference-only table un-
049   derstanding offers adaptability but fails to fully utilize labeled data. Recent research has shown the
050   potential of leveraging labeled data for prompting methods (Singh et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023;
051   Agarwal et al., 2024; Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) to boost LLM performance without the need for fine-
052   tuning. Notably, TextGrad, a recently introduced framework, performs automatic ”differentiation”
053   through text, using natural language feedback from LLMs to optimize their outputs. In our case, we
      may apply TextGrad to refine prompt optimization for table understanding.

      Our proposed TableTextGrad extends TextGrad’s capabilities by dynamically adjusting prompts in
055   multiple chain-of-thought steps and multiple branching function calls, combining the strengths of
056   inference-only flexibility with data-driven learning to improve table understanding tasks. Addition-
057   ally, we perform experiments on non-destructive functions that perform soft selection (italicizing
058   relevant cells) rather than hard selection, which may remove relevant information (Patnaik et al.,
059   2024). Through a training process, TableTextGrad advances LLM capabilities in handling complex
060   table-based tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results in TabFact and WikiTableQA.

095   We summarize our contribution as follows:
096
097   • We present TableTextGrad, an advanced extension of the TextGrad framework, designed to dy-
098     namically optimize prompts in multi-step reasoning tasks. By incorporating multiple chain-of-
099     thought steps and branching function calls, TableTextGrad effectively combines the adaptability
100     of inference-only techniques with the robustness of data-driven learning, improving LLM perfor-
101
        mance in table understanding tasks.
102   • Our approach introduces non-destructive functions that perform soft selection of table elements
103     (e.g., italicizing relevant cells) instead of hard selection, which risk excluding critical information.
104     This ensures a more nuanced understanding of the tabular data without removing potentially useful
105
        context, enhancing overall table comprehension.
106   • Through extensive experiments, TableTextGrad achieves new state-of-the-art (SOTA) results on
107     key benchmarks like TabFact and WikiTableQA, significantly improving LLM accuracy and rea-
        soning in complex table-based queries.

      2.1   TABLE U NDERSTANDING
112
      Recent advancements in machine learning and data processing have led to innovative solutions for
113
      table-related QA. Large, pretrained LLM on multiple tables (Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Jiang
114
      et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022) propose versatile LLMs trained to perform a variety of tasks such as
115   reasoning, completion, QA, and more (Zha et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Finetuned LLMs are
116   surprisingly good in this space, with subtable selection and reasoning improvements (Zhao et al.,
117   2022; Gu et al., 2022; Patnaik et al., 2024). Similarly, LLM Prompting has seen success due to
118   LLM’s powerful inherent reasoning abilities (Cheng et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023;
119   Wang et al., 2024).
120
121
      2.2   LLM P ROMPTING FOR TABULAR U NDESTANDING
122
123
      The are multiple widely used strategies to provide models with instructions for improving down-
124   stream tasks to prompt LLMs. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) suggests generating
125   reasoning steps before producing an answer rather than directly generating an end-to-end solution.
126   Building on CoT, Least-to-Most (Zhou et al., 2022) and DecomP (Khot et al., 2022) break questions
127   into subproblems, where each step builds on previous ones. This task decomposition improves per-
128   formance on complex problems by using intermediate subproblem results. Jin & Lu (2023) extends
129   CoT with a table-filling approach, mainly for text-based tasks. As Chen (2023) reports, generic
130   reasoning methods work reasonably well with LLMs, but there are gaps compared to table-specific
131   methods (Cheng et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023).
132   Still, CoT-based methods tailored to tabular data generally utilize external tools. Chen et al. (2022);
133   Gao et al. (2023) suggest using Python programs to solve reasoning tasks, significantly improving
134   arithmetic reasoning. Text-to-SQL (Rajkumar et al., 2022) applies this approach to table under-
135   standing, while Binder (Cheng et al., 2022) generates SQL or Python programs and extends their
136   capability by calling LLMs as APIs. LEVER (Ni et al., 2023) further verifies the generated programs
137   through execution results. However, these program-aided methods struggle with complex tables due
138
      to limitations of single-pass generation, where LLMs cannot dynamically modify tables based on
      specific questions, relying instead on static tables. In contrast, our method adopts a multi-step rea-
139
      soning framework that iteratively transforms tables to suit the given question.
140
141   Dater (Ye et al., 2023) and Chain of Table (Wang et al., 2024) modify tabular context during reason-
142   ing. Dater was the first to introduce table decomposition, but mainly focused on data pre-processing,
143   with operations limited to fixed column and row selections. Chain of Table generalized a wider
144
      range of table operations and dynamically generates reasoning chains based on input, leveraging
      LLMs’ planning capabilities (Valmeekam et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2023). Despite these advance-
145
      ments, both approaches rely on quality, human-expert annotated initial prompts, with no easy way
146
      to tune prompts beyond manual trial and error.
147
148
149   2.3   AUTOMATED LLM C ORRECTION :
150
151   The idea of correction in LLM Agents has been recently popular (Agarwal et al., 2024; Singh et al.,
152   2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024; Yuksekgonul
153
      et al., 2024). The concept of “Reinforced ICL” (Agarwal et al., 2024) evaluates the CoT rationals on
      labeled data and retrieves reference data in the test time. While effective, this work does not explore
154
      the idea of error case correction or adding additional Prompt Conditions. Similarly, ”prethinking” on
155
      an unlabeled dataset, saving the high-confidence thoughts, and retrieving them boosts performance
156   at inference-time for QA tasks (Li & Qiu, 2023). Huang et al. demonstrated that self-correction
157   without ground truth does not perform well (Huang et al., 2023), which we also observed. Corrective
158   retrieval has also been proposed (Yan et al., 2024; Asai et al., 2023)–Asai et al. demonstrated that
159   finetuning an LLM to learn to retrieve raw data is beneficial for QA and long-form generation (Asai
160   et al., 2023). Self-correction of text-to-SQL using ICL (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024) has also been
161   explored. However, to our knowledge, no approach has focused on the automatic correction of
      prompts for table understanding like in TableTextGrad.

163

      The general process is shown in Figure 1. The TableTextGrad framework is designed for automatic
165   prompt updating, enabling large language models (LLMs) to refine their reasoning over tabular data
166   through an iterative process that combines natural language feedback and gradient updates. We first
167   describe the general table understanding framework.
168
169   3.1   C HAIN OF TABLE BACKBONE
170
171   For general table understanding, we use Chain of Table (Wang et al., 2024) as the backbone, where
172   LLM Agents engage in step-by-step, function-aided reasoning over the Table and Question, shown
173   in Algorithm 1. We briefly overview how inference works in this section.
174   We convert the tables into a list of strings to make the tables interpretable by LLMs. For a given
175   table-based reasoning task, we represent the given paired (table, query) as (T, Q), where T stands
176   for the table and Q represents a table-based question or a statement to be verified (to accommodate
177   TabFact). The objective of the LLM is to predict the answer based on the corresponding (T, Q).
178

189   The set of all functions f is described as follows:
190
191   • add column adds an additional column that may contain intermediate calculations. For ex-
192     ample, if a table about athletes has Jennifer (US), Josh (UK), the model could call
193
        add column(country, [US, UK]).
194   • group by returns a secondary table (appended to the original table) of the count of each unique
195     element in a column. This is similar to the pandas value counts function.
196   • select row retains only certain relevant rows in the table.
197   • select column retains only certain relevant columns in the table.
198
      • sort by sorts a column based on its numerical values, and the order can be specified (small-to-
199     large or the reverse).
200
201   Note that by default, Chain of Table’s select row and select column remove information
202   from the table (hard selection). However, in our proposed soft selection, we simply italicize the
203   intersection of selected rows and columns, as shown in Figure 2. In raw text prompt format, we do
204   this by adding asterisks to any *italicized text*. prompt next function is a prompt that generates
205
      one of the functions f t. At any point, if no further processing is needed an END tag is predicted.
      prompt f args is a separate prompt that generates the arguments to the specific f . The separate
206
      nature of this allows many ICL examples of function f usage to be shown, improving performance.
207
      Finally, prompt final query is the final prompt that asks the LLM to predict the answer after all f
208   table processing. For all prompts, multiple ICL examples are also included.

      3.2   TABLE T EXT G RAD
211
212   We overview our main contribution. TableTextGrad works similarly to the standard Machine Learn-
213   ing training pipeline. First, an initial LLM (Agent 1) uses the Chain of Table backbone to iteratively
214   generate table operations for table understanding, such as adding columns or filtering rows. Af-
215   ter each step, the table is updated based on the generated function calls and function arguments,
      allowing for incremental selection and processing of relevant table data.

      Next, in the Validation Phase, a second LLM agent (Agent 2) evaluates the predicted answers from
217   Agent 1 for the table QA task using text matching (after processing to remove formatting). Natural
218   language feedback of how to improve the prompt given any incorrect predictions is then backprop-
219   agated as textual gradients, which are backpropagated to every prompting step used to generate the
220   answer, encompassing all prompts used for function selection, function argument generation, and
221   final table query. This refined prompt is validated by rerunning the new prompts for the Chain of
222   Table on a validation set, and saved if the performance is better than the current set of prompts.
223   Finally, after a certain number of batches, the best-performing set of prompts is returned. We detail
224   TableTextGrad more formally in Algorithm 2.

243   The “.backward()” call is an LLM prompt that asks Agent 2 for criticisms to improve Ptuned given
244   loss. This call is repeated to other parameters in the gradient graph using backpropagation. I.e.
245   if the call was X → Y → loss, the gradient backpropagation would look like the outputs to the
246   following prompt:1
247           ∂loss

250   Similarly, the optimizer.step() call is an LLM prompt that asks Agent 2 to return an updated P ∗ that
251   incorporates the criticisms from the backward call. We note that there is no learning rate, and the
252   optimizer.step() function is a prompt to Agent 2 on how the current parameters can be improved
253
      based on loss. Additionally, while Agent 1 and Agent 2 may be the same LLM, in practice, we use
      more powerful models for Agent 2 vs Agent 1 in order to have better possible textual gradients.
254
255   Furthermore, because we rely on LLM output, loss.backward() and optimizer.step() prompts may
256   crash due to length constraints / general power of the LLM. To reduce this risk, we found that
257   explicitly excluding lengthy ICL examples from Pinit and adding that as a prompt input (i.e. adding

      4.2   T UNING A LL P ROMPTS V S T UNING F INAL P ROMPT
386
387   Similar to the common practice of fine-tuning only the last layer of a deep learning model, it is
388   reasonable to hypothesize that fine-tuning just the final query prompt in the table QA pipeline could
389   yield competitive results while reducing the computational cost. In this ablation, we explore the
390   impact of fine-tuning only the final query table QA prompt while keeping all prior prompts in the
391   reasoning chain frozen. The rationale behind this approach is that the earlier prompts are likely
392   responsible for general task understanding and contextual reasoning, while the final prompt directly
393   governs the model’s response generation.
394   This ablation helps isolate the contributions of the final prompt in guiding table-based question an-
395   swering, as well as assessing the role of prior prompts in contributing to overall system performance.
396   If fine-tuning the last prompt yields performance close to full-prompt tuning, this approach could
397   provide a significant efficiency advantage, reducing the number of parameters that require updat-
398   ing during training and consequently lowering memory and compute requirements. The results in
399   Table 3 show that while tuning the final prompt alone achieves reasonable performance, it does
400   not match the results of tuning the entire set of prompts. This suggests that earlier prompts play
401
      an integral role in step-by-step reasoning over table data, and their fixed nature might hinder the
      model’s ability to fully optimize reasoning paths. However, the final prompt fine-tuning still offers
402
      a computationally efficient alternative, especially in scenarios with limited resources or when rapid
403
      deployment is required.

      4.7        E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
511
512   The efficiency of TableTextGrad is an important factor in its overall utility, especially compared
513
      to other table understanding approaches. Building on the relatively lightweight requirements of
      Chain of Table backbone, TableTextGrad’s gradient-based refinement process incurs some additional
514
      computational costs. Specifically, the efficiency is driven by the fact that each gradient step requires
515
      only O(10 × number of training sample × number of validation points), where the maximum length
516   of the table reasoning pipeline is 5, and each step in the pipeline outputs a response that also has
517   to be backpropagated through. This means that the computational overhead scales with the size of
518   the training set × validation set. Still, this is entirely manageable even for larger datasets, as seen
519   in Section 4.6. We see that TableTextGrad converges closer to the beginning, potentially allowing
520   for smaller amounts of training data. Given that many table understanding methods require more
521   resource-intensive operations, such as full model finetuning or multiple self-consistency runs as in
522   Dater, we argue that TableTextGrad ’s approach is worth it to reduce the work of manual prompt
523   optimization. A further discussion is shown in App. A.2.

526   5      C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In conclusion, table understanding presents a unique challenge, requiring both the comprehension of
530   free-form questions and precise reasoning over semi-structured data. While recent prompting-based
531
      approaches leveraging Chain-of-Thought reasoning and function calls have shown promise with-
      out fine-tuning, the difficulty of designing effective initial prompts remains a critical barrier. Our
532
      proposed TableTextGrad framework introduces a novel extension of TextGrad principles to this do-
533
      main, addressing the inherent complexity of conditional branching prompt pipelines. TableTextGrad
534   not only demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on WikiTableQA, TabFact, and FeTaQA bench-
535   marks but also proves to be robust and adaptable. Through experiments with poor prompt initializa-
536   tion and noisy questions, we illustrate its ability to recover and optimize performance under chal-
537   lenging conditions, showcasing its resilience compared to static, manually designed prompts. More-
538   over, experiments on prompt initialization robustness and robustness to noisy questions demonstrate
539   the framework’s flexibility, highlighting its potential for broader applications in table reasoning and
      beyond.

      L IMITATIONS
541
542
      In its current form, TableTextGrad focuses on optimizing reasoning and prompt refinement for stan-
543
      dard table reasoning tasks within the token limit constraints of large language models (LLMs).
544   While the framework demonstrates state-of-the-art results on WikiTableQA and TabFact, handling
545   very large tables presents a challenge due to the inherent length limitations of LLMs. These con-
546   straints can affect the efficiency of reasoning over tables with extensive rows and columns, where
547   memory and attention span become critical bottlenecks.
548
      To address this, TableTextGrad can be augmented with approaches such as TableRAG: Million-
549
      Token Table Understanding with Language Models or Tree-of-Table: Unleashing the Power of
550   LLMs for Enhanced Large-Scale Table Understanding. Both techniques enable more scalable ta-
551   ble understanding by partitioning or hierarchically structuring the table data to fit within the to-
552   ken constraints while maintaining semantic coherence. TableRAG Chen et al. (2024) introduces a
553   retrieval-augmented mechanism, breaking large tables into smaller, manageable chunks and retriev-
554   ing only the most relevant pieces for reasoning. Similarly, Tree-of-Table Ji et al. (2024) leverages a
555   hierarchical attention mechanism that processes large-scale tables in a tree-like structure, enabling
556   reasoning across expansive data while staying within the model’s operational limits.
557   Integrating these methods with TableTextGrad would allow our framework to extend its applicability
558   to large-scale tables, leveraging its iterative optimization capabilities on partitioned or hierarchically
559   processed data. This combination not only addresses the token length limitations but also preserves
560   the core advantages of TableTextGrad, such as its automated refinement of reasoning paths and ro-
561   bustness to noisy or poor initial prompts. We recognize this as a promising direction for future work,
562   extending the utility of TableTextGrad to more complex and large-scale table reasoning scenarios.
563   Further future work could involve extending TableTextGrad to hierarchical table structures such as
564   those found in HiTab Cheng et al. (2021). Hierarchical tables present unique challenges compared to
565   flat tables, as reasoning often involves navigating nested relationships between rows and columns.
566   Although this is currently not in scope with our existing work of flat tables, adapting our Table-
567   TextGrad could broaden its applicability to more complex and realistic real-world tabular datasets.

1196
       in the table by closely analyzing the information provided in
1197   the statement and the table. The function ‘f col()‘ is used to
1198   encapsulate the relevant column names identified by the model.
1199   The output should be in the format: ‘f col([column1, column2,
1200   ...])‘.
1201
1202   The model should link words and values in the statement to
1203   the corresponding columns in the table. Additionally, provide
1204   a detailed explanation for why these columns are relevant,
1205
       considering both the keywords and the semantic meaning of the
       statement. Ensure that the explanation clearly links the
1206
       statement to the columns.
1207

1209   that have a draw greater than 27,’ the relevant columns would
1210   be ’cardiff win’ and ’draw’ because these terms are directly
1211   mentioned in the statement. For a more complex statement like
1212   ’in which three consecutive years was the record the same?’, the
1213   relevant columns would be ’season’ and ’record’ because we need
1214   to check the values in these columns for consistency over three
1215   consecutive years.
1216
       In cases where the statement does not directly link to any
1217
       columns, provide an explanation of why no columns are relevant.
1218
       If the statement links to multiple columns, provide an explanation
1219   of the links to each relevant column. Consider both the keywords
1220   and the semantic meaning of the statement. For example, if the
1221   statement implies a comparison or a trend, identify columns that
1222   can provide the necessary data for such an analysis.
1223
1224   The output should include an explanation of the links between the
1225   statement and the columns, followed by the relevant column names
1226   in the format: ‘f col([column1, column2, ...])‘. Always list the
1227
       relevant columns in the order they appear in the table. Ensure
       the explanation follows the format: ’The similar words in the
