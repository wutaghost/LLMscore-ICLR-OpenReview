009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurate real-time monitoring of not only movements, but also internal joint mo-
012            ments or muscle forces that cause movement in unrestricted environments is key
013            for many clinical and sports applications. A minimally obstrusive way to monitor
014            movements is with wearable sensors, such as inertial measurement units, using the
015            fewest sensors possible. Current real-time methods rely on supervised learning,
016
               where a ground truth dataset needs to be measured with laboratory measurement
               systems, such as optical motion capture, which then needs to be processed with
017
               methods that are known to introduce errors. There is a discrepancy between lab-
018
               oratory and real-world movements, and for analysing new motions, new ground
019            truth data would need to be recorded, which is impractical. Therefore, we intro-
020            duce SSPINNpose, a self-supervised physics-informed neural network that esti-
021            mates movement dynamics, including joint angles and joint moments, from in-
022            ertial sensors without the need for ground truth data for training. We run the
023            network output through a physics model of the human body to optimize physical
024            plausibility and generate virtual measurement data. Using this virtual sensor data,
025            the network is trained directly on the measured sensor data instead of a ground
026            truth. Experiments show that SSPINNpose is able to accurately estimate joint an-
027
               gles and joint moments at 8.7◦ and 4.9 BWBH%, respectively, for walking and
               running at up to speeds of 4.9 m s−1 at a latency of 3.5 ms. We further show the
028
               versatility of our method by estimating movement dynamics for a variety of sparse
029
               sensor configurations and inferring the positions where the sensors are placed on
030            the body.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Understanding the biomechanics of injury-causing events is important for injury prevention. How-
036   ever, injuries seldom occur in controlled environments (Wallbank et al., 2024; Heiderscheit et al.,
037   2005). Therefore, in-the-wild capturing of human movement dynamics, e.g. kinematics, joint
038   torques, and ground reaction forces (GRFs), is desirable. Currently, the gold standard for cap-
039
      turing kinematics is optical motion capture (OMC), which is limited to a lab environment. In OMC,
      a person is fitted with reflective markers that are tracked by multiple cameras. Joint torques are
040
      estimated from the kinematics and force data, which are measured using force plates embedded into
041
      the floor, which further limits the environment. Applying the markers by hand is error-prone and
042   the resulting kinematics can vary between different assessors (McGinley et al., 2009). Furthermore,
043   different processing techniques can also lead to different results (Werling et al., 2022).
044
045
      An alternative to the limited setting of OMC is the use of inertial measurement units (IMUs). These
      small, lightweight sensors can be worn during sports activities. Recent studies have explored meth-
046
      ods that, based on inertial sensing, estimate poses (Yi et al., 2021; Van Wouwe et al., 2024; Von Mar-
047
      card et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2022; Roetenberg et al., 2013), forces (Tan et al.,
048   2024) or full dynamics (Karatsidis et al., 2019; Dorschky et al., 2019; 2020; Yi et al., 2022; Li et al.,
049   2021; Winkler et al., 2022). The dynamics estimations are either based on deep learning (Yi et al.,
050   2021; Winkler et al., 2022; Dorschky et al., 2020), trajectory optimization (Dorschky et al., 2019; Li
051   et al., 2021) or static optimization (Karatsidis et al., 2019). Current deep-learning methods rely on
052   supervised learning, which requires labeled data for training and, therefore, inherit the limitations of
053   OMC. As a practical example, motions like high-speed running or sprinting require a large recording
      area, and are absent in widely used public IMU datasets like DIP-IMU and TotalCapture Huang et al.

065   makes them infeasible for analyzing dynamics over a long time period, which, for example, could
066   be a running session leading to an injury. Both deep-learning and optimization-based methods can
067   handle sparse IMU configurations (Winkler et al., 2022; Yi et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Dorschky
068   et al., 2023), where not every body part is equipped with an IMU. This can make a system more
069   practical for the user, but also makes the reconstruction of human movement dynamics even more
070   challenging (Von Marcard et al., 2017). Similar to optical markers, the placement of IMUs can in-
071   troduce errors in kinematic estimation. Therefore, inferring the sensor placement from the data can
072   be highly beneficial.
073   To address these limitations, this work introduces SSPINNpose, which combines the real-time in-
074   ference of learning-based methods with the ability of optimization-based approaches to reconstruct
075   motions without relying on labeled data. The core principle behind SSPINNpose, a self-supervised
076   physics-informed learning method, is that if an estimated motion is physically correct and corre-
077   sponds to the measured IMU data, it is likely to be the correct motion. During training, the network
078   is therefore guided to generate physically plausible motions that align with IMU data through virtual
079
      sensors. We further exploit auxiliary assumptions to accelerate training, mitigate local minima or
      enforce known properties of human movement.
080
081   Our main contribution is to transform the trajectory optimization problems from Li et al. (2021) and
082   Dorschky et al. (2019) into self-supervised learning problem. We show that this method can also be
083   used for real-time inference and with sparse IMU configurations. We further demonstrate that our
084   method can be used to estimate the IMU placement. To our knowledge, SSPINNpose is the first
085
      real-time method for estimating biomechanical variables from inertial sensor data without labeled
      training data. An example of our model’s output is shown in Figure 1.

      Our work focuses on gait analysis, specifically the estimation of human movement dynamics, in-
      cluding both kinematics and the internal/external forces acting on the body. Since most dynamic
092
      motion during straight walking or running occurs in the lower limbs, particularly in the sagittal
093
      plane, we review works that either examine full-body motion or focus on this plane.
094
095
096   Deep learning for movement dynamics: In order to estimate the 3D pose of a person in real-
097   time from sparse IMU configurations, Huang et al. (2018) proposed a deep learning-based method
098   using a recurrent neural network (RNN). Subsequent work enhanced motion accuracy and allowed
099
      for flexible sensor configurations (Yi et al., 2021; Van Wouwe et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2022; Zhang
      et al., 2024). Since visually plausible motion was prioritized in these early methods, physical cor-
100
      rectness, such as accurate force estimation, became a significant next step. Therefore, Winkler et al.
101
      (2022) trained reinforcement learning agents to control torque-driven multibody dynamics models in
102   a physical simulator. Another approach, Physical Inertial Poser (PIP), (Yi et al., 2022) introduced a
103   physics module to create physically plausible motions. The physics module contains a proportional-
104   derivative (PD) controller and a motion optimizer, which also yields joint torques and GRFs, but
105   only the kinematics have been validated so far. Similar to PIP, two-stage inference methods, where
106   kinematics are first estimated with a learned prior and then dynamically updated with a physics
107   model, are established in the domain of video-based pose estimation Shimada et al. (2020); Xie
      et al. (2022); Kocabas et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2021).

      In biomechanical applications, reference data was often recorded with OMC in combination with
109   force plates, from which joint angles and torques can be estimated via inverse kinematics and in-
110   verse kinematics. Supervised deep learning models have demonstrated to accurately predict these
111   outcome variables from IMU data in a single inference step. Examples span a range of applica-
112   tions, such as gait analysis (Lim et al., 2019; Hernandez et al., 2021; Dorschky et al., 2020), slopes
113   and stair climing (Chen et al., 2020), activities of daily living (Wang et al., 2023) or pediatric care
114   Mohammadi Moghadam et al. (2024).
115   All deep-learning-based inertial pose and dynamics estimation methods to date rely on labeled data
116   for training. Therefore, these methods are unable to predict out-of-distribution movements. Further-
117   more, supervised methods inherit limitations from the reference system that was used for labelling,
118   which is usually OMC, such as eventual systemic biases or confinement to laboratory spaces. Our
119   method requires no labeled data for training as we use a fully self-supervised approach.
120
121   Optimization-based movement dynamics: To estimate movement dynamics without labeled
122   data, one can use optimization-based methods. Based on kinematics estimated by Xsens, Karat-
123   sidis et al. (2019) was first to propose the use of inverse methods to estimate GRFs and joint torques.
124   From the estimated kinematics, they used static optimization to infer the GRF, and then used inverse
125   dynamics to estimate the joint torques. They modeled the human body as a 3D musculoskeletal
126
      model with 39 degrees of freedom. However, their method has not been validated on running data
      and is not capable of real-time inference or handling sparse IMU setups. Furthermore, errors can
127
      accumulate during the multiple processing steps.
128
129   Movement dynamics can also be estimated in a single step with a trajectory optimization by finding
130   control inputs, e.g. torques, for a simulation that best fits the IMU data. A solution to this problem
131   can be found using optimal control. In optimal control, an objective function, in this case the distance
132
      between the actual and simulated IMU data, is minimized while satisfying dynamics constraints
      imposed by a multibody dynamics model. Dorschky et al. (2019) solved the resulting optimization
133
      problem with a two-dimensional musculoskeletal model with 9 degrees of freedom and 7 IMUs
134
      using a direct collocation method. However, they assumed the gait to be symmetric and periodic.
135   Furthermore, they only optimised on averaged gait cycles data from multiple trials, while inference
136   took more than 30 minutes for a single gait cycle. They later followed up with a study on sparse
137   IMU configurations under the same settings (Dorschky et al., 2023). Optimal control problems with
138   sparse IMU configurations under no symmetry assumptions have been solved by (Li et al., 2021),
139   but they relied on the detection of gait events instead. Detecting gait events from IMU data is an
140   additional error source and unreliable for fast motions. 3D optimal control problems based on IMU
141   data of 3D movements have not been solved yet, except when synthetic IMU data was used (Nitschke
142   et al., 2023).
143   Our method is conceptually related to optimal control, as we aim to find a motion that minimizes the
144   distance between actual and simulated IMU data and is physically plausible. Unlike optimal con-
145   trol, we create a surrogate model to stochastically map inputs to outputs instead of solving discrete
146   optimization problems as such. A further difference is that optimal control problems use physical
147   correctness as a constraint, while we use it as an optimization objective instead. This is similar to
148   the solving strategy of constraint relaxation in optimization. As our method relies on stochastic op-
149
      timization through a deep learning model, we use first-order solvers, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba,
      2017), instead of second-order solvers that are commonly used in optimal control problems, such
150
      as IPOPT (Wächter & Biegler, 2006). Our method is advantageous in terms of pre-processing, as
151
      we do not need to detect gait events (Li et al., 2021) or extract gait cycles under the assumption that
152   these are periodic (Dorschky et al., 2019).
153
154
      From optimization problems to self-supervised learning: Our work is based on the idea of
155
      transforming an optimization problem into a self-supervised learning problem. This have the ad-
156   vantage of speeding up the simulations, while not requiring labeled data. This approach has been
157   used in various fields. For example, for 3D human (Schmidtke et al., 2023) and hand (Wan et al.,
158   2019) shape matching, the shape of a hand or human body was predicted from a single image with
159   a neural network. The shape, was then (neurally) rendered and compared to the input image. As the
160   rendering process is differentiable, they can backpropagate the error to the neural network. A similar
161   approach was used for the design of RF pulses in MRI (Jang et al., 2024), where the an optimal RF
      pulse prior was learned via MRI simulations. Self-supervised learning is also used in cloth simula-

      tion, where the neural network predicts the mechanics of clothing during movement, which is then
163   evaluated based on physical plausibility (Bertiche et al., 2021; Santesteban et al., 2022). Optimiza-
164   tion problems are very specific to the task, which is also reflected in their respective self-supervised
165   learning methods. In SSPINNpose, we reconstruct our input signal, comparable to Wan et al. (2019),
166   and aim for physical plausibility as in Santesteban et al. (2022).

171   3.1     P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
172
173   Our goal is to reconstruct lower body movement dynamics in the sagittal plane using IMUs. We aim
174   to achieve this in a fully self-supervised manner, meaning that no labeled data for the outputs will
175   be available during training.
176   The input consists of sequential two-dimensional accelerometer and gyroscope measurements from
177   up to seven IMUs placed on the feet, shanks, thighs, and pelvis, alongside body constants that define
178   the parameters of a multibody dynamics model. The outputs are the kinematics of the lower body,
179   including root rotation and translations, joint angles, joint torques and GRF. All outcome parameters
180   are directly estimated by the neural network, except for the GRFs, which are estimated with a ground
181   contact model based on the kinematics output. We describe our method in the following section.
182
183   3.2     SSPINN POSE
184
185   We introduce SSPINNpose, a self-supervised physics-informed neural network designed to learn
186   human movement dynamics from IMU data without labels. The term ”physics-informed” refers to
187   the integration of Kane’s equations and a temporal consistency loss, which ensures that the estimated
188   velocities and accelerations align with changes in position and velocity over time. Temporal consis-
189   tency describes that the velocities and accelerations are consistent with the changes in position and
190   velocities, respectively. The self-supervised aspect relates to the reconstruction of the IMU data,
191
      allowing the model to learn from the inherent structure of the input signals. To ensure stable and
      fast training, we introduce further auxiliary losses that are based on either common assumptions in
192
      human movement or known properties of inertial sensors. In summary, SSPINNpose is trained with
193
      a weighted combination of the core (section 3.2.2) and auxiliary losses (section 3.2.3), which will
194   be introduced in the following sections (see A for more details):

241   3.2.2   P HYSICS I NFORMATION AND S ELF -S UPERVISION
242
      The main idea behind SSPINNpose is that a motion that is physically plausible and consistent with
243   the IMU data is likely to be the correct motion. We enforce this by the following loss functions:
244   Kane’s loss (LK ), temporal consistency loss (LT ) and IMU reconstruction loss (LIM U ). These core
245   components of SSPINNpose are illustrated in Figure 2.
246
247   Multibody Dynamics Model & Kane’s Equations: Our multibody dynamics model is a sagittal-
248   plane lower limb model with 2 translational and 7 rotational degrees of freedom, which correspond
249   to the generalized coordinates. The body consists of 7 segments: one trunk, and a thigh, shank, and
250   foot for each leg. The body constants contain the mass, length, center of mass and moment of inertia
251   for each segment. The body constants are linearly scaled based on the participant’s height (Winter,
252   2009). The forces scale linearly with the bodyweight, therefore, we set it to 1 kg.
253   Using this dynamics model, we calculate the equations of motion based on Kane’s method (Kane &
254   Levinson, 1985), implemented in SymPy (Meurer et al., 2017). Kane’s formulation is advantageous
255   for deep learning as it is the method that requires fewer equations to be solved to describe movement
256   dynamics. Kane defined that the sum of internal (Fr∗ ) and external (Fr ) forces acting on a system is
257   zero. Therefore, we can define a loss term that enforces the physical plausibility of each estimated
258   state:
259
                                        LK = |Fr∗ + Fr | = f (ŷ, θb , Fgc ) .                           (2)

261

262   The vertical component of the GRF is modeled as a linear spring-damper system as in van den
263   Bogert et al. (2011), while the horizontal component is modeled as a friction cone with a learned

288   3.2.3   AUXILIARY L OSSES
289
290
      This section describes the auxiliary losses that we use to accelerate training, mitigate local minima
      or enforce known properties of human movement. For more details and an abliation study to justify
291
      these losses, refer to the supplementary sections A and C.
292
293
      Joint Limit and Ground Contact Force Bounds (LB ): We penalize the model for exceeding
294   joint limits and for violating bounds on maximum velocity and vertical position (see A). Addition-
295   ally, we assume that for each sequence, each foot supports at least 20% of the body weight. In
296   practice, this avoids local minima where the model does not predict any ground contact or skips on
297   one foot.
298
299   Torque Minimization (Lτ ): We apply a small weight on speed-weighted torque minimization, as
300   minimizing effort is a common assumption in human movement and usually leads to more natural
301   motions (van den Bogert et al., 2011). Similar to Dorschky et al. (2019), we normalize the torques
302   by the maximum speed of the root translation in the sagittal plane. As our training data might con-
303   tain some non-movement phases, the speed normalization only applies to sequences with estimated
304
      moving speeds greater than 1 m s−1 .
305
      Sliding Penalty (Lslide ): To prevent foot sliding when a ground reaction force (GRF) is present,
306
      we define sliding as the product of foot-ground speed and vertical GRF. This formulation ensures
307
      that at least one of these variables is constrained to be zero.
308
309   Foot Speed (LF S ): To speed up the training process and make our model less susceptible to
310   local minima, we make use of known properties of foot-worn IMUs by reconstructing their global
311   velocities (ṗK,x ) using a Kalman filter with zero-velocity updates (Solà, 2017; Simon Colomar
312   et al., 2012), as implemented in Küderle et al. (2024). This algorithm is based on integration of the
313   IMU signals which accumulates errors from drift and noise. Furthermore, zero-velocity updates are
314   unreliable during running. In consequence, we treat these reconstructed speeds as erroneous and
315   only apply a penalty when the estimated foot-worn IMU speed from our kinematics differs by more
316   than 30% from its reconstructed maximum speed during the sequence.

      Dataset We use the ”Lower-body Inertial Sensor and Optical Motion Capture Recordings of Walk-
325   ing and Running” dataset for training and evaluation (Dorschky et al., 2024). The dataset contains
326   data of persons walking and running through an area equipped with OMC cameras and a single force
327   plate, along with continuous IMU signals. For every trial, the OMC data contains roughly 5 m of
328   kinematics data and force plate data for a single step. We downsampled the IMU signals to 100 Hz.
329   The dataset includes data from 10 participants, each performing 10 trials at 6 different speeds, rang-
330   ing from 0.9 m s−1 to 4.9 m s−1 . For each condition, the first 7 trials were designated for training,
331   while the remaining 3 were used for evaluation.
332   We selected the training data by applying a heuristic that identifies standing and turning phases
333   based on the foot and pelvis IMU signals, respectively. This was done to include the run-up to the
334   motion capture area and some steps after the motion capture area in our training set, while avoiding
335   turning phases that we cannot reconstruct with a two-dimensional model. In total, our training data
336   consists of 76 minutes of unlabeled IMU data. We processed the OMC and force plate data with
337   addBiomechanics (Werling et al., 2022) to compare the resulting joint angles and joint torques. The
338   first participant was excluded from addBiomechanics because of erroneous force plate readings.
339
      During training, we randomly selected sequences of 256 time steps from the training data, while
      full sequences were used during evaluation. Typical sequences from the datasets are visualized in
340
      Figures 1 and 7. This dataset has been used by several other works focussing on sagittal-plane lower
341
      limb dynamics (Dorschky et al., 2019; 2020; 2023).
342
343
344   Metrics: We use the following metrics to evaluate our model: 1.) Joint Angle Error (JAE): The
345
      root mean square deviation (RMSD) between the estimated joint angles and those obtained from ad-
      dBiomechanics, including the root orientation, in degrees. 2.) Joint Torque Error (JTE): The RMSD
346
      between the estimated joint torques and those obtained from addBiomechanics, in bodyweight-
347
      bodyheight percent (BWBH%). 3.) GRF Error (GRFE): The root mean square error (RMSE)
348   between the estimated GRFs and those obtained from the force plate, normalized by the body-
349   weight, in bodyweight percent (BW%). The GRF is the only outcome variable that can be directly
350   measured, therefore, we consider it to be an error and not a deviation to a reference system. 4.)
351   Speed Error: The RMSD between the estimated average speed and the sagittal-plane speed of the
352   pelvis markers while the participant was crossing the OMC area, in m s−1 . For all metrics, lower
353   values are better. We show an evaluation on metrics that are commonly used in computer graphics
354   in the supplementary B.

432
      is faster and the dynamics are not subject to propagation errors from the kinematics. On the other
433   hand, the two-stage inference can help with generalization towards unseen movements and can be
434   more robust to noise. When testing SSPINNpose with PIP’s PD controller and optimizer, we found
435   that the the PD controller hyperparameters needed to be adjusted. We found that, depending on the
436   hyperparameters, the PD controller could be used to slightly reduce the JTE while raising the JAE
437   and smoothing the motion (see C for implementation details).
438
      Our method contains a number of assumptions and simplifications. As in Yi et al. (2022), we assume
439   that the ground is flat and the foot cannot slide. Information about the ground is present in the IMU
440   data, and has been exposed in recent work (Jiang et al., 2022). The interaction between foot and
441   ground is modeled as a linear spring-damper system. Furthermore, the multibody dynamics model
442   is based on a generic template, which is due to a lack of personalization options. As we fit towards
443   IMU signals that are noisy, our model can learn to replicate that noise and becomes less physically
444   plausible, which we are mitigating, but not eliminating by augmenting the input data with Gaussian
445   noise. Our model is able to accurately estimate human movement dynamics despite these limitations,
446   therefore we consider them to be an opportunity to make the estimations more accurate in the future.
447
448   4.2      F INETUNING FOR P HYSICS AND P ERSONALIZATION
449
450
      In an ideal simulation, the estimated dynamics should perfectly match the actual motion. However,
      achieving a perfect simulation requires physical exactness, meaning that both Kane’s loss and the
451
      temporal consistency loss must be zero. Therefore, we finetuned the Bi-LSTM towards physics by
452
      increasing the weight of the Kane’s loss and the temporal consistency loss by a factor of 10. This
453   reduced the JTE by 10% and the GRFE by 20%. However, as the IMU signals were not followed as
454   strictly, the JAE increased by 5% and the speed error increased by 33%. After finetuning, the biases
455   in knee moment and vertical GRF were substantially reduced and only the bias in the hip torque
456   during the stance phase in running remained. For use cases where the torques are of most interest,
457   this trade-off should be acceptable.

499   4.3   S PARSE IMU C ONFIGURATIONS
500
501
      In practical use, the fewer IMUs one has to wear, the better. We have retrained the Bi-LSTM from
502   scratch on configurations with only the foot-worn IMUs (F), foot and thigh IMUs (FT), and foot and
503   pelvis IMUs (FP). Errors generally increased (Table 2), but the output motion is still physically and
504   visually plausible (see C). For the running motions in F and FT configurations, the ankle angle and
505   therefore the origin of the GRF is visibly shifted. Between the configurations with and without a
506   pelvis IMU, the trunk orientation is different for all motions. Therefore, there is likely a discrepancy
507   between the actual, physically plausible, trunk orientation and the IMU orientation, i.e. the pelvis
508   IMU might not be correctly aligned. Compared to Dorschky et al. (2023), our increases in errors are
509   similar for the F and FP configurations, but higher for the FT configuration. We believe our method
510
      is more affected by soft tissue artefacts, measurement errors caused by the movement of skin and
      muscle, from thigh IMUs compared to the optimal control method. As there is no hard constraint in
511
      SSPINNpose, it can trade off physical correctness for a better fit to the IMU signals, especially when
512
      they contain noise. On the other hand, optimal control’s hard constraints not allowing physically
513   incorrect motions. The pelvis and foot IMUs, on the other hand, are less affected by soft-tissue
514   artefacts.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we present SSPINNpose, a real-time method for the estimation of human movement
532
      dynamics from inertial sensor data that does not require labeled training data. Instead, it relies
533
      on self-supervision and physics information to find plausible motions. We show that SSPINNpose
534   can accurately estimate joint angles, torques, and GRFs from IMU data, while outperforming state-
535   of-the-art methods in terms of horizontal speed estimation. Additionally, SSPINNpose effectively
536   identifies movement patterns from sparse IMU configurations and personalizes IMU placement on
537   the body. Given its capability to work with minimal IMU configurations and allow for personaliza-
538   tion, SSPINNpose is a promising approach for long-term monitoring of athletes and understanding
539   injury mechanisms. In the future, we aim to extend SSPINNpose to 3D applications and adapt it for
      model predictive control tasks.

799   Current state-of-the-art 3D pose estimation methods are typically evaluated on different metrics than
800   those that biomechanists are interested in, which are listed in table 4: 1.) Jitter: The third derivative
801   of the joint positions in km s−3 . 2.) Global Orientation Error (GOE): The mean absolute error
802   (MAE) between estimated global segment orientations and those obtained from addBiomechanics,
803   including the root orientation, in degrees. This term is similar to the SIP error, which measures the
804   accuracy of global limb orientations in 3D. 3.) Mean Absolute Joint Angle Error (JA-MAE): The
805   MAE between estimated joint angles and those obtained from addBiomechanics, including the root
806
      orientation, in degrees. 4. Joint Positioning Error (JPE): The mean distance between the knee and
      ankle position in our estimation and the position of the respective OMC marker, in cm. The greater
807
      trochanter marker was aligned with the hip joint in our estimations.
