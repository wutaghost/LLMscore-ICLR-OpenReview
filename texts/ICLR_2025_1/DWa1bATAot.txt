011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce a method to extract topological features from
014            transformer-based protein language models. Our method leverages the persistent
015            homology of attention maps to generate features for token (per amino-acid) classi-
016
               fication tasks and demonstrate its relevance in a biological context. We implement
               our method on transformer-based protein language models using the family of
017
               ESM-2 models. Specifically, we demonstrate that minimum spanning trees, de-
018
               rived from attention matrices, encode structurally significant information about
019            proteins. In our experiments, we combine these topological features with stan-
020            dard embeddings from ESM-2. Our method outperforms traditional approaches
021            and other transformer-based methods with a similar number of parameters in sev-
022            eral binding site identification tasks and achieves state-of-the-art performance in
023            conservation prediction tasks. Our results highlight the potential of this hybrid
024            approach in advancing the understanding and prediction of protein functions.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Proteins play an essential role in a multitude of biological processes: facilitating chemical reactions,
030   transporting molecules, mediating cellular communication, and providing structural support to both
031   cells and entire organisms. This astonishing functional diversity of proteins is uniquely encoded
032   in their amino acid sequences. With about 20 different standard types of amino acids available, an
033
      infinite number of proteins can be generated by varying their sequence arrangements. These amino
      acid sequences are known as the primary structure of the protein or 1D encoding. Within biological
034
      organisms, proteins are spatially coiled, bent, and spontaneously folded due to the interaction of
035
      amino acids, resulting in a specific three-dimensional molecular structure known as the tertiary
036   structure of the protein, or 3D structure.
037
038   Several unique properties of proteins can be derived from their 3D structure (Kucera et al., 2024;
039
      Sun et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2022). However, despite the progress in solving
      the protein folding problem (Hie et al., 2022; Jumper et al., 2021), existing methods remain com-
040
      putationally demanding. Another approach to predicting protein functions (Kim and Kwon, 2023;
041
      Marquet et al., 2022; Rao et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022b; Xu et al., 2022) involves the use of the
042   protein language models (pLMs) (Ahmed et al., 2020; Heinzinger et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022; 2023;
043   Rives et al., 2021). These models are based on the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017)
044   and take into account the sequential nature of proteins. Statistical patterns within evolutionarily
045   related protein sequences provide insights into their structure and function (Altschuh et al., 1988).
046   This connection arises because the properties of a protein impose constraints on the evolution of
047   its sequences. As pLMs meld knowledge of protein sequences with language modeling techniques,
048   their importance has grown significantly. However, the extent to which these models truly grasp
049   the underlying biophysics of protein structure and, by extension, their functions, remains an open
050
      question.
051   Several approaches have been proposed for analyzing the attention maps of models trained on pro-
052   tein sequences (Bhattacharya et al., 2021; Vig et al., 2020). (Bhattacharya et al., 2021) demonstrated
053   that attention mechanisms offer a systematic and principled model of protein interactions, rooted in
      the inherent properties of protein family data. (Vig et al., 2020) conducted a comprehensive analysis


                                                         1
054
      of the interpretability of protein language models, particularly those based on the BERT architec-
055   ture. Their study focused on understanding how attention mechanisms within these models capture
056   and represent the intricate features of protein sequences. According to (Vig et al., 2020) findings,
057   the attention maps generated by the models: highlight amino acid pairs distant in sequence but
058   close in structure, as indicated by correlations with pairwise contacts, highlight binding sites within
059   proteins and capture local secondary structure, revealing patterns corresponding to structural mo-
060   tifs like alpha-helices and beta-sheets. This results suggest that protein language models can infer
061   structural proximity from sequence data alone, recognize functionally important sites essential for
062   protein activity, and detect common structural motifs inherent in protein sequences. This demon-
063
      strates the capability of attention maps to uncover intricate structural features solely from sequence
      information.
064
065   In this work, we take a step further by proposing a novel approach to analyzing protein language
066   model attention maps using tools from topological data analysis. Specifically, we introduce a min-
067   imum spanning tree (MST)-based method for Residue classification, called RES-MST. We em-
068   pirically demonstrate that topological features defined on minimum spanning trees derived from
069   attention matrices encode structurally significant information about proteins. Moreover, we extend
070   interpretability research of protein language model attention maps by performing a quantitative anal-
071   ysis of the correspondence between nodes of the minimum spanning trees and residues with high
072
      conservation levels.
073   Topological Data Analysis (Barannikov, 1994; Chazal and Michel, 2017; Zomorodian, 2001) is a
074   field focused on the numerical characterization of multi-scale topological properties of data, includ-
075   ing graphs and point clouds. Self-attention maps can naturally be represented as fully connected
076   weighted graphs, where weights, derived from attention scores, indicate similarity (or dissimilarity)
077   in some sense between nodes (amino acids). Previously, approaches to compute topological features
078
      from attention matrices of foundation models were explored in the fields of NLP and speech pro-
      cessing (Cherniavskii et al., 2022; Kushnareva et al., 2021; Tulchinskii et al., 2022), focusing solely
079
      on sequence classification. In contrast, our work aims to make predictions at the individual token
080
      (amino acid residue) level, enabling us to address crucial per-residue tasks. Common examples of
081   per-residue tasks include: identifying which residues are likely to be involved in binding with other
082   molecules such as ligands, DNA, or other proteins (binding sites) and predicting the level of evolu-
083   tionary changes at specific residue positions (conservation) which might affect the protein’s structure
084   and function. Understanding the characteristics of each residue can reveal detailed mechanisms of
085   protein function, interaction, and stability that are not apparent at the whole-protein level. For ex-
086   ample, knowing which residues are involved in binding sites or are critical for structural integrity
087   allows for more precise targeting in drug design (Lu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2024). This specificity
088   can lead to the development of drugs with higher efficacy and fewer side effects. Additionally, dis-
089
      eases caused by genetic mutations often result from changes in single amino acid residues that affect
      protein function. Per-residue binding and conservation predictions can help identify which amino
090
      acids are likely to be exposed to that.
091
092   In summary, we make the following contributions:
093
094          • We introduce a novel non-parametric framework specifically designed to convert attention
095            matrices from transformer models into topological features tailored to token-wise classifi-
096            cation. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that Topological Data Analysis
097            has been directly applied to classification on a per-token basis;
098
099          • Our work is the first one to study the topology of attention maps in protein language models,
100            as opposed to applying it directly to 3D structures (Dey and Mandal, 2018; Koseki et al.,
101
               2023; Swenson et al., 2020);
102
             • We perform a quantitative analysis to demonstrate that the topological structures derived
103            from attention maps, particularly, the minimum spanning tree, capture structurally signifi-
104            cant information about proteins;
105
106          • Finally, we find out that our topological features are informative and, being combined with
107            traditional pLM’s vector representations, outperform sequence-based state-of-the-art meth-
               ods on several tasks such as prediction of binding sites and conservation.

      2       BACKGROUND ON T OPOLOGICAL DATA A NALYSIS
135
136   Topology is often considered to describe the “shape of data”. In this Section, we give a high-level
137
      introduction to the flagship tool of Topological Data Analysis - a persistent homology. For a detailed
      explanation, we refer a reader to (Dey and Wang, 2022).
138
139   Simplicial homology. The central object of this work is a self-attention matrix. After some sym-
140   metrization, we treat it as a fully connected weighted undirected graph G having tokens as vertices.
141   For a weighted graph G, we are interested in studying its subgraphs where only edges with associated
142   weights wij ≤ α are taken, see Figure 1 and Figure 3.
143   The subgraphs G α might have distinct topology, that is, number of connected components, cycles,
144   voids, etc. These features are precisely characterized by k-th Betti numbers βk ∈ N0 by means of a
145   simplicial homology. Formally, the Vietoris-Rips simplicial complex1 is defined as:
146
                                   VRα (G) = {{i0 , . . . , ik }, im ∈ Vert(G) | wi,j ≤ α} ,
147
148   where Vert(G) - is the vertex set of the graph G. The simplicial complex can be considered as a
149   generalization of a graph having high-order relations of vertices. Betti number βk is a dimensionality
150   of a homology group Hk of VRα (G).
151   Persistent homology. A natural issue is the necessity of choosing a value for α. It is solved by
152   a persistent homology, which permits to analyse α ∈ [αmin , αmax ] all together. By considering
153   α1 ≤ α2 ≤ . . . ≤ αm , we get a nested sequence VRα1 (G) ⊆ VRα2 (G) ⊆ . . . ⊆ VRαm (G), which
154   is called a filtration.
155
      The values of α when topological features (like connected components and a cycle on Figure 3)
156
      appear and disappear can be paired and form a segment (αm , αn ). A multiset of such segments is
157   called a persistence barcode and it describes topological features at multiple scales. The longer seg-
158   ment is, the more “persistent” (distinguishable from noise) is the corresponding topological feature,
159   see Figure 2 for an illustration. The whole theory is dubbed a persistent homology (Barannikov,
160   1994; Chazal and Michel, 2017; Zomorodian, 2001).
161
          1
              Simplicial complex is a family of sets that is closed under taking subsets.

162
      Computation of a persistence barcode it computationally demanding. Generally, the barcode com-
163   putation is at worst cubic in the number of simplexes involved. In practice, the computation is faster
164   since the boundary matrix is typically sparse for real datasets. Ablation studies comparing the H0
165   and H1 persistence homology topological features are provided in the Appendix section B.2.
166
167   We derive our method RES-MST based on the H0 persistence homology. The algorithm for building
168   an H0 persistence barcode is essentially an algorithm for finding a Minimum Spanning Tree (MST)
169   of a weighted graph. A bar in the H0 barcode corresponds to an edge in MST, because both are con-
170   structed by incrementally connecting components in a graph based on ascending edge weights. In
171   H0 persistent homology, an interval represents the lifespan of a connected component, ending when
172   it merges with another, which directly maps to the addition of an edge in the MST that connects two
173   disjoint components. This equivalence arises because both processes prioritize edges by weight to
174   form a single connected structure. See Section 3.5.3 from Dey and Wang (2022) for more details.
175
      The basic statistics computed over the edges in the MST, such as the minimum, maximum, sum, and
176
      mean of edge weights, align with those derived from the H0 barcode because the intervals in the
177
      barcode encode the same edge weights. The length of each interval in the H0 barcode corresponds
178
      to the weight of an edge in the MST. Therefore, summarizing these weights through statistics di-
179
      rectly captures the key features of the H0 barcode, making the two representations equivalent in
180
      terms of the structural information they encode. If one is interested only in H0 barcodes, one can
181
      use Kruskal’s algorithm with a complexity O(E log(E)), where E is a number of graph’s edges.

184   3     A TOPOLOGY- BASED FRAMEWORK FOR ATTENTION MATRICES OF P LM S
185
186   Protein language models (pLMs) pretrained on extensive protein sequence corpora have demon-
187   strated impressive results in predicting protein function and structure. These models, typically based
188   on transformer architectures, are trained using a masked protein modeling task. In this task, partial
189
      residues are masked in the input sequence and are predicted based on their context. One such state-
      of-the-art protein language model, ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2022), is trained on protein sequences from the
190
      UniRef database (Suzek et al., 2015). In this model, given an input protein sequence, 15% of amino
191
      acids are masked, and ESM-2 is tasked with predicting these missing positions (Devlin et al., 2018).
192   Although the primary training objective involves predicting these missing amino acids, achieving
193   high accuracy necessitates that the model learn complex internal representations of its input. These
194   representations learn secondary structure prediction, binding site prediction, and contact prediction
195   within amino acids of the protein (Rao et al., 2020; Rives et al., 2021). In this work, we select
196   650-million-parameter (33 layers, 20 heads) and 3-billion-parameter (36 layers, 40 heads) ESM-2
197   models as baselines and seek to enhance their representation power by applying topological data
198   analysis to their attention maps.

205   The attention matrix A of the protein language model denotes the mutual relation of the tokens
206   (amino-acids): the higher the attention is, the stronger is the relation. Each attention matrix is
207   converted to the quasi-distances matrix W computed from attentions: wi,j = 1 − max(ai,j , aj,i ).
208   Classical persistent homology extracts multi-scale topological features of the whole graph. However,
209   we are interested in a per-residue predictions, that is, predictions for each node. As we mentioned
210   before, the H0 persistent homology coincides with a MST and can be calculated by a scalable
211   Kruskal’s algorithm. Each quasi-distances matrix W is converted into a weighted graph and the
212   set of features is derived from an MST of the weighted graph G. Each node i has a set of incident
213   edges in a MST. Thus, for each node, we calculate per-RESidue MST statistics of the incident edges:
214   min, max, sum, mean of the weights from incident edges and a count of incident edges, denoted as
215   MST features. Besides the features extracted from the MST, we also extract additional features di-
      rectly from the attention map for each token: self-attention and the sum of absolute values in the i-th

297   3.3   T OPOLOGICAL PATTERNS OF MST S ENCODE PROTEINS STRUCTURALLY SIGNIFICANT
298         INFORMATION
299
300   Next, we study topological patterns of MSTs and how they are mapped onto amino-acid sequences.
301
      Figure 6, Figure 8 and Figure 9 present a quantitative analysis. Figure 6 shows a mean maximum
302
      degree of a node in MSTs across layers and it has a peak in middle layers and very low values in
303
      initial and last layers. Figure 8 and Figure 9 show a mean distance between nodes incident to edges
304   of MTSs: a distance between tokens in a sequence and amino-acids in Euclidean space respectively.
305   These distances are low in last layers. Based on the quantitative analysis, we draw a conclusion
306
      on MSTs mapping to protein structure. MSTs corresponding to the initial layers are characterized
      by a “chaotic” connectivity, featuring a greater Euclidean distances between nodes and moderate
307
      maximum node degrees. Clearly, in middle layers MSTs have a “star” pattern, with one node
308
      connecting to all the others. In contrast, the MST constructed for the model’s last layer exhibits an
309   almost linear configuration, where each node is connected to its immediate neighbors in a sequence.
310
311   Based on this analysis, we have concluded that the features extracted through topological analysis
312
      of MSTs, corresponding to different layers of the pLM, encode structurally significant information
      that can be used for addressing specific downstream tasks.

460   6      R ELATED WORK
461
462   Topological Data Analysis for Deep Learning. Topological data analysis (TDA) recently started
463   gaining traction in machine learning and deep learning, having a variety of applications (Balabin
464   et al., 2023; Barannikov et al., 2021a;b; Carrière et al., 2020; Hofer et al., 2017; 2019; Hu et al., 2019;
465   Rieck et al., 2018; Trofimov et al., 2023; Zhao and Wang, 2019). Previous works studied topology
466   of attention maps of foundation models in NLP (Cherniavskii et al., 2022; Kushnareva et al., 2021)
467   and speech processing (Tulchinskii et al., 2022). Topology of attention maps proved to be useful for
468   downstream applications: artificial text detection (Kushnareva et al., 2021), acceptability judgement
469
      (Cherniavskii et al., 2022) and speech classification (Tulchinskii et al., 2022). Topological data
      analysis has been performed on 3D structures of proteins (Dey and Mandal, 2018; Koseki et al.,
470
      2023; Swenson et al., 2020).
471
472
473
      Protein language models. The advent of deep learning has revolutionized the field of compu-
      tational biology, particularly through the development of pLMs (Ahmed et al., 2020; Heinzinger
474
      et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2022). These models, often based on the transformer architecture (Vaswani
475
      et al., 2017), have been adeptly repurposed to handle the unique challenges presented by protein
476   sequences. Notable among these is the ESM series, with ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2022) trained on the
477   expansive UniRef database (Suzek et al., 2015). This training involves a masked protein modeling
478   approach, where a percentage of amino acids are intentionally obscured and subsequently predicted
479   to train the model (Devlin et al., 2018). This method, similar to techniques used in natural language
480   processing, necessitates the model’s understanding of the intricate relationships between amino acids
481   to successfully predict obscured segments.
482
483   Per residue downstream tasks. We evaluated our method on several binding site and conservation
484   prediction tasks. Most of other works - PepBind (Zhao et al., 2018), PepNN-Seq (Abdin et al., 2022),
485   and PepBCL (Wang et al., 2022b), AttSec (Kim and Kwon, 2023) - used embeddings of protein
      language models to solve this tasks. AttSec (Kim and Kwon, 2023) also used attention maps as


                                                                 9
486
      features transformed by 2D convolution blocks and provided state-of-the-art on secondary structure
487   prediction task.

490   7   L IMITATIONS
491
492
      While our method offers significant advancements in the application of topological data analysis to
      protein language models, there are several limitations to consider. Monomer-Only Focus. Most of
493
      protein language models are trained exclusively on single protein sequences (monomers). Conse-
494
      quently, our approach, which relies on the attention maps generated by these models, is limited to
495   single proteins and does not extend to protein complexes. This restriction may omit the intricate in-
496   teractions and functionalities that arise in multi-protein assemblies. Computational Efficiency. Our
497   method, which integrates topological features with protein language model (pLM) attention maps, is
498   inherently less computationally efficient compared to using pLM embeddings alone (see Appendix
499   C). This trade-off between computational cost and the enhanced representation power provided by
500   topological features is a factor to consider for practical applications.
501
502   8   C ONCLUSION
503
504   In this paper, we introduced a novel approach for efficient and accurate per-residue prediction tasks.
505   Our method leverages topological data analysis of attention maps of the transformer-based protein
506   language models. The method outperformed several sequence-based state-of-the-art results across
507   diverse per residue benchmarks and prediction tasks in terms of accuracy. Moreover, our method
508   revealed topological structures in attention maps aligned with biological motifs. This opens up
509   the possibility of expanding this approach to other domains. There are several avenues for future
510   research, such as designing further topologically-aware end-to-end training techniques and investi-
511   gating the use of more detailed atom-level interactions in a topological manner.

      A     T ERMS AND DEFINITIONS
757
758
      Sequence profiles. Position Specific Scoring Matrices (PSSMs), commonly referred to as
759   sequence-profiles, are constructed from probabilities assigned to each of the 20 amino acids at every
760   position of an input sequence. These matrices are especially valuable for identifying conservation
761   across sequences: a high value at a position signifies strong conservation, indicative of critical func-
762   tional or structural roles, whereas a low value suggests weak conservation. PSSMs are derived
763   through Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA), a widely employed technique that aligns sequences
764   to explore their evolutionary relationships and to understand the structural and functional constraints
765   within protein families. This method is instrumental in enhancing the accuracy of various prediction
766   pipelines in bioinformatics.
767
768
      “Star”, “linear” and “chaotic” graphs. “Star graph” - a tree having k nodes, one of them is
      internal and it is connected to k − 1 leaves. “Linear graph” - a graph whose vertices can be listed in
769
      the order v1 , v2 , ..., vk such that the edges are (vi , vi+1 ) where i = 1, 2, ..., k − 1. “Chaotic” graph

      to obtain attention maps. A submatrix for each residue is extracted by picking neighbors from the

883   B.2            L OCAL TOPOLOGY PER - RESIDUE FEATURES / M ETHOD RES-LT
884
      Classical persistent homology extracts multi-scale topological features of the whole graph. However,
885
      we are interested in a per-residue predictions, that is, predictions for each node. We hypothesize that
886
      for node i only a local structure of graph G matters. For each amino-acid (node) i of the protein
887   sample we identify a neighborhood of i - the subset of close nodes {j | wij ≤ t} in the graph
888
      represented by the quasi-distances matrix W based on the predefined threshold t 2 ; together with
889   incident edges, they comprise a fully connected subgraph Gi . Then, for each subgraph Gi ⊆ G
890   we calculate its persistence barcode by the giotto-ph library (Pérez et al., 2021). We vectorize
891   persistence barcodes by extracting the following features for H0 and H1 barcodes: the number of
892   essential/non-essential bars; the sum, mean, median, max, min and std of bars’ lengthes; the entropy
893   of a normalized barcodes; the number of bars having length birth/death smaller than predefined
894   thresholds.

      B.2.3    S ECONDARY STRUCTURE PREDICTION TASK .
967
968   Dataset. Protein secondary structures can be used as good features for describing the local proper-
969   ties of protein, but also can serve as key features for predicting the complex 3D structures of protein.
970   Thus, it is very important to accurately predict the secondary structure of the protein, which contains
971   a local structural property assigned by the pattern of hydrogen bonds formed between amino acids.
      The secondary structures are typically assigned by the DSSP (Defne Secondary Structure of Pro-


                                                        18
972
       teins) algorithm (Kabsch and Sander, 1983). The DSSP algorithm checks whether there is hydrogen
973    bond for each amino acid pair by identifying the distance between the elements given the 3D coor-
974    dinate file of the protein. Then, based on the local patterns of these hydrogen bonds, eight types of
975    secondary structure are assigned to amino acids (DSSP8/Q8): 310 -helix (G), 4-helix (α-helix) (H),
976    5-helix (π-helix) (I), hydrogen bonded turn (T), extended strand in parallel and/or anti-parallel β-
977    sheet conformation (E), residue in isolated β-bridge (B), bend (S), and coil (C). The aforementioned
978    types can be further grouped into three larger classes (DSSP3/Q3): helix (H), strand (E), and loop
979    (C). While there are several ways to reduce the 8 types to 3 types, we use general reduction: (G/H/I
980    → H, E/B → E, S/T/C → C).
981    For the comparison analysis of the RES-MST and RES-LT methods we considered the dataset pub-
982    lished with NetSurfP-2.0 (Klausen et al., 2019). Following the previous works (Ahmed et al., 2020;
983    Heinzinger et al., 2023; Kim and Kwon, 2023) the training set of the NetSurfP-2.0 contains 10,792
984    protein sequences both in 3-states (Q3) and 8-states (Q8) DSSP divided into 9,712 training and 1,080
985    validation data samples. The Py-boost model was trained with NetSurfP-2.0 and then evaluated on
986    NEW364 and CASP12 as well as in (Ahmed et al., 2020; Heinzinger et al., 2023; Kim and Kwon,
987
       2023).

       C     C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
1027
1028
       For a sequence of size n, the attention map has a size of n × n. The derived fully-connected
1029   weighted graph has O(n(n − 1)/2) edges. The proposed algorithms scale linearly in terms of a
1030   network’s depth and a number of attention heads.
1031   RES-LT. RES-LT method involves a computation of persistence barcodes which is a main source of
1032   a complexity. An algorithm is applied to n submatrices of size n′ < n. The computation is at worst
1033   cubic in the number of simplices involved in a simplicial complex. The simplicial complexes are
1034   derived from a fully-connected weighted graph of submatrices. In practice, the computation is often
1035   quite fast (takes several seconds) for sequences n < 1024 since the boundary matrix is typically
1036   sparse for real datasets.
1037
       RES-MST. The complexity of finding a minimum spanning tree with a Kruskal’s algorithm is
       O(n2 log(n)). The minimum spanning tree is calculated once for a whole graph and features are
1038
       generated in O(n) time.
