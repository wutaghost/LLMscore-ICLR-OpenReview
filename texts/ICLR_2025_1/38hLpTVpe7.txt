009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Modular addition is, on its face, a simple operation: given N elements in Zq ,
012            compute their sum modulo q. Yet, scalable machine learning solutions to this
013            problem remain elusive: prior work trains ML models that sum N ≤ 6 elements
014            mod q ≤ 1000. Promising applications of ML models for cryptanalysis—which
015            often involve modular arithmetic with large N and q—motivate reconsideration
016
               of this problem. This work proposes three changes to the modular addition model
               training pipeline: more diverse training data, an angular embedding, and a custom
017
               loss function. With these changes, we demonstrate success with our approach for
018
               N = 256, q = 3329, a case which is interesting for cryptographic applications, and
019            a significant increase in N and q over prior work. These techniques also generalize
020            to other modular arithmetic problems, motivating future work.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Modular addition is an important operation commonly used in number theory and cryptography. The
                                                                                   PN
026   operation is simple: given N elements [x1 , x2 ...xN ], xi ∈ Zq , compute s = i=1 xi mod q.
027   Despite its apparent simplicity, prior work has demonstrated that machine learning (ML) models
028   struggle to perform modular arithmetic (Palamas, 2017; Lauter et al., 2024; Stevens et al., 2024). This
029   is surprising because ML models can learn other complex math tasks such as symbolic regression,
030   linear algebra, and computing the greatest common divisor (GCD) (Charton et al., 2021; Charton,
031   2022; 2024). Modular arithmetic, on its face, seems easier, but scalable ML solutions remain elusive.
032   Improved ML performance on modular addition could aid ongoing research efforts and open new
033   research avenues. For example, modular arithmetic is a key component of many cryptographic hard
034   problems, including Learning with Errors (LWE), which is the basis for post-quantum cryptosystems
035   (PQC) standardized by NIST (Chen et al., 2022). ML models capable of modular arithmetic could aid
036   nascent efforts to use ML models for cryptanalysis of LWE (Wenger et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a;b;
037   Stevens et al., 2024) or enable ML-powered cryptanalysis of other cryptosystems. Standardized LWE
038   systems typically involve adding hundreds of random elements modulo q.
039
      Our Contribution. Motivated by these potential use cases, we propose new methods enabling ML
040   models to perform modular addition for a variety of N and q, up to N = 256 and q = 3329. Our
041   method significantly outperforms prior work, which summed N ≤ 6 elements mod q ≤ 1000, and
042   generalizes to other modular arithmetic operations. In developing our methods, we first identify
043   factors that limit models’ ability to learn modular arithmetic: (1) lack of diverse training data, (2)
044   lack of inductive bias for modular addition, and (3) unsuitable loss functions. We address these by:
045
      • Constructing the training data distribution to ensure more diverse elements are represented.
046
      • Introducing an angular embedding (inspired by Stevens et al. (2024)) that represents model inputs
047
        and outputs as coordinates on the unit circle, improving inductive bias for modular addition.
048
      • Designing a new loss function with penalty term discouraging model convergence at local minima.
049
050   The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. §2 discusses related work on ML-enabled modular
051   arithmetic. §3 describes key limitations of prior work and our novel methods to overcome them.
052   §4 presents key results on modular arithmetic problems with varying N and q. §5 reports ablation
053   studies over the methodology changes we introduce. §6 applies our methods to other asymmetric
      functions of interest, and §7 discusses future work.

083   Prior work has investigated whether ML models can learn modular arithmetic operations (Palamas,
084   2017; Lauter et al., 2024; Gromov, 2023; Abbe et al., 2024; Mohamadi et al., 2024; Doshi et al.,
085   2024). Table 2 summarizes the best prior results on modular addition specifically. The best existing
086
      methods train models that sum N ≤ 6 elements for moduli up to q = 1000.
087   We scale ML-enabled modular addition to tackle larger N and q, motivated by problems in number
088   theory and cryptography that involve addition of many elements mod large primes. Prior work has
089   laid groundwork for analytically understanding how models learn modular arithmetic (Gromov, 2023;
090   Doshi et al., 2024). Our methods build on three observations from prior work:
091
092   • Need for representative training data: Mohamadi et al. (2024) showed that models need to be
093     trained on a constant fraction of all possible modular arithmetic behaviors for a given N and q to
094
        generalize. This implies that better designed training datasets could aid learning.
095
      • Importance of appropriate model representations: Nanda et al. (2023) showed that transformers
        trained to perform modular addition inherently learned to convert their inputs to polar coordinates,
096
        combine them, and then decode them back into the resulting integer sum. This suggests that models
097
        with inductive bias towards coordinate representations may perform better on this problem.
098
      • Importance of loss functions: Several works attribute models’ failure to learn more complex
099     modular addition problems to the complexity of the loss space (Gromov, 2023; Jelassi et al., 2023).
100     Because 0 and q − 1 are “close” in a modular field, seemingly different elements must map to the
101     same loss region, making gradient descent difficult. A carefully designed loss function could help.

      3.1   P ROPOSED I MPROVEMENTS
109
110
      More Diverse Training Data to Improve Generalization. Most prior work trains models using
111   randomly generated (x, s) pairs, where x is drawn uniformly at random from ZN        q , i.e. x consists
112                                                       PN
      of elements [x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ], xi ∈ Zq and s = i=1 xi mod q (Jelassi et al., 2023; Doshi et al.,
113
      2024). Building on observations about the importance of training data diversity, we generate our
114
      training data using two distributions, f and g. These help the model generalize by presenting it with
115   simpler versions of the problem (f ) and ensuring the full data distribution is well represented (g).
116
117   Enabling gradual learning via f : We postulate that models may learn better when they see “simpler”
118
      versions of the target operation, in this case modular sums with more zero elements. Seeing these
      simplified problems may help models understand the modular arithmetic structure and learn better.
119
      Thus, we propose adding additional sparse vectors to the training data, in which more coordinates of
120
      x are 0. To generate these, we fix a probability density function (PDF) f : {0, 1, . . . , N } → [0, 1].
121   Then, to create a training instance, we:
122
123   • Sample a random variable z, representing the number of zeros in each vector, from distribution f .
124   • Then, sample N − z integers uniformly from the set {1, 2, . . . , q − 1}. These integers, along with
125     z zeros, are used to construct a vector of length N .
126   • Lastly, shuffle the vector to ensure randomness in element order.
127
128   We experiment with three f s: funi (z) = N1+1 (i.e. uniform density), finv_sqrt (z) ∝ √z+1     1
                                                                                                         and
129                    1√
      finv (z) ∝ z+1+ N , where ∝ means the functions are rescaled by a constant such that the sum of f
130   over all z in its domain equals 1. We compare these to a baseline of fdefault , which is the PDF of the
131   number of zeros in x when x is drawn uniformly from ZN   q . Figure 1 shows the sparsity of examples
132   created using these four sample strategies with N = 20 and q = 257.
133
      Representing distributional tails with g: Wenger et al. (2024) observe that the sum of N elements
134
      from Zq follows the Irwin-Hall distribution, denoted as gdefault in Figure 2. Their analysis shows that
135
      for N = 3 the sum mostly falls in the range [q, 2q], and models struggle predicting modulo q sums of
136   vectors x when their pre-modulo sum lies outside this range. To address this, we augment our training
137                                                                                            PN
      dataset with more instances whose sums (or equivalently their averages µ = N1 i=1 xi since N is
138   fixed) are in the distribution tails. In particular, we fix a new PDF g : {0, 1, . . . , q − 1} → [0, 1] and
139   to create training instances from g we:
140
141   • Sample a random variable µ, representing the target rounded average, from distribution g.
142   • Sample N integers uniformly from the set {0, 1, . . . , q − 1}. If the rounded average of these
143     elements is exactly equal to µ we keep this sample, otherwise we repeat this step.
144
145   Again, we let gdefault be the PDF of µ(x) when x is drawn uniformly from ZN    q , pictured in Figure 2.
146   Next, we introduce ginterval , also pictured in Figure 2, a uniform PDF over a centered range of µs
147   and zero outside this range. Essentially, ginterval is designed to overweight somewhat rare µ values
148   from gdefault , but to exclude very rare ones since we find these are very hard/expensive to generate.
149
      See Appendix A for a more formal definition.
150   Dataset construction: To create the training dataset, we sample repeatedly from f and g as defined
151   above until we have sufficient training data. f data can be generated easily, with no rejection sampling,
152   but generating training samples from g is computationally expensive since it requires significant
153   rejection sampling. Because of this, we generate far fewer samples from g than f . The exact ratio
154   depends on N and q, but g samples typically compose less than 0.05% of the total dataset. During
155
      evaluation, we evaluate models on examples drawn uniformly at random from ZN         q .

156   Inductive Bias via Transformer Model and Angular Embedding. We address our observed lack
157   of inductive bias towards modular arithmetic by adding an angular embedding for input and output
158   data and using an encoder-only transformer model. Stevens et al. (2024) first introduced the angular
159   embedding, which represents input integers mod q as points on the unit circle. The intuition is that
160   this better represents the structure of modular arithmetic, since on the unit circle 0 and 2π—which
161   corresponds to q—are close. Practically, the embedding encodes an integer a ∈ Zq as an angle
      ϕ = 2π aq and then as a point (cos(ϕ), sin(ϕ)) ∈ R2 .

525   7    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
526
527   This work introduces novel techniques to help ML models learn modular addition. These tech-
528   niques—varying the diversity of training data, using an angular embedding for model inputs and
529
      outputs, and introducing a regularized loss function—enable ML models to add hundreds of elements
      mod a large q with high accuracy, a significant improvement over prior work. Our methods also
530
      enable models to learn other modular arithmetic functions, indicating their generalizability.
531
532   Several interesting directions remain for future work. First, as modulus size q increases, our models
533   have lower exact accuracy but consistently high τ = 0.5% accuracy—above 99.5%. This motivates
534   future work to understand this disconnect and improve performance as q scales. Second, transferring
535
      our techniques to other settings (such as ML-enabled cryptanalysis) remains an open challenge. While
      our method achieves success on q used in real cryptosystems and N close to real-world use cases
536
      (N = 512 is used in practice (Avanzi et al., 2021)), transferring general modular addition knowledge
537
      to specific cryptanalysis tasks is nontrivial. Possible approaches include pretraining on this task and
538   fine-tuning on specific application settings, but future research should consider creative approaches.
