009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Understanding generalization of overparametrized neural networks remains a fun-
012            damental challenge in machine learning. Most of the literature mostly studies gen-
013            eralization from an interpolation point of view, taking convergence of parameters
014            towards a global minimum of the training loss for granted. While overparametrized
015            architectures indeed interpolated the data for typical classification tasks, this inter-
016            polation paradigm does not seem valid anymore for more complex tasks such as
017            in-context learning or diffusion. Instead for such tasks, it has been empirically ob-
018            served that the trained models goes from global minima to spurious local minima of
019            the training loss as the number of training samples becomes larger than some level
020
               we call optimization threshold. While the former yields a poor generalization to the
               true population loss, the latter was observed to actually correspond to the minimiser
021
               of this true loss. This paper explores theoretically this phenomenon in the context
022
               of two-layer ReLU networks. We demonstrate that, despite overparametrization,
023            networks often converge toward simpler solutions rather than interpolating the
024            training data, which can lead to a drastic improvement on the test loss with respect
025            to interpolating solutions. Our analysis relies on the so called early alignment
026            phase, during which neurons align towards specific directions. This directional
027            alignment, which occurs in the early stage of training, leads to a simplicity bias,
028            wherein the network approximates the ground truth model without converging to
029            the global minimum of the training loss. Our results suggest that this bias, resulting
030            in an optimization threshold from which interpolation is not reached anymore, is
031
               beneficial and enhances the generalization of trained models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Understanding the generalization capabilities of neural networks remains a fundamental open question
037   in machine learning (Zhang et al., 2021; Neyshabur et al., 2017). Traditionally, research has focused
038   on explaining why neural networks models can achieve zero training loss while still generalizing
039   well to unseen data in supervised learning tasks (Chizat and Bach, 2018; Mei et al., 2018; Rotskoff
040   and Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Chizat and Bach, 2020; Boursier et al., 2022; Boursier and Flammarion,
041   2023). This phenomenon is often attributed to overparametrization enabling models to find solutions
042   that interpolate the training data yet avoid overfitting (Belkin et al., 2019; Bartlett et al., 2021).
043   However, the advent of generative AI paradigms—such as large language models (Vaswani et al.,
044   2017) and diffusion models (Dhariwal and Nichol, 2021)—has introduced a paradigm shift in our
045   understanding of generalization. In these settings, models can generate new data and perform novel
046   tasks without necessarily interpolating the training data, raising fresh questions about how and why
047   they generalize. To illustrate this shift, we consider two seemingly unrelated examples: in-context
048   learning with transformers and generative modeling using diffusion methods.
049   Firstly, in-context learning (ICL) refers to the ability of large pretrained transformer models to learn
050   new tasks from just a few examples, without any parameter updates (Brown et al., 2020; Min et al.,
051   2022). A central question is whether ICL enables models to learn tasks significantly different from
052   those encountered during pretraining. While prior work suggests that ICL leverages mechanisms akin
053   to Bayesian inference (Xie et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023), the limited diversity of
      tasks in pretraining datasets may constrain the model’s ability to generalize. Raventós et al. (2024)


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      investigated this effect by focusing on regression problems to quantify how increasing the variety of
055   tasks during pretraining affects ICL’s capacity to generalize to new, unseen tasks, in context.
056
057   Secondly, diffusion models have made remarkable strides in generating high-quality images from
058
      high-dimensional datasets (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021). These
      models learn to generate new samples by training denoisers to estimate the score function—the
059
      gradient of the log probability density—of the noisy data distribution (Song and Ermon, 2019). A
060
      significant challenge in this context is approximating a continuous density from a relatively small
061   training set without succumbing to the curse of dimensionality. Although deep neural networks may
062   tend to memorize training data when the dataset is small relative to the network’s capacity (Somepalli
063   et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023), Yoon et al. (2023); Kadkhodaie et al. (2023) observed they generalize
064   well when trained on sufficiently large datasets, rendering the model’s behavior nearly independent
065   of the specific training set.
066
      The common thread connecting these examples is a fundamental change in how gradient descent
067   behaves in overparameterized models when the number of data points exceeds a certain threshold.
068   Rather than converging to the global minimum of the training loss, gradient descent converges to a
069   simpler solution closely related to the true loss minimizer. In learning scenarios involving noisy data,
070   the most effective solutions are often those that do not interpolate the data. Despite their capacity
071   to overfit, these models exhibit a simplicity bias, generalizing well to the underlying ground truth
072   instead of merely fitting the noise in the training data. While simplicity bias generally refers to the
073   tendency of models to learn features of increasing complexity, until reaching data interpolation (Arpit
074   et al., 2017; Rahaman et al., 2019; Kalimeris et al., 2019; Huh et al., 2021); this phenomenon seems
075
      to stop before full interpolation in the case of in-context learning and diffusion models (even when
      training for a very long time). This observation underscores a significant shift in our understanding
076
      and approach to generalization in machine learning.
077
078   In this paper, we investigate this phenomenon in the setting of shallow ReLU networks applied
079   to a regression problem. While multilayer perceptrons are foundational elements shared by the
080   aforementioned models, focusing on shallow networks remains a significant simplification with
081
      respect to the architectures and algorithms used for training transformers and diffusion models.
      Despite this simplification, we aim to gain theoretical insights that could shed light on similar
082
      behaviors observed in more complex models.
083
084   Some recent works argued that overparametrized networks do not necessarily converge to global
085   minima. In particular, Qiao et al. (2024) showed this effect for unidimensional data by illustrating the
086   instability of global minima. Boursier and Flammarion (2024) advanced a different reason for this
087
      effect, given by the early alignment phenomenon: when initialized with sufficiently small weights,
      neurons primarily adjust their directions rather than their magnitudes in the early phase of training,
088
      aligning along specific directions determined by the stationary points of a certain function. This
089
      function can be explicitly characterized in simple cases.
090
091   Contributions. Our first contribution is to show that this function driving the early alignment phase
092   concentrates around its expectation, which corresponds to the true loss function. For simple teacher
093   architectures, this expected function possesses only a few critical points. As a result, after the early
094   alignment phase, the neurons become concentrated in a few key directions associated with the ground
095   truth model. This behavior reveals a simplicity bias at the initial stages of training. Moreover, this
096   directional concentration is believed to contribute to the non-convergence to the global minimizer of
097   the training loss. However, this characterization only pertains to the initial stage of training. Therefore
098
      we extend our analysis to provide, under a restricted data model, a comprehensive characterization of
      the training dynamics, demonstrating that the simplicity bias persists until the end of training when
099
      the number of training samples exceeds some optimization threshold.

192   Implications of early alignment. By the end of the early alignment, most if not all neurons are
193
      nearly aligned with some extremal vector D. Maennel et al. (2018); Boursier and Flammarion (2024)

194
      Section 3. As a consequence, only a few directions are represented by the network’s weights at the end
195
      of the early dynamics, even though the neurons cover all possible directions at initialization. Boursier
196   and Flammarion (2024) even show that this quantization of directions can prevent the network from
197   interpolating the training set at convergence despite the overparametrization of the network.
198
199   Although this failure of interpolation has been considered a drawback by Boursier and Flammarion
200   (2024), we show in Section 4 that it can also lead to a beneficial phenomenon of simplicity bias.
201   Specifically, Section 4 illustrates on a simple linear example that for a large number of training
202   samples, the model does not converge to interpolation. Instead, it converges towards the ordinary
203   least square (OLS) estimator of the data. As a consequence, the model fits the true signal of the
204
      data, while effectively ignoring label noise. Before studying this example, we must first understand
      how extremal vectors behave as the number of training samples increases.

263

268   large n, it is sufficient to consider the directions satisfying Equation (6), which is easier to characterize
269   from a statistical perspective. We thus believe that Proposition 1 advances our understanding of how

      4.2   D ISCUSSION
392
393   This section discusses in details Theorem 2 and its limitations.
394
395   Absence of interpolation. For many years, the literature has argued in favor of the fact that, if
396   overparametrized enough, neural networks do converge towards interpolation of the training set,
397
      i.e., to a global minimum of the loss (Jacot et al., 2018; Du et al., 2019; Chizat and Bach, 2018;
      Wojtowytsch, 2020).
398
399   Yet, some recent works argued in the opposite direction that convergence towards global minima might
400   not be achieved for regression tasks, even with infinitely overparametrized networks (Qiao et al., 2024;
401   Boursier and Flammarion, 2024). Indeed, Theorem 2 still holds as m → ∞: although interpolation
402   of the data is possible from a statistical aspect, interpolation does not occur for optimization reasons.
403
      In this direction, Qiao et al. (2024) claim that for large values of n and univariate data, interpolation
      cannot happen because of the large (i.e., finite) stepsizes used for gradient descent. Following
404
      Boursier and Flammarion (2024), we here provide a complementary reason, which is due to the early
405
      alignment phenomenon and loss of omnidirectionality of the weights (i.e., the fact that the weights
406   represent all directions in Rd ). Note that this loss of omnidirectionality is specific to the (leaky) ReLU
407   activation and does not hold for smooth activations (see e.g. Chizat and Bach, 2018, Lemma C.10).
408   We experimentally confirm in Appendix A.5 that both visions are complementary, as interpolation
409   still does not happen for arbitrarily small learning rates.
410
411   Simplicity bias. Simplicity bias has been extensively studied in the literature (Arpit et al., 2017;
412
      Rahaman et al., 2019; Kalimeris et al., 2019; Huh et al., 2021). It is often described as the fact that
      networks learn features of increasing complexity while learning. In other words, simpler features are
413
      first learnt (e.g., a linear estimator), and more complex features might be learnt later. This has been
414
      observed in many empirical studies, leading to improved performance in generalization, except from
415   a few nuanced cases (Shah et al., 2020). Yet in all these studies, the network interpolates the training
416   set after being trained for a long enough time. In consequence, simplicity bias has been characterized
417   by a first feature learning phase; and is then followed by an interpolating phase, where the remaining
418   noise is fitted (Kalimeris et al., 2019).
419
      We here go further by showing that this last interpolating phase does not even happen in some cases.
420
      Theorem 2 indeed claims that after the first feature learning phase, where the network learns a linear
421   estimator, nothing happens in training. The interpolating phase never starts, no matter how long we
422   wait for. While interpolation is often observed for classification problems in practice, it is generally
423   much harder to reach for regression problems (Stewart et al., 2022; Yoon et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie
424   et al., 2023; Raventós et al., 2024). Theorem 2 confirms this tendency by illustrating a regression
425   example where interpolation does not happen at convergence.
426
      Although implicit bias and simplicity bias often refer to the same behavior in the literature, we here
427   distinguish the two terms: implicit bias is generally considered in the regime of interpolation (Soudry
428   et al., 2018; Lyu and Li, 2019; Chizat and Bach, 2020; Ji and Telgarsky, 2019), while simplicity bias
429   still exists in absence of interpolation.
430
431   Improved test loss, due to overparametrization threshold. Theorem 2 states that for a large
      enough number of training samples, the interpolating phase does not happen during training, and


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the estimator then  resembles the OLS estimator of the training set. In that regime, the excess risk
433   scales as O nd (Hsu et al., 2011) and thus quickly decreases to 0 as the number of training samples

434   grows. In contrast when interpolation happens, we either observe a tempered overfitting, where the
435   excess risk does not go down to 0 as the number of samples grows (Mallinar et al., 2022); or even a
436   catastrophic overfitting, where the excess risk instead diverges to infinity as the size of the training
437   set increases (Joshi et al., 2023).
438
      The fact that the excess risk goes down to 0 as n grows in our example of Section 4 could not be
439   due to a benign overfitting (Belkin et al., 2018; Bartlett et al., 2020), as benign overfitting occurs
440   when the dimension d also grows to infinity. We here consider a fixed dimension instead, and this
441   reduced risk is then solely due to the optimization threshold, i.e., the fact that for a large enough n,
442   the interpolating phase does not happen anymore. While some works rely on early stopping before
443   this interpolating phase to guarantee such an improved excess risk (Ji et al., 2021; Mallinar et al.,
444   2022; Frei et al., 2023), it can be guaranteed without any early stopping after this optimization
445   threshold. A similar threshold has been empirically observed in diffusion and in-context learning
446   (Yoon et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2023; Raventós et al., 2024), where the trained model goes
447
      from interpolation to generalization as the number of training samples increases.
448
      Limitations and generality. While Theorem 2 considers a very specific setting, it describes a more
449
      general behavior. Although condition 3 of Assumption 1 and the initialization scheme of Equation (8)
450
      are quite artificial, they are merely required to allow a tractable analysis. The experiments of Section 5
451   are indeed run without these conditions and yield results similar to the predictions of Theorem 2 for
452   large enough n.
453
454
      More particularly, condition 3 of Assumption 1 is required to ensure that only two extremal vectors
      exist. Without this condition, there could be additional extremal vectors, but all concentrated around
455
      these two main extremal ones. On the other hand, Equation (8) is required to enforce the early
456
      alignment phase, so that all neurons are aligned towards extremal vectors at its end. With a more
457   general initialization, some neurons could move arbitrarily slowly in the early alignment dynamics,
458   ending unaligned at the end of early phase. Yet, such neurons would be very rare. Relaxing these
459   two assumptions would make the final convergence point slightly more complex than the one in
460   Theorem 2. Besides the two main ReLU components described in Theorem 2, a few small components
461   could also be added to the final estimator, without significantly changing the reached excess risk, as
462   observed in Section 5. This is observed in Figure 1, where the training loss is only slightly smaller
463   than the training loss of OLS, with a comparable test loss.
464   From a higher level, Theorem 2 is restricted to a linear teacher and a simple network architecture.
465   It remains hard to assess how well the considered setting reflects the behavior of more complex
466   architectures encountered in practice. We believe that the different conclusions of our work remain
467   valid in more complex setups. In particular, additional experiments in Appendix A run with a
468   more complex teacher, GeLU activations or with Adam optimizer yield similar behaviors: the
469   obtained estimator does not interpolate for a large number of training samples, but instead accurately
470   approximates the minimizer of the test loss. Similar behaviors have also been observed on more
471
      complex tasks as generative modeling or in-context learning (Yoon et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al.,
      2023; Raventós et al., 2024). Despite overparametrization, the trained model goes from perfect
472
      interpolation to generalization, as it fails at interpolating for a large number of training samples. In
473
      these works as well, this absence of interpolation does not seem due to an early stopping, but rather
474   to convergence to a local minimum (see e.g., Raventós et al., 2024, Figure 4).
475
476   Lastly, Theorem 2 requires a very large number of samples with respect to the dimension, i.e., n ≳
477
      d3 log d. Our experiments confirm that the optimization threshold only appears for a very large number
      of training samples with respect to the dimension. However, similar behaviors seem to occur for
478
      smaller orders of magnitude for n in more complex learning problems, such as the training of diffusion
479
      models (Yoon et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2023). This dependency in d might indeed be different
480   for more complex architectures (e.g., with attention) and is worth investigating for future work.

524   6                C ONCLUSION
525
526   This work illustrates on a simple linear example the phenomenon of non-convergence of the parame-
527   ters towards a global minimum of the training loss, despite overparametrisation. This non-convergence
528   actually yields a simplicity bias on the final estimator, which can lead to an optimal fit of the true
529   data distribution. A similar phenomenon has been observed on more complex and realistic settings
530   (Yoon et al., 2023; Kadkhodaie et al., 2023; Raventós et al., 2024), for which a theoretical analysis
531   remains intractable.
532   This result is proven via the description of the early alignment phase. Besides the specific data example
533   considered in Section 4, we also provide concentration bounds on the extremal vectors driving this
534   early alignment. We believe this result can be used in subsequent works to better understand this
535   early phase of the training dynamics, and how it yields biases towards simple estimators.

898   A.5                     S TABILITY OF MINIMA
899
900
      Qiao et al. (2024) argue that the non-convergence of the estimator towards interpolation is due to the
      instability of global minima. More precisely they claim that for large stepsizes, gradient descent (GD)
901
      cannot stabilize around global minima of the loss for large values of n. We present an additional
902
      experiment in this section, illustrating that this non-convergence is not due to an instability of the
903   convergence point of (S)GD, but to it being a stationary point of the loss as predicted by our theory.
904
905   For that, we consider a neural network initialized from the final point (warm restart) of training for
906
      8 000 samples in the experiment of Figure 1.4 We then continue training this network on the same
      training dataset, with a decaying learning rate schedule. Precisely, we start with a learning rate of
907
      0.01 as in the main experiment, and multiply the learning rate by 0.85 every 50 000 iterations of
908
      SGD, so that after 4 × 106 iterations, the final learning rate is of order 10−8 .
909
910   We observe on Figure 5 that the training loss does not change much from the point reached at the
911   end of training with the large learning rate 0.01. Indeed, the training loss was around 0.082 at the
912
      end of this initial training, which is slightly less than the noise level (0.09). While there seems to be
      some stabilization happening at the beginning of this decaying schedule, the training loss seems to
913
      converge to slightly more than 0.0815, confirming that the absence of interpolation is not due to an
914
      instability reason, but rather to a convergence towards a spurious stationary point of the loss.
915

            Another relevant experiment is to train from scratch (no warm restart) with a smaller learning rate. When
917   running the experiment of Appendix A.1 with a smaller learning rate 0.001, we observe again that the parameters
      at convergence correspond to the OLS estimator.
