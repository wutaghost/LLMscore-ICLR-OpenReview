009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative models have gained significant prominence in Natural Language Pro-
012            cessing (NLP), especially in tackling the complex task of modeling and evaluat-
013            ing long text sequences. This task is crucial for advancing various downstream
014            applications, such as text generation and machine translation. Recent methods
015            that utilize stochastic processes to capture the intrinsic dynamics of sequences
016            have shown superior performance in generative modeling. However, the accu-
017            rate encoding of both temporal and structural dependencies from text datasets, as
018            well as leveraging this encoded information for sequence evaluation, remains an
019
               open area of research. In this paper, we propose a novel approach to learn the
               stochastic dynamics of long text sequences, utilizing a negative log-likelihood-
020
               based encoder that outperforms contrastive learning methods. We also introduce a
021
               likelihood-based evaluation metric for long-text assessment, which measures se-
022            quence coherence and can be applied to downstream tasks such as Human-AI dis-
023            crimination. Our encoder preserves sequence coherence effectively and performs
024            robustly on out-of-domain datasets. Additionally, the proposed evaluation metric
025            captures both temporal and structural information comprehensively. Theoretical
026            analysis demonstrates the superiority of our metric in sequence evaluation, and
027            experimental results highlight its flexibility and exceptional performance across a
028            variety of tasks, showcasing its utility in diverse NLP applications.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Generative models are rapidly gaining traction in NLP (Zou et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Yi et al.,
034   2024), particularly in tackling the intricate task of modeling and generating long text sequences.
035   This challenge is critical for various downstream applications, including text generation and machine
036   translation. Recently, the use of stochastic representations to model latent spaces has emerged as a
037   promising solution, demonstrating considerable potential in diverse fields such as time-series anal-
038   ysis (Liu et al., 2021), dynamical flow modeling (Albergo et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden,
039
      2023), and video generation (Zhang et al., 2023). In the domain of text generative models, Wang
      et al. (2022) proposed a method that frames long sequences as stochastic dynamical flows, achieving
040
      competitive results in generating coherent long texts. However, the challenge of accurately learning
041
      the time-dependent probability density functions embedded within text datasets remains unresolved.
042   Moreover, fully harnessing the information encoded in the stochastic representation continues to
043   pose a significant challenge that has yet to be comprehensively addressed.

079
      First, we introduce a novel likelihood-based evaluation metric, Stochastic Process Metric (SPM) for
080   long-text assessment that quantifies coherence by leveraging both temporal and structural dependen-
081   cies of the stochastic process, with detailed explanation in Section 2.1. Unlike similar approaches
082   that rely heavily on the training domain (Lai & Tetreault, 2018; Jeon & Strube, 2022) and are lim-
083   ited by their training schemes – which typically evaluate only pairwise data and restrict comparisons
084   to articles of the same length, SPM offers greater flexibility while maintaining comparable perfor-
085   mance. To demonstrate this, we generalize the established Shuffle test (Barzilay & Lapata, 2005;
086   Joty et al., 2018; Moon et al., 2019) into a Mixed Shuffle test. Instead of merely comparing shuf-
087   fled and unshuffled versions of the same article, this new test also compares across the shuffle and
088
      unshuffled versions between different articles, which can be used to evaluate the robustness of the
      metric regardless of individual article properties, e.g. article length. Moreover, SPM proves use-
089
      ful in downstream tasks such as Human-AI discrimination and performs robustly in out-of-domain
090
      (O.O.D.) tasks, likely due to it capture the structural information of the stochastic representation.
091
092   Second, we present a novel text encoder, Stochastic Process Encoder (SP Encoder) for effectively
093   capturing the stochastic dynamics present in extensive collections of long articles. We propose a
094
      negative log-likelihood-based training objective for text encoders, comparing its effectiveness to
      that of contrastive learning methods (Wang et al., 2022). The SP Encoder translates sequences into
095
      a generalized BB while retaining essential information, leading to improved performance on O.O.D.
096
      tests. In contrast, while contrastive learning can be useful for assessing coherence, it may fall short
097   in adequately capturing the representation of the target stochastic process, which could hinder its
098   effectiveness on other tasks, particularly those involving O.O.D. data.
099
100
      In summary, the main contributions of our work are as follows:
101
             • We propose a novel likelihood-based metric (SPM), designed to capture both temporal and
102
               structural dependencies with a solid theoretical foundation. The SPM demonstrates supe-
103
               rior flexibility and effectiveness in sequence evaluation, as evidenced by its performance
104            across various tasks.
105
106          • We hypothesized and validated that fitting the stochastic representation, as measured by the
107            SPM, can serve as an effective and flexible metric for multiple downstream tasks, such as
               distinguishing between human and AI-generated writing.

      2.1   S TOCHASTIC REPRESENTATION
116
117   In this section, we introduce a stochastic representation of the encoded sequences by modeling them
118   using BBs, which are commonly utilized stochastic processes in various applications. We begin by
119   defining a standard BB {B(t) : t ∈ [0, T ]} with B(0) = 0 and B(T ) = 0. For any t ∈ [0, T ], the
120   process B(t) follows a normal distribution B(t) ∼ N (0, t(T − t)/T ). Additionally, for s, t ∈ [0, T ]
121   with s < t, the covariance between B(s) and B(t) is given by Cov(B(s), B(t)) = s(T − t)/T . A
122   general BB can then be constructed as a + (t/T )(b − a) + σB(t), where a and b are fixed start and
123   end points, respectively, and σ is the standard deviation of the process.
124   For an input sequence s̄ = (s0 , . . . , sT ) with st ∈ Rd for t = 0, 1, . . . , T , we capture temporal
125   dependence using standard BBs. To account for structural dependence among the components, we
126   consider d independent standard BBs B1 (t), . . . , Bd (t) over the interval [0, T ]. At each time t, the
127   sequence is modeled as st = µt + W(B1 (t), . . . , Bd (t))⊤ , where W ∈ Rd×d is a transformation
128   matrix and µt = s0 + (t/T )(sT − s0 ) represents the mean at time t. The structural dependence is
129   captured by Σ = WW⊤ . Let s = (s1 , . . . , sT −1 ) denote the sequence excluding the start and end
130   points, and let µ = (µ1 , . . . , µT −1 ) be the corresponding means.

152   2.2   E NCODERS
153
      The encoder architecture consists of two components: a frozen, pretrained GPT-2 model from the
154
      Hugging Face library and a trainable multi-layer perceptron (MLP) network. We extract the hidden
155
      state corresponding to the end-of-sentence (EOS) token from the last layer of the GPT-2 model. This
156   hidden state serves as the input to a four-layer MLP, which is trained to map the input into the latent
157   space. The purpose of the encoder is to learn a nonlinear mapping from the raw input space to the
158   latent space, denoted as fθ : X → S. In practice, we use two loss functions to train the encoder: the
159   contrastive learning loss LCL and the negative log-likelihood loss LNLL . The contrastive learning
160   loss LCL is primarily designed to enhance the encoder’s ability to distinguish between positive and
161   negative samples from the data, following the approach in (van den Oord et al., 2018). In contrast,
      the negative log-likelihood loss LNLL directly enforces the latent space representations to follow

162
      a specified distribution according to the likelihood function. While Wang et al. (2022) utilized
163   contrastive learning for the encoder, our SP Encoder employs the negative log-likelihood loss, which
164   offers certain advantages over their approach, both theoretically and empirically. For comparison
165   purposes, we first introduce the contrastive learning encoder as presented in Wang et al. (2022).

283               that SPM not only excels in basic shuffle and unshuffle tasks but also achieves outstanding
284               results when comparing articles of varying lengths—an evaluation that current state-of-the-
285               art methods cannot perform effectively.
286             • Q2: Can SPM handle the human-AI discrimination task? In Section 4.2, we show that
287               SPM can effectively distinguish between human-written text and text generated by large
288               language models. Compared to recent well-known methods, SPM delivers competitive
289               results with shorter inference times and a more flexible computation scheme.
290             • Q3: Can the SP Encoder enhance the learning of stochastic representations? In Section
291               4.3, we demonstrate that the SP Encoder outperforms the CL Encoder in learning stochastic
292               representations by effectively using latent dimensions and their correlations. Moreover, this
293               advantage leads to more robust performance when applied to unseen datasets.

483   5   R ELATED WORK
484
485   Stochastic processes have demonstrated robust capabilities in modeling complex tasks across various
      fields, including biology (Horne et al., 2007) and finance (Øksendal & Øksendal, 2003). Recently,


                                                                  9
486
      in the realm of machine learning, the use of stochastic representations to model latent spaces has
487   shown considerable promise in diverse applications such as time-series analysis (Liu et al., 2021)
488   and dynamical flow modeling (Albergo et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023). Notably,
489   such methods excel in generation tasks, including video generation (Zhang et al., 2023), and long
490   text generation (Wang et al., 2022). A critical aspect of these tasks is the accurate learning of
491   stochastic representations from datasets, which requires capturing the time-dependent probability
492   density functions embedded within real-world data. Generally, there are two approaches to tackle
493   this challenge. One method is the likelihood-free training paradigm (Durkan et al., 2020), exempli-
494   fied by contrastive learning techniques, which have demonstrated significant effectiveness in han-
495
      dling high-dimensional data (van den Oord et al., 2018). This approach enables the learning of
      predictive density indirectly, rather than through direct reconstruction (Mathieu et al., 2021). The
496
      alternative method is the traditional likelihood-based approach, such as stochastic interpolants (Al-
497
      bergo et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023), which requires the pre-definition of specific
498   target stochastic processes. Both methods exhibit substantial potential in their respective tasks.
499
500   Coherence, as described by Reinhart (1980), defines the discourse structure of a text, where high-
501
      quality writing should exhibit a logical flow with well-connected ideas and topics. Previous studies
      have indicated that neural language models, particularly transformers, often struggle to effectively
502
      capture coherence structures (Deng et al., 2022). To better guide language models in learning the
503
      dynamics of long texts, methods that construct latent spaces have been developed (Bowman et al.,
504   2016; Gao et al., 2021). These methods aim to model sentence embeddings by leveraging informa-
505   tion from neighboring utterances. However, most of these approaches yield static representations
506   and overlook the dynamic nature of the entire text. A recent study introduced the concept of using
507   stochastic representations, specifically the Brownian bridge, to incorporate “temporal dynamics”
508   into modeling latent space (Wang et al., 2022). This approach demonstrates superior performance
509   in modeling long-range text dependencies and achieving notable results in generating long, coherent
510   texts from learned latent plans. However, their training scheme may lead to ineffective dimensions
511   and fail to fully capture the structural and temporal information encoded in the dataset.
512   In addition to latent space modeling, automatically assessing coherence in any given text remains a
513   challenge (Sheng et al., 2024; Maimon & Tsarfaty, 2023). Building on stochastic concepts, Sheng
514   et al. (2024) developed a heuristic metric for coherence assessment, grounded in the unsupervised
515   learning approach proposed by Wang et al. (2022). This score demonstrated considerable perfor-
516   mance on artificial shuffle tasks. However, their method relies on a heuristic understanding of the
517   Brownian bridge and fails to adequately establish a theoretical foundation for their metric setup,
518
      which limit the effectiveness and flexibility of their score, particularly its sensitivity to article length.
519
520
521
      6    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we investigate the structural and temporal properties encoded in the stochastic repre-
524   sentation of latent trajectories and their applications in NLP tasks, both theoretically and empirically.
525
      First, we propose a novel, flexible coherence evaluation metric, SPM, that is not affected by indi-
      vidual article properties, such as text length. To validate this, we design a Mixed Shuffle test based
526
      on the established Shuffle test. We also discover that our metric, which assesses the fit of target
527
      stochastic processes, can aid in distinguishing between human-written and AI-generated data, in-
528   dicating that the stochastic representation encodes properties useful for human-AI discrimination.
529   Second, we introduce a negative log-likelihood-based training method (SP Encoder) to enhance the
530   learning of stochastic representations. Our results show that the SP Encoder outperforms in O.O.D.
531   tasks, due to its effectiveness in using the latent dimensions as suggested by the covariance matrix.
532
      For future work, we aim to conduct a thorough analysis of how to leverage SPM for multi-domain
533
      tasks, such as domain identification, and to utilize its text-length-independent property to develop
534   text generation models that maintain semantic meaning while being robust across varying text
535   lengths. Additionally, compared to existing well-known Human-AI scores, our method is simpler
536   and more flexible, achieving comparable performance with similar inference times; however, it may
537   become inferior as their model complexity increases, such as much more inference times. Thus,
538   extending this method is an intriguing avenue for exploration. Furthermore, a deeper theoretical
539   understanding of the differences between likelihood-based and contrastive learning-based models is
      another direction for future research.

       Due to time and computational resource constraints, we tested our model using LLaMA3-1B and
1083   LLaMA3-3B, with GPT2-117M which is the LLM model used in the main section. The results are
1084   summarized in the table below. Specifically, we compare the following two tasks:
1085
1086         1. Shuffle Test (Global): LLaMA3-3B outperforms both GPT2-117M and the state-of-the-art
1087            method (Moon et al., 2019), demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing global sequence
1088            structure.
1089         2. Mixed Shuffle Test: LLaMA3-3B surpasses GPT2-117M for smaller blocks (b=1, b=2),
1090            but its performance decreases for larger blocks (b=5, b=10). This may be attributed to
1091            our approach, where only an MLP layer was trained without fine-tuning the LLMs. Con-
1092            sequently, GPT2 might better capture certain latent space properties, leading to a more
1093            balanced performance across tasks.
1094
       For models of the same type, increased parameter sizes consistently yield better performance. How-
1095
       ever, in Mixed Shuffled Task, examining the performance drop from b=1 to b=10 reveals an interest-
1096   ing pattern: LLaMA3-3B exhibits a sharper decrease (26%) compared to LLaMA3-1B (18%) and
1097   GPT2-117M (12%). This suggests a trade-off where larger models excel at capturing local details
1098   (b=1) but might sacrifice robustness for global structures (b=10). This insight highlights an intrigu-
1099   ing direction for future exploration — different LLM architectures may facilitate learning stochastic
1100   representations in task-specific ways.
1101

1102   with recent LLMs provide additional evidence supporting our work’s ability to decode spatial and
1103   temporal information from stochastic representations. They further validate our argument that the
1104   fitness of distribution can be leveraged for various downstream tasks and demonstrate the broader
1105   potential of our approach in addressing diverse challenges with advanced LLMs.
