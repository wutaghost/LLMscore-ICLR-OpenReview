011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep learning approaches have revolutionized artificial intelligence, but model
014            opacity and fragility remain significant challenges. The reason for these chal-
015            lenges, we believe, is a knowledge gap at the heart of the field — the lack of
016            well-calibrated metrics quantifying the similarity of the internal representations of
017            models obtained using different architectures, training strategies, different check-
018            points, or under different random initializations. While several metrics have been
019
               proposed, they are poorly calibrated and susceptible to manipulations and con-
               founding factors, as well as being computationally intensive when probed with a
020
               large and diverse set of test samples. We report here an integration of chain nor-
021
               malization of weights and centered kernel alignment that, by focusing on weight
022            similarity instead of activation similarity, overcomes most of the limitations of
023            existing metrics. Our approach is sample-agnostic, symmetric in weight space,
024            computationally efficient, and well-calibrated.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In the last few decades, deep learning has revolutionized the study and development of artificial
031   intelligence (AI). The revolution has been driven by the almost dizzying introduction of novel model
032
      architectures: fully connected perceptrons, deep convolutional neural networks (Krizhevsky et al.,
      2012), transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) and, most recently,
033
      large language models. These approaches have all achieved remarkable success, pushing the state of
034
      the art across numerous fields, including computer vision, natural language processing, and speech
035   recognition.
036
037   In contrast to prior approaches in computer science, where developing parsimonious models using
038
      limited resources was the goal, the path pursued in AI has been one in which increased performance
      has been achieved at the cost of gigantic models using vast amounts of resources. For example, a
039
      modern large language model can easily contain billions of parameters and requires massive com-
040
      putational and data resources to train, resources that are only available to the largest organizations.
041
042   On par with resource requirements is the fact that increasing model complexity comes at the cost
043   of model opacity. The exact nature of what a perceptron model learns still remains elusive (Calude
044
      et al., 2023), but the lack of understanding grows exponentially larger as more complex architectures
      are considered (Rudin, 2019).
045
046   This lack of interpretability of model predictions is made all the more worrisome because many
047   neural network architectures are known to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Goodfellow et al.,
048   2014), exhibit generalization gaps, and are prone to shortcut learning (DeGrave et al., 2021; Geirhos
049   et al., 2020). All these concerns dramatically limit the ability to deploy deep learning models to
050
      high-stakes settings, such as healthcare systems (T. Dhar et al., 2023).
051   In view of these concerns, it is not surprising that multiple lines of research are dedicated to improv-
052   ing the interpretability, generalizability, and robustness of deep learning models. One fruitful ap-
053   proach has been the development of attribution methods Simonyan et al. (2013); Sundararajan et al.
      (2017); Selvaraju et al. (2019) that aim to identify the specific features driving model predictions.

054
      However, recent studies have revealed a lack of consensus and, at times, inaccurate attributions,
055   raising concerns about the fidelity of these methods (Saporta et al.; Rudin, 2019).
056
057   Another fruitful approach is the investigation of how specific training manipulations can address
058
      known concerns — model stitching (Bansal et al., 2021), component ablation experiments (Shah
      et al.), data augmentation such as adversarial training (Goodfellow et al., 2014), early stopping (Pang
059
      et al., 2020), and batch normalization (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015) have all been used in an attempt to
060
      increase model robustness. Despite their widespread utilization, these techniques still rely on “trial
061   and error,” “common practices,” and “rules of thumb,” rather than systematized knowledge.
062
063   It is our contention that the limitations of all the approaches discussed above are due to a gap at
064
      the heart of the field — the lack of a trustworthy, well-calibrated measure of model similarity. To
      address the limitations of current approaches, we need to quantify whether different neural networks
065
      learn in a similar or distinct manner at various stages of internal processing (Klabunde et al.) or
066
      whether there are universal or idiosyncratic mechanisms underlying reported high performance.
067
068   Li et al. (2015) introduced the concept of convergent learning, which asks whether different neural
069   networks — separately trained with varying random initializations, different architectures, disjoint
070
      data samples, or optimization algorithms — ultimately learn the same underlying representations.
      Various research groups have proposed representational similarity metrics aiming to quantify the
071
      (dis)similarity between activation values computed by different layers or models for a given set of
072
      inputs. The most widely studied representational similarity metrics are canonical correlation analy-
073   sis (CCA) (Morcos et al.), Procrustes (Gower & Dijksterhuis, 2004), and centered kernel alignment
074   (CKA) (Kornblith et al., 2019).
075
076
      Despite the valuable insights provided by research on representational similarity in interpreting deep
      neural networks, there remains a lack of consensus on interpreting the outputs of different represen-
077
      tational similarity metrics (Ding et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2024). For example, Davari et al. (2023)
078
      characterized in detail the sensitivity of CKA to data transformations that do not lead to functional
079   changes for neural networks, demonstrating that CKA can be easily manipulated. The dependence of
080   CKA on specific input manipulations is to be expected, given the nature of representation activation
081   values calculated based on a subset of probing inputs (Fig. 1, Fig. 5).
082
      Here, we propose a new approach. We start from the observation that learned knowledge in a neural
083
      network is captured in the values of the weights acquired during training. Despite their fundamental
084
      importance, there is surprisingly little research on the weight similarity of neural networks. Wang
085   et al. (2022) made the first attempt to explore weight similarity by proposing a chained normalization
086   operator that ensures invariance of weight similarity to permutation of neurons, i.e., the shuffling of
087   neurons within the same layer. Inspired by their work, as well as the foundations of CKA, we pro-
088   pose a novel weight similarity metric that applies CKA to a kernel of chained, normalized weights.
089   Our approach — which we denote weights CKA (wCKA) — shifts the focus from activation-based
090   comparisons to weight-based comparisons. This is an important shift because weights capture the
091   learned parameters of a model, independent of specific inputs and invariant to input perturbations.
092   This idea offers more stable and generalizable insights about model similarity and the ability to deal
093
      with input-dependent and spurious similarities.
094   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
095
096          • We propose a novel metric quantifying the similarity of neural networks in terms of learned
097            parameters — model weights. The proposed metric is invariant to permutation and inter-
098            twiner (Godfrey et al., 2022) transformations, independent of probing input, and computa-
099            tionally efficient (Fig. 1c).
100
             • We benchmark wCKA against three existing representational similarity metrics — Pro-
101
               crustes, CKA, and dCKA — with random initialized neural networks and similar neural
102
               networks obtained from successive training epochs. In contrast to other metrics, wCKA
103            demonstrates robust differentiation power in the calibration task towards the number of
104            probing samples and out-of-distribution corruptions,
105
106          • We validate the reliability of wCKA similarity estimates through the analysis of their cor-
107            relation with functional similarity, quantified by the fraction of agreed predictions on a
               variety of test samples, including out-of-distribution corruptions and adversarial attacks.

      2.1     R EPRESENTATION SIMILARITY
111
112   Representational similarity metrics measure the similarity between representations, i.e., activation
113   values of different layers or models in response to a given set of inputs. Let X1 ∈ Rn×d1 and
114   X2 ∈ Rn×d2 denote the activation matrices of two different layers or models, where n is the number
115   of input samples, e.g. images, and d1 and d2 are the dimensionalities of the activations, i.e. number
116   of neurons at correspondent layers. Each row of X1 or X2 corresponds to the activation pattern of
117
      the layer for a specific input example, and each column corresponds to the activation values of n
      input samples for a single neuron.

      2.4     F UNCTIONAL SIMILARITY
241
242   In addition to internal representations and weights, neural networks may be dissimilar in their func-
243   tionality as well. The intrinsic connection between representational and functional dissimilarity is
244   well recognized (Klabunde et al.) and serves as a fundamental rationale for benchmarking represen-
245   tational similarity metrics in Ding et al. (2021).
246
      To facilitate a standard and systematic evaluation of representational similarity metrics, Ding et al.
247   (2021) introduced a benchmarking framework that emphasizes the intrinsic connection between
248   representational and functional similarity. Essentially, the idea is that if two neural network models
249   exhibit different performances on certain tasks, they must learn different internal representations.
250   Therefore, similarity metrics can be evaluated by the rank correlation between metric distance and
251   functional performance.
252
      To further benchmark wCKA against other similarity metrics, we adopt and extend this benchmark-
253   ing framework. Instead of quantifying the functional distance of two models by their accuracy gap,
254   we use the fraction of agreed predictions between two models across clean test samples, out-of-
255   distribution corruptions, and adversarially attacked samples:
256

260   where O is a vector of model predictions, and i and j are indices capturing the evaluated samples
261   and class labels, respectively.

      3.1   W CKA METRIC HAS SUPERIOR CALIBRATION TO CURRENT METRICS
327
328   We first characterize the calibration characteristics of the four metrics considered here: Procrustes,
329   CKA, dCKA, and wCKA metrics. Specifically, we create ensembles of neural network models
330   for which we expect similarity to be close to zero or close to 1. Starting from fully connected
331   neural networks with four hidden layers, each with 32 neurons, which we train handwritten digits
332   of zero, one, and two from the MNIST dataset. We store model checkpoints from adjacent epochs
333
      after 95 epochs of training when the performance of models has already converged. We set 100
      randomly initialized pre-trained networks as the ensemble of random neural networks with expected
334
      zero similarity. We set 100 models from adjacent training epochs after convergence as the ensemble
335
      of similar neural networks with an expected similarity of 1. We compute activation similarity for
336   Procrustes, CKA, and dCKA on the 3,147 test images — the entire test set — probing samples
337   randomly selected from clean or 15 out-of-distribution corruptions. We compute wCKA directly on
338   chain-normalized weight matrices.
339
      As was pointed out by Davari et al. (2023); Cui et al. (2022), the Procrustes and CKA display
340
      spurious similarities for random neural networks, indicating significant estimation bias (Fig. 1a).
341
      Even dCKA, which yields unbiased similarity values, displays a large estimation uncertainty with
342   some values of activation similarity greater than 0.3. as high as 0.5 when it should be zero as some
343   values smaller than 0.8 when it should be close to 1. In contrast, wCKA consistently yields values
344   close to zero for random neural networks and close to one for similar neural networks (Fig. 1a).
345
      However, calibration is not the only concern with Procrustes, CKA, and dCKA. All three approaches
346
      quantify activation similarity. That is, they must be probed with test images. Figure 1b shows that
347
      estimates of similarity for Procrustes and CKA converge slowly with a number of probing images
348   to their biased estimates. Similarly, dCKA displays slow converging estimation uncertainty.

      3.3   W CKA MODEL SIMILARITY MORE ACCURATELY CAPTURES THE FUNCTIONAL
463         SIMILARITY OF FINE - TUNED , CONVOLUTIONAL NEURAL NETWORKS
464
465   For the last decade, transfer learning has become immensely popular Rasmy et al.. The reason is that
466   fine-tuning pre-trained deep learning models for new domain-specific tasks is a promising way to
467   overcome resource limitations. In transfer learning, one typically fine-tunes a layer of perceptrons,
468   i.e., fully connected neural networks. However, transfer learning, if anything, exacerbates the issue
469   of lack of interpretability. Thus, it is also needed, in this context, to develop trustworthy methods
470
      for quantifying model similarity.
471   To test the applicability of wCKA to transfer learning, we train a simple convolutional neural net-
472   work with two convolutional layers, with 64 and 32 kernels of size 5x5, respectively, and one fully
473   connected hidden layer of 1,024 neurons, on the full dataset of MNIST containing ten classes of
474   handwritten images. This illustrative network achieves its highest validation performance on the
475   24th epoch. We then freeze the convolutional layers and fine-tune the weights of specific fully
476
      connected hidden layers on a simpler task: classifying handwritten digits for zero, one, and two.
477   Following again the pipeline described in Fig. 2, we vary four characteristics of the trained mod-
478   els. We consider three distinct architectures for the fully connected layers: one hidden layer of 32
479   neurons, one hidden layer of 1024 neurons, and ten hidden layers of 32 neurons each. We fine-tune
480   the training of the fully connected layer using either standard or PGD adversarial training. We con-
481   sider different random initializations and different checkpoints. This procedure, though conducted
482
      on very simple model architectures and tasks, nonetheless closely resembles the typical transfer
      learning procedure (Ferreira et al.).
483
484   Figure. 4 shows the results of our benchmarking. It is noteworthy that the estimated similarities cal-
485   culated by all metrics are not as negatively affected when comparing models trained using different
      training strategies. This is particularly striking for Procrustes, CKA, and dCKA, which, when con-

      sidering full training, performed so poorly. The explanation is likely the fact that the convolutional
487   layers are frozen, which imposes a higher degree of similarity even under adversarial training. It is
488   nonetheless visually apparent that wCKA estimates model similarities that also consistently display
489   higher correlation with functional similarity than prior metrics. As wCKA is agnostic to frozen
490   original networks, it can be readily integrated with any type of large model and shed light on the
491   fine-tuning process of fully connected layers — highlighting its robustness.

493   4   D ISCUSSION
494
495   We addressed in this study a major knowledge gap at the heart of deep learning — the lack of a
496   trustworthy, well-calibrated measure of model similarity. This gap has so far prevented the type of
497   progress that one would hope for with regard to understanding how neural network models can be so
498   opaque and fragile. Here, we present a well-calibrated metric that can capture model similarity under
499   a number of critical perturbations: model architecture, training strategy, checkpoints, or random
500   initialization. Our benchmarking experiments demonstrate that wCKA displays superior calibration
501   characteristics: matching setpoints and linearity of relationship to functional similarity.
502   Our approach — to the best of our knowledge — pioneers the integration of chain normalization
503   of weight matrices with centered kernel alignment, a widely used similarity metric for estimating
504   similarity between the internal representations of two neural network models. wCKA offers several
505   advantages over competing metrics: it measures directly on the learned parameters (weights) of the
506   model, reflects weight space symmetries, is independent of probing samples, and is computationally
507   efficient.
508   In order to provide a first exploration of the degree to which is applicable outside of fully connected
509   neural networks, we investigated its performance in a simplified implementation of transfer learn-
510   ing. We believe that the consistently high performance of wCKA when estimating the similarity of
511   fine-tuned convolutional neural networks highlights its potential. As wCKA is agnostic to feature
512   extraction layers, it can be incorporated into evaluating the fine-tuning process of dense layers with
513   more sophisticated feature extractor architectures, such as Autoencoders Landi et al..
514   We are not yet presenting here a formulation of wCKA that is able to estimate the similarity of
515   fully-tunable architecture, including convolutional layers, layers with residual connections, or batch
516   normalization layers. Nonetheless, we believe that our study already presents compelling evidence
517   for the potential impact of wCKA in helping researchers better characterize and understand what
518   a deep learning model has learned and how that learning changes under different perturbations.
519   For example, our approach could be used to guide the pruning and compressing of models, for
520   building truly diverse ensembles of models, or for ensuring model similarity to a trusted model. We
521
      believe that further exploration of how to measure weight similarity for more complex structures is
      an important future direction that will bear significant fruit.
