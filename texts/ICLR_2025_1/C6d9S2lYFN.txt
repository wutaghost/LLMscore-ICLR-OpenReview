009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               The rapid development of deepfake techniques has raised serious concerns about
012
               the authenticity and integrity of digital media. To combat the potential mis-
013
               use of deepfakes, it is crucial to develop reliable and robust deepfake detec-
014            tion algorithms. In this paper, we propose a comprehensive Deepfake Detector
015            Assessment Platform (DAP), covering six critical dimensions: benchmark per-
016            formance, forgery algorithm generalization, image distortion robustness, adver-
017            sarial attack resilience, forgery localization accuracy, and attribute bias. Our
018            framework aims to provide a standardized and rigorous approach to assess the
019            performance, generalization ability, robustness, security, localization precision,
020            and fairness of deepfake detection algorithms. Extensive experiments are con-
021            ducted on multiple public and self-built databases, considering various forgery
022
               techniques, image distortions, adversarial attacks, and attributes. The proposed
               framework offers insights into the strengths and limitations of state-of-the-art
023
               deepfake detection algorithms and serves as a valuable tool for researchers and
024
               practitioners to develop and evaluate novel approaches in this field. All codes,
025            scripts, and data described in this paper are open source and available at https:
026            //github.com/tempuser4567/DAP.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The abuse of manipulation and generation technologies will cause chaos in many aspects, such
033   as financial markets. For example, a finance worker was tricked into paying out $25 million to
034   fraudsters in a video conference call generated through deepfake technology (Chen & Magramo,
035   2024). In order to reduce the negative impact brought by the malicious use of deepfake techniques,
036   many deepfake detection techniques(Li & Lyu, 2018; Yan et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2021; Qian et al.,
037   2020; Woo et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Haiwei et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020a) have been proposed.
038   Unfortunately, the detection accuracy of these detectors is actually low. There is a significant gap
039   between the efficacy reported in papers and the actual performance of data in real-world scenarios,
040   which makes it difficult to implement them in practice. Many factors might contribute to this phe-
041   nomenon. Figure 1 outlines the entire pipeline from fake image and video generation to detection.
042   Three main steps contribute to this phenomenon: (1) Fake Result Generation; (2) Image Distortion;
043   and (3) Adversarial Attacks. Fake Result Generation: Based on the type of input data, a proper
044   manipulation or generation algorithm is employed to create a fake face. Any type of deepfake algo-
045
      rithm may potentially be used. Image Distortion: Typically, when users upload images or videos
      on the Internet, social media platforms apply a certain degree of compression, and some users may
046
      even use built-in tools to adjust details like contrast. Additionally, some users might add noise, blur,
047
      or other distortions to the generated results to avoid detection. Adversarial Attacks: Some indi-
048   viduals with advanced technical skills might perform more professional anti-forensic processing on
049   the forged results, such as image reconstruction (Neves et al., 2020) and adversarial attack (Li et al.,
050   2021a). Most forgers do not want their forged fake results to be detected by detection algorithms.
051   Therefore, combining forgery with adversarial attacks poses a significant challenge to detection al-
052   gorithms. Consequently, in practical detection scenarios, besides basic accuracy, the capabilities of
053   Forgery Algorithm Generalization, Image Distortion Robustness, and Adversarial Attack Resilience
      are equally important.

066   Although some evaluations of detection algorithms exist (Yan et al., 2023b; Pei et al., 2024;
067   Deng et al., 2024), they have several shortcomings: (1) They almost exclusively depend on pub-
068   lic databases; (2) They lack actual tests with self-generated fake data; (3) They include too few
069
      evaluation aspects.
070   To more comprehensively assess the detection capabilities of algorithms under various complex
071   conditions, we have built a Deepfake Detector Assessment Platform (DAP). The main functions of
072   this platform are: (1) Benchmark Performance Evaluation, (2) Forgery Algorithm Generalization
073   Assessment, (3) Image Distortion Robustness Assessment, (4) Adversarial Attack Resilience Eval-
074   uation, (5) Attribute Bias Assessment, (6) Forgery Localization Accuracy Evaluation. Image Dis-
075
      tortion Robustness Assessment evaluates the robustness of detection algorithms to common image
      distortions. The platform applies nine types of image distortions, including adding noise, blur, and
076
      compression. Each type consists of 2-8 evaluation tasks. Adversarial Attack Resilience Evaluation
077
      assesses the detector’s resistance to adversarial attacks, such as image reconstruction. Attribute Bias
078   Assessment examines whether detection algorithms exhibit detection bias toward certain attributes,
079   such as gender. Forgery Localization Accuracy Evaluation assesses the detection algorithm’s abil-
080   ity to locate forgeries. It evaluates the accuracy of detecting forged regions in images and forged
081   segments in videos.
082
      This platform serves as a benchmark to continuously validate whether various improvements in
083
      detection algorithms are effective under complex conditions. It helps narrow the gap between the
084   detection performance reported in papers and practice. All codes, scripts, and data described in this
085   paper are publicly available at https://github.com/tempuser4567/DAP.
086
087
      The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
088          • We comprehensively analyzes the entire process from fake image generation to detection,
089            identifying key factors that influence detection, including the diversity of deepfake manip-
090            ulation algorithms, image distortions applied to forgery results, and adversarial attacks on
091            forgery results.
092          • The paper designs a platform architecture for evaluating deepfake detection algorithms,
093            comprising six critical dimensions and 27 detail evaluation items. Notably, it includes the
094            category of Large Multimodal Models, expanding the evaluation coverage.
095          • This paper implements algorithms and scripts for deepfake manipulation, image distortion,
096            and adversarial attack. The platform generates the necessary data (5,976,145 self-generated
097            fake images) for each evaluation category using a unified pipeline, ensuring fair assessment
098            among detection algorithms.
099          • The paper deploys and evaluates 12 popular detection algorithms, conducting practical
100            evaluations and analyses.

      3.3   I MAGE D ISTORTION ROBUSTNESS A SSESSMENT
241
      Images and videos may undergo certain changes before release, such as adjusting brightness, con-
242
      trast, color, etc. Besides, some people intentionally add some noise or perform blur processing
243   to evade detection algorithms Therefore, the robustness of a deepfake detection algorithm is very
244   important.
245
246
      We selected the following nine types as common image disruptions: Compression, Brightness, Con-
      trast, Flip, Rotation, Color, Sharpness, Blur, and Noise. Figure 11 shows the overview of common
247
      image distortion for a fake image.
248
249   Compression: To evaluate the robustness of the detection algorithm to compression, the platform
250   compressed the original images to JPG format in eight degrees: 0.99, 0.95, 0.90, 0.85, 0.80, 0.70,
251   0.60, and 0.50. Brightness, Contrast, sharpness, and Color: These distortions belong to the basic
252
      photo color adjustment. For each type of distortion, we take two degrees with greater change effects
      (-50% and +50%) to make it easier to see whether the detection algorithm can resist the impact of
253
      these distortions. Flip and Rotation: Flip adopts two methods: from left to right and from top to
254
      bottom. The rotation uses four clockwise rotation degrees: 45°, 135°, 225°, and 315°. Blur and
255   Noise: These distortions are often used to hide forgery defects and evade deepfake detection. The
256   evaluation platform uses Gaussian, Mean, and Medium filters to achieve blur effects. It also adds
257   noise in the form of Gaussian, Salt and Pepper, and even tokens.
258
      Through the image distortion robustness assessment, the platform can systematically evaluate the
259
      detection performance of the algorithm under different types and degrees of distortions, facilitating
260
      the analysis of its robustness.

263   3.4   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK R ESILIENCE E VALUATION
264
265   This section is primarily used to evaluate whether the deepfake detection algorithm can resist adver-
266
      sarial attacks designed to evade deepfake detection.
267   Figure 5 shows the pipeline of adversarial attack. Image Reconstruction: The detection algorithm
268   identifies fake images primarily by detecting forgery traces within the image. Autoencoder can be
269   used to reconstruct an image with minimal visual differences between the original and reconstructed
      images (Neves et al., 2020). Adversarial Perturbation: In contrast to image reconstruction, this

      method does not remove the forgery traces but instead interferes with the detection algorithm by
271   adding an adversarial perturbation (Li et al., 2021a), leading to misclassification.
272
273   We can compare the evaluation results with those without adversarial attacks and study the defense
274
      strategies, such as adversarial training, input conversion, model integration, etc., to improve its
      robustness against adversarial attacks.

472
473   5          C ONCLUSION
474
475   This paper analyzes two issues: (1) the difficulty in reproducing experimental results and (2) the
476   low detection accuracy in real-world scenarios. Through a comprehensive review of the entire
477
      process from image forgery to detection, we have identified potential causes of these issues
      and accordingly established a comprehensive and fair deepfake detector evaluation platform.
478
      This platform evaluates detectors from six major dimensions, deploying 16 deepfake algorithms
479
      involving five main categories to generate a large amount of fake data for generalization assessment.
480   Especially, five large multimodal models are included. Additionally, the platform is equipped
481   with adversarial attack-related algorithms to practically test the detectors’ resistance. Finally,
482   considering the practical usage of detectors, the platform introduces attribute bias and forgery
483   localization assessments, both of which have been tested and analyzed. As deepfake algorithms
484   continue to proliferate and evasion techniques mature, the demand for forgery localization of fake
485   videos and images will become more urgent. Therefore, the platform can serve as a benchmark to
      drive the development of deepfake detectors toward more practical applications.

705   A     R ELATED W ORK
706
707   In this section, we introduce deepfake generation techniques, deepfake detection algorithms and
708
      existing evaluation approaches and their limitations.
709
710   A.1   D EEPFAKE G ENERATION T ECHNIQUES
711
712
      Deepfake generation technologies can be categorized into the following four major types: Face
      Swapping, Facial Reenactment, Facial Attribute Manipulation, and Entire Face Synthesis. Face
713
      Swapping is used to replace face A in the target image with face B in the source image. The
714
      most popular face-swapping algorithms are FaceShifter (Li et al., 2019), FSGAN (Nirkin et al.,
715   2019), SimSwap (Chen et al., 2020), and MegaFS (Zhu et al., 2021). Facial Reenactment is used
716   to reenact the facial expressions and movements of a target video on the face in a source image.
717   Face2Face (Thies et al., 2016), Neural Textures (Thies et al., 2019), DG (Hsu et al., 2022), and
718   HyperReenact (Bounareli et al., 2023) are classical algorithms for achieving Facial Reenactment.
719   Facial Attribute Manipulation is also known as face editing. It is used to modify the attributes of
720   a human face, including gender, hair color, age, etc. Many studies pay attention to this category,
721   such as STGAN (Liu et al., 2019), StarGAN2 (Choi et al., 2020), and FDNeRF (Zhang et al.,
722   2022). Entire Face Synthesis is used to generate non-existent human face images or videos. The
723
      classical algorithms are ProGAN (Gao et al., 2019), StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019), StyleGAN-
      v (Skorokhodov et al., 2022). Please note that Entire Face Synthesis can generate videos, such as
724
      StyleGAN-v (Skorokhodov et al., 2022). Large Multimodal Models, which are based on diffusion
725
      models, have become popular in recent years for generating images and videos. These include
726   two generation categories: text-to-image and image-to-video. Due to their realistic effects, they are
727   widely used in face synthesis. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022a), Mini-dalle3 (Zeqiang et al.,
728   2023), and Stable Video Diffusion (Blattmann et al., 2023) are widespread Algorithms.
729
730   A.2   D EEPFAKE D ETECTION A LGORITHMS
731
732   Detection algorithms can be categorized into three types based on detection cues: data-driven detec-
733   tors, spatial artifact-based detectors, and frequency artifact-based detectors. Data-driven detectors
734   use a large dataset of real and fake data (images and videos) to train the detection algorithm to learn
735   the differences between real and fake images. The well-trained model is then used to detect the
736   authenticity of images. Classical detectors are MesoNet (Afchar et al., 2018), EfficientNet-B4 (Tan
737
      & Le, 2019), Capsule-forensics (Nguyen et al., 2019b), and Xception (Rossler et al., 2019). This
      type of detector is relatively simple in design but heavily relies on the training data. If the test data
738
      significantly differs from the training data, the detection performance will degrade substantially.
739
      Spatial artifact detectors take image inconsistencies as clues for detection. Techniques related to
740   Face Swapping, Facial Reenactment, and Facial Attribute Manipulation usually only forge the facial
741   region of an image, leaving other non-facial background areas unchanged. This leads to incon-
742   sistencies between the modified and unmodified regions. Many inconsistencies have already been
743   studied, such as color space (He et al., 2019), saturation cues (McCloskey & Albright, 2019), and
744   noise (Wang & Chow, 2023). The advantage of these detectors lies in their stronger generalization
745   capabilities compared to data-driven detectors. Frequency artifact detectors first transform images
746   from the time domain to the frequency domain. Then, they draw the detection conclusion by ex-
747   amining whether the frequency domain features are abnormal. There are many studies in this field,
748
      such as F3-Net (Qian et al., 2020), FDFL (Li et al., 2021b), and FreqNet (Tan et al., 2024).
749
750   A.3   E XISTING E VALUATION A PPROACHES AND T HEIR L IMITATIONS

      Currently, several studies have been proposed to survey and evaluate the performance of detection
      algorithms (Masood et al., 2023; Juefei-Xu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2024; Seow et al., 2022; Deng
753
      et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2023b)
754
755   Studies (Masood et al., 2023; Juefei-Xu et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2024; Seow et al., 2022) are surveys
      on deepfake. These studies organize deepfake generation and detection algorithms, categorize them

      according to their characteristics, and summarize the highlights and limitations of each algorithm.
757   However, the authors compare and analyze detection algorithms only from the performance results

      Research (Deng et al., 2024) finds that evaluation performance varies for the same detector and

      they propose a fair benchmark to measure the performance of a range of detectors. The authors

      also generate self-generated examples using two face-swapping algorithms to build a private dataset

949   B.3     I MAGE D ISTORTION ROBUSTNESS A SSESSMENT
950
951   The robustness of a deepfake detection algorithm is very important, because the data to be detected
952   may undergo unexpected distortions which affects the detection results. The images and videos
953   spread on the internet, whether real or fake data, are likely to undergo a certain degree of com-
954   pression. The compressed data is different from the original data. Images and videos may undergo
955
      certain optimizations before release, such as adjusting brightness, contrast, color, etc. Besides, some
      people intentionally add some noise or perform blur processing to evade detection algorithms

962   image. Please note, image distortion affects not only fake images but also real images, such as
963   Compression on the Internet. Therefore, the platform performs image common distortion both for
964   real and fake images.
965
      Compression: Compression is an important factor affecting the robustness of detection algorithms.
966   Because the compressed data and the original data are usually not within the same data domain,
967   learning-based detection algorithms may not have learned the difference between compressed real
968   and fake data. As a result, the detection algorithm will perform high false or missed detection rates.
969   To evaluate the robustness of the detection algorithm to compression, the platform compressed the
970   original images to JPG format in eight degrees: 0.99, 0.95, 0.90, 0.85, 0.80, 0.70, 0.60, and 0.50.

1032   B.4     A DVERSARIAL ATTACK R ESILIENCE E VALUATION
1033
1034   This section is primarily used to evaluate whether the deepfake detection algorithm can resist ad-
1035   versarial attacks designed to evade deepfake detection. We have discussed some methods, such as
1036   blur, to hide forgery defects and fool deepfake detection algorithm in Section B.3.1. But those are
1037   only based on common image distortions. In this section, the platform addresses more advanced
1038
       learning-based adversarial attacks, including image reconstruction and adversarial perturbation at-
       tacks.
1039
1040
1041
       B.4.1    A DVERSARIAL ATTACK G ENERATION
1042   Figure 5 shows the pipeline of adversarial attack. When there are no adversarial attacks, a manipu-
1043   lated or generated face image is easy to detect as a fake image. However, when adversarial attacks
1044   are implemented, images that should be detected as fake may be incorrectly classified as real.
1045
       Both image reconstruction and adversarial perturbation attacks have two main objectives: (1) The
1046
       image after the attack should appear as similar as possible to the image before the attack. (2) The
1047   detection algorithm should be highly likely to classify the fake image as a real image.
1048
1049   Image Reconstruction: The detection algorithm identifies fake images primarily by detecting
1050
       forgery traces within the image. If these traces are removed, the fake images can evade deepfake de-
       tection. Autoencoder can be used to reconstruct an image with minimal visual differences between
1051
       the original and reconstructed images (Neves et al., 2020).
1052
1053   Adversarial Perturbation: In contrast to image reconstruction, this method does not remove the
1054   forgery traces but instead interferes with the detection algorithm by adding an adversarial perturba-
1055   tion (Li et al., 2021a), leading to misclassification. The adversarial perturbation can be specifically
1056
       generated for a particular detection algorithm, or it can be a general adversarial perturbation with
       strong generalization capabilities. Please note that the adversarial perturbation can be added not
1057
       only after the fake image is generated, but also within the latent space layer.
1058
1059
       B.4.2    E VALUATION DATA P REPARATION
1060
1061   To facilitate comparison with performance metrics before the adversarial attack, the platform uses
1062   the pre-processed public database data from Section B.1.3. Unlike the image common distortion
1063   in Section B.3.2, the platform does not need to perform adversarial attacks on all data, because the
1064   real image is real and does not require additional processing. Therefore, only the forged data is
1065   processed. The relevant data from the public databases is subjected to image reconstruction and the
1066   addition of adversarial perturbation. These adversarial forged data are then combined with the real
1067
       data to form the evaluation database.
1068
1069   B.4.3    R ESILIENCE E VALUATION AGAINST ATTACKS
1070
       The platform evaluates the detection algorithm using organized evaluation data and obtains its eval-
1071   uation results for different types of adversarial attacks. Then, we can compare the evaluation results
1072   with those without adversarial attacks and study the defense strategies, such as adversarial training,
1073   input conversion, model integration, etc., to improve its robustness against adversarial attacks.

1556   D        L IMITATIONS
1557
1558   The proposed Deepfake Detector Assessment Platform (DAP) offers a comprehensive evaluation
1559   framework for assessing the performance, generalization ability, robustness, security, localization
1560   precision, and fairness of deepfake detection algorithms. However, there are a few limitations that
1561   can be addressed in future work to further enhance the platform’s capabilities and real-world appli-
1562
       cability.
1563   One limitation is that the platform currently focuses on image-based deepfake detection, while
1564   video-based deepfakes are becoming increasingly prevalent and pose unique challenges. Extending
1565   the platform to support video-based deepfake detection would require the incorporation of temporal
       information and the development of suitable evaluation metrics that consider the consistency and


                                                                                   29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       coherence of detected forgeries across frames. This extension would enable the platform to provide
1567   a more comprehensive assessment of deepfake detection algorithms’ performance in real-world sce-
1568   narios.
1569
1570
       Another limitation is related to the diversity and complexity of the datasets used for evaluation.
       While the platform incorporates a wide range of public and self-generated datasets, it could benefit
1571
       from the inclusion of even more challenging datasets that cover extreme poses, occlusions, low-
1572
       resolution images, and other factors that are commonly encountered in real-world settings. Evaluat-
1573   ing detection algorithms on these challenging datasets would provide a more accurate assessment of
1574   their robustness and ability to handle diverse and complex cases.
1575
1576
       Lastly, the computational efficiency of the platform could be improved to handle large-scale evalu-
       ations more effectively. As the number of deepfake generation and detection algorithms continues
1577
       to grow, along with the size and complexity of datasets, optimizing the evaluation pipeline and
1578
       leveraging parallel processing techniques would enable faster and more efficient assessments. This
1579   would facilitate the timely evaluation of new algorithms and the ability to keep pace with the rapidly
1580   evolving landscape of deepfake technologies.

1585
1586
1587
       The development of the Deepfake Detector Assessment Platform opens up several exciting avenues
       for future research and improvement. One key direction is to extend the platform to support video-
1588
       based deepfake detection, as mentioned in the limitations section. This would involve incorporating
1589
       temporal information, such as frame-level consistencies and inconsistencies, into the evaluation
1590   process. Furthermore, developing suitable evaluation metrics that consider the temporal aspects of
1591   video-based deepfakes would provide a more comprehensive assessment of detection algorithms’
1592   performance in this domain.
1593
       Another important future direction is to establish collaborations with the research community to en-
1594
       sure that the platform remains up-to-date with the latest advancements in deepfake generation and
1595
       detection algorithms. By actively engaging with researchers and practitioners, the platform can in-
1596   corporate state-of-the-art techniques and datasets, ensuring its continued relevance and effectiveness
1597   in the rapidly evolving field of deepfake detection. This collaborative approach would also facilitate
1598   the sharing of knowledge and best practices, fostering innovation and accelerating progress in this
1599   critical area.
1600
       Investigating the potential of integrating explainable AI techniques into the platform is another
1601
       promising future direction. Explainable AI aims to provide insights into the decision-making pro-
1602   cess of machine learning models, making them more interpretable and trustworthy. By incorporating
1603   explainable AI techniques into the evaluation process, the platform could offer a deeper understand-
1604   ing of how deepfake detection algorithms arrive at their decisions, identifying the key features and
1605   patterns they rely on. This would not only enhance the interpretability of the detection algorithms
1606   but also facilitate the development of more robust and reliable models.
1607
       Exploring the use of transfer learning and few-shot learning approaches is another area of future
1608   work that could significantly improve the generalization ability of deepfake detection algorithms.
1609   Transfer learning involves leveraging knowledge gained from one task or domain to improve per-
1610   formance on another related task or domain, while few-shot learning aims to learn from a limited
1611   number of examples. By incorporating these approaches into the evaluation process, the platform
1612   could assess the ability of detection algorithms to adapt to new deepfake generation techniques and
1613   datasets with minimal retraining, which is crucial in real-world scenarios where labeled data may be
1614   scarce.
1615   Finally, developing a user-friendly interface and visualization tools for the platform would greatly
1616   enhance its accessibility and usability for researchers, practitioners, and policymakers. An intuitive
1617   interface would allow users to easily configure and run evaluations, while interactive visualization
1618   tools would enable them to explore and analyze the results in a meaningful way. This would pro-
1619   mote wider adoption of the platform and facilitate collaboration among stakeholders, ultimately
       contributing to the development of more effective and trustworthy deepfake detection solutions.
