011
012
               Scale is essential in modern deep learning; however, greater scale brings a greater
               need to make experiments efficient. Often, most of the effort is spent finding good
013
               hyperparameters, so we should consider exactly how much to spend searching
014
               for them—unfortunately this requires a better understanding of hyperparameter
015            search, and how it converges, than we currently have. An emerging approach to
016            such questions is the tuning curve, or the test score as a function of tuning effort. In
017            theory, the tuning curve predicts how the score will increase as search continues;
018            in practice, current estimators use nonparametric assumptions that, while robust,
019            can not extrapolate beyond the current search step. Such extrapolation requires
020            stronger assumptions—realistic assumptions designed for hyperparameter tuning.
021            Thus, we derive an asymptotic theory of random search. Its central result is a new
022            limit theorem that explains random search in terms of four interpretable quantities:
023
               the effective number of hyperparameters, the variance due to random seeds, the
               concentration of probability around the optimum, and the best hyperparameters’
024
               performance. These four quantities parametrize a new probability distribution,
025
               the noisy quadratic, which characterizes the behavior of random search. We test
026            our theory against three practical deep learning scenarios, including pretraining in
027            vision and fine-tuning in language. Based on 1,024 iterations of search in each, we
028            confirm our theory achieves excellent fit. Using the theory, we construct the first
029            confidence bands that extrapolate the tuning curve. Moreover, once fitted, each
030            parameter of the noisy quadratic answers an important question—such as what is
031            the best possible performance. So others may use these tools in their research, we
032            make them available at (URL redacted).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Deep learning advances through experimentation, but as models have grown in scale, experiments
057
      have grown in cost. As cost becomes the bottleneck, researchers must compromise either rigor or
058   speed—unless experiments become more efficient. In many experiments, the most expensive step is
059   finding good hyperparameters; thus, we should only spend the necessary effort to search for them.
060   The issue is: this search is often a blackbox, with few tools for understanding it or how it converges.
061
      To answer such questions, an emerging approach is to estimate the tuning curve, or the test score as
062
      a function of tuning effort (Dodge et al., 2019; Lourie et al., 2024). Figure 1 illustrates an example.
063
      The x-axis measures tuning effort (e.g., iterations, compute), and the y-axis measures performance
064   (e.g., F1, perplexity). The tuning curve reveals where the score levels off and what the best possible
065   score might be, removing many subjective judgments previously required in experiments. However,
066   while the tuning curve might clarify convergence, its estimators are nonparametric and thus do not
067   extrapolate. What is more: the tuning curve describes search, but does not explain it. If a model
068   tunes slowly, is the search space too big or the problem just difficult? Predicting future progress,
069   explaining how search progresses, calls for something of greater strength.
070   Thus, we derive an asymptotic theory of random search. Its central result is a novel limit theorem
071   characterizing the tail of random search. Focusing on the better scores, their distribution converges to
072   a new family: the noisy quadratic distribution. This family explains random search in terms of four
073   interpretable quantities: the effective number of hyperparameters, the variance due to random seeds,
074   the concentration of probability around the optimum, and the best hyperparameters’ performance.
075   The theory is mechanistic—deriving from how random search works—and built on two empirical
076   precepts: the hyperparameter loss is smooth, and the noise when retraining is normal with constant
077   variance. Remarkably, this simple structure emerges as you approach the optimal hyperparameters,
078
      suggesting a new discovery: the asymptotic regime. Empirically, the asymptotic regime governs
      random search after only a few iterations, thus the theory explains much of its behavior in practice.
079
080   Still, the ultimate test of any theory is how well it reflects the data. We validate our theory in three
081   practical deep learning scenarios, including pretraining in vision and fine-tuning in language (§4).
082   With 1,024 iterations in each, we assess how well our theoretical form fits the empirical distribution
083   from random search (§4.1). In all three scenarios, the theoretical form adheres closely to the ground
084
      truth and remains within its 95% confidence bands. Beyond fit, we test whether the noise is actually
      normal with constant variance (§4.2). In fact, it converges to normality long before the asymptotic
085
      regime, and while the variance begins inflated it quickly converges to a constant. Last, we test our
086
      theory’s application. In each scenario, we construct point estimates and confidence bands using 48
087   search iterations (§4.3). The point estimates mostly smooth their nonparametric baselines; however,
088   the confidence bands show a dramatic improvement. While the nonparametric bands become trivial
089   after a fraction of the total iterations, the parametric confidence bands extrapolate beyond them.
090
      Our theory reflects the data, derives from realistic assumptions, and extrapolates the tuning curve
091
      with complete statistical rigor; therefore, it provides a solid foundation for experiments involving
092
      hyperparameters. Beyond tuning curves, it offers other tools for researchers and practitioners to
093   better understand their models. For example, each parameter of the noisy quadratic answers its own
094   question, such as: what is the effective number of hyperparameters? How much variation is due to
095   random seeds? Or, what is the best possible performance? Using standard statistical techniques,
096   each of these parameters can be estimated with confidence. So that others may use these tools in
097   their research, we make them available at (URL redacted).

      2       A N A SYMPTOTIC T HEORY OF R ANDOM S EARCH
109
110   Let us develop the asymptotic theory of random search.1 We begin by describing the formalism,
111   then present the theory in two parts. First, we assume the score is a deterministic function of the
112   hyperparameters. This assumption permits straightforward analysis; however, it is too simple to
113   describe most applications of interest. Thus, we extend this analysis with the additive noise from
114   random elements such as the initialization and data ordering. Empirical results show this approach
115   offers an excellent model of random search in deep learning (§4).
116
117   2.1       F ORMALIZING R ANDOM S EARCH
118
119
      Imagine fitting a neural network—perhaps you pretrain a ResNet on image classification.2 Many
      choices must be made: Which architecture? What learning rate? How much regularization? Each
120
      choice is a hyperparameter, and each hyperparameter takes a value from the hyperparameter search
121
      space, x = [x1 , . . . , xd ] ∈ X. Fixing these values and evaluating the network produces a score,
122   y ∈ Y ⊂ R, such as accuracy, that we wish to optimize.
123
124   Optimizing this score means optimizing the hyperparameters; and for that, we use a hyperparameter
125
      tuning algorithm. Such algorithms typically sample choices from a stochastic policy, so we treat
      both the choices, X 1 , . . . , X n , and their scores, Y1 , . . . , Yn , as random variables. Since sampling
126
      the next choice costs only a fraction of evaluating it, we use the number of evaluations, n, to measure
127
      the cost. Of course, the cost of an evaluation can vary greatly between models and search spaces, so
128   each evaluation should be normalized by its average cost when making such comparisons.
129
130   We capture hyperparameter tuning’s progress as a function of cost by the tuning process:
131                                           Tk := max Yi                                                       (1)
                                                             i=1...k
132
      In general, the tuning process depends on both the model and the hyperparameter tuning algorithm.
133
134   While one could use any algorithm to optimize hyperparameters for deployment, research requires
135   a standard pick to ensure fair comparisons in development. One simple, robust, and surprisingly
136   effective standard is random search. Random search independently draws choices from the search
137   distribution, X i ∼ X . Evaluating a choice then yields a score from the score distribution, Yi ∼ Y.
138   Under random search, analyzing the tuning process is particularly tractable because the choices,
139   Xi , and thus the scores, Yi , are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). Intuitively, since all
140   scores are i.i.d., the best score after k rounds is just the maximum from a sample of size k. As a
141   result, the CDF of Yi , F (y) = P (Yi ≤ y), and that of Tk , Fk (y) = P (Tk ≤ y), share a relationship:

142                                                            Y
143                         Fk (y) = P max Yi ≤ y =                 P (Yi ≤ y) = F (y)k                      (2)
                                             i=1...k
144                                                               i=1...k

145   Essentially, the distribution from one round of random search defines the distribution from k rounds
146   of random search. Then after n rounds, we have a sample of size n with which to estimate that
147
      distribution. Using these insights, we can estimate properties of the entire tuning process.
148   Still, the tuning process is a complex joint distribution that is difficult to interpret or compare. Often,
149   it is more convenient to consider a summary, such as the median. Following Lourie et al. (2024), we
150   define Tk (k ∈ R) as the random variable with CDF F (y)k . Then, letting M[X] denote the median
151   of X, the tuning curve is the function, τ : R → Y:
152                                                τ (k) := M[Tk ]                                            (3)
153   More generally, one might distinguish the median, τm (k)      :=  τ (k), and expected, τe (k) tuning curve:
154
                                                   τe (k) := E[Tk ]                                           (4)
155
156   The tuning curve answers many questions a researcher might ask during model development. To
157
      find the best achievable performance, researchers can look at the tuning curve’s limit. To check if a
      model is undertuned, researchers can see how performance increases with a few more iterations of
158
      search. To compare models while accounting for tuning effort, they can compare the tuning curves
159
      at various budgets, adjusting each curve by the average cost to train that model.

219   often not the case. More commonly, the score varies—even with the same hyperparameters—due

      3       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
271
272
      To test our theory, we need large samples of random search with different models. We obtain 1,024
273   iterations of random search with three models: DeBERTa (He et al., 2021), DeBERTaV3 (He et al.,
274   2023), and ResNet18 (He et al., 2016). DeBERTa and DeBERTaV3 are pretrained transformers for
275   traditional NLP tasks. DeBERTa pretrains with a generative objective, while DeBERTaV3 utilizes a
276   discriminative one. We use data from Lourie et al. (2024) who fine-tuned these models on MultiNLI
277   (Williams et al., 2018), a natural language inference benchmark. Lourie et al. (2024) used the same
278   search space for both models, running 1,024 iterations of random search for each and tuning the
279   learning rate, proportion of the first epoch for warmup, batch size, number of epochs, and dropout.
280   The other model, ResNet18, is a classic convolutional architecture for computer vision (He et al.,
281
      2016). We pretrain ResNet18 on ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015), an image classification
      benchmark. Using the FFCV library,5 we trained ResNet18 with momentum SGD and a 1-cycle
282
      learning rate schedule. We ran 1,024 iterations of random search, tuning the learning rate, peak
283
      epoch, momentum, batch size, epochs, weight decay, label smoothing, and use of blurpool.
284
285   The search distributions for these models can be found in §C. Now, we will describe the details of
286   each analysis.
287   Assessing Goodness of Fit. To assess goodness of fit, we compare the score distribution to the fitted
288   noisy quadratic distribution. We estimate the score distribution using the empirical CDF (eCDF) and
289   highest density LD confidence bands as recommended in Lourie et al. (2024), and we fit the noisy
290   quadratic distribution to the tail via censored maximum spacing estimation (Cheng & Amin, 1983;
291   Ranneby, 1984). The best threshold for the asymptotic regime was selected using visual diagnostics.
292   Estimating and Extrapolating the Tuning Curve. To explore our theory’s practical application,
293   we subsample 48 iterations of random search without replacement from the full 1,024 for each
294   model. We use all 1,024 iterations to estimate the ground truth eCDF. For nonparametric estimates,
295   we construct the eCDF and LD highest density confidence bands from the subsample, as in Lourie
296   et al. (2024). For parametric estimates, we select the asymptotic regime via visual diagnostics using
297   only the subsample, fit the noisy quadratic distribution to the tail via censored maximum spacing
298   estimation (Cheng & Amin, 1983; Ranneby, 1984), and compute parametric confidence bands from
299
      the nonparametric ones as consonance regions (Easterling, 1976). We compute these via brute-force
      search with a grid of 64 log-spaced values for σ, 128 and 256 linearly spaced values for α and β.
300
301   Testing Additive Normal Errors. To test our assumptions about the errors, we took the ResNet18
302   results and picked the hyperparameters at the 12.5th, 25th, up to 100th percentile of performance.
303   We retrained each configuration 128 times, letting the initialization, data order, and so on vary. In
304   this way, we obtained large samples characterizing the score distributions for fixed hyperparameters.

369   4.2   T ESTING A DDITIVE N ORMAL E RRORS
370
371   So, our theory describes random search, but does it really reflect what is actually happening? For
372   example, if you fix the hyperparameters, are the scores normally distributed with constant variance?
373   To test this assumption we do just that. Our theory claims the scores become normal with constant
374   variance as hyperparameters approach the asymptotic regime. The asymptotic regime is defined by
375   a threshold on the score. Thus, we retrain hyperparameters with increasing scores. Namely, we take
376   the random search results from ResNet18, pick the configurations at the 12.5th, 25th, 37.5th, up to
377   the 100th accuracy percentiles, then retrain each 128 times, letting things like the initialization and
      data order vary. Thereby, we obtain large samples characterizing these distributions over the scores.

      we show histograms and kernel density estimates (KDEs) to offer a more intuitive visualization.
419
      Figure 4 displays the results. On the top, the histograms and KDEs reveal that, except for the worst
420   hyperparameters, the distributions exhibit that familiar bell curve. The normal probability plots are
421   even more informative. On the bottom, the sample quantiles almost all fall on that y = x line,
422   equating them with the quantiles of the normal distribution and establishing their fit. Thus, each
423   distribution from the 25th percentile and up achieves a high degree of normality.
424
      Testing Homoskedasticity. We test homoskedasticity by plotting simultaneous confidence intervals
425
      for the standard deviations at the different accuracy percentiles. Since we have confirmed normality,
426   we use the classic confidence interval for the standard deviation of a normal distribution; since
427   the intervals are independent, we make them hold simultaneously using a Šidák correction. These
428   simultaneous intervals then bound how different the standard deviations can be. Figure 5 shows the
429   result. The standard deviation drops to a constant around the 37.5th percentile. From then on, all
430   95% confidence intervals contain a common value for it (e.g., 1.2e-3, the average of the last four).
431   Moreover, as the intervals are so tight, it is unlikely any large differences exist. Thus, we see that the
      standard deviation starts off inflated, but converges to a constant long before the asymptotic regime.

      5       R ELATED W ORK
487
488   Hyperparameters have always been an essential part of deep learning. Today, researchers seek new
489   ways to set them—both theoretical frameworks like µTransfer (Yang et al., 2021) and empirical ones
490   such as scaling laws (Hestness et al., 2017; Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan
491   et al., 2020; Hernandez et al., 2021; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Much of the motivation comes from
492   new challenges posed by foundation models; however, long before the current era, hyperparameters
493   were still the subject of much practical advice (Orr & Müller, 1998; Montavon et al., 2012).
494   Often, this advice was highly effective but ad hoc—focusing on specific hyperparameters and ways
495   to determine them (Mishkin & Matas, 2016; Smith, 2018). Such advice is difficult to adapt to new
496   hyperparameters and changing contexts. Thus, researchers have sought a more systematic approach.
497
      The simplest approach remains most popular: cross-validation (Stone, 1974). Automating it leads
498
      to hyperparameter search, with many such algorithms developed (Bergstra et al., 2011; Bischl et al.,
499
      2023). Early on, Bergstra & Bengio (2012) discovered random search is surprisingly effective, far
500   outperforming grid search. Since then, many have explored more advanced approaches (Snoek et al.,
501   2012; Swersky et al., 2014; Wistuba et al., 2015; Pedregosa, 2016; Olson et al., 2016; Falkner et al.,
502   2018; Awad et al., 2021; Wistuba et al., 2022; Kadra et al., 2023); still, random search remains a
503   strong baseline, with variants obtaining high performance (Li et al., 2018; 2020).
504
      Hyperparameter search excels at finding a good model for production, but in research we face a
505
      different problem. Instead of constructing the best model, we want to understand a new idea or get
506   an insight into some phenomenon—we want to answer questions, such as: are the hyperparameters
507   tuned enough? Why is this model difficult to tune? And, what is the best possible performance?
508
509
      We need tools tailored for research. Accepting the task, Dodge et al. (2019) proposed the tuning
      curve,6 deriving the first point estimator for it. Soon after, Tang et al. (2020) identified the need
510
      for effective confidence bands, discovering that the default approach (the bootstrap) fails to achieve
511
      meaningful coverage. More recently, Lourie et al. (2024) developed such confidence bands. Their
512   bands are simultaneous, exact, and distribution-free—making them quite robust; however, since the
513   bands are nonparametric, they only bound the initial segment of the curve. They do not extrapolate.
514   Enabling such extrapolation was the beginning motivation behind our theory of random search.
515
      Our theory derives a limit for the score distribution’s tail. Similar limits are often explored under
516
      extreme value theory (Coles, 2001; de Haan & Ferreira, 2006). For example, the Pickands-Balkema-
517
      De Haan theorem gives conditions under which the tail converges to a generalized Pareto distribution
518   (Pickands, 1975; Balkema & de Haan, 1974). This distribution relates closely to the (noiseless)
519   quadratic, though the noisy quadratic is distinct. In general, while extreme value theory seeks broad
520   theorems with an abstract approach, we seek specific analyses based on beliefs about the underlying
521   mechanism. Our aim is to build an empirical theory to better understand our deep learning models.
522
523
      6       C ONCLUSION
524
525
      We derived an asymptotic theory of random search. The theory emerges from two ideas: the score
526   is a smooth function of the hyperparameters, and its noise is normal with constant variance. Surpris-
527   ingly, these assumptions describe deep learning quite accurately in practice.
528
529
      Using the theory, we derived a parametric form for the score distribution’s tail. As iterations in-
      crease, this tail determines random search’s behavior. Thus, the parametric distribution can offer
530
      better point estimates and extrapolate confidence bands for tuning curves. The limiting distribution
531
      has two forms: the quadratic in the deterministic case, and the noisy quadratic in the stochastic one.
532   The noisy quadratic generalizes the first, and has four interpretable parameters: α, a measure of the
533   probability in the asymptotic regime, β, the average performance of the best possible hyperparam-
534   eters, γ, the effective number of hyperparameters, and σ, the standard deviation of the scores when
535   you retrain with fixed hyperparameters.
536
      Our theoretical framework offers a new set of tools for deep learning research. We hope they help
537
      practitioners build better models and researchers discover novel insights. Thus, we release a library

831   D    G ENERALIZATION ACROSS A RCHITECTURES
832
833   While our theory is general, it is also asymptotic; thus, it is natural to ask: how quickly does the
834   asymptotic approximation apply in practice? For ResNet18, DeBERTa, and DeBERTaV3, we saw
835   the asymptotic regime covered 34%, 54%, and 57% of the score distribution—applying from the
836   first or second iteration of random search. Still, perhaps the asymptotic regime applies only because
837   these architectures are so advanced, or the search spaces match them particularly well.
838   To investigate such questions, we compare ResNet18 with two other architectures: AlexNet
839   (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Krizhevsky, 2014) and ConvNext Tiny (Liu et al., 2022). AlexNet goes
840   from ResNet into the past: many consider it the first major architecture of the current deep learning
841   renaissance and, as such, it is considerably less advanced than ResNet—missing later innovations
842   such as batch normalization or residual connections. On the other hand, ConvNext goes from ResNet
843   into the future: it starts with the ResNet architecture and applies lessons learned from transformer-
844
      based models. We obtain 170, 495, and 162 iterations of random search for AlexNet, ResNet18, and
      ConvNext, using the same search distribution as before except fixing use blurpool to 0 because
845
      blurpool is not compatible with ConvNext. Moreover, we use the same search distribution across all
846
      three models to guarantee it is not unusually well-suited to any specific one.
847
848   Figure 7 presents the results.
849
850   The asymptotic regime is large in practice. Across all architectures, the asymptotic regime is
851   more than large enough to be practically relevant. Of the search distributions, it covers 17% for
852
      AlexNet, 31% for ResNet18, and 43% for ConvNext Tiny. In other words, it characterizes the tun-
      ing curve after 2-4 iterations of random search. Thus, our theory describes random search with a
853
      realistic budget. The search distribution can not be the driving factor behind this result because we
854
      use the same one across all architectures. Moreover, while better architectures display larger asymp-
855   totic regimes (e.g., ResNet18 and ConvNext), our theory even describes an older less advanced
856   architecture like AlexNet after just a handful of search iterations.
857
858   The effective number of hyperparameters is stable across architectures. An interesting thing
859   happens when we use the same search space across the different architectures: the effective number
860   of hyperparameters (γ) remains constant. For AlexNet, ResNet, and ConvNext Tiny, the estimate of
861   γ is 2. DeBERTa and DeBERTaV3 exhibit a similar phenomenon: Lourie et al. (2024) used the same
862   search space for both, and both models show γ = 1. These results suggest an intuitive conclusion:
863   the effective number of hyperparameters seems to be more a property of the search space, i.e. the
      hyperparameters themselves. Thus, it exhibits some stability across models.
