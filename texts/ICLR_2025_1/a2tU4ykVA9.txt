011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             The past decades have witnessed the rapid development of Information Technol-
014             ogy (IT) systems, such as cloud computing, 5G networks, and financial informa-
015             tion systems. Ensuring the stability of these IT systems has become an important
016             issue. Large language models (LLMs) that have exhibited remarkable capabili-
017
                ties in NLP-related tasks are showing great potential in AIOps, such as root cause
                analysis of failures, generation of operations and maintenance scripts, and sum-
018
                marizing of alert information. Unlike knowledge in general corpora, knowledge
019
                of Ops varies with the different IT systems, encompassing various private sub-
020             domain knowledge, sensitive to prompt engineering due to various sub-domains,
021             and containing numerous terminologies. Existing NLP-related benchmarks (e.g.,
022             C-Eval, MMLU) can not guide the selection of suitable LLMs for Ops (OpsLLM),
023             and current metrics (e.g., BLEU, ROUGE) can not adequately reflect the question-
024             answering (QA) effectiveness in the Ops domain. We propose a comprehensive
025             benchmark suite, OpsEval, including an Ops-oriented evaluation dataset, an Ops
026             evaluation benchmark, and a specially designed Ops QA evaluation method. Our
027             dataset contains 7,334 multiple-choice questions and 1,736 QA questions. We
028
                have carefully selected and released 20% of the dataset written by domain experts
                in various sub-domains to assist current researchers in preliminary evaluations
029
                of OpsLLMs. We test over 24 latest LLMs under various settings such as self-
030
                consistency, chain-of-thought, and in-context learning, revealing findings when
031             applying LLMs to Ops. We also propose an evaluation method for QA in Ops,
032             which has a coefficient of 0.9185 with human experts and is improved by 0.4471
033             and 1.366 compared to BLEU and ROUGE, respectively. Over the past one year,
034             our dataset and leaderboard have been continuously updated.

037   1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039   The IT Operations (Ops) plays a crucial role in maintaining the efficient and stable operation of
040   information systems such as cloud computing, 5G networks1 and financial information systems. As
041   the Internet continues to expand rapidly, the scale and complexity of systems are escalating, lead-
042   ing to the emergence of artificial intelligence-assisted operations as a novel trend. Termed “AIOps”
043   by Gartner (Lerner, 2017), this technique utilizes artificial intelligence to address tasks such as
044   anomaly detection, fault analysis, and performance optimization. In recent years, large language
045   models (LLMs) have witnessed significant advancements. The latest models, such as GPT-4o (Ope-
046
      nAI, 2024), GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023b), Meta-Llama-3 (AI@Meta, 2024), and GLM-4 (Zeng et al.,
      2022), have demonstrated exceptional generalization and task-planning capabilities. As a result,
047
      these models have provided numerous opportunities to enhance downstream domain-specific appli-
048
      cations. With its advanced text generation ability, LLM is well suited for Ops on tasks like question
049   answering, information summarizing, and report analysis. Hereinafter, we refer to the LLM used
050   for Ops as OpsLLM, regardless of whether they have been optimized specifically for Ops.
051
052       1
            Strictly speaking, 5G belongs to the field of communications technology (CT), but given its broad asso-
053   ciation with the information technology (IT) sector, for the sake of generality, we refer to it as IT operations,
      abbreviated as Ops, throughout the remainder of this paper.

054
      While there are benchmarks for assessing general-purpose NLP-related capabilities, no benchmark
055   exists to evaluate the effectiveness of LLMs or OpsLLMs in Ops tasks. There is an urgent need for an
056   Ops benchmark that informs us about the performance of current LLMs on Ops tasks. On the other
057   hand, a good benchmark can significantly aid the optimization process of OpsLLMs tailored for the
058   Ops domain. Nevertheless, due to the specialty of the Ops tasks, constructing an Ops benchmark
059   presents the following challenges:
060
      1) Sensitive data. The Ops data is primarily sensitive and proprietary to companies, with very few
061   publicly available data, making it difficult for any company to independently provide sufficient eval-
062   uation data to ensure confidence in the test results. 2) Sub-domains. The Ops field spans many
063   sub-domains, like 5G communications, cloud computing, and bank transactions, each requiring a
064   mix of capabilities, or “tasks,” such as network configuration or terminology explanation. The sheer
065   number of sub-domains and tasks, combined with the absence of a systematic taxonomy, makes
066   classifying questions challenging. 3) Prompt sensitivity. Due to the relatively proprietary nature
067   of the Ops, existing LLMs have not undergone specialized supervised fine-tuning (SFT) for instruct
068   following within the Ops field, the evaluation results are more sensitive to prompt engineering.
069
      Designing appropriate prompts for robust and accurate evaluation is challenging. 4) QA metric.
      Existing metrics like BLEU focus on linguistic similarity between model output and reference an-
070
      swers, which often fails to capture true performance in Ops tasks. In Ops, it’s essential to assess
071
      whether the model’s answers address key points in the reference and are supported by sufficient
072   evidence, reflecting the precise meanings of domain-specific terms.
073
074   To address these issues, we propose OpsEval, a comprehensive benchmark suite for evaluating
075
      LLMs’ capability in the IT operations domain. First, to tackle the challenge of benchmark data
      mostly being private, we initiated a community around AIOps, which has attracted dozens of com-
076
      panies to participate. We have selected 9 representative sub-domains from the community, allowing
077
      continuous data contributions from community members. We then aggregate data under the same
078   sub-domain to ensure robustness in evaluation. Additionally, we generated multi-choice (MC) and
079   question-answering (QA) questions as supplements based on publicly available network manage-
080   ment books. To address the challenge of classifying the numerous sub-domains and tasks in the Ops
081   field, we employ model-based pre-clustering and manual review to annotate eight tasks and three
082   abilities. Considering the prompt sensitivity of benchmark results, we systematically test model
083   performance under self-consistency (SC), chain-of-thought (CoT), and few-shot in-context learn-
084   ing (ICL). Lastly, to address the inaccuracy of existing metrics in Ops QA evaluation, we design
085   FAE-Score, which evaluates model responses based on fluency, accuracy, and evidence, with each
086
      criterion having its own dedicated assessment method.
087   The contributions of our paper are as follows: 1) We introduce OpsEval, the first bilingual multi-task
088   dataset in the Ops domain, covering 8 tasks and 3 abilities with 9,070 questions. To assist researchers
089   in preliminary evaluating their OpsLLMs, we have carefully selected and released 20% of QAs
090   from our benchmark licensed under CC-BY-NC-4.0, with the remaining 80% of undisclosed data
091   preventing unfair evaluations due to data leakage (Wei & et.al., 2023) 2) Based on the dataset, we
092
      introduce the OpsEval evaluation benchmark, conducting independent and robust evaluations with
      various prompting techniques and a specifically designed evaluation metric, FAE-Score. Compared
093
      to the commonly employed BLEU and ROUGE metrics, FAE-Score exhibits a more pronounced
094
      congruence with the evaluations of human experts. Specifically, FAE-Score attains a correlation
095   coefficient 0.9175 with expert assessments, surpassing the coefficients of 0.6705 for BLEU and -
096   0.3957 for ROUGE. 3) Based on the results of OpsEval evaluation, we provide key observations
097   and practical lessons to help domain practitioners make decisions such as whether existing models
098   are sufficiently applicable within a specific sub-domain, the necessity for fine-tuning and whether
099   model quantization compromises the effectiveness.

      3.2      P REPROCESSING
185
186
      We systematically carried out the preprocessing of our original data in the following stages:
187
188   Deduplication: Any repeated or highly similar questions are identified and removed to avoid redun-
189   dancy in the test set. We calculate the cosine similarity of the question stems by bge-large-zh-v1.5
190   (Xiao et al., 2023) to detect duplicate questions and identify pairs of questions with a similarity
191
      above a certain threshold (th=0.7).
192   Dependance Filtering: We have filtered out questions that rely on external images or document
193   content to ensure the completeness of the question content itself. The filtering process was done by
194   two parallel lists of empirical keywords in the question stems and the responses of GPT-3.5-turbo.
195   The keyword list can be found in the Appendix A.2.
196   Question Categorization: We devise a categorization that captures many tasks that professionals
197   confront in practical applications. The categorization process consists of two steps: automated
198   screening and manual review. We first use GPT-4 for topic modeling to gain rough insights about
199   the dataset and determine the relevance of each question to Ops, which resulted in more than 20
200   tasks but had an imbalanced distribution. We then involved dozens of experts during the manual
201   review process to categorize the questions into eight tasks and three abilities. The distribution of the
202   questions across these eight tasks and three ability levels is shown in Table 2b, and the details of
203
      each task and ability can be found in Appendix A.4.
204   Manual Review: In the manual review step, we asked Ops experts from the industry to inspect the
205   results of the previous three automated steps, including confirming duplicate and invalid questions
206   and examining the classification results of GPT-4. In our work, an expert is defined as an individ-
207   ual with ten or more years of professional experience in their field, whether as an employee or a
208   researcher. Experts were also asked to drop the questions unrelated to the Ops field. We split the
209
      dataset by n-folds and ensure each fold has at least two experts to review. As listed in Table 2a, this
      quality enhancement process resulted in a refined test set of approximately 7,000 multi-choice and
210
      2,000 question-answering questions.

213   3.3      E VALUATION S ETTINGS
214
215   Multi-choice questions offer a structured approach with definitive answers. These questions are
      straightforward and provide a clear metric for assessment. We use accuracy as the metric. A


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      choice-extracting function based on regular expressions is used to extract the predicted answer of
217   LLMs. Then, we calculate the accuracy based on the extracted answer and the ground-truth labels.
218
219   Question-answering questions. We evaluate question-answering tasks using a metric designed
220
      specifically for OpsEval, called FAE-Score, which is explained in detail in the subsequent section.
      Additionally, we perform expert evaluations and calculate BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE
221
      (Lin, 2004) and RAGAS (Es et al., 2024) scores for comparison purposes, as reference to validate
222
      the accuracy of FAE-Score.
223
224   We use the same three criteria to evaluate the responses of various models for both FAE-Score and
225   Expert Evaluation:
226
             • Fluency. Assessment of the linguistic fluency in the model’s output and compliance with
227
               the question-answering question’s answering requirements.
228
229          • Accuracy. Evaluation of the precision and correctness of the model’s output, including
230
               whether it adequately covers key points of the ground-truth answer.
231          • Evidence. Examine whether the model’s output contains sufficient argumentation and evi-
232            dential support to ensure the credibility and reliability of the answer.
233
234   In Expert Evaluation, we asked experts to score it between 0 and 3 for each criterion. During the
235
      scoring, the raw question, the detailed answer and its key points, and the output of an anonymous
      model are given at each iteration.
236
237   Prompting Techniques. We use various settings to evaluate LLMs on OpsEval to get a comprehen-
238   sive overview of their performance. We evaluate LLMs in zero and few-shot (3-shot) settings. For
239   each setting, we evaluate LLMs in four sub-settings of prompt engineering, that is, naive answers
240   (Naive), self-consistency (SC) (Wang et al., 2023b), chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2023), self-
241
      consistency with chain-of-thought (CoT+SC). We set the number of queries in SC to 5.
242   Models. We evaluate popular LLMs covering different weights from different organizations. The
243   model selection was guided by specific criteria: We aimed to include the latest and most advanced
244   large language models, with a particular focus on those capable of handling Chinese input. The
245   detailed information of all 24 LLMs can be found in Table 6 in Appendix C.1.

303   3.5   O PEN - SOURCE P OLICY
304
305   We have released 20% of the OpsEval dataset to the public to foster contributions from the Ops
306   community and support research. To ensure balanced distribution, this subset was randomly sam-
307
      pled from each data source and sub-domain in proportion to their respective weights. Additionally,
      for questions involving proprietary company data, we carefully reviewed and modified the content
308
      to remove any sensitive information. This sample dataset provides researchers with insights into the
309
      types and topics of questions expected in the benchmarks, allowing them to better understand the
310   scope of the evaluation. The sampled dataset also enables model developers to conduct local eval-
311   uations of their models, facilitating faster iterations. Moreover, this dataset can serve as a seed for
312   generating QA pairs through automatic QA generation algorithms (Wang et al., 2023c), contribut-
313   ing to the growth of Ops-specific data for future model development. While this subset is available
314   for users’ self-evaluation, the complete dataset remains undisclosed. By ensuring that the test set
315   answers are not leaked, we guarantee the reliability and non-leakage of the OpsEval benchmark.

415
416   Table 3 presents the evaluation results of 200 question-answering English questions across four met-
417
      rics: ROUGE, BLEU, RAGAS, FAE-Score, and Expert-Evaluation. To gain more insight into how
      different metrics perform in QA evaluation, we use Figure 20 (see in Appendix C.9.2) as a case
418
      analysis. While BLEU and ROUGE are efficient in natural language comparison, they lack seman-
419
      tic information to determine which part of the context is more important than others. Knowing that
420   a given benchmark evaluates QA based on BLEU/ROUGE, there is an obvious way to trick the met-
421   ric: repeat patterns occurring in the question, gaining a higher possibility to match some patterns
422   in the reference answer. Due to their lack of semantic information related to Ops and the potential
423   hack, traditional metrics like BLEU are unsuitable for specialized benchmarks. Instead, with spe-
424   cialized prompting and seperately designed methodology for each criterion (Fluency, Accuracy and
425   Evidence), FAE-Score can comprehensively evaluate models’ QA performance, with the Accuracy
426   metric picking up those important keywords and not be influenced by repeated words that contain
427   no useful information, and the Evidence metric checking the recall of relevant supporting contents.
428
      In Section 5, we discuss the alignment between different metrics and expert evaluation, validating
      the effectiveness of FAE-Score in automated QA evaluation within the Ops domain.
429
430   Insight: In specialized domains, Ops specifically, traditional NLP metrics like BLEU and ROUGE
431   cannot comprehend the key components in the reference answer, resulting their evaluation lacking
      practical significance. FAE-Score is suitable for large-scale qualitative evaluations in the Ops field.

      5.1       B ENCHMARK L EAKAGE T EST
455
456
      For the fairness of a benchmark suited for LLM, avoiding potential bias emerging from test set leak-
457
      age is necessary. We adapted the methodology from Wei & et.al. (2023) to perform a leakage test on
458   OpsEval’s dataset. We evaluate the LLM loss on samples from different datasets for several LLMs
459   and calculate the average loss. For each dataset, we compare LLM loss on the test split (Ltest ) and
460   a specially curated reference set (Lref ) generated by GPT-4, designed to mimic the testing dataset.
461   While Wei & et.al. (2023) only asked GPT-4 to generate similar questions to the GSM8K (Cobbe
462   et al., 2021) dataset, we require GPT-4 to rewrite the question while preserving its original meaning
463   and accuracy.2 We define a key metric: ∆L = Ltest − Lref , with a threshold of ∆L < 0 indi-
464   cating potential test data leakage. A negative ∆L suggests that the LLM’s lower Ltest comes from
465   overfitting the test set rather than understanding the questions, indicating potential leakage. Table 4a
466
      shows the results of leakage measurement. In addition to the two standard evaluation benchmarks
      (CEval (Huang et al., 2023) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021)), we conducted the same exper-
467
      iments on the alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023) and the Alpaca-GPT4 dataset (Peng et al., 2023),
468
      which is likely used in the pre-training of large models, using its ∆L as reference. This demonstrates
469   the unbiased nature and non-leakage of the OpsEval test set. The models used in the leakage test are
470   listed in Appendix C.1.
471
472
473   5.2       E XPERT ALIGNMENT OF FAE-S CORE
474
475   Table 4b shows the correlation coefficients between various automated scoring metrics (ROUGE,
476   BLEU, RAGAS, and FAE-Score) and Expert-Evaluation criteria. The results indicate that ROUGE
477   and BLEU scores often misalign with Expert-Evaluation. This misalignment occurs because LLMs
478
      with poor performance may generate keywords that boost ROUGE and BLEU scores, while stronger
      LLMs might receive lower scores due to different wording from standard answers. While RAGAS
479
      (Es et al., 2024) aligns better with experts than ROUGE and BLEU, there is still a gap between
480
      its scoring rankings for different models and expert judgement standards. In contrast, FAE-Score
481   rankings closely match Expert-Evaluation, particularly with the Accuracy metric. This suggests that
482   FAE-Score is more reliable in assessing the factual accuracy of LLMs’ outputs. Notably, GPT-4’s
483   performance in factual accuracy is reflected in its strong alignment with the Accuracy metric.

      6     D ISCUSSION
487
488   6.1   AUTOMATED QA GENERATION
489
490   During the data collection process, we explored automating question-answer generation. Initially,
491   we sampled QA pairs and manually evaluated their accuracy and domain relevance. Later, we
492   utilized representative examples for few-shot learning, enabling GPT to generate and evaluate QA
493   pairs automatically based on predefined criteria.
494   Recognizing that most existing benchmarks focus primarily on simple knowledge-based questions,
495   we designed various task-specific templates to address this limitation. These templates require the
496   model to complete specific fields within the template using the provided knowledge content, rather
497   than generating entire questions and answers. This prompt engineering approach allows us to gen-
498   erate detailed and context-specific Ops tasks based on extensive operational knowledge while im-
499   proving the model’s instruction-following ability. By focusing on field-level completion, the overall
500   structure of the QA remains consistent and accurate. In the appendix, we provide the prompt tem-
501
      plate used for automatic QA generation (Figure 11), along with some task cases illustrating their
      application (Figure 12). This approach ensures a more diverse and comprehensive evaluation of
502
      model capabilities while maintaining the relevance and quality of generated tasks.
503

      6.2   F UTURE W ORK
505
506   Comprehensive Error Analysis. To better understand the limitations of large models in Ops ques-
507   tion answering, we will further look into the failure cases and identify common error modes, includ-
508   ing lack of domain knowledge, hallucinations, inaccurate reasoning, and overconfidence in incorrect
509   answers. We believe this detailed error analysis provides a clearer picture of the challenges faced
510   by models and informs future research directions to address these issues. Dataset Scale and Real-
511   World Data. While privacy constraints limit real-world company data, our ongoing collaborations
512   aim to expand the dataset with practical scenarios. Expanding the dataset with real-world scenarios
513
      remains a key focus, while the benchmark prioritizes robust evaluation over dataset scale. Agent
      and RAG Introduction: The inclusion of agents and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) tech-
514
      niques is constrained by the current large models’ lack of foundational knowledge in operations. Our
515
      leaderboard will incorporate more complex tasks once open-source models possess sufficient oper-
516   ational capabilities.

518   7     C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we introduced OpsEval, the first comprehensive Ops benchmark suite designed for
521   evaluating the performance of large language models (LLMs) in IT operations. We established a ro-
522
      bust evaluation framework encompassing a wide range of sub-domains and tasks within Ops through
      rigorous data collection from multiple sources and meticulous preprocessing steps. Our benchmark
523
      includes a carefully selected set of 9,070 questions, which we have partially released to aid initial
524
      evaluations while protecting the integrity of the remaining dataset. It has undergone experiments
525   in data leakage detection, ensuring its reliability. Our observations, supported by quantitative and
526   qualitative results, highlight the need for a balanced approach to selecting fundamental models, con-
527   sidering both performance and robustness. During the QA evaluation, the FAE-Score emerges as a
528   more reliable metric than traditional metrics, suggesting its potential as a replacement for manual
529   labeling in large-scale quantitative evaluations. Our failure rate analysis across 8 tasks and 3 abili-
530   ties provides researchers with crucial insights and prospects for future breakthroughs. The identified
531   flexibility within the OpsEval framework presents opportunities for future exploration. This bench-
532   mark’s adaptability facilitates the seamless integration of additional fine-grained tasks, providing a
533
      foundation for continued research and optimization of LLMs tailored for Ops.

731
      into 3 categories. The details of each ability are as follows:

733       and recall core concepts and foundational knowledge. Such questions are akin to situations
734       where a professional might need to identify a standard procedure or recognize a well-known

738

739
          understanding.

741       knowledge or analytical conclusions to provide actionable recommendations for specific sce-

743       solutions based on in-depth analysis and expertise.
