                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Mitigating the detrimental effects of noisy labels on the training process has be-
               come increasingly critical, as obtaining entirely clean or human-annotated sam-
011
               ples for large-scale pre-training tasks is often impractical. Nonetheless, exist-
012
               ing noise mitigation methods often encounter limitations in practical applica-
013            tions due to their task-specific design, model dependency, and significant com-
014            putational overhead. In this work, we exploit the properties of high-dimensional
015            orthogonality to identify a robust and effective boundary in cone space for sep-
016            arating clean and noisy samples. Building on this, we propose One-step Anti-
017            Noise (OSA), a model-agnostic noisy label mitigation paradigm that employs
018            an estimator model and a scoring function to assess the noise level of input
019            pairs through just one-step inference, a cost-efficient process. We empirically
020            demonstrate the superiority of OSA, highlighting its enhanced training robust-
021
               ness, improved task transferability, ease of deployment, and reduced computa-
               tional costs across various benchmarks, models, and tasks. Our code is released at
022
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CLIP_OSN-E86C.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Noise mitigation aims to handle the detriment of noisy labels encountered during the training pro-
028
      cess. The advancement of large-scale pre-training has significantly increased data scale to the tril-
029
      lion level. Much of this data is sourced from the internet, inevitably introducing considerable noise,
030   which severely impedes the training process. This poses a substantial challenge for robust model
031   training in various tasks, such as cross-modal matching (Huang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024),
032   image-classification (Sun et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2019), and image-retrieval (Liu et al., 2021).
033
      Traditional noise mitigation approaches encounter several limitations that constrain their practical
034
      applicability: 1) Task specificity: Existing methods (Huang et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021; Ibrahimi
035
      et al., 2022a) are tailored to specific tasks, limiting their applicability across different tasks. 2)
036   Model dependency: Most noise mitigation techniques (Liu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023a) are
037   tightly coupled with specific models, requiring extensive modifications for adaptation to different
038   models. 3) Computational cost: Numerous existing methods necessitate dual-model collabora-
039   tions (Huang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2019) or multiple training passes (Huang et al., 2021), i.e.,
040   they require at least two backward passes per training step, effectively doubling the computational
041   expense and substantially increasing the training burden (see Figure. 1a).
042   To tackle these challenges, we use an external estimator to assess the noise level of each sample,
043   ensuring a model-agnostic approach. This estimator adjusts the training loss by reducing the in-
044   fluence of noisy samples, driving their weights toward zero. Furthermore, multimodal pre-trained
045   models have demonstrated remarkable task transferability due to their strong semantic capabilities.
046   For instance, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) unifies the paradigms of image-text retrieval and image
047   classification through a shared embedding space (see Figure. 1b). It converts category labels into
048   sentences, maps them into the shared embedding space, and then calculates the cosine similarity with
049   the image representation to perform image classification. Inspired by this, we leverage multimodal
050
      pre-trained models as estimators and apply the shared embedding space to enable task transfer. In
      this case, only one additional inference process is required for each sample, significantly reducing
051
      the computational overhead compared to performing an extra backward pass.
052
053   Nonetheless, this paradigm introduces a new challenge: how to accurately identify noise based
      solely on cosine similarity scores generated by estimators. An ideal solution is to find a decision

075
      2024) typically attempt to build this boundary within the loss space, an isotropic space with uniform
076

077   handling of overlaps by integrating multi-model predictions often results in an unstable decision
078   boundary. In contrast, the shared embedding space of pre-trained models is a high-dimensional,

      In this work, we delve into the decision boundary of pre-trained models employed as estimators
082
      to accurately differentiate between clean and noisy samples. We first investigate the cosine simi-
083   larity distributions of clean and noisy samples, calculated using the multimodal pre-trained models
084   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), on two datasets with a 50% noise ratio:
085   MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014) and SDM, as shown in Figure. 1c-1f. SDM is a dataset with the im-
086   ages generated by the Stable Diffusion Model (SDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) in some uncommon
087   styles (see illustrations in Figure. 4). It is designed to explore how well-pre-trained models can
088   distinguish unfamiliar domains that they rarely encounter during training. There are two interesting
089   observations in Figure. 1c-1f: (1) The clean and noisy distributions of the same model on different
090   datasets have a similar intersection point, suggesting the existence of a natural and stable boundary
091
      in distinguishing between clean and noisy samples. (2) The overlaps between the clean distribution
      and noisy distribution are minimal even in the unfamiliar domain dataset, indicating this boundary
092
      has strong potential for distinguishing between clean and noisy samples.
093
094   Building upon these two observations, we conduct an in-depth investigation and make the following
095   contributions:
096   1. We figure out the origin of the intersection, attributing it to the shift in the orthogonal bound-
097   ary induced by the cone effect. Furthermore, we provide a theoretical framework that proves and
098   elaborates the stability and precision of this boundary in separating noisy and clean samples.
099
      2. We provide a detailed explanation of the reliability of pre-trained models in general noise recog-
100
      nition, even in unfamiliar domains, grounded in the analysis of the pre-training process.
101
102   3. Build on this, we introduce One-Step Anti-Noise (OSA), a general model-agnostic paradigm for
103   noise recognition that requires only one-step inference. Specifically, we utilize a pre-trained model
104
      as the estimator to maintain a shared embedding space. A scoring function, designed based on the
      properties of high-dimensional orthogonality, is then used to accurately handle overlaps by directly
105
      assigning a learning weight to each sample’s loss according to its cosine similarity.
106
107   4. We conduct comprehensive experiments across a variety of challenging benchmarks, models, and
      tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness, generalization capabilities, and efficiency of our method.

115
116   We first elaborate on the gap extent between the positive and negative sides kept by the orthogonal
117   boundary. Then, we present the reasoning behind the hypothesis that the intersection boundary in
118   Figure. 1 is a shifted orthogonal boundary in the cone space.
119
120   The orthogonal boundary largely separates the positive and negative sides. High-dimensional
121   orthogonality is a general phenomenon caused by dimension disaster, where the angles between
122   randomly selected vectors typically approximate 90 degrees, suggesting the cosine similarity that
123   trends toward zero. For instance, in a 1024-dimensional space, the probability of two random vec-
124   tors having a cosine similarity within [−0.1, 0.1] is approximately 99.86% (details are provided in
125   Appendix. C.1). In this case, a natural boundary of cosine similarity zero forms, capably separating
126
      the positive side and negative side with a huge gap.

188   2.3   Q UALITATIVE ANALYSIS OF ROBUSTNESS AND APPLICABILITY
189
190   Next, we perform a qualitative analysis to explore (i) the robustness and generality of the boundary
191   in distinguishing between clean and noisy samples, and (ii) how the boundary’s properties can be
192   leveraged to achieve more reasonable and precise overlap handling.
193
194   How about the boundary robustness even in unfamiliar domains? Although the boundary’s
195   ability to distinguish clean and noisy samples is proven, its robustness and generality still require
196
      further exploration. For practical pre-training, it must maintain accuracy and robustness even in
      unfamiliar domain datasets. Since the capabilities of the pre-trained model are difficult to quantify,
197
      we conduct a qualitative analysis from the perspective of pre-trained model inference. The models
198
      pre-trained on millions of samples already possess somewhat semantic understanding capabilities.
199   Given a positive pair from an unseen domain, due to the contrastive learning process during pre-
200   training, it still has a strong likelihood of moving toward the positive side of the boundary, while
201   the negative pair tends toward the negative side. Although the cosine similarity difference might
202   be slight, as we have shown in Section. 2.1, the boundary constructs a significant gap from the
203   perspective of high-dimensional orthogonality.
204
205   How to handle the overlaps through imbalanced probability? Due to the properties of orthog-
206   onal boundary, as cosine similarity decreases and approaches zero from the positive side, the proba-
207   bility of positive samples sharply decreases. Therefore, we can design a scoring function to annotate
208   the cleanliness of samples. This function should satisfy two requirements: for samples with cosine
209
      similarity less than or equal to zero, which are almost certainly noise, the function should assign
      them a weight of zero. For samples with cosine similarity greater than zero, the function gradient
210
      should increase rapidly as the cosine similarity moves further from zero.

251   3.2     O NE - STEP A NTI -N OISE
252
253   The workflow of our noise mitigation approach OSA is depicted in Figure. 2. Initially, we utilize
254   an estimator model to encode the input pair to a shared embedding space and continue to compute
255   the cosine similarity between the paired embedding. Afterward, the cosine similarity is converted
256
      to a cleanliness score wi , (0 ≤ wi ≤ 1) through a scoring function designed based on orthogonal
      properties (Section. 2.3). This score quantifies the clean degree of the sample, the smaller wi is, the
257
      noisier the sample.
258
259   During the target model training phase, this cleanliness score is used as a weight, directly multiplied
260   by the loss of the corresponding sample to facilitate selective learning. This noise mitigation pro-
261   cess, being solely dependent on the estimator model, is readily adaptable to the training of various
262
      target models by simply adding an extra coefficient to the loss function, ensuring the model-agnostic
      property. Therefore, the key of our noise mitigation approach revolves around the estimator model
263
      and noise score assessment.

266
267   Estimator model selection. In our approach, the Estimator Model must satisfy two critical re-
268   quirements: 1) effectively mapping input pairs into a unified embedding space and 2) possessing
269   basic semantic understanding capabilities. To meet these requirements, we employ CLIP (Rad-
      ford et al., 2021), a commonly used multimodal pre-trained models, as our estimator model. It is

      equipped with a text encoder ϕt (·) and an image encoder ϕv (·), enabling it to perform basic zero-
271   shot tasks efficiently.
272
273   Domain adaptation (Optional). While we have performed a qualitative analysis of the zero-shot
274   pre-trained model’s robustness on out-of-domain data in Section. 2.3, and shown strong robustness
275   for edge cases in Figure. 1, considering the domain diversity in real-world scenarios, we provide
276   an optional Domain Adaptation (DA) approach to enhance the estimator model’s adaptability when

      for our methods. Through multiple experiments, we found that even without domain adaptation, the
      zero-shot CLIP model performs exceptionally well across various scenarios.

469   Noise Detection Accuracy Analysis. To figure out how accurate OSA is in recognizing noise,
470   we evaluate the accuracy and recall on CLIP without Domain-Adaptation (w/o DA) and CLIP with
471   Domain-Adaptation (w DA) on noisy MSCOCO. We utilize zero as the threshold to roughly divide
472   pairs into noise and clean sets, respectively. Concretely, we classify scores less than or equal to 0 as
473   noise, and scores greater than 0 as clean. The Accuracy means the proportion of the clean pairs cor-
474
      rectly classified into the clean set, while the Recall indicates the noisy pairs correctly classified into
      the noisy set. The results presented in Table. 6 indicates the powerful noise recognizing capability
475

476
      Another notable phenomenon is that all recall scores converge towards 100, indicating that OSA
477   achieves greater accuracy in noise detection. This suggests that OSA can almost entirely eliminate
478   the impact of noise on training.

480   Noise Re-weighting Accuracy Comparison. Some anti-noise methods, like NPC, also employ
481   loss re-weighting for optimization. To assess whether our method assigns relatively smaller weights
482   to noise than these methods, we first analyze the weights generated by NPC and OSA. Due to
483   differences in weight scales across methods, a direct comparison is unfair. Therefore, to unify the
484   scale, we adopt a ranking-based approach, sorting weights in descending order and calculating the
485   Mean Noise Rank. This metric evaluates whether smaller weights are consistently assigned to noisy
      samples relative to clean ones. Our experiments use 2,000 randomly selected samples from the

489   OSA achieves a higher Mean Noise Rank compared to NPC, demonstrating greater accuracy in re-

491   optimal; 50% noise: 1520.7 for OSA versus 1524.0 optimal). This near-perfect alignment indicates

524
525
526
      Broader Impacts. In this work, we investigated the possibility of anti-noise in practical large-scale
      training. We introduced a novel model-agnostic anti-noise paradigm with advantages such as task
527
      transferability, model adaptability, and low computational overhead. By leveraging the properties of
528
      high-dimensional spaces, we found a robust and effective boundary for distinguishing between noisy
529   and clean samples. Through rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimentation, we
530   validated the efficacy and robustness of OSA for general noise mitigation. Although our primary ob-
531   jective is to adapt to practical large-scale training, OSA also achieves SOTA performance in standard
532   anti-noise settings. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explore noise mitigation in
533   practical large-scale training scenarios, as well as the first to propose a general anti-noise approach.
534
535   Limitations and Future Works. Limited by the significant computational cost of pre-training,
536   it is difficult for us to evaluate in a real pre-training process. Instead, we simulate large-scale
537   pre-training processes to the greatest extent possible, such as evaluating on the real-world noisy
538   dataset CC120K, which shares similar domains with mainstream pre-training datasets like CC4M
539   and CC12M. Exploring the broad domain adaptability of OSA in real pre-training scenarios will be
      a valuable direction for future work.

750   B.1   N OISE M ITIGATION IN C ROSS -M ODAL M ATCHING
751
752   The cross-modal matching task (Lee et al., 2018; Song & Soleymani, 2019; Li et al., 2019; 2022;
753   Diao et al., 2021) serves as a fundamental component in multimodal learning. However, the inher-
754   ent difference in information density between these modalities leads to high annotation costs and
755   inconsistent annotation quality, rendering cross-modal tasks particularly vulnerable to label noise.
      Some approaches explicitly identify and correct noisy samples through cross-prediction between


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      concurrently trained dual models (Huang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023a; Liang et al., 2022b), while
757   others (Zhang et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2022) implicitly estimate the probability of sample noise,
758   reducing its training impact by adjusting the loss function. NCR (Huang et al., 2021) employs
759   the memorization capacity of its counterpart model for simple clean samples to rectify the output
760   results. BiCro (Yang et al., 2023a) utilizes the consistency of similarity score distributions from
761   a Siamese model ensemble on noisy data, alongside anchors modeled on the loss distribution via
762   a Beta-Mixture-Model (BMM), to filter out noisy samples. NPC (Zhang et al., 2024), deviating
763   from the dual-model training schemes, introduces a two-stage single-model training approach that
764   reduces training overhead by replacing two backward passes with one forward and one backward
765
      pass. Specifically, the first stage estimates the impact of potentially noisy samples on model per-
      formance by constructing a high-quality clean sample bank; the second stage then utilizes these
766
      estimates to reweight the loss function. However, current methods for distinguishing clean from
767
      noisy samples rely on numerous hyperparameters that are closely linked to dataset size and model
768   capacity. This dependency not only limits their adaptability to various downstream tasks but also
769   makes them challenging to deploy in real-world applications.
770
771
772
773   B.2   N OISE M ITIGATION IN I MAGE C LASSIFICATION
774
775
776   Image classification is vulnerable to training data noise, due to varied noise types and strong model
777
      memorization. Noise in datasets manifests in two primary forms: synthetic alterations and those
      arising from real-world scenarios. The former typically involves shuffling the labels of a subset
778
      of the data or retaining the labels while introducing corresponding category images from external
779
      datasets. The latter entails substituting images for a random selection of data points with those
780   sourced from image search engines. Existing approaches are categorized based on their operational
781   focus: loss correction (Yi & Wu, 2019; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018a; Menon et al., 2015; Natarajan
782   et al., 2013; Patrini et al., 2017; Xia et al., 2019; Ghosh et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019a;b; Xu et al.,
783   2019; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018b) and sample selection (Sun et al., 2022; Albert et al., 2023; Yao
784   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Albert et al., 2022). Loss correction methods typically incorporate a
785   regularization term into the loss function, implicitly reweighting clean and noisy samples within
786   the loss. Sample selection strategies, in contrast, explicitly differentiate between clean and noisy
787   samples, applying distinct processing to each category during loss computation. Representative for
788
      the loss correction category, (Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018a) aims to generalize ordinary Cross-Entropy
      loss and MAE loss by setting the loss threshold to iid and ood noisy samples. DivideMix (Li et al.,
789
      2020) concurrently trains two networks, each utilizing the data partitioning from the other network to
790
      distinguish between clean and noisy samples based on loss values, thereby mitigating the influence
791   of confirmation bias inherent within each network. PNP (Sun et al., 2022) framework employs a
792   unified predictive network to estimate the in-distribution (iid), out-of-distribution (ood), and clean
793   probabilities for a given sample. Co-training trained on a sample that has a lower loss, and with the
794   different predictions by its siamese network.
795

798   B.3   N OISE M ITIGATION IN I MAGE R ETRIEVAL .
799
800
801
      Although image retrieval tasks focus on pairwise relationships, the noise predominantly originates
      from image categorization errors. Analogous to image classification tasks, this can be bifurcated into
802
      in-domain (Wang & Tan, 2018) and open-set noise (Liu et al., 2021). In terms of task configuration,
803
      noise retrieval typically operates at the category level, treating images within the same category
804   as positive instances. PRISM (Liu et al., 2021) tries to find noisy image samples by finding the
805   outliers score in the whole similarity matrix from the same category. The generalization ability of
806   the image feature is ensured by a broader query bank restored multi-view of it. TITAN (Yang et al.,
807   2023b) utilizes prototypes to be representative of the anchor of the clean and noisy samples and
808   then generates synthetic samples by a combination of prototypes for substitution of noisy samples.
809   T-SINT (Ibrahimi et al., 2022b) utilizes more negative samples by the interaction between noisy
      samples and negative samples that belong to another category.
