008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            Resampling from a target measure whose density is unknown is a fundamental
011            problem in mathematical statistics and machine learning. A setting that dominates
012            the machine learning literature consists of learning a map from an easy-to-sample
013            prior, such as the Gaussian distribution, to a target measure. Under this model,
014
               samples from the prior are pushed forward to generate a new sample on the target
               measure, which is often difficult to sample from directly. In this paper, we pro-
015
               pose a new model for conditional resampling called mirror Schrödinger bridges.
016
               Our key observation is that solving the Schrödinger bridge problem between a
017            distribution and itself provides a natural way to produce new samples from con-
018            ditional distributions, giving in-distribution variations of an input data point. We
019            show how to efficiently solve this largely overlooked version of the Schrödinger
020            bridge problem. We prove that our proposed method leads to significant algorith-
021            mic simplifications over existing alternatives, in addition to providing control over
022            in-distribution variation. Empirically, we demonstrate how these benefits can be
023            leveraged to produce proximal samples in a number of application domains.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Mapping one probability distribution to another is a central technique in mathematical statistics and
029
      machine learning. Myriad computational tools have been proposed for this critical yet often chal-
030   lenging task. Models and techniques for optimal transport provide one class of examples, where
031   methods like the Hungarian algorithm (Kuhn, 1955) map one distribution to another with optimal
032   cost. Adding entropic regularization to the static optimal transport problem yields efficient algo-
033   rithms like Sinkhorn’s method (Deming & Stephan, 1940; Sinkhorn, 1964), which have been widely
034   adopted in machine learning since their introduction by Cuturi (2013). Static entropy-regularized
035   optimal transportation is equivalent to a dynamical formulation known as the Schrödinger bridge
036   problem (Schrödinger, 1932; Léonard, 2014), which has proven useful to efficiently compute an
037   approximation of the optimal map paired with an interpolant between the input measures.
038   Inspired by these mathematical constructions and efficient optimization algorithms, several meth-
039   ods in machine learning rely on learning a map from one distribution to another. Beyond optimal
040   transport, diffusion models, for instance, learn to reverse a diffusion process that maps data to a
041   noisy prior. Special attention has been given to learning methods that accomplish this in a stochastic
042   manner, i.e., modeling the forward noising process using a stochastic differential equation (SDE).
043   The most common learning applications of distribution mapping attempt to find a map from a simple
044   prior distribution and a complex data distribution, either using a score-matching strategy (Song &
045   Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) or leveraging a formulation of the Schrödinger
046   bridge problem (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022; 2023; Zhou et al., 2024); other learning
047   applications map one complex data distribution to another (Cuturi, 2013; Courty et al., 2017).
048   In this paper, we focus instead on the understudied problem of mapping a probability distribution
049   to itself, that is, finding a joint distribution whose marginals are both the same data distribution
050   π. This task might seem inane at first glance, since two simple couplings satisfy our constraints:
051   one is the independent coupling p(x, y) = π(x)π(y), and the other is the “diagonal” map given by
052   p(x, y) = π(x)δy . The space of couplings between a measure and itself, however, is far richer than
053   these two extremes and includes models whose conditional distributions are neither identical nor
      Dirac measures.

      We focus on the class of self-maps obtained by entropy-regularized transport from a measure to it-
055   self. Formally, we define a mirror Schrödinger bridge to be the minimizer of the KL divergence
056   DKL (P ∥ P0 ) over path measures P with both initial and final marginal distributions equal to
057   π, where P0 is an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process with       noise σ. Mirror Schrödinger bridges are the
058   stochastic counterpart to minimizing DKL p ∥ p0 , where p0 is the probability density of the joint
059   distribution associatedR with the path measure PR0 , over the joint distributions p on Rn ×Rn satisfying
060   the linear constraints p(x, y)dy = π(x) and p(x, y)dx = π(y). While the former minimizes the
061   Kullback-Leibler divergence on path space, the latter is a minimization over density couplings.
062
      Despite its simplicity, this setting of the Schrödinger bridge problem suggests a rich application
063   space. Couplings with the same marginal constraints have already proven useful to enhance model
064   accuracy in vision and natural language processing by reinterpreting attention matrices as transport
065   plans (Sander et al., 2022). Few works, however, consider this task from the perspective of optimiz-
066   ing over path measures or provide control over the entropy of the matching at test time. Albergo
067   et al. (2023) propose a stochastic interpolant between a distribution and itself, but their interpolants
068   are not minimal in the relative entropy sense and do not solve the Schrödinger bridge problem, even
069   with optimization. Minimal interpolants in the relative entropy sense are those with minimal kinec-
070   tic energy, and in applications, minimizing the kinectic energy of a path has been correlated to faster
071
      sampling (Shaul et al., 2023).
072   Contributions. We investigate the mirror Schrödinger bridge problem and demonstrate how it can
073   be leveraged to obtain in-distribution variants of a given input sample. In particular, given a sample
074   x0 ∼ pdata , we build a stochastic process {Xt }t∈[0,1] with minimal relative entropy under which the
075   sample x0 arrives at some x1 ∼ pdata with x1 proximal but not identical to x0 .
076   Our contributions in this direction are twofold: first, on the theoretical side, we use the time symme-
077   try of the mirror Schrödinger bridge to prove that it can be obtained as the limit of iterates produced
078   via an alternating minimization procedure; and second, in applications, the implementation of our
079   method allows for sampling from the conditional distribution X1 | X0 = x0 in such a way that we
080   can control how proximal a generated sample x1 is relative to the input sample x0 .

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
084
085   Entropy regularized optimal transport. A few recent works employ the idea of a coupling with the
086   same marginal constraints. Feydy et al. (2019); Mensch et al. (2019) use static entropy-regularized
087
      optimal transportation from a distribution to itself to build a cost function correlated to uncertainty.
      Sander et al. (2022) reinterpret attention matrices in transformers as transport plans from a distri-
088
      bution to itself, while Agarwal et al. (2024) analyze this reinterpretation in the context of gradient
089
      flows. Also relevant is the work of Kurras (2015), who shows that, over discrete state spaces,
090   Sinkhorn’s algorithm can be simplified in the case of identical marginal constraints. These works
091   do not consider the coupling with the same marginal constraints from the perspective of path mea-
092   sures on continuous-state spaces. In our paper, we focus on the path measure formulation instead of
093   viewing it as a self-transport map and present a practical algorithm to solve it.
094
      Expectation maximization. Our methodology can be broadly categorized under the umbrella of
095
      expectation maximization algorithms, drawing from the theory of information geometry. A number
096   of recent papers introduce related formulations to machine learning; most relevant to us are the works
097   of Brekelmans & Neklyudov (2023); Vargas & Nüsken (2023). These works, however, focus on
098   the case of finding a path measure with two distinct marginal constraints, overlooking the potential
099   application to resampling and algorithmic simplifications obtained for the case in which the marginal
100   constraints are the same. In our work, we derive an algorithm that is distinct, yet similar in flavor, to
101   address this overlooked version of the problem, i.e. the mirror Schrödinger bridge.
102   Schrödinger bridges and stochastic interpolants. Schrödinger bridges have been used to obtain
103   generative models by flowing samples from a prior distribution to an empirical data distribution
104   from which new data is to be sampled. Several methods have been proposed to this end: De Bor-
105   toli et al. (2021); Vargas et al. (2021) iteratively estimate the drift of the SDE associated with the
106   diffusion processes of half-bridge formulations. While the first uses neural networks and score
107   matching, the latter employs Gaussian processes. From these, a number of extensions or alternative
      methods have been presented; most relevant are (Shi et al., 2023; Peluchetti, 2023), which extend


                                                         2

108
      (De Bortoli et al., 2021) but differ with respect to the projection sets used to define their half-bridge
109   formulations. Schrödinger bridge based methods alleviate the computational expense incurred by
110   score-based generative models (SGM) (De Bortoli et al., 2021). The latter requires the forward
111   diffusion process to run for longer times with smaller step sizes. Unlike SGM, our method pro-
112   vides a tool to flow an existing sample in the same data distribution with control over the spread of
113   the newly obtained sample.
                                                                                                                  NEW
114
      To the best of our knowledge, the work of Albergo et al. (2023) is the only one in the literature on
115   generative modeling that maps from a distribution to itself. In their paper, flow matching learns a
116   drift function associated with a stochastic path from the data distribution to itself. Their stochastic
117   interpolants, however, are not optimal with respect to any functional. In particular, they lack opti-
118   mality in the relative entropy sense, a property correlated to sampling effectiveness and generation
119   quality (Shaul et al., 2023) and hence of practical importance. By contrast, our method discovers the
120   coupling with minimal relative entropy, akin to methods such as (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Shi et al.,
121   2023); our method, however, presents certain algorithmic advantages over these, which can only be
122   derived for the mirror case.

126   Definition. Let n > 0 be an integer, and let P0 ∈ P(C ([0, 1], Rn )) be a reference measure in
127   the space of path measures. Following (Jamison, 1975; Léonard, 2014), we define the Schrödinger
128   bridge problem to be the problem of finding a path measure PSB interpolating between prescribed
129   initial and final marginals π0 and π1 that is the closest to the reference measure P0 with respect to the
130   Kullback-Leibler divergence DKL . To be precise, we define PSB to be the solution of the following
131   optimization problem:
                                        PSB := arg min DKL P ∥ P0 ,

132                                                                                                         (1)
                                                 P∈D(π0 ,π1 )
133
134   where D(π0 , π1 ) denotes the set of path measures with marginals π0 and π1 . In other words, we say
135
      that PSB is the direct DKL projection of P0 onto the space D(π0 , π1 ).
136   The reference path measure P0 is typically chosen to be associated with a diffusion process, which
137   is defined to be any stochastic process Xt governed by a forward SDE of the form
138
                                           dXt = ft (Xt )dt + σdWt ,
139
140   where ft denotes the forward drift function, σ > 0 is the noise coefficient, and Wt denotes the
141   Wiener process. Such a process Xt corresponds to a unique path measure once an initial or final
142   condition is specified. An important aspect of diffusion processes is that their time-reversals are
143   diffusion processes of the same noise coefficient σ. Specifically, if Xt is a diffusion process with
144   time-reversal denoted by Yt , then Yt is governed by a backward SDE of the form
145                                        dYt = bt (Yt )dt + σdWt ,
146
147   where bt denotes the backward drift function (see (Winkler et al., 2023, section 2.3)).
148   In the case where P0 arises from a diffusion process, any path measure with finite KL divergence
149   with respect to P0 , including the Schrödinger bridge PSB , necessarily also arises from a diffusion
150   process with noise σ (Vargas et al., 2021). Consequently, by adjusting the initial condition of the
151   reference SDE, we can assume that the reference process P0 has a prescribed initial marginal π0 ,
152   without changing the solution to (1).

156   solving the following pair of half-bridge problems:

      Applications. Suppose π0 is given by a data distribution pdata and take π1 to be an easy-to-sample
      distribution pprior , e.g., pprior = N (0, I). The backward diffusion process associated with PSB
167
      gives a model for sampling from pdata . In practice, the IPFP iterates in (2) can be solved using an
168
      algorithm known as the diffusion Schrödinger bridge (DSB), developed by De Bortoli et al. (2021).
169   DSB relies on the following observation, which is a consequence of Girsanov’s theorem: P2k+1 is
170   the path measure whose backward drift is equal to the time-reversal of the forward drift of P2k , and
171   P2k+2 is the path measure whose forward drift is equal to the time-reversal of the backward drift of
172   P2k+1 . Leveraging this fact, DSB solves for PSB by training neural networks to learn the forward
173   and backward drift functions associated with the IPFP iterates.

      Given a reference path measure P0 and a prescribed marginal distribution π, we consider the
178
      Schrödinger bridge problem between π and itself with respect to P0 . In the case where P0 is time-
179   symmetric, the Schrödinger bridge will inherit the time-symmetry, in which case we call it the mirror
180   Schrödinger bridge from π to itself with respect to P0 . Mathematically, we write
181
                                       PMSB := arg min DKL P ∥ P0 ,

182                                                                                                      (3)
                                                  P∈D(π,π)
183
184   so that PMSB ∈ D(π, π) is the path measure with identical prescribed marginals equal to π that is
185   closest to the reference measure P0 with respect to the KL divergence DKL .
186   A naı̈ve approach to solving the mirror Schrödinger bridge problem (3) is to apply IPFP with both
187   marginals π0 = π1 set equal to π. In practice, this requires iterative training of two neural networks
188   ftθ and bϕt , the first modeling the drift of the forward diffusion process associated to PMSB and the
189   latter modeling the drift of the corresponding backward process. But this straightforward application
190   of IPFP leads to unnecessary computational expense, as it fails to use the time-symmetry of the
191   problem (3). In particular, at optimality the forward and backward drifts of PMSB must be equal,
192   because the mirror Schrödinger bridge PMSB is time-symmetric. Related works in entropic optimal
193   transportation suggest that the use of one optimization variable for the static transport formulation
194   in the symmetric case (see (Kurras, 2015, Section 3) and (Feydy et al., 2019, Equations (24)-(25))),
195   but to our knowledge no approach has been developed to leverage symmetry for the dynamical
196
      formulation in the language of path measures.
                                                                                                                  NEW
197   In section 4.1, we develop a method for solving (3) by leveraging time-symmetry in conjunction with
198   a general technique from information geometry known as the Alternating Minimization Procedure
199   (AMP), which was first formalized by Csiszár & Tusnády (1984). Then, in section 4.3, we derive an
200   efficient algorithm that involves training a single neural network modeling the drift of the diffusion
201   process associated to PMSB and requires half of the computational expense in terms of training
202
      iterations for the mirror problem, when compared to other IPFP-based algorithms.

231   4.2   C ONVERGENCE
232
233   For the scheme in steps (4)-(5) to be practical, we must prove that the iterates Pk converge to the
234   mirror Schrödinger bridge PMSB . The pointwise convergence of schemes like steps (4)-(5) was
235   established by Csiszár & Tusnády (1984) in the special case where the state space is finite. In our
236   setting, however, we work with infinite state spaces of the form Rn for some dimension n > 0. In
237   the following theorem, we prove that the sequence obtained in the AMP scheme converges in total
238
      variation to the mirror Schrödinger bridge, without relying on the finiteness assumption for the state
      space. To our knowledge, this result has not been established previously in the literature.
239
240   Theorem 1. Let Pk be the sequence of path measures obtained via the alternating minimization
241   procedure defined in steps (4)-(5). Then Pk converges to PMSB in total variation as k → ∞.
242   Moreover, the total variation between Pk and Pk+1 decays as o(1/k).
                                                                                                                NEW
243

      4.4     S AMPLING WITH I N - DISTRIBUTION VARIATION
398
399   In this section, we provide a short intuitive explanation of how our method allows for resampling
400   with prescribed proximity to an input sample. Given such a sample x0 ∼ π, we solve the SDE
401   corresponding to the Schrödinger bridge to push x0 forward in time, arriving at a final sample
402   x1 ∈ π. We want x1 to be a variation of x0 , where the proximity of x1 to x0 correlates with the size
403   of the noise coefficient σ. Justifying this mathematically requires understanding how the conditional
404   distribution X1 | X0 = x0 , specifically its mean and variance, depend on σ. While these quantities
405   do not in general have closed form expressions, it is possible to compute them exactly in the case
406
      where π = N (0, I) is a 1-dimensional Gaussian.
407   In this case, let Xt denote the diffusion process associated to the Schrödinger bridge, where the ref-
408   erence path measure corresponds to an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck reference process with drift coefficient
409   −α. In Proposition 4 (see Appendix B for the statement and proof) we determine the joint distribu-
410   tion of X0 and X1 in terms of a quantity β, which is a function of α and σ that grows approximately
411   as 1 + c(α) × σ 2 for some function c. Let p(x, y) denote the probability density function of the joint
412
      distribution of X0 and X1 , and recall that p(x, y) is the product of the conditional PDF of X1 | X0
      with the PDF of X0 . Using this fact in conjunction with Proposition 4, the PDF of X1 | X0 = x0 is
413
414                                                                                   1        2          2    0     x2
                                                                                 − 2(1−β 2 ) (x0 −2βx0 y+y )+ 2
415                         pX1 |X0 =x0 (y) = p(x0 ,y)/pX0 (x0 ) = e                                                      .
416
417   From the right-hand side, we see that X1 | X0 = x0 is Gaussian with mean and variance given by
418
          E[X1 | X0 = x0 ] = x0 (β/1−β 2 ) , E (X1 − E[X1 | X0 = x0 ])2 | X0 = x0 = 1 − β 2 .

419
420   Thus, changing the noise value σ alters both the mean and variance of samples pushed forward
421   via the Schrödinger bridge. Indeed, in the case of the mean, it grows inversely proportional to σ 2 .
422   Consequently, if σ < 1, then we should expect the Schrödinger bridge to push samples away from
423   the distribution mean, whereas if σ > 1, then the opposite occurs, and samples experience mean
424
      reversion. As for the variance, note that 1 − β 2 grows at least as fast as σ 2 , so we should expect
      the Schrödinger bridge to produce samples with spread that increases as σ increases. We expect
425
      that similar effects occur even when the marginal distribution π is not Gaussian: i.e., the value of σ
426
      should be directly related to the proximity of generated samples in an analogous way.
