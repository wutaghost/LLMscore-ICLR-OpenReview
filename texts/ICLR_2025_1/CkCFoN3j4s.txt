                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable advances in supporting
012
               long-context comprehension and processing tasks. However, scaling the genera-
013
               tion inference of LLMs to such long contexts incurs significant additional com-
014            putation load, and demands a substantial GPU memory footprint to maintain the
015            key-value (KV) cache of transformer-based LLMs. Existing KV cache compres-
016            sion methods, such as quantization, face memory bottlenecks as context length
017            increases, while static-sized caches, such as selective eviction, suffer from in-
018            efficient policies. These limitations restrict deployment on consumer-grade de-
019            vices like a single Nvidia 4090 GPU. To overcome this, we propose L OCRET,
020            an efficient framework for long-context LLM inference that introduces retaining
021            heads to evaluate the causal importance of KV cache units, allowing for more
022
               accurate eviction within a fixed cache size. L OCRET is fine-tuned on top of
               the frozen backbone LLM using a minimal amount of data from standard long-
023
               context SFT datasets. During inference, we evict low-importance cache units
024
               along with a chunked prefill pattern, significantly reducing peak GPU memory
025            usage. We conduct an extensive empirical study to evaluate L OCRET, where the
026            experimental results show that L OCRET outperforms the recent popular and com-
027            petitive approaches, including I NF LLM, Quantization, S IR LLM, and MI NFER -
028            ENCE , in terms of memory efficiency and the quality of generated contents —
029            L OCRET achieves over a 20× and 8× KV cache compression ratio compared to
030            the full KV cache for Phi-3-mini-128K and Llama-3.1-8B-instruct.
031            Additionally, L OCRET can be combined with other efficient inference methods,
032            such as quantization and token merging. To the best of our knowledge, L OCRET is
033
               the first framework capable of deploying Llama-3.1-8B or similar models on
               a single Nvidia 4090 GPU, enabling 128K long-context inference without com-
034
               promising generation quality, and requiring little additional system optimizations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized AI development and deployment (Zhao et al.,
040
      2023; Minaee et al., 2024). Recent advancements in LLMs’ ability to handle long-context tasks have
041
      further unlocked the potential of generative AI. State-of-the-art LLMs now support significantly ex-
042   tended context lengths, with GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and Llama-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) handling
043   128K tokens, Yi (Young et al., 2024) and Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2024) supporting 200K tokens, and
044   Gemini-1.5 (Reid et al., 2024) reaching 10 million tokens. These advances enable LLMs to tackle
045   complex tasks like multi-hop reasoning (Li et al., 2024a; Schnitzler et al., 2024), solving needle-
046   in-a-haystack problems (Guerreiro et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a), and powering advanced LLM
047   agents (Qin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b) and AI-driven operating systems (Mei et al., 2024).
048   However, deploying generative inference under long-context settings on consumer-grade GPUs re-
049   quires innovative algorithmic and system optimizations to handle this new paradigm efficiently.
050   Compared to traditional short-context LLM inference, long-context LLM inference shifts the com-
051   puting paradigm in two key ways: i) increased computational overhead for attention mechanisms:
052   as context length grows, the computation required for obtaining attention scores increases quadrati-
053   cally, which results in a higher ratio of the computational budget in a transformer block; ii) higher
      memory footprint for key-value (KV) caching: longer contexts require larger KV caches, which dra-

057   Although various efforts have been made to overcome the bottleneck in LLM inference, these ap-

      proaches fail to enable long-context inference on consumer-grade GPUs. Models with compact
      architectures, e.g. MiniCPM-128K (Hu et al., 2024b) and Phi-3-mini-128K (Abdin et al.,

      2024), reduce the computational load and memory usage, but cannot alleviate the KV cache burden

062   2023; Zhang et al., 2024c), or sparsification (Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) also fall short in
063   effectively reducing the memory usage to a level supported by consumer-grade GPUs.

      Recently, some specific optimizations have been proposed for long-context LLM inference. For
065
      example, sparse attention mechanisms (Jiang et al., 2024a; Ge et al., 2024; Lou et al., 2024) at-

069   issue is that the KV cache grows linearly with sequence length, and the above methods do not ade-
070   quately address this problem. On the contrary, combining chunked prefill with token-dropping tech-
071   niques (Xiao et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024) could offer a more effective solution, as it maintains
072   a static-sized cache where the memory usage can be bounded. However, current token-dropping

      Efforts in long-context LLM inference can be categorized by algorithm and system optimizations:
112   Algorithm optimizations. Optimizations aimed at reducing the size of the KV cache can gen-
113   erally be classified into three categories: quantization-based methods, token dropping methods,
114   and sparsity-based methods. Quantization-based methods (Liu et al., 2024b; Hooper et al., 2024;
115   Zandieh et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a), which store the KV cache in low-bit representations,
116   require hardware support for these formats and may slow down inference due to the overhead of
117
      dequantization. Token dropping methods typically follow two main strategies: eviction or the use of
      an attention pool. Eviction-based approaches, such as H2 O (Zhang et al., 2024e), ScissorHands (Liu
118
      et al., 2024a), and S IR LLM (Yao et al., 2024), rank tokens by certain statistical metrics to identify
119
      the most influential ones, discarding others to reduce memory usage. Attention pool-based meth-
120   ods (Nawrot et al., 2024; Rajput et al., 2024), such as StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024b) and
121   L O C O C O (Cai et al., 2024a), compress multiple adjacent KV cache units into a single unit using
122   a specially designed transformation. Sparsity-based methods (Ge et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024a;
123   Yang et al., 2024; Lou et al., 2024; Lv et al., 2024) focus on leveraging the sparsity patterns of
124   attention heads to reduce both computation and I/O. The combination of these approaches can be
125   further enhanced by identifying specific attention patterns for each head and layer (Ge et al., 2024).
126   For surveys of these methods, please refer to (Yuan et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024).
127   System optimizations. The challenge of long-context inference can also be alleviated from a
128   system-level perspective. Offloading-based methods (Sheng et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024a; Wu
129   et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024) store the KV cache in CPU memory, retrieving only the most relevant
130   chunks to the GPU before computing a new chunk. This approach reduces peak GPU memory us-
131   age, though at the cost of slower inference. Hardware-aware algorithms, such as flash attention (Dao
132   et al., 2022; Dao, 2024; Shah et al., 2024) and page attention (Kwon et al., 2023), exploit GPU ar-
133   chitecture (Ghorpade et al., 2012) to enable more efficient runtime memory management. In addi-
134
      tion, reimplementing inference infra-structure in a more efficient programming language (llama.cpp;
      llama2.c; rustformers), or adopting disaggregated inference (Jiang et al., 2024b; Zhong et al., 2024;
135
      Qin et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024a), can greatly enhance inference efficiency. Algorithmic optimiza-
136
      tions can be seamlessly integrated into such systems (Agrawal et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). For
137   instance, KTransformers (KVCache.AI, 2024) adopts the chunked offloading technique from I N -
138   F LLM (Xiao et al., 2024a). However, system optimizations primarily focus on extending context
139   length by leveraging hardware resources, rather than directly reducing the size of the KV cache.

192   Cache eviction. Cache eviction in long-context inference is defined as follows. Here, we slightly
193   abuse the notation of heads and layers, and treat the key-value vector pair of a single token within one
194   head as the smallest cache unit. We denote the cache unit for the k-th token as ck = (K[k], V[k]).
195   Assume a memory budget b, representing the maximum number of cache units that can be stored
196
      in GPU memory at any given time. The abstract form of attention can then be written as ck =
      f (c1 , c2 , · · · , ck−1 ). With limited cache capacity, this calculation can only be approximated by
197
      c̃k = f (c̃p1 , c̃p2 , · · · , c̃pb′ ), where b′ ≤ b, and p1 , p2 · · · , pb′ ∈ {1, 2, · · · , k − 1}. Intuitively, the
198
      number of prior cache units involved cannot exceed the memory budget. When b′ = b, indicating
199   the cache is full, one cache unit must be evicted. We select the unit to be evicted using a policy
200   pv = Policy(c̃p1 , · · · , c̃pb ; c̃k ). In such stated problem, the key challenge of cache eviction is to
201   develop an effective policy function that minimizes the approximation error ∥c̃k − ck ∥.

      3.2    L OCRET F RAMEWORK
204
205   L OCRET is a training-based KV cache compression framework that works in conjunction with chun-
206   ked prefill. As illustrated in Figure 2, L OCRET operates in two stages: training and inference. In
207   the training stage, we modify the original LLM by appending a retaining head R to each attention
208   module. We then train the retaining heads R while keeping the LLM backbone frozen. During
209   chunked prefill inference, the retaining heads R are used to calculate the importance of each cache
210   unit in the chunk. We retain the cache units with higher scores, along with stabilizers (i.e., the last
211   tokens), in the cache pool located in GPU memory. Through this process, the retaining heads R
212
      learn and predict the patterns discovered by existing methods, as detailed in Appendix L.
213   From a mathematical perspective, cache eviction is performed by assigning each cache unit an im-
214   portance score that reflects its influence on the calculation of subsequent tokens. We refer to this
215   estimation as the causal importance score (CIS) since it is computed in a causal manner. The CIS
      of cache unit k is calculated as sk = S(c1 , c2 , · · · , ck ). By applying top-b sparse attention based on

302   3.4              I NFERENCE I MPLEMENTATION OF L OCRET
303
304   During the inference stage, we use the chunked prefill pattern and perform cache eviction based
305                                                        (i)                       (i)      (i)
      on the predicted CIS. Since the predicted value S̃[k]j depends solely on Q[k]j , K[k]j , and
306                (i)                                                                                                                                                                         (i)
307   V[k]j , and because attention in decoder-only models is causal, S̃[k]j remains consistent once
308   calculated. Thus, we store the KV cache units along with their corresponding causal importance
309
      values. When the cache is full, we evict the units with lower causal importance values, as they
      are deemed less useful for future computations. Cache eviction introduces context discontinuity,
310
      meaning some cache units at certain positions may be absent. This can degrade generation quality
311
      and increase the error between the predicted and accurate CIS, as LLMs are typically not trained
312   on such contexts. To mitigate this, we retain the last tokens of the current chunk at each step of the
313   chunked prefill process, ensuring a local and continuous context to minimize errors. To demonstrate
314   the effectiveness of this design, we perform an ablation study on the length of stabilizers ns , shown
315   in Figure 4. Smaller ns results in severe performance degradation, and the model fails entirely when
316   stabilizers are absent, as context discontinuity leads to instability in CIS prediction, causing errors
317   in cache eviction and amplifying errors in hidden states. More details are discussed in Appendix I.
318   We maintain a cache pool with a capacity of b cache units, discarding units that exceed this limit.
319   For each chunk, the model processes the chunked input tokens alongside the current cache pool.
320   The newly generated KV pairs and their predicted scores are then concatenated with the existing
321   cache. Once the cache pool is full, only the b cache units with the highest CIS values are retained.
322   At each chunked prefill step, except for the final step, we retain the stabilizers, i.e. the last ns
323   cache units. Additionally, we do not compress the last nloc tokens of the prompt, as they are critical
      for maintaining high generation quality due to their strong correlation with the query. Finally, the


                                                                                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      answer is generated according to the compressed KV cache. Algorithm 1 provides the pseudocode
325   for L OCRET, where we formally describe the L OCRET inference process.
326
327   The GPU memory usage during L OCRET inference can be effectively bounded. GPU memory for
328
      KV cache storage is limited to O(b + nloc ), and the runtime memory usage of the attention mecha-
      nism is bounded by O(B×(b+B+nloc )). For comparison, while processing an input with n tokens,
329
      full attention prefill requires O(n) for KV cache storage and O(n2 ) for runtime memory, whereas
330
      chunked prefill requires O(n) for KV cache storage and O(nB) for runtime memory consumption.

396
397   We compare our method with the baselines on both ∞Bench and L-Eval to address Q1. As shown
398   in Table 3, L OCRET outperforms all baselines in terms of end-to-end performance.
399   In the ∞Bench tests, while all methods experience performance degradation compared to FullAttn
400   inference, L OCRET, I NF LLM, and MI NFERENCE exhibits better performance than other methods,
401   with only a modest drop in performance given the reduced memory usage. Quantization, on the
402   other hand, shows significant degradation and fails on all tasks. S IR LLM performs well on compre-
403   hensive tasks such as E.Sum and E.MC, but struggles with tasks that require precise memory, such
404   as R.PassKey and R.Number. L OCRET not only excels in context retrieval tasks but also achieves
405   strong results in comprehensive tasks, earning the highest overall score among all competitors.
406   In the L-Eval tests, all methods show some degree of performance degradation. Nevertheless,
407   L OCRET achieves the best overall result, obtaining the highest score on most tasks. L-Eval is a
408   shorter but more complex dataset, where S IR LLM performs particularly well. Quantization fails on
409   most tasks, resulting in the lowest overall score. Both I NF LLM and MInference suffer significant
410   performance drops compared to FullAttn inference. L OCRET consistently surpasses all competitors.
411   We also report memory consumption in Figure 5. In the extreme long-context scenario (∞Bench),
412   L OCRET uses relatively less memory while achieving the best overall performance. I NF LLM per-
413   forms well with limited GPU memory usage, but it requires a significant amount of CPU memory
414   to store the full KV cache. Quantization and S IR LLM can achieve low memory consumption in
415   some settings, but quantization introduces severe performance degradation. MI NFERENCE employs
416   sparse attention patterns but does not compress the KV cache. As a result, its minimum memory
417   requirement equals the sum of the model weights and the full KV cache. In the shorter context sce-
418
      nario (L-Eval), a similar phenomenon is observed. For Phi-3-mini-128K, which has a larger
      KV cache, I NF LLM and MI NFERENCE exhibit higher memory consumption due to the need to store
419
      the full KV cache. Other methods have similar memory footprints, with L OCRET achieving the best
420
      overall performance while using the least memory. For Llama-3.1-8B-instruct, whose full
421   KV cache is smaller, the memory bottleneck shifts to runtime computational memory for attention
422   and other calculations. All methods exhibit similar memory footprints, with L OCRET delivering the
423   best overall performance. Our experiments demonstrate that L OCRET is both effective and efficient,
424   outperforming all baselines on multiple datasets and models while using less GPU memory.

      5      C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
522
523   We propose L OCRET, a lightweight training-based method that enables memory-efficient infer-
524   ence of long contexts on consumer-grade devices. L OCRET introduces retaining heads to predict
525   the CIS of each cache unit during chunked prefill and performs cache eviction based on the pre-
526   dicted CIS. We conduct extensive experiments across different models and multiple datasets to
527   compare L OCRET with major efficient inference techniques, and results show that L OCRET out-
528   performs all baselines, using less GPU memory and without requiring offloading to CPU mem-
529   ory. The framework of L OCRET, including both training and inference, highlights its suitability
530   for low-resource computing scenarios. L OCRET can be applied to various application scenarios,
531
      such as end-side multi-modal model inference and context compression during disaggregated in-
      ference. In this paper, we explore L OCRET based on two models: Phi-3-mini-128K and
532
      Llama-3.1-8B-instruct, within MHA and GQA architectures, respectively. Future work will
533
      involve testing L OCRET on other model architectures, such as encoder-decoder models and multi-
534   latent models. Currently, L OCRET has been evaluated on only two hardware platforms (A800/H800
535   and 4090), and we plan to extend performance evaluations to other popular hardwares. Addition-
536   ally, we observe that when the cache budget is extremely limited, L OCRET can degrade to the
537   StreamingLLM pattern (Figure 7). In future work, we will investigate enhancement methods for
538   such scenarios. Additionally, we are interested in integrating L OCRET with other efficient meth-
539   ods, such as offloading and speculative decoding. We also plan to explore how to combine existing
      query-aware algorithms with L OCRET to achieve more accurate eviction of local tokens.

851

854   tensor parallelism. I NF LLM is a representative of the offloading-based methods, where the full KV
855

857
      tokens. We denote this method as HF-2 BITS. S IR LLM is an eviction-based token dropping algo-

862   S IR LLM does not have official implementation on Phi-3-mini-128K, thus we implement these

      B    T HE G LOBAL AND L OCAL D ISCREPANCY OF S CORING F UNCTIONS
865
866
      Cache importance scoring functions can generally be categorized into two types: causal and non-
867   causal. Non-causal functions, e.g. H2 O and S NAP KV, require information from subsequent cache
868   units to determine the importance score of a cache unit, making them dependent on prefilling the
869   entire sequence. On the other hand, causal functions, e.g. S IR LLM and L OCRET, predict cache
870   importance without relying on subsequent information. Non-causal scoring functions are incompat-
871   ible with chunked prefill because they cannot calculate scores without access to the full sequence. If
872   such functions are integrated with chunked prefill, they often face a significant discrepancy between
873   the local importance score (without considering subsequent information) and the global importance
874   score (with full context).
875   To investigate this discrepancy, we measure the consistency of the top 10% most important cache
876   positions identified in prefixes of various lengths compared to the full context. For reference, the full
877   context is truncated to 6K tokens. The results shown in Figure 1 highlights that scoring functions
878   requiring future information, such as H2 O and S NAP KV, suffer from significant discrepancies when
879   subsequent cache units are not considered. S IR LLM, while also causal, shows notable inaccuracies,
880   leading to performance degradation as demonstrated in Table 3 and Table 4.
881   We also evaluate the end-to-end performance using H2 O and S NAP KV with chunked prefill on
882   ∞Bench, shown in Table 5. The results demonstrate that discrepancies between local and global im-
883   portance scores in H2O and S NAP KV lead to severe performance drops, particularly in R.Number. It
884   is this discrepancy that leads to the failure of H2O and S NAP KV in accurately retrieving information
885   from the context. Specifically, the model is unable to identify the importance of certain cache units
886   at the time they are first encountered. L OCRET, however, avoids such inconsistencies and achieves
887
      superior performance.
