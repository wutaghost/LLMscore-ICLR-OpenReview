011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has revitalized Large Language Models
014            (LLMs) by injecting non-parametric factual knowledge. Compared with long-
015
               context LLMs, RAG is considered an effective summarization tool in a more con-
               cise and lightweight manner, which can interact with LLMs multiple times using
016
               diverse queries to get comprehensive responses. However, the LLM-generated
017
               historical responses, which contain potentially insightful information, are largely
018            neglected and discarded by existing approaches, leading to suboptimal results. In
019            this paper, we propose graph of records (GoR), which leverages historical re-
020            sponses generated by LLMs to enhance RAG for long-context global summariza-
021            tion. Inspired by the retrieve-then-generate paradigm of RAG, we construct a
022            graph by creating an edge between the retrieved text chunks and the correspond-
023            ing LLM-generated response. To further uncover the sophisticated correlations
024            between them, GoR further features a graph neural network and an elaborately
025            designed BERTScore-based objective for self-supervised model training, enabling
026
               seamless supervision signal backpropagation between reference summaries and
               node embeddings. We comprehensively compare GoR with 12 baselines on four
027
               long-context summarization datasets, and the results indicate that our proposed
028
               method reaches the best performance. Extensive experiments further demonstrate
029            the effectiveness of GoR.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently achieved remarkable performance in sorts of lan-
035   guage modeling tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; AI@Meta, 2024). Among them, the long-context global
036   summarization task is of great importance, which requires ultra-long context understanding capabil-
037   ities of LLMs (Li et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024b). Current attempts to accomplish this task mainly
038   include long-context LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; GLM et al., 2024; Li* et al., 2023; Tworkowski
039
      et al., 2023) and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Ram et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Trivedi
      et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023b; Asai et al., 2023). In comparison with long-context LLMs that
040
      expand their context window to accommodate long-context inputs, RAG performs a cost-effective
041
      retrieve-then-generate paradigm and provides a few retrieved short text chunks from a long docu-
042   ment to LLMs. In a running RAG system (Figure 1), there are usually a large number of historical
043   user queries and LLM-generated responses for a long document. Nevertheless, these historical re-
044   sponses, which contain informative task-related content, are mostly neglected without sufficient
045   utilization by current RAG approaches.
046
      Unfortunately, utilizing LLM historical responses for long-context global summarization present
047
      two major challenges. (1) Sophisticated yet implicit correlations between historical responses and
048   text. Given a long document, there will inevitably be complicated correlations among plentiful user
049   queries (e.g., logical correlations), which are further inherited by LLM-generated responses and the
050   retrieved text chunks. However, uncovering these correlations is non-trivial since most text embed-
051   dings from language models (e.g., SBERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019)) or retrievers (Karpukhin
052   et al., 2020) concentrate on semantic similarity, which faces degrading performance in this case. (2)
053   Lack of supervision signal. In contrast with local (e.g., query-based) summarization (Zhong et al.,
      2021; Wang et al., 2022a) that includes golden reference text as labels, global summarization needs

      Based on the above observations, we propose graph of records (GoR), which utilizes and organizes
072   LLM historical responses as a graph of records for enhancing long-context global summarization in
073   RAG. In detail, we first leverage LLMs to simulate some user queries conditioned on arbitrary text
074   chunks in a long document to obtain historical responses under the paradigm of RAG, and an edge is
075   then created between the retrieved text chunks and the LLM-generated response to construct a graph
076   of records. To learn fine-grained correlations among nodes, we employ a graph neural network and
077   reuse the simulated user queries with the corresponding source text chunk as self-supervised training
078   data. Intuitively, we hope the node embeddings can be adaptively learned to reflect the semantic and
079   logical correlations with a given query. Inspired by the well-received BERTScore (Zhang et al.,
080
      2019) that quantifies the semantic similarity between two paragraphs of text, we rely on it to rank
      the nodes according to their similarity with the self-supervised label of a given simulated query.
081
      In this way, node embeddings can benefit the indirect supervision signal from the self-supervised
082
      labels and be flexibly optimized using a contrastive loss and a pair-wise ranking loss based on the
083   node rankings. In the experiments, we adopt four long-context summarization datasets, and the
084   results demonstrate the superiority and effectiveness of our proposed method. Our contributions are
085   summarized as follows:
086
087          • We propose graph of records (GoR), which utilizes and organizes LLM-generated histor-
088            ical responses as a graph of records to strengthen RAG for long-context global summa-
089            rization. We reveal that the fine-grained correlations between LLM historical responses
090            and text chunks from long documents can be uncovered and utilized effectively to improve
091            RAG performance.
092
093          • We leverage a graph neural network and design a BERTScore-based objective to opti-
094            mize node embeddings, which can be adaptively learned in a self-supervised manner to
095            reflect the semantic and complex correlations with input queries. Furthermore, the indi-
096
               rect supervision signal from self-supervised labels is crucial and conducive to the effective
               optimization of node embeddings.

             • We evaluate our proposed method on four long-context summarization datasets, and the
099
               experimental results show that GoR outperforms several competitive baselines by a signif-
100            icant margin. Extensive experiments and detailed analysis verify the superiority of GoR.

131   2.2   G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION
132
      In this section, we describe how to organize LLM historical responses into a graph of records by
133
      simulating user queries.
134
135   Query Simulation. User queries play a very critical role in the design of GoR since LLM historical
136   responses generated by lots of repetitive, nonsense, or meaningless questions are inherently not
137   beneficial for summarization. One solution is to use doc2query (Nogueira et al., 2019) to simulate
138
      queries for a long document, but the generated results inevitably suffer from simplicity and rigidity
      due to the limited text generation capabilities of T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). To this end, we directly turn
139
      to LLMs for query simulation with temperature sampling instead of greedy decoding for generating
140
      meaningful, insightful, and diverse questions. Specifically, we split a long document into several
141   text chunks C following the standard procedure of RAG and prompt LLMs to generate a query qs
142   based on a randomly selected text chunk cs . We repeat the above process until a certain number of
143   non-duplicate queries are generated, which are gathered in pairs with the corresponding text chunks
144                                      |T|
      to form a corpus T = {(qsi , csi )}i=1 for further model training (Section 2.3). The prompt for query
145   simulation can be found in Appendix C.
146
147
      Organize LLM Historical Responses into A Graph. After obtaining simulated queries, we utilize
      them to perform RAG on the long document. LLM-generated responses during this process include
148
      informative and valuable understanding, summarizing, and answering of retrieved text chunks in the
149
      long document. Moreover, since there may exist sophisticated correlations among simulated queries,
150   the text chunks and responses can inherit these features and potentially assist in answering a more
151   comprehensive query, especially global summarization that needs to be understood from a holistic
152   perspective. Nevertheless, it is a significant challenge to find correlations among complex and mas-
153   sive text at the linguistic level and the embeddings from language models (e.g., SBERT (Reimers
154   & Gurevych, 2019)) or retrievers (Karpukhin et al., 2020) focus on semantic similarity, which also
155   suffers from poor performance in this case. To this end, we propose to break out of this dilemma by
156   organizing these historical responses into a graph.
157   Inspired by the retrieve-then-generate process of RAG, we can connect the retrieved chunks to the
158   corresponding response generated by LLMs since they are naturally relevant in content. Sequen-
159   tially, during the i-th round RAG, given the simulated query qsi , we expand the retrieval corpus C
160   with previously generated responses {r1 , · · · , ri−1 } and then build an edge between each retrieved
161   chunk cj ∈ {c1 , · · · , cK } and the newly generated LLM response ri , resulting in K edges con-
      structed in each round. Note that we append the responses generated by each round of RAG to the

197   2.3   BERTS CORE - BASED O BJECTIVE FOR S ELF - SUPERVISED T RAINING
198
199   So far, we have constructed a graph using LLM-generated historical responses during RAG given
200   the simulated queries. The key in this section lies in designing a reasonable and effective objective
201
      function for model optimization. Considering that some random walk (Grover & Leskovec, 2016)
      or propagation-based (Zhu & Ghahramani, 2002) algorithms are not differentiable, we turn to graph
202
      neural networks (GNNs) for learning node embeddings, which are backpropagation-friendly. Intu-
203
      itively, given a global summarization query q, our ultimate optimization goal is to make the learned
204   node embeddings adaptively reflect the similarity with the query embedding Eq (q) by taking the
205   complicated correlations among nodes into account. However, in global summarization tasks, there
206   are essentially no text chunk indices as labels to indicate which nodes are most relevant for a query
207   since it needs to consider the long document as a whole. Another naive solution is to use global ref-
208   erence summaries as labels, but there is a gap in supervision signal backpropagation between them
209   and node embeddings because we still need to find out which nodes are most relevant to them.
210   Therefore, inspired by BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019), which measures the semantic similarity
211   between the reference and the generated text, we propose to use it to rank all nodes based on the
212   similarity with reference summaries. By this means, BERTScore fills the gap in the backpropagation
213   so that node embeddings can benefit the indirect supervision signal from the reference summaries.
214   Nevertheless, global reference summaries contain broad information about long documents, mak-
215   ing them highly semantically relevant to many nodes, which will confuse the model optimization
      direction and degrade the performance (we will discuss it in Section 3.4).

261
      Datasets. We evaluate our proposed method on four long-context summarization datasets, i.e.,
262
      AcademicEval (Feng et al., 2024), QMSum (Zhong et al., 2021), WCEP (Gholipour Ghalandari
263
      et al., 2020), and BookSum (Kryściński et al., 2021). Among them, AcademicEval collects scien-
264   tific papers from arXiv for abstract writing, given the long inputs of its main body. QMSum is a
265   query-based summarization dataset, and we only use “general queries” for evaluating global sum-
266   marization. WCEP is a multi-document summarization dataset about news events, while BookSum
267   features long-form narrative summarization. For metrics, we adopt Rouge-1 (R-1), Rouge-2 (R-2),
268   and Rouge-L (R-L) (Lin, 2004) to assess the text alignment between the reference summaries and
269   the predicted content generated by our proposed method. More details about adopted datasets are
      described in Appendix A.

      Implementation Details. Following the standard procedure of RAG, we adopt TokenTextSplitter
271   from LangChain1 to split each long document into text chunks. Each chunk has a size of 256,
272   and the chunk overlapping is 32. We generate 30 queries for each long document using Mixtral-
273   8x7B-Instruct-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2024), and the temperature coefficient is set to 0.5 by default in the
274   query simulation stage. For RAG, we use Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021) for query and document
275   embedding and retrieve 6 text chunks by default, which are fed into LLaMA-2-7b-chat (Touvron
276   et al., 2023) with greedy decoding to generate predicted summaries.
277   In the training stage, we initialize the graph neural network as a two-layer graph attention network
278   (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2017), with a 768-dim hidden dimension following the default setting of
279   most retrievers. The batch size is 32, and the max training epoch is set to 150. We use Adam
280   optimizer for model training and gradually decay the learning rate from 1e-3 to 0 with LambdaLR
281   scheduler. The dropout rate and loss coefficient α depend on the specific dataset, and we detail
282   them in Appendix A. We implement our proposed method using PyTorch2 and Deep Graph Library
283   (DGL)3 , and all the experiments are conducted on a single RTX 3080 GPU. As for LLMs, we rely
284   on API calling from Together AI4 to obtain responses.
285   Baselines. To have a comprehensive evaluation, we compare our proposed GoR with dozens of base-
286   lines, including (1) Random Walk based Node Embedding (i.e., Node2Vec (Grover & Leskovec,
287   2016)), (2) Sparse Retriever (i.e., BM25 (Robertson et al., 2009) and TF-IDF (Ramos et al.,
288   2003)), (3) Dense Retriever (i.e., Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021), DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020),
289   Dragon (Lin et al., 2023), and Sentence-BERT (SBERT) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019)5 ), (4) Hy-
290   brid Retriever (i.e., BM25+DPR with Reciprocal Rerank Fusion), (5) Long-context LLMs (i.e.,
291
      Gemma-8K (Team et al., 2024) and Mistral-8K (Jiang et al., 2023a)), (6) Full Context (i.e., feeds
      all inputs to LLMs for summary generation6 ), and (7) Thought Retriever (Thought-R) (Feng et al.,
292
      2024).

295   3.2    M AIN R ESULTS
296
      We conduct comprehensive experiments on QMSum, AcademicEval, WCEP, and BookSum datasets
297
      compared with dozens of baselines to evaluate the long-context global summarization capabilities
298
      of our proposed method. The results are shown in Table 1.
299
300   GoR consistently outperforms retriever-based methods. From Table 1, our proposed GoR beats
301   sparse retrievers, dense retrievers, and hybrid retrievers in every aspect. Thanks to the constructed
302
      graph, which integrates text chunks from long documents and LLM historical responses into a whole,
      node embeddings can better reflect the complicated correlations with given queries, thus signifi-
303
      cantly improving the retrieval performance of GoR. Moreover, the informative content of historical
304
      responses can also potentially enhance the summarization task.
305
306   GoR shows superiority over long-context LLMs. We compare Gemma-8K and Mistral-8K with
307   a longer context window to accommodate long-context inputs. However, longer inputs may contain
308
      minor information, and long-context LLMs struggle with this situation. In contrast, GoR can effec-
      tively differentiate key and topic-related content in long texts using learned node embeddings and
309
      achieve better results with shorter input lengths.
310
311   Additional Findings. (1) Node2Vec produces unsatisfactory results, and the node embeddings
312   cannot be optimized effectively since it is based on a non-differentiable algorithm. (2) Although
313   Thought Retriever demonstrates competitive results, it is still inferior to GoR due to the lack of ex-
314
      ploration of the correlations between retrieved text chunks and LLM-generated responses. (3) Since
      the context window length limit of LLMs is exceeded, “Full Context” truncates the long-context
315
      input, thus losing some information that may be important for global summarization, resulting in
316
      suboptimal results.

      4      R ELATED W ORK
458
459
      Long-context Summarization using LLMs. In recent years, LLMs have demonstrated their ex-
      cellent capabilities in long-context modeling (Achiam et al., 2023; AI@Meta, 2024; Team et al.,
460
      2024; Jiang et al., 2024). Current approaches for summarizing long documents using LLMs are
461
      mainly divided into two categories: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Ram et al., 2023; Yu
462   et al., 2023) and long-context LLMs (GLM et al., 2024; Li* et al., 2023; Tworkowski et al., 2023).
463   Long-context LLMs feature a large context window length to accommodate long-context inputs.
464   However, they may suffer from severe performance degradation when accessing some local key de-
465   tails in the middle of long contexts (Liu et al., 2024b). In contrast, RAG emerges as a promising
466   approach for cost-effective long-context summarization. By equipping with a retriever (Karpukhin
467   et al., 2020; Robertson et al., 2009), RAG can first perform a relevance search based on user queries
468   and then feed the retrieved text into LLMs for summary. For a recent example, GraphRAG (Edge
469   et al., 2024) conducts query-focused summarization by setting up a graph index and detecting graph
470
      communities for summary generation.
471   Nevertheless, most current RAG approaches still focus on enhancing LLMs’ reasoning and question-
472   answering capabilities, which only require retrieving locally relevant information (Trivedi et al.,
473   2022; Jiang et al., 2023b; Asai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). In comparison,
474   our proposed method stands out from these methods by focusing on LLMs’ global summarization
475   capability of long-context inputs.
476   Historical Response Utilization of LLMs. Little work has been done on this under-explored topic.
477   Thought-retriever (Feng et al., 2024) saves the historical responses of each user-LLM interaction as
478   high-level and informative thoughts to expand the retrieval corpus for future user queries. However,
479   the rich correlation among thoughts is neglected, leaving room for further improvement.
480   Another line of work is the Chain-of-Thought (CoT), which is similar to our approach in terms
481   of utilizing LLM historical responses and has been regarded as an effective means to enhance the
482   reasoning ability of LLM during inference time in recent years. Few-shot CoT (Wei et al., 2022)
483   and zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) elicit intermediate reasoning paths by prompting LLMs
484   with several demonstrations or just appending ”Let’s think step by step.” Self-consistency (Wang
485   et al., 2022b) samples diverse reasoning paths and conducts a majority vote to obtain the final an-
      swer. ToT (Yao et al., 2024) and GoT (Besta et al., 2024) take a further step by integrating a


                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      tree or graph structure to manage its historical reasoning paths, enabling more flexible reasoning
487   and reflection of LLMs. By memorizing the solution paradigms of various queries into different
488   templates, BoT (Yang et al., 2024) pushes LLM’s reasoning ability to a new level. Although the
489   above-mentioned CoT-series approaches improve the reasoning capabilities of LLMs by utilizing
490   chains of intermediate reasoning responses, they usually concentrate on one specific QA-type query
491   and cannot generalize to and benefit other queries. Moreover, only a small number of historical
492   reasoning responses are retained for the final generation, while most of the rest are just discarded.
493   Graph-assisted Retrieval-augmented Language Models. As one of the effective structures for
494   modeling data relations, graphs have recently been used to enhance the performance of retrieval-
495   augmented language models on various QA tasks. EtD (Liu et al., 2024a) features a graph neural
496   network (GNN) to traverse a knowledge graph hop by hop to discover more relevant knowledge, thus
497   enhancing LLM generation quality. GNN-RAG (Mavromatis & Karypis, 2024) learns to reason over
498   graphs using GNNs, and retrieves answer candidates for a given question. PG-RAG (Liang et al.,
499   2024) constructs pseudo-graphs with a retrieval indexer by prompting LLMs to organize document
500   knowledge in a self-learning manner. G-RAG (Dong et al., 2024) proposes to rerank documents by
501
      learning graph representation on abstract meaning representation graphs, while GNN-Ret (Li et al.,
      2024b) refines semantic distances between documents and queries by modeling relationships among
502
      related passages. ToG (Sun et al., 2023) and KGP (Wang et al., 2024) treat LLMs as agents to
503
      traverse and reason over knowledge graphs in an iterative way, while RoG (Luo et al., 2023) first
504   generates plans for retrieval and then conducts reasoning. G-Retriever (He et al., 2024) transforms
505   retrieved knowledge subgraphs into graph embeddings by training a graph encoder and textualizes
506   subgraphs to serve as inputs of LLMs.
507
      Different from the above, GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) sets up a graph index for query-focused
508
      summarization, which is more relevant to our proposed approach. Compared with GraphRAG,
509
      which is time-consuming and suffers from huge computational costs, our proposed GoR is
510   lightweight and only draws on a few LLM historical responses with efficient training to achieve
511   competitive performance.

      5   C ONCLUSION
515
516
      In this work, we introduce a method named graph of records (GoR) to improve long-context global
      summarization in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) by utilizing LLM-generated historical re-
517
      sponses. Intuitively, we establish connections between text chunks retrieved from long documents
518
      and LLM-generated historical responses to create a graph of records. To uncover complex correla-
519   tions between these connections, we use a graph neural network and develop a BERTScore-based
520   objective for self-supervised training. This allows for seamless supervision signal backpropaga-
521   tion between self-supervised reference summaries and node embeddings. In our experiments, we
522   assess our proposed method on four long-context summarization datasets, and the results consis-
523   tently demonstrate that GoR outperforms numerous baselines by a significant margin, highlighting
524   its effectiveness.
525   Despite the superiority of our proposed method, GoR has some limitations. (1) Due to a limited
526   budget, we only simulate and generate a small number of user queries, which may cause a bottleneck
527   in further model optimization. (2) The simulated queries may not accurately reflect the real-world
528   distribution, as they do not account for the possibility of users asking many meaningless questions.
529   Therefore, a filtering process may be necessary, which we leave for future work.

541
542
      The security of Large Language Models (LLMs) has always been a concern. Unfortunately, current
543   LLMs sometimes produce harmful and biased information unexpectedly. Our proposed method
544   uses LLMs to generate simulated queries and summary responses, which are only used to construct
545   a graph of records and connect text chunks from long documents. However, more work is needed
546   in real-world applications to ensure that LLMs’ responses are reliable and harmless, so that they do

798           learns unsupervised document embeddings for information retrieval, DPR (Dense Passage

              retrieval accuracy. In our experiments, the retrieval corpus is only the document chunks.

989
       In the era of LLMs, countless interactions take place between users and these models on a daily basis,
990
       resulting in the generation of a vast amount of historical responses. Our proposed method demon-
991    strates that these historical responses hold significant potential and can be effectively leveraged to

994    reduce computational overhead. This approach highlights the untapped value of historical data in
995    optimizing response generation while making the process more efficient and resource-friendly.
