                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We propose a new framework for evaluating the relationship between features
               and generalization via a theoretical analysis of the out-of-distribution (OOD)
011
               generalization problem, in which we simultaneously use two mathematical methods:
012
               a generalization ratio that quantitatively characterizes the degree of generalization,
013            and a generalization decision process (GDP) that formalizes the relationship of loss
014            between seen and unseen domains. By combining the concepts of informativeness
015            and variation in the generalization ratio, we intuitively associate them with OOD
016            problems to derive the generalization inequality. We then introduce it to the GDP to
017            select the best loss from seen domains to gradient descent for backpropagation. In
018            the case where the classifier is defined by fully connected neural network, the entire
019            system is trained with backpropagation. There is no need for any model selection
020            criterion or operating on gradients during training. Experiments demonstrate the
021
               potential of the framework through qualitative and quantitative evaluation of the
               generalization ability.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Traditional supervised learning is highly dependent on the proposition of independent and identically
028
      distributed (i.i.d.), while ignoring out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios commonly encountered in
      real-word applications (Taori et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021;
029
      Wang et al., 2024a). So far, research on the success of gradient operations in generalization algorithms
030
      has emerged (Huang et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2021; Rame et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
031   2021), generally trying to learn generalized representations by directly operating on gradients. These
032   successes have primarily been based on the manipulation and transformation of features. However,
033   theoretical analysis for the OOD generalization problem has less impact, due to the difficulty of
034   providing a metric for evaluating the relationship between features and generalization when features
035   undergo shifts, and due to the complexity in integrating adjustments for generalization into the
036   training procedure. We propose a novel quantitative metric called “generalization ratio” and a new
037   estimation procedure called “generalization decision process” to sidestep these difficulties.
038   In the proposed generalization gradient descent (GGD) framework, an available domain set Eavail
039   includes both the training set Etra and the validation set Eval . The quantitative metric evaluates the
040   degree of generalization between Etra and Eavail . Our aim is to utilize Etra to generalize to Eval , and
041   then achieve generalization to a larger domain set Eavail , which includes all unseen domains. In other
042   words, we consider the validation set as an unseen domain, which is not utilized for backpropagation
043   during training. Firstly, we define ideal feature matrix function, generalization and non-generalization
044   models (Definition 3.1, 3.2 and 3.3), where we refer to the definition of generalization proposed in
045
      the previous theoretical framework (See Ye et al. (2021)). Considering the actual distribution and
      handling of features in gradient descent, we further clarify and redefine these definitions, i.e., we
046
      define the “variation” and “informativeness” (Definition 3.4 and 3.5) of features for classification
047
      task, and propose propositions (Proposition3.6 and 3.7). Meanwhile, to incorporate the definitions
048   of “variation” and “informativeness” of features, we quantitatively define the generalization ratio
049   (Definition 3.8 and Theorem 3.9) based on the definition of expansion function (Definition 3.3
050   in Ye et al. (2021)). In other words, the generalization ratio is a kind of metric to quantitatively
051   characterize the degree of generalization. Then, through the definition of learnability (Definition 3.4
052   in Ye et al. (2021)), the generalization ratio is also a learnable OOD problem (Theorem 3.10). In
053   our experiment, we will introduce easily computable definitions of “variation” and “informativeness”
      through Bayes’ theorem for the estimation procedure (Theorem 2.1). As pointed out by previous

054
      theoretical framework (See Ye et al. (2021)), any high-dimensional joint distributions where the
055   marginal distributions in each dimension are close but non-overlapping. To address this challenge,
056   we theoretically confirm that even with variations in distribution, it is still possible to accurately
057   predict the Eavail dataset using a generalized model (Theorem 4.1), consistent with the definition of
058   generalized model (Definition 3.3). Meanwhile, we prove the generalization inequality (Theorem 4.2)
059   based on the generalization ratio and the linear top model theorem (Theorem 4.2 in Ye et al. (2021)),
060   formalizing the relationship between generalization error and loss on Etra and Eavail . Specifically,
061   we consider both nonlinear and linear neural networks to establish the structure of the model. Based
062   on these models, we derive a generalization inequality to create an estimation procedure.
063   To combine the generalization inequality with gradient descent in an algorithm, inspired by reinforce-
064   ment learning, we introduce the generalization decision process (GDP) as an estimation procedure.
065   In this process, we use the generalization inequality as the basis, defining deterministic transitions
066   (Definition 5.1 and 5.2) for both generalization and non-generalization based on changes in both
067   generalization error and loss on Etra , while providing various magnitudes of rewards. We consider the
068   generalization ratio as the state and the losses on Etra as the actions. This aims to find the maximum
069
      rewards within the deterministic transitions to select the best action, and then utilizes backpropagation
      with gradient descent to optimize this action. In addition, we use T as the stopping criterion of
070
      iterations, allowing the process to stop at any time (not necessarily until total convergence). This
071
      framework can provide specific training algorithms for all types of neural network and optimization
072   algorithm. In this article, we utilize quantitative metrics during model training in simple cases use the
073   GDP for backpropagation with gradient descent.
074
075   Contribution. In summary, this paper will provide the following: (1) We introduce a quantitative
076   metric called “generalization ratio” that characterizes the relation between Etra and Eavail in terms of
077   the variation and informativeness of features. (2) We theoretically prove the generalization inequality
078   in terms of generalization ratio and linear top model theorem. This inequality intuitively guarantees
079   an upper bound on the loss on Eavail , which consists of both seen and unseen domains. (3) We
080   theoretically confirm that accurate predictions remain possible even in the case of distributional
081   variations. (4) We propose an estimation procedure called the “Generalization Decision Process”, a
082   reinforcement learning approach to address the combined problem in the gradient descent algorithm.

084   2    R ELATED W ORKS
085
086   Domain generalization (Zhou et al., 2022) and OOD generalization (Yu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024)
087   have garnered significant attention in recent years, with extensive research addressing the challenge
088   of domain shift. Domain adaptation (DA) (Li et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024), in particular, has been
089   a focal point of this literature, with numerous approaches proposed to mitigate the discrepancies
090   between the training (source) and the test (target) distributions (Chen, 2024; Moreno-Torres et al.,
091   2012; Recht et al., 2019; Ben-David et al., 2010; Taori et al., 2020; Blanchard et al., 2021). Since the
092   formal introduction of domain generalization in 2011 by Blanchard et al. (2011), several methods
093
      have been developed to address the generalization of OOD, addressing the problem of distribution
      change through various strategies (Yang et al., 2024; Teney et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2021; Fan et al.,
094
      2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Pandey et al., 2021; Shu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2024; 2023; Cha et al.,
095
      2021).
096
097   Many works have emerged from the perspectives of causal discovery, distributional robustness, and
098   conditional independence, with the aim of providing robust solutions to the OOD generalization
099
      problem (Gui et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Ahuja et al., 2020a; Bai et al., 2021; Creager et al., 2020;
      Chang et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2020; Koyama & Yamaguchi, 2020; Krueger et al., 2021; Parascandolo
100
      et al., 2020; Sagawa et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020). These approaches often focus on defining test
101
      domains around the training domain using distribution distance measures or using causal frameworks
102   that remain invariant under interventions (Shao et al., 2024; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Heinze-Deml &
103   Meinshausen, 2021; Magliacane et al., 2018; Meinshausen, 2018; Müller et al., 2021; Pfister et al.,
104   2021; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018; Schölkopf et al., 2012). The principle underlying these methods is
105   that a causal model, which achieves minimal worst-case risk, is invariant to distribution shifts (Wu
106   et al., 2024; Aldrich, 1989; Haavelmo, 1944; Pearl, 2009; Rojas-Carulla et al., 2018). However, the
107   unknown nature of test distributions necessitates additional assumptions for effective generalization
      analysis.

109   and experimental perspectives, highlighting areas where current approaches fall short (Yu et al., 2024;
110   Feder et al., 2024; Ahuja et al., 2020b; Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020; Kamath et al., 2021; Nagarajan

310   In this section, we introduce the Generalization Decision Process (GDP), which incorporates the
311   reinforcement learning method. In GDP, we set the generalization ratio as the environment, and select
312
      different losses from the training set L (e ′′ , f ), e ′′ ∈ Etra to minimize the loss from the available set
      L (Eavail , f ) based on the maximum cumulative reward.

533   9   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We take the first step towards combining generalization with reinforcement learning to train models,
536   and propose a generalization ratio to quantitatively characterize the degree of generalization and
537   a Generalization Decision Process to formalize the relationship between loss in seen and unseen
538   domains. Based on our framework, we derive a generalization inequality and design a Generalization
539   Gradient Descent algorithm to optimize loss selection for backpropagation. Finally, the results of our
      experiments show that our algorithm significantly outperforms traditional gradient descent methods.
