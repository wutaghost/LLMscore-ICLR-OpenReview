009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have found successful deployment in numerous
012            vision perception systems. However, their susceptibility to adversarial attacks
013            has prompted concerns regarding their practical applications, specifically in the
014            context of autonomous driving. Existing research on defenses often suffers from
015            cost inefficiency, rendering their deployment impractical for resource-constrained
016            applications. In this work, we propose an efficient and effective adversarial attack
017            detection scheme leveraging the multi-task perception within a complex vision
018
               system. Adversarial perturbations are detected by the inconsistencies between the
               inference outputs of multiple vision tasks, e.g., objection detection and instance
019
               segmentation. To this end, we developed a consistency score metric to measure the
020
               inconsistency between vision tasks. Next, we designed an approach to select the
021            best model pairs for detecting this inconsistency effectively. Finally, we evaluated
022            our defense by implementing PGD attacks across multiple vision models on the
023            BDD100k validation dataset. The experimental results demonstrated that our de-
024            fense achieved a ROC-AUC performance of 99.9% detection within the considered
025            attacker model.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The camera-based perception system is critical to enable automated driving (AD). Indeed, camera
031   is the only sensor able to read traffic signs, identify lane markings or drivable areas, and see traffic
032   light colors. To perform such perception tasks (e.g., object detection, classification, segmentation), a
033
      wide range of machine learning models were developed, each with its own objective and network
      architecture (Zou et al., 2023). For example, from an input image, 2D object detection models output
034
      bounding boxes, while semantic segmentation models output masks, or multi-object tracking models
035
      output track identifiers. The model outputs help to understand the scene and allow the automated
036   vehicle to maneuver appropriately.
037
038   However, camera inputs can be maliciously manipulated to affect the performance of perception tasks,
039
      or even downstream tasks of automated vehicles (e.g., path planning, motion control). The idea of
      adversarial inputs (commonly called adversarial examples) is to add specially-crafted noise to images
040
      such that the underlying machine learning models do not perform as originally intended (Madry
041
      et al., 2018). Adversarial examples have been demonstrated in the form of full image perturbations or
042   patches, realized digitally or physically, and with some high attack success rate and universality (Chow
043   et al., 2020). Because of their low level of sophistication and effectiveness, it is key to deploy defenses
044   to protect automated vehicles against such threats. Defenses range from preemptive techniques (e.g.,
045   adversarial training (Shafahi et al., 2019), certified robustness (Xiang et al., 2024)) to reactive
046   techniques (e.g., real-time detection of perturbations (Xiang et al., 2022), image compression (Das
047   et al., 2018)).
048   In this paper, we focus on reactive techniques, aiming at real-time detection of perturbations, because
049   it does not require any adversarial data generation or additional training. Especially, we propose to
050   leverage the output of multiple perception tasks to identify perturbations on every image prior to
051   use by downstream tasks. Prior work showed the effectiveness of checking inconsistencies of edge
052   extractions between outputs of semantic segmentation and depth estimation (Klingner et al., 2022),
053   but with some limitations. Their inconsistency check only detects adversarial perturbations on the
      entire image and might show limited performance on local perturbations. Therefore, we propose


                                                          1

054
      a consistency-based detection technique that is effective regardless of the perturbation’s location.
055   As long as the perturbation causes inconsistent inference output across models, locally or globally,
056   our defense can capture the inconsistency. Especially, we demonstrate the benefits of cross-model
057   consistency by using 2D object detection and instance segmentation models. Indeed, 2D object
058   detection models are commonly used in AD to detect obstacles or traffic signs, and then to convert 2D
059   bounding boxes to 3D bounding boxes (Feng et al., 2020; Arnold et al., 2019). Instance segmentation
060   is also used in AD to provide finer object boundaries (Zhou et al., 2020). Both model share the
061   objective of detecting objects, and hence, can be used to identify inconsistencies.
062   Our contributions are as follows:
063
064
      • We propose a lightweight consistency detector based on outputs from object detection and instance
        segmentation models.
065
      • We develop a technique to select the optimal model pair, deriving requirements w.r.t model
066
        architecture.
067   • We define a metric to capture the consistency score between two models’ output.
068   • We generate and publish an adversarial BDD100k dataset to assess the effectiveness of our defense,
069     and allow reproducibility and comparison of future defenses.

      2.1   V ISION M ULTI -TASK S YSTEM
074
075   Perception systems perform multiple vision tasks such as object detection, segmentation, and depth
076   estimation. Because of its better generalization performance and efficiency (Guo et al., 2020b),
077   one architecture considered for automated driving is Multi-Task Learning (MTL) (Miraliev et al.,
078   2023). A common approach in MTL is to have a shared feature extractor and multiple task-specific
079   heads (Caruana, 1997; Kokkinos, 2017; Lu et al., 2017). In this paper, because our detection method
080   must work with MTL and non-MTL architecture, our architecture consists of one model per task.
081   With this flexible approach, we can evaluate the performance of our detector when the tasks share
082
      (or not) the same backbone. Indeed,the attack success rate strongly correlates with the architecture
      similarity between tasks as highlighted by Xie et al. (2017). Interestingly, from a security perspective,
083
      it may be more robust to have an architecture with different backbone per task than a common
084
      backbone architecture for all tasks (like in the MTL architecture).

088   We follow the same attacker model as defined by Xiang et al. (2022), where the attacker performs a
089   white-box attack (i.e., has access to the model’s architecture and weights). We assume a model F
090   with an underlying data distribution D over pairs consisting of image x and its corresponding ground
091   truth y. X denotes the image space. The attacker adds the perturbation δ to the genuine image x
092   to create an adversarial image (x′ = x + δ) (with ||δ||p ≤ ϵ, where ϵ is the bound on the Lp norm
093   perturbation) such as x′ ∈ A(x) ⊂ X , where constraint A defines the attacker’s capability. The goal
094
      of the attacker is to minimize the alteration of the genuine image x while ensuring the attack succeed,
      and is formulated as:

140   3.1      C ONSISTENCY BETWEEN V ISION TASKS
141
      As shown in Figure 1, the inference outputs for object detection and instance segmentation on
142
      clean images exhibit overall consistency. Indeed, the object bounding boxes match with the object
143
      masks. However, on the perturbed images, discrepancies arise. For instance, in Figures 1c-1d, the
144   perturbation optimized for the object detection model successfully deceived the object detector,
145   leading to numerous false positive and false negative predictions. On the other hand, the same
146   perturbation did not fool the instance segmentation model, which accurately predicted the bounding
147   boxes and masks1 . Similar impact is observed in Figure 1e-1f where the perturbation is optimized for
148   instance segmentation. In fact, we can identify two types of consistency between the model outputs:
149
150   • Location Consistency: refers to detecting an object at the same location within an input image
151
        using both an object detection model and an instance segmentation model. It involves calculating
        the Intersection over Union (IoU) between each detected object from both models. If the IoU
152
        exceeds a predefined threshold (e.g., 50%), the object pair is considered location consistent.
153
154   • Semantic Consistency: goes beyond location and ensures that the labels of the object pair are
155
        identical as well. In this paper, we consider a detection as consistent if both location and semantic
        consistency are proven.
156
157
      Consistency Score. In this work, we call consistent detection (CD) a matching pair of box and
158
      mask (location and label wise). In order to measure the overall consistency of a single image,
159   Equation 3 defines the Consistency Score Ctask as the ratio of total number of consistent detection
160   over the total number of detection from either model (Ntask ).

      Notations. In order to formulate the problem, we denote the output of the object detection model as
217   a set of annotations of detected objects Sdet = {(BBoxdet,i , Labeldet,i )|i = 1, ...Kdet } where BBoxdet,i
218   is the bounding box coordinates for the i-th detection, Labeldet,i is its corresponding class label, and
219   Kdet is the total number of detection by the object detection model. Similarly, we denote the output
220   of the instance segmentation model as Sseg = {(BBoxseg,j , Labelseg,j )|j = 1, ...Kseg }.
221
222   Step 1: Consistency Score Calculation. Following Equation 3, the consistency score is calculated
223   between the two tasks output. In Appendix A.1, we propose Algorithm 1 as an implementation of the
224   Consistency Score Calculation module of Figure 3.
225
226   Step 2: Adversarial determination. With the consistency score generated for the input image, the
227   next step is to decide if it is a clean or perturbed image. As shown in Figure 3, a threshold-based
228   binary classification takes the consistency score as input. If the consistency score is lower than
229
      the predefined threshold, the input is labelled as “perturbed”. As shown in Figure 2, setting a high
      cut-off threshold (e.g., 0.75) would trigger false positives. Conversely, selecting a low threshold
230
      (e.g., 0.2) would trigger false negatives. Therefore, there is a trade-off between false positive rate
231
      and false negative rate. Implementers would have to pick the appropriate threshold using known
232   techniques (Lan et al., 2020).
233
234   Cross-task model selection. When designing a multi-task consistency detector, it is important
235   to select the appropriate models used for each task. Indeed, the two models2 could share the same
236   backbone and underlying structure, or only share the same backbone, or share similar backbone
237   but with different layer depth. We aim at answering the question “What model architectures or
238   parameters affect the ability to detect adversarial inputs via multi-task consistency?”. For example,
239   should the feature extractors be different? if so, to what extent? Ghamizi et al. (2022) hinted that
240   one should carefully select the auxiliary tasks added to reduce model vulnerability. Indeed, the
241
      addition of auxiliary tasks can have negative effects (e.g., larger model size, slower convergence
      of the common encoder layers, deterioration of clean performance). They raised the (still open)
242
      question of how to select the combination that yields the lowest vulnerability. One could think that
243
      picking the most adversarially robust backbone would be preferable. For example, when investigating
244   ResNet50 and ResNet101 backbones, the only difference is that ResNet101 has 23 conv4_x layers
245   while ResNet50 has 6 (so a total of 51 additional convolution layers as the name indicates). This
246   means that ResNet101 has larger receptive fields than ResNet50. As shown by Xiang et al. (2024),
247   smaller receptive fields impose a bound on the number of features that can be corrupted, hence more
248   adversarially robust. This could justify the use of ResNet50 backbone over ResNet101. However,
249   in our context, we select models that, even if fooled by the attack, yield to inconsistent outputs. So,
250   having two weak models could be acceptable as long as their outputs are inconsistent.

254   In this section, we outline the implementation details of the datasets, models, attack parameters,

259   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
260
261   Datasets. Our evaluation relies on a set of genuine and adversarial datasets based on the BDD100k
262   dataset. The description of the BDD100k dataset can be found in the Appendix( A.2).
263
264   Models. We use 11 existing models from BDD100k model zoo (Huang, 2021) and from mmde-
265
      tection 2.0 framework (Chen et al., 2019): six models for object detection (OD) and five models
      for instance segmentation (SEG). All models are fine-tuned on the BDD100k dataset. Our selection
266
      of models aims to maximize the diversity of models for a given vision task to understand how it
267
      affects the performance of our defense. Indeed, an adversarial attack may transfer from one model to

271   The first one is the type of architecture (e.g., transformer or CNN). A second criteria is the depth of
272   the backbone (ResNet50 versus ResNet101). The last criteria is to ensure a diversity of heads among

275   Attack. We utilized 1,000 clean images from the BDD100k instance segmentation validation

283
284   Evaluation Metrics. To evaluate the prediction performance of the models on both the clean dataset
285   and the eleven adversarial datasets, we utilize the mean Average Precision (mAP), a widely accepted
286   metric for assessing computer vision models. For evaluating our detection scheme, we employ the
287
      receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, a popular metric that illustrates the performance of a
      classification model across all classification thresholds. The area under the curve (AUC) provides a
288
      measure of our adversarial attack detection performance.

      Next, we compare our detector with RobustDet. Our detector functions as a binary classifier,
      determining whether an input is adversarial or not. In contrast, RobustDet, similar to adversarial
463
      training, enhances the model’s robustness. To ensure a fair comparison, we introduce the metric
464
      Detection Rate, which represents the true positive rate for a given adversarial dataset. Specifically,
465   we utilize one of our best model pairs (FRCNN R50, MRCNN R50) for our detector and assess
466   its detection rate on the adversarial dataset for FRCNN R50. For RobustFRCNN, we evaluate its
467   performance by calculating the consistency score between its output and the ground truth annotations
468   under adversarial conditions. A high consistency score indicates that RobustFRCNN successfully
469   mitigates the perturbation, whereas a low score signifies failure. Therefore, the detection rate is the
470   ratio of adversarial inputs with a consistency score above the consistency threshold.
471   As shown in Table 4, our consistency-based detector successfully identifies all adversarial inputs
472   (99.9%) in the adversarial datasets, thanks to the high inconsistency between the outputs of the model
473   pair. In contrast, RobustFRCNN performs poorly (mAP = 6.2), failing to ensure prediction outputs
474   align with the ground truth, resulting in a very low detection rate (19%). On top of being less able to
475   detect adversarial inputs, RobustFRCNN employs a dynamic convolution kernel that is four times
476   the size of a regular convolution kernel, significantly increasing its model size. In comparison, our
477   detector has a combined weight size of only 350MB for OD and SEG. Finally, our detector achieves
478   faster inference speeds on the same hardware due to its less complex architecture. In summary, our
479
      detector demonstrates stronger performance compared to RobustDet.

      5    O PEN C HALLENGES
487
488
489
      Stronger attacker model. Our attacker model targets only one perception task. A stronger attacker
      could target both tasks. Prior work have demonstrated attacks fooling both semantic segmentation
490
      and object detection with some success (Xie et al., 2017). More generally, techniques were designed
491
      to improve the adversarial transferability cross-model or cross-task (Gu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024;
492   Hu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2020). We expect that an attacker that uses these techniques would be
493   able to bypass our multi-task consistency detector. However, we noted that these techniques, despite
494   being able to fool both tasks in silo, do not create cross-task consistent adversarial output (e.g., a fake
495   bounding box in OD does not match the location or size of the fake instance segmentation mask).
496
497   Generalization. In this paper, we investigated object detection and instance segmentation models,
498   finding the best model pairs to use in a multi-task consistency detector. We are interested in
499   generalizing the approach to any combination of tasks. Especially, we would like to understand if the
500
      recommendations (about the model architectures) generalized across tasks.
501
      Tuple multi-task consistency. We propose to extend the detector with more tasks and investigate
502   how the detection rate correlates to the number of tasks. Though, Ghamizi et al. (2022) demonstrated
503   that what matters the most is not the number of tasks or how they correlate, but how much the tasks
504   individually impact the vulnerability of the model. Indeed, the more vulnerable the tasks in the model
505   are, the less likely adding new tasks increases the robustness of the model; and adding a vulnerable
506   task may actually decrease the robustness of the whole model. Thus, a comprehensive analysis is
507   required to answer this challenge.

510   6    R ELATED W ORK
511
512   In recent years, many defenses were created to detect (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liu et al., 2019;
513   Tian et al., 2021; Sperl et al., 2020) or to improve the robustness of vision systems against adversarial
514   perturbations (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Madry ˛ et al., 2018). Especially, a strong emphasis has been put
515   on the security of image classification task. Examples of defenses used in image classification include:
516   use of additional detection networks (Liu et al., 2019), analysis of network output (Hendrycks &
517
      Gimpel, 2016; Tian et al., 2021), or use of certain activation patterns within the hidden layers (Sperl
      et al., 2020). These detection methods focus on the output structure or network topology of an image
518
      classifier and are thereby not transferable to more complex vision tasks.
519
520   As described earlier, multi-task learning (MTL) (Kendall et al., 2018) tackles a wide range of vision
521   tasks in an efficient way. Mao et al. (2020) showed that MTL increases the adversarial robustness due
522   to the increased difficulty of successfully attacking several tasks. Thus, subsequent work explored
523
      other task combinations (Xie et al., 2017; Klingner et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2021),
      or compared the effectiveness of adding different auxiliary tasks (Ghamizi et al., 2022; Gurulingan
524
      et al., 2021; Haleta et al., 2021). While the positive effects of MTL on adversarial robustness are
525
      quite well-explored, we are the first to check the consistency between outputs from object detection
526   and instance segmentation models, deriving recommendations to select best model pairs.

529   7    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      Vision models are paramount to many applications such as autonomous driving. Their robustness
532
      have been shown to be brittle under adversarial setting. From the observation that adversarial inputs
533
      yield different effects when fed to different models, we propose an adversarial perturbation detection
534   method based on multi-task perception. We showed an example of our lightweight defense using
535   instance segmentation and object detection tasks. We generated adversarial BDD100k datasets
536   and demonstrated our consistency score can effectively detect perturbations. Then, we empirically
537   identified the optimal model pairs, demonstrating that even if sharing the same backbone, the attack
538   can be detected because of uncoordinated perturbations on both models. The optimal models pair
539   had a 99.9% detection rate. Future work will focus on joint multi-task perturbations and assess the
      effectiveness of our defense against stronger attacker models.

731

733   pairs, focusing on the number and size of the detected objects. As previously mentioned, the attack
734   exhibits high transferability between models with similar architectures and backbones, but it also
735   leads to significant inconsistencies in the model outputs. For instance, Figure 8 shows the attack
736   transfer between FRCNN R50 and MRCNN R50, but the number and size of hallucinated objects
737   across the categories vary. For model pairs with different baseline architectures or backbones, such
738

739

740
      not fool MASK2FORMER whose object areas are close to ground truth. Although the number of

742   number of objects even on clean inputs, as shown in Figure 7.

744
      This observation also supports the conclusion in Section 4.2.2. The consistency scores for model
      pairs with similar architectures are low because the attack transfers between them, resulting in
745
      distinct impacts, and thus, high inconsistency. For model pairs with different architectures, the
746
      attack does not transfer well, leading to low inconsistency. However, when one model in these
747   pairs performs very poorly even on a clean dataset, it will output many hallucinated objects despite

749   MASK2FORMER SwinT.

       B     OTHER D EFENSES
1783
1784
       To assess the benefits of our multi-task consistency detector, it is important to compare it against other
1785
       known defenses. Defenses usually fall into three categories: data-based, model-based, detection-
1786
       based. Data-based defenses use data augmentation at training to improve adversarial robustness.
1787   Adversarial training is a common data-based defense (Qian et al., 2022). Model-based focuses on
1788   selecting a specific network architecture that provides intrinsic adversarial robustness (Ye et al.,
1789   2019; Guo et al., 2020a; Dong et al., 2022). Finally, detection-based defenses do not require data
1790   augmentation or model changes, but add a processing (on the input or the output of the model) in
1791   order to detect adversarial inputs. PatchCleanser (Xiang et al., 2022) is one example of a double
1792   masking technique used to detect presence of adversarial patches in images.
1793
1794
       B.1     A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
1795
1796   The concept of adversarial training (AT) is to train a model on a dataset containing both genuine
1797   and adversarial examples in order to build resilience against perturbations. Previous work such as
1798   MTD (Zhang & Wang, 2019) and Class-Wise Adversarial Training (CWAT) (Chen et al., 2021)
1799   defined loss functions to train the model to accurately localize and classify objects in an image despite
1800   the presence of adversarial noise. Unfortunately, all AT schemes demonstrated a drop in model
1801   accuracy, which is not desirable.
1802
1803   B.2     M ODEL - BASED D EFENSE : ROBUST N ETWORK
1804
1805   B.2.1    A DVERSARIALLY-AWARE ROBUST O BJECT D ETECTOR (ROBUST D ET )
1806
1807   Dong et al. (2022) proposed a counter-proposal to adversarial training by modifying the model
1808   architecture. The proposal, named RobustDet, aims to modify an existing backbone (e.g., SSD)
1809   by adding three security components: an adversarial image discriminator (AID), an "adversarially-
1810
       aware convolution" (AAconv), and a consistent features with reconstruction (CFR). The AID is a
       discriminator that outputs a probability vector based on the category of the image. For instance, if the
1811
       AID discriminates the image as genuine, then the AID will output the probability vector for a genuine
1812
       image. Otherwise, if the image is adversarial, then the AID will output the probability vector for an
1813   adversarial image. For the training phase, the author formulated a dedicated loss function for the AID
1814   to generate a probability vector specific to the category of the image (genuine or adversarial). This
1815   probability vector will serve as an input for the next module: AAconv. Unlike in adversarial training,
1816   AAconv aims to use specific weights for the model based on the category of the image. To achieve
1817   this goal, AAconv uses the concept of dynamic convolution to generate different convolution kernels
1818   based on the category of the image. The generation of those convolution kernels is possible thanks to
1819   the (genuine or adversarial) probability vector provided by the AID. The probability vector serves
1820   as the weights to generate convolution kernels. This approach allows to have dedicated weights for
1821
       genuine images and adversarial images instead of having a single set of weights for both categories
       of images (like in AT). Lastly, the CFR reconstructs the adversarial image into a clean image.
1822
1823   Looking at their mAP evaluation, RobustDet has higher mAP scores than adversarial training methods
1824   such as MTD and CWAT on both genuine and adversarial datasets. However, RobustDet still has at
1825   best a 20 mAP score difference between the genuine dataset and the adversarial dataset. This issue
1826   means RobustDet do not completely mitigate the mAP loss caused by adversarial examples.
1827
1828   B.2.2    A PPLICATION OF ROBUST D ET ON FASTER RCNN
1829
1830   Dong et al. (2022) evaluated RobustDet on the object detection model SSD with a VGG16 backbone,
1831   which was trained on the COCO dataset. However, in our paper, the models evaluated are trained on
1832
       BDD100k dataset and we do not use SSD. To fairly compare the performance of our consistency-
       based detector with RobustDet, we applied the AAconv technique to one of the models, namely Faster
1833
       RCNN with RestNet50 backbone (FRCNN R50 in short).
1834
1835   As previously explained, the core idea of AAconv is to replace any regular convolution kernel in a
       model network with a weighted sum of a set of dynamic convolution kernels, expressed as:
