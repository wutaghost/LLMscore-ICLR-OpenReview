                                                       A BSTRACT
009
               This paper proposes a control-based framework for aligning large language models (LLMs)
010
               by leveraging a control barrier function (CBF) to ensure user-desirable text generation. The
011
               presented framework applies the CBF safety ﬁlter to the predicted token generated from the
012            baseline LLM, to intervene in the generated text. The safety ﬁlter includes two signiﬁcant
013            advantages: this safety ﬁlter is an add-on type, allowing it to be used for alignment purposes
014            without ﬁne-tuning the baseline LLM, and if there is an evaluation model regarding the desired
015            alignment, it can be directly applied to the ﬁlter design. The overall text-generation system
016            is implemented with Llama 3 and a BERT model, aiming to generate positive text. Finally,
017            further applications and limitations of the CBF-LLM for other alignment tasks, including topic-
018            keeping and hallucination mitigating, are discussed.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   While large language models (LLMs) are known to have strong language understanding and generation abilities,
024
      they can also generate harmful, biased, or toxic content (Shen et al., 2023; Minaee et al., 2024). Alignment of
      LLMs ensures that they generate content that is “desirable” for the user, typically meaning content that is safe
025
      and ethical. Various approaches for LLM alignment have been presented (see the works by Shen et al. (2023);
026
      Minaee et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023) and reference therein).
027
028   The major approach to the alignment is reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF, Ouyang et al.
029   (2022)), where a reward model is constructed by human feedback and used for the training of LLMs. Variants of
030
      RLHF architectures are also proposed, such as Safe-RLHF by Dai et al. (2024), SENSEI by Liu et al. (2022), and
      f-DPG by Go et al. (2023), and their implementations are presented, such as training pre-trained LLMs (Bai et al.,
031
      2022a; Zhou et al., 2023), and applications like information-seeking chatbot (Glaese et al., 2022). Collecting
032
      human feedback with data is time-consuming and expensive. To overcome the drawback, RL from AI Feedback
033   (RLAIF) is presented instead of using human labels (Bai et al., 2022b). In addition, the method to construct the
034   training data automatically is proposed (Kim et al., 2023). Furthermore, to reduce the computational cost, direct
035   preference optimization (DPO) is proposed (Rafailov et al., 2023), where the training data is directly used for
036   training LLMs without accessing the reward model. A common feature of alignment methods like RLHF and
037   SFT is that they modify LLMs’ model parameters.
038
      An alternative approach for LLM alignment is to directly intervene in the input prompt or output of LLMs,
039   rather than modifying the model parameters. In-context learning (ICL, Dong et al. (2024)) is a major approach
040   for intervening in the input prompt. In ICL, a few demonstrations are provided in prompt to instruct the LLMs
041   on the task, including few-shot learning (Brown et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024). As the methods for intervening
042   in the output, the work by Cao et al. (2021) proposes a method to format output for retrieval application, and
043   the work by Keskar et al. (2019) proposes a repetition penalty to prevent LLMs from repeating the same words
044   and expressions. In addition, the Transformers module provides some functions to modify the output, such as
045   NoBadWordsLogitsProcessor and MinLengthLogitsProcessor (HuggingFace).
046   One can view the intervention approach to alignment, which avoids undesirable output, as analogous to “col-
047   lision avoidance”, the most fundamental problem in control engineering. In control engineering, various stud-
048   ies are conducted for safety assurance, including collision avoidance (Isaly et al., 2024; Dawson et al., 2023;
049   Nishimura & Hoshino, 2024). A promising approach for collision avoidance is the control barrier function
050   (CBF), as studied in theoretical works by Ames et al. (2019); Taylor & Ames (2020); Lopez et al. (2021) and
051   in real-world applications by Hu et al. (2023). Just as a vehicle’s trajectory is intervened to avoid collisions,
052   LLM’s output can be intervened to prevent undesirable content. This paper draws an analogy between vehicle
      and LLM, as illustrated in 1.

      This paper proposes a framework for control-based LLM alignment by applying a safety ﬁlter that intervenes in
064
      the LLM output to generate user-desirable outcomes. To this end, we leverage the idea of the CBF to improve the
065   safety and controllability of the outputs of LLMs. We aim to design a CBF-based safety ﬁlter that intervenes in
066   the output of LLMs into the user’s desired content. The CBF ﬁlter and the baseline LLM constitute a novel text
067   generation system, which we call “CBF-LLM”. This paper also conducts the text-generation experiment on CBF-
068   LLM. In the experiment, the CBF control enabled the alignment task to be completed with fewer interventions.
069
      CBF-LLM is one of the controlled decoding alignment methods. Controlled decoding alignment has been ad-
070
      dressed in the literature, including works such as Mudgal et al. (2024), Zingale & Kalita (2024), Safedecoding
071   (Xu et al., 2024), FUDGE (Yang & Klein, 2021), and DeAL (Huang et al., 2024). The common feature is to in-
072   tervene in the token probability based on a reward of generated text. On the other hand, our CBF-LLM intervenes
073   in the token probability based on the change in reward caused by adding a token, rather than just the generated
074   text. Moreover, CBF-LLM is distinct from previous research in that CBF-LLM considers the constraint of
075   preventing undesirable text from being generated. This paper also attempts to bridge the gap between control
076   engineering and natural language processing. While the theoretical analysis of LLMs, such as reachability, has
077   been studied in the works by Bhargava et al. (2024) and Soatto et al. (2023), this paper presents a design method
078   of LLM-based text-generation systems.
079   The other contributions of this paper are as follows: 1. The proposed CBF-LLM is realized in an add-on manner
080   to a baseline LLM: an external ﬁlter is simply added to the LLMs without accessing their model parameters.
081   In this sense, CBF-LLM is broadly applicable to various LLMs, as is designed independently of the underlying
082   LLM. 2. CBF-LLM is implemented with Llama 3 and a RoBERTa model in Section 4. The result shows that
083   CBF-LLM is more reliable compared with the previous approaches, in the sense of output alignment.
084   “Safe Control” addressed in this paper reﬂects a broader interpretation of “safety”. We mean safe control as
085   the ability to regulate LLM output to produce text that not only adheres to ethical standards but also aligns with
086   user-speciﬁc objectives.
087
      The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, the basic theory of CBF is provided, and the structure
088
      of nominal LLM is reviewed. In Section 3, the concept and design of CBF-LLM are proposed. In Section 4, the
089   experiment of CBF-LLM is conducted. Finally, in Section 5, the conclusion of this paper is presented.

      2.1   C ONTROL BARRIER F UNCTION FOR S AFE C ONTROL
096
097   Control barrier function (CBF), developed in the control community, provides safety assurance in control systems
098   (Ames et al., 2019). This section brieﬂy reviews CBF and CBF-based safety control.
099
      Consider the following dynamical system to be controlled:
100
101                                                        ẋ = g(x, u),                                                                            (1)
102
103   where x ∈ Rn is the state variable of the object being controlled, and u ∈ Rm is the action applied to the object.
104   The function g represents the system dynamics; how this object is affected by the current state x and action u. A
105   typical example of the system (1) includes a vehicle dynamics, where the state x is coordinate, velocity, angle,
      etc, and the action u is accelerator pedal depression, steering angle, etc.


                                                           2
106
      We aim to design the assisted control system with safety assurance. As for safety, we let the safe and unsafe sets
107   be denoted by S ⊆ Rn , S̄ ⊆ Rn , respectively. Then, the safety means to constrain x within the safe set S, i.e.,
108   x ∈ S. Consider that the nominal action unom ∈ Rm is provided which might violate the safety, i.e., the driver
109   unom might generate the unsafe state x ∈ S̄ on a car. Then, we address the problem of designing “safety ﬁlter”
110   F : Rm → Rm , as follows:
111   Problem 1 (Safety Filter). Find the safety ﬁlter F : Rm → Rm such that the system (1) with u = F (unom )
112   generates x(t) ∈ S for all nominal actions unom for all time t.
113
114   As a preliminary, we design a continuously differentiable function h : Rn → R, called a “constraint function”,
115   such that h(x) ≥ 0 if x ∈ S, and h(x) < 0 if x ∈ S̄ hold. The safety is equivalent to the constraint: h(x) ≥ 0.
116   In the assisted driving example, the nominal action unom is a manual action by the driver, and the unsafe set S̄
117
      includes the positions of obstacles like pedestrians. The function h can be the distance between the vehicle and
      the obstacle. With the manual action by the driver unom , the vehicle may enter the unsafe set, such as colliding
118
      with obstacles. The safety ﬁlter F needs to modify unom to output u such that h(x) ≥ 0 holds, meaning that the
119
      vehicle never collides with obstacles.
120
121   To construct the safety ﬁlter F that keeps the safety constraint h(x) ≥ 0, the control barrier function ﬁlter (CBF
122   ﬁlter, Gurriet et al. (2018); Ames et al. (2019)) is presented. The CBF ﬁlter intervenes in the nominal action
123
      value unom to introduce a safe state of the object by ﬁnding u as follows:
124                                               min (unom − u)2 ,                                                  (2)
                                                    u

                                                                                                         dx > 0. In

129   CBF ﬁlter intervenes in unom to provide safe driving. To constrain the action u by the CBF ﬁlter, the following

      The alignment discussed in this paper aims to ensure desirable text generation by weak intervention to the output
186   of LLMs. To clarify the meaning of “desirable”, we let the desirable and undesirable text sets be S ⊆ X and
187   S̄ ⊆ X , respectively, based on the respective alignment goals.
188   Example 1. Suppose that the alignment goal is set to generate non-toxic content. Then, S is the set of non-
189   toxic text, and S̄ is the set of toxic texts. For mathematical procedures, we consider S and S̄ to represent “all”
190   non-toxic and toxic text samples. More examples of S and S̄ are seen in Section 4.
191
192   One simple idea of achieving the alignment goal is to force the text generation to stop when the generated text x
193   turns undesirable, i.e., x ∈ S̄. However, this method involves a strong intervention in the baseline LLM, which
194
      renders the original capabilities of the baseline LLM meaningless. To overcome the drawback, we make the
      intervention strength adjustable, which enables the text-generation system to achieve the alignment goal with a
195
      weak intervention.

      Recall that this paper aims to ensure positive text generation by weak intervention to the output of LLMs. Align-
372   ing LLMs has a tradeoff between two indicators: task quality and intervention weakness. The task quality
373   indicates how the generated text is aligned with the speciﬁed objective. To measure the task quality, we used G-
374   Eval (Liu et al., 2023), a framework for evaluating any texts by LLMs, to assess the positiveness of the generated
375   texts. The intervention weakness indicates how the CBF-LLM text-generation system is close to the baseline
376   LLM. We use the number of disallowed tokens per generation, naturalness, and generation time per token. The
377   average naturalness of texts generated by each ﬁlter is evaluated by G-Eval.
378   The results are shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. 6. In Fig. 5, the black points show the number of disallowed tokens
379   per generation, and the red bars show the generation time per token. The horizontal axis is the α value and
380   recall that CBF-LLM with α = 1 equals the Blocklist. We can see that as the number of disallowed tokens
381   increases, generation time tends to increase. The generation time with CBF(α = 0.8) is less than that with
382   Blocklist, and the numbers of disallowed tokens with CBF(α = 0.4) and CBF(α = 0.8) are less than that with
383   Blocklist. In Fig. 6, the black points show the average naturalness scores and the red bars show the average
384   positiveness scores. The naturalness score was the highest at α = 0.6, and the positiveness score was the highest
385   at α = 0.2. These results reveal a trade-off between task quality, displayed by positiveness, and intervention
386
      weakness, displayed by disallowed tokens and naturalness, and the trade-off is calibrated by the hyperparameter
      α. Generation time is also affected by α, and it can be shorter than that with Blocklist in some α values. Based
387
      on the results on naturalness, positiveness, the number of disallowed tokens, and the generation time, we see
388
      that the hyperparameter α should be chosen from within (0, 1) rather than choosing 1, which is equivalent to the

      4.4   F URTHER A PPLICATIONS AND L IMITATIONS OF CBF-LLM
433
434   To explore further applications and limitations of CBF-LLM, we conducted an experiment focusing on mitigating
435   hallucinations generated by the baseline LLM. According to the works by Kadavath et al. (2022); Farquhar et al.
436   (2024), the high entropy of token probabilities indicates the uncertainty of the output text, implying that it may
437   be used as the detector of incorrect generation, while it is not fully sophisticated.
438   We set the control goal to reduce hallucination. To this end, we construct the L-CF as follows:
439
440
                                                                          ∑
                                                                          N
                                    h(x) = 2.5 − H(P ),       H(P ) = −         P [t] ln P [t],                      (14)
441
                                                                          t=1
442
      where P is the output of token predictor G in CBF-LLM, and H denotes the entropy. This L-CF shows positive
443
      when the entropy of P is less than 2.5. In this sense, this ﬁlter chooses tokens that produce lower entropy of the
444   next token probabilities during generations, aiming to avoid hallucinations. Note that the model used in L-CF
445   and the token predictor G are the same in this experiment.
446
447
      In this experiment, we employ Llama 3 8b Instruct, which is ﬁne-tuned to respond to user instructions
      (Dubey et al., 2024), as the model for the token predictor G. We conduct the text-generation experiment by
448
      CBF-LLM with the input prompt as “Write a unique ﬁzz buzz python in a single row. Just output the code.” and
449
      all other settings remain the same as in the previous experiment, in Subsection 4.2.
450
451   We evaluate the output text by determining the generated code by following three labels: [OK]Success: the
452   code has no syntax errors and outputs ﬁzz buzz properly; [TE]Task Error: the code has no syntax errors but
453
      does not output ﬁzz buzz properly; [SE]Syntax Error: The code has syntax errors and is non-executable. We
      hypothesize that as the hallucination is suppressed the number of errors ([SE]and [TE]) decreases and the
454
      number of success ([OK]) increases.
455
456   The result shows that the CBF ﬁlter does not signiﬁcantly reduce syntax error ([SE]) rates or improve the success
457   ([OK]) rates. The generated codes and their evaluations are listed in Appendix G.3. It should be emphasized
458   that in CBF-LLM, task accuracy heavily relies on the design of L-CF h(x). In particular, as in the case of this
459
      experiment, where the formulation of L-CF is based on underlying evaluation methods, the impact on the task
      accuracy is even greater. Although the CBF-LLM designed in this experiment requires further updates, we ﬁnd
460
      that it is broadly applicable to formulate a wide range of control objects of LLM.

      4.5   E XTENSION TO M ULTI -S TEP A HEAD CBF-LLM
463
464   The CBF ﬁlter, formulated in (9), compares the current text x(k) with the text after adding only one token
465   concat(x(k), ti ) to output the modiﬁed probabilities Q as illustrated in Fig. 2. The ﬁlter prevents the text from
466   becoming undesirable for all time. A drawback of CBF-LLM is that it may ﬁlter out text that appears undesirable
467   initially but becomes desirable when read to the last, e.g., “You are clumsy, but you have high aspirations!”.
468   To overcome the drawback of the CBF ﬁlter with one-step ahead token prediction, we extend the ﬁlter with
469   multi-step ahead token prediction. Let H ∈ {1, 2, . . .} denote “prediction horizon”, and further let y denote the
470   sequence composed of H tokens, i.e.,y = [t1 , t2 , . . . , tH ]. At each time, the method collects K ∈ {1, 2, . . .}
471
      candidates of H-token sequences y1 , y2 , . . . , yK generated from the baseline LLM that continues from x(k) such
      that the CBF inequality holds, i.e., (4) with h(k) = h(x(k)) and h(k + 1) = h(concat(x(k), y)). The next
472
      H-token sequence is selected from these candidates according to the probability distribution P (y|x(k)) derived
473
      by the baseline LLM.
474
475   The controlled decoding with multi-step ahead prediction is also presented as the Blockwise best-of-K method
476   in the work (Mudgal et al., 2024). The method selects the best of K candidates of H-token sequences generated
      from the baseline LLM without imposing any constraints.

500   5   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
501
502   This paper proposed the control-based LLM alignment framework, called CBF-LLM. This framework utilizes
503   the control barrier function (CBF), commonly used in control engineering to ensure the safety of physical objects,
504   such as the collision avoidance function in assisted driving vehicles. Based on an analogy between the control
505   theory and the LLM alignment task, we employed the CBF-based safety ﬁlter to ensure that the text-generation
506
      system generates desirable content. The key feature of CBF-LLM is that the CBF ﬁlter can be attached to the
      baseline LLM in an add-on manner: it intervenes in the output of the baseline LLM without any additional train-
507
      ing of LLMs. This paper also presented the implementation of CBF-LLM by Llama 3 and a sentiment analysis
508
      RoBERTa model to ensure that the text-generation system generates positive content. The text-generation exper-
509   iment showed that CBF-LLM outperforms the baseline method in terms of naturalness, positiveness, generation
510   time, and the reliability of controlled decoding.
511
512
      In CBF-LLM, the key challenge is to effectively incorporate human feedback and existing evaluation models to
      reﬂect human preferences into the L-CF. The design of L-CF is similarly challenging to construct a high-quality
513
      reward model in RLHF approaches. The value of CBF ﬁlters lies in their ability to facilitate easy modiﬁcations.
514
      To illustrate this, we show two scenarios: In a scenario, consider that an aligned LLM is developed and integrated
515   into a service system. Suppose that an ethical or other critical issue is discovered with the original data used
516   for alignment. Then, it becomes challenging to remove the inﬂuence of the data from the LLM using RLHF-
517   based methods, such as unlearning (Isonuma & Titov, 2024). This can lead to the suspension of the service
518   system. In contrast, in CBF-LLM, an add-on type alignment method, we can simply disable the CBF ﬁlter
519   to maintain the service operation while modifying the speciﬁcations. In the other scenario, consider an LLM
520   initially trained or controlled to produce positive text. Later, suppose that an additional requirement is added
521   such as ensuring that the generated text is easy for children to comprehend. In CBF-LLM, we can independently
522   design a readability CBF ﬁlter without modifying the existing positivity CBF ﬁlter, allowing the system to meet
523
      the updated requirements without having to retrain the entire LLM. This approach enables us to easily adapt
      to changing speciﬁcations and requirements. In these scenarios, the CBF-LLM approach offers a signiﬁcant
524
      advantage.

735   B    V EHICLE C ONTROL AND LLM C ONTROL
736
737   An analogy between vehicle collision avoidance and intervention-based LLM alignment can be drawn as illus-
738   trated in Fig. 1 in Section 1. Consider the LLM as an analogy to a vehicle, and the generated text as an analogy to
739   the vehicle’s trajectory. Both vehicle collision avoidance and LLM alignment aim to guide the complex system
740   away from undesirable states by designing proper control strategies. The goal of vehicle collision avoidance is
741   to prevent collisions with obstacles by intervening in the vehicle’s trajectory. For example, if there are obsta-
      cles ahead of the vehicle, it is necessary to operate the steering or use the brakes to avoid colliding with them.


                                                       14
742
      Similarly, LLM alignment aims to prevent undesirable outputs, such as harmful content. To this end, CBF-LLM
743   intervenes in the token probabilities at each step during the generation to generate the desirable “trajectory” of
744   the token sequence.
745
746
      However, there is a key difference between the two systems: while vehicle collision avoidance involves direct
      access to physical quantities such as steering angle and brake pedal position, intervention-based LLM alignment
747
      involves access to the probability distribution of generated tokens. This difference will be taken into account in
748
      the development of our control strategy.
