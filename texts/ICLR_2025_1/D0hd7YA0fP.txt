011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is essential for enhancing the robustness and
014            security of deep learning models in unknown and dynamic data environments.
015            Gradient-based OOD detection methods, such as GAIA, analyse the explanation
016            pattern representations of in-distribution (ID) and OOD samples by examining
017            the sensitivity of model outputs w.r.t. model inputs, resulting in superior per-
018            formance compared to traditional OOD detection methods. However, we argue
019            that the non-zero gradient behaviors of OOD samples do not exhibit significant
020
               distinguishability, especially when ID samples are perturbed by random noise in
               high-dimensional spaces, which negatively impacts the accuracy of OOD detec-
021
               tion. In this paper, we propose a novel OOD detection method called S & I based
022
               on layer Splitting and gradient Integration via Adversarial Gradient Attribution.
023            Specifically, our approach involves splitting the model’s intermediate layers and
024            iteratively updating adversarial examples layer-by-layer. We then integrate the at-
025            tribution gradients from each intermediate layer along the attribution path from
026            adversarial examples to the actual input, yielding true explanation pattern repre-
027            sentations for both ID and OOD samples. Experiments demonstrate that our S &
028            I algorithm achieves state-of-the-art results, with the average FPR95 of 29.05%
029            (38.61%) and 37.31% on the CIFAR100 and ImageNet benchmarks, respectively.
030            Our code is available at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/S-I-F6F7/

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Deep neural networks have achieved remarkable success in a variety of domains, including au-
037   tonomous driving (Chen et al., 2021) and medical diagnosis (Yadav & Jadhav, 2019). However,
038   their performance and reliability are strongly influenced by the assumption that the test data orig-
039
      inates from the same distribution as the training data. In practical applications, this assumption is
      frequently violated, as models often face inputs that deviate significantly from the in-distribution
040
      (ID) training data. Such inputs, known as out-of-distribution (OOD) samples, present a major chal-
041
      lenge for deep neural networks, which can produce overconfident yet incorrect predictions.
042
043   Therefore, performing OOD detection is essential for ensuring the safe and reliable deployment of
044   deep neural networks in real-world applications. Currently post-hoc OOD detection methods can
045
      be mainly divided into three categories: output-based methods (Hsu et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020;
      Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liang et al., 2017), feature representation-based methods (Sun et al.,
046
      2021; Sastry & Oore, 2020; Song et al., 2022) and gradient-based methods (Huang et al., 2021;
047
      Lee & AlRegib, 2020; Igoe et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Among them, output-based methods
048   rely on the confidence score of the model output to determine whether the input sample belongs to
049   the training data distribution, while feature representation-based methods detect OOD samples by
050   analyzing the feature vectors of the intermediate layers of the neural network. However, compared
051   with gradient-based methods that identify OOD samples by calculating the gradient information of
052   input samples w.r.t. model parameters (or a certain layer output), they are easily deceived by some
053   OOD samples with high output similarity or easily affected by the quality of feature representation.
      Therefore, in this paper we focus on gradient-based methods.

067   gates the explanation pattern representations of ID and OOD samples from the sensitivity of model
068   outputs w.r.t model inputs, i.e., the attribution gradients (Simonyan, 2013). Specifically, by back-
069   propagating the attribution gradient ∂f∂x  (x;θ)
                                                       of the model output f (x; θ) w.r.t. the input sample x
070   on each intermediate layer, GAIA considers input samples with a large number of non-zero attribu-
071   tion gradients as OOD samples. As shown in Fig. 1, we find that for OOD samples, the attribution
072   map often does not focus on certain key features and shows a scattered pattern, which means that
073   the model has no clear understanding of OOD samples. Therefore, we argue that this phenomenon
074   indicates that the model may have higher sensitivity (i.e., larger gradient value) to any feature under
075   an unseen distribution, and even some irrelevant details will get high gradient values. This char-
076   acteristic makes the non-zero gradient behavior of OOD samples not significantly differentiating,
077
      especially when ID samples are subject to random noise in high-dimensional space. At this time,
      the gradient fluctuation caused by small input changes of the model will make it difficult for the
078
      gradient sensitivity to stably reflect the actual relationship between the model output and the input,
079
      affecting the distinction between the explanation pattern representations of ID and OOD samples.

111   Besides, it is worth emphasizing that traditional gradient-based methods such as GAIA assume that

      the influence of each intermediate layer of the model on the input features is uniform and linearly cu-

114
      focusing on high-level semantic features. In deeper neural networks, this may introduce unstable
115   gradient explosions or cumulative errors in inter-layer features, reducing the representation accu-
116   racy of the explained pattern. To address this problem, we introduce the concept of layer splitting
117   for the first time. Assuming that the neural network has a total of l intermediate layers, we split the
118   current j-th intermediate layer from the subsequent (j + 1 ∼ l)-th intermediate layer while updating
119   the adversarial example layer by layer. Based on these insights, we propose a novel OOD detection
120   method called S & I based on layer Splitting and gradient Integration via Adversarial Gradient At-
121   tribution. Comprehensive experiments on both CIFAR100 benchmark and large-scale ImageNet-1K
122   benchmark validate the effectiveness of our S & I algorithm. Fig. 2 shows the algorithm flowchart.
123   Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
124
125          • Given the observation that the attribution gradients of OOD samples are not significantly
126            distinguishable, in order to reduce the abnormal gradient fluctuations caused by random
127            noise in ID samples in high-dimensional space, we first introduce adversarial examples to
128

               ture explanation pattern representations.

130          • We, for the first time, propose the concept of layer splitting and adversarial attribution gra-
131            dient integration for OOD detection. By decomposing intermediate layers and iteratively
132

133

136
               FPR95 of 29.05% (38.61%) and 37.31% on the CIFAR100 and ImageNet benchmarks,
               respectively. We have also open-sourced the relevant code.

142
143
      Given a deep neural network f with parameters θ, for a supervised task, the output of the network
      for the input sample space X can be expressed as f (X; θ; Y ). Here Y represents the label space,
144
      and in the following we omit Y for convenience. The goal of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection is
145
      to identify input data that comes from a distribution different from the training data. Let xin ∈ X
146   represents the in-distribution (ID) samples, and xout ∈ X represents the OOD samples. Typically,
147   there is no intersection between the label sets yin ∈ Y and yout ∈ Y for ID and OOD samples.
148   Taking an image classification task as an example, since the model f has never seen OOD data
149   xout during training, it tends to produce overconfident predictions for such inputs. Based on this
150   characteristic, OOD detection can be formulated as a binary classification problem as follows:
151
152

                                                            OOD , if     Ω(X) ≥ ξ
153                           Binary Classif ier =                                                          (1)
                                                            ID  , if     Ω(X) < ξ
154
155   Here ξ represents the threshold for distinguishing OOD and ID samples, and Ω(X) is the confidence
156   score function for the binary classification. We consider input samples x with confidence scores
157   greater than or equal to ξ as OOD samples xout .

      2.3   D EFINITION OF ADVERSARIAL ATTACKS
209
210   Given a deep neural network f and an original input sample x ∈ RR×S×K , for a standard image
211   classification task, where the true label corresponding to x is t ∈ yin , the objective of adversarial
212   attacks is to generate an adversarial example xadv by adding perturbations to x. These perturbations
213   are designed to mislead the model into making incorrect predictions while maintaining the semantic
214   similarity to the original input. In this scenario, the label of the adversarial example is manipulated
215   to be t′ . It is important to note that, according to the characteristic of adversarial attacks, the label
      t′ is manipulated by the model during training, and therefore, t′ still belongs to the ID label set yin .

501   5     R ELATED WORK
502
503
504   In this paper, we focus on post-hoc OOD detection methods as they can perform OOD detection after
505   the model is deployed without retraining the model or accessing the original training data. Among
506
      them, output-based methods rely on the confidence score of the model output to determine whether
      the input sample belongs to the training data distribution, which is common in OOD detection based
507
      on the maximum softmax probability (MSP) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016). Liang et al. proposed
508
      the ODIN algorithm, which utilizes temperature scaling and random perturbations to differentiate
509   the softmax score distributions of ID and OOD samples (Liang et al., 2017). In order to explore the
510   applicability of ODIN in different scenarios, Hus et al. proposed a confidence score decomposition
511   approach and an improved input preprocessing approach based on the existing ODIN algorithm (Hsu
512   et al., 2020). Liu et al. proposed a unified OOD detection framework based on energy scores to re-
513   place the traditional softmax score, thereby reducing the effect of overconfident output for softmax
514   scores when inputting OOD samples (Liu et al., 2020). Considering the problem that output-based
515   methods have poor discrimination effect in high-dimensional feature space, feature representation-
516   based methods detect OOD samples by capturing structural information in feature space. Sun et al.
517
      proposed the ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) algorithm based on the analysis of the internal activation pat-
      tern of the model to reduce the overconfidence of neural networks on OOD samples. By removing
518
      the rank-1 matrix consisting of the largest singular value and its corresponding singular vector in the
519
      feature matrix, Song et al. proposed the Rankfeat (Song et al., 2022) algorithm for OOD detection.
520   Gradient-based methods are dedicated to analyzing the gradient information of input samples rela-
521   tive to model parameters (or output of a certain layer) (Huang et al., 2021; Lee & AlRegib, 2020;
522   Igoe et al., 2022). Chen et al. proposed the state-of-the-art GAIA (Chen et al., 2023) algorithm to
523   investigate the different representations of attribution gradients (Simonyan, 2013) on ID and OOD
524   samples for the first time. We further explore the true explanatory pattern representations by layer
525   splitting and adversarial attribution gradient integration to enhance the accuracy of OOD detection.
526
527
528
529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we contend that non-zero gradient behaviors of OOD samples lack sufficient differ-
532
      entiation, particularly when ID samples are perturbed by random noise in high-dimensional spaces,
533
      which hampers the accuracy of OOD detection. To tackle this issue, we propose the S & I algo-
534   rithm. Specifically, we first split the model’s intermediate layers and iteratively update adversarial
535   examples layer-by-layer. The attribution gradients of each intermediate layer along the attribution
536   path from adversarial examples to the actual input are integrated to obtain true explanation pattern
537   representations for ID and OOD samples. Experimental results demonstrate that our S & I algorithm
538   achieves superior performance compared to SOTA post-hoc OOD detection methods. The results
539   highlight the effectiveness of S & I algorithm in enhancing the robustness of OOD detection method
      in dynamic data environments, paving the way for more secure applications in real-world scenarios.
