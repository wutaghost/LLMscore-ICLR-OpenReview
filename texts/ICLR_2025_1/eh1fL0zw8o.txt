009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Predicting the types and affinities of protein-protein interactions (PPIs) is cru-
012            cial for understanding biological processes and developing novel therapeutic ap-
013            proaches. While encoding proteins themselves is essential, PPI networks can also
014            provide rich prior knowledge for these predictive tasks. However, existing meth-
015            ods oversimplify the problem of PPI prediction in a semi-supervised manner when
016            utilizing PPI networks, limiting their practical application. Furthermore, how to
017            effectively use the rich prior knowledge of PPI networks for novel proteins not
018
               present in the network remains an unexplored issue. Additionally, due to in-
               flexible architectures, existing methods cannot handle complexes containing an
019
               flexible number of proteins. To overcome these limitations, we introduce LLaPA
020
               (Large Language and Protein Assistant), a multimodal large language model that
021            integrates proteins and PPI networks. LLaPA offers a more rational approach
022            to utilizing PPI networks for PPI prediction and can fully exploit the informa-
023            tion of PPI networks for unseen proteins. Through natural language instructions,
024            LLaPA can accept flexible number of protein sequences and has the potential to
025            perform various protein tasks. Experiments show that LLaPA achieves state-of-
026            the-art performance in multi-label PPI type prediction and is capable of predicting
027            the binding affinity between multiple interacting proteins based on sequence data.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Protein-protein interactions (PPIs) are fundamental to biological processes and critical in drug dis-
033
      covery (Wells & McClendon, 2007; Braun & Gingras, 2012). Traditional high-throughput screening
      methods, such as yeast two-hybrid screens (Ito et al., 2001) and tandem affinity purification (Gavin
034
      et al., 2002), are both expensive and time-consuming. Recently, advancements in deep learning have
035
      led to numerous approaches for predicting PPIs. These approaches can be divided into those that
036   utilize PPI networks and those that do not. Methods that do not use PPI networks include DPPI
037   (Hashemifar et al., 2018), DNN-PPI (Li et al., 2018), PIPR (Chen et al., 2019), TAGPPI (Song et al.,
038   2022), and Geo-PPI (Liu et al., 2021). These methods encode proteins individually and then concate-
039   nate the features of two proteins for downstream tasks. Specifically, DPPI and DNN-PPI perform
040   binary classification to determine protein binding, while PIPR and TAGPPI also predict multi-label
041   PPI types (mPPI). Geo-PPI focuses on changes in protein binding affinity due to mutations.
042   Methods based on PPI networks encode not only proteins but also the PPI network. In a PPI net-
043   work, nodes represent proteins, and edges, often multi-labeled, indicate relationships between them.
044   PPI networks are essential for predicting PPIs, as protein interactions depend on both individual fea-
045   tures and their positions within the larger network (Lee, 2023). GNN-PPI (Lv et al., 2021) was the
046   pioneering method leveraging PPI networks, achieving significant improvements in the mPPI task.
047   Subsequent methods, such as SemiGNN-PPI (Zhao et al., 2023), HIGH-PPI (Gao et al., 2023b),
048   and MAPE-PPI (Wu et al., 2024b), built upon GNN-PPI’s settings and demonstrated even better
049   performance in mPPI prediction.
050   Despite significant advancements, current these methods face three critical limitations: (1) Over-
051   simplified mPPI Task Setting: Existing methods utilize connection information between unseen
052   proteins in a semi-supervised manner (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Lv et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023b;
053   Zhao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b), which oversimplifies task difficulty. Current benchmarks sep-
      arate a portion of the PPI network data as the test set, and the topological information of the test


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      set is also input into the model. This approach explicitly informs the model of relationships be-
055   tween protein pairs being tested, facilitating information exchange and simplifying PPI prediction.
056   Unlike readily available protein sequences, acquiring connection information between proteins of-
057   ten requires extensive biological experiments and analysis, making this approach impractical for
058   real-world applications. (2) Limitations in Handling Unseen Proteins:(2) Ineffectiveness of PPI
059   Network Information for Unseen Proteins: In real-world scenarios, we frequently encounter unseen
060   proteins that do not exist in any PPI network. Existing methods fail to effectively utilize PPI net-
061   work information in such cases, as the model cannot extract useful information from the network
062   topology, thereby affecting prediction accuracy and practicality. (3) Limitations in Multi-Protein
063
      Interactions: Current These models, with their fixed architectures, can only handle interactions
      between two proteins and cannot predict relationships involving multiple proteins or the affinity of
064
      multi-protein complexes. Many biological processes depend on multi-protein complexes, such as
065
      antigen-antibody complexes, which typically consist of three chains: the antigen, the heavy chain
066   of the antibody, and the light chain of the antibody (Wu et al., 2024a). The challenge lies in the
067   unknown number of proteins, requiring models to be flexible enough to accept an arbitrary number
068   of proteins as input. Existing These methods struggle with such complex multi-protein interactions,
069   limiting their applicability in practical biological research.
070
      Recently, some studies have achieved notable performance in protein encoding and understanding
071
      through joint learning of proteins and natural language, such as ProtLLM (Zhuo et al., 2024), Pro-
072   LLama (Lv et al., 2024), Prot2Text (Abdine et al., 2024), and ProteinGPT (Xiao et al., 2024). Pre-
073   trained on large-scale protein databases, these methods exhibit strong generalization capabilities.
074   The flexibility of LLMs enables them to handle tasks involving multiple protein sequences, address-
075   ing Challenge (3) effectively. Nonetheless, they did not further explore the task of multi-sequence
076   proteins, nor did they utilize the rich information provided by the PPI network.
077
      To address these challenges In this work, we propose a multimodal model called LLaPA (Large
078   Language and Protein Assistant), which effectively addresses the aforementioned three challenges
079   simultaneously. LLaPA integrates protein representations and PPI networks into a large language
080   model (LLM). We construct a more general PPI network, inputting both network topology infor-
081   mation and protein information into the LLM to assist in decision-making. During both training
082   and inference, we completely remove edges that overlap between the PPI network and the test set.
083   Treating the PPI network as external knowledge, we inject this knowledge into the LLM prompt
084   using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Gao et al., 2023a). For proteins not present in the
085   PPI network, we find similar protein nodes within the PPI network and provide their topology as ad-
086
      ditional information. Leveraging the flexibility of large language models, LLaPA can accept flexible
      number of proteins as input and use natural language instructions for downstream tasks.
087
088   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
089
090          • We reveal the limitations of existing methods in utilizing PPI networks and provide a
091
               straightforward method for more reasonable utilization of PPI networks.
092          • We propose treating the PPI network as external knowledge and injecting it into LLMs
093            through RAG to assist downstream tasks. This approach is also effective for unseen pro-
094            teins. We also constructed a more general PPI network called UPPIN.
095          • We develop a protein natural multimodal large language model, LLaPA, which integrates
096            the protein encoder EMS-2 (Lin et al., 2022), the PPI network encoder SGC (Wu et al.,
097            2019), and the large language model llama3-8b (Touvron et al., 2023). LLaPA can handle
098            flexible numbers of proteins and has the potential perform diverse protein tasks.
099          • Experiments show that LLaPA achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance on the mPPI
100            task and demonstrates significant accuracy in multi-sequence affinity prediction.

104   2.1   P ROTEIN - PROTEIN INTERACTIONS
105
106   Protein-Protein Interactions (PPIs) are crucial components of cellular activities and play significant
107   roles in various biological functions (Lu et al., 2020; Bryant et al., 2022; Richards et al., 2021). The
      interactions among multiple proteins form complex PPI networks, which implicitly represent the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      signaling processes and pathways of various life activities within organisms. Understanding PPIs
109   not only helps us decipher complex biological systems but also aids in identifying potential targets
110   for disease intervention.
111
112
      With the rise of deep learning technologies, researchers have proposed numerous deep learning-
      based methods for PPI prediction. From a task perspective, PPI tasks include: (1) Binary Classifi-
113
      cation: This task involves inputting a pair of protein sequences and determining whether these two
114
      proteins can interact. Methods such as DPPI, DNN-PPI, PIPR, and TAGPPI typically include a con-
115   volutional neural network module as the protein encoder. After encoding the two proteins separately,
116   a feature fusion module combines the encoded features, and a binary classifier outputs the classi-
117   fication result. (2) Multi-label PPI Type Prediction: This task focuses on identifying the types of
118   interactions between two proteins. PIPR and TAGPPI can also handle this task. GNN-PPI introduces
119   the topological information of the PPI network, combining the topological information of proteins in
120   the PPI network with protein features, achieving significant improvements in the mPPI task. Subse-
121   quent works like HIGH-PPI and MAPE-PPI use the same PPI network. (3) Protein-Protein Binding
122   Affinity Prediction: This task typically focuses on predicting changes in binding affinity between
123
      protein complexes due to mutations, as seen in works like Geo-PPI, DDAffinity (Yu et al., 2024),
      and topNettree (Wang et al., 2020). These methods input the original and mutated protein features
124
      to predict the affinity changes caused by specific mutations. Few works directly predict the binding
125
      affinity of protein complexes, with PIPR being one known example. These methods can only han-
126   dle pairwise protein interactions and cannot predict the affinity of multi-sequence complexes. (4)
127   PPI Binding Site Prediction: This task requires amino acid-level encoding. Representative works
128   include DeepHomo (Yan & Huang, 2021), GLINTER (Xie & Xu, 2022), and DeepInter (Lin et al.,
129   2023), which are beyond the scope of this discussion. (5) Protein-Protein Conformation Prediction:
130   Similar to task (4), this also requires amino acid-level encoding and is not covered in this paper.
131
132   2.2   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
133
134   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) are dedicated to enabling LLMs to recognize and
135   understand non-natural language modality data, such as images, sounds, etc. From a methodological
136   perspective, A common approach involves first using multimodal encoders to encode data from
137   various modalities. Then, a projector module aligns the output space of these modalities with the
138
      input space of the LLM. This process injects multimodal output features into the LLM, enabling
      it to understand non-natural language modalities. Subsequently, Multimodal Instruction Tuning is
139
      employed, which mixes natural language instructions with multimodal data, allowing the MLLM to
140
      perform downstream tasks based on the given multimodal data and natural language instructions.
141   Representative works in this area include LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a), InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023),
142   VisionLLM (Wang et al., 2024), and MultiModal-GPT (Gong et al., 2023). These models exemplify
143   the integration of multimodal data into LLMs, enhancing their ability to process and understand
144   diverse types of data. Recently, some efforts have been made to integrate protein modality into
145   LLMs, endowing LLMs with the ability to understand proteins. Relevant work includes ProtLLM
146   (Zhuo et al., 2024), Prot2Text (Abdine et al., 2024), and ProteinGPT (Xiao et al., 2024), which have
147   achieved noteworthy performance in gene ontology term prediction, as well as understanding of
148   protein sequences and structures.

233   3.3   L IMITATIONS OF OVERSIMPLIFIED M PPI TASK S ETTING
234
235
      PPI networks are essential for PPI-related tasks, as a protein’s position within the network provides
      valuable prior knowledge. Lv et al. (2021) were the first to apply PPI networks to the mPPI task with
236
      their GNN-PPI model, which uses a graph isomorphism network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018) to encode
237
      PPI network topology. They validated the model using three data partitioning methods: random,
238   DFS (depth-first search), and BFS (breadth-first search). As shown in Figure 2-(a), (b), and (c), these
239   methods partition a portion of the edges as the test set, which the labels are not used for training but
240   the structural information are retained for message passing in the GNN model. This allows message
241   exchange between the protein pairs being tested, reducing prediction difficulty. During training,
242   GNN-PPI uses the training edges (green solid edges in Figure 2), but during testing, the test edges
243   (dashed edges in Figure 2) are also included within the graph encoder. Subsequent work, HIGH-PPI,
244   followed GNN-PPI’s setup, while MAPE-PPI used all topological information during both training
245   and inference phases.
246   Unlike protein sequences, which are relatively easy to obtain, acquiring connection information
247   between proteins requires extensive biological experiments and analysis. This makes it challenging
248   to effectively use connection information between test proteins in practical applications. However,
249   removing test set edges during testing may bring another issue, as shown in Figure 2-(d), (e), and
250   (f), where gray nodes become isolated and cannot obtain useful information from the PPI network.
251   Additionally, an unseen protein inherently has no edges in the PPI network, making it an isolated
252
      node. Existing methods do not address how to handle this situation.
253
254   3.4   G RAPH - BASED R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED P ROMPT P REPARATION
255
      To address the aforementioned issues, we propose a novel method that utilizes the PPI network as an
256
      external knowledge source. By employing the RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) technique,
257
      we integrate the knowledge from the PPI network into the input of the large language model (LLM).
258   Given a set of proteins P = {p1 , p2 , ..., pm } and a textual task prompt W , we first locate the
259   corresponding nodes V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vm } in the PPI network for each protein. Next, we construct
260   an enhanced natural language instruction WRAG . Finally, we input this set of proteins, along with
261   the graph node information and the enhanced instruction into the LLM.
262
      In real-world applications, we may lack prior knowledge about a new protein, meaning it might not
263
      exist in any PPI network. In the biological domain, Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) (Edgar &
264   Batzoglou, 2006) is a common method for analyzing protein functions. MSA aligns multiple protein
265   sequences to study the structure, function, and evolutionary relationships of the target protein. A key
266   aspect of MSA is the use of reference sequences. Inspired by MSA, for a protein that is an isolated
267   node in the PPI network, we can approximate its topological reference by comparing it with proteins
268   in the PPI network. Specifically, for a protein p not present in the PPI graph G, we calculate its
269   similarity to each protein in G, denoted as S = {s(p, pi )|pi ∈ G}. This similarity can be computed
      using methods such as sequence alignment scores, structural similarity, or other biological metrics;


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      here, we use the cosine similarity between protein features. With the similarity scores, we retrieve
271   the most similar proteins in the PPI network and use their topological information as a proxy for the
272   isolated protein. This enables us to construct an enriched instruction WRAG that includes relevant
273   topological features, which we then integrate into the LLM prompt for prediction tasks. We did not
274   perform any additional processing on the similarity scores; instead, we directly placed the similarity
275   scores explicitly into WRAG .
276
      Our instruction template, shown in APPENDIX Figure 3, uses a structured data representation,
277   separating text, protein sequences, and graph node indices. In the text, we use two special tokens,
278   <|proteinHere|> and <|graphEmbeddingHere|>, to denote the protein embedding and
279   the PPI network node embedding, respectively. This method can also address isolated nodes caused
280   by the removal of test edges. When constructing WRAG , we initially assess the degree of the target
281   protein p within the PPI network. If the degree is 0, indicating that p is an isolated node, we employ
282   the same methodology used for an unseen protein to derive the topological information of similar
283   proteins in the PPI network.

285   3.4.1    U NIFIED PPI NETWORK
286   Although leveraging protein similarity to utilize the topological information of similar proteins in the
287   PPI network is beneficial, the number and diversity of protein nodes remain a limitation. Intuitively,
288   a PPI network with more proteins and greater diversity enhances the model’s generalization ability.
289   To achieve better generalization, we constructed a larger PPI network called UPPIN (Unified PPI
290   Network), which consists of three sub-datasets: STRING (Homo sapiens subset) (Szklarczyk et al.,
291   2016), PDBBind (Liu et al., 2017), and SAbDab (Dunbar et al., 2014).
292   STRING (Homo sapiens subset) is a multi-source PPI network comprising 15,202 unique proteins
293   and 581,161 unique edges. It includes seven types of protein interactions: activation, binding, catal-
294   ysis, expression, inhibition, posttranslational modification (ptmod), and reaction. For UPPIN, we
295   retained all nodes and edges from STRING but removed the edge labels.
296
      PDBBind is a database derived from the PDB (Protein Data Bank) (Berman et al., 2000), containing
297
      biomolecular complexes with experimentally determined binding affinities. We used the 2020 ver-
298   sion of PDBBind, which includes 2,852 protein-protein complexes, totaling 5,711 unique proteins.
299   Due to often incomplete protein sequences in the crystal data, we first obtained the fasta data for
300   each protein from the PDB. We then connected each pair of proteins within a complex with an edge,
301   resulting in a total of 5,978 edges.
302
      SAbDab is an antibody-antigen database that includes complexes and experimental information,
303
      and it is continuously updated. We used data up to PDB 8cds, comprising 16,226 complexes and
304   6,315 unique proteins. As with PDBBind, we first obtained the fasta data for each complex from the
305   Protein Data Bank and then constructed edges between each pair of proteins within a complex.
306
307
      Finally, we merged these three datasets to create our UPPIN, which includes a total of 26,180 unique
      proteins and 594,216 unique edges. Detailed information is provided in Appendix Table 6.
308
309   By constructing UPPIN, we expanded the coverage and diversity of the PPI network, enhancing
310   the model’s generalization capability. This larger-scale PPI network provides richer topological
311   information and better supports prediction tasks for unseen proteins.

385   Datasets. For the mPPI task, we used SHS27k and SHS148k, two subsets of STRING constructed
386   by Chen et al. (2019). They randomly selected 3,000 and 8,000 proteins with sequence identity
387   less than 40% from STRING to form these datasets. SHS27k and SHS148k contain 26,945 and
388   148,051 interaction cases, respectively. We adopted the same data splitting algorithms as GNN-
389
      PPI: random (randomly selecting test edges from the PPI network), DFS (using depth-first search
      to obtain test edges), and BFS (using breadth-first search to obtain test edges). Depending on the
390
      splitting algorithm, the test protein pairs can be categorized into three modes: BS (both proteins
391
      have been seen), ES (either one protein has been seen), and NS (neither one has been seen). For
392   more detailed information, please refer to the GNN-PPI (Lv et al., 2021) and MAPE-PPI (Wu et al.,
393   2024b) papers. We split these two datasets into training, validation, and test sets in a 60%:20%:20%
394   ratio. For the MA task, we used PDB2020 for validation. We split PDB2020 into a training set and
395   a test set at a ratio of 80%:20%.
396
      Baselines. For the mPPI task, we compared against DPPI, DNN-PPI, PIPR, ESM2-3B (fixed),
397
      ESM2-3B (ft), ProtLLM, GNN-PPI, HIGH-PPI, and MAPE-PPI. Since GNN-PPI, HIGH-PPI, and
398   MAPE-PPI can see the complete PPI network during the test phase, for a fairer comparison, we
399   removed the edges contained in the test set from the PPI network during the test phase, denoted
400   as GNN-PPI/R, HIGH-PPI/R, and MAPE-PPI/R, respectively. For ESM2-3B (fixed), we fixed the
401   parameters of ESM2-3B and trained a multi-classifier on the top of it; For ESM2-3B (ft), in addition
402   to training multi-classifiers, we also fine-tuned all the weights of ESM2-3B, with all training hy-
403   perparameters kept consistent with LLaPA; For ProtLLM, we modified the original code provided
404   by the authors to support the mPPI task and fine-tuned it using the pre-trained weights and hyper-
405   parameters supplied by the authors. For the MA task, to the best of our knowledge, LLaPA is the
406
      first model capable of handling flexible number of sequences, so we did not find suitable baselines.
      For reference, For the MA task, we used PIPR to predict the binding affinity of all two-protein
407
      complexes in the test set. Additionally, we trained three affinity prediction models using ESM2-3B,
408
      named E(2), E(3), and E(4). E(2) is used to predict the binding affinity of two-protein complexes,
409   while E(3) predicts the binding affinity of three-protein complexes, and E(4) for four-protein com-
410   plexes.

      5.1   M ULTI - LABEL PPI T YPE P REDICTION
433
434   The experimental results are shown in Table 1. From these results, we can draw two key insights:
435
      (1) Underperformance of PPI network-Based Methods After Edge Removal. The PPI network-
436
      based methods yield unsatisfactory results after removing the edges contained in the test set. Among
437
      the three PPI network-based algorithms, MAPE-PPI/R achieves the best results across various task
438   settings. However, it still falls short of PIPR in several settings. Specifically, on SHS27k (random),
439   MAPE-PPI/R is 2.75 points lower than PIPR; on SHS27k (DFS), it is 0.5 points lower; on SHS148k
440   (random), it is 2.85 points lower; and on SHS148k (BFS), it is 1.89 points lower. The other two
441   PPI network-based models, GNN-PPI/R and HIGH-PPI/R, perform worse than the non-PPI network
442   models across all task settings. These results are expected because graph encoders heavily rely on the
443   graph structure. When the edges in the test set are removed, the graph structure changes significantly,
444   making it difficult for the weights learned on the training set to be effective. This is particularly
445   problematic for the DFS and BFS data splitting methods, which may result in a number of isolated
446
      nodes that cannot obtain any useful information during the graph message-passing process. It is
      worth noting that MAPE-PPI/R still performs relatively well in this scenario, likely due to its pre-
447
      training based on VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), which demonstrates the effectiveness of its
448
      proposed micro-environment-based protein encoding.
449
450   (2) Superior Performance of LLaPA. LLaPA achieves the best results across all task settings. Un-
451   der the random splitting method, LLaPA outperforms the second-best model by 2.90 on the SHS27k
452
      dataset and by 2.97 on the SHS148k dataset. The improvement is modest, which is expected because,
      under the random method, most proteins in the test set can be well-trained, making the learning task
453
      relatively easy. However, the improvements are much more significant under the DFS and BFS
454
      methods. Specifically, LLaPA outperforms the second-best model by 17.35 6.16 points on SHS27k
455   (DFS), by 12.00 points on SHS27k (BFS), by 11.80 7.01 points on SHS148k (DFS), and by 12.33
456   9.02 points on SHS148k (BFS). The performance on SHS148k are better than those on SHS27k,
457   likely because the SHS148k dataset is approximately 5.5 times larger than SHS27k, making it easier
458   for the model to fit the data. We found that training a multi-classifier with fixed ESM2-3B pa-
459   rameters underperformed. While full parameter fine-tuning of ESM2-3B improved results, it still
460   didn’t match LLaPA. Despite ProtLLM has similar architectures with LLaPA, it lack of PPI network
461   information led to suboptimal performance on the mPPI task.
462   These results highlight the robustness and effectiveness of LLaPA, especially in scenarios where the
463   graph structure is altered or when dealing with more challenging data splits.

523   5.4   C ONCLUSION
524
525   We identified and addressed limitation in current multi-label PPI type predictions based on PPI net-
526   works. Our solution, a multimodal large language model named LLaPA, treats the PPI network
527   as external knowledge, integrating it into the model via RAG. We innovated a modality alignment
528   method that leverages pre-aligned protein modalities to expedite graph modality alignment. LLaPA
529   not only sets a new standard in multi-label PPI type prediction but also stands as the first model capa-
530
      ble of predicting affinities for multi-sequence complexes with flexible number of protein sequences.
      Beyond this, LLaPA holds promise for a wide range of other protein-related tasks.

      Graph data structures have a wide range of real-world applications, such as in financial networks,
857
      social networks, and protein interaction networks. When it comes to modeling complex network
858   relationships, graph data structures offer unique advantages. Graph-structured data can also be con-
859   sidered a type of multimodal data, and the approach of injecting multimodal features into LLMs is
860   equally applicable to graph data. For example: GraphLLM (Chai et al., 2023) uses Prefix-tuning (Li

862   (Liu et al., 2024b) and Molca (Liu et al., 2023): These models use Q-former (Li et al., 2023) to align
863   graph features with LLMs. The Q-former helps in bridging the gap between graph representations
      and the input space of LLMs. InstructMol (Cao et al., 2023) employs a mapping matrix W to map

912   A.8   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORKS
913
914   As a large model integrating proteins, PPI networks, and natural language, LLaPA utilizes natural
915   language instructions and a unified training method for downstream tasks. It can accept a flexible
916   number of protein inputs and has the potential to handle more complex protein tasks. However,
917   LLaPA focuses on protein-level features and is ineffective for tasks requiring amino acid-level fea-
      tures, such as PPI binding site prediction and PPI conformation prediction. Additionally, since


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      we directly input protein embeddings into the LLM, we cannot leverage the textual features corre-
919   sponding to protein entities. This is not an issue for novel proteins, but for well-studied proteins
920   with existing literature, utilizing these resources for better analysis is crucial. Furthermore, con-
921   structing a larger and more diverse UPPIN is also very important. Therefore, our future work may
922   focus on three aspects: (1) using LLaPA for more comprehensive and challenging protein tasks; (2)
923   incorporating amino acid-level features into LLaPA; (3) enabling LLaPA to better utilize literature
924   resources; (4) constructing a more comprehensive and diverse UPPIN network.
