009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalized Category Discovery (GCD) is a challenging task that aims to recog-
012            nize seen and novel categories within unlabeled data by leveraging labeled data.
013            Designing a prototype classifier to identify unlabeled samples instead of relying on
014            traditional time-consuming clustering is well recognized as a milestone in GCD.
015            However, we discover there exists a bias in this classifier: some seen categories are
016            mistakenly classified as novel ones, leading to imbalanced pseudo-labeling during
017            classifier learning. Based on this finding, we identify the low discriminability
018
               between seen and novel prototypes as the key issue. To address this issue, we pro-
               pose DebiasGCD, an effective debiasing method that integrates dynamic prototype
019
               debiasing (DPD) and local representation alignment (LRA). DPD dynamically
020
               maintains inter-prototype margins, encouraging the network to strengthen the learn-
021            ing of class-specific features and enhance prototype discrimination. Additionally,
022            LRA promotes local representation learning, enabling DPD to capture subtle de-
023            tails that further refine the understanding of class-specific features. In this way, it
024            successfully improves prototype discriminability and generates more reliable pre-
025            dictions for seen classes. Extensive experiments validate that our method effectively
026            mitigates pseudo-labeling bias across all datasets, especially on fine-grained ones.
027            For instance, it delivers a 10.7% boost on ‘Old’ classes in CUB. Our code is avail-
028            able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DebiasGCD-34F0.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

033
      Deep learning has achieved great success in computer vision (He et al., 2017; Ren et al., 2015;
      Ronneberger et al., 2015; Szegedy et al., 2015; Redmon et al., 2016; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Caron
034
      et al., 2021). However, these achievements are based on a close-world assumption, where test data
035
      shares the same classes as the training data. This assumption fails in open-world scenarios (Geng et al.,
036   2021; Han et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b), where unlabeled data may encounter
037   unseen categories. To address this, a new paradigm called Generalized Category Discovery (GCD)
038   Vaze et al. (2022b) has been proposed and is gaining increasing attention. GCD requires models to
039   recognize seen and novel categories in unlabeled data by leveraging knowledge from labeled data,
040   making it suitable for open-world scenarios with vast amounts of unlabeled data.
041
      There are mainly two existing approches for GCD: k-means clustering (Vaze et al., 2022b; Fei et al.,
042   2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Pu et al., 2023) and prototype classification (Wen et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
043   2024). The former identifies unlabeled samples by clustering their representations. However, it
044   often becomes computationally expensive with larger datasets due to the quadratic complexity of
045   clustering algorithms. Instead, Wen et al. Wen et al. (2023) adopts the latter approach, and proposes
046   SimGCD, replacing the clustering-based approach with a classifier. Specifically, they found that
047   using a classifier directly in GCD led to overfitting on seen categories, causing novel categories to
048   be misclassified as seen ones. To fix this, they introduced a mean-entropy-maximization regularizer
049   (Assran et al., 2022) to activate novel prototypes learning and improve pseudo-label reliability for the
050
      prototype classifier. As a result, SimGCD replaces the time-consuming clustering with a prototype
      classifier and achieves competitive performance, establishing itself as a robust baseline in GCD.
051
052   Although SimGCD successfully mitigates the overfitting bias on seen categories, a detailed investiga-
053   tion reveals that there also exists a new bias in the classifier: some seen categories are mistakenly
      classified as novel ones, leading to imbalanced pseudo-labels during classifier learning. This issue

085
086          To tackle the above issues, we propose an effective method called DebiasGCD to calibrate bias
087
             between prototypes. First, we introduce a dynamic prototype debiasing (DPD) technique to reduce the
             prediction bias induced by prototypes dynamically. This technique maintains inter-prototype margins
088
             between classes, enhancing the network’s ability to learn more class-specific features, and reinforcing
089
             the prototype discrimination. As a result, the classifier can generate more reliable pseudo-labels.
090          Second, we propose a local representation alignment (LRA) module to align the local representations
091          of different sample views in semi-supervised learning. This helps the classifier learn detailed class-
092          specific representations and facilitates the optimization of DPD. Additionally, we also adopt strong
093          and weak augmentation for instances following Vaze et al. (2023); Sohn et al. (2020). Finally, by
094          combining DPD and LRA, DebiasGCD creates clear boundaries for class prototypes, especially for
095          seen and novel classes, thus mitigating imbalanced pseudo-labeling.
096          To evaluate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct extensive experiments on six datasets,
097          including fine-grained and generic object classification datasets. Our approach significantly reduces
098          pseudo-labels bias, outperforming SimGCD (Wen et al., 2023) by 10.7%/5.2% on the ‘Old’ and ‘New’
099          classes in CUB, respectively. Meanwhile, DebiasGCD prevents a 10% drop in pseudo-label accuracy,
100          as shown in Fig. 1a. Fig. 1c further shows DebiasGCD produces less noise in the red rectangle

102           In summary, our key contributions are as follows: 1) We investigate and discover a bias exists in
103           the classifier that leads to imbalanced pseudo-labeling in GCD semi-supervised learning; 2) We
104           propose effective debiased imbalanced pseudo-labeling, DebiasGCD, which combines DPD and LRA
105           to expand the distances between prototypes and extracts more local discriminative representation
106           features for recognizing instances in unlabeled data; 3) Extensive experimental results demonstrate
107           our proposed debiased learning removes bias effectively, improving the performance by a large
              margin e.g., 10.7%/5.2% on ‘Old’ and ‘New’ classes in CUB, respectively.

      Semi-supervised Learning (SSL) tackles the issue of limited labeled data by integrating unlabeled
111   data from pre-defined classes (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Laine & Aila, 2017; Huang et al., 2021). Most
112   SSL methods adopt techniques like consistency-based regularization (Menon et al., 2021; Tarvainen
113   & Valpola, 2017; Miyato et al., 2019; Xie et al., 2020), pseudo-labeling (Sohn et al., 2020; Zhang
114   et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023), and transfer learning (Chen et al., 2020b). Notably, pseudo-labeling
115   is an effective baseline using a weak augmented view’s prediction as a pseudo-label for a strong
116   view Berthelot et al. (2020); Sohn et al. (2020). Meanwhile, Wang et al. (2023a); Xu et al. (2021);
117   Zhang et al. (2021) improves performance by adjusting thresholds to select high-quality pseudo-
118   labels. However, traditional SSL methods assume the labeled and unlabeled data share the same
119
      distribution, limiting its effectiveness in open-world settings. Recently, SSL has been extended to
      out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Huang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
120
      2023). For instance, FlatMatch Huang et al. (2023) minimizes cross-sharpness for consistent learning,
121
      InPL Yu et al. (2023) uses energy-based pseudo-labeling to select pseudo-labeling for OOD data,
122   and ATOL Zheng et al. (2023) employs generated OOD data to improve detection. Increasingly, SSL
123   methods are being adapted for real-world scenarios.
124
125
      Generalized Category Discovery (GCD) Vaze et al. (2022b) aims to train a model that can recognize
      both seen and novel categories within unlabeled data. Unlike Novel Class Discovery (NCD) (Han
126
      et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), which treats unlabeled data as entirely new classes,
127
      GCD assumes a mix of seen and novel classes. Current GCD methods can be categorized into two
128   paradigms: k-means clustering (Vaze et al., 2022b) and learnable classifiers (Wen et al., 2023). First,
129   most works adopt a clustering strategy to learn the representation center to recognize unlabeled
130   instances. XCon Fei et al. (2022) partitions datasets into visually similar sub-datasets using k-
131   means clustering, forcing the model to learn fine-grained features. GPC Zhao et al. (2023) uses a
132   Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) and representation learning to cluster categories. AGCD Ma et al.
133   (2024) incorporates active learning to increase labeled data for clustering. PromptCAL Zhang et al.
134   (2023b) enhances semantic representations with prompt learning and contrastive affinity, but still
135   relies on SemiKMeans clustering (Vaze et al., 2022b). However, these methods are computationally
136
      expensive, particularly with large datasets. To address this, parametric classifier methods have
      emerged. SimGCD Wen et al. (2023) introduces a prototype classifier establishing a new GCD
137
      baseline. µGCD Vaze et al. (2023) utilizes mean-teacher to enhance pseudo-label quality for SSL,
138
      building upon SimGCD. It initially trains with the clustering-based GCD (Vaze et al., 2022b) before
139   fine-tuning with the classification head, which is time-intensive. In this paper, we also improve the
140   quality of pseudo-label for SSL in a time-saving manner based on SimGCD.
141
142
      Prototypes Learning regards the class-specific representations in feature space as prototype centers
      for each category (Snell et al., 2017). In most GCD works, instances are matched to the category with
143
      a larger similarity to its prototype. For instance, DPN An et al. (2023) uses a decoupled prototypical
144
      network to separate seen and novel categories, aligning them in labeled and unlabeled data to transfer
145   knowledge and capture semantics. TAN An et al. (2024) trains a model to align instances with
146   prototypes and estimate novel prototypes in unlabeled data based on category similarities. Some
147   works combine contrastive learning (Caron et al., 2021) with classifiers to learn the prototypes.
148   SimGCD Wen et al. (2023) first proposes to construct a parametric classifier with prototypes for
149   categories. It employs contrastive learning technique in labeled and unlabeled data to train the model
150   to learn the class discriminative features and then employs self-distillation (Caron et al., 2021; Assran
151   et al., 2022) to generate pseudo-labels for further optimization. Obviously, the parametric classifier
152   relieves the cost of transferring and aligning prototypes and is more effective in recognizing the
153
      instances. Unfortunately, the classifier in SimGCD ignores training a set of more discriminative class
      prototypes, leading to unreliable pseudo-labels.

240
241   Motivation. Although the prototype classifier learning (Wen et al., 2023) has alleviated the costly
242   clustering process and improved accuracy compared to GCD (Vaze et al., 2022b), it encounters
243   a bias in generating imbalanced pseudo-labels for semi-supervised learning, as shown in Fig. 1a
244   and Fig. 1b. This bias arises because SimGCD relies solely on contrastive learning (see Sec. 3)
245
      and does not directly optimize prototypes for pseudo-label generation. Therefore, we argue that
      enhancing the discriminability of each prototype is essential. To tackle this, we propose the Dynamic
246
      Prototype Debiasing (DPD) strategy, which dynamically augments class-specific discriminability
247
      among prototypes to mitigate bias in a pseudo-label generation.
248

      by applying prototype ranking loss. Specifically, we enforce this ranking-based loss on labeled data to
252
      dynamically increase the margin between prototypes, enhancing their ability to distinguish between
253
      classes. This enhanced discriminability in the classification of our method is shown in Fig. 3.

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
528
      In this paper, we first investigate the previously unrecognized bias of imbalanced pseudo-labeling
      in the GCD task. We then propose an effective debiasing method, DebiasGCD, to address this
529
      imbalance between seen and novel categories in classifier prototype learning. To implement this
530
      debiasing, we propose a dynamic prototype debiasing technique to maintain a margin between
531   prototypes dynamically, encouraging the network to explore category-specific features and enhance
532   prototype distinction. Furthermore, to improve the learning for more discriminable representations
533   in DPD, we propose a local representations alignment module to discover more subtle features that
534   benefit classification, especially in fine-grained datasets. Extensive results show that our DebiasGCD
535   significantly outperforms the baseline SimGCD, effectively mitigating the pseudo-labeling bias.
