                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
                 We present a simple meta quantization approach that quantizes different layers of
012
                 a large language model (LLM) at different bit levels, and is independent of the
013              underlying quantization technique. Specifically, we quantize the most important
014              layers to higher bit precision and less important layers to lower bits. We propose
015              two effective strategies to measure the importance of layers within LLMs: the first
016              measures the importance of a layer based on how different its output embeddings are
017              from the input embeddings (higher is better); the second estimates the importance of
018              a layer using the number of layer weights that are much larger than average (smaller
019              is better). We show that quantizing different layers at varying bits according
020              to our importance scores results in minimal performance drop with a far more
021
                 compressed model size. Finally, we present several practical key takeaways from
                 our variable layer-wise quantization experiments: (a) LLM performance under
022
                 variable quantization remains close to the original model until 25–50% of layers
023
                 are moved in lower quantization using our proposed ordering but only until 5–10%
024              if moved using no specific ordering; (b) Adding layer importance to inherently
025              dynamic quantization techniques can further improve their performance, showing
026              that our approach is complementary to other dynamic quantization methods; (c)
027              Quantizing LLMs to lower bits performs substantially better than pruning unless
028              extreme quantization (2-bit) is used; and (d) Layer-wise quantization to lower bits
029              works better in the case of larger LLMs with more layers compared to smaller
030              LLMs with fewer layers. Our code is publicly available at URL hidden for review.

      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable performance on a variety of tasks,
      especially when scaled to billions of parameters Jiang et al. (2023; 2024); Touvron et al. (2023);
036
      Zhang et al. (2022); Team et al. (2023). The largest open-source models available can have an
037
      upwards of 400B parameters, such as LLaMa3-400B Touvron et al. (2023). Even small models such
038   as LLaMa3-8B require as much as 20GB of VRAM to run on a GPU at the original precision, making
039   them unusable for low resource settings. In such settings model compression techniques such as
040   quantization are critical Zhu et al. (2023); Wan et al. (2023).
041
      Most prominent techniques for model compression broadly cover pruning Ma et al. (2023), knowledge
042
      distillation Gu et al. (2023), and quantization Zhu et al. (2023). Pruning yields improvements in
043
      inference speed, but often results in substantial performance drop Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Men
044   et al. (2024). On the other hand, quantization has proven to be a more robust solution for model size
045   compression with comparatively much smaller performance drops Lin et al. (2024). In our work, we
046   primarily focus on memory reduction through quantization. Further, quantization can be training
047   specific or post-training Yao et al. (2024), where trained models are quantized without requiring any
048   further training. We focus on post-training quantization due to its practicality.
049
      The majority of quantization techniques proposed recently (Frantar et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023;
050   Yao et al., 2022, inter alia) focus on the quantization of all the LLM layers to a single precision
051   bit, or quantizing only specific weights of the network Lin et al. (2024). This has shown to be
052   costly, their effectiveness is data dependent, and their implementations can be considered moderately

146   A few works have highlighted 4-bit precision as a robust quantization limit for wide variety of NLP

      tasks (Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2023). Our results (fig. 2 and 3) also show similar findings that

      more on quantizing LLMs below 4-bits to highlight the effectiveness of layer-wise quantization based

      A few previous works have studied layer importance in transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Simoulin
154
      & Crabbé, 2021). Some of the recent, (unpublished) concurrent works such as ShortGPT (Men et al.,
155
      2024) have also proposed utilizing layer importance but primarily focusing on pruning. Shen et al.
156   (2020) had utilized hessian information from each layer as an importance measure to quantize specific
157   weight matrices at different bits. Different from these, our dynamic strategy focuses on utilizing layer
158   importance for quantizing more important layers in higher bits and less important layers in lower
159   bits. Importantly, our proposed approach of layer-wise quantization of LLMs based on their layer
160   importance is a meta method that can be coupled with any quantization techniques such as GPT-Q
161   and Quanto (section 3.3). We also compare ShortGPT’s layer importance-based pruning with our
      layerwise quantization approach in section 6.1, and show that they are largely complementary.

185   3     M ETHOD
186
187   At a high level, our meta quantization strategy operates in two steps: first, given an LLM with N layers
188   denoted as {L1 , L2 , L3 , ....LN }, we first compute the importance of each layer. Second, we then
189   employ an existing quantization technique to quantize layers differently based on their importance.
190
191   3.1   L AYER I MPORTANCE S CORES
192
193
      We propose two layer importance scoring methods.
194   We call our first layer importance score layer input modification (LIM). The intuition behind LIM is
195   that the more a layer changes its received input embeddings the more important it must be. More
196   formally, the LIM score for a specific layer Li measures the modification of the representations of
197   Li ’s input (denoted by LIi ) to create its output representations (denoted by LO
                                                                                      i ). A near similar score
198   has been also studied for pruning (layer removal) in a concurrent (unpublished) work (Men et al.,
199
      2024). The LIM score is the negative of cosine similarity between LIi and LO     i as shown below:
200
201                                                              LIi · LO
                                                                        i
                                              LIM(Li ) = −                                                      (1)
202                                                             ∥LIi ∥∥LOi ∥
203
204   The dot product LIi · LOi quantifies the alignment between the input and output vectors, while the
205   normalization factor ∥LIi ∥∥LOi ∥ scales the dot product to the range of [-1, 1], corresponding to the
206   cosine of the angle between the two vectors. The negative sign in the LIM score indicates that a
207   higher similarity (cosine similarity closer to 1) between LIi and LO i results in a lower importance
208   score. For measuring change in LIi to LO i , we use all 50 documents  of pg19! Rae et al. (2019a) (an
209
      unlabelled text corpus) as calibration corpus.
210   Our second score, called z-score distribution (ZD), is based on the distribution of parameter values
211   and does not require any calibration data. Intuitively, this score considers a layer as more important if
212   it contains more weights that are much higher than average.
213   We examine the proportion of weights in a layer exhibiting a z-score greater than 1. The z-score of a
214   weight wi is defined as

233   3.2     C HOOSING THE N UMBER OF L AYERS IN H IGHER P RECISION G IVEN A M EMORY B UDGET
234
235   Under a fixed memory budget, our approach aims to keep as many layers as possible in higher
236   precision. The maximum number of layers to keep in higher precision given available memory is easy
237   to compute. In particular, assume we have Mavailable memory available. Having all Nlayers layers in
238
      the network in lower precision requires Mlower memory, while having all of them in higher precision
      requires Mhigher memory. The maximum number of layers that can be kept in higher precision is
239
      then calculated as:
240
241
                                                      Mavailable − Mlower
242                                    Nhigher = ⌊                        ∗ Nlayers ⌋
243                                                   Mhigher − Mlower
244   For example, if we have 20GB of VRAM available; a model with 32 layers takes 17GB in lower
245   precision (e.g., 2-bits) and 34GB in higher precision (e.g., 4-bits), then the number of layers using
                                          20−17
246   higher precision is: Nhigher = ⌊ 34−17    ∗ 32⌋ = 5. This means that 5 of the layers can be in
247   4-bit precision and the other 32 − 5 = 27 have to be in 2-bit precision, for a total memory of
248   19.65GB < 20GB. This also underscores the practicality of our technique, as we do not add an
249   extra hyper-parameter that requires tuning and we make use of most of the available memory.

251   3.3     Q UANTIZATION T ECHNIQUES
252
      To show that our meta quantization approach is independent of the underlying quantization tech-
253
      nique, we couple it with two well-known post-training quantization techniques: GPT-Q and Quanto.
254
      While our focus is not on low-level quantization methods, we summarize both methods below for
255   completeness.
256
257   3.3.1      Q UANTO
258
259   Quanto is a fast-acting quantization method3 that simplifies the process of reducing precision after
260   training. In practice, Quanto achieves quicker quantization by applying uniform scaling factors across
261
      all layers of a model, avoiding the need for detailed data-driven analysis of each layer’s distribution.
      Similarly to most techniques, it allows for int2, int4, int8, and fp8 quantization. The Quanto library
262
      is part of Hugging Face, with symmetric per-tensor or per-channel projection for int8 and float8,
263
      and group-wise affine (with a shift or ’zero-point’) for lower bit-widths, such as int2 or int4. In this
264   paper, we used Quanto in the 2-bit and 4-bit setting, utilizing an asymmetric quantization method
265   with RTN (round to the nearest), with the exception of Table 5 where we used 4-bit and 8-bit settings.
266   In our implementation for variable quantization of different layers, we quantized the same model to
267   two different bit levels (int2 and int4). Then, based on the layer importance order, we selected less
268   important layers from the int2 quantized sets and more important layers from the int4 quantized sets.

306   3.3.2                    GPT-Q
307
308   GPT-Q is a post-training quantization technique specifically designed for GPT models (Frantar et al.,
309   2023). Utilizing a dataset, it calculates the necessary scaling factors for quantization. After training,
310
      GPT-Q assesses the distribution of weights using this dataset to determine optimal scaling factors for
      converting floating-point representations to lower-bit formats such as int8 or int4. We only used a
311
      few data points for GPT-Q as it adds significant execution time overhead to our experiments. We
312
      modified the GPT-Q implementation in the Hugging Face library (Wolf et al., 2019) for our use-case
313   in the same way as described above for Quanto.

526
      We introduced a simple, flexible quantization approach that quantizes different layers at different bits
      based on their importance. We presented two layer importance scoring techniques which when used
527
      to select more important layers for quantizing them in 4 bits and less important layers in 2-bits lead to
528
      strong performance retention across several LLMs even until 2.85 overall bit size. Our work presents
529   several key practical findings such as layer-wise quantization is more effective for larger LLMs (with
530   more number of layers), in the same memory setting; quantization is better than pruning until a
531   certain bit precision level, text generation tasks are affected more with quantization, etc. Overall,
532   our work introduces layer-wise quantization and presents detailed empirical findings for motivating
533   future research in this direction.

671   A.1    Q UANTIZING L AYERS U SING 3 L EVELS
672
673   In all of our experiments, we have focused on two level quantization, i.e., either less important layers
674   are quantized in 2-bits and more important layers in 4-bits or less important in 4-bits and more
675   important in 8-bits. As a plausible variant, LLM layers can also be easily quantized using three levels,
676   i.e., least important layers in 2-bits, moderately important in 4 bits, and the most important ones in
677   8-bits. In our study, we observed three level quantization almost always performs worse than two
678
      level quantization. We show three level quantization of LLaMa2-7b with fixed overall model bit size
      of 4-bits. We first quantize all the 32 layers in 4-bits as shown by the leftmost bar of appendix A.1.
679
      We then convert two least important layers to 2-bits each and one most important layer to 8-bits, thus
680
      maintaining the overall bit size of the model to 4-bits. This is represented by second bar from the left
681   in fig. 7. We repeat the same process of converting two more layers in 2-bits and a more important
682   layer to 8-bits represented by the consecutive bars in fig. 7. As observed, three level quantization
683   always performs worse than one level quantization when the target bit-level is the same, thus we
684   don’t propose the technique as a way to achieve better performance for a set quantization level, but as
685   a way to achieve a variable level of quantization while retaining maximum performance.

693   A.3    C OMMONALITY BETWEEN LAYER IMPORTANCE
694
      To the best of our knowledge, we are the first one to propose layer importance and utilize the
695
      importance order to quantize different layers at different bits. in Figure 6, we show the intensity bars
696   below for each layer based on their importance for four different LLMs with different number of
697   layers and sizes. As observed, there is a substantial pattern overlap of least important layers across
698   multiple LLMs. We observe that first and the last layer are the most two important layers. Many of
699   the important layers also tend to be the initial few set of layers. Lesser important layers (shown by
700   block of layers with faded intensity) tend to be towards halfway of middle and end of the network.
701   These observations suggest generalized patterns in layer importance across LLMs and pre-computed
      layer importance orders can be roughly utilized to quantize a wide variety of LLMs.
