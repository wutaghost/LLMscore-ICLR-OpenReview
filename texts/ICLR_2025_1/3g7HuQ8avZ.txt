009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we present OmniContrast, a unified contrastive learning model tai-
012            lored for vision, language, and vision-language-interleaved understanding within
013            multi-modal web documents. Unlike traditional image-caption data with clear
014            vision-language correspondence, we explore a new contrastive fashion on maxi-
015            mizing the similarity between consecutive snippets sampled from image-text in-
016            terleaved web documents. Moreover, to enable CLIP to handle long-form text
017            and image-text interleaved content from web documents, OmniContrast unifies
018
               all modalities into pixel space, where text is rendered visually. This unifica-
               tion simplifies the processing and representation of diverse multi-modal inputs,
019
               enabling a single vision model to process any modality. To evaluate the omni-
020
               modality understanding of OmniContrast, we design three consecutive informa-
021            tion retrieval benchmarks AnyCIR, SeqCIR, and CSR. Extensive experimental
022            results demonstrate that OmniContrast achieves superior or competitive omni-
023            modality understanding performance to existing standard CLIP models trained on
024            image-text pairs. This highlights the potential of multi-modal web documents as
025            a rich and valuable resource for advancing vision-language learning.

      2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE L EARNING FROM W EB DATA
101
102   The pioneer work CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) establishes a breakthrough learning paradigm by ap-
103   plying contrastive learning on large-scale noisy image/alt-text paired data from the internet. Follow-
104   up studies scale the image-text pairs data (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Gadre et al., 2024) and the model
105   design (Li et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2023) to further improve the performance. More
106   recently, with the rapid development of Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Li et al.,
107   2023; Liu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024), multi-modal web documents data, such as MMC4 (Zhu et al.,
      2024) and OBELICS (Laurençon et al., 2024), have emerged as new sources of training data. These


                                                                  2
108
      multi-modal documents typically consist of sequences of coherent text paragraphs interleaved with
109   images. Several research (Lin et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024) demonstrate that joint training
110   with image-text data and multi-modal web documents outperforms solely image-text pairs, which
111   indicates the multi-modal documents contain useful vision-language correspondence from image-
112   text pairs. Moreover, (Ma et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Jang et al., 2024) leverage MLLMs to encode
113   multi-modal document information for question answering or document retrieval. In contrast to
114   prior research focusing on MLLMs, we serve as the first step in studying the potential of contrastive
115   learning on image-text interleaved web document data.
116
117   2.2   V ISUAL R EPRESENTATION FOR L ANGUAGE M ODELING
118
119   Despite the impressive results achieved by text tokenization (Devlin, 2018; Sennrich, 2015) in
120   language modeling (Devlin, 2018; Brown, 2020), text tokenization is vulnerable to text permuta-
121
      tions (Salesky et al., 2021), such as misspellings and has limited scalability to other languages (Rust
      et al., 2022). To address these challenges, a line of works explores the tokenizer-free solution based
122
      on the visual representation of text. (Meng et al., 2019) use glyph-vectors from Chinese characters
123
      images to enhance the text representation. (Salesky et al., 2021) proposed visual text representation
124   as open-vocabularies to improve the robustness of machine translation. Recently, to close the gaps
125   between the visual text representation and text tokenization, (Rust et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2024;
126   Gao et al., 2024; Chai et al., 2024) further explore different pre-training strategies, such as next
127   patch prediction, next token prediction, and contrastive learning.
128
      In the vision-language domain, the most closely related work is CLIPPO (Tschannen et al., 2023).
129
      CLIPPO utilizes rendered alt-text and image pairs to train the vision encoder using contrastive learn-
130   ing the same as CLIP. In contrast, OmniContrast marks the first attempt at exploration in image-text
131   interleaved documents contrastive learning and omni-modality learning. Additionally, screenshot
132   understanding (Gao et al., 2024) is also closely related to visual text representation learning, which
133   involves language modeling from documents (Kim et al., 2022), web pages (Lee et al., 2023) or UI
134   images (Li & Li, 2022). Despite these screenshot language models directly learning text information
135   from the input image, they still can not handle omni-modality input.

      As shown in Fig. 2, OmniContrast uses rendered consecutive snippets sampled from multi-modal
140   web documents as training data. After data pre-processing and augmentation, each snippet in pos-
141   itive pairs can be either image-only, text-only or an interleaved image-text rendered image. During
142   training, the single vision model is optimized by contrastive loss on these consecutive data pairs.
143
144   3.1   I NTERLEAVED W EB DATA P ROCESSING
145
146   Document Pre-processing. Given a web document, our goal is to sample a pair of semantically
147   relevant image-text snippets for training. Firstly, we split a document text into multiple text segments
148   with a maximum of 1,100 characters in each segment. Then, we use the CLIP similarity annotation
149   provided in MMC4 dataset (Zhu et al., 2024) to assign the image to the corresponding segments.
150   Each interleaved snippet at least contains text while can be without images or assigned multiple
151   images. For the multiple image cases, we only randomly sample one image for training.
152   Data Augmentation. Next, we apply two types of augmentations to obtain augmented snippets, i.e.,
153   modality masking and text masking. In modality masking, we only mask snippets with both text and
154   image contents. During training, we apply modality masking with a masking rate of 40% on snippets
155   to randomly drop one modality content. With modality masking, we are able to sample diverse
156   training matching targets. For text masking, we randomly remove sentences from the beginning or
157   end of the text content in 40% of the snippets. This augmentation enhances the model’s language
158
      understanding by preventing the model from overfitting on recurring words.
159   Multi-modal Snippet Rendering. Given a multimodal snippet containing both image and text, we
160   render its content into a 2×2 grid. Each grid has a resolution of 224×224 pixels. If the snippet
161   includes an image, we resize it to fit the grid and place it in a randomly selected grid cell. For
      visual text rendering, we follow the approach in (Tschannen et al., 2023) using the GNU Unifont

      4       C ONSECUTIVE I NFORMATION R ETRIEVAL

196
      To evaluate the consecutive information retrieval capabilities, we design two multi-modal snippet
197
      retrieval benchmarks based on OBELICS (Laurençon et al., 2024) and zero-shot slide retrieval based
198   on Slideshare-1M (Araujo et al., 2016). Compared to the training dataset MMC4, the OBELICS
199   preserves the original image text interleaved order, which is closer to real-world scenes. The slides
200   in Slidershare-1M are naively interleaved multi-modal data with more complex interleaved forms.
201
      Any-to-Any Consecutive Information Retrieval (AnyCIR). In this task, we aim to retrieve any
202
      modality consecutive information given any modality queries, as shown in Fig. 3(a). The types of
203
      modality include interleaved (IN), Text only (Tx), and Image only (Im), resulting in 9 tasks in total
204   with different combinations. The AnyCIR consists of 20,000 randomly sampled consecutive snippet
205   pairs from distinct documents. Each snippet in the pair includes text and at least one image content.
206   During inference, all the tasks share the same snippet pair source. For retrieval tasks with a single
207   modality, we simply mask other modalities during rendering. We render images into a randomly
208   chosen grid for both queries and candidates.
209
      Sequential Consecutive Information Retrieval (SeqCIR). This task aims to evaluate the fine-
210   grained consecutive information modeling capacity. For each query, the candidate pool consists
211   of 26,433 snippets from 5,000 distinct documents. For each snippet, we use the full text and one
212   randomly selected image if applicable. We use 2,524 snippets as the initial query set, which are the
213   first snippets of the documents. For this task, we iteratively retrieve the next consecutive snippets
214   and only successful retrieval queries are passed to the next iteration. For each iteration, we ignore
215   the preceding snippets of the query snippet in the documents. The Pass@k rate denotes the success
      rate of sequential retrieval at the nth round, as shown in Fig. 3(b). The SeqCIR is a very challenging

224   Zero-Shot Consecutive Slide Retrieval (CSR). To better examine the transferability of Omni-

236   Data Variant Baselines. To better understand the model capacity learned from interleaved data, we
237   further construct different positive pair data as our baselines as illustrated in Fig. 4. Our baselines
238   include 1). Image-Text (Im-Tx) pairs sampled from a LAION subset; 2). Image-Text (Im-Tx) pairs
239
      from the same snippet of MMC4, where we use the MMC4 annotation to generate the pairs, i.e. the
      CLIP similarity assignment; 3). Text-Text (Tx-Tx) pairs by masking all the images in the snippets;
240
      4). Interleaved-Interleaved (IN-IN) pairs by sampling from the snippets pairs containing both image
241
      and text content; 5). Omni224 pairs first rendering in 448 × 448 resolution then resize to 224 × 224
242   resolution for fair comparison with original CLIP model; 6). Omni+/++ denotes 20%/40% of pairs
243   are sampled from one-hop pairs. All baselines use the same training setting.

      Implementation Details. Our implementation is based on OpenCLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021). In all
      experiments, we use ViT-B-16 (Dosovitskiy, 2020) with an input resolution 448×448. We use a
246
      batch size of 1024 and a learning rate of 1e-4 for training 20 epochs. Our pretraining dataset uses
247
      the MMC4-core-fewer-face (Zhu et al., 2024) subset, comprising 5 million documents with both
248   images and text, totaling 17 million images. We use CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) checkpoint as our
249   initialization due to the small scale of our training data.

367   Motivation. In real-world scenarios, much of image-text interleaved content is natively present in
368   visual formats such as screenshots. Therefore, it is natural to develop a single end-to-end modal
369   that can process any modality. Unifying everything into pixels can reduce specialized design for
370   diverse modalities. Moreover, CLIPPO (Tschannen et al., 2023) demonstrates that the vision en-
371   coder can learn meaningful textual representation directly from pixels. While OmniContrast taking
372   a further step towards a more general-purpose vision-centric encoder that can seamlessly understand
373   image, scene text, and their relationship. We acknowledge that layout information (size and posi-
374
      tion) of image-text can be one major benefit of unified pixel space, which has not been fully explored
      in OmniContrast. Because it requires acquiring the exact snippet location from screenshots and is
375
      non-trivial to manipulate the data content, which we left for future work.
376
377   Separate Encoder Baseline. Besides unifying in pixel space, another straightforward approach to
      training CLIP on image-text interleaved data is fusing the image-text in the feature space, similar

474   7          C ONCLUSION
475
476   We introduce OmniContrast, a unified vision model that learns the loosely vision-language corre-
477   spondence from multi-modal documents in a contrastive fashion. To achieve this, OmniContrast use
478   consecutive image-text interleaved snippets as contrast targets and unify all the modalities into the
479   pixel space. Moreover, we propose three consecutive information retrieval benchmarks to demon-
480   strate that multi-modal web documents can empower the CLIP model with new omni-modality un-
481   derstanding capacity. We hope that OmniContrast serves as a stepping stone for exploring multi-
482
      modal documents as valuable training data in the vision-language research community.
483   Although our presented OmniContrast can process any modality input from pixel space using a sin-
484   gle model, its efficiency and scalability are limited by its fixed input size. Future work on designing
485   a dynamic input strategy or specific architecture could significantly enhance the performance and
      unlock more application scenarios for multi-modal web document understanding.

      A primary concern in our work is that the multi-modal document datasets collected from the Internet
489   through common web crawlers may contain unfair or biased data. Despite employing multiple filter-
490   ing steps during the dataset collection process, the presence of unwanted data remains a possibility.
491   Additionally, using a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) checkpoint for model initialization
492   could propagate existing biases inherent in the pre-trained model into our methodology. We are
493   committed to continuously monitoring and mitigating potential biases in both our model and dataset
494   as they are identified. We hope that our research contributes positively and fairly to the field of
495   vision-language understanding research.

664
665   Data Pre-processing. Given a document, we chunked the document into several snippets in a
666   sliding window strategy based on text sequence. For MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024), the document text is
667   stored in a list of sentences. To create snippets, we merge consecutive sentences until their combined
668   length reaches 1100 characters or less. Then we use the image-text assignment provided by MMC4
669   to assign each image to the corresponding snippet. For OBELICS (Laurençon et al., 2024), we first
670   split the text content based on the newline character and then use the same sliding window strategy to
671   generate text snippets. Differently, OBELICS organizes the documents as an image-text interleaved
672   sequence, where the image position is extracted from the original HTML files. In both AnyCIR and
673
      SeqCIR, we assign each image to the closest preceding text snippet, while images appearing at the
      beginning of the document are assigned to the first text snippet.
674
675   Training Data Details. During training, to maintain optimal text length, we apply text masking
676   augmentation only to snippets containing more than four sentences and exceeding 250 characters.
677   Empirically, we found that a maximum text length of 768 characters during training led to better
678   performance. During testing, the model can handle up to 1,100 characters without any degradation
679
      in performance. Therefore, we set the maximum training text length to 768 characters and 1,100
      characters for testing. After initialization from the CLIP pre-trained checkpoint, the positional em-
680
      bedding is randomly initiated for 448×448 input size. For each training batch, the data modalities
681
      are mixed from image, text, and image-text interleaved without specialized balance.
