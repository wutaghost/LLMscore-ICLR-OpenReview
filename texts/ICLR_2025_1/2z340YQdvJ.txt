009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               The modified universality hypothesis proposed by Jones et al. (2022) suggests
012            that adversarially robust models trained for a given task are highly similar. We
013            revisit the hypothesis and test its generality. We find that predictive behavior
014            does not converge with increasing robustness and thus is not universal. Further,
015            with additional similarity measures, we uncover differences in the representations
016            that were invisible with the measures used in prior work. While robust models
017            tend to be more similar than standard models, robust models remain distinct
018            in important aspects. Moreover, the importance of similarity measures when
019            comparing representations is highlighted as the absolute level of similarity—and
020
               thus the assessment of universality—is heavily dependent on the measure used.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The universality hypothesis (Olah et al., 2020) suggests that all trained neural networks for a given
026   task are highly similar. If this hypothesis held generally, interpretability research would be simplified,
027   as insights for a specific model could be more easily transferred to other models. While the hypothesis
028   is unlikely to hold in a strict sense (Li et al., 2015; Breiman, 2001), Jones et al. (2022) proposed
029   and presented evidence fo a modified universality hypothesis (MUH): adversarial robustness may
030   function as a strong prior on neural networks such that adversarially robust models will learn similar
031   representations “regardless of exact training conditions (i.e., architecture, random initialization,
032
      learning parameters)”. They showed empirically that robust CNNs trained on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
      2009) are highly similar in the input features of the data that they use and in the representations they
033
      produce, whereas standard models are not. Thus, training a single robust model is sufficient to mimic
034
      the behavior of any other or in their words ”if you’ve trained one, you’ve trained them all”.
035
036   However, their work has three key limitations which motivate us to revisit the link between robustness
037   and universality. First, the experiments were centered around representational similarity, while one
038
      of the direct and arguably practically most relevant ways to study model similarity is to compare
      their predictions. Second, a key part of the evidence was gathered with Centered Kernel Alignment
039
      (CKA) (Kornblith et al., 2019), a method to measure similarity of representations, which adopts a
040
      specific perspective on neural network similarity and was recently shown to have multiple pitfalls
041   (Cui et al., 2022; Dujmović et al., 2022; Davari et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022). Numerous other
042   similarity measures have been proposed (Klabunde et al., 2023; Sucholutsky et al., 2023), which
043   provide alternative views on neural network similarity. Third, experiments exclusively used ImageNet
044   as input data, which leaves the role of data uncertain, e.g., whether results transfer to other vision
045   datasets or out-of-distribution data.
046
      In this work, we thus critically reassess the modified universality hypothesis that suggests that all
047   adversarially robust models for a given task are highly similar. We conduct an extensive empirical
048   study that involves multiple similarity measures, model architectures and datasets. In contrast to
049   previously published results, our study indicates that robust models cannot be considered universal.
050   Our main contributions are:
051
052         1. We show that predictions of robust models are not universal (see Figure 1). Their agreement
053            scores do not converge with increasing robustness and the variance of Jensen-Shannon
               Divergence (JSD) scores increases with higher robustness levels (Section 3.1).

084   Adversarial Robustness While neural networks achieve high performance in many tasks, they
085   are susceptible to —often imperceptible— modifications of inputs that lead to wrong predictions
086   (Szegedy et al., 2014). These modifications δ are usually computed via a constrained optimization
087   problem:
088                                                            δ ∗ = arg max L(f (x + δ), y) s.t.                                    ∥δ∥p ≤ ϵ,                                       (1)
                                                                                     δ
089
090   where L is the loss function, x, y the input and target, respectively, and ϵ is the strength of the
091   adversarial attack, i.e., the maximal allowed modification of the input. By augmenting training data
092   with adversarial examples, the space of potentially good models is constrained and robust models
093   are produced, which are less susceptible to such attacks (Madry et al., 2019). For these models,
094   perturbations need to be larger to induce misclassifications.
095

099    the agreement rate, respectively. For JSD, we normalize the outputs of the last network layer with a

      Comparing Representations Another approach at testing universality is comparing the internal
115
      representations, i.e., the activation of a layer for some input. Again, if models are universal, we
116   expect that their internal processes are highly similar, which should lead to similar representations.
117   To measure representational similarity, activations are collected for a set of inputs resulting in a
118   matrix R ∈ RN ×D , where N is the number of inputs and D is the number of neurons in the layer. A
119   representational similarity measure typically takes two such matrices as input and produces a single
120   number that quantifies the similarity of these matrices. The matrices may come from different layers
121   or models, but are based on the same set of inputs such that the rows between the matrices correspond.
122   The similarity score respects certain transformations between representations that would keep them
123   equivalent, e.g., switching neuron order, which would result in a different order of the columns of the
124
      compared matrices. For a detailed introduction, we refer to the survey by Klabunde et al. (2023).
125   In this work, we use three similarity measures: linear CKA (Kornblith et al., 2019), Orthogonal
126   Procrustes (Procrustes) (Ding et al., 2021; Williams et al., 2021), and k-NN Jaccard Similarity
127   (Jaccard). Intuitively, these measures summarize the similarity of representations across multiple
128   different aspects, e.g., specific properties of their geometry. These measures have been empirically
129   shown to give meaningful similarity assessments (Klabunde et al., 2024), but highlight different
130
      discrepancies between representations. Thus, employing a set of similarity measures enables a more
      multi-faceted comparison of representations. At the same time, the measures we use consider the
131
      same representations equivalent, i.e., any representations that only differ in rotation, reflection, scale,
132
      and translation. This means we should expect similar similarity scores when representations are close
133   to equivalent.
134

      Image inversion (Ilyas et al., 2019) presents a way to create a model-specific variant of an input that
192
      produces nearly the same representation as the original input, but only consistently contains the input
193   features that the model actually uses. Hence, inverted images enable the study of the similarity of
194   representation mechanisms. If one model has a different mechanism that relies on another set of input
195   features, it will not find those features in the inverted image of another model and thus will be unable
196   to produce a similar representation. Comparing the representations given inverted images gives us
197   information about the similarity of the mechanisms.
198
      To create an inverted image x̃ for a given target image x, a seed image s from a different class is
199   modified such that it produces a representation similar to the representation of the target image. More
200   precisely, let f L (x) ∈ RD be the representation of model f for the target image x in the penultimate
201   layer L, then the inverted image x̃ is computed as the output of
202
                                                  ∥f L (s) − f L (x)∥2
203                                         min                        .                                   (7)
204
                                              s        ∥f L (x)∥2
205   The optimization is done with gradient descent, so the naive solution of s = x is not reached. Instead,
206   the most relevant input features for the model f are introduced to the seed image. As the seed image
207   is sampled randomly from all images with a different class than the target image, feature cooccurrence
208
      in natural images, e.g., dog fur texture and dog ears, can be eliminated if only of those features is
      relevant for f . See Figure 2 for an example.

       3.1              P REDICTIONS OF ROBUST M ODELS N OT U NIVERSAL
243
244
       If adversarially robust models are universal in a strict sense, we would expect that their predictions
245    overlap to a very high degree. Figure 1A shows that this is not the case. On regular images, the
246    agreement between predictions of highly robust models is much lower than the theoretical maximum
247    imposed by small accuracy differences (Fort et al., 2019) and comparable to the agreement between
248    standard models. Comparing the predicted distributions with Jensen-Shannon divergence (Figure 1B)
249    instead of just the final predictions leads to the same conclusion: the predictive behavior is not
250    universal.
251    However, using inverted images as input, which highlight differences in the representation mechanism,
252    reveals that robustness does have a profound impact on similarity of models. Figure 3 shows how
253    predictive behavior on these kind of inputs become more similar with increasing robustness. Jones
254    et al. (2022) showed similar effects for similarity of the representations directly. Nevertheless, the
255    differences in predictions given regular data must have an origin. In the following section, we will
256    present a possible explanation.
257
258    3.2              D IFFERENCES IN R EPRESENTATION M ECHANISMS OF ROBUST M ODELS
259
260    Closely connected to the final predictions are the representations at the penultimate layer of the neural
261    network as they are the input for the final classification layers. Should these representations be highly
262    similar, we intuitively expect that the classification layers will lead to similar predictions. Conversely,
263    if the representations are not similar, similar predictions might still be possible, but we expect them
264    to be less likely. Hence, inspecting the representations may lead to an explanation why predictions of
265    models are different.
266    Using inverted images as inputs lets us study similarity of the whole representation mechanism, i.e.,
267    the combination of input feature reliance and feature processing, as mentioned in Section 2. If this
268    mechanism is similar, predictions should be similar as well as the only degrees of freedom remain in
269    the final classifier layers. Jones et al. (2022) found high similarity between the representations of
       inverted images using CKA, a highly popular similarity measure. We now compare the representation

309    3.3               H OW ROBUST IS THE E FFECT OF ROBUSTNESS ON S IMILARITY ?
310
311    So far, we have shown on ImageNet1k and CIFAR-10 that robustness increases similarity of neural
312
       networks in some aspects, but also that increasing robustness does not eliminate all differences
       between models. However, the degree of model similarity might be impacted by the training data
313
       and we have yet to examine the influence of data in detail. On the one hand, larger datasets might
314
       lead to increased model similarity as more data could lead to less overfitting. On the other hand,
315    larger datasets tend to be more complex, i.e., contain more classes as well as a larger variety of
316    images, which could lead to a more complex loss landscape that makes it difficult to converge to
317    similar optima. Additionally, out-of-distribution (OOD) behavior might differ significantly from
318    in-distribution behavior and is particularly well suited to highlight differences between models (Ding
319    et al., 2021).
320

383   4   D ISCUSSION
384
385   MUH Only Holds Partially With our experiments, we demonstrated that predictions of robust
386   models do not converge with increasing robustness and highlighted the existence of differences in the
387
      representation mechanism. Hence, the MUH does not hold in full. However, consistent with Jones
      et al. (2022), we also observed that representation mechanisms consistently become more similar
388
      with increased robustness, when measured by CKA and Jaccard—albeit slowly. While more work
389
      is needed to understand the origin of the observations in detail, they point towards an interesting
390   direction of future work: why is it that some aspects of models seem to be strongly constrained by
391   robustness whereas others are not?
392
393   (Un-)Interpretability of Representational Similarity Measures As we found in Section 3.2, Pro-
394   crustes highlights that there is some difference in the representations from inverted images. However,
395   in contrast to interpretable measures like agreement on predictions or Jaccard on representations, the
396   score of Procrustes is hard to interpret. While we know from the definition of the similarity measure
397   that representations must be unable to be aligned well, what exactly makes this alignment difficult is
398   unclear. The similarity scores point us towards model pairs that appear dissimilar and is thus useful
399   as a first step, but ultimately gives limited insights into the representation space. We conducted some
400
      exploratory analysis, but finding the origin of a low representational similarity score is not straight
      forward. At the same time, the observation is unlikely to be a defect of the similarity measure as
401
      it performs well in benchmarking tests (Klabunde et al., 2024). This may highlight a fundamental
402
      problem of representational similarity measures trying to break down similarity between objects with
403   many facets into a single number.
404
405
      Impact on Interpretability Research On the one hand, the observed differences point out a attack
406   point for future work to understand both robust models and representational similarity measures
407   better. This could lead to more effective use of similarity measures as explorative tools. On the other
408   hand, our work is another point of evidence against universality in a strong sense, where all parts of a
409   model are highly similar, and towards a world where models consist of universal and non-universal
410   parts. Studying universal parts may be of general interest, whereas non-universal parts may be only
411   interesting for frontier models or specific models with high interest. Hence, identifying universal
412   components is an interesting direction of future work.
413
414   Increasing Robustness Beyond Our Experiments We observed that increasing robustness up
415   to ϵ = 3 for ImageNet models lead to increased similarity in some aspects of the models, e.g., the
416   trend for CKA similarity appears to continue further. Thus, extremely robust models may be a way to
417
      studying the whole model class at once–at least with respect to the aspects that make them similar
      from the CKA perspective. However, increasing robustness even further would likely lead to further
418
      accuracy degradation. Ultimately, such models may be not comparable to more widely used models,
419
      which could make detailed study of these models not worth it despite the aforementioned benefit.
420
421
      Value of Multi-Faceted Similarity Analysis Our work demonstrates that reliance on a single
422
      measure when evaluating model similarity can lead to over- or underestimation of similarity. Com-
423   bining similarity measures and analyzing predictions as well as representations provides a more
424   comprehensive view of relations between models. Thus, the similarity assessment is less prone to rely
425   on shortcomings of individual measures. While this approach comes at a (small) cost—the popular
426   CKA is very fast compared to other measures—we argue that the trade-off is worth it.
427
428   Exchanging Models in Production When a model that delivers predictions to users should be
429   exchanged, e.g., by a more efficient variant, consistency of predictions may be desired (Milani Fard
430   et al., 2016a). The MUH suggested an easy approach to increase prediction consistency by using
431   (slightly) adversarially robust models. As we find that predictions do not become more similar, other
      methods to improve consistency are necessary.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
433
434
435
      Universality The question to what extent models are universal has attracted significant interest in
      prior work. On the one hand, model multiplicity, i.e., the existence of multiple models with almost
436
      equal performance but different input-output behavior or representations, has been studied extensively
437
      (Breiman, 2001; Black et al., 2022; Heljakka et al., 2023). Architecturally similar models trained or
438   updated on near-identical data can differ significantly (Klabunde and Lemmerich, 2023; Somepalli
439   et al., 2022; Marx et al., 2020; Black and Fredrikson, 2021; Liu et al., 2022; McCoy et al., 2020; Li
440   et al., 2015). Modifications to training or inference may be necessary to enforce consistent behavior
441   between different models (Milani Fard et al., 2016b; Summers and Dinneen, 2021). In mechanistic
442   interpretability, a more fine-grained view on universality is taken, i.e., whether the input-output
443   behavior of a network is also implemented in the same way. It leads to further evidence against
444   universality (Zhong et al., 2023; Chughtai et al., 2023).
445   On the other hand, there is evidence for universality in certain scenarios. Some features consistently
446   appear in CNNs (Schubert et al., 2021). Further, attention heads with specific functionality can be
447   found across many transformer-based language models (Olsson et al., 2022; Gould et al., 2023).
448   Additionally, some of their internal processes for tasks such as indirect object identification (Merullo
449   et al., 2023) and retrieval (Variengien and Winsor, 2023) seem to be universal, at least across
450   certain model classes. On the smallest scale, certain neurons appear universal (Gurnee et al., 2024).
451   Further, parts of two different models (trained for the same task) can be connected using simple
452
      transformations with little accuracy loss (Csiszárik et al., 2021; Bansal et al., 2021; Lähner and
      Moeller, 2023; Moschella et al., 2023) indicating representational compatibility (Brown et al., 2023).
453
454   While the two above collections of evidence for and against universality might seem contradicting,
455   the scope of universality as well as what would be considered equivalent between networks differs
456   drastically. In fact, universality has multiple non-binary facets (Gurnee et al., 2024). Further,
457   universality may only occur for certain types of models (Jones et al., 2022).
458
459   Neural Network Similarity To measure similarity of neural networks, especially of their represen-
460   tations, numerous similarity measures have been proposed across machine learning and neuroscience
461   (Klabunde et al., 2023; Sucholutsky et al., 2023). These measures represent different views on what
462   kind of behavior is considered equivalent. Due to its popularity, Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA)
463   (Kornblith et al., 2019) has attracted particular interest and was also used by Jones et al. (2022) who
464   propose the hypothesis of universality across robust models. However, several caveats of CKA are
465
      known: few data points may dominate the similarity score (Nguyen et al., 2022), the choice of inputs
      may determine similarity measurements in early layers (Cui et al., 2022), and scores are generally
466
      brittle (Davari et al., 2022).
467
468
469   6    C ONCLUSION
470
471
      We revisit the modified universality hypothesis which states that adversarially trained models are
472
      highly similar. We show that predictions of robust models are not universal as their agreement on
473
      regular images does not converge with robustness. Additionally, the similarity of representation
474   mechanisms, i.e., the combination of input feature reliance and processing of the input into a
475   representation, is highly dependent on the way similarity is measured. While CKA shows that robust
476   representation mechanisms are highly similar, ProcrustesSim does not increase with robustness.
477   Our multi-faceted similarity analysis reveals that while adversarial training causes robust models to
478   converge to some degree, they still exhibit critical differences that some similarity measures fail to
479   detect, but affect predictive behavior. Our results show that the modified universality hypothesis in its
480   original form does not hold and highlights the importance of using a broader set of measures when
481   evaluating model similarity.

770   A.1     I MAGE N ET 1 K M ODELS
771
772   Table 1 shows all model architectures with their accuracy and number of parameters. We use seven
773   L2 -robust CNNs: ResNet-18, ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016), Wide ResNet-50-2, Wide ResNet-50-4
774   (Zagoruyko and Komodakis, 2017), ResNeXt-50 32x4d (Xie et al., 2017), Densenet-161 (Huang
775   et al., 2017), and VGG-16-BN (Simonyan and Zisserman, 2015) along with a TinyViT (Wu et al.,
776
      2022) trained from scratch.
777
778
      Training Details (Salman et al., 2020) trained their L2 -robust ImageNet models for 90 epochs
      using an initial learning rate of 0.1 which is reduced every 30 epochs by a factor of 10. The training
779
      uses stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a batch size of 512, a momentum of 0.9 and weight
780
      decay of 1e−4 . For standard training, cross-entropy was used as a loss function. Robust training was
781   conducted using projected gradient descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2019) allowing L2 perturbation of
782   the respective ϵ value. Adversarial examples were generate in three attack steps with a step size of 23 ϵ.
783   The TinyViT models were trained with the same setup but with a lower batch size of 256. We used an
784   identical setting for training the remaining ImageNet1k models.
785
786   Inverted Images Inverted images were generated on the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition
787   Challenge 2012 (ILSVRC2012) validation set using the Robustness library (Engstrom et al., 2019).
788   First 10,000 target images were randomly sampled from the dataset. Then, for each sampled image, a
789   seed image was sampled at random. If the sampled seed image had the same class as the target, a
790
      new image was sampled until seed and target classes were different. To generate an inverted image,
      the seed image was modified in three steps and the best result taken.

      Training Details The training procedure for ImageNet50 models was the same as for ImageNet1k
      with a few deviations. As models converged faster on the smaller datasets, we reduced the epochs to
827
      60 with the exception of VGG-16-BN for ϵ = 1 and ϵ = 0.5. As the VGG-16-BN models with ϵ = 3
828
      and ϵ = 0.25 initially did not converge, we reduced the learning rate to 0.05 and trained them with
829   batch size 256. Due to availability of hardware, other models were trained with batch size 128 for
830   regular training, except DenseNet-161 with batch size 64. For adversarial training, batch size was
831   reduced to 64 and 32, respectively.
