009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Domain-Incremental Learning (DIL) involves the progressive adaptation of a model
012            to new concepts across different domains. While recent advances in pre-trained
013            models provide a solid foundation for DIL, learning new concepts often results
014            in the catastrophic forgetting of pre-trained knowledge. Specifically, sequential
015            model updates can overwrite both the representation and the classifier with knowl-
016            edge from the latest domain. Thus, it is crucial to develop a representation and
017            corresponding classifier that accommodate all seen domains throughout the learn-
018
               ing process. To this end, we propose DUal ConsolidaTion (D UCT) to unify and
               consolidate historical knowledge at both the representation and classifier levels.
019
               By merging the backbone of different stages, we create a representation space
020
               suitable for multiple domains incrementally. The merged representation serves as a
021            balanced intermediary that captures task-specific features from all seen domains.
022            Additionally, to address the mismatch between consolidated embeddings and the
023            classifier, we introduce an extra classifier consolidation process. Leveraging class-
024            wise semantic information, we estimate the classifier weights of old domains within
025            the latest embedding space. By merging historical and estimated classifiers, we
026            align them with the consolidated embedding space, facilitating incremental classi-
027            fication. Extensive experimental results on four benchmark datasets demonstrate
028            D UCT’s state-of-the-art performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent years have seen the rise of deep learning, demonstrating its strong potential in real-world
      applications (He et al., 2016; Deng et al., 2009; Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020). However, in a dynamic
034
      and ever-changing world, data often evolves in a stream format (Aggarwal, 2018), necessitating
035
      continuous updates with data from new domains (Gama et al., 2014). For instance, autonomous
036   vehicles must handle driving tasks across different seasons and weather conditions (Bojarski et al.,
037   2016), and face recognition systems are supposed to recognize users despite the varying lighting
038   conditions (Zhao et al., 2003). Correspondingly, Domain-Incremental Learning (DIL) (van de Ven
039   et al., 2022) addresses this challenge by absorbing new knowledge from new data distributions while
040   preserving existing knowledge. However, sequentially updating the model with new domains often
041   biases the embedding (Yu et al., 2020) and classifier (Zhao et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019) modules
042   toward the latest domain, leading to catastrophic forgetting (Mermillod et al., 2013; De Lange et al.,
043   2021) of previous knowledge. Therefore, developing methods for learning new domains without
044
      forgetting existing knowledge becomes a critical challenge for the machine learning community.
045   To combat catastrophic forgetting, recent works leverage strong pre-trained models (PTMs)(Han et al.,
046   2021) as the initialization for DIL(Wang et al., 2022d;c;b). PTMs’ robust representation abilities
047   provide a powerful starting point, showing promising results in DIL benchmarks. These approaches
048   freeze the pre-trained backbone to preserve existing knowledge and append lightweight modules (Jia
049   et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022; Lian et al., 2022) to capture new patterns. However, since incoming
050
      domains will also overwrite the appended modules, the representations still suffer from forgetting,
      leading to corresponding bias in the classifier.
051
052   In DIL, there are two primary sources of forgetting: the overwriting of features and classifiers.
053   Continually adjusting the features to capture the latest task biases the representations toward the
      newest domain. For example, suppose the previous task involves real-world photos while the


                                                        1

054
      subsequent task includes quick-draw images. The features will be overwhelmingly adjusted to
055   highlight non-objective details, which are less helpful in distinguishing the previous domain, thus
056   leading to forgetting. In light of this, an ideal feature space in DIL should fit all domains and
057   equally highlight the domain-specific features for all seen tasks. However, as features become less
058   generalizable due to overwriting, the classifier also becomes biased toward the latest domain. Since
059   the classifier plays the pivotal role of mapping embeddings to classes, using the biased classifier can
060   cause detrimental effects to decision-making. Consequently, it is imperative to consolidate existing
061   knowledge both in the feature and classifier to resist forgetting.
062   There are two main challenges to achieving this goal. 1) Building a unified embedding space that
063   suits all tasks continually. Since the training data arrives in a stream format, we cannot access all
064   training instances simultaneously, preventing us from achieving the ‘oracle embedding’ that favors
065   all seen domains. 2) Calibrating the prediction of biased classifiers. Even if we achieve a holistic
066   embedding suitable for all tasks, we still cannot build an accurate mapping between features and
067   classes due to the lack of previous instances. Consequently, a classifier consolidation process is
068   needed to align the classifier weights with the changing features continually.
069   In this paper, we propose DUal ConsolidaTion (D UCT) to address these challenges. To counter
070   feature drift, we introduce a representation merging technique that continuously integrates historical
071   backbones. This merged embedding consolidates task-specific information from all previous domains,
072   providing informative features without forgetting. Consequently, we achieve non-forgettable features
073   incrementally and resist feature-level forgetting. To map updated features to classes, we design
074   a classifier consolidation process that merges historical classifier weights with calibrated weights.
075
      By extracting class-wise semantic relationships in the joint space, we develop a classifier transport
      mechanism that estimates classifiers of previous domain classes in the latest embedding space.
076
      Through consolidating features and classifiers in this coordinated way, D UCT balances all seen
077
      domains and robustly resists forgetting, achieving competitive performance on various benchmarks.

080   2    R ELATED W ORK
081
082
      Continual Learning/Incremental Learning is a popular topic in the machine learning field, aiming
083
      to incorporate new knowledge within streaming data (De Lange et al., 2021; Masana et al., 2023;
084
      Wang et al., 2024). It can be further classified into three subcategories, i.e., task-incremental
085   learning (TIL) (De Lange et al., 2021), class-incremental learning (CIL) (Masana et al., 2023),
086   and domain-incremental learning (DIL) (van de Ven et al., 2022). Among them, TIL and CIL are
087   faced with emerging new classes and are required to learn them without forgetting. By contrast,
088   DIL mainly focuses on the scenario where label space is fixed while incoming data involves new
089   domains (Wang et al., 2022b; Shi & Wang, 2023). In continual learning, typical solutions include
090   using data replay (Bang et al., 2021; Aljundi et al., 2019b; Isele & Cosgun, 2018; Rolnick et al., 2019;
091   Ratcliff, 1990) to review former knowledge, using knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015; Rebuffi
092   et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017) or parameter regularization (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al.,
093
      2017; Chaudhry et al., 2018; Aljundi et al., 2018; 2019a) to maintain existing knowledge. Recent
      advances also resort to bias correction (Hou et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2020; Ahn et al., 2021; Castro
094
      et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Belouadah & Popescu, 2019) to rectify the model bias or expand the
095
      network structure as data evolves (Rusu et al., 2016; Aljundi et al., 2017; Yan et al., 2021; Wang
096   et al., 2022a; Zhou et al., 2023).
097
098   Domain-Incremental Learning with Pre-Trained Models: With the rapid development of pre-
099
      training techniques, PTMs provide a strong initialization for DIL models to boost the feature gen-
      eralizability (Smith et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022c;d;b). Current PTM-based DIL methods mainly
100
      resort to visual prompt tuning (Jia et al., 2022) to adjust the pre-trained features. With the pre-trained
101
      features frozen, it learns a prompt pool as external knowledge and selects instance-specific prompts
102   to encode task information. L2P (Wang et al., 2022d) utilizes the query-key matching mechanism
103   to select instance-specific prompts, while DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022c) extends it by learning
104   task-specific and instance-specific prompts jointly. By contrast, S-Prompts (Wang et al., 2022b) learns
105   domain-specific prompts for DIL and retrieves prompts via KNN search. To alleviate the prompt
106   selection cost, CODA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023) modifies the prompt selection process into an
107   attention-based weighted combination. Apart from prompt tuning, there are also some methods that
      directly build classifiers upon the pre-trained features (Zhou et al., 2024a; McDonnell et al., 2023).

108
      Model Merging: Recent advances in pre-training have made weight interpolation among different
109   models a useful technique in model editing (Ainsworth et al., 2023; Frankle et al., 2020; Ilharco et al.,
110   2023; Matena & Raffel, 2021; Wortsman et al., 2022a;b). Most work focuses on simple averaging
111   of multiple models, aiming to enhance model in terms of its performance on the single task and
112   robustness to out-of-distribution data. In this paper, we consider merging the pre-trained weights and
113   task vector parameters to cultivate multitask representation for all seen domains.

169   specific knowledge into the embedding, but also comes with the risk of feature being fully overwritten
170   by new domains since there is no restriction on retaining previous knowledge. Besides, although
171   Eq. 3 freezes the pre-trained weights, the trainable prompts appended may also incur forgetting. Since
172   the prompted feature relies on instance-specific prompt selection, optimizing the prompts for new
173   domains still has the risk of overwriting previous ones. With the features being biased toward the
174   latest domain, the classifier is also adjusted upon it. Consequently, the classifiers of previous tasks are
175   incompatible with the unstable and ever-changing features, resulting in the classifier-level forgetting.

      4     D UCT : D UAL C ONSOLIDATION FOR D OMAIN -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
178
179
      Observing the risk of feature-level and classifier-level overwriting, we aim to resist forgetting in DIL
180   from two aspects. Firstly, alleviating feature-level forgetting requires obtaining a unified embedding
181   space at a low cost. Since sequentially updating the backbone or prompts will both result in forgetting,
182   a proper solution is needed to make full use of all historical features and consolidate them into a
183   unified one. Secondly, as the representation changes from task to task, the mismatch between classifier
184   and embedding becomes more severe as the data evolves. Consequently, a classifier consolidation
185   step is needed to calibrate them to be compatible with the embeddings.
186   In the following sections, we introduce the learning paradigm for representation consolidation and
187   classifier consolidation. Lastly, we provide detailed guidelines for training and inference.
188
189
      4.1   R EPRESENTATION C ONSOLIDATION
190
191   Observing the challenge of striking a balance between all seen domains, we need to opt for another
192   feature updating policy to avoid sequential overwriting and resist forgetting. Let us assume an ideal
193   scenario where we can separately train each model for each incoming domain, obtaining a set of
194   models {ϕ1 (·), W1 }, · · · , {ϕB (·), WB }. Those models are obtained by optimizing the same PTM
195   via Eq. 2, thus having the discriminability for each specific domain and can be seen as the ‘domain
196   expert.’ In an oversimplified scenario where we know which domain the instance is from, we can
197   directly utilize the corresponding expert for prediction. However, since such auxiliary information is
198
      unavailable in DIL, we need to obtain a omnipotent embedding space that suits all domains. In this
      way, there is no need to decide which embedding to use since the omnipotent embedding is strong
199
      enough to capture all domain-specific features.
200

202   et al., 2023) that an ideal model for multiple domains can be achieved by combining multiple task
203   vectors. We denote the relative change of the embedding function as δϕi = ϕi − ϕ0 , where ϕ0 is the
204
      weight of the pre-trained model. Hence, δϕi represents the relative change of the weights in the i-th
      domain, and we can build the unified embedding via:

261   4.2    C LASSIFIER C ONSOLIDATION
262
      In Eq. 5, we design the feature consolidation process, which aggregates multiple fine-tuned models
263
      by accumulating task vectors. However, a fatal problem still exists, i.e., there is a mismatch between
264   the classifiers and the consolidated embeddings. Since the classifiers are optimized to match the
265   embeddings to the corresponding class, the matching degree drastically decays as the backbone is
266   replaced with another one. Recalling the background information in Section 3.1 that we utilize an
267   independent classifier for each domain, we need to design the extra classifier consolidation process
268   to align the classifiers to the embedding space. In each training stage, we denote the classifier for
269   previous domains as ‘old classifier’ (Wo ) and the classifier for the current domain as ‘new classifier’
      (Wn ). We then discuss how to align them with the consolidated features.

278   Old Classifier Transport: Eq. 8 aligns the new classifier to the latest embedding space. However, a
279   fatal problem still exists, i.e., the old classifier is also incompatible with the merged embedding space.
280   Consequently, the previous knowledge of old domains shall be forgotten in the incremental learning
281   process. If we have plenty of training instances of previous domains, a similar calibration process can
282   be done as in Eq. 8. However, due to the exemplar-free restrictions, we cannot save any previous
283   instances in the memory. Hence, we need to find another way to estimate the relative calibration
284   weight for the old classifier. For example, if we have the retrained classifier to classify a ‘lion’ in
285   the clip art style, it can be mostly reused to classify the lion in the real photo style. We call such a
286
      relationship ‘semantic information,’ and the goal is to utilize such information to assist old classifier
287
      alignment. We denote the calibrated classifier using semantic information as Ŵo = T (Wn , S), i.e.,
      the estimated classifier is obtained by transforming the new classifier using semantic information S.
288
      Hence, there remain two core problems to be solved: 1) How to define the transformation function
289
      T ? 2) How to define the semantic information S?

291   Implementing T via Optimal Transport: The function T encodes the correlation between the set
292   of features among two tasks. Considering that the weight matrix of a linear layer reveals the relative
293
      relationship among feature-class pairs and the final logit is the aggregation of all features, we can
      design the recombination of exiting classifiers with a linear mapping T ∈ Rβ×γ . T encodes the
294
      cross-task correlation between the current and the previous domains, where larger values in T denote
295
      higher class-wise similarity. Since the decision is made by matching the classifier with corresponding
296   features, we can utilize and recombine the weights of similar classes in the current domain to obtain
297   those of previous domains. For example, important features that discriminate lions in the clip art style
298   should be assigned higher coefficients to help classify lions in the photo style and vice versa.
299

      uniform distribution. To map a set of classes to another between old and new domains, a cost matrix

             +    is further introduced to guide the transition. The larger weight of Qi,j indicates we need

       Parameter Robustness: There are two major consolidation steps in D UCT, i.e., the representation
472
       consolidation in Eq. 5 and the classifier consolidation in Eq. 10. These consolidation steps include
473
       the merging parameters between a set of models/classifiers, i.e., the backbone merge ratio αϕ and the
474    classifier merge ratio αW . In this section, we conduct ablations on the choice of these parameters
475    to investigate the robustness of D UCT with change of them. Specifically, we choose αϕ and αW
476    among {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0}, resulting in 25 parameter combinations. We conduct experiments
477    with these parameter combinations on the Office-Home dataset and show the average performance in
478    Figure 3(c). As we can infer from the figure, D UCT is generally robust to parameter changes. Besides,
479    since the merge parameters are designed to trade-off between old and new knowledge, we find a
480    small value (e.g., 0.1) or large value (e.g., 1.0) works poorly. The poor performance of these marginal
481    parameters also indicates the importance of the merging process since a small value or large value
482
       equals to ignoring part of the important information during consolidation. By contrast, both of them
       prefer a mild value, i.e., choosing them around {0.25, 0.5} shows better performance. Hence, we
483
       suggest setting αϕ = 0.5, αw = 0.5 as the default setting.

529   6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
532   Domain-incremental learning is a desired ability for real-world learning systems to obtain new
533   knowledge. In this paper, we propose dual consolidation (D UCT) to achieve this goal. Since the
534   forgetting in DIL occurs in two aspects, i.e., the embedding and the classifier, we separately design
535   the consolidation technique to handle them. Specifically, we consider merging the pre-trained model
536
      with domain-specific task vectors to achieve the unified embedding. To compensate for the mismatch
      between consolidated features and classifiers, we design the classifier consolidation process by
537
      introducing semantic-guided transport. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of D UCT.
538
539   Limitations: Possible limitations include the utilization of PTMs since feature consolidation relies
      on generalizable initialization. Future work includes extending D UCT to non-PTM scenarios.

902
903             • DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019)1 is a dataset of common objects in six different domains.
904               All domains include 345 categories of objects, such as Bracelets, planes, birds, and cellos.
905               The domains include clipart — a collection of clipart images; real — photos and real-world
906               images; sketch — sketches of specific objects; infograph — infographic images with specific

908               — drawings of the worldwide players of the game ‘Quick Draw!’. We use the officially
909               recommended version ‘Cleaned’ in this paper.

911               which contains four domains where each domain consists of 65 categories. The four domains
912               are Art — artistic images in the form of sketches, paintings, ornamentation, etc.; Clipart
913               — a collection of clipart images; Product — images of objects without a background; and
914               Real-World — images of objects captured with a regular camera. It contains 15,500 images,
915               with an average of around 70 images per class and a maximum of 99 images in a class.

1006   In this section, we introduce the methods that were compared in the main paper. Note that we
1007   re-implement all methods using the same pre-trained model as initialization. They are listed as
1008   follows.
1009
                  • Finetune is a simple baseline in DIL, which directly optimizes the model with cross-entropy
1010
                    loss. It will suffer catastrophic forgetting since there is no restriction on preserving previous
1011
                    knowledge.
1012
1013
                  • Replay (Ratcliff, 1990) is an exemplar-based method, which saves a set of exemplars from
                    previous domains (i.e., in this paper, we save 10 exemplars per class) and replay them
1014
                    when learning new domains. Hence, forgetting can be alleviated since the model can revisit
1015
                    informative instances from previous domains when learning new ones. Of note, for classes
1016                with fewer than 10 instances, repeatable sampling is allowed to conform to the requirement.
1017
                  • iCaRL (Rebuffi et al., 2017) is a knowledge distillation-based continual learning algorithm,
1018
                    which saves the previous model in memory. During updating, apart from the cross-entropy
1019
                    loss for learning new tasks, it also introduces the knowledge distillation loss between old and
1020                new models to avoid forgetting. It also requires saving an increasing number of exemplars.
1021
                  • MEMO (Zhou et al., 2023) is an expansion-based continual learning algorithm that partially
1022
                    expands the network to catch new features. As for the implementation, we follow the
1023
                    original paper to decouple the network and expand the last transformer block for each new
1024                task.

             • SimpleCIL (Zhou et al., 2024a) proposes this simple baseline in pre-trained model-based
               continual learning. It freezes the backbone representation, extracts the class center of each
1046
               class, and utilizes a cosine classifier updated by assigning class centers to the classifier
1047
               weights.
1048
1049
             • L2P (Wang et al., 2022d) is the first work introducing prompt tuning in continual learning.
               With the pre-trained weights frozen, it learns a prompt pool containing many prompts.
1050
               During training and inference, instance-specific prompts are selected to produce the instance-
1051
               specific embeddings. However, as alluded to before, learning new domains will lead to the
1052           overwriting of existing prompts, thus triggering forgetting.
1053
             • DualPrompt (Wang et al., 2022c) extends L2P in two aspects. Apart from the prompt
1054
               pool and prompt selection mechanism, it further introduces prompts instilled at different
1055
               depths and task-specific prompts. During training and inference, the instance-specific and
1056           task-specific prompts work together to adjust the embeddings.
1057
             • CODA-Prompt (Smith et al., 2023) aims to avoid the prompt selection cost in L2P. It treats
1058
               prompts in the prompt pool as bases and utilizes the attention results to combine multiple
1059
               prompts as the instance-specific prompt.
1060
1061
             • EASE (Zhou et al., 2024b) designs lightweight feature expansion technique with adapters
               to learn new features as data devolves. To fetch a classifier with the same dimension as ever-
1062
               expanding features, it utilizes class-wise similarity to complete missing class prototypes.
1063
1064         • RanPAC (McDonnell et al., 2023) extends SimpleCIL by randomly projecting the features
1065
               into the high-dimensional space and learning the online LDA classifier for final classification.
1066         • S-iPrompt (Wang et al., 2022b) is specially designed for pre-trained model-based domain-
1067           incremental learning. It learns task-specific prompts for each domain and saves domain
1068           centers in the memory with K-Means. During inference, it first forwards the features to
1069
               select the nearest domain center via KNN search. Afterward, the selected prompt will be
               appended to the input.
