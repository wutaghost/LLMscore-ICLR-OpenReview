                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Diffusion models have emerged as powerful tools for solving inverse problems
010
               due to their exceptional ability to model complex prior distributions. However,
011
               existing methods predominantly assume known forward operators (i.e., non-blind),
012            limiting their applicability in practical settings where acquiring such operators
013            is costly. Additionally, many current approaches rely on pixel-space diffusion
014            models, leaving the potential of more powerful latent diffusion models (LDMs)
015            underexplored. In this paper, we introduce LatentDEM, an innovative technique
016            that addresses more challenging blind inverse problems using latent diffusion priors.
017            At the core of our method is solving blind inverse problems within an iterative
018            Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework: (1) the E-step recovers clean images
019            from corrupted observations using LDM priors and a known forward model, and
020
               (2) the M-step estimates the forward operator based on the recovered images.
               Additionally, we propose two novel optimization techniques tailored for LDM
021
               priors and EM frameworks, yielding more accurate and efficient blind inversion
022
               results. As a general framework, LatentDEM supports both linear and non-linear
023            inverse problems. Beyond common 2D image restoration tasks, it enables new
024            capabilities in non-linear 3D inverse rendering problems. We validate LatentDEMвҖҷs
025            performance on representative 2D blind deblurring and 3D pose-free sparse-view
026            reconstruction tasks, demonstrating its superior efficacy over prior arts.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Inverse problems aim to recover underlying signals x from partial or corrupted observations y
032   generated by a forward operator AП• (В·). Such problems are prevalent in computer vision and graphics,
033
      encompassing a variety of tasks ranging from 2D image restoration(denoising, deblurring, and
      inpainting (Bertero et al., 2021; Bertalmio et al., 2000)) to 3D reconstruction(CT, NLOS, inverse
034
      rendering (Marschner, 1998; Mait et al., 2018; Faccio et al., 2020)), etc. Typically, inverse problem
035
      solvers assume the forward model A and its physical parameters П• are known (i.e., non-blind) (Schuler
036   et al., 2013). However, acquiring accurate forward models is often challenging or impractical in
037   real-world settings. This necessitates solving blind inverse problems, where both the hidden signals
038   x and the forward model parameters П• must be jointly estimated.
039
      Being heavily ill-posed, inverse problems largely rely on data priors in their computation. Traditional
040
      supervised learning approaches train an end-to-end neural network to map observations directly
041
      to hidden images (y вҶ’ x) (Li et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2017; McCann et al., 2017). Recently,
042   diffusion models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) have emerged
043   as powerful inverse problem solvers due to their exceptional ability to model the complex data
044   distribution p(x) of underlying signals x. DMs approximate p(x) by learning the distributionвҖҷs score
045   function вҲҮxt log pt (xt ) (Song & Ermon, 2019), allowing data-driven priors to be integrated into
046   Bayesian inverse problem solvers (e.g., diffusion posterior sampling (Chung et al., 2022b)). Later,
047   latent diffusion models (LDMs) have evolved as a new foundational model standard (Rombach et al.,
048   2022) by projecting signals into a lower-dimensional latent space z and performing diffusion there.
049   This strategy mitigates the curse of dimensionality typical in pixel-space DMs and demonstrates
050
      superior capability, flexibility, and efficiency in modeling complex, high-dimensional distributions,
      such as those of videos, audio, and 3D objects (Rombach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Stan et al.,
051
      2023; Blattmann et al., 2023).
052

      Diffusion Models for 2D Inverse Problems. DMs have been applied to a wide range of 2D inverse
114
      problems, including natural image deblurring, denoising, super-resolution and fusion tasks (Wang
115   et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022b;c;d; Feng & Bouman, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), as well as medical
116   and astronomy image enhancement (Song et al., 2021a; Chung & Ye, 2022; Wang et al., 2022).
117   Diffusion Posterior Sampling (DPS) pioneered the use of DMs as strong data priors to solve non-blind
118   2D inverse problems in a maximum-a-posteriori (MAP) manner (Chung et al., 2022b;c;d). Later
119   works (Rout et al., 2024; Song et al., 2023) evolved DPS with Latent Diffusion Model (LDM)
120   priors, demonstrating improved performance due to better priors. While these methods (Chung
121   et al., 2022b; Rout et al., 2024; Song et al., 2023) all address non-blind problems, BlindDPS (Chung
122   et al., 2022a) extends DPS to the blind setting by modeling diffusion priors of both data and forward
123
      model parameters. Similar to our approach, FastEM (Laroche et al., 2024) proposes to address blind
      inversion within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. However, Chung et al. (2022a);
124
      Laroche et al. (2024) remain limited to pixel-based DMs, as the instability of LDMs makes the
125
      optimization even harder. In this paper, we investigate how to integrate more powerful LDM priors
126   with EM frameworks in blind inversion tasks and demonstrate new state-of-the-art results.
127
128   Diffusion Models for 3D Inverse Problems. 3D reconstruction from 2D images, also known as
129
      inverse graphics, has long been a significant goal in the fields of vision and graphics (Loper & Black,
      2014; Chen et al., 2019; Mildenhall et al., 2020). Recently, diffusion models are also largely involved
130
      in tackling this problem (Poole et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; MГјller et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023b;
131
      Liu et al., 2023b). In this context, the underlying signals x and the observation y represent 3D data
132   and 2D images, while A denotes the forward rendering process and П• are the camera parameters.
133   Although the most straightforward way is to directly model 3D distributions (MГјller et al., 2023;
134   Zeng et al., 2022), this way is not feasible due to the scarcity of 3D data (Chang et al., 2015; Deitke
135   et al., 2023). Alternatively, recent works focus on utilizing 2D diffusion priors to recover 3D scenes
136   with SDS loss (Poole et al., 2022) but suffer from view inconsistency issues (Lin et al., 2023; Tang
137   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023).
138   To mitigate this problem, a branch of work fine-tunes Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) with multi-
139   view images, transforming LDMs into conditional renderers (Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023b;
140   Tewari et al., 2023). Given an image and its camera parameter, they predict the corresponding
141   novel views of the same 3D object. In other words, these models can also be utilized to provide 3D
142   data priors. However, existing methods typically operate in a feed-forward fashion, still leading to
143   accumulated inconsistency during novel view synthesis and requiring further correction designs (Shi
144   et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024; 2023a). In contrast, LatentDEM treats the sparse-view 3D reconstruc-
145   tion (Jiang et al., 2023) task as a blind inverse problem. Given sparse-view input images without
146
      knowing their poses, we apply Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023b) priors to jointly optimize their relative cam-
      era parameters and synthesize new views. Our method utilizes information of all input views (Song
147
      et al., 2023) and produces significantly better view-consistent objects compared to feed-forward
148
      baselines (Liu et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023).

227   We now describe how to solve blind inverse problems using latent diffusion priors. Our method is
228   formulated within a variational Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework, where the signals and
229   forward operators are iteratively optimized through EM iterations. In the E-step (Sec.4.1), we leverage
230
      latent diffusion priors to draw signal posterior samples, where we introduce an annealing technique
      to stabilize the optimization process. In the M-step (Sec.4.2), we estimate forward operators in a
231
      maximum-a-posteriori (MAP) manner, and adopt a skip-gradient method to improve the efficiency.
232
      In Sec.4.3, we show how our framework can be applied to solve representative problems such as 2D
233   blind deblurring and 3D pose-free sparse-view reconstruction.

      Different from pixel-space diffusion models, latent diffusion models require encoding and decoding
298
      operations that map between latent space and pixel space (i.e., x = DвҲ— (z) and z = E вҲ— (x)),
299   which takes primary time consumption. Therefore, we further design an acceleration method

301   EM iteration, the E-step comprises two sub-steps: prior-based diffusion (вҲҮzt log pt (z t )) and data
302   likelihood-based diffusion (вҲҮzt log pt (y|z t )). (See Eq. 6 for the two terms). The former happens

305   these operations to accelerate the training process:
306
      Technique 2 (Skip gradient) In early stages (t > ST ), performing K times E-step in latent space
307
      with вҲҮzt log pt (z t ) only, then perform the whole EM-step in both latent space and image space.
308
309   We refer to this new technique as Skip gradient. We find it largely accelerates the training process
310   without hurting the performance for two reasons. First, similar to the annealing case, in the early
311   stages of the diffusion posterior sampling process, the sampled data xМӮ0 and forward parameters П• are
312   far from the true value, making frequent LDM encoding and decoding unnecessary, as they wonвҖҷt
313   provide useful gradient signals. Second, while the skip-gradient steps rely only on unconditional
314   latent diffusion (вҲҮzt log pt (z t )), the optimization still partially follows the previous conditional

509   5.3   A BLATION S TUDIES
510
      Annealed Consistency. A major problem when using LDMs instead of pixel-based DMs is the
511
      vulnerable latent space optimization. In the context of blind inverse problems, the inaccurate
512
      forward operators at the beginning could make the problem even worse, where the optimal solutions
513   significantly deviate from the true value and contain strong image artifacts, as demonstrated in Fig. 5
514   right. To ensure stable optimization, we should set our empirical annealing coefficients(О¶t anneals
515   linearly from 10 at t = 1000 to 1 at t = 600 and then holds) based on the forward modeling errors,
516   as shown in Fig. 5. This technique show stabilizes the optimization process and produces a more
517   accurate estimation (Fig. 5 right). We provide more annealing analysis in Appendix B, which explains
518   why annealing consistency aligns better with the blind inversion problem and brings more stable
519   optimization, as well as superior performance.
520
521   Skip Gradient for Acceleration. We also investigate the influence of the skip-gradient technique,
522   where we compute the full EM step every K steps while in the middle steps we only run the latent
523
      space diffusion. We validate 4 different groups of hyperparameters and compare their running
      times and imaging quality on 2D blind deblurring task (Table 2). We find the running time for
524
      LatentDEM linearly decreases as the number of accelerated steps M increases. In the accelerated
525
      steps, the encoder-decoder inference and gradients are skipped, therefore significantly reducing the
526   total optimization time. Moreover, though we have skipped a lot of computation burden, due to the
527   fact that the diffusion tends to follow the previous optimization trajectory, it still results in meaningful
528   convergence. In an extreme setting (case f) where we skip 900 gradients, our method still outperforms
529   baselines, as well as achieving the fastest optimization speed.

533
      In this work, we proposed LatentDEM, a novel method that incorporates powerful latent diffusion
534   priors for solving blind inverse problems. Our method jointly estimates underlying signals and
535   forward operator parameters using an EM framework with two carefully designed optimization
536   techniques, which reveals more accurate and efficient 2D blind debluring performance than prior arts,
537   as well as demonstrates new capabilities in 3D pose-free sparse-view reconstruction. Its limitation
538   includes that in 3D tasks it still relies on LDMs fine-tuned with multi-view images. It is interesting to
539   think about how to combine LatentDEM with SDS loss to directly run 3D inference from purely 2D
      diffusion models. The code and project page are available upon request.

       Annealing of View-consistent Diffusion. An annealing strategy is essential for the view-consistent
1027   diffusion process in pose-free 3D reconstruction due to initial inaccuracies in estimated camera poses.
1028   In a two-image-based 3D reconstruction problem, for instance, model errors are quantified by ОҪt in
1029   Eq. 25. This error term starts large and gradually decreases to zero as the camera pose П•2 becomes
1030   increasingly accurate. Consequently, Оіt progressively increases from nearly 0 to 0.5. During early
1031   stages, the diffusion process primarily relies on a single reference view y 1 , and the intermediate
1032   generative images are utilized to calibrate camera poses of other images. As camera poses gain
1033   accuracy, the other images exert growing influence on the diffusion process, ultimately achieving
1034   view-consistent results.

       Vanilla EM v.s. Diffusion EM. Traditional EM algorithms perform both E-step and M-step until
1212   convergence at each iteration before alternating. This approach guarantees a local optimal solution,
1213   as demonstrated by numerous EM studies. However, in the context of diffusion posterior sampling,
1214   which involves multiple reverse diffusion steps, this paradigm proves inefficient and computationally
1215   expensive.
1216
       Our implementation of the EMDiffusion algorithm for blind inverse tasks deviates from this conven-
1217
       tional approach. Instead of waiting for the diffusion process to converge (typically 1,000 reverse
1218   steps with a DDIM scheduler), we perform model parameter estimation (M-step) after each single
1219   diffusion reverse step, except during the initial stage where we employ the skip gradient technique, as
1220   detailed in Sec. 4.2. Table 6 compares the performance of vanilla EM and our method on the blind
1221   deblurring task. Our approach, LatentDEM, requires only 1.5 minutes compared to vanilla EMвҖҷs 120
1222   minutes, while achieving superior reconstruction quality and kernel estimation.
1223
       Besides, weвҖҷve tested running a second iteration of the full diffusion process once the forward model
1224   parameters are determined. Specifically, we first used our original setting to estimate the blur kernel,
1225   then fixed the kernel and ran another 1000 diffusion steps (equivalent to 1000 E-steps only) from
1226   Gaussian noise. As reported in Fig. 11, the quality of reconstructed images from the second iteration
1227   is comparable to the original single-iteration implementation. This is likely because our annealing
1228   techniques help avoid overfitting to wrong images with the guidance of inaccurate blur kernels at
1229   early diffusion stages, so the second iteration slightly helps but will not significantly improve results.
1230   These results demonstrate that performing the M-step after each reverse step is both effective and
1231   efficient for blind inverse tasks. Moreover, this strategy offers improved escape from local minima
1232   and convergence to better solutions compared to vanilla EM, which completes the entire diffusion
1233   reverse process in each EM iteration.
