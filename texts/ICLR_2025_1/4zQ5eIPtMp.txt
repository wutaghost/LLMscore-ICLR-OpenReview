009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Preference optimization, particularly through Reinforcement Learning from Hu-
012            man Feedback (RLHF), has achieved significant success in aligning Large Lan-
013            guage Models (LLMs) to adhere to human intentions. Unlike offline alignment
014            with a fixed dataset, online feedback collection from humans or AI on model gen-
015            erations typically leads to more capable reward models and better-aligned LLMs
016            through an iterative process. However, achieving a globally accurate reward model
017            requires systematic exploration to generate diverse responses that span the vast
018            space of natural language. Random sampling from standard reward-maximizing
019            LLMs alone is insufficient to fulfill this requirement. To address this issue, we
020
               propose a bilevel objective optimistically biased towards potentially high-reward
               responses to actively explore out-of-distribution regions. By solving the inner-
021
               level problem with the reparameterized reward function, the resulting algorithm,
022
               named Self-Exploring Language Models (SELM), eliminates the need for a sepa-
023            rate RM and iteratively updates the LLM with a straightforward objective. Com-
024            pared to Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), the SELM objective reduces in-
025            discriminate favor of unseen extrapolations and enhances exploration efficiency.
026            Our experimental results demonstrate that when fine-tuned on Zephyr-7B-SFT
027            and Llama-3-8B-Instruct models, SELM significantly boosts the performance on
028            instruction-following benchmarks such as MT-Bench and AlpacaEval 2.0, as well
029            as various standard academic benchmarks in different settings.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently achieved significant success largely due to their abil-
035
      ity to follow instructions with human intent. As the defacto method for aligning LLMs, Reinforce-
036   ment Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) works by maximizing the reward function, either a
037   separate model (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023) or reparameterized by the
038   LLM policy (Rafailov et al., 2024b;a; Azar et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), which is learned from
039   the prompt-response preference data labeled by humans. The key to the success of alignment is the
040   response diversity within the preference data, which prevents reward models (RMs) from getting
041   stuck in local optima, thereby producing more capable language models.
042   Offline alignment methods (Rafailov et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2024) attempt to manually construct
043   diverse responses for fixed prompts (Cui et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023), which,
044   unfortunately, struggles to span the nearly infinite space of natural language. On the other hand,
045   online alignment follows an iterative procedure: sampling responses from the LLM and receiving
046   feedback to form new preference data for RM training (Ouyang et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024).
047   The former step helps explore out-of-distribution (OOD) regions through randomness in sampling.
048   However, in standard online RLHF frameworks, maximizing the expected reward learned from the
049   collected data is the only objective for the LLM, sampling from which often leads to responses
050
      clustered around local optima. This passive exploration mechanism can suffer from overfitting and
      premature convergence, leaving the potentially high-reward regions unexplored.
051
052   To address this issue, we propose an active exploration method for online alignment that elicits
053   novel favorable responses. In its simplest form, an optimism term α maxy r(x, y) is added to the
      reward-fitting objective (e.g., logistic regression on dataset D), denoted as −Llr , resulting in a bilevel

220   It has been observed recently (Rafailov et al., 2024a; Pal et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024) that DPO
221   decreases the likelihood of responses generated by the reference policy. It is because for any prompt
222   x, at convergence when πθ ̸= πref , it holds that
223

224
225
      while at the beginning of training when πθ = πref , the above terms are zero. Thus, the expected im-
      plicit reward rbθ as well as the likelihood of πθ will decrease on the reference model’s responses. This
226
      indicates that DPO stimulates a biased distribution favoring unseen extrapolated responses. In the
227
      online iterative setting that we consider, the LLM policy generates responses and receives preference
228   feedback alternately, where biasing towards OOD regions may sometimes help discover outstanding
229   novel responses. However, DPO indiscriminately favors unseen extrapolations and passively ex-
230   plores based purely on the randomness inherent in sampling from the LLM. As a consequence, the
231   vast space of natural language makes it almost impossible to exhaustively explore all the possible
232   responses and identify those that most effectively benefit alignment.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
290   Data Synthesis for LLMs. A key challenge for fine-tuning language models to align with users’
291   intentions lies in the collection of demonstrations, including both the SFT instruction-following
292   expert data and the RLHF preference data. Gathering such data from human labelers is expensive,
293   time-consuming, and sometimes suffers from variant quality (Ouyang et al., 2022; Köpf et al., 2024).
294   To address this issue, synthetic data (Liu et al., 2024a) has been used for aligning LLMs. One line of
295   work focuses on generating plausible instruction prompts for unlabeled data by regarding the target
296   output as instruction-following responses (Li et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023; Josifoski et al., 2023;
297   Taori et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a). Besides, high-quality data can also be distilled from strong
298
      models for fine-tuning weaker ones (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b;
      Ding et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023). To construct synthetic datasets for offline RLHF, a popular
299
      pipeline (Cui et al., 2023; Tunstall et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024b; Ivison et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
300
      2023) involves selecting responses sampled from various LLMs on a set of prompts in the hope to
301   increase the diversity of the data that can span the whole language space. However, data manually
302   collected in such a passive way does not consider what improves the model most through its training,
303   leaving the potentially high-reward regions unexplored.
304
305   Iterative Online Preference Optimization. Compared to offline RLHF algorithms (Rafailov
306
      et al., 2024b; Zhao et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2023) that collect preference datasets ahead of train-
      ing, online RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024), especially the iterative/batched online
307
      RLHF (Bai et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Hoang Tran, 2024;
308
      Xiong et al., 2023; Calandriello et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024) has the potential to gather better and
309   better synthetic data as the model improves. As a special case, self-aligned models match their re-
310   sponses with desired behaviors, such as model-generated feedback (Yuan et al., 2024; Yuanzhe Pang
311   et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). Unfortunately, the above methods still passively
312   explore by relying on the randomness during sampling and easily get stuck at local optima and over-
313   fit to the current data due to the vast space of natural language. A notable exception is Dwaracherla
314   et al. (2024), which proposed to use ensembles of RMs to approximately measure the uncertainty for
315   posterior-sampling active exploration. On the contrary, our method explores based on the optimistic
316   bias and does not estimate the uncertainty explicitly, bypassing the need to fit multiple RMs.
317
      Active Exploration. In fact, active exploration has been widely studied beyond LLMs. Similar to
318   Dwaracherla et al. (2024), most existing sample-efficient RL algorithms first estimate the uncertainty
319   of the environment using historical data and then either plan with optimism (Auer, 2002; Russo &
320   Van Roy, 2013; Jin et al., 2020; Mehta et al., 2023; Das et al., 2024), or select the optimal action from
321   a statistically plausibly set of values sampled from the posterior distribution (Strens, 2000; Osband
322   et al., 2013; 2023; Zhang, 2022; Li et al., 2024c). The proposed self-exploration objective can be
323   categorized as an optimism-based exploration method. However, most previous works require the
      estimation of the upper confidence bound, which is often intractable. Ensemble methods (Osband


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      et al., 2024; Chua et al., 2018; Lu & Van Roy, 2017) can serve as approximations to estimate the
325   uncertainty but are still computationally inefficient. MEX (Liu et al., 2024b) proposed to combine
326   estimation and planning in a single objective similar to ours and established theoretical guarantees
327   under traditional RL setups. RPO (Liu et al., 2024c) proposed to use an adversarially chosen reward
328   model for policy optimization, but focuses on mitigating overoptimization in offline settings.

334
      We adopt UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) as our training dataset, which contains 61k preference
335
      pairs of single-turn conversations. For the external ranker during online alignment, we choose the
336
      small-sized PairRM (0.4B) (Jiang et al., 2023). All experiments are conducted on 8xA100 GPUs.
337
338   Due to the absence of performant open-source online direct alignment codebases at the time of this
339   study, we first implement an iterative version of DPO as the baseline, adhering to the same steps
340
      as Algorithm 1 but training the LLM with the standard DPO objective. Then we conduct a grid
      search over hyperparameters, such as the batch size, learning rate, and iteration number, to identify
341
      the optimal settings for the iterative DPO baseline. We follow these best settings to train SELM.
342
      In addition, we apply iterative DPO and SELM on instruction fine-tuned models. Specifically, we
343   consider two series of LLMs: Zephyr (Tunstall et al., 2023b) and Llama-3 (Meta, 2024), to demon-
344   strate the robustness of SELM. Since the official Zephyr-7B-β model is fine-tuned with DPO on
345   the same UltraFeedback dataset, to avoid overoptimization, we choose Zephyr-7B-SFT1 as the base
346   model and perform 3 iterations of SELM after a single iteration of standard DPO training on the first
347   portion of the training data (we refer to this model as Zephyr-7B-DPO). For Llama-3-8B-Instruct2
348   that is already fine-tuned with RLHF, we directly apply 3 iterations of SELM training.

521   7    C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
522
523   In this paper, we introduced an active preference elicitation method for the online alignment of large
524   language models. By incorporating an optimism term into the reward-fitting objective, the proposed
525
      bilevel self-exploring objective effectively balances between exploiting observed data and exploring
      potentially high-reward regions. Unlike standard online RLHF algorithms that passively explore
526
      the response space by sampling from the training LLM, whose sole objective is maximizing the
527
      expected learned reward, our method actively seeks diverse and high-quality responses. This self-
528   exploration mechanism helps mitigate the risk of premature convergence and overfitting when the
529   reward model is only locally accurate. To optimize this bilevel objective, we solve the inner-level
530   problem and reparameterize the reward with the LLM policy, resulting in a simple yet novel iterative
531   alignment algorithm called Self-Exploring Language Models (SELM). Compared to DPO, SELM is
532   provably sample-efficient and improves the exploration efficiency by selectively favoring responses
533   with high potential rewards rather than indiscriminately sampling unseen responses.
534   Our experiments, conducted with Zephyr-7B-SFT and Llama-3-8B-Instruct models, demonstrate
535   the efficacy of SELM with consistent improvements on AlpacaEval 2.0, MT-Bench, and academic
536   benchmarks with minimal computational overhead. These results underscore the ability of SELM
537   to enhance the alignment and capabilities of LLMs by promoting more diverse and high-quality
538   responses. Since the proposed technique is orthogonal to the adopted online RLHF workflow, it will
539   be interesting to apply our method within more sophisticated alignment frameworks with advanced
      designs, which we would like to leave as future work.

977
978    In experiments, we use the Alignment Handbook (Tunstall et al., 2023a) framework as our codebase.
979
       We find the best hyperparameter settings for the strong iterative DPO baseline by conducting a grid
       search over the iteration number, batch size, learning rate, and label update rule. The results for the
980
       Zephyr-based models are shown in Figure 6. Specifically, we find that using the same amount of
981
       data, updating the model too many iterations can lead to instability. So we set the iteration number
982    to 3 for Llama3-It-based and Zephyr-based models (excluding the first iteration of DPO training).
983    Besides, we observe that choosing different batch sizes has a large effect on the models’ performance
984    and the optimal batch size heavily depends on the model architecture. In experiments, we set the
985    batch size to 256 and 128 for the Zephyr-based and Llama3-It-based models, respectively. For the
986    learning rate, we consider three design choices: cyclic learning rate with constant cycle amplitude,
987    linearly decayed cycle amplitude, and decayed cycle amplitude at the last iteration. We find that a
988    decaying cycle amplitude performs better than constant amplitudes in general. Thus, for Zephyr-
989    based models, we set the learning to 5e − 7 for the first three iterations and 1e − 7 for the last
990
       iteration. In each iteration, the warmup ratio is 0.1. For Llama3-It-based models, we use a linearly
       decayed learning rate from 5e − 7 to 1e − 7 within 3 iterations with the same warmup ratio. We
991
       also test two update ways for the preference data. One is to rank yw , yl , yref and keep the best and
992
       worst responses in the updated dataset, which is the setting that is described in the main paper. The
993    other is to compare yw and yref and replace the chosen or rejected response by yref based on the
994    comparison result. We find that the former design performs better than the latter. We also compared
995    with stepwise DPO (Kim et al., 2024), which updates the reference model at each iteration but uses
996    the original dataset instead of the updated one. This demonstrates that exploring and collecting new
997    data is necessary.
