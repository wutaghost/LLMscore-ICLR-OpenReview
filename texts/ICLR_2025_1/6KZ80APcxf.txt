009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We introduce PASTA (Perceptual Assessment System for explanaTion of Artifi-
012
               cial Intelligence), a novel framework for a human-centric evaluation of eXplain-
013
               able AI (XAI) techniques in computer vision. Our first key contribution is a human
014            evaluation of XAI explanations on four diverse datasets—COCO, Pascal Parts,
015            Cats Dogs Cars, and MonumAI—which constitutes the first large-scale bench-
016            mark dataset for XAI, with annotations at both the image and concept levels. This
017            dataset allows for robust evaluation and comparison across various XAI meth-
018            ods. Our second major contribution is a data-based metric for assessing the in-
019            terpretability of explanations. It mimics human preferences, based on a database
020            of human evaluations of explanations in the PASTA-dataset. With its dataset and
021            metric, the PASTA framework provides consistent and reliable comparisons be-
022
               tween XAI techniques, in a way that is scalable but still aligned with human eval-
               uations. Additionally, our benchmark allows for comparisons between explana-
023
               tions across different modalities, an aspect previously unaddressed. Our findings
024
               indicate that humans tend to prefer saliency maps over other explanation types.
025            Moreover, we provide evidence that human assessments show a low correlation
026            with existing XAI metrics that are numerically simulated by probing the model.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are used in increasingly high stakes domains (e.g., legal, medical)
033   (Surden, 2021; Litjens et al., 2017), it is essential for humans to interpret how they reach their
034   conclusions (Bender et al., 2021). Their lack of transparency has led them to be characterized as
035   “black boxes” (Castelvecchi, 2016), which is particularly problematic in critical applications where
036   understanding the decision-making process is essential for trust and accountability (Vereschak et al.,
037   2024), leading to the creation of a relatively new field: explainable AI (XAI) (Gunning et al., 2019).
038   XAI aims to make the workings of DNNs more transparent and interpretable. XAI methods fall into
039
      two main categories: post-hoc techniques (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg &
      Lee, 2017) and ante-hoc techniques (Bennetot et al., 2022; Koh et al., 2020). Post-hoc techniques
040
      generally explain the output of a frozen, pretrained DNN, while ante-hoc techniques modify the
041
      architecture of the DNN to improve its interpretability from the outset. Each of these categories can
042   be further subdivided into various sub-families, offering a wide array of XAI approaches.
043
044   The diversity of XAI techniques calls for an effort to standardize their evaluation and comparison.
045
      Although there are toolkits in computer vision that offer a range of computational evaluation tech-
      niques (Agarwal et al., 2022b; Hedström et al., 2023; Fel et al., 2022a), to our knowledge there has
046
      been no effort to standardize their evaluation from a perceptual point of view (Nauta et al., 2023),
047
      i.e., the way the explanation is perceived by the human for whom it was intended. Currently, preva-
048   lent approaches (Dawoud et al., 2023; Colin et al., 2022) to evaluate XAI techniques involve human
049   annotators assessing and ranking their interpretability. This approach aligns with XAI’s goal of
050   improving the human interpretability of DNN models. Yet, this method is costly since it requires
051   paying annotators and is impractical for widespread use, as each new XAI technique necessitates a
052   fresh round of human evaluation. It is also at risk of being inconsistent and unreliable since evalu-
053   ations may differ from one annotator to another and depend on factors such as fatigue and even the
      time of day (Schmidt et al., 2007).

054
      To address the challenges associated with evaluating XAI techniques, we propose the Perceptual
055   Assessment System for explanaTion of Artificial intelligence (PASTA). PASTA aims to automate
056   the evaluation of XAI techniques by providing an evaluation metric that mimics human preferences.
057
058
      The first component of PASTA is a dataset composed of four diverse datasets (COCO, Pascal Part,
      Cats Dogs Cars, and Monumai), which include both image and concept annotations. Using this
059
      dataset, we compare 21 XAI methods across multiple model architectures. We subject the resulting
060
      explanations to a rigorous evaluation by human annotators, along a comprehensive set of criteria
061   that cover a variety of desired properties.
062
063   The second component of PASTA is a metric designed to replicate human evaluation on the PASTA-
064
      dataset. While there are benchmarks that focus on perceptual evaluation of XAI methods (Colin
      et al., 2022; Dawoud et al., 2023), to the best of our knowledge, we are the first to integrate both
065
      saliency-based and concept-based explanations into a unified framework. Additionally, our approach
066
      addresses multiple dimensions of human assessment by incorporating a diverse set of questions for
067   users. The primary contributions of this paper are as follows:
068
069    • Comprehensive XAI Benchmark: We establish a dataset, the PASTA-dataset, designed to eval-
070      uate XAI methods across various modalities, including vision and concept-based explanations
071      (Sec. 3.1).
072    • Extensive Evaluation of XAI Methods: We conduct a large-scale evaluation of 21 XAI meth-
073      ods, comparing both post-hoc and ante-hoc approaches across multiple datasets (Sec. 3.2—3.4).
074      Our findings indicate that saliency and input perturbation-based techniques, such as LIME and
075      SHAP, are favored for their effectiveness in interpreting model predictions (Sec. 3.5).
076    • Human-AI Correlation: Our findings reveal a low correlation between widely used XAI met-
077
         rics and human assessments, suggesting that these metrics cover complementary aspects of XAI
         quality (Sect 3.6).
078
079
       • Human-aligned Perception Metric for Explanations: We introduce a novel, data-based metric,
         which we call the PASTA-metric, that automates the scoring of XAI techniques along human-like
080
         interpretability criteria (Sec. 4).
081
082   Automated yet human-aligned metrics such as the PASTA-metric may serve not only to streamline
083   the evaluation process of XAI techniques but also to foster a more transparent and trustworthy AI
084   ecosystem, where DNNs are comprehensible and their decisions justifiable. The complete PASTA
085   framework (code, annotation, and models) will be released after the reviews.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Automated scoring. Automated scoring involves developing models that assign scores to inputs
090   based on a reference dataset, often derived from human ratings. A particularly active area of re-
091   search in this domain is automated essay scoring. Traditionally, this has been addressed through
092   handcrafted feature extraction (Yannakoudakis et al., 2011), but modern methods tend to be closer to
093   model as a judge (Lee et al., 2024; Taghipour & Ng, 2016; Chiang et al., 2024). More recently, there
094   has been a growing interest in using embeddings from large language models (LLMs) as features
095   for scoring. The first successful attempt in this direction was made by Yang et al. (2020). Build-
096   ing on this trend, other approaches have incorporated LLM embeddings with models like LSTMs
097
      (Wang et al., 2022), integrated text generation into the training loop (Xiao et al., 2024), or introduced
      multi-scale aspects to enhance performance (Li et al., 2023).
098
099
      Explainable AI. To address the challenge of explaining DNNs, several specialized tools have been
100
      proposed, often categorized into post-hoc and ante-hoc methods (Arrieta et al., 2020; Rudin et al.,
101
      2022). Post-hoc methods encompass any tool external to the model, allowing us to gain insights
102   from any pre-trained DNN. Popular examples are GradCAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), LIME (Ribeiro
103   et al., 2016), and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). While most post-hoc explainers agree in providing
104   input regions most responsible for a certain prediction, they differ in many non-trivial details, and
105   selecting and evaluating the most appropriate explainer for each task can be challenging (Leavitt
106   & Morcos, 2020; Roy et al., 2022). Ante-hoc methods, instead, aim at modifying the underlying
107   model architecture so as to provide explanations by design. This can be done in the framework of
      Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020) by prompting the model to first predict a set

      Evaluating explainability. While several methods have been proposed to quantitatively measure
134
      explanation quality, such as faithfulness (Petsiuk et al., 2018; Dasgupta et al., 2022), sparsity (Cha-
135   lasani et al., 2020; Bénard et al., 2021), robustness (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018b; Montavon
136   et al., 2018), sensitivity (Adebayo et al., 2018; Hedström et al.) and alignment to an assumed ground
137   truth (Colin et al., 2022; Mohseni et al., 2021; Dawoud et al., 2023), they inherently overlook the
138   perceptual aspect with respect to the human, which is the expected consumer of such explanations.
139   Evaluating explanations via user studies, e.g. where annotators are asked to rate and evaluate ex-
140   planations (Chen et al., 2018; Shu et al., 2019), are however very costly, prone to unreproducibility
141   issues (Nauta et al., 2023), and often unfeasible for tasks that require trained users, like in the med-
142   ical domain (Miró-Nicolau et al., 2022; Muddamsetty et al., 2021). In this work, we take the first
143
      step towards standardizing the evaluation of human perception preferences of explanations (Nauta
      et al., 2023). We propose to overcome the issues of hard-to-reproduce large-scale user studies by
144
      automatizing the evaluation of XAI techniques through a multi-value scoring system that mimics hu-
145
      man preferences while taking into account the users’ diverse expectations, which naturally emerge
146   in user-based studies.

      3     C REATING A HUMAN PREFERENCE DATASET FOR XAI INTERPRETABILITY
150
151
      To evaluate the quality of XAI explanations from a human-centric point of view, we proceed along
      the following steps, which are detailed in the subsections below. First, we build a dataset from anno-
152
      tated images. Using our codebase of classifiers and XAI techniques, we compute label predictions
153
      along with XAI explanations. The explanations are then evaluated by human annotators following
154   a rigorous evaluation protocol. Finally, we compare the different XAI techniques with the human
155   evaluations to assess quality of their explanations. We also investigate how human scores correlate
156   with popular automated XAI metrics to see whether they are complementary.
157
158   3.1   DATASET C OMPOSITION
159
160   To evaluate the performance of different perceptual metrics, we collect a large-scale dataset com-
161   posed of images of four highly heterogeneous datasets: COCO (Lin et al., 2014), Pascal Part (Chen
      et al., 2014), Cats Dogs Cars (Kazmierczak et al., 2024), and Monumai (Lamas et al., 2021). This


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      collection is referred to as the classifier’s dataset. The precise set of labels used for each classifier’s
163   dataset is described in Section A.1.1.. It is important to distinguish this from the PASTA-dataset,
164   which comprises the final set of images, annotations, labels, and explanations obtained through
165   our evaluation process. Each dataset includes two levels of annotations: image-based annotations
166   and concept-based annotations. This dual-level annotation framework allows for the application of
167   Concept-Based and other XAI methods, enabling a robust evaluation across different approaches.
168   For this, we use for the computation of explanations a subset of 25 images of the classifier’s dataset
169   test split per dataset, resulting in a comprehensive evaluation of 100 images, that serve as the basis
170   of the PASTA-dataset. This diverse selection ensures a broader generalization of the XAI techniques
171
      across datasets being assessed. Note that, unlike traditional datasets, our benchmark dataset com-
      prises a triplet of images, explanations, and labels. This triplet enables us to quantitatively assess
172
      the quality of XAI techniques (see Figure 1).

175   3.2   XAI M ETHODS
176
177
      To ensure representativity, we consider two distinct types of explanations. The first type comprises
      saliency methods, which generate explanations by assigning an importance score to each pixel of
178
      the input image, indicating the significance of each pixel in the prediction process. The second type
179
      consists of concept-based explanations, which highlight the importance of human-understandable
180   concepts in the explanation. A detailed list of the methods and more details on each technique are
181   given in Appendix A.2. Notably, we present in this appendix a succinct definition of each XAI
182   method used.
183
      Among saliency-based methods, we consider model-agnostic explanations (LIME (Ribeiro et al.,
184
      2016) and SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017)), gradient-based (FullGrad (Srinivas & Fleuret, 2019))
185
      and model-specific techniques. The model-specific methods include GradCAM (Selvaraju et al.,
186   2017), HiResCAM (Draelos & Carin, 2020), GradCAMElementWise (Pillai & Pirsiavash, 2021),
187   GradCAM++ (Chattopadhay et al., 2018), XGradCAM (Fu et al., 2020), AblationCAM (Ra-
188   maswamy et al., 2020), ScoreCAM (Wang et al., 2020), EigenCAM (Muhammad & Yeasin, 2020),
189   EigenGradCAM (Muhammad & Yeasin, 2020), LayerCAM (Jiang et al., 2021), Deep Feature Fac-
190   torizations (Collins et al., 2018), and BCos (Böhle et al., 2024).
191
      Among concept-based methods, we explore those that produce explanations through counterfac-
192   tuals (CLIP-QDA-sample (Kazmierczak et al., 2024)), and feature importance (X-NeSyL (Dı́az-
193   Rodrı́guez et al., 2022), LaBo (Yang et al., 2023), CLIP-linear (Yan et al., 2023), LIME-CBM
194   (Kazmierczak et al., 2024), RISE (Petsiuk et al., 2018) and SHAP-CBM (Kazmierczak et al., 2024)).
195   Additionally, we employ various strategies for concept extraction: zero-shot methods, training from
196   concept annotations, and training from bounding boxes.
197
      A beneficial aspect of our approach is that many of the methods are tested on multiple backbone
198   architectures (see Figure 7). In such cases, the XAI method is applied to independently trained
199   models. For instance, GradCAM is evaluated on ResNet50, ViT-B, and CLIP-zero-shot models,
200   while SHAP-CBM is applied to both CLIP-QDA and the original Concept Bottleneck model as
201   proposed by Koh et al. (2020).

      3.4   H UMAN EVALUATION PROTOCOL
239
      We aim to quantify the interpretability and usefulness of XAI techniques accurately, using a human
240   evaluation of the quality of evaluations. The resulting dataset serves as a benchmark, enabling us to
241   compare and validate current and future XAI methods. Our human-centric approach complements
242   existing approaches that focus primarily on assessing the model’s internal behavior. For exam-
243   ple, traditional evaluations of faithfulness measure how closely an explanation corresponds to the
244   model’s true functioning while we assess in our dataset how the explanation fit human expectations.
245
      We take a structured approach to ensure that the explanations are not only technically sound but
246   also align with human cognitive processes and expectations, fostering the development of more
247   transparent and interpretable AI systems. First, we establish a comprehensive set of assessment
248   criteria that are evaluated on a graded scale. Then, we apply a meticulous evaluation protocol,
249   developed with the help of a psychologist, to ensure that annotators fully understand the task and
250   the expectations. This includes annotator training and close monitoring throughout the process.
251
252   Evaluation Criteria We consolidate different criteria from the literature into the following set
253   of desiderata for XAI explanations that we wish to evaluate (equations, definitions and algorithms
254   defined in Appendix B.2):
255
256    • Faithfulness (Arrieta et al., 2020; Fel et al., 2022b) measures the extent to which an explanation
257      accurately reflects the true behaviour of the model.
258    • Robustness (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017; Agarwal et al., 2022a; Yeh et al., 2019) assesses the
259      stability and relevance of the explanation across a broad range of models and inputs.
260    • Complexity (Nauta et al., 2023; Nguyen & Martı́nez, 2020; Bhatt et al., 2021) checks whether the
261      explanation is both simple and informative, balancing clarity and detail.
262    • Objectivity (Bennetot et al., 2022) evaluates whether the explanation is interpreted consistently
263      by the majority within a given audience.
264
265   Evaluation Protocol Gathering human preferences from surveys is an active field of research in
266   psychology (Fowler Jr, 2013), but also in the field of machine learning e.g., with the recent advent
267   of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Kaufmann et al., 2023). Interfaces, as
268   well as the formulation of questions, play a key role in the quality of the annotations (Pommeranz
269   et al., 2012), and their design must be considered cautiously to avoid confounding cognitive biases.
      The following human evaluation protocol has been designed with the help of a psychologist. The


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      formulation of the questions has been carefully chosen to ensure that they are fully understood by
271   each annotator.
272
273   To maintain consistency and reliability, all annotators undergo a training session before starting
274
      the actual annotation task. This training familiarizes them with the XAI techniques, evaluation
      criteria, rating scale, and datasets, ensuring a uniform understanding of the task and the expectations.
275
      More details about the annotation protocol are given in Appendix A.3, including an example of how
276
      questions are presented to the annotators (Fig. 9).
277
278   During the evaluation process, annotators are shown an image, a prediction, and an explanation.
279   They are then asked a list of questions that we now describe in more detail. A first set of questions
280
      aims at having annotators establish a baseline, i.e., by interpreting and explaining what makes an
      image recognizable as a specific object or class. This prompts the human annotator to think about
281
      what they are relying on to classify the image themselves, before having to evaluate explanations
282
      produced by the XAI techniques. The first two questions in the annotation process are:
283
284    • Q0.1: What part makes you classify this image as ***? (write an explanation extracting concepts)
285    • Q0.2: What part of the input helps the prediction? (draw bounding boxes on the image)
286
287   Each explanation generated by the XAI techniques is then evaluated regarding each of the desiderata
288   given above (indicated in italics):
289
290    • Q1: The provided explanation is consistent with how I would explain the predicted class? Fidelity
291    • Q2: Overall the explanation provided for the model prediction can be trusted? Complexity
292    • Q3: Is the explanation easy to understand? Complexity
293    • Q4: The explanation can be understood by a large number of people, independently of their
294      demographics (age, gender, country, etc.) and culture? Objectivity
295    • Q5: With this perturbed image, to what extent has the explanation changed ? (Examples with
296      good predictions and light perturbations) Robustness
297    • Q6: With this perturbed image, to what extent has the explanation changed? (Examples with bad
298      predictions and strong perturbations) Robustness
299
300   Annotators evaluate how well the explanations conform to their expectations (Q1), whether the ex-
301
      planations are clear (Q2 and Q3), and if they would rely on these explanations (Q4). They also
      assess how much the explanations change when the images are perturbed (Q5 and Q6), for both ac-
302
      curate and inaccurate predictions, as studied by (Fel et al., 2022b). An example is shown in Figure 2.
303
      Each explanation is rated on a scale from one to five stars, where one star indicates the explanation
304   is entirely uncorrelated with the annotator’s reasoning, and five stars represent perfect correlation.
305   This star rating system allows for a nuanced assessment of the quality of the explanations, reflecting
306   how closely they align with human understanding.

360   We turn to the question of how the human scores in the PASTA-dataset correlate with standard
361   XAI metrics. An analysis based on the Pearson Correlation Coefficient and the Spearman rank
362   Correlation Coefficient for different perturbation strategies, shown in Table 1 indicates a rather weak

364
      faithfulness. We conclude that our human scores indeed cover an aspect of explanation quality
      unrelated to that of perceptual quality, as predicted by Biessmann & Refiano (2021). Additional
365
      results, including results for other axioms, are available in Appendix B.2 and B.3.
366
367   Then, our findings reinforce the idea that human evaluations and computational metrics measure
368   complementary aspects of XAI methods. Human evaluations excel at assessing the usefulness of
369   explanations, aligning with their primary purpose of serving a human audience. In contrast, com-
370
      putational metrics, such as faithfulness, focus on evaluating the alignment between the explanation
      and the model’s actual internal functioning. This aspect lies beyond the reach of human judgment,
371
      as humans cannot directly access or fully comprehend the internal mechanisms of the model.

514   5   C ONCLUSIONS
515
516   In this paper, we introduce PASTA, a novel perceptual assessment system designed to benchmark
517   explainable AI (XAI) techniques in a human-centric manner. We integrate four diverse datasets —
518   COCO, Pascal Parts, Cats Dogs Cars, and Monumai — to form a large-scale benchmark dataset for
519   XAI, and used it for an assessment of XAI explanations by human annotators. We also develop an
520   automated evaluation metric that mimics human preferences based on a comprehensive database of
521   human evaluations. This framework offers a scalable and reliable way to compare different XAI
522   methods, facilitating robust evaluations across modalities previously unaddressed.
523   Our findings demonstrate a clear preference for saliency-based explanations, particularly techniques
524   such as LIME and SHAP, which align well with human intuition. These results affirm the scalability
525   and reliability of our perceptual metric, which provides consistency with human assessment while
526   automating much of the evaluation process.
527
      However, there are limitations to our approach. The current study focuses on a fixed set of datasets
528   and XAI techniques. Human evaluations can be influenced by subjective factors that may affect
529   the consistency of results. Furthermore, annotators can inadvertently introduce biases. Perceptual
530   metrics like the ones proposed here are therefore not intended to serve as absolute measures of XAI
531   performance. Rather, we consider them simply as complementary to other XAI metrics.
532
      Looking ahead, dynamic scoring approaches could be explored to capture the evolving nature of
533
      XAI techniques and their use in real-world applications. In conclusion, PASTA intends to take a
534   step towards creating a transparent and trustworthy AI ecosystem. By aligning AI explanations
535   with human cognitive processes, we aim to foster the development of more interpretable AI systems
536   that can be understood and trusted by users across various domains. This work also introduces a
537   perceptual metric, paving the way for future research to implement the PASTA-metric as a perceptual
538   loss aimed at enhancing the trustworthiness of networks, drawing for example inspiration from the
539   emerging use of LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) in tasks such as image generation (Jo et al., 2020).
      Another possible use is XAI method hyperparameter fine-tuning, as proposed in Section E.2.

922   A.1.1    DATASET
923
924   The dataset used in PASTA is designed to provide a benchmark for evaluating a wide range of
925
      XAI techniques across different explanation modalities. To ensure robustness and versatility, we
      have built a benchmark dataset consisting of four diverse, publicly available datasets, each bringing
926
      distinct characteristics in terms of visual content and concept annotations. Choosing which task
927
      to focus on is a tough question. We have chosen to focus on the image classification task. This
928   task can be performed in many different domains, but in order not to be too domain-specific, we
929   decided to work on general datasets. These datasets enable the evaluation of both image-based and
930   concept-based XAI methods.
931
      The dataset used to train our inference model integrates four datasets, each chosen for its unique
932
      attributes that align with the requirements of evaluating explainability methods. The datasets are as
933
      follows:
934
935           • COCO: A widely-used dataset known for its complexity and variety, containing 117k train-
936             ing images, 4.5k validation images annotated with 80 object categories, which we consider
937             to be concepts in the images. The labels correspond for this specific dataset to indoor scene
938             labeling, to do so, we took the subset of images of indoor scenes (53,051 images). Then,
939             we labeled the images using a scene label DNN trained on the MIT SUN.
940           • Pascal Part: This dataset focuses on detailed part-level annotations, providing fine-grained
941             insights into object structure and component relationships. It is composed of 13,192 train-
942             ing images, 39 concepts, and 16 classes.
943           • Cats Dogs Cars: A curated dataset featuring images of cats, dogs, and cars. The goal of this
944             dataset is to explore if color biases are present in the model or not. It is composed of 3,858
945             training images, 39 concepts, and 3 classes. Since this network does not include annotated
946             concepts, we used Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2023) as an annotator. Since the number
947             of images that constitute Cate Dogs Cars is sufficiently small, we manually checked the
948             bounding boxes generated and found no significant errors.
949           • Monumai: A specialized dataset containing images of monuments, with annotations that
950             include both the overall structures and specific architectural features. It is composed of 908
951             images, 15 concepts, and 4 classes.
952
953   Each dataset in the classifier’s training datasets is annotated at two levels:
954
              • Image-level annotations: These are traditional class labels (Table 6) or object categories
955
                that describe the primary content of the image.
956
957           • Concept-level annotations: These describe specific, human-understandable features within
958
                the image, enabling the application of Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) and other
                concept-based XAI methods. The list of concepts for each dataset is detailed in Table 5.
959
960   This dual-level annotation setup ensures that XAI methods can be evaluated not only for their ability
961   to explain class predictions but also for how well they handle concept-based explanations. The
962   presence of both granular (part-level, concept) and holistic (object, scene-level) annotations provides
963   a comprehensive evaluation environment for various XAI methods.
964
      In Figure 5, we observe the class distribution across the different datasets. While the distributions
965
      are not perfectly uniform, they generally reflect the original composition of the datasets, ensuring
966   that the diversity of the data is preserved in the evaluation process.

1324   Faithfulness: How much does the explanation describe the true behavior of the model? A num-
1325   ber of different ways to compute faithfulness exist, but they all broadly fit the same framework
1326   of measuring how much model predictions change in response to input perturbations (Bhatt et al.,
1327
       2021; Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018a; Yeh et al., 2019; Rieger & Hansen, 2020; Arya et al.,
       2019; Nguyen & Martı́nez, 2020; Bach et al., 2015; Samek et al., 2016; Montavon et al., 2018;
1328
       Ancona et al., 2017; Dasgupta et al., 2022). Intuitively, an explanation is faithful if perturbing
1329
       regions deemed irrelevant by the explanation bring little to no change in model output, whereas
1330   perturbing regions deemed relevant bring a considerable change. In this analysis, we resort to the
1331   evaluation protocol outlined in Azzolin et al. (2024), which generalized a number of common faith-
1332   fulness metrics into a common mold1 . Specifically, faithfulness is estimated as the harmonic mean
1333   of sufficiency (Suf) and necessity (Nec), which account for the degree of prediction changes after
1334   perturbing irrelevant or relevant portions of the input, respectively. Formally, given an input image
1335   x with associated explanation e, and a model to be explained pθ (Y | x), sufficiency and necessity
1336   are defined as:

       Next, we aggregated the scores using majority voting and calculated QWC scores to measure the

1519   We also investigated potential biases in the annotations themselves by examining the differences in
1520   how annotators approached CBM-based and saliency-based explanations. For CBM explanations,
1521   we focused on the text written by annotators in response to question Q0.1, assessing whether anno-
1522   tators preferred explanations that closely resembled their own textual responses. To quantify this,
1523   we transformed CBM explanations into text by concatenating the top concepts used in the explana-
1524   tion and calculated the BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE scores (Lin, 2004) between these
1525   explanations and the annotators’ text responses. As shown in Figure 11, the ROUGE score reveals
1526   a slight correlation between the explanations and the annotators’ expectations for questions Q1, Q2,
1527
       Q3, and Q4. This suggests that annotators are inclined to favor explanations that align with their
       preconceived notions, potentially introducing a bias toward consistency with their initial answers.
1528
1529   Moreover, we observed that questions Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q4 exhibit high intercorrelation, as do ques-
1530   tions Q5 and Q6. This clustering indicates that annotators tend to evaluate explanations similarly
1531   across these sets of questions, which may reflect underlying patterns in how different types of ex-
1532   planations are perceived.
1533   For saliency-based explanations, we analyzed the bounding boxes provided by annotators in re-
1534   sponse to question Q0.2. We evaluated the correlation between various metrics and the annotators’
1535   answers, including:
1536
1537         1. the total sum of pixel intensities in the saliency map (“SUM all”),
1538         2. the sum of pixel intensities within the area of the image identified by the bounding box
1539            (“SUM pos”),
1540         3. the sum of pixel intensities outside the bounding box (“SUM neg”),

             4. the entropy of the saliency map (“Entropy”).

1543   Figure 11 shows that questions Q1, Q2, Q3, and Q4 are highly correlated with each other, as are
1544   questions Q5 and Q6. Additionally, all metrics except for “SUM pos” show some correlation with
1545   questions Q1–Q4. This suggests that annotators may focus heavily on background features and
1546   salient objects when answering these questions, potentially overlooking finer details in the bounding
1547   box area.
1548   Overall, these analyses highlight several potential biases in the dataset. Annotators exhibit a prefer-
1549   ence for certain types of explanations, particularly saliency maps, and tend to favor explanations that
1550   align with their expectations, as evidenced by the correlation between their text responses and the
1551   explanations. Additionally, while demographic factors such as age and gender do not significantly
1552   impact the overall evaluation, the slight decrease in consistency among older annotators warrants
1553   further investigation. The study involved 15 annotators, all from the same cultural background,
1554   which may introduce some shared perspectives or biases. To mitigate this, future studies could

1771   ically, we encode each predicted label as a one-hot vector and concatenate it with the embedding.
1772   The results of this modified approach are presented in Table 16.
1773
       As observed in Table 16, incorporating label information into our framework appears to degrade
1774   performance. This phenomenon may be attributed to the relatively high number of labels used
1775   across all datasets (26), which is comparable to the number of distinct images. Consequently, the
1776   added label information may introduce redundancy or overfitting, ultimately impacting the overall
1777   scoring process.
