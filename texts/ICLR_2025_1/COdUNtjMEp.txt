009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               While transformers have demonstrated impressive capacities for in-context learn-
               ing (ICL) in practice, theoretical understanding of the underlying mechanism en-
013
               abling transformers to perform ICL is still in its infant stage. This work aims to
014
               theoretically study the training dynamics of transformers for in-context classifica-
015            tion tasks. We demonstrate that, for in-context classification of Gaussian mixtures
016            under certain assumptions, a single-layer transformer trained via gradient descent
017            converges to a globally optimal model at a linear rate. We further quantify the
018            impact of the training and testing prompt lengths on the ICL inference error of
019            the trained transformer. We show that when the lengths of training and testing
020            prompts are sufficiently large, the prediction of the trained transformer approaches
021            the Bayes-optimal classifier. Experimental results corroborate the theoretical find-
022            ings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) based on the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) have
029   demonstrated remarkable in-context learning (ICL) abilities (Brown et al., 2020). When given a
030   prompt consisting of examples of a learning task, these models can learn to solve this task for new
031   test examples without any parameter updating. This behavior has been empirically demonstrated in
032   state-of-the-art models on real-world tasks (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023).
033   This impressive capacity of transformer-based models has inspired many recent works aiming to
034   understand the ICL abilities of transformers. A more comprehensive literature review can be found
035   in Appendix B. Garg et al. (2022) was the first to study the ICL abilities of transformers for various
036   function classes. They empirically showed that transformers can learn linear regression models in
037   context. Later on, a line of research was developed to theoretically explain how transformers perform
038   in-context linear regression. For example, Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023); Bai et al.
039
      (2024); Fu et al. (2023); Giannou et al. (2024) showed by construction that, some specially-designed
      transformers can perform linear regression in context. Moreover, some recent works like Zhang
040
      et al. (2023a); Huang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024) studied the training dynamics of a single-
041
      layer transformer for in-context linear regression. They proved the convergence of their single-layer
042   transformers and showed their trained transformer are able to perform linear regression in context.
043
044   Building on the earlier works that largely focus on linear regression problems, several recent pa-
045
      pers have started to investigate the ICL capabilities of transformers for non-linear problems such as
      classification. For instance, Bai et al. (2024) showed that, by construction, multi-layer transformers
046
      can be approximately viewed as multiple steps of gradient descents for logistic regression. Gian-
047
      nou et al. (2024) further showcased that the constructed transformers can approximately perform
048   Newton’s method for logistic regression. Recently, Li et al. (2024) studied the training dynamics
049   of transformers for in-context binary classification. However, their analysis requires the data to be
050   pairwise orthogonal and the possible distribution of their data is highly limited. The learning dy-
051   namics of transformers for more general in-context classification problems is not well understood.
052   Moreover, to the best of our knowledge, existing literature (Bai et al., 2024; Giannou et al., 2024; Li
053   et al., 2024) studying the in-context classification of transformers focus only on binary classification.
      How transformers perform in-context multi-class classification remains unexplored.

      In this work, we study the learning dynamics of a singly-layer transformer for both in-context binary
055   and multi-class classification of Gaussian mixtures, a fundamental problem in machine learning. Our
056   main contributions can be summarized as follows:
057
058   • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to study the learning dynamics of transformers for
059
        in-context classification of Gaussian mixtures, and we are the first to prove the training conver-
        gence of transformers for in-context multi-class classification. We prove that with appropriately
060
        distributed training data (Assumptions 3.1, 4.1), a single-layer transformer trained via gradient de-
061
        scent will converge to its global minimizer at a linear rate (Theorems 3.1, 4.1) for both in-context
062     binary or multi-class classification problems.
063   • Due to the high non-linearity of our loss function, we cannot directly find the closed-form expres-
064     sion of the global minimizer. Instead, we prove an important property that the global minimizer
065     consists of a constant plus an error term that is induced by the finite training prompt length (N ).
066     We further show that the max norm of this error term is bounded, and converges to zero at a rate
067     of O(1/N ).
068   • With properly distributed test prompts (Assumptions 3.2, 4.2), we establish an upper bound of
069     the inference error (defined in Equation (3)) of the trained transformer and quantify the impact of
070
        the training and testing prompt lengths on this error. We further prove that when the lengths of
        training prompts (N ) and
                                √ testing prompts (M ) approach infinity, this error converges to zero at
071
072
        a rate of O(1/N + 1/ M ) (Theorems 3.2, 4.2), and the prediction of the trained transformer is
        Bayes-optimal, i.e., the optimal classifier given the data distribution.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We studied the learning dynamics of transformers for in-context classification of Gaussian mixtures,
532
      and showed that with properly distributed data, a single-layer transformer trained via gradient de-
533
      scent converges to its global minimum. Moreover, we established the upper bounds of the inference
534   errors of the trained transformers and discussed how the training and test prompt lengths influence
535   the performance of the model. Experimental results also corroborated the theoretical claims. There
536   are some directions worth further exploring. One potential avenue is to investigate whether the
537   assumptions regrading the training and test prompts can be relaxed. Additionally, we have only ex-
538   amined single-layer transformers with linear attention and sparse parameters. The learning dynam-
539   ics of multi-layer transformers with nonlinear attention (e.g., softmax) for in-context classification
      problems remain an interesting area for future investigation.

      B    R ELATED WORK
671
672
      It has been observed that transformer-based models have impressive ICL abilities in natural language
673   processing (Brown et al., 2020; Nye et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Dasgupta et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
674   2022). Garg et al. (2022) first initiated the study of the ICL abilities of transformers in a mathemati-
675   cal framework and they empirically showed that transformers can in-context learn linear regression,
676   two-layer ReLU networks, and decision trees. Subsequently, numerous works have been developed
677   to explain the ICL capacities of transformers in solving in-context mathematical problems. These
678   works mainly use two approaches: constructing specific transformers capable of performing certain
679   in-context learning tasks, and studying the training dynamics of transformers for such tasks.
680   Constructions of transformers. Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023) showed by con-
681   struction that multi-layer transformers can be viewed as multiple steps of gradient descent for lin-
682   ear regression. Akyürek et al. (2022) also showed that constructed transformers can implement
683   closed-form ridge regression. Guo et al. (2023) showed that constructed transformers can perform
684   in-context learning with representations. Bai et al. (2024) proved that constructed transformers
685   can perform various statistical machine learning algorithms through in-context gradient descent and
686   showed that constructed transformers can perform in-context model selection. Lin et al. (2023)
687
      demonstrated that constructed transformers can approximate several in-context reinforcement learn-
      ing algorithms. Fu et al. (2023); Giannou et al. (2024) further proved that constructed transform-
688
      ers can perform higher-order optimization algorithms like Newton’s method. Pathak et al. (2023)
689
      showed that transformers can learn mixtures of linear regressions. Giannou et al. (2023) proved that
690   looped transformers that can emulate various in-context learning algorithms. Cheng et al. (2023)
691   showed that transformers can perform functional gradient descent for learning non-linear functions
692   in context. Zhang et al. (2024) showed that a linear attention layer followed by a linear layer can
693   learn and encode a mean signal vector for in-context linear regression.
694
      Training dynamics of transformers. Mahankali et al. (2023); Ahn et al. (2024) proved that the
695
      global minimizer of the in-context learning loss of linear transformer can be equivalently viewed
696   as one-step preconditioned gradient descent for linear regression. Zhang et al. (2023a) proved the
697   convergence of gradient flow on a single-layer linear transformer and discussed how training and
698   test prompt length will influence the prediction error of transformers for linear regression. Huang
699   et al. (2023) proved the convergence of gradient descent on a single-layer transformer with softmax
700   attention with certain orthogonality assumptions on the data features. Li et al. (2023b) showed that
701   trained transformers can learn topic structure. Wu et al. (2023) analyzed the task complexity bound
      for pretraining single-layer linear transformers on in-context linear regression tasks. Tarzanagh

      et al. (2023) built the connections between single-layer transformers and support vector machines
703   (SVMs). Nichani et al. (2024) showed that transformers trained via gradient descent can learn causal
704   structure. Chen et al. (2024) proved the convergence of gradient flow on a multi-head softmax
705   attention model for in-context multi-task linear regression. Kim & Suzuki (2024); Yang et al. (2024)
706   proved that trained transformers can learn nonlinear features in context.

      Recently, Li et al. (2024) studied the training dynamics of a single layer transformer for in-context

721   ity of the ICL abilities of transformers. Hahn & Goyal (2023) showed that ICL can arise through
722   recombination of compositional structure found in linguistic data. They derived an information-
