009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce the novel problem of remote reinforcement learning (RRL) with a
012            communication constraint, in which the actor that takes the actions in the environ-
013            ment lacks direct access to the reward signal. Instead, the rewards are observed
014            by a controller, which communicates with the agent through a communication-
015            constrained channel. This can model a remote control scenario over a wireless
016            channel, where the communication link from the controller to the agent has lim-
017            ited capacity due to power, bandwidth, or delay constraints. In the proposed solu-
018
               tion, rather than transmitting the reward values to the agent over the rate-limited
               channel, the controller learns the optimal policy, and at each round, signals the
019
               action that the agent should take over the channel. However, instead of sending
020
               the precise action–which can be prohibitive when the action set is large–we use an
021            importance sampling approach to reduce the communication load, which allows
022            the agent to sample an action from the current policy. The actor, sampling from
023            the desired policy at each turn, can also learn the optimal policy, albeit at a slower
024            pace, using supervised learning. We exploit the learned policy at the actor to fur-
025            ther reduce the communication load. Our solution, called Guided Remote Action
026            Sampling Policy (GRASP), exhibits a significant reduction in communication re-
027            quirements, achieving an average of 12-fold decrease in data transmission across
028            all experiments, and 50-fold reduction for environments with continuous action
029
               spaces. We also show the applicability of GRASP beyond single-agent scenarios,
               including parallel and multi-agent environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Reinforcement learning (RL) enables the solution of complex, sequential tasks through interaction
035
      with the environment alone. This is accomplished by identifying a sequence of actions that maximize
036   the cumulative expected rewards. However, the reward signal is not always readily available to
037   the agent, as it can be difficult to evaluate or costly to acquire. For instance, in human-in-the-
038   loop systems, the reward may need to be evaluated and provided by a human, which can cause
039   delays (Knox & Stone, 2009; Daniel et al., 2014), or be learned from demonstrations (Abbeel &
040   Ng, 2004; Schaal, 1996; Arora & Doshi, 2021). In other complex engineering systems, such as
041   communication networks or multi-processor systems, evaluating the reward may require solving
042   complex optimization problems or accumulating information distributed across a large network.
043   The challenges of lacking or costly reward acquisition in RL have been studied in the context of
044
      active learning (Krueger et al., 2020; Eberhard et al., 2024).
045   In this work, we consider a distributed learning scenario with two agents: a controller and an actor.
046   Only the controller has access to the reward signal, while the actor takes the actions. This setting
047   is depicted in Figure 1. The actor observes the state of the environment, either fully or partially,
048   and decides on an action; however, it does not have access to the reward. The controller observes
049   both the state of the environment and the reward signal, but relies on the actor to take actions. The
050
      controller communicates with the actor over a rate-limited channel to help guide it toward the correct
      action. We dub this problem remote reinforcement learning (RRL) with a communication constraint.
051
052   If the controller is able to convey the reward signal to the actor through the communication channel,
053   the actor would have all the necessary information to perform RL; that is, it could learn a policy
      that probabilistically maps states to actions to maximize the sum of future rewards. However, this

074   approach encounters four primary limitations in our scenario: limited communication, feasibility,
075   parallelism, and coordination. Firstly, the reward is usually a real number, and it may not be possible
076   to represent it exactly with the finite number of bits dictated by the capacity of the communication
077   channel between the controller and the actor. Secondly, the actor may be deployed on a resource-
078   constrained environment, such as an edge devices or a sensor, and may not be capable of running a
079   complex RL algorithm locally, even if it has full or partial access to the reward signal. Thirdly, to
080   accelerate learning, multiple concurrent agents are often used to collect experiences independently
081   (Mnih et al., 2016; Heess et al., 2017). In our framework, this corresponds to communicating with
082
      multiple actors, each interacting with the same but parallel environments. However, if the actors re-
      ceived individual reward signals, they would develop distinct policies, failing to benefit from shared
083
      experiences. Lastly, in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) scenarios, where multiple actors
084
      jointly influence the same environment, simply conveying individual reward signals would result in
085   a distributed training algorithm that struggles with action coordination. These limitations suggest
086   that direct communication of the reward signal is an inefficient solution for RRL.
087
088
      Shifting the focus to the controller, it has full knowledge of the state and rewards. If it also had
      access to the actions, it could effectively run a RL algorithm locally to obtain the optimal policy.
089
      This would emulate the best possible performance of a centralized learning scenario, provided the
090
      controller can select and communicate the subsequent actions to the actor at each decision step. In
091   scenarios involving small discrete action spaces, this method can result in smaller message sizes
092   compared to conveying the reward signal (or a quantized version of it) to the actor. On the other
093   hand, for continuous action spaces, one might initially think that communicating actions would face
094   similar bandwidth limitations as with reward transmissions, given that actions in such spaces can
095   assume an uncountably infinite number of values, necessitating some form of quantization and com-
096   pression. However, crucially, the actor does not need to take a specific action from the controller’s
097   policy, but any sample from it would suffice. Let P be the distribution of actions dictated by the
098   controller’s policy in a given state, while Q represents the actor’s belief about the policy in this state.
099
      From an information-theoretic perspective, the number of bits required to communicate a particular
      sample from P (i.e., a specific action) is approximately H(P ) + DKL [P ||Q]-—the entropy of the
100
      action plus the cost of using the ‘wrong’ distribution Q to compress it. Instead, by generating candi-
101
      date samples from Q and using P only to select a single candidate via an importance-sampling-like
102   criterion, the cost of communicating the index of the accepted sample can be reduced to approxi-
103   mately DKL [P ||Q] (Cuff, 2008; Li & El Gamal, 2018). This method of conveying random actions
104   is particularly effective in systems with multiple parallel agents. By centrally processing all collected
105   experiences, the controller can learn the most informed policy, benefiting from the experiences of all
106   the actors in parallel. The controller can then enable each actor to take an action based on the most
107   up-to-date policy in the next round. We call this approach the Guided Remote Action Sampling
      Policy (GRASP) method.

      MARL extends the traditional RL framework to multiple agents, where the agents collectively in-
109   fluence the environment’s state. This scenario is particularly relevant to RRL because the reward is
110   often tied to the overall system’s performance; and thus, may not be directly accessible to each actor.
111   Moreover, decentralized MARL suffers from a high degree of non-stationarity (Du & Ding, 2021;
112   Wong et al., 2023). If each agent views others as part of the environment, the learning and policy
113   updates by other agents alter the environment, rendering it highly non-stationary and challenging
114   to learn from. To address this issue, a centralized-learning decentralized-execution approach is typ-
115   ically employed (Lowe et al., 2017). During training, this method involves centrally learning the
116   policies of all agents using global information, thereby avoiding the non-stationarity problem. After
117
      training, these policies are fixed, ensuring that even though the agents execute them independently,
      the environment remains consistent for each agent. Multiple agents in MARL translate into multi-
118
      ple actors in RRL, while a single centralized controller is ideally suited to oversee the centralized
119
      training stage, enabling the actors to take correlated actions at each step.

122   related works. Section 3 mathematically defines the framework for RRL. Section 4 empirically
123
      evaluates the proposed approach, comparing it against other solutions. Finally, the paper concludes
      with a summary of findings and proposes potential future research directions.

131   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
132
133   2.1   RL WITH COMMUNICATION CONSTRAINTS
134
      RL literature includes many connections to communications. Relevant works include federated RL
135
      (Nadiger et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2022), where multiple agents collaborate to learn a common policy
136
      while keeping data localized to each agent. This contrasts with RRL, where both the controller and
137   the actors have access to the state and the actions. MARL with communication among agents is
138   an extensively studied topic, where the agents exchange messages over a dedicated link (Foerster
139   et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2020), including over noisy channels (Tung et al., 2021; Roig & Gündüz,
140   2020), to achieve a common goal. In these works, reward is known to all the actor(s), unlike in
141   our setting, where it is only accessible to a remote controller. Our work is orthogonal to these
142   approaches; GRASP can be applied to solve MARL problems through centralized training, with or
143   without communication between actors. In the presence of communication, an agent’s messages can
144   be considered as part of its action space; thus, during training, they would be chosen by and known
145
      to the controller. Furthermore, in this scenario, the centralized training with decentralized execution
      paradigm is often employed, to which GRASP is particularly well-suited.
146
147   Communication constraints have also been recently considered for distributed multi-armed bandit
148   problems in Hanna et al. (2022); Mitra et al. (2023); Salgia & Zhao (2023). These works focus on
149   the compression of the reward signal, or the model, to minimize regret. Differently from our setting,
150   in these papers, the agents taking the actions observe the corresponding reward, which they then
151
      report to the learning agent over a limited channel. The work closest to ours is Pase et al. (2022),
      which studies sending actions over a communication-limited channel in a contextual multi-armed
152
      bandit problem. In contrast to our work, the states are independent across time, and the agents cannot
153
      learn the policy. The authors study the regret behavior for a certain class of policies, focusing on the
154   asymptotic regime of infinitely many agents.

      2.2   R EMOTE S AMPLING
157
158   Let P be the distribution we wish to sample from in RRL, representing the controller’s policy, and
159   Q be a reference distribution, representing the actor’s policy, which is also known to the controller.
160   Our goal is to enable the agent to sample from P by communicating as few bits as possible. This re-
161   mote sampling problem is also known as ‘reverse channel coding’ (Bennett et al., 2002) or ‘channel
      simulation’ (Cuff, 2008) in the literature. In their quest to obtain the entanglement-assisted capacity

173   A naive approach to this problem would be to sample an action from P at the controller and send it
174   using universal lossless data compression. The advantage of the channel simulation approach over
175   directly sampling from P has been shown in Li & El Gamal (2018); Theis & Yosri (2022): the

177

      Importantly, this approach allows for communicating samples from a continuous distribution P by
178
      transmitting a finite number of bits, provided that DKL [P ||Q] < ∞.

      2.3   I MITATION LEARNING
194
195   In the proposed solution to the RRL problem, actions need to be effectively communicated from the
196   controller to the actor. To facilitate this, we use channel simulation, which enables the transmission
197   of actions using approximately DKL [P ||Q] bits, where P represents the action probability distribu-
198   tion under the controller’s policy in a given state, and Q is a probability distribution known to both
199   the controller and the actor. What should Q be? One solution is to periodically transmit the con-
200   troller’s current policy to the actor and use it as the reference distribution Q. This method involves
201   resending updates to account for the evolving policy as the controller learns. Since the policies are
202
      represented as neural networks, this approach requires periodically transmitting all the parameters,
      which is very costly from a communication perspective.
203
204   Alternatively, since the actor can observe the current state and receives a sample from the desired
205   policy, it can learn the controller’s policy—-a probability distribution over actions conditioned on the
206   state—-in a supervised manner. This concept is known as behavioral cloning and is an application
207   within imitation learning, a field focused on learning policies from demonstrations (Pomerleau,
208
      1988; Torabi et al., 2018; Abbeel & Ng, 2004; Schaal, 1996; Arora & Doshi, 2021). Inverse RL
      (Arora & Doshi, 2021) offers another approach, where the objective is to recover the reward function
209
      from a set of state-action trajectories. While this approach can succeed in scenarios where behavioral
210
      cloning fails, it is also more complex, often requiring the solution of RL problems as a subroutine.
211   A combination of these two approaches was proposed by Ho & Ermon (2016), where a policy is
212   learned directly as if learning from rewards recovered through inverse RL, without explicitly solving
213   the inverse problem. In our experiments, we found that behavioral cloning alone was sufficient for
214   our purposes, and we provide a more thorough examination of this in Section 4.

454   5     L IMITATIONS
455
456
      To perform channel simulation, both parties require access to a common reference distribution Q.
457
      In GRASP, this is achieved by training an additional policy at the actor, which aims to follow the
458   controller’s policy as closely as possible. The closer the two policies are, the smaller the communi-
459   cation cost. This requirement introduces increased computational cost at the actor in order to reduce
460   the communication rate. As previously mentioned, the need for a common distribution Q can be
461   circumvented by periodically transmitting the controller’s current policy to the actor. This approach
462   can reduce the need for training a separate policy at the actor, but it may lead to periodic spikes in
463   communication load, depending on the frequency and size of the transmitted policy updates.
464   RRL assumes that both the agent and the controller have access to the same state/observation. In
465   situations where this is not the case, a common policy cannot be trained, and thus GRASP cannot
466   be implemented. However, there exists a potential avenue due to recent advances in the information
467   theory literature regarding the error rates of performing channel simulation when the encoder and
468   decoder do not share the same policies (Li & Anantharam, 2021). It remains to be determined how
469   best to exploit the different information available to the controller and the actor in such situations to
470   find a good policy in a computation- and communication-efficient manner.
471

      6     C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this work, we have introduced the novel problem of RRL, in which the reward signal is only avail-
476   able to a controller, removed from the action-choosing agent, called the actor. The actor relies on
477
      messages transmitted by the controller to decide on its actions. There are two obvious benchmarks:
      In the first, the controller conveys the reward signal to the actor, so that the actor can learn the op-
478
      timal policy by applying its favourite RL algorithm. In the second, the controller learns the optimal
479
      policy and transmits the optimal action to the actor at each step. Both of these options may become
480   infeasible when the reward function takes real values or when the action set is prohibitively large
481   (even continuous). We have proposed a novel alternative method, called GRASP, based on impor-
482   tance sampling and behavioral cloning. The controller sends a sample from the desired policy to the
483   actor, and to further reduce the communication cost, the actor attempts to estimate the controller’s
484   policy through supervised learning. Our experiments have shown that the proposed method vastly
485   outperforms the baselines, achieving a 12-fold reduction in communication rate while maintaining
      the same reward.

765   Algorithm 3 GRASP Actor
766   Require: P, Q, N
767    1: t, n, s⋆ ← 0, 1, ∞
768    2: w = minx P (x)/Q(x)
769    3: repeat
770    4:    z ← sample P
771    5:    v ← N/(N − n + 1)
772    6:    s ← t · P (z)/Q(z)
773    7:    if s < s⋆ then
774
       8:       s⋆ ← s
       9:       n⋆ ← n
775
      10:    end if
776
      11:    n←n+1
777   12: until s⋆ ≤ t · w or n > N
778   13: return n⋆
