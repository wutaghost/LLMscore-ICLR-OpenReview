009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Protein function inference relies on annotating protein domains via sequence simi-
012
               larity, often modeled through profile Hidden Markov Models (profile HMMs),
013
               which capture evolutionary diversity within related domains. However, pro-
014            file HMMs make strong simplifying independence assumptions when modeling
015            residues in a sequence. Here, we introduce PSALM (Protein Sequence Annotation
016            using Language Models), a hierarchical approach that relaxes these assumptions
017            and uses representations of protein sequences learned by protein language models to
018            enable high-sensitivity, high-specificity residue-level protein sequence annotation.
019            We also develop the Multi-Domain Protein Homology Benchmark (MDPH-Bench),
020            a benchmark for protein sequence domain annotation, where training and test
021            sequences have been rigorously split to share no similarity between any of their
022
               domains at a given threshold of sequence identity. Prior benchmarks, which split
               one domain family at a time, do not support methods for annotating multi-domain
023
               proteins, where training and test sequences need to have multiple domains from
024
               different families. We validate PSALM’s performance on MDPH-Bench and high-
025            light PSALM as a promising alternative to HMMER, a state-of-the-art profile
026            HMM-based method, for protein sequence annotation.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Proteins are composed of distinct structural and functional units conserved through evolution, known
033   as domains. The primary aim of protein sequence annotation is to locate and characterize these
034   domains within a given sequence. Insight into the individual functions of these domains, which may
035   act independently or in concert with neighboring domains, may shed light on the overall biological
036   role of the protein (Fig. 1). Since experimental characterization of protein function can be difficult,
037   function is often inferred and annotated through sequence similarity (homology) to domains with
038   known function (Pearson, 2013; Eddy, 1998). As the size of protein sequence databases and the
039
      number of protein sequences with unknown function continue to grow rapidly (UniProt Consortium,
      2023), methods for annotating protein sequences have been critical for exploiting this wealth of
040
      information about the molecular basis and evolutionary trajectory of life.
041
042   The state of the art in protein domain sequence annotation uses profile hidden Markov models (profile
043   HMMs) to detect domains (Eddy, 2011) and profile/profile comparison to identify homologous
044   domains (Remmert et al., 2012). Databases of protein domain families, like Pfam (Mistry et al.,
045
      2021), categorize millions of protein sequences into approximately 20,000 domains. The state of
      the art uses profile hidden Markov models (profile HMMs) to identify domains (Eddy, 2011) and
046
      profile/profile comparison to identify homologous domains (Remmert et al., 2012). Annotation can
047
      be achieved by using databases of protein families to compare a protein sequence against 20,000
048   profiles rather than scanning against millions of individual sequences. Domain-based annotation
049   goes beyond classifying the whole protein sequence; it identifies the domain composition as well as
050   the boundaries of each domain. This “domain annotation” requires annotation at the residue level,
051   labeling each amino acid symbol in the sequence. Domain annotation helps with function inference
052   and avoids the “transitive identification catastrophe”, where sequence-level annotations inferred from
053   the presence of one domain can erroneously transfer between sequences due to homology of an
      unrelated domain (Doerks et al., 1998).

070   Profile HMMs make simplifying independence assumptions when modeling residues in protein
071   sequences, and there is considerable interest in developing more powerful methods that can better
072   recognize distant evolutionary relationships with greater sensitivity. While most deep learning
073   approaches to protein sequence similarity recognition focus on whole-protein or single-domain
074   classification (Bileschi et al., 2022; Heinzinger et al., 2022; Nallapareddy et al., 2023; Kaminski et al.,
075   2023; Hamamsy et al., 2023), they do not address the challenge of identifying individual domain
076   subsequences within longer target sequences, which requires careful benchmarking and data curation
077   to assess performance.
078   In this work, we introduce Protein Sequence Annotation with Language Models (PSALM), a novel
079   approach that extends the capabilities of ESM-2, a pre-trained protein language model (pLM) (Lin
080   et al., 2023), to predict residue-level sequence annotations. Our contributions include:
081
082          • First deep learning model for residue-level protein domain annotation: PSALM is
083            the first deep learning approach to annotate domain boundaries and subsequences within
084
               multidomain proteins.
085          • Relaxation of HMM independence assumptions for improved sensitivity and specificity:
086            PSALM leverages pLMs to overcome the simplifying assumptions of profile HMMs, allow-
087            ing for greater sensitivity in detecting conserved domains across distantly-related sequences
088
               and higher specificity in identifying previously unannotated domains.
089          • First benchmark for multidomain protein annotation, MDPH-Bench: To enable robust
090            evaluation, we introduce the Multi-Domain Protein Homology Benchmark (MDPH-Bench).
091
               This benchmark rigorously curates training and test sets to prevent any domain similarity
               above a predefined threshold, enabling realistic assessments of model performance across
092
               diverse domain families and multidomain proteins, which previous benchmarks do not
093
               support.

098   2.1   P ROFILE HMM S
099
      Profile HMMs use curated multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) of related domains, which reveal
100
      patterns of conservation and variability at the residue level, to model consensus using “match”,
101
      “insert”, and “delete” hidden states (Durbin et al., 1998; Eddy, 1998). Profile HMMs assume that
102   the observed residues are conditionally independent given the hidden state. While this assumption
103   simplifies the modeling process, it may limit the ability of profile HMMs to capture complex
104   dependencies between residues in a sequence. These models serve as templates for comparison
105   against the sequence of interest, enabling the identification of domains by finding subsequences
106   that match the profile HMMs. Sequences with multiple, unrelated domains will require the use of
107   multiple profile HMMs for annotation. HMMER (Eddy, 2011) is the state-of-the-art protein sequence
      domain annotation method and underlies many different databases, which organize related domains

108
      into MSAs and profile HMMs at varying levels of granularity, enabling profile-based annotations at
109   the superfamily (Pandurangan et al., 2019), family (Mistry et al., 2021), and sub-family (Thomas
110   et al., 2022) levels.
111
112   2.2   D EEP M ODELS
113
114   Recent efforts to apply deep learning methods to predict protein function from sequence have either
115   focused on predicting ontology-based functional annotation at the sequence level (Cao & Shen, 2021;
116   Hong et al., 2020; Kulmanov & Hoehndorf, 2020) or recognizing homology at the sequence level
117   (Heinzinger et al., 2022; Nallapareddy et al., 2023; Kaminski et al., 2023; Hamamsy et al., 2023). To
118   our knowledge, ProtENN, an ensemble of convolutional neural networks, represents the first attempt
119
      to predict Pfam domains directly from protein sequences (Bileschi et al., 2022). ProtENN, however,
      is constrained to make one domain prediction per input sequence and cannot natively identify domain
120
      boundaries or multiple domains within a sequence without ad hoc post-processing. Additionally,
121
      ProtENN cannot provide information on the contribution of an individual residue to a predicted
122   annotation.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
128
      Here, we formalize the residue-level sequence annotation problem as a mapping from a protein
129   sequence x = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xL ) to a sequence of protein domain families y = (y1 , y2 , . . . , yL ). For
130   residue i in a sequence, xi is an index 1 . . . 25 representing the i-th amino acid character (20 canonical,
131   2 non-canonical, and 3 ambiguous amino acid characters), and yi is an index 1 . . . D representing
132   the i-th protein domain family annotation, with D + 1 for none. Approximately 23% of protein
133   sequences and 47% of residues across all sequences in UniProt do not belong to any Pfam domain
134   family (Mistry et al., 2021). The goal of residue-level annotation is to learn a model that predicts
135   domain family annotations for each residue in a protein sequence:
136
137                                          ŷi = arg max P (Yi = f |x),                                       (1)
138                                                    f

139
      where P (Yi ) is the distribution over D + 1 family annotations for a given residue i.

      3.2   P ROTEIN L ANGUAGE M ODEL
142
143   Numerically encoding protein sequences is necessary as an input for machine learning tasks such
144   as classification. Protein language models (pLMs) learn vector representations of both individual
145   residues and full-length protein sequences, which capture long-range interactions, predict function
146   via transfer learning, and achieve state-of-the-art performance in several structure prediction tasks
147   Bepler & Berger (2021); Rao et al. (2020); Meier et al. (2021); Elnaggar et al. (2021). We use ESM-2
148   (specifically, the 8M, 35M, 150M, and 650M parameter models), a pre-trained general-purpose pLM
149   (Lin et al., 2023), to generate residue-level sequence embeddings x′ for a given sequence x. ESM-2
150   was trained using a BERT-style masked token prediction task (Devlin et al., 2018), enabling it to
151
      capture contextual information and dependencies within protein sequences and allowing us to replace
      x with x′ .

      3.3   PSALM
155
      We introduce PSALM (Protein Sequence Annotation using Language Models), a method to predict
156   domains across a protein sequence at the residue-level. PSALM uses a hierarchical approach that
157   considers both individual protein domain families and clans, which are collections of evolutionarily
158   related (homologous) protein domain families categorized by Pfam (Finn et al., 2006). In Pfam 35.0,
159   approximately 45% of the 19,632 Pfam families are grouped into 655 clans, and a family can only
160   belong to at most one clan. While our primary aim is to predict protein domain families at each
161   residue, modeling clans – the super class – is an interpretable intermediate step that aids in identifying
      areas of functional or structural importance that may not have clear family-level annotations.

      where C(f ) is the clan label to which family f belongs, and P (Zi ) is the distribution over all C + 2
      clan annotations for a given residue i. The inclusion of a separate clan prediction task ensures the
193
      interpretability of the clan model, preventing it from becoming an abstract hidden state. The family
194
      model is trained via the teacher forcing algorithm (Williams & Zipser, 1989), where it is provided the
195   correct clan annotation for each residue in order to mitigate error propagation.
196
197   The clan and family models follow a similar structure and are trained separately. Protein sequences
198
      are initially embedded at the residue level using a pre-trained and frozen instance of ESM-2. The
      resulting embeddings are then passed into a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (BiLSTM) layer
199
      to capture sequential dependencies (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) in the forwards and backwards
200
      directions, each of which, like profile HMMs, are strongly linear, left-right models (Eddy, 1998). The
201   choice of BiLSTM was made deliberately to introduce as few changes as possible, ensuring that the
202   observed performance improvements could be attributed primarily to the use of the protein language
203   model, rather than architectural differences from profile HMMs. The output from the BiLSTM layer
204   is subsequently decoded using a stack of three dense layers, scaled to the number of clans or family
205   labels, to produce logits across the prediction space. Probabilities are computed by applying softmax
206   to the logits generated by each model.

231   4.1     B ENCHMARK C REATION
232
233   We begin by collecting the 1.2M “seed domains” from Pfam-A Seed 35.0, a set of curated, represen-
234   tative domains for each domain family that are used to build the 20K Pfam profile HMMs (Mistry
235   et al., 2021). We apply BLUE (Petti & Eddy, 2022), a graph-based sequence splitting algorithm,
236
      to partition the seed domains into preliminary training and test sets, defining an edge between two
      domains as their pairwise percent identity (Appendix A.1.1). We use a PID threshold of 25% to split
237
      the seed domains, resulting in 560K training domains and 190K test domains. The remaining 450K
238
      seed domains were discarded due to sharing > 25% PID with both test and training sets. We then
239   retrieve the full-length sequences corresponding to these representative training and test domains
240   from UniProt release March 2021, a comprehensive database of 230M protein sequences. This results
241   in 517K training sequences. From the test set, we eliminate duplicate sequences also present in the
242   training set. All sequences across both training and test sets are annotated via the hmmscan tool
243   from HMMER (Eddy, 2011) with strict inclusion thresholds (E-value < 0.001, bitscore ≥ 30) in
244   order to identify domain hits that constitute a “ground truth”, with special care to nested, contiguous
245   domains, which may escape typical processing methods (Appendix A.1.2). For training and test
246   purposes, family and clan labels are only assigned to ground truth domains. We discard sequences
247
      in test that do not contain an annotated ground truth domain represented by at least 20 ground truth
      domains from the same family in the training set, resulting in 73K test sequences. For each test
248
      sequence, we compute the maximum PID between any of its domains to any domain in the training
249
      set as a proxy for its distance from the training set (Appendix A.1.3). We partition the test set into five
250   subsets based on this maximum PID (Table 1), and a total of 6K validation sequences are sampled
251   uniformly across the test subsets. Such a partition may result in test sequences that, for example, may
252   be placed in the 80 < PID ≤ 100 subset due to a single domain closely related to one in the training
253   set, whereas the test sequence may have several other domains that share significantly lower PID
254   with domains in the training set (this is why the average PID is near the lower bound of the max PID
255   range for many of the test subsets in Table 1). The domain coverage, defined as the average percent
256   of residues in a sequence that are labeled by Pfam domains, is similar across all test subsets.
257
258   4.2     A DDRESSING P OSSIBLE L EAKAGE
259
260   We address the potential for unannotated domains to introduce data leakage across the training and
261
      test sets by shuffling all subsequences without family and clan labels in the test sequences to disrupt
      possible domain structures, preserving 0th order residue-composition (Pearson, 2013; Eddy, 2011).
262
      The ground truth for protein sequence annotation is fundamentally unknown, relying on inference
263
      rather than complete structural and evolutionary knowledge – this is why we must assume natrual
264   sequences contain unannotated true domains that new methods may discover. Since PSALM may be
265   sensitive enough to identify unannotated domains, it is trained with these regions shuffled, to mitigate
266   penalties for “false positives” (with respect to the ground truth annotations). Another source of data
267   leakage may arise from the millions of representative sequences from the UniRef50 database release
268   April 2021 (Suzek et al., 2015) used to train ESM-2. We identify that none of the 4,087 sequences in
269   the 0 < PID ≤ 20 test subset were present as representative sequences in this version of UniRef50.
      However, UniRef50 may contain close homologs to the sequences in this test subset.

283   5.1   BASELINES
284
285   We establish two baseline methods for comparison. We use HMMER, the current state-of-the-art
286   protein sequence annotation method, to build profile HMMs from MSAs of the ground truth domains
287
      in the training set, denoted as HMMER*, and use these profiles to annotate the test sequences
      with hmmscan. This allows us to evaluate how a state-of-the-art profile HMM method compares
288
      to PSALM when using the same training and testing sets. Additionally, we implement a variant
289
      of PSALM, denoted as PSALMOH , where one-hot embeddings for each amino acid in a protein
290   sequence are utilized instead of embeddings from the pre-trained protein language model ESM-2.
291   This comparison helps discern whether differences in performance between PSALM and HMMER*
292   are influenced by ESM-2 or solely by the subsequent neural network architecture. Additionally, we
293   assess the scalability of PSALM by evaluating performance across different ESM-2 model sizes (8M,
294   35M, 150M, and 650M parameters).
295
      More recent deep learning approaches (e.g., ProtENN) are not included as baselines because they
296   address sequence-level or single-domain classification, which is a different problem that is largely
297   irrelevant to domain- or residue-level annotation. We discuss the difference between the two in
298   our related work section (Section 2.2). Biologists prefer domain-level annotation for many reasons,
299   including avoiding the “transitive catastrophe” (lines 27-33), where unrelated sequences cluster
300   through homologous domains (e.g., protein AB shares homology with BC, BC with CD, and all three
301   cluster; but AB shares no homology with CD). Biologists rely on extensive domain-level annotation
302   resources built on a state of the art of profile HMMs.

      (Kingma & Ba, 2014) with initial learning rate 5e−4 for the clan model and 5e−5 for the family
308
      model. These values were selected via hyperparameter tuning from across the following learning
309   rates: [1e−3, 5e−4, 1e−4, 5e−5, 1e−5]. A similar hyperparameter search results in a learning rate
310   of 1e−4 for the PSALMOH clan model and 1e−5 for√the family model. We employ a learning rate
311   scheduler that reduces the learning rate by a factor of 10 if the validation loss fails to decrease over
312   consecutive epochs with an additional early stopping criterion of 5 epochs with no improvement. The
313   effective batch size is 32,768 tokens.
314

315

368   We highlight the key observations from the sequence annotation benchmark (Table 3). PSALM650

472   7    C ONCLUSIONS
473
474   We introduce PSALM, a highly sensitive and specific pLM-based protein sequence annotation
475   method. PSALM extends the capabilities of self-supervised pLMs with just a few hundred thousand
476   protein sequences, enabling interpretable residue-level annotations at both the clan and family levels.
477   Comparisons with InterPro show PSALM’s ability to detect multiple domains, including those
478   currently unannotated. Ablation experiments confirm the importance of pLM embeddings over
479   one-hot encodings and the importance of clan annotations in achieving higher family-level sensitivity
480
      and specificity. We find that PSALM performance improves with larger ESM-2 models. We also
      introduce MDPH-Bench, the first protein sequence benchmark that splits training and test sequences
481
      by domain-level sequence similarity for multi-domain proteins. This benchmark minimizes data
482
      leakage and enables model evaluation across an evolutionarily-diverse set of proteins.

      8     L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
493
      We address the current limitations and potential future research directions of this approach with the
494
      following points.
495
496
      8.1   DATA L EAKAGE
497
498   Despite our efforts to mitigate it, information from the test set may still contribute to training through
499   the millions of sequences used to train ESM-2. While we exclude sequences used to train ESM-2
500   from our test subset with the lowest maximum PID, homology could still lead to indirect leakage.
501   However, our maximum PID guarantees help minimize this risk. Ideally, retraining ESM-2 on our
502   training data would further alleviate this concern. We have not done this in the present work because
503   of the compute demand for training a pLM like ESM-2, but we hope to rigorously split a much larger
504   set of proteins to train a pLM from scratch.
505
506   8.2   D OMAIN C ALLING
507
      PSALM cannot distinguish between repeated domains occurring consecutively or accurately resolve
508
      split domains. For example, if a domain repeats immediately after itself, PSALM labels the entire
509
      two domain block instead of recognizing two separate domains within it. Similarly, when a domain
510   is split, PSALM identifies the two halves as separate domains from the same family, rather than
511   as originating from a single domain. We aim to address this by explicitly modeling the domain
512   boundaries and developing domain-calling algorithms.
